Abstract
The Fr´echet distance is a central measure for comparing curves.
Our main
result relates the Fr´echet distance for two paths in a 3D Grid graph to the
Orthogonal Vectors problem. We show that, if no algorithm with strongly sub-
quadratic runtime exists to solve the Orthogonal Vectors problem, then no (1 +
10−4) approximative algorithm with runtime O(n
4
3 −δ) exists for calculating the
discrete Fr´echet distance for two paths in a 3D Grid graph for δ > 0.
2
Introduction
The Fr´echet distance is a widely recognised measure of similarity between two
curves or trajectories. Conceptually, it can be understood as the shortest leash
length needed to connect a human walking along one path and a dog walking
along the other path, with both moving forward without backtracking. In con-
trast to other distance measures, such as the Hausdorff distance, the Fr´echet
distance considers the sequence of points, making it suitable for many natu-
ral settings where the order of the data matters. The Fr´echet distance is used
in various domains, including signature verification[1], the study of coastline
retrievals[2], the study of protein structures [3], and is widely used in the anal-
ysis of trajectories of moving objects such as vehicles, animals, and athletes [4]
[5] [6] [7] [8] [9] [10].
The study of the Fr´echet distance in computational geometry was introduced
by Alt and Godau [11] [12], who provided the first algorithmic analysis of the
problem. They modelled two trajectories as polygonal curves with n and m
vertices respectively, and presented an algorithm that computes the continuous
Fr´echet distance in O(mn log(n+m)) time. Since then, improvements have been
made: Buchin et al. [13] present a slightly better algorithm. The runtime of the
algorithm is, however, still super-quadratic. Building on this line of research,
more efficient algorithms have been studied for realistic curve input[14] [15] [16].
In this thesis, we consider the discrete Fr´echet distance, where trajectories are
modelled as sequences of discrete points. This variant was introduced initially
in [17], which presents an algorithm with runtime O(mn).
1
==PAGE:5==
In 2014, Bringmann [18] presented a conditional lower bound for calculating
the Fr´echet distance for curves in the plane. Assuming the Strong-Exponential
Time Hypothesis, they showed that no (1 + 10−3) approximative algorithm
can compute the Fr´echet distance between two curves in strongly sub-quadratic
time. Here, strongly sub-quadratic refers to a runtime of O(n2−δ) for δ > 0.
Bringmann shows the same lower bound for the continuous and the discrete
case. Since then, conditional lower bounds have been presented for other vari-
ants and extensions.
Bunic, Ophelders and Speckmann [19] showed that no
3-approximative algorithms with a strongly sub-quadratic run time can com-
pute the Fr´echet distance between curves in one dimension unless the Strong-
Exponential Time Hypothesis fails.
Driemel, van der Hoog, and Rotenberg [20] introduced Fr´echet distance in
the context of graphs. In this variant, the input consists of a weighted graph
G, where trajectories are represented as walks along the graph. The distance
between any two vertices is defined as the length of the shortest path connecting
them. Van der Hoog, van der Horst, Rotenberg and Wulf [21] show that the
discrete Fr´echet distance of two disjoint simple paths in an unweighted planar
graph can not be approximated better than a factor 1.25 in sub-quadratic time,
unless the Orthogonal Vector Hypothesis fails. However, they also show that, if
the graph is a regular tiling, with unit-weighted edges, such as a grid graph, then
there exists an ˜O((n + m)1.5)-time algorithm to compute the discrete Fr´echet
distance. This motivates the exploration of conditional lower bounds for the
Fr´echet distance for paths in grid graphs. A dataset such as satellite images of
a coastline, where each pixel is labelled as sea or shore, could lead to trajectories
in a grid graph.
Our contribution Our main result relates the Fr´echet distance for paths
in a 3D Grid graph to the Orthogonal Vectors problem. We show that, if no
algorithm with strongly sub-quadratic runtime exists to solve the Orthogonal
Vectors problem, then no (1 + 10−4) approximative algorithm with runtime
O(n
4
3 −δ) exists for calculating the discrete Fr´echet distance for paths in a 3D
Grid graph for δ > 0.
2.1
Fr´echet distance in a Grid Graph
In graph theory, the infinite 2D grid graph is the graph G = (V, E), where V =
Z2 is the set of all integer lattice points in the plane and E = {{(x, y), (x′, y′)} ∈
V × V | |x −x′| + |y −y′| = 1}.
The infinite 3D grid graph is the graph
G = (V, E), where V = Z3, is the set of all integer lattice points in 3D space
and E = {{(x, y, z), (x′, y′, z′)} ∈V × V | |x −x′| + |y −y′| + |z −z′| = 1}.
Let G = (V, E) be an infinite grid graph, and let P = (p0, p1, p2, . . . , pm)
and ˆP = (ˆp0, ˆp1, ˆp2, . . . , ˆpn) be two simple paths on G, where each element in P
and ˆP is a vertex in V and consecutive vertices are connected by an edge in G.
The discrete Fr´echet distance between P and ˆP is defined as:

and ending at (pm, ˆpn). For any p, ˆp let dG(p, ˆp) denote the length of the short-
est path in G between p and ˆp. If G is a grid graph then dG(p, ˆp) is simply the
Manhattan distance dM(p, ˆp).
Fr´echet distance between two paths is often illustrated through the analogy
of a human walking a dog. Imagine a human walking along one path and a dog
walking along another. Both start at their respective starting points and move
forward without backtracking. The Fr´echet distance corresponds to the length
of the shortest leash required so that both can walk from start to finish while
staying connected by the leash. When working in the grid graph, the length
of the leash connecting the dog and the human should be thought of as the
Manhattan distance between the dog and the human.
The paired sequence σ will be referred to as a traversal of P and ˆP, where
each pair (p, ˆp) ∈σ will be referred to as a state of the traversal. The distance
of a state refers to the distance dG(p, ˆp). The time of a state refers to the rank
of the state in the paired sequence σ. An example is shown in figure 1

2.2
Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis
The Exponential Time Hypothesis, ETH, and the Strong Exponential Time
Hypothesis, SETH, introduced by Impagliazzo, Paturi, and Zane [22][23], are
fundamental assumptions used to derive conditional lower bounds in complexity
theory. ETH states that there is no 2o(N)-time algorithm for solving 3-SAT,
where N denotes the number of variables.
SETH strengthens ETH by stating that there is no constant δ > 0 such
that k-SAT can be solved in time O((2 −δ)N) for all k. Informally, SETH
asserts that satisfiability cannot be solved faster than exhaustive search, even
when the clause width is bounded. Since SETH defines the exact exponent in
contrast to ETH, it is easier to work with when showing polynomial time lower
bounds.
3
==PAGE:7==
SETH’ is a more flexible variant of SETH, which applies to general CNF-SAT
instances, where the clause width is unbounded. SETH’ states there is no al-
gorithm for CNF-SAT running in time O∗((2 −δ)N) for any δ > 0 where O∗
hides factors polynomial in the number of variables N and the number of clauses
M. Because SETH’ applies to CNF-SAT instances without restricting clause
width, it is strictly weaker than SETH and is thereby considered more plausible.
However, both SETH and SETH’ are widely regarded as unlikely to fail.
2.3
Orthogonal Vectors Hypothesis
The Orthogonal Vectors problem is defined as:
Orthogonal Vectors problem: Given two sets of bitvectors V, ˆV ⊆
{0, 1}d of each N vectors, determine if there exists a pair of bitvectors v ∈V,
ˆv ∈ˆV such that v is orthogonal to ˆv. This will be denoted v ⊥ˆv. Call V and ˆV
a Yes-instance if there exist a pair of vectors v ∈V, ˆv ∈ˆV such that v ⊥ˆv.
The Orthogonal Vectors problem can not be solved in strongly sub-quadratic
time unless SETH’ fails [24]. More precisely, the Orthogonal Vector Hypothe-
sis, OVH, states:
OVH: There is no algorithm for solving the Orthogonal Vectors problem
with runtime O(N 2−δdO(1)) for any δ > 0.
Many lower bounds for different variations of calculating Fr´echet distances
are related to SETH by going through OVH. The relation between SETH,
SETH’ and OVH is shown in figure 2.
Bringmann [18] presents a construction of two polygonal curves in R2, given
an instance of CNF-SAT. The construction shows that there does not exist
an (1 + 10−3) approximate algorithm with runtime O(n2−δ) to calculate the
(continuous or discrete) Fr´echet distance for any δ > 0, unless SETH’ fails.
Here n denotes the length of the curves. The reduction we present is similar

presented by Bringmann, we will phrase section 2.5 as if Bringmann’s reduction
were based on OVH rather than SETH’.
2.4
Main result
Our main result relates the Fr´echet distance for two paths in a 3D Grid graph
to OVH:
Theorem 2.1. There exists no (1+10−4) approximative algorithm with runtime
O(n
4
3 −δ) for computing the discrete Fr´echet distance for two paths in a 3D Grid
graph for any δ > 0, unless OVH fails.
Given two sets V, ˆV ⊆{0, 1}d of each N vectors, we prove theorem 2.1 by
constructing two paths P and ˆP in a 3D Grid graph. The paths are constructed
such that the Fr´echet distance between P and ˆP determines whether there exists
a pair of vectors v ∈V, ˆv ∈ˆV such that v ⊥ˆv.
2.5
Intuition for the conditional lower bound
In this section, we provide a brief explanation of why the conditional lower
bound applies to paths in the 3D grid graph instead of the 2D grid graph.
Furthermore, we will give intuition for why our construction only excludes an
algorithm of runtime O(n
4
3 −δ) for δ > 0.
Bringmann [18] presents a construction of two curves in R2, given an instance
of the Orthogonal Vectors problem, such that the Fr´echet distance be-
tween the curves determines whether or not there exists a pair of orthogonal
vectors. Bringmann introduces two sets of vertices in the plane, which are used
to construct the two curves.
The pairwise distance between any two points
in this set is constant. Each of the two constructed curves may traverse ev-
ery vertex O(Nd) times, and is hence self-intersecting.
Here N denotes the
number of bitvectors and d denotes the dimension of each bitvector from the
Orthogonal Vectors problem instance. The curves visit O(d) vertices per
bit vector, so the length of the two curves generated is O(Nd).

there exists a unused path to an unvisited vertex l ∈Lq for every time we
wish to visits a vertex q in Bringmann’s construction (see figure 3). We will
refer to q as a centroid of Lq as the set of vertices Lq is placed around the point q.

Next, we consider whether it is possible to arrange these sets of vertices in
the plane in a meaningful way. Suppose the set of vertices Lq can be contained
in a square of side length ϕ. Hence, any two vertices in Lq are within distance
O(ϕ) of each other. To maintain the property that vertices in Lq remain roughly
equidistant to the vertices in Lq′, adding or subtracting O(ϕ) to their pairwise
distances should be redundant. The distance between the centroids q and q′
should therefore be Ω(ϕ) (see Figure 4). To achieve this, we scale the whole
construction by a factor Ω(ϕ). Suppose Bringmann uses a curve segment from
the centroid q to the centroid q′ in their construction. The length of the curve
segments is O(1) as it simply consists of the two points q and q′. Say we want
to mimic the same curve segment in our construction. The path will no longer
go from centroid to centroid but from a vertex near one centroid, l ∈Lq, to a
vertex near the other centroid, l′ ∈Lq′. As the entire construction has been
scaled by a factor Ω(ϕ), the points l and l′ are more than a unit distance apart.
Therefore, we need to connect them with a path in Z2. The length of such a
path will be Ω(ϕ). Scaling is necessary to ensure that the paths we construct do
not overlap. However, when scaling the construction, we also scale the length
of the paths we create. Excessive scaling of the construction will ultimately
result in a weaker conditional lower bound. We therefore need to determine an
appropriate scaling factor for the construction.

3.2.1
Intuition for placement of points in Q and ˆQ
To get a better intuition of the placement of the centroid points and the function
of each point, we will give a rough sketch of the construction of P and ˆP, and
state some of the main properties of the centroid points. Say a dog is walking
on the path ˆP and a human is walking on P. Whenever we say a point near a
centroid point q, we mean a point in Lq.
Each bitvector v ∈V and bitvector ˆv ∈ˆV will contribute to a subpath in
P and ˆP, respectively. The points near the centroids B(0), B(1) and w will be

used to create subpaths corresponding to bitvectors in V. The human will visit
a point near B(0) or B(1), depending on each bit in the bitvector v, and visit a
point near the point w between each consecutive bit. The human will start each
of these subpaths at a point near r. Intuitively, one may imagine the human
reading the full bitvector v when standing close to r, and writing bits one by
one when near w. The dog interacts with points near the centroid points ˆB(0),
ˆB(1), ˆw and ˆr in a similar manner for each bitvector in ˆV.
The placement of the points B(0), B(1), ˆB(0), and ˆB(1) ensures that the

staying within a distance ∆+ ϕ + 4 of one another, if and only if the corre-
sponding bitvectors are orthogonal. The whole construction of P and ˆP will
force the human and the dog to traverse one pair of subpaths corresponding to
a pair of bitvectors, to stay within distance ∆+ϕ+4 of each other. The human
and the dog can thereby only traverse the whole of P and ˆP while remaining
within distance ∆+ϕ+4 of each other if there exists a pair of orthogonal vectors.
If there exists a pair of orthogonal vectors, the dog and the human can line
up the corresponding subpaths so they can traverse them in unison. To do so,
they must traverse the paths of P and ˆP strategically, by waiting in one place
while the other traverses a section of their path. In particular, the human can
traverse any point in P while the dog is standing close to ˆs or ˆt. The dog can
traverse most of ˆP while the human is standing close to the points s or t. In the
human walking a dog analogy, these points can be thought of as park benches,
where either the human or the dog can sit on a bench, whilst the other traverses
a large number of subpaths. The human will have the opportunity to rest at a
bench in between each subpath corresponding to a bitvector, whereas the dog
only has the opportunity to rest at a bench before traversing any of the subpaths
and after completing all of them. This will be used to ensure that the dog and
the human can align their movements to get to a state where both the dog and
the human are standing at the beginning of their respective subpaths encoding
orthogonal vectors.

remaining a distance of ∆+ ϕ + 4 if there exists a pair of orthogonal vectors.

paths P and ˆP has been shown.

4.1.1
Types of Traversals
We will now introduce three different kinds of traversals. Both the Fixed vertex
traversal and the Inside Lq traversal are subcases of the Unison Traversal or a
sequence of Unison Traversals. We have introduced three names, so hopefully
it becomes more apparent to the reader what each traversal does. In the Fixed
vertex traversal, either the dog or the human stands still in one vertex while
the other traverses a subpath. In the Unison traversal, both the dog and the
human walk simultaneously to traverse each of their subpaths. For the Inside
Lq traversal, the dog and the human are allowed to perform any sequence of
Unison Traversals as long as they both stay inside each of their set of vertices
Lq or Lˆq.

5.1
Future work
5.1.1
Gap between lower bound and upper bound
Van der Hoog, van der Horst, Rotenberg and Wulf [21] present an algorithm
for calculating the Fr´echet distance between two paths in the 2D grid graph
with a runtime of ˜O((n + m)1.5). Unfortunately, the conditional lower bound
we establish fails to match both the runtime and the dimensionality of the grid
graph. So far, there is no non-trivial conditional lower bound for calculating the
Fr´echet distance between two paths in the 2D grid graph, nor a strongly sub-
quadratic algorithm for calculating the Fr´echet distance between two paths in
the 3D grid. Consequently, it remains unknown whether a tighter lower bound
exists for computing the Fr´echet distance in either the 3D grid or the 2D graph,
or whether the algorithms can be improved. The techniques presented in [21]
may apply to the case of the 3D grid graph, potentially resulting in a strongly
sub-quadratic algorithm for calculating the Fr´echet distance between two paths
in the 3D grid. Such an approach would most likely lead to a running time
exceeding that of ˜O((n + m)1.5), which consequently fails to close the gap with
the lower bound established in this work.
5.1.2
Starting from a 1D construction
Say we no longer constrain the paths to be non-self-intersecting: In other words,
instead of working with paths, we now consider curves in the Grid graph, then
the lower bound presented by Bunic, Ophelders and Speckmann [19] naturally
applies. They showed that no 3-approximative algorithms with strongly sub-
quadratic running time can compute the Fr´echet distance between curves in
one dimension unless the Strong-Exponential Time Hypothesis fails.
In this
project, we managed to take a construction for curves in R2 and scale it to
embed simple paths in the 3D grid graph. It might be possible to embed a
scaled version of their 1D construction to construct non-self-intersecting paths
in the 2D grid graph. However, this approach undoubtedly introduces its own
set of challenges.

5.1.3
Conditional lower bound for c-packed curves in 3D gridgraph
To enable efficient computation of Fr´echet distance, it is often helpful to re-
strict the input to well-behaved classes curves. One widely studied model of
that is c-packed curves. A polygonal curve P in Rd is said to be c-packed if,
for any point x ∈Rd and any radius r > 0, the total length of P contained
within the ball B(x, r) is at most c·r. In other words, c-packedness bounds how
much the curve can fold or wiggle within any local region, preventing arbitrarily
dense self-intersections. This model captures many realistic trajectories, such
as movement paths or road networks, while excluding highly oscillatory inputs.
The paths we construct are clearly not c-packed for a constant factor c. A ball
