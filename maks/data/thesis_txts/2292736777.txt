Abstract 
 
One of the most challenging issues in every real estate organization is the 
transfer and utilization of the knowledge that derives from the building operation of 
previous constructions, to the new construction projects. According to the literature, it 
is of great importance that the organizational knowledge, which can be divided into 
explicit and tacit knowledge, after created or acquired, to be codified and transferred, 
in order to be utilized by the organization in future projects. In a real estate 
organization, this knowledge arises from the units that are responsible for the 
operation and the maintenance of the facilities. Although the use of this operation and 
maintenance knowledge can assist the project management of a new project, there are 
multiple barriers that prevent its integration. Over the last years several tools and 
processes have been developed in order to facilitate this knowledge transfer as well as 
the intradepartmental knowledge sharing, however it is apparent that the best results 
can be achieved only if the different actors of a construction project collaborate 
aiming to the objectives. 
The case company analyzed in this master thesis is DTU Campus Service, the 
facilities management organization of the Technical University of Denmark. DTU 
Campus Service seems to be aware of the importance of sharing and transferring their 
organizational knowledge. The last five years, the organization has developed 
different tools and has adopted several processes, so that they can achieve a high level 
of integration of the knowledge that they possess from all the years of operation and 
maintenance of the existing buildings. However, there are many cases where the tools 
==PAGE:8==
ii 
 
and processes do not work efficiently, and therefore the knowledge transfer is not 
effective enough. In order to improve the effectiveness of the knowledge transfer in 
DTU Campus Service, the POKI (process, organization, knowledge, information) 
management tool, which has been developed by a group of experienced individuals 
that have worked in the Danish construction and facilities management industry, has 
been used in this thesis. This tool has been used as the main framework for analyzing 
and discussing the case, on which the implementation of specific changes or additions 
has been recommended.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
 
1.1   Problem Statement 
This thesis is dealing with a challenging topic within the Construction Industry 
and the Knowledge Management (KM) discipline. The issue that is examined in this 
thesis is:  
‘Knowledge Transfer between Building Operation and Project Management in 
Construction Projects. 
Although it could be assumed that in this case Knowledge Transfer (KT) can 
be approached in the same way as it is in other kind of industries, the nature of the 
construction industry makes it difficult and in some cases even impossible. 
Construction firms move from one building project to another, which usually differs 
substantially from the previous one. The lack of distinct similarities between these 
building projects makes the project management team more reluctant to consider, 
extract and reuse knowledge that has been acquired during past projects. In some 
instances, the project team of a building project is not even aware of the benefits that 
can emerge from the fully utilization of the knowledge that has been generated during 
the building operation of previous projects, as they are not involved in the life cycle of 
the building. 
Taking this into consideration, it is important to comprehend and analyze 
different aspects of this topic, thus the following sub-questions can be answered: 
==PAGE:24==
 
2 
 
 Which kind of knowledge does derive from a building project and how can be 
stored and transferred in order to be used in a future project? 
 Which are the benefits of KT in building projects and which are the 
limitations of successful KT? 
 Which existing tools and frameworks facilitate the effective and efficient KT? 
 
In order to understand better how KT between building operations and project 
management works, and answer to these questions in a realistic way, a case from 
DTU Campus Service (CAS) has been researched. Examining DTU CAS’ 
intradepartmental knowledge sharing (KS), as well as the KT between the building 
operation and maintenance (O&M) sections and the construction project managers, 
this thesis aims to clarify the benefits that occur from it and the degree to which the 
participants are aware of it, the barriers that are preventing KT between these two 
parties, as well as the KS and KT tools and processes that have been developed and is 
being used within the organization. 
 
1.2   Research Methodology  
The methodological approach that was used during the research was divided 
into three different stages. 
In the first stage, the main focus was given on a broad literature review sub-
divided into three parts. Initially the field of KM was examined thoroughly, giving 
weight to the aspects that are useful and assist the understanding of the importance of 
KM within an organization. Secondly, research regarding KS and KT in construction 
industry and particularly in KT between facilities management (FM) and building 
design was conducted, even though the available literature is rather limited. To end 
with, literature with respect to KT in other kind of fields and industries, such as the 
educational sector and the offshore drilling industry, was reviewed. 
The second stage focused on the data collection, using semi-structured 
interviews with people of DTU CAS. As the research is qualitative, interviews were 
considered to be the most suitable method of data collection. Having developed an 
interview guide with different topics and questions based on the literature review, 
which were expected to be covered during the interviews, the conversation was 
mainly led by the interviewee, while ensuring that all topics were covered. This type 
of interview had as a result the generation of additional questions, which were

1.3   Objectives 
The objectives that have been set in the beginning of this project are the study 
and deep understanding of the KM theory, focusing on the KS and KT processes. 
Moreover, how KT processes are implemented in the construction industry and which 
are the benefits of it, as well as the barriers that are usually confronted during this 
process.  
Coming after, a comprehensive examination of DTU CAS KS and KT 
processes has been aimed. This analysis attempts to give a description of how DTU 
CAS approaches KS and KT and up to which level they have integrated them into 
their organizational culture. Having comprehended these processes, this thesis aims at 
using the POKI management tool as a framework, in order to provide some 
recommendations that could increase the KT and optimize the outcome of the 
activities used at the moment.   
 
1.4   Structure of the thesis 
To understand the importance and the benefits that successful KS can bring to 
a building project, it is essential to understand its significance for any kind 
organization, regardless the industry they belong into. Therefore, the second chapter is 
describing thoroughly the fundamentals of KM theory as it has been portrayed in the 
literature. 
The third chapter examines closer the construction industry and the significant 
benefits that can arise from sharing the knowledge that has been gained from previous 
building projects, as well as the most usual barriers that prevent the successful KT and 
techniques, mechanisms and frameworks that have been developed and used so far.    
Following, the fourth chapter introduces the case company, DTU CAS. After a 
description of the company, a discussion about the KS activities that take place in 
DTU CAS follows. In order to understand better the KS attitude within the sections 
that are examined, it is also essential to have a look into the intradepartmental KS 
activities of Facilities Maintenance and Projects department, and specifically of the 
Buildings Maintenance section, as well as the Real Estate PMO department and the 
project teams.  
The fifth chapter focuses on the KT processes between the Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects department, and the project leaders from Real Estate PMO

Finally, the seventh chapter provides a summary of the literature, the case

Chapter 2 
Knowledge Management 
 
2.1   Knowledge Management 
The creation, the acquirement and the communication of knowledge, in order 
to be reused in the future is an issue that has concerned humanity for centuries. 
However, the field that started investigating extensively how knowledge can be 
created and utilized effectively, emerged only during the last decades. 
Knowledge Management is a relatively new management field, established on 
the argument that it is a challenging task for an organization to fully utilize the 
knowledge that they create or possess. The amounts of data and information that an 
organization generates during its operation are large, and their effective management 
can assist the organization to recognize and detect the appropriate information that 
can be reused in a future case. Moreover, the continuous and progressive changes and 
novelties in production and information technologies constitute a threat for the 
survival and the prosperity of an organization and thus, necessitate the use of 
intellectual capital along with the building of strong professional competences of the 
employees (Milner 2001). Figure 2.1 illustrates the KM process model, which’s main 
functions are analyzed thoroughly later in this chapter. 
The intellectual capital is one of the most important assets of an organization. 
Either it is individual knowledge or collective knowledge that is coming from 
previous experiences; the successful synergy between the human, intellectual and 
physical resources of an organization can maximize its business performance and 
==PAGE:30==
 
8 
 
enables them to pursue their organizational goals. Therefore, as Dubey and Kalwale 
(2010) mention, KM assists the organization to compete successfully within its 
environment by mapping its intellectual assets, capturing or creating knowledge that 
will sustain and strengthen its competitive advantage, and sharing and exploiting large 
amounts of business knowledge. As Taylor (Taylor cited in Dubey & Kalwale 2010) 
suggests, KM enables an organization to move from a knowledge-chaotic 
environment to a knowledge-centric enterprise, supported by a comprehensive 
knowledge system, through a five-stage process: Knowledge-Chaotic, Knowledge-
Aware, Knowledge-Enabled, Knowledge-Managed and Knowledge-Centric.   
 
 
Figure 2.1 - KM process model (King 2009) 
 
The information revolution was one of the crucial reasons why the access to 
knowledge became possible. However, it is of great importance the discrimination 
between data, information and knowledge. According to Fleming (Fleming cited in 
Dubey & Kalwale 2010), the collection of data is not information, as well as, the 
collection of information is not knowledge. That means that the collection of data and 
information are not usable knowledge, but just collections. Information can be 
portrayed as the transformation of data, while knowledge as the transformation of 
information. Therefore, KM can be described as the strategy that aims to the 
development of organizational knowledge through the accumulation of organizational 
data and information, along with past experience derived from the human resources 
(Dubey & Kalwale 2010). 
Nowadays, both the importance of KM and its benefits are recognized from 
the business community. KM has been accepted as a useful tool that can manage the 
intellectual capital of different types of organizations and enhance the effectiveness of 
their operations while boosting their performance. Figure 2.2 illustrates the 
revolutionary changes in technologies at the end of the twentieth century and the 
beginning of the twenty-first century, and how these changes have influenced the 
management activity by investing on organizational KM.  
 
==PAGE:31==
 
9 
 
 
Figure 2.2 – Technological changes and organizational knowledge (Milner 2001) 
 
The main benefits KM can bring to an organization, concern processes, 
employees, customers, finance and innovation. It ensures the acceleration of the 
processes, as the redundant activities are minimized with the utilization of previous 
experiences; builds up employees’ competencies as mentioned above; increases the 
customer satisfaction, as the quality of products and services offered is increased; 
supports financially the organization with the mitigation of risk, the reduced 
administration costs and the increased turnover; and finally, promotes the expansion 
of scientific research and innovation, with the development of new products and 
services, and the introduction and application of new technologies (North et al. 2004). 
Among these benefits, it should be mentioned that KM supports a more efficient and 
better substantiated decision-making process, and enables an adequate management of 
the constant changes within the dynamic environment of the organization.  
 
2.2   Organizational Knowledge 
As aforementioned, the organizational knowledge can be described as the 
transformation of the internal information collected from previous organizational 
processes (collective knowledge) or personal experiences and competences of the 
employees (individual knowledge), and it is considered to be an intellectual asset for a 
company.  
==PAGE:32==
 
10 
 
There are different kinds of knowledge categorization in the literature. One of 
them, established by Lundvall (2004), is the separation of knowledge into four types: 
‘know-what’, ‘know-why’, ‘know-how’ and ‘know-who’. ‘Know-what’ is related to 
information, as it has to do with ‘facts’ and it can be located in fragmented forms and 
shared as data. ‘Know-why’ is about the background basic knowledge, such as 
principles or laws of motion in nature, in human and in society, which is extremely 
important to technological achievements. ‘Know-how’ refers to skills and 
competences of individuals within an organization or a research team, which are 
fundamental for the creation of networks, where ‘know-how’ elements are shared or 
combined. Finally, ‘know-who’, which nowadays becomes of great importance, refers 
to the knowledge regarding who knows what, and who knows what to do, and it can 
assist the allocation of the right tasks to the right people.  
A more general and more popular way to distinguish knowledge is into two 
fundamentally different categories; explicit and tacit. 
The explicit knowledge is useful technical knowledge, which comes from the 
employees and can be explained by them, sometimes with some effort or extra 
assistance, thus can be easily codified. Once codified and stored, this explicit 
knowledge can be easily scattered within the organization and be communicated and 
available for future use. Therefore, it can either assist the organization to deal with 
problems that have similar characteristics with those faced in the past, or enhance 
employees’ competences, through information retrieval systems, drawings, standard 
operational procedures, policies, specifications or manuals of best practice. For 
instance, forms of explicit knowledge are templates, patents, reports and copyrights.  
As tacit, is classified the knowledge of the individuals within the organization, 
which is inherently personal. Tacit knowledge is therefore associated with the ‘know-
who’ type of knowledge that was explained above. It is personal, which makes it 
difficult to be extracted out of the human minds, be formalized and be disclosed in 
manuals in order to be shared or transferred. Another difference with explicit 
knowledge is that tacit knowledge consists of two dimensions; the technical and the 
cognitive. While explicit knowledge has a solely technical dimension, tacit includes 
individual experience along with personal belief, perspective and values. This feature 
constitutes an obstacle to the transferability of the tacit knowledge.  
In many cases, the significance and the usefulness of tacit knowledge is being 
neglected, and explicit is the only accepted knowledge. This might happen because 
explicit knowledge is easier to be accessed, collected and codified, which simplifies 
the utilization from technical and science based sectors. However, even in these 
==PAGE:33==
 
11 
 
sectors, tacit knowledge can serve as a critical factor which is able to assist the 
enhancement of organization’s competitive advantage if it is taken into consideration 
(Lundvall 2004).  
 
2.3   Knowledge creation and acquisition 
Knowledge creation has been put under the microscope and examined by 
many researchers. There have been different approaches to learning and some of them 
link learning with the building of competences. The most important include learning 
by doing, which reflects the creation of experience-based knowledge during the 
production process; learning by using, which refers to the knowledge generated by the 
actual use and the observation of product’s performance; learning by interacting, 
which shows how interaction between producers and users can produce knowledge 
while developing new skills for both of them; learning by absorbing new scientific 
and technological achievements; and, finally, learning by querying the performance of 
other companies within the industry (Storgaard et al. 2010; Ahmed-Kristensen & 
Vianello 2015).   
Regardless this categorization of the different kinds of learning, knowledge 
creation and competences building are considered to be inadvertent side effects of 
production, use, marketing, or innovation processes.  
Knowledge creation has also been divided in two more general categories, 
which describe the nature of the knowledge creation. One consists of activities that 
take place in universities or other educational or R&D institutes, and therefore, they 
produce new knowledge without involving routines; while the other involves 
activities that are linked to everyday functions, and the produced knowledge 
constitutes by-product of production processes, representing learning by doing or 
learning by using (Lundvall 2004). 
 
2.3.1   Knowledge creation at organizational level 
There are different approaches regarding the creation and the transformation 
of explicit and tacit knowledge. Isaacs (Isaacs cited in Smith 2001) supports that the 
transformation of explicit knowledge into tacit knowledge cannot be achieved, while 
on the contrary, there are other points of view which support that if appropriate 
==PAGE:34==
 
12 
 
methods and techniques are used, tacit knowledge can be transformed into explicit 
knowledge and vice versa. 
Nonaka and Taleuchi (Nonaka & Taleuchi cited in Appel-Meulenbroek 2014) 
formulated different learning models in which they looked at knowledge creation and 
knowledge transformation between the two main types of knowledge - explicit 
knowledge and tacit knowledge. In a simple four field model (Figure 2.3), known as 
SECI (socialization, explication, combination, internalization) model, was obtained 
four spaces for creating knowledge. From Tacit knowledge to Tacit knowledge: 
Socialization; from Tacit to Explicit: Explication; from Explicit to Explicit: 
Combination; and from Explicit to Tacit knowledge: Internalization.  
 
 
Figure 2.3 – The SECI Model (Nonaka & Taleuchi cited in Appel-Meulenbroek 2014) 
 
From tacit to tacit  
In this step, the knowledge is not explicit and is created by socialization. 
Individuals learn from each other or from mentors, by observing, imitating and 
iterating their actions. In this step, valuable tools that can be used for the successful 
creation of knowledge are individuals’ imagination, as well as, visualization tools.  
 
==PAGE:35==
 
13 
 
From tacit to explicit 
The conversion of tacit knowledge into explicit knowledge, known also as 
explication, is based on the process of externalization. In this case, illustrations and 
other types of art can be of great use, as the aim of this step is to disclose, 
communicate and record a form of knowledge that is inexpressible, such as 
discussions, descriptions or innovations. 
 
From explicit to explicit 
Transforming one type of explicit knowledge to another can be achieved with 
the process of combination, a relatively easy process, where different parts of explicit 
knowledge are merged into a new explicit knowledge entity through factual 
propagation, like the use of data sources in order to write a financial report. 
 
From explicit to tacit 
This process of knowledge transformation, which supports the development of 
new individual’s tacit knowledge, using current explicit knowledge, is assisted by 
internalization.  Medialization is a tool being used to assist the comprehension and 
personalization of the existing explicit knowledge, which, if it is combined with 
individual’s unique tacit knowledge, can be implemented to expand or reframe an 
existing idea. 
Furthermore, SECI model supports that these types of knowledge 
transformations take place in a spiral process, where each time you run through the 
four stages will come to new levels. 
 
2.4   Knowledge codification  
As illustrated in Figure 2.1 (KM process model), the creation or acquisition of 
new knowledge is followed by its codification. Codification mechanisms ensure that 
the new knowledge becomes part of the organizational memory, expanding its 
reusability in order to deal with future issues similar to past as well as link individuals 
with beneficial knowledge (Smith 2001).   
Knowledge codification is an important part of the knowledge refinement 
process, which includes the techniques that extract, filter, clean and reform the new 
==PAGE:36==
 
14 
 
knowledge in order to enter the various knowledge repositories. These knowledge 
repositories hold both organizational knowledge and information, either in an 
electronic form, such as knowledge databases, or in a documented form. According to 
Davenport et al. (1998), the knowledge repositories can be divided into three types 
with regard to their context:  
 Repositories that store external knowledge, for instance about organization’s 
environment (competitive intelligence).  
 Repositories that store organized internal knowledge, such as research reports, 
product-oriented marketing data, as well as, practices and methodologies. 
 Repositories that store informal internal knowledge (explicit or tacit), such as 
discussion databases that contain organizational ‘know-how’.  
 
Among the KM approaches developed by Earl (Earl, cited in King 2009), 
there are four codification-oriented sub-strategies established empirically by 
observing KM processes of several organizations: systems, process, commercial and 
strategic. Systems is about building and formulating knowledge repositories and 
motivating individuals to supply them with organizational knowledge; process refers 
to the use of newly established repeatable operations assisted by organizational 
knowledge gained from past operations; commercial is related to the management of 
organization’s intellectual property, such as copyrights, patents, trademarks, etc.; and 
strategic refers to the establishment of a competitive strategy that is built on internal 
‘knowledge capabilities’. 
It was stated above that knowledge codification can be found in electronic or 
documented form. However, technology has a crucial role in the acquirement and 
codification of organizational knowledge, which makes the electronic document 
systems more popular, as they can store large amounts of organizational knowledge, 
allowing the smooth distribution and re-use of it. Therefore, it is of great importance 
to have robust information technology (IT) infrastructure to support both the 
codification and storage of the organizational knowledge. Nonetheless, the providers 
of the knowledge are the individuals and not IT itself; thus, the selection of the 
appropriate technology should be aligned with different organizational aspects. The 
organizational culture is the most important aspect as it is the one that affects internal 
communication and KS, with operational, technical and cost aspects being significant 
as well (Ettore, cited in Smith 2001).  
As it can be assumed, there is a substantial difference between the codification 
of tacit and explicit knowledge. Before being codified, tacit knowledge must be 
==PAGE:37==
 
15 
 
explicated. However, this is not always possible, as knowledge can be tacit because of 
lack of incentives but also because of its nature. For instance, it is difficult to 
explicate the knowledge that is linked to individuals’ ‘know-how’ (Cowan & Foray, 
cited in Lundvall 2004). On the other hand, explicit knowledge can be codified 
without any pre-process. After codified, both tacit and explicit knowledge need to be 
formatted appropriately and assessed, before become part of the organizational 
memory (King 2009). 
 
2.5   Knowledge transfer and knowledge sharing 
In order for knowledge to be spread and affect extensively the organization, it 
has to be either transferred or shared. KT and KS seem to be decisive parameters of 
the successful efforts regarding innovation and competitiveness. Although sometimes 
in the literature there is blurriness about the use of these two terms, the distinction 
between the two of them is necessary. 
According to King (King, cited in King 2009), in KT, the knowledge is 
communicated from a sender to a known receiver, having a specific focus and 
purpose. On the other hand, in KS, knowledge is exchanged among individuals, 
groups or organizational units, either being focused or not, but usually, KS does not 
serve any specific purpose (Paulin & Suneson 2012).  
Therefore, for the rest of this report, KS will refer to the knowledge that is 
shared between the participants of the same group, network or organizational unit, 
while KT will refer to the knowledge that is transferred from a group, a network or an 
organizational unit, to another. 
 
2.5.1   Knowledge sharing through networks 
There are different perspectives from which KS can be analyzed, such as KS 
through networks, between relevant participants in this field. KS through networks 
can be either within the same organizational unit or between different organizations.  
Having as primary function the exchange and the transfer, networks facilitate a 
more effective sharing of the created or acquired knowledge in an organization, in 
order to be reused and promote the creation of new knowledge. This effectiveness of 
the KS through networks examined by Inkpen and Tsang (Inkpen & Tsang cited in 
Attila 2012) and was summarized in three parameters: structural conditions (network 
==PAGE:38==
 
16 
 
links, network structure and network stability), cognitive conditions (common 
objectives and common culture) and relational conditions (trust).   
The importance of networks creation and further development is apparent, as 
the performance of an organization can be exponentially increased when the 
knowledge that is captured and shared comes from more resources. Examples of this 
are organizations that due to powerful network creation demonstrated superior 
performance with higher innovation and productivity levels. 
The parties that participate in the creation of a knowledge network comprise a 
knowledge community with a clear and precise goal. Most of the times, the 
knowledge groups are organized with respect to similarities and common interest 
objectives of the participants. Their strength and effectiveness are defined by the 
accumulated knowledge as well as the possibility to use it within the network (Attila 
2012).  
A crucial factor of the successful KS through networks is the existence of 
cooperative behaviour between the participants. Kahn (Kahn cited in Appel-
Meulenbroek 2014) distinguished cooperative behaviour into two main types; 
interaction and collaboration. According to him, “interaction adds structure to how 
departments interrelate” while “collaboration represents the unstructured, affective 
nature of interdepartmental relationships”. In other words, interaction describes a 
more formal kind of cooperation with routine activities, such as scheduled meetings 
and teleconferences, routine calls or standardized documentation. On the other hand, 
collaboration portrays a more informal process, characterized by mutual 
understanding between the different parties which work together sharing a common 
vision in order to achieve the same objective. 
The different nature of these two types of cooperation that takes place in KS 
networks makes interaction easier to quantify and assess and therefore to improve and 
make more efficient than collaboration, which is dependent of the participants’ 
behaviour and organization’s culture.  
Kahn (Kahn cited in Appel-Meulenbroek 2014), who evaluated both 
interaction and collaboration related to innovation, supported that the latter can 
provide greater benefits. This seems to be valid, as closer relations between the 
participants of the networks lead to extended KS. Kahn’s analysis outcome (Table 
2.1), were suggested actions and means of communication that can facilitate effective 
KS within the network.

2.5.2   Knowledge transfer activities 
According to Argote and Ingram (Argote & Ingram cited in Ahmed-
Kristensen & Vianello 2015), knowledge repositories in an organization are three 
essential organizational features – tools, tasks and members – and their combination. 
Tools represent technological means that the organization acquires; tasks represent the 
objectives; and members represent the knowledge resources of the organization. It is 
very important to ensure that the networks that are formed by these combinations are 
functioning compatibly internally, within the network, and externally, with the rest of 
organization’s networks, in order to reap the benefits of both KS and KT, and increase 
the organization’s competitiveness.  
From all the possible combinations that can be arranged by these three 
elements, the most effective seems to be the member-to-member repository. 
Assuming that the collaboration levels in an internally compatible member-to-
member network are high, KT becomes a process that aims to the achievement of 
common goals between its participants. As described in this chapter, collaboration 
implies that members are sharing mutual objectives and are influenced by the 
organizational culture. Consequently, the successful KT of the member-to-member 
repository, makes it difficult for other organizations to duplicate, and thus enhances 
their competitive advantage.

2.6   Knowledge Management Strategy 
In order to get the maximum benefit of the use of previous operational 
experience and organizational knowledge, it is essential for the organization to 
establish and follow an effective knowledge management strategy (KMS) which will 
have as an ultimate goal to increase the quality of the final product or service. As has 
been shown in research studies, a clear strategy in KT could lead an organization to 
perform better and enhance its competitive advantage through the efficient 
management of the organizational knowledge (Zander & Kogut cited in Vianello 
2011 & Ahmed-Kristensen & Vianello 2015). 
As supported by Chua and Lam (Chua & Lam cited in Ahmed-Kristensen & 
Vianello 2015), a KMS without a clear objective can lead to ineffective KM. 
According to their analysis, an unsuccessful KM can have its roots in four major 
factors: technology, culture, content and project management. The organizational 
culture factor refers to the lack of trust within the organization, as well as the lack of 
management commitment and lack of specific objectives. This highlights the 
importance of developing and following a well-targeted KMS that is tailored to a 
specific context of use. The first and most important step towards this direction is to 
specify the exact organizational knowledge needs of the users, in order to design KM 
activities that align with these needs. The next steps include the creation or capturing 
of this relevant knowledge and the assurance of the access that the users will have to 
it, in order to reuse it. Thus, to develop a successful KMS the organization should be 
able to answer thoroughly to the following questions: 
 What knowledge to share? 
 Whom to share with? 
 Why should knowledge be shared? 
 How will knowledge be shared? 
It is essential to understand that the development of a successful KMS is not 
dependent to the quantity of the organizational knowledge that is accumulated into the 
repositories, but to the way that this knowledge is being reused, through KM 
activities, to satisfy the objectives that have been set.    
Moreover, the established KMS should take into consideration the products or 
services that are being developed by the organization. In addition, there is a business 
trend towards the development of a strategy which, apart from providing a high 
quality product or service, includes the assistance and support to the client throughout 
the life cycle of the product or service. Therefore, the support and services provided 
during maintenance processes could be a source of useful organizational knowledge 
==PAGE:45==
 
23 
 
and should constitute another parameter to be considered (Vianello 2011; Ahmed-
Kristensen & Vianello 2015).    
As can be assumed, each organization uses a different KMS that is relevant to 
its culture and objectives. According to Dubey and Kalwale (2010), the main 
categories of KMS that are usually adopted by an organization are: 
 Knowledge Strategy as Business Strategy, focusing on the creation or 
acquisition of organizational knowledge, and its sharing and reuse in every 
activity or operation, providing the most relevant knowledge at every moment. 
 Intellectual – Assets management Strategy, focusing on the management of the 
most valuable intellectual assets of the organization (patents, technologies, 
etc.) by renewing, arranging, assessing and securing them, along with 
increasing their availability and marketing. 
 Personal Knowledge Strategy, focusing on the personal responsibility for 
investment, innovation, renewal, fruitful use and availability of knowledge 
resources within each employee’s area of accountability.   
 Knowledge Creating Strategy, focusing on the creation of organizational 
knowledge through the development of research within the organization, and 
the encouragement of employees to innovate and acquire new knowledge from 
past experiences. 
 Knowledge Transfer Strategy, focusing on the transfer of the knowledge 
wherever would be useful or necessary through KS activities.

FM as a supporting function of an organization, according to Bröchner

However, when it comes to the integration of FM knowledge into the construction 
project phases, the ideal situation would be that FM is a part of the owner 
organization, integrating FM knowledge from the user organization and the FM 
service providers, as the latter usually operate using more state-of-the-art building 
systems.   
 
3.2.1   The benefits of FM involvement in a construction project 
As it is suggested in Table 3.1, the FM personnel should be involved in each 
and every phase of the building project and participate to KT activities. According to 
Kelly et al. (Kelly et al. cited in Hansen, Damgaard & de Haas 2010), it is of great 
importance to involve FM from the early programming phase, when the project 
requirements need to be specified. Considering and integrating the FM knowledge to 
the project since its early phases benefits all the project participants; clients, FM units, 
designers and end users (Meng 2013). 
The project client has as main concern the monetary perspective of the project 
(Sarshar & Pitt cited in Meng 2013). By involving the FM unit to the project from the 
beginning, the client ensures the effective and efficient function of the building, 
reducing in this way the O&M costs. Often, during the early design phase, decisions 
are made that may lead to future dysfunctions. When the FM unit is part of this phase, 
it can provide advices so that this kind of decisions will be avoided. Therefore, the 
focus of the clients, which usually is on the capital investment, may switch to the life-
cycle costs of the building. In addition, the chances of meeting the requirements that 
have been set by the client are higher. 
==PAGE:51==
 
29 
 
On the other hand, the focus of the FM unit in a construction project is on the 
complexity level of its future O&M activities; thus, their active involvement in the 
project phases can guarantee that the optimal choices respecting equipment or 
materials have been made. Moreover, they will assure the minimal exposure to any 
kind of risk, for instance cleaning windows where the access is difficult. Their 
participation in the early phases of the project will enable them to make an early 
estimation of the expected FM performance and to be aware of whether the FM 
contract will be fulfilled. Working together with the client, the designer and the 
project team since the beginning of the project, enhances the collaboration attitude of 
the participants. 
Usually the level of complexity of a construction project, in terms of technical 
systems and support services, influences the complexity of its design. Hence, there are 
many cases where the developed design does not consider the everyday functionality 
of the facility. The involvement of FM assists the designers to the early identification 
of design defects, and therefore the achievement of more accurate design results. 
Taking into account the existing FM knowledge, helps designers to provide solutions 
that improve the facility’s operability, maintainability and serviceability, along with 
encouraging them to make more sustainable choices (e.g. energy saving).    
Considering that the end users of the facility are its occupants, it is crucial to 
comprehend and fulfill their requirements as well. According to the research of Meng 
(2013), the FM providers’ knowledge, benefits the end users by considering their 
expectations during the design phase. Furthermore, the occupants’ productivity and 
work efficiency tend to be improved, as their working environment becomes safer, 
healthier and more attractive. Finally, the involvement of FM in the project 
development can provide flexibility to the end users, in case of future building 
refurbishments (Meng 2013).

with, is the difficulty concerning the achievement of an adequate degree of 
organizational learning from experiences of use and operation of existing buildings 
(BUR; Bröchner; Jensen; Emmitt; Lê; cited in Jensen 2009). Therefore, integrating 
existing FM knowledge into a new construction project could be a rigorous process. 
This can be easily understood, considering that a construction project is divided into 
different phases, with different parties involved in each of them; therefore, the FM

Although literature studies indicate the importance of the FM knowledge 
integration in the building process, it seems that in practice FM knowledge is 
transferred inadequately, and the involvement of previous experiences is limited 
during the building process (Jensen cited in Hansen, Damgaard & de Haas 2010). The 
barriers that prevent the transfer of the FM knowledge and limit its integration in the 
building process, according to Damgaard and Erichsen (Damgaard & Erichsen cited 
in Due & Stephensen 2011), fall into five categories (Table 3.2); project related; 
structural; legislative; competence-related; and sociological.    
 
Project-related barriers 
The project-based nature of the construction industry requires the temporary 
involvement of different actors during the progress of a building project, which makes 
==PAGE:53==
 
31 
 
it rather hard for the participants to be assigned to responsibilities. If the 
responsibilities have not been clarified by the end of the project, external advisors or 
contractors may not be held accountable for additional costs or design issues, as well 
as for defects that may be detected in the future. Sometimes the only permanent actor 
in the project is the client, thus he has to be the one to consider the integration of FM 
knowledge in the building processes.  
Apart from the nature of the industry, a limitation can occur from the nature of 
the project. Usually in large and complex projects, where intelligent and 
technologically advanced facilities are developed, traditional processes have to be 
combined with innovative ways of designing and constructing, creating more 
knowledge challenges. Thus, during this kind of projects it has to be assured that the 
generated knowledge has been transferred to the project group. 
 
Structural barriers 
Often the client and the project team (advisors, architects, engineers) are 
focusing on the capital investment, which includes the construction costs, without 
taking into consideration the costs that will occur during the life-cycle of the project. 
As the FM unit is the one that is focusing on the future costs, it can be understood that 
the multiple project actors have different focus areas. Moreover, their interaction is 
defined by tender rules and legislation, hence it is usually short-term.   
 
Legislative barriers 
As aforementioned, legislation limits do not always allow the recruitment of 
the same participants in every project, which does not assist the facilitation of KT 
between different projects. 
 
Competence-related barriers  
FM is a relatively new profession in the construction industry and frequently 
project participants are not aware of the existing FM literature. As a result, FM is not 
always considered to be one of the strategic issues for the project and tend to be 
ignored in the early project phases. Another limitation occurs from the deficiency of 
FM competences and know-how, between the client and its consultants, as well as the 
end users. However, the lack of know-how and communication skills of the FM 
personnel is one of the most discussed barriers. According to Jaunzens et al. 
==PAGE:54==
 
32 
 
(Jaunzens et al. cited in Jensen 2009), often the FM representatives in a building 
project are not qualified enough and lack of effective communication skills, which 
obstructs their participation and contribution in the dialogues that are taking place 
during the design phase.    
Sociological barriers 
The lacking awareness about the benefits of integrating existing FM 
knowledge into a new construction project, along with the inadequate competences 
and communication skills among the FM personnel, results in the low status of FM 
between the project participants. Moreover, the FM units are usually overloaded with 
other FM activities, such as the O&M of existing buildings, and therefore tend to be 
unwilling to participate in the construction phase. On the other hand, the focus of the 
client on the capital investment, instead of the whole life-cycle of the project, does not 
lead to an embracing attitude towards FM as well. Another sociological barrier that 
often appears during a project is the internal conflicts with regard to the power 
distribution between the participants.  
 
3.3   KT tools and mechanisms 
Before the identification of some important KT tools and frameworks that are 
used in the construction industry, it would be useful to present some definitions 
regarding the characteristics of these mechanisms that have been introduced by the 
literature. The characteristics that have been taken into consideration during the 
progress of this project research concern the initiation mechanisms of the KT and the 
strategies for capturing and transferring knowledge   
The initiation KT mechanisms can be categorized into push, pull and fixed (or 
symmetric) mechanisms. It should be mentioned that the participants in the KT 
(sender/receiver) play a crucial role in whether the mechanism will be knowledge 
push, pull or fixed. Knowledge push represents an initiation mechanism where the 
sender provides knowledge without any particular demand for it, knowledge pull is a 
mechanism where the receiver is the one that requests the knowledge that is 
necessary, while a fixed KT initiation mechanism depicts the scheduled KT activities, 
such as regular meetings, where both sender and receiver play an active role through 
established interaction activities (McMahon et al. cited in Vianello & Ahmed 2009a; 
Vianello & Ahmed 2009b; Vianello 2011; Ahmed-Kristensen & Vianello 2015).   
==PAGE:55==
 
33 
 
Another type of categorization of the KT mechanisms that can be indicated, is 
in respect to the strategies for capturing and transferring knowledge, and it is 
illustrated in Figure 3.2; personalization or codification strategies (Hansen et al.; 
McMahon; cited in in Vianello & Ahmed 2009a; Vianello & Ahmed 2009b; Vianello 
2011; Ahmed-Kristensen & Vianello 2015). The personalization strategies represent 
a more informal communication between the participants and can be related to the 
collaboration KS activities that were introduced in the second chapter. Usually 
through the personalization strategies new knowledge is generated and existing tacit 
knowledge becomes available to the receiver. On the other hand, the codification 
strategies refer to the transfer of the explicit knowledge that is captured into 
knowledge repositories, related more to the interaction activities. However, the 
codification strategies do not ensure that he captured knowledge will be used 
(Vianello 2011). Sometimes, in both type of strategies, the facilitation of KT through 
an intermediary knowledge broker is necessary. The main role of a knowledge broker 
is to assist the KT process by providing links, pointing to relevant knowledge sources 
or directly supplying knowledge to the participants (Hargadon cited in Vianello & 
Ahmed 2009a; Vianello 2011; Ahmed-Kristensen & Vianello 2015).

3.3.1   ICT-based tools 
As can be assumed, from the two strategies presented above, the one that can 
be described to a great extent as technology-dependent is the codification strategy. 
According to Jensen (2009), codification strategy is based on the use of knowledge 
repositories, which are mainly ICT-based, as KT tools. Moreover, it was explained in 
the second chapter that the ICT-based tools are the most sufficient way for capturing, 
encoding, storing and sharing or transferring codified knowledge (Appel-
Meulenbroek 2014).  
Useful ICT-based tools for KT are the web-based databases, which are 
developed in order to assist KT and online collaboration between the project 
participants, while saving time and unnecessary costs from the decision-making that 
takes place during the project progress. The web-based databases can manage 
specifications, drawings, construction records, asset management performance data 
and other information that are required during the project phases and, therefore, deal 
with some typical problems (Ling et al. 2014). For instance, an information platform 
can assist the project participants to locate equipment (Jog et al. cited in Ling et al. 
2014), or can help contractors and suppliers to ensure that there will not be any 
material shortage (Xue et al. cited in Ling et al. 2014). Furthermore, the knowledge 
stored in the web-based databases will be available to the actors that may join the 
project after its initial phases. 
Another type of control ICT-based tools is the Building Management Systems 
(BMS) or Building Automation Systems (BAS), developed and used extensively over 
the last decades (Derek & Celements-Croome cited in Wang & Xie 2002). BMS 
gather building operation information, such as operational costs (maintenance, 
cleaning etc.) or energy consumption, on a regular basis and provide a better 
arrangement and management of the several building services systems (Jensen 2009). 
Usually, in a BMS coexist several types of control devices (controllers, control 
stations, sensors, actuators) from different manufacturers, and their interconnection is 
necessary in order to achieve a high degree of integration (Wang & Xie 2002). The 
accomplishment of the desired interoperability between these devices is supported by 
internet or intranet solutions, which provide an effective, convenient and inexpensive 
way for intelligent building network integration (Bushby; Ivanovich & Gustavson 
cited in Wang & Xie 2002). 
The list with the ICT-based KT tools includes also more recently developed 
tools, such as digital tendering and digital handover of the buildings, along with 
==PAGE:57==
 
35 
 
Building Information Modelling (BIM), 3D design, project-webs and computer-aided 
FM (CAFM) systems, analyzed in the following chapters.     
 
3.3.2   The POKI management tool 
The POKI (Process, Organization, Knowledge, Information) management tool 
was developed in order to assist an organization within the construction industry to 
overcome all the barriers that prevent the integration of the FM knowledge during the 
construction phases. As the barriers presented in Table 3.2 are multifaceted, it would 
not be effective enough to use some new tools in order to overcome them; a change of 
the management mindset of the organization seems to be a more appropriate 
approach.  
The strategic level decision for the implementation of the POKI framework is 
made by the project client and consultants; however, POKI can be used by all project 
participants. As it is illustrated in Figure 3.3, in order to get the maximum benefits of 
its use, the POKI tool should be applied to all project phases, emphasizing the 
beginning of the project when an extra effort is necessary (Due & Stephensen 2011). 
 
 
Figure 3.3 - The POKI tool in a construction project (Due & Stephensen 2011) 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:58==
 
36 
 
Process 
In the beginning of every construction project, it is important for all the project 
participants to comprehend that the involvement of the FM unit into the project aims 
to the development of a user-friendly facility, which will serve the purpose of its 
construction, while being aligned with the vision, mission and values of the 
organization. Therefore, sufficient time and resources should be used in the early 
project phases, as, according to Due and Stephensen (2011), “resources spent on 
thorough planning are resources well spent”. 
During the feasibility study phase, the project goals and the purpose of the 
construction project is defined; thus, it is crucial to prioritize highly among the main 
project goals the development of a user- and FM-friendly facility. In the briefing 
phase, the end user and the FM provider involvement is necessary, in order to assure 
that their requirements have been taken into consideration. The design phases are 
those where the demands are specified and it is still possible to make changes. It is 
important to mention that the earlier the alterations are occurring, the less the final 
cost are. During these phases, the FM unit should screen and evaluate systematically 
the decisions that have been made and ensure that they meet the FM demands. In the 
construction phase, the role of FM is to ensure that the user and FM requirements that 
have been set in the agreement with the contractor are fulfilled. 
 
Organization 
To ensure the success of a construction project, the most suitable persons 
should be chosen for its execution. The selection of competent and appropriate project 
participants is a factor that also assists the elimination of the loss of information 
during the project phases. Therefore, a “linking pin” (Likert cited in Due & 
Stephensen 2011) should be appointed, usually a person that belongs to the project 
team, which will ensure that the FM-related knowledge and user information are 
transferred and integrated into each project phase.  
 
Knowledge & tools 
As it facilitates the transfer of FM-related knowledge into a construction 
project phases, the POKI framework focuses on the use of sufficient tools during the 
progress of the project. This element of the POKI tool aims to ensure that the existing 
knowledge is formalized and codified, so that existing FM knowledge is available to 
the project participants at every moment. Therefore, a development of a project can be 
==PAGE:59==
 
37 
 
assisted by O&M specialists or the use of tools, such as Life Cycle Costing (LCC) and 
BIM.  
 
Information & communication 
According to the POKI tool, an extra focus should be given to the information 
that is communicated during the project phases. Over the last decades, the 
development of the technology and digital communication methods increased the 
amount of the information transferred. Therefore, it should be assured that the 
information and knowledge that is collected, is relevant and useful for the project, and 
subsequently, that it is prioritized, focused and transferred to the right participants 
when it is truly required. To achieve this, it is crucial for the organization to develop a 
suitable KMS which will define clearly what type of information and knowledge is 
transferred, when it should be communicated, to whom it would be beneficial, and 
how it is more efficient and effective to be transferred (Due & Stephensen 2011).  
 
 
Another tool that has been detected during the literature research but has been 
decided not to be described nor used in this thesis, is the typology for front- and back-
end KT mechanisms from FM to the building process, developed by Jensen (2012). 
This typology suggests initiation, both push and pull, mechanisms that support the 
front-end KT, which refers to the knowledge regarding the requirements set for the 
building to be designed and constructed, as well as the back-end KT, which refers to 
the knowledge regarding the performance of the finished building.

4.4   Knowledge Sharing in DTU CAS 
DTU CAS seems to be aware of the important outcome that KT within its 
organization can deliver. Effective KT can support DTU CAS constructing more user- 
and operational-friendly buildings for its users, as well as maintaining efficiently the 
existing ones. Therefore, as DTU CAS is moving to a direction towards extensive KT 
between its different departments, several techniques that support this KT can be 
observed. Some of them have been developed over the years in order to assure that the 
employees of every department are participating in the KT activities, while others are 
currently under development in order to provide even more effective and efficient KT 
in the future. In this thesis these methods have been divided into two general 
categories; cooperative activities and ICT-related techniques.

4.4.1   Cooperation 
In the second chapter, the necessity of cooperative behavior between the 
different parties of a network in order to achieve successful KS or KT was discussed. 
As explained, the cooperative behavior was distinguished by Kahn (Kahn cited in 
Appel-Meulenbroek 2014) into interaction and collaboration. Characteristics of these 
two main types of cooperative behavior can be found in the organization of DTU 
CAS.  
 
4.4.1.1   Interaction 
It has been described above that interaction as a cooperative behavior is more 
structured and formal. Every organization has methods, systems and processes to 
build and support interaction between its different units. DTU CAS establishes its 
interaction on a phase-gate model that has been developed based in the principles of 
PRINCE2 model in order to support every new construction project, in which all the 
departments of CAS, as well as external actors, are involved. In Figure 4.4 is 
presented the phase-gate model that is applied to every construction projects of DTU 
CAS. 
As illustrated in Figure 4.4, a building project in DTU CAS is divided into 
four main stages; conceive, design, implement and operate. Each of these stages is 
subdivided into different phases, which represent the activities that take place during 
the project execution. All DTU CAS departments are involved in a building project, 
depending on the phase that the project is in. Each phase is followed by a gate-point, 
where mainly activities that support the interaction of the several parties involved in 
the project, occur. These activities are usually scheduled meetings or exchange of 
documented information for review or approval from DTU CAS units or other project 
participants.  
 
First Stage – Conceive 
Every building project is initiated with the ‘predesign and concept 
development’ phase. In this phase, where the Real Estate and Space Management 
department is involved, a general idea of the future project is determined, including 
the identification of the needs, vision and strategy of the project. During this phase, 
the location of the building and a framework regarding the budget of the project is 
established.

47 
 
 
 
Figure 4.4 - The Phase-gate model of DTU CAS 
==PAGE:70==

When the first phase is completed and the concept of the project has been 
defined, the Real Estate and Space Management department calls a meeting to 
announce to the Real Estate PMO department the decision that has been made. 
Following, the head of Real Estate PMO has to decide who the most appropriate 
project manager of his department to undertake the project is. He assigns the project 
manager to the building project during a meeting, where the future project is 
described. These activities comprise the first gate of the model.   
The next phase is the ‘design brief’ phase. The product of this phase is a one 
of the most important documents in the building project, as the wishes and the 
requirements that are set by the users of the building, are listed. In the beginning of 
this phase, the project manager, who is assigned to the project, has to form a project 
team which consists of external advisors that will assist him/her during the different 
stages of the project. This project team usually has a size of 8-10 people, and includes 
architects and engineers specialized in different fields, such as electrical or HVAC 
installations. In order to define the content of the project in the design brief document, 
meetings with a user group of the future building are held. As the future users of the 
building have more accurate knowledge about the work processes and workflows of 
personnel and students, it is necessary to incorporate their knowledge into the brief 
document. At the same time, and considering that the future users of the building are 
not the only ones involved during the life cycle of the building, it is crucial to involve 
in this phase the personnel from the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department, 
who will maintain it in the future. This is happening during the scheduled meetings 
that the project team holds with the leaders of each FM section. In the first meeting 
between these entities, the section leaders distribute the building requirements that 
they have set, through standards that have been developed by them the previous years.   
The second gate, that follows the design brief phase, stands for the finalization 
and the approval of the design brief. The project managers forward the document that 
has been developed, to the management of the department, the section leaders and the 
executive board of DTU, for review and proposals for modifications or 
improvements. After receiving back the comments on the design brief, the project 
managers modify the document, having taken into consideration these comments. 
Sometimes they need to contact again the section leaders in order to explain or discuss 
some issues that may have occurred, so that they will reach a common decision 
regarding the project. This happens with the exchange of e-mails or meetings, 
depending on the significance of the issue. When a common decision cannot be made, 
the point in question is discussed and solved in the next higher level of the 
organization, with meetings between the heads of the Real Estate PMO, and Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects departments. If there is no common decision in this level as 
==PAGE:71==
 
49 
 
well, the director of DTU CAS is the one that takes the final decision. When the 
design brief is finalized, it is sent to the executive board of DTU for the final 
approval.    
 
Second Stage – Design 
During the third phase, the ‘outline design’ of the project is prepared on the 
basis of the design brief and in close consultation with the user group, as its focus is 
on the overall distribution and layout of the rooms. It is the most important of the 
design phases, as the decisions that are taken here, have the greatest cost impact on 
the overall project. The subsequent design phases offer limited freedom to conduct 
changes to the project.  
 
 
Figure 4.5 - The correspondence in meetings between Real Estate PMO and Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects representatives 
 
Following this phase, and during the third gate of the model, the project team 
meets the section leaders of the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department, in 
order to review together the outline design and ensure that it complies with the design 
brief. Usually during each meeting the project manager and the engineers of a specific 
field, meet the leader of the equivalent section; for instance, the engineers specialized 
in electrical installations meet the leader of the Electrical section, the architects meet 
the leader of the Buildings Maintenance section and so on (Figure 4.5). In case of 
disagreement on a decision, the same decision-making process as described above is 
==PAGE:72==
 
50 
 
followed. When the two parties conclude on the outline design, and before the next 
phase begins, it has to be approved by the executive board of DTU.  
The fourth phase is the ‘preliminary design’ phase, where the outline design is 
being further detailed, along with the user group, having as main focus the rooms 
layout and the choice of furniture and other equipment. The preliminary design of the 
building project, assists the project team as it can be the basis for obtaining 
construction permit and other regulatory approvals from the local planning authority. 
The fourth gate is where the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department 
has to ensure that the input from each of its sections is taken into account. Therefore, 
the project manager sends the design that has been developed, to each section leader, 
requesting them to review and comment on it. Usually the comments are submitted 
through a standardized format document that the project managers attach to the 
design, in order to classify efficiently the input of the section leaders and avoid 
receiving comments that is time-consuming to comprehend. Sometimes, if there are 
issues that need to be discussed thoroughly with the section leaders, project managers 
schedule meetings, as described in the previous phase (Figure 4.5). After being 
reviewed by the FM section leaders, the preliminary designed is sent to the executive 
board of DTU for approval, and the application for the construction permit is sent to 
the local planning authority.  
The fifth phase concerns the development of the detailed design of the project, 
which represents the final design solution that the contractors will compete for, during 
the tender process. In this phase, usually is only the project team that is involved, 
unless the user group needs to evaluate certain elements. 
After the completion of the detailed design phase, during the fifth gate, the 
project team arranges meetings with the FM section leaders, in order to get the 
approval of all the finalized project documents before the tenders are invited in the 
next phase. 
In the sixth phase, the contractor(s) that are going to execute the building 
project is selected through a tendering process with private, public or EU invitations. 
The criteria that a contractor needs to meet in order to win a project, depend on the 
type of the project and are set by the project team.  
Once the project manager has decided about the contractor(s), he/she prepares 
a recommendation that will get the final approval from the executive board of DTU 
during the sixth gate of the model. Subsequently, the contract can be signed. 
Sometimes, before taking their final decision about the contractor(s), the project 
==PAGE:73==
 
51 
 
managers need to schedule a meeting with the FM section leaders in order to agree on 
the selected contractor(s). That happens in order to avoid selecting contractors that in 
the past delivered work of insufficient level to DTU. 
 
Third Stage – Implement 
The seventh phase deals with the site operations and the realization of the 
building project, according to the contract that has been signed in the previous phase. 
Throughout this phase, all daily users of the surrounding buildings experience 
disruptions; for instance noise, vibration, dust, operation of cranes or unavailability of 
parking facilities and access routes; thus their tolerance is required. Project managers 
conduct several on-site inspections of the project to assure that the ‘time, cost, 
quality’ framework is respected, and inform the user group regarding the progress of 
the building project.  
The seventh gate is following the end of the construction operations. It 
includes the cleaning of the premises in order to be ready for occupation, the fire 
safety inspection by an expert, and the acquisition of the certificate of occupation 
from the local authority.  
The eighth phase is the last phase of the implementation stage of the phase-
gate model, and concerns the handover of the building. It starts with a building 
defects inspection that the project team conducts. The defects are recorded and it is 
concluded if they can impede the occupation of the building. If the extend of the 
defects is such that they need to be fixed, their remedy is decided in conjunction with 
the contractor(s).  
During the eight gate, and after the defects have been remedied, the actual 
handover takes place. The contractor(s) signs the handover document and hands the 
keys of the building over to the client (DTU) along with any further responsibility. 
Moreover, the user manuals are prepared, and the Facilities Maintenance and projects 
department is introduced to the new building.  
 
Fourth Stage – Operate  
The next phase initiates the operation stage of the model, as it concerns the 
occupation of the new building. This phase begins with the moving of furniture, and 
the installation of IT equipment that are not part of the contract, as well as the 
adjustment of the building security through its lock systems. Subsequently, the users 
==PAGE:74==
 
52 
 
along with the FM sections that will take care of the building during its life cycle, are 
introduced to the facilities and instructed regarding its operation and maintenance. 
The actual occupation and operation of the building by its users can be considered as 
the gate that follows this phase. 
The phase that determines the finalization of the model includes the one- and 
five-year inspections of the building. During this five-year period, users play a critical 
role as they have to list any defects that they detect. When these defects are serious, 
such as large cracks on the floors/ walls or malfunctions of heating systems, they have 
to report to the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department immediately, in order 
to prevent the deterioration of the facility. This defect list, containing both 
construction deficiencies and defects caused by the facilities operation, is part of a 
document called ‘schedule of defects’ that is formed by DTU CAS. The contractor(s) 
issues a five-year warranty for the building, and therefore no other entity is allowed to 
remedy defects during the first five years of operation. The Facilities Maintenance 
and Projects department takes the control of the building after the one-year inspection 
(Danmarks Tekniske Universitet Campus Service 2014b).  
As described thoroughly above, DTU CAS ensures the cooperative behavior 
within its organizational departments in terms of interaction, with activities that take 
place through its phase-gate model that is being used in every building project. The 
most usual interaction activities are the meetings between the participants, where 
decisions are made; the exchange of documented information, which usually aim to 
the review or approval of a recommendation; and the exchange of e-mails in order to 
determine details or make decisions of a lower level of importance.  
 
4.4.1.2   Collaboration 
It has been explained above, that collaboration as a type of cooperative 
behavior, includes more unstructured and informal organizational processes, 
characterized by mutual understanding between the participants, as well as sharing of 
common objectives. Although DTU CAS seems to have a structured cooperative 
behavior in terms of interaction, when it comes to collaboration, it can be noticed that 
they have some way ahead of them before they achieve an adequate level of 
collaboration within the whole organization. 
Kahn (Kahn cited in Appel-Meulenbroek 2014), after conducting his research, 
suggested a number of collaboration activities that can assist an effective KS within a 
network (Table 2.1), such as share a common vision for the organization or work 
==PAGE:75==
 
53 
 
together as a team. Kahn was referring to networks with regard to ‘interdepartmental 
relationships’; however it can be easily understood that the formulation of common 
objectives, and the adoption of informal activities that support the achievement of 
these objectives between all departments of an organization, can be a difficult and 
complicated process, compared to the development of a common goal within a 
department in order to obtain collaboration and effective KS.   
Although everyone in DTU CAS is aware of the importance of KS, and that it 
is something that the whole organization needs to focus on, a clear common goal 
regarding KS has not been defined. As Anders B. Møller claimed, “There is a clear 
mind about KS but it is not written down in any form”, which according to the FM 
section leaders and the project manager that have been interviewed, could be an 
obstacle in increasing the collaboration levels within the organization. Employees in 
DTU CAS know that they have to share the knowledge between them and transfer 
knowledge to another department when it is necessary, but it has not been formally 
stated any kind of strategy that clarifies why and how they should do it. 
For instance, according to the overall DTU strategy for the period 2014-2019, 
sustainability is one of the factors that every department and every employee in DTU 
should focus on. Research and education in DTU are pointing to the creation of 
sustainable value for the Danish society and internationally, and DTU’s personnel 
makes effort to think sustainable in all aspects of their work (Danmarks Tekniske 
Universitet 2014c). DTU CAS integrates sustainability in every project and sets it as 
an ultimate objective, thus it can be said that the whole organization is aware of this 
goal. Therefore, all the activities in each department aim to this direction of DTU 
being a sustainable university, enhancing the collaboration within each department. 
However, the fact that they do not have a formulated clear common strategy towards 
KS, impedes them from following the DTU strategy collectively and debilitates the 
collaborative behavior.   
On the other hand, the heads of DTU CAS’ departments collaborate in a 
higher degree than the employees in their departments do. As Ole Kristian Bottheim 
mentioned, the heads of the three departments of DTU CAS, along with the director 
of DTU CAS, have a close relationship and report directly to each other any problem 
that might occur during a project. However, they are aware about the necessity of 
increasing the collaboration behavior between the people employed in their 
departments; thus, at the moment they are constructing their own building. The 
building of DTU CAS is estimated to be finished in 2016, and when the three 
departments will move in, it will be the first time since DTU CAS was founded, that 
the whole organization will be under the same roof. 
==PAGE:76==
 
54 
 
Having the three departments of an organization sitting in different buildings 
may result in the development of subcultures within the organization. Even though 
subcultures can be considered as a positive consequence, because employees feel as a 
part of a community, and therefore there are more chances to collaborate and perform 
better; it may prevent the development of a common organizational culture. 
Therefore, the gathering of DTU CAS in the same building aims to the development 
of a strong universal organizational culture. As Ole Kristian Bottheim supports, the 
congregation of the three directors of DTU CAS departments and the general director 
of DTU CAS in the same building, as well as working in the same environment, will 
also serve as a “symbolic” action which will enhance the interdepartmental relations 
and the collaboration behavior.   
 
4.4.2   Knowledge Sharing through ICT-related methods 
It has been explained in the third chapter that KM depends heavily on ICT-
based tools, as they constitute a reliable and sufficient way to share codified 
knowledge.  Since DTU CAS is moving to a direction where the knowledge that has 
arisen from previous projects will be shared in order to be used in the future, they try 
to invest in ICT systems that will assure the competent and efficient KS throughout 
the organization. As detailed below, DTU CAS is developing and using different 
systems and methods, as well as CAFM systems, for the accumulation of information 
and the sharing of the generated organizational knowledge. Two valuable systems for 
DTU CAS are the BIM method and the DTU FM. 
 
4.4.2.1   Building Information Modelling  
Building Information Modelling is a substantial ICT-based development for 
the construction industry. Through this integrated method, the construction process 
can be digitized, during the building life cycle, from idea to demolition. Figure 4.6 
illustrates the way that the digital building models become a central point for all the 
construction activities, as well as for the cooperation and the expansion throughout 
the different entities associated with the project. Nonetheless, the use of BIM models 
is an exhaustive and demanding process that excludes the individuals that have no 
knowledge of BIM to participate in an integrated project (Danmarks Tekniske 
Universitet BIM lab 2013).  
 
==PAGE:77==
 
55 
 
 
Figure 4.6 - BIM cycle (Danmarks Tekniske Universitet BIM lab 2013)  
 
In charge of the digitization of all the building information in DTU CAS, is 
the BMS section, which is part of the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department. 
The primary task of BIM office is to unify and streamline all the analog information 
of DTU’s buildings as digital information, and make them available for all the parties 
associated to the life cycle of the constructions (Danmarks Tekniske Universitet 
2014d). 
Since its establishment in 2010, the BIM office is responsible for the 
digitization of all the paper drawings related to the existing buildings of DTU. 
Initially, these drawings were converted to 2D drawings in Autodesk AutoCAD and 
some in 3D models in Autodesk Revit. For a whole year an employee was walking all 
around Lyngby campus, describing thoroughly every building and its use, along with 
mapping all the necessary operating characteristics, such as building element 
producers, materials, and all the technical values related to the existing building 
elements and systems. Therefore, the building models that the BIM office developed 
were supplemented by a variety of detailed information that can be used during the 
maintenance or the refurbishment of a building, reducing time and costs (Lampe 
2014). Currently, BIM office’s tasks include the development of 3D Revit models of 
DTU buildings on Level 2, as well as the formation of a database, where all the digital 
drawings and the documents will be available to the users and new information will 
be reported (Danmarks Tekniske Universitet 2014d).  
Even though the BIM office managed to create a digitalized archive where all 
the information has been collected in a consistent way using BIM, they still have a

quite long way ahead. According to Markus Lampe, the BIM manager of DTU CAS, 
by now approximately 33% of the information digitalization has finished, and 25% of 
the digital models have been developed; both of them are expected to be completed in 
2016. 
 
4.4.2.2   DTU FM 
Another ICT-based mechanism that DTU CAS is developing is a space and 
maintenance management software called DaluxFM, provided by Dalux. DaluxFM, 
which has been renamed DTU FM, is a quite new system introduced in DTU CAS and 
it has not come into use yet. 
DaluxFM is a cloud-based software that supports the FM activities of an 
organization and it is used by the largest operators in Denmark, such as government 
agencies, universities and municipalities. As it uses web as a platform, it can be 
accessed by its users anytime and from anywhere, enabling them to obtain directly 
information that they need, and thus, save a lot of time. Another important aspect of 
using this software is the fact that it is developed and tailored according to the client’s 
needs and requirements. That means that the users are not loaded with features and 
functions that they will not use, which makes the software even more user-friendly 
(Dalux 2015a). 
DTU CAS will use DaluxFM as a FM database which has a link to the 3D 
models and the building information mentioned above. In collaboration with DTU 
CAS, Dalux developed a new and improved operation and maintenance module, 
which has been added to DaluxFM. 
This new module enables DTU FM to manage everything, from scheduled or 
preventive maintenance to emergency maintenance incidents, which have been 
register through Fejlmeld, an ICT-based system that has been developed to allow its 
users (DTU personnel, students, etc.) to record all the faults that are detected around 
campus. Moreover, it can plan and prioritize tasks, as well as handle the latest FM 
principles, service contracts, standards, tasks at fixed intervals and employees in 
maintenance departments. DaluxFM provides a FM organization with a full history of 
the tasks that have been performed to a room or building, as it supports the direct link 
of every task related to this room or building (from the internet or a BIM model), to 
all the relevant documents and building information. Another important and useful 
characteristic of DaluxFM is that provides the users with a full overview of all the 
==PAGE:79==
 
57 
 
ongoing tasks as well as all the possible delays for a building or a department, section 
and employee assigned to each task (Dalux 2015b). 
However, as Synne Remvig, the Buildings Maintenance section leader 
mentioned, “A system is only as good as the information you put in it”. Thus, 
considering the size of Lyngby campus and all the available data and information 
related to all the buildings, it can be easily understood that their registration into the 
system is an extremely time-consuming process. As DTU FM is a maintenance 
database, accountable for the registration of all the data and information are the 
sections in the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department. However, the 
employees in these sections are usually occupied with all the emergency maintenance 
tasks around the campus that are coming from Fejlmeld, which leaves them no time to 
devote to the set-up of DTU FM. Synne Remvig, for instance, estimates that it will 
take around two years to finish the registration of all the information and data that her 
section possess, not only because of their amount, but also because of lack of human 
resources (Remvig 2015). 
 
4.5   Knowledge Sharing in Real Estate PMO department 
The Real Estate PMO department is a relatively new department. As DTU had 
all its buildings constructed at once when it moved its facilities to Lyngby campus 40 
years ago, the main activities of DTU CAS were concerning the maintenance of these 
facilities. The Real Estate PMO department existed but it was composed of a small 
number of project managers (around five persons). When a need for a new building 
was occurring, the traditional way to construct it was through an external consultant 
who was hired to undertake both the design of the building and the decision about the 
contractor(s) who was going to build it. Thus, the project managers were practically 
unaware of the time frame, the quality, the costs involved in the project and the 
activities that were going on in the construction site (Bottheim 2015).    
In 2009, when Ole Kristian Bottheim was hired as the head of the Real Estate 
PMO department, the future plans of DTU included the expansion of the Lyngby 
campus, in order to gather there some of the facilities that were sitting in other DTU 
campuses. According to Ole Kristian Bottheim, in 2009, the total investments were 
about 2 billion Danish kroner while today this amount has been raised to 4.5 billion 
Danish kroner. In order to prepare his department for this enormous future 
investment, he decided to make some drastic changes to the way that the projects 
where executed until then, and issue DTU CAS with more control on the projects by 
==PAGE:80==
 
58 
 
involving the project managers to the whole construction process (Figure 4.7). Along 
with this “change management trip”, a project model (Figure 4.4) was developed 
based on the principles of the PRINCE2 model. The head of the department, in 
conjunction with the project managers, created a skeleton of the PRINCE2 model 
based on their previous project experiences, in order to define the way that they would 
like to work with their project model.  
 
 
Figure 4.7 - Real Estate PMO department's change of structure 
 
The following years the number of the projects expanded and the personnel of 
Real Estate PMO increased to 30 people; hence, the department divided into three 
different sections, supervised by three section leaders, and took its current structure. 
Each section is in charge of different projects and the 2/3 of the department is 
employed directly from DTU CAS. The rest of the department’s personnel is external 
partners coming from the engineering consultancy companies NIRAS A/S and V2C 
(Value 2 Construction) ApS.  
As Real Estate PMO is a rather newly established department having its 
current structure, the KS within the department has not been developed sufficiently. 
Even though they often hold meetings, Lisbet Sand, a project manager that has been 
in the department before its separation into the three subsections, supports that the 
previous structure allowed a more efficient KS between the project managers. Hence, 
the work overload because of the numerous projects that they are in charge of, can be 
considered to be one of the factors that constrain them from focusing adequately on 
this area. Nonetheless, the project managers are aware of the fact that many of their 
colleagues are facing – or have faced in the past – similar difficulties and 
complications with their projects. Therefore, they understand that they can help each 
other by sharing their individual knowledge, instead of spending time solving their 
issues on their own, and they request the establishment of KS techniques within their 
==PAGE:81==

department. As Lisbet Sand mentions, if a new big organization wants to act in a 
professional way, optimal processes should be established, instead of carrying out 
tasks in different ways.  
The center of Real Estate PMO’s intradepartmental KS, is an intranet where 
their project model, which has been described above, has been developed for each of 
their projects. Every member of a project has authorized access to this project model 
platform, where all the information regarding every project is available. However, 
sometimes the size of this model seems to be impractical. Its massive extent and 
capacity with all the phases, is necessary for a large project, but when it comes to 
smaller refurbishment projects its use can be complicated; therefore, according to Ole 
Kristian Bottheim, a simpler version of the model could be more convenient and 
applicable. Moreover, its high level of complexity may prevent other entities related 
to the project, such as the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department, from using 
it.      
As mentioned above, when the construction projects in DTU CAS increased, 
the Real Estate PMO department was divided into three sections (PMO1, PMO2 and 
PMO3), which until the beginning of 2015 they were sitting in different buildings. 
According to the head of the department, this distribution of the department into three 
different buildings constituted a problem, as it obstructed him from having a clear 
view of what was happening to the department and act fast when an issue was 
appearing. Moreover, the three sections started developing their own subcultures and 
goals, while they should define common objectives for the department. Finally, this 
segregation was a barrier for KS between the project managers, as they were not 
aware of the problems that their colleagues were dealing with. 
Apart from gathering the department under the same roof, a new shared office 
layout was adopted in Real Estate PMO. The three sections have been placed in a 
common space, where all the project managers, as well as the head of the department, 
work together. As it can be assumed, the prevalence of an everyday face-to-face 
contact is more likely to stimulate collaboration and therefore lead to the expansion of 
KS and innovation. In this way, the project managers see and talk to each other about 
their projects, raising awareness about the activities and tasks they are engaged in. 
Besides increasing KS, this new office layout aims to examine how this structure 
works in practice. Real Estate PMO comprehends how important to save space is, 
hence they use to try several office layouts, furniture, etc., calling themselves an 
“office laboratory”. In this way, it is easier for them to persuade a DTU institute to 
adopt a space-saving room layout during the design stage of a project (Bottheim 
2015).    
==PAGE:82==
 
60 
 
However, in order to ensure the effective function of a shared office layout, it 
is necessary to establish some internal guidelines. People are different and not 
everyone enjoys or can be productive when sitting together with others. The noise 
levels and other distractions, such as ringing cell phones or office machines, are 
factors that can induce frustration and block the collaboration attitude, which 
following may prevent KS. Moreover, it is crucial for the personnel to adopt a 
positive attitude towards KS and be willing to share their individual knowledge, as 
according to Ole Kristian Bottheim KS is a bidirectional process.  
 
4.6   Knowledge Sharing in Facilities Maintenance and Projects 
department 
Facilities Maintenance and Projects is the largest department of DTU CAS. It 
employs around 60 people and it is divided in several different sections, as it is 
illustrated in Figure 4.2. However, the structure of the department was not always as 
presented in Figure 4.2.  
When Anders B. Møller, the current director of the department, started 
working in DTU Lyngby campus in 2010, the FM – Planning and Projects section 
was not formed yet, and all the project managers that constitute it today were 
dispersed in the four FM sections; Parks and roads; Buildings maintenance; Electrical; 
and HVAC. These four sections were responsible of all the basic maintenance as well 
as the small refurbishment projects, thus their personnel was involved in both of them. 
However, the personnel of the four sections was trained and skilled in maintaining 
facilities and fixing defects, but not in dealing with bigger projects, such as roofing or 
windows replacement in a building. Frequently, they had to put extra effort in 
executing a refurbishment project, and consequently they did not have the required 
time in order to do all the building maintenance and ensure that everything is 
functioning right in the existing facilities. Therefore, the department was not 
performing sufficiently either in basic maintenance or in refurbishment projects 
(Møller 2015).   
The big change in the structure of the department (Figure 4.8) came when 
Anders B. Møller decided to gather all the specialized projects personnel that was 
sitting in the four sections, in a distinct subsection that he formed (FM – Planning and 
Projects), responsible for the projects that were deriving from the FM sections. Thus, 
the four FM sections personnel could focus on the basic maintenance operations that 
were competent and skilled enough to perform, providing top-class services.

KS in Facilities Maintenance and Projects department can be subdivided in KS 
within the same section, and between different sections of the department. The most 
common methods that are used in both cases include activities that are linked with 
interaction as well as collaboration behavior, as it has been defined by Kahn (Kahn 
cited in Appel-Meulenbroek 2014).

Scheduled meetings are the most preferable and efficient method that is being 
used within a section. According to the section leader of Buildings Maintenance, 
Synne Remvig, there are scheduled meetings in her unit every two weeks, which 
usually aim to the communication of information coming from the higher levels of the 
organization and is relevant for the employees of the section. During these meetings 
individual experiences of the personnel are shared, along with knowledge that has 
been acquired by these experiences. Sometimes, when the section leader needs to talk 
with a part of the personnel, for instance only with the painters of the section, 
meetings are held just with them. When it is not possible to gather all the members of 
the desired group and the reason of meeting is the communication of information, e-
mails are used in order to deliver this information. 
Another key activity for sharing knowledge in Facilities Maintenance and 
Projects is the exchange of documented information in terms of writing and 
distributing reports within the department. Every year, the whole department 
composes a report that contains all the activities and goals that have been achieved in 
every section separately, along with information about the function of the section 
during the whole year. After reporting the previous year’s achievements, each section 
==PAGE:84==

leader presents a plan and sets some new goals for the next year. Apart from the 
annual report of the department, there is also a long-term investment plan which has 
been developed by composing the future goals of each section, regarding investments. 
When a new maintenance project that has to be done in a building is identified 
(roofing replacement, toilettes replacement, etc.), the section leader needs to develop 
a plan that will present a cost schedule regarding the investment itself, as well as the 
future maintenance costs. Following, this plan is added to the annual report and the 
long-term investment plan of the department. All these information is necessary to be 
shared within the department, as other sections need to take them into consideration 
for their future planning. For instance, FM – Planning and Projects section needs to be 
aware of the future agenda of each section in order to plan and arrange the work and 
the distribution of its project managers in the future (Møller 2015). 
During a maintenance project or a refurbishment of a building that is 
conducted by FM –Planning and Projects section, the KS between the FM sections 
and the project managers is usually less formal, and it is achieved with both 
interaction and collaboration activities. These projects derive from the section leaders, 
and meetings with the project managers are both scheduled, when important decisions 
have to be made, and unscheduled, when details need to be discussed. Moreover, e-
mails and phone calls are used in order to determine less important issues. As the 
project managers were part of the FM sections in the past, a mutual understanding and 
trust has been developed between these units. Unfortunately, data regarding the KS 
between the rest of the sections in Facilities Maintenance and Projects (Building 
Management Systems and CAS sections in other campuses) could not be collected 
from the interviews that have been conducted during the research stage (Remvig 
2015).  
Another question, that section leaders contemplate, is how to extract 
individual tacit knowledge from personnel that is employed in the department for a 
long period. Section leaders try to extract this individual knowledge and codify it, but 
there has not been established any formal process for achieving this. The section 
leaders are aware of the importance of obtaining and formalizing this knowledge, and 
that having key employees in an organization, whose individual knowledge has not 
been codified, could be fragile. The absence of these employees, even for a short 
period, could create a problem to the whole organization. The method that is being 
used currently, is trying to extract as much information and knowledge as possible 
from a key employee when an issue occurs. However, section leaders recognize that it

to ask for, as usually you need the knowledge when you meet a problem.

Knowledge Transfer in DTU CAS 
 
Over the last five years, DTU CAS is moving towards a direction that 
promotes interdepartmental KT. As the Buildings Maintenance section leader, Synne 
Remvig, says, “Everybody knows that KT is very important and it is the only way that 
we can do effective FM”. However, in the past, the level of KT between the Real

adequate. There were project managers from the Real Estate PMO department who

from the analysis of the phase-gate project model that is being used (Chapter 4), KT 
between these two departments is a crucial issue for the organization, which is 
supported by several ICT-based tools and it is facilitated through different processes.   
An issue that is still challenging the relations of the two departments is the 
issue of the ownership of the building projects. According to the heads of the two 
departments, the effectiveness of the KT between the two parties, in terms of 
utilization of the existing knowledge, can be related to the mutual understanding, 
acceptance and respect of each other’s role. The issue of the project ownership 
derived from the head of the Real Estate PMO department, who was the first to 
indicate the importance for each department to comprehend the role distribution 
within a project. According to him, even though project managers are a big part of the 
project and they are responsible for it during the development of every project phase, 
after its completion and for the rest of the project life cycle, the responsibility 
==PAGE:86==
 
64 
 
relocates to the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department; hence, the FM 
section leaders should be treated as the actual owners of the project. Thus, it is crucial 
for both sides to be aware of these roles and respect them during the development of a 
project. As it can be presumed, to achieve the desired level of respect between the two 
departments, a high level of collaboration should be obtained.  
As Anders B. Møller, head of the Facilities Maintenance and Projects 
department, explained, the difficulty in establishing this project ownership mainly 
arises from FM section leaders’ attitude. The Real Estate PMO department comprises 
project managers that are competent professionals and in the past they were involved 
in the management of large projects. As a result, mental barriers for FM section 
leaders can be created and their attitude towards the project managers may become 
quite reluctant. In other words, FM section leaders may hesitate to request, or even 
demand in some cases, changes in some of the project decisions that have been made, 
or in ongoing processes that do not follow the FM requirements. Moreover, according 
to Synne Remvig, conflicts about some design decisions might create tensions and 
frustration between the FM section leaders and the Real Estate PMO department’s 
project managers.   
However, according to the interviewees from DTU CAS, this ownership issue 
seems to be gradually solved, and acceptance, as well as respect, about each other’s 
role is becoming something incontrovertible. Essential factors towards this major 
change of perspectives have been the continuous enhancement of the FM section 
leaders’ ownership feeling, through continuous encouragement by the head of the 
Facilities Maintenance and Projects department, along with the development of the 
design standards that are described later in this chapter.

5.1.1   KT through Design Standards 
As it has been previously stated, the Facilities Maintenance and Projects 
department focuses on the preservation of a harmonic building structure within the 
Lyngby campus. However, this is not always easy to achieve, as sometimes the people 
that are involved in the construction projects do not share the same perspectives.  
The design standards that have been developed over the last years, according 
to Lisbet Sand, was a Real Estate PMO project managers’ request, who wanted to 
simplify the facilitation of KT from the Facilities Maintenance and Projects 
department. These standards are applicable to both Real Estate PMO department’s, 
and FM – Planning and Project section’s projects, and define the design requirements 
==PAGE:90==
 
68 
 
that have been set by the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department, having as 
main parameters the design consistency, the level of complexity and the cost of 
maintenance. For instance, a toilette standard aims to prevent designing different 
toilettes around the campus, while saving time. Moreover, the implementation of 
these design standards has a positive impact on the maintenance cost of the facilities, 
as dealing with technical failures in the buildings does not require different type of 
training or equipment. Apart from saving maintenance time for the Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects department, the design standards assist the project team to 
design buildings that meet the FM, to take design decisions that will be approved by 
both the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department and the board of directors, 
and to determine faster and more precisely the overall budget of the project. 
Moreover, according to Ole Kristian Bottheim, the development and use of the design 
standards has decreased the number of conflicts between section leaders and project 
managers. However, a balance between standardized and personalized knowledge 
should be found in order to be sure that the design phase will not turn into a stiff 
process, without any personal interaction between the two departments. 
The standards are distributed to the project team by the FM section leaders in 
the design brief phase of each project; thus, the KT through the design standards can 
be described as knowledge push (Figure 5.2). From this moment the responsibility 
regarding their implementation on the design of the project passes to the project 
managers. However, often FM section leaders discover that the decisions that have 
been made during the project phases are not compatible with the standards’ 
requirements. This can happen either because the requirements set in the standards 
could not be applied to the specific project or because the project team disagrees with 
them. In this instance, FM section leaders need to remind the project team about the 
implementation of the standards or, in case of disagreement, try to find a common 
ground (Remvig 2015).

The first design manual that had been developed by the Buildings 
Maintenance section leader was called ‘Building basis’ and included requirements 
regarding several design aspects. As the benefits of the standards application started 
to be visible, Synne Remvig started selecting different design elements included in the 
‘Building basis’ and developing detailed design standards focused on each of them.  
The production of the standards is a responsibility of the FM section leaders. 
However, due to their limited available time, it is not always possible to be developed 
by them. For instance, in the Buildings Maintenance section, the toilette standard was 
developed by a project manager from FM- Planning and Projects section, while 
currently, the production of a design manual regarding the main entrances of the 
buildings is being developed by an external company, provided with information from 
the Buildings Maintenance section. An obstacle when producing a design standard is

Therefore, the developer of the standard needs to be very careful when listing all the

one; after the final approval from the board of directors, the FM requirements are 
officially established, and they are unquestionable by the project managers (Remvig 
2015).   
The design standards are dynamic documents; therefore, they should be 
updated from time to time. As there is not a specified time frame or schedule, the 
standards are updated when it is considered necessary. This necessity, according to 
Synne Remvig, can be defined by different occasions. Sometimes, during the 
implementation of the standards, it is realized that there are elements which have not 
been included in the standards, or that some of the decisions that have been made are 
not efficient enough. For instance, during the production of the toilettes standard a 
decision about the toilette floor tiles was made, which afterwards was proved to be 
inefficient. The floor tiles had a long delivery time and this was generating delay 
problems to many projects; hence, the standard needed to be updated.

not being used.

5.2.1.1   Project web – iBinder  
The main ICT-based system that the Real Estate PMO department uses, in 
order to transfer knowledge and digital data from their projects to the Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects department, is a construction project web called iBinder. 
The use of a project web in building projects is something very popular in the 
construction industry and has increased over the last decade. The selection and use of 
the right project web as a mean of exchanging information and transferring 
knowledge between the project participants, can increase the efficiency of the various 
project processes, as well as the productivity, and benefit the project and the people 
involved in it (Andresen, Christensen & Howard 2003). 
iBinder is a web-based construction project management system, which has 
started being popular in the construction industry over the last years. Its logic and 
functions are based on a simple and user-friendly interface, which reminds a regular 
ring binder, and makes it a tool that can be used without any special training. It can 
support a project regardless its size, therefore it is being used for both small and large 
projects with different levels of complexity (iBinder 2015).   
The Real Estate PMO department uses iBinder as a platform that facilitates the 
KT from the projects to the members of the project team, as well as FM section 
leaders. Project team adds all the project material (drawings, documents, etc.) of each 
phase, to this system and subsequently informs the FM section leaders by e-mail 
messages, that they are available to be reviewed. The project web, apart from 
allowing access to the material, authorizes section leaders to comment on it (Bottheim 
2015).

5.2.2   Process methods 
It was mentioned above that the processes that are established within the

5.2.2.1   The Soft Landings framework 
Another tool that DTU CAS considers to start using regularly in the future, in 
order to assist the KT from the ‘Real Estate PMO’ department to the ‘Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects’ department, is the Soft Landings framework.  
The Soft Landings Framework is a quite new joint initiative between BSRIA 
(Building Services Research and Information Association) and UBT (Usable 
Buildings Trust) in the UK, published in 2009. It is a framework which refers to a 
strategy that is adopted to optimize the operational performance, by narrowing the gap 
between design intentions and operational outcomes; therefore, it minimizes strain 
and frustration that often follow the handover phase. According to the principles of 
the Soft Landings framework, the transition is important to be considered by the client 
from the early project phases of briefing and feasibility (not just at the handover 
phase) and to include the FM personnel, as well as the building users. In that way, 
realistic targets and expectations are set, the budget can be allocated more precisely in 
every project phase, and certain requirements regarding the smooth transmission can 
be included in the briefing document and the appointment agreements. These 
requirements include all the necessary information for commissioning, personnel 
training, FM and BIM. Moreover, extending the aftercare period with defect 
inspections, performance assessment and feedback helps the users to benefit from the 
building, and the clients, designers, contractors and managers to gain knowledge 
about what they have to do differently in the future.  
The implementation of the Soft Landings framework can be summarized with 
the following additions in five main project phases (BSRIA 2015), illustrated also in 
Figure 5.5. 
==PAGE:97==
 
75 
 
 
Figure 5.5 - The Soft Landings framework 
 
1. During the Concept and Briefing phase, the tasks and responsibilities of the 
different teams that are involved in the project (client, user, project 
management and contractor) should be established, and specifications 
regarding the building performance expectations should be set.  
2. In the Design Development and Review phase (including tendering and 
construction) the focus is on the implementation of the procedures that were 
established in the Briefing phase, on the assessment of the expected 
performance compared to the initial expectations and on the accomplishment 
of specific goals. 
3. The Pre-handover phase is constituted by the active involvement and 
interaction of the designers, the contractors, the FM personnel, as well as the 
commissioning and control consultants, to assure that the building is ready to 
be occupied.  
4. During the Initial aftercare phase, a Soft Landings aftercare team provides 
information and support so that the occupiers will be introduced in using the 
new building, and the FM personnel in operating and maintain its systems. 
Therefore, it is important for the occupiers and the FM personnel to be 
involved as much as possible. Furthermore, any problem or question that 
emerges during this phase is investigated by the aftercare team. 
==PAGE:98==
 
76 
 
5. The Aftercare phase, which runs in 1 to 3 years after the handover, is a phase 
that periodic reviews of the building are held. The FM department has the full 
responsibility of the building operation, and the aftercare team reviews the 
building performance and provides insights and advices to the users and the 
FM personnel, in order to benefit fully from it.    
  
As explained above, to implement successfully the Soft Landings framework, 
several activities should be adopted in different phases of a building project. DTU 
CAS is aware of the complexity of applying the framework to every current project, 
as both Real Estate PMO and, Facilities Maintenance and Projects departments are 
overloaded. Moreover, as aforementioned, the lack of human resources in the latter 
department does not allow them to use their personnel in every project phase. Hence, 
instead of applying the Soft Landings framework altogether, DTU CAS has decided 
to add building commissioning in some of their projects. 
 
5.2.2.2   Building commissioning 
Building commissioning can be defined as a process that guarantees the 
quality of the project, by ensuring that the building and its technical systems are 
functioning sufficiently. Moreover, it ensures that the performance expectations that 
have been set by the owner during the pre-design project phase are met.  
It is a combination of different activities, including the interaction between the 
building project participants; the exchange of documented information between the 
owner, the project management team, the contractor and the FM personnel; the report 
of the project defects and the performance failures compared to the original 
expectations, along with their remedies; the assurance that the owner’s expected 
quality is met, through systematic test and verification processes; and the assurance 
that the FM personnel is capable of maintaining the new building. Therefore, building 
commissioning can be described as a process that focuses initially on the end result, 
and then on the means that were used to achieve it.  
Although it is a rather new concept for the construction industry, 
commissioning has been used extensively in the shipping industry, where the risk of 
life lost if the equipment or technical system fails to function properly is high. 
However, the implementation of commissioning in building projects follows the same 
principles as in shipbuilding projects; the commissioning activities mentioned above, 
are incorporated into all construction phases, from pre-design to occupation and 
==PAGE:99==
 
77 
 
operation, in order to assure the expected level of project quality and performance. 
(Grondzik; California Commissioning Collaborative; Heinz & Casault cited in 
Ágústsson & Jensen 2012) 
DTU CAS is aware of the importance of transferring the knowledge that is 
created during a building project from the Real Estate PMO to the Facilities 
Maintenance and Projects department. They comprehend that the reuse of this 
knowledge can assist them in order to provide better maintenance services, as well as 
avoid future mistakes that have already occurred during the different phases of a 
project. Therefore, they have started implementing commissioning to their largest 
projects that are composed of more complex systems, such as the building 202, also 
known as the Life Sciences building.  
As people in DTU CAS have not been trained to do in-house commissioning, 
it has been outsourced to the engineering consulting company COWI A/S, which is 
providing them with a commissioning package that includes a commissioning leader 
and a team of specialized consultants. This external commissioning group, together 
with the project team and the personnel of the Facilities Maintenance and Projects 
department, perform all the commissioning activities.  
The biggest challenge for DTU CAS, according to Ole Kristian Bottheim, is 
the lack of human resources in the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department. 
Being occupied with all the maintenance activities, it is very difficult for the FM 
personnel to find time to work also with the building commissioning for the new 
constructions. An obvious solution to this lack of human resources problem would be 
to outsource entirely the commissioning process. However, this solution would lead to 
the exclusion of the Facilities Maintenance and Projects department from all the 
knowledge that is created during the commissioning process, as the knowledge would 
be absorbed by the external consultants.     
 
 
As described previously, Soft Landings framework aims to ensure that the 
design performance targets that have been set, are possible to be met during the 
operation a building. This is happening through an on-going process that takes place 
from the early project phases. The Soft Landings framework targets on the seamless 
transition from construction to occupancy; thus, the involvement of the FM personnel 
and the user group is necessary, assuring that their requirements are comprehended 
and taken into consideration. On the other hand, building commissioning can be seen 
==PAGE:100==
 
78 
 
as a sub-process of Soft Landings that focuses on the successful performance of the 
technical systems and subsystems of the construction. It is important to be 
implemented from the early beginning of the project as well, but the main 
commissioning activities take place in the Pre-handover phase, when the systems and 
subsystems are tested. As it focuses on more technical issues, it can be understood 
that the involvement of the future occupants is not essential during the building 
commissioning, whereas FM personnel needs to be included in order to be prepared 
for the future O&M functions, and absorb the knowledge that derives through the 
commissioning activities.

Chapter 6 
Discussion& Recommendations 
 
Following the literature review and the thorough analysis of DTU CAS, it has 
been decided to use the POKI management tool in order to map the current situation 
regarding KT within the organization. Therefore, the deficiencies and weak points 
that prevent DTU CAS from integrating the existing FM knowledge were identified 
and several suggestions that are pointing to the improvement of the KT level were 
made.  
 
6.1   The POKI management tool 
As explained in the third chapter, the POKI management tool aims to assist an 
organization in overcoming the usual barriers that the integration of FM expertise into 
new construction projects deals with. However, the use of extra tools is not always an 
effective way, and a change of the management mindset towards KT seems to be 
crucial. The POKI management tool approaches this change by involving processes, 
organization, know-how and tools, and information and communication.    
 
==PAGE:102==
 
80 
 
6.1.1   The Process 
In the fourth chapter, the phase-gate model that DTU CAS uses in every 
project was analyzed thoroughly. This model, which represents all the phases of a 
construction project, was developed by DTU CAS and enriched with interaction 
activities, so that the FM unit, end users and other important stakeholders will be 
involved in the project from its early beginning. A critical parameter in the ‘process’ 
element is the assurance that all project participants are aware of the common goal of 
achieving a user- and FM-friendly building.  
The data collected through the interviews with representatives of the Real 
Estate PMO department during the research stage, reveal that the Real Estate PMO 
department is gradually becoming more aware of the aforementioned parameter. For 
instance, the “gift metaphor” that Ole Kristian Bottheim used, along with the role 
distribution and the ownership issue that he has posed – described in the previous 
chapter – show how critical the development of FM-friendly buildings, that will fulfill 
the FM and user requirements, is for him. Moreover, the project manager that was 
interviewed underlined the importance and the focus on delivering a functional 
building that will serve its purpose and will satisfy its future users.  
However, even though the FM unit is involved in the processes, there should 
be a continuous reminder from the management of the Real Estate PMO department 
towards the project managers, about the importance of FM role in the project. This 
might be a challenging process, as part of the project managers in the Real Estate 
PMO department and all the advisors that constitute the project teams are external 
consultants; thus, they do not feel part of the organizational culture and they do not 
hold responsibility after the delivery of a building project. In addition, the project 
managers should be the focal point in the communication of the rest of participants, 
and they should be fully aware of the state of their projects at every moment, so that 
they will ensure that every decision made, agrees with the requirements of the FM 
unit. 
 
6.1.2   The organization 
The ‘organization’ element of the POKI management tool underlines the 
importance of including the right persons into the KT processes. The involvement of 
representatives of future users, FM personnel and technical consultants is considered 
to be essential. As described thoroughly in the previous chapters, DTU CAS ensures 
==PAGE:103==
 
81 
 
that all the aforementioned actors are part of the construction projects, by involving 
them in the different phases of the phase-gate project.  
 
6.1.2.1   Development of training programmes 
Several times during the interviews with the heads of the departments of DTU 
CAS, they seemed to doubt personnel’s competences regarding using the ICT-based 
tools that are implemented in the organization.  A suggestion that could help DTU 
CAS to improve personnel’s skills and ensure that everyone involved in the KT is 
competent enough to work effectively with the existing tools, is to provide them with 
the appropriate training. Even though training may seem to DTU CAS as a time-
consuming process, especially when they are overloaded with their everyday tasks, it 
should be approached as an investment. Introducing employees, which are involved in 
KT, to the existing tools and processes, will ensure the efficient use of these methods 
in the future. The three-stage framework suggested by Korsten (2002) could be used 
by DTU CAS in order to develop a successful training programme.  
During the first stage, the training requirements should be analyzed by the 
heads of Real Estate PMO and Facilities Maintenance and Projects departments. After 
assessing the current competences of their personnel regarding their ability to use the 
existing tools, the managers should determine the desired future level of these 
competences.  
The second stage suggests the translation of the training requirements 
identified during the first stage, into an action plan. The training requirements are 
prioritized, and a more detailed programme, which will indicate the preferred type of 
learning, should be developed. According to Korsten (2002) the ‘horizontal’ learning, 
such as learning from each other, constitutes a more sufficient way of learning for FM 
employees, compared to ‘vertical’ learning methods, such as top-down presentation of 
a programme. Furthermore, it should be mentioned that a training programme, apart 
from focusing on the development of competences and skills, should also assist 
employees to comprehend the benefits that they will gain through the use of the 
existing tools, as well as motivate them to use these tools extensively.  
The third stage focuses on the evaluation of the training programme, which 
usually cannot take place immediately after the completion of the programme, as the 
results need some time to be seen. What would be valuable for DTU CAS, after 
implementing a training programme, is to ask for feedback from the participants, as it 
==PAGE:104==
 
82 
 
has been noticed that often FM employees come up with useful or challenging ideas 
and suggestions about different ways of doing things. 
 
6.1.3   Know-how and tools 
As described thoroughly in the previous chapters, during the last years DTU 
CAS has developed – and is still developing – processes and ICT-based tools that help 
facilitating the transfer of the organizational knowledge between and within its 
departments. These KT methods and processes include the phase-gate project model, 
the design standards, the Soft Landings framework, building commissioning, while 
the ICT-based tools include BIM, DTU FM, iBinder, as well as the use of an intranet. 
According to Uffe G. Thomsen, head of the Real Estate- and Space Management 
department, even though the amount of the existing tools and techniques is sufficient, 
their effectiveness is often in dispute. Below some suggestions that could improve the 
functionality of some of these methods and tools, are presented.  
 
6.1.3.1   The phase-gate project model 
In the fourth chapter, it was discussed that Ole Kristian Bottheim believes that 
the current project model is applicable in the large and complicated projects of DTU 
CAS but quite inconvenient when it comes to smaller or less complicated projects. 
Hence, considering that the current project model represents a rather comprehensive 
process, a suggestion that could assist the effective use of the current phase-gate 
model is to scale it and develop two additional project models that will represent a 
medium and a small process. The small and medium process project model could be 
developed through a workshop that could be facilitated by the head of the Real Estate 
PMO department and having as participants the project managers from PMO1, PMO2 
and PMO3, FM section leaders and, optionally, a user group. Following, through a 
simple framework, like the one illustrated in Figure 6.1, each project could be 
categorized according to its size and its complexity level. As Figure 6.1 suggests, for 
small or large projects with low complexity, a simple process is recommended, 
underlining the importance of the complexity in a project. A project with small size 
but high complexity should adopt a medium process project model, while the current 
project model (comprehensive process) is recommended only when the project is of 
large size and high complexity.  
 
==PAGE:105==
 
83 
 
 
Figure 6.1 - Project categorization framework 
 
Thus, in the beginning of every project, the project manager that is assigned to 
the project will decide whether the project needs to be approached with a simple, 
medium or comprehensive process project model. Following, this decision should be 
approved by the head of the Real Estate PMO department and, if approved, the 
execution of the project can begin. Thus, when a project will be registered, for 
instance, as ‘large but of low complexity’, the simple process project model will 
assure that the different parties will not go through an extensive and sometimes 
tiresome process with unnecessary time- and cost-consuming actions.  
 
6.1.3.2   DTU FM 
DTU FM is a large ICT-related investment for DTU CAS. Although, as 
described in the fourth chapter, the benefits arising from its implementation are many, 
there are some implications that obstruct DTU CAS from using it to its fullest extent. 
According to Uffe G. Thomsen, not more than five people in DTU CAS are skilled 
enough to utilize it at its full capacity. Moreover, many project managers feel more 
comfortable to contact an external advisor than look for information in DTU FM. In 
order to put DTU FM into operation a two-step procedure is suggested to be followed.  
The first step includes the complete development of the system. All the data, 
information and knowledge that exist in all the different locations of the organization 
should be gathered, organized and registered into the system, which will work as a 
knowledge repository. As Synne Remvig explained for instance, the Buildings 
Maintenance department has already started inserting the existing knowledge into the 
==PAGE:106==
 
84 
 
system; however, the everyday work overload does not allow her to assign part of her 
section’s personnel to work on the set-up of DTU FM. A suggestion that could 
accelerate the use of DTU FM is to outsource the task of knowledge registration. 
Nonetheless, assigning an external party, which will not have any benefit of the 
system, to assist its development, is a quite risky decision. Therefore, and without 
employing more human resources in the FM sections, the Facilities Maintenance and 
Projects department should consider assigning full-time a small group (2-3 
employees) of each FM section for a short period of time, which after being trained by 
Dalux will fulfill this task. As it is obvious, this is not an easy decision to make and, 
according to Poul Henrik Due from Grontmij, management should be “brave” to 
make it. However, it should be considered as an investment that will benefit DTU 
CAS by accelerating the use of an important CAFM system.  
The second step is about informing the people that will benefit from using 
DTU FM – project managers both from Real Estate PMO, and Facilities Maintenance 
and Projects departments, as well as FM section leaders – about the knowledge that is 
stored in the system and how it can contribute to their projects, and afterwards 
providing them with sufficient training. The training programme should be developed 
as described previously in this chapter and involve representatives of Dalux as 
trainers. As it can be easily assumed, if the parties that will benefit from using DTU 
FM, are aware of the system’s functionalities and if they are skilled enough to use it, 
the chances to do it are higher.  
 
6.1.3.3   Development of checklists 
Another tool that could be adopted by DTU CAS is checklists that will assure 
that the most critical demands for each project phase have been satisfied. Maintenance 
checklists have been developed and have been more popular over the last years within 
the construction industry. For instance, during their study, Hassanain et al. (2015a; 
2015b) developed maintainability design review checklists about electrical and, water 
supply and drainage systems for campus maintenance departments in universities in 
Saudi Arabia. These checklists were developed to be used by design teams in order to 
reduce the occurrence of the most frequent defects. Checklists have been also used in 
major Danish construction projects, such as municipalities, hospitals or schools. 
Construction projects in DTU CAS could be divided in two categories; offices 
or teaching facilities, and laboratories. Even though these two categories have the 
same requirements regarding some of their characteristics, usually the focus is on 
different demands for each of them. Thus, it could be suggested the development of 
==PAGE:107==
 
85 
 
two different types of checklists, one implemented during the construction of offices 
and teaching facilities, and the other implemented during the construction of 
laboratories.  
The development of checklists is not a time-consuming process and can be 
outsourced to an external consultant. Figure 6.2 illustrates a simple procedure that is 
suggested for the development of a checklist. An external consultant along with the 
section leader and representatives of the respective section, arrange a meeting. During 
these meeting the participants brainstorm in order to come up with the most 
commonly met issues. Moreover, defects that are expensive for DTU CAS to amend 
or that generate additional operational costs if they are not designed in a specific way 
(e.g. the placement of the elevators), is important to be listed. A risk assessment from 
the FM department could also assist the identification of the most significant defects. 
It would be also beneficial if the representatives of the FM section are experienced 
personnel employed in DTU CAS for many years. Subsequently, the list with the 
most usual defects should be assessed and prioritized. During this assessment process 
it is likely that some of the points that the participants came up with in the first place, 
will be discarded. The list that will occur after the assessment will be the final 
section’s checklist. The same procedure is followed for the rest of the FM sections in 
order to develop checklists for each one. 
 
 
Figure 6.2 - Suggested procedure for the development of a checklist 
 
As mentioned above, these checklists assist project participants to identify the 
most critical requirements, but also serve as a basis on which more demands can be 
added during the evolvement of the project. These additional requirements may regard 
the expectations that the participants have set about the building project for the short 
future. An example of a suggested checklist is presented in Appendix C.

6.1.3.4   Implementing Life Cycle Costing 
Another tool that its implementation could benefit DTU CAS is LCC. LCC is 
a tool which provides a macro economical perspective of a construction, visualizing 
the consequences of the investment over time. As illustrated in Figure 6.3, the Danish 
Building Research Institute (SBi) suggests several tools that support the successful 
implementation of LCC during the life cycle of a construction project (Jensen 2014).  
 
 
Figure 6.3 - Tools for successful LCC implementation (Jensen 2014) 
 
In the case of DTU CAS, LCC evaluations can assist the decision-making 
during briefing and design phases of a project. As explained in previous chapters, 
sometimes during these phases decision-making becomes a frustrating process and 
tension is created, due to disagreements among project managers of Real Estate PMO 
department and FM section leaders. Thus, LCC can assist the standardization of 
decision-making process, reducing this way the conflicts between the two entities. 
Decisions during the development of the projects in DTU CAS are made by the 
project managers; therefore, they should be the ones using LCC for selecting among 
the alternatives, supplied with data and information from the Facilities Maintenance 
and Projects department.  
In the early 2000s, the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 
developed ISO 15868 (International Organization for Standardization 2002), 
regarding service life planning, with its fifth part addressing ‘Whole life costing’, later 
renamed ‘Life-cycle costing’. However, the necessity for a different standardization 
that fits better the cost classification and tools that are used in the Nordic countries, 
led to the development of a Nordic cost classification project based on the principles 
==PAGE:109==
 
87 
 
of ISO 15868, ‘LCC for byggverk’, by the Norwegian government’s building and real 
estate agency Statsbygg together with the Danish Building Development Council 
(Haugbølle & Hansen 2005). Thus, DTU CAS can launch successfully the 
implementation of LCC by following this Nordic cost classification. 
Alternatively, another new tool that can be used by DTU CAS, in order to 
support LCC calculations, is LCCbyg. LCCbyg, which was launched in spring 2015, 
has been developed by the Danish Energy Agency and it is provided along with an 
associated installation guide and a user’s guide, which details how the tool is used.  
 
6.1.4   Information and communication 
In the second chapter, the importance for an organization to establish an 
effective KMS, in order to benefit as much as possible from the existing 
organizational knowledge, was discussed. However, usually the management of an 
FM organization does not realize the necessity of forming a KMS, and therefore 
managers often have to deal with problems that occur because of the lack of a clear 
KMS. In DTU CAS, even though there is no established KMS, according Synne 
Remvig, a change in the organizational culture within her department regarding KS 
has been noticed over the last years.   
It is interesting to mention that usually in an organization like DTU CAS, 
where both construction projects and FM activities take place, two different mindsets 
are met; ‘hunters’ and ‘farmers’ (Johnstone cited in Jensen 2012; Johnstone 2007). 
According to Johnstone et al. (2007), a ‘hunter’s’ mindset is orientated towards the 
successful development and execution of high-profile projects within the provided 
time frame. In DTU CAS this mindset describes the project managers from the Real 
Estate PMO department, as according to the heads of the departments they are 
described as “butterflies” that after the completion of a project fly away to the next 
one. On the other hand, ‘farmers’ mindset represents a long-term focus, where 
continuous improvement and benefit through sharing knowledge deriving from past 
experience, are main goals. This mindset can be linked to the Facilities Maintenance 
and Projects department’s mindset, as they are more concerned about the operation of 
the facilities in long-term.  
The co-existence of these two kinds of mindsets within an organization like 
DTU CAS, and the development of a common KS-oriented culture is not dependent 
on the replacement of the hunters’ mindset with a farmers’ mindset, as it would be a 
hard and time-consuming process. The most effective way to combine them both in 
==PAGE:110==
 
88 
 
the same organizational culture is to find a balance between them (Bowen et al. cited 
in Johnstone et al. 2007). The development of a KMS can assist this effort and 
therefore can assist the development of a common organizational KS culture.  
 
6.1.4.1   Development of a Knowledge Management Strategy 
It was mentioned in the second chapter that an efficient KMS is the one that is 
tailored to a specific context of use; hence, it should be able to answer the questions 
‘What knowledge to share?’, ‘Whom to share with?’, ‘Why should knowledge be 
shared?’ and ‘How will knowledge be shared?’.  
A framework that has been developed to support the establishment of a KMS, 
which is addressing a specific KM issue within an organization, is the CLEVER 
(cross-sectoral learning in the virtual enterprise) framework (Anumba et al. 2005). 
Therefore, in order DTU CAS to develop a successful KMS, which will aim to the 
optimization of the KT between the Facilities Maintenance and Projects, and the Real 
Estate PMO departments, the use of the CLEVER framework (also applied by 
Ahmed-Kristensen & Vianello 2015), is suggested. The four stages in the CLEVER 
framework along with their goals and their outcomes are introduced in Figure 6.4. 
These four stages are supported by several tools (e.g. a problem definition template 
that assists the definition of the KM issue in the first stage) that are used by the 
participants. 
As the problem that will be addressed by the CLEVER framework in DTU 
CAS, involves two of the organizational units, it would be beneficial if individuals 
from both departments will participate in the procedure. The framework can be used 
by the participants individually or collectively. In the case of DTU CAS, due to the 
work overload of the organization, the collective way can be selected, through the 
facilitation of a workshop. The average time for the completion of the workshop, 
according to the implementation time of the CLEVER framework during its 
development, is half to two days. Apart from the paper-based version of the CLEVER 
framework, a prototype software (CleverKM™) has been developed.

6.1.4.2   Organizational culture and incentive systems  
As mentioned above, a big challenge for an organization like DTU CAS, 
where both ‘hunters’ and ‘farmers’ mindsets exist, is the way to bring these cultures 
together and make them co-exist and chase the same goals. To achieve this, the 
development of an organizational culture, which is built in mutual trust among the 
participants and is based on collaboration processes, is crucial. The two different units 
of DTU CAS, that have been analyzed in this thesis, need to develop a more holistic 
point of view, without focusing exclusively on their own success, as according to Poul 
Henrik Due “If you only focus on your own bottom line, then for sure it will become 
red”.  
The development of an organizational culture in DTU CAS, where KS and KT 
will be the norm and knowledge will be regarded as an asset to be shared with the 
others, is not an easy process and it cannot be done overnight. The people from the 
organization that were interviewed and participate in the KT (the project manager 
from the Real Estate PMO department and the FM section leaders) claim that over the 
last years the organizational culture seems to be changing towards a more KT-
==PAGE:112==
 
90 
 
oriented direction. In order to continue this change in the organizational culture, it is 
essential for the management of the organization to be engaged in some crucial 
activities. These activities comprise the continuous encouragement of the personnel to 
work together effectively, to collaborate and share, as well as the use of incentive 
systems that will assist and keep the participants motivated. 
The development of a KT incentive system within an organization is a rather 
tough process, as it usually involves the study of the factors that motivate the 
participants to share or transfer the organizational knowledge. According to Yang and 
Wu (Yang & Wu cited in Pemsel & Blomé 2011) individuals in an organization are 
usually motivated by competition, reciprocity, reputation, ego, satisfaction and 
organizational climate.  
Pemsel and Blomé (2011) conducted a research on real estate organizations in 
Sweden, which tried to identify what motivates the employees in the construction 
industry to perform successfully in their job, and proposed appropriate incentive 
systems. Their study revealed that the two fundamental motives of the employees are 
their eagerness to develop a construction project that will satisfy the client, and 
undertake more challenging projects. What is interesting in this study is that the 
employees are seldom motivated by improving their relationships with their 
colleagues or their consultants, while a possible increase of their salary does not 
constitute a motive at all. The recommendations regarding the establishment of 
incentives in this study, are based on the major motives of the employees; their 
reward in accordance with client’s satisfaction and their individual performance. 
Thus, it is suggested that DTU CAS should follow the same procedure by first 
identifying what motivates the main participants in the KT activities, and then 
proceed to the development of suitable incentive systems.

Chapter 7 
Conclusion 
 
This chapter provides an overview of the context of this thesis, which aimed to 
examine the KT between the building operations units and the construction project 
management in facilities management organizations. As the project-based nature of 
the construction industry makes the project managers of a building project rather 
unwilling to take into consideration the knowledge that has been acquired from past 
projects, the research was built on the analysis of three aspects; the knowledge that 
derives from the O&M of a building and how it is managed in order to be transferred 
and used in a future project; the benefits of KT and the barriers that prevent the 
integration of existing knowledge into a new project; and the existing tools and 
frameworks that facilitate the effective and efficient KT. The case company that has 
been analyzed, in order to provide a real view to these three aspects, is DTU Campus 
Service, the organization that is responsible for the FM activities within the DTU 
campuses, focusing on Lyngby campus.    
Knowledge is considered to be one of the most valuable assets within an 
organization; therefore, its effective management and reuse can assist the organization 
to enhance its competitive advantage, as it provides benefits to existing processes, 
employees, customers, finances and innovation. This knowledge can be categorized 
into explicit knowledge, which is technical and easily-codified knowledge, and tacit 
knowledge, which is personal knowledge, difficult to be extracted and codified. Thus, 
it can be understood that explicit knowledge is the one that is usually accepted in the 
technical- and science-based sectors, as it can be codified, stored and used easier than 
tacit knowledge. However, in order to exploit the existing knowledge, an organization 
==PAGE:114==
 
92 
 
needs to develop a cooperative behavior that will assist the KS within its departments, 
as well as the interdepartmental KT. This cooperative behavior can be supported by 
interaction and collaboration activities. Interaction activities include formal 
predetermined processes, such as scheduled meetings, while collaboration activities 
are based on the informal relations and aim to the achievement of common objectives. 
Therefore, interaction activities are easier to be established, compared to collaboration 
activities, as the former require the development of an organizational culture and the 
establishment of collective goals. For this reason, the formation of a KMS, that will 
aid the organization to achieve of the desired levels of KS and KT, is often necessary.    
The type of knowledge, whose sharing and transfer has been examined in this 
thesis, is the one that derives from the O&M of existing buildings and it is owned by 
the FM departments of the real estate organizations. According to the literature, the 
involvement of FM in a construction project from its early phases is of great 
importance. FM units can provide the project team in charge of the project, with 
valuable knowledge that supports the decision-making, ensuring that decisions with 
long-term benefits are made. Moreover, their active involvement guarantees that the 
decisions that are made take into consideration the FM requirements, assuring that the 
O&M of the future building will be efficient, in terms of financial and complexity 
perspectives.  
For the facilitation of this KT from the FM units to the project team, several 
tools and frameworks have been developed. Over the last years, ICT-based tools play 
the key role in the facilitation of this KT, such as web-based databases, digital 
tendering, project-webs, as well as BMS, BIM and CAFM systems. However, it 
should be mentioned that these systems just serve as knowledge depositories that can 
store huge amount of data, information and knowledge. A framework that has been 
designed to assist an organization to overcome the usual barriers that obstruct the KT 
from FM units to new construction projects, is the POKI management tool. This 
framework supports a change of the existing mindset towards the holistic integration 
of the FM knowledge into four aspects; processes, organization, know-how and 
information. 
DTU CAS is an organization that has been taking care of the O&M of DTU 
campuses for many years; therefore, it possesses huge amounts of knowledge that can 
be used in the new construction projects. Over the last years the importance of 
utilizing the existing FM knowledge has become apparent. For this reason, the 
management of the organization has developed and established different tools and 
processes that facilitate the sharing of the existing knowledge throughout the 
organization or within the departments, as well as the interdepartmental transfer of the 
==PAGE:115==
 
93 
 
knowledge, from the FM unit to the project management of the new constructions. 
However, during the research it has been discovered that DTU CAS has given more 
attention to the interaction activities, through the formation of a phase-gate project 
model and investing on the development of several ICT-based systems (BMS, BIM, 
DTU FM), without focusing on the collaboration activities within the organization. 
This lack of collaboration and universal objectives within DTU CAS creates several 
issues that lead to inefficient KT and frustration between the participants and impose 
the formation of a KMS.    
According to the people that have been interviewed during the research stage 
of this thesis, KT within DTU CAS is improving over the last years, respect from both 
parties involved in KT – project managers and FM section leaders – has been 
increased, and the personnel is becoming aware of the importance of the KT 
activities. To facilitate the KT from their department to the new constructions, FM 
section leaders have created several design standards, which ensure that the FM 
requirements regarding the new projects are set, and the project team should consider 
during the design phases. On the other hand, project managers ensure that all the 
available data and information that derive throughout every phase of their projects are 
communicated to the FM sections, through the use of an ICT-based project web 
(iBinder). Furthermore, KT processes from the building projects to the FM sections, 
such as building commissioning and Soft Landings, have been started to be 
introduced.  
In order to recommend some improvements that can assist DTU CAS to 
present more effective and efficient KT within its organization, the POKI framework 
was implemented. The recommendations are summarized below: 
 
Process 
 Continuous reminder to the project managers about the importance of FM role 
in the project, as well as being in the central point of the communication 
between the project participants.  
 
Organization 
 Development of training programmes that will assure that DTU CAS’ 
personnel is competent enough to use the provided KT-related tools and 
processes. 
 
==PAGE:116==
 
94 
 
Know-how & Tools  
 Project categorization regarding their size and complexity, and development of 
less comprehensive versions of the existing phase-gate project model. 
 Completion of DTU FM’s development and beginning of its operation. 
 Development of checklists which assure that the most critical demands for 
each project phase have been satisfied. 
 Implementing LCC in order to support the decision-making processes during 
the design phases 
 
Information & Communication 
 Development of KMS through the CLEVER framework in order to assist DTU 
CAS to address the KT issue. 
 Establishment of an organizational culture where KT will be a norm, and 
development of incentive systems that will motivate the KT participants.
