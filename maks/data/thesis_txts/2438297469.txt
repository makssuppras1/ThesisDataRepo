Abstract
Peptides play crucial roles in a wide range of biological processes, and they possess high selectivity
and potency towards their respective receptor. For this reason peptide drugs are of great interest in
the industry, but the rapid proteolytic degradation of bioactive peptides results in short half-lifes in
vivo, which limit their use as pharmaceuticals. Chemical modifications, such as lipidation, have been
shown to extend the half-life of these peptides. In the treatment of Type 2 diabetes, lipidation of the
truncated Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 (GLP-1) (7-36) amide peptide (1), an active insulinotropic agent,
has resulted in the formation of the once daily treatment Liraglutide. This peptide self-associates to
form heptamers in solution, resulting in slower absorption, and thereby its prolonged half-life, in vivo.
In order to explore the oligomerization of lipidated GLP-1 analogues, a small library of shortened
GLP-1 peptide analogues were synthesized by automated solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS). Fatty
acids to be coupled to the peptides included commercially available fatty acids, and fatty acids linked
by a L-γ-glutamyl spacer to the peptide.
These were synthesized manually in solution, and were
subsequently coupled to the peptide by automated SPPS. Getting the peptide analogues dissolved
with an appropriate viscosity was achieved by adjusting the pH of the peptide mixtures (pH>9).
Identification, purification and quantification of the peptide analogues were obtained by the use of
ultra-performance liquid chromatography mass spectrometry (UPLC-MS), high-performance liquid
chromatography (HPLC) and preparative HPLC.
For analyzing the secondary structure, and hereby possible oligomerization, of the peptide analogues
NMR spectroscopy was utilized. Two-dimensional (2D) NMR experiments, performed in conditions
promoting secondary structure formation, were used to obtain the full assignment of proton chemical
shifts within the structure of a shortened GLP-1 analogue without fatty acid-functionalization (Com-
pound 2). Data from the 2D experiments indicated the presence of a helical secondary structure in
the peptide analogue.
The assignment of Compound 2 was used to make general observations from the NMR data obtained
for five peptide analogues containing fatty acid-functionalizations. What was observed were the change
in chemical shift, and in some cases the complete disappearance, of resonances in the HHαNH cross-
peak regions of the 2D spectra. A full NMR assignment of all peptide analogues was not obtained,
but the observations made from the comparison with Compound 2 indicated the loss of the helical
secondary structure of the peptide analogues.

Resumé
Peptider spiller afgørende roller i en lang række af biologiske processer, og de har høj selektivitet og
potens for deres respektive receptor. Af denne grund er peptidbaserede lægemidler af stor interesse for
industrien, men den hurtige proteolytiske nedbrydning af biologisk aktive peptider resulterer i korte
halveringstider in vivo, som begrænser deres anvendelse som lægemidler. Kemiske modifikationer,
såsom lipidering, har vist sig at forlænge halveringstiden for disse peptider. I behandling af type 2
diabetes har lipidering af det afkortede peptid glukagonlignende peptid-1 (GLP-1) (7-36) amid (1), et
aktivt insulinotropisk middel, resulteret i dannelsen af én gang daglig behandlingen Liraglutid. Dette
peptid selvassocierer for at danne heptamerer i opløsning, hvilket resulterer i langsommere absorption,
og dermed dets forlængede halveringstid, in vivo.
For at udforske oligomeriseringen af lipiderede GLP-1 analoger blev et lille bibliotek af afkortede GLP-1
peptid-analoger syntetiseret ved hjælp af automatiseret fast-fase peptidsyntese (SPPS). Fedtsyrer, som
skulle kobles til peptiderne, inkluderede kommercielt tilgængelige fedtsyrer og fedtsyrer der var koblet
til peptidet via en L-γ-glutamyl spacer. Disse blev syntetiseret manuelt i opløsning, og blev derefter
koblet til peptidet ved hjælp af automatiseret SPPS. At få peptid-analogerne i opløsning med en
passende viskositet blev opnået ved at justere pH-værdien af peptidblandingerne (pH>9). Identifika-
tion, oprensning og kvantificering af peptid-analogerne blev opnået ved anvendelse af ultra-præstations
væskekromatografi-massespektrometri (UPLC-MS), højtydende væskekromatografi (HPLC) og præ-
parativ HPLC.
NMR spektroskopi blev anvendt til at analysere den sekundære struktur, og hermed mulig oligomeris-
ering, af peptideanalogerne. Todimensionale (2D) NMR-eksperimenter, udført under betingelser der
fremmer dannelse af sekundære strukturer, blev anvendt til at opnå fuld bestemmelse af kemiske
skift for protoner inden for strukturen af en afkortet GLP-1 analog uden fedtsyre-funktionalisering
(Compound 2). Data fra 2D-ekperimenterne viste tilstedeværelsen af en helisk sekundær struktur i
peptid-analogen.
Bestemmelsen af Compound 2 blev brugt til at lave generelle observationer ud fra den NMR-data,
som blev opnået for fem af peptid-analogerne indeholdende fedtsyre-funktionaliseringer. Det der blev
observeret, var forandringen i kemisk skift, og i nogle tilfælde den fulstændige forsvinden af resonanser
i HHαNH cross-peak regionerne i 2D-spektrene. En fuld NMR-bestemmelse af alle peptiderne blev
ikke opnået, men observationer gjort på baggrund af sammenligningen med Compound 2, indikerede
at den heliske sekundære struktur af peptid-analogerne blev tabt.

Introduction
Peptides are naturally occurring molecules which play crucial roles in a wide range of biological
processes in the human body. Bioactive peptides have high selectivity and potency towards their
respective receptor, and for this reason naturally occurring peptides and their synthetic analogues
have a major relevance to the pharmaceutical industry [1]. Peptide drugs reach the clinical phases
more easily than small molecules, since they are believed to be less toxic and are more predictable in
their in vivo behaviour [2,3].
Peptides are highly beneficial agents for treatment of diseases such as cancer and diabetes, but the
down-site of using bioactive peptides as pharmaceuticals lies in the rapid proteolytic degradation of
peptides in the body. This degradation leads to rapid clearance of the peptides. Besides this the
peptides are also limited by low membrane permeability and in some cases the peptides have low
solubility [1].
Studies have shown that the modification of biologically active peptide pharmaceuticals can prolong
their life-time in the human body [4,5]. The aim of this project was to synthesize and analyze a library
of such peptides to better understand the molecular mechanisms of this effect.
The peptides which were synthesized in this project consisted of shortened glucagon-like peptide-
1 (GLP-1) analogues containing different modifications. These modifications were functionalization
with fatty acids at one specific amino acid in the peptide sequence.
According to previous studies it has been found that the stability of such modified peptide pharma-
ceuticals can be explained by an oligomerization between monomer species [6]. In this project the
oligomerization of the peptide analogues was investigated by Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR)
spectroscopy and Circular Dichroism (CD). These techniques were used to determine the molecular
and/or energetic determinants that stabilize the formed oligomers.

Prolonging the Half-Life of Naturally Occurring Peptides
Peptides in general have short biological half-lifes due to proteolytic degradation, and this hinders
their pharmacological use [5,7]. Several different strategies have been developed with the purpose of
prolonging the biological half-life of peptide pharmaceuticals, and they include chemical modification
of relevant amino acids. These modifications can for example be achieved by utilizing peptidomimetics
or retro-inverso peptides. These strategies help in reducing the proteolytic degradation and thereby
prolonging the life-time of the peptide pharmaceuticals [5].
Peptidomimetics are molecules containing regions in the peptide structure which are altered compared
to the original structure. Examples of such molecules are sugar-based peptidomimetics. These peptides
have in their back-bone structure an implemented sugar moiety, which can confer peptide resistance
towards proteolytic cleavage [8,9].
Retro-inverso peptides have been designed with the purpose of avoiding proteolytic degradation of
peptides.
The structure of retro-inverso peptides is similar to the parent peptide, but the entire
structure is reversed. This includes the usage of D-amino acids and coupling these together in the
reversed order of the original sequence of the parent peptide consisting of L-amino acids. In theory,
the three-dimensional structure of the peptide should be similar to the parent structure, which would
help in retaining the biological activity of the peptide [10].
Other chemical modifications of amino acids include wholly/partly swapping the natural L-amino acids
with D-amino acids or functionalizing amino acid side-chains with fatty acids [1,11]. The disadvantage
of synthesizing peptides from L-amino acid is, as mentioned, their degradation by proteolysis. D-amino
acids can be used instead, with the purpose of improving the stability of the peptide [10,12]. Especially
fatty acid-functionalization has been utilized for the synthesis of pharmaceutical peptides which should
withstand proteolytic degradation [1], and the focus of this project was the synthesis and study of
such peptides.
2.1
Fatty Acid-Functionalized Peptides
Covalent attachment of fatty acids to peptides (lipidation/acetylation) is a widely used technique for
obtaining better pharmacokinetic properties of bioactive peptides, and it can be performed without
disrupting the therapeutic potency of the peptide. These lipidated peptides often show superior phar-
macological properties compared to their unmodified peptide analogue. They have for example been
shown to acquire higher metabolic stability and longer circulating time. One example is Liraglutide, a
commercially available acetylated GLP-1 analogue used in the treatment of type 2 diabetes mellitus.
Liraglutide has been shown to form oligomers in solution, hereby reducing metabolic degradation as
well as renal clearance. The agonist has also shown tendency towards interaction with serum albumin,
which prolongs its life-time due to reduced filtration by the kidney. Prolonged half-life has also been
suggested to result from the steric hindrance which the fatty acid employs against degradation by
3
==PAGE:22==
DPP IV [1,6,13].
Peptide lipidation have been seen to improve pharmacological properties of a broad range of peptides,
including peptides of different amino acid compositions, as well as varying position and structure of
the acyl group. This also suggests that the effect of lipidation may be different, varying from improved
propensity to form oligomers, to improved binding affinity to human serum albumin (HSA) [1].
These two generally accepted explanations for the prolonged half-life of Liraglutide in the body will
be explained in more detail below.
2.1.1
Oligomerization
Lipidated peptides have amphiphilic characteristics, and as such can form micelle-like oligomers in
solution. These oligomers are less susceptible to renal clearance and metabolic degradation (due to
their increased size), which is the main reason for the short half-lifes of natural peptides [1]. The
properties of the assemblies of these peptides depend on a series of factors, including the temperature
and pH of the peptide solution [7].
An understanding of the oligomerization of lipidated peptides is crucial in order to better understand
the increased life-time of these peptides. Also, it is important to ensure that the oligomeric state is
stable, since uncontrolled oligomerization can lead to fibrillation. A stable formulation of the peptides
can in turn result in longer shelf-life of the pharmaceutical product in liquid formulation [6].
Liraglutide has been shown to mainly form concentration-independent heptamers in a concentration
range of 0.004 - 4.501 mg/mL [6].
2.1.2
Serum Albumin Binding
Another explanation of the prolonged half-life of lipidated peptides is that the fatty acid functionality
on the peptides promotes albumin interaction. This happens due to the high affinity of fatty acids for
HSA. The binding to HSA is reversible/non-covalent, and leads to a longer circulation life-time, since
the filtration in the kidney is reduced [7,14].
Studies have found that the peptide oligomers do not bind to HSA. Only the peptide monomers have
shown affinity towards HSA, since it is the lipid entity that binds to HSA [1].
2.1.3
Fatty Acid-Functionalized Peptides as Drugs
Fatty acid-functionalization of peptides is not a general solution for the elongation of the biological
half-life of peptide pharmaceuticals, since the functionalization can lead to a reduction in the solubility
of the peptide drug. This will subsequently lead to reduced absorption of the drug in the body [14].
When considering the formulation of peptides as drugs given to patients, then oral administration is the
preferred method of drug administration. This method of administration makes it easier for patients
4
==PAGE:23==
to take hand of their own treatment, but it also put demands on the drug, as it has to survive the first
pass effect. The first pass effect is a phenomenon of drug metabolism, where the drug concentration is
substantially reduced when taken orally. Importantly, lipidated peptides have been shown to withstand
metabolism, which allow peptide drugs to be used as oral administration. Another limitation with
peptide drugs is their size and hydrophilic nature, which prevent them from being absorbed through
the intestinal epithelial barrier. Also here, lipidation is beneficial, as acetylation has been shown to
be capable of increasing the intestinal permeability of the natural peptide drugs [13,15].
One area where functionalization of peptides with fatty acids has shown to be of great importance is
in the treatment of Type 2 diabetes.

Diabetes Mellitus
Diabetes mellitus is a metabolic disorder, which results in elevated blood glucose concentrations, also
known as hyperglycemia. The disorder is characterized by multiple etiologies, the most prominent
being Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. These two types of diabetes will be explained in more detail in
the following sections [16,17].
Diabetes is a complex disease characterized by the overproduction of glucose in the liver and/or
the underutilization of glucose by different organs in the body, thereby resulting in an elevated blood
glucose concentration [18]. Diabetes is a major public health problem, with cases all over the world [16].
The incidence of diabetes in 2014 was around 8.5% on a worldwide plan [19].
People diagnosed with diabetes suffer from life-long implications, which differ from the type of diabetes
[17].
3.1
Insulin
Insulin is a peptide hormone responsible for stimulating the glucose uptake from blood in people with
diabetes [20]. It is produced in the pancreas in response to food intake, and its production takes place
in the islet of Langerhans by the β-cells. Insulin plays a key role in regulating the energy homeostasis,
and deficiency of the protein results in hyperglycemia [16,18,21].
3.1.1
Mechanism of Action in Muscle
The largest amount of glucose (85%), which is obtained through food intake, is used by the muscles,
and Figure 1 explains its mechanism of action in this part of the body. Initially, insulin binds to its
receptor, which results in the cross-phosphorylation of tyrosine residues on the receptor. This activates
the receptor towards binding with insulin-receptor substrates (IRSs), which are then phosphorylated,
hereby beginning the insulin-signaling pathway (1). The phosphorylated IRS-1 (in the case of Figure 1)
then binds to phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K) and activates it. PI3K then catalyzes the reaction,
forming phosphatidylinositol 3,4,5-trisphosphate (PIP3) from phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate
(PIP2) (2). This molecule functions as a second messenger, passing on the signal received at the
receptor. PIP3 then activates the phosphatidylinositol-dependent protein kinase (PDK) (3), which
then activates the kinase Akt (4). [18].
The activation of Akt leads to the phosphorylation and inhibition of glycogen synthase kinase, which
inhibits the glycogen synthase. This is one of the mechanisms, which results in a reduction of the
blood/glucose concentration [18].

3.2
Type 1 Diabetes
One of the two prominent etiologies of diabetes is Type 1 diabetes. The cause of Type 1 diabetes lies
in the autoimmune destruction of the insulin-secreting β-cells. This disease is referred to as insulin-
dependent diabetes, since people who are affected by the disease require the administration of insulin.
People are usually below the age of 20 when acquiring the disease [18].
3.3
Type 2 Diabetes
Patients with Type 2 diabetes typically acquire the disease later in life than patients suffering from
Type 1 diabetes. Type 2 diabetes is characterized by normal or higher levels of insulin in the blood,
but the patient’s cells have developed resistance towards insulin. Type 2 diabetes is the most common
metabolic disease in the world, and it accounts for approximately 90% of all diabetic cases around the
world. Type 2 diabetes can lead to a range of other serious conditions, including blindness, kidney
failure and amputation [18].
A common predisposing factor for the development of Type 2 diabetes is obesity [18].
3.3.1
Insulin Resistance
Metabolic syndrome is a clustering of medical pathologies thought to be a predecessor leading up to
Type 2 diabetes. The cluster of pathologies includes insulin resistance, hyperglycemia and dyslipidemia
[18].
In obese people the accumulation of fat also takes place in the cytoplasm, leading to an increased
level of diacylglucerol and ceramide. Diacylglycerols activate protein kinase C, which phosphorylates
8
==PAGE:27==
IRS, thereby reducing its ability to generate the insulin signal. Ceramide, which is a component of
sphingolipids, inhibits glucose uptake and the glycogen synthesis [18].
To understand insulin resistance leading to Type 2 diabetes, the mechanism with which insulin is
released needs to be understood:
In presence of glucose in the blood, the pancreas will respond by secreting insulin to the surroundings
(see Figure 2). Glucose only enters the pancreatic β-cells when the concentration in the blood is high.
The presence of glucose in the cells will lead to cellular respiration, breaking down the glucose, and
hereby generating adenosine triphosphate (ATP). The heightened ATP/adenosine diphosphate (ADP)
ratio closes the K+ channel, which in turn alters the ionic environment resulting in the opening of
the Ca2+ channel. The influx of Ca2+ ions causes the vesicles containing insulin to fuse with the cell
membrane, resulting in insulin release [18].

the K+ channel. The figure was adapted from [18].
When insulin resistance is developed in the muscles, the β-cells synthesize even more insulin in order
to force the action of insulin. This results in the formation of what is known as endoplasmic reticulum
(ER) stress. ER stress leads to mis-/unfolded proteins, and generates the so-called ”unfolded protein
response” (UPR), which is a signaling pathway responsible for alleviating the ER stress. A failure in
UPR will trigger cell death, resulting in Type 2 diabetes.

GLP-1 is a hormone consisting of 37 amino acids. The hormone exists in multiple biologically active
forms; of which the truncated forms GLP-1 (7 - 36) amide (1, Figure 3) and GLP-1 (6 - 37) have blood

suppression of the inappropriately elevated levels of glucagon secretion. Besides this, the truncated
peptide also promotes the feeling of satiety. All these physiological actions contribute to normalizing
elevated blood glucose levels [24–27].

4.2
Mechanism of Action
The primary cause of Type 2 diabetes is an impaired incretin function. Treatment with GLP-1 can
compensate for the incretin defect by reducing hyperglycemia in patients. Several studies on native
GLP-1 has led to the following primary mechanisms of action:
1. GLP-1 stimulates the insulin secretion from the β-cells of the pancreas and it decreases the
glucagon secretion,
2. GLP-1 delays gastric emptying,
3. GLP-1 induces the feeling of satiety [4,18].
It does so by binding to and activating the GLP-1 receptor found on the pancreatic β-cells, which
then exerts its regulatory functions, resulting in increased levels of cyclic adenosine monophosphate
(cAMP) and intracellular Ca2+. The increase in the level of these two species results in the exocytosis
of insulin in a glucose-dependent manner [22].
The insulinotropic effect of GLP-1 subsides once the blood glucose level is lowered, thereby reducing
the risk of hypoglycemia [22].
Besides this, GLP-1 also inhibits the apoptosis of the pancreatic β-cell, while at the same time stim-
ulating their proliferation and neogenesis to make up for lost cells [22].
4.3
GLP-1 Degradation by dipeptidyl peptidase-IV (DPP-IV)
When GLP-1 was first given as a subcutaneous injection, a very weak effect of insulin secretion
and plasma glucose levels was observed. This was due to the proteolytic cleavage of the N-terminal
Histidine (His) and Alanine (Ala) of GLP-1, resulting in the inactive form GLP-1 (9-36). The cleavage
was caused by the metabolic enzyme DPP-IV, which is found both in the blood stream and on cell
membranes [23].
Two options have been suggested for avoiding this proteolytic degradation, including the development
of synthetic GLP-1 analogues or GLP-1 receptor agonists, which are resistant towards the degradation.
Furthermore, designing drugs, which are capable of inhibiting the enzymatic activity of DPP-IV
has been suggested. However, since DPP-IV regulates the action of many peptides in the body, its
inhibition could lead to various side-effects [23].
4.4
GLP-1 Receptor Agonists
Drugs have been designed, which have a pharmacologic effect equivalent to that of native GLP-1.
These drugs are known as GLP-1 receptor agonists, and they function by reducing hyperglycemia and
body weight in patients suffering from Type 2 diabetes. This can be achieved, since the drugs still
have the same qualities as those of GLP-1, but with improved metabolic stability and lowered renal
clearance [25,30].

GLP-1 Analogues on Market
Various GLP-1 analogues have been successful in the treatment of Type 2 diabetes, many of them
being fatty acid-functionalized peptide analogues of GLP-1 [30]. In this section three of them will be
presented.
5.1
Exenatide
Exenatide was the first approved compound for treatment of Type 2 diabetes.
The compound is
a 39 amino acid-long peptide and was isolated from the saliva of the Gila monster. It needs to be
administered twice daily, since its biological half-life is 2 - 3 h after subcutaneous administration [24,30].
Exenatide is not metabolized by DPP-IV as GLP-1, since it contains a His and glycine (Gly) at the
N-terminus, making the site unrecognizable by the enzyme [23]. The antidiabetic features of exenatide
are similar to those of GLP-1, including its glucose-dependent enhancement of insulin secretion and
reduction of food intake (feeling of satiety). Some of the antidiabetic features may be mediated by
activation of the GLP-1 receptor [31].
5.2
Liraglutide
Liraglutide is one of the most successful treatments for Type 2 diabetes, proving the beneficial effect
of functionalizing peptides with fatty acids. The half-life of about 1 - 3 minutes for GLP-1 is extended
to 13 - 15 h for Liraglutide, making it a once daily treatment [14,30].
Liraglutide (see Figure 4) has 97% amino acid homology to the human GLP-1 (7-37) with only two
modifications. A palmitoyl group linked to a L-γ-glutamyl spacer is attached to the Lysine (Lys) in
position 26. In addition, the Lys at position 34 of the native GLP-1 sequence has been substituted
with an Argenine (Arg). This was done to allow selective palmitoylation at Lys26 [6,25].

Peptide Synthesis and Purification
Peptide synthesis is performed by selectively coupling together members of the 20 natural amino
acids (see Appendix A.1) or unnatural variants of these, using well-established and reliable coupling
strategies. Major improvements within this field now allow computer-controlled automatic peptide
synthesis for routine synthesis of peptide pharmaceuticals in the industry [33,34].
Peptides can be synthesized by solution-phase synthesis, solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) or by a
combination of the two, and they can consist of many differing reactive groups, which can complicate
the continuing peptide synthesis. These reactive groups are not only present in the side-chains of
amino acids, but also in their N- and C-terminus [35].
The focus of this report will be on SPPS, since this is the most common way of synthesizing peptides.

6.1.1
Solid Support
Solid supports (resins) used in SPPS consist of insoluble polymeric material, which needs to be swelled
in order to make the active site accessible. Many variations of resins exist and the choice of resin
depends on the synthetic strategy chosen for the specific peptide, as well as on the desired C-terminal
functionality. Different resins are designed to result in, for example, the peptide acids/-amides once
the peptide is cleaved from the resin. However, a special functionality (linkers) needs to be introduced
to release the peptide as an amide [37].
6.1.2
Linkers
Linkers are bifunctional molecules which anchor the growing peptide to the resin, and they are known
as being ”integral” or ”non-integral”. This means that they are either a part of the resin or have to
be attached to the resin in an initial synthetic step. The focus in this thesis will be on non-integral
linkers, as these are most commonly used and are known to result in better yields [2,35].
A broad range of linkers have been developed, which are cleaved under various conditions, including
acidic, basic and photolytic conditions. Besides this, linkers have been developed to prepare various
peptides, including peptides containing C-terminal -COOH and peptide amides [2,37].
6.1.3
Orthogonal Protecting Groups
Protecting groups are a necessity when synthesizing peptides, since a large amount of amino acids
contain reactive groups in their side-chains. Beside this, the amino-group on the incoming amino acid
needs to be protected to ensure the desired coupling. On this basis, a broad range of protecting groups
have been developed [33].
There are two main categories of protecting groups: groups that allow temporary protection of the
α-amino group on the incoming amino acid, and ”permanent” groups which block the reactive groups
in the side-chains. [35].
In SPPS, permanent side-chain protecting groups are usually removed at the same time as cleavage of
the peptide from the solid support [2]. All protecting groups should be cleaved under mild conditions,
so the reaction would not result in decomposition of the target molecule [33]. In Table 2 some common
protecting groups used in peptide synthesis can be seen.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy
NMR spectroscopy is a widely used method for characterization of molecules. It gives information
about the number of magnetically distinct atoms in a given molecule. Hydrogen-1 (1H) and carbon-13
(13C) NMR spectroscopy are the most commonly used NMR experiments [43].
When studying structurally complex molecules, such as peptides, by NMR spectroscopy, 2D NMR
experiments can be utilized to determine their secondary/tertiary structure [44]. The application of
NMR techniques to study the secondary structure of peptides will be explained in more details below.
7.1
One-dimensional (1D) 1H-NMR Spectroscopy
The structure of a molecule is elucidated from chemical shifts, integrals and J-couplings, being read
from the NMR spectrum [43].
In peptide chemistry the chemical shifts of NH and α-protons in the backbone can be an indicative
of secondary structure formation. In helices the chemical shift of these will move upfield from that of
the average for extended chain conformations (random coils) [44].
The following 2D experiments all utilize 1H-NMR homonuclear correlations [45].
7.2
2D Double Quantum Filtered Correlation Spectroscopy (DQF-COSY)
One of the principle experiments used for determining the molecular structure of peptides is DQF-
COSY. This method relies on the correlation between through-bond J-couplings, which lies within the
same spin system. In this case, spin systems consist of parts of a peptide sequence, which are divided
by quaternary carbon atoms. The correlation between the J-couplings results in the formation of cross
peaks between neighbouring protons [46,47].
DQF-COSY provides a greater clarity of the diagonal peaks as they do not suffer from broadness as
is the case in normal phase-sensitive COSY experiments [45].
7.3
2D Total Correlation Spectroscopy (TOCSY)
TOCSY experiments are also used to determine the intraresidue connectivities seen in a spin system
[46]. A TOCSY spectrum is obtained by applying a spin-locking field, which allows the spins to become
temporarily equal. This is called isotropic mixing, and it is a result of cross-polarization between the
bonds within a spin system [44].
Isotropic mixing generates cross peaks to the diagonal between all protons within the same spin
system. This makes TOCSY spectra very useful for the analysis of molecules containing more than
one spin system, such as peptides. If one resonance in a spin system can be determined, the remaining
resonances within the same spin system can be located by the use of a TOCSY spectrum. [47].
19
==PAGE:38==
7.4
2D Nuclear Overhauser Effect Spectroscopy (NOESY)
NOESY experiments give information about the distances between nuclei via cross-relaxation.
A
nuclear overhauser effect (NOE) can detect the distance between two nuclei which is useful for deter-
mining the location of an amino acid residue within a peptide sequence and thereby, for correlating
the separate spin systems. For this reason NOESY experiments are commonly used for obtaining
stereochemical information about a molecule [46–48].
In a peptide sequence the key resonances are those from the amide protons, thus it is important to
use conditions favouring these protons. This includes a solution with acidic pH, hereby minimizing
the exchange of the amide protons [46].
7.5
Sequential Assignment of Peptides Using NMR Spectroscopy
The assignment of small peptides by NMR spectroscopy requires the combined use of all of the NMR
experiments mentioned above, and the 1H-NMR lines corresponding to unique amino acid residues are
the first to be identified within a peptide sequence. A sequential assignment can then be obtained by
matching the peptide residues found by NMR spectroscopy to the independently determined amino
acid sequence. The procedure of sequential assignment goes through the following steps:
1. DQF-COSY and TOCSY experiments of the peptide are used for identification of the spin
systems of non-labile protons in the individual amino acid residues within the sequence.
2. From DQF-COSY and TOCSY studies, the amino acid spin systems of the individual residues
can be correlated with the labile amide/amine protons by identifying the mutual J connectivities.
3. Sequential NOESY connectivities are useful for determining which signals arise from protons that
are close in space, even if they are not bonded. NOESY experiments can hereby also be indicative
of the formation of a secondary structure in a peptide, however, they do not provide enough
evidence for the presence of folded conformations in dynamically moving peptide structures, since
the spectra contain the average information of all the conformational structures the peptide can
ensemble in solution [44,46].

When determining the secondary structure of a peptide it is also useful to know the distances between
intermolecular nuclei. These distances can be obtained from NOESY experiments. The cross peak
volumes in NOESY spectra can be converted into distances using the R−6 dependency of the volume
on the intermolecular distance (R). Programs utilizing distance geometry can be used to predict
structures, that are consistent with the observed distances obtained [46].
NOESY experiments will in this thesis only be used to qualitatively determine the secondary structure
of peptides.
7.7
Solvent Systems
In peptide chemistry, research has been focused on developing buffers that help bring the peptides
into solution, as well as aid analysis of peptides [50].
For determining the peptide back-bone and side-chain amide protons, the optimal pH-value is found
to be around 3. At this pH-value the exchange process between water and the amide protons are at
their minimum. If the pH is too high, then the exchange with water leads to a broadening of their
resonances, making them difficult to see in the NMR spectrum [46].
It has been found that the solvent system; 2,2,2-trifluoroethanol (TFE)/water stabilize the secondary
structure of peptides, especially helical regions. The stabilizing effect is caused by the preferential
aggregation of TFE molecules around the peptide, hereby displacing water, providing an environ-
ment which favours intramolecular hydrogen-bonding. TFE does not promote formation of helices in
peptides lacking the properties to form such structures, but it stabilizes nascent helices [44,51].
7.7.1
Solvent Suppression
The presence of water in a protein sample will complicate the detection of the protein resonances, since
the water results in such high resonances in NMR spectroscopy. There are two types of approaches
for achieving water suppression. One is to saturate (sat.) the water signal with a weak, specific pulse.
The other requires advanced use of pulse sequences with gradients. [44].

structure of larger polypeptides use near-UV CD spectra (320 - 250 nm) [52].

10.6
Purification of the Shortened GLP-1 Analogues
When the peptide analogues had been synthesized by SPPS and cleaved from the resin, the crude
product contained a lot of impurities which needed to be removed before the peptides could be ana-
lyzed.
Different techniques and machines were used for the identification and purification of the peptide
analogues synthesized in this thesis, and a few obstacles had to be overcome in order to get the
purification needed for the NMR studies.
10.6.1
Peptide Solubility
The main problem in the beginning of this project was to get the peptides into solution without
obtaining a viscous peptide mixture. If the peptide mixtures were too viscous, the machines performing
the analyzes could not collect the samples mixtures.
The first time a crude peptide analogue was tried to be dissolved, it was done in a mixture of MeCN,
DMF and H2O (11 mg peptide in 1 mL). The resulting mixture was too viscous for analysis. To
examine the solubility of the crude peptide, 4 × 20 µL of the viscous mixture was taken out, and a
few drops of different solvents were added to the mixture, with the purpose of seeing if they affected
the viscosity of the sample. The results are shown in Table 6.

The tests showed that the addition of base lowered the viscosity of the dissolved peptide analogue
significantly. To test this further, a new portion of crude peptide (20 mg) was dissolved in MeCN
and DMF, and then 0.1 M NaOH was added dropwise to the solution to observe when fluidity was
obtained. The pH of the crude peptide solution was monitored during this process by the use of pH
paper, and a pH above 9 was required to obtain a fluid peptide mixture. The total sample volume
ended up being approximately 0.5 mL.
These findings lead to the conclusion that every GLP-1 analogue synthesized during this thesis was
to be dissolved in a mixture of MeCN and DMF (as little as possible), and then the pH was to be
adjusted with the use of 0.5 M NaOH (pH > 9).

Mass Analysis and Purification
To validate the formation of each peptide analogue, UPLC-MS analysis was utilized. This analysis

to the mass of the GLP-1 analogues, then the UPLC-MS results could be used as templates for the
purification of the peptide analogues by preparative HPLC.
One to three portions of each of the shortened GLP-1 analogues (2, 3 and 12 to 20) were purified by
preparative HPLC, and the results are presented in Section 12.7. The yields obtained for the shortened
GLP-1 analogues ranged from 2 - 72%, and their purity ranged from 63 - 95%.

lower concentrations than the natural GLP-1 analogues, which made it more difficult to purify them
by preparative HPLC (the column used for purification required a loading of approximately 25 mg
sample).

to time-limitations the experimental data for this peptide could not be included in the thesis.

The loss of resonances
in these regions indicated the loss of secondary structure elements. This was explained by the fact
that interactions, leading to the stabilization of the amide proton in the peptide back-bone, were no
longer possible due to steric hindrance from the fatty acid. Using TFE as cosolvent helps stabilizing
the secondary structure formation of peptides possessing structural properties to form a helix, so
when peptides lack these properties, TFE cannot induce the secondary structure, and then the amide
protons are not stabilized by intramolecular hydrogen-bonding anymore. This leads to proton exchange
between the amide protons and other molecules present in the solution mixture, resulting in the
disappearance of the proton resonances [44].

Compound 12, 14 and 17 all contained a carboxylic acid in the terminal of the coupled fatty acid.
From Figure 20 it could be seen that these peptides did not show as great a downfield change in
chemical shifts as those of Compound 13 and 16. This could suggest that the presence of the carboxylic
acid had an effect on the conservation of the helical structure of the peptides. However, a conclusion
could not be made on the basis of these observations. The peptide analogues should be compared
pairwise to peptides containing fatty acids of equivalent length, with or without the carboxylic acid
end group.
Compound 16 (Figure 20e) was the most diverse of the peptide analogues analyzed, as it lost a large
amount of the HHαNH cross-peaks. Looking at the structure of this molecule, it can be seen that it is
the shortened version of Liraglutide, which is known to form heptamers in solution. So as explained
above, the reason why the amide resonances was lost could be explained by the rapid proton exchange
between monomer peptide units forming an oligomer in solution. This can however not be deter-
mined without supporting information on the oligomerization of the peptide analogues. Supporting
information can be obtained from SAXS studies, which are used for estimating the average solution
shape of molecules. SAXS studies can hence be used to elucidate the overall structure of oligomeric
species [63]. SAXS experiments has been performed by Ph.D. student Tine Maja Frederiksen on the
fatty acid-functionalized GLP-1 analogues, and the data obtained from these experiments can in the
future help elaborate on the observations made in this project.

Conclusion and Perspective
The aim of this project was to synthesize a peptide library, consisting of fatty acid-functionalized
GLP-1 analogues, and analyzing the solution structure of these using NMR spectroscopy and CD.
This was done in order to investigate the role of fatty acids in the oligomerization of peptides and to
determine the structural determinants that stabilizes the oligomerization of fatty acid-functionalized
peptides.
The experimental work of this project has led to the synthesis of a small library of 12 shortened GLP-1
peptide analogues, along with the synthesis of 3 relevant fatty acids linked to a L-γ-glutamyl spacer.
The synthesis and purification of these fatty acids were performed with satisfying yields.
Purification of the peptide analogues proved difficult in the beginning of the project, due to problems
concerning the solubility. The dissolved solutions were too viscous, but upon addition of base (pH>9)
fluid mixtures were obtained. UPLC-MS analysis of the crude peptide analogues confirmed the for-
mation of the peptides, and these results were used as templates for the purification of the peptides
by preparative HPLC.
The purification of the peptides resulted in yields varying from 2-72%, and UPLC-MS and HPLC
studies proved the purification and isolation of the peptide analogues with purities ranging from 63-
95%.
NMR studies were performed using DQF-COSY, TOCSY and NOESY experiments.
To be able
to assign and determine structural changes of the fatty acid-functionalized peptide analogues, the
shortened analogue without fatty acid-functionalization (Compound 2) was fully assigned from its
obtained NMR data. The analysis of this compound resulted in the observation of helix formation
throughout most of the peptide sequence.
Due to lack of time at the end of the project, Compound 2 was the only peptide analogue which was
fully assigned. General trends and observations were made based on the NMR data obtained for five
of the fatty acid-functionalized peptide analogues (Compound 12-14, 16 and 17).
The observations which were made from the processed NMR data, were the disappearance and change
in the chemical shifts of resonances in the HHαNH cross-peak regions of the 2D spectra. These changes
could result from conformational changes in the peptide analogues. Especially Compound 16 showed
diversity compared to Compound 2.
This could be explained by the oligomerization also seen in
Liraglutide, since Compound 16 is a shortened analogue of Liraglutide.
The observations made from the NMR spectra of the fatty acid-functionalized peptide analogues, led
to the conclusion that the helical structure of the peptide analogues disappeared, but nothing could be
completely determined without fully analyzing the obtained NMR data and without the supporting
data on oligomerization, which are to be obtained from SAXS studies performed by Ph.D. student
Tine Maja Frederiksen at DTU Chemistry.
51
==PAGE:70==
By analyzing the peptide analogues in different environments, these studies could lead to an un-
derstanding of which molecular and/or energetic determinants effect the stabilization of potentially
formed oligomers.
A future perspective of this project is to finish the full characterization of the different peptide ana-
logues synthesized during the experimental work described in this thesis. Besides this, the synthesis
of a larger peptide library is desired. This library should consist of shortened, as well as full-length,
GLP-1 analogues, and these should be analyzed using different pH-values and different solvent sys-
tems. The optimal study would be performed at physiological pH, in a solvent system similar to the
conditions met in the human body.
Data is desirably obtained from both NMR spectroscopy and CD experiments in the future.

This section describes the experimental work which was performed in the laboratory during the time

All starting materials, reagents and solvents were available from commercial suppliers, and they were

Chemical shifts of the different products are reported in parts per million (ppm) and the corresponding
coupling constants are reported in hertz (Hz).

a mixing period of 500 ms. Water suppression for all the peptide experiments was achieved using
excitation sculpting with a 2 ms 180 degree shaped pulse centered at the water resonance.
All NMR data were analyzed in the program TopSpinTM [62].
Calibrations of the obtained NMR-spectra were done using the residual peaks of CDCl3 (δH = 7.26
ppm), d6-DMSO (δH = 2.50 ppm), CD3OD (δH = 3.31 ppm) and TFE-d3 (δH = 3.88 ppm) as reference
for 1H-NMR. The residual peaks are the signal which appears for the proton of the isotopomer with
one less deuterium than the perdeuterated compound. CDCl3 (δC = 77.16 ppm), d6-DMSO (δC =
39.52 ppm), CD3OD (δC = 49.00 ppm) and TFE-d3 (δC = 61.50 ppm) were used as a reference for
13C-NMR [64]. Multiplets are reported as singlet (s), doublet (d), doublet of triplets (dt), triplet (t),
quintet (q) and multiplet (m).

12.2
Manual Fmoc-Deprotection
The peptide-coupled resin was swelled in DMF which was removed again by suction filtration. Then
20% piperidine (1 ml/100 mg resin) was added and frequently stirred for 5 minutes, before it was
removed by suction filtration.
The peptide-coupled resin was washed with 3 × DMF. Then 20%
piperidine (1 ml/100 mg resin) was added and frequently stirred for 15 minutes, before it was removed
by suction filtration. The peptide-coupled resin was washed with 3 × DMF, 3 × MeOH and 3 ×
DCM.
12.3
Deprotection of Lys(Alloc) in the Shortened GLP-1 Analogues
The peptide-coupled resin was dried in a vacuum desiccator overnight. Dry DCM (4 mL corresponding
to 250 mg resin), phenylsilane (0.3 mL, 32.418 mmol) and Pd(PPh3)4 (29 mg, 0.025 mmol) were added
to the peptide-coupled resin under N2 atmosphere. The mixture was shaken under N2 atmosphere for
1 h. The deprotection was repeated again, after washing the peptide-coupled resin with dry DCM (3
× 10 mL).
The peptide-coupled resin was washed with DCM (5 × 10 mL), 0.5% (v/v) DIPEA-DMF (3 × 10
mL), 0.5% (w/v) sodium diethylthiocarbamate-DMF (5 × 10 mL) and 1:1 DCM-DMF (5 × 10 mL).
12.4
Cleavage from Rink Amide-Linked Resin
After the synthesis was complete (including coupling with fatty acids), the peptide was cleaved from
the resin in a standard cleavage cocktail [55]: 95% TFA/2.5% TIPS/2.5% H2O (1 mL/30 mg coupled
resin). The mixture was shaken for 1-4 h depending on the amount of peptide-coupled resin being
cleaved.
The solution was filtered from the resin, and subsequently a milky-white crude peptide
precipitated upon addition of diethyl ether. The crude peptide was separated from the solution by
centrifugation. The extraction was repeated one more time to get rid of excess TFA cocktail.
