Abstract
In the modern world of civil engineering, the importance of sustainable materials cannot be overstated.
As such, cross-laminated timber (CLT) structures have become a crucial design choice for many civil
engineers when building structures due to their sustainable and structural advantages over other exist-
ing options.
This thesis is to studies in-plane forces distribution within CLT diaphragms, particularly focusing on
the distribution of horizontal forces to the lateral load-resisting system. The conventional methodolo-
gies for distributing shear forces are very complex and often rely on Finite Element Method (FEM)
models, which are time-consuming to create and analyze and susceptible to errors. In response to this,
the thesis proposes an approach based on machine learning, aiming to establish linear and non-linear
connections between reaction forces and diaphragm parameters. The study successfully identified key
parameters influencing force distribution and managed to predict reactions with impressive errors as
low as 2–3 percent when compared to FEM models. Notably, this thesis is the beginning of how math-
ematical models can be implemented in civil engineering, presenting a promising technique for future
applications. While constrained by a limited dataset from ROBOT, the mathematical methods exhibit
potential for practical use in structural engineering. Furthermore, by obtaining more data and imple-
menting more advanced modeling techniques while making sure to methodically and accurately deal
with each dataset by cross-validation and hyperoptimization, future errors will inevitably become lower.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In contemporary constructions, Cross-Laminated Timber(CLT) has emerged as a sustainable material
with unique structural advantages for a variety of applications [2]. It is a sustainable material that is
lightweight and flexible, showing favorable performance as a structural material.CLT offers numerous
applications as a building material, diaphragms being one of them. CLT diaphragms play a decisive
role in transferring the lateral loads within a building structure [15]. Understanding the in-plane distri-
bution of forces is essential for designing a robust and resilient structure. The project delves into the
intricacies of timber diaphragm, focusing on developing a simplified and accurate method to predict
the reaction on the walls.
1.1
Cross-laminated Timber
Cross-laminated timber (CLT) stands out as an innovative engineered wood panel product, made from
gluing together at least three layers of solid-sawn lumber. The layers are arranged perpendicularly to
the adjacent layers of boards. For structural advantages, it is common to have an odd number of layers.
The CLT panel can be built with large spans, from a minimum of 0.9 meters to 3 meters. Their lengths
typically fall within the range of 6 to 18 meters.
The material is known for exceptional seismic performance, substantial load-bearing capacities, and
fire resistance. It is also a sustainable material and energy-eﬀicient to build. CLT is biodegradable,
reducing the carbon footprint. Its lightweight nature allows for more eﬀicient transportation and use
in construction, minimizing environmental impact.

1.2
Diaphragm definition
Diaphragms serve as crucial components within the structural framework of buildings, playing a pivotal
role in transferring horizontal loads to the lateral load-resisting system. According to Wallner-Novak
et al.[16], diaphragms act as integral unifying elements that stabilize together all structural and non-
structural components, thereby contributing to the overall integrity of the building. While floors and
roofs are primarily engineered for vertical load support, they inherently possess in-plane strength and
stiffness, facilitating diaphragm action. Despite this, a prevalent misconception assumes that all di-
aphragms are universally rigid and robust, capable of withstanding arbitrary loads and deformations
without repercussions. This misconception persists due to deficiencies in design codes and literature,
where the intricacies of diaphragm design are often overlooked.
Floor diaphragms are a part of the basic load-bearing elements; as Canadian standards state [17], their
main function is to transfer horizontal loads, such as wind action or seismic forces, to the shear walls.
The diaphragms usually have a rigid behavior as the main material is reinforced concrete. However,
when the material shifts to wood, such as in the case of CLT floor diaphragms, the dynamics of di-
aphragm behavior undergo a notable transformation. Timber introduces a unique set of characteristics,
including material flexibility and connection properties, which can influence the diaphragm’s response
to lateral loads. Therefore, the rigid behavior is undermined, and the diaphragm has a certain flexibility,
which makes the distribution of forces an intricate and complex problem.

1.3
Current research
The investigation of Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) diaphragms is a prominent subject within timber
and structural engineering. Existing literature reveals a multitude of studies focused on understand-
ing the intricate behavior of CLT diaphragms under various loading conditions. Current methods for
predicting reactions often involve complex numerical analyses, such as Finite Element Models (FEM).
While the FEM is the most accurate tool used in the industry, is also resource-intensive and may slow
down the eﬀiciency of structural design processes.
Various analysis methods for timber diaphragms are meticulously discussed in comprehensive work by
Moroder, D. (2016) [11]. One highlighted approach described is ”The equivalent truss method for mas-
sive timber diaphragms”, where each panel is defined by the equivalent stiffness of the sheathing panel
connected to framing elements via flexible fasteners. The method allows a very precise calculation of
the shear wall reactions without using FEM software, however, this method requires a very long and
complex hand calculation.
Several researchers have explored simplified approaches to address this complexity, emphasizing the
need for practical, simplified solutions. A notable example is described in ”Lukacs In plane capacity
and stiffness of floor diaphragms” called deep beam analogy. It simplifies the complex behavior of the
diaphragms by likening them to deep beams. The analysis focuses on key parameters such as shear
span-to-depth ratios, offering a more straightforward and practical means of predicting reaction dis-
tributions. However, the method lacks complexity; therefore, only for straightforward layouts can the
method be applied and still some complex hand calculation has to be carried out to get the result.
The stiffness of the diaphragm becomes a key factor influencing the behavior of the structure as either
flexible or rigid. The flexibility of the floor diaphragm introduces notable changes to structural behavior,
particularly in altering the load distribution between shear walls. The Eurocode 8 [13] and ASCE 07-10
[1] define a diaphragm as rigid when its midpoint displacement is less than twice the walls’ displace-
ments. The properties of the connection between CLT panels have a great impact on the definition, the
behavior of the connection has been studied, as shown in several studies [6],[9]. Many studies such as
”Rigid or Flexible? A Numerical Investigation on the In-Plane Behaviour of CLT Floor Diaphragms”
studied the intricacy of such categorization and provided simple, straightforward boundaries between
these cases [5]. However, further studies are needed to draw conclusive findings on this matter.
One aspect that is key in the definition of the diaphragm is the influence of the connection. Typically,
screws connect adjacent Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) panels at interfaces such as wall-wall or wall-
floor. Numerous studies have investigated the load-deformation behavior and ultimate load capacity
of these connections, exploring various alignments of screws and panels [10],[7],[8],[14]. Their findings
underscore significant contributions to deformation arising from connections such as steel-steel, steel-
timber, and timber-timber. The importance of the connection is illustrated in a study done by D’Arenz
et al. (2019), suggesting that is the leading factor for the diaphragm deformation [4].
The study of Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) diaphragms holds a significant position in timber and
structural engineering. The existing simplification methods, such as the multiple spring method de-
scribed in work done by Chen at al.(2014), provided inaccurate results for distributing forces and are
ineﬀicient for design purposes [3]. Relying mainly on Finite Element Method (FEM) software, exploring
numerical analyses could offer simpler methods for in-plane force distribution in diaphragms, providing
crucial insights into the factors influencing force distribution.

1.4
Problem Formulation
The calculation of load distribution within Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) diaphragms poses a chal-
lenge, as evident from the analysis methods outlined in Moroder’s study (2016), table 3.3 [11]. The
existing literature underscores the absence of simplified analyses for the structural design of construct-
ing wood diaphragms.
Despite the complexity of the assumption of rigid and flexible diaphragms,
this project aims to provide analytical expressions to distribute in-plane reactions to the Lateral Load
Resisting System (LLRS). Through parametric studies conducted via Finite Element Method (FEM)
models and mathematical analyses, this thesis aims to create a straightforward and reliable solution for
predicting reactions to the shear walls.

CHAPTER 2
Methods
The investigation into the in-plane force distribution within a CLT diaphragm relies on the most ad-
vanced and effective methods currently available. By identifying the factors that might influence the
reaction to LLRS, the research aims to study each importance, using the capabilities of finite element
method (FEM) design to capture intricate details. Mathematical models will serve as indispensable
tools in crafting analytical expressions that encapsulate the complex behavior of CLT diaphragms un-
der horizontal forces. This comprehensive approach focuses on contributing significant insights to the
realm of timber structural engineering, providing valuable knowledge for the design and analysis of
these innovative structural elements.
2.1
Finite Element Method Choice
To achieve precise predictions of force distribution within the lateral load-resisting system, a preferred
methodology involves leveraging Finite Element Method (FEM) software. This approach ensures a
high level of precision in the analysis. Notably, Robot Structural Analysis emerges as a prominent and
widely acknowledged software within the industry for structural design. Its extensive toolset proves
invaluable for constructing realistic simulations across diverse structures. The software’s capabilities
span multiple material choices, providing flexibility in geometry and incorporating a wide array of func-
tions to effectively model the intricate details inherent in designing wood diaphragms. The utilization
of such advanced software contributes to a comprehensive understanding of the structural dynamics
under lateral loads.
2.1.1
Model Validation
Before proceeding with specific analyses, it is essential to have a clear understanding of the fundamental
functions of ROBOT. Modeling a timber diaphragm poses a challenging task. While assessing how the
Finite Element Method (FEM) behaves in modeling complex structures is intricate, it is more sustain-
able to conduct basic checks to confirm the reliability of numerical analyses. Creating a realistic model
in ROBOT FEM analysis requires making certain assumptions. Therefore, gaining a thorough under-
standing of the software’s assumptions before configuring the model is crucial. This preliminary insight
into the software’s functionalities enables more precise decision-making when selecting the appropriate
functions for an accurate study. Various critical aspects of the model have been rigorously tested and
thoroughly investigated to enhance trust for future analyses.
2.1.2
Boundary conditions
The boundary condition is examined to determine if the diaphragm is effectively modeled. To approach
the complexity of the problem, the walls are represented as an elastic spring, with the elastic spring
modulus serving to illustrate the physical properties of the wall. Several analyses were conducted, sim-
plifying the diaphragm as a rigid body function. The objective is to verify if the Finite Element Method

2.1.3
Material properties
Perhaps one of the more challenging tasks is incorporating Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) panels into
the analysis. Given that this material is relatively less common compared to concrete or steel, to recre-
ate the industry standards, the guidelines from Stora Enso are utilized. The material properties are
determined based on the stiffness matrix of the material, taking into consideration components such as
flexural stiffness, shear stiffness, and axial stiffness in the plane.

against calculations for existing panels. The method for calculating material deflections is outlined in
Moroder’s thesis [11]. In simplifying the problem, the connection aspect was omitted, focusing solely
on two components for deflection: bending and shear.

2.2
FEM Analysis Choice
The objective of this study is to analyze and predict the distribution of in-plane forces within timber
diaphragms. Given the vastness of this problem, setting certain limitations provides a starting point
for the study. To accomplish this goal, several basic models are chosen, with a primary focus on estab-
lishing a robust correlation between reactions and key parameters that exert a significant influence on
shaping the in-plane force distribution.
Informed by both primary observations and a comprehensive literature review, the following param-
eters will be assessed in this study:
2.2.1
Geometry
In the context of building structures, one of the most commonly employed geometries is rectangular,
where the ratio between length and width is typically set around 1.8. This standardized aspect ratio
serves as a prevalent configuration in construction practices, providing a balanced and practical foun-
dation for architectural design and structural stability.
2.2.2
Force application
The applied force is assumed from wind action following the guidelines outlined in EN1991-1-4 Wind
Actions. In this scenario, the wind is conceptualized as a uniformly distributed load, specifically for
zones D and E. In ROBOT Structural Analysis, this uniform load is implemented as a member load,
with the magnitude expressed in kN/m. To accurately simulate this wind-induced force, a timber bar is
introduced, serving as the designated element to receive the distributed load. It’s noteworthy that the
timber bar is assigned no stiffness, ensuring that its presence does not influence the structural response
within the model.

2.2.3
Number of panels
Naturally, one important element in wood diaphragms represents the number of panels along the struc-

the width falls within the range of 2 to 3 meters.
Given that ROBOT is not a code-based software, the facilitation of geometry alterations, including
panel design adjustments, is created through the utilization of Dynamo. This Dynamo is compatible
with ROBOT, providing several advantages, notably the ability to modify the geometry, thereby en-
hancing the eﬀiciency of the overall process.

2.4.7
Standardization
Standardization is a common practice in multiple regression problems, serving various purposes. The
process is used to rescale and center the variables in a data set, bringing them to a common scale. This
process transforms variables to have a mean value of 0 and a standard deviation of 1. By equalizing
the differences between scales, it guarantees that each variable contributes equally to the model.
The formula for standardization used is:
Xstd = Xn −µ
σ
(2.14)
Where:
• Xn- the original value of the variable.
• Xstd - the standardized value.
• µ - the mean of the variable.
• σ- the standard deviation of the variable.
This equalization is particularly advantageous for enhancing the interpretability of each factor.
It
simplifies the process of drawing conclusions and understanding which aspect of the problem plays a
leading role. Without standardization, variables with larger scales might dominate the learning process.
Secondly, standardization aids in the interpretation of regression coeﬀicients. When applying transfor-
mations to variables, the resulting transformed variables might have different units and scales. Stan-
dardization ensures that these transformed variables are on a common scale, making it easier to compare
the relative importance of each feature in the model.
Moreover, through standardization, concluding becomes more straightforward as the process equalizes
contributions on the same scale. This clarity facilitates the identification of the dominant factors in the
analysis, offering enhanced interpretability of the model’s outcomes.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
4.1
Model Limitation
It is noteworthy that ROBOT, despite its extensive utilization, comes with its own set of limitations.
The software’s non-code-based nature restricts numerous possibilities when aiming to thoroughly inves-
tigate a specific model. From a mathematical perspective, the amount of data is directly proportional to
the depth of findings in this field. Manually storing data is not the optimal solution. It also leaves room
for potential errors. Opting for software that facilitates easy parameter adjustments and automated
result recording would significantly enhance the value of the study.
While the mathematical models employed in this study offer valuable insights into the prediction of
reactions in multi-wall diaphragms, several limitations should be acknowledged. The linear regression
models, while providing a foundational understanding, may oversimplify the complex behavior of tim-
ber diaphragms under varying conditions.
Despite the incorporation of transformations to address
non-linearity, certain nuances of the diaphragm’s response remain elusive. As seen in the variation of
errors across different support directions, the models exhibit sensitivity to the underlying assumptions
and may struggle to capture the full intricacies of the diaphragm’s behavior. Additionally, the minimal
impact of certain parameters, such as the number of panels and stiffness of connections, on force distri-
bution raises questions about the comprehensiveness of the chosen predictors.
4.2
Conclusion
This paper presents a comprehensive investigation into the in-plane force distribution of CLT di-
aphragms.
As existing methods heavily rely on Finite element models, alternative methods are in-
vestigated in this research. Mathematical models are implemented to find analytical expressions to
calculate reaction forces on the shear walls based on the data set provided by ROBOT.
The study identified several parameters, like wall and connection stiffness, geometry, panel number,
and geometry, to be the parameters to shape the outcome of the lateral load-resisting system (LLRS).
Three experiments are analyzed in ROBOT, investigating how each parameter affects the total response.
Additionally, this study emphasizes the significance of each parameter, highlighting that the primary
factors shaping reactions are the stiffness of the walls and geometry, the ones used to predict the reac-
tion through the use of the rigid diaphragm method. Meanwhile, the stiffness of the connection and the
number of panels offer little influence. The mathematical model shows that the relation between the
chosen parameters has a non-linear relationship. The logarithmic function tends to describe it better
than other transformations in their interaction. Looking ahead, acquiring more extensive data with
more parameters of interest and implementing advanced modeling techniques, coupled with meticulous
cross-validation and hyperoptimization, holds the promise of significantly reducing errors in future ap-
plications.

4.3
Future investigation
Future research endeavors may consider incorporating additional parameters to enrich the analysis of in-
plane force distribution in timber diaphragms. Introducing factors such as openings in the diaphragm,
diverse load applications, or dynamic loading scenarios could provide valuable insights into the intri-
cate dynamics of force distribution within timber structures. Exploring these aspects would contribute
to a more comprehensive understanding of the behavior of timber diaphragms under varying conditions.
A prospective study of significant interest involves a detailed exploration of the definitions, specifically
focusing on distinguishing between flexible and rigid diaphragm types and clarifying their unique char-
acteristics. Incorporating the consideration of deflection into these investigations would only increase
accuracy. In addition, it will also contribute to a more comprehensive analysis, capturing the nuanced
behavior and responses associated with different diaphragm types. This approach aims to provide a
deeper understanding of the diverse aspects of timber diaphragms, paving the way for more informed
and precise structural analyses in the future.
A future consideration would be to incorporate cross-validation for optimization of the hyper-parameters.
Cross-validation ensures that the model’s performance is ROBOT for different subsets of the data. Also,
the use of other mathematical models such as Decision Tree Regressor or XGB Regressor is left for fur-
ther research.
