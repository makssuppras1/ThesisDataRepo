Abstract
A multitude of methodologies exist in order to manage, monitor and execute business
processes, consisting of sequences of events. One of these is the family of declarative
languages known as DECLARE. DECLARE and its variants, provide a set of patterns
formally grounded in Temporal Logics, in order to constrain the behaviour of a process.
However, processes not or only partially comprised of sequences of events, cannot be
fully captured. Such processes can be commonly found in CyberВӯPhysical Systems. SigВӯ
nal Temporal Logic is a temporal language utilized by CPS designers to specify and monВӯ
itor these complex systems. Since the first publication on STL, various dialects have
emerged, including STLВӯMX and STL*. STLВӯMX integrates components for digital and
analogue systems in a manner that allows to separate discrete and denseВӯtime elements.
STL* focuses on the representation of properties like Oscillations, which require referencВӯ
ing past values of a signal.
This thesis proposes a STLВӯMX*, a combination of the two STL dialects, motivated by the
desire to represent processes entailing signals, as well as discrete events. Subsequently,
this logic is used to formalize a version of DECLARE called SigCLARE, which can be
used to encode denseВӯtime and realВӯvalued signals. The DECLARE constraint templates
are adopted, and a set of templates for new elements called States are introduced. The
application of the language is illustrated by a running example.

1.1
Business Processes and their modelling languages
In the Age of Industry 4.0, with topics such as Connectivity, Analytics and Automation
being one of the top strategic priorities for a majority of companies [1], it is no surprise that
the operations of Organizations have increased in complexity. This issue is exacerbated
by the growing size of companies, wherein companies with higher revenue tend to focus
more processes [2].
One methodology proposed to tackle this increase in complexity is Business Process
Management (BPM), which tries to structure and document key aspects of operation. The
importance of BPM is further emphasized by including a вҖқProcess ApproachвҖқ as a core
quality management principle, on which the ISO 9001:2015 certification is based upon
[3]. BPM is a useful tool for overall business improvement by establishing a framework to
consciously and continuously improve the organization.
The Association for Business Process Management Professionals defines BPM as вҖқ[...]
a disciplined management approach to identify, design, execute, document, measure,
monitor, and control both automated and nonВӯautomated business processes to achieve
consistent, targeted results aligned with an organizationвҖҷs strategic goalsвҖқ [4].
A Business Process can be defined as вҖң[вҖҰ] a continuously repeated set of activities with
a defined startВӯ and endpoint. It processes information as a mean to generate an OutputвҖқ
[5], or more loosely as вҖңa collection of activities that takes one or more kinds of input and
create an output that is of value to the customerвҖқ [6]. An even more abstract definition,
as proposed by Dumas describes Processes as [...] вҖқwhat companies do whenever they
deliver a service or a product to customersвҖқ [7]. The term Business Process might be
overВӯspecified, as Processes are not strictly limited to business contexts. In fact, they
occur almost anywhere!
The omnipresence of processes demands a structured approach for representation and
management. Intuitively, a process could be depicted graphically Вӯ in the sequential orВӯ
dering in which the activities occur. This allows Users to gain a visual overview of the
process and easily interpret and understand the relations. The standard Business ProВӯ
cess Model and Notation (BPMN), as defined as by the Object Management Group, has
established itself as one of the most common Process Modelling Standards. BPMN enВӯ
compasses more than graphical elements, as it also regulates the interaction with other
systems, which made it possible to use BPMN models as Execution Engines in WorkВӯ
flowmanagementsystems (WfMS). BPMN Models are imperative Models 1. These type
of models describe the behaviour which is explicitly allowedНҫ all execution sequences
which do not adhere to this strict definition are not permitted. This works well for highlyВӯ
structured processes, however it runs the risk of overВӯspecification [8].
Declarative languages have been proposed as an alternative for looselyВӯstructured proВӯ
cesses requiring a high degree of flexibility [8]. DECLARE is one of the widely adopted
declarative languages and is a patternВӯbased language, which employs sets of constraints
for model definition. The constraints can be chosen from a wide range of constraint temВӯ
plates, which are formally defined using Linear Temporal Logic over finite traces (LTLf).
LTLf is a formal logic system used for specifying and reasoning about temporal properties

1.2
CyberВӯPhysical Systems
Technological advancement allows for the creation of increasingly complex systems. CyberВӯ
Physical systems (CPS) combine analog and digital components in order to create selfВӯ
regulating systems [9]. Communication and controller devices are deeply embedded into
the physical components [10]. This enables novel applications in various domains, inВӯ
cluding topics such as unmanned vehicles, automated health care, smart electricity grids
[11] or robotic networks [9]. These represent domains, in which safety, security and susВӯ
tainable operations is critical 2[11]. As such, continuous monitoring and validation of the
system is particularly important. While monitoring the selfВӯcontained aspect of CPS is
vital, providing a way to communicate and interact with their physical environment is simВӯ
ilarly important. Whether it be autonomous vehicles, smart health care appliances, or
intelligent electricity grids Вӯ human decision making is part of the holistic system. MoreВӯ
over, even the most sophisticated, autonomous system requires human intervention in
emergency situations, highlighting the need to explicitly account for these.
With the rise of modern computing capabilities and communication infrastructure, CPS will
become an increasingly important aspect of modern society. In the strive to become more
dataВӯdriven and improve operations, business will adopt CPS to support their business
processes.

Background & Research Statement
Traditional WfMS generate execution sequences of the processes they represent, comВӯ
monly also referred to as traces. There are numerous ways to represent these: in strucВӯ
tured graphs such as Petri nets, via a finite sequences of letters, or state machines. EiВӯ
ther way, classical process systems generate discrete outputsНҫ the processes they repВӯ
resent are discrete in nature, either an event happens, or it does not. Moreover, an acВӯ
tion has a clearly defined beginning and end, and is uniquely distinguishable from all
other events. As mentioned previously, imperative process models are suitable to exВӯ
press the controlВӯflow of highly structured and preВӯdetermined processes. While complex
behaviour of loosely structured processes can be captured, it quickly becomes cumberВӯ
some. Repeated Actions, exclusive disjunction between actions, or limited allowance of
occurrences of an event are examples of troublesome behaviour.
CPS require a high degree of flexibility in order to operate optimally. Their behaviour is
closely coupled with their physical environment, making an explicit definition of all possiВӯ
ble behaviours practically impossible. The important aspect is, that the systems adhere
to their corresponding certain safety requirements. As such, the declarative approach to
process specification is more appropriate to represent these systems. However, unlike
traditional business processes, CPS operate in the continuous domain, as they need to
react to changes in their environment in real time. Moreover, in most instances the enВӯ
vironment is governed by realВӯvalued input such as coordinates or physical properties
(temperature, pressure, humidity, . . . ), which discrete execution sequences are not able
to fully capture.
2.1
Declarative Process Specification Languages
While Imperative Process Modelling, also known as Procedural Process Modelling, enВӯ
joys popularity in industry and academia alike, interest in Declarative Specifications is
continuously increasing. A growing body of research has emerged focusing on the topics
of Reasoning and Discovery, as well as Monitoring and Verification of declarative specifiВӯ
cations [13]. Declarative specifications have been proposed as an alternative for looselyВӯ
structured processes requiring a high degree of flexibility, as they represent the desired
behaviour of the processes more closely. By integrating constraints into the language,
one is able to specify the expected behaviour in a positive way (what is expected to hapВӯ
pen) as well as a negative way (which behaviour is forbidden) [14].

2.1.1
DECLARE
As introduced previously, DECLARE is a constraint based language, formally grounded in
LTLf. The templates are inspired by a set of LTL patterns [14, 15]. In addition to the conВӯ
straint templates, DECLARE provides a graphical notation in order to represent process
specifications visually. An overview of various templates, alongside their corresponding
LTLf formula is shown in table 2.1. A inВӯdepth explanation of LTLf is given in section
2.1.1. Formally, a DECLARE specification consists of 1) a (nonВӯempty) set of templates,
2) a (nonВӯempty) set of activities, and a mapping from the set of activities to the set of temВӯ
plates, such that the constraints are instantiated versions of the set of templates. Given a
state sequence (or trace) t of activities, and a DECLARE specification DS, it is possible to
identify if t is accepted by DS by constructing a finite state automaton (FSA). One crucial
assumption is, that at each time instant in t, one, and only one, activity holds [14]. The
trace t satisfies DS if, and only if trace t is accepted by the language of the automaton.
Automatons can be utilized as runВӯtime engines, to facilitate the execution and monitorВӯ
ing of process specifications in real time. The notion of Conformance Checking provides
methods to quantify how much a given trace deviates from the behaviour specified in DS.
The templates shown in table 2.1 model the dependency of an activation x and a target
y. If the activation condition never happens, the constraint is vacuously satisfied. The
distinction between satisfied and vacuously satisfied is of importance when trying to infer
a specification from data.

Flexibility by Design

Flexibility by UnderВӯspecification
If the process is not known a priori, a system should allow to leave

Flexibility by Change
As processes are not static and immutable, a system should allow for
the alteration of specifications.
Flexibility by Deviation
As a mean to handle special instances the system should not force a

Design Support
Providing feedback on the correctness of the processes
RunВӯtime Support
RunВӯtime support consists of Monitoring and supporting correct execuВӯ
tion. If changes are made during runВӯtime, these should be validated.

2.2.1
Temporal Logics
Temporal Logics stem from the field of Formal Systems and Software Verification. They
provide a formal framework used to precisely specify complex temporal behaviours within
state sequences, and find application for ensuring safety properties, deadlock freedom,
or fair resource allocation [16]. Researchers are continuously investigating the expresВӯ
siveness and decideability of various dialects, with new variants emerging continuously.
Temporal Logics are usually evaluated on sequences of infinite length, however for the
purpose of monitoring systems finite interpretations have been adopted, as all systems
are expected to eventually terminate.
Temporal Logics for Signals vary from their discrete counterparts in two primary aspects:
1) instead of (timed) state sequences, they are interpreted over a continuous input space,
and 2) they express properties over realВӯvalued input.

Related Domains
This section touches upon related domains to the topics of process execution and system

2.3.1
Process Aware Information Systems
Process Aware Information Systems (PAIS) are specialized information systems designed
for the management and (automated) execution of business processes [31, 32]. These
systems offer operational support by enabling the design, enactment, and monitoring of
processes, if provided with a suitable executable process model [31]. While imperative
process models enjoy a high degree of popularity, declarative representations are being
considered as well [32]. In this way, the declarative approaches above could be utilized
to support the process representation.
Specialized PAIS are able to act as execution engines of processes, which we referred to
earlier as WfMS. As these processes are in a discrete context, PAIS pose no additional
benefit when trying to reason over signals. However, it might be possible to incorporate
signals in the future.
2.3.2
Model Predictive Control
Model Predictive Control represents a set of methods to regulate and model complex timeВӯ
invariant systems [33]. The goal is to find an optimal control strategy for some time in the
future [34]. Often, the system of interest is formulated in discreteВӯtime, however strategies
for continuousВӯtime systems exist as well [33]. While MPC is indispensable in regulating
complex systems, it requires a degree of specialization to be able to implement and unВӯ
derstand the dynamics. In [34] a possible application of STL in MPC is demonstrated,
and in [35] the use of MPC methods for STL specification synthesis.
Putting it into the context of CPS, the methods of MPC are inadequate to capture the
complexity of such systems, due to the nature of uncertainty in their environments. MoreВӯ
over, it is challenging to incorporate business process knowledge into the set of equations
governing a MPC strategy.

3.2.1
Reasoning over SigCLARE specifications
In [14] the authors introduce three concepts when addressing the topic of Reasoning: 1)
consistent specifications, 2) dead activities and 3) subsumptionНҫ all three of which are
relevant in SigCLARE as well. Just as in any of the DECLARE variants mentioned in
section 2.1, a specification in SigCLARE might entail conflicting constraints. This issue is
more pronounced when reasoning about signals, as states themselves might include conВӯ
flicting formulae. A trivial example follows: a state consisting of the combination of both
Monotonic Rising Time and Monotonic Falling Time. Furthermore, by allowing overlapВӯ
ping states, unintended consequences quickly emerge if one is not exceedingly careful.
For example two states s1 and s2, with inverse state formulae can never be active at the
same time, however as the rise operator shifts the evaluation to the first occurrence, the
Constraint ConcurrentResponse(s1, s2) would allow for an overlap. It is simple to create a
faulty specification SCS, however it is exceedingly difficult to identify it manually. ProperВӯ
ties of DECLARE specifications can be checked utilizing automata, specifically whether
the automaton corresponding to the DECLARE specification accepts at least one trace
[14]. When abstracting a STL specification8 into its corresponding boolean satisfaction
signals, the resulting formula are instances of MTL formulae. Nickovic and Piterman show
a way to construct deterministic timed automata for MTL assuming bounded variability
[38]. As STL specifies properties of signals of finite variability [25], their resulting satisВӯ
faction signals are also of bounded variability. Following this line of thought, constructing
an automaton from an STL specification would theoretically be possible, however, the
complexity of the resulting automaton would be significant 9.
ModelВӯchecking methods relying on other approaches can be found in the literature. DonzГ©
et. al. propose the integration of methods from MPC in order to identify whether a forВӯ
mula is feasible, via solving a Mixed Integer Linear Program [35]. An simulation based
approach to formula validation is introduced in [40]. Alternative methods include the use
of treeВӯstructures [41] or computationВӯgraphs [42]. The authors of [43] propose the use
of methods from the field of Reachability analysis in order to identify satisfiable specifiВӯ
cations. Another research area is the application of Machine Learning in order to identify
satisfying and nonВӯsatisfying traces. In [44] the authors address the use of LSTM to genВӯ
erate controllers. Lastly, Bartocci et. al. propose algorithms to emulate a system for
subsequent verification [45].
Unfortunately, all of these approaches assume STL or a different dialect. As none of these
methods incorporate frozen values, they can only be utilized if a specification without
freezing operator is used. This task in itself is already computationally complex, as this
requires checking all possible combinations of input values. Consequently, reducing the
searchВӯspace to the subset of acceptable conditions as defined by the nature of the system
is essential.
Furthermore, in [46] the authors propose a simulator, which is able to automatically genВӯ
erate satisfying and violating traces for STL specifications. If the set of satisfying traces
is empty, the specification must be inconsistent. Based on the theory of syntactic separaВӯ
tion, the authors of [47, 48] introduce a method to check and validate bounded formulae.
This approach is of high interest, as it utilizes hybrid automata to check for inconsistency
in a similar way as done for LTLf.

4.3
Evaluation
Section 2.1.4 highlights key characteristics which a declarative language should possess,
and any version of DECLARE ought to fulfill these as well. SigCLARE, as introduced in
section 3.2, entails the DECLARE set of templates and its graphical notation. FurtherВӯ
more, custom constraints can be defined, which however relies on the user being familiar
with temporal logics. The patterns presented in section 2.2.3 and table 3.1 show concrete
examples of the catalogue of templates. with these, SigCLARE fulfills most of the propВӯ
erties illustrated by van der Aalst and Pesic [8, 22, 23]. As the is no implementation as of
now, there is no system to support SigCLARE specifications. Of course, this means no
statements about the fulfillment of the requirements on userВӯ and runВӯtime support can be
made.
4.4
Threats to Validity / Critical Reflection
The primary assumption of this work is that freezing operator of STL* extends into STLВӯMX
(or vice versa). STL* is based on a fragment of STL allowing only bounded, nonВӯsingular
intervals, in contrast to STLВӯMX which relies on singular intervals to represent events or
transient states 1. STLВӯMX adopts a strict interpretation of the Until operator, such that
the satisfaction of formula ПҶUПҲ relies on the satisfaction of ПҲ at a time tвҖІ in the future, and
is not vacuously satisfied if ПҲ is satisfied at time t [49]. While section 3.1 provides formal
background on how to approach this assumption, the interaction between the freezing
operator and mapping operators remains open. This extends to the reasoning tasks disВӯ
cussed in section 3.2. The primary body of work presented on STL covers dialects other
than STL*. While the underlying approaches to problem solving are transferable, due to

Conclusions and Future Research
5.1
Conclusions
As illustrated with the example of the Annealing process, the integration of realВӯvalued
signals into process specifications has not yet been tackled. Even the most expressive
variant of DECLARE is not able to fully represent the behaviour. CyberВӯphysical systems
operate in a continuous environment and output a continuous stream of information. As
such, they require more advanced approaches than classical information systems, which
generate execution sequences. Consequently, Research Question 1 can be answered
with no.
One of the possible options to represent behaviours of signals is Signal Temporal Logic.
STL is a widely adopted formalism for property checking and finds application in CPS
already. STLВӯMX provides a succinct way to reason about the hybrid nature of CPS. By
integrating discrete time formulae with continuous time ones, STLВӯMX enables system
designers to express both time dimensions independently. However, neither STL nor
STLВӯMX provide a way to specify safety properties which require referencing previous
values without assuming an extended signal including preВӯcomputed derivatives. The
variant STL* introduces signal value freezing, motivated by this challenge, however it
adopts concessions originally present in STL and since then lifted.
The language STLВӯMX* integrates the two variants to utilize the properties of both, and
builds the backbone for SigCLARE. SigCLARE takes the elements of DECLARE and apВӯ
plies them to signals in such a manner that the DECLARE semantics emerge if no signal
information is used. Apart from the Constraint Templates already available in DECLARE,
SigCLARE provides additional State templates to specify behaviours over signals. States
represent a durative property of the signal. By lifting the assumption of mutual excluВӯ
sivity between states, which is a necessary condition for Signals, the expressivity of the
language increases. Two of the primary advantages this entails is 1) the dependency beВӯ
tween multiple states, such that constraints only activate if two or more states hold at the
same time, and 2) the explicit modelling of overlapping intervals. This provides answers
for research question 1.2 and 1.3.
The reasoning tasks carry over from DECLARE. The identification of consistent specificaВӯ
tions is significantly more complex for SigCLARE, as the number of possible interactions
between elements is significantly larger. The same applies to the detection of dead activВӯ
ities. However, the popularity of STL has prompted a rise in research, resulting in novel
methods to tackle this challenge. The python based software library stlcg has been used

5.2
Future Research
5.2.1
Proof and Interactions
The underlying logic for SigCLARE, STLВӯMX*, obtains its semantics from STLВӯMX [25]
and STL*[27, 28]. However, the fundamental assumption is that the freeze operator вҲ—ПҶ
transfers into the domain of STLВӯMX. The two primary topics are the adoption of a strict
interpretation of the temporal operators, and assessing properties of the time mapping
operators @dc and @cd. Both aspects have been addressed in section 3.1, however the
a precise interaction of the freezing operator remains unclear.
Mining and Monitoring
Moreover, assessing the possibilities to apply existing methods for Mining and Monitoring
remains of interest. Numerous approaches for automatic identification of STL specifiВӯ
cations have been investigated [49, 50, 26, 42, 41], however, these all cover different
variants of STL. Adapting these to STLВӯMX* would facilitate the automatic discovery of
SigCLARE specifications.
Similarily follows for monitoring algorithms, specifically in the context of online or streamВӯ
ing monitoring. Furthermore, Online monitors are of special interest in the context of alert
systems, or if storing the entire signal becomes infeasible [24].
5.2.2
Evaluating Quantitative Semantics
The notion of Robustness expresses the degree to which a Signal satisfies a STL formula,
where positive values imply satisfaction, whereas negative values imply dissatisfaction
[49, 51]. This quantitative metric could be used to enhance monitoring (e.g. identify
hotspots of nonВӯconformity) or to supplement specification mining.
5.2.3
Implementation
In order to show the practical applications of SigCLARE, it is paramount to implement
the procedures in a framework. Furthermore, it would be of particular interest to evaluate
the performance of SigCLARE monitors in comparison to DECLARE. This would allow to
quantify the cost accrued by adopting a signal representation. The open source Process
Mining framework ProM already supports a wide range of languages, therefore it would
be a natural choice to provide a SigCLARE plugin.
5.2.4
Additional Signal Dimensions
Alternative logics have been proposed that incorporate domains other than temporal, for
instance spatial or frequency information [49]. A supplemented frequency domain might
assist in detecting more complex oscillatory behaviour as described in section 2.2, by
enabling the use of methods from the field of signal processing [49]. However, this would
most probably necessitate the definition of a new declarative language.
