Abstract
As wind energy technology continues to develop and its share keeps growing, new technologies
and lines of research are emerging. One of these technologies is Hybrid power Plants (HPPs)
combining wind and solar pv technologies, which is the main topic of this study. It aims to
compare optimal HPPs layouts in different U.S markets, with different wind and solar resource
characteristics, and to investigate further how different parameters affect the layout design and
output metrics.
The study firstly focused on a market research to find patterns and features in each of them.
Once selected the sites in CAISO, ERCOT and NYISO a layout optimization was carried out
leveraging NREL’s open-source software HOPP. Not only it allowed to use different models and
approaches for the layout optimization, but it also offered a great variety of great output metrics
such as AEP of the different technologies, losses, performances, etc. Secondly a sensitivity
analysis was conducted changing the input installed capacity of the HPP and other analyses
such as power generation availability. The thesis continued with a financial study using a self-
developed discounted cash flow (DCF) model which whom a sensitivity analysis was carried
out from, to observe how different input parameters affected the financial metrics.
According to the results of the study it was corroborated that the wind components were the
main drivers for the layout, AEP and LCOE results. combining wind with solar pv technology
expanded the value of the HPP beyond LCOE, and this was measured in terms of power
generation availability.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Figure 1.1: Hybrid Power Plants in the U.S [1].
The environmental situation urges to seek for a transition into renewable power generation in
order to cope with the increasing load demand and taking advantage of the decrease in costs
while renewable energy is growing 10%-20% on overall electricity generation [2]. It has been
proved multiple times that it is feasible to provide the load demand necessary to run large areas
using renewable technologies, such as in Portugal that achieved to run for four days straight
on renewable energy alone [3] back in 2016 or the recent case in California where its grid ran
on 97% renewable energy in April 2022 due to an extended period of solar production [4]. As
the wind and solar market penetration increases, these power sources will need to deal with
the concepts of reliability, resilience and flexibility of the power grid [5][6]. A way to reduce
variability, address local grid stability issues, provide higher quality power to the grid and
improve economics is through co-locating wind and solar in Hybrid Power Plants (HPP).
==PAGE:14==
1.2 Project Scope
2
Wind and solar generation are a natural fit for HPPs, because of the generally negative cor-
relation between wind and solar resources [7] and because of the easiness to built solar arrays
in the areas between wind turbines (WTs). Even though this favourable negative correlation
helps providing more consistent power for HPPs, it is still not enough to completely remove
instances or periods of minimal power generation. This paper [8] investigates aforementioned
topic in the Western interconnection in the U.S.
HPPs combining wind and solar technologies, and potentially battery storage, can represent
many advantages compared to single-technology power plants. One of these advantages is the
reduction on variability and curtailment due to the complementary effect of both technologies
that has been proofed to share a power generation negative correlation and a combined ca-
pacity factor that increases significantly for hybrid power plants, reaching up to 60% in some
areas of the U.S landscape [9]. Other advantages are the synergies and potential infrastructure

Moving from single generation power plants to HPP is motivated by the need to increase prof-
itability by moving beyond Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) to look at the variable costs and
revenues for the power plants over their lifetime. The challenge is that the design of these
plants involves a lot of complexity [2]. Previous work has pointed out the diﬀiculties associated

area [12]. HPP optimization research has predominantly focused on technology sizing or objec-
tives such as reliability, making most problems suitable for mixed integer linear programming
(MILP). In this thesis, the goal is to go beyond sizing and cover the topic of physical layout
optimization.

1.3
Problem definition
The physical layout design of a solar and wind Hybrid Power plant (HPP) is an iterative process
that consists of finding the optimal positions of wind turbines (WTs) and solar pv panels inside
a wind farm, so as to maximize and/or minimize a single objective or multiple objectives, while
satisfying a set of constraints [13]. In this Thesis, the problem will be formulated to maximize
the Annual Energy Production (AEP). The model description will be covered in Section 3.6,
where the problem formulation and the mathematical model will be explained, including the
objective function, design variables, parameterization and constraints selected.
The optimization is conducted using the Hybrid Optimization and Performance Platform
(HOPP) from NREL. The software is able to maximize the desired objective function, by
setting up some design variables, parameters and constraints. The constraints involve power
generation, installed capacity and boundary constraints. The following models from HOPP
will be used in the optimization process: Hybrid model, Wind model, Solar model and shadow
flicker model and they will be explained in Section 3.8.
To carry out the layout optimization, first it will be given a certain sizing to select how much
capacity will be allocated to each technology for the same parameters, design variables and
constraints to be used for the layout optimization. By inserting the HPP coordinates, HOPP
is able to extract the hourly electricity prices, as well as importing wind resource data from the
WindToolkit [14] and and solar resource data from The National Solar Radiation DataBase
(NSRDB)[15]. The model is applied to a case study scenario in order to compare the perfor-
mance of the model in three different markets in the U.S.

2.1
Hybrid Power Plants
Hybrid renewable energy systems are proposals that have been around for a while but have not
been enough exploited or developed at a large scale to overcome the randomness and variability
of a single technology power source. By definition, HPPs are power plants that contain two
or more technologies that may potentially include wind turbines, solar pv, solar CSP, storage,
geothermal power, hydropower, biomass, natural gas, oil, coal, or nuclear power. These hybrid
plants can be used to generate electricity or other products such as hydrogen.
The HPP
studied in this thesis is the one including wind and solar pv connected to the grid through a
common point and managed and operated as a single power plant. This is a potential disruptive
technology as the costs of solar photovoltaic, wind and battery supplies are decreasing while the
penetration of renewables is increasing in parallel with the new trends where more sustainable
standards, policies and regulations are being implemented globally [12].
The concept behind combining solar and wind is the complementary of these two technologies,
even thought that the fluctuation cannot be entirely erased, especially for off-grid applications.
Therefore it can be stated that wind and pv are complementary under certain conditions
that depend on locations and time-scales, because their power generations are directly related
to weather conditions and climate [16]. One of the challenges of HPPs is to overcome the
high probability of curtailment, because of the unpredictability and sizing of the power source
technologies. This scenario can be reduced with the addition to the system of a battery that
will charge during generation periods and discharge load to the grid when the power sources
are under-performing [12].
When thinking about wind and solar pv HPP, there can be three main different co-location
configurations. First, wind and solar can share the same substation and grid connections, but
not necessarily be located in the same boundary area. Secondly, they can share the previous and
also be co-located in the same area to optimize space. Third configuration is the so-called virtual
HPP where the two technologies have separated substations and grid connections. Previous
works [17] write about the different advantages and disadvantages of the different co-location
configurations. Figure 2.1 shows the physical architecture of a co-located HPP in contrast with
Figure B.2 in the Appendix which shows a virtual one. It can be viewed how the co-located
option shares the same substation.
This thesis will study only the second option because it is the most relevant to layout optimiza-
tion. Some of the advantages mentioned are the reduction in capital costs and permitting time

HPP design and optimization are set to be complex owing to the several different variables,
characteristics, constraints and interactions among the systems under certain assumptions and
conditions. This is why it is convenient to follow a design optimization process as described in
figure 2.2 formulated by Joaquim R.R.A. Martins and Andrew King.

Figure 2.2: Design Optimization Process [19].
Historically until recent times, the design of these plants have been focused on an objective
function that would always maximize energy production and would supply constant load for
specific certain times [12]. An important consideration when designing a HPP is to consider
the market context, opportunities and trends.
The possible market scenarios considered during the design and optimization process are listed
below [12]:
• Fixed-rate energy payment.
This scenario has been used historically as baseline
because of the low renewable shares that aligned with the the times where the power
plant gets paid a fixed rate per energy unit produced regardless of time. The objective
is to minimize the COE by the power plant.
• Energy-market dominant.
This market resembles better actuality because of the
higher renewable generation shares (up to 25% or even more). In this context power
plants participate in time-varying energy markets and may receive some payments for
capacity value and even penalties if the production is not as forecasted. Therefore this
translates to a bigger focus on predictability of the variability and to a multi-objective
function where maximizing of the revenues and minimizing the over all costs take the
lead in prioritization.
• Capacity- and service-dominant. Considers the future energy scenario with high
shares of renewable generation with abundant and low cost energy. The objective in this
market is to maximize the NPV or similar metrics and to provide ancillary services on
demand.
In addition, there are other considerations of the market structure such as the incentives avail-
able.
Several investment tax credits (ITCs), rebates, grants and loan programs have been
offered along with production tax credits (PTCs) to support renewable energy adoption glob-
ally. It is of big importance to understand the inventive scenario when assessing the different
locations for the design of a hybrid power plant.

The design of an integrated Balance of System (BoS) includes the turbine foundations and
infrastructure for roads, the electrical collection system, substation, interconnection, erection
of turbines, etc. Depending on the degree of simplification some of these elements may not be
taken into account during the optimization design process. The electrical collection system has
particularly importance for Hybrid power plant design because of the potential opportunity for
integrated design and leverage of common infrastructure for cost saving by creating synergies.
For physical design of hybrid power plants that integrate wind solar pv, and potentially storage
special resource analysis considerations has to be used. An approach could be to use joint
probability density functions (PDFs) for wind energy and extend this to more dimensions
for computational simplification during the simulations of the optimization. For example a
3-dimensional joint frequency distributions (or the often so-called 3D-wind rose) where the
dimensions are yearly wind speed, wind direction and electricity price site [12].
Modelling of the different technologies is assessed individually such as the wake losses effect,
but because of the integration of the subsystems in one same system, extra models enter the
equation such as the flickering on the solar panels caused by the dynamic shading from the
rotating turbines or the shading of the turbine towers depending on the sun position[12].
Existing literature focuses on challenges with the system objectives, constraints, and design
variables associated with HPP design optimization [20]. These studies show the complexity of
HPP design and they have covered traditional optimization methods encompassing dynamic
programming, mixed integer linear programming (MILP), artificial intelligence (AI) methods
and specifically developed software tools [21] [22]. The Hybrid Optimization and Performance
Platform (HOPP) will be the software tool to be used in this thesis. The tool developed by
NREL is presented in Section 2.3.

ERCOT´s electric grid connects more than 610 generation units and it covers more than

new transmission lines in West Texas to accommodate the growth [55]. Such transmission
investments increase reliability and resilience of the grid, so the effects of catastrophic
results, such as the Winter Storm Uri that was seen in February 2021, can be mitigated
[56].
This paper [57] covers the topic of grid resilience and reliability during natural
disasters.
To mitigate the risk of congestion, ERCOT market players can purchase congestion Rev-
enue Rights (CRRs), which are financial instruments, or hedges that can produce a charge
or a payment to the participant when the transmission grid is congested in the DA market
[55]. Since early 2018 ERCOT has been implementing CRR auctions [53].

Methodology
3.1
Wind and solar data
Before carrying out the site selection for the layout optimization, it was decided to look at the
correlation between wind and solar resource. To be aligned with HOPP data source, both were
obtained from state-of-the-art NREL´s Wind Integration National Dataset (WIND) and the
National Solar Radiation Data Base (NSRDB).
The WIND toolkit [14] was developed by NREL in collaborations with the National Center for
Atmospheric Research’s Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF). It is a state-of-the-art wind
resource data set offering US wind data from 2007 to 2013 and was created to facilitate wind
resource assessments and promote deployment [70]. HOPP is connected to it and downloads
barometric pressure, wind speed and direction, relative humidity, temperature and air density
data from the desired sites. The WIND toolkit carries out a data cleaning process excluding
wind speed Values below 0 m/s or above 40 m/s and wind directions below 0◦or above 360◦.
Similarly, The solar data including Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) and Direct Normal
Irradiance (DNI) has been extracted from the National Solar Radiation Data Base (NSRDB)
[15]. The current NSRDB yields solar radiance data at a 4 km horizontal resolution from 1998
to 2016. It is computed by a combination of NREL’s Physical Solar Model (PSM) with products
from the National Aeronautics and Space Administration’s (NASA)[71], the National Oceanic
and Atmospheric Administration’s (NOAA) [72], the Geostationary Operational Environmental
Satellite (GOES)[73] and the National Ice Center’s (NIC) [74] among others.
3.2
Site Selection
Site selection in this thesis was conducted focusing on the correlation between wind and solar
resource. This correlation could be calculated between wind speed and GHI or wind direction
and GHI. This thesis focused on the first correlation because wind speed is one of the most
critical characteristics in wind power generation [75], but the wind direction correlation was
also shown. The idea was to find both a high correlation and a low correlation site in CAISO,
ERCOT and NYISO markets, resulting in 6 different sites in total. It was then computed using
contiguous data sets from 2012 for both wind and solar data sources introduced in Section 3.1.
Data from 2012 was chosen as it is the default year in HOPP. Siting was considered out of

scope in this thesis, but please note that when developing a HPP feasibility study, it should be
conducted a siting assessment.
In general terms, a wind farm siting assessment has several priorities, whose main ones are
economics and access [76]. The economics topic covers studies about wind resource assessment,
land purchase/lease availability, permitting, transmission/grid upgrades and costs, electricity
prices, market structure and policy initiatives. On the other hand the access priorities focus
on assessments about transmission infrastructure access, land purchase or lease availability,
community proximity, environmental or historical constrained areas and sensitive sites (i.e.
military). Therefore siting study itself could be easily a whole other thesis and will be omitted.
A Pearson R hourly correlation coeﬀicient analysis was performed to assess the correlation
between wind speed and solar GHI in several locations across the three markets for 2012.
Hourly wind speed and wind direction data was extracted from the Wind Toolkit at 80 m hub
height, to align with the wind turbine´s hub height. Hourly solar GHI was extracted from the
NSRDB. The Pearson R correlation coeﬀicient [77] is a dimensionless unit that determines the
linear correlation between two variables. It is computed using Equation 3.1 where ¯x and ¯y
represent the mean of the two sample variables, which in this case are GHI and wind speed.
Pearson R correlation coeﬀicient could range from −1 to 1, meaning that the closer its absolute
value is to 1 the more correlated the sample values of wind speed and GHI are. The correlation
sign is determined by the regression slope: positive correlation implies that both variables are
directly proportional, whereas a negative one means they are inversely proportional. To get
the most value out of a HPP and be able to improve grid stability, it was decided to choose the
most negative correlation values possible (or at least low positive), so the HPP could benefit
from the complementary behaviour between solar and wind profiles.

To determine what sites to choose and what range of correlation values, it was leveraged
NREL´s paper [78], in which an analysis of the hourly Pearson R correlation across the U.S.
was carried out and plot in a map as represented in Figure A.5. It indicated that the correlation
of wind and solar resource on an hourly basis was commonly low and negative, meaning they
were often complementary. Highest absolute correlation was approximately 0.5 and it was set
as a benchmark for ”high correlation”, even though it was still fairly low. This low correlation
offers a dual benefit of simultaneously smooth the overall energy provided from the HPP and
increase the energy production [79]. This confers a large number of advantages for resiliency
and grid stability [80].
An additional consideration is that the more complementary that wind and solar resources are
on a daily basis, the lesser the expected variation in daily resource is, translating into a less
need for storage solutions to smooth energy supply [78]. Based on this, an iterative process was
carried leveraging Figure A.5, to look for ”low negative correlation sites” with values ranging
from 0 to -0.1 in each market, and also ”high absolute negative correlation sites” with values
ranging from -0.2 to -0.3. Please note that each site could have different wind speeds and wind
direction, as well as different GHI. The idea was to use different scenarios but that could be
comparable in numbers.

Site area
Even though it does not exist an accepted single metric to figure out the land use for wind
farms, the so-called total area of a wind farm (measured in hectares per MW) or the capacity
density area (in MW per square kilometer) are often used[81]. The total area is often called
the ”project area” or the ”site boundary” since it takes into consideration the whole area of the

3.6
Optimization Model Formulation
As a recapitulation, the main focus and problem formulation in this research study is to analyze
how in different energy markets across the USA, layout-optimized hybrid power plants are
affected in their different parameters such as financial or performance depending on this hybrid
technology and sizing scenarios with different Pearson Correlation between the two technology
generations. Because of the nature of this problem with high dimensionality where it becomes
complex to allow all the dimensions to be optimized as free variables, it is decisive to break up
the problem in different parts. This helps parameterize and simplify the problem variables to
the needs of the optimization problem.
3.6.1
Optimization Approach
One single technology is already a complex topic when referring to design of a power plant and
layout optimization, especially with wind energy technology [89]. Therefore aiming for a HPP
layout optimization becomes an even more complicated challenge with a high dimensionality of
design variables and parameters which translates to high computational costs. This problem is
addressed in a study [90] that develops a method to reduce the high dimensionality of variables
and increase the computational eﬀiciency using a boundary grid method.
Stochastic-optimization of low-dimensional parameterized layouts is an effective method for
producing eﬀicient hybrid plant layouts [91]. Parallel to this, the Evolution Strategies (frame-
work displayed in Algorithm 1 of figure 3.4) approach to stochastic optimization is suitable
to the HPP problem because of the highly non convex objective function (that translates to
a higher probability of getting stuck in a local minima), the diﬀiculty in obtaining derivatives
(because of the non convexity) and the capacity to produce eﬀicient layout options.

3.6.3
Parametrization Design of HPP
Because of the nature of this problem with high dimensionality where it becomes complex to
allow all the dimensions to be optimized as free variables, it is crucial to find a parametrization
of the problem that will help in its simplification. There are several papers that address the high
dimensionality and analayze different parametrizations that are suitable and can be applied to
HPP. The chosen design parametrization is inspired by the research paper done to simplify the
layout optimization of wind plants [90] but adapted to a hybrid power plant combining wind
and solar [91].
This parametrization reduces the number of dimensions and because of this reason it also
eliminates certain possible layout configurations. Nevertheless, the overall results are proved
to be very good [91]. It consists on assuming a circular boundary grid for the wind turbine
layout problem where the wind turbines are dispersed in a mesh disposition complying with
different constraints such as minimum distance between wind turbines, minimum distance to
the boundary perimeter, number of wind turbines on perimeter etc. The solar area is modeled
as a compound array of PV panels (generally in rectangular form except for when the solar
area is on the boundaries) where the length and width of the optimized disposition and number
of panels will determine its dimensions and will depend on the installed capacity determined.
The solar area contains a buffer zone where there are no PV panels designed to reduce the
flickering losses and where wind turbines cannot be placed.

3.6.5
Assumptions and limitations
Because of the complexity of the problem and HOPP itself, there have been some assumptions
taken into account that have led to some sort of limitations.
These assumptions and its
limitations are as stated in the following list:
• The flickering data set selected has been generalized for all case scenarios due to the
diﬀiculties to generate new flickering data for each different location. Therefore it has
been fetched from one same site but there is minimum impact as consequence to the
parameterization of the buffer solar zone that forces the creation of larger distances
between the solar PV panels and the wind turbines (added to the minimum spacing
distance of 100 m) in the simulation.
• A simple wind direction standard deviation was computed. However, it could be com-
puted using more advanced methods such as the Yamartino Method [97].
• For comparison reasons between the different markets it was decided to use the same
circular boundary with a radius of 3 km.
• Due to data availability reasons and alignment with HOPP, the wind and solar data were
fetched for every case form the year 2012.
• The percentages of wake losses were calculated by considering the layout of the wind
farm, applying them individually to one wind turbine and then scaling it.
• For the sensitivity analysis where in each case scenario the installed wind capacity was
varying (+10%, -40%, etc) from the baseline of 64.5 MW, the number of turbines was
rounded up in order to have integer numbers and fulfill the percentage variation (i.e. for
the +20% in wind installed capacity that translates to 77.4 MW, the number of WTs
which has been rounded up from 51.6 to 52).

• The randomness in HOPP simulations forces the program to have small variations in the
results even for identical input runs.
• HOPP uses a penalization method where the penalty scale is set to 0.1 in order to avoid

• As previously discussed in section 3.6.3 the solar area and array of PV panels is computed

irradiance, soiling factors and the string voltage of each subarray to apply self-shading. It is
then transformed to the DC output taking into account system parameters such as DC/AC
ratio or inverter eﬀiciency to finally apply AC losses. The model components include surface
angles, sun position, self shading, snow loss temperature, among others.

3.8.4
Flickering Model
The flickering model leveraged by HOPP simulates a wind turbine’s flicker over a grid for a
given location. The shadow cast by the tower and the three blades are calculated for each of
the simulation steps, number of blade angles (evenly spaced) per step of the hour. The turbine
is located at (0, 0) and a set of 2D arrays give the flicker losses at grid cell/coordinate. The
calculated maps are the following:
• The Plane of Array (POA) heat map is produced as a loss ratio relative to un-shaded
areas (0 - 1). This loss ratio is with respect to plane-of-array irradiance
• The Power heat map is another loss ratio (0 - 1), but with respect to power production
of an unshaded string of panels.
• The Time heat map is weighted by the number of timesteps each grid cell is shaded over
the total timesteps simulated.
All heat maps generated are normalized by the number of timesteps simulated. For this model
the parameters are the number of hours in year, number of time steps to run each hour, the
distance of the heat map’s north, west and east end from the turbine at (0, 0) (in number of
turbine diameters), and similarly, the number of turbine diameters the heatmap extends from
(0, 0) to south.

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Analysis of the results
Layout results revealed that WTs tended to be located in the boundary to free up space.
Similarly, WTs were prone to create wake-free corridors in the dominant wind direction of
the sites. Additionally, the more concentrated the wind roses were towards a wind dominant
direction, the less wake losses were and the easier it was to distinguish the aforementioned
wake-free corridors. Logically, the larger the solar area was the less the distance was left for
WTs and the more concentrated they ended up. With respect to the solar array allocations, it
was hard distinguish clear patterns.
Wind and solar resources complemented each other the higher the negative Pearson correlation
was in each site. This improved the HPP value beyond an economical perspective because it
increased power availability. High and low correlations did not affect as much as it was first
thought to the overall AEP of the HPP. Wind speed was the main driver of the AEP. High
correlation affected the power generation availability as it was observed in CAISO. both sites
having similar resource characteristics, the high correlated one increased its power availability
more than the low one (37.53% increase compared to 25.91%). Despite this, it was also proven
that even in low correlated sites, the HPP benefited from a power availability gain.
From an LCOE perspective, the no solar scenario seemed to have better economic results in
terms of LCOE and NPV, but from a beyond LCOE perspective, adding solar also increased the
HPP power generation availability. The best sizing change that was recommended to implement
in CAISO high was to increase 10% the wind installed capacity, secondly to reduce 40% the
solar capacity and third, a 10% solar increase. On the other hand, the worst sizing change
would be to decrease 40% the wind capacity or increase 40% the solar capacity. However, from
a beyond LCEO perspective, it would not make sense to reduce the solar capacity so much
because it would also minimize the aforementioned complementary advantage of HPPs.
The financial sensitivity analysis performed in CAISO high site came to the conclusion that
the two inputs that affected the most the LCOE of the HPP in the DCF Model were the wind
AEP and the wind CAPEX. In opposition, inflation and AEP solar were the ones who affected
the less to the LCOE.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
Due to the wind share growth and constant development, the rush to create more eﬀicient and
consistent technologies inspired this thesis to investigate about Hybrid Power Plants combining
wind and solar pv. In this study the main goal which was to find the optimized layout of
different markets in the US and to see if different factors such as resource correlation affected
them.
The study was split into two parts, one more technical where the main output metrics discussed
were the AEP, the wake losses, and power generation availability. Followed by a second part
which focused on the financial parameters were the main output metric was the LCOE.
To carry out this study a whole investigation was executed to select 6 sites in different regions
in the U.S. with different Pearson R correlations between wind speed and GHI. Similarly, a
HPP layout opitimization was chosen and a parameterization approach was conducted. In this
process, different models, output metrics and analyses were included.
Results were compared across the different sites and in general it was proven that the wind
components in these HPPs were the main driving factor in AEP. Having said this, the addition
of solar pv technology complimented wind and increased the power generation availability in all
sites. Being the best gains observed in the high correlated sites. Regarding layout results, some
layout patters were identified such as the WT boundary allocation, and wake-free corridors in
the dominant wind directions of the site.
6.2
Future Work
The complexity of the Hybrid Power Plants physical layout design offers many ways to expand
the research and analysis carried out. Therefore some of the future work opportunities are
listed below:
• A great improvement would be the addition of a battery as complementary power resource
to reduce curtailment. HOPP includes a battery dispatch model that could be included
in the optimization problem.
==PAGE:69==
6.2 Future Work
57
• The coordinates chosen in each market have been selected based on the wind and solar
resource maps. A more detailed wind resource assessment could have been conducted
by carrying out a feasibility study and leveraging some state-of-the-art software like
WAsP engineering which is suitable for wind resource assessment, siting and energy yield
calculations.
• Regarding the Wind turbine model selected, it would be a great approach to consider
different types of Wind turbine generators with different characteristics like Power ratings,
Hub heights or rotor diameters and see how they affect the optimal layout configurations.
• The solar area could be improved by considering more complex configurations with dif-
ferent solar arrays. Similarly, this optimization approach could look further into solar
losses and do not assume them constant.
• A multi-objective function approach including minimization of the LCOE could be con-
ducted.
For the CMA-ES optimization approach, a larger population size would be interesting to
investigate. In this thesis we tried a candidate population of 50, whereas a size of 200
would probably offer more accurate results and the probabilities to get stuck into local
maxima would be lower but the computational cost would increase.
• Revenues of the HPP were assumed to be coming from a PPA scheme. For this reason it
could be interesting to expand the analysis using market power prices.
• A 3-dimensional wind rose which combines yearly market prices, wind speeds and direc-
tions could also be developed for the revenues section.
• The DCF Analysis could include a more detailed cost-component Model, or HOPP’s cost
model could be implemented.
