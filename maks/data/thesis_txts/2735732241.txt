Abstract
Anomaly detection in water quality data streams is an important task for ensuring the safety and
efficiency of water treatment systems. However, this task faces several challenges, such as high
data volume, high data velocity, high data variability, and concept drift. In this thesis, we propose
a novel solution that combines machine learning and model­switching techniques to address these
challenges. We train and evaluate different machine learning models, such as decision trees (DT),
k­nearest neighbors (KNN), support vector machines (SVM), XGBoost (XGB), Naïve Bayes (NB)
and Stochastic Gradient Descent(SGD), on a real­world water quality dataset. We apply different
model update strategies, such as Detecting Data Shift Training, scheduled training, and threshold­
triggered training, to adapt the models to changes in the data distribution and system dynamics.
We also apply different post­processing techniques, such as smoothing and confirmation delay, to
reduce the number of false positives and improve the reliability of the anomaly detection solution.
We compare the performance of the models and the strategies using various metrics, such as accu­
racy, balanced accuracy, precision, Specificity, Sensitivity, F1­score, and AUROC. We find that
different models and strategies have different strengths and weaknesses depending on the charac­
teristics of the data stream and the trade­off between accuracy and efficiency. We conclude that
there is no single best model or strategy that works for all scenarios, and that model switching is a
useful technique to select the most suitable model or strategy for each scenario.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction
Water quality is essential for life, health, and well­being, but it is threatened by various natural and
anthropogenic factors, such as pollution, climate change, population growth, urbanization, and in­
dustrialization. These factors can cause water quality issues, such as eutrophication, acidification,
contamination, and infection, which can affect the safety and efficiency of water treatment sys­
tems, as well as the protection of aquatic ecosystems and human health [1] [2]. Therefore, water
quality monitoring is crucial for detecting and addressing these issues. Water quality monitoring
involves the measurement and analysis of various physical, chemical, and biological parameters of
water using different methods. Traditionally, these methods are manual sampling and laboratory
analysis, which are costly, time­consuming, labor­intensive, and prone to errors. Moreover, they
provide limited spatial and temporal coverage of water quality data, which may not capture the
dynamic and complex nature of water systems [3]. Therefore, there is a need for more advanced
and automated methods that can provide high­frequency, high­resolution, and real­time data.
In recent years, the development of sensor technologies and data analytics has enabled the use of
automated in situ sensors for water quality monitoring. These sensors can measure various water
quality parameters continuously and remotely, and transmit the data to a central server or a cloud
platform. This can provide timely and accurate information on the status and trends of water qual­
ity [4]. However, this also poses several challenges for data analysis and interpretation, such as:
The sensor data may contain noise or errors due to technical issues or environmental factors [5].
The sensor data may exhibit high variability or non­stationarity due to seasonal or diurnal cycles or
sudden changes in water conditions [6]. The sensor data may be affected by concept drift, which
means that the relationship between the input data and the output variable may change over time
due to changes in the underlying data distribution or system dynamics [7]. These challenges make
it difficult to identify and understand the anomalies in water quality data streams. Anomalies are
defined as data points that deviate significantly from the normal or expected behavior of the data
[8]. Anomalies can indicate potential problems or threats in water systems, such as equipment
malfunction, sensor failure, contamination events, algal blooms, or cyber­attacks [9]. Therefore,
anomaly detection is an important task for water quality monitoring that can enable timely inter­
vention and mitigation actions.
However, anomaly detection in water quality data streams is not a trivial task. It requires effective
methods that can handle the high volume and velocity of data, cope with the high variability and
non­stationarity of data, adapt to the concept drift in data, distinguish between normal and abnormal
variations in data, reduce the false positives and false negatives in detection results, and provide
interpretable and actionable insights for decision making [10].
Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence that can provide powerful and flexible meth­
ods for anomaly detection in water quality data streams. Machine learning methods can learn from
data without explicit rules or assumptions, and can handle complex and nonlinear relationships
between variables. In machine learning, supervised and unsupervised learning are recognized as
two primary categories widely utilized. [11].
Supervised learning methods require labeled data for training and testing. They learn a function
that maps the input data to the output labels. They can be used for anomaly detection by treating it
as a classification problem, where anomalies are assigned a different class or category than normal
data points. However, supervised learning methods have some limitations for anomaly detection,
such as:
1
==PAGE:10==
­ The need for sufficient and representative labeled data, which may not be available or feasible in
many cases [12].
­ The risk of poor generalization to new or unseen data, especially if the data distribution changes
over time [13].
­ The inability to capture the diversity or novelty of anomalies, as they are based on predefined
classes or categories.
Some examples of supervised learning methods that have been applied for anomaly detection in
water quality data streams are decision trees (DT), k­nearest neighbors (KNN), support vector
machines (SVM), XGBoost (XGB), Naïve Bayes (NB) and Stochastic Gradient Descent(SGD)
[14].
Unsupervised learning methods do not require labeled data for training or testing. They learn the
intrinsic structure or patterns of the data without any guidance. They can be used for anomaly
detection by treating it as an outlier detection problem, where anomalies are identified as data
points that deviate significantly from the normal behavior of the data. However, unsupervised
learning methods also have some limitations for anomaly detection, such as:
­ The difficulty of distinguishing between normal and abnormal variations in data, as they do not
have any prior knowledge or feedback [15].
­ The sensitivity to the choice of parameters or thresholds, which may affect the detection perfor­
mance or accuracy [15].
­ The lack of interpretability or explainability of the results, as they do not have any labels or
references to compare with [15].
Some examples of unsupervised learning methods that have been applied for anomaly detection in
water quality data streams are isolation forest (IF), one­class SVM (OCSVM) [14].
In this thesis, we propose a novel solution for anomaly detection in water quality data streams us­
ing machine learning and model­switching techniques. We aim to address the challenges of data
analysis and interpretation posed by the high volume, velocity, variability, and non­stationarity of
sensor data, as well as the concept drift in data distribution and system dynamics. Our method
involves applying various techniques to clean, normalize, select, and transform the sensor data,
and then training and evaluating different machine learning models, such as DT, KNN, SVM,NB,
XGB,SGD, IF, OCSVM, on the transformed data. We also apply different model update strategies
to adapt the models to changes in the data and system, and different model switching techniques
to select the best model for each scenario. Our proposed method is novel and innovative in sev­
eral aspects. Firstly, we have devised a process aimed at feature selection and parameter tuning
for individual models. This ensures that each model optimally utilizes the most suitable features
and parameters, thereby enhancing the efficiency and accuracy of our machine­learning models.
Secondly, our study revolves around an exceptionally unbalanced dataset. We experimented with
oversampling and undersampling algorithms to address this dataset imbalance and conducted a
thorough analysis of each model’s performance, thus shedding light on the effectiveness of these
algorithms. Third, we use a combination of model update and model switching techniques to cope
with concept drift in water quality data streams. This can enhance the adaptability and flexibility
of the machine learning models.
The main contributions of this thesis are:
• We propose a novel solution for anomaly detection in water quality data streams using ma­
chine learning and model­switching techniques.

• We conduct extensive experiments on a real­world water quality dataset using different ma­

• We compare and analyze the performance and impact of different machine learning models,

3
Methodology
3.1
Overview
Figure 3.1 illustrates our methodology for anomaly detection in water quality data streams using
machine learning and model switching techniques. Our methodology comprises two clearly de­
fined phases: Phase 1 involves Model Preparation, while Phase 2 focuses on Model Update and
Switching.
The initial phase comprises three stages, with the primary objective being the meticulous prepara­
tion of algorithms to enhance their performance on the dataset. The overarching aim is to ensure
their optimal utilization in the subsequent phase. The first stage in Phase 1 is Data Pre­processing,
where we apply various techniques, such as outlier removal and missing values removal, to clean
and normalize the sensor data. This ensures the data quality and consistency for the subsequent
analysis. The second stage of our methodology is a standardized process that is uniformly applied
to all selected machine learning algorithms for subsequent stages, incorporating techniques such
as feature selection algorithms to identify optimal subsets of features tailored to each algorithm.
This approach not only reduces data dimensionality and complexity but also addresses potential
imbalances within the dataset through oversampling or undersampling methodologies. Moreover,
parameters are adjusted by each algorithm, resulting in the optimization of algorithmic perfor­
mance for maximal efficacy on the given dataset. The third stage, Model Selection, involves the
careful curation of models with the highest performance from those trained in the preceding stage.
The selection process is guided by rigorous evaluation using performance metrics such as accuracy,
precision, recall, AUC, and ROC. The model chosen through this meticulous assessment becomes
the focal point for the subsequent phase, namely Model Update and Switching.
Phase 2 is Model Update and Switching, where we apply different model update strategies, such as
continuous training, to adapt the models to changes in the data distribution and system dynamics.
We also apply different model­switching techniques, based on performance metrics, to select the
best model for each scenario. This enhances the adaptability and flexibility of the models and
ensures the system’s efficiency and reliability. The following subsections will describe each stage
of our methodology in more detail.

AdaBoost is a popular ensemble learning algorithm for classification tasks[28]. In AdaBoost, the
weak learners are usually decision trees with a single split, often referred to as ”stumps”. During
training, each weak learner is trained sequentially on the same dataset, focusing on instances that
were misclassified by the previous weak learner.
The key idea behind AdaBoost is to assign higher weights to misclassified instances and lower
weights to correctly classified instances. At each iteration, AdaBoost adjusts the weights of the
training instances based on their classification errors. AdaBoost is a powerful and versatile al­
gorithm that can achieve high accuracy by combining predictions from multiple weak learners.
However, it can be sensitive to noisy data and uniform noise.
(9) Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
SGD belongs to the family of gradient descent algorithms, which iteratively update the parameters
of a model in the direction that minimizes the loss function[29]. In classification tasks, the loss
function measures the difference between the model’s predictions and the true labels.The basic
principle of SGD is to reduce the value of the loss function by calculating the gradient of the
loss function with respect to the model parameters and updating the parameters along the negative
direction of the gradient. Since only a portion of the data (a randomly selected sample) is used for
each update, it is called stochastic gradient descent.
The basic update rule of SGD for updating the parameters θ of the model is given by:
θt+1 = θt −η∇J(θt;xi,yi)
where θt is the parameter vector at iteration t, η is the learning rate, and ∇J(θt;xi,yi) is the gradient
of the loss function J with respect to the parameters θt evaluated on the training example (xi,yi).
In summary, Stochastic Gradient Descent is a powerful optimization algorithm widely used in ma­
chine learning and deep learning. While it offers efficiency and can prevent overfitting, it requires
careful tuning of hyperparameters and may suffer from noisy updates.
Algorithm­specific feature selection
Feature selection, also referred to as feature subset selection, is a critical aspect of machine learning
aimed at choosing a subset of n valuable features (where n is less than or equal to m) from an initial
set of m features. The primary objective is to diminish the dimensionality of features, leading to
a reduction in computational complexity while simultaneously optimizing the effectiveness of the
model[30]. This process not only streamlines model complexity but also guards against overfitting.
Machine learning algorithms differ due to their reliance on distinct mathematical and statistical
principles, problem assumptions, and optimization goals[31]. These differences result in unique
characteristics related to complexity and expressive capabilities. Some algorithms prefer fewer
features to prevent overfitting, while others favor a larger set to capture complex relationships. Al­
gorithms also have preferences for handling sparse or dense data. Noise and sample size variations
in the dataset can influence feature selection. Sensitivities to noise and proficiency in addressing
small sample sizes vary among algorithms. Hence, it is crucial to perform algorithm­specific fea­
ture selection, choosing the optimal subset for each algorithm, aligning with their characteristics
and preferences, and enhancing overall model effectiveness[32].
The feature selection process considers a subset of features ranging from 3 to 6, given that the
dataset comprises only 6 features. Having less than 3 features in a subset is insufficient. The ob­
jective is to ensure the selection of the most suitable features for each machine learning algorithm.
This research employs a feature selection algorithm to generate four subsets of features, each com­

Algorithm­specific Hyperparameter Opimization
Every machine learning algorithm comes with hyperparameters, and how these hyperparameters
are set greatly influences the actual performance of the algorithm. Among all the algorithms for
hyperparameter optimization, grid search is the most basic and classic method. Before kicking
off the search, we need to manually list out the possible values for each hyperparameter. We then
arrange the different values of multiple hyperparameters in combinations, ultimately forming a
parameter space[39]. The grid search algorithm takes all the parameter combinations from this
space, plugs them into the model for training, and uses cross­validation to showcase the model’s
generalization ability. In the end, it picks the combination with the strongest generalization ability
as the final hyperparameter for the model. Similarly, since our dataset is imbalanced, we also
consider multiple evaluation criteria when assessing the results of the grid search.

4.3
Model selection
We have gathered performance metrics for each model after parameter tuning, which we will utilize
to exclude certain models from our model­switching strategies.
Firstly, models with significantly lower accuracy compared to others will be excluded. Despite our
dataset’s imbalance, accuracy remains a crucial metric for comparing model performance.
Secondly, models with relatively low performance in predicting True class data will also be re­
moved. Our objective is to detect anomalies in water quality, making it essential to uphold high
performance levels in predicting True class instances.
• OneClass SVM The OneClass SVM algorithm was not utilized in Phase 2 due to its sig­
nificantly lower accuracy compared to other models. This indicates its poor performance in
correctly predicting both true and false class data. Furthermore, its Area Under the Curve
(AUC) is notably low, reflecting its overall inadequate predictive capability.
• Isolation Tree The Isolation Tree algorithm has been removed from consideration as well.
This decision was made because its precision in predicting true class data is markedly low,
at only 0.0935. Additionally, its specificity does not demonstrate a sufficiently high value to
compensate for the low precision, rendering its inclusion in subsequent steps unnecessary.
• AdaBoost and Decision Tree The overall accuracy falls below 0.9, even accounting for the
imbalanced nature of the dataset. This implies that the performance metrics for accurately
predicting both true and false class data are unsatisfactory.

Discussion
5.1
Model Preparation
5.1.1
Model training and tuning based on algorithms
Regarding the section Algorithm­specific feature selection, the performance of the same algorithm
trained on distinct subsets of features varies, indicating the importance of choosing the appropriate
feature subset tailored to each machine learning method.
Given that our dataset is highly imbalanced, we employed both oversampling and undersampling
algorithms for each machine learning method and evaluated their performance. Nonetheless, we
observed that the application of oversampling and undersampling techniques did not markedly
enhance the performance of the individual machine learning algorithms. The potential reasons for
this observation are outlined below:
• Bias introduction When oversampling data, new data points are introduced manually or
through algorithms like SMOTE. SMOTE addresses imbalance by generating synthetic sam­
ples for the minority class. It randomly selects minority class samples and creates synthetic
ones by interpolating between them and their nearest neighbors. However, the synthetic
samples may not accurately represent the underlying distribution of the minority class, po­
tentially impacting the model’s performance[53]. Moreover, when different class data over­
lap in features, synthetic samples generated by SMOTE may widen the overlap between
classes, leading to blurred classification boundaries and reduced model generalization[54].
Undersampling, on the other hand, involves removing observations from the majority class
to balance the dataset. This process may introduce bias if critical observations are removed,
and the new sample may not accurately reflect the true test data.
• The dataset exhibits extreme imbalance Over­synthesis of minority class samples. In
datasets with extreme imbalances, the number of minority class samples may be extremely
small. Consequently, SMOTE may over­generate new minority class samples, potentially
resulting in overfitting or the introduction of noise. This can ultimately degrade the perfor­
mance of the model[55].
Although ”oversampling” and ”undersampling” methods are commonly used to solve unbalanced
dataset problems, they should not be used in our machine learning algorithm in this case[56].
Nevertheless, we can find some advantages in the results. Specifically, these methods significantly
improve specificity in most cases, suggesting that the model can effectively detect most of the few
categories of data through prediction. If the goal is solely around identifying anomalous data, with
no emphasis on accuracy or precision, then these methods can be considered.
5.1.2
Model selection
During this phase of analysis, the models OneClass SVM, Isolation Tree, AdaBoost, and Deci­
sion Tree have been eliminated from consideration for the subsequent phase. The exclusion of
the two unsupervised learning methods, OneClass SVM and Isolation Tree is attributed to their
low performance, suggesting that unsupervised learning approaches may not be well­suited for ex­
tremely imbalanced datasets. Moreover, the Decision Tree method has been ruled out; a possible
reason is that the Decision Tree may be too simplistic when compared to other methods that em­
ploy Decision Trees as weaker classifiers, such as XGB. Additionally, despite being an ensemble
learning algorithm like XGB, AdaBoost is also excluded because of its low performance, and on
this imbalanced dataset, AdaBoost does not surpass that of XGB.

5.2
Model Update Strategy
The dataset utilized in this study is extremely imbalanced, reflecting the reality wherein anomalies
in water quality data are rare, comprising only a small fraction of the total dataset. Given the high
accuracy scores typically associated with such datasets, we have chosen not to focus our analysis
on accuracy, as it may not provide meaningful insights. Instead, we prioritize Balanced Accuracy
as our metric because it captures the model’s performance across both the majority and minority
classes, penalizing any bias toward the predominant class.
Through the analysis of the results from both model update strategies, we have confirmed that the
Model Update Strategy is effective in selecting models that either achieve the highest or second­
highest Balanced Accuracy. While most models struggle to maintain high performance, the Model
Update Strategy manages to attain the best or second­best Balanced Accuracy relative to other
models.
Additionally, a comparative review of the outcomes for each model—with and without continuous
retraining—reveals a significant disparity in Balanced Accuracy, highlighting the necessity of con­
sistent retraining to adapt to data shifts. However, this approach necessitates vigilance to prevent
overfitting in each model as we strive to maintain their relevance and accuracy over time.
The Detecting Data Shift Training approach resulted in the models being retrained on 24 separate
occasions, correlating with the detection of data shifts across 24 days. In contrast, the Scheduled
Training strategy mandated retraining 8 times, while Threshold­Triggered Training called for 7
retrainings. From a computational efficiency standpoint, Threshold­Triggered Training proves
to be the most resource­conservative, as it necessitates the fewest number of retraining sessions.
Furthermore, when examining the balanced accuracy results depicted in Figure 5.1, it is evident
that the performance of the Threshold­Triggered Training is comparable to that of the Scheduled

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
The methodology encompasses a comprehensive process that begins with model preparation and
extends to the implementation of Model Update and Switching strategies. Three distinct model
update strategies were explored to examine their impact on performance, with efficacy gauged by
analyzing the resulting performance metrics.
Effects of different feature sets on various machine learning algorithms were investigated. Feature
selection algorithms were applied to identify unique feature subsets for each algorithm, followed
by experiments and recording the outcomes. Findings indicate that the choice of feature subsets
conserves computational resources and significantly affects algorithm performance, making the
selection of the appropriate feature subset crucial for optimizing different machine learning algo­
rithms.
Given the dataset’s imbalanced nature, attempts were made to mitigate this issue using ”oversam­
pling” and ”undersampling” techniques. However, these methods, while often employed to address
imbalances, did not prove effective in this study and even had a detrimental effect on the perfor­
mance of certain algorithms, indicating their unsuitability for this case. The extreme imbalance of
the dataset suggests that unsupervised learning approaches might be ill­suited for such data.
Three different model update strategies were recorded and contrasted with the results of standalone
models, with a focus on balanced accuracy. Comparing outcomes from the model update strategies
with those of the isolated models revealed that the update strategies could effectively select dif­
ferent models for optimal predictive performance. Additionally, results of each standalone model
subjected to continuous training through the update strategies were compared against those without
such training, confirming that the Model Update Strategies are robust and capable of adapting to
new data trends, yielding improved predictive outcomes.
During the study, overfitting was not encountered; however, continuous training carries a substan­
tial risk of overfitting, which could potentially impair performance.
6.2
Future work
6.2.1
Extremely imbalanced dataset processing
In this study, we utilized a dataset with extreme imbalance, posing significant difficulties for pre­
dictive modeling. Traditional techniques for imbalanced datasets such as oversampling and un­
dersampling were applied, yet they did not produce the desired outcomes. This ineffectiveness
in addressing the severe imbalance implies a limitation in conventional methods when confronted
with such skewed data distributions. The findings highlight a compelling need for a specialized al­
gorithm designed explicitly to manage the challenges of extremely imbalanced datasets, indicating
a promising avenue for future research and development.
6.2.2
The method of selecting the best model
The results from the three Model Update and Switching strategies highlight the importance of
continuous model retraining to maintain performance. Currently, we utilize cross­validation and
performance metrics to guide the selection of the best model for forecasting new data and iden­
tifying outliers. In future efforts, it would be beneficial to investigate different model selection
techniques during retraining. This exploration could potentially improve the effectiveness of the
model selection process, ensuring better adaptation to new data patterns and anomalies.
71
==PAGE:80==
6.2.3
The method of predicting
Currently, our approach involves selecting the best­performing model during the retraining phase
and employing it for subsequent predictions of new incoming data. While this technique generally
outperforms other single models, there are instances where the selected model does not yield the
most optimal results for certain days.
To address this variability, future work could entertain alternative prediction methodologies. For
instance, rather than relying on a single best model, we could identify the top three models and
leverage ensemble methods such as Voting or Stacking for making predictions[26]. This could po­
tentially provide a more robust and consistent performance across different periods. The outcomes
of this new, ensemble­based Model Update Strategy could then be compared with the current strat­
egy to evaluate any improvements in predictive accuracy and reliability.
6.2.4
Overfitting
The examination of learning curves for all models within this study shows no current signs of
overfitting. However, the possibility of overfitting remains an ongoing concern due to the regular
addition of new data and the iterative nature of model retraining. To proactively manage this risk,
continuous monitoring and experimental adjustments will be essential.
We are considering a strategy that involves periodic updates to the learning curves to identify any
substantial divergences between training and validation score trends. If a significant decline in
validation score is detected, we would document the corresponding training sample size at which
this change occurs. In subsequent training sessions, we would limit the data to this specific sample
size, focusing on incorporating fresh data. This method is anticipated to curtail the prospect of
overfitting as we persist in evolving our model training practices.
