Abstract 
The salt frost scaling is a problem in many countries with cold climate and the damage mechanism behind it 
is not fully understood yet. This experiment tries to contribute to the understanding of this mechanism by 
investigating if a pumping effect is presents and affects this damage mechanism. In this thesis a freeze/thaw 
test and chloride diffusion test are performed. The experiments take basis in the guidelines DS/CEN/TS 12390-
9 and NT BUILD 443 respectively with some variations in the exposure temperature to test its influence. 
Furthermore, specimens with different amount of air and water-cement-ratios (w/c-ratios) were tested on to 
see the influence of these factors as well. In connection with this an air void analysis according to ASTM C457 
was performed. The mixing of the concrete was fulfilled on a mixing plant at DTU and in the mixes 
superplasticisers (SP) as well as air entraining agencies (AEA) was added to represent concrete from the 
industry. 
The results indicated that a pumping effect may be present which especially the air content had an influence 
on. Furthermore, this thesis concludes that a higher air content and lower w/c-ratio contribute to the frost 
resistance of concrete. Similar results were seen in the diffusion of chlorides. Moreover, does the report 
conclude that the exposure temperature interval of a freeze/thaw test is important and that a thawing plateau 
of 5°C results in more scaling.

1. Introduction 
Last year in Denmark the average days with temperatures going below 0°C was 81.2 days while the number 
of days with temperatures below 0°C throughout the day was 15.9 [1]. Water freezes at 0°C and the 
transformation of water to ice is accompanied by an expansion of 9%. Most of us have experienced this 
expansion by putting a can of soda in the freezer and afterwards found it all bend outwards. This expansion of 
phase shift in water can also result in damage of concrete. Previous studies have shown that concretes that 
additionally are exposed to a saline solution are particularly vulnerable and even more so if the saline solution 
have a concentration of 3% sodium chloride. This specific damage mechanism is known as salt frost scaling 
[2] [3].  
 
Denmark has a practice of salting the roads with sodium chloride to prevent snow accumulation and ice 
formation. This is good for the safety of the road users but the splashing of saline solution on bridge shoulders 
or bridge peers at overpasses, results in a cocktail that can be severe for the concrete. Denmark is furthermore 
an island nation with a lot of coast and seas with a salt concentration around 3% [4]. When a wave hit a 
concrete structure in one of the many harbours around the country the so-called splash zone is an area 
especially exposed to frost scaling. With the many days with temperatures below 0°C and the many areas 
exposed to salt-frost scaling Denmark is one of the many countries that tries to prevent this damage 
mechanism. Both because of the cost of repair or replacement of existing structures but also in terms of saving 
CO2 from performing the latter. It is not only the salt frost scaling that is a concern in the splash zone. The 
diffusion of chlorides and access to air, results over time in what is called pitting corrosion of the reinforcement 
inside the concrete. Ultimately inducing cracks from internal expansion, decreasing the cross-sectional area 
of the reinforcement, and together decreasing the structure’s strength. 
 
In a previous Ph.D. study by Hasholt [5] a tendency was shown where the water uptake increased during the 
freezing period and decreased again during the thawing period of a freeze/thaw experiment. This tendency 
could indicate a pumping effect. This report will try and test if a pumping effect is present for concrete exposed 
to varying temperatures and what effect it has on both salt frost scaling and chloride diffusion.

2.1 Portland Cement 
 
Rapid (CEM I 52.5 N (MS) (LA)) is an OPC with the strength class 52.5 N [6] meaning that the cement has a 
normal strength progress that will reach a compressive strength of 52.5MPa within 28 days of hardening. Rapid 
consists of 95-100% clinkers and 5-0% gypsum [6]. The first mentioned is made from limestone, sand, clay, 
and iron rich materials which when heated to temperatures above 1300°C creates the main clinker materials: 
 
- 
Belite (2CaO.SiO2, C2S) 
- 
Alite (3CaO.SiO2, C3S) 
- 
Aluminate (3CaO.Al2O3, C3A) 
- 
Ferrite (4CaO.Al2O3.Fe2O3, C4AF) 
 
As seen from the chemical notations above the clinkers Belite and Alite is made from a sintering process 
between CaO and SiO2, while Aluminate and Ferrite is formed during the cooling phase from crystallisation of 
Al2O3 and Fe2O3 bound with calcium silicate. 
Beforehand, the calcium oxide has calcined from the limestone while releasing CO2 during the 450-900°C 
range of the heating process as seen by the equation below. 
 
CaCO3 →CaO + CO2 
(1) 
 
Aluminate contributes to the early strength development, Blite contributes to the late development, while Alite 
contributes to both early and late development. Ferrite has only a limited contribution to the strength 
development [7].

2.2 Frost damage mechanism 
 
There are several hypotheses for the mechanism for frost damage of concrete. The frost damage can be 
separated into inner damage and outer/surface damage. The inner damage consists of internal cracks while 
the surface damage is the local flaking or peeling of the surface that is significantly worse with saline-solutions 
as freezing medium. The latter is also known as salt frost scaling. There are several hypotheses why these 
phenomena occurs but a common factor in them all is the 9% expansion of water during ice formation that 
leads to destructive stresses. 
 
The inner damage is typically caused by the internal water freezing and expanding in the capillary pores. If the 
distance to a nearby air void are too long this expansion will appear under confinement and will apply pressure 
on the inner surfaces of the concrete. This pressure will lead to internal stresses and cracks which allow more 
water to enter the body and ultimately lead to corrosion of the reinforcement. To prevent this phenomenon the 
concrete either needs to be strong enough to withstand these induced stresses or allow for the expansion to 
continue until all the internal water is frozen. The latter can be achieved by adding Air Entrained Agencies 
(AEA) to implement more air bubbles that are placed closer to each other. This will allow the unfrozen water 
during the freezing period to travel through the capillary pores between air bubbles, thus distribute the pressure 
and preventing cracking.  
The distance between air bubbles (described by the spacing factor) has therefore proven to be an important 
factor for frost resistance and should be less than 8.47-6.35 microns to prevent the hydraulic pressure from 
causing damage. However, with a combination of a strong concrete the maximum spacing factor can be as 
large as 250 microns (0.25mm or 0.01 in.) [2]. 
 
The outer frost damage does not appear directly from the same phenomena as for the inner damage. In fact, 
the salt frost scaling has multiple hypotheses none of which has been completely proven yet. One of the 
hypotheses is the Glue spall mechanism which proposes that cracks in the freezing medium on top of the 
concrete is allowed to propagate into the concrete due to the ice “gluing” itself to the surface of concrete. The 
theory behind this hypothesis is that a thermo-mechanical mismatch appears between the frozen layer and 
concrete which results in cracks in the concrete. If no salt is mixed into the freezing medium the ice will be too 
strong which means no cracks will go through the ice. This is seen in Figure 1 a).  On the other hand, too much 
salt mixed into the freezing medium will result in brine pockets that make the ice too weak to afflict any stresses 
into the concrete. This is seen in Figure 1 b). At the pessimum concentration of 3% salt-solution some pockets 
of brine will form and lead to cracks in the frozen layer. Meanwhile, the rest of the freezing medium have a 
strong bond to the underlying concrete which allows the cracks to propagate into the surface of the concrete 
[2]. This is seen in Figure 1 c). 
 
 
Figure 1. Glue-spalling mechanisms. Figure 5-12, Concrete: Microstructure, Properties, and Materials [2].

Another hypothesis is the cryo-suction mechanism which proposes that the freezing medium is sucked into 
the surface of the concrete, where micro ice bodies have formed due to a lower salt-concentration of the pore-
solution. The freezing medium is then either sucked directly to the micro ice bodies, or the water from adjacent 
pores is sucked to local ice formation. The latter is then followed by freezing medium being sucked into the 
now empty adjacent pores. This suction may result in a micro ice body growth which expands too fast for the 
pressure to be dispersed between the adjacent pores [3] [10]. 
 
A factor beside the air content that in general improves the frost resistance is the strength of the cement. 
Moreover, the strength of concretes with small water-cement-ratios (w/c-ratios) has proven to be higher due 
to the simultaneously lower porosity and increase in the strength of the interfacial transition zone (ITZ) [2].

The amount of air in the hardened concrete can be controlled by adding an AEA to the mixing procedure and 
adjusting the mixing speed on the fresh concrete. An AEA does not introduce air into the concrete but merely 
lowers the surface tension of the water to promote bubble formation and stabilizes the small air bubbles by 
creating a protective layer at the air-water interface. Note that too much AEA added to the mix will delay the 
cement hydration because the AEA makes the cement particles hydrophobic so that air can attach to them [2]. 
The amount of air that is added to the fresh concrete is a result of the rough surface of the aggregates. During 
the mixing procedure air is pushed into the fresh concrete with the aggregates, possibly by the dents of the 
rough surface [14].    
 
 
2.4 Effect of superplasticiser 
 
A superplasticiser is a high range water-reducing admixture that contains long-chain anionic surfactants. 
A surfactant is a surface-active-agent that when mixed with water reduces its surface tension and increases 
the viscosity thus enhancing the workability of the concrete in fresh state [15]. A superplasticiser can also 
increase the hydration process due to the increased dispersion of the cement matrix. The previously mentioned 
AEA is also a surfactant and may therefore contribute to the workability depending on the type of admixtures 
used. A relatively large amount of superplasticizer can be added to a fresh concrete without causing bleeding 
compared to ordinary water-reducing admixture. This is probably due to the long-chain particles of the 
admixture [2]. 
 
The modern superplasticisers are made of polymer chains that have a comb-like structure. The polymer chains 
work by one side of the chain being absorbed on the surface of the cement particles while the other side 
creates steric repulsion that decreases the friction between cement particles [2]. 
 
 
Figure 2. Illustration of the mechanisms decreases the friction between cement particles. Figure 8-5 from 
Concrete: Microstructure, Properties, and Materials [2]. 
 
These features of better workability, faster hydration and low amount of additive is why superplasticisers are 
so commonly used in the concrete industry today.

2.5 Chloride ingress and binding 
 
Chloride ingress in concrete can be a problem to the underlying reinforcement, as the chlorides can break the 
protective film placed around the reinforcement and cause pitting corrosion. The protective film consists of 
layers of mainly Fe3O4 and Fe3O3 that is formed because of the alkaline conditions caused by the high content 
of calcium hydroxides (CH) that ensures a PH-level between 12 to 14. When the Cl−/OH− molar ratio goes 
above 0.6 the protective film is destroyed patch wise [2]. 
 
The ingress of chlorides occurs with four different transport mechanisms, permeation, capillary action, diffusion 
inside a solution, and/or migration due to an electric current being precent. The latter mentioned occurs less 
frequent than the others as it is uncommon for an electric current to be present in concrete structures. The 
transport by diffusion is the transport mechanism that will be focused on in this report. 
Diffusion is the transfer of free molecules in a solution from a region with high concentration to a region with 
low concentration. For a diffusion to take place inside a concrete a liquid must therefore be transported into 
the pore structure beforehand. Here the air content of the concrete plays a big role. More air voids allow for 
more water to transport into the concrete and thus allows for a greater diffusion [16] [17].  
 
In case of chloride diffusion, it is the free chloride ions that is transported from an exposure solution on the 
outside into the pore solution of the concrete. A way to describe the rate in which the chlorides diffuse through 
some concrete is with the diffusion coefficient Df. The diffusion coefficient can be determined by Equation (11) 
that is the solution to Fick’s second law due to the non-steady-state diffusion process of concrete.

From the equation above the diffusion coefficient can be estimated by a best-fit curve to a measured chloride

2.6 Potentiometric chloride titration 
 
Potentiometric titration is a technique to measure the chloride content in a solution by measuring the potential 
change while adding silver nitrate (AgNO3) to the solution.  
When silver nitrate is dissolved in the solution silver and chloride ions will create the following reaction: 
 
Ag+ + Cl−→AgCl (s) 
(13) 
 
When all the chloride ions have been bound to form AgCl the double derivative of the potential measured will 
change sign. By measuring the potential with an Ag/AgCl electrode while gradually adding silver nitrate one 
can then measure the content of chloride [22]. The Chloride content is calculated from the difference in molar 
mass between silver and chloride ions. Usually, the above-mentioned method is performed by adding a 
0.1mol/l silver nitrate solution which mean that the chloride content can be calculated by the following equation: 
 
Weight%Cl−= 3.545 ⋅V ⋅N
m  
(14) 
 
Here V is the amount of added silver nitrate solution (ml) with the normality N and m is the dry mass of the 
sample. The multiplication of 3.545 comes from chloride’s molar mass of 35.45 g/mol with the change from ml 
to l (divide by 1000) and change to percentage (multiply by 100) [23].

2.8 Hypotheses 
 
From the previously described theory, the air content of a concrete has influence on both the frost resistance 
and the chloride diffusion. Furthermore, the temperature also plays a role in the chloride transport. With this 
the following hypotheses will be analysed and tried answered in this report: 
 
- 
A pumping effect will occur inside concrete when exposed to varying temperatures. 
 
- 
A lower thawing plateau in the freeze/thaw-test temperature curves will result in worse scaling.

3. Materials and methods 
3.1 Mix design, mixing procedure & specimen processing 
 
The mix designs used in this project was based on concretes used in the industry. After the initial mix designs 
had been laid a total of twenty trial castings was performed to optimize the workability and air content with as 
little paste content as possible. Here the content of sand, paste, SP and AEA was adjusted as well as the 
mixing speed of the stirrer and mixing pan.  
 
A total of five mixes were mixed with Rapid cement. Three of the five mixes were made with a w/c-ratio of 0.40 
with varying air contents, while the last two mixes were made with a w/c-ratio of 0.45. Two of the mixes with 
w/c-ratio of 0.40 were added different amount of AEA to create a high and medium air content. One of the 
mixes with w/c-ratio of 0.45 was added AEA to match the highest air content of mixes with w/c-ratio of 0.40. 
For each mix three Ø150x300mm cylinders were casted for a freeze/thaw test and three Ø100x200mm 
cylinders were cast for a compressive strength test following the dimension requirements stated in the 
European standard DS/EN 12390-1 [24]. Furthermore, six Ø100x200mm cylinders were cast from the mixes 
with the highest air content and w/c-ratio of 0.40 along with two Ø100x200mm from the other mixes. These 
were used for a chloride diffusion test. The larger number of specimens from the air rich mixes are to observe 
whether there are variances over the individual specimens.

o 100x200mm

After the initial trials DTU’s custom concrete mixing plant made by Eirich was used to mimic the mixing 
procedure in the industry. The Mixing plant can be seen in Figure 3. 
 
 
Figure 3. Eirich concrete mixing plant. Silos with constituents (left), Mixer (right). 
 
Due to an error with the mixing plant the mixes where casted over two days instead of the initially planned one 
day. 
Each mixing started by writing in the mix design on the control panel and determine the amount one wanted 
out. In this case, a batch of 60L was made for all mixes to fill the required cylinders and to test on the fresh 
concrete, as wells as to have a little extra in case something was spilled. After the amounts had been specified, 
the machine would then drive a cart underneath the silos (see the left image of Figure 3) and collect the dry 
constituents in the following order: 1. Cement, 2. Sand, 3. Small stones, and 4. Large stones. The cart would 
then drive back to its initial position where it would be lifted, rotated horizontally, and driven over the mixer 
(see the right image of Figure 3). The mixer would then start rotating the barrel clockwise and stirring arm 
counterclockwise. The barrel was set to rotate with a speed of 1 m/s and the stirring arm with 6 m/s. The speed 
of the barrel was satisfying from the start while the speed of the stirring arm was adjusted doing the trials to 
make the SP work most sufficiently and thus lowering the water/paste content. 
 
After the mixer had started rotating the dry constituents would be dumped from the cart into the mixer via a 
tube. The dry constituents would then be mixed for 15 seconds, followed by the addition of water over a 15 
second duration. Thereafter, the mixer would mix for another 180 seconds. The SP and AEA were manually 
added within the first 30 seconds after the water intake. 
 
When the concrete was properly mixed, it was dumped out of the mixer into a large bucket and transported for 
a minute close over beside a vibration table. Here a slump test and air content measurement were done as 
==PAGE:18==
14 of 84 
 
described in the next section. After the fresh concrete property tests, the moulds were filled halfway and 
vibrated on the vibration table at a minimum frequency of 40 Hz as stated by DS/EN 12390-1 [24]. The concrete 
was vibrated until no more large air bubbles exited the concrete. Thereafter, the mould was filled up to the top 
and vibrated again. Any excess concrete was scraped off with a float and the lid was mounted and tightened. 
Lastly the filled moulds were cleaning on the outside and prepared for storing. 
 
 
Slump and air content test on fresh concrete 
 
Material list: 
- Materials listed in the following standards: 
o DS/EN 12350-2 [27]. 
o DS/EN 12350-7 [28]. 
 
The slump test was performed by the procedure described by DS/EN 12350-2 [27]. A minimum slump of 80mm 
was sought after to compare with the industry. Slumps lower than 80mm are hard to work with and air in large 
structures are hard to vibrate out.  
The Air content test on fresh concrete was performed with the pressure gauge method described in DS/EN 
12350-7 [28]. The bucket of the pressure gauge meter was filled halfway (approximately 4L) and compacted 
with the same vibration table as used to cast the cylinders. The rest of the bucket was filled and vibrated again. 
The standard says that removal of excess concrete should be avoided, however due to practical reasons the 
bucket was overfilled, and as little as possible excess material was scraped off with a trowel. 
The bucket was weighted before and after being filled with fresh concrete to estimate the density of the fresh 
concrete.

3.2 Air void analysis on hardened concrete 
 
Material list: 
- Materials stated in the standard ASTM C457 [11]. 
- Silicon carbide abrasives of No. 1200. 
- Ultrasonic cleaner 
 
For the air void analysis on the hardened concrete procedure B in ASTM C457 [11] was used. This method is 
also known as the modified point-count method. A single 20x95x95mm square was as mentioned previously 
cut from the thin discs. The discs were taken randomly from one of the 3 cylinders casted of each mix. The 
dimensions of the cut-out ensured that the later analysed area would satisfy the requirement of a total area of 
71 cm2 to be analysed. A dimension of 8.5x8.5cm gives an area of 72.25 cm2 which would have been enough. 
However, any skewness of the cut edges or bad grinding quality near the edges could cause problems with 
the photography and analysis of the specimens. Therefore, a size of 9.5x9.5cm was chosen. 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
17 of 84 
 
Before procedure B in ASTM C457 [11] could be used the samples had to be grinded to a smooth surface to 
make the contrast between constituents and air void as sharp as possible. The method of doing so was to first 
plain the specimens with a diamond grinding disc. Afterwards the specimens were grinded with silicon carbide 
abrasives of No. 220, 600, 800, and 1200 in the presented order changing to a finer abrasive when no 
improvements could be detected. In between each shift of abrasive, the grinding disc was cleaned with 
demineralized water, and the specimens was cleaned in an ultrasonic cleaner. The ultrasonic cleaner was set 
to a 130Hz frequency with only 50% power to shake out the small particles while preventing damages to the 
microstructure. The specimens were first cleaned standing upright with 1 minute on each of the four sides and 
hereafter for 1 minute on each of the flat surfaces.  
 
All abrasives were used for all specimens except one where only N0. 220 and 1200 was used. This was done 
to test whether all the different abrasives were necessary to use. No clear differences were observed, and the 
grinding time used was close to the same. The difference was evaluated by an untrained eye, so there might 
be a slight difference. It was however judged that the possible difference would not affect the quality of the air 
void analysis. The images below show the grinding and cleaning process of the specimens. 
 
 
Figure 5. Grinding process (left), cleaning process of specimens (middle), and ultrasonic cleaner (right). 
 
After processing of the specimens, the point count analysis was performed with a 2mm grid. This resulted in 
a total transverse line of 3528mm and 1764 points to be categorised fulfilling the requirement by ASTM C457 
[11]. ASTM C457 [11] requires a total transverse line of 2286mm and 1350 points.

freezing medium had taken placed without interference.

The specimens for the chloride diffusion test were fully saturated in the Ca(OH)2-solution by soaking the 
specimens for a week. The standard says that one should weigh the specimens every day until constant mass 
is reached. However, a week should be more than sufficient to fully saturate the specimens. After the

separate plastic container with a thermocouple. The containers were filled with the NaCl-solution stated in NT

containers with the specimens were then placed in one of the two temperature-controlled/programmable 
cupboards and the exposure experiment started.

temperatures inside the containers had resulted in an expansion of the air. This caused it to leak out with some 
of the solution as well. This sealing did however not work for long as the high moisture content and low flexibility 
resulted in the duct tape losing its grip. Therefore, the duct tape was replaced with a wide insulation tape with 
a higher flexibility on the 25th day of exposure which seemed to work for the rest of the experiment.

As seen from Table 8 and the left graph of Figure 9 the air content in the hardened concrete is higher than that

fresh concrete are close to the same no matter the amount of air. The fresh air content on the x-axis of the 
right graph of Figure 9 is calculated with Equation (16) and shows that the measured values are higher than 
the estimated ones from densities.

This method of estimating the air content based on densities is uncertain due to variation in amounts and 
individual densities of constituents. It should therefore only be used to roughly estimate the air content. Note 
that two values go below zero in air content. As the amounts of constituents in all mixes were close to what 
was intended, as seen in Appendix 7.5, the error must be due to higher densities of the constituents than 
anticipated. This just shows how uncertain this method of estimating air content is. 
 
The paste content stated in Table 8 differs from the mix designs with an intended paste content of 32.4%. The 
paste content of mixes with w/c-ratio of 0.40 were higher while the rest was lower than the intended paste 
content. This shows that the distribution of the constituents was not dispersed to perfection. As the content 
only differs 5.9% at max with a standard deviation of 3.9%, they do not differ significantly as a completely 
uniformly distributed concrete is nearly impossible to produce. Furthermore, the analysis only gives results for 
a single depth in the concrete. Results that before gave a lower paste content than intended might give a 
higher value if a depth is just changed a little. As long as the paste content only deviates a small percentage 
from the intended value it is considered dispersed adequately. 
 
As seen from Equation (9) the paste-air ratio determines how the spacing factor is calculated. Based on the 
values stated in Table 8 all the mixes are calculated with the same equation as all are above 4.342. With the 
specific surfaces found from the counted air voids the spacing factor was calculated. Both sets of values are 
shown in Table 8. A clear relation between the air content and spacing factor can be seen as high air content 
results in small spacing factor and vice versa. There is however not a linear relation as can be seen by R-0.40-

4.4 Compressive strength and densities 
 
As seen from Table 9 and Figure 13 there is a good correlation between the compressive strength, the density, 
and thus also the air content. Note that R-0.40-L sticks out from this trend with a lower strength than R-0.40-
M even though the density is larger. This is due to one of the three cylinders tested on for  
R-0.40-L that had an abnormal failure curve as seen from Figure 12. The reason for this is unclear but could 
be due to large air voids or a result of uneven dispersion of constituents. The abnormal failure curve was in 
most cases also accompanied with an unsatisfying failure shape of the cylinder. As seen from the last colon 
in Table 9 several unsatisfying failure shapes was recorded which in some cases results in a larger standard 
deviation. If the abnormal cylinder of R-0.40-L is neglected the compressive strength would be 66.3 MPa which 
is a 7.85% increase in strength. This value also agrees with the trend of higher compressive strength for lower 
air content. 
 
Interestingly R-0.45-H had an unsatisfying failure shape on one of the cylinders but did not disturb the failure 
curve nor resulted in a different compressive strength than the other two. The standard deviation of R-0.40-H 
is a little high due to another abnormal failure of one of the cylinders. Before this cylinder was tested on it was 
observed that the top was a little porous. This was probably due to a bleeding effect inflicted during the vibration 
procedure of the fresh concrete. 
 
When comparing the mixes with the equivalent amount of air the mixes made with a w/c-ratio of 0.45 has a 
lower compressive strength than those with a w/c-ratio of 0.40 as expected.

From the figures below can be seen zoomed parts of the temperature curves around the transition from liquid 
to solid state of the freezing medium. As seen the transition generally appears around -2°C which is close to

freezing point depression formula shown below. 
 
𝑇𝑓_𝑁𝑎𝐶𝑙= 𝑇𝑓_𝑤−∆𝑇𝑓= 𝑇𝑓_𝑤−𝑚𝑁𝑎𝐶𝑙⋅𝐾𝑓⋅𝑖 
(17) 
 
Here 𝑇𝑓_𝑁𝑎𝐶𝑙 is the freezing point of the NaCl-solution, 𝑇𝑓_𝑤 is the freezing point of water (0°C), and ∆𝑇𝑓 is the 
freezing point depression. ∆𝑇𝑓 is calculated from the solution’s molality 𝑚𝑁𝑎𝐶𝑙= 0.529 𝑚𝑜𝑙/𝑘𝑔, The cryoscopic 
constant for the freezing point of water 𝐾𝑓= 1.86°𝐶 𝑘𝑔/𝑚𝑜𝑙, and the Van’t Hoff factor 𝑖= 2 [35].

different temperature intervals. The difference in scaled material, ΔS56, is calculated by subtracting S56 
exposed to HT with that of LT. The percentage change, ΔP56, is calculated by dividing ΔS56 with the scaled 
material exposed to HT. As seen in Table 12, the negative difference between the scaled material between 
HT and LT is lower for mixes with higher air content, and in case of R-0.40-M the HT is even larger resulting 
in a positive difference.   
 
This tendency could indicate that pumping effect has taken place. Both temperature curves would 
theoretically result in a pumping effect because of the temperature change. However, the high temperature 
interval should result in a larger amount of material pumped into the concrete. This combined with the fact 
that specimens with higher air content have a bigger void volume to allow for more material to be pumped in. 
As more solution would have entered these specimens more ice would also have formed and resulted in 
more scaled material. This does however not explain why R-0.40-M have a higher ΔP56 than R-0.40-H. 
 
Two other factors that could explain the above-mentioned tendency is the diffusion and time in which the 
exposure solution in LT and HT are in liquid state. As specimens exposed to HT are longer above the 
freezing point and the diffusion have longer time to work then more chlorides can be bound. The amount of 
freeze-able water should therefore also increase and hereby increase the scaling. The reason why R-0.40-H 
and R-0.45-H still have a higher scaling for LT could be that the cryosuction is simply more dominant than 
diffusion.

Theoretical volume of sucked exposure liquid: 
 
The theoretical values stated in the figure below are based on the air content of the hardened concrete and 
the volume from the height of the specimens. It is assumed that the pressure and the amount of substance 
(air) remains constant, but that the volume of the air change and sucks in a volume of solution. Here the 
previously mentioned oscillate temperatures was used. As seen from the theoretical values of Figure 25 the 
air content dictates how much solution that can be sucked in. This is because it is the only factor that changes 
between mixes in the calculations. 
 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
41 of 84 
 
 
Figure 25. Theoretical volume of solution sucked into the concrete due to temperature change. 
 
These calculations that leads to the results above does not consider the resistance in the concrete nor the 
amount of saturation. They therefore merely indicate how much solution that could potentially be sucked in 
given enough time. It does however give a sense of how much solution that the specimens can contain in the 
air voids. 
 
 
Measured chloride profiles: 
 
The figures on the next few pages shows the chloride profiles of each mix. All the chloride profiles reach or 
gets really close to the initial chloride content before the depth of 30mm. This means the chloride front analysis 
was successful in estimating the required depth.

spread seems to also be affected by the uncertainty of the skew exposure surface. The chloride profile of

Comparing the chloride profiles by their w/c-ratio it is visible that the chloride content at a depth of 17.5mm is 
higher for mixes with w/c-ratios of 0.45. This indicates that more chlorides have diffused into these mixes.

4.7 Summary of discussion 
 
Now that the results have been represented and the individual subject have been briefly discussed where 
seemed fit. This section will summarize some of the important discussions before going to the conclusion. 
 
As seen from the comparison between scaled material and diffusion coefficient it is hard to make a connection 
that could prove that a common pumping effect is present. Both because of the trends shown but also because 
of the uncertainties. This does however not exclude the possibility that a pumping effect may be present as 
some of the results could indicate this.  
In the freeze/thaw test the results showed that specimens with higher air content had a lower difference in 
scaled material at 56 cycles between LT and HT, which could likely be due to a pumping effect increasing the 
amount of water in the specimen that could freeze. The results of the highest air content were however lower 
in difference than the medium air content which disturbs this theory. 
In the chloride diffusion the mean chloride content indicated that the overall number of chlorides in the concrete 
were increasing for higher air contents, which could indicate a pumping effect. However, because of high 
uncertainties and an unclear trend in the diffusion coefficients (which one usually evaluate the chloride 
resistance with) a pumping effect may not be present.

5. Conclusion 
With the above-mentioned results and discussion, it can with certainty be said that the exposure temperature 
interval has influence on the scaling, where specimens without AEA and specimen with a w/c-ratio of 0.45 
scaled more. Furthermore, it can be concluded that the amount of air and size of spacing factor have an 
influence on the frost resistance.  
 
Likewise, the air content plays a role in the diffusion of chlorides by increasing the diffusion for higher air 
contents. The exposure time between 35 and 44 days can be concluded to have a significantly reduced 
influence on the chloride profiles than the exposure time between 0 and 35 days. 
 
The results from the freeze/thaw-test and chloride diffusion test indicated that a pumping effect may be present. 
However, because of the non-strict tendencies of both experiments and the number of uncertainties in the 
chloride diffusion test it cannot be concluded that a pumping effect is present. The theory of an existing 
pumping effect can on the other hand not be discarded as some tendencies indicated towards this. 
 
From the compressive strength test, one can conclude that the specimens reached an expected compressive 
strength after 28 days of hardening.  
 
 
5.1 Recommendations for future studies 
 
The possible pumping effect observed from the amounts of scaled materials should be investigated further 
with different temperature curves and higher number of specimens. Furthermore, the rubber sheets used 
should be changed to rule out an edge effect. The method of applying the rubber sheets should also be 
optimised to prevent leakages. 
 
A new chloride diffusion experiment is recommended where the specimens are cut with a straight diamond 
saw and the chloride concentration should be shaken more thoroughly before used. Furthermore, the 
containers should be sealed with insulation tape that is more stretchable and does not lose its grip at high 
moisture content. Moreover, to rule out any influence the phase change of Friedel’s salt may have the 
experiment is recommended to change the varying temperature to between 0°C and 30°C. Lastly more 
specimens of the same mix design should be tested on to see the uncertainties of mixes with different air 
contents and w/c-ratios.

7.2 Design procedure of Grinding box 
 
Some previous students from DTU had made a box for the grinding procedure that consisted of a plastic box, 
a lid with a groove, and two sheets of plexiglass. The design can be seen in the figure below. 
 
 
Figure 44. Original design of grinding box. 
 
The original design worked relatively okay but had its flaws. As the box was made of plastic the hot speeding 
powder shards created from the grinding procedure had over time made the inner surface rough. This 
resulted in the finer powder to stick hereon. Furthermore, one had to constantly move the sheets of 
plexiglass as the grinder moved sideways. Even as the plexiglass prevented the larger concrete particles to 
leave the container the finer powder would fly out through the large gabs as seen to the left on the above 
figure. Beside the problems with the collections of powder the original box also had a problem with the 
dimensions. The specimen had to be clogged at least once as the height inside the box was too small and 
the grinding head could not pass all the way over the specimen. 
It should be mentioned that some of these problems was first discovered on the original design during the 
testing in the design procedure. 
To eliminate these uncertainties and inconvenience a design process was initiated. The new box had to 
solve the following challenges: 
 
1. The box and lid had to be smooth to prevent powder to stick to it. 
2. The box had to contain the specimen while holding it still during the grinding procedure. 
3. The lid had to allow the grinder to move horizontally and vertically while preventing powder from 
leaving the box. 
4. The powder collected by the box had to be easy to collect after each layer.  
 
Challenge 3 was proven to be the most difficult challenge to surpass. 
 
 
==PAGE:66==
62 of 84 
 
First design: 
 
The first design concept took basis in the original design. The design consisted of a metal box to prevent the 
hot powder from creating a rough surface and thus solving challenge 1. The box had to have a hole with a 
diameter of 103mm in the middle of the bottom part to allow for the specimen to stick through into the box. 
The specimen would then have to be held firm by a vice underneath the box. This is like the original design. 
The lid would be made of two plexiglass sheets with a groove as wide as the fitting above the grinding head. 
The lids had to be large enough to always cover the whole box during the movement of the grinder. The lids 
would be mounted opposite each other only allowing a small gap to form around the fitting above the 
grinding head. The design can be seen by the sketch below. The red parts are the grinding parts.  
 
 
Figure 45. Sketch of the first design proposal. 
 
After some talk with the technicians the design proposal was parked as it was proposed to just update the 
original design. 
 
 
Update of original design: 
 
The original design was looked upon again and it was concluded that the lid was the largest problem in 
original design. To keep the powder from leaving the box it was proposed that the already existing groove 
could be covered with two rubber mats. The rubber mats were to span over the gap and meet in the middle 
thus sealing the groove while allowing the grinder to move. This was tested by taping the rubber mats 
temporarily onto the lid and trying the grinder on some dummies. As seen by the figure below the fitting 
above the grinding head would spread the rubber sheets and create a triangular shaped gap for powder to 
leave. A second try was made where the rubber mats would overlap over the gap. This only resulted in the 
same result. 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
63 of 84 
 
 
Figure 46. First and second concept of sealing rubber mats. 
 
During these trials it was discovered that the grinding head could not move all the way over the specimen 
due to the placement of the hole in the bottom and the side of the box. Therefore, a new and longer box 
would have to be made. This time with the hole in the middle and made of steel at initially intended. 
While the box was being produced several trials was made with the rubber mats to see if the gap could be 
eliminated by for example flipping the mats up or downwards. None of it eliminated the gap and the flipping 
upwards only resulting the tearing of the rubber mat as seen from the left image below. 
 
 
 
Figure 47. Tearing of the rubber mat. Second (left) and third (right) concept of sealing rubber mats. 
 
==PAGE:68==
64 of 84 
 
After some thought about using brushes like the ones used under a door it was tried implementing the same 
concept in the rubber mats by cutting the mats into smaller slats overlapping each other. This did solve the 
large triangular gap but did instead allow for several smaller gaps to form thus allowing the same amount of 
powder to leave. Furthermore, the friction between the rubber would tear off the rubber slats as seen in the 
right image above. 
 
The weak rubber mats lead the design process to take a step back to the properties of the harder plexiglass. 
The original and first proposal was combined in a design of the lid to consist of two sheets of plexiglass with 
a half circle cut into the side allowing the fitting above the grinding head to rotate in between the pair. The 
sheets of plexiglass then had to be connected to each other around the fitting with a hinge lock and cover the 
groove in the lid. A challenge occurred as the original lid was placed a little below the top of the box as seen 
in figure of the original design. This prevented the plexiglass to move outside the edges of the box. A 
proposal was made to cut off a part of the edges in the lid and box to allow for the plexiglass to slide out. 
This would however not guarantee the lid confine the powder to the box. Furthermore, there was some 
concern whether the plexiglass would melt over time due to the friction caused by the grinder dragging the 
plexiglass. 
 
With these last two problems the concept for the original design formed.  
 
 
Final design: 
 
As the plexiglass had to move outside the edges of the box they were combined with the lid and changed to 
now lay on top of the edges of the box. Furthermore, the lid was design to be in one piece with a hole in the 
middle instead of the two sheets of plexiglass. This meant that the lid had to be semi-permanently mounted 
on the grinder but eliminated any seams in the previous design concept. The lid was bent in the sides around 
the outside of the box to prevent the lid from rotating when in contact with the grinder. The lid was decided to 
be made of metal to prevent melting of the edge of the centre hole and to prevent the powder to stick to the 
lid. The height of the box allows for a total grinding depth of around 35mm without changing the clogging 
during the grinding procedure. The design can be seen in the figure below.

New design challenges: 
 
The new design works but still have some flaws. First off, the gaps around the grinder and specimen allows 
for powder to fly out as seen from the figure below. Furthermore, it was observed the powder was slowly 
seep out of the seam between the lid and the box. The amount of material leaving the box was minimal but 
further design tweak might reduce it even further.

Further notes: 
It should be mentioned that several design concepts with rails and moving parts was thought about during 
the whole design process. Common for them all was the challenge of keeping the lid tight while moving in 
several directions. Some of the concepts are listed below. 
 
- 
Rail system with multiple lids that could move sideways (somewhat like an expanding selfie stick) 
- 
Ventilation tube formed to be square in the bottom and round in the bottom and tightened with 
reusable strips. 
- 
One large rubber matt with a hole in the middle that could be mounted around the top part of the 
grinder with reusable strips. The edged of the rubber mat had to have some weights attached to 
make the matt lay flush with the edges of the box.

7.3 Grinding procedure at DTU for DS/EN 12390-11 and NT BUILD 443 
By Alexander Stæhr Rasmussen, Master student at DTU 
 
Introduction: 
In DS/EN 12390-11 and NT BUILD 443 it is described that one must grind the exposed specimens in layers 
to a fine powder. This guide goes step by step through the process of grinding these required layers on the 
grinder placed at DTU. 
 
Apparatus: 
A. Grinder with Jenix DSC803M readout system. 
B. Custom made metal box and the belonging lid. 
C. 50mm paint brush. 
D. Plastic bags with sip ties for collected material. 
E. Vacuum cleaner. 
F. Ventilation hood. 
G. Pieces of wood or steel for chocking. 
H. Vice with wrench or hand crank. 
I. 
Umbraco key. 
 
 
Figure 50. Materials needed for the grinding (minus the vacuum cleaner). 
 
Procedure:  
 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
67 of 84 
 
1. Mount the custom lid on the rotary fitting above the grinding head by removing the rotary fitting  with 
the grinding head from the rotation rod.  
a. Loosen first the rotation rod from the grinder by rotating the rotation rod on top of the grinder 
while holding the rotary fitting and grinding head stille. The there is a skrew under the head 
that can be used hold the grinding head firm (see Figure 51). 
b. Once the rotation rod have been separated from the grinder the grinding head can be 
removed from the rotary fitting by slamming the rotary fitting on a vice separate from that on 
the grinder and rotating the grinder head with a large clamp. 
c. Once the grinder head is off mount the box lid by sticking the rotary fitting through the hole in 
the lid and fasten everything back into place with the now mounted lid. 
 
2. Turn on the grinder on the switch on the left side of the maschine. 
 
3. Clamp in the specimen to be grinded in the bench vice mounted on the grinding bench*.  
 
4. Place the box over the specimen with the specimen sticking through the hole in the bottom of the 
box. 
 
5. Place the grinding head directly above the specimen by rotating the handcranks on the grinding 
bench. The grinding head should not touch the specimens but simply hover above. 
a. The hand crank on the side (left or right) change the bench from side to side**.  
b. The hand crank in the front in the middle changes the bench forward or backward. 
c. The hand crank in the front to the left changes the bench up or down. 
 
6. Place the lever on the right side under the bench in the middle position.  
a. This lever controls whether the bench should move to the left or the right. The upper position 
moves the bench to the left and the lower position moves the bench to the right, while the 
middle position is the neutral state. 
 
7. Place the lever on the left side under the bench in the middle position. 
a. This lever controls whether the lever mentioned in point 6. should control the back/forward, 
up/downward, or left/right movement. The upper position moves the bench up or down and 
the lower position moves the bench back and forward, while the middle position Is for the left 
and right movement needed for this document. 
 
8. Start the grinder by pushing the green button in the front right side of the bench (under the rubbber 
mat). 
 
9. Gently rotate the front left hand crank described in point 5.c so that the bench moved up towards the 
now spinning grinder head. Stop when a low noice appears from the grinding heads teath hitting the 
specimen. 
 
10. Rotate the hand crank decribed in point 5.a clockwise to move the bench to the left until the grinding 
head is clear of the specimen. This can be estimated by the safety blocks and the small lever 
underneath the rubbermat. 
 
11. Once the grinding head is clear reset the X, Y, and Z coordinates on the digital display by pressing 
X0, Y0, and Z0. 
 
==PAGE:72==
68 of 84 
 
12. Turn on the ventilation and move the ventilation hood close to the grinder and box. 
 
13. Rotate the hand crank described in point 5.c to the desired depth. One can see the current depth on 
the digital display after the reset in point 11. 
a. If the decired depth is more than 3mm one should divide the layer into more sublayers that 
at max are 3mm due to the capacity of the grinder head. 
 
14. Push the lever underneath the rubber mat in front of the bench down to enable the automatic 
movement of the bench. If it is impossible the gears inside the maschine is not alligned and can be 
fixed by one or both of the following things: 
a. Rotate the hand crank from point 5.a a little in ether direction.  
b. Pushing the lever described in point 6 up or down and back into the neutral state. 
 
15. Once the small lever is down and the rod above is up the lever described in point 6 can be pushed 
down which actives the bench to moved to the right and grinding a layer of the specimen. 
 
16. When the grinder have grinded off a layer and the rod above the small lever have reached the safety 
block stop the movement of the grinder by pulling the small lever from point 13 up and then do either 
of the following things: 
a. If the sought out layer have been grinded off place the lever from point 6 in the neutral 
position, push the red button beside the green button described in point 8, and follow point 
17. 
b. If only some of the sought out layer have been grinded of place the lever from point 6 in the 
neutral position and follow point 13-16. One should however at this point pull the lever from 
point 6 upwards to move the bench to the left instead. 
 
17. Once the sought out layer have been grinded of rotate the hand crank described in 5.c to move the 
bench all the way down to the bottom. 
 
18. Rotate the lid and brush off the powder from on top the specimen into the box. 
 
19. Remove the box from the specimen by lifting it gently trying not to spill any powder. 
 
20. Remove any large rocks or shards and empty the content of metal box with the brush into a plastic 
bag with sip tie and seal it***. 
 
21. Clean everything of excess powder to prevent the next layer to be affected by the present grinding. 
This includes the box, the underside of the lid, the grinding head, the vice, and the specimen****. 
 
22. Place the clean box over the specimen as in point 4 and rotate the lid to run parallel with the box. 
 
23. Follow point 13-21 until all desired layers have been grinded and collected 
 
24. Remove the specimen from the vice and either place a new specimen in the vice or clean the 
grinding machine, bench, and vice. 
 
* The Auther found that if the specimens are 100mm tall a clocking height 20-25mm was optimal.

*** Any powder stuck in the corners can be loosened by hitting the outside of the box with the hard part of
