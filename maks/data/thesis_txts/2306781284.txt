Abstract 
The main objective of this master thesis is to investigate the interaction of flexural buckling and lateral 
torsional buckling in beam-columns. The main focus of this investigation has been to perform a 
reliability analysis on an IPE 160 beam-column subjected to both axial compression and strong axis 
bending moment. The analysis is conducted according to EN 1990:2002 [1] and uses a first order 
reliability method with a semi-probabilistic approach where the resistance of the member is treated as 
a stochastic variable. Additionally, a minor investigation into the use of geometrical imperfections in 
the shape of buckling modes, when both axial force and bending moment are present, is carried out. 
The Eurocode handle the buckling interaction phenomenon through two interaction equations which 
have to be fulfilled. These equations are in a linear additive format and uses different interaction 
factors to account for the interaction between lateral torsional buckling and flexural buckling in the 
member. Furthermore is the calculation of these interaction factors divided into two separate methods, 
called Method 1 and Method 2. Both interaction formulae uses the Eurocode buckling curves for both 
flexural and lateral torsional buckling and while it is common knowledge that the flexural buckling 
curve are generally well calibrated the same cannot be said about the buckling curve for lateral 
torsional buckling [2] [3]. It is therefore of interest to investigate whether or not the buckling 
interaction formulae can be considered to be safe according to the Eurocodes own safety requirements.  
All simulations were done in the Finite Element program Abaqus, and the results from the reliability 
analysis were then compared to the Eurocode interaction formulae calculated using both Method 1 and 
Method 2. The conclusions that could be drawn from the analysis were that the Eurocode prescribes an 
unsafe design when the member is loaded predominately in bending for members with a lateral 
torsional slenderness larger than 0.4. Conversely it could be concluded that the design according to the 
Eurocode were uneconomical due to a conservative estimation of the load carrying capacity for 
members loaded predominately in compression for beam-columns where the lateral torsional 
slenderness were larger than 0.8. Additionally, the interaction formulae did not fully approximate the 
curvature of the results in the MN-diagrams for higher slenderness values.

1 Introduction 
When I-shaped steel members are subjected to both axial compression and strong axis bending the 
structural response is highly complex and depends on many variables such as initial geometric 
imperfections, which can vary in both amplitude and shape, residual stress, plasticity and second order 
effects. This in turn as a consequence makes the modelling of such behaviour a difficult task, which 
has been under scrutiny ever since the work with the Eurocodes started over 40 years ago. The current 
Eurocode, EN 1993-1-1 [4], handles the interaction between axial compression and bending moment 
through two separate interaction formulae which are on a linear additive form using interaction factors 
which can be calculated using two different methods, namely Method 1 and Method 2. These methods 
were developed by two separate teams of researchers from different universities in Austria, Germany, 
France and Belgium over the course of nine years. The methods share the same theoretical background 
[5], b        y            ‘            ’                                                 1 can be said to 
aim for transparency by having effects represented by individual factors, while Method 2 aims at being 
simple and user friendly with compact interaction factors. 
The intention of the Eurocode formulae is to provide engineers with a simple, economic and safe way 
of designing structures and the formulae should thus comply with the general safety requirements 
given in EN 1990 [1]. This part of the Eurocode provides the basis of structural design and contains 
specific guidelines for the level reliability of structures. The reliability of structures are traditionally 
found by deterministic methods using conservative estimators of the variables that influence the load 
carrying capacity [1] [6], even though it is usually expressed in probabilistic terms. The code does 
however provide the necessary guidelines for determining the reliability using a semi-probabilistic or 
even a fully probabilistic approach. 
1.1 Problem statement and scope of the thesis 
In the present document an IPE 160 beam-column will be subject to a reliability analysis according to 
the Eurocode [1] using a First Order Reliability Method (FORM) with a semi-probabilistic approach 
where the resistance of the beam-column is treated as a stochastic variable which has a Gaussian 
probability distribution. The resistance found in the analysis is then compared to the Eurocode 
interaction formulae calculated using both Method 1 and Method 2 in order to determine whether or 
not the formulae, and their respective coefficients, meets the required level of reliability. The 
reliability analysis will be carried out by using Geometrically and Materially Nonlinear Imperfect 
Analyses (GMNIA) in the Finite Element (FE) software Abaqus.  
The thesis starts with an introduction to the fundamental theory regarding beam-column stability and 
the design of such members according to the Eurocode, before the theoretical background of the two 
methods for calculating the interaction factors in the Eurocode formulae and a condensed introduction 
to the probabilistic theory utilized in the reliability analysis is outlined. These parts are included in 
order to give the average reader the necessary theoretical background to follow and understand how 
the results of the simulations in this thesis are handled and discussed.  
Furthermore the FE-model setup is explained in detail in order to facilitate reproduction of the results 
that are presented, before extensive model validation is done and discussed by performing preliminary 
testing. In these preliminary tests the appropriate choice of mode shape for the geometrical 
imperfections for simulations with combined bending moment and axial force were investigated. 
Throughout this thesis Christian Gamst has been primarily responsible for the derivation of the theory 
regarding beam-column stability, how this is treated in the Eurocode, and Method 1, while Magnus

2 Preliminary theory 
This thesis involves the interaction between flexural and lateral torsional buckling, and the use of 
probabilistic methods to determine the resulting ultimate capacity of beam-columns susceptible to 
these phenomena. As these concepts are quite theoretical, a significant portion of the thesis will be 
dedicated to explaining the theory behind these concepts. In this and the following chapters (2.1, 2.2, 
and 2.3) the basic concepts of both flexural buckling and lateral torsional buckling will be explained, 
and by utilizing elastic theory the equations for elastic critical loads for axial force, bending moment, 
and both combined, will be derived. In addition concepts relating to the non-linear influence on 
ultimate capacity are discussed. In chapter 3 the practical approach is covered as the design equations 
from the Eurocode are described and the most important aspects of the background theory are 
explained. Here, the design equations describing the interaction between axial forces and bending 
moment in beam-columns susceptible to lateral torsional and flexural buckling are highly dependent of 
the two sets of interaction factors described in EC 1991-1-3 annex A and B [4]. These sets of 
interaction formula are referred to in this thesis as Method 1 and Method 2, and these two approaches 
and their parameters are explained in chapter 4.2 and 4.3. Finally a chapter explaining the basic 
concepts and statistical background of the reliability analysis carried out in this thesis is included in 
chapter 5. 
2.1 Axially loaded members  
In this subchapter the capacity and the elastic critical load of a member susceptible to lateral 
deformation is to be explained similar to the derivation shown in [7]. A structural member loaded only 
with axial force will have an ultimate capacity dictated by several factors, where the most critical 
influence is the sideways deformation of the member. This is referred to as flexural buckling and is a 
stability phenomenon characterized by a sudden failure of the member due to lateral deflection when 
exposed to high compression forces. This effect is most important when designing columns as the 
critical buckling load is in many cases much lower than the pure cross section resistance.

2.2 Members in pure bending 
In this section the focus will be to explain the concept of lateral torsional buckling (LTB) briefly and 
show the steps involved in deriving the expression for the elastic critical moment Mcr in an intuitive 
manner, simultaneously showcasing the important effects involved in lateral torsional buckling. 
 
Figure 2.2: Degrees of freedom involved in the deformed lateral torsional buckling shape 
Lateral torsional buckling is the stability phenomenon that occurs when an unrestrained member is 
subjected to moment loads. Here the compression force caused by the moment in one of the flanges 
will make the flange buckle out of plane, but the tension force in the other flange will hold back 
causing a twisting motion of the member. For pure constant bending of a double symmetric cross 
section this phenomenon can be modelled using equilibrium equations for two degrees of freedom: 
lateral deformation v, and rotation ϕ, both shown in Figure 2.2. The third degree of freedom, in-plane 
deformation w, is of no importance when it comes to LTB and is thus neglected [8].

3 Beam-column design according to the Eurocode 
In this section the aim is to familiarize the reader with the principles behind the design methods for 
beam column instability used in the Eurocode. The first part will be the theory behind the flexural 
buckling curves, and how imperfections in the steel from the manufacturing process are included in 
these through an equivalent imperfection factor vi. A similar process is used to explain the LT 
buckling curves in subchapter 3.2, leading up to the familiar design equations used in the Eurocode in 
subchapter 3.3. This theory is addressed as this thesis in large part deals with comparing the design 
capacity of beam-columns with the ultimate capacities found through GMNIA-simulations, which 
both are heavily influenced by these stability effects. The understanding of how the Eurocode handles 
the underlying phenomena shown in chapter 2 is thus of importance.  
3.1 Flexural buckling curves – pure axial force 
This subsection illustrates the derivation of the well-known flexural buckling reduction factor χ found 
in the Eurocode [4]. When calculating the reduction factor the Eurocode utilizes several buckling 
curves in order to deal with the effect of different amounts of imperfections present in the steel from 
the manufacturing process. Such effects can be an initial non-straightness of the member, or residual 
stress from the rolling or welding process. This effect varies in value along with a number of 
parameters related to section geometry, such as cross section profile shape and h/b-ratio, but the most 
important influence on stability issues such as buckling is the member length. Thus, in order to 
develop a design curve based on the theoretically derived axial capacity of a member, which includes 
the presence of initial imperfections, a slenderness definition is useful. The slenderness of a member 
determines its level of susceptibility towards the different instability phenomena by comparing the 
cross section capacity with the critical load based on elastic theory, meaning a low critical load caused 
by having a long member will result in a high slenderness. This definition is used in the Eurocode for 
both flexural and lateral torsional buckling, represented by the slenderness factor λ. In this thesis the

with both axial force and bending moment, and at the same time possesses boundary conditions in a

separately and then combine the effects using interaction factors, meaning the effect on the ultimate 
capacity from flexural and LT buckling can be said to be linked. The equations are also made to be 
physically transparent, and do therefore consist of three physically transparent parts: One term for the

is done using two linear design equations (3.40) and (3.41) for the capacity of a beam-column

It is always the most conservative, i.e. the highest value of the two equations that should be considered 
in a design situation. Another point worth noting is that LT buckling is only considered about the

interaction factors utilized in the equations. Essentially the interaction factor k needs to account for 
several effects [17]. First and maybe most important is the moment amplification caused by second 
order effects from axial force as described in chapter 2. Secondly plasticity in the cross section needs 
to be accounted for. For cross sections where material plasticity can be developed without being 
affected by local buckling before this happens, the plasticity can result in a different moment 
amplification [17] possibly leading to a beneficial interaction between My and Mz. Finally, the shape of 
the moment diagram resulting from both second order effects and the first order moment from external 
load is accounted for. Due to the somewhat complex process of including such effects into one factor, 
the beam-column interaction factor k is calculated by two independent methods referred to as Method 
1 and Method 2 located in the annex of Eurocode 1993-1-1 [4] 
.

4 Design formulae for the beam-column interaction factor k 
The Eurocode includes two separate methods to calculate the interaction factors used in the design 
equations (3.40) and (3.41), for members loaded with both axial force and bending moment. This 
section aims at giving the reader a brief introduction and a condensed overview of the theoretical 
background of the common aspects of the two methods. The individual terms involved in each of the 
separate methods are explained more thoroughly in section 4.2 for Method 1, and section 4.3 for 
Method 2. 
Up until the introduction of the Eurocodes beam-column interaction had been handled in a number of 
different ways varying from code to code throughout Europe. As the Eurocodes got ratified the need 
for a unified method of design rose, which lead to the development of the two methods seen in the 
Eurocode today. Both methods were developed independently by two different research teams, but 
common for both methods is that the background investigations in which the two methods are based 
on is mainly conducted on double symmetric cross sections, which are simply supported with fork-end 
conditions, thus the design formulae are in principle only valid for such sections . Another important 
feature of both methods is that they distinguish between elastic-plastic cross sections (class 1 and 2) 
and elastic cross sections (class 3 and 4). For cross sections defined as class 3 and 4 plastic capacity is 
either omitted or only partly taken into account [7, p. 35], as such cross sections does have some level 
of plasticity increasing the ultimate capacity. Additionally extensions of the “       y”  q        are 
made for LT buckling in both of the methods, rather than developing new terms to include this effect, 
“since fully consistent analytical derivations were found to be too complex” [7, p. 35].  
Furthermore both methods are based on second order elastic theory and rely on several common 
concepts. First, the amplification concept which was introduced in chapter 2 which is simply the 
amplification of an acting moment caused by axial compression coupled with a deformation of a 
beam-column, this is considered a second order effect as an increase in axial compression will give a 
higher deflection and vice versa. Another common concept is the use of equivalent moment factors. 
4.1 The equivalent moment factor Cm 
This subchapter will explain the conceptual idea of utilizing an equivalent moment factor when 
dealing with members loaded by both axial force and bending moment, where initial geometrical 
imperfections are present. Naturally, in structural design equations the part of the cross section most 
heavily influenced by the load actions will be the part which is designed for. And as bending moment 
often vary across the structural member, the part where the peak of the moment diagram is located, is 
the most critical. For first order moments MI, this is often at mid span or at one of the member ends. 
However, when a second order moment MII is introduced by an axial force as described in section 2.1, 
its moment diagram is assumed to have a sinusoidal shape with a peak at mid span. Now the total 
moment from both MI and MII is somewhere along the beam length, as seen in Figure 4.1, depending 
on the shape of the first order moment diagram. To avoid locating the location of the combined 
maximum moment, the interaction factors described by Method 1 and 2 instead convert the shape of 
the first order moment into one which gives a maximum at mid-span. This is done by the so called 
equivalent moment factor Cm. Meaning, the equivalent moment factor is a solution to the drawback of 
having to locate the maximum bending moment, caused by the addition of an axial force to a member 
already loaded in bending.

5 Preliminary theory related to structural reliability  
The objective of this thesis is to perform a reliability analysis of an IPE 160 beam loaded in both axial 
compression and bending moment using a first order reliability method with a semi-probabilistic 
approach. This section will give a short introduction to the theory behind structural reliability and 
Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS). 
Traditionally deterministic methods, where the load on the member and the resistance of said member 
were determined without the occurrence of randomness, have been used in the development of the 
Eurocode [1, Pt. Annex C]. A single safety factor was then used in the check were the engineer would 
see if the effects on the member would surpass the allowable level. This approach can lead to a very 
uneconomical design as there are many uncertainties related to the loads. Wind, snow live load etc are 
highly stochastic in nature and the probability for a maximum value of all the loads to occur at the 
same time is very unlikely. Naturally there are also uncertainties related to the resistance such as 
material and geometrical properties. 
 A probabilistic approach as opposed to a deterministic one means that both the load and the resistance 
are treated as a stochastic variable. This means that each variable has a mean or expected value (which 
is the value considered in the deterministic approach) and a distribution function, e.g. the normal 
distribution or the log-normal distribution. These distributions are additionally described by their 
standard deviation. The load and resistance are further augmented by partial safety factors in order to 
reach the desired reliability level, which in turn relates to the intersection between the two probability 
distributions (load and resistance).This intersection also represents the probability of failure, which not 
necessarily is synonymous with collapse of the structure or member [24]. 
 
Figure 5.1: Sketch of an arbitrary probability distribution of the action and the resistance. The intersection representing the 
probability of failure can be observed in the middle [25] 
There are two methods of conducting a probabilistic reliability analysis; fully probabilistic or semi-
probabilistic. A fully probabilistic approach entails modelling both the load and the resistance as 
stochastic variables before performing for instance a Monte Carlo simulation in order to determine the 
failure surface, i.e. the intersection between the two probability distributions. In a semi-probabilistic 
approach only the distribution of the resistance is known and the probability of failure can be 
estimated using the First Order Reliability Method (FORM), which will be elaborated on in the next 
sub-chapter.

5.2 Population sampling 
Over the past decades computing power has increased tremendously and has opened up for the 
opportunity to run computer simulations instead of actual physical experiments. This has the obvious 
benefit of saving costs by reducing the need of expensive testing equipment. Although one could make 
the argument that computing power is far from cheap it is however a lot more versatile than 
conventional lab equipment, and given the right software the possibilities are practically endless. 
Other cost saving aspects worth mentioning includes the elimination of the need for physical specimen 
and time saving, as many parts of an experiment that would normally be quite laborious can be 
automated in the computer code. Not to mention the fact that the sheer number of simulations possible 
to conduct far outnumbers what is possible by physical experiments.  
The number of simulations possible to execute also brings forth a new challenge. Experiments are 
often meant to emulate real life situations under controlled circumstances suitable for extracting data 
that can later be processed and used in research. With live experiments one gets the natural variations 
in the specimen that would occur in real life situations automatically. This can on one hand cause a 
problem when trying to control the variables in the experiment, but the opposite can also pose a 
problem. A computer code which is given an input will always produce the same output, and what is 
the point of running hundreds of simulations if the result will always be the same? 
It is easy to understand that running simulations with all possible combinations of all the different 
input variables is for most experiments impossible as this would easily require millions of simulations.

5.2.1 Random variable sampling using Latin Hypercube Sampling 
There are different methods for sampling input parameters from a population. One possibility could be 
to just randomly select realisations of a variable, where all realisations have the same probability of 
being chosen. This can be a fine approach if a vast amount of realisations are required, but the problem 
arises when only a few values of a given variable are needed. In this case, due to the nature of 
randomness one runs the risk of only sampling values in just one end of the sample space. Take for 
example the height of a beam profile with a mean value of 200.00 mm and a standard deviation of 
1.50 mm. Say then that 3 realisations are required and that they are sampled at random. It is not hard 
to imagine that one could end up with realisations such as; h1 = 201.01mm, h2 = 200.97mm and h3 = 
201.47mm, all of which are larger than the mean value. This is overrepresentation of one side of the 
sample space is of course not desirable. Latin Hypercube Sampling is a method for population 
sampling that mitigates this phenomenon and the concept will be outlined in the following paragraphs. 
For further reading [28] is recommended.

variables are required in order to get a statistically valid result from the computer code.

To properly investigate the different formulae for beam-column interaction used in the Eurocode 
multiple tests of the type Geometrically and Materially Nonlinear Analysis with Imperfections 
(GMNIA) is executed using a finite element (FE) model in Abaqus. The inclusion of the non-linearity 
and imperfections are considered essential in this thesis as these phenomena are an important part of 
the interaction formulae in Method 1 and Method 2, and by including or excluding these effects in the 
simulations a proper study of the individual factors in these methods can be accomplished. In this 
chapter the setup of this model will be explained along with some preliminary investigations in order 
to end up with a sufficiently accurate model of beam-column behaviour. Here the first subchapters will 
try to explain the procedure of implementing a nonlinear analysis in Abaqus, followed by subchapters 
explaining the chosen parameters in the model, before ending with the preliminary investigations.  
When modelling stability problems in beam-column two types of analyses are frequently used. The 
first is the previously mentioned GMNIA analysis, which is the most complete sort of analysis as it 
includes material plasticity, geometrical imperfections, and residual stress. Usually the term GMNIA 
may or may not include the modelling of the residual stress which is present in the steel members from 
the manufacturing process. This is sometimes referred to by the acronym GMNIAr in order to separate 
it from cases where this residual stress is not included, but this in this thesis residual stress is included 
in the GMNIA-term unless otherwise specified. Another often used analysis is the Geometrically 
Nonlinear Analysis with Imperfections (GNIA). This analysis is similar to the GMNIA as it is a non-
linear analysis method induced by initial imperfections, but it does not include the effect of material 
plasticity. This implies that investigated members do not hold any capacity past the elastic range and 
failure of the member is assumed when the material yield stress is reached, which later is referred to as 
a first yield criterion.

used to automate the process. This is possible as Abaqus does come with in-built Python support 
without any further modifications, there is also a number of references on how to use Python to 
automate different Abaqus operations on internet forums and similar. Stan and Larsen [29] has put in a 
significant effort in making a script to automate the Abaqus post-buckling analysis which is easily 
understood and modifiable, and much logic in the scripts used in this thesis is taken from here.

6.1 GMNIA analysis procedure 
In the simulations for this assignment an Abaqus FE model is used for both the elastic buckling test 
and the nonlinear buckling test which accounts for plasticity, and this section explains how the two 
analyses were carried out in this assignment. First one model is first created with a specific type of 
mesh, geometry and boundary conditions. This is referred to as the elastic model as the buckling 
analysis it is used for does not include any plasticity effects. Then this model is duplicated and 
material plasticity is added along with the final loads. This model is referred to as the elastic-plastic-, 
or just static model. The separation of the models is useful as it allows for the nonlinear analysis to be 
carried out directly following a buckling job is completed for a combination of axial force and bending 
moment without having to add or remove steps or material properties between each force combination 
for a given length.  When using this approach, it is important that the models are identical in geometry 
and mesh as the imperfections based on the elastic buckling test needs to be transposed to the model 
used in the nonlinear analysis, as this analysis requires an imperfect member in order to initiate the 
calculation [31].

6.3 Finite element properties 
In order to properly simulate real world behaviour of steel beam-columns, the finite element model 
needs to be sufficiently accurate. The main measure of accuracy in such a model is the density of the 
elements, referred to as the mesh of the model. This subchapter explains the choice of both what 
element to use, and the density of the mesh. 
Regarding the type of element used in the model, shell elements are chosen. Shell elements are often 
used over solid elements in modelling of steel structures, as they are better at modelling the behaviour 
of structures in which one of the dimensions is significantly smaller than the other [31]. In this case of 
a beam-column, this significantly smaller dimension here is the thickness of the flange and web. 
Furthermore, Boissonnade [34] suggest the use of quadrangular 4-noded shell elements, and previous 
work on beam-column interaction done by Stan and Larsen here at DTU [29] using S4 shell elements. 
The S4 element is a general purpose 4-node shell element, which is not sensitive to distortion and 
should provide accurate solutions to bending problems [31]. Abaqus allows for the use of reduced 
integration in the integration point of each element, characte  z   by    “R”                           
classification. The use of such elements offers an increase in computation speed, but can have a 
significant effect on the accuracy of the results [31].  
To see the influence of the reduced integration on ultimate capacity, a small test is carried out using 
the standard quadrangular shell elements S4, S4R, and the 8-node element S8R as comparison, similar 
to [29]. The S8R5 element with only 5 degrees of freedom is tested as well, but analyses using this 
element failed to yield any reasonable results. From Figure 6.4 the influence on ultimate capacity of 
the different quadrangular shell-elements is shown. It can be seen that the highest capacity is reached 
with the ordinary S4 element without reduced integration, giving a capacity up to 0.84% higher than 
the S8R element. The reduced integration in the S4R element does not offer a significant increase in 
calculation speed in this small test. The 8-node S8R element however does have a noticeably higher 
calculation time than both the S4 and S4R elements, and will of this reason not be considered a viable 
alternative. Even though the S4R does produce the most conservative values it is not considered as 
accurate as the S4 element, and as it does not offer any noticeable increase in calculation speed, the 
shell elements used in the model was chosen to be the quadrangular 4-noded S4 elements without 
reduced integration.

6.6 Geometrical imperfections 
Geometrical imperfections are of great importance when modelling beam-columns as they heavily 
influence the second order effects (see chapter 2) and thus the ultimate load carrying capacity of such 
members when the slenderness is starting to become significant. Geometrical imperfections refers to 
the initial out of straightness of the member and is sub-divided into two distinct types; global and local 
imperfections. Local imperfections mean imperfections which are related to the individual plate parts 
of the beam-columns profile, such as initial deformations of the flange or the web. Analogously, 
global imperfections are imperfections related to the member as a whole such as initial deflections of 
the entire member. The present document focuses mainly on class 1 and 2 cross sections and thus local 
imperfections are neglected in the simulations.

the mode shape from the linear buckling analysis is imported into the non-linear model, such a mode 
shape can be seen in Figure 6.10.  The scale factor of the imperfections in the non-linear model is set

many papers. Modelling the stress distribution with a direct vertical transition in one point produces 
poor results as changes or rounding errors will result in a distribution which is not in equilibrium, and 
thus essentially adding an extra load to the member. The slope allows for easier script automation as 
the field in the transition area does not need to be explicitly defined as its own analytical field, and

6.8 Material properties 
The objective of the present document is to perform a reliability analysis on a beam-column utilizing 
real material data from a steel mill. The real stress-strain properties of the steel were not a part of the 
dataset however, and therefore a material model had to be implemented in the Abaqus simulations in 
order to adequately simulate the buckling behaviour. The Eurocode [30, Pt. Annex C] proposes four 
different assumptions for the material behaviour: 
a) Elastic-plastic without strain hardening.

b) Elastic-plastic with a nominal plateau slope of tan-1(E/1000) or similarly small value. 
c) Elastic-plastic with linear strain hardening and a slope of tan-1(E/100). 
d) True stress-strain curve modified from test results 
Boissonnade and Somja [34] proposes an additional material behaviour model where a plateau is 
introduced at first yield and lasts until the strain is ten times the yield strain. At this point strain 
hardening with a slope of tan-1(E/50) is assumed.

7.3 Sub-conclusion 
In this chapter the properties of buckling mode shaped geometrical imperfections and its effect on 
carrying capacity has been looked into. It was found that when using either a LT or flexural buckling 
shape of the initial imperfections, the flexural buckling shape should be used for load combinations 
where N>0.5Npl and M<0.5Mply, when ordinary rolled cross sections are investigated. For welded 
cross sections a clear limit could not be established, but beam-columns with higher values of 
slenderness (λz≥1.5) should utilize the same limits as the rolled case of N>0.5Npl and M<0.5Mply. This 
limiting value will be utilized in further investigations in this thesis, and the reliability analysis. 
It was then looked into whether an expression for the ratio between the amount of lateral and torsional 
imperfection, vi and ϕi, in the LTB buckling shape could be described when axial forces are present. 
And whether or not this could be used to create a first yield expression which accounts for both 
imperfections using only one imperfection scale factor.  
The expression in equation (7.6) proved to relatively accurately describe the ratio between the two 
imperfections vi and ϕi as the axial force was increased, with a relative error below 5% for λz>0.5.

amplification term (1-N/Ncr,z) did not emulate the beam-column behaviour when Ncr,z > Npl.

9.2 Issues with large scale GMNIA simulations 
When executing such a vast number of non-linear analyses in the timeframe an assignment such as this 
usually has, some form of automation is essential. This automation in conjunction with the relatively 
large number of different parameters investigated, such as member length, can cause range of issues. 
This is often caused by trade-offs which are made in order to finish the simulations within the time 
required, while at the same time not compromising accuracy in any significant matter. While running 
the analyses a few distinct problems arose repeatedly, often needing several re-runs of the affected 
analyses.   
For the range of lateral torsional slenderness values smaller than 0.5 the ultimate capacity of the beam-
columns were higher than expected in a large portion of the sets, this was mainly due to the fact that in 
these cases the beams were so short that the global buckling mode was no longer the first mode to 
appear in the buckling analysis. . This was unexpected at least for slenderness values higher than 0.2, 
as these had not shown this behaviour when testing the model. It became apparent that small changes 
in the cross section geometry have a significant effect on the elastic buckling mode. This meant that 
e.g. a cross section with marginally thicker flanges would have a higher eigenvalue, and as the 
investigated profile is relatively small and slender this eigenvalue could surpass the ones for local 
buckling and end up as high as the 30th mode. This obstacle was mitigated by switching the 
eigensolver from subspace to Lanczos and then carefully selecting the right modes manually before re-
running the flawed simulations. Due to the stockiness of the member at the smallest slendernesses the 
eigenvalues were closely spaced, the correct buckling mode could not be accurately predicted. 
Meaning that the buckling modes to which the imperfections were to be based on had to be chosen 
manually for all sets, which was a time consuming process. 
For the range of lateral torsional slenderness values larger than 0.5 the issue was mainly the selected 
maximum arc length increment in the static Riks step. This was initially set to 0.25 in order to decrease 
calculation time, which did not pose any problems or reduced accuracy in pre-simulation tests. Here 
however, this increment caused the simulations in some instances to step over the correct buckling 
mode, making the beam buckle incorrectly resulting in significant spikes in the capacity curve, see 
Figure 9.1. Meaning that if the weak axis buckling mode was skipped, the model could potentially 
buckle about the strong axis instead, giving a massively overestimated ultimate capacity. The input 
sets that had these problems were mainly the ones with the smallest value of the imperfection scale 
factor e0′ , which likely contributed to the maximum increment value being insufficient. This error was 
easily corrected by decreasing the value of the maximum arc increment to a sufficiently low value and 
then re-running the simulations for the affected data sets. In hindsight a lower increment could have 
been utilized from the beginning of the simulations to reduce the amount of re-runs, but this would 
==PAGE:113==

again lead to an even longer initial running time, meaning that the total amount of time saved would 
possibly not be that high.

9.3 Processing of the data 
The output data from Abaqus comes in the form of thousands of Load Proportionality Factors (LPF). 
To facilitate the extraction of sensible information from this vast data pool all the maximum values of 
the LPFs, for all the individual data sets, for all the different slendernesses and for all the different load 
combinations were extracted. Since both the axial force and the bending moment were applied in the 
Riks step of the simulations each maximum value of the LPFs had to be multiplied by their 
corresponding load case ratio, giving the ultimate capacity for both axial force and bending moment in 
the respective load case. 
In order for the results to be statistically valid it had to be verified that the results were Gauss 
distributed. This was done in a Matlab script which took all the data from each relative slenderness 
value in the simulations and tested them using an Anderson-Darling test with unknown mean values 
and standard deviations. The Anderson-Darling test is a goodness-of-fit test developed by Theodore 
Wilbur Anderson and Donald A. Darling in 1952, and is used to check the hypothesis H0 that the 
population sample has a certain probability distribution. The test did not reject the hypothesis of 
normal distribution of the results with a significant level of 5% for all but one value of the lateral 
torsional slenderness, namely λLT = 0.8. For the results corresponding to this beam length the 
hypothesis was not rejected at a significant level of 1%. The reason why the aforementioned results 
were rejected at α = 5% is not immediately clear. One possible explanation could be that these results 
suffer from minor fluctuations due to the issues that were previously discussed related to this 
slenderness range. These fluctuations are in that case too small to be detected during the inspection of 
the results, but could still affect the Anderson-Darling test. 
It is important to note that the Anderson-Darling test does not indicate that you definitely have the 
probability distribution you are testing for. It only indicates that it is unlikely that you do not have the 
distribution in question. Nevertheless it was concluded based on the results of the Anderson-Darling 
test that the results from the simulations, hence the load-carrying capacity of the beam can be regarded 
as normal distributed.  
To get data from the simulations that are comparable to the Eurocode formulations of interaction 
between flexural buckling and lateral torsional buckling, EN 1990:2002+A1 [1] have to be consulted. 
This volume of the Eurocode contains the basis of structural design and verification. Moreover it

establishes principles and requirements for safety, serviceability and durability of structures and is 
intended to be used in conjunction with EN-1991 to EN-1999. 
The Eurocode is based on a limit-state design method where a design value of resistance is compared 
to a design value of the load. When the design value of the load exceeds the design value of resistance 
the structure or member is considered to no longer fulfil its limit state. There are two types of limit 
states namely the ultimate limit state (ULS) and the serviceability limit state (SLS). The ultimate limit 
state is related to the structural safety, e.g. collapse or other forms of structural failure [1, Sec. 1] while 
the serviceability limit state is related to the service requirements for a structure or structural member 
[1, Sec. 1] such as deflection, crack width etc.  
In order to determine the design load and resistances, partial safety factors are used [1, Sec. 3]. There 
are two main methods of establishing appropriate partial factors; deterministic methods and 
probabilistic methods, see Figure 9.2. In the development of the Eurocodes deterministic methods, 
which are models where no randomness occur, has primarily been used [1, Sec. Annex C].

9.4.6  Steel quality 
The information on the yield strength of the steel used in these simulations is taken from the same data 
base as used in [41]. These data are more than ten years old and it should be remarked that there is a 
possibility that advancements in steel production over the past decade can have rendered these data 
obsolete. If this is true a similar analysis as performed in this thesis with updated data on the steel 
quality might not yield the same results. This is however pure speculations as the steel producers no 
longer disclose such data and newer information were not available. Furthermore due to the time 
restraints it was only possible to conduct tests using one steel quality, namely S235. A higher steel 
grade might yield different results. 
9.4.7  Partial safety factors 
[1] clause (C3(3)) states that the partial safety factors for the different materials and actions for 
ultimate limit states should be calibrated so that the required reliability levels are reached. A fully 
probabilistic analysis of lateral torsional buckling on IPE steel beams in the ultimate limit state has 
been conducted in [6]. Here it was found that a more balanced failure probability could be achieved by 
having a higher partial safety factor γM, than the one prescribed by the Eurocode when the lateral 
==PAGE:133==
Reliability analysis 
 
119 
torsional slenderness was higher. It was however concluded that this was an unfeasible way of 
achieving the target reliability in practice due to the risk of human errors in the estimation of 
slenderness in practical design. It was therefore recommended that the buckling curve for lateral 
torsional buckling was recalibrated rather than the partial safety factors. The same logic applies to 
beam-columns subjected to both bending moment and axial compression, where in addition to having 
to introduce slenderness dependent partial factors, separate partial factors for bending moment and 
axial force would have had to be implemented in order to not make the axial force dominated load 
cases even more conservative. This would have led to an overly complicated design process and as a 
consequence the partial safety factor in the previous calculations and plots has been kept at the 
prescribed value of 1. 
9.5 Sub-conclusion 
In this chapter an extensive simulation of the ultimate capacity of an IPE160 cross section profile, 
loaded with both constant end moment and axial force, has been compared to the design formulae for 
beam-column interaction from the Eurocode. The results were compared using the 0.1% quantile of 
the results from the 30 mutually statistically independent datasets in order to be able to shed light on 
the level of reliability that the Eurocode formulae provides. To investigate the two Eurocode methods 
further the interaction factors kyy and kzy were recalculated from the quantile and compared to the ones 
calculated using both Method 1 and Method 2. 
In the case of pure axial force the Eurocode weak axis flexural buckling reduction factor χz provided 
conservative results compared to the 0.1% quantile of the results for nearly all slenderness values 
tested. It was showed in Figure 9.9 and Figure 9.10 that the residual stress and geometrical 
imperfection were both influential to the results in the range of slenderness (0.5≤λz≤1.0) where the 
results were the closest to the EC curve, as opposed to the lower range where residual stress had high 
correlation with the results, and the higher range of slenderness values where geometrical 
imperfections dominated.  
For pure bending the Eurocode design values were non-conservative throughout the range of 
slenderness values, where the 0.1% quantile only started approaching the design curve for LT buckling 
for very high slenderness values (λLT>2.0).  Due to the limited amount of datasets included, it was hard 
to determine a sole cause of the poor fit. But geometrical imperfections did seem to have a large 
influence for all slenderness values, suggesting that omitting the initial rotation ϕi in the calibration of 
the Eurocode LT buckling curves might contribute towards the poor fit.  
Concerning the beam-column interaction, it could be seen that factors from both Method 1 and 
Method 2 did a decent job of approximating the results for lower to intermediate slenderness values. 
For higher slenderness values however, the fit of the curves deteriorated and it became apparent that 
the Eurocode formulae did not properly emulate the complex behaviour of the very slender beam-
column. 
Method 1 did at lower slenderness values (λLT≤0.6) prove to be a better fit than Method 2 in the load 
cases dominated by axial load, due to the plasticity interaction factor Czy implemented in the weak axis 
interaction factor kzy. As the λLT –value increased this fit was deteriorated, and Method 2 provided 
better results, as the weak axis interaction formula started to curve inwards. When increasing the 
slenderness value further (λLT≥1.3), using Method 1 factors will again become the more economical of 
the two methods. This is because the Method 1 is better suited to model the behaviour at a high 
slenderness range due to its individual terms not being dependent on linear λ-expressions as in Method 
2. The strong axis interaction factor kyy does a good job of approximating the recalculated kyy0.1%

10 Further research 
The time restraint on a thesis such as this naturally limits the scope of the investigations. A lot of time 
was put down in literature studies, coding and testing the Abaqus model, not to mention creating 
Python and Matlab scripts in order to fully automate and post process the simulations. The simulations 
conducted in the reliability analysis was another rather time consuming procedure in itself. Even with 
full automation the simulations ran on two computers nonstop for more than three weeks, and adding 
the time that was needed in order to re-run bad results etc the total time spent on simulating for the 
reliability analysis ended up being more than five weeks. This has led to restrictions in the 
investigations since altering individual variables such as cross section profile, steel quality etc and 
then run the analysis again would require a huge amount of time. This restriction left a few different 
aspects which can be further researched at a later time, some of which is briefly discussed in the 
following paragraphs: 
10.1 Different cross section profiles 
A few profiles has been used throughout this thesis, IPE160, IPE500, HEB300 and HEB500 have all 
been used in the initial phases in order to validate the Abaqus model and in the minor investigations 
such as the imperfection study. The profile investigated in the reliability analysis and thus the main 
focus of the thesis however has been the IPE160. As both Method 1 and 2 in the Eurocode makes a 
distinction between class 1 and 2 cross sections and class 3 and 4 cross sections, a similar reliability 
analysis as the one carried out in this thesis, using a cross section from one of the latter classes would 
be of interest.  
10.2 Steel grades 
The only steel quality that has been tested throughout this thesis has been S235. This was a 
consequence of the data available and a similar investigation utilizing data from a higher steel quality, 
such as s355, and possibly a newer dataset would make for an interesting comparison. 
10.3 Load combinations and weak axis bending 
The load situation investigated in this thesis has been constant strong axis bending in conjunction with 
compression. An examination of the impact of different moment diagrams and the influence of weak 
axis bending on a reliability analysis such as this should be appealing. 
10.4 Different residual stress distribution 
As seen in the preliminary testing chapter (chapter 8) the residual stress distribution has a big 
influence on the accuracy of the pure loading cases, and thus any solid conclusion on the accuracy of 
the beam-column interaction formulae in the case of a welded profile was hard to determine. The 
reduction factor for both flexural and lateral torsional buckling in the Eurocode is also dependent on 
whether the profile is rolled or welded. It would therefore be on interest to see an investigation of the 
influence of the residual stress distribution on the reliability analysis.   
10.5 Fully probabilistic analysis 
Lastly it should be mentioned that a fully probabilistic analysis similar to the one conducted in [6]  
with the inclusion of axial force would give even more accurate verification than the semi-
probabilistic approach utilized in this thesis. Based on the results found here it should therefore be 
justifiable and appealing to conduct a fully probabilistic reliability analysis in order to further verify 
these findings.

11 Conclusions 
In this thesis the interaction between bending moment and axial force in beam-columns susceptible to 
lateral torsional- and flexural buckling, has been looked into by performing a reliability analysis. The 
reliability analysis was done by setting up a Finite Element model of the beam-column in the software 
Abaqus and running 6600 simulations with variation of input parameters such as geometrical data, 
loading and imperfections. The simulations were of the type “Geometrically and Materially Nonlinear 
Analysis with Imperfections”, or GMNIA. These input data were taken from a data set which 
contained real life samples from a Czech steel producer. The high amount of simulations was made 
possible through the use of Python scripts, which allowed the simulations to be automated while still 
being supervised for potential errors. The results were then further processed utilizing Matlab code to 
extract the relevant outputs and confirming their probability distribution as Gaussian. When this was 
confirmed the 0.1% quantile of the resistance distribution could be calculated and the results could be 
compared the Eurocode design formulae, in which the interaction factors kzy and kyy were calculated 
using both Method 1 and Method 2. These plots were presented in separate figures for each individual 
value of lateral torsional slenderness that had been investigated, which facilitated a verification on how 
well the Eurocode formulae predict the load carrying capacity within the requisite level of safety as it 
is stated in [1]. 
Before the reliability analysis was carried out, extensive preparations were made to ensure decent 
results. This involved extensive research into the stability phenomena lateral torsional and flexural 
buckling, and how these phenomena are handled by the Eurocode. To prepare the setup of the finite 
element model, recommendations from [34], and previous experience from DTU based projects from 
[29] and [12], were followed. This allowed the model parameters such as element type, element 
density, residual stress modelling, and plasticity modelling to be performed in manner which should 
produce the most accurate results. As the shape of the geometrical imperfections in the model was to 
be based on the deformed elastic buckling shape, an investigation was made into how this should be 
done when both axial force and bending moment are present. Here it was shown that the most 
conservative approach was to utilize a LT-buckling mode shape in the bending moment dominated 
load cases, and a flexural buckling mode shape in the axial force dominated load cases. Also that this 
change of shapes should occur at the intermediate load case N=0.5Npl and M=0.5Mply, as the presence 
of a rotational imperfection ϕi would yield non-conservative results for higher axial forces. This 
transition limit was found to be adequate for all tested cross sections, namely the IPE160, IPE500, 
HEB300, and HEB500 profiles.   
A preliminary test of the GMNIA model was carried out for several cross section profiles in order to 
examine the Eurocode interaction formulae accuracy compared to the results. From this it was seen 
that the poor fit of the LT-buckling curve gave several non-conservative values from the interaction 
formulae when small amounts of axial force was added. The IPE160 profile was selected to the

It should be noted that these conclusions can only be said to be valid for an IPE160 profile of the steel

The interaction factor kyy did for Method 1 properly approximate the increase in strong axis interaction 
as the axial force was increased for all slenderness values. The linear format of the same factor in 
Method 2 did not follow this trend, but as the kyy factor is only relevant for Method 2 for small 
slenderness values, this has little effect on the resulting capacity. For the weak axis interaction factor 
kzy, this was seen to be non-conservative for low slenderness values (λLT≤0.6) when calculated using 
Method 1. This is due to the plasticity interaction in this range, and lead to the accurate fit in the NM-
diagrams for Method 1 in this range. As the slenderness is further increased, the kzy factor was better 
approximated from Method 1, as the linear slope of the Method 2 kzy factor did not approximate the 
reduction of kzy for higher axial forces.

probabilistic approach. It should be mentioned that an even more accurate verification can be done by 
doing a reliability analysis with a fully probabilistic approach, where the probability distribution of
