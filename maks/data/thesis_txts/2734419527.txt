1. Introduction
1.1. Background and Context
The motivation behind discussing the significance of user research comes from our 1.5 year
journey navigating building a startup. We had many challenges along the way but one we truly
struggled with was figuring out how to properly conduct user interviews and leverage user
research findings. The uncertainty about whether we were validating our assumptions correctly
led to significant time delays and we remained unsure if our efforts were steering us in the right
direction towards creating the right product for our users. Our main struggles revolved around
the difficulty in understanding user needs, obtaining honest and unbiased feedback, balancing
qualitative and quantitative data, limited access to target users, over-reliance on solutions
instead of problems, not validating the problem and solution space separately, not being able to
apply research findings effectively, resource and time constraints for user research and avoiding
bias.
As first-time founders, we received a lot of advice such as reading the "The Mom Test" to
enhance our skill in asking the right questions. And while it did help in certain aspects it did not
help us in a more holistic methodological and practical approach to user research that would
lead us to PMF in the quickest and smartest possible way. User research, we soon realized,
extends beyond merely asking the right questions—it's a multifaceted domain with complexities
that need to be interpreted and used in the right manner.
One of our problems was figuring out how much our customers were actually bothered by the
issues we were trying to solve. Getting honest and open feedback from them was harder than
we anticipated - it turns out it can be quite psychological. We couldn't quite understand the
depth of their experiences without unintentionally influencing them. We struggled to evaluate
consistently and continuously with them the solution that we had created based on their
feedback.

When talking to other founders, we realized we weren't alone in this struggle. Many others faced
the same challenges when it came to product discovery and understanding what are the
priorities to focus on to get to PMF.
The primary goal of every product company and founder is to develop a product that customers
genuinely want and love. However, the reality is more challenging, of course. Despite existing
frameworks, and best practices in user research and product discovery techniques startups,
whether in their early or later stages, still end up investing time and money building the wrong
product without validating the problem and solution spaces separately and in the leanest
possible way. Many product leaders such as Marty Cagan (2018) discuss how B2B startups can
address these challenges and avoid such pitfalls. But there still seems to be many traps that
many product managers, founders and product teams fall into. On one hand this thesis argues
that user research is not getting enough attention and on the other it calls for new innovative
user research practices and applications that are leaner, more transparent and more objective.
In addition, the fast-paced development of technologies and SaaS companies means that not
only achieving but also maintaining a strong product-market fit is becoming harder. What kind of
change does user research require? How can it become more flexible and responsive to the
market's changing needs?
It is no secret that startups often face financial constraints. However, Lam and Ebben argue that
the key to enhancing new venture creation lies not solely in easing financial constraints but in
educating start-ups on how to navigate and thrive within those limitations. This perspective is
reinforced by Paternoster et al. (2014), emphasizing the crucial role of understanding how
start-ups can leverage work practices to support the creation of new ventures.
User research is an essential component of achieving product market fit and creating a great
user experience (Ghosh, 2018). Understanding customer needs, preferences, and behaviors,

1.2. Problem Statement
In many startups and companies leaders and product managers do not invest enough time and
resources on user research. The reason being that user research remains very time consuming
and often not impactful enough for the business, if not executed well. It is also a continuous
process since software products are never finished and market demands evolve, which means
that in bigger companies, there is a lot of data that gets forgotten or misinterpreted. In smaller
and earlier stage companies, there are rarely dedicated user researchers, so it often falls to
product managers or designers, who spend a significant amount of time — over 30% of their
day-to-day on user research.
Judd Antin, who has spent 15 years leading research and design teams at companies like
Airbnb, Meta, and Yahoo makes a strong statement in one of his blogs “It’s not just the
economic crisis. The UX Research discipline of the last 15 years is dying. The reckoning is here.
The discipline can still survive and thrive, but we’d better adapt, and quick” (2023). His main
argument is that user research is dying because the majority of it has not been done the proper
way and therefore does not deliver enough business value.

2. Frame of Reference
Through the process of conducting user research for Motionshift, a startup I have been building,
it became clear that user research is far more complex and unpredictable. The commonly held
notion discussed by many such as Eric Reis (2011), revolves around a seemingly
straightforward formula: 1. Design What People Want: If your goal is to create products that
people will buy and use, it seems logical to design what aligns with their desires. 2. Know What
People Want: To accomplish this, you need to understand precisely what people want. 3. Ask
People What They Want: Find individuals and inquire about their preferences. 4. Create Based
on Their Feedback: Armed with their input, proceed to build what they've told you. However,
here's the stark reality: this formula doesn't hold up in the world of user research. In fact, it
contradicts the first rule of user research, which is to never directly ask anyone what they want.
But why? The reason is simple yet profound: people want to be liked and are complex beings.
When you ask someone directly what they want, you run the risk of receiving answers that are
shaped by their perception of what you want to hear or how they wish to perceive themselves.
Furthermore, it's impossible to desire something you can't even imagine, which means that by
relying solely on direct feedback, you risk constraining the scope of your ideas to the limits of
others' imaginations. That's precisely why the field of user research exists. A field with
professionals dedicated to understanding and representing the user's perspective. The goal of
this chapter is to gain an understanding about the best practices within the field providing a
foundation for building upon.

2.1. Best Practices In User Research and Product Discovery
2.1.1. Historical Context and Evolution of User Research in SaaS
Historically, user research primarily encompassed usability testing, where researchers observed
users interacting with software or products. This practice aimed to identify usability issues and
improve user interfaces. Books like "The Design of Everyday Things" by Norman (2013) shed
light on the significance of usability in early design.
With the evolution of user research is closely intertwined with technological advancements. The
rise of the internet and digital products in the late 20th century brought about a shift in user
research. With the advent of web-based applications, understanding user behavior and
preferences became crucial. This marked the era when web analytics and user surveys gained
prominence.
Books like "Don't Make Me Think" by Steve Krug (2000) were instrumental in spreading the
importance of web usability and accessibility. The focus transitioned from evaluating products
for usability to studying user behavior and gathering insights into their digital interactions. The
evolution of user research is also evident in the expansion of research methodologies.
Traditional methods like surveys, interviews, and usability testing have been complemented by
newer techniques such as A/B testing, eye-tracking studies, heatmaps, and customer journey
mapping.
In 2001, Jeff Gothelf (2021) started advocating for agile methodologies and the integration of
user research throughout the product development process. Which also led to a cohort of
frustrated engineers deciding to tackle these issues head-on. Inspired by industry-wide
discussions on the challenges of software development, they crafted the Agile manifesto. These
software engineers recognized that projects were often too massive and teams invested
excessive time developing products that failed to meet customer demands.This marked a
significant shift toward continuous user feedback and rapid iterations.

User research has increasingly drawn from fields such as psychology and behavioral science.
The understanding of user motivations, emotions, and cognitive processes has become a
central element of user research. In "Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products" Nir Eyal
(2013) explores the psychology behind user engagement and habit formation.
User research started placing users at the center of the design and development process.
Concepts like user personas, user journey mapping, and empathy mapping gained prominence
and books like "The User Experience Team of One" by Leah Buley (2013) emphasized the
importance of cross-functional collaboration and user-centric design.
The evolution of user research mirrors the transformative journey of the digital age itself. From
early usability testing to today's user-centered, data-driven, and psychologically informed
approaches, user research has evolved to meet the needs of both users and the organizations
serving them.
Today user research usually consists of different interconnected steps (Figure 01). Starting with
a strategic recruitment of diverse participants, followed by user interviews and surveys unveils
qualitative and quantitative insights, respectively. Initial data analysis identifies key patterns,
guiding further investigation. Usability tests then provide actionable feedback on product
interactions. Subsequent analysis refines usability insights, and findings are documented in a
comprehensive report. Active sharing of insights ensures impactful integration into product
development, fostering a continuous loop of learning and refinement.

2.1.2. Hypothesis validation process
Conducting user research goes beyond mere data collection; it necessitates a methodical
approach encompassing the validation of hypotheses at various stages. Every product concept
relies on a foundation of assumptions. It can be helpful to initially comprehensively list these
assumptions and break them down into their most basic forms to be able to evaluate the
product through the lenses of feasibility, desirability, and viability.
In the realm of user research, making the distinction between validating macro hypotheses and
micro problems is important. These two levels require distinct approaches and methodologies to

ensure that a product addresses user needs comprehensively and effectively. Judd Antin (2024)
who has spent 15 years leading research and design teams at companies like Yahoo, Meta, and
Airbnb talks about macro, middle range, and micro research. Judd sees “macro research as the
big picture, strategic, business focused, forward-looking innovation, looking at the market,
competitors, and long-term research to understand where the product should go next. Micro
research is the part into which a lot of really technical usability testing falls into as well as
laser-focused research to understand A/B test results. Then there is the middle range, which is
this blobular place where the research questions are middle altitude (Antin, 2024).
In macro research there are macro problems, also known as high-level problems,
encompassing the overarching issues that users face. These problems often reflect the broader
needs, goals, and frustrations of the user base. They serve as a starting point for the entire
product development process and require validation at an abstract level. Validating macro
problems necessitates a qualitative and exploratory approach. Researchers often employ
methods such as in-depth interviews, ethnographic studies, and user observations. These
techniques help uncover and understand the root causes of high-level problems (Kuniavsky,
2003). Macro problems often inform the research and design direction for a product. Once these
high-level issues are identified and validated, they serve as a guiding framework for addressing
detailed problems. Micro problems should be validated only after the macro problems are
validated.
Dan Olsen (2015) creates a Product-Market Fit Pyramid, where he breaks down product-market
fit down into five key components: your target, customer, your customer’s underserved needs,
your value proposition, your feature set, and your user experience (Figure 02).

Problem space and solution space are an integral part of the Product-Market Fit Pyramid, as
shown in Figure 02. Microresearch and testing micro problems usually come at the solution
stage—they’re what customers can see and react to. The other three layers of the pyramid live
in problem space. The relationship between problem space and solution space is also the
relationship between your value proposition and your feature set (Olsen, 2015).
Keeping problem space and solution space separate and alternating between them as you
iteratively test and improve your hypotheses is the best way to achieve product-market fit
(Olsen, 2015). This approach prevents premature solutioning, ensuring that solutions are
directly tied to validated problems, enhancing the chances of creating meaningful impact. It also
allows for unbiased assessment and iteration of proposed solutions, focusing solely on their
effectiveness in addressing the identified problems without being influenced by preconceived
notions. By separating these validations, it enables a more precise and systematic approach,
fostering innovation and increasing the likelihood of developing successful, user-centered
solutions that precisely match validated problems.

Sometimes despite asking the above mentioned questions it's still hard to uncover the
underlying reasons behind a particular issue or problem. One useful technique is the "5 Whys"
method (1930), invented by Sakichi Toyoda the Japanese industrialist, inventor, and founder of
Toyota Industries. This method is a root cause analysis technique. The core idea behind it is to
ask "why" multiple times to dig deeper into the causes of a problem, typically reaching a root
cause after around five iterations of asking "why" (Figure 04, Liker, 2021, p.13). The "5 Whys"
method helps prevent the application of superficial solutions that may not address the real
issues. It is a valuable tool for problem-solving and continuous improvement in a wide range of
contexts.

2.1.5. Micro research and usability testing
Micro research primarily focuses on the examination and validation of the solution space within
a defined scope or context. Usability tests are one of the ways to do micro research and can
significantly impact product development by pinpointing user experience issues, identifying
areas needing improvement, and refining features iteratively to meet user expectations more
effectively. By understanding specific user needs and preferences, these tests facilitate tailored
product iterations that align with market demands and help to get closer to PMF.
Establishing an iterative feedback loop, usability tests continuously gather user insights, guiding
precise product adjustments. This cyclical process aligns the product more closely with market
requirements, contributing significantly to achieving better product-market fit. Usability testing
serves as a crucial component within companies aiming to enhance product profitability and
user satisfaction (Rubin & Chisnell, 2008). By gathering data from representative users, this
approach informs design decisions, identifying potential usability and product design issues
before product release.
Usability testing employs techniques to collect empirical data while observing representative
end users using the product to perform realistic tasks. (Barnum, 2021) Testing is roughly divided
into two main approaches. The first approach involves formal tests conducted as true
experiments, in order to confirm or refute specific hypotheses. The second approach, a less
formal but still rigorous one (and the one we emphasize in this book), employs an iterative cycle
of tests intended to expose usability deficiencies and gradually shape or mold the product.
(Rubin & Chisnell, 2008)
.31
==PAGE:31==

In the handbook of usability testing (Rubin & Chisnell, 2008) usability tests are split into three
subgroups: exploratory (or formative), assessment (or summative), and validation (or
verification) tests — at a high level, according to the approximate point in the product
development lifecycle at which each would be administered. The fourth type of test, the
comparison test, can be used as an integral part of any of the other three tests and is not
associated with any specific lifecycle phase (Rubin & Chisnell, 2008). However, each test will
vary in its emphasis on qualitative vs. quantitative measures, and by the amount of interaction
between test moderator and participant.
Test 1: Exploratory study or Formative study
The exploratory study takes place during the initial stages of product development when the
product is in its formative phase, often referred to as the early stages of definition and design.
By this stage, the user profile and usage model, or task analysis, should have been established.
At this point, the project team is likely grappling with the functional specifications and early
product models. Alternatively, it may have completed the requirements and specifications phase
and be on the verge of commencing the design phase. The primary aim of the exploratory study
is to assess the effectiveness of initial design concepts, focusing on the broader aspects of a
user interface or document. For instance, in the case of a Web application interface, it aims to
determine how well users understand the fundamental elements and navigate through the
interface efficiently, supporting their tasks and workflow. Similarly, concerning a task-oriented
user guide for a software product, the study might investigate higher-level aspects such as the
overall organization of content, the suitability of graphical versus verbal approaches, and the
effectiveness of the proposed format in facilitating easy information retrieval. The implications of
these high-level issues go beyond the product, because you are also interested in verifying your
assumptions about the users. Understanding one is necessary to define the other. Some typical
user-oriented questions that an exploratory study would attempt to answer might include the
following (Rubin & Chisnell, 2008) :

2.2.2. The Lean Startup Methodology
Eric Ries's book, "The Lean Startup" (2011), is widely recognized for its discussion of the steps
toward achieving PMF through a lean methodology for developing businesses and products.
The central idea behind the approach is to help startups avoid wasteful practices and focus on
creating a sustainable business model. It introduces the concept of Build-Measure-Learn which
encourages a cycle of building a minimal viable product (MVP), measuring its performance, and
learning from the feedback. This iterative process allows companies to adapt quickly to
changing market conditions and customer needs. He's the one who popularized the term
"Minimum Viable Product" (MVP). Today, many entrepreneurs apply to lean startup framework
and know that spending excessive time and resources on developing a fully-featured product is
a mistake. Despite this knowledge, putting it into practice is a lot harder than it seems.
Reis (2011) also talks about six key pillars to succeed. Those pillars are validated learning,
pivoting and persevering, continuous deployment, lean thinking, innovation accounting and
cross-functional teams. Validated learning emphasizes the importance of learning from actual
user feedback and data rather than relying on assumptions. It involves testing hypotheses and
adjusting strategies based on what is learned from the market. Pivot and persevere means that
startups often need to make critical decisions about whether to pivot or persevere - change or
continue with the current strategy. Eric's methodology encourages quick and decisive action
based on validated learning. Continuous deployment means that new features or improvements
are released to users as soon as they are ready. This allows for rapid adaptation to changing
circumstances and quick response to user needs. The lean thinking methodology draws
inspiration from lean manufacturing principles, aiming to eliminate waste in the product
development process. This includes avoiding unnecessary features, reducing time to market,

and optimizing resource utilization. Innovation accounting measures progress in a startup,
traditional financial metrics may not be sufficient. It involves tracking metrics that provide
insights into the development of a sustainable business, such as validated learning and the rate
of acquiring new customers. And finally the idea of cross-functional teams encourages the
formation of small, cross-functional teams that can work independently to develop and iterate on
the product. This promotes agility and responsiveness to changes in the market.

2.2.5. The role of dual track agile in PMF
Dual-track Agile, a methodology at the intersection of Agile principles and the dual-track
approach, represents a nuanced strategy for product development. This method seamlessly
integrates two complementary but distinct tracks: the discovery track and the delivery track.
Agile development and UX design often find themselves at odds with one another. This is
strange considering the fact that both have the same underlying goal: to build the best possible
product. It also follows the principle of Dan Olsens (2014) Product-Market Fit Pyramid seen in
2.1.2.
The essence of Dual-track Agile lies in its emphasis on continuous learning and adaptation, with
regular feedback loops between the discovery and delivery tracks. This approach enables
teams to work on research and product development in parallel, leading to faster delivery time,
improved stakeholder management, better team communication, more efficient product
development, and higher quality products (Productboard, 2024), (Bornfight, 2020). It also allows
for the gathering and validation of insights more quickly, making it easier to change the future
focus of the project and ensure higher quality final products (Airfocus, 2024).
It correlates well with the double diamond approach where the goal is to understand customer
needs and explore innovative ways product teams can solve their problems developed by
Productboard (Figure 27). Teams first begin by exploring the problem space—talking to
customers and prospects and exploring user needs before nailing down the precise problem
users are experiencing. Once teams understand how customers feel and how they’re currently

tackling those problems, they propose potential solutions and validate their ideas before landing
on a valuable solution.

project's end. They engage in collaborative product definition and design, steering clear of the

2.3.1. Cognitive biases
Cognitive biases serve as the brain's efficient shortcuts, helping manage mental load and
facilitating swift decisions by making rapid inferences. These biases permeate every aspect of
our lives, often operating beneath our conscious awareness. Initially meant to streamline
information processing, cognitive biases can cause misjudgments, fostering assumptions, rigid
beliefs, and overlooked blind spots (Dong, 2022). Judd Antin (2024) explains that there's all
these implicit ways that people engage in that kind of user performance. A lot of it comes down
to cognitive biases, confirmation bias, ego. One of Antin's big mantras is "We don't validate, we
falsify. We are looking to be wrong. "He believes that as a product manager, startup founder this
mindset is mandatory when approaching insights and research. "I want to be wrong. I want you
to do research that shows we were off base in the following ways."
In his book "Thinking, Fast and Slow" Daniel Kahneman (2011) delves into various cognitive
biases, including confirmation bias. This bias refers to our tendency to favor information that
.55
==PAGE:55==
Lucie Krulichova
Master Thesis 2024
confirms our existing beliefs or hypotheses while ignoring or downplaying contradictory
evidence. Kahneman (2011) explores how these biases influence decision-making, often
leading to errors in judgment and flawed reasoning.
This bias significantly impacts decision-making processes. When individuals seek out
information that confirms what they already believe, it can lead to flawed judgments and
reinforce mistaken beliefs, ultimately hindering objective decision-making. Confirmation bias
also extends into social interactions. People often seek validation from others who share their
beliefs, leading to a reinforcement of shared biases within social groups. This can strengthen
groupthink and polarization, making it challenging to consider alternative viewpoints.
By recognizing the prevalence of confirmation bias, individuals can strive to approach
information more objectively, consider diverse viewpoints, and make more informed decisions.
It's an essential aspect of critical thinking to actively challenge one's own beliefs and remain
open to new evidence and perspectives.
In the Decisive, Chip and Dan Heath (2013) also touch upon confirmation bias, especially
concerning user research and founders. When it comes to user research, confirmation bias can
heavily impact the collection and interpretation of data. Researchers might subconsciously seek
out information that validates their assumptions about user behavior or preferences, potentially
skewing the findings. For founders, confirmation bias can lead them to favor information that
aligns with their vision or initial hypotheses about their product or market, possibly blinding them
to critical insights that challenge their assumptions. This is because evaluating evidence takes
time and energy, and so our brain looks for shortcuts to make the process more efficient.
Confirmation bias is aided by several processes that all act on different stages to protect the
individual from cognitive dissonance or the discomfort associated with the violation of one’s
beliefs.

2.3.4. Challenges in continuous product discovery
Few key directions in product development and product management have been evolving
slowly. The first is that, if we want to decide about what we should build, we need to talk to the
customer. The second is to recognise that a digital product is never done because customer
needs are always evolving. Therefore, building in continuous feedback loops has become the
best way to build products. In some organization teams have come to think that product
managers own defining the problem and that designers and software engineers own defining
the solution. This sounds nice in theory, but it quickly falls apart in practice. Nigel Cross (2004),
Professor of Design Studies at the Open University, conducted a comprehensive comparison of
the knowledge, skills, and abilities of expert designers and novice designers across various
disciplines. His findings revealed that the most proficient designers are good at evolving both
the problem space and the solution space at the same time. As they navigate potential
solutions, their understanding of the problem deepens, leading to the emergence of new, viable
solutions. These two activities are inherently interconnected, with the problem space and
solution space evolving in tandem.

make strategic decisions about which opportunities to address, which customers to serve, and
which path to take toward their desired outcome. When managing software products and agile
development teams that create them a common problem nearly always seems to emerge: The
development team feels like they get involved too late in the planning process. The team is

attributed to the entire team's effort. However, when a product fails, it often falls on the product
manager's shoulders. While the mechanics of the job may not be the tricky part, the real
challenge lies in ensuring that what goes on the product backlog is genuinely worth building. In
today's top-notch teams, engineers and designers want to see evidence that what's being asked
to build is truly valuable. One of the main ways to make sure that the product or feature is
valuable is to give product team problems to solve (Cagan, 2022). Working with a team to solve
a given problem isn't easy. The hard part is finding a solution that actually works – it needs to be
valuable, feasible, usable, and viable. While the engineer definitely owns feasible and the
designer definitely owns usable, the product manager owns the viable and valuable.
Patrik Collison (YC, 2018) believes that while user research does inform the understanding of
users and customers, solely relying on it to dictate what to build might be limiting. Instead, user
research should shape the model of the user, which in turn guides product development
decisions. Marty Cagan (2022) echoes a similar sentiment, cautioning against spending
excessive time validating the problem.
In user research, the aim should be to uncover why people might not use a product. This
distinction between generative and evaluative user research is crucial. The bulk of the research

should lean towards the evaluative side—understanding the reasons users might reject the
product. Moreover, it's suggested that researchers shouldn't operate in isolation but involve the
product manager, designer, and ideally even engineers in the research process. This
collaborative approach ensures a comprehensive understanding of user needs and challenges,
enabling the team to craft more effective and user-centric solutions (Jobs, 2000). Patrik Collison
(YC, 2018) shares the same sentiment: “a lot of people think that user research leads to what

2.3.5. Making research useful and impactful
In Lenny's podcast Judd Antin (Appendix 08, 2024) talks about the core dilemma in
research—bridging the gap between intriguing but non-impactful inquiries and their relevance to
business needs. The conversation starts with the acknowledgment that research often focuses
on understanding user behavior and sentiments but frequently lacks the necessary impact on
business outcomes.
There's a recognition that researchers engage in such studies because they find them
interesting, and these are the projects they're often asked to undertake. Yet, the crux lies in the
misalignment between the captivating nature of the questions asked and their inability to
generate actionable insights for the business. These middle-range research projects, while
fascinating, tend to lack specificity in addressing direct business problems.
This issue arises due to research often being seen as a service function, called upon at the end
rather than integrated from the project's inception. This reactive approach delays
decision-making, fosters a lack of trust in gut instincts among product teams, and perpetuates a
cycle where researchers become sidelined and their impact undervalued.
The discussion highlights the differences between middle-range research, which delves into
specific but not precisely targeted inquiries, and macro research, which involves strategic
planning and broader landscape analysis. It's emphasized that middle-range research, while
interesting, often lacks the sharp focus required for direct business impact.
The key takeaway is the necessity for a paradigm shift: integrating researchers more deeply into
the product development process from the outset. This proactive engagement is seen as crucial
in obtaining insights that drive meaningful outcomes for businesses and teams. The prevailing
challenge lies in reshaping the perception of research from a reactive service discipline to an
active contributor to strategic decision-making and product evolution.

5.1.2. The Future of User Research and Prototyping
The conversation with Jonathan, co-founder of Maze reveals insights about how user research
and prototyping could change in the future. Jonathan sees Maze as a vital validation layer
bridging the gap between conceptualization and product roadmap planning. Furthermore,
conventional prototyping methods are challenged, hinting at potential paradigm shifts in future
research methodologies. “Our role at Maze is building for the way people build today but also try

5.2.1. Community co-design and co-creation
The findings in chapter 4.1.2 brought to light the challenge in recruiting the right participants,
effectively engaging a diverse user base, and being able to gather honest user feedback to build

ideas into the roadmap. Goals Alignment: Both teams present their objectives, aiming to identify
intersections and shared goals. Reporting Insights: Exchange of relevant data insights and
metrics to foster a data-driven perspective. Ongoing Relationship Management: Discussion on

5.2.4. User research and prototypes for AI enabled products
As seen in Productboards case in 4.2.3. in the era of AI many companies would benefit by
thinking about a similar concept in terms of ‘AI-Problem fit’. This is a make-or-break factor for
the success of AI initiatives, yet it’s surprisingly overlooked. AI-Problem fit is all about
understanding the business problems deeply and then asking: “Is AI the best solution to this
problem?”. If it’s not, you should solve the problem with some other solution. If it is, then you
have found AI-Problem fit and you’re onto something great.
Firstly, it ensures the feasibility of the chosen approach by scrutinizing the available data's
sufficiency and quality for model training. Simultaneously, an assessment of the machine
learning model's complexity and limitations is crucial to ascertain its ability to effectively address
the identified problem. This feasibility check significantly influences design decisions. For
instance, if the available data is constrained, the design may need to incorporate features that
account for uncertainties or communicate these limitations transparently to users.

Understanding the outputs generated by the model is equally vital. It enables designers to align
the user interface and interactions with the AI's capabilities, fostering realistic user expectations
and leveraging the strengths of the model.
Optimizing the user experience is another key outcome of early data model validation.
Designers can iterate on user interfaces based on the behavior and outputs of the AI model,

Moreover, early validation helps identify and address ethical concerns such as biases inherited

6.2. Limitations of the study
In our thesis, we conducted interviews with a restricted number of founders, introducing
limitations in the representativeness of our findings. The selected founders may not fully capture
the diversity of startup stages, potentially leading to a skewed understanding of the challenges
and strategies associated with achieving product-market fit. A broader and more varied sample
would have provided a more comprehensive view. Interviews, being reliant on respondents'
recollection of events, introduce recall bias. Moreover, the risk of respondents providing biased
responses to present a more favorable image ("brushing up reality") is a concern that could
impact the reliability of our gathered insights.
Our focus on understanding the reasons behind actions taken in interviews might result in an
incomplete picture of the decision-making process. The exploration of why certain strategies or
actions were not pursued could help in a more holistic analysis towards emphasized
approaches, potentially neglecting valuable insights into why certain paths were not chosen.
The case studies suffer from a similar limitation. The absence of a diverse range of cases
representing various startup stages limits the depth of insights gained. More varied case studies
would have allowed for cross-referencing patterns and trends, enhancing the robustness of our
analysis and conclusions.
Furthermore, it is important to recognize that despite building on established practical verified
foundations, the absence of direct testing of the proposed solutions leaves room for

7. Conclusion
This thesis is designed to help SaaS startup founders tackle the challenges in user research,
whether in the early stages or later on and provide tools and creative methods that can speed
up the process of finding the best market fit for their products. Chapter 1 shows that conducting
user research is a multifaceted process that extends beyond data collection - it requires a
systematic approach. Chapter 2 outlines that there are observable indicators when a company
reaches product-market fit, but achieving this milestone can vary significantly in terms of the
path and time required. As seen in 2.2.3. the process requires a tailored approach aligned with
the individual characteristics of each startup's business. While there may be variations in the
metrics considered important for a business, in user research there are universal patterns that
are applied across industries.
We have seen a few constant principles that have been evolving consistently. The first is that, if
we want to decide about what we should build, we need to talk to the customer. The second is
to recognise that a digital product is never done because customer needs are always evolving.
After the analysis of existing frameworks we identified that they do not effectively address
cognitive biases influencing decision-making processes, particularly in the handling of customer
feedback. The literature emphasizes the need for external strategies to mitigate these biases
and offers solutions for entrepreneurs and product managers. Furthermore, challenges in
customer feedback interpretation were identified, ranging from the establishment of effective
feedback loops in early-stage startups to the complexity of extracting insights from extensive
feedback in post-PMF stages. The study underscores the necessity for agile methods for
early-stage startups and strategies for mature startups to navigate and extract valuable insights
from customer feedback. Evaluation of the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) concept revealed
potential confusion and inefficiencies, prompting opportunities to propose refined definitions,

guidelines emphasizing prototyping, and exploration of alternative concepts like the "Minimum
Remarkable Product." In continuous product discovery, challenges include misalignment
between product managers, designers, and engineers in defining problem and solution spaces,
suggesting the need for a holistic approach and structured opportunity spaces. Lastly, the
analysis reveals that research is often perceived as a service function called upon at the end
rather than integrated from the project's inception. This highlights an opportunity for a paradigm
shift, advocating for the proactive integration of researchers into the product development
process to drive meaningful outcomes for businesses and teams. These identified gaps provide
fertile ground for innovative solutions and further advancements in the fields of entrepreneurship
and product management.
Findings from interviews with startup founders, product managers, and user researchers echoed
challenges documented in literature: participant recruitment challenges, a lack of methods for
identifying pain intensity during customer interviews, user engagement and feedback
challenges, struggle to navigate the complexity and relevance of user research due to
decentralized feedback and struggles to define the right metrics. To help startups face the
challenges that we have identified, the results from chapter 4 and 5 show that the following
approaches in user research for SaaS startups can accelerate the process of finding product
market fit:
AI for Insight Extraction: The use of AI for extracting valuable insights enhances the efficiency
of user research. Automation in data analysis can speed up the process of identifying user
preferences and behaviors, providing a quicker path to product improvements.The integration of
AI is seen as a way to level the playing field by enabling rapid development. This aligns with the
need for shorter product development cycles, allowing SaaS organizations to move swiftly from
research to a full-fledged product.
Community Participation in Design: The study emphasizes the involvement of the community
in the design process and creates a step by step guide on how to go about it. This collaborative

Minimum Remarkable Opportunity/Solution/Prototype: The transition from the minimum
viable product (MVP) concept to the minimum remarkable opportunity/solution/prototype
underscores a commitment to delivering outstanding value. This shift in mindset empowers
entrepreneurs to streamline their efforts, ensuring they're directed towards creating a product
that truly stands out in the market. Reconceptualizing MVP to for "Minimum Remarkable
Prototype" (MRP) introduces an approach to prototype to radically facilitate early feedback but
also aligns with the industry's move towards a more user-centric and iterative product
development ethos.
User research for AI products: User research for AI based products requires a different
approach as it often involves testing not only the usability of the interface but also the reliability
and accuracy of AI algorithms. This may require specialized methodologies such as
scenario-based testing, where users interact with simulated AI-generated outputs to evaluate
their effectiveness in real-world scenarios. Thereby this thesis also draws attention to the
concept of 'AI-Problem fit'. It emphasizes the need for a thorough understanding of business
problems to determine whether AI is the best solution. If so, it's crucial to assess the feasibility
of the approach by examining data sufficiency and quality for model training, as well as
understanding the limitations and complexity of the machine learning model.
Validating the data model early in the design process helps to optimize the user experience,
address ethical concerns such as biases in the training data, and facilitate iterative improvement
through collaboration between UX designers and data scientists. This integrated approach
ensures that the design not only meets user needs but also effectively leverages the capabilities
of AI technology.
