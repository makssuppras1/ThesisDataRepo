Abstract
The problem evaluated during this project, was whether it is possible to tailor the
expanded austenite zone,γN, formed by diﬀusion of nitrogen into stainless steel. The
tailoring was in respect to the stresses, the concentration and possibly the diﬀusion
depth(layer thickness).
All of these were investigated via two diﬀerent methods. Firstly, nitriding and de-
nitriding, secondly; nitriding under applied stress.
Based on the results, it could be concluded that reducing the stresses in the
expanded austenite layer, was possible via de-nitriding as determined using the
grazing incidence X-ray diﬀraction methods. The large negative stresses could be
reduces to a zero stress state, while still maintaining an increased nitrogen content.
The stresses were going from -4.80GPa for a sample nitrided with the nitriding
potential KN = 400atm

2, to 336MPa for similar samples de-nitrided at a nitriding
potential of KN = 0.
Despite this, the de-nitriding did not however seem to inﬂuence the hardness of
the expanded austenite layer considerably. The measured micro hardness for a load
of 10g was up to 1284HV for samples with nitriding potential of KN = 400atm

Using these methods, the applied stress did not seem to inﬂuence the chemical
potential and consequently no eﬀect eﬀect on diﬀusion of nitrogen was observed.

Intro
For a long time nitriding and carburizing of stainless steels have been related with
a lot of issues. This has mainly been due to the fact that the carbon and nitrogen
have tendencies of forming nitrides and carbides with the chromium in the stainless
steel[1]. This issue has been solved by reducing the temperature. This new nitrid-
ing technology known as; Low temperature gaseous nitriding. A technique where
the chromium is maintained as chromium, and free to form protective oxides. In-
stead of forming nitrides and carbides, the nitrogen or carbon diﬀuse into the surface
creating a layer of expanded austenite, which has improved mechanical properties[1].
This process can though be improved. During the project the main goal has
been to experience diﬀerent ways to tailor stresses and nitrogen content in the ex-
panded austenite phase of stainless steel. This has been done mainly in two ways;
by nitriding and de-nitriding, and stress induced nitriding.
Both ways are build upon ideas which have earlier been explored in some form.
The stress induced nitriding originates from a series of articles, based on the work of
Li, Li and Dong[2], where they have reported a remarkable increase in layer thick-
ness of expanded austenite, when applying a tensile stress. These experiments have
been done using carburizing, and thus the eﬀect should be possible to see under
nitriding which is in many ways a similar process.
The nitriding and de-nitriding builds on former work by T. L. Christiansen and
M. A. J. Somers where diﬀerent nitriding potentials have been investigated. Sam-
ples have been nitrided and de-nitrided and unpublished results suggested that the
residual stresses could be relaxed by doing so. This was investigated via stepwise
de-nitriding.

Theory
In this section the applied theory for the project will be explained. An overview of
the heat treatment technique and used X-ray diﬀraction methods will ge given, as
well as a brief intro to the mechanical aspects of applied stress and other character-

2.1
Heat treatment
Nitriding
The heat treatment mainly focused on within this project is low temperature gaseous
nitriding. Main points of this process is enhancing fatigue properties, wear resis-
tance and further improvement of localized corrosion, pitting and crevice corrosion
for stainless steels. In classic nitriding this is done by forming nitrides. Either with
iron(γ′ −Fe4N1−x and ϵ −Fe2N1−z or chromium (CrN)) [1]. However this is un-
wanted for stainless steel. Thus the process is mainly based on forming an expanded
austenite layer also know as the S-phase[2]. This is essentially a solid solution of
nitrogen in steel.

2.1.4
Mechanical enhancement
In low temperature gaseous nitriding the main goal is to enhance the fatigue prop-
erties, while maintaining the good corrosion properties of stainless steel. However
wear, galling(adhesive wear) and localized corrosion is also sought to be improved[6].
Because free chromium is essential to corrosion resistance, chromium nitrides are un-
wanted.
From the following schematic time temperature transformation(TTT) diagram
in ﬁgure 1 it is clear, that the chromium nitrides can be avoided either at high
temperatures or at lower temperatures.
When the nitrogen diﬀuse into the surface, an expanded austenite,γN, region
is formed. Here nitrogen is placed in the octahedrally coordinated interstitial sites
in the f.c.c.
lattice of steel.
The assicuated lattice expansion, hence expanded
austenite, leads to very large compressive stresses in the γN-zone. The gradient in
nitrogen content and associated stress proﬁle improves the fatigue properties. At
the same time the nitriding process increases the yield stress and the hardness of
the nitrided layer considerably. This enhances the mechanical properties as well.

2.1.6
Eﬀects of applied stress on nitriding
Earlier work suggests that elastic stresses can positively inﬂuence the diﬀusion in
stainless steel. This was shown by Li, Li and Dong in 2011. They showed that by
applying stresses from 0-80 MPa the diﬀusion, activation energy could be reduced
considerable in carburizing.
This was done by applying tensile stresses on 316L austenitic stainless steel
foils. The samples were examined by microsectioning, X-ray diﬀraction and electron
microscopy. The experiments showed an increase in layer thickness with the applied
load.

2.3
X-Ray Diﬀraction
X-ray diﬀraction(XRD) is a characterization technique, relying on diﬀraction of
X-rays on the crystaline structure.
The technique was discovered in Munich in
1912 by Laue, Friedrich and Knipping. However, it was ﬁrst really applied and
described eﬃciently by W. H. Bragg and W. L. Bragg a few years later[10]. What
Bragg suggested (later to be described as Bragg’s Law) was that diﬀraction from
the crystal planes occurred under a certain condition. This condition is formulated
by the equation which was later given his name, Bragg’s law:
nλ = 2dhklsinθ
(17)
In an X-ray experiment for a given wavelength, the lattice spacing(dhkl) leading
to diﬀraction occurs for a certain diﬀraction angle, θ. This can be used for ﬁnding
the crystallographic spacing, for certain planes. This is a useful technique in mate-
rials characterization and for describing strains and stresses in the material.

The iso-inclination and grazing incidence can be used for stress measurements.

3.2.1
Preparation of samples
The as-recieved material for applying stress, were cold rolled sheet of steel AISI

The 0.5mm only seemed to endure elastic

bolt, it has been sharpened in order to only apply pressure at one line.
The beam samples were put in the ﬁxture, only hold in place by the friction at
the ends. Thus leaving the sample free for volume expansion during heating.
The length between the simple supports in each end is 102mm. Samples can be
up to 20mm in width.

Interpretation
The result was very unexpected. From the slope of the ϵ, sin2(ψ), the measured

The applied stress state was positive(tensile).
The following hypotheses were put forward to explain this:
28

• There could be large compressive residual stresses in the material. This would
cause the applied stress to reduce the stresses, however not nearly enough to
form tensile stresses.
• The problems could be caused by large grains. The large grains could cause
trouble, with the intensity of the peaks. However with the amount of scans,
this would reduce the error considerably.
• In former experiments[18] probing the (200) lattice planes indicated an overes-
timation of compressive stress values. This eﬀect however has only been seen
for larger stress values in expanded austenite and originates from non-linear
elastic behavior of elastically deformed austenite.
• The alignment and focus of the diﬀractometer could be oﬀ. This would cause
the measured results to have a diﬀerent value, than the actual.

Calibration remarks
From the null-test experiments it was concluded, that because of the grain size a
large beam was necessary. For the grazing incidence no reduction in beam size was
used further on.
For the Bragg-Brentano and iso-inclination setup a slit size of

a high amount of steps for the scans are needed, to make up for the large grain size.
For further scans it was decided to avoid the (200) lattice planes, because of the

The focus point of the machine was corrected by the use of calibration samples.

thickness, on the upper side of the sample and the lower side. This former result
51
==PAGE:61==
can however also be explained by the yield stress being exceeded, due to residual
stresses building up, during the nitriding process. Especially at higher temperatures
this could be the case, since the yield stress decreases with increasing temperature.
This concludes that no results have indicated that an applied stress state should
inﬂuence the diﬀusion of nitriding in stainless steel. The eﬀects on the chemical
potential seen in diﬀusion experiments with carbon by Li, Li and Dong, is not seen
with nitrogen. However since there is large magnitudes of diﬀerence in the residual
stress formed by the two experiments, the eﬀect could be an elastic eﬀect, which
is impossible to see in nitriding due to plastic nature of the expanded austenite
containing nitrogen.

Conclusion
6.1
Problem
The main problem during the project, was to apply and test diﬀerent methods of
tailoring properties of expanded austenite,γN, formed by diﬀusion based nitriding
of stainless steel. The tailoring was in respect to the stresses, the concentration and
possibly the diﬀusion depth(layer thickness).
The problem was investigated via two diﬀerent methods.
Nitriding and de-
nitriding and nitriding under applied stress. These methods has formerly indicated
possibilities in the area, described in former published articles by Li, Li and Dong[2]
and non-published work by the materials section at DTU and Marcel Somers.
6.2
Results
The main ﬁnding was that reducing stresses in the expanded austenite layer has been
possible via de-nitriding. The investigated samples were nitrided at a potential of
KN = 400atm
1
2, and then de-nitrided at diﬀerent potentials as seen in table 12. The
stresses were investigated using the grazing incidence X-ray diﬀraction method. The
large negative stresses could be reduced to a zero stress state, while still maintaining
increased nitrogen content. The stresses were ranging from -4.80GPa for a sample
nitrided with the nitriding potential KN = 400atm

got trapped by the so called trapping eﬀect.

6.2.1
Future work
Regarding the de-nitriding samples, they have been ground before nitriding and
measurements. In future work the samples could be polished before nitriding.
The experiments could be done by de-nitriding and XRD measuring the same
sample continuously. Thus removing the texture eﬀects which had caused abnor-
malities in the intensity count for the diﬀerent samples. This however removes the
opportunity for making a concentration analysis. This analysis however, could be
made on reference samples which have been de-nitrided at the same time.
Furthermore the de-nitriding samples had experiences zones of rapid diﬀusion.
This could also be investigated further.
Regarding the diﬀusion under applied stress, it is not recommended to be inves-
tigated further.
