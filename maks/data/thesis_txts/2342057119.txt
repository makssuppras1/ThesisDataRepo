ABSTRACT
Palm Beach is located by the city Gold Coast situated on the Paciﬁc coastline of Queensland in Eastern
Australia. Due to the regular occurrence of high energy waves from the Paciﬁc, the surﬁng amenity of
Palm Beach is highly valued by local surfers and frequently visited by surf tourists from around the world.
Paradoxically, the waves which is feeding the tourism economy of Palm Beach also pose a threat to it
due to wave-induced coastal erosion. To prevent coastal erosion, the construction of submerged control
structures have been discussed. Coastal protection strategies aim to make the best of these structures
or ’artiﬁcial reefs’ by further enhancing the surﬁng quality or ’surfability’ of the waves which break over
the reef, thus beneﬁting local economies (Mortensen et al. (2015)). Past work to optimize the surfability
of waves breaking over artiﬁcial reefs have been based on empirical laboratory-scale physical models uti-
lizing wave basins and wave ﬂumes (Pattiaratchi et al. (1999)). In such physical models it is diﬃcult to
accurately reproduce the existing beach structure and even more diﬃcult to iteratively optimize design
parameters like the slope, shape and porosity of the artiﬁcial reef. In addition to being time-consuming
(and thus expensive), physical modeling does not allow researches to accurately measure parameters
like the peeling speed and the shape of the overturning wave (breaking intensity), which are essential
parameters in quantifying surfability. To investigate wave breaking conditions and surfability, I use large
scale computational ﬂuid dynamic (CFD) simulations, based on the equations governing the motion of
ﬂuids. These simulations allow me to test and compare how diﬀerent types of incoming waves break over
the proposed reef structure and allow me to investigate the eﬀect of using a porous reef rather than the
’conventional’ impermeable reef.
Based on historical wave data from the Gold Coast Waverider Buoy recorded within a period of 7 years
from (2007-2013), I consider four representative wave scenarios and investigate the diﬀerent behavior of
these waves as they break over a porous and an impermeable reef. I develop a scoring system which
allow me to quantify the surfability of the breaking waves and the surﬁng athlete safety. I found that
waves which approach the reef crest-line parallel (wave event 3,0.39◦) provide poor surﬁng conditions,
as little or no pealing is observed in this scenario while the surﬁng safety estimate is very poor due to
the relatively low water depth at the trough of the wave. On the other hand, when the wave approaches
the reef from the left of the crest-line at an angle which is too large (wave event 2, −24.61◦) the wave is
strongly refracted, resulting in a very high peeling speed, which will only allow short surf ride lengths.
Using my scoring system, I have shown that waves approaching the reef at intermediate angles (wave
event 1 and 4, -14.61◦and -9.61◦) provide good surfability, i.e. long surf rides.
I show for all the wave events that the breaking point is displaced between 6 m to 8 m closer to the crest
of the porous reef. I show that the breaking intensity is up to 18% larger when a wave breaks on an
impermeable reef compared to the implemented porous reef. Finally I quantiﬁed that the peeling speed
is more than 12% larger when the wave breaks on the impermeable reef than on the porous reef.

Chapter 2
Background
The aim of this numerical study is to set up a large scale CFD wave model as a state-of-the-art tool
for the analysis of the wave dynamics over the artiﬁcial reef proposed for Palm Beach.
The wave
breaking is analyzed with regards to surfability and surﬁng athlete safety. This section will pose an
introduction to the theoretical background relevant to this study. Firstly, to provide theoretical insight
to the fundamentals of this CFD wave model, a description of the implementation of the governing
equations is given. Secondly, relevant background will lead to an understanding of what a good quality
surﬁng wave is.
2.1 Governing equations
In this thesis an OpenFoam wave model is set up to investigate the eﬀect of the porosity on the wave
dynamics over the proposed reef for Palm Beach. The OpenFoam wave toolbox waves2Faom is applied
to generate the waves. Waves2Foam is based on the application interFoam, where two ﬂuids of diﬀerent
viscosities (water and air) is set in motion. In waves2Foam, the boundary between the two ﬂuids can
attain a wave shape approximated by an analytical wave function. A ramping function is converting
the analytic shape of the ﬂuid boundary to a numeric representation of water motion determined by the
equation of motion.

the reef, which is recommended in the strategy protection plan for Palm Beach.
The porosity is a
characteristic material property. The porosity of a material is deﬁned as a ratio of the volume of the
void in the material and the total volume of the material. With regards to the porous reef, the porosity

2.2 Discretization
At the moment, all equations are represented in their analytical form. Regarding conservation laws,
the physical domain is build up by small CVs as described by Bingham et al. (2010), where the ﬂux
of information through a boundary face of two CVs is determined based on the local conservation
of information of each CV, and the global conservation of information of the whole model domain.
A temporal and spatial discretization of the governing equations are needed to solve the equations
numerically, where all derivatives are expressed as discrete diﬀerences. In ﬂuid mechanics, it is convenient
to conduct a dimensional analysis of the forces acting on the ﬂuid element. In general, three forces are
acting on a ﬂuid element; gravity, inertia and the viscous forces. The dimensional analysis will reveal
the relative importance of each force in the system.
The scaling of the forces will identify relevant
non-dimensional quantities e.g. the Reynolds number (Re), as described by Bingham et al. (2010). It
describes the ratio between the inertia force and the viscous force. In an inertia dominated regime, the

2.3 Shoaling
As the wave travels from deep water towards shallow water with a celerity (or speed) c, the wave envelope
is traveling with a group celerity cg. Furthermore, the energy ﬂux per unit crest-length Ecg of the wave
is conserved along the propagation of the wave. This is true if there is no loss in wave energy due to
frictional forces, as described in Fredsø (2008). When the wave approaches shallow water, cg decreases as
the wave celerity decreases. This is easily explained from the dispersion relation, as described in Fredsø
(2008)

A water surface gravity wave propagates by deforming the water surface. This deformation induces
orbital velocities in the water below. The velocities are periodic and in phase with the wave. In shallow
water, the velocities in the water are mostly horizontal with maximum in the crest and minimum in the
trough of the wave.

2.6 Surfability
When it comes to surﬁng, not all types of breaking waves are considered good surfability. To quantify
the surfability of the breaking wave, the following key parameters are of greatest importance (Mortensen
et al. (2014)):
• Wave breaking height Hb
• Pealing velocity Up
• Breaking intensity I
• Length of surf ride l
13
==PAGE:19==
Taking into account the experience of the surﬁng athlete, the only surfable wave breaking types are
the spilling and the plunging breaking waves. For beginners, spilling breaking waves are the preferred,
while the majority of the surﬁng community considers the plunging wave the desirable wave. Both the
surging and the collapsing waves are considered unsurfable, as described by Mead and Black (2001).
The Spilling breaking wave is characterized by a turbulent mixture of dispersed air-bubbles and water
(white water) running down the wave face and traveling with the wave front. The momentum of the
moving white water, accelerates the surfer and provides an easy, straight ride for beginners. Whereas the
plunging breaking wave undergoes a steep overturn of the wave crest, resulting in a jet-stream colliding
with the wave, trough producing a strong vortex. When surﬁng a plunging wave, small surf crafts are
often used since a quick take-oﬀis needed to follow the fast traveling wave. The overturning of the
wave crest can be violent enough to form an elliptic, at times circular, shape, into which the surfer can
position himself, as described by Mortensen et al. (2014). The present days’ diﬀerent designs of surﬁng
crafts allow the surﬁng athlete to surf a wave with a breaking height ranging from 0.15 m to 20 m.
However, most of the world’s recreational surﬁng athletes surf waves from one meters to ﬁve meters,
as described by Mortensen et al. (2014). Most surﬁng athletes do not aim for every wave; they are
selective and choose the largest wave heights. Based on the deﬁnition of the surﬁng wave height made
by Scarfe et al. (2003), only 10% of the largest wave heights H1/10 are considered a good proxy with
regards to surfability. Following relation is used: H1/10 = 1.27Hs, where Hs is the signiﬁcant wave height.
When a wave meets its breaking point, the overturn of the wave moves along the wave crest (peeling).
The velocity of the peeling depends on the local water depth h, the wave period, the wave height and
the angle of attack to the shoreline. The point that separates the wave crest from the overturning wave
crest is deﬁned as the peeling point P. Assuming an inﬁnitely wide plane slope, the overturn of the
wave occurs at the same time along the wave crest, hence there is no peeling of the overturning wave
(close-out). At locations where a sand-bar or a natural reef contracts the depth contour-lines, the wave
energy is focused. Since the water depth is larger in one side of the reef, the wave undergoes refraction,
as the wave celerity depends on the water depth as deﬁned from shallow water wave theory. As the wave
starts to refract, the velocity of the breaking point attains a vector component along the wave crest (see
Figure 2.5).

When a wave breaks, it does not always break with a constant peeling speed, since the natural bed is

next cut-back. The total horizontal distance that the surﬁng athlete has moved from the take-oﬀto the
point at which the requirements for surﬁng is no longer met, relative to a ﬁxed point on the ground, is
deﬁned as the surﬁng ride length l. The reef recommended for Palm Beach is expected to deliver surﬁng
rides of 50-70 m on a regular basis, based on the study of Mortensen et al. (2015).

Chapter 3
Model validation
When working with numerical models, it is of highest importance to validate the model against real
physical measurements. However it is not always possible to ﬁnd experimental data to compare with.
Fortunately, experimental data for this validation exists and is utilized to tune the model settings to
achieve the best reproduction of the physics of wave dynamics. The data is obtained from the experimen-
tal work by Beji and Battjes (1993) and Ting and Kirby (1994). The validation cases provide detailed
settings leading to the most comprehensive results regarding the shoaling eﬀect, the breaking point and
the breaking height. The found settings are applied in the numerical investigation of the reef proposed
for Palm Beach. In the following chapters, Chapter 3 and Chapter 4 important deﬁnitions regarding
the boundary conditions are made. A no-slip boundary conditions in OpenFoam is characterized by a
homogeneous Dirichlet boundary condition imposed on the velocity (U = 0), this clariﬁes that both
the vertical velocity w and the tangential velocities u,v is zero. Further a (slip) boundary condition is
characterized by a homogeneous Dirichlet boundary condition imposed on the vertical velocity w and a
homogeneous Neumann boundary condition on the tangential velocities ∂u/∂x = 0, ∂v/∂y = 0.

3.2 Model validation of plunging and spilling wave breaking
This second validation study is based on the experimental study by Ting and Kirby (1994), who examined
the dynamics of breaking waves on a sloping bed. They investigated two main types of breaking waves; a
spilling breaking wave (ξ < 0.5) and a plunging breaking wave (0.5 < ξ < 3.3), as described by (Battjes,

the wave crest resulting in a jet stream colliding with the wave trough, producing a strong vortex. In the
validation of the wave model, a comparison to the data from Ting and Kirby (1994) is conducted. The

The characteristic design of the reef is thought to provide peeling waves on a regular basis, as described
by Mortensen et al. (2015).
When the wave approaches the oﬀshore toe of the reef, it undergoes a
shoaling process as the water depth decreases. The diﬀerence of the slopes in the left and right side of
the reef makes the wave refract around the reef on the less steep side (right) of the reef, since the water
depth here is larger. The breaking point of the wave is most likely to occur close to the crest of the
reef (lowest water depth), subsequently the breaking point starts moving to the right from the crest as
the wave refracts producing a ”right-hander” wave (see Chapter 2 about peeling). The wave peels until
the unbroken wave section meets deeper water further out on the right hand side of the reef. However,
the wave only peels to the right, hence the steepness of the left side of the reef prevents the wave from
breaking, as the water depth increases rapidly.

Cnoidal ﬁrst order wave theory is applied as the analytical approximation of the two phase ﬂuid bound-
ary at the inlet zone. Since no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between Cnoidal ﬁrst and Stokes 5th order wave
theory is found in the validation of the wave model, a consistent choice of using Cnoidal ﬁrst order is
taken. As a consequence of the size of the model, a maximum allowable C-number is set to 0.3. in con-
tradiction to the validation cases, where it was demonstrated that a C = 0.2 provides satisfying results.
Nevertheless, to mitigate the large computational time, it is decided to retain a slightly larger C-number.
The bathymetry of the reef is created by DHI Gold Australia, which has provided a stereolithography
(stl) ﬁle, build of triangles with vertices deﬁned by topographic coordinates. The bathymetry of the reef
is implemented by the use of the snappyHexMesh (SMH) procedure, which snaps the curvature of the stl
surface to the block-mesh, resulting in an introduction of triangular cells near the bed of the reef. Prior
to the snapping procedure, the ambient mesh is reﬁned up to three times the ambient mesh around the
free surface. However, close to the crest of the structure, cells of reﬁnement level 3 are attained all the
way to the bed.
The representation of the free surface is inﬂuenced by a loss to numerical diﬀusion as a consequence of
the coarse resolution in the inlet zone, where the waves are generated. To reduce the impact from the
numerical diﬀusion, the inlet wave height is multiplied by a gain factor of 1.08 to assure the right wave
height before the wave approaches the reef. This approach is inspired by physical model experiments
in e.g. wave ﬂumes, where a calibration of the generated wave is often conducted prior to the actual
experiment.

simulation run-time, as the orbital velocities change due to a shoaling eﬀect.

models are decomposed and processed to 60 machines, it is remarkable how computationally expensive

4.2 Model investigation
The impermeable and porous wave model setups are used to investigate the eﬀect of the implemented
porosity on the physics of breaking waves. Furthermore, they are used as a tool to evaluate the surfability

along the porous reef compared to the impermeable reef.
5.3 Surﬁng athlete safety
The safety assessment is comprised by an evaluation of how violent the wave breaking is, compared to
the water depth in the wave trough. The more violent the wave breaking is, the larger water depth
required to prevent the athlete from hitting the bottom.

be thrown more horizontally, producing a circular shape of the air (plunging wave).

5.3.2
Wave breaking pressure distribution
The pressure distribution coheres directly with the water velocity. When the wave shoals, a low pressure
is observed in the front and at the rear of the wave due to a convergence of water in the wave crest,
enabling the wave to gain height. When the wave breaks, a ﬂow of water from the trough feeds the
overturning of the wave, as is also evident in the velocity plots. A high pressure forms in the wave crest
due to the convergence of water. When the plunging jet hits the trough in front of the wave, it applies
a tremendous pressure on the water surface (and potentially the surﬁng athlete) in the trough.
As
shown in the velocity plots, the overturning of the wave creates a strong vortex, where water is thrown
outwards from the center, providing a low pressure ﬁeld in the center. The low pressure ﬁeld in the
vortex is focused in the lower part of the vortex. The water beneath the vortex is dragged away from
the center and sent to the wave crest. In some of the wave breaking events on the impermeable reef,
the low pressure ﬁeld extends all the way to the reef due to the divergence of water close to the reef.
On the porous reef, water is held inside the reef, supplying the vortex with water through the breaking
sequence.

Chapter 6
Discussion
During this numerical study, a large scale CFD wave-model is set up as a state-of-the-art tool for in-
vestigating wave breaking on a submerged structure. First, a model validation was conducted to verify
the performance of the OpenFoam wave toolbox waves2Foam. As the ﬁrst validation test, the wave
transformation on a bar based on the experimental study by Beji and Battjes (1993) was investigated. I
found, that with a reasonable reﬁnement of the mesh and adjustments of the C-number, accurate results
is attained. To improve even further, a surface based reﬁnement on the bed can be conducted to resolve
the near bed dynamics better in the shallow region close to the bed.
The second validation test was grounded in the experimental study by Ting and Kirby (1994), who
investigated the dynamics of spilling and plunging breaking waves. I set up a wave model based on the
original experimental setup. I showed that good results can be obtained in 3D without implementing
a turbulence model. Ensemble averaged envelope plots were conducted for two diﬀerent model setups
for both spilling and plunging breaking waves. It is likely, that the additional spatial averaging of the
surface elevation in the transverse direction resulted in smoothing the envelope plots due to a reduction
in spatial ﬂuctuation of both the breaking point and the hydrodynamics just after the breaking point.
It is clear, that a better resolution of the bed is important to achieve better results of the undertow
as seen in the numerical study by Christensen (2006). The aim of the validation cases were to test if
waves2Foam is capable to represent the free surface in wave breaking scenarios. The velocities close to
the bed are extremely important, and have a large impact on the representation of the free surface during
wave breaking. However, since the envelope plots were found satisfying, plots of velocities depth proﬁles
was omitted. It would have been correct to show the depth proﬁles of the velocities and compare with
the measured by Ting and Kirby (1994).
In the second validation case, I found that an extension of the model from a 2D to a 3D wave model
had a signiﬁcant impact on the location of wave breaking. When compared to experimental data, the
3D wave model provided a better representation of the free surface and yielded a better estimate of
the location of the breaking point. Interestingly, this ﬁnding demonstrates that the software-user must
be aware of the displacement of the breaking point when analyzing results from less computationally
demanding 2D wave model setups. In addition, I found that the conversion from a 2D to a 3D setup
allowed the software-user to run simulations with larger cell sizes and still attain better results. In the
end, the computational time for the coarse 3D models were lower than the 2D models and still the 3D
models performed most accurately.
This computational study has been characterized by tremendous computation times. In agreement with
an analysis by Jacobsen et al. (2012), it appears that the exchange of momentum between water and
air is the primary reason for this. When the water moves, it causes the air to move due to frictional
forces. Very high air velocities occur when the plunging jet accelerates towards the water. The large air
velocities require a lower time-step to maintain a constant C-number. A reduced computation time will
greatly improve the applicability of the modeling approach used here, hence (as suggested by Jacobsen
et al. (2012)), the possibility to neglect the air in these kinds of models should be further investigated.
81
==PAGE:87==
The end product of this thesis is a large scale CFD wave model setup designed for the numerical investi-
gation in the coastal protection project at Palm Beach, Gold Coast Australia. The settings of the ﬁnal
setup are based on the settings from the validation cases, which acted as a tune-in process to optimize
the representation of the free surface in the breaking waves. In the validation case based on Ting and
Kirby (1994), I found that gradual reﬁnement from the bed towards the free surface (still with reﬁnement
zones around the free surface) improved the accuracy of the model. This was grounded in the better
representation of the undertow and near-bed dynamics. This technicality complicated the implementa-
tion of the Palm Beach wave model, as the irregular bathymetry of the reef made it diﬃcult to reﬁne the
mesh near the bed: Diﬀerent mesh utility functions crashed. These crashes seem to be explained by the
two diﬀerent reﬁnement procedures. The reﬁneMesh procedure uses cell zones marked by the topoSet
utility and the marked cells could not be reﬁned afterwards by applying the snappyHexMesh utility.
Regarding the impermeable model setup, a choice of extending the last reﬁnement zone all the way to
the crest of the reef, where wave breaking occurs was made. Then the bathymetry was snapped to the
model domain, resulting in ﬁne cells all the way to the reef crest. In the porous model setup, reﬁnement
was carried out in the porous zone, and here the same problem occurred. I worked around the problem
by lowering the number of cells between each reﬁnement level to 1. The last reﬁnement zone was now
extended to the edge of the porous zone. It may be beneﬁcial to debug the mesh reﬁnement algorithms
to ease the application of the CFD-based wave modeling approach in future studies.
The local hydrodynamics of a breaking surface gravity wave are quantiﬁed in this thesis. The overturning
of the wave is fed by a constant water supply from the wave trough produced by the strong pressure
gradient force pointing towards the crest of the wave. The water is dragged up into the crest and thrown
in the direction of wave propagation, thus producing a bore of turbulent water in front of the wave. When
a surfer takes oﬀon the steep wave face of the overturning wave, he accelerates down the wave face due
to gravity. Meanwhile, a counter-directional water ﬂow enhances the frictional force on the surﬁng craft.
This explains why a surfer can maintain his position on the wave face and follow the peeling speed of
the wave.
In future studies, it will also be interesting to further optimize the design of the reef. Conducting simula-
tions with ﬁxed wave properties and varying reef orientations is of great importance to determine which
orientation leads to the best surﬁng conditions. It is clear, that the optimal design for one wave event
will not be suﬃcient for other wave events, hence the design should be focused on the most frequent
waves with a wave period large enough to assure plunging wave breaking.
Also ﬁne-tuning of the stone size and the slope of the reef allow for further optimization of surfability
and safety. Especially, the diﬀerence between the slope parallel and perpendicular to the crest line is
an interesting parameter to investigate. As this parameter has proven to inﬂuence the refraction of the
wave (and thus the pealing velocity) signiﬁcantly.
Tidal induced currents are neglected in this numerical study. However, the changing water level remain
an important factor to account for, as a low tide might result in partial or complete drain out of the reef
in severe wave events. On the other hand, a high tide might make the water level too deep to ensure
regular wave breaking and prevent waves traveling over the reef crest.
In this numerical wave study air velocities were caused by an exchange in momentum between water
and air. It will be of highest interest to include wind speed and direction in the computations since
the wave breaking and thus the surfability and surﬁng athlete safety will depend on this, as mentioned
by Scarfe et al. (2003). Strong oﬀshore wind will delay the wave breaking, hence the wave breaks on
shallower water making the shape of the wave breaking more tubular. Whereas strong onshore wind has
the opposite eﬀect.
The safety evaluation of the surﬁng athlete is only quantiﬁed based on the ratio between the breaking
height and the water depth in front of the wave. A more comprehensive determination of the safety can
be conducted by investigating the forces in the breaking wave. The surfer can crash in diﬀerent ways; 1)
he can fall down from the crest of the wave hitting the wave face or 2) he can get caught by the plunging
82
==PAGE:88==
jet and follow it onto the wave trough. In both situations there are in general two forces of interest; a
downward force super imposed by the Gravity and the force from the plunging jet. Likely, the second
scenario (when the surfer is caught in the plunging jet) is most threatening to the surﬁng athlete. In this
scenario the two forces (gravity and force from the plunging jet) act in the same direction, accelerating
of the surﬁng athlete violently into the through of the wave. An analysis of the two forces will reveal if
the surfer might hit the reef bed.
In this thesis, the surﬁng athlete safety was characterized only by the ratio between the breaking height
and the local water depth in the wave trough; when Hb/hthr is low, the surﬁng athlete safety is valued
high. However, an implementation of the breaking intensity (W/L) into the evaluation af the safety is
interesting for future studies. Assuming a constant Hb/hthr ratio, the surﬁng athlete safety will depend
on the vortex ratio (L/W); a spilling wave is not as dangerous as a plunging wave with a low vortex
ratio. I propose, that the surﬁng athlete safety parameter can be characterized by;

It is important to keep in mind that the main objective of the proposed artiﬁcial reef is to prevent coastal
erosion of the coastline by Palm Beach. Besides, the reef is intended to further enhance the surfability of
the waves. In preparation for coastal protection wave induced currents are of greatest importance. The
reef is installed oﬀshore the ambient surf zone to focus the waves to break on a regular basis, hence the
along shore transport will decrease in the lee of the reef. Furthermore, the high energetic water, produced
by the wave breaking, travels over the reef towards land. It is important that most of the energy will
dissipate over the reef, otherwise this will pose a risk to swimmers or other recreational visitors on the
beach. The increased dissipation of the wave energy in a porous reef explains why the porous reef type
was initially proposed.

surf rides insight the tube of the wave can be generated based on wave properties (wave period, angle of
attack and deep water wave height). Based on these surﬁng parameters an application for mobile devices
can be developed as a tool to plan surﬁng sessions.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
During my thesis I have set up a large scale CFD wave model as a numerical tool for use in the ongoing
coastal protection project at Palm Beach to quantify the eﬀect on the wave breaking from an implemen-
tation of a porosity on the artiﬁcial reef as proposed for Palm Beach.
By comparing model results to experimental measurements from previous studies, I have validated the
model and shown that it can accurately reproduce the physics inside and outside a breaking surface
wave without implementing turbulence models. During this model validation procedure, I found that
the wave breaking is delayed for both spilling and plunging waves in 2D model setups, while the wave
breaking is only slightly too early in 3D model setups. Thus, I have showed that a 3D model is essential
for accurately modeling large scale wave breaking.
I have tested four wave events which are representative of waves recorded in a period of seven years
(2007-2013) by Palm Beach. The diﬀerent wave events were all deﬁned from a surf similarity parameter
corresponding to a plunging wave, which is the most desirable wave type for surﬁng. I have formulated a
scoring system to evaluate the surfability of the waves and the surﬁng athlete safety. Using this scoring
system, it was immediately apparent that waves which approach the reef crest-line parallel (wave event 3,
0.39◦) provide poor surﬁng conditions, as little or no pealing is observed in this scenario while the surﬁng
safety estimate is very poor due to the relatively low water depth at the trough of the wave. On the other
hand, when the wave approaches the reef from the left of the crest-line at an angle which is too large
(wave event 2, −24.61◦) the wave is strongly refracted, resulting in a very high pealing velocity, which
will only allow short surf ride lengths. Using my scoring system, I have shown that waves approaching
the reef at intermediate angles (wave event 1 and 4, -14.61◦and -9.61◦) provide good surfability, i.e.
long surf rides. I showed, that the best surfability is found for wave event 4. It provides a long surﬁng
ride (up to 56 m) with wave sections of surﬁng insight the tube of the wave. However this wave event
also poses the largest risk to the surﬁng athlete due to a small depth in the wave trough in front of the
breaking wave of only 70 cm meanwhile the breaking height is 2.86 m. I propose to develop a means to
inform surﬁng athletes of the current surfability and surf safety, for example through a mobile surﬁng app.
In closing, the investigation of the four wave events lead to the following conclusions regarding the eﬀect
of the porous reef;
• For all the wave events the breaking point are displaced from 6 m to 8 m closer to the crest of
the reef.
• The breaking intensity is up to 18% larger when a wave breaks on an impermeable bed com-
pared to the implemented porous bed.
• The peeling velocity is more than 12% larger when the wave breaks on the impermeable bed
than on the porous bed.
• The overall surfability was quantiﬁed by a scoring system taking into account the above surf
parameters and in general the porous reef was evaluated at the same level or higher than the
impermeable reef.
• The surﬁng athlete safety was evaluated and the impermeable reef delivered waves at the same
85
==PAGE:91==
safety level or higher than the porous reef.
• In general, the best wave conditions are attained when waves approaching with an angle of
attack between -14.61 ◦to -9.61 ◦to the crest-line of the reef.
• The porous reef will reduce violent water currents induced by the wave breaking.
