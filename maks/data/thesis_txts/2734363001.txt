Abstract
Objective: This master’s thesis addresses the challenges faced by children aged 4-9
years in pediatric emergency department (PED) of Herlev Hospital, with a focus on
anxiety caused by unfamiliar environments, long waiting times, and the typical clinical
atmosphere of hospitals. The overall objectives are to evaluate the effectiveness of
child-friendly mobile app interfaces, with and without physical toys, in managing
anxiety in pediatric emergency care waiting areas.
Through iterative prototyping
based on user feedback, this project develops gamified interfaces designed to engage
and distract children during their waiting time.
Methodology: A mixed-methods approach was used, consisting of qualitative and
quantitative methods to enhance understanding of anxiety mitigation strategies and
child-friendly interface design in pediatric emergency care. Through iterative pro-
cesses and literature review, key challenges were identified, leading to the development
of physical solutions. Employing the double diamond model, the approach empha-
sizes iterative problem exploration and solution development, ensuring prototypes
evolve based on user feedback. To assess anxiety, scoring systems like Visual Ana-
log Scale (VAS) and modified Yale Preoperative Anxiety Scale (m-YPAS) are used.
Further, biometric data from the Empatica E4 wearable was collected and analyzed.
Results:
The tests were conducted with 19 children.
The seamless integration
between hardware and software was achieved via serial communication, with the
hardware consisting of the ESP8266 microcontroller, the YF-S201 flowmeter and a
normally-open microswitch. These sensors were integrated into the physical interfaces.
In the effectiveness and user experience test, 100% of participants would recommend
playing with the physical interfaces to a friend. Based on VAS and m-YPAS, none of
the participants had high levels of anxiety, either before starting or after completing
the tests. Thus, no analysis regarding mitigation of anxiety can be performed. The
feasibility of data collection with the Empatica E4 wristband was successful, as 71%
of the data sets could be analyzed in the first test and 100% in the second test.
Conclusion: Overall, this thesis contributes to advance in pediatric emergency care
through the development and evaluation of innovative technological interventions to
manage anxiety. Through empathetic design and a user-centered approach, gamified
interfaces were successfully integrated into a mobile app and tested in real-world
environments, resulting in positive outcomes.
Future work should include testing
with anxious children in different environments, engaging healthcare professionals,
and further improving the game’s interactive elements.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
”Anxiety doesn’t always look like a nervous child. Sometimes
it looks like a quiet, angry, or diﬀicult child.” – Unknown [1]
1.1
Introduction to Pediatric Emergency Care
In the domain of pediatric healthcare, the experience of an emergency room visit can
be an emotionally challenging and anxiety-causing event for both children and their
caregivers.[19] The anxiety experienced by pediatric patients during such encounters
not only hinders the delivery of effective medical care but also has a post-treatment
impact on the child’s overall well-being.[42, 13] Recognizing the promising role that
technology plays in the current healthcare system, this Master’s Thesis aims to ad-
dress a critical issue. It does so by investigating the development and evaluation of
child-friendly interfaces for a mobile application. The primary focus is on mitigating
anxiety in a pediatric emergency care setting.
The collaboration between DTU and Herlev Hospital represents a special opportunity
to bridge the gap between modern technology and applied medical practice. This in-
terdisciplinary partnership connects the expertise of DTU’s software engineering and
design departments with the clinical insights and experience of healthcare profes-
sionals at Herlev Hospital. In tailoring a mobile application specifically to mitigate
anxiety in young patients, the research has two goals. First, it seeks to advance the
field of pediatric emergency care. Second, it endeavors to offer innovative solutions
that significantly enhance the overall patient experience. The primary focus is on
improving the healthcare journey, particularly for young patients.
By merging the two domains of technology and healthcare, this project hopes to
lay the foundation for a more empathetic, eﬀicient, and child-focused approach to
pediatric emergency care. The goal is to not only manage children’s anxiety during
medical treatment but, also, to create an environment where treatment is delivered
without inducing fear. This aims to mitigate potential post-treatment issues, ensuring
a smoother recovery process for all young patients. Through this collaborative effort,
an attempt to encourage pediatric emergency care, with particular attention to Herlev
Hospital, was made, to prioritize empathy in their waiting areas.
The PED of the children’s department of Herlev Hospital already has various forms
of distractions to offer (see in chapter 3.3).

1 Introduction
1.2
Problem Definition
The commonness of anxiety in pediatric patients within emergency care settings is
a well-documented challenge. Anxiety not only aggravates the challenges faced by
healthcare providers but also increases the discomfort experienced by young patients
and their caregivers.[24] To tackle this multifaceted issue, a mobile application with
gamified interfaces, or child-friendly toys, tailored specifically to children’s needs and
preferences was developed in the parallel-running project by Petkova [34]. This inno-
vative game was designed in prior projects of Kjeldsen [22] and Folmer and Meyers
[16]. Building upon this base, Petkova [34] further implemented and developed the
mobile application in her Master’s project.
This application will serve as a supportive tool, offering various features to engage
and comfort pediatric patients within the emergency care environment. The gamified
interfaces connected to the application aim to engage and comfort young patients.
Specifically, this occurs during the crucial time they spend in the waiting area before
undergoing treatment. Those physical toys suggest a feeling of Virtual Reality (VR)
which is proven to have an effective impact in reducing pain and stress as a distraction
or an exposure tool. [47]. The overall goal is to transform the waiting experience.
This involves offering a dynamic and interactive platform that not only distracts
young patients from their anxiety or boredom but also promotes fun and control over
their situation.
Research has shown that anxiety experienced by pediatric patients before receiving
treatment can lead to lasting issues and fears afterward. [42] The emotional impact of
the initial anxiety can linger, potentially influencing the child’s perception of medical
procedures and creating worries during future visits. Recognizing the long-term con-
sequences of pre-treatment anxiety, the mobile application aims not only to provide
immediate comfort and distraction but also to contribute to a positive post-treatment
experience. By getting to the root of their anxiety during the waiting period before
treatment, we aim to mitigate future fears and challenges and enable young patients
to have a more resilient and positive attitude throughout their medical treatment.
This approach aligns with the overall goal of establishing a more empathetic and
child-centric approach in pediatric emergency care.
1.3
Research Objectives and Questions
This research addresses the theoretical framework for anxiety in pediatric emergency
care and explores the role of technology in alleviating anxiety. It also provides insights
into the design and development process of the child-friendly, gamified interfaces and
shows how user-centered principles were applied to create a supportive and engaging
platform. In addition, this report discusses the evaluation phase in which the effec-
tiveness of the toy in alleviating anxiety will be assessed through empirical studies
with pediatric patients.
==PAGE:21==
1.4 Report Structure
3
Empirical studies with children will evaluate the effectiveness of child-friendly mo-
bile app interfaces, both with and without physical toys, in alleviating anxiety in
the pediatric emergency care waiting area. This evaluation aims to determine user
preferences, levels of interaction, and ease of use to determine the effectiveness of
these interfaces in managing pediatric anxiety. In addition, the study will investigate
whether there is a difference in anxiety reduction between a mobile app that uses only
screen-based interaction and an app that is connected to specially designed gamified
interfaces (toys). The analysis will look at user engagement, satisfaction, playabil-
ity and anxiety levels to determine the potential benefits of incorporating gamified
interfaces into pediatric emergency care.
Ultimately, the goal of this master’s thesis is to contribute to the improvement of
pediatric emergency care through the integration of innovative technology and em-
pathetic design. In doing so, it aims to reduce the anxiety of young patients, their
caregivers and the healthcare system and pave the way for a more compassionate and
effective approach to pediatric emergency care. In addition to integrating innovative
technology and empathetic design, the goal is to create physical interfaces that allow
children to interact with a mobile game designed to alleviate their anxiety. In this
way, young patients can actively participate in therapeutic activities that give them
a sense of control and distraction in an otherwise stressful environment. Ultimately,
this project should not only alleviate the anxiety of the children and their caregivers,
but also improve the overall quality and eﬀiciency of pediatric emergency care. Based
on these, the primary questions for this research are as follows:
1. Through empirical studies with children, how well does the child-friendly mo-
bile app (with/ without physical toys) mitigate anxiety in the waiting area of
pediatric emergency care, and how can these findings contribute to the overall
enhancement of the pediatric emergency care experience?
2. Is there a difference in mitigation between the mobile app only with the screen
and connected with the specially developed gamified interfaces (physical toys)?
1.4
Report Structure
This report follows primarily the IMRaD model as it is a well-known and used for-
mat for systematic research reports in computer science. IMRaD refers to Introduc-
tion, Methods, Results, and Discussion (see Figure 1.1).
[50] In addition to this,
background information, including a literary review, will be included, as well as, a
conclusion containing suggestions for future work to round it off.
In the current chapter, an overview of pediatric emergency care, the definition of
the problem, and the significance of the research are offered. For these, the research
objectives and questions are determined.
In chapter 2 Methodology details about the research approach, methods, data collec-
tion, and analysis techniques are explained.

2.2
Design Methods
The Double Diamond model is a design thinking framework developed by the Design
Council in 2003 [23] used to structure and guide the design process. It consists of two
diamond shapes which are seen below in Figure 2.3 and represents a visual illustration
of the design thinking process.
The first diamond (left) has its focus on problem exploration whereas the second
diamond (right) centres around solution finding. Each diamond is divided into two
halves, first divergent thinking and then convergent thinking. That means first explor-
ing the whole problem (left) or solution (right) space and then coming to a conclusion
about either the only problem statement and then the specific solution.
==PAGE:26==
8
2 Methodology
Figure 2.3: Double Diamond Design Thinking Model [11, 23, 35, 36, 37]
The Discover Phase involves deep research, user empathy, and understanding of the
problem or challenge. The status quo should be addressed and explored. The goal is
to gain a broad range of insights and ideas by immersing in the problem space. User
needs, pain points, and perspectives are key considerations in this phase.
After exploration, the model narrows down to reach the problem definition before
getting to the second diamond. This is called the Define Phase. This second half is
about converging possible opportunities. A specific problem is defined, often framed
as a ”How might we...” question.
For example, how can we mitigate anxiety for
children in waiting areas of a PED?
Clear, user-centered goals are set to ensure
everyone understands the problem to be solved.
After reaching diamond number two the process returns to divergence in the third
phase. In the Develop Phase is where creative thinking and brainstorming (see Figure
2.4) come into play. A wide range of ideas and multiple solutions should be generated
and the most promising ones will be turned into prototypes. Prototypes are tested
and refined through user feedback. The amount of iterations depends on either time
constraints or satisfaction with the respective outcome.
In the final diamond half, the Deliver Phase, the focus is on convergent thinking
again.
Precise prototypes or refined designs of the solution are created based on
feedback and testing results. The solution is continuously tested to ensure it meets
user needs and provides the desired experience. Once validated, the solution is ready
for implementation and deployment in the real world.
After the final phase, the result is one or multiple specific design solutions ready to
be implemented, hence the implementation phase starts.
The Double Diamond model promotes a holistic and iterative approach to design.
It encourages exploration of possibilities, clear problem definition, diverse idea gen-
eration, and thorough testing and refinement. It places a strong emphasis on user-
centered design, problem-solving, and iterative improvement. This approach is par-
ticularly valuable for complex projects where understanding user needs is crucial.

design.[21] Throughout the experiments, thinking-out-loud methods were used to get

3.1
Anxiety
3.1.1
Definition
Anxiety is a complex and versatile emotional response characterized by feelings of
unease, fear, and tension. [9, 42] It is a natural part of the human experience and can
be seen in different forms, from mild uneasiness to severe panic. Anxiety often occurs
in response to perceived threats or unknown situations, and while sometimes, it can
be a normal reaction, it is a concern when it becomes chronic or significantly impacts
someone’s life. In the context of pediatric emergency care, anxiety is a common emo-
tional state experienced by patients in all age groups, but especially younger ones, due
to the unfamiliar and often intimidating hospital environment and the awaiting med-
ical treatments.[24, 13] The children are unaware of what is to be expected, and they
are probably at the PED due to a painful condition. Anxiety in pediatric emergency
care includes a range of emotional responses to medical procedures, often influenced
by age-specific factors. Dreuning et al. [13] emphasizes the importance of tailored
information for children and their families, highlighting the need to define anxiety
within the context of pediatric patients. According to Dreuning et al. [13], providing
age-specific and family-centered information plays a crucial role in addressing anxi-
ety, forming the basis for understanding the phenomenon in this specific healthcare
setting. It is important to mention that pre-treatment anxiety not only causes chal-
lenges in the present but can also have a significant impact on the overall healing
process, as well as mental and physical recovery. [42]
3.1.2
Assessment and Mitigation
The assessment of anxiety involves a comprehensive evaluation of both subjective and
objective factors. In hospital settings, healthcare professionals commonly use stan-
dardized questionnaires and scales to measure anxiety levels such as the m-YPAS
[42], Venham Picture Test, a child-reported anxiety level used in Stunden et al. [46]
or the VAS, which is a subjective pain measure.
Subjective assessment relies on
self-reporting by patients or their caregivers, while objective measures may include
physiological indicators such as heart rate and blood pressure. Additionally, behav-
ioral observations and interviews can provide valuable insights into the extent and
nature of anxiety experienced by individuals, especially children who may have dif-
ficulty expressing their emotions verbally.
A mixed-methods design that involves
both qualitative and quantitative data can offer valuable insights into the assessment.
This aligns with the full understanding needed for assessing the impact of the inter-
ventions. [7] Similarly, the user-centered design approach has to be emphasized for
valuable insights into anxiety assessment. [49]
Mitigating anxiety involves a multi-faceted approach that can include both pharma-
cological and non-pharmacological techniques (NPT). Non-pharmacological methods
often focus on psychological and environmental factors, including relaxation tech-

3.1.3
Age-related Variability
Anxiety manifests differently in children compared to adults due to developmental
factors. [48] Children may not possess the cognitive and emotional maturity to com-
municate their feelings, leading to physical complaints, or behavioral changes as out-
ward signs of anxiety. Understanding the emotions of younger patients becomes in-
creasingly more complex as their expression of those may not completely reflect their
feelings. Moreover, pediatric anxiety often involves separation anxiety and specific
phobias related to childhood experiences or fears. [48] Recognizing and addressing
these differences is crucial in providing effective support and intervention for anxious
patients. Children of varying ages may express distinct responses to anxiety-inducing
situations in emergency care. Tas et al. [47] explores the effects of virtual reality in
pediatrics, revealing age-specific insights. The study highlights the effectiveness of
VR as an intervention, underscoring the crucial need to recognize and address age-
related variability in anxiety. Despite the research not establishing a visible difference
in eﬀiciency, neither for younger nor older patients, it emphasizes the importance of
considering age-related factors in anxiety management strategies.

collaborative effort that combines the expertise of healthcare professionals and tech-

to pediatric emergency care, ultimately reducing the burden of anxiety on young
patients and their families.

Pediatric emergency care often triggers anxiety among young patients, presenting
challenges in providing effective medical interventions. Recent technological advance-
ments, particularly in mobile applications and VR, promise to mitigate anxiety within
this context. The literature review included a total of thirteen articles, mainly based
on the search string illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Six of those thirteen articles (46%) used VR as an intervention method. Just four
times articles used mobile apps (31%) to mitigate pre-treatment anxiety. Only one
of the thirteen articles used toys and games (8%). In the following, the main points
of the articles are summarized.
VR emerges as a significant tool in distracting pediatric patients during medical
procedures. Tas et al. [47], Stunden et al. [46] and Chiu et al. [10] highlight its ef-
fectiveness, indicating a substantial reduction in patient-reported pain and anxiety
levels. Moreover, VR applications designed specifically for medical settings, such as
VR-MRI, have shown comparative effectiveness to traditional preparatory methods
like manuals or hospital-based programs. [46]
Rashid et al. [39] delve into the domain of health apps tailored for overweight children,
emphasizing the potential of personalized interventions to positively impact anxiety
and overall well-being. Their study underlines the importance of tailored mobile ap-
plications in addressing anxiety issues among pediatric demographics.
Wiguna et al. [49] and Chen et al. [9] underscore the significance of user-centered
design methodologies in developing digital tools. Iteratively refined prototypes and
interdisciplinary collaborations play a pivotal role in creating effective digital inter-
ventions for pediatric patients, as observed in their research.
Scarano et al. [42] and Ashmore et al. [4] showcase the eﬀicacy of non-pharmacological
techniques in managing preoperative anxiety.
Their findings highlight the impor-
tance of approaches beyond medication, suggesting that such methods significantly
contribute to anxiety reduction among pediatric patients.
Chen et al. [9] evaluate integrated care models and their impact on preoperative anx-
iety, emphasizing the significance of holistic care approaches that consider psycholog-
ical aspects in pediatric procedures. Also Lerwick [24] highlights the introduction of
the CARE (Choices, Agenda, Resilience, Emotion) protocol, which aims to empower
pediatric patients, mitigate the risk of anxiety and trauma reactions, and promote
trust in healthcare providers.
Petersen et al. [33] explore the feasibility of using heart rate-based measures in real-

in pediatric anxiety management. Balaskas et al. [6] also uses technology to explore

corporating physiological interventions such as biofeedback or relaxation techniques
into technology-based interventions. This project aims to address this gap by explor-

applications to deliver tailored, comprehensive strategies for anxiety mitigation in
pediatric emergency care. By combining physiological techniques with user-friendly
digital interfaces, this project aims to provide a comprehensive approach to ease anxi-

4.1
Double Diamond Design Thinking Process
The Double Diamond Model (see chapter 2.2) served as the guiding structure through-
out the design process for the gamified interfaces.
As outlined in the preceding
chapter, this framework comprises two distinct phases: problem exploration and solu-
tion finding, each characterized by divergent and convergent subspaces. The process
through the Double Diamond Model involved research, experimenting, and iterative
ideation to address the challenge of mitigating anxiety in pediatric emergency care
settings.
The starting point of this model is the problem statement which reads as follows:
In pediatric emergency care settings, children often experience heightened anxiety,
which can adversely impact their overall well-being and care experience.

of the flow meter further hindered the pinwheels’ movement. This resulted, firstly, in
the decision to purchase a pre-made pinwheel. Secondly, rather than integrating the
flow meter into the pinwheel, a different approach was adopted. The flow meter was
used like a straw, allowing users to blow onto the pinwheel and set it into motion.

(thanks to Julie and William). To facilitate the pinwheel’s movement, various de-
vices were considered, including a straw and the YF-S201 flow meter [28] capable of
integration as a sensor with an Arduino, specifically the ESP8266. After evaluating
options, the flow meter was selected due to its ability to transmit data effectively,
addressing the project’s requirements.

4.2.2.2
Input Mechanism: Serial Communication
Serial Communication is a mechanism enabling the transmission of data between
electronic devices. It is often used to connect devices with sensors and/or microcon-
trollers. In this case, the UART was used to communicate between ESP8266 to the
Unity application. As the name suggests, it is asynchronous data transmission. Data
is transferred in a series of bits containing different parts of bits. There are the start
bits, the data bits, some optional bits, and the end bits. Data is not sent in parallel
but in chunks and timely after one another. It doesn’t rely on timestamps as it is
asynchronous and it uses predefined baud rates to specify the data chunks.[45]
An advantage of serial communication is found in the simplicity of the method. Only
one cable is needed for the transmission. In Figure 4.19) below, the difference is visible:
for sending an 8-bit message eight cables would be needed for parallel communication
==PAGE:64==
46
4 Results
whereas serial communication only requires one cable. This simplicity translates to
ease of implementation and cost-effectiveness.
Figure 4.19: Serial / Parallel Communication [45]
Comparing this method with Wi-Fi connectivity, multiple advantages can be high-
lighted. While Wi-Fi connection offers more flexibility and convenience to interact
between physical devices and mobile games, wired connections are in general more
reliable and stable. The potential for loss of signal can immensely impact usability,
whilst wired connections ensure stable performance. In addition, a wired connection
requires usually less power than a wireless connection. Moreover, since devices usually
require a power cable, using the same cable for two tasks further promotes simplicity.
To use this protocol the method Serial.print() needed to be integrated. First,
the relevant pins were identified and it has to be started with the chosen baud
rate.
Then, depending on the input of which sensor is chosen, data is transmit-
ted to the connected device.
In the case of the microswitch if the switchState
== LOW, which means the lever is pressed, Serial.println("SwitchState :1") is
sent to the listening device. For the airflow sensor, the code is a bit more elabo-
rate. First, the incoming airflow needs to be calculated based on the specifications of
the flowmeter. According to the pulse characteristics of the YF-S201 [28], there are
450 pulses/liter. This led to the following calculations to achieve an output of ml/s:
float airflow = (flowCount / 0.45) / 60. This output is sent to the listening
devices with Serial.println("Airflow:" + String(airflow)). The full Arduino
IDE Sketch can be found in the Appendix D.1.
4.2.2.3
Important Learnings
While working with the hardware, three big learnings have happened as they cost a
lot of time during the process.
First, when utilizing Serial Communication with the Arduino IDE, the Serial Monitor
occupies the port, allowing it to be accessed by only one task at a time which is in this
case the Arduino IDE. Therefore, if the Serial Monitor is open, the port is occupied,
==PAGE:65==
4.2 Hardware Connectivity & Software Integration
47
leading to the occurrence of the following error message: Error opening serial
port 'COM7'. (Port busy)
Second, when linking a microcontroller to Wi-Fi make sure the connection is at-
tempted to a 2.4 GHz network, as the connection won’t work with a 5.0 GHz network.
Routers commonly offer dual-band functionality, and some phones can maximize their
compatibility to successfully connect.
Third, when using Unity Hub, ensure that each script is added correctly via the
inspector to the relevant scene. Otherwise, despite having the listener established the
connection will not come to life.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This chapter contains a critical reflection and interpretation of the results within the
broader context of the research questions. The goal is to analyze the results in relation
to existing research and highlight their implications for the field. This chapter aims
to clarify the significance of the study and its potential contribution to advancing
knowledge in this area. In addition, the inherent limitations encountered throughout
the data collection process will be addressed. Finally, avenues for future opportunities
and potential progress in this area are outlined.
5.1
Interpretation of Results
Through empirical studies with children, how well does the child-friendly
mobile app (with/ without physical toys) mitigate anxiety in the waiting
area of pediatric emergency care, and how can these findings contribute
to the overall enhancement of the pediatric emergency care experience?
The prototypes developed included blowing and squeezing, which are physiological
techniques that help to reduce anxiety. This is also evidenced by Petersen et al. [33].
The design test focused on evaluating the aesthetics and ergonomics of playful, child-
friendly interfaces (physical toys). While this phase did not directly measure anxiety
reduction, it provided valuable insight into design preferences and potential improve-
ments for pediatric care experiences, laying the groundwork for further research and
success in reducing anxiety. Overall, the experiment was rated 4.2/5, indicating a
positive result for the first end-user trial and meaning that the devices are popular
and liked.
The mock-up test focused on evaluating the game mechanics and usability for chil-
dren using a mobile application and child-friendly interfaces. Despite the lack of a
tangible reduction in anxiety based on physiological indicators, the feasibility test
within this test had a positive outcome. 100% of the participants were able to wear
the Empatica E4 biometric data collection bracelet throughout the test, while only
71% of the collected data sets were usable afterwards due to handling errors. Later,
the collected data could provide information about anxiety or stress levels, for ex-
ample based on body temperature or heart rate. Unfortunately, the data sets did
not show any attenuation of anxiety, as the participating children showed no signs of
anxiety beforehand. Based on the VAS and m-YPAS scores used before the start of

was 56/100. According to Scarano et al. [42], the m-YPAS is a good measure in the

their stress and pain with the lowest value of the VAS both before and after playing

The test focusing on the effectiveness and user experience analyzed the integration of

the children did not want to participate on their own initiative. This shows that for
further research on this topic, it is important to involve hospital staff and conduct
the tests in a different environment, e.g. in the triage room immediately before or
after treatment. Another possibility would be to involve the parents in the gaming
experience, as Dreuning et al. [13] mentions in his study. It could lead to enormous

improving overall pediatric emergency care.
5.2
Limitations
Although this project had the goal of thoroughly addressing challenges faced by chil-
dren in pediatric emergency care settings, four major limitations were encountered
throughout the research process.
It was found that the mood and anxiety levels of the caregivers were closely related to
those of the children. Therefore, the presence of highly anxious caregivers may have
influenced the children’s participation in the experiments, which makes conducting
tests with anxious children challenging.
Recruiting a suﬀicient sample size proved a challenge due to the unpredictability of
the number of children in the relevant age group present in the waiting area of the
hospital. As a result, the study was limited to a small group of children, which could
affect the generalizability of the findings.
The age range of the participating children showed a considerable gap between the
lower and upper limits. These differences in developmental stages among children may
have led to variations in their responses and preferences, which affects the consistency
of the results.
Conducting the experiments by university students, rather than hospital staff, may
have influenced the trust levels of anxious caregivers. Working with hospital staff
could have created greater confidence in the research process and increased participa-
tion rates among caregivers.
Despite these limitations, the project provides valuable insights into the challenges
faced by children in hospital environments and provides a basis for future research to
enhance pediatric emergency care experiences.

Future experiments should involve testing with real anxious children with high m-YPAS
scores in environments outside of the waiting area of a hospital. While the waiting
area is a convenient location, it may not accurately represent the level of anxiety ex-
perienced by children undergoing medical procedures. Testing in environments where
children experience heightened anxiety, such as examination rooms or pre-surgery ar-
eas, would provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the developed interfaces
in reducing anxiety in real-life scenarios.
Involving nurses, doctors, and caregivers in the testing process could provide addi-
tional perspectives and support. Anxious caregivers may be more inclined to agree
to their child’s participation if they understand the potential benefits of the medical
gaming application. Additionally, involving healthcare professionals in the testing
process would provide insights into the practical implementation of the technology in
the clinical setting and ensure alignment with medical protocols.
To further increase user engagement, the game should be adapted to provide inter-
active feedback based on input from the physical interfaces. For example, on-screen
feedback indicating whether a child is blowing too much or too little could encourage
more active participation. In addition, different animations corresponding to the in-
tensity of airflow or trigger input would add variability and increase the immersive
experience for the children.
Switching to a computer-generated voice that sounds like a human voice would of-
fer greater flexibility and customization options.
Unlike human voices, computer-
generated voices can be easily modified and adapted to suit different languages, ac-
cents, or cultural preferences. This would facilitate future iterations of the game by
simplifying the process of updating and localizing the narration content.
Expanding the flexibility of the game to allow players to return to the hospital scene
after completing each subgame would improve flexibility and engagement. Addition-
ally, enabling players to complete scenes in a flexible order would cater to individual
preferences and accommodate varying levels of comfort and familiarity with different
gameplay elements. This adaptive approach would enhance the overall user experi-
ence and encourage longer engagement with the game.
