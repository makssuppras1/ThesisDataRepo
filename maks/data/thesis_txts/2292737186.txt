i. Resumé 
Baggrund: Øjet er et kompleks organ, der er kendt som et immune privilegeret sted. Den mest udbredte 
celle type i den uveale tragt i øjet, som består af iris, strålelegemet og chorioidea, er uveale melanocytter 
(UM). Bruchs membran adskiller chorioidea fra de retinale pigment epithel (RPE) celler og resten af retina. 
RPE cellerne har vist at være involveret i immunereguleringen i øjet ved, blandt andet, at inhibere 
proliferationen af T-celler. RPE cellerne er den mest studerede celletype i forbindelse med aldersrelateret 
makula degeneration (AMD), som er den mest hyppige årsag til tab af syn hos personer over 60 år i den 
vestlige verden. Udviklingen af AMD er ikke helt klar, men involverer blandt andet RPE atrofi og dermed 
fotoreceptor død. Desuden er makrofager blevet forbundet med AMD, hvor en forøget ratio af M1 til M2 
makrofager er observeret i AMD patienter. Vi ønskede at opnå en større forståelse af UMs rolle i 
chorioidea, især deres effekt på T celler og monocytter  
Metoder: In vitro co-kultur modeller blev anvendt, hvor tre forskellige primære UM cellekulturer blev co-
kultureret med CD3/CD28 activerede T celler, oprenset fra buffy coats. Aktiviteten ad indoleamine2,3-
dioxygenase (IDO) i UM blev kvantificeret ved hjælp af l-kynurenine analyse. Endvidere blev anti-IDO, levo-
1-methyl tryptophan, tilsat nogle af co-kultur forsøgene. T celle proliferationen blev bestemt ved hjælp af 
3H-thymidin inkorporerings analyse. Medie fra primære UM cellekulturer enten ubehandlet eller behandlet 
med IFN-γ og TNF-α blev anvendt til at differentiere M0 makrofager. Disse blev analyseret ved hjælp af de 
karakteristiske markører CD40 og CD64 for M1 makrofager og CD163 og CD206 for M2 makrofager og 
undersøgt ved flow cytometri. 
Resultater: UM inhiberer proliferationen af T celler gennem direkte kontakt. Aktiviteten af IDO var 
opreguleret i UM, når disse var kultureret med supernatant fra aktiverede T celler. Ved tilsætning af anti-
IDO til co-kultur af UM og aktiverede T celler, sås en delvis genoprettelse af T celle proliferationen.  
M0 makrofager behandlet med medie fra både ubehandlede UM samt IFN-γ og TNF-α stimulerede UM 
resulterede i en blandet M1/M2 makrofag population.  
Konklusion: UM ser ud til at være involveret i immunereguleringen i øjet gennem inhibering af 
proliferationen af T celler. Mekanismen bag denne hæmning er ukendt, men IDO kan være involveret, 
muligvis i kombination med andre inhiberende mekanismer 
Medie fra ubehandlede UM ser ud til at inducere en blandet population af M1 og M2 makrofager, og medie 
fra UM stimuleret med IFN-γ og TNF-α synes at yderligere inducere CD40 specifikke M1 makrofager. 
Grundet manglende kontroller kan der dog ikke konkluderes på data.

ii. Abstract 
Background: The human eye is a complex organ and known to be an immune privilege site. The uveal tract 
of the eye consists of the iris, the ciliary body and the choroid, where the main cell type is melanocytes. 
Little work has been done on the uveal melanocytes (UM). Bruch’s membrane separates the choroid from 
the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells and retina. A lot of work has been done on the RPE cells. They 
have been shown to be involved in the immune regulation of the eye by, among other things, inhibiting T 
cell proliferation. Furthermore, it is the most studied cell type in regard to age-related macular 
degeneration (AMD), the leading cause of blindness among people over the age of 60 years in the western 
world. The pathogenesis of AMD in not completely understood but involves RPE atrophy and thereby death 
of photoreceptors. Moreover, macrophages have been associated with AMD where an increased ratio of 
M1 to M2 macrophage phenotype is observed in AMD patient compared to age-matched controls. Because 
little is known about the UM we wanted to obtain a better understanding of the role of these cells, in 
particular their effect on T cells and monocytes.  
Methods: In vitro co-culture systems were used, in which three different primary UM cell cultures were co-
cultured with CD3/CD28 activated T cells purified from buffy-coats. Different ratios of UM to T cells were 
used; 1:1, 1:2, 1:4 and 1:8. Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) activity in UM was measured with l-
kynurenine assay, and the anti-IDO compound, levo-1-methyl tryptophan, was included in co-culture of UM 
with activated T cells. Proliferation of T cells was determined by 3H-Thymidine incorporation assay. 
Conditioned medium derived from primary UM cell cultures, either untreated or stimulated with IFN-γ and 
TNF-α was used to differentiate matured M0 macrophages. The differentiated macrophages were analyzed 
by flow cytometry using the characteristic markers CD40 and CD64 for M1 macrophages and CD163 and 
CD206 for M2 macrophages 
Results: UM co-cultured with activated T cells were able to inhibit T cell proliferation through direct cell to 
cell contact. Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) activity was uregulated in UM when exposed to 
supernatant from activated T cells, suggesting a role in the UM mediated inhibition of T cell proliferation. 
Addition of anti-IDO seemed to partly restore the T cell proliferation in some experiments.  
Conditioned medium derived from untreated as well as stimulated UM generated a mixed population of M1 
and M2 macrophages.  
Conclusion: UM seem to be involved in the immune regulation in the eye, through inhibition of T cell 
proliferation. The mechanism behind is still unknown, but IDO might be involved, possibly in combination 
with other inhibitory mechanisms. 
Conditioned medium derived from untreated UM might induce a mixed population of M1 and M2 
macrophages, and there seems to be an induced expression of the M1 macrophage marker CD40 when 
conditioned medium derived from IFN-γ and TNF-α stimulated UM were exposed to M0 macrophages. 
However these data is not conclusive due to lack of proper controls.

1. Background 
1.1 Anatomy and physiology of the eye 
The human eye is a complex organ, in which three layers can be distinguished. The outer layer consists of 
the cornea and the sclera, the middle layer is composed of the iris, the ciliary body and the choroid, 
collectively known as the uveal tract, and the most inner layer of the eye is the retina (Figure 1) [1]. The 
cornea is located in front of the iris and the pupil, and is covered and protected by a tear film layer. The 
cornea refracts and transmits light to the lens and the retina. The sclera forms a connective tissue coat that 
protects the eye from both external an internal forces[1], [2]. The iris is controlling the amount of light that 
reaches the retina by regulating the size of the pupil. The 
ciliary body controls the shape of the lens via the ciliary 
muscles, and thereby focuses light on the retina. The last 
part of the uveal tract, the choroid, supports and provides 
nutrients to the retina [2]. Bruch’s membrane separates 
the choroid from the retina. This extracellular matrix is 
involved in the exchange of several biological molecules, 
oxygen, nutrients and waste products between the two 
tissues, and plays an essential role in cell-cell 
communication, tissue remodeling and cellular 
differentiation to mention a few [3]. The retina covers 
most of the inner surface of the eye and is composed of 
the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) cells and the neural 
retina, where the photoreceptors are located [1]. 
Light enters the eye, goes through the pupil and is 
refracted as it passes through the cornea, the aqueous 
humor in the ciliary body, the lens and the vitreous 
humor to be focused on the retina. The photoreceptors in 
the retina respond to the light by producing nervous 
impulses. These impulses leave the retina and eye 
through the optic nerve to the brain where 
interpretations of the nervous impulses end in ‘seeing’ [4] 
                       
                       
1.1.1 Melanocytes 
Melanocytes are found in various sites in the human body. They provide important physiological functions 
in the skin, eyes, inner ears and covering of the brain. The main cell type in the uveal tract of the eye is the 
uveal melanocytes (UM), and is comprised of the iridal melanocytes, the ciliary melanocytes and choroidal 
melanocytes [6]. The melanocytes are melanin pigment producing cells, and function, among other things, 
as photoprotecters, trapping reactive oxygen species (ROS) and binding of drugs and organic chemicals [7].  
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
Melanin is produced and stored in the melanosomes in the cytoplasm of melanocytes. There exist two 
kinds of melanins; eumelanin (EM) and pheomelanin (PM). Both of these seem to have the same protective 
properties; protect the ocular cells and tissue by absorbing light and UV radiations as well as working as an 
antioxidant. However the higher level of EM to PM the greater the antioxidant effect seems to be [8].The 
melanin has been proposed to protect the eye against several ocular diseases that, can cause blindness, 
including age-related macular degeneration (AMD) and uveal melanoma[8],[9]. However, other studies, 
trying to connect the melanin content to the pathogenesis of the early stages of AMD, have concluded, that 
there seems to be no protective effect of melanin against AMD [10], [11].  
The UM do not directly participate in the detection and transfer of light. They do however have functions 
that support the neural retina and other tissue types in the eye, and are therefore essential for 
maintenance of vision [6]. The UM differ from the cutaneous (skin) melanocytes. For example, the UM are 
attached to each other and the melanocytes of the choroid have easy access to blood-borne factors. The 
anterior layer of the eye (Bruch’s membrane, the RPE cells etc.), which covers the choroidal melanocytes, 
absorbs and scatters most of the visible, UV and infrared radiation from the sun, thus the melanocytes of 
the choroid receive less to no exposure to light[7]. 
In the choroid, melanocytes are organized in a three dimensional network of parallel sheets, which is a 
network comprised of heterogeneous population of melanocytes [12]. Figure 2 shows a section of the 
choroid, where the deep brown color of the choroid results from the large number of melanocytes found 
around the blood vessels.

Different cells of the immune system have been found in the choroid. These include CD4+ T cells and CD8+ 
T cells as well as monocytes/macrophages expressing CD14 [14] 
1.1.2 RPE cells 
The RPE cells are multifunctional cells, which are highly important for homeostasis of the neural retina and 
visual function [15]They degrade and phagocytose used photoreceptor outer segments and are responsible 
for removing waste products from the photoreceptor as well as removing water and ions from the retina to 
the choroid. They form the outer blood-retina barrier, transport nutrients to the photoreceptors and are 
also involved in the renewal of photoreceptor outer segment [15], [16]. The RPE cells cannot renew 
themselves by cell division and loss or damage of RPE cells is fatal to the photoreceptors and vision [16]. 
1.2 The immune privilege of the eye 
Immune privileged was first discovered by Peter Medawar in the 1940s [17]. He found, that foreign tissue 
grafts placed in the anterior chamber of the eye could survive for an extended time, and in this way defined 
immune privileged organs. These organs include the eye, brain, pregnant uterus and testis (see table) [18].  
The maintaining of immune homeostasis in the eye is important. Many of the cells residing the eye are not 
able to regenerate and so inflammation can have devastating consequences. One way it is maintained is 
through physical barrier, like the blood-retina barrier. This prohibits inflammatory cells and 
macromolecules in entering the eye [19]. However cells of the immune system do occasionally enter the 
eye and produce immune-mediated inflammation, like in uveitis. In these cases the ocular 
microenvironment helps uphold the privileged. The iris/ciliary body and the RPE cells express and secrete 
inhibitory molecules, where the latter have been shown to inhibit T cell proliferation [20]–[23], convert 
CD4+ T cells to regulatory T (Treg) cells [24] and induce T cell apoptosis[25]. Several molecules have been 
proposed to play a role in the RPE-induced regulation including programmed cell death 1  ligand 1 (PD-L1) 
[23], [26], Transforming growth factor (TGF)-β[22], prostaglandin [27]and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen-2 
(CTLA-2)α[24]. 
The RPE cells have been shown to express indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO)[28]. IDO have been found to 
contribute to immune regulation by catalyzing the essential amino acid tryptophan along with the 
kynurenine pathway. It modifies immune response by producing kynurenine and by depleting tryptophan. 
The expression of IDO is tightly regulated by exogenous signals and upregulation of IDO happens rapidly in 
response to inflammatory stimuli. IDO is expressed intracellular so the metabolic effects begin as 
intrinsically signals. The depletion of tryptophan and secretion of produced kynurenine however do not 
only impact cells expressing IDO, but neighboring cells (e.g. T cells that interact with the IDO expressing 
cell) may respond to these signals [29]. It has been proposed that this depletion of tryptophan and the 
associated production of l-kynurenine may play a role in the RPE-mediated immune suppression and ocular 
immune privilege [28]. 
1.3 Age-related macular degeneration 
AMD is the leading cause of blindness among people over the age of 60 years in the western world, where 
more than 50% of the blind registration is recognized by AMD [30]–[33]. The elderly population is 
increasing in the western world, and AMD is therefore likely to increase significant over the next period of 
years [34]. AMD is found in two forms; dry and wet AMD (figure 3), where the dry form is the most 
common (90% of cases) [35]. In the dry form, also called geographic atrophy, vision loss is usually gradually 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
happening, because of RPE atrophy and thereby photoreceptor death. It is characterized by the presence of 
drusen, which are extracellular deposit that accumulates on Bruch’s membrane below the RPE cells and the 
clinical hallmark of AMD [35], [36]. The wet and more severe form of AMD occurs when new blood vessels 
from the choroid grows into the outer retina (choroidal neovascularization), and leak fluid, lipid and blood 
that leads to fibrous scarring [37]. There is no treatment for dry AMD, but anti-vascular endothelial growth 
factor (VEGF) treatment is available for those affected by wet AMD [38]. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
At present the molecular mechanism behind AMD pathogenesis is only incompletely understood. A number 
of risk factors are however identified such as age [30]–[33], smoking and obesity [40] as well as genetic 
factors [41], including studies where individuals which are homozygote for mutations in complement factor 
H (CFH) alleles have an increase by a factor of 7.4 in developing AMD [42]. It is also known that significant 
changes occur in the immune system with age, which lead to increased levels of pro-inflammatory 
cytokines in the blood [43], and that inflammation is an important process in the AMD pathogenesis [44]. 
Macrophages have been found in the choroid [14] and increasing in numbers as AMD progresses 
[45].Furthermore it has been suggested ,that drusen is associated with recruitment of macrophages to the 
Bruch’s membrane, and the altering phenotype of macrophages present in the choroid [46]. Macrophages 
can polarize to at least two phenotypes: M1 and M2. M1 phenotypes have a pro-inflammatory and anti-
angiogenic effect and therefore a tissue destructive role, where the M2 have a pro-angiogenic and anti-
inflammatory effect [47]. Experiments have shown that there is an increased M1 to M2 ratio in patients with 
AMD compared to age-matched controls [48], and it is suggested that these M1 macrophages may mediate 
RPE injuri in the pathogenesis of AMD [48], [49]. 
The most studied cell type in regard to AMD is the RPE cells, and little attention has been paid to the UM. It 
has been shown in vitro, that both the RPE cells and the UM, when stimulated to induce inflammatory 
conditions, produce several pro-inflammatory cytokines and growth factors [50]–[52]. It has been 
suggested that these cells may play a role in the development of ocular inflammation processes and the 
pathogenesis of various ocular diseases including age-related macular degeneration (AMD) [16], [51], [52].

2. The aim of the project 
The overall purpose of the project was to obtain a better understanding of the role of the UM located in 
the uveal tract of the eye, in particular their effect on T cells and monocytes. 
 
The first part of the project focuses on the effect of UM on T cell proliferation.  
Previous work within the laboratory and by other groups, have shown the RPE cells are able inhibit 
T cell proliferation. As mentioned in the section about the immune privilege of the eye, several 
factors have been proposed to be involved in this inhibition. In this project we will investigate if 
primary UM cultures are able to inhibit T cell proliferation both by direct contact as well as by 
soluble factors. We will furthermore examine if IDO plays a role in the regulation. 
 
The second part of the project investigates the effect of conditioned medium from UM on 
monocytes and their differentiation into M1 or M2 macrophage phenotypes. 
The role of the UM in macrophage differentiation could be interesting to elucidate because of their 
different roles in inflammatory processes in the back of the eye.

3.1.1 Flow cytometry control of UM purity 
The purity of the isolated UM was checked by flow cytometry. Potentially three different cell types could be 
present; fibroblasts, RPE cells and melanocytes. The purity was checked with two different markers, S-100 
which is expressed on RPE cells and melanocytes and Pan cytokeratin, which is present on RPE cells. 
Fibroblast expresses none of these markers. Briefly, cells were harvested, fixated and permeabilized with 
the Cytofix/Cytoperm Kit (BD Biosciences). The cells were incubated for 30 min at 4 °C  with an Alexa Fluor® 
488 conjugated antibody to the cytokeratin peptides 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 13 and 18 (pan cytokeratin) (Clone C11, 
Exbio, Vestec u Prahy, Czech Republic), the corresponding isotype control (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, Ca) and the 
primary antibody S-100 (Clone B32.1 Santa Cruz, Dallas, Tx). After washing, the cells labeled with the 
primary antibody S-100 were incubated for 30 min at 4 °C with a fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) 
conjugated F(ab’)2 specific secondary antibody (Sigma) which was diluted 1:100. Cells were analyzed on a 
LSR II flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) using CellQuest software. Data was analyzed using FlowJo version 
10.0.7. 
The 3 eyes used were above 98 % pure. As a representative the purity of eye 7 can be seen in figure 6.  Live 
cells were first gated based on the forward-side scatter plot (FSC-SSC) (6A). Afterward the single cells were 
gated (6B) and expressions of S-100 and Pan Cytokeratin were analyzed on these cells (6C and 6D). The UM 
isolated from eye 7 are 99,4 % pure (6C).

cells should not influence the result of the T cell proliferation.  
3.5.1 Theory behind 3H-thymidine incorporation assay 
The 3H thymidine proliferation assay can determine the amount of growing cells under different cultures. 
3H thymidine is added to cell cultures and will be incorporated into the replication strand of DNA produced 
during cell division. The more cell divisions, the higher the proliferation rate and the more radioactive 
thymidine will be incorporated into the DNA. After incubation the cells are harvested onto filter plates, 
where cell fragments and intact DNA, will be captured on the filter membrane and excess 3H thymidine 
washed away. Scintillation liquid is added, and the amount of β radiation emitting from the cells can be 
measured on a scintillation counter [53], [54]

3.10.1 Theory behind flow cytometry 
Flow cytometry is a technique where one is able to make rapid measurements on cells, as they flow in a 
fluid stream through a sensing point. One important aspect of flow cytometry is, that the measurements 
are made on individual cells within a suspension and not just an average value of the cell population. 
Multiple cellular parameters can be analyzed via flow cytometry, like nuclear acid content, enzyme activity 
and, if using conjugation of fluorescent dyes to monoclonal antibodies, distribution of cell-surface 
receptors. Flow cytometry can also be used for cell sorting [57]. 
A suspension of single cells is injected into a fluidic system were sheet fluid is flowing. The system is 
responsible for delivering the cells in a single stream to the light beam (figure 11). In the beginning of the 
channel the velocity of the fluid is the same at the walls and in the center. As 
the fluid moves through the channel viscous drag at the walls slow down the 
fluid near the walls creating a gradient where fluid is moving fast in the 
center of the stream and slower near the walls. This phenomenon creates a 
lower pressure in the middle of the channels and draws the cell particles 
toward the center of the flow, a process known as hydrodynamic focusing. 
The sheet fluid flows continuously and is normally controlled by regulated 
positive air pressure. The sample flow rate is controlled by another pressure 
regulator [57], [58].  
After focusing, each particle passes through a beam of light. The light beam 
can be a laser or an arc lamp, and the cells deflect light when hit by the

beam. The forward scatter light (FSC) is proportional to the cell size/cell surface area and is detected by the 
forward scatter. The side scattered light (SSC) is proportional to cell granularity or the internal complexity 
of the cell. Measurements of FSC and SSC can provide information of cell types in a heterogeneous cell 
population. If a fluorescent dye is conjugated to a monoclonal antibody, it can be used to identify a 
particular cell type based on the specific surface marker on the cell. In a mixed population of cells, different 
fluorochromes can be used to separate and distinguish cells, and in combination with FSC and SSC one is 
able to identify, which cells are present and their relative percentage in a sample[58] 
When in contact with the light beam, cells that are labeled with a fluorochrome, will absorb light energy at 
one wavelength, and the electrons are raised from the ground state to an exited state. The electrons return 
to the ground state by emitting light of a longer wavelength than the exciting light. The transition of the 
energy is called fluorescence, and the difference between the absorption and emission is referred to as 
Stokes shift (figure 12). Both the excitation and emission wavelengths are specific for each fluorophore 
[57], [60] 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The FSC signal is collected by a photodiode that converts the 
intensity of the light into voltage. The emitted SSC and 
fluorescence signals are directed to the photomultiplier tubes 
(PMTs) (figure 13) 
In the PMTs the light signal excites the electrons in the 
photocathode, which results in the release of photoelectrons 
emitting into the PMT. The photoelectron then hits the first 
dynode, where the multiple secondary electrons are being 
released. This electron multiplication continues down the PMT 
where several dynodes are placed. The number of produced 
electrons depends on the number of dynodes, as well as the 
voltage applied to the PMT. The electrons are captured by the anode and converted to a voltage pulse.

The highest point of voltage pulse happens, when the cell is in the center of the laser, where the maximum 
amount of scatter of fluorescence occurs. As the cell leaves the laser, the voltage pulse returns to baseline 
(see figure 14) [62], [63] 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Filters are placed in front of the PMTs, which allow only a narrow range of wavelength to reach the PMT 
and detector. These filters come in two forms; Bandpass filters or edge filters. The bandpass filters transmit 
the light close to the wanted wavelength. The edge filter is characterized as either short wavelength filters, 
where light, equal or below a given wavelength, is transmitted, or long wavelength filters where 
wavelength, equal to or longer than a specific wavelength, is transmitted. The edge filters are often used as 
dichroic mirrors (also known as beam splitters) to direct the light of different wavelengths in the direction 
of different detectors [57], [58]. Figure 15A shows a simple overview of a flow cytometry system and figure 
15B shows an example of how different colors are directed to the detector by filters on an LSR II from bd 
bioscience.

5. Discussion and conclusion 
5.1 UM effect on T cell 
As mentioned in the introduction, it has previous been found that RPE cells are able to inhibit T cell 
proliferation and is thought to do so in a way to maintain the immune privilege of the eye [20]–[23]. The 
inhibition has been found to be a product of direct cell to cell contact as well as through soluble factors. It 
was of interest to study UM cells, the main cell type in the uveal tract, to see if they might also contribute 
to the immune privilege in the posterior part of the eye. 
This thesis shows that UMs are also able to inhibit T cell proliferation (figure 17). Previous studies on gene 
expression by Affymetrix gene array, showed that IDO was highly up-regulated when UM were exposed to 
pro-inflammatory cytokines (IFN-γ and TNF-α) or supernatant from activated T cells, compared to UM 
cultured alone. IDO deplete tryptophan from the medium, thereby deprive the T cells of an essential amino 
acid [29]. In order to validate if IDO is active, we measured l-kynurenine, the product produced and 
secreted in IDO catabolism. L-kynurenine was shown to be secreted in higher concentrations from 
supernatant of stimulated UM compared to untreated UM (figure 18). Therefore, it was interesting to study 
if this IDO regulation could be involved in the UM mediated inhibition of T cell proliferation.  
We tried to add additional tryptophan to the co-culture of UM and T cells to prevent tryptophan depletion 
(figure 19. However, this did not restore the T cell proliferation.  The continual inhibition of T cell 
proliferation could be caused by further depletion of tryptophan and/or the produced metabolites in IDO 
catabolism. 
Levo-1-methyl tryptophan (1-L-MT) has been shown to block the activation of IDO. This anti-IDO compound 
is an analog of tryptophan and is resistant to degradation by IDO [28]. When adding anti-IDO to the co-
culture of UM and T cells, it seemed, that in some of the experiments, the T cell proliferation could be 
partly restored (figure 20]. However, it also appears that the anti-IDO itself have an effect on the T cell 
proliferation, both stimulatory and inhibitory. These contradicting functions of anti-IDO on T cell 
proliferation are difficult to explain. The logical explanation would be if the solvent, that anti-IDO is 
dissolved in, is harmful for the T cells and therefore has an effect on the proliferation. It does seem like the 
proliferation of T cells, when exposed to the solvent, show the same tendency for the proliferation profile 
as T cells exposed to anti-IDO. Their proliferation counts are however not equal and so the solvent may not 
be the sole reason for the effect of anti-IDO on T cell proliferation 
Since IDO is an intracellular molecule that deplete tryptophan from the medium and secrete l-kynurenine, 
neighboring T cell should also be affected by IDO metabolism. However, in an indirect set-up, UM did not 
inhibit T cell proliferation (data not shown). IDO might have a role in the inhibition of T cell when co-
cultured with UM, but it does not seem to be the sole molecule responsible. Other UM immunosuppressive 
mechanisms might be involved in the contact based inhibition of T cell proliferation.  One could be the 
surface protein programmed cell death 1 ligand 1 (PD-L1). PD-L1 is one of the ligands of PD-1, which have 
been found to be expressed on various cell types, included activated T cells [67]. PD-L1 has been shown to 
be involved in the RPE cell inhibition of T cell proliferation [23], [26] and Affymetrix gene expression data 
showed that PD-L1 is induced on UM when exposed to IFN-γ and TNF-α or supernatant from activated T 
cells. Affymetrix data also showed that PD-1 was slightly expressed on activated T cells.  
==PAGE:35==
34 
 
To sum up; UM seems to be involved in immune regulation in the eye, through the inhibition of T cell 
proliferation. The mechanism behind this immune regulation is unknown, but IDO might be involved 
perhaps in combination with other regulatory molecules. 
5.2 UM effect on monocytes 
The differentiation and maturation of monocytes to M0, M1 and M2 macrophages have been described by 
others [56]. M1 are pro-inflammatory macrophages that can inhibit cell proliferation and causes tissue 
damage, while the M2 macrophages work opposite as anti-inflammatory and repair cell tissue and 
promotes cell proliferation [47]. We were also able to regulate M0 macrophage differentiation and induce 
specific macrophage phenotypes with IFN-γ/LPS or IL-4.  
IFN-γ and LPS induced M1 macrophages seen by the up-regulation of CD40 and CD64 (figure 23A and B), 
which are specific M1 marker and predominantly spindled shaped cells were observed (figure 21B). 
IL-4 could induce M0 macrophages to M2 phenotypes, shown by the up-regulation of expression marker 
CD206 (figure 23D). The other M2 marker was not induced when M0 macrophages were differentiated with 
IL-4 (figure 23C). The shape of the IL-4 stimulated M0 macrophages were mostly of a circular shape (figure 
21C).  
Untreated M0 macrophages expressed basal level of the M1 marker CD40 and CD64 and for the M2 
macrophage marker CD206. For the other M2 marker, CD163, the untreated M0 macrophages were slightly 
more induced (figure 24). 
M0 macrophages exposed to conditioned medium derived from untreated UM showed an induced 
expression of the M1 marker CD40, as well as the two M2 markers CD163 and CD206 (figure 24).  
CD 40 was highly induced when the M0 macrophages were exposed to conditioned medium derived from 
stimulated UM. No major difference in expression of the other M1 marker CD64 was seen. The expression 
of both CD163 and CD206 were induced (figure 24).  
So, both M0 macrophages exposed to conditioned medium from untreated UM and conditioned medium 
from activated UM induced expression of CD40, CD163 and CD206. This means that UM are able to 
differentiate M0 macrophages to both M1 and M2 macrophages and suggest that a heterogenic population 
of macrophages are be present.  
One can speculate what function macrophages and in particular their phenotypes (M1/M2) have in AMD. 
The number of M2 to M1 macrophages is increased in normal aging eyes. However, in AMD the number of 
M1 is increased [48]. Since M1 are the pro-inflammatory macrophage phenotype they can contribute to the 
inflammation seen in AMD and to tissue damage. On the other hand the M2 macrophages, which are anti-
inflammatory and repair cell tissue, might be beneficial in AMD. However, M2 macrophages are also known 
to be pro-angiogenetic and promote blood vessel growth and may in this way be harmful in wet AMD. 
Since macrophages are phagocytotic cells, another beneficial aspect of macrophages in AMD could be that 
they help in the clearing of drusen deposit, at the beginning of its development in dry AMD. It might be that 
one of the phenotypes is better in the phagocytosis of drusen particles and thus preventing dry AMD.   
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
Our study indicates that conditioned medium from untreated UM induce a mixed population of M1/M2 
macrophages. Though, when macrophages were exposed to conditioned medium from UM stimulated with 
IFN-γ and TNF-α, we could observe a higher induction of M1 macrophages expressing CD40. However, we 
cannot draw any conclusions on these results, since we are lacking important control experiments; the UM 
are in 50% X-vivo-50% M2 medium when experiments are conducted, which means that the conditioned 
medium derived from UM is also composed of X-vivo/M2 (50/50). Since the monocytes and macrophages 
were maintained in DMEM medium, the medium combination used in UM experiments should have been 
tested on M0 macrophages alone, to rule out that the medium has an effect on the differentiation of 
macrophages. Unfortunately this was not done, and therefore any conclusion on the macrophage 
differentiation by conditioned medium derived from UM cannot be made.  
Furthermore, one needs to be careful drawing conclusions from in vitro study to their behavior in AMD. 
Since macrophages have a high plasticity and can easily switch between M1 and M2 macrophages their 
phenotype probably also depend on the time point of the disease [48]. Also other environment factors and 
cells types in the choroid might influence their polarization.

6. Future work 
6.1. UM effect on T cells 
To validate the found UM mediated inhibition on T cell proliferation, another method could be 
implemented. This could be e.g. by using caboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE) and flow cytometry 
measurement. The CFSE bind covalently to intracellular molecules without affecting cellular function. When 
CFSE labeled cells divide, the CFSE will be equally distributed between progeny, and cell division can be 
evaluated by measuring the decrease in cell fluorescence. Furthermore, it could be interesting to 
investigate if the inhibition is caused to CD4+ T cells and/or CD8+ T cells. 
The inhibition of T cell proliferation also needs to be replicated using other T cells donors. Including more 
UM donors to elucidate if e.g. the UM mediated inhibition is eye donor specific could also be of interest. 
The aspect of IDO and the fact that it seems, in some cases, to have a stimulatory effect on T cells 
proliferation and in other cases to have an inhibitory effect, should be investigated more thoroughly 
through additional set-up of the originally experiment. Furthermore, it could be interesting to look at other 
molecules (e.g. PD-L1) and investigate if they are involved in the UM mediated T cell proliferation, either 
alone or in combination with IDO.  
 
6.2. UM effect on monocytes 
The observed results of macrophage differentiation should be confirmed using more UM donors and 
efficient control experiments with untreated M0 macrophages in combination with the different media 
used. 
It was seen that IFN-γ and LPS could induce M1 macrophages. Conditioned medium derived from stimulated 
UM contains IFN-γ and TNF-α. It could be of interest to neutralize the IFN-γ in the conditioned medium 
before exposure to M0 macrophages, in order to validate that the observed phenotype is a result of the 
conditioned medium and not IFN-γ.
