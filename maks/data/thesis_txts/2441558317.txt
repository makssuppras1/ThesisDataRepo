Abstract
Internet of Things is a very broad term, encompassing networks of devices varying from
smart grids to environmental issues such as ﬂoods and temperature monitoring and even crit-
ical applications in healthcare, like insulin controlling or even remote surgery. Although it is
not a very new concept and machine-to-machine communication has been present for some
time, the number of devices connected is increasing exponentially, and so is the number of
solutions emerging to ﬁx the need for this type of communication. One of these technologies
is Low Power Wide Area Networks (LPWAN) and two communication technologies from this
category: LoRaWAN (Long Range Wide-Area Network) and NB-IoT ( Narrow-Band IoT) are
further discussed in this study.
Among all diﬀerent classiﬁcations of the Internet of Things(IoT), two of the most common
categories are Massive IoT and Critical IoT. These represent the great diversity that can be
found in this ﬁeld, as they are have very distinct requirements. Massive IoT consists of use
cases such as smart buildings, logistics tracking, industrial monitoring and agriculture. These
are all characterized by a large amount of connected devices, which require a long battery life
and low cost, but which are not very sensitive to latency. On the opposite end, the critical
IoT is concerned with use cases such as autonomous vehicles and remote health care. This
proves how diﬀerent the requirements can be, but there can be some unifying characteristics
found in all, such as a long range, low power operation and scalability.
This thesis consists of evaluations of the two aforementioned technologies, in terms of coverage.
This is an empirical study, based on ﬁeld tests taken in diﬀerent conditions and presented
comparatively, regarding the placement of the end devices (deep indoor and outdoor) and also
in terms of diﬀerent types of network communications, as NB-IoT operates in the licensed
spectrum and LoRaWAN in the ISM bands (Industrial, Scientiﬁc and Medical). The concept of
this paper emerged to meet the needs of those use cases that require the end devices to be placed
in remote places. For this purpose, parameters such as the Received Signal Strength Indicator
(RSSI) and Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR) are evaluated to determine what is characteristic
to each type of technology. The results of the study showed that both technologies provide
coverage in deep indoor settings, maintaining communication when the signal is attenuated
by up to 40 dBm.

Introduction
As technology evolves, an obvious demand arises - that of interconnecting everyday objects
and having them represented in the form of information, rather than a physical gadget. By
equipping these objects with intelligence and integrating them, there can be a strong increase in
eﬃciency, starting from environment monitoring to health-care and countless other applications.
As more and more objects are integrated, several solutions have emerged to fulﬁll the need for
wireless connectivity and low power consumption. There is a big variety of applications with
diﬀerent requirements. IoT solves a large number of problems and increases eﬃciency, oﬀering
solutions such as goods tracking, climate monitoring, medical monitoring, smoke detectors,
waste management, smart grid, among others - represented in Figure 1. All of these require
diﬀerent performances in terms of battery life, reliability and Quality of Service, coverage,
capacity and cost.
Figure 1: IoT applications and their diﬀerent requirements
Among other factors, some that are deﬁning for the IoT industry are the need for high capacity
(as a number of 12 billion devices will be interconnected by 2020, as estimated by Cisco[2]) as
well as long range coverage for most of the IoT use cases. As several competing technologies
have emerged, the aim is to assess them in comparison to each other and determine to which
extent they fulﬁll the necessities of the desirable coverage needed to support the IoT services.
1.1
Motivation
As a lot of applications consist of end devices located in basements or in the ground, the main
interest is not in the signal levels outdoor, on ground level or in line of sight, but in deep
indoor. When deploying an IoT application, it is necessary to have knowledge of actual values
in terms of battery life, transmit power and signal levels. When implementing a sensor network
in conﬁned places, it is critical to know how it will behave. As there is high attenuation, the
devices transmit with high power, which translates in a high battery consumption. This will
eﬀectively decrease the battery life, which should typically be of up to ten years.
Coverage is a very critical parameter to guide by when choosing to implement an IoT solution.

2
Technologies for IoT - LPWAN
2.1
LPWAN Requirements
Low Power Wide Area Networks (LPWAN) fulﬁll the IoT requirements, some of the charac-
teristics that set them apart from other technologies being long range, low cost and low power
consumption of the end devices and a scalable architecture. Among the features considered in
an IoT application, bitrate does not count, so even though LPWAN does not oﬀer high speeds,
it compensates with a very long range. A comparison between LPWAN and other wireless
technologies, is presented in Figure 2. Each of the wireless technologies satisﬁes particular ap-
plication speciﬁc needs. PAN technology, such as Bluetooth, or Zigbee can also be implemented
for Internet of Things applications, but they have the restraint of very short range, which brings
the need for a personal ﬁxed gateway for connecting to the internet, such as a smart phone,
or a Wi-Fi access point. LPWAN devices are compatible with long range wireless protocols,
reaching distances of even 10 kilometers, depending on the modulation technique used. Cellular
networks, on the other hand, even though they provide long range as well as bitrate, have a
considerable battery consumption, which makes them impractical in the use of massive ma-
chine to machine communication, where the end devices are required to have a long battery life.
Figure 2: A comparison between LPWAN and other wireless solutions based on range versus
bitrate

2.1.1
Long range
After doing research on the diﬀerent implementations of IoT, it was found that in a lot of the
cases most of the devices are indoor, or even in a deep indoor setting. Therefore the necessity
for high coverage is essential, as the signal is highly attenuated and the communication needs
to be maintained without interruptions.
For determining the range of a transmission, the key parameter is the link budget, which gives
information about the propagation of a signal, based on the received power, the transmitted
power, the antenna gains and the losses that occur, such as path loss, receiver and transmitter
loss.
Figure 3: Link budget illustration
The link budget is expressed in the form of an equation as:
ReceivedPower(dBm) = TransmittedPower(dBm) + Gains(dB) −Losses(dB)
A large link budget results in a long range. The size of the area in which a signal can be
received, depends on the sensitivity of the receiver. The more sensitive a receiver is, the weaker
signals it can receive, thus resulting in a higher coverage. LPWAN devices can detect signals
even lower than -130 dBm, unlike other types of technologies, such as 2G or 3G who have a
sensitivity of at most -110 dBm.[7]
Receiver sensitivity varies inversely with the modulation rate. The modulation rate, or symbol
rate is directly proportional with the bitrate:
SymbolRate =
BitRate
BitsPerSymbol
The lack of a high bit rate requirement allows the reduction of the symbol rate, for the purpose
of an increase in the range of the communication. The bit rate is the measurement for the
number of bits transmitted in a unit of time on the communication channel. Not to be confused
with the symbol rate, which represents the number of symbols per unit of time. A symbol
occurs each time a signal changes state. More complex modulation techniques allow for a
larger number of states, meaning that the number of bits per symbol is larger. In Figure 4
there can be seen three types of diﬀerent modulations. BPSK - Binary Phase Shift Keying, is
a simple modulation, where only one bit is transmitted with each symbol. In this case, the
symbol rate is equal to the bit rate. With QPSK - Quadrature Phase Shift Keying, there are
four states, therefore two bits are transmitted with each symbol and the symbol rate is half
of the bit rate. The symbol rate can be further decreased, as can be seen for 8PSK, however,
10
==PAGE:13==
having more states makes the transmission more susceptible to interference and errors caused
by noise. [13]
Figure 4: The constellation diagrams of three types of Phase Shift Keying modulation
The signal bandwidth of a channel depends on the symbol rate. Long range can be achieved
by employing the right kind of modulation technique, because with a larger number of bits per
symbol, at the same bit rate, the symbol rate decreases and therefore an equivalent amount
of data can be sent over a narrower bandwidth. With the proper modulation technique, a
range of tens of kilometers can be reached. Two of the modulation standards employed by
the technologies that will be further detailed in this paper are Ultra Narrow Band Modulation
- UNB and Spread Spectrum Modulation - SSM. The physical layer communication link of
NB-IoT is supported by UNB, and for LoRa by SSM.
Most commonly, in LPWAN (with a few exceptions which are not the focus of this paper) the
Sub-1 GHz band is used, which provides good propagation characteristics that lead to longer
range and lower power consumption. This is due to the fact that signals with a lower frequency
have less attenuation and multipath fading introduced by dense obstacles. Another reason is
that the sub-1Ghz band is less congested compared to higher frequency bands, such as the 2.4
GHz band. By being less congested, the network is more reliable, and therefore the signal can
reach further, providing a higher coverage. [17]
2.1.2
Low power operation
An indisputable requirement for battery powered User Equipment (UE) is an eﬃcient power
consumption. A long battery lifetime is an aspect that must be taken into consideration
when designing a UE that is a sensor in a deep indoor environment, hard to reach, that
has to perform its function for years on end without accessibility for changing the battery fre-
quently. LPWAN technologies typically provide a lifetime of ten years or more of the end nodes.
The topology of a network, or the shape and structure that deﬁnes the network, inﬂuences the
functionality of the UEs. By connecting the end devices directly to a gateway, a lot is saved
in terms of power, as the UEs do not need to receive and forward packets from the other end
users to the base station. This implies the use of a star topology, instead of a mesh where not
11
==PAGE:14==
all end devices have a one hop path to the gateway.
Figure 5: Mesh topology vs. Star topology
When the structure of a network consists of a massive number of devices gathering data from
the real world and centralizing the information in a cloud, via a gateway, the obvious choice is
a star topology. While a mesh topology provides a higher reliability and higher speed, a star
topology is still a better alternative for an IoT architecture, as the faulty devices are easier to
detect and replace and it is easier to expand. The main problems with mesh topologies are
higher battery utilization and security, since traﬃc from the neighbour nodes is passed through
each node. The data transmitted by IoT end devices is in the form of small packets, with a
small payload (for example 255 bytes for LoRa [24]), which are not transmitted continuously,
so there is no need for high protection against congestion, which can often occur in star networks.
A further aspect that is enabled by star topologies is oﬄoading the complexity from the end
devices. Since only the UEs are the ones constrained by battery power, oﬄoading the complex
and power consuming tasks to the base station is another opportunity to conserve energy in
the UE and increase battery life.
A concept eﬀective in diminishing the power consumption is duty cycling. A duty cycle is one
fraction of the time when a system is active. By turning oﬀthe power consuming components
such as the data transceiver when not transmitting (which is the majority of time in case
of IoT UEs), a lot of power is saved. Depending on the type of service and the use case, if
a sensor only needs to transmit data when a certain threshold is exceeded, it only needs to
keep awake the component that monitors the level of the measured parameter, and only when
it is over the threshold, the transceiver is activated and the information is sent. Of course,
this example is only valid for a case where the application is designed such that only uplink
transmissions are required. If the base station needs to transmit in downlink as well to the end
device certain commands for example, a mutual agreement on a listening schedule needs to
be put in place between the two ends. This way, it is assured that the end device is listening
when the downlink transmission takes place. For some devices, this is done by having a short
period of time to listen and wait for a reply after having transmitted uplink. The issue can
also be solved by establishing scheduled times for the waking up, according to the listening
schedule.[17] This will further be detailed in Chapter 2.4, as the LoRaWAN protocol deﬁnes

multiple classes of devices that act diﬀerently, depending on the need of the operation.
2.1.3
Scalability
When dealing with a massive number of devices, it is imperative to be able to add or remove
a device without aﬀecting the functionality of the network. As it has been forecast by several
organizations such as Cisco and Ericsson, the number of interconnected devices is expected
to grow exponentially in the near future. Therefore, it is essential that the networks are
expandable, to support the large amount of devices. Managing the increase in the density
of the devices is done by deploying additional base stations in dense areas with a multitude
of devices. Capacity is improved by enabling the base stations and the back end systems to
transmit and receive in parallel to multiple devices. As it is expected, the scalability factor is
settled within the base station, not in the low complexity end devices.
2.1.4
Low cost
Due to the star-type connectivity, the complexity is held in the base station, which results in
a reduced end device complexity, and therefore a low cost.
Having a network infrastructure where each gateway connects thousands of devices spread on
a large area substantially reduces the costs of deploying such a network, for the operators.
Open source or proprietary technologies such as LoRaWAN and Sigfox further lower the ex-
penses by making use of the Industrial Scientiﬁc and Medical (ISM) bands. Being deployed in
the license exempt bands removes the need to pay for the use of the licensed spectrum.

2.2
General Telecommunication Concepts
2.2.1
Received Signal Strength Indicator - RSSI
One of the most utilized measures of the quality of a signal in wireless networking is the
Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI). This parameter is of interest when evaluating
the range of a device and is commonly expressed in dBm (which means decibels relative to a
milliwatt). The RSSI represents the signal power received by an end device and it fades at
larger distance from the signal source. It is a measure of the relation between the transmitted
power and the received power.
2.2.2
Propagation loss
The signal propagation is determined by several components such as the path loss (which
is the decay of signals with distance) and ﬂuctuations caused by obstructions between the
transmitter and receiver. Free space loss is also accountable for the degradation of signals. The
value of the RSSI is inﬂuenced by a number of factors, such as reﬂection, refraction, diﬀraction,
objects positioned between the transmitter and receiver, interference from other signals on the
frequency band, type of antenna among others. [4]
2.2.3
Signal to Noise Ratio - SNR
Another important parameter of the quality of a transmission is the Signal to Noise Ratio. As
any physical path between a transmitter and a receiver encounters interference, it is essential
to evaluate the ratio between the useful information relative to the unwanted noise. A system
has a required SNR, representing the measure of noise that can be tolerated and still provide
a certain eﬃciency. As the RSSI, the Signal to Noise Ratio is also expected to decrease with
distance from the source. The SNR is calculated as the diﬀerence (in decibels) between the
received signal and the noise level.
15
==PAGE:18==
2.2.4
Coverage measurement
When deﬁning the coverage of a technology, it is speciﬁed by the lowest signal level where the
ability to reach a certain throughput target is achieved. The technologies evaluated in this
paper reach bit rate of up to 250 kbps - NB-IoT [8] and 50 kbps - LoRaWAN [24].
A metric used in deﬁning coverage is the Maximum Coupling Loss (MCL), which represents
the highest amount of loss in the power level that can occur and still have an operable system,
calculated as the diﬀerence between the transmitted and received conducted power levels. [8]
When the loss is higher than the MCL, the coverage is lost. Providing the coverage results in
a distance dimension, such as meters or kilometers is not suitable, as there are several types
of environments with diﬀerent properties that inﬂuence the physical distance. Another factor
that inﬂuences this is the frequency at which the technology operates, as lower frequencies
experience less attenuation than the high frequencies. For these reasons, the MCL is a good
choice of measuring coverage. 3GPP’s Release 14 deﬁnes a requirement of 164 dB MCL for NB-
IoT, which is an improvement of 20 dB from GPRS, at a data rate of at least 160 bps. LoRa has
a MCL of 154 dB in uplink and 152 dB in downlink. [3] For coverage measurements presented
in the form of distance, the environment needs to be speciﬁed and explained thoroughly, for it
to make sense.
2.3
Narrow Band Internet of Things (NB-IoT)
2.3.1
Design Principles
Narrow Band IoT is a cellular technology which operates in the licensed spectrum. NB-IoT
is a Long Term Evolution (LTE) feature introduced in 3GPP’s Release 13, designed for IoT
radio access. The aim was to have a technology than can support high capacity, low device
complexity and low power consumption (and therefore, long battery life of the devices) and
high coverage. Some of the requirements speciﬁed are to have a coverage of 20 dB more than
GPRS, a battery life for the UE of at least 10 years (at a battery capacity of 5 Wh) and a
capacity of 52K devices/channel/cell [16].
A low device complexity is achieved by having the baseband processing reduced, not only
during initial cell selection, but also during connection. When the device performs the initial
cell selection, it only needs one synchronization sequence, to obtain time and frequency syn-
chronization to the network. During connection, the DL transport block size is restricted to
maximum 680 bits and channel coding is also simpliﬁed. The lack of Multiple Input Multiple
Output and only half duplex functionality are some other methods that provide means for low
device complexity [8].
A long battery life is vital, as a big part of the mobile devices in IoT are sensors in remote
places. This is done by taking advantage of the fact that in IoT implementations, most of
the time the devices do not have an active data session. In R13, two power saving states
are introduced: extended Discontinuous Reception (eDRX) and Power Saving Mode (PSM).
When the device shifts from idle mode to active, a context is kept in the device and the network
as well, to reduce signalling and therefore optimizing power consumption [8].
16
==PAGE:19==
Coverage enhancement is achieved through a compromise, meaning that data rate is traded
oﬀfor high coverage, by employing repetitions. Compared to GPRS, NB-IoT oﬀers an im-
proved coverage of 20 dB corresponding to a Maximum Coupling Loss (MCL) of 164 dB while
supporting a data rate of 160 bps at the application layer. [15]
Flexibility of deployment is another key aspect, carried out with three possible modes
of functioning: as a stand-alone mode of operation (a GSM carrier - or more is refarmed),
in-band operation (by using one or more Physical Resource Blocks (PRBs) from the existing
LTE network), or operation in the guard-band of an LTE carrier.
In the stand alone mode of deployment, one way of operation is to have one or more GSM
carriers replaced by NB-IoT. Therefore, to assist the progress of technology migration, one NB-
IoT carrier has a bandwidth of 200 kHz same as the GSM channel size. In 3GPP’s Technical
Speciﬁcation 36.104 version 13.3.0 Release 13 [25], it is recommended to have a guard-band
of 200 kHz between the GSM and NB-IoT carriers. However, if there is the same operator
deploying the two technologies, in 3GPP’s Cellular System Support for Ultra Low Complexity
and Low Throughput Internet of Things [3] it is advised to have a 100 kHz guard band on each
side of the NB-IoT carrier, as is shown in Figure 9.
Figure 9: NB-IoT stand-alone deployment [8]
For in band deployment, the operator allocates an LTE Physical Resource Block (PRB) for
NB-IoT, as can be seen illustrated in Figure 10. This can be accomplished because NB-IoT
is part of the 3GPP standardization Release 13 and shares a lot of features with LTE. An
NB-IoT carrier has a 180 kHz bandwidth, which corresponds to one PRB in LTE. A PRB is
the smallest chunk of data transmitted and it is comprised of 12 subcarriers of 15 kHz each
along a timeslot of one millisecond divided into 14 symbols.

To avoid interfering with the LTE transmission, no NB-IoT physical channels are mapped onto
the essential LTE control channels and signals, such as the Synchronization and Reference
signals and the Physical Downlink Control Channel. The synchronization signal, in the form
of the Primary Synchronization Signal - PBS and Secondary Synchronization Signal - SSS are
critical in the initial cell search process. The reference signal, Cell-speciﬁc Reference Signal
- CRS is what assists the UE in diﬀerentiating between the cells. The Physical Downlink
Control Channel is used to inform each UE of the resources scheduled to them in the shared
channel and what resources are assigned to them in the uplink. [18]
In downlink, in NB-IoT the form of multiplexing used is Orthogonal Frequency Division
Multiple Access - OFDMA. This involves a large number of orthogonal subcarriers, of 15 kHz
each. Since NB-IoT uses a single PRB of 180kHz, this constitutes 12 subcarriers in a subframe
of 1 ms. This requires scheduling in frequency, as well as time.
In uplink, the multiplexing is done rather diﬀerently, by the use of Single Carrier Frequency
Division Multiple Access - SC-FDMA. The reason for a diﬀerent technique is the power con-
sumption, as the end device can not manage the same power consumption as the base station.
Therefore, as the name states - the 15kHz subcarriers are combined into a single carrier. In
UL, the carriers can also be 3.75kHz, with a 2ms time slot.
The modulation technique adopted is QPSK in both UL and DL, with the possibility of BPSK
as well, only in DL. Release 13 deﬁnes, based on the LTE bands, 14 frequency bands for
NB-IoT(Table 1).
As stated in the 3GPP standardization, the maximum transmit power in uplink is of 23dBm,
and in downlink of 46 dBm. This is, clearly, in order to limit the UE power consumption.

2.4
LoRaWAN
Unlike the technology previously presented, LoRaWAN operates in the unlicensed spectrum
and is an open technology, established by the Lora Alliance [10], the cost emerging only from
hardware setup, network establishment and management. LoRaWAN is a layer 2 communica-
tion protocol that runs on top of the LoRa modulation scheme at the physical layer, technology
owned and patented by a company named Semtech[21].
The protocol stack is simpler compared to the OSI model, as not all 7 layers need to be
implemented. All is based on the LoRa physical, which encodes the raw bits using the Chirp
Spread Spectrum modulation. On top of this, the data is delivered by LoRaWAN, a MAC layer
protocol which handles point-to-point connections. On top of this, there is the application
layer, which is up to the user to deﬁne.

2.4.1
Physical Layer
One of the most signiﬁcant disadvantages of operating in the license exempt spectrum - Indus-
trial Scientiﬁc and Medical bands (ISM) is the duty cycle. As it is required to be shared with
the other technologies in these radio bands, there is the need for restrictions in the power level
and the duty cycle - the amount of time a device is allowed to transmit. These regulations
are set in place for reducing the interference created and diﬀer for several frequencies - Figure
15. In Europe, LoRaWAN operates in 863-870 MHz band, where it can have a restriction of
1% or even 0.1% or in the 433 MHz band, where the restriction is of 10%. The LoRa Alliance
speciﬁes three channels that must be supported by all devices implementing LoRaWAN: 868.10
MHz, 868.30 MHZ and 868.50 MHz. A maximum Eﬀective Radiated Power (ERP) of 14 is
also speciﬁed in the technical documentation for regional parameters from the LoRa Alliance,
as well as a maximum payload size of 250 bytes [9].
Figure 15: European duty cycle restrictions in the ISM bands
As LoRa is closed source, there is a limited amount of data available about the physical
layer, mostly from experiments where researchers have reverse-engineered it. The modulation
technique utilized is Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS), a way of encoding information by linearly
increasing or decreasing frequency of modulated pulses over time[20]. This technique allows
the eﬃcient decoding of signals with a power of 19.5 dB below the noise ﬂoor, which results
in a long distance transmission [5].

Network Architecture
The architecture of LoRaWAN (Figure 16) consists of a massive number of end devices, con-
nected to a gateway, which forwards the packets to a network server through a standard IP
connection. The topology of the network is a star-of-stars, as several gateway nodes relay all
information from the end nodes to the central network server.

• class B that have additional scheduling windows that allow downlink communication

2.5
Summary
LPWAN technologies are characterized by the requirements of long range, low power consump-
tion, scalability and low cost. Two technologies that fulﬁll these requirements are LoRaWAN
and Narrow-Band IoT. LoRaWAN operates in the unlicensed spectrum and is an open source
layer 2 protocol which runs on top of LoRa, a proprietary physical layer. NB-IoT is a cellular
technology, operating in the licensed spectrum, deployed and maintained by mobile operators.

Related work
Numerous studies have been carried in the past few years regarding the capabilities of the
new emerging IoT technologies, individually as well as comparatively. Even though several of
the papers that will be mentioned cover GPRS and Sigfox, since the purpose of this thesis is
LoRaWAN and NB-IoT, only these will be discussed further.
One comparative study is concerning the coverage and capacity of four diﬀerent technologies
that enable IoT: Coverage and Capacity Analysis of Sigfox, LoRa, GPRS, and NB-IoT [29].
This was carried out in the Northern part of Denmark, known as North Jutland, on a surface
of 8000 km2. The analysis was performed in two types of areas: urban (1648people/km2) and
rural (44people/km2), using the sub 1 GHz network of the cellular operator Telenor. All users
were assumed to be outdoor, and when considering indoor coverage, a penetration loss between
10-30 dB was added to the calculations. The results were that outdoor coverage was provided
with a 99% probability for all technologies. When considering an added 20 dB penetration loss
(simulating indoor users), the coverage probability dropped slightly for LoRa, at 97%, while
remaining the same for NB-IoT. In the case of deep indoor, with an added 30 dB loss, LoRA
and NB-IoT covered 76% and 90% of the users, respectively. The conclusion of the paper
is that NB-IoT triumphs over the other technologies analyzed, having the best coverage and
capacity, at the expense of the longest time on air.
A similar paper is Coverage comparison of GPRS, NB-IoT, LoRa, and SigFox in a 7800 km2
area [6], where simulations are performed in order to determine which of the aforementioned
technologies provides the highest coverage. The model was designed in a MatLab simulator,
based on two types of regions: rural and urban, and varying several parameters such as antenna
gain, path loss, number of sites and sectors and adding indoor loss of 10, 20 or 30 dB. The
results presented showed that all the technologies tested provided full coverage outdoor and
indoor in urban areas (with the exception that GPRS had a 3% outage indoor, with the added
20 dB penetration loss). In rural areas however, even though the outdoor coverage is almost the
same as in the urban area, the indoor makes the diﬀerence. With the added 20 dB penetration
loss, the outage for the technologies is about 3%. In deep indoor (simulated by an added 30
dB penetration loss), NB-IoT proved to oﬀer 90% coverage, better than LoRa, which had 76%.

high SNR and they vary almost linearly, but with a steeper slope in the outdoor, where there
is less attenuation and therefore a higher signal value related to noise.

Ping Response Time
Another measure of coverage is the time elapsed that it takes a packet to reach a destination and
echo back to the transmitter. With the Sodaq Sara N211, there is the possibility to measure
the roundtrip time, with the command AT+NPING, which replies with three parameters, the
number of packets sent before receiving a response, the time to live in the response packet
and the round trip time, in milliseconds. An 8 byte packet was sent to Google’s DNS server

In the outdoor measurements the response time was approximately constant, around the value
of 500 ms, with no observable tendency of increasing with distance from the base station. In

taking place. This can be one of the explanations why the ping response time is so high in the

LoRaWAN
4.2.1
Methodology
Similar to the Narrow Band IoT results, the following presented outcomes are based on
experimental measurements. Knowing the location of the LoRa gateway, the route was planned
around it, restricted only by the deployment of tunnels which oﬀered the ground for deep indoor
tests. A range as large as possible of values for the distance between the end device and the
gateway was also the scope for the trail designed. The positions for measuring are placed on a
range of 0-400 meters from the gateway. For increasing the accuracy, 10 measurements were
taken in each of the 45 positions, with a delay of 4 seconds between them. This time delay was
determined by trial and error, gradually increasing, starting at 200 ms. This was inﬂuenced
by the time on air and the processing time of transmitting the packets. Even though the need
for accuracy is as important as NB-IoT, there were only 10 measurements taken on each point
(as opposed to 50 for NB-IoT) due to attempting not to violate the duty cycle restrictions to
a great extent. The parameters of interest evaluated are the Signal to Noise Ratio - SNR and
the Received Signal Strength Indicator - RSSI.
The end device used was a PICtail Daughter Board with the Microchip RN2483 LoRa trans-
ceiver module. As seen in Figure 34 a. the development board has the possibility of connecting
two diﬀerent types of antennas, 433 MHz or 868 MHz. For these experiments the 868 MHz
antenna was connected. The RN2483 module carries a LoRaWAN class A protocol stack [11].
The gateway that relayed the packets from the end device was a Kerlink iBTS (Figure 34 b.).
37
==PAGE:40==
Figure 34: PICtail development board and Gateway
The end device was reached by a serial USB connection, where the commands were sent via
the QCOM serial port terminal application. In compliance with the LoRaWAN addressing
scheme, the device has a hard coded 64 bits unique identiﬁer - DevEUI that is used to register
the device on the application platform of choice - Teracom in this case [26]. By registering the
hardware identiﬁer on the application platform, the DevAddr, NwksSKey and AppSKey are
generated and loaded to the device, which subsequently allow it to join the network and send
data.
A one byte packet was sent every 4 seconds, and the data could be seen in real time on
the Teracom website[26], consisting of information such as the RSSI, SNR, the time of the
transmission, the frequency, the data rate and the payload - Figure 35. The frequency recorded
is either 868.1 MHz, 868.3 MHz, or 868.5 MHz, which are the default channels that need to be
implemented in all LoRaWAN devices. In order to obtain a maximum range, the spreading
factor was set to 12, which is the maximum one, but which has the lowest bit rate. The data
was downloaded from the website for further processing.

Summary
The techniques used in both cases were very similar. Typically when using a LoRaWAN

5
Discussion: NB-IoT and LoRaWAN Results Com-
parison
The measurements regarding LoRaWAN were taken on a shorter distance than NB-IoT, of up
to 300m compared to 600m. This is due to the attenuation being too high, which made it more
diﬃcult for the device to stay attached to the network. As the results shown in the previous
chapter, the SNR is much higher when it comes to NB-IoT. The diﬀerence is noticeable when
examining the results at the same distance between the end device and the gateway. The
maximum distance where measurements were performed for LoRaWAN is around 300 meters.
The SNR is in the range of -5 dB for most of the measurement points, with some values even
reaching -12 dB in the indoor and around 5dB outdoor. On the other hand, when looking at
NB-IoT, the SNR at the same distance is much higher, between 20 dBm and 30 dBm when
measured outside and in the range of 10-20 dBm in the tunnels.
Regarding the RSSI, the same behaviour is observed, with the signal reaching -90 dBm outdoor
and -120 dBm indoor for the LoRaWAN technology. In Narrow Band IoT the RSSI is recorded
around -60 dBm outdoor and about -110 dBm in deep indoor.
The extended coverage provided by NB-IoT is a consequence of several factors. First of all, the
ability to transmit with a power of up to 23 dBm, adaptable to the radio conditions, is a great
advantage compared to the maximum power of 14 dBm provided by LoRaWAN. The lack of
more power makes it hard for the signal to penetrate the walls, ground and other materials.
On the other hand, this means an increased battery life for LoRa end devices. Another factor
is the licensed spectrum, which is more regulated than the license-exempt frequency bands,
where there is a lot of interference. This is one of the main aspects why the SNR is quite low
for LoRaWAN.
The two technologies are expected to have dissimilar results, as one of them is a cellular IoT
technology, with base stations deployed by the operators, whereas the other is an open source
technology, with reasonably cheap equipment so that anyone who wants to implement a sensor
network to monitor some parameters can deploy their own gateways.
Another aspect worth considering is the accuracy of the measurements. Even though there
were 50 measurements taken in each position for NB-IoT, and only 10 for LoRaWAN, the
latter yielded more accurate values, in terms of the variance of SNR and RSSI. The diﬀerence
could be caused by the type of antenna of the end devices, which had diﬀerent designs (Figure
23 b. vs Figure 34 a.).

==PAGE:47==
6
Conclusion
The purpose of this report was to provide information about two types of LPWAN technologies
in terms of coverage. By covering areas of up to 600 meters between the end device and the
base station for NB-IoT and 300 meters for LoRaWAN, an overview was provided of how the
signal propagates on this range of distances. Furthermore, tests were conducted in two distinct
types of environments, deep indoor and outdoor. Analyzing the signal under these conditions
provided a good insight into the performance ability of the technologies.
As the measurements were taken in a real life scenario, with buildings and other objects
obstructing the signal and causing reﬂection and refraction, the results are beneﬁcial both for
users in the need to deploy their own IoT application, as well as operators looking to improve
their network. The ability to measure under ground on such a large area is very valuable, as it
provides a good model of how the technologies perform and of their challenges and limitations.
As IoT requirements are largely application speciﬁc, there is no right answer when choosing
the type of technology to implement. Knowledge of the coverage capabilities of a technology
is necessary, whether it is a critical IoT use case that requires high Quality of Service and low
latency, or a massive IoT platform that has the need for high capacity and low cost of end
devices.
Both technologies have proven to perform well under severe attenuation, although for LoR-
aWAN the SNR and RSSI values have turned out lower than NB-IoT. However, this comes
with the price of high battery consumption. If there is a need for a cheap system with a high
number of devices, LoRaWAN could perform as well as NB-IoT, but probably more than one
gateway would be necessary. Judging by the results, NB-IoT is more reliable, as the signal
reaches further and has higher RSSI and SNR.
6.1
Future work
As this study was mainly empirical, based on ﬁeld measurements, a further improvement can
consist of more in depth evaluations of theoretical propagation loss, to have an expectation
of how the signals should be transmitted and also consider more parameters. More precise
devices would also be a good enhancement of the experimental setup. The SNR and RSSI
measured had quite big variances, especially in NB-IoT and this can be improved by using
devices that provide a higher accuracy. The range on which the measurements were taken
can also be extended and for LoRaWAN it would be interesting to see how the results are
improved if more than one gateway is within the range of the end device.

deﬁne the Packet Data Protocol(PDP) context. The module is then set to full functionality:
