Abstract
The proper control strategy is of great importance for either heating system or photovoltaic­
battery (PV­B) system interconnecting with the building. Therefore, it is necessary to
identify and evaluate the characteristics of different control strategies for engineering ap­
plication. In this study, three control strategies including, basic, price­based and model
predictive control are compared comprehensively for two case study buildings separately.
Several important performance aspects, including thermal comfort, techno­economic per­
formance and battery charge/discharge process are analyzed and compared. The results
show that the model predictive control has grater flexibility than the price­based control
to adapt to time­varying electricity prices, and has the advantage of achieving better eco­
nomic performance as well as ensuring uniform temperatures over a period of time with
large differences in electricity prices.

The introduction consists of 4 subsections. The first subsection describes the current state
of research, existing problems, solutions, and related concepts and terminology which is
helpful for understanding the problems. The second and third subsection presents the
literature review on control strategies and the objectives of this study, respectively. The
last subsection introduces the structure of this thesis.
1.1
Background and motivation
As the fight against climate change and global warming is becoming more and more ur­
gent, drastic changes are needed in our energy systems. The goal of decarbonization
of the market requires that more Renewable Energy Sources (RES) are integrated in the
global energy mix. Many nations have understood the emergency and the challenges
posed by these requirements and have fixed quantifiable targets to be achieved in the
coming years. For instance, Denmark claims to reach a share of 50% of their final energy
produced by RES by 2030, and 100% by 2050 [1]. As a result of these ambitions, the
sector of renewable energies has considerably grown over the last years. The stability of
energy networks is threatened by the increasing share of RES as well as their variable
and unpredictable characteristics. Therefore, the traditional vision where the “production
follows the demand” needs a paradigm shift towards a concept where the “demand follows
the production” [2]. To deal with the disadvantages associated with renewable energy, de­
mand side management (DSM) contributes a group of solutions, which has concentrated
a large amount of research [3].
DSM in electricity grid is a well­known concept, however, only focus on electricity system
is inefficient, as district heating system has been shown to have a significant potential
to support the integration of fluctuating renewable energy sources into power systems
by offering cost effective flexibility [4]. This is due to the characteristics of the thermal
energy systems that have both greater inertia than power systems and are significantly
less sensitive to the mismatch between the demand and supply. Lund et al. [5] defined
the 4th generation district heating system(4GDH) that can utilize heat from central indus­
trial waste heat and local solar collectors. The 4GDH also equipped with thermal energy
storage (TES) that can be found either centralized through a borehole thermal energy
storage (BTES) or distributed in the buildings themselves and the water tanks. The dif­
ferent clusters of building are all connected to the backbone, which connects them to the
central units. The sources of flexibility in thermal networks are the heat (or cold) of the
heat (or cold) carrier fluid, thermal storage devices (e.g. heat storage tanks) and ther­
mal inertia of the buildings connected to the system [6]. In the present work, attention is
drawn to DSM applied to buildings and their embedded systems, heat pumps in particu­
lar. This is because the current electrification of dwellings. This trend has notably been
pushed by governmental decisions: for instance, the recast of the European Performance
of Buildings Directive [7] obliges its member states to only build nZEBs from 2020. Sartori
et al. [8] proposed a framework definition of nZEB in 2012. Its basic principles consist
in reducing the energy use of the building while providing the remaining with renewable
sources, aiming to achieve an annual zero energy balance. For all these reasons, build­
ings equipped with heat pumps are deemed worthy of further investigations with regards
to their potential for energy flexibility. They can become active elements in the energy
grids, providing flexibility services when needed [9]. In order to unlock the flexibility of
buildings, advanced control strategies are studied and implemented.

1.2
Literature review
The control strategies reviewed have been classified in two distinct categories: price­
based controls (PBC) and Model Predictive Control (MPC), because of their conceptual
difference. Price­based controls are simple heuristic methods which generally have the
form “if (condition is verified), then (action is triggered)”. PBC relies on the monitoring of a
specific “trigger” parameter of electricity price on which a threshold value has been fixed.
When the threshold is reached, the operation of the heat pump is changed, according to
the predefined strategy. On the other hand, Model Predictive Control is a more complex
strategy, which relies on a model of the building to project its behavior in the future. MPC is
an optimization problem, therefore it intends to find the best solution for the management
of the heat pump operation, over a certain time horizon and within certain constraints.
MPC is an ideal framework to use weather and price predictions in order to make use of
the thermal storage of a building appropriately [10].
The price­based control strategies rely on the variations of energy price in time with the
objective of reducing the energy costs for the end­users. Schibuola et al. [11] propose two
different approaches: the first one analyzes the price data of two entire years (2012 and
2013), and fixes thresholds based on this distribution. The second approach compares
the current electricity price with the forecasted price over the next 12 h, hence relying on
prediction data rather than on past data. Both methods enabled to reduce the yearly elec­
tricity costs of around 15%. Le Dréau and Heiselberg [12] also based their approach on
recorded past data: their thresholds were calculated using the first and the third quartiles
from the price distribution of the two weeks prior to the current moment. Changes of ±2 K
in the heating setpoints were then implemented when the current electricity price reached
the thresholds. A high flexibility was thus obtained and the cost savings ranged from 3
to 10%. This type of price­based control is bound to grow in the coming years with the
increasing development of smart­grids, where dynamic pricing can easily be sent to the
end users. The performance of the controls then highly depends on the processing of this
price data and the definition of the threshold parameters.
The most common objective of MPC is to reduce monetary costs, a strategy known as
Economic MPC (EMPC) as shown in Fig.1.1. Ref. [13] use EMPC precisely to compare

1.3
Research objectives
This thesis presents different types of models for implementing and comparing different
control strategies; the focus is on designing, validating and tunning the model predictive
control for heating and PV­B systems. The research goals of this thesis can be sum­
marised as follows:
• Exploring how to develop a simplified grey­box model. Low order model that can
be identified from the fundamental principles of energy dynamics in buildings. The
RC parameters of these types of models should be easily calculated based on the
thermal properties data of the building, then calibrated with data generated from the
white­box simulator, and without using extensive data and information to develop
the statistical models.
• Step­by­step implementation of MPC: from developing the plant and plant model,
designing the MPC algorithm (objective function and constraints), coding and de­
bugging as well as tunning the MPC design parameters to produce better results.
• Compare different scenarios, gain insight into the MPC logic and how it works, and
recognize the advantages and disadvantages of different control strategies from the
results.
It is hoped that this thesis could be a good learning record for the author, easy to recall
in the future when the details of MPC are not clear as well as being a basis for future
work. It is also hoped that this thesis could give some great tips and inspiration for MPC
beginners who have less knowledge of this subject.

2.1
Modelling
2.1.1
White­box model
In this study, a detailed building thermal dynamic and heating system model is developed
in Modelica language. The case study building is simplified as a single zone. A zone is
assumed to have perfectly homogeneous air and is modelled using the MixedAir compo­
nent from the Modelica Buildings library. Data generated from the building model will be
used to identify its grey­box model. Then, outputs of the model predictive controller will
be applied to the building model.
• Modelica & Dymola
Since the model should be easily readable, usable and extendible, the object­oriented
equation­based programming language Modelica [15] has been chosen. Modelica also
enables multi­domain, textual and graphical modelling. It is domain neutral and allows for
modelling of systems containing subcomponents from different domains, including their
control. By using Modelica instead of imperative languages, the complication of extracting
model equations used for some optimization methods will be overcome. The model was
compiled and simulated with Dymola which is a commercial Modelica simulation environ­
ment. The solver used in the study was the standard DASSL solver included in Dymola.
• Buildings library
All the components used for modelling are from the Buildings library [16], a free open­
source, well­established library that is implemented in Modelica. This library has been

2.1.2
Grey­box model
One of the critical targets in developing a grey­box model for an MPC applications is to
identify a suitable model which is consistent with the physical response of the system.
At the same time, the complexity of the model should be able to embed the information
contained in the data, which means that the model should neither be under­fitted nor
over­fitted. In buildings, grey­box models commonly use the Resistance­Capacitance (R­
C) network analogy with an electric circuitry to describe the thermal dynamics of a system
(thermal delays caused by a building envelope and internal thermal mass effects). Al­
though there have been many improvements in the RC model over the years, the three
resistances and two capacitances (3R2C) arrangement is widely used for modeling tran­
sient heat transfer in building envelopes [17].
RC Model structure
Fig.2.2 shows the three most common model structures. In the first order model the
entire thermal mass of the building is lumped to a single capacity Call and no distinction is
made between the external structural mass (envelope) and the internal mass (including
internal structural mass and indoor air mass). The 2nd order model takes into account
this difference by including a second capacity (Cex and Cin). The 3rd order model instead,
has 3 different capacities for the envelope (Cenv), the internal walls (Cmass) and the indoor
air (Cair).

For these models the inputs are: the ambient temperature (Tamb), solar gains (Qs), inter­

Mathematical formulation
An illustration of a simple building unit and its heat flows are shown in Fig.2.3. The three
common forms of heat transfer in buildings are conduction, convection and radiation.
Conduction is the transfer of energy between objects that are in physical contact or within
one solid object that has uneven temperatures transmission. Convection is the transfer
of energy between objects that are in physical contact or within one solid object that has
uneven temperatures transmission, including natural or forced convection. Radiation is
heat transfer by thermal radiation which is carried by electromagnetic waves between
surfaces. Radiation does not need a medium and can occur in vacuum.
Figure 2.3: Heat transfer between building and outside environment
The calculation of grey­box model parameters (R) in this work mainly focus on conduction
and convection, ignoring the effect of radiation.

X (k + 1) = AdX (k) + BdU (k) + EdV (k)

2.1.3
Black­box model
Black­box models are developed by fitting parameters of a model to historical behavior of
the system and do not require full knowledge of the system or the process. The black­box
approach considers the system as a box with inputs and outputs and depends crucially
on the availability of appropriate input data sets. It becomes particularly suitable for pre­
dicting the behavior of processes where a priori deterministic knowledge of the physical
relationship between input and output is not univocally defined (e.g. evolution of climate
disturbances and occupant behavior related disturbances).
2.1.4
System identification and parameter estimation
System identification and parameter estimation are closely related concepts in the field of
modeling and analysis of dynamic systems.
System identification refers to the process of constructing mathematical models that ac­
curately represent the behavior of a real­world system based on measured input­output
data. The goal is to determine the underlying structure and dynamics of the system, in­
cluding its transfer functions, state­space representation, or other mathematical models.
The approach used here relies on state­space representation (for building) and mathe­
matical model (for battery).
Parameter estimation, on the other hand, focuses on determining the values of unknown
parameters within a given system model. These parameters represent the system’s char­
acteristics or properties that influence its behavior. By estimating these parameters, one
can obtain a more accurate representation of the system and improve the model’s pre­
dictive capabilities.

Common characteristics
Control inputs
The control strategies act upon certain parameters, which are called control inputs (or

identified:
• Temperature set­points

• Power of the heat pump
This variable can be binary, which means the heat pump is completely switched on/off

• Power measurements
When applicable, the energy consumption of the building can be monitored, through a

inverter and the battery inverter.
• Outside conditions
When weather compensation is utilized (see Section 3.2.1), a probe needs to measure
the outside temperature to adapt the system supply temperature consequently.
2.2.2
Basic control
This section presents classic control for the heating system and battery separately with
the aim of providing a reference (baseline operation) for Price­based and model predictive
control.
Heating system
Frederiksen and Werner [19] describe classic control of thermal networks, by splitting the
control up into four parts: differential pressure control, supply temperature control, heat
demand control and flow control. No flexibility is intended at this stage, only the users’
needs and the outside conditions are taken into account, that is why such strategies are
normally served as a reference.
Differential pressure control ensures the pressure difference between the supply and
return pipe is sufficient to ensure adequate mass flow in the periphery of the network. This
control is managed by the circulating pump in the thermal network.
Supply temperature control ensures the supply temperature in the network is sufficiently
high by injecting the correct amount of heat. The supply temperature is determined by
the use of curves that describe the relation between the outdoor temperature and the
supply temperature. This set­point temperature, combined with the mass flow and the
return temperature of the network identifies the heat or cold that should be injected into
the network.

Heat demand control ensures the comfort demands of the customers are met. This
control can be found in the each of connected buildings where it is used to meet both the
space heating and DHW demand.
Flow control ensures the valves in a substation are opened such that the mass flow rate
is sufficiently large to deliver the demanded heat or cold to the substation.

2.2.4
Model predictive control
In a control problem, the goal of the controller is to calculate the input to the plant such
that the plant output follows a desired objective. As shown in Fig.2.6, model predictive
control (MPC) is a control algorithm that optimizes a sequence of manipulated variables
(control inputs) adjustments over a prediction horizon by utilizing a plant (process) model
to optimize forecasts of process behavior based on a linear or quadratic objective, which is
subject to quality or inequality constraints. In MPC, the optimization is preformed repeat­
edly on­line. The optimizer inside the MPC controller finds the sequence of future control
inputs that will minimize the objective function over the receding horizon, taking into ac­
count the constraints, disturbances, and behavior of the model. Only the first control input
is then applied to the real system.
Figure 2.6: Schematic of general MPC control algorithm
The plant is implemented in Modelica, then exported as FMU file and co­simulation with
MPC algorithm in MATLAB (YALMIP [22] optimization environment and GUROBI solver).
Fig.2.7 illustrates one of the simplest MPC Simulink models, and all MPC Simulink models
developed in this study are similar in structure to this simplest model. All optimization­
related equations (objective, grey­box model, constraints) are implemented in Matlab
functions and inserted into Simulink block ’Interpreted MATLAB Fcn’. Here a continu­
ous state space model is used to represent the plant, but in reality the plant is presented
by the FMU file exported from Modelica.
16

==PAGE:25==
Figure 2.7: Schematic of general MPC control algorithm [23]
A heating system is used here to illustrate how the MPC works. At the current moment
(k), the MPC controller uses the plant model to simulate the zone’s indoor temperature
in the next N time steps if the input power would be changed. N is a measure of how
far ahead MPC looks into the future and is referred to as the Prediction horizon. The
MPC controller needs to find the best predicted temperature which is the closest to the
reference (temperature set­point in this case). So, it simulates multiple future scenarios in
a systematic way where the optimizer comes into the picture. By solving an optimization
problem, the MPC controller tries to minimize the error between the set­points and the
predicted temperature of the zone. It can also minimize the electricity cost for operating
the heat pump while keeping the zone temperature in prespecified intervals.
Figure 2.8: Schematic of the principle of receding horizon. The difference between the left
and the right figure is one­time step. The top and bottom figures depict predicted outputs
and control inputs respectively (Prediction horizon N=4).
The design and implementation of MPC at k moments is divided into 3 steps:
Step 1: Measure the current system state yk known at moment k.
Comparative study of different control strategies for integrated building energy systems
17
==PAGE:26==
Step 2: Optimization is performed based on uk, uk+1...uk+N−1 and the system perfor­
mance is predicted according to the plant model if the input uk is applied at moment k.
Although MPC is digit control, the system performance is continuous as shown in Fig.2.8.
Step 3: At the current moment k, MPC applies only the first step of this optimal sequence
uk to the plant and disregards the rest. Based on the applied input, the plant changes
accordingly. In the next moment k + 1, the controller gets a new measurement of the
system state. The prediction horizon shifts forward by one time step and the controller
repeats the same cycle of calculations to compute the optimal input power for the next
time steps. Because of the forward­moving nature of the prediction horizon, MPC is also
referred to as receding horizon control.
At each time step k, the best sequence of future control inputs is calculated by the opti­
mizer, and only the first one uk is then applied to the actual systems. This is because the
predictive (plant) model is difficult to perfectly describe the real system, and there may be
disturbances in the real system, in reality there is a high probability of deviation from the
prediction.
Design parameters
Choosing proper values for MPC design parameters is important as they affect not only
the controller performance but also the computational complexity of the MPC algorithm
that solves an online optimization problem at each time step.
According to Fig.2.8, the design parameters for MPC typically include the following:
• Current time step (k): The discrete variable k is generally used to refer to the current
moment.
• Sampling time (Ts):The sampling time determines the rate at which the controller
receives measurements from the system and updates the control actions. It can be
also recognized as the time between the current time step k and the next time step
k + 1. It should be chosen based on the system dynamics, control objectives, and
computational capabilities.
• Prediction horizon (N): The prediction horizon defines the length of the future time
horizon over which the control action is optimized. It determines how far into the fu­
ture the controller predicts the system’s response and makes control decisions. The
prediction horizon should be chosen carefully to balance computational complexity
and control performance.
• Control horizon: The number of possible different values the manipulated variables
can take in the future. For example, in Fig.2.8, the prediction horizon is 4 whereas
the control horizon is 2.
• Weights: Weighting factors assign relative importance to different terms in the ob­
jective function or constraints. By adjusting these weights, the controller designer
can prioritize certain control objectives or constraints over others. Proper selec­
tion of weighting factors is crucial to achieving the desired control performance and
behavior.

significant effect on the system response (not within the scope of this study).

building energy system and building energy management:
• Weather disturbances (e.g., ambient temperature, solar radiation, wind velocity,
ground temperature, etc.)
• Occupancy behavior related disturbances (e.g., occupied/ unoccupied, variation in
the scheduled comfort set­points, internal heat gains, etc.)
• Grid and energy distributor related disturbances (e.g., Nordpool time­varying elec­

Heating system
As shown in Fig.3.6 the district heating application type of the Sønderborg buildings is
combined room heating (HE) and domestic hot water (DHW) where HE system is directly
connected to the DH network with a mixing loop. The secondary side flow temperature is
adapted to the heat demand of the building by means of a mixing loop. To avoid ‘back­
flow’, a check valve is mounted in the mixing loop. DHW exchanges heat with the DH
network through a heat exchanger and let it into a storage charging tank.
Figure 3.6: Schematics of heating system for the real system
• Room heating
Secondary side supply water temperature is controlled by the motorized valve based on
the weather data. The valve regulates the mass flow of secondary side return water that
flows through the mixing shunt and mixes with the primary side supply water.
Circulation pump in HE loop is specified by the nominal pressure drop, but has variable
mass flow rate depending on the heat demand of each radiator adjusted by the thermostat
valve opening.
Radiator thermostatic valve is a device used to control the temperature of a radiator in
a heating system. It works by regulating the flow of hot water into the radiator, thereby
adjusting the heat output and maintaining the desired room temperature. P­band (Pro­
portional Band) is a parameter used in a proportional control system, which is a common
control strategy for regulating the operation of the thermostatic valve. In a proportional
control system, the valve’s position is determined based on the difference between the
desired temperature (setpoint) and the actual temperature (measured by a sensor). The
P­band essentially sets the sensitivity of the control system. A smaller P­band makes the
system more responsive but might cause more frequent adjustments, leading to poten­
tial temperature oscillations. On the other hand, a larger P­band makes the system less
sensitive but can result in slower response times.
• Domestic hot water
The circulation pump has a pre­setting constant mass flow rate when it turns on, and the
tank water volume is always constant, so the cold water mass flow from the water mains

Low load operation: When the demand is lower than circulation pump pre­setting mass
flow (low load operation), the cold water flows in the system is not enough for pump, so
the remaining amount of water needs to be removed from the bottom of the tank then
these two flows together go into heat exchanger and get heat up to 55◦C, the amount of
cold water goes to demand and the remaining goes back to the top of the tank.
Peak load operation: During peak load operation (higher demand that can be supplied by
the available heating power), the pre­set cold water flow flows through the heat exchanger
and the residual water streams from the bottom to the top of the storage tank. The tap
output is covered by the heat exchanger while the domestic water storage tank is being
simultaneously discharged. Once tap operation ceases, the storage tank is charged as
described below.
Charging operation (no consumption): If there is no consumption the storage tank is being
charged. The total charging flow is removed through the bottom of the tank and returned
through the top of the storage tank via heat exchanger. If the storage tank is completely
charged and no output consumption occurs, the control valve closes and interrupts the
heating water flow.

Battery mathematical modelling
Mathematical modelling of batteries involves developing mathematical equations or al­
gorithms to describes the behavior and performance of batteries. These models help
understand and predict various aspects of battery operation, such as voltage, current,
capacity, and state of charge (SoC) or state of health (SoH).
Battery characteristics
• Energy capacity
The energy capacity of a battery refers to the amount of energy it can store. It is typically
measured in kilowatt­hours (kWh).
• voltage

The voltage of the battery should match the system’s voltage requirements in order to
ensure compatibility and efficient operation.
• Cycle life
Cycle life represents the number of charge­discharge cycles a battery can undergo while
maintaining a specified performance level.
• Efficiency
Battery efficiency refers to the ratio of energy output to energy input during charge and dis­
charge cycles. Higher efficiency batteries result in reduced energy losses and improved
overall system performance.
• Charging and Discharging Rate
The charging and discharging rate of a battery determines how quickly it can be charged
or discharged.

6.5
Limitations and future work
The limitations of this paper come mainly from the fact that, due to time constraints, sim­
plifications and assumptions have been made accordingly in each part of the work. For
example, in modeling: simplifying the three­story multi­zone building into a single zone,
using a low­order grey­box model and calculating the parameters manually instead of
estimating the parameters using commonly used algorithms, and directly precludes the
flexibility study of the domestic hot water tank due to the difference methods in modeling
with the thermal dynamics of the building. When implementing MPC, real past data were
used directly for disturbances or based on Standards assumptions instead of predicted
data, the most common Economic MPC was chosen for the design of the MPC optimiza­
tion problem, and so on. These simplifications, while helpful in reducing the complexity
of the study to ensure its comprehensiveness, make it suffer in terms of veracity and
accuracy.
Based on the limitations mentioned above, the following future work is proposed:
1. Create a multi­zone white­box model to investigate how best to achieve the coordina­
tion between the low­level control loops (switch/PID controller) widely used for each zone
and the top­level MPC­based energy management systems.
2. Study of higher order grey­box models (5R3C, 7R5C) [34] with algorithmic (e.g., genetic
algorithm[35]) estimation of parameters. Try to better understand how to make a balance
between the computational complexity and model accuracy. It is also possible to study
the most suitable RC model structure for different types of buildings (e.g. single zone,
multi­storey, residential, office, etc.). Because different types of buildings have different
thermodynamic, occupant, energy use, and demand characteristics, it makes sense to
conduct such a study.
3. For activating the energy flexibility of buildings, a thermal storage is necessary; in this
regard, the thermal storage capacity of domestic hot water should also be taken into ac­
count. Mathematical modeling of heat transfer between different layers of a heat storage
48

tank [36] and implement MPC for DHW storage and compare to see whether the building
envelope or the DHW storage tank has more flexibility.
4. Investigating more flexibility­related objectives other than economic MPC such as max­
imizing renewable energy use or minimizing primary energy consumption, reducing CO2
intensity, etc. These different objectives are usually combined in a single objective func­
tion. The most general formulation of such a multi­objective problem is to present a global
objective function which use linear combinations of the different objective terms, setting
different weights to put more emphasis on certain aspects of the optimization.
5. Scalability analysis: Study of MPC from buildings to cluster or district level. Study
of MPC control architecture and investigation of the applicability of different MPC archi­
tectural formulations (either centralized, decentralized or distributed MPC formulation) for
different scales of control.
6. It can be observed that for the most related literature about MPC, a great majority of
them relies on computer simulations rather than implementations in real contexts, there­
fore more experimental work is needed to implement and validate the MPC strategy.

7
Conclusion
In summary, to harness the full potential of the increased share of renewables, energy
must be used more efficiently, effectively, and intelligently. Flexible consumption and
smart energy systems must be developed with correlative dependence and interplay to
meet the challenge of integrated fluctuating RESs. Buildings play a crucial role in this
process, especially for buildings with large thermal storage capacity.
In this study, model predictive control strategies for activating energy flexibility with heat
pump have been investigated, and comparisons have been done between basic control,
price­based control and model predictive control. The strength of the basic control and
PBC resides on their simplicity of implementation, and they already achieve satisfactory
performance in reality, though not optimal. The good performance of the PBC obviously
depends a lot on the good choice of the thresholds values placed on the “trigger” pa­
rameters (e.g. the electricity price). It is therefore crucial to draw some attention on the
tuning of these values. Another advantage of PBC resides in its simplicity. No complex
models are required, neither computationally demanding algorithms. However, PBC also
feature several lacks, usually concerning their poor dynamics. The fact that the trigger
parameters or threshold values are fixed makes it difficult for this kind of control to adapt
to changing external conditions. Therefore, better control strategies based on optimiza­
tion methods are needed to address this deficiency. MPC is more complex and costly to
integrate, but yields better results. From both the results of MPC for heating and PV­B
system, the MPC can recognize the relative price of electricity in a future time period and
pre­heat the building or charge the batteries in a time period when demand is not as high
but the price of electricity is relatively low. This way, when the price is high, the building
may not have a thermal demand because it has been pre­heated, or if it does have a
demand, the batteries may be able to carry the load in advance.
As mentioned in the previous section, since some simplifications were made in the current
work, future work can investigate the simplifications in more detail and depth based on
current results.
