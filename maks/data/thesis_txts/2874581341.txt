Abstract
Most current wind turbine blades are made of composites, due to their good stiﬀness and
strength to weight ratio. However, other structural materials present some advantages
with respect to composites, in terms of cost or sustainability.
A blade model has
been developed to evaluate the inﬂuence of using glass ﬁbre reinforced plastic, carbon
ﬁbre reinforced plastic, diﬀerent aluminium alloys or bamboo-based composite as the
load carrying beam material; namely, blade beam weight, tip deﬂection, cost and
environmental footprint are predicted.
On the one hand, it can be seen that the
design of blades made of glass ﬁbre reinforced plastic or bamboo-based composite is
driven by blade deﬂection; for these cases, an optimisation algorithm to ﬁnd the lowest
blade weight to meet the deﬂection requirement has been included. On the other hand,
using aluminium or carbon ﬁbre reinforced plastic lead to a design controlled by fatigue
strength. Comparing against the most common glass ﬁbre blades, it is found that using
carbon ﬁbre reinforced plastic leads to much lighter blades, at a higher cost that might
be worth, whereas bamboo-based composite blades present excellent environmental
qualities at low cost and reasonable weight.
Aluminium blades result to be heavier
and more expensive than the other options.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind turbine blades are considered one of the key components of the entire wind turbine
system, because the extraction of energy depends on their design. A well-designed wind
turbine blade must fulﬁl, as far as possible, these requirements [1]:
• Maximise annual energy production
• Withstand extreme and fatigue loads
• Avoid the impact with the tower, when the blade is deﬂected
• Avoid resonance
• Minimise weight
• Minimise cost
Very often, some of these objectives cannot be satisﬁed simultaneously. The design
process has two important stages which cover the above-mentioned points: aerodynamic
design and structural design. The materials selection is part of the latter.
Blade material is one of the factors that have direct inﬂuence on the turbine eﬃciency.
As a result of the change in the material, the power coeﬃcient CP can vary up to 5 %,
because of the eﬀects of friction in the hub bearings caused by the blade weight [2].
Considering that the lifetime of a wind turbine is 20 – 25 years, this 5 % eﬃciency
margin due to the correctly selected material becomes relevant from an economic point
of view. In addition, diﬀerent materials can lead to a reduction in blade weight, and
that weight saving could be used to make the blades longer, increasing the rotor swept
area and therefore generating more power.
1
==PAGE:17==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
2
In general, a suitable material for wind turbine blades should present high material
stiﬀness to maintain optimal shape of performance, low density to reduce gravity forces,
and long fatigue life to lessen material degradation [3].
In addition, the economic
considerations (availability, cost of material, cost of manufacturing, reliability...) are
equally important.
Consequently, the selection of a proper material is a key point,
which will aﬀect both the design and the manufacturing process. In the same way,
the design and the manufacturing can set some constraints and criteria on the eligible
materials.
Moreover, nowadays taking into consideration environmental impact and sustainability
is mandatory. On the one hand, producing 1 kg of material consumes a certain amount
of energy; sometimes the energy to recycle a material is less than that required for
its production, but the material properties are usually degraded and the costs are
higher. On the other hand, material production is usually associated with undesired
gas emissions, such as CO2, NOx and SOx, which contribute to the global warming [4].
Even though most of current wind turbine blades are made of composites and their
manufacturing processes are already clearly deﬁned, the continuously increasing rotor
diameters, the industry’s rising awareness of the use of more sustainable materials,
and the interest in improving the eﬃciency to lower the cost of energy and be more
competitive, oﬀer an opportunity to analyse the feasibility of other options and judge
the impact that other materials can have on the blades.
The aim of this study is to analyse diﬀerent materials, and to evaluate their impact on the
resulting blade characteristics. This report is divided in the following chapters: Chapter
2 presents the diﬀerent materials that will be used for the blade analysis, focusing
on their mechanical properties. Chapter 3 describes the beam model that has been
developed to calculate the blade weight and deﬂection, and presents the results obtained
using the selected materials.
Chapter 4 includes the economic and environmental
considerations, and discusses the disposal and recycling of blade materials. Finally, in
Chapter 5, the results of the study are discussed, and the main conclusions are drawn.

Chapter 2
Materials selection
2.1
Introduction
The materials selection is part of the structural design of the rotor blade. In general
terms, the ideal material for a wind turbine blade should have high strength to weight
ratio, good fatigue life and stiﬀness, and low cost. The selection of the material has to
be considered along with the conceptual design and the production method [5].
Traditionally, the design of blades has been based at a certain degree on the aeronautics
industry; as a consequence, the materials for wind turbine blades could be in principle
the same that have been used for aircraft wings or helicopter rotor blades. From all the
materials used in engineering, those which have a good balance between stiﬀness and
density are composites, certain metals and some types of wood, as it is shown in ﬁg. 2.1.
For those materials, Young’s modulus is in the range of 20 – 110 GPa approximately,

2.3.1
Fibre-Reinforced Composites
A composite material is made from two constituent materials (ﬁbres and matrix), each
one with signiﬁcantly diﬀerent properties that, when combined, produce a material with
characteristics that are diﬀerent from the individual original components. In general, a
material on ﬁbre form will show higher strength than the same material on bulk form,
because it is less sensitive to defects and it has an improved orientation of internal
structures.
However, a ﬁbre bundle in itself has low resistance to loading in other
direction than tension, such as loading in compression or shear.
By embedding the
ﬁbres in a matrix that can transfer the external loading to the ﬁbres, this is solved. In
composites, stiﬀand strong ﬁbres are embedded in a softer matrix.
The type of of ﬁbre material used is mainly responsible for the strength and stiﬀness of
the composite, whereas the type of resin used as matrix material will largely determine
the manufacturing process as well as the fatigue properties. On the one hand, polyester
resins are generally inexpensive and suitable for medium stresses; moreover, they cure
at room temperature, and that makes the processing easier. However, they have lower
strength and signiﬁcant shrinkage during drying (up to 8 % [5]). On the other hand,
epoxy resins are high-quality but expensive. They are increasingly used, because of
their better strength characteristics and no shrinkage, which allow that the weight of
the ﬁnal component could be reduced. Amongst their drawbacks, their higher costs and
the fact that they cannot be cured at room temperature, which makes the processing
more complex.
The possibility of producing stable components in almost any geometry, with relatively
low costs of manufacturing, the good mechanical properties, and the low weight, have
made that current wind turbine blades are almost without exception made of ﬁbre-
reinforced composites. One of the ﬁrst rotor blades made of composite was the 17-m-long
blade of the W-34 turbine, constructed in glass ﬁbre composite by U. H¨utter, in 1959
[5].
Glass Fibre Reinforced Plastic (GFRP)
Glass ﬁbres are the most widely used type of ﬁbre for composites.
Although their
strength properties are high, their modulus of elasticity is not as high as the strength.
Consequently, the stiﬀness of components made of GFRP is not very high, and that is
one of the reasons why very large wind turbine blades cannot be based only on GFRP
structures [5].

Both stiﬀness and strength are determined by the ﬁbre volume fraction, and the
obtainable fraction depends on the moulding process. For example, for hand lay-up,
typical ﬁbre volume content is in the range of 30 – 40 %, while vacuum infusion process
allows reaching a higher ﬁbre content (50 – 60 %), besides being a safer and cleaner
process [1].
As it will be explained in subsequent chapters, the main loads in the blade structure
are due to gravitational and aerodynamic loads, that lead to almost pure bending loads.
Consequently, mainly unidirectional (UD) composites are used for the main load carrying
parts of the blades [9], that is, spar caps, which take up the bending forces in the ﬂapwise
direction (from aerodynamics), and leading and trailing edges, which take up edgewise

Carbon Fibre Reinforced Plastic (CFRP)
Carbon ﬁbres consist of graphite layers of carbon atoms in hexagonal units, arranged in
parallel, with their planes in the direction of the ﬁbre. This arrangement, together with
the strong carbon-carbon bonds, provides carbon ﬁbres with high mechanical properties.
Compared with glass ﬁbres, carbon ﬁbres have lower density and higher stiﬀness, and
their fatigue strength properties are good too. However, their main disadvantage is
the much higher price, even an order of magnitude more costly than other materials;
as a consequence, producing rotor blades entirely of CFRP is expensive for current
commercial wind turbines. It is for that reason that CFRP is used in small amounts as
reinforcing material at the highly loaded areas of the blades.
As an example of this group, a carbon ﬁbre/epoxy composite, with 60 % ﬁbre volume
fraction and UD lay-up has been chosen. Its mechanical properties are given in [1]:
density, ρmat = 1580 kg/m3; longitudinal Young’s modulus, E = 142 GPa; and fatigue
strength at 107 cycles, σ0 = 350 MPa. Using (2.6), the corresponding fatigue strain is
ε0 = 0.25 %.

Bamboo-Based Composite
The increasing awareness of the environmental and economic issues of the disposal of
the ﬁbre-reinforced composite wind turbine blades has made that green materials are
being considered as an alternative for the use in load-carrying elements of rotor blades.
In the past, several types of natural wood were considered, such as birch, Douglas ﬁr
and spruce, but bamboo has some advantages over those wood-based materials [16]:
• Higher fracture toughness
• Higher speciﬁc strength and modulus
• Low processing costs
• Rapid growth rate and well managed commercial forests
• High carbon sequestration ability and excellent recycling potential
However, wood is an inherently variable material; that means that its properties vary
according to the part of the tree from which wood is taken, but also to the grow
conditions of the tree. In the case of bamboo, the volume fraction of ﬁbres increases
towards the outer culm wall (ﬁg. 2.13), reaching around 50 – 60 % of the total material
in the culm wall. This has a direct impact on the mechanical properties: because of
the higher ﬁbre volume, the axial strength and modulus of bamboo are higher near the

3.8
Tower clearance
As it was shown in section 3.5, wind turbine blades are deﬂected during the turbine
operation. It is extremely important that there is no collision with the tower under
any circumstances, because this will probably result in catastrophic failure of the wind
turbine. In order to prevent this, there are several alternatives, apart from stiﬀening
the blade [24]. Fig. 3.10a shows a wind turbine with straight blades and horizontal rotor
axis; in this situation, the blades are very close to the tower, and there is little margin
in case the blade is deﬂected towards the tower. Fig. 3.10b shows a tilted rotor plane,
so the rotor axis is not horizontal anymore; tilt angles are within the range 5 – 6 deg.
Fig. 3.10c shows pre-bent blades during their production. Finally, the blades can be
coned at a certain angle (typically 2 – 4 deg) at the root (ﬁg. 3.10d).
These three
options, which can be applied simultaneously, aim at increasing the distance between
the tower and the blade tip (tower clearance), and thus increasing the margin to avoid
possible impacts.

3.10
Blade design process
Once all the equations which govern the blade model have been derived, the next step
is to deﬁne the blade design process. There are diﬀerent criteria that have to be taken
==PAGE:47==

into consideration, based on the critical material design parameters (fatigue strength,
stiﬀness and density):
• The blade material has to be able to withstand the number of load cycles
experienced by the wind turbine throughout its lifetime. That means that the
stresses on the blade should be lower than or equal to the fatigue strength:
σflap(x) ≤σ0, σedge(x) ≤σ0.
• The blade has to be stiﬀenough to avoid any collision with the tower when it is
deﬂected. That means that the tip displacement should be lower than or equal to
the maximum allowable tip deﬂection: wflap(x = L) ≤δ.

a lower amount of material would imply reducing the environmental footprint and
the blade cost.

Chapter 4
Cost and sustainability
Like in any engineering activity, cost plays an important role in blade design and
materials selection.
Besides having good performance, a wind turbine blade should
be cost-eﬀective.
Moreover, although wind energy is a renewable energy which
contributes to create a greener and more sustainable world, the environmental impact of
manufacturing and disposal of wind turbines components, particularly the blades, can
be considerable. Both questions are discussed in this chapter.
4.1
Blade cost
A wind turbine blade has a number of inherent costs, such as manufacturing, testing,
transport... As a rule of thumb, the share of one rotor blade in the total costs of a wind
turbine is approximately 5 – 6 % [5]. In this study, only the manufacturing cost will be
considered, and that is determined to a great extent by the cost of the materials.
4.1.1

In general, material properties such as Young’s modulus, strength and density do not
change with time.
However, cost does.

possible, the same source to obtain the price of the diﬀerent materials. In that way, the
absolute cost might not be updated, but the relative cost diﬀerences will be acceptable.

4.2
Environmental footprint
The environmental (or ecological) footprint is deﬁned as the eﬀect that an activity has
on the environment. In the case of industrial activities, the manufacturing and use of
products have the largest impact, because of the consumption of materials and energy.
Nowadays, there is a big concern about all environmental issues, which aﬀects the
engineering processes. Concerning the design, two approaches can be distinguished [4]:
design for the environment, which consists of adjusting the current design methods to
reduce environmental degradation; and design for sustainability, that is, the adaptation
to a lifestyle that meets our necessities without putting at risk the needs of future
generations.
The time scale of the former is the product’s expected lifetime (order
of years), whereas the latter is a long-term strategy (order of decades) that requires
global social and political changes. The choice of materials is part of the design for the
environment approach.
The life-cycle of a product, and in particular a wind turbine blade, can be divided into
the following phases:
• Material production
• Product manufacture
• Use
• Product disposal
Every phase has a diﬀerent contribution to the environmental impact, that, in turn,
depends on the chosen material. For rotor blades, material production and product
disposal are the most relevant phases.
4.2.1
Material production
When material production phase is analysed, materials selection should be based on
minimising consumed energy and associated gas emissions.
Embodied energy
The embodied energy is the energy required to produce 1 kg of material, considered as if
that energy was incorporated in the material itself. Polymers are energy-intense because
==PAGE:69==
CHAPTER 4. COST AND SUSTAINABILITY
54
they are derived from oil, but natural materials also have intrinsic energy; for example,
wood has energy from absorbed solar radiation. The existence of this retained energy
explains the fact that the energy needed to recycle a material is very often less than
that required for producing it for the ﬁrst time.
According to [4], the embodied energy of GFRP ranges from 107 to 118 MJ/kg; other
sources state that it is equal to 100 MJ/kg [31]. The consumed energy of CFRP is higher
than that corresponding to GFRP, being 259 - 286 MJ/kg [4]. These high values are
due to the amount of heat that is consumed during the manufacture of ﬁbre-reinforced
composites.
The value of aluminium alloys is also signiﬁcant, 200 – 220 MJ/kg [4]. However, the
embodied energy for recycled aluminium is just a small fraction of that for virgin
aluminium, 29 MJ/kg and 218 MJ/kg, respectively [31]. This is because the required
temperature for melting recycled aluminium is 600 ◦C, whereas extracting aluminium
from ore requires 900 ◦C, and much more energy is needed so as to reach this higher
temperature.
Finally, the energy required for producing bio-based composites is much lower: 4 – 6
MJ/kg for bamboo, according to [4]. A bio-based composite product consumes only
4.8 % of the energy used to manufacture the same product with conventional composites
[32]. This clearly shows how selecting a bio-based material can reduce extraordinarily
the environmental impact compared to traditional composites.

Additional considerations
Besides the embodied energy and the CO2 footprint, there are other considerations that
aﬀect the material production phase and should be mentioned.
Water consumption is an additional variable that might be taken into account to evaluate
the environmental impact of a material. Manufacturing materials requires a certain
amount of water, which can be consumed, evaporated, or polluted. A more productive
use of water is beneﬁcial for the environment, because climate change has already
aﬀected the water cycle (prolonged periods of drought, extreme rainfalls...). A kind
of water footprint could then be analysed.
Moreover, there is an additional amount of material that is used in the maintenance
stage of wind turbine blades. It is generally between 1.5 and 4.5 % of the blade weight
[34], and it has an equivalent embodied energy and CO2 emission.

4.2.2
Blade decommissioning
After the end of their lifetime (usually 20 – 25 years), wind turbines are decommissioned.
In some cases, old rotors are replaced by newer and more eﬃcient ones. In any case,
the decommissioning phase might have a large environmental impact, and consequently
it is necessary to deﬁne a sustainable way for dealing with out-of-action wind turbines.
Due to the fact that the wind industry is relatively young, there is limited experience in
removal and recycling of wind turbines. While some components are already recyclable
(foundations, towers...), blades constitute a big challenge because of the materials used.
As shown in ﬁg. 4.7, blades are the most damaging component, and at the same time,
the uncertainty on how to cope with their dismantling properly and safely is still high
[35].
Figure
4.7:
Assessment of the environmental impact and uncertainties involved in

The process of blade decommissioning has two main stages. The ﬁrst step consists of
dismantling and cutting the blades into smaller sections. This can be carried out using
diﬀerent methods [36]:
• Water jet cutter
• Wire saw
• Circular saw
• Jaw cutter
==PAGE:75==
CHAPTER 4. COST AND SUSTAINABILITY
60
These methods have diﬀerent degrees of environmental impact, mainly in terms of dust,
noise and water consumption. Jaw cutter is the most common method for sectioning
the blades.
The next step is the blade waste treatment. There are a number of ways to treat the
waste; some of them are already commercially available, whereas others are only limited
to laboratory scale [36]:
• Biotechnological
• Chemical
• Electrochemical
• Fluidised bed pyrolysis
• High voltage fragmentation (HVF)
• Incineration
• Landﬁll
• Mechanical
• Microwave pyrolysis
• Pyrolysis
Incineration allows electricity and heat recovery, and the generated ashes can be used
in other applications; it is the main alternative to landﬁll. Landﬁll is one of the most
used methods at commercial scale because of its low cost, although it is taxed and
even banned in some countries. Mechanical and pyrolysis methods allow recovering of a
certain amount of material, but with reduced mechanical properties; they are not used
on a large scale, as they are expensive.
The European Waste Framework Directive establishes an “obligation to handle waste
in a way that does not have a negative impact on the environment or human health”
[37], and it encourages the recovery of waste and the use of recovered materials so as
to conserve natural resources. In order to do that, a waste hierarchy is established,
consisting of a priority order of waste treatment methods: a) prevention, b) preparing
for re-use, c) recycling, d) other recovery, e.g. energy recovery, and e) disposal. The
waste hierarchy model is shown in ﬁg. 4.8.
Some speciﬁc applications for wind turbine blades for every hierarchical category are
listed below [36]:
==PAGE:76==
CHAPTER 4. COST AND SUSTAINABILITY
61
Figure 4.8: Waste hierarchy model. Taken from [36]
• Prevent: extending the lifetime of the blade through blade refurbishment and
reparation.
• Re-use: repairing a blade and installing it on an existing wind turbine or on a
new one; second-hand sales. This is also known as primary recycling, because the
re-used product has equivalent properties and value.
• Recycle: the blade becomes a new product or material, with a totally diﬀerent
function, for example building structures, furniture or sport material. It requires
additional energy. Also called secondary recycling.
• Recover: energy recovery by means of some waste treatment methods, or cement
production from blade waste. It is also referred as tertiary recycling.
• Disposal: the least preferred option, because no material nor energy are recovered,
e.g. landﬁll.
4.2.3
Recycling of blade materials
Blade recycling methods have been maturing, due to several factors: expected amount of
blade waste in the near future, waste policies, wider producer responsibility, demand for
more sustainable manufacturing processes, and reduced availability of landﬁll, amongst
others.
==PAGE:77==
CHAPTER 4. COST AND SUSTAINABILITY
62
Recycling implies recovering part of the material. However, in the case of wind turbine
blades, this process is quite challenging. First, it is diﬃcult to recycle blades into any
other applications than blades; moreover, the material to be recycled will be varying in
quality, quantity and design [38]. But probably the most relevant issue is the type of
material used.
Recycling of ﬁbre-reinforced composites
The development of the wind energy industry has led to a fast increasing consumption
of ﬁbre-reinforced plastic (FRP), see ﬁg. 4.9. It is estimated that the composite material
used in wind industry is between 12 and 15 tonnes per MW [36]. As a consequence,
composite waste is expected to increase in the next years.
Figure 4.9: Annual use of FRP composites in wind turbine blades until 2030. Taken from
[36]
The constituents of composites are matrix and ﬁbres.
For wind turbine blades,
thermosetting polymers are the preferred type of matrix polymer, although new
composites of reactive thermoplastics are being investigated [39]. Unlike thermoplastic
polymers, which can be melted down and reused, thermosettings cannot be remoulded,
they burn. Consequently, only ﬁbres can be recovered.
The most common recovery processes are based on chemical and thermal principles,
requiring temperatures between 300 and 700 ◦C [38].
Such high temperatures are
detrimental for the mechanical properties of the ﬁbres. Therefore, recycled ﬁbres can
rarely be used in high-performance composites. In addition, processing costs are very
high, and that means that recovered ﬁbres are costlier than pristine ones, so commercial
==PAGE:78==
CHAPTER 4. COST AND SUSTAINABILITY
63
applications are limited. Currently, recycled blade material could be used in automotive
manufacturing, leisure and sport sectors.
Other alternatives are producing cement using waste composites as fuel and raw material
[35], or reusing the blades for less demanding constructions or street furniture (ﬁg. 4.10).
Figure 4.10: Practical examples of reusing wind turbine blades: playground (left) and public
benches (right). Taken from [36]
Summing up, although recycling ﬁbre-reinforced composites is technically feasible, it is
not cost-eﬀective and the recycled material has lower mechanical properties, so it cannot
compete with virgin material.
Recycling of aluminium
Aluminium has outstanding advantages in terms of sustainability.
It is inﬁnitely
recyclable; that means that it can be recycled over and over without losing its essential
properties, because the recycling process does not change the structure of the metal
(ﬁg. 4.11). Furthermore, the energy used for its primary manufacture remains inside
the material, consequently just a fraction of the original power is required for the
recycling process, saving up to 95 % of the energy needed to produce new aluminium.
An equivalent amount of greenhouse gases is also saved.
The process of aluminium recycling follows these steps:
• Collection of scrap from products that have reached the end of their service life.
• Sorting and treatment, including shredding, separation of other metals using
magnets, and de-coating.
==PAGE:79==
CHAPTER 4. COST AND SUSTAINABILITY
64
Figure 4.11: Aluminium production and recycling loop. Taken from [40]
• Remelting and reﬁning, to produce either wrought or casting alloys.
It is estimated that 75 % of all the aluminium ever produced is still being used, due
to its unique ability to be recycled. In Europe, around 90 % of the aluminium used in
construction and automotive sectors, and 60 % of that used in packaging is recycled [40].
All the mentioned characteristics make aluminium an almost unbeatable blade material
regarding recyclability.
Recycling of bamboo-based composite
Bamboo-based composites have the same problem than ﬁbre-reinforced ones concerning
the thermosetting matrix. Nevertheless, bamboo ﬁbres have higher recyclability than
glass and carbon ﬁbres.
Almost all the scrap from bamboo blades can be used to manufacture other products:
furniture, compost for gardens, chipboard, ﬂooring installations... Moreover, bamboo is
naturally biodegradable. This has two main advantages with regard to its end-of-life:
ﬁrstly, if it is landﬁlled, it decomposes more easily than other materials; secondly, it can
be burnt as biomass to produce bioenergy (electricity or heat), reducing the use of fossil
fuels.

Using GFRP leads to a blade design controlled by deﬂection. As a consequence, the
blade weight is increased to make the blade stiﬀer and to meet the minimum clearance
requirement. Despite this, the blade weight has intermediate values, even a bit lower
than those of actual rotor blades.
The blade cost is reasonable, in the mid-range.
However, the environmental indicators are not so good; both the embodied energy and
the CO2 footprint are high, being the highest values of all the composites. Moreover, the
recycling of this material is costly, and the recovered ﬁbres have lower quality than the
pristine ones. Therefore, GFRP blades have intermediate performances, at a suitable
cost, but with poor sustainability.
CFRP has outstanding structural properties, hence the very good characteristics CFRP
blades have. These are the lightest blades by a large margin, and the tip deﬂection does
not reach the maximum allowable value, so it is not necessary to increase the weight.
However, this material is the most expensive of all the candidates (more than four times
more expensive than GFRP), so the blade cost is 22 % higher than that made of GFRP,
in spite of such a low weight. The environmental footprint is lower than for GFRP,
both in terms of consumed energy and undesired gas emissions, but the recyclability is
as bad. In general, CFRP overcomes GFRP to be used in blades, but at a higher cost,
that might be worth depending on the case.
Aluminium blades are stiﬀand controlled by fatigue, but they are considerably heavier
than composite blades. In general, blades made of aerospace alloys (2024 and 7075) show
better performances than 6061, that is, they are lighter and have less environmental
impact, but on the other hand they are more expensive.
The great advantage of
using aluminium in wind turbine blades is the excellent recyclability, which makes that
recycled aluminium has the same properties than virgin one. If blades are made of
recycled aluminium, the consumed energy and the carbon footprint will be even lower

than those of GFRP and CFRP blades. However, the weight is a big drawback, that

(around 300 % costlier than GFRP blades); as a consequence, using aluminium does not
seem recommendable for large wind turbine blades.
Finally, the use of bamboo-based composites for rotor blades looks promising. Bamboo
blades are deﬂection-designed due to their ﬂexibility, but their mass is in the same
order of GFRP blades. From a sustainability perspective, they are unbeatable, with
very low values of embodied energy and CO2 production, and good recyclability. Apart

from being the most environmentally friendly option, they are economic, with a cost
reduction of 74 % with respect to GFRP blades.
As blades get longer, deﬂection and stiﬀness become more important in blade design and
performance. Therefore, it seems reasonable to include CFRP as reinforcing material,
because it is an eﬃcient way to reduce weight to length ratio and increase blade stiﬀness
so as to avoid tower impacts.
The mass reduction caused by CFRP usage could become substantial. For the Sandia
100-meter blade, a 28 % weight reduction is achieved by replacing the glass ﬁbre spar
cap with a carbon ﬁbre one, and a 5 % reduction is due to the replacement of glass
ﬁbre with carbon ﬁbre in the trailing edge reinforcement [41].
Moreover, reducing
mass not only reduces the gravitational loads on the blades (and thus improves the
fatigue performance), but it also allows associated secondary reductions in the laminates,
reaching a total weight reduction of 35 % (from 114172 to 73995 kg for the Sandia blade
[42]). Any blade weight saving would beneﬁt the whole wind turbine.
In addition, special attention should be paid to sustainability concerns. As the consumed
energy and CO2 emissions of CFRP are two times those of GFRP, the environmental
impact would be higher when widely using CFRP. Comparing a full GFRP blade with a
similar-size partial CFRP blade (CFRP spar cap, rest of the blade is GFRP), the energy
consumption might be increased up to 50 %, and the CO2 footprint up to 60 % [34].
Some blade manufacturers already use pultruded, carbon ﬁbre-reinforced spar caps to
reinforce the blades. The higher cost can be counterbalanced by greater annual energy
production. Another approach is the hybrid technology, which aims at getting the best
of both GFRP and CFRP. It is the case of the blade manufacturer LM Wind Power,
who has developed a technology consisting of standard glass ﬁbre/polyester base shell
laminate strengthened by an embedded hybrid carbon/glass ﬁbre main spar cap. In
that way, the blade becomes stiﬀer and some weight is saved, but at limited cost and
environmental impact increase.
Concerning the bamboo blades, it is true that the obtained blade performances
are appealing, but some potential problems might arise.
Firstly, blade erosion, as
bamboo easily gets scratches; secondly, damage caused by water, because of bamboo’s
vulnerability to excessive moisture.
Although bamboo-based wind turbine blades
are just in a start-up phase, they might become a promising contributor to a more
sustainable wind energy industry. Therefore, further research is needed to evaluate the
actual performance of bamboo in commercial large rotor blades.
There are several ﬁelds of research for the future. Material choice and blade design
should consider the improvement of performances and the eﬃciency of manufacturing
processes, as well as the sustainability of the materials and the alignment with recycling

methods.
Some interesting areas could be developing alternative manufacturing
processes using carbon-ﬁbre to reduce costs, consolidating the application of bio-based
composites on rotor blades, or investigating other recyclable materials, such as
thermoplastics.
Any of these innovations might have positive eﬀects on production,
maintenance, cost, or environmental impact of the blades.
Finally, although the developed blade model is not excessively complex, it estimates
fairly well the characteristics of the blade, as it has been checked with the weight and
tip deﬂection of publicly available blades. It can be used to quickly compare the eﬀect
of diﬀerent materials on blade design, and use these results as a preliminary stage of
a more comprehensive design. Improvements to the model might be done, and new
materials can be easily added to be simulated. The implemented Python code has been
included in an appendix to this report.
