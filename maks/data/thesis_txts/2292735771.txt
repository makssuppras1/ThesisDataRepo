DANISH TECHNICAL UNIVERSITY
Abstract
Department of Wind Energy
Master of Science
Evaluation of obstacle models based on full-scale scanning lidar
measurements
by Davide Conti
Today due to less available high wind potential sites on land, more wind turbines are
being installed in areas of great topography complexity and near obstacles. The latter
has a strong inﬂuence on the microscale wind resources, as obstacles can highly reduce
the wind speed and increase the turbulence intensity, thus aﬀecting negatively both the
power and loading of turbines. Particularly, small wind turbines lifetime is observed to
be drastically reduced due to obstacle eﬀects.
In the present study to assist evaluation of computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) and an-
alytical obstacle models, the shelter of a 3-m tall and 30-m long solid fence is investigated
with measurements from a full-scale ﬁeld experiment. Three short-range lidars are used
to measure the velocity ﬁeld on a vertical plane extending 2.5 and 11 obstacles heights in
the vertical and downstream directions, respectively. The obstacle eﬀect is analysed for
diﬀerent wind directions and atmospheric conditions. The results include the evaluation
of obstacle models, the models’ error estimation and possible improvements of models
are discussed.
From the comparison of models and observations, it is concluded that the theory used as
basis of the analytical obstacle models can be used in the far wake, deﬁned by 6 obstacle
heights downstream. It is also shown that the CFD model can accurately predict the
obstacle’s eﬀects in both near and far wake regimes, when correct inﬂow and surface
boundary conditions are used. Moreover this analysis provides an understanding of the
accuracy levels and limitations of the analytical obstacle models, which are commonly
used for wind resource assessment.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the last 25 years wind turbines have been erected over ﬂat and homogeneous
terrain, at high wind site conditions in order to maximize performance and lifetime.
Today, as most of the ideal on land areas have been already deployed, more turbines are
being installed in the vicinity of forests, at the top of escarpment, in hilly terrain and in
areas surrounded by obstacles. Obstacles are widespread over the surface with diﬀerent
shapes and sizes. They are strongly concentrated in urban areas such as buildings and
industrial blocks, but they can also be found in rural areas such as isolated houses, trees
and shelterbelts. Regardless of the functionality and nature, they have a strong impact
on the local wind resources, causing velocity reduction and increasing the turbulence
intensity at the vicinity site. These eﬀects have a negative impact on the performance
of wind turbines as structural loading increases with high turbulence and the power
production decrease with the cube of the wind speed. Nowadays the small wind industry
has raised the challenges that installed turbines are facing around the world. Many small
wind turbine locations are near obstacles such as buildings, trees and shelterbelts, which
have been proved to have a signiﬁcant detrimental eﬀect on the turbines’ performance. In
addition, current planning and zoning regulations are often based on old guidelines which
recommend installation at distances in proximity of existing buildings.
In Denmark
municipalities are often to recommend turbines installation at 20 m distance from houses.
Over the last years the Energy Agency’s Secretariat for wind turbines at DTU Wind
Energy, have been working to report statistics about damages and failures of turbines
and to improve installation guidelines. In 2014 more than 25 small turbines broke down
due to high loading (see Fig. 1.1).
1

However the wind ﬂow behind obstacles is one of the most complex to measure and to
accurately predict. The International Energy Agency IEA TASK 27 with nine country
members, is promoting scientiﬁc research and analysing obstacle eﬀects on turbines per-
formances. The main objective is to develop recommended practices, for siting of small
turbines in highly turbulent sites. But yet there are few existing measurements dataset
of 3D wind speed which can be used for comparative analysis and model evaluations.
Eventually the ability to accurately predict the obstacle eﬀects will improve the accuracy
of existing model commonly use in siting of wind turbines.
Historically, the obstacles eﬀects have been investigated for wide ranges of application
ﬁelds.
One of the ﬁrst application was related to agricultural meteorology.
In fact
shelter belts have been used to improve climatic conditions to serve agricultural needs.
The necessity to protect crops from harsh wind conditions blowing from North and Baltic
Seas, was already apparent in the 50s in Denmark. Jensen (1954) is one of the ﬁrst to
analyse the aerodynamic of shelter belts and to estimate its eﬀects on crops. Nageli
(1941) demonstrated how the porosity of belts can be designed to obtain the desired
conditions. Kaiser (1959) shows how a medium porous screen reduced the velocity by at
least 20% over a larger distance than either a very dense screen or a screen with very high
porosity. Around that time the shelter belts were investigated in a systematic manner in
==PAGE:18==
Symbols
3
order to optimize the aerodynamic for micro-environment protection purposes. However
due to the complexity of the analytical problem, most of the research has been done
experimentally and by trial and error and by evaluating the wind reduction of existing
belts.
Few years later a wind tunnel experiment was conducted at Colorado State
University, to measure the ﬂow in the wake of a solid obstruction (Chang, 1966). This
comprehensive dataset was a milestone for the ﬁrst attempts of developing analytical
theory to describe the ﬂow. Plate (1971) demonstrated that the drag on the shelter is
governed not only by the shape and porosity of the shelter but also by the aerodynamic
characteristics of the approaching boundary layer ﬂow.
Counihan (1974) deduced a
simple eddy-viscosity theory to describe the mean velocity behind a two-dimensional
obstacle in a turbulent boundary layer. Counihan’s study demonstrated that the wake
strength can be described by the downwind component of the momentum deﬁcit, which
is constant throughout the far wake. Based on the Counihan theory and wind tunnel
measurements, Perera (1981) developed a set of empirical correlations which estimate
the velocity deﬁcit and Reynolds stresses at any point downstream of a two-dimensional
porous fence. Perera’s model prediction were limited to the far wake beyond 7.5 ∗h,
where h is the height of the obstacle. The WAsP shelter model (Troen I, 1989) adopted a
version of Perera’s empirical formula to predict the wakes of three-dimensional obstacles.
Furthermore, the lateral wake was modelled assuming a lateral spreading of the wakes
of 12 degree.
Taylor and Salmon (1993) developed a model to correct anemometer
measurements for sheltering by obstacles. The velocity deﬁcit formulation is based on
the work of Perera, while the wake moment coeﬃcient is adjusted to count for three-
dimensional eﬀects. Lemberg (1973) estimated the wake moment coeﬃcient for diﬀerent
obstacles, including a cube, a rectangular prism and a cylinder by demonstrating the
coeﬃcient dependency on wind direction.
For diagonal wind the wake moment was
measured to be lower than for perpendicular wind. The Taylor and Salmon (1993) model
was evaluated with Lemberg (1973) wind tunnel study in the far wake of a 3D cube.
The correction for lateral wake eﬀects was assumed to be symmetrical with a Gaussian
form along the obstacle length. One of the most comprehensive experimental data set
was reported by Martinuzzi and Tropea (1993) for ﬂow past a cube mounted on a wall
of a plane channel at high Reynolds number. Few years later Shah and Ferziger (1997)
applied Large eddy simulation of ﬂow, (LES) and compared results to Martinuzzi study.
In the recent years computationally ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) models have been developed
and applied in several studies to simulate steady turbulent ﬂow over surface-mounted

Symbols
4
cubes (Yakhot et al., 2006) (Yassina et al., 2014), (Ganesan and Awang, 2012). But
yet available literature lack of extensive validation of CFD simulation for accurately
describing the ﬂow behind obstacles.
The present work extends previous knowledge of the ﬂow around obstacle through a full-
scale ﬁeld experiment, combining analytical and CFD models with ﬁeld observation and
data analysis. A similar study was conducted in Risø National Laboratory by Landberg
(2000). The present study is intended to fulﬁll the following objectives:
• Measure the wind ﬂow in the wake of a fence
• Evaluate accuracy and limitation of analytical and CFD obstacle models
• Estimate the impact of wind direction variability on model accuracy
Chapter 2 is dedicated to provide the background knowledge and the description of the
obstacle models evaluated in the study. In chapter 3 the analytical models are compared
with previous wind tunnel experiment, including Perera (1981), Lemberg (1973), Castro
and Robins (1977). Chapter 4 describes the experimental set up, including instrumenta-
tions, measurement strategies. The methodology and models’ conﬁgurations including
for wind direction variability are presented in chapter 5. The measurement campaign
analysis is presented in chapter 6, where the correlation and the error of measurements
are studied. The evaluation of obstacle models is performed in 7, where models results
are compared to measurements and a number of study cases are presented. Furthermore
the error introduced by models is estimated. The discussion on ﬁndings, implications
and limitation of results is provided in chapter 8. The suggestions for future work in 9.
Eventually the main conclusions are listed in chapter 10.

2.1
Surface Boundary layer
The surface boundary layer (SBL), develops from the ground covering the lower 5−10%
of the atmospheric boundary layer.
The height of the surface layer varies with the
atmospheric stability, from few meters under stable conditions to several hundreds meter
for unstable conditions. This area is characterized by a strong vertical gradient of wind
and temperature proﬁles which are aﬀected by the stability conditions of the above layer
in the atmosphere (Pe˜na et al., 2009). The SBL is also known as the constant ﬂux layer
in which vertical ﬂuxes of momentum, caused by surface obstacles, heat and moisture
ﬂuxes are considered constant (Garratt, 1994).
In the SBL the wind speed proﬁle over homogeneous terrain in stationary conditions
is well-predicted by Monin-Obukhov similarity theory (MOST) (see e.g. (Carl et al.,
1973), (Tennekes, 1973), (Hogstrom, 1988) and (Gryning et al., 2007)).

2.6
The CFD model
The Ellipsys3D code (Sørensen et al., 1995) (Michelsen et al., 1992) (Michelsen et al.,
1994) is a general-purpose ﬂow solver used for many applications within wind energy,
but it was originally implemented to solve ﬂow over hills. The EllipSys3D code is a
multiblock ﬁnite volume discretization of the incompressible Reynolds Averaged Navier-
Stokes (RANS) equations in general curvilinear coordinates. The convective terms are
discretized using a third order QUICK upwind scheme, implemented using the deferred
correction approach ﬁrst suggested by Khosla and Rubin (1974). Central diﬀerences are
used for the remaining terms. It is parallelized with MPI for execution on distributed
memory machines, using a non-overlapping domain decomposition technique and is fur-
ther accelerated, using a multi-level grid sequence in steady state computations. The
numerical solution of the CFD simulations is only stopped when all variable residuals
have converged to under 5E-05.
Turbulence is modeled using the two-equation k-ϵ RANS model by Launder and Spalding
(1974) that has been validated against other modelling approaches models in the Bolund
model blind comparison (described in (Bechmann et al., 2010)). Fixed model constants
calibrated for atmospheric ﬂows are used.

Computational Grid
The width and height of the computational domain is chosen as a balance between
minimizing the blockage eﬀect of the fence and computational resources spent.
The
used domain has a height of 60m, width of 300m and length of 400m. The fence has a
height of 3m and width of 30m. The ratio between the frontal areal of the fence and
the cross-sectional area of the domain is 0.5% indicating that blockage from the domain
boundaries has little inﬂuence.
The fence is placed 40m from the inlet; providing a
long (360m) downstream fetch for the wake to recover before reaching the outlet of the
domain – this helps convergence of the simulations. In order to be able to run diﬀerent
wind directions, the domain has outlet conditions (Neumann boundary conditions) at
two horizontal planes and inlet conditions at the others (see ﬁgure). Inlet conditions
were also used at the top of the domain.
The domain is divided into 1494 blocks each of 163 cells. The domain has a length of
12 blocks in the x-direction, a width of 10 blocks in the y-direction and a height of 13
blocks. Since no mesh block is needed at fence location a total of 1494 blocks or 6.1
million cells are used (left Fig 2.8).
A rectangular grid stretched towards the walls was used for the fence simulations. In
order to capture the high velocity gradients the near-wall grid cells are only 0.03m
tall and coarsen with distance to the wall. Downstream of the fence the vertical grid
resolution is kept in order to accurately model the development of the obstacle wake (Fig
2.8). Due to the mesh topology the fence has a thickness of 0.5m. This was done to get
a gradual coarsening of the grid cells necessary to achieve fast convergence of the CFD

Lidar
In this study ground-based continuous-wave coherent lidars were deployed for the full-
scale wind measurements of the shelter behind a fence. The working principles of these
lidars is based on the Dopller shift phenomenon (Werner, 2005), (Branlard et al., 2013),
(Sj¨oholm et al., 2014).
Lidars measure the Doppler shifts by comparing the frequency of backscattered radiation
to that of a reference beam via a light beating process. The backscattering signals is
derived by particles in the atmosphere, which genreally consist of dust and organic
matters. Their motions is assumed to be the same of the wind ﬂow.
The frequency of the original beam is dependent of the wavelength λ0 as f0 = c/λ0,
where c, is the speed of the light. If the target moves towards the lidar it causes a
compression of the wave and an increasing of frequency. Whereas if it moves away the
wave are stretched and the frequency is reduced. The relative shift of optical frequencies,
named Doppler shift (∆f/f) is proportional to v/c, where v is the relative speed along
the line of sight, and it is presented in 2.51
f = f0 + ∆f = f0 · (1 + 2 · v/c)
(2.51)
==PAGE:39==
Symbols
24
The notation adopted is that a positive frequency shift is seen when the aerosols move
towards the lidar and therefore characterized by a positive line-of-sight velocity v.
To assess this frequency diﬀerence, the beat signal between the reference beam (or local
oscillator) and the backscattered radiation is Fourier transformed and absolute squared
in order to obtain a spectrum density distribution. The background noise of the spectrum
is removed and the spectra is scaled by scaling factors as described in Angelou et al.
(2012). Finally the frequency is determined as the median of the spectrum (Angelou
et al., 2012).
The frequency spectra are converted to velocity spectra according to 2.52 then averaged
over a given period, typically 10 min or 30 min, and normalised to have unit area.

4.5
Lidar measurements
Wind velocity components were measured usings three ground-based continuous-wave
lidars. The instruments are part of the ”WindScanner.dk” project at DTU Wind Energy,
and they are improved versions of the ZephIR instrument described in Smith et al.
(2006), Angelou et al. (2012). The rotating mechanism of the focused beam is controlled
by two independently moving prisms in a patented conﬁguration Mikkelsen et al. (2011).
This conﬁguration has a larger eﬀective aperture, a more sensitive detector, and a local
oscillator able to distinguish the sign of the line-of-sight velocity (Mann et al., 2014).
For the fence experiment, the short-range WindScanners R2D1, R2D2, R2D3 are posi-
tioned in the area behind the fence, as shown in ﬁgure 4.7. R2D1, R2D2 were positioned
≈2 m from the fence to avoid hitting the cross beams at the fence with the laser. More-
over since the most complex ﬂow, characterized by fast ﬂow variation and recirculation,
is expected close to the fence, the R2D1, R2D2 locations are chosen to minimize the
error of the measurements (see Sect. 5.5). A new coordinate system is established in a
Cartesian grid with x along the direction normal to the fence and zero at the centre of
the fence. The positions of instruments are listed in table 4.2.

Lidar observations
The vertical component of the wind speed was not properly measured by the Wind-
Scanners. For 1013 full scans, ≈70% of the entire campaign, measuring errors of the
w-component were found. The following analysis is conducted for the horizontal wind
speed (u, v).
Figure 6.12 shows the measured speed up on the vertical plane behind the fence for
diﬀerent wind direction intervals. It is seen that the shelter of the fence is highest for
wind directions normal to the fence. For directions less and less perpendicular to the
fence the shelter eﬀect decreases. For directions between 0◦± 15◦, the wind speed is
strongly aﬀected in the area delimited by x/h < 6 and z/h < 1.5. In this region the
speed up is negative as the wind ﬂow is inverted. Commonly the area where complex
phenomena including ﬂow separation, inversion and fast variations occur is called near-
wake. The same deﬁnition will be used in the present work. For larger direction from
the normal, the near wake develops over a shorter distance. Important to mention is

that in this region the vertical component of the wind is expected to be relevant, though
it was not measured from the WindScanners.
The far wake is deﬁned as the region characterized by a wind ﬂow which is recovering
towards undisturbed conditions. Figure 6.12 shows that for increasing wind directions
the obstacle eﬀects are nearly null in the far wake. This is observed as the wind is not
sheltered for larger directions due to the ﬁnite length of the fence.
In the region above the obstacle within z/h < 2, the wind ﬂow experience a strong veloc-

CFD fence model
Figure 7.3 shows the comparison between the observed speed-up, calculated as the mean
of all the observations, and the simulated speed-up with the CFD model. In the same
graph the result of ﬁve diﬀerent set ups of the CFD model are presented. It is seen that
the accuracy of the model can be improved by considering the variability of wind direc-
tion within the analysed interval. The best ﬁt is found when the speed-up is modelled
by simulating each of the observation. However counting for the direction variability
over the average time of each observation does not improve the accuracy. Modelling the
speed-up on a weighted Gaussian distribution provide a better ﬁt than simulating at a
single wind direction, either the central or the mean of the interval. The observed speed-
up, which was deﬁned as the average over the bins size is dependent of the variability
of the direction in the speciﬁc bin. Therefore if the model can accurately predict the
shelter eﬀects for any direction, than simulating the speed for each of the observations
provides the best agreement with the measurements.

WAsP shelter and WEMOD
Figure 7.8 shows the comparison between the speed-up simulated in WAsP shelter,
WEMOD and the observations for the bin size 0◦± 25◦(Case I). Both models predict
the same recovery in the far wake. However they are not able to predict the complex

variability. This is because the correction factor which model the spreading of the lateral
wake in WAsP shelter and WEMOD underestimates the faster recovering in the far wake,
for direction less and less perpendicular. In other words the model consider the fence
has a nearly 2D inﬁnite obstacle for distances x/h < 11.

7.4
Models accuracy
The deﬁnition of the error is given in section 5.5. Considering a number observations
as the bin size of case I, thus 0◦± 25◦, the error is estimated between each observations
and the relative simulation. Eventually the mean over all the single errors is calculated
and presented. Moreover, the mean errors evaluated for a range of heights or distances
are calculated as an average of single errors within the range. The standard deviation
of the errors have been omitted from the graphs but it is shown in appendix. Negative
values indicate that the model underestimates the speed-up.
CFD fence model
Figure 7.20 illustrates the estimated mean error for direction 0±25◦, (Case I). It is seen
that for all the observed heights the error is decreasing to zero with further distance from
the fence. For heights lower than z/h < 0.96 the error in the near-wake is very high,
in cases more than 100% in respect to the observation. However it must be noticed
that the speed-up assumes values around 0 ± 0.1 for this region. Another ﬁnding is
that the accuracy of models is improved considering the variability of wind direction in
the far-wake. This was the region for which the recovering of the speed is aﬀected by
unsheltered wind. The error reduction achieved by simulating the direction variability
is ≈35 % at z/h = 1.46, and 20% at z/h = 1.96. For heights lower than the obstacle
the errors are reduced by 60%. factor.
Regarding the near-wake the speed-up is independent from the direction variability as
the wake is strongly aﬀected by the obstacle. An error of 30% is estimated in the near-
wake at z/h = 1.46. For higher heights the obstacle eﬀects is limited and the error is
within +5%.
For increasing wind directions from the normal, the errors become constant with the
distances in the far wake and independent from direction variability (see Fig. 7.21). For
heights z/h > 1.46 the error is less then +6%. For z/h < 0.96 the error is ≈+15%,
which indicates that CFD simulates a faster recovers of the wind speed.

Chapter 8
Discussion
WAsP shelter and WEMOD models
In the present study the deﬁnitions of near and far wake were given accordingly to ﬂow
conditions. Perera (1981) demonstrated the validity of the semi-empirical formula at
the basis of the analytical models only in the far-wake, deﬁned by x/h > 7.5 for a 2D
inﬁnite fence. Taylor and Salmon (1993) demonstrated that the WEMOD was able to
predict the speed up behind a 3D cube in the far-wake, denoted as x/h > 5. The fence
experiment demonstrates the validity of analytical models in the far-wake for distances
x/h > 6. It is found that the development of the far-wake region varies for type of
obstacles, and therefore analytical models have to be corrected accordingly.
In the common practice the speed-up eﬀects caused by an obstacle are evaluated for
a bin size of wind directions. It was demonstrated that for increasing directions, the
spreading of the lateral wake cause the faster recovering of the wind to undisturbed
conditions. As a consequence the shorter is the obstacle the closer is the far wake and
the larger is the region for which analytical models can be applied. This imply that if
analytical models are corrected to count the 3D eﬀects, they may ﬁnd application for
estimating the sheltering eﬀects of isolated obstacles in addition 2D inﬁnite obstacles.
The correction factor counting for lateral wake eﬀects was demonstrated to not accu-
rately predict the speed-up for a 30 m long fence and nearly perpendicular directions.
However this correction is function of the length of the obstacle, based on a geometrical
92
==PAGE:108==
Symbols
93
relationship. The shorter the obstacle the more relevant is the correction on the speed-
up. In the present work in order to cover for the deﬁcit of the lateral wake correction,
it was demonstrated that for lower wake moment coeﬃcient the strength of the wake
was reduced in order to ﬁt observations. Lemberg (1973) demonstrated that the wake
moment coeﬃcient for a cube, a vertical cylinder and a rectangular prism, decreases for
diagonal wind ﬂow compared to perpendicular ﬂow. Therefore the analytical models
should be evaluated for shorter obstacles to assess the combine eﬀects of correction fac-
tor and reduction of the wake moment coeﬃcient. For example if the spreading of the
lateral wake is well-predicted then the model may be accurate even for a wake coeﬃcient
of Ch = 0.8.
CFD model
It was observed that the accuracy of the model is improved by considering the direction
variability. This was evident for relatively perpendicular wind direction to the fence.
However CFD simulation are computationally expensive and in the common practice to
run a simulation for each individual observation it is not a feasible approach. One way
to circumnavigate this, it is to evaluate the speed up eﬀects for narrower direction bins.
In the present study the size of the relatively perpendicular direction was 50◦width due
to lack of data. For a 10◦bin size the direction variability dependency of the model is
expected to be lower.
In addition, it was not observed any fundamental improvement by accounting for vari-
ability of wind direction of each observation. However the averaging time of the analysed
time series was ≈39.1 s. For a 10 min average time series, the model accuracy is expected
to be improved.
The larger error was observed for the region above the fence. In a range of ≈1.5 m the
wind speed vary from nearly zero to undisturbed velocity. It may be further investigated
the sensitivity of the speed-up with varying heights in this region.

Measurements
The dataset for perpendicular wind directions was limited and more measurements are
needed to study in more details the sheltering eﬀects of an obstacle. It was found that the
uncertainty on the roughness length upstream the fence did not inﬂuence the resulting
speed-up for heights z/h < 2.5. Moreover topographical eﬀects were not relevant as
well. However it is found that the region above the obstacle was the most critical as
the measurements are aﬀected by the larger error due to probe volume characteristic.
Diﬀerent scanning pattern should be employed both to minimize the error and to increase
the number of observations in this region.
In addition, in order to reduce the uncertainty of measurements, the time series of
velocities should be determined from an average Doppler spectrum rather than for each
individual Doppler spectra as done in the present work and as in described in (Angelou
et al., 2012),

Chapter 9
Future Work
The fence experiment should be extended in order to collect more measurements from
perpendicular wind direction. The accuracy of models was found to be dependent on
direction variability, and with a larger dataset the direction dependency can be evaluated
for narrower bin size as done in the common practice. Moreover a more detailed analysis
may be conducted to evaluate the sheltering eﬀects dependency on wind speed and
atmospheric conditions.
A diﬀerent scanning pattern is suggested with more focus on the region above the obsta-
cle, where the WindScanner measurements are mostly aﬀected by uncertainty and the
CFD simulation give the largest error. A simple solution is to scan more lines within
the vertical plane 1 < z/h < 2.
A new experiment for a shorter obstacle should be conducted to evaluate the modelling
of the lateral wake eﬀects and estimating a corresponding wake moment coeﬃcient, for
analytical models.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The main conclusions of this study are listed below
• There are two regions characterizing the wake of the fence. The near wake, where
the ﬂow separation and inversion occur for x/h < 6. The far wake where the ﬂow
recovers to undisturbed condition, x/h > 6.
• WAsP shelter and WEMOD do not predict the wind ﬂow in the near wake.
• WAsP shelter and WEMOD predict the wind ﬂow in the far wake with an accuracy
of 4% for heights z/h > 1.46 and ≈23% for lower heights.
• The correction for 3D eﬀects are underestimated in WAsP shelter and WEMOD.
By assuming a wake moment coeﬃcient Ch = 0.5 the accuracy is improved by 10%
for height z/h < 0.96.
• The CFD model can predict both the near and the far wake. The accuracy is
improved given correct inﬂow and atmospheric conditions.
• The CFD model accuracy is signiﬁcantly improved by accounting for wind direction
variability. The error is reduced by 60% for z/h < 0.96, by 35% at z/h = 1..46
and 20% at z/h = 1.96, in the far wake.
