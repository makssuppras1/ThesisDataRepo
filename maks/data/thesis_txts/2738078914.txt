Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive techno-economic analysis of the Bornholm Energy
Island and the implementation of Power-to-X (PtX) technologies. The analysis is struc-
tured into three distinct steps: Wind power generation, hydrogen and ammonia production.
By examining multiple scenarios, including best-case and extreme cases, this study pro-
vides valuable insights into risk management and the economic feasibility of the project.
The objective of this thesis is to understand the synergies between offshore wind power,
hydrogen and ammonia production. Key objectives include exploring the integration of
offshore wind power with PtX technologies through a literature review, identifying research
gaps, developing a mathematical model to simulate different scenarios and conducting a
sensitivity analysis of key parameters and a risk mapping. The study also extends and
extrapolates parameters to other initiatives to support informed decision-making in similar
projects.
Financial analysis reveals that the wind power generation step is more feasible than the
electrofuel production steps. However, certain extreme scenarios, such as readiness
delays of technology implementation, can severely impact overall feasibility. Among the
electrofuel production steps, the PtA pathway appears slightly more feasible than the PtH
pathway across the five scenarios examined. Notably, the Weighted Average Cost of
Capital (WACC) significantly influences financial metrics, demonstrating the sensitivity of
key financial parameters to changes in WACC.
The results highlight that the Bornholm Energy Island demonstrates lower levelised values
compared to the Floating Offshore Wind Canarias (FOWCA) project, indicating a compet-
itive advantage in electricity generation costs. Key technical parameters were selected
to quantitatively and graphically illustrate their sensitivity within the cost production met-
rics. The sensitivity analysis underscores the significant impact that variations in these
parameters can have on the overall project feasibility and cost-effectiveness.
The comparison with other initiatives, such as the Canary Island offshore hub, highlights
the influence of varying environmental, regulatory, and economic conditions on project
feasibility. This analysis emphasizes the importance of contextual factors in determin-
ing project viability and provides a broader perspective on the potential challenges and
opportunities associated with offshore energy projects.
To advance beyond recent research, this thesis developed a preliminary methodology
and risk mapping to quantitatively evaluate the impact of various factors on the business
case results. The risk mapping tool provides a visual representation of potential impacts,
enhancing the ability to anticipate and mitigate risks effectively. This methodology offers
stakeholders and investors a clearer understanding of the risks involved, enabling more
informed decision-making with a particular focus on risk management.

Introduction
The decarbonization of the energy system has been a priority for many countries for years,
driven not only by the Paris Climate Agreement, in which is agreed to keep the rise in
global warming to well below 2°C, but also by the increasingly extreme weather impact.
The energy sector, as a significant contributor to Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions, must
transform to reduce these emissions. Part of the approach includes the large-scale de-
ployment of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) and rethinking system infrastructure and
energy business models. RES not only help mitigate climate change but also offers a way
to avoid fuel crises exacerbated by geopolitical conflicts, as seen with the war in Ukraine,
and are the cheapest option [1].
1.1
Background
Denmark has long been a leader in decarbonization, serving as an inspiration globally. Its
fast technological advancements in offshore wind, biomethane, district heating and car-
bon capture and storage demonstrate its commitment to sustainability. Currently, Den-
mark has the highest share of wind electricity in the International Energy Agency (IEA),
comprising 54% of its power mix along with bioenergy and solar photovoltaic. The district
heating sector has effectively phased out coal, reducing the country’s reliance on fossil
fuels from 75% in 2011 to 53% in 2022, well below the IEA average [2].
Nevertheless, despite Denmark’s impressive progress and alignment with decarboniza-
tion objectives, there is still significant work to be done. Continued efforts are necessary
to further reduce fossil fuel dependence, enhance energy efficiency and expand renew-
able energy capacity to meet future climate targets fully.
In this context, the emergence of energy islands offer a promising solution to accelerate
renewable energy deployment. These hubs, capable of transmitting power from multiple
Offshore Wind Farms (OWFs) to several countries, hold significant potential for expediting
the transition to sustainable energy. Large-scale energy islands, unconstrained by land
use limitations, present substantial advantages over onshore wind and photovoltaic in-
stallations. By facilitating the efficient transmission and distribution of offshore renewable
energy, energy islands can play a crucial role in meeting the ambitious climate targets
and fostering a sustainable blue economy.
Denmark, known for its pioneering climate policies, has set ambitious targets for reducing
CO2 emissions and achieving climate neutrality. Certainly, the Danish Energy Agreement,
signed in June 2018, outlines specific initiatives to accelerate the transition to renewable
energy, including the development of new OWFs, coal phase-out in electricity production,
investment in green transportation and funding for energy and climate research.
In line with these policies, this thesis master seeks to conduct a techno-economic evalu-
ation of Bornholm Energy Island, focusing on the integration of offshore wind energy with
electrofuels production. PtX technologies are pivotal in enabling the storage and utiliza-
tion of renewable energy. By examining the economic viability, regulatory frameworks
and technological capabilities of PtX integration, the thesis aims to provide insights for
accelerating the transition to renewable energy and achieving climate neutrality goals.

1.2
Objectives and scope
The objective of this Master’s thesis is to conduct a comprehensive three-step techno-
economic analysis of different scenarios regarding the Bornholm Energy Island project.
The aim is to understand the synergies between offshore wind power, hydrogen and am-
monia production. The specific objectives include:
• Explore the current integration of offshore wind power with PtX technologies us-
ing the Bornholm Energy Island configuration through a comprehensive literature
review. This review will provide insights into existing configurations, technological
advancements and challenges in integrating PtX technologies.
• Identifying research gaps: Highlighting the limitations and gaps in current research
with the aim of developing strategies to address and overcome these shortcomings.
• Develop a mathematical model using Microsoft Excel to simulate different scenarios
and assess the techno-economic feasibility of various configurations of electrolysers
for hydrogen and ammonia production. The model will incorporate variables such
as Levelised Values, Capital Expenditure (CAPEX), Operation Expenditure (OPEX),
revenue projections, cost-benefit analyses and financial Key Performance Indicators
(KPIs).
• Explore extreme cases and emulate hypothetical scenarios to assess their potential
impact on the techno-economic feasibility of the Bornholm Energy Island project.
• Adopt a stakeholder and investor approach to the analysis, considering their per-
spectives and priorities in decision-making processes.
• Conduct sensitivity analysis of key parameters to understand their influence on
project outcomes and identify critical factors affecting feasibility.
• Extend and extrapolate parameters and values to other initiatives to facilitate quanti-
tative and qualitative comparisons and support informed decision-making in similar
projects.
• Develop a preliminary methodology to evaluate quantitatively key factors and plot
risk maps to visually represent key parameters, identifying risks and their potential
implications for project feasibility.
The thesis will adopt a structured approach consisting of three steps:
• Step 1: Wind case evaluation: Evaluating the economic feasibility of offshore wind
power generation for the Bornholm energy island using the developed Excel model.
Considering technical and financial factors in order to calculate production costs and
revenue projections.
• Step 2: Hydrogen production through 1 GW electrolyser: Utilize the Excel model to
analyse the techno-economic aspects of hydrogen production using a 1 GW electrol-
yser. Evaluating investment requirements, production costs and revenue potential.
• Step 3: Ammonia production: Assessing the feasibility of producing ammonia from
hydrogen generated by the electrolyser using the Excel model. Considering market
demand, production costs and potential revenue streams.
In short, the thesis aims to provide insights into a business case for the Bornholm En-
ergy Island and a detailed techno-economic analysis conducted using a developed Excel
model. This approach will contribute on understanding the offshore energy integration
and sustainability while facilitating informed decision-making processes.

1.3
Methodology and assumptions
The methodology for assessing the business case on the Bornholm Energy Island involves
developing a detailed mathematical model using Microsoft Excel. Excel was chosen for
its accessibility, flexibility, and robust functionality, making it suitable for handling complex
calculations and scenario simulations. Additionally, its data visualization tools facilitate
clear presentation of results, aiding in effective communication of findings.
To thoroughly assess the potential of the Bornholm Energy Island project, the model will
simulate different scenarios, allowing for a comprehensive risk assessment and testing
the robustness of proposed configurations under different conditions. A preliminary exam-
ination will compare the impact of each indicator separately within each production step,
ensuring a clear understanding of how each variable influences the feasibility of electricity,
hydrogen and ammonia production. This method prevents confusing effects and provides
specific insights into the impact of individual factors. Additionally, risk mapping will be de-
veloped to represent the likelihood of various factors.
Through this project, extensive research has been conducted to gather technical param-
eters on the state-of-the-art of selected technologies. The values used are derived from
a combination of real data from Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) catalogues and
estimations from other research studies. Information from Danish Energy Agency (DEA),
Energinet’s database and other sources has been consulted to understand the current
situation and future perspectives on the development of the Bornholm Energy Island.

Setting assumptions is essential in a study of this scope, as it provides a foundation for
realistic and consistent analysis. By simplifying complex systems and modeling potential
future scenarios, assumptions enable a structured evaluation of technical and economic
outcomes. The following are the main assumptions of the model:
SYSTEM CONFIGURATION
• Integrated onshore and offshore technologies: The study assumes the integra-
tion of various advanced technologies onshore in Bornholm. This includes a 1 GW
electrolyser, a desalination plant, a 1 GW compressor station, storage tanks, land-
based pipelines, an air separation unit and an ammonia reactor connected to con-
densers, compressors and heat exchangers, among other components. Addition-
ally, a 3 GW OWF is connected to these onshore technologies. These specifications
form the foundational basis for evaluating the techno-economic feasibility of hydro-
gen and ammonia production in the Bornholm Energy Island project.
• Technology maturity: The technologies considered in this business case are as-
sumed to be mature and reliable enough for effective implementation. This assump-
tion implies that the integrated onshore and offshore technologies have undergone
extensive development and rigorous testing, achieving a Technology Readiness
Level (TRL) of 8 or 9. Such high TRL levels indicate that the technologies have
been proven in operational environments, ensuring their performance, integration
capabilities and reliability during the project’s execution. This maturity reduces the

2.2
The future potential of PtX
Renewable PtX is emerging as a transformative platform for storing excess renewable en-
ergy for subsequent use and providing a low capital-intensive pathway to produce green
fuels and chemicals. PtX leverages underutilized solar and wind resources to power tech-
nologies capable of converting abundant molecules, such as water into hydrogen; CO2
and water into methane, syngas and oxyhydrocarbons; and air and water into hydrogen
peroxide (H2O2) and ammonia (NH3). These energy carriers and chemical products offer
significant versatility in renewable energy storage, solving intermittency issues, enabling
energy transport and supporting the decarbonization of energy infrastructure. Adoption of
PtX technologies will further facilitate the integration of renewable power into other energy-
consuming sectors such as transportation, agriculture and manufacturing, displacing the
need for fossil fuels [8].
At the core of renewable PtX technologies is electrolysis (Figure 2.3), which uses renew-
able electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen or chlorine, convert nitrogen (N2)
into ammonia, and CO2 into CO, syngas and formic acid. Renewable hydrogen can also
be used in secondary conversion processes such as methanation, hydrogenation and
Fischer–Tropsch synthesis to produce various hydrocarbons and ammonia [9].
Department of Wind and Energy Systems

==PAGE:20==
Figure 2.3: Schematics of power-to-X infrastructure.
Despite the technical maturity and some commercial deployment of key technologies,
most renewable PtX products remain costlier than their conventional fossil-fuel-based
counterparts. This cost disparity arises from the decentralized nature of PtX plants, which
are typically small-scale, while traditional chemical and energy industries benefit from
economies of scale and lower feedstock prices. Additionally, PtX technologies face high
capital costs and significant electricity requirements. The cost-competitiveness of PtX de-
pends on the availability of cheap and excess renewable electricity. However, the evolv-
ing economics of PtX technologies are promising, with potential for commercial viability
and scale-up driven by advances in catalyst and system development for electrolysis and
secondary conversion processes [10].
In addition to capital costs and electricity pricing, feedstock costs will also determine the
price of PtX products. Particularly for secondary conversion processes, the economics
of renewable power-to-hydrogen will be critical. While renewable hydrogen is currently
more expensive than hydrogen from fossil fuels, declining electrolyser capital costs, the
development of cost-effective catalysts, and lower electricity prices are leading to compet-
itive levelised costs. Further research into using wastewater and seawater as feedstock
for electrolysis is also crucial, especially in regions facing water scarcity. In this context,
early opportunities for PtX technologies are expected to begin with small-scale applica-
tions before scaling up to larger commercial and industrial uses, ultimately supporting the
development of a robust PtX economy [11].
In 2021, Denmark presented the PtX Strategy as a key enabler for its climate targets,
industrial development and international export ambitions, establishing four key objectives
(Figure 2.4). In this regard, Denmark is creating the regulatory framework and developing
the infrastructure to enable the industry to operate on market terms in the long run, thus
improving the interaction between PtX and the Danish energy system with a view to export-
related PtX products and technologies [2].

Another important index to consider in the techno-economic analysis is the WACC, which
represents the average rate that a company expects to pay to finance its business [28].
In private economic analysis of projects, the capital cost has two sources: 1) debt, and
2) equity, each having a different weight in the investor financing strategy. The WACC is
the discount rate used to bring future values into present values, in other words it is the
rate used to evaluate the investment value to the capital investors. It is measured after
tax according to the ratios between debt and equity and their corresponding cost:

LCOE calculation
The LCOE is an indicator that represents the average lifetime levelised cost of energy/-
electricity generation. Therefore, it is the minimum price at which the energy/electricity
produced should be sold to make the project attractive. It varies by energy technology, by
country and project, but when measured in the same units can be used to compare their
relative cost.
In this regard, it is worth noting that the calculation of LCOE predominantly centers on
the infrastructure pertinent to energy generation, encompassing turbines, foundations,
nacelle rotor, tower, as well as project management and installation costs. Additionally,
expenses associated with the grid connection for transmitting electricity onshore to the
electrolyser, as well as offshore substations, transformers, shunt reactors and cables for
transporting electricity to Zealand and Germany, are critical components because they
ensure the efficient transmission and distribution of electricity generated by OWFs. There-
fore, these elements are also included in the LCOE calculation [31].
Regarding the previous parameters, the LCOE is equal to the total lifetime costs of the
project divided by the total energy output generated over its operational lifespan. Mathe-

STEP 2: Power-to-Hydrogen Case
With the deployment of a 1 GW electrolyser, surplus electricity generated by OWFs is har-
nessed to produce green hydrogen. This step represents a crucial advancement in Den-
mark’s renewable energy strategy, as hydrogen emerges as a versatile and sustainable
energy carrier. By exploring the technical and economic feasibility of PtH technologies,
Step 2 aims to unlock the potential of hydrogen production as a key electrofuel.
3.2.1
PEM Electrolysis
The system devised for this study revolves around the concept of an energy island, where
a portion of the OWFs installed capacity is aligned with the combined capacity of the elec-
trolysers. Unlike aiming to replicate the Bornholm Energy Island’s exact portfolio plan,
this study focuses on comprehensively assessing the suitability of electrolysers and hy-
drogen production pathways for exclusively harnessing energy from an OWF. Therefore,
for analysis purposes, we examine a 3 GW OWF alongside a 1 GW onshore hydrogen
production plant.
An electrolyser is a device that receives DC electricity and pure water and splits the
molecules of water into hydrogen and oxygen atoms through an electrochemical reaction.
The resulting molecules are stored separately with a high percentage of purity. Although
the technologies operate slightly differently, all have an anode and cathode that are sep-
arated by an electrolyte.
In this study, three primary electrolyser technologies are considered for the Bornholm
Energy Island project: AEL, PEM and Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOEC) (Figure 3.9).
Each technology has distinct characteristics and advantages that influence their suitability
for large-scale hydrogen production: [32]
Figure 3.9: Electrolyser technologies [33]
AEL
AELs use a liquid alkaline electrolyte solution, such as potassium hydroxide (KOH) or
sodium hydroxide (NaOH), to facilitate the transfer of hydroxide ions through the elec-
trolyte from the cathode to the anode, generating hydrogen at the cathode. Although
AELs are a mature and commercially available technology, they have several drawbacks
[34]:
Department of Wind and Energy Systems
25
==PAGE:38==
• Lower current density: AELs operate at lower current densities, resulting in larger
cell sizes compared to PEM electrolysers.
• Corrosive electrolyte: The use of highly corrosive potassium hydroxide presents
maintenance and safety challenges.
• Gas purity and response time: AELs have issues with gas permeation and slower
charge responses.
SOEC
SOECs are a high-temperature electrolysis technology that uses a solid oxide electrolyte
to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. While SOECs offer the potential for high effi-
ciency (around 100%) and the use of thermal energy, they are still in the research and
development stage. Key drawbacks include [35]:
• High operating temperatures: SOECs operate at very high temperatures (800–
1300 K), leading to faster degradation rates and higher maintenance costs.
• Commercial viability: The high degradation rate and current technological imma-
turity make SOECs unsuitable for immediate commercial deployment.
PEM Electrolysers
PEM electrolysers use a solid polymer electrolyte to conduct protons from the anode to the
cathode while insulating the electrodes electrically. The advantages of PEM technology
include [36]:
• High efficiency: PEM electrolysers can operate at higher current densities and
pressures, resulting in high-purity hydrogen production.
• Compact size: They occupy less space, making them ideal for space-constrained
configurations like islands.
• Flexibility and durability: PEM electrolysers are flexible and efficient, offering
rapid response times and the ability to handle variable power inputs without sig-
nificant performance degradation.
In the context of the Bornholm Energy Island project, PEM electrolysers have been cho-
sen over AEL and SOEC technologies due to their superior efficiency, flexibility, compact
size and ability to produce high-quality hydrogen [37]. The choice of PEM technology
aligns with the project’s need for reliable, high-performance electrolysis systems that can
efficiently integrate into the island’s renewable energy infrastructure.
System configuration
On the other hand, the hydrogen production considers three key system configuration
concepts: shoreline, offshore platform and in-turbine production (Figure 3.10). Each con-
cept presents distinct advantages and challenges. However, the shoreline configuration
is chosen for this study due to its cost-effectiveness compared to offshore setups. On-
shore electrolyser placement benefits from lower installation and maintenance costs, eas-
ier scalability, and the efficient recovery of byproducts such as waste heat and oxygen.
Additionally, it offers improved accessibility for maintenance, control, and monitoring, as
well as enhanced power system stability through reliable backup power [38].

3.2.3
Hydrogen price
The determination of hydrogen prices is crucial for evaluating the financial feasibility and
attractiveness of the PtH step within the Bornholm Energy Island project. The willingness
of hydrogen end-use sectors to pay for high-quality green hydrogen significantly impacts
project revenues and profitability.
The green hydrogen produced on the island energy island is suitable for various appli-
cations, including energy storage and transportation fuel. The selling prices of hydrogen
across different sectors in Denmark, ranging from 1.2 €2017/kg for ammonia production
and oil processing to 7 €2017/kg for forklifts [44].
In this regard, considering various factors and uncertainties surrounding hydrogen pricing
and, especially, other reports assumptions such as Feasibility assessment model for using
island-based Power to X to facilitate offshore wind integration [16]), a selling price of 4
€/kg has been adopted and maintained throughout the model. This price point reflects a
balance between sector demand, production costs and market dynamics.
However, fluctuations in hydrogen prices could significantly impact the project’s feasibility.
Evidently, if market prices for hydrogen drop below the assumed 4 €/kg, the project may
face reduced profitability, making it less attractive to investors and stakeholders. Con-
versely, if prices rise above this level, the project could see increased revenues and en-
hanced financial viability. Such price variations could stem from changes in supply and
demand dynamics, technological advancements, policy shifts and competitive pressures
in the hydrogen market.
Therefore, understanding and anticipating these fluctuations is critical for accurate finan-
cial modeling and risk assessment. By considering sector demand, production costs, and
market dynamics, the established selling price of 4 €/kg serves as a foundational assump-
tion for financial modeling and decision-making, but it also highlights the importance of
flexibility and responsiveness to market changes.

Regarding the ammonia synthesis process, it follows a specific sequence of steps, vital
for the conversion of hydrogen and nitrogen into ammonia. Beginning with the mixing of
hydrogen, produced from water electrolysis, and nitrogen, separated from the air through
the ASU, the process ensures high purification to eliminate the presence of oxygen, which
can disrupt the reaction. Subsequently, the mixture undergoes compression before enter-
ing the catalytic reactor, where an iron-based catalyst facilitates the exothermic reaction,
yielding gases including ammonia and unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen. These gases
are then directed to an ammonia separation unit operational at intermediate pressure,
where unreacted gases are separated from the produced ammonia. The unreacted gases
undergo recycling through recompression before being reintroduced into the catalytic re-
actor [48].
With a designated operational lifetime of 20 years, the synthesis process demands spe-
cific inputs to facilitate efficient conversion. Notably, each ton of ammonia production
requires a substantial electricity demand, estimated at 0.738 kWh/kg NH3 [53], under-
scoring the process’s energy-intensive nature. Additionally, the high conversion rate of
both hydrogen and nitrogen, estimated at 99%, ensures optimal utilization of these inputs,
contributing to the overall efficiency of the ammonia synthesis process [14]. These factors
collectively shape the operational dynamics of ammonia production within the Bornholm
Energy Island framework, emphasizing the importance of accurate parameter estimation
and strategic planning for sustainable and economically viable outcomes.

Risks and extreme scenarios
Bornholm Energy Island is driven by a sense of urgency imposed by the imperatives of
addressing climate change. The ambitious vision for Bornholm Energy Island’s imple-
mentation underscores its status as a high-risk venture, where decisions are not only
shaped by environmental objectives but also by external pressures to expedite progress.
Moreover, the reliance on emerging and immature technologies introduces an element of
uncertainty, compounded by the potential for an overheated market driven by concurrent
green transitions in neighboring regions [55].
Additionally, the integration of PtH and PtA steps adds layers of complexity to the project’s
risk landscape. The successful implementation of these steps relies on an intricate inter-
play between technology readiness, market demand and regulatory frameworks, further
heightening the project’s risk profile. Ensuring the timely readiness and optimal perfor-
mance of electrolysis facilities and ammonia production plants emerges as a critical prior-
ity, with delays posing significant implications for project economics and viability.
Understanding and mitigating these risks is critical for the Bornholm Energy Island project.
A comprehensive risk analysis is essential to identify potential challenges and develop
strategies to address them, ensuring project feasibility and long-term success. The techno-
economic analysis for Bornholm Energy Island transcends conventional parameters, re-
quiring a broader spectrum of assumptions. This expanded scope amplifies the project’s
overall risk profile, encompassing challenges related to schedule, budget and technical
solutions. In recognition of these complexities, a three-step risk analysis through differ-
ent scenarios has been undertaken to mitigate uncertainties and shed light on project
feasibility.

Preliminary examination limitations
• Arbitrary Value Selection: The choice of a 20% variation for the factors under ex-
amination is inherently arbitrary. This percentage was selected to provide a uniform
basis for comparison across different scenarios, but it may not reflect real-world
conditions accurately. The 20% figure is useful for illustrative purposes, showing
how variations can impact the business case quantitatively. However, actual vari-
ations could be higher or lower and the specific choice of 20% does not account
for the potential range of real-world deviations. Thus, while this approach provides
a clear framework for understanding potential impacts, it lacks specificity and may
oversimplify complex realities.
• Subjectivity in Risk Mapping: The risk mapping process involves a degree of sub-
jectivity, particularly in estimating the likelihood of each factor’s occurrence. The
likelihood ratings are based on reasonable assumptions derived from literature and
expert judgment, but they are not calculated using a precise, quantitative method.
This introduces an element of uncertainty into the risk assessment. Different stake-
holders or experts might have varying opinions on the probabilities of certain risks,
leading to potential discrepancies in the risk mapping outcomes. Despite these sub-
jective elements, the risk mapping serves as a valuable tool for highlighting areas
of potential concern and focusing attention on significant risks.
72
Department of Wind and Energy Systems
==PAGE:85==
• Extrapolation Methodology: The financial and production impacts were derived
through an extrapolation and rounding up methodology that converts risk impact
values from a percentage to a 1-5 scale, with the most severe impact assigned a
value of 5, as it is shown in the appendix A.3. This method simplifies the repre-
sentation of risks, making it easier to communicate the relative severity of different
factors. However, the rounding and scaling involved may introduce inaccuracies,
particularly when dealing with nonlinear relationships between variables. This ap-
proach is a preliminary one, designed to provide an initial, accessible overview of
potential impacts. While it facilitates risk visualization and comparison, it may not
fully capture the intricacies of each factor’s influence on the overall project.
• Simultaneous 20% Variation Assumption: The assumption that all factors will
experience a 20% variation simultaneously is a significant simplification. In reality,
different factors are likely to vary independently and to different extents. The si-
multaneous variation assumption helps to create a worst-case scenario, enabling
a robust comparison of the eleven factors and highlighting the potential maximum
impact on the project. However, this scenario is unlikely to occur exactly as mod-
eled. The real-world interactions between these factors are more complex and some
may change while others remain stable. Despite this limitation, the assumption is
useful for providing a comprehensive and extreme case analysis, which can help
investors understand the boundaries of risk and the resilience of the project under
highly adverse conditions.
• Simplified Risk Representation: The preliminary nature of this assessment means
that it uses simplified risk representation techniques. The extrapolation and risk
mapping methods, while effective for a broad overview, do not account for all possi-
ble interactions and dependencies between factors. For instance, certain risks might
be interrelated, amplifying or mitigating each other’s effects. These interdependen-
cies are not fully captured in the current model, which could lead to an underestima-
tion or overestimation of certain risks. Nonetheless, the simplified representation is
a practical starting point for investors, offering a clear and concise view of potential
risks and their impacts.
• Lack of External Factors Consideration: This examination does not account for
external factors that could influence the project. Political, economic, social and en-
vironmental changes could all affect the likelihood and impact of the risks analysed.
While the focus on internal project factors provides a controlled analysis, it limits
the scope of risk assessment. Incorporating external factors in future assessments
would provide a more holistic view of the project’s risk landscape.
Overall, these limitations highlight the preliminary nature of the assessment. Despite
these constraints, the analysis provides valuable quantitative insights into potential risks,
enabling investors to make informed decisions and identify areas requiring further de-
tailed study. The approach offers a foundation for understanding risk, facilitating initial
risk management strategies and prioritization in the early stages of project planning.

Conclusions
This thesis provides a comprehensive techno-economic analysis of the Bornholm Energy
Island and the implementation of PtX technologies. The analysis is structured into three
distinct steps: the Wind, the PtH and the PtA Case. By examining multiple scenarios,
including best-case and extreme cases, this study offers valuable insights into risk man-
agement and economic feasibility, directly addressing the research objectives outlined.
A key outcome of this analysis is the calculation of the levelised value for each of the three
steps. This metric serves as a critical benchmark for comparing the cost-effectiveness
of different energy generation methods, fulfilling the objective of exploring the synergies
between offshore wind power, hydrogen and ammonia production.
Key technical parameters were selected to quantitatively and graphically illustrate their
sensitivity within the cost production metrics, aligning with the objective of developing a
mathematical model to simulate different scenarios. The sensitivity analysis reveals the
substantial effect of these parameters on overall project feasibility and cost-effectiveness,
fulfilling the objective of understanding critical factors affecting feasibility.
The financial results reveal that the wind case step is far more feasible than the electrofuel
production steps. However, certain extreme scenarios can have a severe impact on the
overall feasibility of the project such as readiness delays of the technology implementation.
Within the electrofuel production steps, the PtA pathway appears slightly more feasible
than the PtH pathway across the five scenarios examined. Notably, the WACC has a
pronounced effect on the financial metrics, with the range provided in the business case
demonstrating the sensitivity of key financial parameters to changes in WACC.
The comparison with other initiatives, such as the Canary Island offshore hub, highlights
the influence of varying environmental, regulatory and economic conditions on the feasi-
bility of such projects. Although the projects compared differ in specific technical char-
acteristics, this analysis sheds light on how geographical context can impact the overall
business case. This comparison emphasizes the importance of contextual factors in deter-
mining project feasibility and provides a broader perspective on the potential challenges
and opportunities associated with offshore energy projects.
To advance beyond recent research, this thesis developed a preliminary methodology
and risk mapping to quantitatively evaluate the impact of various factors on the business
case results. This methodology offers stakeholders and investors a clearer understanding
of the risks involved, enabling more informed decision-making with a particular focus on
risk management. The risk mapping tool provides a visual representation of the potential
impacts, enhancing the ability to anticipate and mitigate risks effectively.
Future research should aim to refine the risk assessment methodology by incorporating
more precise likelihood calculations and expanding the range of scenarios considered.
Additionally, policymakers should consider the insights gained from this study to support
the development of robust regulatory frameworks and financial incentives that can en-
hance the feasibility of PtX projects. Collaboration between industry, universities and
government will be crucial in driving innovation and ensuring the successful implementa-
tion of large-scale renewable energy initiatives like the Bornholm Energy Island.

A.3
Key factors description
Table A.1: Description of key factors and parameters: Impact and stakeholder perspec-
tives
C1. Wind speed variability
Ensuring stability in offshore wind speeds is crucial for mitigating risk and enhancing project fea-
sibility. The relatively consistent wind conditions at offshore locations, due to fewer obstacles
(e.g., onshore terrain features) and more predictable atmospheric conditions, offer a more sta-
ble energy generation profile for investors. This stability can ensure consistent energy output
over time, reducing the impact of wind speed variability on project feasibility. To further ensure
stability, investors can leverage advanced forecasting techniques and historical data analysis.
These methods help predict wind speed patterns and mitigate risks associated with offshore
wind speed variability, despite its inherent unpredictability as a natural resource.
C2. Electrolyser load factor fluctuations
The load factor indicates average power demand relative to peak demand. Understanding
the correlation between electrolyser load factor and wind capacity factor is crucial for optimal
sizing and efficient operation. In this regard, considering an installed renewable capacity of
3GW, a 1.5 GW electrolyser capacity can achieve a load factor over 0.55, whereas a 150 MW
electrolyser can achieve the maximum load factor. Therefore, investors can strategically select
the electrolyser size based on anticipated power demand patterns and system requirements to
optimize performance and enhance project feasibility.
C3. Electrolyser efficiency variation
The overall efficiency of the electrolyser installation is the combined effect of stack, system
and plant efficiencies. Therefore, electrolyser efficiency is influenced by various factors, in-
cluding technology design and operating conditions. Real-world performance may differ from
theoretical efficiency values, so analysis based on a realistic efficiency range highlights the im-
portance of comprehensive optimization strategies to maximize efficiency and minimize energy
consumption. By carefully assessing the following key parameters, investors can maximize
efficiency and minimize energy consumption:
• Stability of energy supply: Offshore wind power provides more stability compared to on-
shore sources, leading to higher electrolyser efficiency due to reduced fluctuations.
• Pressure considerations: Low-pressure electrolysers are less costly than high-pressure
ones of the same size. So, choosing the appropriate pressure level based on the appli-
cation can enhance overall cost-effectiveness.
• Temperature management: Higher operating temperatures lead to higher efficiency in
electrolysers. Optimizing temperature conditions can significantly improve performance.
• Current density optimization: Low current density results in higher electrolyser voltage
efficiency. Balancing current density to achieve optimal voltage efficiency is crucial.
• Electrode arrangement: Reducing the distance between electrodes can increase cell
voltage efficiency by minimizing the distance charge carriers must travel. However, this
also raises the risk of short circuits and gas mixing.

C4. Wind turbine capacity factor uncertainty
Capacity factor is a critical parameter influenced by various factors such as maintenance prac-
tices, environmental conditions and technology specifications. These factors directly affect en-
ergy generation and project profitability. Investors recognise the importance of selecting tur-
bine technologies that not only align with project requirements but also optimize performance
under prevailing conditions. By leveraging advanced turbine designs, incorporating predictive
maintenance strategies and considering site-specific factors, investors can enhance turbine per-
formance. This proactive approach not only mitigates uncertainties but also improves project
feasibility and long-term profitability in offshore wind energy ventures, in spite of the uncontrol-
lable factors subject to natural resource.
C5. WACC fluctuations
WACC serves as a crucial financial parameter which reflects the cost of financing a project
and is subjected to annual fluctuations influenced by industry conditions and perceived risk.
These fluctuations underscore the dynamic nature of project financing and its implications for
investment value over time. While sensitivity analysis of WACC values range offers valuable
insights along the three main steps project under various economic scenarios, it is important
to note that from an investor’s perspective, WACC is a financial parameter uncontrollable and
it can fluctuate throughout the project’s time frame. However, conducting sensitivity analysis
that quantifies the impact of a specific increment provides stakeholders with essential insights
into the potential impact on financial performance, enabling informed decision-making and risk
assessment. It is crucial to note that WACC is influenced by the location of the project. Setting
up an energy island in a Spanish location may be perceived as riskier compared to a Danish
location due to factors such as regulatory environment, market conditions and economic stability.
This geographical consideration adds more complexity to the risk assessment process.
C6. OPEX variation
Operating expenses, influenced by location-specific factors such as labour costs and regulatory
requirements, directly impact project economics. Adjusting OPEX assumptions based on cost-
of-living differentials between locations allows for more accurate financial modeling and risk
assessment. Lower OPEX estimates for Gran Canaria reflect the relative cost advantages of
operating in that region. From an investor’s perspective, OPEX represents a factor that can be
controlled to some extent. By carefully considering the location and its associated cost advan-
tages or disadvantages, investors can make informed decisions regarding OPEX projections.
C7. Electricity price fluctuations
Investors play a crucial role in agreeing on prices with the production company through fixed
compensation mechanisms (PPAs, CfDs, FITs, IRA, etc.). However, considering the assump-
tion in the techno-economic analysis, the price forecast for 20 years provided by Energinet will
be the fixed price set. Therefore, based on the assumption, the electricity price forecast, de-
rived from technical reports, significantly influences project feasibility and financial performance
across all project stages. Given this fixed compensation agreement, the price limitations guar-
antee revenues without taking into account the real market prices making the business less
risky. So, the project can maximize revenues such as by increasing the amount of electricity
produced, but that is related to technical parameters not financial ones.

C8. Delayed readiness
Project delays are subject to supply chain interruptions, pose significant risks to project
timelines and financial performance. A delay, not only leads to delayed revenues but
also incurs additional management costs, so it has a considerable negative impact
on overall project economy. While stakeholders can implement measures to expedite
processes and minimize delays, the extent to which project readiness is extended until
full operation is achieved remains uncertain. Consequently, investors must carefully
assess and manage the inherent risks associated with project delays to protect their
investments and ensure project success.
C9. Electrofuel demand reduction
Market dynamics, such as economic downturns or changes in policy, may lead to reduced
demand for electrofuels. Decreased demand affects revenue projections and overall project
viability, emphasizing the importance of scenario analysis and risk mitigation strategies to adapt
to changing market conditions. Implementing scenario analysis allows investors to anticipate
and assess the potential impact of fluctuating demand on project economy, enabling them to
adjust strategies accordingly.
C10. Electrofuel price reduction
Based on the assumption made in the electricity price agreement, in the worst-case scenario of
a drop in electrofuel prices, it is possible that the agreed-upon fixed price could be lower than the
forecasts provided by technical reports. This situation could arise from negotiations with energy
producers who decide to set a lower price due to potential economies of scale. Forecasted
reductions in electrofuel production costs, attributable to economies of scale and technological
advancements, have the potential to drive down selling prices in the market. These price drops
can significantly impact revenue projections and overall profitability, highlighting the importance
of closely monitoring market trends and competitive dynamics.
C11. Electrolyser CAPEX increment
Unexpected increases in electrolyser CAPEX can have a moderate impact on project feasibility,
potentially affecting the overall economics of the project. However, it is important to note that
potential cost reductions resulting from economies of scale may help offset these impacts over
time. From an investor perspective, it is crucial to conduct thorough due diligence and risk
assessment to anticipate and mitigate the effects of CAPEX fluctuations. This includes closely
monitoring market trends, technological advancements and supply chain dynamics that may
influence electrolyser costs.
