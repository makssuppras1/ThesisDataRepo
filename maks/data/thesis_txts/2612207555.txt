Abstract
Cardiovascular diseases have been a leading cause of death for decades and continue until now
to aﬀect millions of people worldwide. In our times, biomedical research advances with swift
pace and the last year more than ever this fact has been proved life-saving due to COVID-19
pandemic. This new era demands a new approach to medical treatment, making use of the
latest technological advancements.
Next-generation sequencing is a powerful tool that keeps
evolving and can add a great value in human genetics research. This project presents a long-read
sequencing approach of a standard transcriptomic analysis. A gene called RBM20, associated
with the cardiac disease phenotype, is to be further investigated. This particular study involves
rats, as model organisms, carrying a loss-of-function mutation of RBM20 and thus expected
to present a phenotype related to this deﬁciency. RBM20 operates as an alternative splicing
regulator that regulates titin, a sarcomere protein, which has shown to be associated with a
plethora of cardiac diseases, including atrial ﬁbrillation. This study provides useful insights into
the sensitive experimental process that is crucial for eﬃcient transcriptomic long-read sequencing.
Moreover, a thorough data analysis framework recommended by Oxford Nanopore Technologies
is tested for diﬀerential transcript usage and diﬀerential expression analysis. The results point
towards changes in both transcript and protein level that could cause a disease phenotype in rats
carrying the mutation. However, since the experimental process does not include ampliﬁcation
aiming to reduce PCR bias, the available material is insuﬃcient to provide statistically robust
results rather than a broader picture that needs to be veriﬁed with more precise tools.

Introduction 1
1.1
Heart and Sarcomere
1.1.1
The human heart
The structure of the human heart has been thoroughly described over the years, with four
distinctive chambers, two atria and two ventricles as depicted in Figure 1.1. The right side of the
heart, composed of the right atrium connected with the right ventricle with the tricuspid valve,
is responsible for receiving the deoxygenated blood from the systemic circulation and pumping
it through the pulmonary artery, to the lungs to be reoxygenated. The left side of the heart,
composed of the left atrium connected with the left ventricle with the mitral valve, is responsible
for receiving the oxygenated blood from the lungs, and pumping it across the whole human body
through the aorta [1].
Figure 1.1: Human heart section visualising all the chambers [1]
In Figure 1.2, the pulmonary and the systemic circulation are schematically illustrated,
emphasising that the coordinated function of all the individual components of the heart is crucial
for human survival. It should be noticed that both atria are chambers that do not develop high
pressure compared to the ventricles due to their functional role; hence their walls appear thinner
and with less muscle tissue.

1.1.2
Sarcomeric structure
Human heart functions in a complex yet perfectly coordinated way. To further understand it,
it is necessary to observe the diﬀerent layers that compose it. From the outer part to the inner
part, there is the epicardium, the myocardium and the endocardium as illustrated in Figure 1.3.
There is also a surrounding layer of the heart and roots of great vessels called pericadium. It is
apparent that myocardium composes proportionally the greatest part and thus it is considered
its principal component.
To further understand heart’s complex yet perfectly coordinated function, it is necessary to
observe the diﬀerent layers that compose the heart and, therefore, focus on its principal
component, the muscular part known as myocardium, illustrated in ﬁgure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: Heart layers and pericardium[1]
Myocardium consists of cardiac muscle cells, also known as cardiomyocytes, which reside in the
wall of the heart as well as the base of the large veins that empty in the heart. These cells
contain several myoﬁbrils, which are long structures of multiple sarcomeres. The sarcomere is
myocyte’s smallest functional unit. As shown in Figure 1.4, is a repetitive structure of striated
muscles. Further deconstruction shows that it is a network of ﬁlaments, thick and thin ones,
and regulatory proteins, whose role is to generate contraction forces and pump the blood [1][2].
Thick ﬁlaments are composed of myosin II while thin ﬁlaments are mainly composed of actin.

Among other regulatory proteins involved,there is titin (TTN) which plays a critical role in the
mechanical support and passive stiﬀness of the sarcomere by anchoring the thick ﬁlaments on

1.2
Titin protein (TTN)
Titin (TTN) is the largest known protein, and its crucial role in providing elasticity was described
more than thirty years ago [5]. TTN derives after the expression of a gene which is 294 kilo-bases
long, producing 363 coding exons that can be transcribed into an mRNA molecule at least 100
kilo-bases long [6]. Instead, several splice isoforms of titin have been reported, with two of them
being more abundant in cardiomyocytes, N2BA and N2B, illustrated in Figure 1.5 [6][7][4]. This
fact points towards a highly complex splicing mechanism and, as a consequence, high association
with various human cardiac diseases [8][9][10].

1.3
RNA Binding Motif Protein 20 (RBM20)
Gene regulation for eukaryotes, especially vertebrates, is complex machinery which requires the
cooperation of various elements such as regulatory proteins and non-coding RNA molecules.
Processing of pre-mRNA, through mechanisms such as splicing and polyadenylation, is carried
out by RNA Binding Proteins (RBPs) and trans-acting RNAs [11]. The RBM20 belongs in
these regulatory proteins and, by observing its structure, it is possible to further understand its
function. RBM20 resides on chromosome 10 and consists of 14 exons, which produce a protein
of 1,227 amino acids. This RBP is encountered only in vertebrates and its functional role is to
regulate alternative splicing [12][13]. In 2012, Guo et al., revealed that RBM20 is involved in the
alternative splicing of several cardiac genes, including TTN [8].
The conserved regions of the human RBM20 protein are illustrated in section A of Figure 1.6.
Whereas in section B, an alignment of an arginine/serine(RS)-rich region between several species
is presented. The highlighted region has been strongly associated with missense mutations that
lead to both familial and sporadic dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) [14][15].
Figure 1.6: RBM20 structure [12]
1.4
Atrial ﬁbrillation
Atrial ﬁbrillation (AF) is the most common type of arrhythmia, aﬀecting more than 33,5 million
people worldwide [16]. It is associated with high morbidity and mortality, mostly attributed to
the signiﬁcantly increased risk of stroke, heart failure, and premature death [17][16].
An episode of AF is characterised by rapid and irregular atrial electrical activation, which leads
to an overall irregular heart rate, hence heart ﬁbrillation. Focal ectopic activity and formations
of re-entry circuits are the fundamental triggering mechanisms. AF as a disease is caused by
genetics, age, environmental factors, and underlying cardiovascular diseases [18][19].
Diagnosis and therapy of AF constitute a complex issue and a signiﬁcant burden on the health
care system. AF is associated with frequent hospitalisation, limited eﬃcacy treatments, and
signiﬁcant adverse eﬀects of antiarrhythmic drugs [17][19].

1.5. Next Generation Sequencing - Oxford Nanopore Technologies
1.5
Next Generation Sequencing - Oxford Nanopore
Technologies
Nanopore sequencing is perhaps the most promising technology of third-generation sequencing
development. It came as a branch of a broader ﬁeld using nanopores to detect and quantify
various biological and chemical molecules.
The prospects of nanopore sequencing were ﬁrst
demonstrated before second-generation sequencing had emerged when researchers showed that
single-stranded DNA or RNA could be driven across a lipid bilayer through large α-hemolysin
ion channels by electrophoresis. Furthermore, passage through the channel obstructs ion ﬂow,
reducing the current for a length of time proportional to the nucleic acid’s length. It is also
possible to use non-biological, solid-state technology to generate suitable nanopores [20].
Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) was the ﬁrst company oﬀering nanopore sequencers and
has generated a great deal of excitement over their ﬁrst nanopore platform MinION, a pocket-
sized USB device, which was ﬁrst released in an early access trial in 2014. The MinION, due to
its portability, oﬀered the opportunity to be deployed in the ﬁeld, as proved by Joshua Quick
and Nicholas Loman in 2015 when they sequenced Ebola viruses in Guinea two days after sample
collection [21].
Figure 1.7: MinION (Oxford Nanopore Technologies, UK)
Since then, ONT continued in developing both bench-top and portable sequencers and other
related products. The scaled-up GridION was commercially launched in 2017 and PromethION
in 2018, with the largest device, the PromethION 48, ﬁrst shipped in 2019.
The nanopore
sequencing technology and the available platforms have enabled the generation of long reads
which is crucial for ameliorating de novo assembly. Moreover, achieving long reads allows progress
in understanding highly complex and repetitive regions in genomes that are otherwise hard to
resolve.
In general, long-read sequencing oﬀers increased resolution compared to short-read

In the early years, nanopore technology’s main drawback was that the throughput and accuracy
had to be signiﬁcantly improved. Recent studies proved that after improvements to the protein

been improved. The sequencing and assembly of a reference genome for the human GM12878

1.6
Previous Work
During the last decade, several studies have demonstrated that titin mutations or mutations that
lead to titin-truncating variants (TTNtv) cause cardiac diseases, with dilated cardiomyopathy
being the most common one [9][10]. More recently, whole-exome sequencing studies of families
with diagnosed AF and genome-wide association studies (GWAS) linked familial and early-onset
AF with rare truncating variants of titin protein [24][25]. At the same time, another GWAS study
linked AF and RBM20 [26]. As already stated, RBM20 has been found to aﬀect the alternative
splicing of the titin protein. A loss-of-function, 95-kb frame-shift mutation of RBM20 has been
shown to hinder titin splicing, resulting in abnormal isoform expression.
The heterozygotes
carrying the mutation express the N2BA isoform predominantly, while the homozygotes express
an extraordinary long isoform, called N2BA-G [8][12]. In 2020, a DCM study suggested that other
RBM20 splicing targets might contribute to the diseased phenotype [13]. Another study reported
AF phenotype in two groups of mice, one carrying a missense mutation in the RSRSP amino
acid region of RBM20 and the other one carrying a knockout mutation of RBM20. However, it
is not clear what the underlying mechanism is, which caused the speciﬁc phenotype [15]. Finally,
unpublished data from our group indicate that RBM20 rare variants aﬀect TTN splicing and
might link to AF.
1.7
Aim of the study
Based on all the aforementioned, it is evident that the correlation of atrial ﬁbrillation and RBM20
through titin is yet to be revealed. Interestingly, we have unpublished genetic data from our own
AF patients suggesting that RBM20 also play a role in AF pathogenesis. Hence, we believe
that a better understanding of the role of RMB20 in atrial function can aid us in our quest to
understand AF pathogenesis in greater detail.
This master’s thesis project aims to investigate the eﬀects of RBM20 loss-of-function at
transcriptional and expressional level. To achieve this, samples of atrial tissue, acquired from
RBM20-deﬁcient rats, will be analysed using long-read sequencing technology and will be
compared to atrial tissue from wild type rats. We aim to understand the changes that occur in
the left atrial tissue as it is reported to be structurally more aﬀected in AF patients with TTNtv
[27].

Materials & Methods 2
2.1
Tissue characteristics
During the experimental process, tissue of human and rat origin was used. The protocol was
initially tested on human heart tissue due to the higher amount of tissue available in the
laboratory. After the optimisation process, rat heart atrial tissue was used. As controls, ﬁve
unrelated Sprague Dawley rats were used, referred as wild type (WT) and 13 descendants of
Norwegian Brown rats outcrossed with Sprague Dawley rats; eight heterozygotes (RBM20+/-)
and ﬁve homozygotes (RBM20-/-) carriers of the investigated loss-of-function mutation. This
particular mutation results from a 95-kb deletion at the RBM20 gene, which removes the exons
2-14 [8].
2.2
RNA isolation
2.2.1
Total RNA
RNA was isolated from tissues kept at -80°, and puriﬁed using TRIzol Reagent (Thermo Fischer
Scientiﬁc, USA) combined with the RNeasy Micro kit (Qiagen, Germany). TRIzol maintains
the integrity of RNA, due to its inhibitory action against RNases, while disruption of cells is
performed by homogenisation. Addition of chloroform follows, which separates the solution in
two phases: the upper aqueous phase contains the RNA, and the lower organic phase contains
the DNA and the proteins. The supernatant containing the RNA is used for further processing
with the RNeasy Micro kit.
It has to be noted that initially only the RNeasy Micro kit (Qiagen, Germany) was used, following
the manufacturer’s guidelines. However, during the optimisation process, the addition of TRIzol
resulted in signiﬁcantly higher quality (See Section 3.1), so a combination was ﬁnally used as
described below.
Detailed experimental process
In a 1.5 ml RNase-free Eppendorf containing the desired amount of tissue (≈10 mg of heart
tissue), 250 ul of Trizol were added. The tissue was homogenised using a hand homogeniser until
there were no visible tissue parts. A total of 250 ul of Trizol was added again, followed by further
homogenisation. The sample was incubated for 5 minutes at room temperature. Then, 100 ul
of chloroform were added, and the tube was intensely shaken for 1 minute. The sample was
incubated for 3 minutes at room temperature. Spinning down follows, at 10.000 g, at 4 °C for
7
==PAGE:19==
2. Materials & Methods
20 minutes. The supernatant was carefully transferred to an RNase-free Eppendorf tube (≈200
ul). An equal volume of absolute ethanol was added slowly, and the sample was transferred to
an RNeasy column. Spinning down followed, at 10.000 g, for 15 seconds. Then, 350 ul of RW1
buﬀer were added, and the sample was spun down again, at 10.000 g, for 15 seconds. RNase-free
DNase, 10 ul vials were used at this point, after being mixed carefully with 70 ul of RDD buﬀer
in a separate tube. The 80 ul mixture was added directly on the column’s membrane, followed
by incubation at room temperature for 15 minutes. Then, 350 ul of RW1 buﬀer were added, and
the sample was spun down, at 10.000 g, for 15 seconds. The sample was further washed with
500 ul of RPE buﬀer, and spun down, at 10.000 g, for 15 seconds. Finally, 500 ul 80% ethanol,
freshly made, were added on the column, followed by spinning down, at 10.000 g, for 2 minutes.
The RNeasy column was transferred to a clean collection tube and was centrifuged for 5 minutes,
at 13.000 g. The sample was eluted in 15 ul of RNase-free water directly added to the column
and incubated for 1 minute at room temperature. The column was centrifuged for 60 seconds,
at 10.000 g. The total RNA was collected in the ﬂow-through.
Subsequently, the quality and quantity of the RNA were accessed with Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer,
using the Agilent RNA 6000 Nano kit (Agilent Technologies, USA). A RIN threshold, above which
a sample was used for library preparation was set. RNA derived from bovine heart tissue has
been shown to deviated slightly around 6 [28], hence the minimum accepted RIN was set at 5.

Before the library preparation, it is necessary to select,the messenger RNA from the total RNA,

In this project, the selection of poly(A)+ RNA molecules was performed using Lexogen’s Poly(A)
Selection RNA Kit (Lexogen GmbH, Vienna, Austria). The manufacturer estimates the expected
recovery percentage to 1-3% of the input.

The library preparation begins with the addition of poly(T) primers which anneal to the poly(A)+
RNA input to initiate the reverse transcription, followed by the addition of strand switching
primers to create an initiation point for the second strand synthesis.
The sample is treated
with an RNase solution to digest the remaining RNA, and the second strand of the cDNA is
synthesised with the addition of high-ﬁdelity DNA polymerase. Then, the ends are repaired
during the end-prep process in order for them to have 5’ prime end phosphorylated and 3’ prime
end dA-tailed [31]. At this step, the cDNA concentration is determined with Qubit Fluorometer
(Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc, USA) using the DNA HS Assay Kit (Thermo Fischer Scientiﬁc, USA).

The particular protocol oﬀers the possibility of multiplexing up to twenty-six samples in one
sequencing run, and this requires the ﬂanking of the cDNA fragments between distinctive short
sequences before pooling them together in one library. The Native Barcoding Expansion 1-12
(EXP-NBD104) kit was only required in this case due to the number of samples. It is important
to mention that both the barcoding kits associated with this protocol oﬀer the advantage of
being PCR-free methods.
After barcoding, each sample is tested for quality and quantity with Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer,
using the Agilent High Sensitivity DNA kit as instructed by the manufacturer (Agilent
Technologies, USA). It is important to consider the concentration and the molarity of each
barcoded sample and pool them together without exceeding the upper threshold of 200 fmol in
total, for the whole library.
Once all the samples are pooled together, the adapter protein has to be ligated at both ends
of each molecule. After ligation, the library concentration is measured to ensure the amount of
mass loaded on the ﬂowcell for sequencing. The loading of the ﬂowcell is performed according
to the instructions using the EXP-FLP002 priming mix.

2.4.1
Basecalling
After completion of the sequencing, the output format is .fast5. This type of ﬁle contains all
the information on the ionic current ﬂuctuations, which are the ones to be translated into the
equivalent nucleotide.
To achieve this, these data have to undergo the basecalling process.
Basecalling is oﬀered either by a MinKNOW build-in and real-time function, but also as a
separate process after sequencing is completed. In this case, basecalling was performed manually
after sequencing was completed. The software used was Guppy Basecalling Software: Version
4.4.0+3a263d4b1 (Oxford Nanopore Technologies, UK) and the run was performed on an Ubuntu
18.04 personal computer following the instructions for the GPU option for faster results [32]. The
system speciﬁcations are a 8-core Intel Core i9-10980HK CPU with 32GB RAM equipped with an
Nvidia GeForce RTX 2070 Super Max-Q GPU and 1TB OS and data disk. Each run’s duration
was between 4-8 hours depending on the size of the sequenced library. The command below
shows the exact parameters used for the basecalling.
guppy_basecaller --input_path /rawdata --save_path .. --flowcell FLO-MIN106 --kit
SQK-DCS109 --qscore_filtering --min_qscore 7 --progress_stats_frequency 120
--barcode_kits EXP-NBD104 --trim_barcodes -r -x

2.4. Data acquisition and analysis
Command line
The basecalling process creates a folder with all the reads that have passed the quality threshold.
The reads are further separated according to the detected barcode. Each barcode folder contains
several fastq ﬁles which can be concatenated and compressed into one ﬁle for further analysis.
The speciﬁc command used for the concatenation is presented below.
cat fastq_runid_* > barcodeXX_chipXX_sampleID.fastq.gz
The analysis is performed according to the Oxford Nanopore’s cDNA transcriptome tutorial,
which includes both diﬀerential gene expression (DGE) and diﬀerential transcript usage (DTU)
analyses [33]. Thus, following these guidelines, all the compressed ﬁles created in the previous
step (one ﬁle for each barcode) should be collected in a new folder called RawData. Moreover,
the conﬁguration ﬁle provided should be modiﬁed to match the data of our experiment. Once
all the modiﬁcations are completed for the tutorial to ﬁt the particular analysis, it is initiated
by executing only one command, which is presented below.
snakemake -j 4
This command is part of Snakemake, a workﬂow management system implemented in Python
[34]. The snakemake workﬂow ﬁle of the speciﬁc tutorial follows a straightforward array of com-
monly used methods and tools. First, it downloads the reference transcriptome and the genome
annotations, speciﬁed in the conﬁguration ﬁle. In our case these were, ftp://ftp.ensembl.org/
pub/release-97/fasta/rattus_norvegicus/cdna/Rattus_norvegicus.Rnor_6.0.cdna.all.fa.
gz and ftp://ftp.ensembl.org/pub/release-97/gtf/rattus_norvegicus/Rattus_norvegicus.
Rnor_6.0.97.gtf.gz, respectively. Then it calls, a tool called minimap2 which indexes the ref-
erence genome and maps the cDNA reads against this reference transcriptome index [35]. The
output of minimap2 is a SAM ﬁle and thus to facilitate the downstream analysis, it is converted
into a BAM ﬁle using the tool, called samtools [36].
Afterwards, samtools ﬂagstat function
is used to create a summary of mapping statistics, and a tool called salmon is used to count
the cDNA sequence reads assigned to individual transcripts or gene isoforms [37][38]. All the
aforementioned steps produce output ﬁles which will be visualised in RStudio.
RStudio
For this experiment, the option of extracting the R code from the Rmarkdown ﬁle was used with
the purl command.
knitr::purl("Nanopore_Transcriptome_Tutorial.Rmd", quiet=TRUE)

Post sequencing analysis
When it comes to diﬀerential gene expression analysis, it is a common practice to analyse 10-
30 million reads per sample. This is not the case in this project due to the PCR-free method
used, and the long-read technology throughput estimated to be between 106-107 reads. Hence,
samples with the same genotype and age were merged, attempting to reach the threshold of one
million reads. A million reads have been shown to be similarly representative of the transcript
abundance of highly expressed genes to those from thirty million reads [39].

Discussion 4
4.1
General observations
4.1.1
From tissue to sequencing run
During this study, it became clearer that experimental design might be a key element of a
successful project but it is also optimisation of processes and troubleshooting that add great
value and experience. In this study, unexpected obstacles were encountered, which in order to
be handled, further research and more laboratory tests were conducted to reassure that the rat
tissue samples will be used in the best possible way. To begin with, the RNA extraction gave
poor results and another approach had to be seeked. Once the protocol was optimised in a way
more suitable for limited amount of heart tissue, it was tested several times in another heart
tissue to verify that the RNA yield and quality was improved.
The next obstacle was the library preparation. Initially, total RNA was used which was quickly
characterised as ineﬃcient and, subsequently, a poly(A)+ RNA selection step was added. During
the library preparation, the maintenance of high quality unfragmented genetic material was
the most tricky part and it was handled with several quality control steps.
Although ONT
recommendations include some quality control steps, an extra assessment of cDNA with Qubit
Fluorometer was added, using the DNA HS Assay Kit.
That step ensured that the cDNA
library will have the proper concentration and molarity to avoid overloading of the sequencing
ﬂowcell. Finally, after the ﬁrst sequencing run of twelve multiplexed samples, it was decided
that multiplexing six samples is a better option since it would allow higher concentration of each
sample.
4.1.2
Sequencing output without ampliﬁcation
As already mentioned in Section 3.3, in order to produce the least biased results, no PCR was
used to amplify the isolated genetic material.
In this study, where the available tissue was
only enough for one sequencing run of each sample, the trade-oﬀwas the number of sequenced
reads. ONT produces fewer sequenced reads per run, estimated between 106-107 reads [39]. The
output of each sample for this project ranged between roughly 300,000 - 3,000,000 unﬁltered
reads, limiting the experiment’s resolution.
This problem was partially tackled by merging
samples with the same genotype and age. Nevertheless, this option was not always a possibility,
sometimes resulting in a partial loss of data.
29
==PAGE:41==
4. Discussion
4.1.3
Quality control of reads indicates fragmentation
When conducting experimental processes involving RNA handling, it is recommended to avoid
unnecessary delays to prevent RNA degradation. Moreover, RNA-free environment and materials
should be ensured for better results. The particular experiment had a time-frame of ﬁve and
a half months during COVID-19 pandemic. Unfortunately, this brought unpredictable delays
that, combined with regular laboratory oversights, might have resulted in partial fragmentation
of the RNA, as it is demonstrated in Sections 3.3.1 and 3.3.2. However, it should be noted that
the samples were quality tested in various steps of the library preparation and samples that did
not qualify for further analysis were discarded.
4.1.4
Diﬀerential gene expression of numerous genes in both LA and RA
analyses
The analyses were performed based on an ONT framework. The outputs report several genes
that show signiﬁcant diﬀerential expression in the mutated LA and RA tissue. According to
Ensembl and RGB, the top twenty hits in both studies indicate individuals under stress with
conditions such as anaemia, skeletal, metabolic and immune disorders with FDR<3.392034e-05
and FDR<1.0008377e-09 respectively. Moreover, RA top hits (see Section 3.3.2) included titin
cap which was marked as up-regulated under RBM20 deﬁciency. The produced datasets should
be thoroughly investigated as more genes not presented in the top lists could be signiﬁcantly up-
or down-regulated.
4.1.5
Diﬀerential transcript usage appears to be limited
This part of the analysis reported limited results, with only few genes with maximum two
transcripts showing a diﬀerent proﬁle between the tested conditions.
After running stageR,
few more genes appeared to have transcripts with diﬀerent abundances in both LA and RA
dataset. These genes and the according transcripts need to be further investigated, something
that was not possible due to time restrictions of this project.
4.1.6
Inspection of the raw mapped reads for genes of interest revealed
interesting trends
Since the statistical tests produced narrow results (see Section 3.3), a manual inspection of the
mapped raw reads was conducted, aiming to observe the relative expression of RBM20 and
genes reported to be regulated by RBM20. There is no golden standard for normalisation when
it comes to RNA-seq data. Since the knowledge on the expected expression levels is minimal, a
superﬁcial investigation was performed, aiming to understand the diﬀerent levels of expression
for each individual gene between the three genotypes of the experiment. The read counts were
normalised to facilitate the comparison and plotted according to the genotype of the initial
library. This method resulted in exciting patterns. For both LA and RA libraries, there is an
apparent down-regulation of RBM20 in homozygous rats for the loss-of-function mutation. In
addition, the two transcripts that are annotated as TTN and the transcript annotated as RYR2

down-regulation in the homozygous rats which is consistent with the ﬁndings of Guo et al. [8].
The SORBS1 in RA libraries shows great variation between individuals. CAMK2G, CAMK2D,
SH3KPB, CACNA1C and TRDN do not seem to follow a clear trend in both libraries.
All the aforementioned are based on interpretation of raw reads which is not safe by itself. Thus,
further and possibly more targeted investigation is recommended.
4.2
Limitations
The current project aimed to investigate the eﬀects of a loss-of-function mutation using rat tissue
as a model organism. This fact itself brings the most signiﬁcant limitation, especially considering
the fact that collaborating laboratories supply the rats. First, the tissue is limited for a project
designed to last less than six months, so it is diﬃcult to receive more tissue if something goes
wrong. On top of that, the amount of atrial tissue from young rats often is a bottleneck and
maintaining a suﬃcient amount throughout the process is nearly impossible.
The next most signiﬁcant limitation is that the whole analysis is based on one annotated rat
transcriptome, meaning that if the investigated gene is poorly annotated, which happened with
TTN annotations, the whole analysis might fail to provide safe results.
Other more generalised limitations include the extrapolation of results from model organisms to
human biology and technical issues such as bias related to polyadenylated RNA selection.
4.3
Future work
Undoubtedly, a correlation between RBM20 and AF underlies and deserves to be revealed. First
of all, there is space for future work on this dataset, which includes using a custom annotation
of TTN by liftover, which could drastically shift the experiment results.
Additionally, a repetition of the presented pipeline could be promising, given that there is an
abundance of fresh atrial tissue to analyse. Moreover, another approach potentially not as cost-
eﬀective but more suitable for handling RNA could be the direct RNA sequencing in combination
with rRNA depletion instead of polyA selection. This method could capture and analyse RNA
data with the least possible bias without eliminating RNA molecules that are not polyA-tailed
and might hold vital information.
Furthermore, using diﬀerent model organisms could beneﬁt in various ways; for example,
zebraﬁsh could be more ﬂexible in replicating mutations, whereas pigs could produce more
translatable results for humans.
4.4
Conclusion
Overall, the goal of this study to enhance our knowledge of the eﬀects of RBM20 deﬁciency was
reached. The produced data illustrated changes that align with the relevant literature. RBM20
