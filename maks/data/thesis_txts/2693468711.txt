Abstract
This thesis focused on the Photoluminescence Imaging for Diagnostics of PV Modules. The aim of
the thesis was to characterize the different sources of illumination available and ﬁgure out what works.
This involved performing PL imaging tests for both indoors and outdoors. The outdoor tests in-
volved testing out the 2 approaches, contact PL imaging and the contactless PL method. The PL tests
have been carried out on both the individual module level and the string level in the PV plant.
The work to be carried out was done in stages beginning with a detailed understanding of how Pho-
toluminescence works. Different light sources like the ﬂashlight, halogen lightbulbs and the LED
Theatre Lights when performing indoor diagnosis. The outdoor PL tests were done with 2 tech-
niques. One technique, the contact PL method involved the use of modulation. The other technique
used the contactless manipulation of the modules to perform imaging.
The images were then processed using the analysis shell software and the ImageJ software using
the Denoisin By Averaging, Fast Fourier Transform and Standard Deviation methods. The results
about the various techniques involved when conducting the tests are discussed in detail.

1
Introduction
Climate change has become an existential crises today and going forward the world at large needs
to move away from fossil fuels based energy sources and towards renewable forms of energy. The
landmark special report in 2018 about the impacts of global warming of 1.5◦C above pre-industrial
levels and related global greenhouse gas emissions by the Intergovernmental panel on climate change
(IPCC) relayed about the dangers of exceeding the 1.5◦C warming to the world. The newly formed
government of Denmark has set very ambitious renewable energy goals, to reduce Denmark’s green-
house gas emissions by 70% in 2030 compared with 1990 levels[1] . Achieving this goal will require
signiﬁcantly more renewable energy generating capacities to be installed in the danish energy grid.
The increasingly ambitious goals being set by countries to cut their carbon emissions and becoming
carbon neutral means an exponential increase in the renewable energy generation. This translates to
more and more solar plants being installed both in Denmark and around the world. It brings on a new
challenge for the operations and maintenance industry, servicing these large pv plants with installed
capacity at gigawatt scale as seen with the opening of the Bhadla solar park in India with a capacity
of 2245 MW[2]. Pavagada solar plant in India with an installed capacity of 2050 MW is expected
to provide electricity to 700,000 households[3]. This showcases how many households rely on it for
their daily electricity needs. PV plants at such a large scale need to ensure power production and
cannot have unexpected down time or decreased production as they provide energy to a large number
of people. It is imperative that the modules being installed are in good shape and the solar cells have
continuous operation.
1.1
Project Background
Luminescence imaging techniques are widely in use today for performing diagnosis on the PV panels
as they help in understanding the faults that are present both module wide and for the individual solar
cell. Defects present in the PV modules must be detected in time to make sure that the operation of
the PV plant runs smooth and the electricity generation of the plant is optimal. There are two main
methods of detecting defects in the module i.e. electroluminescence and photoluminescence. The
electroluminescence or EL is well established for indoors and for nighttime imaging [4]. Photolumi-
nescence or PL can offer different diagnosis information and has the advantage of being potentially
contactless[5]. A contactless way of imaging for fault detection can speed up the diagnosis and can
simplify the imaging procedure. The night-time EL imaging technique [4] and contactless PL imaging
technique[6] for fault detection are in use already.
1.1.1
Electroluminescence
Electroluminescence is a light emission due to the recombination of carriers and holes which happens
when electricity is supplied to the PV panel. This signal can be sensed by an external sensor like
a silicon charge-coupled device or InGaAs camera[6]. When performing indoor EL imaging, the
equipment required are the PV panel, a camera and a simple power supply unit to set the voltage at
open-circuit so as to not exceed the short-circuit current according to the panel’s speciﬁcations. EL
imaging can be performed with and without ﬁlters as the EL signal is stronger due to the power being
supplied to the module. Outdoors EL imaging also has the same requirements. The solar irradiance
is a disadvantage to the process as it can increase noise in the images. An example of outdoor EL can
be seen in the image in ﬁgure 1.

1.1.2
Photoluminescence
Photoluminescence is the light emission similar as to above, but a light source usually like a laser is
needed to create a electron-hole pairs in the material. This leads to photon emission corresponding
to the PL signal[6]. When performing indoors, there is need for a light source, and a camera as no
external power needs to be supplied to the module. PL imaging needs ﬁlters present on the camera
to remove the wavelengths of light that is unnecessary, as the PL signal is weaker when compared
with EL. The outdoor imaging can be done both with and without contact. When performing outdoor
contact PL imaging, a power supply unit is necessary to help switch the module between open circuit
and maximum power point. The solar irradiance is necessary to induce the carriers, but the sunlight
reﬂected in the images acquired are noise should be removed to be to see the luminescence signal.
An example of outdoor PL can be seen in the image in ﬁgure 1. It can be observed here that both EL
and PL images provide different diagnosis information. The cell D4 shows the bottom part of the cell
as not working in the EL. Whereas in the PL, the same part of the cell is not damaged. This means
the cell has some fault in the connection. The cell F5 shows damage in both the EL and PL images,
meaning that there is deﬁnitely damage present in the solar cell.
Figure 1: Indoor EL and PL[7]
Luminescence signal is created by the radiative recombination of the majority carriers inside the solar
cell material. The different ways of the signal creation allows for different methods of performing
diagnostics. EL imaging helps to detect defaults in the PV panel like cell cracks, potential induced
degradations, electrical connection problems[8]. PL imaging helps in detecting series resistance and
minority carrier lifetime [9]. An additional advantage of PL is that it can be performed on any point
of the panel’s lifetime, whereas EL imaging needs electrical connections as it needs external power.
The daylight PL technique uses the sunlight already available as the source of excitation for Si. When
performing outdoor measurements, it is important that the sun’s irradiation remain homogeneous.
This is especially important when performing batch measurements i.e. changing the working point of
the module helps to capture two images, one with the luminescence and one without [10]. Changing
the working point method helps to ﬁlter out the sun’s irradiation as it is very strong in a clear sky[11].
Subtracting the background images helps in isolating the PL signal. This allows for fast imaging and
diagnosis.
The amount of luminescence radiation created depends on the working point(open circuit or short
circuit) of the solar cell. When in open circuit no current ﬂows and short circuit has maximum current
ﬂow. As daylight PL technique uses the sun as the source of illumination, it’s spectrum is present in
the short-wave infrared wavelength range where Si radiates. The water vapour present in the Earth’s
atmosphere helps in reducing the radiation that reaches the surface signiﬁcantly in the 1100 nm to
1170 nm wavelength range. This drop coincides with the Si luminescence radiation spectra[10]. This
allows for differentiation of luminescence signal from sunlight. The luminescence signal captured is
3% of the total signal captured. Hence the removal of unwanted radiation from the sun becomes nec-

essary, which is why imaging is done at different working points[11]. Temperature is also a big factor
in luminescence signal intensity although being out of scope of this project. The total luminescence
intensity decreases to about 80% of the initial value by increasing the temperature[11].
One outdoor PL technique used a novel contactless approach. Here all the cells in series connected
to the same bypass diode in the panel are manipulated using a high power white light LED[10]. The
control cell is covered with a cover containing the LED. The current ﬂowing through one cell can be
ﬁgured out by the current ﬂowing through the cell with the lowest light-induced current. If the con-

allows for switching the cells between OC and SC. The LED simulated full sun, so the measurements
outdoors were done for control cell completely shaded(zero sun illumination) and not shaded(full sun
illumination).
Contact EL for outdoor imaging involves using a power supply unit to act as a load. This method
involves using the capability of the solar module to generate power by itself. This technique involves
extracting electric energy from the module, boosting it up to a higher voltage and then injecting the
energy back into the same module[12]. Similar principle was used to perform outdoor PL.
Laser is a very good source of excitation for PL imaging. Line scan using laser would help to detect
faults not shown in uniformly illuminated PL like sunlight[13]. The line scan sends the current in the
cell which helps to identify contact related faults. The InGaAs camera allows for fast line scanning
imaging.
1.2
Project Motivation
The opportunity to work on a project relevant with a part of industry that is quickly becoming very
relevant due to the fast paced adoption of renewable energy was very exciting. This project would
help create guidelines for photoluminescence imaging technique for analysing faults on both cell level
and module level and adapt that for PV plants. This has been an important reason to work on this
project.
The goal of the project was to understand the luminescence imaging technique and use different light
sources to compare the outcome for indoors and to test the outdoor PL imaging techniques seen in
the literature for Danish weather. Details regarding the ambient and weather conditions required, the
kind of faults they can detect and compare them with EL images acquired. The feasibility of these PL
techniques for ﬁeld applications was needed. Such a study is still not present in the literature, and it
will be of great importance in the PV Operation and Maintenance (O&M) industry.

2.1.2
InGaAs
The InGaAs camera used is "OWL 640" manufactured by Raptor Photonics[16] and the camera model
used in the tests is "OWL1.7-VS-CL-640". The camera has a lens attached in front of it where the
ﬁlters can be attached externally as per use. The InGaAs camera acquires the entire c-Si luminescence
spectra which makes it possible to do PL during daytime. The image in ﬁgure 5 shows the quantum
efﬁciency of the InGaAs camera. The ﬁlters used during the imaging are bandpass which cuts the
wavelength in the range of 1150±12.5 nm and the longpass ﬁlter which blocks the wavelengths
below 950 nm. The bandpass ﬁlter has a width of 25 nm. This means that 12.5 nm above and below
the wavelength of 1150 nm is allowed to pass i.e. light in the wavelength range of 1137.5 nm to
1162.5 nm is allowed and the rest is cut out[17]. The 950 nm ﬁlter is a longpass ﬁlter and it blocks
the wavelengths of light below 938 nm and similarly allows the light in the wavelength in the range
of 962 nm to 2150 nm[18] to pass. The images in the ﬁgure 6 and 7 show the camera setup along
with the ﬁlters used. When performing tests outdoor, the InGaAs camera was the only one that was
used. The camera is capable of capturing images at 120 frames per second. The InGaAs camera had
the highgain function kept "on" when performing indoor imaging. This is because, the way the sensor
works is similar to a well but in a quantum sense. The well ﬁlls up with photons and this is how
the camera is sensitive to the light. When performing indoor PL imaging, if the highgain function is
"on" then the well is open for the photons to enter. Similarly, when performing outdoor PL imaging
if the highgain function in the camera is "on" then due to the Sun’s irradiation the well would ﬁll
up quickly and the camera would not be able to capture the PL images with precision. Hence, when
doing outdoors PL imaging the highgain function is kept "off".

The image processing softwares for analysis of the images captured are described below.
2.7.1
ImageJ
ImageJ is an open source software for processing and analyzing scientiﬁc images[27]. The images
were processed using ImageJ software. It was also used for standard deviation image analysis[27].
ImageJ also allowed for processing of the images for contactless PL performed in the ﬁeld as well as
the images captured indoors using the different light sources. It helped with measuring the mean pixel
number in the stack of images present to ﬁgure out if the contactless PL imaging using the bypass
diode manipulation method was working or not. The images analysed using ImageJ are presented and
discussed in the results section below.
2.7.2
Analysis Shell
All the images that were captured using the PSU for the modulation were processed using the anal-
ysis shell software being developed by Thøger Hass, post-doc at DTU Fotonik. The images were
processed in the software using 2 techniques i.e. Denoising by averaging and Fast Fourier Transform
method. The software was initially developed for processing EL images but can work for PL image
analysis as well. The working of the working principle of the denoising by averaging and the fast
fourier transform method used in the software are described in brief below. The images analysed for
outdoors were with the modulation approach with square waves of 15 Hz and 30Hz for PL imaging.

2.7.2.1
Denoising By Averaging
There is signiﬁcant noise in the images captured outdoors because it is not a controlled environment.
The high frequency noise in the signal spectrum tends to cancel itself when computed along the
entire image series. When the noise is integrated along an inﬁnite number of images, the limit of the
noise tends to 0. The signal within the signal spectrum tends towards a constant value, meaning the
limit of the signal when integrated tends towards a constant value. The analysis shell software uses
this mathematical approach for removing the noise and helping the underlying PL signal to become
visible.
2.7.2.2
Fast Fourier Transform
The FFT approach in practical terms means observing the raw signal from the image sequence as a
more intricate collection of many simple signals with their own respective properties associated with
a signal such as phase, amplitude and frequency. The software then breaks down this raw signal ac-
quired from the image sequence into its individual components to identify and isolate the modulation
signal and the simpler signals. The simpler signals are then compared with the modulation signal to
ﬁnd which ones have the same property as the modulation signal. There 2 ﬁlters, LP D3 and LP D5 in
the software. LP D3 reduces the noise components with a period smaller than 3 and the LP D5 does
the same for the period smaller than 5.
The Sun’s radiation and the continuous ﬂuctuations make majority of the low frequency noise. The
main concept being that Fourier series allows breaking down the main periodic function into indi-
vidual components: a constant and a series of sinusoids. The sinusoidal series is determined by its
frequency, with a frequency corresponding coefﬁcient.
The Fourier transform is a frequency domain representation of the signal applied in the calculus of
correlations which, relying on an analysing function, can be used to ﬁnd these coefﬁcients. In the
analysis shell software, a discrete Fourier transform is used. This is done so because of the limited
number of samples and ﬁnite time frame.

4
Conclusion and Future work
The project involved using different types of light sources to induce photoluminescence in the PV
panel so as to determine their usefulness for indoors and outdoors. Outdoor PL tests are important as
they have the potential for cheaper and faster diagnosis.
4.1
Cameras
The Nikon camera proved to be unhelpful in the different tests performed. The reason appears to
be the limitations of the ﬁlters. The literature supports this as PL imaging has been performed with
DSLR camera but with better ﬁlters. This needs to be investigated further both about the ﬁlter re-
quired as well as the exposure times needed to perform PL imaging with a DSLR camera. This can be
particularly advantageous due to the cost factor. If PL imaging done using the DSLR camera provides
even 80% of the results in comparision to the InGaAs camera then there can be signiﬁcant cost saving
as the InGaAs cameras are quite expensive.
The InGaAs camera was able to perform PL imaging both indoors and outdoors without any issues
with bandpass ﬁlter attached when performing outdoors.
4.2
Indoors
When performing PL imaging indoors, the source of excitation to induce PL in the solar cells need
to be strong. This can be observed from the results displayed in the results and discussion section.
The different light sources provided insight as to what is the best option but also as to what is the best
trade-off. The initial tests using the LED theater lights to image the PV module 2 columns at a time
gives better results when compared it with the LED theater lights illuminating half module. But, this
also needs more work, to keep moving the lights and to position them so as to get perform the PL
imaging accurately.
There were 4 light sources that were tested and characterized. The ﬂashlight, the 4 x LED Theater
lights and the laser produced good results with deﬁnitive proof that PL was being induced. The halo-
gen lightbulbs did not yield any results. It cannot be said with certainty that they were unsuccessful
in inducing PL in the module as more tests with better ﬁlter needs to be carried out.
The ﬂashlight and the laser produced the best indoor PL images. This is due to the high intensity of
the both the sources of lights. The time for imaging and diagnosis increases when using ﬂashlight
or the laser setup. The 4 x 100 W Eurolites while yielded less details but there is reduction in time
required for diagnosis as instead of imaging 2 columns at a time, the half of the module was imaged.
4.3
Outdoors
The tests conducted outdoors involved the use of 2 techniques i.e. with contact and contactless. The
contact approach involved the use of power supply unit to modulate the panel or the string(PV plant)
to perform batch measurements. All the contact PL imaging performed outdoors were batch measure-
ments, hence image subtraction with the help of image processing software like ImageJ or Analysis
shell was performed. The batch images were processed with 3 mathematical approaches to better
understand which approach provided the best results. The methods Denoising by averaging and the
Fast Fourier transform approaches were done with the analysis shell software. The other method, a
simple standard deviation method in the ImageJ software.
94
==PAGE:96==
August 8, 2021
The images processed with the FFT approach in the analysis shell software resulted in the best noise
removal. The images processed with the denoising by averaging and standard deviation did yield
some interesting results but not to the same details as the FFT approach.
Contact PL imaging outdoors is a very useful technique as it uses the already available solar irradi-
ance. The PL imaging of the backside of the panel in string 3.2, tracker 6 is the ﬁrst time such results
have been recorded. This is very interesting as PL imaging does not need power from the PSU. The
outdoor imaging for PL yielded good correspondence with the respective EL images, especially the
FFT approach to process the images. PL can be recommended for outdoor diagnosis. The results from
performing outdoor PL imaging show that Danish weather is not a limiting factor for both contact and
contactless approaches.
The contactless PL test to use the ﬂashlight to manipulate the bypass diode with the control cell did
not show usable results. This could be because the ﬂashlight was not able to switch on and off in high
speed manually. This would have been necessary for batch imaging.
4.4
Futurework
The contactless PL technique yielded some interesting results. There was deﬁnitively modulation
happening when the cover was placed on the panel. More tests need to be conducted to further char-
acterize contactless PL diagnosis. The potential of contactless PL is huge, if the results correspond to
the EL images. This can drastically improve the speed for diagnosis of PV plants. The SNR values
also yielded interesting results as the trends with image quality increase was not constant. More tests
need to be conducted as well to get more understanding about the relationship between SNR and
image quality. This will be very useful for diagnosing solar plants at gigawatt capacity.
