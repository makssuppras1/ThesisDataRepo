Background 
Sugar beet is grown to produce sugar and provides about a third of the worlds sugar production. In 
Denmark more than 2.5 million tons sugar beet is grown per year, solely for sugar production. After 
extraction of sugar from the sugar beet, an insoluble fibrous pulp remains. The pulp from sugar beet 
is rich in functional plant cell wall polysaccharides, such as cellulose, pectin and pectin-associated 
arabinogalactans. Currently, the sugar beet pulp is not upcycled but used mainly for animal feed. 
However, pectin which constitutes a significant part of the pulp fraction ~25% (w/w), are rich in 
arabinose with highly arabinose substituted side chains in the rhamnogalacturonan I (RGI) fraction 
of the pectin. Arabino RGI have shown to have health promoting bioactivities, such as prebiotic and 
anti-inflammatory effects. The RGI segments of the sugar beet pectin with intact arabino side 
chains, cannot with current methods be extracted. The SURE project (SUgar beet REvolution) aims 
to enzymatically cut and loosen the recalcitrant sugar beet pulp, to retrieve arabino RGI side chains 
and cellulose fibers which can be used for nutraceuticals and recyclable nanocellulose materials.  
Two classes of carbohydrate active enzymes (CAZymes) polysaccharide lyase family 1 (PL1) and 
glycoside hydrolase family 28 (GH28) possess the ability to cleave pectin with different catalytic 
mechanisms. To cut and loosen the recalcitrant sugar beet pulp, it is hypothesized that these 
enzymes along with other enzymes can aid in the processes, while keeping the arabino side chains 
intact. 
Aim of the project 
The project aims to investigate several PL1s and GH28 from sugar beet degrading organisms with 
their optimal catalytic parameters by enzymatic assays using a well-defined substrate. The 
enzymatic assessment include investigation of optimal parameters: ions, temperature, pH and 
kinetic investigation of the enzymes to determine the catalytic constants.  
Depending on enzyme activity uncovered in the first part of the project, the enzymes are to be used 
on complex substrate pectin, including pectin from various sources to determine the activity on 
sugar beet associated substrates. Further investigation of synergies with other enzymes, such as 
AA17 LPMOs and carbohydrate esterases are to be conducted if time permits.

Gain experience in quantitative data handling and visualization. Learn about PL1 and GH28, their

Abstract  
 
After sugar extraction from sugar beets, a fibrous sugar beet pulp is left over, which is mainly used 
as animal feed. The sugar beet pulp is rich in pectin, with a high arabinose side chains content 
which could be used for its health beneficial properties if extracted. To try and find a way release 
these sidechains two pectate lyases belonging to Polysaccharide lyase family 1 (PL1) named 
PbPL1A and PbPL1B and two endo-polygalacturonases belonging to Glycoside Hydrolase family 
28 (GH28) named RsGH28A and PbGH28A had their temperature stability, divalent ion 
dependency, pH- and temperature optimum, kinetics on demethylated- and methylated substrates, 
and sizes of products determined. 
 
PbPL1A had a Tm of 53.5±0.1ºC and optimal conditions with 0.2mM Ca2+ and Mn2+, with pH 
optimum at 8.5 and 50ºC used for kinetics. PGA showed a kcat of 751±10min-1 and kcat/Km of 
6827.27±90.91g·L-1·min-1, citrus pectin kcat of 2734±52min-1 and kcat/Km of 749.04±30.24g·L-1·min-
1, and apple pectin kcat/Km of 15.23±0.50g·L-1·min-1, with products being Δ4,5-DP2-Δ4,5-DP6.   
 
PbPL1B had a Tm of 49.0±0.0ºC, 0.6mM Ca2+, pH optimum at 9.5 with 50ºC used for kinetics. PGA 
showed a kcat of 12516±272min-1 and kcat/Km of 40374.19±2748.60g·L-1·min-1 and citrus pectin a 
kcat/Km of 2492.67±12.22g·L-1·min-1, and cleaved Δ4,5-DP3 most often. 
 
RsGH28A had a Tm of 53.2±0.0ºC, no ion dependency, pH optimum at 6.0 with 50ºC used for 
kinetics. PGA showed a kcat of 48.0±2.0min-1 and a kcat/Km of 12.73±0.76g·L-1·min-1, with product 
sizes of DP2-DP6.  
PbGH28A had a Tm of 66.7±0.0ºC, optimal conditions with 0.5mM Mn2+, pH optimum at 7.5 with 
60ºC used for kinetics. PGA showed a kcat of 40.4±2.0min-1 and a kcat/Km of 46.44±5.81g·L-1·min-1, 
with a product size of DP2.  
From the four enzymes characterized PbPL1B would be the best candidate to removed RG-I 
sidechains, but more experiments are needed using substrates more like sugar beet pulp.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Carbohydrates and pectin 
Carbohydrates are used vigorously by all organic life as energy storage, communications both intra-
, and extracellularly and as structural molecules. One of these structural molecules are pectin which 
is synthesized and used by plants and fruits as a central component in the plant cell wall along with 
cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Since pectin is part of the plant cell wall it is highly involved in 
the properties of the cell wall such as porosity, surface charge, ion balance and as an extension the 
pH of the cell (1). 
 
Pectin is the overall name for a group of molecules predominantly having a galacturonic acid 
(GalA) backbone but can come in multiple structural assortments. Homogalacturonan is the most 
basic of these being a continuous chain of α-1,4-linked GalA units, which can have various degrees 
of methyl esterification on the C6 and/or acetylation at O2 and/or O3 (2–4). If there is very few 
methyl ester and acetyl groups attached to homogalacturonan it is instead referred to as 
polygalacturonic acid (PGA).  
Pectin is generally described as having a “smooth” region and a “hairy” region which have different 
backbone structure and sidechain substitutions. The “Smooth” regions have a homogalacturonan 
backbone while the “hairy” regions has rhamnogalacturonan I (RG-I) as a backbone consisting of 
repeating α-ᴅ-galacturonic acid units linked to the C2-position on α-L-rhamnose (Rha) units which 
in turn is linked to the C4-position on α-ᴅ-GalA (5,6). These two backbone structures can be seen in 
Figure 1 alongside PGA. RG-I contains neutral sugar sidechains often situated on the Rha units 
consisting mainly of galactosyl and/or arabinosyl residues placed at the O4-position (4,7,8). Other 
examples of backbone linked sidechains could be xylogalacturonan, which has a single side chain 
of β-ᴅ-Xylopyranose on the C3-position of the GalA backbone (4,9), or more complex types which 
have large sidechains of different sugars like rhamnogalacturonan II (4,10).  
Figure 1: Different backbones found in pectin. From the top; Homogalacturonan the main backbone in “smooth” regions, Rhamnogalacturonan-I 
(RG-I) the main backbone in “hairy” regions, and polygalacturonic acid (PGA) the demethylated and deacetylated version of homogalacturonan. R 
in RG-I indicates location of neutral sugar side chain.

Pectin is mainly used as a gelling and thickening agent in food products such as jams, jellies and 
dairy drinks (11,12). Pectin as a dietary fiber has been shown to have various positive health 
benefits such as lowering blood cholesterol levels (4,13), having a immunoregulatory effects (14), 
to being a carbon source for commensal gut bacteria (15). Sugar beets constitutes 20% of the worlds 
sugar production according to the European commission (16). In Denmark more than 2.3 million 
tons was produced in 2019 (17). After the sugar extraction from the sugar beet, a fibrous sugar beet 
pulp is left over, which is mainly used as animal feed. The sugar beet pulp is rich in pectin, 
especially RG-I which has a high arabinose content (18), which if extracted could be used for its 
health beneficial effect. However, there is currently no way to extract this RG-I part of the sugar 
beet pulp pectin in its intact form. The Novo Nordisk Fond has funded the SUgar beet REvolution 
project (SURE project) to try and find a way to extract RG-I from the sugar beet pulp using 
enzymes to gently liberate these segments from the sugar beet pulp. This report is part of the SURE 
project and have looked at different enzymes for their pectin degrading properties.  
 
1.2 CAZy 
For the assembly and degradation of large carbohydrate structures like pectin enzymes such as 
glycosyltransferases, glycoside hydrolases, polysaccharide lyases are needed. Together these groups 
of enzymes are categorized as Carbohydrate Active enZymes (CAZymes). Classification of these 
CAZymes is done by grouping them into families based on their amino acid sequence similarity 
compared to a founding member of that family (19,20). Because of this classification enzymes are 
grouped not by their substrate specificity but on their sequence and by extension their mechanism as 
these are often correlated.  The CAZymes can sometimes be divided further into subfamilies which 
are subgroups within the family that share a more recent common ancestor resulting in a more 
similar molecular function. A step above families are clans which are currently only defined for 
Glycoside Hydrolases. Clans contains groups of families which share fold and catalytic mechanism 
(21). 
The wide arrays of families which have been classified have been available on Cazy.org since 1998, 
and currently holds more than 380 different families belonging to six classes. These six classes and 
their functions are (20–22); 
 
- 
Glycoside hydrolases (GHs) which hydrolyse and/or transglycosylate glycosidic bonds. 
- 
Glycosyltransferases (GTs) which biosynthesise glycosidic bonds from phosphor-activated 
sugar donors. 
- 
Polysaccharide lyases (PLs) which cleave the glycosidic bonds of uronic acid-containing 
polysaccharides. 
- 
Carbohydrate esterases (CEs) which remove ester-based modification on every form of 
saccharide. 
- 
Carbohydrate-binding modules (CBMs) which have no enzymatic activity themselves but 
increase the activity of other CAZymes by prolonging interactions with their substrate. 
- 
Auxiliary Activities (AAs) which cover redox enzymes that act in conjunction with other 
CAZymes.  
 
To achieve the goal set out by the SURE project two families from two different classes will be 
tested for their pectin degradation abilities. These are Glycoside Hydrolase family 28 (GH28) and 
Polysaccharide Family 1 (PL1).

1.3 PL1 
Members of PL1, are used by their hosts to degrade pectin in its various forms such as when 
ripening fruits(23,24) and in pollen (25,26). Plant pathogenic organisms, like bacteria and fungi, use 
these enzymes to get past physical defenses by degrade primary cell walls and middle lamellae 
which contain large amount of pectin (27,28). PL1 members have a β-helix structure with between 
8 and 14 turns. Generally, the helix is closed by an amphipathic α-helix at the N-terminal end. The 
inner part of the β-helix is lined with hydrophobic amino acids (29). The active site can have a bit 
of variation as different family members can act on different substrates. The enzymes which will be 
tested in this project all belong to pectate lyases (EC. 4.2.2.2) which prefer non-methylated 
substrates and cleave α-1,4 linkage between GalA residues. They do this by acting on the carboxylic 
group at C5 using a β-elimination mechanism to cleave the glycosidic bond linking the sugars at the 
C4 position. The mechanism occurs via three steps first proposed by Gacesa et al. (30), and can be 
seen in Figure 2: 
1. The carboxyl group is neutralized by a positive charge. This lowers the pKa of the 
proton which sits on the C5. 
2. The proton is removed via abstraction and an enolate anion intermediate is 
created. 
3. Electron transfer from the carboxyl group created the double bond between the C4 
and C5. This leads to the breakage of the C4 glycosidic bond 
This β-elimination requires a base to accept the proton from step 2 and an acid to donate a proton to 
the leaving group to recreate a new reduced end. Most members of PL1 uses a metal-assisted 
method where often Ca2+ ion is responsible for the neutralization happening in step 1 of the 
reaction. Lys or Arg in the active site of the enzyme act as the catalytic base which accepts the 
proton and water or an amino acid like Lys act as the catalytic acid which donates a proton (29,31). 
Protonation of the leaving group is unknown but could be from O3 shuffling a proton from Arg or 
facilitated by Ca2+ (31). The pectate lyase enzyme often have multiple Ca2+ binding sites, with one 
already mentioned being responsible for neutralization of the carboxyl group while the other Ca2+ 
are there to help bind the substrate (31). This way of breaking the glycosidic bond leads to two 
easily distinguishable different ends. The non-reduced sugar end which after cleavage possess an 
unsaturated double bond between C4 and C5 and the reduced end sugar which remain saturated. 
The formation of the unsaturated double bond can be measured directly in a spectrophotometer at 
235nm (32).

1.4 GH28 
Members of the GH28 family, such as endo-polygalacturonases and xylogalacturonan hydrolase, 
are used by their host organism for a plethora of different purposes. The focus of this project will be 
on endo-polygalacturonase enzymes (EC 3.2.1.15). Polygalacturonases are used by plants, such as 
tomato and papaya, when ripening their fruits by breaking down pectin (33–35) as well as used in 
pollen by plants such as trees and grasses (36,37). Likewise, due to their pectin degrading nature a 
variety of pathogens produce GH28 members as virulence factors such as Rhizoctonia solani (38), 
Pectobacterium betavasculorum (39), Fusarium oxysporum (40) and Stereum purpureum (41) to 
help with their infection of the hosts. 
 
Endo-polygalacturonases, like members of PL1, have parallel β-helix structures with a “cleft” 
active site which corelates nicely with them being endo-acting enzymes (42). This “cleft” consists 
of highly conserved regions that vary little to none between the enzymes. There are four of these 
motifs being the NTD-, GDD-, GHG-, and RIK-motifs (43,44). All these motifs were found in the 
GH28 characterized in this project (see Appendix 1). These sites are responsible for binding the 
substrate via hydrogen bonds, orienting it correctly and cutting the polygalacturonic backbone. The 
active site itself is within these conserved regions and the entire enzymatic reaction can be seen in 
Figure 3. The His in the GHG-motif works as a proton donor to the glycoside bond cleaving it. The 
first Asp in the GDD-motif makes a covalent bond to the substrate where the cut occurred, while the 
second Asp positions water for a nucleophilic attack. This attack releases the substrate and returns 
the amino acids in the active site to a standby position ready to act again, after Asp has dropped its 
proton (43). The enzymatic reaction results in the release of two sugars, one having a reducing end 
which is an inversion of the anomeric configuration (42) which is also seen in other endo-
polygalacturonases (45). This product can not be detected as easily as for the double bond for PL1, 
and a different method is needed.

2.2 Temperature stability, Tm 
Thermostability Tm of the enzymes were determined using differential scanning fluorimetry. Two 
glass capillaries containing 5µM of enzyme were placed in a DSF instrument Prometheus NanoDSF 
(NanoTemper Technologies GmbH, Munich, Germany). The following parameters were used: 
excitation power of 35%, start temperature of 25ºC, end temperature of 95ºC and temperature 
increase of 1ºC pr. Min. Tm were plotted directly by the machine based on the ration of integrated 
fluorescence at 350nm/330nm. 
2.3 Influence of divalent ions on activity 
The optimal divalent ion(s) for each enzyme was found using optimal pH and temperature 
determined during initial experiments (data not shown). The enzyme concentration used in every 
experiment varied based on enzyme and can be seen in Table 2. Ions tested were Ca2+, Zn2+, Mg2+ 
and Mn2+ with a reaction using no ion and a reaction with EDTA. Every reaction was conducted in 
triplicates unless stated otherwise. PbPL1A was tested with an ion concentration of 0.25mM in MIB 
buffer (50mM, pH 8.5) with 1g/L PGA dissolved in 20mM NaOH at 50°C, while PbPL1B was 
tested using 0.5mM ion concentrations in MIB (50mM, pH 9.5) with the same temperature and 
substrate concentration. Similarly, GH28 was tested with 1mM ions concentrations in 50mM, pH 6 
or 7.5 UB4 buffer at 50°C and 60°C for RsGH28A and PbGH28A, respectively, with 2g/L PGA 
dissolved in 20mM NaOH as substrate. The ions showing the highest rates were further tested at 
varying concentrations to find the optimal concentration. If more than one ion was found to

2.4 Optimal pH and Temperature determination 
PL1 assays was done directly in UV capable 96-well plates with wells containing enzyme, 50mM 
buffer, 1 g/L PGA, and the optimal divalent ion found conducted in triplicates. Reactions had pH 
values ranging from pH 6.0-10.0 in 0.5 pH steps, as well as varying temperatures from 25-65°C 
with 5ºC step increases, along with enzyme only and substrate only controls. Reactions ran for 15-
30 minutes and was measured continuously. 
Reactions for GH28A were done using two methods; a master mix (2g/L Polygalacturonic acid, 
50mM UB4 buffer with varying pH and water) was made and distributed to six 1.5mL Eppendorf 
tubes in which enzyme was added individually to each tube. The reactions ran with a time delay and 
was stopped using DNS, one reaction at a time. Alternatively, three tubes were used to run the 
reactions and samples were taken from each Eppendorf tube and transferred to a tube containing 
equal amount of DNS.  
Regardless of method the Eppendorf tubes were preheated with everything except enzyme for a 
minimum of 10min to the desired temperature in triplicates. Appropriate enzyme concentrations 
(see Table 2) were added to each tube, with reaction time depended on temperature, pH and enzyme 
but generally 20-50 seconds for PbGH28A and 4-10min for RsGH28A. Reactions were stopped at 0 
seconds and at five other appropriate times, which varied depending on total reaction time. The 
reaction was stopped by mixing the enzymatic reaction with a 1:1 volume of DNS. A glucose 
standard curve was done with each reaction temperature and averages made for each. DNS was 
added in 1:1 ratio to Eppendorf tubes containing different glucose concentrations when the final 
reaction sample was taken. Glucose concentrations used were 0.25g/L, 0.5g/L, 0.75g/L, 1g/L and 
1.5g/L. Afterwards, sample and standard curve Eppendorf tubes were heated to 95ºC for 18min. 
The tubes were centrifuged, and the supernatant was transferred to a 96-well plate and measured at 
540nm. The standard curves were used to find the glucose equivalents created in the enzymatic 
reactions based on absorbance measured, and thereby find the rate in µM using the molecular 
weight of glucose, 180.156g/mol. 
Temperature/pH/relative activity 3D-plots were created for each enzyme using Visual studio Code 
version 1.90 (Microsoft, Redmond, Washington, USA). The optimal conditions found were used 
when doing kinetics on PGA, citrus pectin and apple pectin.

4 Discussion 
Different novel characteristics for PbPL1A, PbPL1B, RsGH28A and PbGH28A have been 
determined throughout this report, with some being more trustworthy than others. The methods 
used throughout the report will be looked at for improvements, optimization and reliability, as well 
as discussing the values that were obtained and what may have been factors in why some is most 
likely overestimated.  
The determination of divalent ions was done by firstly testing the different ions at one concentration 
of either 0.5mM for PL1 or 1mM for GH28A. From this the ion showing the highest activity had a 
range of concentrations tested to find the optimal concentration. In the experiments it was 
concluded that the ion showing highest activity at the first test would be the ion that showed the 
highest activity for all concentration, but this may not necessarily be true. E.g. if Ca2+ showed the 
highest activity in the first test but Mg2+ would show an even higher activity at a concentration not 
tested for. To circumvent this every ion should have a range of concentrations tested to see if this is 
the case. Likewise, a test of other divalent ions such as Ni2+, Cu2+ or Fe2+ not used here would be 
optimal. PbPL1B had a dependence on Ca2+ which is to be expected as pectate lyases use Ca2+ to 
help in the enzymatic reaction, as mentioned in section 1.3. Interestingly, PbPL1A had a 
dependence on both Ca2+ and Mn2+ which could indicate the presence of distinct binding sites on 
the enzyme, as there was lower activity when using only one enzyme which have also been reported 
elsewhere (48). If they competed for the same binding site, higher activity would be seen when 
using ions alone compared to when in use together, due to the lower activity ion blocking the higher 
activity ones from binding.  
There were several problems with the DNS method that made it hard to find the optimal way to do 
the experiments, and some problems that sadly were discovered after the experiments were done. 
Several different methods were tried to varying success, before a consistent method was found. 
Firstly, it was tried to run reactions directly in a 96-well plate as this would have the highest 
throughput and many samples could be ran at the same time. However, two problems arose from 
this method. Firstly, the wells became cloudy which was first though to be ions, but later confirmed 
to be PGA precipitating when the DNS drastically changed the pH of the reaction. Secondly, 
heating of the reactions to 95ºC led to the evaporation of water which would increase the 
concentration of the rest of the reactants, making comparisons between wells impossible as levels of 
evaporations differed. The lid used had no easy way of reintroducing the water to the reaction, and 
the problem of precipitation persisted. These problems led to the discarding of the 96-well plate 
method and DNS was thereafter exclusively done in Eppendorf tubes, as they could be centrifuged 
thereby separation the precipitated substrate and reintroducing the water to the mix. A downside to 
this method however was throughputs were low, so much more time was used running experiments. 
Furthermore, tinkering and optimization of the method as the project progressed led to the use of 
two methods as described in section 2.4. This is of course not optimal and to be more precise and 
have less error and variables only one method should have been used. From these two methods the 
one using only three reaction tubes would have been optimal as there is less pipetting and therefore 
less variation and error. 
When DNS was added to a reaction the concentration of glucose or glucose equivalents was halved 
so the standard curve should have half the g/L reported. But as this was not considered for the 
==PAGE:35==
Page 34 of 52 
 
reactions either it has no effect on the final concentration calculated. E.g. a standard curve glucose 
of 0.5g/L shows an absorbance of 0.4. After DNS is added, this is halved to 0.25g/L. An enzyme 
reaction likewise shows 0.4 absorbance which is then translated to 0.25g/L glucose equivalents in 
the reaction. However, half of this is DNS so the concentration of the reaction should be doubled to 
0.5g/L which was the original glucose concentration used in the standard curve, so any halving or 
doubling can be ignored, and the standard curves used as is before DNS adding.  
By far the biggest issue with the DNS method, discovered well after experiments ended, is that it 
has been reported to overestimate methylated substrates (49). The alkaline conditions created by the 
addition of DNS and the high temperatures needed when provoking the color change causes 
degradation of the polysaccharides. In pectin structures with higher levels of methyl-esterification 
like citrus pectin, this causes de-esterification and cleavage of the glycosidic bonds via trans-β-
elimination (50). As PGA contain no methyl-ester groups DNS should work fine on this substrate 
(49). This causes a bit of a discrepancy however, as the kcat and catalytic efficiency of PbGH28A on 
PGA reported in Table 6 was found to be unbelievably high at 224760±10100min-1 and 
258344±31883 g·L-1·min-1, respectively. No explanation for why the numbers were so big for 
PbGH28A was found in the data, but it may result from a mistake made somewhere doing 
concentration calculations in the lab, but none were found in the logs. Others have reported just as 
high numbers and even higher ones upwards of 3,600,000min-1 (51), however these numbers seem 
very unlikely for a polygalacturonase. Likewise, the values found for PbPL1B of kcat 
12516±272min-1 and kcat/Km of 40374.19±2748.60 may also be quite large, however more 
believable. One paper found a kcat of 78±1.8 and 105.6±3 (52) while another found something 
more comparable of kcat of 6840±660-12960±720min-1 (53).  
When doing kinetics with higher substrate concentrations there were an increase in error and 
RsGH28A even saw a small decease in activity. This is most likely due to the high substrate 
concentration increasing the viscosity of the mixture thereby reducing the ability of enzymes to 
move freely.  
Similarly, the conditions in which determination of pH and temperature could be optimized. pH was 
corrected for in each buffer. When everything is added to the reaction the reaction pH differs 
somewhat from the intended pH meant for the reaction. To try and counter this pH was corrected 
for the substrate as well, but anything above approximately pH 6 would instantly change the pH to 
10+. UB4 buffer has a buffer range of 2.0-8.2 pH (54), which is one of the reasons MIB buffer was 
used for PL1 as experiments showed they needed higher pH. Interestingly, PL1 also showed an 
increase in activity when using MIB buffer above pH 7.5 of on average 35.4±10.34%, which was 
not seen for GH28 that had similar activities for both buffers. PL1 reactions with MIB buffer at pH 
7 (-4.6±14.6%) showed a small decrease in activity but with a large standard deviation.

example of this loss in activity can be seen below in Table 7 for RsGH28A at pH 6. Due to only the 
linear part of the rate measurements being used, the point when the enzyme started to denature and 
the curve flatten was of great importance to the rate calculated. Some measurements only had three 
data points for a straight line making them very unreliable and could help explain contributing to 
the minima and maxima of the 3D-plot seen for RsGH28A. This was not seen as much for PL1

The size analysis using LC-MS results consistent with the enzymes being endo except for 
PbGH28A which mostly created DP2 which is more characteristic for an exo-acting enzyme. This 
is also consistent with the structure of PbGH28A seen in Figure 6 which shows it having a closed 
active site compared to the open “clefts” seen in the other enzymes. 
Generally, there was a large decrease in activity between PGA and the more methylated substrates. 
As mentioned above GH28 data for methylated substrates are not reliable so RsGH28A with citrus 
pectin will be overlooked. PL1A had a much higher affinity for PGA with a Km of 0.11±0.00g/L 
with the Km for citrus pectin being much higher at 3.65±0.13g/L. Even if kcat for citrus pectin was 
higher for PbPL1A there is an approximately 89.03% decrease in catalytic efficiency from PGA to 
citrus pectin. Similarly, for PBPL1B there is a 93.83% decrease from 40374.19±2748.60g·L-1·min-1 
to 2492.67±12.22g·L-1·min-1. PbPL1B is still 333% better than PbPL1A on citrus pectin substrate. 
Sadly, PbPL1B was not tested on apple pectin so a comparison with PbPL1As kcat/Km of 
15.23±0.50g·L-1·min-1 was not possible.  
As the SURE project’s goal is to find enzymes that can release arabinose sidechains from fibrous 
sugar beet pulp, the tested enzymes should be able to digest substrates that contain high degrees of 
methylation and acetylation. PbPL1A showed some activity for methylated substrates, however 
PbPL1B worked better on citrus pectin but was sadly not tested on apple pectin. However, PbPL1B 
high activity may be somewhat of a false positive as Tm for PbPL1B was 49.0ºC but kinetics was 
done at 50ºC, making it the only enzyme ran above its Tm. Every enzyme showed increased activity 
when using higher temperatures so PbPL1B may have had an advantage as seen in the 3D-plots 
where higher temperatures have a large impact on relative activity.

5 Conclusion 
Two PL1 and two GH28 enzymes had their ion dependence, temperature stability, pH and 
temperature optimum, kinetics on simple and methylated substrates and created oligosaccharide 
sizes determined to try and find a candidate for the SURE project.  
PbPL1A had a melting temperature Tm of 53.5±0.1ºC, showed maximum activity with 0.2mM Ca2+ 
and Mn2+, had pH optimum at 8.5 with 50ºC used as the temperature when doing kinetics. Kinetics 
for PGA showed a kcat of 751±10min-1 and catalytic efficiency of 6827.27±90.91g·L-1·min-1, for 
citrus pectin a kcat of 2734±52min-1 and catalytic efficiency of 749.04±30.24g·L-1·min-1, and lastly 
an apple pectin catalytic efficiency of 15.23±0.50g·L-1·min-1. LC-MS showed that the size of 
oligosaccharides created by PbPL1A was Δ4,5-DP2-Δ4,5-DP6.  
PbPL1B had a melting temperature Tm of 49.0±0.0ºC, showed maximum activity with 0.6mM Ca2+, 
had pH optimum at 9.5 with 50ºC used as the temperature when doing kinetics. Kinetics for PGA 
showed a kcat of 12516±272min-1 and catalytic efficiency of 40374.19±2748.60g·L-1·min-1 and for 
citrus pectin a catalytic efficiency of 2492.67±12.22g·L-1·min-1. PbPL1B cleaved Δ4,5-DP2 and 
Δ4,5-DP3 with the latter created most frequently. 
RsGH28A had a melting temperature Tm of 53.2±0.0ºC, showed no dependence for ions, had pH 
optimum at 6.0 with 50ºC used as the temperature when doing kinetics. Kinetics for PGA showed a 
kcat of 48.0±2.0min-1 and a catalytic efficiency of 12.73±0.76g·L-1·min-1 while citrus pectin was 
unreliable due to DNS interference with methylated substrates. Oligosaccharides created by 
RsGH28A had sizes of DP2-DP6. 
PbGH28A had a melting temperature Tm of 66.7±0.0ºC, showed maximum activity with 0.5mM 
Mn2+, had pH optimum at 7.5 with 60ºC used as the temperature when doing kinetics. Kinetics for 
PGA showed a kcat of 40.4±2.0min-1 and a catalytic efficiency of 46.44±5.81g·L-1·min-1. 
Oligosaccharides with a size of DP2 was heavily seen suggesting PbGH8A is an endo-acting 
enzyme. 
The enzyme that showed the highest activity on methylated substrates was PbPL1B, with PbPL1A 
also showing some activity although much less. Methylated substrates were not able to be tested for 
GH28 due to DNS interference. Therefore, of the enzymes characterized in this report PbPL1B is 
the best candidate for an enzyme which can free RG-I sidechains, however further testing is needed 
on more methylated substrates like apple pectin and on sugar beet pectin.
