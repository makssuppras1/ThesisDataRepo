been conducted as a joint collaboration between DTU, Rambøll, and HOK Marineconsult

1
Introduction
In 2020 Rambøll and HOK Marineconsult ApS conducted a report into the production
of carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions produced by vessels in Copenhagen harbour. This
project hopes to expand on this report by incorporating considerations for other pollutants
such as nitrous oxides (NOx), sulfur oxides (SOx), and particulate matter (PM), as well
as estimating how these emissions are transported throughout the urban area of Copen-
hagen.
The effects and potential significance of these emissions will be discussed further in sub-
section 1.1.1 and subsection 1.1.2.
1.1
Background and Motivation
1.1.1
Climate change
In 1988, the International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) was set up by the United
Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and the World Meteorological Organisation
(WMO) with the intention of collecting and coordinating scientific research into global cli-

1.1.2
Air quality effects on human health
The consequences of global warming and climate change aside, ambient air quality has
significant direct effects on human health. Several existing studies have established a
detrimental link between ambient air quality and human health as the biological mecha-
nisms by which air pollutants affect human health include systemic inflammation and ox-
idative stress, increased blood pressure, as well as changes in serum metabolites such
as stress hormones, insulin, glucose, amino acids, and lipids [6]. Often this can culminate
in cardiovascular or lung disease in patients.
Air quality studies generally focus on the influence of particulate matter (PMx, where x
denotes the diameter of particles in terms of microns), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulfur diox-
ide (SO2), and ozone (O3) [7]. Other pollutants investigated include black carbon. A study
investigating the long-term exposure of the Danish population to the aforementioned pol-
lutants indicated that cardiovascular mortality was approximately 29% more likely due to
long term exposure to particulate matter. Increased risk factors of 16% and 11% were
also noted for black carbon and nitrogen dioxide respectively. Stratified analysis also in-
dicated that these increased risk factors applied primarily to males, likely due to different
mortality baseline hazards and endogenous oestrogen [6].
A 2021 study carried out by Imperial College London indicated that exposure to high pol-
lutant concentrations can increase risk of contraction and severity of infectious diseases
such as COVID-19 [8].
Particulate Matter (PM)
As per the World Health Organisation’s (WHO) website, PM is a common proxy indicator
for air pollution. The major components of PM are sulfates, nitrates, ammonia, sodium
chloride, black carbon, mineral dust, and water [7]. PM of varying diameters can have a
range of potential health effects, varying for certain demographics. Studies have linked
increased exposure to PM to lung cancer [7] and cardiovascular disease [6]. A correlation
with perinatal issues has also been investigated, with 3 million low birth weight (LBW) and
6 million preterm birth (PTB) cases attributed globally to air pollution [9]. Pollution-related
diseases are more prevalent in low-income countries, likely due to these their reliance on
solid fuels burned indoors for cooking and heating. In 2019, 92% of the world’s population
lived in areas that exceeded WHO guidelines for PM2.5, and 3.8 billion people (49% of the
global population) were exposed to household pollution for cooking [9].

Literature Review
This literature review will examine the objective of this project in relation to previous scien-
tific studies carried out, i.e. examine previous works which establish emission inventories
from ships, and/or works which examine the dispersion of these emissions. The aim of this
literature review is to establish the different methodologies used, their respective advan-

2.1
Ship emission inventories
Although alternative fuel sources such as hydrogen and liquefied natural gas are being
examined, the majority of the global shipping fleet depends on the combustion of diesel
oil in order to run its engines and auxiliary services. Diesel oil is a complex mixture pro-
duced by the distillation of crude oil [21]. Small amounts of sulfur, nitrogen, and oxy-
gen (so called heteroatoms) are present in this fuel. When heteroatoms are bound into
molecular structures with carbon and hydrogen, the resulting compounds are not char-
acterised as hydrocarbons. Typical examples of nonhydrocarbon compounds found in
diesel include dibenzothiophene (sulfur compound) and carbazole (nitrogen compound)
[22]. When these hydrocarbons, and sulfur and nitrogen based compounds are combined
with oxygen during the combustion process, they release the CO2, NOx, and SOx. In ad-
dition, emission particles can coagulate, or vapours can be condensed together, to form
particulate matter of varying compositions (PM) [23]. These pollutants will be the focus of
the current study.
There are currently 2 primary methods of calculating emission inventories from ships:
top-down methods and bottom-up methods. Both top-down and bottom-up methods cal-
culate emissions by applying emission factors to some sort of quantification of the fuel
consumed. The relationship between fuel consumed and emissions produced is affected
by a wide range of variables such as ship engine age, ship size, ship operations, ship
engine fuel type, origin of fuel etc.
The top-down approach is based on regression analysis of fuel sale statistics. By ap-
plying fuel sale records to some sort of database of vessels, emissions can be calculated.
Top-down emissions are typically calculated similarly to the equation presented below[24]:

The bottom-up approach analyses the characteristics and behaviour of each ship in an

from international shipping in Belgian waters by De Meyer et al. [26].
Even in scenarios where adequate information was obtained, vessel behaviours were
systematically categorised and emissions averaged across time spent within a certain
category. Typically these categories included vessel cruising, anchoring, in-port manoeu-
vring, and hotelling. Such categorisation can be seen in a 2004 study of air pollution
sourced from the Danish ports of Copenhagen, Elsinore, and Køge [18], and the afore-
mentioned study by De Meyer et al. [26].

2.1.1
Dispersion models
Dispersion modelling examines how various materials are transported in an environment.
Generally, these materials are gaseous pollutants that are transported and altered via me-
teorological/physical and chemical processes. During the literature review of the current
project, numerous methods of dispersion modelling were encountered. These include
Eulerian chemical transport models, computational fluid dynamic models, and Gaussian
dispersion models.

Computational Fluid Dynamics Model
The principle of Computation Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is to solve the Navier-Stokes equa-
tions. These equations utilise theories for conservation of mass and momentum for New-
tonian fluids. In principle, Navier-Stokes equations describe all fluid flows [30] and the
present literature review showed them to estimate emission dispersion patterns to a high
degree of accuracy [31]. However, application of these models can be very computation-
ally intensive, meaning they are unlikely to be suitable for the scale of the current project
which examines a large spatial area (Copenhagen Harbour) over a significant temporal
length (3 calendar years). However, studies have shown results from this model type
to agree well with physical results [31], meaning CFD models could offer an excellent
calibration tool for short-term predictions.
Gaussian Dispersion models
Gaussian models assume that pollutant concentrations from a source can be represented
by assuming the pollutants are dispersed according to a normal distribution. Under this
assumption, concentrations relative to the source will vary with x, y, and z coordinates,
with pollutant diffusion being dictated by turbulence conditions induced by parameters
such as terrain roughness and weather conditions such as wind speed, wind direction,
precipitation, temperature gradients etc.. Figure 2.1 shows a simple sketch created by

Shipping Inventory and Dispersion case studies
Several studies have been conducted in an effort to quantify emissions from ships, with
some even examining how these emissions are distributed in the local environment. This

Emissions from international shipping in the Belgian part of the North Sea and the
Belgian seaports, P. De Meyer, F. Maes, and Annemie Volckaert - 2007 [26]
This study examined the emission inventory for Belgian waters in the North Sea as well
as the port cities of Antwerp, Ghent, Ostend, and Zeebrugge. It utilises a bottom-up ap-
proach for most vessels, while supplementary top-down emissions are applied for vessels
which are known to not travel outside of Belgian waters such as dredgers and tugboats.
In order to establish vessels behaviours (which determine engine load factors - see Equa-
tion (2.2)) in the bottom-up methodology, data is collected from operators and contractors.
However, the study cites poor quality data from port authorities, meaning significant as-
sumptions regarding vessel behaviour were applied. The study cites difficulties approx-
imating vessel behaviours in the bottom-up approach, especially vessel trips that transi-
tion from international waters to Belgian waters. This study found that most vessel turn
off their main engines at berth, except for oil tanker and passenger vessels due to quay
side activities. The study found that shipping emissions contributed 1.5% to total annual
CO2 emissions in Belgium, while contribution 30% and 22% to SO2 and NO2 respectively.
Development of an integrated modelling-measurement system for near-real-time
estimates of harbour activity impact to atmospheric pollution in coastal cities; E.
Merico, A Dinoi, and D. Contini - 2019 [34]
This study adopted a holistic approach to emission calculations, using AIS technology
to categorise ship behaviours as per EMEP/EEA bottom-up emissions calculations - AIS
technology will be discussed more in Section 4.1. Emissions from vehicles, cranes, and
other port activities were also considered and emission factors were applied in line with
EMEP/EEA documentation. Emissions were applied to dispersion point sources located
at quay locations and fed into a Gaussian plume model (ADMS-5). Assumed values
for exit velocity of the emissions (10m/s) from the vessel exhaust and the temperature
(160°C) of these emissions were implemented in the dispersion model. Assumed values
of stack height were assumed based on vessel categorisation. Emission generation and
dispersion were combined with local meteorological data and then calibrated against in-
situ monitoring devices in an effort to establish real-time estimates of harbour contribution
to atmospheric pollution. All calculations were conducted at an hourly temporal resolution.
The study cited that the emission factors presented in EMEP/EEA can vary from 20%
to 50% on true emission factors, due to variations in engine fuel oil, engine performance
etc.. However, it also considers that these sources of error are random and independent,
thus underestimations could be counter-acted by overestimation. It concludes, that 30%
is a reasonable margin of error to apply to emission factors.

Assessment of the impact of ship emissions on air quality based on a complete

This study is quite similar to the study performed in Bari by Merico et al. in that it uses
AIS data to classify ship behaviours in accordance with EMEP/EEA procedures. AIS data
for the year 2018 was utilised. These behaviour classes include entry to port, navigation
in port, beginning, middle and end of mooring phase, exit from port, and engine start and

Average vessel velocities are then applied in line with ship behaviour classification. Ves-
sel funnel height was assumed a function of the vessel length (lbp), while funnel diameters
were determined as a function of engine load. The release speed of emissions was calcu-
lated as a function of vessel activity in line with EMEP and EEA procedures. A regression

Assessment of the impact of gaseous ship emissions in ports using physical and
numerical models: The case of Naples: Toscano et al. - 2021 [31]
This study compares wind tunnel, computational fluid dynamic (CFD) and Gaussian dis-
persion models in relation to the dispersion of emissions from 3 cruise ships in the port
of Naples, Italy. The study recognises that Gaussian dispersion models are the most
commonly used, however they are often poor at calculating dispersion in an urban envi-
ronment which is strongly influenced by interactions between the atmospheric flow and the
buildings which gives rise to a wide variety of dispersion patterns, i.e. enhanced disper-
sion in the wake of a building, the trapping pollutants in street canyons, the channelling of
pollutants along canyon axes, and mixing and dispersion within street intersections [31].
However the study also recognises weaknesses regarding computational times of CFD
models, which make the method impractical for emission studies of ports which often con-
sider an entire calendar year. Therefore the purpose of this study is to calibrate Gaussian
puff models with results from wind tunnel and CFD models.
CALPUFF is the Gaussian dispersion model used in this particular study. As a Gaussian
puff model, CALPUFF simulates pollutant release via a series of puffs, each conatining a
mass of pollutant, this pollutant can be transported and convected by atmospheric con-
ditions. The CALPUFF model calculates the evolution of the puff spread (σx and σy) via
several options, namely i) direct turbulence measurements, ii) simliarity theory, and iii)
Pasquill-Gifford (rural) and McElroy-Pooler (urban) coefficients.
Agreement between the numerical models and the wind tunnel experiments results are
quantified with the normalised mean square error (NMSE), the fractional bias (FB), the
correlation factor (R), and the fraction of predictions within a factor of 2 (FAC2). The pa-
per cites a 2011 study by Hanna and Chang regarding targets for these metrics in an
urban setting, with NMSE <= 6, FAC2 >= 0.3 and FB <= 0.67 [43].
The study found CFD model results and wind tunnel results agreed well, however CALPUFF
measurements generally failed to achieve the agreement criteria mentioned above. The
authors of the paper attempted to calibrate CALPUFF results against wind tunnel and
CFD results in order to improve agreement. It was noted that an increase in UR ratio
i.e. the ratio of funnel gas velocity to wind speed resulted in a noticeable improvement in
accuracy. This indicates that initial propulsion effects will be an important consideration in
dispersion studies. The study also cites a 2010 study by Meneter and Egorov [44] stating
that the buoyancy effect does not have a major influence on ship emission dispersion,
although does not elaborate why.

modelling in EPISODE-CityChem v1.3; Pan et al. 2021 [45]
This study utilises the chemistry transport model CityChem-EPISODE, developed at Helmholtz-

combined. This study aims to analyse emission dispersion from ships operating in the

source”.
In the line source model setup, the ship’s emissions are assumed to be constantly emitted
along the ship’s route (which is assumed as a straight line) of one hour. For the spatially
averaged point source, a single fixed point is used to represent the average position of

Evaluation of the Urban Background Model (UBM) and AERMOD for Mumbai City:
Kumar et al. -2018 [46]
This report compares 2 Gaussian dispersion models and their modelling of emission dis-

directly, it offers interesting observations regarding the performance of the two models.
Note also that Mumbai is an industrial city, and the Gaussian plume models were applied
to stationary sources such as factories.
The two models considered are the Urban Background Model (UBM), developed by Aarhus
University, and AERMOD, developed by the United States Environmental Protection As-

input: temperature, global radiation, humidity, wind speed, wind direction, and mixing
height. The paper notes that AERMOD takes into account these parameters, and also
additionally considers pressure, rainfall and cloud cover. Despite this, the paper finds
that UBM performs better for gaseous emissions when compared with measured data,
while AERMOD performs better for particulate matter. This could suggest that rainfall
considerations are important in relation to the transport of particulate matter, but less so
for gaseous materials. The paper also cites that in India, suspension of a particulate mat-
ter is a common problem not accounted for in initial emissions inventories.

port model DEHM (Danish Eulerian Hemisphere Model), suggesting that different models
can be combined in order to achieve optimum results. After this calibration percentage
differences between modelled and observed emission results fell below +/- 3%, indicat-
ing that Gaussian models can give good approximations for stationary, time-invariant
sources. However, significant efforts to quantify background emissions and chemical
process may be required
A review of dispersion modelling and its application to the dispersion of particles:
An overview of different dispersion models available: Holmes N.S. and Morawska
L. - 2006 [47]
This study examines Lagrangian/Eulerian transport models, CFD models, and Gaussian
plume models. The study states that a major weakness of plume models is that the use of
steady state approximations do not take into account the time required for the pollutant to
travel to the receptor, and often they do not consider secondary chemical processes, such
as interactions between plumes from different sources which can be significant in urban
environments. For these reasons, Gaussian model results are often innacurate, particu-
larly in low wind conditions. However, the study also recognises that some of the issues
regarding steady state approximations can be overcome by approximating the emissions
as a series of puffs over time as this method allows puffs to behave and react to external
forces independently.

Literature review discussion
The above literature review unearthed many interesting findings, which will aid the current
study greatly. This includes:
• Passenger ships, especially cruise ships are generally the biggest contributor to ves-
sel emissions in port areas. This is due largely to their long hotelling durations where
auxiliary engines are running for hours at a time to maintain the vessel’s amenities
for passengers. In fact, the investigation into emissions in Copenhagen harbour by
Rambøll found that approximately 90% of cruise ship emissions occurred at quay
[51]. In fact, this Rambøll report found that quayside emissions were the primary
source of emissions across all vessel types, except for Ro-Ro freight ships. Stud-
ies by Aarhus University confirmed these findings, indicating that 93% of emissions
occurred at quay compared to manoeuvring phases.
• Ships are historically the biggest contributor to SO2 concentrations, however strict
regulations regarding fuel sulfur content have seen this contributions decline dra-
matically in recent years.
• Previous studies from Aarhus university indicate that ozone levels in Copenhagen
are not sufficiently high to convert all NOx emitted from ships in to NO2. For this
reason, 15% of NOx emissions will be assumed to be converted to NO2. As no
guidance is offered in relation to SOx and SO2, a one to one conversion scale will
be assumed. In any case SOx emissions are expected to be low due to MARPOL
requirements and SO2 limits are not expected to be threatened.
• The advent of AIS technology has made calculating emissions via the bottom-up
method a lot more viable. AIS data has improved the accuracy and ease at which
similar emission inventories for ports can be analysed by providing easily- available,
detailed information on shipping activities and shipping properties. However, it is
important to note that the information provided by AIS is largely dependent on the
vessel operator’s own diligence. AIS data can provide everything from the vessel
dimensions, operational phase, heading, etc., but only if the operator makes this
information available. In many cases, only the vessel’s speed over ground (SOG)
and maritime mobile service identity (MMSI) are readily available. This means that
consultation with port registers and similar vessel registers are often necessary for
accurate calculation of bottom-up emissions.

• Several different types of dispersion models have been used to estimate the disper-
sion of emissions from ships, these include the use of Eulerian chemical transport
models, CFD models, and Gaussian plume and puff models. While Gaussian mod-
els are considered the least accurate of these, they are also the most commonly
used due to their relative simplicity and fast computational times. Problems regard-
ing assumptions of stationarity and steady state can be overcome by utilising time
varying puff models and discretising shipping routes into a series of time varying
sources [37].

trapping of pollutants in street canyons, and channelling of pollutants along canyon

such as CO2 could prevent it from being used to form compounds such as NO2 and SO2.

project and assumptions will be applied in conjunction with the literature.

Literature review conclusions
The present study should utilise AIS data in order to obtain the most accurate emission in-

time series, and when combined with information regarding ship size, a highly accurate
emission inventory can be obtained. Calculating emission inventories with AIS also al-

latitude and longitude). This will allow the generation of several point sources related to
these emissions, which can potentially be combined with a Gaussian puff model, which
will cater to time varying emissions from these sources. However, the literature review

avoid inaccuracies associated with modelling dispersion in the urban environment.

3
Background Theory
The subject of ship emission modelling, emissions dispersion modelling, and the effects
of said emissions are a complex topic and have been investigated in several reports and
studies. Due to the complexity of each topic, many reports/studies focus on one aspect,
or, sub-aspect, of the aforementioned topics. However, comprehensive studies examin-
ing multiple aspects exist.
The aim of the literature review was to gain an appreciation of the methods and results
of these works and quantify a state of the art prior to commencement of the project. This
would aid in focusing methodologies for the current project with a view to optimising ef-
ficiency and accuracy. The sub-chapters below aim to highlight the main findings of this
literature review.
3.1
Automatic Identification System (AIS)
The Automatic Identification System, or AIS for short, was conceived as a navigational
aid for ship monitoring and collision avoidance at sea [38]. In 2000, IMO adopted a new
requirement ships to be fitted with AIS. The regulation requires AIS to be fitted aboard
all ships of 300 gross tonnage and upwards engaged on international voyages, cargo
ships of 500 gross tonnage and upwards not engaged on international voyages and all
passenger ships irrespective of size. The requirement became effective on the 31st of
December, 2004 [60]. The regulation requires that AIS shall:
• provide information - including the ship’s identity, type, position, course, speed, nav-
igational status and other safety-related information - automatically to appropriately
equipped shore stations, other ships, and aircraft;
• receive automatically such information from similarly fitted ships;
• monitor and track ships;
• exchange data with shore-based facilities.
The AIS system is useful for the evaluation of ship emissions, as it provides continuously
automatic information on the vessel positions and instantaneous speeds of ships [39]. Its
system regularly transmits static and voyage-related information every 6 min in addition
to dynamic information with a frequency related to the vessel’s speed underway (2–10
s) and navigational status (3 min when anchored)[38]. This data can be used to estab-
lish the relationship between vessel speed and required engine output, in turn leading to
an accurate estimation of emissions from the vessel [51] [61]. Ship traffic and emission
studies prior to 2004 largely relied on port register tables, behavioural approximations,
top-down regression models based on fuel sale statistics, or some combination of these
[18][62] [16]
The above means that detailed data of individual vessel position and activity are available
at a global scale. However is should be noted that the resolution of this data depends on
the location of the vessels as they emit their respective signals. For example, even if a
vessel emits AIS signals on a minute-by-minute basis, these signals may be out of range
of all terrestrial AIS receivers. When out of range of terrestrial receivers, the AIS data
are picked up by satellite receivers. The resolution of this data depends on how often the
satellite orbits earth [41]. This is highly variable and can exceed several hours, leading to
Investigation of exhaust gas dispersion from ships over urban areas in Copenhagen
31
==PAGE:46==
potentially thousands of kilometres between consecutive AIS signals for vessels [39]. It
is not envisaged that this problem will be encountered during this project concerned with
Copenhagen port, however, it is possible that duplicate AIS signals will be encountered
as multiple stations receive the same AIS signal.
The application of AIS data to estimation of port emissions inventories has been utilised
all over the world. Tichavska and Tovar[38] utilised AIS data in tandem with the STEAM
emissions modelling software [39], IHS Fairplay ship register , engine manufacturer spec-
ifications, local authority registers, and consultation with ship owners to determine emis-
sions at Las Palmas port in the Canary Islands. Toscano et al. [37] combined AIS with
EMEP/EEA guidelines to estimate the annual emission inventory for the port of Naples,
Italy. Similar studies were also noted in Bari, Italy [34], China [63], and Copenhagen,
Denmark [51].
The current project hopes to utilise AIS data from dma.dk to calculate emission rates
corresponding to velocity time series presented by AIS data. These calculations will be
aided by a calls table provided by the port of Copenhagen for the year 2019 providing
additional vessel information such as each individual vessel’s gross tonnage.
3.2
Ship Emissions Inventory Calculations (SHIP-DESMO)
For the purpose of this project, the emissions inventory will be calculated with the SHIP-
DESMO suite of calculation spreadsheets. Hans Otto Kristensen of HOK Marineconsult
ApS has kindly provided these spreadsheets, along with supporting documentation and
his personal guidance throughout the project.
The SHIP-DESMO spreadsheets are extensive and comprehensive calculation tools based
on a combination of ship-propulsion properties, hydrodynamic resistance properties, and
utilisation of empirical and regression analysis-based relations [64][65][66]. This thesis
will not attempt to explain and derive the calculations in detail, but rather summarise key
aspects and and how these relate to the project at hand.
3.2.1
SHIP-DESMO Power Calculations
As a vessel moves through water, a certain amount of power is required to generate the
forward movement to overcome not only frictional resistance from the water, but also
resistance from the ambient air and wave conditions. (Although, weather conditions were
not considered in the current project due to the focus on a sheltered harbour area, SHIP-
DESMO can incorporate inclement weather conditions via a Beaufort number input [67]).
In SHIP-DESMO, the total resistance experienced by a vessel can be calculated as per
Equation (3.1).
RT = 1
2 · CT · ρ · S · V 2
(3.1)
where CT is the total resistance coefficient as the vessel moves through the water, ρ rep-
resents the density of water (assumed as 1,025 kg/m3), S is the wetted surface area of the
hull (m2), and V is the vessel velocity (m/s). Note that AIS dtata generally provides vessel
velocity in terms of knots, but SHIP-DESMO includes conversion factors to maintain SI
units in its formulae. This resistance coefficient takes into account several parameters via
the relation CT = CF + CA + CAA + CR [66] where:

• CF is the frictional resistance coefficient. This coefficient helps quantify the sum
of tangential stresses along the wetted surface in the direction of the motion. This
takes into account the Reynolds number as the ship moves through the water at
velocity V .
• CA is the incremental resistance coefficient. The frictional resistance mentioned
above is related to physical model studies. The incremental resistance coefficient
caters for the extrapolation of roughness parameters from model scale to full-size
vessel scale.
• CAA is the air resistance coefficient. This coefficient quantifies the resistance as the
ship moves through the air.
• CR is the residual resistance coefficient. The residual resistance includes wave
resistance , the viscous pressure resistance, and the additional resistance due to
the form or curvature of the hull [66].
Once the resistance encountered by a ship is known, the power required to overcome
that resistance and achieve the observed velocity can be found by Equation (3.2)
PE = RT · V
(3.2)
where PE (W) represents the effective/ observed power, RT (N) represents the total ship
resistance, and V (m/s) represents the the observed velocity of the ship.
In addition to this, the efficiency of the engine, propeller, and even hull form, can exacer-
bate the amount of initial propulsion power required to achieve a certain speed. Efficiency
parameters are applied to the observed power, PE, in order to calculate the actual propul-
sion power produced by the engine, PP . The relationship between PE and PP can be
seen in Equation (3.3)
Pp = PE · ηT
(3.3)
where ηT is the total efficiency of the vessel. It is calculated as ηT = ηH · ηO · ηR · ηS,
where:
• ηH is the hull efficiency. This quantifies the ratio between the effective power of a
hull as it moves through water to the thrust power developed by the propellers.
• ηO is propeller in open water condition. This quantifies the efficiency of a propeller
type in open water condition.
• ηR is the relative rotation efficiency. This quantifies the reduction in propulsive power
generated by a propeller due to its location close to the hull, compared to the open
water condition.
• ηS is the transmission efficiency, relating engine power output to propeller power
output.
Once the power exerted by a vessel is known, the amount of fuel it is consuming can be
estimated by considering the actual power and the specific fuel oil consumption of the
engine.

3.3
Dispersion Modelling
Throughout the literature review for this project, a number of methods of dispersion mod-
elling were encountered. These usually consisted of already-existing software packages
- some private, some open-source - based on an array of methods including 3-D Eulerian,
computational fluid dynamic models, Gaussian plume models, and Gaussian puff mod-
els. After considering the monetary cost of softwares and ease of software use, the author
decided that modelling dispersion through either Gaussian plume models, Gaussian puff
models, or some combination of both was the optimal solution. However both methods
have their advantages and disadvantages.
Gaussian plume models assume that pollutant concentrations from a source can be rep-
resented assuming the pollutants are dispersed according to a normal distribution. Under
this assumption, concentrations relative to the source will vary with x, y, and z coordinates,
with standard deviations of the formula being dictated by turbulence conditions induced by
parameters such as terrain roughness and weather conditons such as wind speed, wind
direction, precipitation, temperature gradients etc.. Mixing with the adjacent atmosphere
is greatest at the edge of the plume, leading to statistically lower concentrations in this
area [33]. A simple Gaussian plume model, neglecting reflexion terms for simplicity, can
be seen in Equation (3.6) [45]

plume constantly emitting at the same rate, individual puffs containing a certain mass of

3.3.1
RIMPUFF
After assessing all possible dispersion options available, it was thought that the application
of a Gaussian puff dispersion model was the best compromise between model accuracy,
computational efficiency, and user accessibility. In this project, the Risø Mesoscale PUFF
model (RIMPUFF) will be applied to a series of temporally-varying point sources deter-
mined from well defined shipping routes in the harbour. This software, first developed dur-
ing the 1980s at DTU’s Risø campus, can model continuous, steady pollutant releases
through a series of consecutively released puffs. At each timestep the model advects,
diffuses and deposits individual puffs according to meteorological parameter values and
calculates concentrations from puffs and deposited materials [70][71]. The model can
calculate concentrations for each grid point, based on the average concentration over a
timestep. The duration of these timesteps can be changed manually, but in the current
project a timestep of 1 hour is chosen in order to return hourly average concentrations.
3.3.2
Overview of RIMPUFF
A rough overview of how pollutants are dispersed according to RIMPUFF is presented in
Figure 3.1. Puffs are assumed to be generated by ships as they enter harbour. Pollutant
material is assigned to puffs, with the amount of pollutant material in a puff dictated by
the emission release rate multiplied by the time elapsed between puffs. Therefore, longer
time intervals between puffs mean less puffs with higher concentrations, while shortening
the timestep will create more puffs at a lower concentration [70]. From an accuracy point
of view, shortening the timestep is considered more accurate. In the current project, this
release time between puffs was set as 600s (10 minutes), however future iterations of the
project should consider shortening this timestep. This means a source could potentially
release up to 6 puffs in 1 hour. The concentration distribution of the pollutant within the
puff is Gaussian in all three dimensions.
At each timestep the model advects, diffuses and deposits individual puffs according to
similarity theory which accounts for friction velocity, the surface heat flux, the height of the
atmospheric boundary layer, the Monin-Obukhov length and the Lagragian time scale for
the turbulence, all as described by a 1992 study by Carruthers [72].

Phase 1- AIS Data Processing
The purpose of this project is to determine the contribution of vessels utilising Copenhagen
harbour to air pollution concentrations in the surrounding urban area. Emissions will be
calculated based on ship activity in the harbour, while dispersion of these emissions will
be assessed using a Gaussian puff model. The first step in this process is to quantify ship
activity in Copenhagen harbour. This is done by processing the AIS data which vessels
transmit upon entering the harbour.

4.1.4
Sub-step 1C: Sort and cleaning the data
After the data has been cut down to the relevant area and IMOs as per the port log tables,
the data for the entire calendar year is compiled, and then sorted according to individual
vessels and “trips” into the harbour. Categorising AIS data signals into different “trips”
allows for easy calculation of emission inventories on a “trip-by-trip” basis
Vessel trips
The definition of a vessel trip into the harbour is important as it allows an accurate quantifi-
cation of a vessel’s time in a harbour. This mitigates against errors of integrating emission
rates across unreasonable time frames. For example, if a vessel leaves the harbour on
Sunday, and re-enters the following Wednesday, a possible error might be to interpolate
emission rates from the last signal on Sunday to the first signal on Wednesday. This would
be erroneous as no emissions should be considered if the vessel is not in the harbour.
Considering emissions outside of the harbour would lead to a significant overestimation
of emissions produced in Copenhagen harbour and is outside the scope of the current
project. Therefore, establishing well-defined trips is necessary for the calculation of an
accurate emission inventory. In this project a trip is defined as a series of data for a single
IMO-registered vessel, where the interval between consecutive timesteps is less than 1
hour. As soon as this limit is exceeded, as new trip is defined.
In addition to this, another significant assumption is to only consider “trips” greater than
one hour duration. This is to mitigate against making emission calculation efforts for ves-
sels which pass the port boundaries en route to another location. This can occur a lot in
the south-western boundary of Copenhagen harbour as smaller vessels such as private
yachts and dredgers travel along the periphery of the port’s boundary. The emission con-
tribution of these smaller trips is very minor compared to larger tankers and cruise liners
entering the port [51].
Cleaning the data
As well as establishing well-defined trips and activities in the port, the elimination of er-
roneous data velocity signals is important in order to prevent against overestimation of
emissions. Several different procedures were trialled, including determining vessel ac-
celerations and eliminating data points exceeding a certain threshold. However, the “sig-
nal.find_peaks” function was deemed to produce the best results in terms of eliminating

4.1.5
Sub-step 1D:AIS resampling
As SHIP-DESMO is available as a Microsoft Excel-based tool for the purpose of this
project, a way to easily input the sorted and cleaned AIS data into the relevant excel
tool was required. In order to achieve this, it was decided to print the data to a central in-
put sheet, to which the SHIP-DESMO models were linked. However, this required that the
amount of data points in the input sheet to be consistent with the relevant SHIP-DESMO
data entries in order to avoid errors. After consultation with supervisors, it was thought
that 300 data points across platforms would offer a good compromise between data res-
olution and computational speed.
Despite the sorting and cleaning procedures mentioned above, the data for a single ves-
sel’s trip can vary greatly from less than 50 timesteps to over several thousand. Many
datapoints in particular were noted when vessels remain docked in the harbour for a long
time, as often occurred due to travel restrictions incurred by the COVID-19 pandemic.
Therefore, a method of reducing (or increasing) a data set to the desired length of 300
data points was required. This could be done via simple linear interpolation of speed vs.
time, however it was feared that this would fail to capture some important information as
the vessels briefly accelerate and decelerate on their way into the harbour.
In order to overcome this, a method of clever resampling was taken from a draft pa-
per written by Michael Zevin and Mads Sørensen [79], the details of which will be made
available as soon as possible. The aim of this method is to resample the data based on
the quantifiable rate of change between data points, rather than the simple progression of
time. This method should see a low resolution of resampled data points when the vessel
behaviour is unchanged and predictable, i.e. when it is stationary at quay. Conversely,
increasing resolution will be noted when the ship behaviour is changing and is less pre-
dictable, i.e. when the vessel is accelerating or decelerating in and out of the harbour.
This method leads to a more accurate calculation of emissions as a single data point
whilst the ship is at quay can be representative of the entire time the ship is at quay i.e.
the velocity is 0 knots for the entire time the ship is hotelling, and only one data point is
needed to represent this. This means the remaining data points can focus on more vari-
able phases of the vessel’s journey.

5
Method and Assumption Validation
This section will compare the results of the present study with previous and similar studies
in order to assess the validity of the models, techniques, and assumptions employed. This
process will facilitate more accurate analysis of results in Chapter 6.
5.1
Resampling technique
When a data set has been altered, it is easy to compromise on the accuracy of that data
set. In order to validate the resampling method described in Section 4.1.5 the coefficient
of determination between the original dataset and the re-sampled dataset is calculated for
each trip. In addition, this section tries to identify instances where the resampling method
did not meet expectations and not only establish reasons for this, but also if these in-
stances had a detrimental effect on the calculation of emissions in the project.
Recall from Section 4.1.5 that the current project required the resampling technique re-
duce (or in a minority of cases, expand) a velocity vs. time data series to 300 points.
The greater the amount of data in the original series, the greater the chances of losing
important information about the vessel trip upon resampling. In particular, the calendar
years 2020 and 2021 saw some vessels restricted to port for extensive lengths of time
due to COVID-19 travel restrictions; the Crown Seaways was recorded as being at quay
from 02/11/2020 to 01/07/2021. That means a single “trip” defined in Section 4.1 lasted
from 01/01//2021 to 01/07/2021 - 7 months (note that trip numbers are also reset every
new calendar year, otherwise this trip would have lasted 9 months). This resulted in over
130,000 data points for a single trip. Furthermore, the dredger Elisabeth Hoej registered
321,866 data points in a single trip between May and June 2021. Resampling this to 300
points could prove erroneous to even the most adept resampling techniques. This section
will identify instances where the resampling technique has failed and quantify their effect
on emission studies.
In order to quantify the similarity between original and resampled data, the coefficient
of determination is calculated between each trip’s original and resampled data series.
The coefficient of determination, or the R2 value, is a statistical measurement that relates
the differences in one variable to the differences of another [84]. Thus, a high R2 value
between the resampled and original data sets can indicate that the resampled data offers
a good approximation of the original data, even if the number of data points varies greatly.
On average in the current project, the R2 value between original and resampled data for
each trip was 0.99. However, a small number of trips achieved R2 values below 0.6.

5.1.2
Resampling technique conclusions
The resampling technique, which resampled data from their original length to a fixed data
series 300 points long, was seen to perform excellently throughout, producing an R2 co-
efficient of 0.99 between original and resampled data across approximately 6,000 vessel
trips into the harbour. The effectiveness of this measure even saw one data series of
approximately 130,000 data points resampled to 300 with an R2 value of 0.94. Poor per-
formance of the resampling techniques were seldom, and are outlined above, however the
following lessons were learned about application of the resampling technique for future
projects.
• The resamping tecnnique can perform poorly if there is simply too much activity to
resample. See the case of the Elisabeth Hoej above. In these instances it could be
worth sub-dividing the original data length based on length of time series.
• The resampling technique performs can perform poorly if the vessel is stationary
for the entire trip as there is no discernible measure of change - the basis of the
resampling technique. However in the current project this was not a problem, as
when a vessel is stationary for its entire “trip”, then a velocity of 0 can be applied
across the entire resampled time series without any interpolation.

7
Discussion
7.1
Emission and dispersion results
Results from this project indicated that the COVID-19 pandemic, which initiated a series of
various travel restrictions in Europe and the wider world from approximately March 2020
to the present, had a significant impact on emissions from ships in Copenhagen harbour.
This is primarily due to reduced cruise ship activity as travel restrictions impacted the
tourism industry significantly. Total CO2 emissions drop by approximately 20% year on
year, leading to an approximately 40 % reduction in CO2 originating from ships in the har-
bour between 2019 and 2021.
Despite reductions in overall emissions, pollutant concentrations from ship sources alone
were noted as breaching WHO 24-hour mean NO2 limits on several occasions, even at
elevations as low as 10m at Copenhagen International School. This is an alarming finding
for several reasons as no background concentrations were applied to the model, indicating
that real concentration levels are greater than that indicated by RIMPUFF. In addition to
this, Section 5.4 indicated that RIMPUFF underestimates concentrations, further indicat-
ing that pollutant levels in this busy residential area exceed dangerous levels as dictated
by the WHO.
In many more instances, 24-hour mean NO2 limits were exceeded at elevations of 30m
and 50m. While it is difficult to quantify the effect these emissions have on ground level
concentrations in an urban environment, it could indicated that air quality levels around
the port area are sometimes unsafe.
It was noted that WHO and Danish health authority limits were never exceeded for SO2
and PM2.5, indicating that measures implemented by MARPOL have had a positive effect.
Nevertheless, a wider review of air quality in Copenhagen could be warranted. This could
incorporate emissions from other sources such as traffic and other industries aside from
shipping.
In a port setting, the vast majority (over 85%) of these emissions come from the ship idling
at quay for significant periods of time. Ways to reduce power usage while at quay could
result in a significant decrease in emission inventories and pollutant concentrations. This
could include the provision of onshore power, removing the need for ships to run auxiliary
engines which contribute a huge amount to emissions. Also, moving ferry terminals away
from busy residential areas would result in lower local pollutant concentrations. Concen-
tration plots in Section 6.2 suggest areas around Nordhavn and Østerbro are significantly
affected by emissions from ships.
7.2
Future improvements and potential
There are many ways the current project could be improved and developed. These are
described below:
• The current project developed a model which combined historical AIS data and me-
teorological data to estimate emission generation and dispersion from ships. Future
projects could implement live input data, to provide up-to-date information to port or
Investigation of exhaust gas dispersion from ships over urban areas in Copenhagen
107
==PAGE:122==
public authorities. This could be used as an early detection method for high pol-
lutant levels, allowing local authorities to issue relative health warnings. It could
also be used as a method to incentivise more environmentally friendly practices,
with penalties/rewards being introduced for vessel operators in relation to certain
emission thresholds.
• The current project was limited by the information available, primarily the 2019 calls
table which did not offer information on vessel which arrived in harbour after 2019.
Future iterations of the project could employ a system whereby emission are cal-
culated for “most similar vessel” where some information is unknown. This method
has been used to good effect in previous emission studies [39].
• Incorporating background concentrations, and calibrating dispersion models with
monitoring data could greatly improve the accuracy and confidence in dispersion
calculations. In particular, the advent of machine learning methods could aid cali-
bration methods.
• The method of resampling data sometimes performed poorly due to a over saturation
of datapoints/activity to resampled. Future iterations of the project should perhaps
alter the definition of a “trip” so that data accuracy is not compromised.

8
Conclusion
The current project successfully combined historical AIS data and historical meteorolog-
ical data to assess the effect of emissions in Copenhagen urban area. This study found
that emission generation from vessels in the harbour has reduced significantly due to
travel restrictions imposed by the COVID-19 pandemic. These restrictions limited cruise
ship activity in the harbour, which contributed approximately 57% of the total emission
inventory in 2019. This contribution from cruise ships fell to 21% and 19% in 2020 and
2021 respectively.
Another goal of the project was to quantify the effect of these emissions on the surrounding
urban area. Results indicate that residential zones in Nordhavn and Østerbro are signifi-
cantly affected by shipping emissions. Even without adding background concentrations,
or emissions from other sources, dispersion modelling indicated that NO2 concentrations
are prone to exceeding WHO-stipulated limits. In particular, these areas are sensitive
to southern and easterly winds, as pollutants from cruise ships docked at Langelinie are
carried over the surrounding urban area.
In conclusion, the current project showed that it is possible to effectively combine AIS
data and emission generation models to quantify the effect of pollutants from shipping in
an urban area. As AIS data is utilised by most of the global fleet, methods in this project
can be extended to other port studies.

RIMPUFF CBRN source input
Poul Astrup
DTU Wind Energy
CBRN means Chemical,Biological,Radiological,Nuclear. The two last
types (RN) both concerns radioactive items for which reason they are
treated equally. Chemical and biological (CB) items are also treated
equally, but differently from the radioactive items. A CB or a RN run is
selected by setting "I_SourceType" in file "rimpar.dat" to 2 (CB) 
respectively to 0 (RN), see "RIMPUFF_specification_input". I.e. the two 
types cannot be mixed for a single RIMPUFF calculation.
RIMPUFF can run on specified source terms or it can be run on output 
from SourceModel or on output from URD taking over puffs created by 
these programs. 
RIMPUFF with no puff takeover
-----------------------------
Needed files
------------
"rimiso.dat"
"rimsrc_NNN.dat", one for each source, NNN the three digit source 
number.
Optional file
-------------
"Depext.dat"
For normal runs with no puff take over from neither SourceModel nor URD,
the source information consists of the file "rimiso.dat" holding a list 
of the items/nuclides to take into account, given as their correctly 
formatted names and a number of source files "rimsrc_NNN.dat", where NNN
is the 3-digit source number, 001, 002, to maximum 100, holding the 
release information. The "rimsrc_001.dat" is mandatory, for more sources
the higher numbers come into play. The correct format for nuclides names
can be seen in the model data file "isotopes.dat", delivered with the 
code.
For nuclear cases a maximum of 60 nuclides can be handled in a single 
run.
Source files for chemical and biological releases have a somewhat 
different format than those for radioactive isotopes, and release rates 
can be specified for only a single item in each source file. RIMPUFF has
no internal database for chemicals or biological agents. Depending on 
information in the "rimsrc_NNN.dat" the chemicals are deposited as 
aerosols and particles or are like noble gasses not deposited as all.
The file "depext.dat" is a source file for deposition of matter other 
than that in the puffs. It deposits the specified amouts at the 
specified times. The deposits are equally distributed within an ellipse 
==PAGE:172==
given by the center, the major and minor axes, and the direction of 
major axis. The items are given as strings, and for a non-nuclear 
release, these strings have to be given also in the "rimiso.dat" file. 
The ground concentration of so deposited items are added to the 
concentration of deposition from the puffs.
XML
---
RIMPUFF contains an XML parser to generate the needed "rimiso.dat", all 
"rimsrc_NNN.dat" an the optional "depext.dat" from a correctly formatted
xml source term file. The content and format of this is described in 
report "XML_source_input".
rimiso.dat
----------
"rimiso.dat" shall contain a list of items given with their name in
apostrophes, one per line, terminated with '**' in the last line.
RN example
----------
'I -131'
'Xe-133'
'Cs-137'
'**'
----------
CB example
----------
'NH3'
'Sulphur dioxide'
'SF6'
'**'
----------
rimsrc_NNN.dat
--------------
"rimsrc_NNN.dat", where NNN is the source number (001 to 100), is the
source specification file. "rimsrc_001.dat" is mandatory, only with more
sources are higher numbers also needed. The source file is different for
RN and CB items.
For RN items, i.e. nuclides, it consists of an initial specification of
source position and particle diameter and density
  x_UTM/longitude    y_UTM/latitude    UTM-zone   | [m]  [m]  [-]    or
                                                  | [deg]  [deg]  -1
  particle_diameter      particle_density         | [m]  [kg/m3]
followed by groups of release data, one group for each period of
constant release. The group format is the following:
  start_time_for_group   duration_for group       | [s] [s]
  release_height         release_power            | [m] [kW]
==PAGE:173==
  SigmaXY                SigmaZ                   | [m] [m]
  elementary     organic_bound   aerosol_bound    | Iodine fractions
  release_rate   '1st_nuclide_Name'               | [Bq/s] [string]
  release_rate   '2nd_nuclide_Name'
  release_rate   '3rd_nuclide_Name'
  .
  .
  .
  release_rate   'Nth_nuclide_name'
  0.0            '**'                             | group end
A diameter of zero means a gas and the specified density then has no 
influence.
The "start_time_for_group" is time after initial release, the time of 
which is specified in input file "lsmc.sup" (see 
"RIMPUFF_specification_input").
The "release_power" is the power released to the atmosphere due to
excess temperature of the release. This causes plume rise.
SigmaXY and SigmaZ are the (so called) puff sizes horizontally and
vertically at release time, i.e. the standard deviations of the assumed
Gaussian concentration distributions in the puffs.
After the number of release groups a final line shall hold
  end_time_of_release     -1

   0.000   0.000   1.000

     90600        -1            end of release    | [s] [-]
----------
For CB items each "rimsrc_NNN.dat" file can contain release information
for only one item.
Again the first entries are the position plus particle diameter and
density:
  x_UTM/longitude    y_UTM/latitude    UTM-zone   | [m]  [m]  [-]   or
                                                  | [deg]  [deg]  -1
  particle_diameter      particle_density         | [m]  [kg/m3]
followed by groups of release information. The group format is the
following:
  start_time_for_group   duration_for group       | [s] [s]
  release_height         release_power            | [m] [kW]
  SigmaXY                SigmaZ                   | [m] [m]
  release_rate    0/1    'Item name'              | [?/s]  [-]  string
  0.0             0/1    '**'                     | group end
The 0 or 1 after the release rate specifies if the item does not deposit
(0) or deposits as a particle/aerosole (1). The release rate unit is
[?/s] where "?" can be whatever. The output air concentration is
correspondingly [?/m3] and the output ground deposition concentration
[?/m2]. After the release groups a final line shall hold:
  end_time_of_release    -1                       | [s] [-]

----------

 0.0000E+00  0  '**'            end of time group
  1200       12000              Second time group
 95.00  0.0000E+00
  8.0   5.0
 6.1700E+00  0  'SF6'
 0.0000E+00  0  '**'
 13200          -1              end of release
----------

Depext.dat
----------
"depext.dat" stands for "external deposition", i.e. deposition from 
other sources than puffs, e.g. initial deposition of large items from an
explosion where only the dust/aerosoles are carried away with the wind 
and to be modelled with RIMPUFF.
In case such deposition is wanted, the file "depext.dat" shall be
present in the same directory as "rimiso.dat" and shall only specify 
items listed in that file.
The external deposition is given as groups in the following format:
  time_of_depostion   | Time after initial release [s]
  x  y  UTM_zone      | Central point coordinates of deposition: [m] in 
                      | UTM_zone or decimal degrees for UTM_zone -1
  a  b                | Deposition ellipse major and minor half axis [m]
  dir                 | Compass bearing [deg] of major axis
  Nitems              | Number of items
  Amount_deposited  '1st_Item_name'
  Amount_deposited  '2nd_Item_name'
  .
  .
  .
  Amount_deposited  'Nitems'th_Item_name'
"time_of_deposition" is in seconds after initial release.
If UTM_zone equals -1, x and y shall be longitude and latitude given in
decimal degrees, negative west of Greenwich, south of Equator, else in
metres, again negative south of the Equator, no false northing allowed.
"Amount_deposited" shall be in the unit corresponding to the source unit
in "rimsrc_NNN.dat", i.e. [Bq] or [?].
The item names are checked against those given in "rimiso.dat", so if
item names are not found there, external deposition shall not work.
The external concentration on ground is calculated as "Amount_deposited"
divided by the ellipse area, and this concentration is added to the
otherwise calculated grid and detector point concentrations within the 
ellipse.

RIMPUFF source files with puff take over from SourceModel
---------------------------------------------------------
The SourceModel puff files each contain release information for a single
chemical only. For RIMPUFF to operate with more SourceModel puff files 
in the same run, an equivalent number of sources have to be specified. 
They can have coinciding release coordinates if relevant.
Needed files
------------
"rimiso.dat"
"rimsrc_NNN.dat", one for each source, NNN the three digit source 
number.
"puff_chem_NNN.txt"
"puff_chem_NNN.che"
rimiso.dat
----------
==PAGE:177==
As for a no puff takeover chemical release the "rimiso.dat" file has 
here to hold the names of the released chemicals.
rimsrc_NNN.dat
--------------
From each "rimsrc_NNN.dat" only the first two lines with the source 
position and the particle diameter and density are read and so the only 
needed content.
SourceModel output files to update
----------------------------------
SourceModel is a tank blowdown model, possibly forming a liquid pool on
the ground from where gasses evaporate as puffs.
The output files from SourceModel are named "puff_chem.txt", 
"concmap.cnc", and "dosemap.tic". As input to RIMPUFF they need to be 
renamed "puff_chem_NNN.txt", "concmap_NNN.cnc", and "dosemap_NNN.tic", 
where NNN is the applicable three digit source number. The file 
"puff_chem_NNN.dat" describes groups of puff releases.
Be aware, that the puff masses in "puff_chem_NNN.txt" are in [g], the 
chemical concentrations in "concmap_NNN.cnc" in [g/m3], and the time 
integrated concentrations in "dosemap_NNN.tic" in [g s/m3].
Example of "puff_chem_NNN.txt" file
-----------------------------------
       0     5
1     127.00   -440.64   274.92     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
2     127.00   -451.22   254.15     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
3     127.00   -461.80   233.39     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
4     127.00   -472.38   212.62     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
5     127.00   -482.96   191.86     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
      60     5
6     127.00   -440.64   274.92     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
7     127.00   -451.22   254.15     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
8     127.00   -461.80   233.39     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
9     127.00   -472.38   212.62     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
10    127.00   -482.96   191.86     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
First line of a release group:
group time   number of puffs in group
       0     5
Each released puff in the group then has a line:
no.   Time         x        y        z     sigxy    sigz        Q
1     127.00   -440.64   274.92     9.89   10.49    6.59   17426.80
where
no.:   puff number
Time:  release time relative to the group time [s]
x:     puff release x-position relative to source point [m]
y:     puff release y-position relative to source point [m]
z:     puff release z-position (height above ground) [m]
sigxy: horizontal puff size (i.e. concentration standard deviation) [m]
sigz:  vertical puff size [m]
Q:     puff mass [g]
==PAGE:178==
puff_chem_NNN.che
-----------------
For each source a file named "puff_chem_NNN.che" is to be created. It 
shall contain the name of the chemical from source NNN - same name as 
specified in "rimiso.dat" - and the time [s] between the initial release
specified in the "lsmc.sup" and the time corresponding to group time 0 
in the puff release file "puff_chem_NNN.txt". This time delay is by 
RIMPUFF applied to the puff releases and to the output times in the 
RIMPUFF formatted binary files generated from "concmap_NNN.cnc", and 
"dosemap_NNN.tic".
Example of "puff_chem_NNN.che" file
-----------------------------------
'Ammonia'   200.0
XML
---
Specifying the sources in RIMPUFF's xml-format, the xml reader creates 
the "puff_chem_NNN.che" together with the "rimiso.dat" and the 
"rimsrc_NNN.dat" files. But the renaming of the individual 
"puff_chem.txt", "concmap.cnc", and "dosemap.tic" has to be done by the 
user before the run of RIMPUFF.
RIMPUFF source files with puff take over from URD
-------------------------------------------------
RIMPUFF can take over the URD puffs which have left the URD calculation 
area and those remaining in this area, when the URD calculation stops. 
RIMPUFF takes over all items/nuclides specified from the URD run, and 
URD generates all source files needed by RIMPUFF. This include the ascii
file "items.dat" holding the names of the released items/nuclides – that
is in stead of "rimiso.dat", - the "rimsrc_001.dat" only containing the 
normal first line giving the source position of first source, and the 
binary file "URD_Puff_Leave.dat" with the puffs for RIMPUFF to take 
over.
