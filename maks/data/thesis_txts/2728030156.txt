Abstract  
With the emergence of Industry 4.0 (I4.0), the manufacturing processes get further advanced and 
complex. At the same time, I4.0 presents opportunities for sustainability, offering economic, ecological 
and social advantages. This development has driven companies to reassess their business models and 
explore new approaches. However, Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) face challenges in 
implementing I4.0 and leveraging sustainability to survive in today's competitive environment. 
This thesis aims to identify the technologies widely adopted to achieve sustainable results in 
manufacturing automation. The impacts of different I4.0 technologies (I4.0T) are analyzed and 
compared based on the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) principle, including environmental, economic and 
social aspects. Additionally, for SMEs the key characteristics of the transition are identified as key 
barriers and enablers. Finally, a framework is proposed, suggesting different approaches to help SMEs 
adopt sustainability through I4.0T in their operations. 
This research is based on a comprehensive literature review focused on assessing key features of the 
transition. The objective is to thoroughly evaluate the impacts of various sustainability-related 
technologies while considering the specific characteristics of SMEs. This analysis aims to help SMEs to 
develop a strategic plan for implementing I4.0 manufacturing processes and accessing sustainability 
advantages by evaluating and comparing different approaches. 
The framework proposed in this study provides key considerations and actions for SMEs to follow. 
While it is flexible to accommodate SMEs' unique challenges, it does not offer solutions to fundamental 
issues. Rather, by carefully considering the critical barriers and identifying ways to transform barriers 
into enablers, SMEs can develop a tailored approach for a successful implementation. This work guides 
choosing the right technologies, methods and approaches by pointing out important considerations 
and assessing the pros and cons in a holistic discussion.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Potential of I4.0T to Enable Sustainable Manufacturing Processes 
I4.0, also known as the "Industrial Internet," the "Internet of Things" or "Smart Manufacturing," is a 
ubiquitous term that defines the fourth industrial revolution. The term originated in Germany and was 
initially defined as a government strategy to transform traditional manufacturing systems by combining 
advanced digital technologies with physical operations (Drath and Horch 2014). Today, this definition 
still applies to the technologies and concepts of I4.0. The industry can collect data throughout the 
supply chain and respond to real-time changes through the digitalization and integration of 
manufacturing systems. 
At the same time, the growing corporate commitment to sustainable development has made Corporate 
Sustainability (CS) an integral part of business strategy and processes. As a result, many companies are 
rethinking how they do business and developing strategies to manage their supply chain and product 
lifecycle sustainably. CS is usually associated with the economic, social and environmental dimensions 
known as the triple bottom line (TBL). (Cochran and Rauch 2020, p. 1238) In this context, I4.0 is 
emerging as an alternative to help companies manage sustainability issues and has gained increasing 
popularity among scholars and practitioners (Beltrami M. 2019). Such an integrated approach could be 
guided by the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG), defined by the United Nations 2030 Agenda for 
ensuring sustainable technological progress. They provide essential targets for energy, climate action 
and beyond. Similarly, the multiple perspectives on TBL dimensions provide a holistic framework for 
thinking about a strategy for sustainable business design.  
Achieving sustainable development through the transformation of industrial production is a significant 
challenge. Therefore, there are high expectations of the contribution of I4.0 to master this challenge. 
I4.0 reflects the transformation of traditional production systems into smart manufacturing through 
the connectivity of advanced digital technologies and physical operations. By leveraging the benefits of 
connectivity and information pooling, sustainable actions are more accessible. 
Manufacturing is the primary driver of human welfare and prosperity. However, it is also a prominent 
direct and indirect contributor to natural resource depletion, environmental pollution and social 
conflict. These negative impacts grow with the global demand for manufactured goods, which will 
continue to increase as the world's population grows. As Least Developed Countries (LDC) aim to 
achieve the same standard of living as richer countries, the overall standard of living is improving. It is 
essential to consider how sustainable manufacturing systems and the digital transformation of the 
==PAGE:7==
industry can work together to achieve this goal. A closer look reveals the benefits and limitations of 
this situation. 
Conclusively, companies need help to integrate these trends into their long-term business strategy and 
design. I4.0 promises strategic advantages for companies in many ways, but the digital transition 
requires more tools and approaches for integrating digital manufacturing technologies into an overall 
enterprise system design. In addition, a framework needs to address sustainability issues to enable 
long-term benefits. Not all companies face similar challenges in adapting to technology and 
sustainability. The transformation process is especially challenging for SMEs, as for smaller and midsize 
companies the access hurdles seem to be higher than for big companies. As SMEs are the backbone of 
the manufacturing value chain, it is crucial to enable them to make a smooth transition to ensure the 
overall sustainable growth of the industry. (Kumar et al. 2022)  
1.2 Definitions and Scope of This Study 
The relationship between sustainability and I4.0 is significant and impacts various industries and 
aspects of the supply chain. For easier investigation, several constrictions assist in narrowing down the 
topic and enable better results formulation. 
This study focuses specifically on the manufacturing industry to deepen the topic. In 2011, 
manufacturing contributed 15% to the overall Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and employed around 
16% of the working population in Europe. (Dijkman et al. 2015, p. 1351) However, the manufacturing 
sector faces more sustainability issues than other industries, such as service industries. (Yadav et al. 
2020b, p. 13) Manufacturing also contributed around 20% of the global Carbon Dioxide (CO2) emissions 
(Dijkman et al. 2015, p. 1352) in 2010, which are expected to increase with current developments. 
(Dijkman et al. 2015, p. 1352) With industry accounting for nearly one-third of global greenhouse gas 
emissions, adopting sustainable practices and reducing its environmental impact is crucial (Edenhofer 
2014). Existing studies emphasize that incorporating I4.0 and sustainability theories can promote 
greener practices in production by utilizing the latest engineering discoveries. (Ejsmont et al. 2020) 
The focus narrows down to central European manufacturing companies, as Europe is a significant 
player in new technologies. These companies have the resources to transform towards I4.0, providing 
more data on the effects of the transition and helping to derive barriers and enablers.  
The focus now turns to SMEs, which require assistance in efficiently transitioning towards Smart 
Manufacturing (SM), given their limited resources compared to Multi-National Enterprises (MNE). 
SMEs are businesses that meet specific criteria established by the European Commission. Typically, an 
SME has less than 250 employees, an annual turnover of 50 million euros or less and a yearly balance 
sheet of under 43 million euros. These guidelines help distinguish SMEs from larger corporations in the 
business landscape. SMEs are vital in promoting innovation, job creation and economic growth. Finally, 
the scope of this study is to assist SMEs in decision-making, enabling I4.0T to derive sustainable results. 
Throughout the thesis, the term I4.0 and I4.0T are to be used similarly, as both terms enable each other. 
Finally, the framework is applicable to any company lacking resources for this transformation.  
A key success factor for sustainable manufacturing is energy efficiency, which is enabled by reducing 
the environmental impact of the production process. Enhancing the energy efficiency of manufacturing 
systems contributes to long-term energy sustainability (Kluczek 2019). Adopting methods and tools 
such as energy analysis, energy evaluation and energy-saving measures is vital to manage energy 
resources effectively (Menghi et al. 2019). By implementing these strategies, manufacturers can 
optimize its consumption, minimize waste and contribute to a more sustainable and environmentally 
friendly production process. Given the challenges of improving living standards and managing a 
growing population, a practical solution would be to focus on transforming the manufacturing sector, 
which has become intricately linked to wide-reaching supply chains. (Bag and Pretorius 2022, p. 872) 
==PAGE:8==
Understanding the impact of manufacturing becomes easier with energy efficiency. Sustainability plays 
a necessary role in measuring efficiency, as it considers the utilization of resources while acknowledging 
their depletion and how they harm the environment (Ibarra et al. 2018). Sustainable manufacturing 
also aims to minimize or eliminate negative environmental impacts throughout the manufacturing 
process. The priority in this practice is the safety and well-being of employees, society and the 
environment. By integrating sustainable principles into manufacturing, resource-efficient production is 
promoted, which reduces pollution and enhances awareness of social and environmental 
responsibility. (EPA report 2020) 
1.3 Research Justification 
The concept of combining I4.0 and sustainability matters is relatively new. The combination of both in 
specific environments needs further research, especially when applying I4.0T and identifying their 
environmental and social effects instead of just economic benefits. However, there is little discussion 
about the barriers and how to overcome them by utilizing critical success factors through technology. 
SMEs have been widely forgotten in this approach, as very few studies take care of the specific 
resources and structure SMEs bring when implementing I4.0 solutions. SMEs are often handled 
similarly to MNEs when making strategic and operational decisions. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 196)  
In addition, the results show that SMEs perceive risks in the context of I4.0 differently, especially as 
they do not fully grasp the opportunities of new business models in contrast to larger enterprises 
(Müller et al. 2018; Yan 2017). 
SMEs face new challenges due to the fast-paced changes in MNEs and the growing focus on 
sustainability. With the expansion of supply chains, SMEs are now part of a larger network of 
collaborating companies. Furthermore, sharing data has become an essential aspect of supplier 
relationships. To stay competitive in this market, SMEs must adopt Industry 4.0 and sustainable 
practices to adapt their traditional business strategies. 
This thesis aims to create a practical maturity model for SMEs in the context of I4.0. This model will 
accurately represent the industrial essential facts of SMEs. By using tailored maturity models adapted 
to the specific needs of SMEs, stakeholders will have a clearer understanding of their I4.0 vision, 
roadmap and strategic projects. This results in lower barriers to entry and reduced risks during the 
transition process towards I4.0, facilitating a cultural shift supporting this transformation. (Mittal et al. 
2018), p. 194

2. Method 
2.1 Research Design 
This research utilized a multiple case study approach that involved analyzing secondary data from 
academic literature and performing practical research. As shown before, the interaction between I4.0T 
and sustainability is part of a further investigation, to address the lack of existing guidelines for 
successful transformation, particularly for SMEs. By employing multiple cases instead of relying on 
individual case studies, the study develops a more generalizable and accurate theory, ensuring the 
reliability and credibility of the findings. 
The approach for the data analysis employs a dual approach, including academic literature and practical 
research. The academic literature has helped to improve the understanding of state-of-the-art concepts 
and design principles, the intercorrelation of I4.0T and sustainability and existing measures to 
transform towards digitalization. Practical research, on the other hand, has mainly focused on current 
studies, leading to framework and guideline proposals. By presenting these findings collaboratively, it 
is ensured that the evaluations are comprehensive and accurate. 
2.2 Data collection 
Academic Literature  
The academic literature review follows a multistep method. First, an extensive literature review was 
conducted to derive a general understanding and form state of the art of technologies, concepts and 
design principles.  
The literature review used Scopus, Science Direct and Web of Science as a basis for the main scientific 
databases, as they all match academic standard. The main criteria for filtering the results were the 
language (English), the document type (article), the publication stage (final), the source type (journal) 
and the date the paper was published (recent 5-10 years). Context-wise key terms according to I4.0T, 
sustainability and SMEs. The topic under discussion is highly relevant and dynamic, necessitating the 
selection of recent papers to capture the ongoing and ever-changing nature of developments in this 
field. The study focused on recent literature and prioritized high-ranked journals. Older sources contain 
knowledge valuable for introducing the topic and providing a general understanding. The papers were 
chosen based on their relevance and connections to previous works. Recent literature emphasizes the 
actuality of the topic, ensuring comprehensive coverage of the latest developments and trends. 
Specifically, papers from high-ranked journals published in 2020 were selected, while older sources 
were used only to introduce the topic.  
After identifying the relevant papers, the forward and backward search methods allow the analysis of 
their citations and references. Connected Papers as a tool helps to identify citational relationships. The 
assessment of each paper is based on its importance to the scenario, focusing on studies with a high 
citation count that are published in reputable journals. The resulting papers meeting these criteria were 
included in the analysis.

2.3 Data Analysis Approach 
An in-depth evaluation of how I4.0 contributes to promoting sustainability is yet to be conducted (G.B. 
Nieuwenhuize 2016). As a result, a research gap exists between the sustainability assessment field and 
the specific technologies utilized in various sectors. Developing decision-making models is one 
potential avenue to fill this gap and enhance research in this area. (Ejsmont et al. 2020) Using a maturity 
model, a company can assess its status and take important steps toward sustainability by implementing 
I4.0T.  
A framework will support this by giving an overview of the situation and providing a guideline for the 
correlation between actions and results. Frameworks are structured selections of processes, 
approaches and tools used to design and develop systems. They provide a coherent approach and 
principles to ensure consistency and efficiency in the system development process. (Mittal et al. 2018, 
p. 199) 
An approach that analyzes each I4.0T individually can enable clearer results by distinguishing separate 
impacts. Further, an approach that determines different industries can derive better results, as I4.0T 
impacts on each industry differ (Bai et al. 2020, p. 11). Due to the novelty of the topic, the impacts of 
I4.0T in the long run are yet to be determined (Bonilla et al. 2018). 
LDC face similar barriers as SMEs due to limited economic power and higher risks of investments. The 
framework applies to SMEs and companies in countries with less resources and digitalization 
infrastructure, such as LDC companies. The multi-applicability can enable the creation of the first 
linkages between I4.0 and sustainability and serve as an example for other companies to follow. An 
existing digitalized infrastructure along the supply chain will lead the way for further investments of 
other companies. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 43)

3.1 State of the Art: Concepts and Technology Definitions 
The following chapter is an introduction to current concepts, technologies and design principles 
according to I4.0 and sustainability. The descriptive analyses provide definitions, to help form a clearer 
picture of recent developments so that the reader can get a good overview of the topic.

Corporate Sustainability 
To better understand I4.0, it is necessary first to discuss sustainable development. Corporate 
sustainability (CS) has become increasingly important in recent years as companies recognize their 
environmental and societal responsibilities. CS has emerged in response to the negative impact that 
industrial, commercial and service processes can have on the environment and society. By considering 
economic, environmental and social issues, CS connects different aspects. With its social, ecological 
and economic dimensions, the TBL concept provides a pragmatic basis for implementing CS. The 
environmental dimension focuses on balancing resource consumption and the limited availability of 
natural resources (Espuny et al. 2021). It encompasses initiatives such as recycling, regenerating or 
substituting non-renewable resources with renewable alternatives, implementing Circular Economy 
(CE) principles and adopting greener production strategies. CE will be further described later in this 
study. Additionally, the social dimension cultivates an organizational culture that values and develops 
human capital, considering aspects such as job satisfaction, quality of life, solidarity and equitable 
distribution of resources and services (Saeidi et al. 2021). Finally, the economic dimension involves 
enhancing the value of goods and services, improving management processes, investing in innovation 
and technology and undertaking initiatives that bolster market position and profitability (Braccini and 
Margherita 2019). By integrating CS into their operations, companies can foster environmentally 
conscious practices, promote social well-being and enhance economic performance. This 
comprehensive approach paves the way for sustainable business strategies that effectively address 
organizations' interconnected challenges. (Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022) 
I4.0T 
This chapter will provide a more detailed description of the I4.0 key technologies. Examples of 
applications showcase the proper achievement of sustainable impact. 
The primary enabling technologies of I4.0 encompasses AM, augmented and virtual reality, Big Data 
Analytics (BDA), blockchain, cloud computing, cyber-physical systems (CPS), flexible manufacturing 
systems, machine learning, smart sensors, reconfigurability and the Internet of Things (IoT) (Jesús 
Hamilton Ortiz 2020). 
I4.0T is categorized as physical and digital technology. The former describes elements, that play a major 
role in manufacturing, such as AM (Gibson et al. 2021), drones or sensors (Morrar and Arman 2017). 
Digital technologies support the industry by collecting, analyzing and communicating data, such as 
BDA, blockchain, cloud computing, simulation or IoT (Liao et al. 2017). 
Additive Manufacturing  
Additive Manufacturing (AM), also known as 3D printing, is an innovative technology that enables the 
creation of complex geometries and structures from three-dimensional model data. The process 
involves printing layers of materials that build upon each other to create the desired object. AM 
encompasses various methods, materials and equipment and has evolved to revolutionize 
manufacturing and logistics processes. This versatile technology has applications in industries as 
diverse as biomechanics, prototyping and construction. (Kagermann et al. 2013) 
One of the key advantages of AM is its resource utilization and environmental benefits. Unlike 
traditional manufacturing methods, AM can use alternative materials not solely derived from nature. 
AM includes using biodegradable or edible materials, allowing industries to adopt more 
environmentally responsible practices when sourcing production inputs. In addition, AM enables the 
use of non-conforming or surplus materials from the industry, turning them into viable components for 
new products. This reduces waste generation and eliminates the need to mine or manufacture new 
materials. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 39) 
==PAGE:12==
Regarding practical applications, AM, is primarily used for prototyping and producing individual 
components, particularly in 3D printing. It excels at facilitating the production of small batches of 
customized products (Rüßmann et al. 2015).The benefits of 3D printing include low-cost, low-volume 
production, which enables a high degree of customization not possible with traditional manufacturing 
processes. It also democratizes manufacturing by allowing anyone with the necessary skills to use 
standard computer-aided design (CAD) software to design and manufacture products anywhere in the 
world. In addition, 3D printing overcomes the complexities and limitations of traditional methods, 
resulting in reduced waste and time savings. The ability to produce goods based on CAD software 
wherever compatible 3D printers are available adds to its flexibility and accessibility. (Jansen et al. 2014)  
Overall, AM and 3D printing offer groundbreaking opportunities for manufacturing innovation, 
customization and sustainable resource use. 
Artificial intelligence 
Artificial Intelligence (AI) encompasses digital systems capable of performing advanced classification 
and decision-making tasks. Recent advances in AI have focused primarily on pattern recognition and 
classification using neural networks, also known as Machine Learning . This breakthrough enables the 
development of operations such as image recognition. Unlike customized solutions, AI technology can 
adapt to different use cases by using various data sets for training. The choice and quantity of training 
data play a key role in optimizing the performance of neural networks. One of the drawbacks of this 
approach is that understanding how the AI system arrives at its decisions can take time and effort. This 
lack of transparency can create additional complexity when deconstructing the decision-making 
process. (EEA 2021)  
AM can benefit from decision-making software, but it is only effective if properly configured, making 
specialists a crucial component of the process. 
Augmented reality 
Augmented Reality (AR) is a technique that combines virtual objects with the real world, transforming 
them into digital space using computer graphics. It involves the integration of virtual and real objects, 
real-time interactivity and interactions between them (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 32). One possible 
approach to achieve this integration involves utilizing mobile devices, like phones and tablets, or 
specialized AR glasses that utilize cameras to display virtual content. 
AR offers real-time guidance and instructions for performing specific tasks, enhancing productivity and 
production quality. It enables the simulation or use of real-time data to accurately represent the 
physical world within a virtual model. This model can include various elements such as machines, 
products and people (Dalmarco et al. 2019). This capability allows operators to thoroughly test and 
refine machine configurations for upcoming products in virtual space before a real implementation 
occurs. (Rüßmann et al. 2015) 
AR enables the creation and virtual visualization of plans before execution, minimizing design errors 
and wasted resources. The simulations provided by AR technology allow designers, engineers, 
operators and technicians to assess product-wide compliance and identify necessary modifications in 
advance. In addition, AR enables remote viewing of specific points or locations within an industrial site, 
facilitating real-time awareness for informed decision-making and efficient action or repair. (Jesús 
Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 38)

Blockchain 
Blockchains are decentralized databases and ledgers with linked blocks stored on multiple machines. 
This distributed design ensures a permanent record of all changes made to the database, facilitating 
public access to all records (Crosby et al. 2016). Blockchains are expected to revolutionize traceability 
and improve data integrity, offering new possibilities for reducing or eliminating the need for a trusted 
intermediary in various operations. These include validating legal provisions, establishing patents, 
facilitating secure payments and ensuring certified renewable electricity supply from distributed 
energy generation. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 26) 
Blockchain enables data security for several data types by storing and encrypting it incorruptibly. (EEA 
2021) Its importance will be further explained in the following paragraph. 
Big data and analytics 
Big data has emerged as the second pillar of I4.0, driven by the exponential growth of data generated 
from diverse sources. It includes expanding new analytical devices in sectors such as healthcare and 
the increasing number of sensors and connected objects associated with the IoT, which consistently 
generate substantial volumes of data. Traditional data processing and analysis tools have become 
inadequate to handle the volume, velocity and variety of data, necessitating the adoption of BDA 
(Rüßmann et al. 2015). To overcome this issue, BDA encompasses the process and tools used to analyze 
data and the associated infrastructure, visualization techniques and presentation of insights (Espada 
and Rosa 2018). 
By utilizing BDA, organizations can implement a robust system for gathering, storing, analyzing and 
distributing data, resulting in improved performance and data-driven decision-making (Jesús Hamilton 
Ortiz 2020, p. 32). The advantages of using BDA include better-informed decisions, increased visibility, 
decreased supply risks, improved bargaining power with vendors and clients, greater efficiency in the 
supply chain, improved demand planning, enhanced sales and operations planning and increased 
agility (Schoenherr and Speier-Pero 2015). With the help of BDA insights, industrial organizations can 
shift from reactive to proactive decision-making, optimizing resource utilization and improving overall 
management practices. (Ben Youssef 2020) 
In addition, big data has the potential to support sustainability efforts by providing relevant statistics 
for informed decision-making across economic, environmental and social domains (Bulger et al. 2014). 
In Environmental Management 4.0, BDA allows for the examination and utilization of data pertaining 
to resources, machine performance, energy efficiency, waste production, waste utilization and 
pollution levels. It can also facilitate the identification of historical data on resource reuse, resource 
shortages or machine breakdowns, thus optimizing production processes and suggesting 
improvements. In combination with Cloud Manufacturing (CMfg), suggestions concerning the adaption 
of the production process can be derived. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, pp. 37–38) 
==PAGE:14==
Cloud-based solutions are particularly useful in logistics, with over 40% of new technology 2016 being 
cloud-based (Chandra and van Hillegersberg 2015). 
Overall, the extensive capabilities of BDA, including its data storage capacity, enable it to serve as a 
proactive tool for identifying opportunities and corrective actions in production, resource use and 
environmental impact (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, pp. 37–38). 
Cloud computing 
Cloud computing describes a collection of technologies for intense companies, offering capabilities as 
a service. Cloud computing lays the foundation for BDA to work by providing easy access to data and 
big data technologies. The Internet provides several advantages, such as cost cuts, extensibility and 
flexibility (Braccini 2010). 
CMfg is an area of research that combines Cloud Computing with traditional product design and 
manufacturing practices. CMfg is characterized as a service-oriented model for product development 
that allows consumers to use information technology and online manufacturing resources to design 
and create products (Zhong et al. 2017). By adopting CMfg; customers can actively participate in the 
customization of on-demand products to meet their specific preferences and needs (Jesús Hamilton 
Ortiz 2020, p. 32). 
Cyber-Physical systems 
Significant advances in information and communication technologies have led to the emergence of 
Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS). These systems involve computational collaboration closely linked to the 
real world and its correlating processes. They provide data access services and use internet-based 
computing capabilities. (Chen 2017) CPS integrates human interactions, data and information and 
machinery and devices to facilitate efficient data flow, analysis, interpretation and decision-making. 
(Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 32) 
In addition, applying CPS in manufacturing has led to the emergence of Cyber-Physical Production 
Systems (CPPS). CPPS consist of autonomous and collaborative computational entities, known as 
subsystems, intricately linked to the physical world and its production processes. These subsystems 
interact dynamically across all production levels, including processes, machines and production and 
logistics networks. CPPS digitally represents physical assets within its framework. (EEA 2021) 
In the context of I4.0, three fundamental concepts are noteworthy in developing these systems: I4.0 
Reference Architecture Model (RAMI 4.0), the Digital Twin and the Asset Administration Shell (AAS). 
Rami 4.0 is a layer model providing the fundamental architecture for implementing I4.0. The digital is 
explained later in this chapter. AAS supports standardized data exchange throughout the production 
lifecycle and serves as a standardized communication interface within a network, connecting I4.0 to 
physical assets. It is a repository for storing comprehensive data and information about the asset. Some 
of the models are late referred to in the Framework proposal. (EEA 2021) 
CPS serves as the foundational technology in I4.0, offering the potential for mass customization through 
modularity and adaptability in production systems. It connects physical entities, enables human-
machine interaction and facilitates the integration of infrastructure, processes and other system 
activities when linked to IoT. This interaction between IoT and CPS enables the exchange of data 
collected by sensors and generated by manufacturing activities, bridging the physical entities and the 
virtual world. Introducing innovative and disruptive technologies in I4.0 allows organizations to 
transform the management of projects, production systems and services, making processes more 
efficient and adaptable to demand fluctuations. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020; Jiang 2018)

Drones 
Uncrewed aerial vehicles, also known as drones, are small aircraft that do not need steering from a

Digital Twin 
A digital twin is a virtual representation of a process, including products or services and establishes a 
connection between the physical and virtual worlds (Grösser 2018). It uses data from sensors installed 
in the physical environment to monitor various factors, such as working conditions and machine 
positions. Connecting the virtual and real world facilitates the analysis of data and monitoring of 
systems. This makes it possible to predict and prevent problems before they occur, reducing downtime, 
opening new opportunities and facilitating future planning through computer simulations (EEA 2021) 
Internet of Things  
Originally coined by Kevin Ashton, the IoT refers to a concept in which a network of interconnected 
smart objects, embedded sensors and miniaturized computers primarily performs data generation and 
processing. These entities sense their environment, process data and communicate machine-to-
machine. Examples of IoT implementations range from connected watches and cars in everyday life to 
industrial applications in manufacturing. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017)  
The term 'Internet of Things' encompasses the increasing expansion of connectivity, sensors and 
actuators in various products, enabling the creation of an accurate digital representation of the physical 
world. The information gathered through the IoT enables trend forecasting and comprehensive data 
collection. This fundamental concept of connecting sensor-equipped devices is highly relevant in I4.0. 
(EEA 2021)  
I4.0 relies on the IoT, which uses smart and independent machines, advanced predictive analytics and 
connections between humans and machines to enhance efficiency, productivity and reliability (Wong 
and Kim 2019). The IoT utilizes radio frequency identification, wireless network sensors, middleware, 
cloud computing and IoT application software to enable object recognition, data collection, physical 
condition monitoring and interconnectedness. (Lee and Lee 2015) 
Similarly, the Industrial IIoT is an expansion of IoT, demonstrating a system that includes intelligent 
networks, cyber-physical systems and cloud computing platforms that enable real-time access, 
collection and evaluation of communications and data exchanges within the industry (Jiang 2018). It 
contributes to the optimization of production value and ensures the integration of data and 
communication between different stakeholders. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 33) 
RFID 
Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) is a technology that enables communication between two 
elements by enhancing wireless technology. An object or tag can communicate with a device, also 
referred to as a reader, to automatically identify and track the aforementioned object. (Bai et al. 2020) 
Rapid Prototyping 
==PAGE:16==
According to Kruth et al. (1998), rapid prototyping encompasses several complementary technologies, 
including additive layer manufacturing, mostly known as 3D printing and computer-aided design. This 
approach enables the rapid production of parts and prototypes, providing a faster and more cost-
effective alternative to traditional removal approaches and material forming (Kruth et al. 1998). 
Although the term has been in use for a long time, it has gained new relevance with the advent of I4.0T, 
opening up new possibilities for this concept. 
The resource savings offered by rapid prototyping have significant implications for mass customization 
and innovation, making it more efficient to test innovative designs. In addition, technologies like 
additive layer manufacturing have proven advantageous for prototyping and series production. A 
notable example is General Electric's LEAP engine, where 3D-printed fuel nozzles reduced the weight 
of the component by a quarter and led to five times more durability while achieving optimized 
geometry for improved combustion efficiency. This has resulted in fuel savings over the engine's life 
and reduced CO2 emissions. (Ford and Despeisse 2016) 
Sensors and actuators 
Sensors are vital in bridging the gap between the physical world and the digital production 
environment. They collect data on the current state of processes and facilitate communication with 
various systems. The applications of sensor technology are extensive, particularly in smart 
manufacturing. By tracking and recording the state of production machines and goods, sensors enable 
process control by measuring and converting these states into digital values. This technology enables 
industries to effectively adapt and respond to internal and external needs within the process chain. 
(EEA 2021) 
There are various methods of transferring data from sensors to the digital world and controlling 
production machines when it comes to connectivity. Stationary machines and high data rates prefer 
wired connections. On the other hand, wireless connections are the prerequisites for tracking mobile 
units such as finished goods, with options ranging from adapted Wi-Fi to cellular networks or custom-
designed wireless networks. Optimal performance depends on factors such as speed, range, stability, 
power consumption and other relevant characteristics that vary depending on the application. (EEA 
2021) 
Managing the data collection, processing and storage logistics requires careful planning due to the 
digital nature, high volume and detailed resolution of the data collected. In an I4.0 environment, where 
high amounts of data are rapidly generated and utilized, the underlying technological infrastructure 
must be in place to process and store this information. As a critical infrastructure component, edge 
computing performs computations on networked peripheral devices rather than centralized cloud or 
mainframe computing. By eliminating communication steps and using dedicated processing units, edge 
computing increases computing speed, reduces latency and minimizes network load by transmitting 
distilled information rather than raw data. (EEA 2021) 
Furthermore, smart sensors have a prominent position in the future smart grid. They enable remote 
monitoring at any point in the network, allowing real-time system performance assessment and 
identification of potential faults (EEA 2021).  
Simulation 
Simulation is a technology that imitates physical elements, such as processes, products or systems, 
using computer technology as a fundamental basis (Bai et al. 2020).

Concepts and Design Principals  
When speaking of I4.0, several concepts are included in this term, mainly: "flexible automation, cyber-
physical systems, (industrial) IoT, sensors, collaborative and cognitive robotics, cloud computing, big 
data, computer modelling and simulations and AM (3D printing) (Da Xu et al. 2018) Some of the I4.0T, 
introduced in the chapter before are forming a concept, as they enable a new way of handling things 
in the manufacturing context.  
The rise of technological innovation in computing, data storage and sensors has brought about a 
permanent transformation in the business landscape, leading to industrial revolutions. (EEA 2021) In 
this context, I4.0 represents the current trend of data exchange and automation among manufacturing 
technologies and activities. I4.0 paves the way for "smart" factories that adapt to dynamic 
manufacturing scenarios, management objectives and new business models. The new technologies 
connect various physical devices enriched with embedded electronics (such as sensors, actuators, RFID) 
to networks or the internet, enabling their interaction. 
Key characteristics of I4.0 include the digitization and interconnection of equipment and devices, 
leading to the creation of intelligent networks and the monitoring of independent and autonomous 
production modules. This integration facilitates identification and communication throughout the value 
stream, enabling IT-enabled mass customization in manufacturing. There are six design principles that 
I4.0 compromises and enable the transformation of processes from I3.0 to I4.0: decentralization, 
interoperability, modularity, real-time capability, service orientation and virtualization (Li et al. 2019).  
Decentralization offers the potential to optimize local resources, while interoperability extends the 
lifespan of machinery and reduces industrial waste. Embracing modularity prolongs machine longevity 
and enables more efficient utilization of industrial resources. Real-time capabilities enhance resource 
optimization, adaptability to demand fluctuations and swift response to energy supply changes. 
Adopting a service-oriented approach enhances end-product utilization and expands opportunities for 
recycling and reuse. Virtualization is crucial in minimizing industrial waste, promoting environmentally 
friendly practices and maximizing recycling possibilities. (Carvalho et al. 2018) 
Four maturity levels for digitalization can differ between the integration level of I4.0 solutions and help 
to retrieve the status of I4.0. First, visibility envisions the collected data to create transparency to 
emphasize several root causes. Further, the prediction level comprises future conversion functionalities 
for future scenarios, resulting in the automation of the process. (Thiede 2021, p. 3) 
Additionally aiding in the process of orientation, I4.0 categorizes data analytics into five levels: 
descriptive, diagnostic, discovery, predictive and prescriptive. The first two levels, descriptive and 
diagnostic, focus on historical data and provide insights into past processes. The discovery level takes 
a present-oriented approach, using complex analytical methods to gain more detailed insights and 
discover non-trivial knowledge from the available information and data. This level enables the 
identification of new and more efficient material cascades and the analysis of end-user behavior to 
predict and optimize recycle streams. The predictive level projects future probabilities and trends to 
identify likely future developments. Predictive maintenance, for example, uses real data extrapolation 
to schedule repairs and replacements proactively. At the top of the hierarchy, the prescriptive level 
introduces a greater degree of technological autonomy and relies on knowledge gained from previous 
levels. It takes a dynamic approach to future resource use and may involve enabling digital technologies 
to make autonomous decisions, such as predicting and scheduling maintenance or other life-extending 
actions. (Kristoffersen et al. 2020) 
In the context of I4.0, several key business model patterns have emerged. Integration entails the 
development of innovative business models that incorporate unique processes and integrate different 
supply chain elements. Servitization involves integrating new products and services within a business 
==PAGE:19==
model, offering a comprehensive solution to customers. It describes a business model trend, shifting 
away from the pure sale of a product towards the provision of (additional) services. On the other hand, 
expertization focuses on business models that revolve around product and process expertise, 
combining hybrid elements to deliver customer value. (Weking et al. 2020) 
The principles, characteristics and concepts that define manufacturing in the context of I4.0 are 
essential to understand. Additionally, it is crucial to consider the enabling factors and impacts and 
implementation considerations for I4.0 to grasp the picture fully. 
I4.0 Implementation Considerations 
To implement I4.0, horizontal and vertical digital integration is essential. Horizontal integration involves 
connecting multiple smart factories through value networks within and across different factories. 
Vertical and horizontal integration enables end-to-end integration across the entire value chain. Smart 
products play a critical role within the I4.0 framework, facilitating communication and cooperative 
control between products and machines. These smart products, equipped with sensors, software 
control and Internet connectivity, enable the realization of collaborative industrial processes and 
associated business networks. Meeting customers' evolving demands, who seek fast delivery, reliable 
services and product availability, drive organizations to create value and embrace I4.0. (Oláh et al. 2020; 
Ghanbari et al. 2017) 
I4.0, enabled by digitalization, encompasses four principal aspects that shape its characteristics. First, 
horizontal integration within value networks involves implementing new concepts across the entire 
value chain. This integration enhances connectivity, improves information flows and processes and 
requires suitable information technology infrastructure. Second, vertical integration occurs within a 
factory or manufacturing plant, addressing all layers of the automation pyramid. It involves connecting 
physical products through sensors, digitalizing and optimizing internal processes, increasing 
connectivity and rethinking business models and collaboration methods. Third, lifecycle management 
and consistency of engineering ensure the coherence, traceability and interpretability of information 
throughout the value chain (Fraunhofer IOSB).; Fourth, people play a vital role as conductors in the 
value network, overseeing value creation processes and bearing ultimate responsibility for their 
outcomes. (VDI and Zentralverband 2015)  
The impact of I4.0 on global value chains is significant, influencing the activities and strategic decisions 
of MNEs. MNEs will need to manage the design and distribution stages of global value chains and 
technologies like 3D printing and virtual engineering empower customers to provide input on product 
design and influence manufacturing decisions. The location of manufacturing facilities may shift closer 
to end customers, considering the reduced costs associated with robotic systems. Key capabilities 
driving future organizational performance include BDA and IoT, enabling companies to anticipate and 
shape customer demands and enhance manufacturing, logistics and distribution efficiency. While some 
critical technologies like cloud computing, mobile computing and modularization have matured, 
others, such as AM, robotics and augmented, virtual and mixed reality, are still in the early stages of 
development. (Bai et al. 2020; Ramanujan et al. 2023)

SME Characteristics 
As mentioned earlier, SMEs have different resources compared to MNEs. The thesis will emphasize the 
specific traits of SMEs, particularly in implementing I4.0T, to gain a better understanding. 
The European Commission defines SMEs as firms with fewer than 250 employees, an annual turnover 
of at most 50 million euros and an annual total balance sheet of less than 43 million euros (Birkel et al. 
2019). They form the backbone of supply chain management for worldwide manufacturing economies. 
(Sarath Menon and Satya Shah 2020, p. 1)  
The critical role in producing and delivering goods is defined by competitive prices, crucial quality 
standards maintenance and delivery deadlines. While specific processes may vary from competitor to 
competitor, common challenges facing SMEs include digitalization, lack of financial power, convincing 
of employees, technological equipment and the increasing complexity of supply chains. (Sarath Menon 
and Satya Shah 2020, p. 206)(Mittal et al. 2018, p. 207) 
Various disparities between SMEs and MNEs hinder the former from progressing in certain strategic 
areas. A crucial difference is the monetary resource of SMEs, which conflicts with realizing 
opportunities, like upgrading new advanced manufacturing technologies. (Müller et al. 2018; Musa and 
Dabo 2016; Piccarozzi et al. 2018). As these technologies tend to cost a lot and still need extensive 
research, new investment is connected to many risks. The research and development area could be 
more developed. Developing IT integration and software or data analytic tools is crucial in transforming 
to I4.0 and must be noticed. It is rather custom tailored to the specific problems SMEs face. As a result, 
miscalculating the investment in I4.0 can have a high impact on the overall business of the small 
company. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 195) There is a strong emphasis on developing and promoting 
customer-centric business models. This includes the creation of innovative hybrid solutions that 
combine products and services, as well as customization, to enhance competitiveness. Medium-sized 
providers of I4.0-based solutions can explore the potential of retrofitting existing machines with 
customized services, thereby tailoring their offerings to the specific needs of their customers. This 
approach holds great promise for their future success and growth. (Müller and Däschle 2018) (Kiel et 
al. 2017) 
MNE are known for their strict adherence to standards such as ISO, whereas SMEs experience less 
pressure to apply such standards. This discrepancy is partly due to the resources required to prepare 
==PAGE:21==
for and obtain certification. As a result, SMEs are relying on knowledge acquired and shared by MNEs, 
as study by Blind and Mangelsdorf derived by analyzing manufacturing firms in Germany. However, 
they are also concerned that standardization could expose their essential information to competitors, 
as competition is high. (Blind and Mangelsdorf 2013) 
I4.0 inherits more positive correlations between operational advantages and performance outcomes 
for large companies than SMEs. Larger companies can attribute their success to their strategic and 
forward-thinking approach. They operate with a perspective on the business model and have a long-
term orientation (Polo Redondo and Cambra Fierro 2007). In contrast, SMEs tend to focus more on 
short to medium-term activities related to production and supply. Their flat hierarchies, short decision 
paths and overall flexibility enable them to respond more quickly to changing market conditions. 
(Braglia and Petroni 2000). Therefore, operational advantages are more relevant antecedents for SMEs 
than strategic advantages. Limited R&D has led to product specialization. As competition is fierce, 
differentiation to surpass others is desirable. SMEs are already in a good position to produce specialized 
products. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 196) 
Additionally, organizational behavior is of great importance for SMEs. While MNEs have more complex 
and formal organizational structures, SMEs tend to have less complex and informal structures. The 
organizational culture within SMEs often lacks the flexibility required to experiment and implement 
I4.0 T (van de Vrande et al. 2009). As a result, SMEs may be unable to devote comparable resources to 
market research and analysis, leading to less informed decision-making. The intuition of the manager 
or decision-maker drives decisions in an SME due to the inherent uncertainty of the business 
environment (Salles 2006). In contrast, MNEs base their decisions on extensive market research and 
extensive analysis, often supported by panels of advisers or consultants (Trott 2001). 
According to the readiness for I4.0T adoption, three categories describe the potential for adaptation 
and intention. With rising potential: dormant, captives and adopters (G.B. Nieuwenhuize 2016). 
However, to fully implement I4.0, systematic consideration of risks is necessary. While the risks 
associated with I4.0, especially for SMEs, have yet to be extensively studied, there needs to be further 
investigation to provide a comprehensive understanding of these risks. Identified risks should apply to 
the TBL principles that arise during the implementation of I4.0 in the context of SMEs. By emphasizing 
the importance of SMEs as critical components of the value network, the scope of economic research 
will extend to a more holistic approach. The specific risks are to be addressed, as well as the appropriate 
mitigation strategies, that need to be determined on a case-by-case and company-specific basis. (Birkel 
et al. 2019) 
Although I4.0 has attracted public awareness for a while and several studies have derived various 
benefits, there is a big gap for SMEs to adopt new technologies. (Bai et al. 2020, p. 2) Considering the 
differences in resources and opportunities, an individualized roadmap for the maturity model for SMEs 
is needed to implement I4.0T to derive sustainability successfully. (Jamwal et al. 2021a) Having 
identified the core principals and technologies of I4.0 and the main characteristics of sustainability, the 
next chapter investigates the correlation between I4.0T and sustainability, acknowledging the 
characteristics of SMEs. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 195) 
While MNEs adapt highly to new digitalization concepts, SMEs need help implementing a new business 
model. This chapter presents the characteristics of SMEs to gain further knowledge about the different 
resources. The next chapter analyzes I4.0T and sustainable principles before focusing on SMEs 
adaptation opportunities.

Key Practices Combining I4.0T and Sustainability 
The emerging trends of I4.0 and sustainability are closely connected, supporting and complementing 
each other. As to which extent is to be derived from individual scenarios.  
Sustainability and I4.0 are emerging technological research areas, aiming to improve processes. I4.0T, 
such as Information and Communication Technologies (ICT) and automation, use interconnectivity to 
optimize performance. Whereas, sustainability focuses on companies' contributions to improve the 
==PAGE:23==
quality of life in society and the planet's well-being. These two elements are fundamental to industry, 
working together to maximize benefits. (Jayashree et al. 2021) 
“The main practices in these two areas are sustainable designing, lifecycle management, 
remanufacturing, CE, lean and green management and environmental management.” (Jamwal et al. 
2021a) Integrating I4.0 and corporate sustainability offer a new perspective for companies, enabling 
the development of fully digitalized, connected, smart and decentralized value chains, while improving 
social and environmental conditions (Brozzi et al. 2020). Furthermore, I4.0T contribute to strategic 
management and sustainable operations through data collection and sharing systems (Stock and 
Seliger 2016; Man and Strandhagen 2017). The synergy between I4.0 and business sustainability can 
address social, environmental and economic development needs (Bonilla et al. 2018).  
In the analysis of sustainable manufacturing in the context of I4.0, both macro and micro perspectives 
show the contribution of I4.0T to sustainable value creation in all three dimensions of sustainability 
(Stock and Seliger 2016). Several new opportunities arise from I4.0, including business models driven 
by smart data, closed-loop supply chain networks, digitalization in SMEs, employee training using ICT, 
sustainable product design and distributed production lines (Jamwal et al. 2021a), giving a few 
examples. 
In developing CS through I4.0, the emphasis is on adopting advanced product and project portfolio 
management technologies to design and produce smart products that consider TBL aspects (Bibaud-
Alves et al. 2019). This is enabled by including TBL aspects already in the production and process 
planning phase, but also extending it throughout the supply chain, to reduce the carbon footprint (Bag 
et al. 2018). Similarly emphasized is the creation of a digital platform for continuous engagement with 
consumers and real-time data collection, to address their sustainability needs, as well as the 
enhancement of workers' skills and knowledge in smart and creative environments facilitated by I4.0 
(Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022).  
Recapitulating, these opportunities stress the potential of I4.0 to drive sustainable practices and 
outcomes from improved product design to supply chain sustainability. 
How Can I4.0T Impact Sustainability  
While digitization has gained significant momentum in industrialized countries, its main driver is the 
pursuit of increased competitiveness through process optimization and automation. Although energy 
and resource efficiency gains are favorable by-products, they are rarely the primary objectives when 
implementing I4.0T (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 33). This chapter 
explains general impacts concerning I4.0T and provides several application examples. Overall it mirrors 
the motivation of companies to invest in I4.0T to derive sustainability. The results form a basis for 
further research directions in this thesis. Additional focus will be layed on each TBL category as well as 
the negative side effects. 
Smart manufacturing, driven by I4.0, is a sustainable manufacturing system that effectively addresses 
waste reduction, increases manufacturing efficiency and minimizes environmental impact by reducing 
carbon footprint. As automation becomes more widespread, with high levels of automation and smart 
manufacturing replacing certain human labour, I4.0 will eventually find global adoption to keep up with 
the demands of production and services. Therefore, it is advantageous to participate early in this 
transformative journey to ensure survival in a competitive market and protect the environment for 
future generations. This builds preassure on companies to digitalize. (Jena et al. 2020, p. 10)  
The positive effects of I4.0T are resource savings, reducing waste, making processes leaner and 
extending equipment lifecycles (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 29). To assure this outcome several key 
factors have to be enabled. For instanse: expertise in resource management, transparency and specific 
workforce knowledge are key factors for I4.0T implementation (Luthra et al. 2020, p. 1516)(Ejsmont et 
al. 2020). Besides, emphasizing sustainability and innovative business practices will improve processes, 
technology, skills and production (Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022, p. 22). The central statement emerges:

in manufacturing companies (EEA 2021).

Numerous studies have highlighted the benefits of integrating I4.0 practices in manufacturing. Many 
companies have yet to adjust, to keep up with this trend. In Sweden, for instance, SMEs have not yet 
adopted I4.0 practices as part of their long-term production strategy, despite recommendations from 
various studies (Ericsson et al. 2022, p. 118). This is just one example of how developed European 
countries have the resources to make the transition but are failing to take advantage of the opportunity. 
However, some companies already use digital technologies, to improve energy efficiency and the 
effects are tremendous. For example, a German car manufacturer achieved a 30 % improvement in the 
energy efficiency of a robotic system by using I4.0T alongside other optimization (Daimler 2017). 
Similarly, a Canadian company reported energy savings of up to 15 % by implementing I4.0T in its 
production processe (Thilmany 2017). Reducing energy consumption and increasing productivity 
simultaneously. 
To provide a tangible case, the study by the United Nations Industrial Development Organization 
reveales several examples on how sustainable energy concepts can be incorporated into a digital 
factory:  
The combination of I4.0 and sustainability is a relatively new approach that has yet to receive much 
research attention. The sustainable energy concepts can be integrated into a digital factory by 
establishing a data connection between the factory and the energy system. This data link enables the 
convergence of energy production and consumption. This allows for more efficient use of renewable 
energy, better grid flexibility and optimization of energy storage capacity. (United Nations Industrial 
Development Organization 2017, p. 28) 
Also mentioned is power-to-heat, where excess renewable energy is converted into thermal energy, 
such as steam, for storage or direct use. Similarly, power-to-gas is another approach, using surplus 
renewable energy to produce gas, typically methane or hydrogen. Power-to-gas has the advantage of 
large-scale storage and the ability to convert the gas back into electricity. Another concept discussed is 
demand response, which focuses on the flexibility of energy consumption over time. This concept is 
particularly relevant for energy-intensive production processes that can be time-flexible. The aim is to 
align production phases with the availability of cheap electricity on the energy market, using the 
surplus of renewable energy. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 28) 
An example of optimizing a specific technology is the control of linked robots, using an algorithm that 
reduces their energy consumption. Minimizing robot acceleration can reduce energy consumption by 
up to 30 % without increasing overall production time (Riazi et al. 2016). In addition, innovative digital 
technologies also offer the opportunity to replace traditional, often more energy-intensive 
manufacturing processes. For example, rapid prototyping technologies can be a cheaper, faster and 
more energy-efficient alternative to traditional machining processes for producing prototypes or low-
volume products. Another innovative approach is the transformation of business processes. Digitizing 
the entire value chain enables direct connection with customers and integration of their user 
experience. This enables the development of customized products on demand, eliminating 
unnecessary functionalities and saving resources. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 
2017, p. 29) 
The study concludes that the digitization of manufacturing processes in I4.0 offers opportunities for 
energy savings through optimizing or replacing technologies, applying new software tools and adapting 
business processes. These approaches can contribute to more efficient and sustainable energy use in 
the manufacturing sector. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 28)  
 
Regardless, there are several disadvantages, aligning I4.0T and sustainability. As previously noted, the 
implementation of I4.0T require a significant amount of energy throughout the process. More factors 
to consider are, lack of understanding, associated costs, necessary alterations to legacy systems, 
==PAGE:26==
significant waste creation resulting from water-, energy use, extraction of materials and the 
exploitation of rare earths (Tao et al. 2014) (Saberi et al. 2019).  
However, in summary, smart applications in production processes can provide transparency on energy 
consumption patterns, optimize energy management and contribute to the stability of the energy 
system, thus, contributing to energy savings. Integrating I4.0T and sustainable energy initiatives can 
lead to more sustainable economies and a reduction in the carbon footprint. (United Nations Industrial 
Development Organization 2017, p. 33)  
Unlocking the Power of Big Data 
Data collection and evaluation, with big data analysis, is identified as the major key role player. Thus, 
this chapter provides a solely focus on the I4.0T connected to big data. Individual examination helps to 
derive clearer results, by distinguishing separate impacts (Bai et al. 2020, p. 11). 
Data collection and analysis offer companies new opportunities to develop products tailored to 
individual customer needs. I4.0 production processes offer greater flexibility, allowing for rapid 
changes, particularly in product design. Gathering valuable insights from data analytics facilitates rapid 
iterations in product design, enabling the production of customized or personalized products, such as 
lot-size-one production (Fraunhofer-Gesellschaft 2023). Furthermore, data analytics is pivotal in 
obtaining profound insights and enhancing product development. This is facilitated by examining the 
data gathered from smart products during their lifecycle, which provides indispensable knowledge to 
refine future versions. (EEA 2021) 
Capturing and analyzing real-time data, enables the measurement and monitoring of sustainability 
performance in the production environment. This leads to improved resource efficiency, reduction of 
inefficient operations and access to reliable and up-to-date information on production efficiency. 
Embracing digitalization is pivotal in optimizing sustainability outcomes by harnessing the potential of 
I4.0T. (Ejsmont et al. 2020) 
Moreover, data science offers numerous benefits also to SMEs, including enhancing operational 
efficiency and accuracy, optimizing production processes, managing supply chains, enabling product 
customization, facilitating predictive maintenance and preventing machine breakdowns. These 
advantages make data science a valuable technology for business transformation. (Ghobakhloo 2018)  
IoT, digitization, CPS and sensors are big data technologies, enable monitoring and collection of data. 
For sustainable terms, benefits are reducing machine operating time and waste, while improving the 
overall utilization of machines and resources. By using these technologies, companies can achieve cost-
effective operations. Sensors play a crucial role in production by collecting data on machine status, 
enabling machine utilization analysis. This data-driven analysis helps prevent unexpected failures and 
ensure product quality. (Ejsmont et al. 2020) 
A recent study by Bai et al. (Bai et al. 2020, p. 11) evaluated and ranked the sustainability potential of 
I4.0T. The study has found that sensors and actuators are the most environmentally sustainable 
technologies. Following closely behind are AI, big data and analytics and cloud. The combination of all 
provides the necessary physical and digital infrastructure to improve energy and resource efficiency 
during production processes.  
Nevertheless, encryption and authentication are critical to protect data privacy. BDA requires 
encryption of collected data before it is stored and transmitted to prevent unauthorized access and 
protect intellectual property. Identification and authentication may also be required for secure 
communications. (EEA 2021) 
==PAGE:27==
Ultimately, the collection and analysis of big data can greatly benefit companies in their digitalization 
processes, but it is important to address data security concerns. 
TBL Aspects 
 
This study emphasizes the importance of the TBL principles, which include the social, environmental, 
and economic dimensions. By considering multiple outcomes, businesses can achieve holistic 
profitability. It is crucial to acknowledge all three dimensions simultaneously to achieve the best results. 
Furthermore, the significance of TBL in implementing I4.0T is acknowledged, including identifying the 
opportunities and restrictions that come with it. 
Considering all three dimensions and balancing the unique challenges for each dimension, is necessary 
for sustainably generating value (Kiel et al. 2017). I4.0T can align with the TBL principles, if implemented 
correctly. They can increase productivity and product quality, reduce energy consumption, promote 
sustainability and prioritize worker safety (Braccini and Margherita 2019).  
However, the interrelated and sometimes conflicting impacts of the different I4.0T on the three 
dimensions of sustainability add complexity and uncertainty due to the policy-making process. For 
example, encouraging investment in autonomous robots and robotics systems may improve 
environmental sustainability but negatively impact employment in the automotive industry and reduce 
social sustainability. (Bai et al. 2020, p. 13)  
 
The SDGs are a comprehensive set of indicators that support sustainability across all three dimensions. 
They effectively evaluate processes and technologies and their potential for contributing to a 
sustainable future. Economic sustainability includes ending poverty, providing decent work, promoting 
economic growth, investing in industry and infrastructure and reducing inequalities. Social 
sustainability covers ending hunger, improving health and well-being, providing quality education, 
promoting gender equality and establishing peace, justice and strong institutions. Environmental 
sustainability focuses on clean water and sanitation, affordable and clean energy, sustainable cities and 
communities, responsible consumption and production, taking climate action and preserving life 
underwater and on land. (Bai and Sarkis 2019) 
I4.0 has potential to benefit all 17 SDGs (Bai et al. 2020, p. 3). Yet, the current approach focuses on 
economic parameters rather than realizing the transformative opportunities associated with I4.0, 
necessary to achieve the sustainability goals outlined in the SDGs and the Paris Climate Agreement. 
Some empirical studies support this statement and underline that organizations focus solely on the 
economic dimension while neglecting the impacts on other dimensions, considered positive 
externalities (Thiede 2018; Lee et al. 2018). It is crucial to incorporate comprehensive sustainability 
considerations into designing and implementing I4.0. Organizations should prioritize designing I4.0 
adoptions with all aspects of sustainability in mind. (Beier et al. 2021) 
 
The connection between I4.0T and sustainability is complex. To understand the individual effects, it is 
recommendable to examine each of the three dimensions of the TBL principles independently. 
However, these three categories cannot be entirely separated, so any impact on another category is 
considered secondary. As a compromise, each category is given its own space to be addressed 
individually while acknowledging their interconnected nature. 
Environment Aspects 
Digital transformation significantly impacts the environment, particularly in terms of energy and 
material consumption. Emissions are expected to rise with the expansion of digital technologies and 
the digitalization of manufacturing lines. One reason is the environmental backpack of a new digital 
==PAGE:28==
solution, that consists of the direct energy and material it consumed. As such, the environmental effects 
of I4.0T and its direct and indirect influences are being closely evaluated. (EEA 2021) 
 
On the one hand, I4.0 enables several environmental benefits if applied in an appropriate manner. It is 
possible to incorporate sustainable aspects into management practices: Environmental Management 
4.0 is an innovative approach, integrating advanced technologies into manufacturing processes to 
promote sustainability. Utilizing conscious sourcing, resource utilization, waste reduction and 
stakeholder communication allows for a more efficient and eco-friendly production process. However, 
achieving sustainable behavior requires collective actions and awareness. I4.0T should encourage the 
systematic implementation of sustainable practices to support environmental excellence. (Jesús 
Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 41) 
Moreover, ecological I4.0 aims to reduce energy and resource consumption and decrease waste or CO2 
through transparency, data-centered decisions and carbon footprint analyses (Gabriel and Pessel 2016; 
Sarkis and Zhu 2018). For ecological benefits, products can be disassembled and reused or recycled 
(Ejsmont et al. 2020). Autonomous robots and cobotic (cooperative robots) systems have the most 
significant favorable impact on the environment in the automotive and electronics industries (Bai et al. 
2020). 
 
On the other hand, the potential adverse effects of I4.0T' sustainability benefits are unpredictable and 
have unforeseen consequences. While the deployment and maintenance of virtual and physical 
infrastructures require environmental resources, it is difficult to predict the extent of their effects on 
the environment (Smit et al. 2016). This work aims to provide insights on how I4.0 can affect the flow 
of resources, energy, products, waste, assets and information and how it can have positive or negative 
environmental implications. The digitalization of manufacturing poses significant environmental risks, 
particularly regarding resource and energy requirements. It is essential to closely monitor and conduct 
further research to understand these technologies' environmental impacts completely. Efforts must be 
made to ensure the sustainability of digital manufacturing and prevent any rebound effects. Developing 
environmental impact assessment methodologies is crucial as they provide the basis for future 
indicators to measure digitalization's real and net benefits. In other words, ensuring that the 
environmental benefits outweigh the environmental impacts associated with I4.0 implementation is 
important. (Bonilla et al. 2018)(EEA 2021) 
 
To better understand the connection between I4.0T and environmental sustainability, in the next step 
of this study particular technologies related to environmental issues are analyzed. The automotive and 
electronics sectors are relevant, as they present important opportunities for practical outcomes. 
Robots 
Robots and cobotic systems can specify tasks and assist companies in the automotive industry to 
optimize resource use, reduce waste and minimize their environmental impact. Technological advances 
are helping companies and the industry make informed decisions in line with sustainable practices and 
goals. (Bai et al. 2020, p. 12) 
Technological improvements are important in emerging markets where efficiency gains are crucial. Due 
to high competition and limited resources, this is also applying to SMEs. In the automotive industry, 
nanotechnology shows promise as a key technology of I4.0 for achieving sustainability goals. It can help 
develop lightweight bio-based plastics and composites, which can reduce fuel consumption, lower CO2 
==PAGE:29==
emissions and lower reliance on petroleum-based plastics, promoting economic and environmental 
sustainability. (Esposito 2018)  
Although robots' carbon footprint contribution may be slight at the cell or line level, reducing their 
electricity consumption during operation can significantly impact their overall carbon footprint and 
sustainability profile. Therefore, robot producers should implement measures to minimize electricity 
usage during operation to improve their sustainability credentials. (Dijkman et al. 2015, p. 1351) 
Concluding, the integration of AI, robotics and advanced technologies in various sectors of the 
economy, including supply chains, distribution channels and manufacturing, has positively impacted 
the environment by reducing pollution, greenhouse gas emissions and energy consumption (Ejsmont 
et al. 2020). Yet, the energy efficiency in enhancing the environmental sustainability of robots is 
important to assure overall ecological results. 
AM 
Companies can reap the rewards of AM by incorporating materials that are not just sourced from 
nature. Depending on the industry's needs, these materials can consist of biodegradable items or even 
edible substances. By embracing these alternative materials, industries can adopt a more 
environmentally friendly approach to obtaining production inputs. AM also helps to reduce waste by 
reusing unused or leftover materials, eliminating the need for new resource mining or manufacturing. 
This sustainable practice encourages responsible manufacturing processes. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, 
p. 39) 
Sensors and BDA 
The Smart Sensor system can detect errors or non-conformities in materials, products, machines, steps 
or production lines and measures levels of waste or pollution generated. It can communicate with a 
central computer or mobile device and even act independently when programmed to do so. Location 
tracking, facilitated by tagging systems, is a valuable technology in many industries. Accurately tracking 
the location of machines, equipment and products throughout the production process can result in 
significant time savings, improved accuracy and reduced waste, production disruptions and pollution. 
(Cochran and Rauch 2020, p. 1241) 
Enabling the detection of machines and equipment failures also helps to identify service opportunities 
for stakeholders. This helps to prevent environmental waste caused by the loss or malfunction of 
production machinery or equipment and facilitates efficient communication with customers (Jesús 
Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 38). Additionally, I4.0 in logistics aims to bring production back to the place of 
consumption (reshoring) and produce on demand (AM) (Ancarani and Di Mauro 2018; Fratocchi 2018).  
CMfg is key in facilitating Environmental Management 4.0 by enabling the receipt, storage, processing, 
presentation and sharing of production data and information. This technology addresses internal 
environmental factors and responds to external environmental stimuli. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, 
p. 38) 
At last, with digital devices and visualization technologies, many traditional paper-based processes can 
be replaced, minimizing the environmental impact of paper production and waste. Adopting digital 
alternatives leads to greater efficiency and reduced reliance on physical documents. (Cochran and 
Rauch 2020, p. 1241) 
CPS 
CPS can receive production plans, outline production steps and provide real-time data on resource 
usage and production inputs. By analyzing this data, CPS can ensure that natural resources are used 
effectively and only when needed to produce the desired product. This optimized use of resources 
==PAGE:30==
minimizes waste by utilizing the right materials in the right quantities based on actual demand. 
Moreover, the CPS is interconnected through the Internet, acting as a network system that promotes 
communication and coordination. Changes in the production process can be communicated through 
messages to computer systems and mobile devices, enabling the CPS to make the necessary 
adjustments autonomously or collaboratively (in conjunction with humans). This ability increases 
manufacturing flexibility and responsiveness, contributing to better environmental sustainability. 
(Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 37) 
 
Energy consumption 
After all, these are just some exemplary evaluations of I4.0T in respect to environmental aspects. On 
the downside, increased use of digitalization and the Internet has significantly increased energy 
consumption. The Internet currently utilizes approximately 5% of the world's energy and could increase 
to 21% by 2030 (Berg et al. 2020). To address the increased energy demand associated with I4.0, 
adopting low-carbon energy systems that can meet these demands while minimizing carbon emissions 
is essential (Wang et al. 2016). This shift to sustainable energy sources is crucial to ensure the long-
term environmental viability of digital technologies and the overall sustainability of I4.0 (Wang et al. 
2016). The main challenge lies in balancing the increased energy usage with the environmental benefits 
of implementing I4.0T. 
Process heating is the largest energy consumer in the manufacturing industry, particularly in process 
industries, while Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning (HVAC) is the next significant energy-
consuming aspect. However, there are differences between sectors, as process industries prioritize 
process heating and discrete manufacturing sectors focus more on HVAC, lighting and process energy 
consumption. These differences highlight the sectoral variations in energy use patterns. To improve 
energy consumption, selecting energy-efficient technologies, properly sizing equipment and 
implementing effective process and factory control systems to minimize idle losses can be helpful. 
(Thiede 2021, p. 2) 
As countermeasure to increasing power demand, it is advisable to secure alternative energy sources to 
ensure a reliable energy supply for I4.0T (Brozzi et al. 2020). I4.0T can help address the challenge of 
fluctuating energy supply from renewable sources. Companies can align their production cycles with 
peak generation periods to better match the demand for electricity with the actual supply. (United 
Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 43) 
Material consumption 
With the increasing prevalence of digitalization technologies, there will be a corresponding increase in 
the demand for raw materials. Balancing this demand with environmental concerns is crucial to ensure 
sustainable transformation. Achieving the right balance between resource consumption and 
environmental benefits is essential. According to the International Resource Panel (IRP), the extraction 
and processing of natural resources, such as minerals, energy and food, contribute to around half of 
global greenhouse gas emissions and over 90% of the impacts of water stress and biodiversity loss 
(Oberle et al. 2019). Moreover, demand for raw materials is projected to more than double from 2011 
levels to 167 gigatons (Gt) by 2060. Unless absolute decoupling is achieved, environmental impacts 
associated with the extraction and processing of raw materials are expected to increase globally. (OECD 
2019) 
Improving material efficiency can be achieved through mixed means, including waste prevention, 
quality improvement, improved material utilization and effective recycling practices. This reduces 
upstream energy demand and mitigates associated environmental impacts. Nevertheless, it is vital to 
implement measures that effectively manage the impacts associated with material substitution in 
==PAGE:31==
manufacturing processes. Overall, by emphasizing material efficiency, implementing energy-flexible 
technologies and promoting responsible material substitution practices, the materials industry can 
significantly reduce energy demand, improve environmental performance and promote sustainability. 
(Thiede 2021, p. 3) 
In summary, the proposed implementation and productivity scenarios align with environmental 
sustainability goals and benefit horizontal and vertical integration. Environmental sustainability is 
promoted by improving working conditions, reducing pollution through flexible work arrangements, 
optimizing energy use, adopting low-cost production methods and leveraging BDA to minimize waste 
and pollution. (Oláh et al. 2020)

Social Aspects 
Despite acknowledging the importance of social aspects in driving the implementation of I4.0, these 
aspects are considered of secondary importance in terms of overall sustainability, emphasizing the 
need to address them appropriately (Brozzi et al. 2020). Therefore, this section explores the features 
of I4.0T and its effects on social aspects. 
 
The social dimension of I4.0 is characterized by changes in occupational profiles, working habits and 
potential job losses due to the automation of tasks. The impact of I4.0 on employment is still a subject 
of debate, with different opinions on whether it will lead to job growth or job losses (Beier et al. 2017; 
Kong et al. 2015; Bonekamp and Sure 2015). 
On the one hand, challenges arise in retaining skilled workers and addressing social gaps, leading 
especially to concerns for SMEs, as they have less leverage in attracting highly demanded specialists 
due to less stability of social benefits (Birkel et al. 2019; Luthra and Mangla 2018; Guo et al. 2016). 
Further performance pressure is put on workers due to the high degree of controllability and 
transparency facilitated by information and communication technologies (Stock et al. 2018).  
On the other hand, there is an opportunity to transform workers with traditional craftsmanship skills 
into supervisors and agents of continuous improvement, thus ensuring continued employment 
(Braccini and Margherita 2019). The term 'Operator 4.0' arises from this concept, as employees 
enhance their skills and capabilities, together with I4.0T development (Romero et al. 2016). Including 
I4.0T in sustainability training is important to improve workers' competencies, motivation and 
productivity (Hirsch-Kreinsen 2016). The creation of new employment opportunities in data 
management, data science, robot programming and the development of augmented reality 
applications (MDS 2019), can lead to further attractiveness of jobs (Cochran and Rauch 2020, p. 1242). 
 
Focusing on the automotive industry, big data and analytics have shown to be effective in addressing 
social sustainability concerns (Dremel et al. 2018; Kim and Chun 2019). Cloud technology enables big 
data analysis, offering the necessary framework. Although cloud-based applications concern privacy 
and personal autonomy they can make a significant contribution to a range of socially impactful 
applications. These include workforce augmentation, robotic disassembly for remanufacturing bio-
based plastics and composites, digital traceability of minerals, advanced design automation, precision 
agriculture and advanced bio-farming and vertical farming. (Esposito 2018; Isaias et al. 2015) 
The use of technologies such as smart cameras, sensors, AI-based localization systems and autonomous 
robots can enhance the human-machine interface, eliminate hazardous conditions and reduce mental 
stress among employees, thereby promoting workplace health and safety (Bai et al. 2020; Brozzi et al. 
2020; Stock et al. 2018). Also, smart and autonomous production systems can support this, by taking 
over monotonous and repetitive tasks; resulting in less absenteeism (Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022, p. 18), 
higher employee satisfaction and motivation (Müller et al. 2018). Big data and analytics can as well 
address social sustainability issues, such as fair labor practices (Bai et al. 2020, p. 12).  
Intelligent assistance systems and human-machine interfaces can boost employee satisfaction and 
increase flexibility in task processing, including the option to work from home. Such benefits promote 
the integration of work and family life. In addition, the use of I4.0T can contribute to improving the 
organizational climate, by using internet-based information networks to connect people, machines and 
devices, facilitating the flow of information and creating a more participative and dynamic work 
environment. (Birkel et al. 2019) 
 
==PAGE:33==
These social aspects are best addressed when companies collaborate with stakeholders, propose 
legislative measures and consider current research (Birkel et al. 2019). Cooperation can lead to better 
employment opportunities, improved living and working conditions and fewer accidents. Also, 
establishing standards and guidelines supports collaborations and avoids data ownership and privacy 
concerns (Birkel et al. 2019; Hirsch-Kreinsen 2016).  
 
A holistic approach that considers the social dimension, collaboration between stakeholders, worker 
competencies and proactive measures is crucial. It remains important to prioritize and efficiently 
implement I4.0T to achieve the UN Sustainable Development Goals and the Paris Agreement while 
addressing inequality and promoting inclusive and sustainable industrial development (United Nations 
Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 44). Yet, there are questions about accountability and 
responsibility for errors made by AI systems and who should be held responsible in the case of a 
tremendous failure (Birkel et al. 2019). 
In summary, the implementation of I4.0T can have a positive impact on social sustainability by 
improving workers' health and safety, creating new job opportunities, enhancing workers' skills, 
improving the organizational climate and promoting work-life balance. However, to ensure a truly 
sustainable and inclusive transition to I4.0, it is essential to address challenges and limitations such as 
job losses, information security issues and resource constraints. (Birkel et al. 2019)

Economic Aspects 
The primary reason for investing in I4.0T is to achieve economic sustainability, with social and 
environmental sustainability being considered a positive byproduct. This is particularly relevant for 
SMEs, where resources need to be utilized efficiently. (Jamwal et al. 2021a)  
Economic sustainability is one of the immediate outcomes that I4.0 can offer. Successfully delivering 
the manufacturing-economic sustainability functions of I4.0 can then contribute to environmental and 
socioeconomic sustainability (Ghobakhloo 2020, p. 16). With the ability to collect, process, structure 
and utilize data in decision-making is increasingly viewed as a competitive advantage for manufacturing 
companies (EEA 2021). Blockchain has the highest economic sustainability impact, followed by mobile 
technology (Zhang 2019; Grigoras et al. 2018; Cocco et al. 2017). 
 
Digitalization of manufacturing processes has brought significant economic benefits to industries, 
including cost savings, improved planning, material savings, flexbility, shorter lead times, and increased 
energy- and resource efficiency (Ejsmont et al. 2020; Beier et al. 2017; Rüßmann et al. 2015; Dalenogare 
et al. 2018).  
==PAGE:35==
On the contrary, there may be limitations and concerns, such as investment requirements, training 
needs, potential labor resistance, lack of management support and limited understanding of the 
implications of I4.0 (Jamwal et al. 2021a) (Luthra and Mangla 2018, p. 176). Mechanical and plant 
engineering companies face additional challenges in implementing I4.0 due to their focus on hardware 
and products rather than IT and software expertise. These companies may fear becoming dependent 
on external companies with more advanced data, software and IT competencies (Müller et al. 2018).  
Particularly challenging for SMEs are financial constraints, including the immense resources required 
to acquire new technologies (Selim Erol et al. 2016) and the delayed and uncertain profitability of I4.0T 
investments, resulting in a trade-off between general IT experts and specialized skills (Birkel et al. 2019).  
 
Companies must invest and commit to stay ahead of the I4.0 trend. To fully evaluate the impact of I4.0, 
it is essential to consider its effect on sustainable value creation, efficiency and profit and its impact on 
market share, competitiveness, supply chain, security and new business models (Jamwal et al. 
2021a)(Ghobakhloo 2020, p. 16).  
Concluding, the connectivity of the three TBL aspects plays an important role as derived from these 
insights. By considering these factors, industries can fully realize the benefits of I4.0 and drive economic 
growth. Therefore, a wholistic approach must consider all three aspects simultaneously and carefully 
evaluate their interrelations.

CE as Sustainability Enabler of I4.0T 
The CE is an economic concept that strives to keep the value of products, materials and resources intact 
for as long as possible in the manufacturing environment. This is achieved by reducing the use of new 
materials and minimizing waste generation (EC 2015). The CE is increasingly being implemented in I4.0 
and is considered a key element in the further development of society. Repairing, adapting and 
updating have become common individual practices, promoting innovation, recycling and conservation 
(Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, p. 26). According to Bressanelli et al. (2018), the main contributions of I4.0T 
to CE and sustainability include improved product design, marketing activities, monitoring and tracking 
product activity, provision of technical support, preventive and predictive maintenance, optimization 
of product usage, product upgrading and renovation and end-of-life activities (Bressanelli et al. 2018). 
The main contributing technologies are IoT, big data and analytics technologies (Bressanelli et al. 2018). 
Moreover, I4.0T, can support the Digital Product Passport (DPP) proposed by the European Union, 
which is essential for enabling CE and promoting sustainable decision-making. Scaling up CE in a digital 
world without digital technologies would be challenging. However, I4.0 technology must focus on CE 
to avoid hindering climate protection and environmental impact reduction goals. The current decade 
presents an opportunity for technological development that addresses the challenges of climate 
change and CE. Delaying action increases the risk of unfavorable lock-ins, stranded assets and sunk 
costs as digitalization progresses without a clear link to environmental impact reduction. (EEA 2021)  
 
Reverse logistics plays a strategic role in recovering used products at the end of their life, facilitating 
circular inputs and flows and supporting the implementation of circular systems (Julianelli et al. 2020). 
The transition to a CE is a gradual process that involves identifying problems and transforming them 
into circular business opportunities. Analytical tools such as qualitative questionnaires and quantitative 
material flow analysis can be used to review the status. Life cycle assessment is highlighted as a 
methodology for assessing environmental impacts in different application areas (Schebek et al. 2019, 
p. v). Transforming a traditional business model into a circular one requires top management 
commitment and support, as it involves a significant change in the organization’s value proposition and 
capacity building. Life cycle costing and analysis tools are useful in eliminating unfavorable solutions 
and assessing performance after adopting the best solution (Chen et al. 2020). Managers can align their 
business strategy with CE practices by identifying the relationship between digital technologies, CE 
value drivers and life cycle phases. Further research and development are necessary to scale existing 
approaches (Bressanelli et al. 2018).  
 
Nonetheless, the adoption and expansion of CE face multiple challenges. These include high initial 
setup costs, complex supply chains, lack of cooperation among companies, insufficient product design 
and manufacturing information, skill gaps, quality concessions, long disassembly lead times, and high 
costs associated with such processes (Jaeger and Upadhyay 2020). 
To overcome these hurdles, various measures can be taken. These include automating the circular 
supply chain, establishing semantic communication between machines and products, capturing 
information throughout the system, and enhancing sustainable operations by improving production 
and consumption rates (Rajput and Singh 2021, p. 1732). 
==PAGE:38==
 
The ReSOLVE framework is useful for organizations, seeking to apply CE principles. It consists of several 
key elements:  
The Regenerate approach, which involves using renewable energy and materials and biological cycles 
to enable the circulation of energy and materials. Additionally, it involves converting organic waste into 
energy and raw materials. 
The Share approach emphasizes sharing goods and assets between individuals, leading to longer-
lasting products and the availability of maintenance services to reuse and extend product life.  
The Optimize approach uses digital manufacturing technologies such as sensors, automation, RFID, big 
data and remote monitoring to reduce waste in production systems across supply chains. This approach 
leads to improved performance and predictive maintenance. (MacArthur and Waughray 2016) 
The Loop approach involves using both biological and technical cycles to recapture value. Biological 
loops include anaerobic digestion to recover value from organic waste. In contrast, technical loops 
include repair, reuse, refurbishment and recycling to recover value from post-consumer products and 
packaging. (Loomba and Nakashima 2012) 
The Virtualization approach focuses on service-oriented strategies that replace physical products with 
virtual and dematerialized alternatives. Lastly, the Replace approach involves replacing old and non-
renewable goods with advanced and renewable alternatives to mitigate supply chain unavailability. 
By implementing these approaches, organizations can promote resource efficiency, reduce waste and 
close material loops, ultimately contributing to the CE. (Lopes de Sousa Jabbour et al. 2018, pp. 275–
276)

General Barriers and Constriction Criteria  
Having analyzed the interplay between I4.0T and sustainable concepts, the insights provide a basis for 
deriving key aspects. It is important to consider how addressing certain factors can lead to 
improvement and which factors have a negative impact when not considered thoroughly.  
In the upcoming chapters, the emphasis will shift away from identifying sustainable arguments based 
on the TBL. Instead, better outcomes can be achieved by focusing on I4.0T, which facilitate sustainable 
development, as derived in the preceding chapter. 
To fully benefit from I4.0, it is crucial to understand its advantages and potential risks. Managers should 
carefully evaluate whether the benefits outweigh the challenges and risks. Even though implementing 
I4.0 can be challenging, it presents significant opportunities and benefits. Therefore, companies should 
take advantage of these opportunities and be encouraged by the challenges and risks of digitalization 
and connectivity in industrial value creation. (Müller et al. 2018) A table at the end of each chapter 
summarized the main factors, together with the associated literature. 
Expectations and hopes regarding the sustainability impacts of I4.0 are high, but often not grounded in 
empirical evidence. Creating a challenge due to uncertainty of success (Luthra et al. 2020, p. 1516). The 
digital transformation of the industry leads to conflicts and trade-offs between the environmental, 
economic and social dimensions of sustainability, requiring difficult compromises (Beier et al. 2021). 
 
Data security and data privacy are major concerns for companies implementing I4.0, as connectivity of 
processes and complex software increase vulnerability to cyber threats and intellectual property 
breaches. Appropriate policies and regulations must be developed to address these security and 
privacy challenges. (Brink et al. 2015) 
However, the rapid pace of technological progress can outpace the formulation of policies and 
regulations to control digital and technological developments. Governments are overburdened in 
creating appropriate policy frameworks and global standards for digitalization and I4.0. Simultaneously, 
the slow development of models and tools to support real-time quantification of metrics and 
engineering analysis, limits the expected benefits of standardized approaches (Ramanujan et al. 2023, 
p. 14). In addition, the rapid pace of technological development can lead to a race to innovate. 
Pioneering countries or companies gain a first-mover advantage, giving them significant economic 
leverage and the ability to bypass social and environmental standards where regulation is weak. 
==PAGE:40==
Proprietary software formats can lead to lock-in effects, limiting competition and potentially leading to 
monopolies (Acemoglu et al. 2020). Therefore, dependence on a few countries for technology 
endangers the independence of others (Hobbs 2020). Fast product iterations require lifelong learning, 
potentially disadvantaging those who have yet to have digital skills training. Finally, the shift from 
human labor to machines in a digitalized world may impact tax revenue as well as social and medical 
welfare services funding. (Acemoglu et al. 2020) 
 
Significant financial resources are required to standardize infrastructure, develop smart devices and 
sensor technologies and invest in digital operations (Kumar et al. 2022, p. 3). Furthermore, integration 
challenges of new technologies with traditional production systems and uncertainties due to different 
contexts and industries of application are key issues for I4.0T (Bai et al. 2020). Consequently, integrating 
emerging technologies into the overall business system design faces difficulties, due to a lack of tools 
and concepts to support this integration. This hinders long-term enterprise strategy and design related 
to I4.0. (Cochran and Rauch 2020, p. 1237)  
 
The absence of investment in research and development activities related to I4.0 prevents the effective 
adoption and utilization of these technologies. The lack of data exchange protocols, inadequate 
infrastructure and poor data quality can limit the effectiveness of I4.0 initiatives (Machado et al. 2021).  
Before investing in digital technologies, it is recommendable to be aware of the potential impacts, such 
as energy consumption and amounts of waste. Increased efficiency in production processes may result 
in higher production volumes, leading to more waste and negatively impacting sustainability goals 
(Golde 2016). The primary reason for disposing of digital devices is digital obsolescence, where devices 
become outdated and stop functioning due to new software versions (Harris 2020). While digitalization 
drives economic growth (Katz and Koutroumpis 2013), it can be detrimental to sustainable 
development, if it occurs in industries that harm the environment, such as those that rely on fossil fuels 
(Schneidewind 2018). 
 
This thesis´ literature review identified several barriers and challenges that hinder the implementation 
of I4.0. These challenges include poor understanding of I4.0 implications, insufficient research and 
development, legal issues, lack of management support, commitment and sustainable intention, 
resistance to change, lack of digital culture, internet networks and ensuring supplier sustainability 
(Luthra and Mangla 2018, p. 174), (Machado et al. 2021)(Müller et al. 2018), (Salimzadeh and 
Courvisanos 2015). Additionally, risks include changing business models, dependencies on suppliers, 
training requirements and concerns regarding AI and manufacturing relocation (Kiel et al. 2017; 
Tjahjadi et al. 2021; Birkel et al. 2019).

General Enabling Factors and Key Benefits for I4.0T Implementation  
It is important to understand that enablers are not quick-fix solutions for implementing I4.0T. Instead, 
they can help companies identify key areas that require attention and a customized approach. This 
==PAGE:43==
thesis focuses on the available enablers, to provide a comprehensive framework for succesfull 
digitalization. However, the results provide a detailed analysis of potential strategies for overcoming 
obstacles and shed light on the factors that can facilitate success. 
To enable I4.0, businesses can control critical processes and promote transparency in sharing 
economic, environmental and social data (Tiwari and Khan 2020). Using intelligent systems provides 
top management with systematized and analyzed data, enabling faster and more reliable decision-
making processes, ultimately benefiting sustainability development. Adopting global standards and 
protocols for data sharing is recommended to facilitate real-time communication between intelligent 
systems. This permits effective information exchange not only between companies but also with 
stakeholders, thereby promoting economic sustainability (Tiwari and Khan 2020). Additionally, 
companies are advised to conduct audits to identify cyber vulnerabilities and weaknesses, such as 
inadequate crisis management strategies, to ensure successful implementation(Bag et al. 2021). 
 
Additionally, the resulting facilitators have been sorted into categories to provide clarity and ease of 
navigation. 
Top managmeent: 
Proactive risk management and top management involvement are identified as prominent factors, that 
highly influence successful implementation of I4.0T (Birkel et al. 2019). 
Management should view the investment in I4.0 as a strategic decision with long-term benefits. This 
includes creating roadmaps, strategic planning, developing standardized processes and investing 
appropriate resources to improve internal capabilities (Garg et al. 2021, p. 190). Careful planning and 
potential public investments are necessary to support implementation in the manufacturing 
environment (Luthra et al. 2020, p. 1516). Consulting firms can assist in prioritizing resource allocation 
(Raj et al. 2020, p. 15). Strategies could focus on creating multidisciplinary project teams, implementing 
digital factories, creating systems departments and collaborating (Moica et al. 2018, p. 56).  
Industry managers and practitioners must understand the causal interactions between I4.0 
sustainability-oriented drivers to embed sustainability in supply chains. By improving collaboration with 
suppliers and external stakeholders and formulating effective plans to increase process transparency, 
manufacturing organizations can improve their eco-economic efficiency and enhance human welfare 
through I4.0 initiatives. (Luthra et al. 2020, p. 1516) 
It is also important to consider social and economic factors when creating new business models (Kiel 
et al. 2017). By selling functionality rather than tangible products, value can be provided to more 
people with less resource consumption (Stock et al. 2018, p. 260). As strategic, operational, 
environmental and social opportunities drive the implementation of I4.0, it is crucial to consider the 
potential competitive advantage that investments in I4.0T can bring (Bosman et al. 2020). 
Finally, to adopt I4.0, accurate knowledge, financial resources, skills development and the 
establishment of an open and flexible organizational culture are necessary. Proper planning, data 
management, security and adaptable organizational structures are crucial factors for successful 
implementation. (Veile et al. 2020; Zangiacomi et al. 2020)  
Employee: 
Companies should adopt a collaborative, exploratory and entrepreneurial mindset and address 
employees' concerns while convincing them of the benefits of I4.0. Training and development programs 
should enhance specific competencies and skills necessary for I4.0, such as data analytics, IT, software 
and human-machine interaction. In easing employee fears regarding I4.0-related technologies, 
==PAGE:44==
employees with a lot of knowledge and experience in simple tasks can assist in training new colleagues, 
guiding customers and supporting the implementation of new machines. Evaluating customers' 
changing demands and acceptance towards these technologies is also crucial. (Birkel et al. 2019) 
LDC: 
In developing industrial economies, integrating I4.0T in manufacturing processes is often driven by 
market demand and the need to comply with regulations or requirements from large commercial 
customers. These customers play a vital role in promoting the adoption of digitalized, energy-efficient 
and resource-efficient production technologies. Local showcases and integration of I4.0 with renewable 
energy and energy efficiency can help assess the benefits and challenges of implementation, especially 
in emerging and developing economies. (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, 
p. 43)  
To promote sustainable manufacturing and increase awareness among consumers and producers, it is 
necessary to implement intention awareness programs as an additional requirement and address 
infrastructure and connectivity gaps (Salimzadeh and Courvisanos 2015; Birkel et al. 2019).  
Collaboration: 
The implementation of I4.0T requires the integration of different systems and the exchange of 
standardized information. This integration can be achieved through horizontal-, vertical- and end-to-
end digital integration of engineering along the value chain (Ben Youssef 2020). Horizontal growth takes 
place on the same level, vertical growth along the supply chain and end to end digital integration 
delivers a service to the client as a whole from the beginning to the end. 
Industries in the same segment can work together to form partnerships and networks, allowing them 
to utilize resources no longer needed in their production processes. Cooperation reduces costs and 
waste and provides other industries with the necessary resources. Sharing machinery and equipment 
between industries in I4.0 can also optimize operational capacity. As a result, industries can provide 
services to each other, ensuring continuous operation and revenue generation for the supplying 
industry. (Jesús Hamilton Ortiz 2020, pp. 35–36) 
Scalable digital solutions can assist with sustainability assessments on multiple suppliers and promote 
strategic alignment on sustainability objectives between partners (Matthess et al. 2022, pp. 100050–
100051). Learning from each other can lead to mutual benefits and valuable programs (Müller and 
Voigt 2018). Online platforms can help ensure better quality inputs and security in business 
negotiations (Birkel et al. 2019). Cross-border cooperation can be beneficial at macro and 
microeconomic levels. In the digital age, transparent information exchange with consumers is crucial 
for promoting responsible consumption. By empowering consumers with environmental awareness, 
businesses can make informed choices and contribute to sustainability efforts (Bonilla et al. 2018). 
To strengthen relationships with customers and society, companies should disclose their sustainability 
actions through reports that include data processed by smart devices without human intervention, 
avoiding greenwashing practices (Bonilla et al. 2018), (Stock et al. 2018). 
Government and policies: 
Various policy measures need to be implemented to improve recycling rates and move towards a more 
sustainable CE. These include reflecting climate costs in prices, promoting recyclability through 
standards and incentives and investing in recycling infrastructure. Clear targets and defined 
responsibilities are also necessary for effective implementation (Sun et al., p. 444). Policies also 
promote the competitiveness of local actors and ensure the convergence of I4.0T with sustainability. 
==PAGE:45==
An integrated framework incorporating I4.0T on IT platforms is expected to promote proactive decision-
making and ensure performance, products, processes and people sustainability. (Ejsmont et al. 2020) 
Previous studies have emphasized the importance of supportive government policies in enabling 
sustainable industrial systems and maximizing the effectiveness of initiatives (Luthra et al. 2020, 
p. 1505). The European Union recognizes the impact of digitalization on sustainability. It aims to 
transform into a society with a resource-efficient and competitive economy, decoupled from 
greenhouse gas emissions and resource use. The European Green Deal addresses the interlinked 
challenges of environmental and digital transformation. (EEA 2021) 
Manufacturing and logistics: 
Incorporating digital technologies into logistic processes under the term logistics monitoring involves 
optimizing material flows, improving transportation efficiency, resource allocation and reduce delivery 
errors (Birkel et al. 2019; Braccini and Margherita 2019). I4.0T such as IoT, CPS and big data enable 
establishing decentralized distribution centers near consumption areas, reducing logistics costs and 
environmental impacts (Brozzi et al. 2020; Ghobakhloo 2020).  
Digital tools for automatic data pre-processing and connectivity for automated and self-optimized 
production can improve decision-making. Moreover, product design should focus on increased 
recyclability while waste segregation and processing should be improved to address heterogeneity and 
contamination in waste streams (Sun et al., p. 444). By adopting these elements, systems and machines 
can be better integrated, human-machine interfaces can be improved and production processes can 
become more agile, intelligent and decentralized, positively impacting all dimensions of sustainability 
(Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022, p. 19). 
Finally, maintenance plays a noteworthy role in the industrial ecosystem and has greater potential to 
enhance sustainability in manufacturing than many industrialists realize, by adopting performance 
metrics that promote sustainability (Garetti and Taisch 2012).  
Models: 
To better understand the complexity of I4.0T, advanced Multiple Attribute Decision Making (MADM) 
method should be utilized to find the best fitting technology to invest in. The methods: Decision Making 
Trial and Evaluation Laboratory (DEMATEL) and Analytic Network Process (ANP) are effective tools that 
provide a comprehensive and integrative analysis of different factors and their interdependencies. They 
are picked up again later in the thesis but need to be examined more, as they do not fit SMEs yet. Using 
these methods, researchers can derive more knowledge of I4.0 dynamics and make more informed 
decisions. (Bai et al. 2020) 
Effective decision-making for sustainable manufacturing across financial, environmental and social 
dimensions requires strategic and operational attention. Existing Decision Support Systems (DSSs) focus 
more on strategic planning, with limited emphasis on the operational level and social planning 
objectives. Improvements in DSSs are needed to address these gaps. (Zarte et al. 2019) 
 
A study by Da Torres Rocha et al. identifies several axes that provide recommendations for the 
successful implementation of I4.0T (Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022, p. 19). Some axes are demonstrated 
to complete the analysis.  
The continuous improvement axis emphasizes using I4.0 to improve process performance and product 
quality, contributing to economic and environmental sustainability (Stock et al. 2018), (Müller et al. 
2018), (Da Torres Rocha et al. 2022, p. 19). The production monitoring axis involves assessing the 
==PAGE:46==
performance of production processes to improve quality and productivity, utilizing strategies such as 
RFID systems, handheld terminals and sensors. The production planning axis proposes using digital 
twins and AI models to plan, control and optimize the use of resources and personalized production, 
leading to economic sustainability through improved productivity and resource planning (Ghobakhloo 
2020). Finally, the data sharing axis emphasizes the importance of smart platforms and blockchain 
technology in achieving greater integration among supply chain members, enabling efficient and secure 
data sharing within the supply chain. (Braccini and Margherita 2019; Luthra et al. 2020; Stock et al. 
2018)  
The customization axis involves using I4.0T to create products and processes that meet customers' 
specific needs. This involves utilizing the IoT and the IoS to connect with consumers and gather data 
on their consumption habits. By analyzing this data, companies can develop or adjust their products to 
fit their customers' needs better. Furthermore, they can use advanced simulation software to prototype 
and simulate production lines virtually. (Beier et al. 2020) 
The organizational performance axis focuses on improving product and process performance during 
the design phase. Smart product design allows for effective monitoring throughout its lifespan, 
enabling data collection and analysis for better resource utilization and waste management in real-
time, supporting CE principles. For process design, adopting cyber-physical production systems (CPPSs), 
intelligent tracking and data protection systems and using IIoT and AI enables efficient production 
planning, autonomous operations and enhanced equipment reliability (Tiwari and Khan 2020). By 
implementing these technologies, companies can achieve sustainable energy usage, reduce 
greenhouse gas emissions and improve cybersecurity. Furthermore, the axis recommends preventive 
and predictive maintenance processes through I4.0 technologies, which contribute to extending 
equipment life, ensuring employee safety and replacing environmentally harmful products with eco-
friendly alternatives (Müller et al. 2018). It is suggested to retrofit parts, machines and software with 
newer versions to save material and energy and increase productivity. Adopting these elements 
facilitates more sustainable practices within the organization, positively impacting both environmental 
and economic aspects. (Müller et al. 2018) 
 
After analyzing the general limiting and enhancing factors, it is evident that implementing I4.0 is 
complex. It can cause uncertainty and confusion regarding its impact on sustainability. On the one 
hand, digitalization can improve equality and economic factors. On the other hand, it can also lead to 
an increase in energy requirements and potential resource depletion or environmental damage. 
Several identified factors can enhance and limit the process, depending on the way they are taken care 
of. Hence, it is crucial to carefully weigh the potential benefits and drawbacks of I4.0 applications. This 
will ensure that sustainability considerations are adequately addressed and maintained. (Bai et al. 
2020)

SME Tailored Results 
The previous chapter discussed the general criteria for factors that can restrict or enable certain 
outcomes. This chapter examines, whether these criteria apply to SMEs and how they should be 
assessed in the context of SMEs. Initially focus is on key factors that limit digitalization when they are 
not addressed carefully. Following, the factors that can facilitate the implementation of I4.0 are 
discussed. 
Key Barriers  
SMEs face unique challenges and barriers compared to larger enterprises (Sarath Menon and Satya 
Shah 2020, p. 206; Wuest Thorsten et al. 2018). While some SMEs have successfully undergone digital 
transformation, the majority still struggle to effectively move into the digital realm of manufacturing 
(Sarath Menon and Satya Shah 2020, p. 206). Existing frameworks often fail to address real-world 
problems and lack implementation mechanisms (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 194). Therefore, there is a need 
for specialized roadmaps and maturity models tailored to SMEs to unlock the sustainability benefits of 
I4.0 practices (Akkucuk 2019).  
 
==PAGE:49==
There are significant differences in the opportunities and exposure that employees in SMEs and MNEs 
receive. Understanding the characteristics of SMEs can help clarify the contrast. 
Employees in SMEs often adopt a 'jack of all trades' approach, handling various responsibilities in 
different areas, which limits the development of expertise in specific areas (Bublitz and Noseleit 2014). 
In contrast, employees in MNEs tend to specialize in specific areas and are considered experts, 
especially in areas such as automation technology (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 196). Furthermore, employees 
in SMEs often lack exposure to mentors, workshops, employee participation and supervised 
comprehensive training, which hinders their professional growth areas (McAdam and Reid 2001; Mittal 
et al. 2018). Farther, there is a strong association between company size and the perception of I4.0 in 
terms of achieving a more flexible organizational structure (Brozzi et al. 2020). Operational 
opportunities are the main driving force behind the implementation of I4.0 for SMEs. In contrast, large 
companies are primarily driven by strategic benefits. (Müller et al. 2018)  
Additional differences arise in readiness to adopt digital technologies and services across regions and 
firm sizes. While MNEs are integrating digital technologies into their manufacturing processes to 
optimize them, smaller companies are exploring the opportunities and benefits they can gain (United 
Nations Industrial Development Organization 2017, p. 43). However, all firms face challenges and 
uncertainties in selecting appropriate technologies and making investment decisions. Smaller firms 
with limited resources prioritize shop-floor level technologies, while larger firms with more resources 
focus on operations-related technologies. (Bosman et al. 2020)  
SMEs, however, have shown to be more flexible and adaptable in responding to the changing market 
environments, due to smaller batches production (Kapasuwan et al. 2007; Sciascia et al. 2015). 
 
Business model infrastructure: 
The adoption of I4.0 often involves changes to business models, which can be a challenge for SMEs. 
They may worry that their key assets will become less appealing to customers, potentially losing core 
competencies, market success, or profitability. Traditional thinking, which is product-driven rather than 
data-driven and a need for knowledge about generating value from data pose additional challenges for 
SMEs in deriving value from I4.0T. (Birkel et al. 2019) 
SMEs rely heavily on customer support to fund their research and development activities (Birkel et al. 
2019). Therefore, it is crucial to examine weather customers are ready to pay for an I4.0 produced 
product.  
Policy:  
The lack of an implementation plan and policy for sustainability is considered a significant barrier, as 
it affects the sustainability competence of organizations and hinders the development of short-term 
strategies of SMEs (Kumar et al. 2022, p. 16). 
To enable the widespread adoption of I4.0, new ethical, technical and legal standards are needed. 
Emphasizes lays on standardization based on widely used standards that facilitate interoperability, 
communication and security. Adopting I4.0 standards and relevant policies and regulations is essential 
to build trust between SMEs and their partners, thereby supporting collaborative efforts. (Han and 
Trimi 2022, p. 10) 
External support: 
==PAGE:50==
SMEs may lack the skills and resources to develop and implement I4.0T on their own (Birkel et al. 2019). 
Consequently they need more expertise to address technological challenges, often requiring external 
support from research institutions, government organizations, or industry working groups (Sarath 
Menon and Satya Shah 2020, pp. 205–206). Dependence on external expertise hinders the acquisition 
of knowledge.  
Aliances, supply chain: 
SMEs may be reluctant to share information and data digitally due to fear of reduced bargaining power 
and contributing data without receiving adequate benefits in return (Birkel et al. 2019). In a competitive  
environment the trust barrier plays an important role. 
Understanding the role of SMEs in larger supply chains is key to tailoring the supply chain's 
characteristics, advantages and disadvantages to their traits. By doing so, SMEs can be more relevant 
to suppliers, partners and other companies. (Matthess et al. 2022, p. 100038) 
SMEs face limitations in cross-disciplinary networking opportunities, both nationally and 
internationally. This limitation makes it difficult for SMEs to stay up to date with cutting-edge research 
and establish partnerships with universities and research institutions. Collaborative strategies are 
crucial to success, but SMEs often lack partnerships with academic and research institutions, which 
limits their access to shared knowledge. Unlike MNEs, SMEs' knowledge and expertise are often 
concentrated in specific areas, leading them to outsource key activities. SMEs typically have a smaller 
product portfolio and a limited collaborative network, which makes them more dependent on a certain 
number of suppliers or vendors (Singh et al. 2007). In contrast, MNEs have a wider range of supplier or 
vendor options, reducing their dependence on a single supplier or vendor. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 196) 
Top management: 
There is a positive relationship between management support and I4.0 adoption, highlighting the 
critical role of top management in decision making and coordination (Agostini and Nosella 2020). It is 
essential, that top management is involved in the whole process of transformation, driving the process 
of change (Moeuf et al. 2020). This is especially important for SMEs, as they typically have a flatter 
organizational structure, making top management's impact more direct. Understanding the challenges 
and opportunities of I4.0 adoption is crucial for SMEs to stay competitive and thrive in today's digital 
landscape. Resource management, organizational competence, lack of strategy and internal resistance 
can be derived as key factors, which are best addressed by top management (Ghobakhloo and 
Iranmanesh 2021). In SMEs the number of decision makers is limited to only a few mangers. If they fail 
to implement new business models on time, the SME goes the wrong direction. 
Resources general: 
SMEs have restricted resources, which makes the significant investment costs involved in I4.0 adoption 
seem intimidating. Moreover, some SMEs believe that their environmental impact is minimal, making 
it challenging to comprehend the advantages of sustainability and the necessity of adopting I4.0T 
customized to their requirements (Matthess et al. 2022, p. 100038). Due to resource constraints, SMEs 
often struggle to integrate environmental strategies (Eikelenboom and Jong 2019).  
Furthermore, the short-term focus of SMEs poses a challenge to long-term planning and investment in 
technology, leading to limited access to crucial resources and information required for full technology 
adoption (Eggers 2020). Conjointly, organizational strategy, structure, human resources, 
competitiveness and operational challenges influence the adoption of I4.0 in SMEs (Nwaiwu et al. 
2020). 
==PAGE:51==
In conclusion, SMEs face multiple barriers to adopting I4.0 to achieve sustainability in the supply chain 
due to limited resources. These challenges include reluctance to invest, difficulty integrating 
environmental strategies, short-term focus and the need for more comprehensive support in navigating 
the complexities of I4.0. Overcoming these obstacles requires tailored approaches, greater awareness 
and access to the necessary resources to embrace sustainable and innovative practices.

Enabling Key Consideration  
The following key considerations are critical to ensure the smooth implementation of I4.0T to SMEs 
achieving sustainable outcome in terms of TBL principles.  
Mittal et al. identified six requirements to integrate I4.0T in SMEs successfully: technical resource 
availability, standards, organizational culture, employee participation, alliances and collaboration 
(Mittal et al. 2018, p. 195). These criteria are used as a foundation for orientation. 
Economic: 
One approach to overcome the financial barriers is to consider implementing I4.0 to customize 
products and improve a company's competitive position (Müller and Voigt 2018, p. 666). More 
recommendations motivate companies to address various aspects such as knowledge and awareness, 
strategic planning, cultural change, infrastructure development and regulatory framework (Luthra and 
Mangla 2018, p. 174) (Machado et al. 2021). SMEs must assess whether they require I4.0T and if they 
can afford them, as financial resources are difficult to alter. Opting for the most cost-effective 
technologies can release funds for further investments. The enablers suggest comprehensive 
evaluation and planning to prevent undesirable economic expenses. 
Vision and Strategy: 
SMEs should use a self-assessment tool to align their current level with their I4.0 vision. The tool should 
be integrated into an appropriate maturity model that guides the process, eliminating the need for 
external consultants. The maturity model should reflect the diversity of SMEs and be integrated with a 
self-assessment tool that SME managers can use at different stages of their journey towards smart 
manufacturing. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 212) 
Self-assessment maturity models can help SMEs assess their readiness for I4.0 adoption and address 
research gaps. Many SMEs have a different digitalization and smart manufacturing maturity level than 
what is currently considered "level 1" in most maturity models (tailored to MNE). This is why a new 
"Level 0" is suggested to reflect the real starting point. To move from this new baseline to the standard 
Level 1, SMEs need to make a significant effort, including a change in mindset, investment and human 
resources. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 194) 
While a tailored vision addresses the specific needs of SMEs, it does not consider the importance of 
SMEs assessing their strengths and weaknesses. SMEs can leverage strategies such as conducting an 
analysis to identify their Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities and Threats (SWOT) (Cerezo-Narváez 
et al. 2019). By conducting a readiness assessment, SMEs can determine their current capabilities and 
gaps, which will help them create a more comprehensive and effective strategy for their digital 
transformation. (Mittal et al. 2018, p. 212) 
The current maturity models focus on MNEs and do not consider the characteristics of SMEs. 
Employee participation: 
For SMEs to succeed, it is important, that they prioritize improving the technical skills of their 
employees to manage data and implement changes effectively. A cost-effective way to achieve this is 
by implementing virtual training programs that offer employees skills development opportunities 
regardless of location. Training programs should be user-centric to ensure a practical understanding of 
I4.0T and their application in the industry. In addition to technical skills, SMEs should also focus on 
developing digital and soft skills such as problem-solving, communication, and adaptability for 
employees to thrive in the I4.0 environment. Finally, SMEs can build on their experience in 
manufacturing systems and integrate I4.0T into their existing processes. Additionally, new training 
==PAGE:53==
models should emphasize developing transversal skills to prepare workers for the challenges of I4.0. 
(Luthra et al. 2020; Yadav et al. 2020a; Jamwal et al. 2021b; Papadopoulos et al. 2022) 
Subsequently, linking employee bonuses and promotions to the knowledge of sustainability and I4.0 
can encourage skills development and reduce resistance to change (Kumar et al. 2022, pp. 16–17). To 
be digitally ready, SMEs should focus on utilizing data analytics and knowledge acquisition, investing in 
employee training and developing infrastructure for big data analysis in the supply chain (Pirola et al. 
2020). 
Government and policies: 
Government regulations and incentives encourage SMEs to promote sustainability and adopt I4.0 
practices (Kumar et al. 2022, pp. 16–17). Despite that, adaptations and accompanying programs 
specifically designed for SMEs are necessary to address concerns about larger companies, gaining an 
advantage through technology. The government should address data security, ownership issues and 
standardization of processes. (Müller and Voigt 2018, p. 666)  
Also, publicly funded research programs in I4.0 should prioritize balancing the opportunities and risks 
associated with digitalization. Public policy is vital in encouraging companies to integrate digital support 
into their sustainability management, using successful flagship projects as examples. Key objectives are 
supporting SMEs in their digital modernization efforts and designing digital products that reduce 
energy and material demand. (Beier et al. 2021) 
Collaboration and Alliances: 
Collaboration between SMEs and the supply chain is derived as an important aspect. Cloud computing 
is essential in linking and integrating this collaboration. By forming horizontal alliances with universities 
and research institutions, knowledge sharing and creation can be promoted. One example of 
collaboration is the Cloud Process Execution Engine (CPEE), an open-source platform SMEs can use to 
manage their business processes (Mangler et al. 2014).  
Aligning business models with the value network and implementing strategic risk management is 
necessary for profitability, sustainability and value co-creation within the ecosystem (Ghanbari et al. 
2017; Dellermann et al. 2017). 
It is essential to consider the use of technology in assessing supplier sustainability to simplify and 
standardize the process of collecting and exchanging sustainability data (Wilhelm and Villena 2021). 
Adopting a strategic approach to this implementation can help. Overall, it is important to have scalable 
solutions tailored to their specific needs while also being compatible with the broader I4.0 framework 
(Kiel et al. 2017). 
Organizational Culture and Strategy: 
SMEs that adopt innovation-based strategies tend to focus on environmental and social changes, while 
sustainability-rooted strategies prioritize product, process and innovation principles (Klewitz and 
Hansen 2014). By committing to sustainable practices, SMEs can enhance product quality, build 
stronger relationships with employees and customers and boost their reputation and economic 
growth. Therefore, organizational culture and strategy must be adapted to ensure internal 
compatibility with sustainable plans. 
During the planning, addressing the foundational bottom-level barriers is crucial, as they can act as 
drivers for other barriers. By tackling relatively independent barriers in the short term, they become 
more manageable to overcome. (Kumar et al. 2022, p. 17) 
==PAGE:54==
Moreover, overcoming obstacles to sustainable manufacturing requires top management 
commitment, regulatory support, expertise and access to alternative raw materials and energy sources. 
SMEs can benefit from enhancing top management commitment, strategic alignment and receiving 
government and institutional support. (Machado et al. 2021)  
Knowledge Sharing and Standardization: 
To take advantage of the benefits of digitalization, SMEs need to innovate their business models. 
Adopting new and modern business logic, such as cloud-based (Zhang et al. 2010), service-oriented 
(Bulger et al. 2014) and platform business models (Porter and Heppelmann 2014) that rely on data and 
information, can help prioritize customer needs and ensure future customer loyalty. Collecting data is 
only of use if evaluated and used correctly. Activities like employee collaboration, attending 
international workshops or conferences and working with academia and industry partners are crucial 
to fostering knowledge (Vasudevan and Chawan 2014). 
While private cloud solutions are typically associated with high costs, that make them too expensive 
for many SMEs, the Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) data science platform offers an affordable solution 
(Singh et al. 2016). It benefits SMEs in data science by providing a data management shell that enables 
consistent and standardized access to data, promoting efficient and effective use of data across the 
organization. Additionally, the platform facilitates using historical or real-time data for tasks such as 
system diagnosis or prediction, providing valuable insights for decision-making. (Bianchini and 
Michalkova 2019) 
By using data science, SMEs can unlock new opportunities and better understand their internal 
processes and the needs of their stakeholders. Cloud computing, supported by SaaS, is a fundamental 
enabler of the I4.0 system, providing scalability, flexibility and cost-effectiveness. It allows organizations 
to access hardware and software resources on a pay-as-you-go basis. At the same time, the platform 
helps SMEs build and manage an intelligent virtual system. (Han and Trimi 2022, p. 10) 
 
To sum up, SMEs encounter several obstacles when incorporating I4.0T in their organizational structure 
to attain sustainable supply chain processes. Still, SMEs can overcome these challenges and build a 
prosperous and sustainable future in the digital age by utilizing inventive business strategies, promoting 
sustainable practices, supporting employees, finding appropriate partnerships and using the correct 
tools.

Results for Framework  
This section summarizes the predictive analysis of I4.0T concerning sustainable factors. The potential 
impacts of various scenarios have been identified and those deemed relevant will be included in the 
framework developed later in the study. 
The analysis of the relationship between I4.0 or I4.0T and sustainability, as assessed by the TBL aspects, 
shows that it is a complex issue. It was found that an action or investment can have various impacts on 
different sustainability aspects. Therefore, planning before implementing I4.0T is crucial to ensure 
success. It is essential to research existing technologies and processes to understand how they interact 
before proceeding. Key players have been identified in the literature review. 
I4.T enables data analytics, such as IoT, CPS, cloud computing, or sensors, which is an essential 
technology foundation for further digitization steps. Sensors are easy to implement and, with low-cost 
software such as SaaS, suitable for SMEs with limited economic resources. Various models can assist in 
analyzing the maturity level. Gathering information and knowledge about the company's situation is 
essential to formulate the next steps.  
==PAGE:56==
Tailoring strategies to the maturity and resources of the SME is critical. Promoting sustainability in the 
company is essential and employees need to be convinced of the usability of I4.0T. Employees also 
need to be trained and informed. Several strategies and approaches to support employees are 
presented. The importance of top management is also emphasized, as they need to motivate the rest 
of the organization. 
Collaboration with research institutions, stakeholders, customers and the supply chain is crucial, also 
benefiting employees’ knowledge acquisition. Partnerships can help build more knowledge and learn 
from each other's mistakes and successes, motivating others in the immediate environment to digitize.  
Finally, helpful external guidelines and government regulations are needed to enable SMEs to keep up 
with MNEs moving towards I4.0. Guidelines and regulations for data exchange are also essential, to 
assure compatible data sets. The implementation of network facilities and automation capabilities will 
support this process. In this way, SMEs can increase the attractiveness of their company and thus 
maintain a competitive environment, while also addressing TBL aspects of sustainability. 
Consequently, SMEs can develop a successful and sustainable strategy by using innovative business 
strategies, promoting sustainable practices, supporting employees, finding appropriate partnerships 
and using the right tools. The next chapter will explain the recommended tools and models in detail, 
by presenting a SME tailored framework. The framework demonstrates one approach to answer the 
research question: 
Which approaches can SMEs adopt to assess the sustainability effects of I4.0T prior to making 
investments and how can the development of guidelines aid their decision-making process?

4. Framework for SMEs 
This chapter presents a framework that can assist SMEs in their transition towards sustainability using 
I4.0T. The framework has been developed by combining the results of prescriptive and predictive 
analysis of the literature review. The framework takes a multidimensional approach and is connected 
to other tools. The six main drivers displayed in the framework are listed below. To round the 
framework off, the chapter describes additional models and tools that can support the transition 
process.  
4.1 Framework 
The findings of this thesis are visualized in Figure 1, providing a framework for SMEs to achieve 
sustainable development by enabling I4.0. The framework consists of a central organizational structure 
including key topics for internal considerations. Further supporting linkages are included in tables 
(T1(table2), T2(table12)), models (M1, M2(figure3), M3(figure4)), and drivers (D1-D5) for in additional 
input concerning the topic. The graph illustrates the connection via vertical and horizontal integration. 
Vertical integration is directed to research associations and universities, whereas horizontal integration 
connects the business with customers and other supply chain members. SMEs can identify their area 
of interest and follow the reference to acquire further knowledge. To not repeat knowledge, that has 
already been derived, references to previous chapters are included.

Furthermore, this thesis provides an exemplary guideline on how to apply the framework: 
The very first step should be a SWOT analysis (M1) to assess the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, 
and Threats of the given SME. This helps to lay the foundation for further planning. The primary goal 
should focus on knowledge acquisition, to form an overview of the individual situation. SMEs vary in 
their readiness to adapt to I4.0. The Maturity Model (M2) is tailored to SME's characteristics and builds 
on the SWOT analysis. In this step, the status regarding readiness towards digitalization is in focus. M2 
includes a level 0 readiness, to better suit the circumstances of SMEs. Thus, M2 can identify early on 
what the basic needs for deriving level 1 maturity of I4.0 are and offer guidance to reach level 1. Moving 
==PAGE:58==
from level 0 to level 1 will already require a substantial effort, due to a lack of resources and 
infrastructure. Still focusing on collection know-how should include understanding I4.0- technologies 
and concepts, key enablers and barriers of I4.0T, sustainability aspects and how those concepts apply 
to SMEs particularly. This thesis provides a holistic analysis of the topics through literature analysis to 
help derive a good overview of I4.0T and sustainable aspects. T1 explains major I4.0T and associates 
critical factors and impacts, enabling SMEs to choose appropriate technologies based on their 
requirements. In addition, T2 summarizes the main arguments of the literature review by connecting 
key barriers and enablers for SMEs. Internal preparations include informing and educating employees, 
so they are prepared for changes and understand the necessity of them. For instance, bonuses and 
promotions related to I4.0 skills can encourage the process. More detailed recommendations can be 
found in the chapter: SME focused enabling key considerations. Also, D3 summarizes key aspects for 
considering employees in planning. Conclusively, investing in know-how is the first- and most important 
step in laying the foundation for further action.  
This knowledge helps to formulate a strategy, tailored to the maturity level and resources of the SME. 
The product and process driver (D2) and the smart production driver (D4) provide relevant 
considerations of the manufacturing side. Understanding the concept of digitalization benefits better 
strategy planning and promotion of I4.0 internally. Promoting sustainable practices and using 
innovative and tailored business strategies are identified as key targets. 
The framework highlights the connectivity of SMEs. Communicating and exchanging data, through 
horizontal and vertical integrations, can profit I4.0- preparation and implementation. The previous 
chapter emphasized the importance of collaborations with research institutions, stakeholders, 
customers and the supply chain and provided strategies to develop partnerships. The last driver D5, 
collects key factors for suppliers to support the strategy formulation. The key benefits are an increased 
knowledge transfer and a more significant role as a supplier to stay competitive. Furthermore, sensors 
are generally essential for data acquisition and to reap the benefits of BDA. For SMEs, they represent a 
first simple investment, as they are cheap and build the basis for digitalization technology.   
The acquisition of knowledge helps justify strategic decisions and can prevent wrong initial 
investments. A good overview works as a foundation for any further actions.  
SMEs must form a tailored vision and strategy to define their personal roadmap towards I4.0. The 
sustainable aspects should be considered throughout the whole process of planning and executing. 
The literature review provides key thoughts concerning several aspects of sustainability. Only then can 
the implementation of I4.0T benefit sustainability. Finally, the Rami model (M3) provides a roadmap 
toward I4.0 that considers all stakeholders and communication, facilitating process tracking, which can 
be used throughout the whole process.

The following models can be adapted to SME characteristics. Further information can be derived from

Three models to further investigate are: DREAMY (Digital REadiness Assessment MaturitY) model,

SWOT (M1) 
The SWOT analysis is one approach to recognize and evaluate internal strengths and weaknesses, as 
well as external opportunities and threats, that impact present and future operations and aid in 
formulating strategic objectives (Kumar et al. 2022).  
Maturity Model (M2) 
According to a pre-analysis, it is important to consider the relevance and potential for improvement of 
a specific case, as well as the environmental impact during operation and potential savings. It is also 
necessary to determine the most efficient and effective setup for the given case, viewing the possible 
multiple uses of ICT components for several applications. The SME Maturity Model recommends 
demonstrating an organization's readiness towards I4.0 through activities performed in the SME, 
allowing the creation of its own tailored vision of I4.0 and guidance along the process. For example, an 
SME currently at "level 0" may need to adopt a more reliable and accessible data storage system, such 
as a cloud-based system, to move towards an SM reality. (Thiede 2021, p. 5; Mittal et al. 2019) 
Figure 3 outlines various maturity dimensions for creating a maturity model. Companies can use it to 
pinpoint specific areas for assessing their maturity level. An honest evaluation of these maturity items 
helps to determine the level of maturity for digitalization.

SaaS 
The Development of service infrastructures offers computational services for SMEs, which utilize 
service-oriented architecture (SOA) based software and applications to support cross-company 
business processes and collaborative networks. By adopting SaaS on top of SOA, SMEs can select and 
integrate necessary software components for collaboration. This empowers SMEs with flexibility, agility 
and innovation capabilities (Wisner et al. 2014). Cloud computing extends SaaS by providing both 
hardware and software on a pay-as-you-go basis, which reduces the need for a large upfront 
investment for SMEs. SOA-based software applications can help SMEs transform into smart businesses 
and enable them to create or join collaborative networks. (Kagermann 2015) 
RAMI4.0 (M3) 
Figure Y depicts the Reference Architectural Model I4.0 (RAMI 4.0), which serves as a foundational 
framework for digitalization in the industrial context. RAMI 4.0 adopts a three-dimensional approach 
to organize and present the key aspects of the I4.0 architecture. Three categories define the axes: 
1. Architecture Layers (in the figure ‘Layers’): This dimension consists of six facets of business 
operations, including the organization of business processes, the desired functionality of assets, data 
output, communication technologies for data generation and processing and the interconnection of 
assets with digitally controlled processes. 
==PAGE:64==
2. Life Cycle Value Stream: This axis reflects the different stages of a product's life cycle, primarily 
focusing on the development and usage phases. The asset, production or construction and 
maintenance plans are established during development. The usage phase involves production, asset 
identification with serial numbers and maintenance and service activities. 
3. Hierarchy Levels: This axis illustrates the hierarchical structure of the I4.0 network. As the networks 
may consist of diverse entities like production plants, machines and products from different companies, 
a standardized hierarchical structure facilitates seamless communication across the network. 
(Plattform Industrie 4.0 2018)

5. Conclusion 
This thesis covers the basic elements of I4.0 and sustainability thoroughly, exploring the various aspects 
of each element. The literature review revealed the complexity of assessing sustainability, due to the 
need of clearer measurements. While many studies focus on organizational sustainability, few consider 
the broader societal and ecological sustainability aspects of I4.0. This complexity highlights the need of 
a comprehensive analysis, to understand the interactional relationship between I4.0 and sustainability. 
Additionally, most studies on I4.0 implementation and sustainable benefits do not cover the position 
of SMEs. There is a huge demand for additional guidelines and information, to implement I4.0 and 
sustainability at SMEs, to achieve the benefits out it. This research aims to bridge the identified 
research gaps.  
The thesis contributes by collecting and sorting existing research and evaluating the results, starting 
with focus on MNEs and secondly pointing out the characteristics for SMEs. The sustainability impact 
of I4.0 was critically assessed by breaking down relevant aspects. By tailoring the results to the 
==PAGE:65==
characteristics of SMEs, a solid guideline to implement I4.0 at SMEs was worked out. The research 
results in the following key statements are: 
1. SMEs should assess their readiness and determine the necessity of pursuing I4.0 before 
implementation. Planning and understanding the interaction of I4.0T and processes is essential. 
2. Tailoring strategies to the maturity and resources of the SME is important. To promote sustainability 
within the company is essential, including employee training and convincing them of the usability and 
benefits of I4.0T. 
3. Exploring simpler digitalization methods, starting with sensors as foundation for further digitization 
and gradually progressing step by step, can be a more practical approach before investing in advanced 
I4.0T.   
4. Assessing the sustainability impact of I4.0T can be challenging. But also, the need for clarity in 
evaluating sustainability contributions makes it difficult to adopt these technologies strategically. 
Therefore, a comprehensive analysis is needed to understand the relationship between I4.0 and 
sustainability, considering organizational and broader societal aspects. 
5. Collaboration with research institutions, stakeholders, customers and the supply chain is crucial to 
acquire knowledge and build partnerships, which helps SMEs in their digitization journey. 
6. Helpful external guidelines and government regulations are needed to enable SMEs to keep up with 
larger enterprises moving towards I4.0. This includes guidelines for data exchange and the 
implementation of network facilities and automation capabilities. 
Overall, the thesis emphasizes the importance of careful assessment, planning, and collaboration for 
SMEs to successfully adopt and benefit from I4.0T, while considering sustainability. In a next step the 
thesis pointed out an SME-tailored framework to further elaborate on these findings. 
A framework model was developed to address the research question and identify key factors for SMEs 
to transition to digitization successfully. This framework guides companies to identify potential 
enablers and barriers, allowing them to focus on the essentials and avoid unnecessary hurdles. 
However, it is important to note that the framework is not a guaranteed solution but a helpful overview 
of strategies, to overcome barriers and achieve positive outcomes. SMEs are advised to tailor their 
approach to digitalization based on their unique characteristics. 
While literature sources were used to develop the framework, empirical data and real-life cases could 
enhance the study and provide a more practical model with clearer recommendations. It is suggested 
that companies take advantage of I4.0T to improve their sustainability efforts. However, careful 
evaluation and prioritization are needed to ensure their successful integration and maximum positive 
impact on sustainability. 
To successfully implement the framework, it is recommended that SMEs assess their readiness and 
explore other adaptation methods, if necessary. Future studies should focus on gathering more 
empirical evidence and data to formulate a realistic guideline. In addition, it is crucial to understand 
the overall environmental impact of I4.0 and validate the proposed theories and concepts through 
simulations, prototypes, experiments, and case studies. Local showcases can inspire policymakers to 
develop appropriate policies to promote the adoption of I4.0 and to encourage research and 
innovation.
