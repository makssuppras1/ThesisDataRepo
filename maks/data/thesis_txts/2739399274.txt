Introduction 
Throughout human history, the activity that we today call entrepreneurship, have been one 
of the main drivers of progress. From the first human inventing flint tools to today’s 
advancements in technology, certain intents and behaviors are likely to have been present in 
the people involved in the innovation, creation, and dissemination of new inventions. 
However, it is only within the recent 70 years that social sciences have investigated this field 
of human behavior outside the realm of mere profits or outcomes and has regarded the 
concept of motivation in widely different ways, and within the last 20 years, neuroscience has 
been the most promising area of study to better understand the basis of entrepreneurship. 
For entrepreneurs, maintaining a persistent level of motivation is paramount to pursuing goals 
and realize strategies towards desired outcomes in new ventures. Persistent actions need to 
be undertaken during the life cycle of the startup or venture, but every so often, 
entrepreneurs find themselves running out of steam or hitting the proverbial wall. It appears 
that levels of motivation can diminish over time. This can be thought of as somewhat of a 
paradox; how can successful ventures demotivate its actors? 
On a societal level, it could also be considered how inherited cultural traditions might 
influence the way we think we ought to act in business life with disregard to actual basic 
human nature. Considering the old adage “the journey is more important than the destination” 
seem to be much better supported by findings in neuroscience, we might start to see patterns 
emerging that can further our understanding of motivation, and hopefully start to implement 
them across all sectors - including entrepreneurship – leading to better outcomes and more 
satisfying endeavors.

Literature Review 
The desire to understand what it is that drives us to perform tasks and accomplish goals has 
long been the focus of study. For a considerable time, we came to understand this process 
through the lens of different schools of philosophy and can for brevity in the post-industrial 
world be condensed into “if you do more of that, you get more of this”.  
However, beginning in earnest with Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs (1943) it began to become 
more apparent that there are other factors at stake than mere punishment and/or reward, 
and was further explained and refined with Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory (1959) that took 
into consideration the inner drive towards achieving goals and completing tasks, and how 
external factors such as rewards only built towards the establishment of a new homeostasis. 
Herzberg separated into Hygiene (reducing dissatisfaction) and Motivation (induce 
satisfaction) and today, Pink (2011) has aggregated much of the contemporary findings into a 
workable model that has gained a lot of traction. Within the recent decades, the field of 
neuroscience, genetics, and endocrinology has been implemented in various ways in the 
study of motivation and entrepreneurship. In this thesis, however, we will focus on 
neuroscience and the neurotransmitter dopamine and how it may relate to neuroreceptors 
within the field of entrepreneurship. 
Entrepreneurial Motivations 
In the literature review, we will divide the data in two parts. Firstly, we briefly investigate 
McClelland’s 6 entrepreneurial motivations and Stephan et al’s 7 dimensions, Herzberg’s two-
factor theory, and Daniel Pink’s findings aggregated in Drive (2011). Following this, we will 
propose a correlation between the entrepreneurial motivations (understood as a personal 
consideration/decision) and how they relate to current understanding of intrinsic and 
extrinsic motivation. Lastly, we will devote the bulk of this section to the second part that 
explores neuroscientific findings and theories. 
McCelland’s Entrepreneurial Motivations 
Need for Achievement 
In the current research, Need for Achievement (nAch) as a personality trait have garnered a 
lot of attention. McClelland (1961) has argued that people who who express a greater degree 
of nAch are much more likely to engage in entrepreneurial activities that involves higher 
levels of individual responsibility for outcomes, requires skill and effort, moderate risk levels, 
and includes clearer feedback on performance, than those who do not. McClellan further 
argues that entrepreneurial roles are characterized as having a higher degree of task 
attributes than other careers. This appears to make people high in nAch more likely to engage 
in entrepreneurial activities than those that do not.

However, Hansemark (2003) has found in an 11-year longitudinal study that there does not 
appear to be any clear correlation between nArch and entrepreneurial activity, ostensibly due 
to the fact that changes in social contexts can also change the entrepreneurial activity and 
therefore not necessarily as a predictor of it. Rather, Locus of Control of Reinforcement 
appears to serve as a predictor, which we will discuss in a later section. 
Risk Taking 
Another motivation of interest stemming from McClelland’s work is the propensity towards 
risk taking, where McClelland claims that a high level of nArch necessitates a moderate 
propensity toward risk as entrepreneurs are often acting in the face of uncertainty, such as 
financial well-being, psychic/emotional well-being, career safety, and relationships. 
Interestingly, successful founders appear to gravitate to intermediate to low risk scenarios, 
but does not differ from managers, but does so in comparison with the general population. 
Furthermore, Sarasvathy, Simon, and Lave (1998) found that when experienced 
entrepreneurs and bankers are faced with uncertainty, entrepreneurs tend to find 
opportunities in the data, where the banker would merely identify risk (and likely attempt to 
avoid or mitigate).  
Note: It is possible that risk taking propensity should be measured and confounded with self-
efficacy, as taking risk also involves the possibility of personal or professional growth. 
Tolerance for Ambiguity 
Since the successful outcome for a startup or business venture by nature is unpredictable, 
tolerance for ambiguity becomes an important trait for entrepreneurial motivation. By 
Budner’s (1982) definition, tolerance for ambiguity can be seen as the propensity to regard 
situations without clear outcomes as attractive rather than threatening. Because of this, 
entrepreneurs tend to score higher for this trait than managers according to several sources. 
E.g. Sexton and Bowman (1986) identifies tolerance for ambiguity as the defining 
psychological characteristic for entrepreneurs in comparison to managers.  
However, some studies find no significant differences, such as Babb and Babb (1992) and 
Begley (1995). The inconsistency in these findings suggest that we do not know if this trait 
bears any significant influence on entrepreneurial motivation or activity. 
Locus of Control of Reinforcement 
Another entrepreneurial trait is the belief that both external and internal characteristics can 
influence outcomes through actions or reactions. The external can be regarded as events and 
conditions that have little bearing on the outcome meaning that it is outside the control of the 
entrepreneur, but a means of “moving” rather than “motivating”1. Conversely, the internal

People high in nArch prefer situations where they can directly observe the results based on

Self-Efficacy 
Self-efficacy is the belief in one’s ability to muster and implement the necessary personal 
resources, skills, and competencies to attain a certain level of achievement on a given task 
(Bandura, 1997).  
In this regard, self-efficacy can be seen as a form of task-specific self-confidence. Task-
based self-efficacy has been shown to be an excellent predictor of individual performance in 
a given task and can help explain why people with comparable abilities and skills can perform 
very differently when engaging in identical tasks. Individuals with high self-efficacy for any 
given task will be able to exert greater effort for a substantially longer period of time, persist 
in face of setbacks and failures, set and accept higher goals, and be able to construct better 
strategies and plans. Also, they will be able to handle negative feedback, and be more adept 
in discovering the “silver lining” in the face of adversarial conditions. Given that

Goal Setting 
In their 1998 study, Tracy, Locke, and Renard delved into the dynamics of small printing firm 
owners. Utilizing concurrent and longitudinal measures, the study scrutinized four key 
performance aspects: financial, performance, growth, and innovation. The results 
demonstrated a noteworthy correlation between entrepreneurs' quantitative goals and the 
corresponding outcomes, both in the immediate and extended periods. Interestingly, the 
study uncovered an unrelated relationship between the need for achievement (nAch) and 
performance. Extending this line of inquiry, Baum, Locke, and Smith (2001) found a parallel 
connection, highlighting the significant link between growth goals and subsequent growth in 
architectural woodworking firms. Despite the multitude of studies on entrepreneurial goals,

The Stephan, Hart, and Drewes’ 7 Dimensions 
In their review article “Understanding Motivations for Entrepreneurship - A Review of Recent 
Research Evidence” (2015), Stephan, Hart, and Drews reviewed 51 relevant studies looking 
into the motivations for engaging in entrepreneurial activity. Although there are overlap with 
McClelland, there are some variations that need to be considered in our understanding of 
what underpins the personal motivations of entrepreneurs, how internal and external factors 
can influence decision making, and to which extent government involvement can affect and 
direct decisions. 
 
The following details the emergence of characteristics found within this review, separated 
into 7 dimensions: 
Achievement, challenge & learning 
This dimension represents the pursuit of personal development within the context of 
entrepreneurship. It involves elements such as engaging in meaningful work, accepting 
responsibilities, and gaining insights from the challenges associated with creating and 
==PAGE:9==
Lars Egholm Fischmann, DTU, 2024 
 
9 
 
managing a business. Moreover, it incorporates aspects of self-actualization, including the 
realization of one's individual vision and goals. 
Independence & autonomy 
This dimension emphasizes the entrepreneurial motivation to achieve mastery over one's 
occupational life, characterized by control over personal time and tasks, the freedom to make 
autonomous decisions, and the flexibility to balance professional responsibilities with 
personal life. 
Income security & financial success 
This dimension highlights the pivotal role of financial returns in entrepreneurship. 
Interestingly, motives associated with income security and financial success are often 
intertwined, contrary to the expectations set by opportunity-necessity motivation studies. 
Studies measuring both aspects often consolidate them into a singular dimension. There is a 
notable trend of merging financial success with family financial security, especially in 
research involving entrepreneurs from economically disadvantaged regions within 
developed nations and in developing countries. This trend may reflect the impact of cultural 
factors, including the pronounced significance of family in collectivist cultures and 
subcultures. 
Recognition & status 
This dimension reflects elements related to social standing, highlighting the desire to achieve 
acknowledgment and esteem from peers, family members, and the wider societal context 
for one's work as an entrepreneur. 
Family & Roles 
This dimension encapsulates the aspiration to perpetuate a family tradition and emulate the 
example set by various role models, which are typically not further delineated in the studies. 
In some instances, this dimension was in some cases highlighted as the importance of 
establishing a family legacy. 
Dissatisfaction 
This dimension pertains to entrepreneurial motivation driven by discontent with former work 
situations. Accordingly, it exhibits parallels to necessity motivation, a factor that is infrequently 
explicitly considered in motivational studies. 
Community & social motivations 
This dimension encompasses the entrepreneur's aspiration to give back to their community, 
either through philanthropic efforts or via their business endeavors, exemplified by social 
entrepreneurship. Additionally, it includes considerations such as employee welfare and 
maintaining environmentally sustainable business practices.

Motivational Theory 
Motivation, as it is understood today, is a critical component that dictates an individual’s effort 
to attain or complete a set goal by the activation of the intent or physical movement toward 
(approach) or away (withdraw) from a given situation, consequence or outcome. Most, if not 
all, modern theory of motivation distinguishes between extrinsic and intrinsic factors (Ryan 
and Deci, 2000), where extrinsic motivation can be understood as the drive to act or behave 
in a certain way with the expectation of achieving or experiencing external consequence (eg. 
reward, acknowledgement, recognition, etc.), while intrinsic motivation is focused on interest 
or inherent satisfaction for a given activity (Di Domenico and Ryan, 2017). Intrinsic motivation 
is therefore an inherent tendency or characteristic to seek out novelty and challenge, and 
explore and investigate, in order to expand and extend one's capacities beyond the status 
quo. 
The distinction made between intrinsic motivation and extrinsic motivation is often 
investigated in their relationship to an individual’s overall values and general well-being (Deci 
et al., 1999; Ryan and Deci, 2000; Deci et al., 2001; Deci and Ryan, 2008). Intrinsic motivation is 
enhanced when individuals can identify with the importance and value of the activity they are 
attempting to accomplish (Deci et al., 2017). Such states may promote growth, social 
development, and well-being (Ryan and Deci, 2000; Ryan and Deci, 2017). Intrinsically 
motivated states may also enhance goal accomplishment, as intrinsically motivated activities 
lead to less physical burnout, emotional exhaustion, and make individuals feel like they 
perform better.  
 
Maslow 
Within the realms of business, management, and leadership literature, one of the most 
influential figures in understanding the needs of individuals in organizations is Abraham 
Maslow, renowned for his seminal “Hierarchy of Needs” theory introduced and expanded on 
beginning in 1943. Maslow's theory delineates a hierarchy of factors critical to human 
organizational wellbeing, often represented as a pyramid. Originally, Maslow identified five 
tiers: physiological, safety, love and belongingness, esteem, and self-actualization. However, 
he later introduced a sixth tier, self-transcendence, recognizing that an overemphasis on 
individualism and financial gain led to ethical dilemmas and detracted from collective 
welfare. 
 
Over the years, a restrictive and narrowly interpreted understanding of Maslow's hierarchy of 
needs has become deeply rooted, unduly prioritizing self-actualization as the foremost 
aspect of motivation. This constrained viewpoint has led to a skewed conception of success, 
heavily reliant on financial achievements and material rewards. Consequently, organizations 
focused solely on profit are at risk of encouraging employees to engage in self-serving and 
==PAGE:11==
Lars Egholm Fischmann, DTU, 2024 
 
11 
 
ethically dubious behaviors, often resulting in the manipulation of rules and regulations. 
(Venter, 2010, 2012, 2016). 
 
Given the profound and lasting impact of Maslow’s work on motivational theory, as well as its 
inadvertent negative consequences on the understanding of motivation, this thesis 
incorporates this brief examination of Maslow’s hierarchy of needs. The aim is to 
recontextualize and realign the theory with its original intent, thereby addressing the 
misconceptions and misapplications that is still inherent in some company cultures. 
 
Herzberg 
As a contemporary of Maslow, Frederick Herzberg was a psychologist who also studied the 
field of motivation. In his published work starting in 1959, Herzberg introduced a two-factor 
model of motivation, looking into intrinsic and extrinsic factors that he branded as ‘motivation’ 
and ‘hygiene’, and in order to sustain motivation in an actor in an organization, hygiene criteria 
must be met in order to prevent dissatisfaction, while motivational factors are to be met in 
order to promote satisfaction. Thus, a hygiene factor cannot be the cause of satisfaction (e.g. 
salary, bonus, benefits, status, etc.) but will be the causal link of dissatisfaction and create 
reduced levels of motivation if needs are not met or taken away. In other words, hygiene is 
what is required to maintain a neutral motivational baseline of an individual but does not 
construe a source of satisfaction and will only have short-term effects on motivation and job 
performance. Conversely, he found from his research data, that achievement, recognition, the 
work itself, responsibility, and advancement where strong determinants of job satisfaction 
leading to long-term positive effects on job performance. 
Pink 
In his seminal work “Drive: The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us” (2011), Daniel H 
Pink has built upon the preceding decades of research in the field of motivational psychology 
and has condensed the field into three segments. In this regard, Maslow and Herzberg are 
considered to be the originators of this field of study and that Pink’s model was conceived 
within the field of managerial research. 
Autonomy – The desire to direct our own lives 
Daniel Pink posits that autonomy within the workplace serves as a significant motivational 
factor, contributing to enhanced job satisfaction. When employees are granted greater 
control over their work processes and assignments, it can lead to improved performance 
outcomes. Autonomy in this context can be operationalized through various means, such as 
allowing employees to manage their work schedules, empowering them to determine the 
most suitable methods for problem-solving (while still providing supervisory guidance), and 
enabling them to form and lead their own teams. Such measures are likely to foster a more 
engaged and productive workforce. 
==PAGE:12==
Lars Egholm Fischmann, DTU, 2024 
 
12 
 
Mastery – The urge to get better and better at something that matters 
Mastery refers to the intrinsic human drive for continuous development and personal growth. 
To facilitate mastery within an organizational context, it is essential for company leadership 
to assign tasks that are appropriately challenging. These tasks are designed to be neither 
overly simplistic nor excessively difficult, but rather to present manageable challenges that 
progressively enhance employees’ skill levels and competencies, thereby fostering their 
ongoing development. 
Purpose – The yearning to do what we do in the service of something larger than 
ourselves 
In Daniel Pink’s framework, purpose represents the pinnacle of motivation, where employees 
can align themselves with a cause greater than their individual interests. This connection to a 
higher purpose fosters a deep sense of meaning and fulfillment in their work. To enhance this 
sense of purpose, organizations should integrate employees more deeply into the company 
culture and clearly communicate the broader organizational objectives, beyond mere 
financial goals. By doing so, employees are more likely to develop a strong sense of purpose, 
which can significantly enhance their motivation and commitment to the organization.

Partial Conclusion on Motivational Theory 
As we have discussed in this chapter, the theory of what motivates us and what our 
motivations are for choosing entrepreneurship has long been the focus of study in the social 
sciences. As years of research has added up to deepen our understandings, it is possible to 
arrive at conclusions with a higher degree of confidence about what cognitive mechanisms 
that are in effect, and furthermore also how to guide employers and entrepreneurs to 
enhance and cultivate motivation in themselves and their organizations.  
Organizational psychology together with behavioral science, has provided us with a lot of 
actionable answers, though as we have also discussed, there is still a disconnect in some 
instances of how to apply the models in the way they were intended. 
However, to further broaden our understanding and solidify the findings, observations and 
experimentation, traditional motivational theory is insufficient to gain a deeper and more 
complete understanding of why the findings are meaningful. In this regard, the field of

neuroscience has started to examine the significance that neural pathways and the structures 
of the brain have on motivation. It is a burgeoning field of study, and despite that research is 
ongoing and still in its infancy, it has become possible to use findings to underpin theory.  
In the following chapter, we will take a closer look at a selection of neuroscientific literature 
that could be applicable in this regard. 
Neuroscientific Basis for Motivation 
In “The Emerging Neuroscience of Intrinsic Motivation: A New Frontier in Self-Determination 
Research” (2017) Di Dominico & Ryan completed a literature review of a selection of significant 
sources from both past and recent findings in intrinsic motivational research related to 
neuroscientific findings. In the review they conflate these sources to build two propositions 
based on the evidence found. In both, they found dopamine to be a significant substrate. 
There are three lines of evidence that points to the importance of dopamine as a key 
substrate of intrinsic motivation: 
● In humans, intrinsic motivation is an elaboration of the SEEKING system (see below), 
and dopamine is the main actor in the neurochemistry of it.  
● Dopamine has been observed in association with an increase in positive affect, 
cognitive flexibility, creativity, behavioral persistence, and exploration of novelties. It is 
important to mention that positively affected states in conjunction with dopamine is 
connected to “wanting” rather than “liking”. 
● There is some direct evidence of the connection between dopamine and intrinsic 
motivation, where PET scans have shown that people with greater dopamine D2 
receptor availability is much more likely to experience intrinsically motivated flow 
states.  
These three observations make a reasonable case for proposing that dopamine might be a 
key substrate of intrinsic motivation. 
The SEEKING System 
Within the concept of the affective neuroethological perspective, researchers have argued 
that all mammals are hardwired with general-purpose SEEKING system that provides energy 
and incentives to a variety of exploratory activities. While it does service homeostatic 
imbalances such as energizing learned appetitive behaviors it still provides a motivation in 
animals to seek exploratory engagement with their respective environments. So even though 
it is an objectless system that encourages exploration, the direction it causes is to discovery 
and useful regularities. 
 
==PAGE:15==
Lars Egholm Fischmann, DTU, 2024 
 
15 
 
Panksepp and Biven defines it as: 
The SEEKING system is a spontaneous, unconditional behavior generator that takes 
animals to places, actively and inquisitively, where associated learning mechanisms 
allow them to develop knowledge structures, to guide their foremost evolutionary action 
tools (inbuilt emotional systems) to create more structures - more higher mental 
processes - which facilitate survival (Panksepp and Biven, 2012, pg. 135) 
The core neural structures that make up the SEEKING system in rats (and by extension 
humans), are the ventral tegmental area (VTA), the nucleus accumbens (NAcc), the 
ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vPFC), and the dopaminergic projections that originate from 
the VTA. These regions are often colloquially referred to as the “brain reward network”. In 
more sophisticated animals, such as humans, the urges stemming from the SEEKING system 
allows us to conduct more advanced manipulations and explorations of complex objects, and 
our cognition provides us with the opportunity to form ideas from abstract objects and 
concepts, opening up the possibility to manipulate them with our minds. It is thus believed to 
energize “many mental complexities that humans experience as persistent feelings of interest, 
curiosity, sensation seeking, and, in the presence of a sufficiently complex cortex, the search for 
higher meaning” (Panksepp, 1998, pg. 145). 
Tonic and phasic modes 
Dopamine neurons originate in the midbrain region and serves two modes of activity; tonic 
and phasic (Grace, 1991). While in tonic mode, the neurons express a steady and stable 
baseline, directing dopamine to the relevant target structures. This type of activity promotes 
normal functioning of neural circuits (Schulz, 2007) and is believed to be reflected in strong 
exploratory behaviors in animals originating from their SEEKING system (Alcaro and 
Panksepp, 2011). In the phasic mode, neurons respond to specific events causing either an 
increase or decrease of dopamine (above or below baseline) in the target structures. These 
phasic bursts can last up to several seconds. The phasic mode of dopamine transmission may 
“transiently activate SEEKING patterns in coincidence with specific cue- or context-dependent 
information, attribution to such information and incentive motivational, action-oriented effect” 
(Alcaro and Panksepp, 2011, p. 1810). It is likely that both tonic and phasic modes interact in 
complex ways to regulate intrinsic motivation. 
Alcaro et al (2007) advanced the hypothesis that moderately high levels of dopamine 
optimize the SEEKING behavior promoted by phasic dopamine release, and that when tonic 
levels of dopamine are too low, phasic signals lack the efficacy to promote exploration. But 
when tonic levels are too high, phasic signals lose their informational value and exploratory 
behavior patterns are uncoupled from relevant contextual stimuli.

Value-coding and salience-coding neurons 
Value-coding (VC) neurons are phasically excited by unexpected rewarding events and 
inhibited by unexpected aversive events; events that are wholly expected elicit little to no 
response. VC neurons are found in the ventromedial substantia nigra pars compacta (SN) and 
throughout the VTA. From these midbrain regions, these neurons project axons that innervate 
the NAcc shell, the dorsal striatum (caudate and putamen), and the vPFC, where they send 
signals about the availability of rewards, evaluation of outcomes, and learning. The phasic 
signals emitted by VC neurons are classically recognized as “reward-prediction errors” within 
neurobehaviorist theories and are believed to be an important mechanism through which 
animals learn about external reinforcement contingencies (Schultz, 2007). Di Domenico and 
Ryan proposes that “a high rate of dopaminergic signaling within the value system is inherent to 
the performance of intrinsically motivating activities.” 
 
In addition to the VC neurons, Bromberg-Martin et al. (2010) identified salience-coding 
neurons (SC). These dopamine neurons are phasically excited by both unexpected rewarding 
and punishing events. The neurons are found in the dorsolateral SN and medial VTA, and 
project to the NAcc core, the dorsal striatum, and the dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC). The regions 
innervated by SC neurons support the orientating of attention, cognitive processing, and the 
invigoration of actions. In relation to this, Di Domenico and Ryan proposes that “the salience-
coding system also subserves intrinsic motivation.” 
 
Sharma 
In their publication “Neuroentrepreneurship: an integrative review and research agenda” from 
2021, Sharma et al reviewed 167 papers examining the interlinkages between neuroscience 
and entrepreneurial intention through six antecedents: molecular neuroscience, systems 
neuroscience, behavioral neuroscience, cognitive neuroscience, social neuroscience, and 
computational neuroscience. 
Molecular Neuroscience 
Molecular neuroscience focuses on brain molecules, particularly neurotransmitters that 
facilitate neural network formation and information transfer (Bear, Connors & Paradiso, 2005). 
It aids in understanding cognitive neuroscience and the behavioral aspects of decision-
making, with genes influencing neuronal protein synthesis (Ramsøy and Skov, 2010). 
Research links genetic factors to entrepreneurial traits, with genetic variation accounting for 
differences in creativity and intention (Nicolaou and Shane, 2010; Yadav and Bansal, 2021). 
Genetic markers, such as serotonin and dopamine polymorphisms, are associated with risk-
taking behaviors, impacting entrepreneurial decisions (Beauchamp et al., 2011; Kuhnen, Chiao, 
and Harpending, 2009).

Systems Neuroscience 
Systems neuroscience explores the brain's neuronal circuits and their role in sensory input 
processing, perception, decision-making, and execution (Bear, Connors & Paradiso, 2005). 
This understanding is crucial for entrepreneurial decision-making (Krueger, 2003; Shane & 
Nicolaou, 2013; Zhong, 2018). While neuroimaging techniques like fMRI and EEG provide 
insights into entrepreneurial brain function, opinions on their use vary (de Holan, 2014; Tracey 
and Schluppeck, 2014). Zheng (2018) found that an EEG-based neurofeedback systems 
improves entrepreneurial innovation, linked to specific personality traits. Differences in brain 
structure among innovative individuals highlight the importance of brain plasticity in decision-
making (Erkut et al., 2018). 
Behavioural Neuroscience 
Behavioral neuroscience examines the biological underpinnings of behavior and the neural 
networks supporting complex behaviors (Bear, Connors & Paradiso, 2005). Zhang (2018) 
advises that businesses should focus on nurturing, directing, and maintaining goal-oriented 
behaviors for a positive impact. Research in entrepreneurship reveals that disorders like 
Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) significantly affect entrepreneurial behaviors 
both positively and negatively (Wiklund, Patzelt, and Dimov, 2016). Verheul et al. (2015) report 
that increased ADHD behaviors are positively associated with entrepreneurial intentions, with 
hyperactivity being particularly linked to self-employment (Verheul et al., 2016). Thus, an in-
depth analysis of ADHD is crucial for understanding entrepreneurial intentions. 
Cognitive Neuroscience 
Cognitive neuroscience examines the neural mechanisms behind higher mental activities 
(Bear, Connors & Paradiso, 2005), offering insights into entrepreneurial actions (Baron and 
Ward, 2004; Grégoire, Corbett, and McMullen, 2011; Randolph-Seng et al., 2015). 
Entrepreneurial experiences trigger neural activity in cortical and subcortical areas, leading 
to episodic experiences and affective valuations. This can be categorized into two sub-
themes: the Cognitive Behavioral Perspective and Cognitive Determinants. 
The cognitive behavioral approach addresses challenges by altering thought patterns to 
change behavior. Baron (2007) highlights the role of 'affect' in generating ideas, recognizing 
opportunities, and securing resources for ventures. Lortie and Castogiovanni (2015) relate the 
Theory of Planned Behaviour to entrepreneurship, explaining opportunity recognition and 
venture creation. Cognitive and discursive methods explain entrepreneurial behavior through 
individual and social cognition while phenomenology focuses on lived experiences 
(Berglund, 2015). Cognitive factors in corporate governance influence managerial behavior 
and financial discipline (Wirtz, 2011). Cognitive and behavioral impacts drive value creation 
and capability development in entrepreneurship (Pryor et al., 2016). 
The cognitive perspective of entrepreneurship aims to connect human cognition with 
entrepreneurial activities. Literature indicates that cognitive processes are prone to bias and 
==PAGE:18==
Lars Egholm Fischmann, DTU, 2024 
 
18 
 
error, leading to irrational decision-making. Entrepreneurs are particularly vulnerable to 
cognitive biases under conditions of information overload, uncertainty, novelty, emotion, and 
time pressure (Baron, 1998). Cognitive neuroscience can provide valuable insights into the 
motivations behind entrepreneurial decisions. Baron (2004) uses prospect theory to explain 
risk reduction and signal detection, and regulatory focus theory to demonstrate 
entrepreneurial alertness. 
Researchers can utilize cognitive tools such as reaction time, priming, working memory, and 
creative cognition measures to advance entrepreneurial cognitive research (Baron and Ward, 
2004). Ferreira et al. (2015) employ multiple criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to gauge 
entrepreneurial orientation and improve decisions on partnerships and funding. Grillo et al. 
(2018) use MCDA for cognitive mapping of innovative SMEs. Karayev et al. (2018) explore 
cognitive tools for dynamic strategy analysis in enterprises. Enterprise Cognitive Computing 
Applications (ECC) can optimize business processes, operational excellence, customer 
satisfaction, and employee experience (Tarafdar, Beath, and Ross, 2017). 
The integration of neuroscientific methods into entrepreneurial research offers a promising 
avenue to enrich scholarship in the field. By utilizing neuroscientific techniques, researchers 
can delve into the neural underpinnings of entrepreneurial intentions and behaviors, thereby 
contributing to a deeper understanding of entrepreneurship (Nofal et al., 2018). 
Social Neuroscience 
The interaction between environmental factors, such as social networks, and the cognitive 
biases of entrepreneurs, contributes significantly to entrepreneurial behavior (De Carolis and 
Saparito, 2006). Understanding the connection between cognitive aspects (counterfactual 
thinking, over-confidence, cognitive biases) and social psychology (social competence, 
perception, pursuance, adaptability) is essential for entrepreneurial success (Baron, 2000). 
Tran and Von Korflesch (2016) introduce a model linking personality traits, social 
entrepreneurial outcome expectations, and self-efficacy with social cognitive career theory 
(SCCT). Research indicates a positive association between specific personality traits like 
aesthetic interest and cognitive adaptability, and a negative association between self-
reproach and cognitive adaptability fit (Botha and Morallane, 2019). Liguori, Bendickson, and 
McDowell (2018) demonstrate the application of SCCT to environmental and personal inputs 
and their influence on self-efficacy and entrepreneurial outcomes. Randolph-Seng et al. 
(2015) present a framework for the dynamism of entrepreneurial cognition, and Sassetti et al. 
(2018) emphasize the endogenous nature of entrepreneurial action, advocating for its 
exploration within the cognitive domain. 
Computational Neuroscience 
Nordqvist (2012) characterizes computational neuroscience as the application of computers 
to model brain functions, using techniques from mathematics and physics. This field is 
applied in enterprises through genetic algorithms and genetic programming to optimize 
solutions (Whitley 2001; Zhang et al. 2013). These algorithms simulate evolutionary processes 
==PAGE:19==

19 
 
to improve problem-solving and partner selection in virtual enterprises (Wang, Xu, and Zhan 
2009). Genetic programming encodes computer programs as genes, adhering to Darwinian 
survival principles (Bian, Li, and Cong 1998). Therefore, effective partner selection via genetic 
algorithms and programming enhances entrepreneurial performance (Savoiu 2010). 
 
Dopamine Receptor D4 Genes 
Thirdly, we will investigate research data pertaining to the dopamine receptor DRD4 in 
relation to entrepreneurial motivation. Findings are not conclusive in this regard and is merely 
included to cast a glimpse on what current research looking for candidate genes has 
uncovered. 
 
In their study, Dreber et al (2010) have found, based on emergent literature looking at the 
potential role of genetic factors’ contribution, how individuals handle economic decision 
making. Molecular genetics and twin studies have investigated levels of altruism in various 
games, such as the ‘dictator game’, rejection in the ‘ultimatum game’, trust behavior in the 
‘trust game’, risk preferences, sensitivity to the framing effect, and behavioral aggression in 
economic games. 
 
The research suggests that genetic contributions to individual biological variations have 
substantial implications for economic and behavioral studies. Genetic inheritance is an 
important factor to consider when examining preference correlations between parents and 
offspring and in understanding the determinants of preferences. Although genetic factors do 
not entirely dictate behavior, as environmental influences are also significant, incorporating 
genetic components into economic models is likely to refine our understanding of behavior, 
thereby enhancing the models' explanatory and predictive power. 
 
The study focuses on the dopamine receptor gene DRD4, previously associated with risk 
preferences in economic behavior, though results have varied. Two studies demonstrated a 
positive link between the 7R+ genotype and economic risk-taking, one found a similar 
association with bridge (card game) risk-taking, another identified a negative link with 
economic risk-taking, and one found no significant association. 
While this cannot be construed as a link between the data and entrepreneurship to explain 
any causality to any satisfactory level, it is nonetheless relevant at this stage to be mindful of 
potential candidate genes in relation to the effect of dopamine receptor gene DRD4 and 
entrepreneurial motivation.

Benefits of Neuroscience on Entrepreneurship Research 
In ongoing entrepreneurial research there is a debate whether neuroscience is relevant to 
further our understanding of the field. Although most of the literature tends to favor applying 
a neuroscientific approach, proposing that it can further our understanding, critics, such as 
Tracey and Schluppeck (2014), question if neuroscience is even capable of understanding the 
processes that underpin cognitive functions such as decision making, as the processes are 
more complex and involves great uncertainties than what neuroscience may be able to 
explain to a satisfactory level. They liken the approach to “brain pornography”, indicating that 
novelty biases may influence the way researchers employ neuroscience in the field of 
entrepreneurship (personal interpretation). 
In their study “Exploring the potential and limits of a neuroscientific approach to 
entrepreneurship” Nicolaou et al (2019) critically examines the potential limitations and 
benefits of applying neuroscience to the study of entrepreneurship, though they align 
themselves to neuroscience being mostly beneficial. The concerns they raise are centered 
around the validity of reverse inference studies, that entrepreneurship is a social activity that 
is influenced by social constructs, events, changes in environment, a.s.o., and that in this 
context, neuroscience may not be able to explain the behaviors of entrepreneurs to a greater 
satisfactory level. Also, ethical and moral implications (neuroethics) should be kept in mind. 
However, their conclusion is that applying NS to the study of entrepreneurship is mostly 
beneficial and can provide valuable data for neuroscience in general as well, but that it is 
important to keep its limitations in mind. 
 
Summation 
Despite much past work examining the behavioral outcomes of intrinsic motivations, little 
work has investigated the neural correlates of expectancy as an intrinsic motivator (Di 
Domenico and Ryan, 2017) when compared to existing literature pertaining to motivation. Past 
work has primarily focused on intrinsic motivation and neural correlates tied to manipulations 
of autonomy, confederate manipulations, or conceptually different neural correlates of 
motivation (Murayama et al., 2010). 
More research on different forms of intrinsic motivation is still needed in order to further 
examine the neural correlates, but overarching the converging research appears to be 
suggesting that intrinsic motivation is linked to phylogenetic tendencies which are subserved 
by dopaminergic systems across large-scale neural networks, while, as others have 
suggested, there appears to be correlation only between dopamine receptor genes and 
entrepreneurial motivation.

Partial Conclusion 
Neuroscience is a field of research that is growing in volume, and the past decades have seen 
a rise in how it applies to entrepreneurship. Some have argued that there could be such a 
thing as an “entrepreneurial gene”, though as shown, there is insufficient data to suggest such 
a link with any degree of confidence.  
The findings from research stemming from social sciences, and the emerging data coming 
from neuroscience does indicate a possible causal link between dopamine activation and 
maintenance for sustaining stable levels of motivation for entrepreneurs coupled with the 
personal motivations for the entrepreneur, which we covered in a previous chapter. 
However, given its level of maturity, it can be very difficult to make any direct conclusions of 
exactly how neuroscience explains the precise mechanisms of neural functioning in 
entrepreneurs. There is ongoing debate on findings, e.g. several candidates for genetic 
variations (in relation to neuronal development and functioning) are inconclusive2. 
What does appear possible to argue at some level of confidence is the connection between 
Pink’s three areas that relate to autonomy, mastery, and purpose, since actions and behaviors 
tied to these are mostly consistent with findings in NS, most notably dopaminergic triggers 
(phasic and tonic) and the SEEKING system.

Delimitations 
It is outside the scope of the thesis to focus in greater detail on the effects of pro-social 
motivation (such as the effects of group dynamics and sense of commitment to team mates), 
despite having a tremendous significance and outcome of motivated activity in the team and 
organizational activity and processes. This is due to the fact that the scope of the thesis places 
emphasis on which processes, both psychologically and neurologically, that are activated 
and plays out in significant ways in the individual actor in the startup. 
While the antecedents from Sharma; molecular neuroscience, systems neuroscience, 
behavioral neuroscience, cognitive neuroscience, social neuroscience, and computational 
neuroscience applies to different aspects of how neuroscience can be utilized. For this 
reason, only molecular, systems, behavioral, and cognitive neuroscience will be considered 
for the analysis.  
We will disregard any considerations about the different external societal and socio-
economic conditions and pressures that may or may not also encourage need or desire for 
engaging in entrepreneurial activities especially subsistence motivations. Motivations 
stemming from dissatisfactions will still be included. 
While there are at least 6 major components of studying classic motivations within the 
context of entrepreneurship (need for achievement, risk taking, tolerance of ambiguity, locus 
of control, self-efficacy, and goal setting) we will utilize these in the manner to account for 
how they may disseminate into intrinsic and extrinsic motivation groups, but also look into 
these traits to a lesser degree, as all traits are of significance to better understand 
psychological artifacts and how they might correlate to neurological processes. 
It must also be considered that a large part of the neurological theory used stems from 
research into the neurological processes present in people with learning disabilities, 
neurodivergence, autism spectrum disorders, and both pre- and post-natal brain damage. 
However, as we will also discuss, many of these findings are being transferred into more 
mainstream psychology, sociology, and anthropology, as it helps to better understand and 
explain certain behaviors, personality traits, and their root causes embedded within certain 
brain regions and both neural activity and release of neurotransmitters in more neurotypical 
individuals. Though briefly touched upon the aspect of neurodivergence will not play any 
significant part of the analysis, except in the case of one respondent There will be no major 
emphasis on the overall assessment in this regard.

may be necessity-driven, it can also be considered opportunity-driven under the right 
circumstances. 
 
For some entrepreneurs, dissatisfaction can be aimed at a greater systemic problem that

Neuroscience 
In this section neuroscience as described in the literature review will be considered and 
analyzed in context of the responses given by the interviewees. Throughout, references are 
made to previous segments and statements given in the first two sections of the analysis. 
 
Most prominently, evaluating for behaviors and actions that can be related to the SEEKING 
system, which is the structure of the brain that prompts exploratory and novelty seeking 
behaviors, combined with potential indicators of dopamine phasic triggers and their potential 
effects on tonic baselines will be prioritized. To a lesser degree, value-coding and salience-
coding will be used to support this as well as considerations taken from cognitive 
neuroscience.  
 
Though a brief look at a potential candidate gene for entrepreneurial propensity was 
discussed in the literature review, it is not possible to determine whether the interviewees 
possess the DRD4 gene, which would require a DNA test to show. However, most 
respondents report having family members that are either entrepreneurs, or have an 
entrepreneurial mindset, loosely indicating that there could be a genetic component at work.

you were expecting, or getting new discoveries that can open new doors.”

In this response, GY describes the process of engaging in activities that involve exploring the 
outcomes of lab work. In this way, SEEKING behavior is engaged and outcomes that either 
confirm or provide unexpected results are both tied to phasic modes. While salience- or 
value-coding is at the core, a neural activation takes place in which an unexpected result can 
first create a salience-neuron response where an abnormality or outlier is observed, followed 
by activation of value-coding neurons assisting in discovering how this new knowledge could 
be beneficial and for which application. As GY also states previously, there is a drive towards 
exploratory behavior as a conscious motivation for entrepreneurship that also give credence 
to the possibility of SEEKING activation. 
 
GY: “The so the last time that we reached a milestone, … that was really thrilling,

This response speaks to the long-term effect of phasic mode activations. It is possible that 
this can have influenced tonic baseline levels of dopamine, elevating the need for stronger 
phasic triggers later, as is indicated in the following response:

actually. I only recognized it few weeks ago that there was a great mismatch,

Here, the absence of dopamine activation from expected outcomes could be construed as a 
reward-prediction error caused by an absence of informational significance stemming from 
elevated tonic baselines. As GY is aware of the functioning of dopamine as a neurotransmitter, 
they speculate that the resulting decrease in motivation could be a result of this as stated in 
the following response: 
 
GY (personal opinion on whether dopamine withdrawal could have been a 
source of experienced stress): “… it was this period of nothing really happening … 
it was not rejection, but emails after emails saying vacation, reach back later, so

Discussion 
In the analysis it has been shown that there are correlations between theory, research, and 
collected data from the interviewees. While the sample size is too small to argue for causal 
links based on empirical data alone, findings in other research does seem to support the 
possibility of such a link. 
 
The sample group was relatively diverse culturally, though with more Danish nationals than 
non-Danish. Cultural influences stemming from more collectivist or individualistic cultures is 
a factor that can be considered to be significant to an extent. As we saw in the case of MG 
and TS, collectivist cultures appear to be more focused on community and social implications 
than those of GY, AN and JO. Although they do have considerations towards social and 
environmental impacts they are of lesser importance compared to other factors, with JO 
being the exception, where it seems to be evenly distributed. The difference being that GY, 
AN and JO have a stronger focus on other aspects of being entrepreneurs, most specifically 
their need and desire to achieve outcomes, independence, learning, control, and self-
efficacy. 
 
It was also shown that all respondents, with the exception of JO, had family that are, or have 
been, engaged in entrepreneurial activities. This appears to suggest that there could be a 
genetic component as some researchers suggest, but it could also be a result of behavioral 
conditioning. It is also possible that it could be both and would then be considered an 
epigenetic effect. As the data is not significant to make any assumptions it will not be included 
in the conclusion and detractors have also posited that genetics may not be sufficient to 
support a more complex cognitive neural explanation.

Despite the sample size, it was possible to posit correlations between intents, motivations, 
events, and neuroscience as it has been described in literature and throughout the analysis. 
It is, however, given the sample size and lack of access to measuring tests, insufficient to 
show any causality. However, as other research has shown, there is a possibility that such a 
link exist. 
 
Though not examined in the analysis, there seem to be a correlation between the 
respondent’s hobbies and the way they report their motivations and experiences of 
outcomes. As an example, GY play the game Dungeons & Dragons, which is a fantasy role-
playing game with no defined objectives or end. This relates well to their relationship with 
taking risk, and the character they play in the game (druid) is a type that can change forms to 
fit a situation, which also lends to the reported positive attitude towards adapting to change 
in their startup. 
Conclusion 
Motivation is a crucial component in not just entrepreneurial life but also our professional and 
personal lives. It has been a focus of study for a long time and is one of the most well-
documented fields of study in social sciences, albeit, often misunderstood. Many still believe 
in reward and punishment as the means to motivate ourselves and each other, and thus the 
results from studies are either ignored, rejected or not trusted. With new findings in 
neuroscience we are now closer to being able to validate previous findings and can say with 
a higher degree of confidence that there is a link between what social science says and what 
biology says. Hopefully, this will prompt more to embrace this knowledge so better outcomes 
and satisfaction with our work and success in entrepreneurial life can move improve. 
 
For entrepreneurs, motivation is perhaps the most important component for each individual 
actor engaged in the startup, as it is the fuel that drives the person and their projects further. 
With the knowledge that has been put forth in this thesis, the important question is how it is 
to be used. 
 
As SEEKING behaviors and tonic and phasic modes are not restricted to the nature of working 
or navigating projects in a professional manner, a way that could help entrepreneurs (and 
people in general), would be to be mindful of the other arenas in which such activations are 
created. E.g. if a person is highly engaged on social media, it is important to be aware that 
such platforms are designed to exploit these activations to keep users engaged. This is an 
important aspect to realize, as dependence on “doom scrolling” may be the cause for not only 
addiction, but also adverse effects impacting tonic baselines on activities in life, such as 
entrepreneurship. For entrepreneurs specifically in this context, it may be highly advisable to 
strictly manage usage of social media to avoid dependence and raise or lower tonic 
baselines.

Another arena that may be of importance is diet, sleep and exercise. Though not being 
covered in this thesis, the beneficial outcomes of these activities are well documented in 
other sources. In the context of this thesis, it is possible that, as they are instrumental in good 
brain health and development in general, they may allow for managing dopamine levels 
more efficiently and create greater resilience. To determine this with any degree of 
confidence, it will be necessary to conduct more research. 
 
If educators, managers, and entrepreneurs are aware of the potential novelty bias and the 
ethical challenges, neuroscience seem capable to provide new knowledge that could help 
to ensure that existing and future entrepreneurs can maintain motivation by creating 
awareness of how dopamine and motivation are related, both to each other, but also in 
relation to other activities, and that it is important to manage expectations to stabilize a 
healthy tonic baseline. Doing so, might lead to better outcomes, and a lower rate of burnout 
and depressions. 
 
Answering the Research Question 
Can neuroscientific findings expand our understanding of motivational goals and ongoing 
motivation in entrepreneurs? 
Can dopamine related behavior be attributed to motivation(s)? 
We have looked at compositionally varied respondents (relatively speaking) despite the small 
sample size, but it is not certain that dopamine is at play directly from motivations from the 
onset, but it is possible that as soon as the ‘want’ to engage emerges, dopamine will be a key 
neurotransmitter moving forward. The internally expected outcomes were reported to “feel 
invigorating” when met. 
Is it possible to determine phasic dopamine triggers from entrepreneurial action(s)? 
From the reported experiences, there are several statements that appear to suggest that 
certain events and outcomes can be related to phasic events of dopamine release. To 
increase the confidence to make this assertion more probable, it will be necessary to create 
a research design with much greater resolution, including brain scans and blood samples and 
for a much longer duration. 
Does entrepreneurial intent correlate with SEEKING behaviors? 
As we saw in the analysis, respondents reported satisfaction and motivation when engaging 
in activities that correlate with exploratory and novelty seeking behaviors that have been 
shown to originate from the SEEKING system of the brain. In this sense, it is not unreasonable 
to assume that activation of the SEEKING system is at the core of entrepreneurial activity. 
==PAGE:47==

47 
 
Can ongoing motivational incentives be correlated with dopamine relevant behavior 
and action? 
Respondents reported satisfaction when outcomes of entrepreneurial activities were either 
achieved or failed to reach expectations. When intrinsically stimulated, the respondents 
report a heightened sensation of motivation, while when extrinsically stimulated outcomes 
fail to manifest, a lowered sense of motivation was experienced. This is possibly tied to 
dopamine, but more research is required to determine this. 
Is there any indication that phasic dopamine triggers could raise tonic dopamine 
baseline, possibly due to expectations of extrinsic rewards? 
While, as mentioned previously, the sample size of the respondents is not significantly large 
enough to make any definitive conclusions, there were reported incidents where it is possible 
that an elevated tonic baseline of dopamine could have led to reward-prediction errors. While 
this is only tangentially indicative of such a state, more research with access to more 
advanced methods and materials will be needed to make any conclusion viable. 
 
In closing, it appears that neuroscience can be a valid field of study of motivation for 
entrepreneurs, but that it will require more research to determine if the mechanisms 
described is at work, and to build actionable models and methods for entrepreneurs. Despite 
this, we can likely already now disseminate this current level of knowledge in entrepreneurial 
education and support.
