Abstract
Myocardial infarction, commonly known as a heart attack, is a serious disease and a common
consequence of coronary artery narrowing, known as coronary artery disease. This narrowing
reduces blood supply to the heart muscle, leading to myocardial tissue death. In response, the
left ventricle undergoes a remodelling process, altering its size and shape to compensate for the
loss of function. However, over time, this remodelling leads to a decline in the heart’s pumping
ability. Therefore, examining the shape of the left ventricle is critical for assessing cardiac health.
This study focuses on statistical shape analysis of the left ventricle to detect myocardial in-
farction using only a single-phase cardiac computed tomography scan in the end-diastole phase.
The statistical analysis aims to classify healthy individuals and patients with prior myocardial
infarction. Previous studies have primarily utilized both end-diastole and end-systole phases,
or motion-based features, with some also incorporating clinical measurements. In addition to
shape-based analysis, this study examines Hounsfield Unit values within the myocardium to
assess whether myocardial infarction causes significant changes in tissue density that could aid
in detection.
To apply statistical classification methods such as Canonical Discriminant Analysis, Contrastive
Principal Component Analysis, and Partial Least Squares, an image registration process is
required to align the images for comparison. This is accomplished using template registration
with Implicit Neural Representations.
The best-performing classification model with the data that is based on the shape of the left
ventricle was Partial Least Squares with three components, achieving an accuracy of 96% with
only one misclassification. For the data of the Hounsfield Unit values inside the myocardium,
the Multi-Layer Perceptron model performed best, with an accuracy of 92% and two misclassi-
fications. The combined dataset of the left ventricle shape and the Hounsfield Unit values, one
of the top-performing models was Na¨ıve Bayes, which achieved an accuracy of 88% with three
misclassifications.
Additionally, this study explores individual assessment of myocardial infarction location using
Canonical Discriminant Analysis and Partial Least Squares contributions. The results indicate
that no clear and consistent pattern was found regarding which myocardial segments were most
influential compared to the Myocardial infarction scar tissue. This aligns with the unique nature
of myocardial infarction, where infarct location varies significantly among individuals.
When comparing these results to state-of-the-art methods, the three highlighted classification
models outperformed the baseline model from the Statistical Atlases and Computational Models
of the Heart left ventricle statistical shape challenge in one or two metrics. Although the mod-
els in this project did not surpass the best-performing state-of-the-art methods, they achieved
strong results using simpler, single-phase data, highlighting their effectiveness despite the re-
duced complexity of the input data.

Introduction
The heart plays a vital role in the human body and is essential for sustaining life. Proper heart
function is important, since it is crucial to survival. However, heart diseases are the leading
cause of death worldwide, with Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) responsible for 13% of global
deaths in 2021 [45].
CAD occurs when the coronary arteries become narrowed, often due to the build-up of fat, which
reduces blood flow. This narrowing can lead to a myocardial infarction (MI), commonly known
as a heart attack, a condition in which the heart muscle (myocardium) does not receive enough
blood. The left myocardium is especially important, as it is responsible for pumping oxygenated
blood throughout the body. When the left myocardium does not receive an adequate supply of
blood, it can impair its ability to pump, potentially leading to the death of heart muscle cells.
To compensate, the left ventricle (LV) undergoes a remodelling process, which over time may
lead to a further decline in the heart’s ability to pump blood throughout the body.
The remodelling process changes the size and shape of the LV, making an examination of its
shape vital for detecting and monitoring heart health, as well as for gaining insights into the
development and progression of heart disease. Therefore, this project will conduct a statistical
shape analysis of the LV to detect MI using various statistical models. The results will be used
to determine whether it is possible to identify the location of the MI. In addition to analysing
the shape of the LV, an analysis of the Hounsfield units (HU) within the myocardium could
provide further insight into heart health, as the death of heart muscle tissue may cause the HU
value to differ from that of normal muscle tissue. Furthermore, a combined shape and HU values
will be analysed.
1.1
State of the Art
Various LV shape analysis methods have been developed to predict MI, traditionally relying
on biomarkers. The Statistical Atlases and Computational Models of the Heart (STACOM)
challenge [40] focused on the statistical analysis of LV shape and function after MI. Participants
were provided with surface points derived from cine Magnetic Resonance (MR) images at two
cardiac phases: end-diastole (ED) and end-systole (ES). From both phases, the epicardial and
endocardial surfaces were extracted.
Eleven research groups participated in this challenge, proposing various models aimed at outper-
forming the baseline method; a binary multiple logistic regression based on clinical parameters,
including end-diastolic volume (EDV), end-systolic volume (ESV), left ventricular mass (LVM),
and ejection fraction (EF). This approach calculated the probability of a case belonging to the
infarcted group, using a generalized linear model as the prediction framework. All eleven meth-

One of the methods, developed by Piras et al. [35], used geometric morphometrics to analyze

tion, Principal Components Analysis (PCA) was used to reduce the dimensionality of the feature
space. Various classification methods were tested, with Support Vector Machines (SVM) yield-
ing the best performance.
The data, centred on the average shape, incorporating both the
endocardium and epicardium, proved to be most effective for classification.
Lekadir et al. [25] proposed a method based on Partial Least Square (PLS) regression, which
identified new axes that captured shape variances most strongly correlated with the class labels.
The LV surface points, combined with the shape labels, served as inputs to the PLS model.
Multiple PLS regression models with varying numbers of latent variables were generated, and
the final classification decision was derived by calculating the median value of these models’
outputs.
Bai et al. [3] combined shape and motion features to classify MI. They used 3D surface meshes
from both the ED and ES phases, aligned with a rigid transformation. Shape features were
extracted and reduced using PCA for both ED and ES. Motion features, including wall thick-
ening and vertex displacement in radial, circumferential, and longitudinal directions, were also
defined.
For classification, they applied SVM with an Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel,
trained with 10-fold cross-validation.
The best model used PCA components, absolute wall
thickening, and displacement features in the circumferential direction for the endocardium and
the radial direction for the epicardium.
Roh´e M-M et al. [37] applied Polyaffine Transformations to the 17-segment Standardized My-
ocardial Segmentation model to align data from different cardiac phases (ED and ES) for com-
parison. These transformations captured the heart’s motion, from which they extracted clinical
parameters like strain, displacement in radial, circumferential, and longitudinal directions, and
regional wall thickness. To reduce feature dimensionality, they used PCA and PLS. The best
classifier for distinguishing between healthy and MI hearts was the SVM with a RBF kernel,
using the reduced feature set from PLS. The selected methods from the STACOM challenge

1.2
Aim of thesis
This thesis aims to detect MI using different statistical models, trained on static single-time
Computed Tomography (CT) images in ED-phase. Previous studies have used ES shape data
or both ED and ES or with incorporating biomarkers. This thesis explores MI detection from
a single ED-phase CT image, without biomarkers or motion features. This approach is based
on the hypothesis that examining the shape of the LV in a single time frame in ED-phase can
determine whether the patient has an MI. The hypothesis is revised since MI leads to structural
remodelling of the LV. Furthermore, this thesis explores whether it is possible to assess the
specific location of the infarction in the LV, as its occurrence varies between individuals. In
addition to examining the shape of the LV, the HU value within the myocardium is examined,
based on the hypothesis that MI alters the HU value. The model is called the HU-Intensity
model. Lastly, both the shape of LV and HU values inside the myocardium together is explored.
To fulfil the aim of this thesis, an overview of the heart’s anatomy and function, specifically
the LV, will first be provided, followed by an explanation of the cardiac cycle. Since the focus
is on detecting MI, a description of the disease and the remodelling process will be included.
To enhance the understanding of the results, the standardized myocardial segmentation system,
the 17-segment model will be presented, followed by an explanation of: CT.
Next, the relevant theory necessary for understanding the method is presented, followed by
a detailed description of the dataset used in this project. Subsequently, the methodology is
outlined, covering the entire process from CT scans to the final results. This includes details
on TotalSegmentator, the image registration process, the statistical models employed, and the
approach used for individual prediction.
The results section will then present selected findings to confirm or refute the hypotheses. Fi-
nally, the thesis will conclude with a discussion and a conclusion of the project’s findings.

2.1
Anatomy of the Heart
The human heart is a muscular organ maintaining a continuous circulation of blood throughout
the body. It consists of four chambers: the right and left atria and the right and left ventricles.
The chambers are divided into two sides each containing one atrium and one ventricle. The
interventricular septum separates the right and left ventricles (see Figure 2.1a).
The right
atrium (RA) receives deoxygenated blood from the body and passes it to the main pump of the
right side, the right ventricle (RV). From there, the blood flows to the lungs to be oxygenated,
then returns to the left atrium (LA) before reaching the main pump on the left side, the LV
[36]. The LV pumps oxygen-rich blood throughout the body via the aorta. As a result, the
LV is larger than the RV and has more robust walls. The heart’s walls consist of three layers:
the endocardium, myocardium, and epicardium. These layers enable the powerful contractions
needed to keep blood circulating efficiently. The endocardium is the thin inner membrane, the
myocardium is the middle layer and the heart muscle itself, making it the thickest layer, and
the epicardium is the outer layer, where the coronary arteries are located. The coronary arteries
supply the myocardium with oxygenated blood, enabling it to function properly. See Figure
2.1a for the heart seen from inside.
2.1.1
The Left Ventricle
The LV is a crucial component of the cardiovascular system, responsible for pumping oxygenated
blood throughout the body. It has a cone-like shape, with a narrow apex and a broader base,
making it larger and narrower than the RV. The apex is the tip of the LV, where the muscle
wall is thinner compared to the rest of the heart. While this makes it more vulnerable, it also
provides advantages in certain medical procedures. The base is located at the upper part of the
ventricle, near the mitral valve, which regulates blood flow from the LA into the LV, ensuring
the blood moves in the correct direction.
Inside the LV, there are three key elements: the
free volume, papillary muscles, and trabeculation. The free volume refers to the space within
the ventricle that accommodates the flow of blood. The papillary muscles play a vital role in
supporting the mitral valve, helping it to open and close properly to prevent backflow. Lastly,

2.1.2
The Cardiac Cycle
The cardiac cycle, which is the sequence of events from one heartbeat to the next, controls blood
circulation through a rhythmic pattern of contraction and relaxation. The contraction phase
is called systole, while the relaxation phase is called diastole. During systole, the heart muscle
contracts, leading to the pumping of blood out of the chambers. In diastole, the muscle relaxes
and allows the chambers to fill with blood. Both the atria and the ventricles go through these
phases: atrial systole and atrial diastole for the atria, and ventricular systole and ventricular
diastole for the ventricles. When the atria are in diastole, the ventricles are in systole, and vice
versa. Therefore, when speaking generally about the diastole and systole phases, the phases
often refer to the ventricular phases [10].
During these phases, the blood volume in the ventricles can be analyzed, including the ED
volume (EDV) and the ES volume (ESV). The EDV is the amount of blood in the ventricles
just before the systolic phase begins, while the EVS is the amount of blood remaining in the
ventricles just before the diastolic phase starts (see Figure 2.2). The EDV represents the highest
volume of blood in the ventricles, and the ESV represents the lowest volume. These two volumes
are used to calculate the stroke volume, which is the amount of blood ejected by the LV during
each cardiac cycle. It is determined by subtracting ESV from the EDV. Stroke volume can

minute. Cardiac output is calculated by multiplying stroke volume by heart rate.
Three main factors affect stroke volume: preload, afterload, and contractility. Preload refers to
the stretch of the myocardial cells (cardiac myocytes) at the end of the diastolic phase. This
stretch is caused by the volume of blood in the ventricle, which exerts tension on the myocardial
walls.
Afterload is the pressure in the systemic circulation that the LV must overcome to
eject blood into the body. Contractility refers to the strength of contraction of the myocytes,
which determines how effectively the heart can pump blood. Another measurement is the left
ventricular ejection fraction(LVEF), which is the stroke volume divided by the EDV. The LVEF
represents the percentage of blood ejected from the LV with each heartbeat. These measurements
are used by medical professionals to assess the overall health of the heart [7].
2.2
Myocardial Infarction
For the cardiac cycle to function properly, the myocardium requires a continuous supply of
oxygenated blood, which it receives from the coronary arteries. This might not be the case
if these arteries narrow or block, the myocardium may not receive adequate blood flow. This
condition is known as CAD. A common symptom of CAD, or myocardial ischemia, is stable
angina pectoris, the medical term for a specific type of chest pain.
It occurs when certain

during situations where the heart needs more oxygen, such as during exercise. However, pain
typically subsides with rest or the use of nitroglycerin (medicine). When the blood supply to
a portion of the heart muscle is severely reduced or cut off, it can lead to the death of the
affected tissue, which is MI or more commonly, a heart attack. MI weakens the heart’s ability

The 17-segment model
2.3
The 17-segment model
The 17-segment model is a standardized myocardial segmentation system that divides the LV
myocardium into 17 segments based on anatomical landmarks. It was developed by the American
Heart Association to provide a consistent framework for comparing different cardiovascular
imaging modalities and analysing variations in left ventricular anatomy across individuals.
In the 17-segment model, the orientation of the heart is based on the long axis of the LV, which
runs through the apex (tip) of the LV and the center of the mitral valve (see Figure 2.1b for
the anatomical reference). All imaging planes are oriented at 90◦relative to this axis. There
are three primary planes that provide comprehensive views of the heart. The Short-Axis View
(Parasternal short-axis view) displays the heart in cross-sections, the Vertical Long-Axis (Apical
2-chamber view) shows the heart from the side, focusing on the LV and LA and the Horizontal
Long-Axis (Apical 4-chamber view) captures the heart from above or below, providing a full
view of all four chambers (see Figure 2.4).
Figure 2.4: Standardized 17-segment model of the left ventricular myocardium, illustrat-
ing various echocardiographic views: Apical 4-chamber (A4C), Apical 2-chamber (A2C),
Parasternal long-axis (PLAX), and Parasternal short-axis (PSAX) at basal, mid-cavity,
and apical levels. Figure from [12].
To better assess left ventricular function and myocardial blood flow, the 17-segment model
divides the myocardium into three sections along the short-axis view: basal, mid-cavity, and
==PAGE:18==

apical, which can be seen in Figure 2.4. These sections represent the upper, middle, and lower
parts of the LV/myocardium. The basal section extends from the mitral valve to the tips of
the papillary muscles at end diastole. The mid-cavity section includes the region containing
the papillary muscles. The apical section extends just beyond the papillary muscles, stopping
just before the left ventricular cavity ends. The myocardial mass in each of these regions is
distributed as follows: 35% in the basal region, 35% in the mid-cavity, and 30% in the apical
region.
The basal and mid-cavity regions are divided into six segments each, while the apical region is
divided into four segments. The final segment, segment 17, is the true apex, located at the tip
of the LV, which sometimes goes under the apical region. Each segment is named according
to its position along the long axis of the heart and its circumferential location. This naming
system helps to precisely identify and analyse specific areas of the myocardium or the surface
of the LV(see Figure 2.5).
One way to visualize the 17-segment model is through a bullseye plot (also called a polar plot),
which projects the short-axis view of the LV onto a 2D plane. In this plot, the center represents
the apex, the second ring represents the apical segments, the third ring corresponds to the mid-
cavity segments, and the outer ring represents the basal segments. Figure 2.5 illustrates this
visualization, including the names of the segments.
The plot also divides the segments according to the major coronary arteries that supply blood
to each area of the myocardium.
The three major coronary arteries are the Left Anterior
Descending artery (LAD), Right Coronary artery (RCA), and Left Circumflex artery (LCx).
Because of individual anatomical variability, the myocardial segments may receive blood supply
from different coronary arteries. However, the general territorial assignment helps guide the
understanding of coronary artery perfusion in relation to myocardial segments [6].

2.4
Computed Tomography
CT is a medical imaging technique that combines X-ray technology with computer algorithms
to generate detailed cross-sectional images of the body. A rotating X-ray tube emits beams
as it moves around the patient, while detectors on the opposite side capture the transmitted
X-rays. The patient lies on a movable bed, which positions them within the circular opening
of the scanner. Using mathematical reconstruction techniques, 2D slices are generated from the
collected data, which can be viewed individually or stacked to form a 3D image [28].
CT images appear in shades of grey, depending on how much X-rays are absorbed by different
tissues. For example, bone absorbs more X-rays, appearing bright, while air absorbs less, ap-
pearing dark. The CT linear attenuation coefficient describes this phenomenon and is used to
calculate the HU, a normalized attenuation value, as shown in Equation 2.1:
HU = 1000 · µ −µwater

attenuation coefficient of water [20]. HU values are standardized, HUwater = 0 and HUair =

Distinguishing between different soft tissues can be challenging, so contrast agents are often used
to enhance visibility. These X-ray-absorbing fluids improve image clarity, especially in cardiac
CT, where the heart consists primarily of soft tissue. CT scans that use contrast agents are
referred to as CT Angiography (CTA).
The spatial resolution of a CT scan determines how well small structures can be visualized.
It consists of in-plane resolution, which refers to the level of detail within a 2D image slice,
and through-plane resolution, which describes the detail along the axial direction. The latter
is influenced by slice thickness and patient movement during scanning. Higher resolution cor-
responds to smaller pixel sizes, capturing finer details, while larger pixel sizes may lead to a
loss of structural clarity. Pixel size is measured from the centre of one pixel to the centre of a
neighbouring pixel, typically in millimetres (mm).
In cardiac CT, scan timing is crucial. Imaging is typically performed during the diastolic phase
(best phase), when the heart is at its most stationary, reducing motion artifacts and improving
image clarity [28].

Technical Background
This chapter provides an overview of the technical background necessary to understand the
methods used in this project. It begins with an introduction to the Marching Cubes algorithm,
followed by an explanation of the No-new-Net (nnU-Net) segmentation framework. Next, key
technical aspects of image registration are presented. The chapter then covers various statistical
methods relevant to this study.
3.1
Marching Cubes
Marching Cubes is a computer graphics algorithm used to construct 3D surfaces from medical
images.
The algorithm works by dividing the input volume into small cubes, each defined
by eight vertices. For each cube, it determines whether each vertex lies inside or outside the
target label. Based on this classification, a precomputed lookup table is used to identify the
edges where the target label intersects the cube. Triangles are then constructed along these
intersected edges to approximate the target label within the cube. This process is repeated for
all cubes in the volume, and the resulting triangles are combined to form a complete polygonal
mesh representing the target label. A key distinction between Marching Cubes and Discrete
Marching Cubes is whether the input volume is continuous or discrete. In this project, the
input label is discrete, meaning interpolation is unnecessary before generating the final mesh
[26].
3.2
No-new-Net
The nnU-Net is a deep learning-based segmentation method, presented by Isensee et al. [18],
that builds upon the existing U-Net architecture [38] and serves as a self-adapting framework
for biomedical images. This means it adapts to each new input through pre-processing, network
architecture selection, and post-processing. Depending on the input, the network determines
which architecture to use.
The framework includes three different U-Net architectures: 2D
U-Net, 3D U-Net, and U-Net Cascade.
The 2D U-Net is applied to 3D medical images, but it only utilizes 2D slices of the data,
meaning that the z-axis is not taken into account. In contrast, the 3D U-Net considers all three
dimensions. However, running the 3D U-Net on the entire 3D image can consume a significant
amount of GPU memory, as the network processes 3D patches of the image. The U-Net Cascade
consists of two 3D U-Nets. It works by first downsampling the image and then running a 3D
11
==PAGE:21==
12
3.3
Technical Details about Deformable Image Registration
U-Net on the downsampled images. This results in a rough segmentation at a lower resolution,
which is then upsampled to the original resolution. The upsampled segmentation is used as an
additional input for the next 3D U-Net, which is trained on patches at full resolution. During
the training process, they employ five-fold cross-validation, and at the end, the model with the
highest mean foreground dice score is chosen as the final model.
3.3
Image Registration
Image registration is an image processing technique used to align images into the same coordinate
system for comparison.
This process is particularly important for medical images taken at
different times, from different patients, or using different scanners, as these factors can introduce
variations in object representation. During registration, a reference (target) image is selected
as the baseline to which all other images are aligned [33].
The registration process can be performed using rigid, affine, or deformable registration methods.
Rigid registration uses simple alignment techniques that apply only rotation and translation,
preserving the size and shape of objects. This method can be extended to affine registration,
which additionally allows shearing and scaling. Affine registration provides more flexibility than
rigid registration but remains more constrained than Deformable Image Registration (DIR).
In contrast, DIR applies more complex transformations, enabling changes in both size and
shape while maintaining anatomical structure. This makes DIR particularly useful for medical
imaging applications where objects, such as the LV, vary in size from patient to patient [9].
The following subsections describe the technical details of two different registration methods.
The first is Iterative Closest Point (ICP), which is used in this project as an affine registration
method.
The second is DIR, which allows for more flexible transformations to better align
anatomical structures.

3.3.3
Generalized Procrustes Analysis
Using INRs alone does not completely align objects in physical space. To address this, GPA is
applied to the objects after they have been registered with INR.
GPA is a statistical method used in shape analysis to compare the geometric shapes of multiple
objects/shapes. GPA focuses exclusively on geometric differences between shapes by aligning
them, allowing for the analysis of shape variation without interference from differences in size,
position, or orientation. The GPA is a rigid transformation that preserves relative distances and
angles, retaining the essential shape of each object. The steps of the analysis are as follows:
1. Select an initial shape as an estimate for the mean shape, often using the first shape in
the dataset.
2. Align all shapes to this current mean shape.
3. Recompute the mean shape from the newly aligned shapes.
==PAGE:24==
CHAPTER 3. TECHNICAL BACKGROUND
15
4. If the mean shape has significantly changed, return to step two and repeat the process
until the mean shape stabilizes.
In GPA, all shapes must have the same number of corresponding points, or landmarks, to
ensure accurate alignment. The alignment of shapes involves a series of transformations applied
uniformly to each shape.
First, each shape is translated so that its centroid is positioned
at the origin, ensuring that all shapes share the same centre. Next, each shape is scaled to
standardize size, making all shapes comparable without size differences. Finally, each shape
is rotated to minimize the Procrustes distance to the mean shape, optimizing alignment by
reducing differences in orientation.
The Procrustes distance is a measure of the difference
between two sets of landmarks, calculated after alignment and reflecting the dissimilarity in
shape alone [32, 39].

4.1
The Copenhagen General Population Study
The data for this project consists of 124 3D CT scans from The Copenhagen General Population
Study (also known as Herlev-Østerbro undersøgelsen). Before 2003, the study was named The
Copenhagen City Heart Study (Østerbro undersøgelsen). The Copenhagen City Heart Study
was initiated in 1975 as a cardiovascular population study involving 20, 000 women and men
aged between 20 and 93 years. The population lived in the Østerbro and Nørrebro areas of
Copenhagen (including all of Østerbro and one-third of Nørrebro). The study was originally
designed to prevent CAD and stroke but has since expanded to include other aspects, such as
pulmonary diseases, heart failure, and additional conditions. The study has been conducted in
several phases, with the first three surveys carried out from 1975 to 1997 at Rigshospitalet, and
the final survey conducted at Bispebjerg University Hospital between 2001 and 2003.
In addition to the CT scans of the heart and lungs, the study collected measurements such as
participants’ height, weight, blood pressure, and other variables, as well as information about
their diet and lifestyle. Blood samples were also collected. In 2003, the study expanded its
population base to include participants from additional areas, prompting a name change. Ex-
aminations of participants were then conducted at Herlev Hospital. By 2010, the study was
further expanded to include more participants aged 40 years or older, eventually encompassing
over 100, 000 Danes [2, 13, 34].
4.1.1
Dataset Characteristics
The 3D CT scans in this project were taken during the cardiac phase ED, referred to as the ”best
phase”, as it minimizes motion and provides the clearest images. Based on the participants’

5.1.1
Implementation Details
The code for this project is written in Python, utilizing various packages. Mesh generation and
handling are performed using the VTK package, CT images are processed using the SimpleITK
package. Statistical methods are implemented using the scikit-learn library. The remaining
code, including setup and execution, was developed by the author with some guidance and
inspiration from the supervisors.
5.2
TotalSegmentator
One of the initial steps in progressing from the CT scans to the results that differentiate between
the healthy and infarcted groups is obtaining the heart segmentations using the TotalSegmen-
tator tool. TotalSegmentator is a whole-body segmentation tool for CT images, presented by
Wasserthal et al. [42]. It is a pre-trained network that utilizes the nnU-Net segmentation algo-
rithm (Section 3.2) and has been trained on 1204 diverse CT images. Initially, the network was
capable of segmenting 104 anatomical structures in a single CT scan. However, further train-
ing and developments have enhanced the tool’s capabilities to segment 117 different anatomical
structures, and it now also works on MR images. These further developments have made it
possible to achieve higher resolution in the segmentation of the heart. In this project, the To-
talSegmentator segmentation tool is used to segment the LV and the left myocardium in order to
develop a statistical shape model of the LV and sample HU values from inside the myocardium
to the HU-Intensity model.
5.2.1
Generating Left Ventricle and Myocardium Meshes
The segmentations created by TotalSegmentator are then used to generate the LV and My-
ocardium meshes. For each patient, the LV and myocardium segmentations are extracted, and
the VTK function vtkDiscreteMarchingCubes is applied to generate the meshes. This function
is a specialized version of vtkMarchingCubes [21], which itself is based on the Marching Cubes
algorithm (Section 3.1). The process from LV segmentation to mesh creation is shown in Figure
5.1. The process for generating the myocardium is the same, but with the myocardium segmen-
tation instead. The resulting meshes are then reduced to contain only 5005 points to simplify
the computation and are subsequently smoothed. For the image registration, a random sample
from the healthy dataset is selected as the target mesh.

5.3
Applied Image Registration
After generating the LV and myocardium meshes, image registration is required to enable direct
comparison and ensure point correspondence across all patients. The image registration process
in this project consists of three main steps. First, affine registration using the ICP algorithm
(Subsection 3.3.1) is performed as a pre-alignment step to align the source mesh with the target
mesh. This step is crucial because patient positioning in the scanner may vary, as the meshes
are derived from different patients at different times. The ICP transformation is first computed
based on the mesh alignment, and its inverse is then applied to the source image to align it with
the target image. The transformed source image is subsequently used in the next registration
step.
The second step involves DIR using INR (Subsection 3.3.2), following the implementation by
Lowes et al. [27]. This registration is optimized using both the HU values from the CT images
and the SDF of the LV or myocardium. The transformed source image is then processed through
the network to ensure complete alignment with the target image.
Once the first two steps of image registration are complete, the mesh transformation process
follows a reverse approach. Unlike image registration, where the source image is aligned with the
target, mesh transformation aligns the target mesh with the source mesh. First, the inverse INR-
based registration is applied to the target mesh, followed by the inverse ICP transformation.
By applying these inverse transformations, the coordinates of the target mesh are mapped
back to the source patient, ensuring point correspondence across patients.
Since the same
mesh structure is deformed and registered for each patient, this approach guarantees consistent
anatomical alignment. The mesh transformation is performed separately for both the LV meshes
and the myocardium meshes, as illustrated for the LV mesh in Figure 5.2. The final step in the
image registration process involves GPA (Subsection 3.3.3), ensuring that all meshes are aligned
within the same coordinate system, thereby maintaining consistency across patients. Figure 5.3
shows the LV meshes before the GPA, while the right-side illustration depicts the meshes after

5.5.1
Classification Models
When the data is in the correct form, the classification task begins. For this purpose, different
models are used. The dataset consists of three different versions.
The first is the full dataset, where all available features are included. In this case, CDA is
not only used as a classification model but also serves as a dimensionality reduction technique.
Subsection 3.4.1 provides a detailed explanation of CDA along with relevant parameters. In
this project, there are k = 2 groups: the healthy group and the infarcted group. For the shape
model, there are p = 15015 variables, corresponding to the x,y,z coordinates of the 5005 points
on the LV mesh. In contrast, the HU-Intensity model consists of p = 10000 variables, while for
the combined model, p = 25015. For all models, the maximum number of canonical discriminant
functions is given by min(k −1, p) = 1, meaning a single function is used to achieve optimal
separation between the two groups.
The second is the PCA-reduced dataset (Subsection 3.4.2), where PCA is applied to reduce
the number of features while retaining 95% of the variance in the dataset. This dimensionality
reduction results in 39 PCs for the shape data and 78 PCs for both the HU-Intensity data and
the combined dataset. For this dataset, cPCA (Subsection 3.4.3) is also applied. In this method,
the algorithm selects different values of α, where α controls the contrast between the target and
background datasets. The selection of different α values ensures that the results provide diverse
perspectives on the data rather than being too similar to one another.
The third is the PLS-reduced dataset (Subsection 3.4.4). To analyse the PLS dataset, different
numbers of PLS components are tested to observe how they influence classification performance.
Therefore, three different PLS-reduced datasets are used, where m = 1, m = 2, and m = 3. To
make a prediction, a decision threshold of 0.5 is applied.
Other classification models were also applied to both the full dataset and the PCA-reduced
dataset, with the exception of Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA), which was only used on
the full dataset. These models are listed in Table 5.1.
To evaluate the statistical models, standard classification metrics were used, including precision,
recall (sensitivity), F1-score, accuracy and Area Under the Curve (AUC).

Discussion
In the following chapter, the results presented in the results section will be discussed.
The
discussion will begin with a reflection on the dataset and its limitations, followed by an analysis
of the individual assessments.

7.1
Data Reflections
Looking at the results for the Shape model in Table 6.1, some classification models (e.g. Lo-
gistic Regression with PCA-reduced dataset and PLS with three components) indicate that it
is possible to distinguish between patients with and without MI based solely on shape features
from a single-phase CT scan. However, the size of the dataset impacts the performance met-
rics. With only 26 patients in the test set, the classification metrics become highly sensitive to
misclassification. A single misclassified instance can lead to a noticeable decrease in metrics.
This effect is evident in Table 6.5, where PLS with three components misclassifies only one
infarcted patient as healthy, resulting in a recall of 0.89. In contrast, Bai et al. [3] from the
state-of-the-art methods misclassifies two infarcted patients as healthy and achieves a recall of
0.98, highlighting the instability of performance metrics in small datasets.
Another important consideration is the AUC metric, as it is also affected by the limited sample
size.
In many cases, the AUC is relatively high compared to accuracy, indicating that the
models are effective at distinguishing between healthy and MI patients. However, since accuracy
remains comparatively lower, this suggests that the default classification threshold of 0.5 may
not be optimal. Lowering the threshold could potentially improve classification performance by
adjusting the sensitivity of the model. However, the fact that the model assigns a probability
lower than 0.5 for MI classification indicates that it is not highly confident in its predictions for
MI cases. This uncertainty further complicates threshold optimization. Additionally, due to the
small dataset size, it remains difficult to draw definitive conclusions about which classification
model is superior.
In addition to the small dataset size, the dataset is also highly dimensional due to the large
number of features. A key factor to consider is the feature-to-sample ratio. If this ratio is too
low, many models struggle to classify the data correctly due to an increased risk of overfitting.
This is particularly evident in QDA. In the Shape model, QDA misclassifies 12 instances, whereas
in the HU-Intensity model, it misclassifies 10 instances. However, in the Combined model, the
number of misclassified instances increases to 16, suggesting that the model is overfitting as
more features are introduced. Since the HU-Intensity dataset contains fewer features than both
the shape and combined datasets, this pattern indicates that adding more features without

increasing the number of samples leads to worse generalization.
Another important observation is that models with linear decision boundaries, such as Logistic
Regression and SVM, tend to exhibit more stable performance across all datasets. For instance,
Logistic Regression maintains a consistent misclassification count of around two to four across
different datasets, whereas more complex models, such as QDA and MLP, show greater vari-

high-dimensional spaces compared to linear models. Interestingly, while MLP struggles with
the shape dataset, it performs significantly better on the HU-Intensity model. This suggests
that shape features may be too complex for MLP to extract meaningful patterns, whereas HU-
Intensity values provide a clearer numerical structure that is easier for MLP to learn from. The
results of the HU-Intensity model using the full dataset indicates that the HU values inside the
myocardium also provide some meaningful information about MI.
A possible way to address the high dimensional issue is by reducing the dimensionality of the data
using PCA or PLS. This effect is evident in the performance metrics of the Decision Tree model.
Decision Trees tend to overfit when the dataset is both small and high-dimensional, memorizing
the training data rather than learning generalizable patterns. The benefits of dimensionality
reduction can be seen in the Shape model, where the Decision Tree achieves an F1-score of 0.50
on the full dataset, compared to 0.86 on the PCA-reduced dataset. However, not all models
benefit from dimensionality reduction.
This is particularly evident in models like Random
Forest, as shown in Table 6.4, where the F1-score drops from 0.71 to 0.00 after PCA. This
decline is likely due to the feature selection mechanism of Random Forest, since Random Forest
naturally selects the most important features during training, applying PCA may remove critical
information that the model would otherwise use for classification.
Additionally, PLS transformation appears to improve performance for the Shape model when
using only three components.
PLS optimizes both for label prediction and for maximizing
feature correlation, making it particularly effective for datasets with highly correlated features,
as is the case for shape data. The results suggest that shape features are linearly correlated
with the target label.
However, PLS does not improve performance for the HU-Intensity or Combined models, suggest-
ing that these datasets do not exhibit strong linear correlations with the target variable. One
possible explanation for the poor performance of PLS in the Combined model is that the dataset
combines coordinate-based shape features with HU values. This mix may make it difficult for
PLS to extract meaningful components, as PLS works best when all features are on the same
scale (e.g., standardized data). Standardizing shape data is not straightforward, as the spatial
relationships between coordinates must remain intact before and after standardization. This
complexity may explain why PLS is effective for shape data alone but not for the Combined
dataset.

Individual Assessment
The results in Table 6.2 suggest that it is difficult to identify a consistent pattern or perform an
individual assessment of the infarction location in each patient. This is likely due to the unique
nature of each infarct, making it challenging to detect recurring patterns that may not exist.
However, it is important to note that the identification of infarcted tissue is based on the
doctor’s interpretation and the assumed blood supply from the coronary arteries. Since the
coronary artery anatomy varies between patients, the expected blood supply to a specific region
may differ from the actual supply, leading to inconsistencies. Additionally, the 17-segment model
is based on the typical coronary supply to different myocardial segments, but this anatomical
distribution does not hold true for every patient.
Furthermore, as seen in Figure 5.7b, the 17-segment divisions on the mesh are not straight lines
but wavy, introducing potential inaccuracies when transforming the contribution mesh into the
bullseye plot.

Comparisons to State of the Art
The performance of the three models was compared to five models from the STACOM challenge.
While the classification models based on Shape, HU-Intensity, or a combination of both out-
performed the baseline method in one or two metrics, they did not achieve higher performance
across any metric compared to the other state-of-the-art models.
However, despite not surpassing the state-of-the-art models in overall performance, the models
developed in this project demonstrate several important strengths. One of the most significant
advantages is that these models require only a single-phase CT scan, making them both simpler
and more clinically accessible. In real-world clinical practice, it is not always guaranteed that
cardiac CT scans will capture multiple phases across the cardiac cycle. The ability to achieve
high classification accuracy with only a single time point highlights the practical relevance of
this approach. Furthermore, the HU-Intensity model performed well, making it an even simpler
alternative, as it relies purely on intensity values without requiring spatial information.
On the other hand, the slightly better performance of the state-of-the-art models suggests that
incorporating multiple phases of the cardiac cycle could provide additional valuable information
for classification. MI is generally easier to detect in the ES phase, as remodelling effects may be
more pronounced. However, the ED phase is more commonly captured in clinical practice, as it
is considered the ”best phase” due to minimal motion artifacts. While the use of a single-phase
scan contributes to the simplicity and feasibility of the approach, the lack of multi-phase data
may explain the slight performance gap compared to the state-of-the-art models.
Additionally, the small sample size likely played a role in the observed performance differences,
as discussed earlier.
A larger dataset could improve model robustness and generalizability,
potentially narrowing the gap between the proposed models and the state-of-the-art approaches.

Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to detect MI patients using different statistical models applied to
static single-time CT images in the ED phase, based on the shape of the LV (Shape model),
HU values in the myocardium (HU-Intensity model), and a combination of the two (Combined
model). The Shape model was developed based on the hypothesis that it is possible to detect
whether a patient has MI using only the shape from a single time frame. The findings in this
study support this hypothesis, demonstrating that MI detection is feasible using only the shape
of the LV. The classification model with the lowest misclassification rate in the test set was PLS
with three components, achieving an accuracy of 0.96, F1-score of 0.94, and Recall of 0.89.
Another hypothesis was that MI alters the HU values within the myocardium, making it possible
to detect MI patients based on these changes. The results of this study indicate that HU values
do change following an MI, making it possible to some extent to distinguish between healthy
and MI patients.
The best-performing model for the HU-Intensity dataset was MLP with
the full dataset, which achieved an accuracy of 0.92, F1-score of 0.88, Recall of 0.78, and a
misclassification rate of two.
Following these findings, a Combined model was tested, as both individual models had demon-
strated some capability in classifying infarcts. The results varied, with Na¨ıve Bayes (full dataset)
achieving one of the lowest misclassification rates, with only three misclassifications. It obtained
an accuracy of 0.88, F1-score of 0.84, and recall of 0.89. The reduction in performance metrics
was most likely due to the small sample size and the high dimensionality of the feature set or
the different scale of the features.
The individual assessment of MI location was challenging using both CDA and PLS contri-
butions, as there was no consistent pattern indicating which segments were most influential
compared to the MI scar. This is closely related to the unique nature of MI. However, in some
cases, CDA contributions successfully identified the specific location where the LV was deformed.
When compared to state-of-the-art methods, the highlighted models in this study outperformed
the baseline method from the STACOM challenge in one or two metrics. This further supports
the hypothesis that shape and HU values alone contain valuable information for distinguishing
between healthy and MI patients. While the proposed models did not outperform the best-
performing state-of-the-art methods, they achieved strong classification results using simpler,
single-phase data, making them potentially more practical for clinical applications.
Overall, the findings in this study suggest that MI detection from single-time CT images is
feasible using both shape and HU-Intensity data. However, due to the small sample size and high-
dimensional feature set, it remains difficult to determine which classification model is definitively
superior.

Statistical analysis of left ventricular remodeling
following a myocardial infarct
Anonymous authors
Anonymous Institute
Abstract. Myocardial infarct (MI) is a leading cause of mortality world-
wide. It is known to cause left ventricular remodeling, characterized by
changes in left ventricle myocardial size, shape, and function. Quantifying
these changes is crucial for evaluating the progression of heart diseases
following MI. Traditional clinical measurements primarily rely on the
volume of the left ventricle (LV) from temporal imaging. In this work,
we focus on detecting left ventricular remodeling from a single-phase
Cardiac Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA) using statistical
shape analysis. Our pipeline consists of a template registration using im-
plicit neural representations, followed by a statistical shape analysis on
the mesh of the LV to classify between healthy individuals and infarct
individuals with previous MI. Several methods for dimensionality reduc-
tion and classification are evaluated, with Partial Least Squares (PLS)
regression achieving the highest classification accuracy of 96%. The PLS
components can also be interpreted as directions of healthy vs. infarct
LV shapes.
Keywords: Cardiac Computed Tomography Angiography · Statistical
Shape Analysis · Implicit Neural Representations · Partial Least Squares.
1
Introduction
A myocardial infarct (MI), commonly known as a heart attack, is one of the
leading causes of death and morbidity worldwide [1]. It occurs when blood flow
in one of the coronary arteries is severely limited or obstructed, causing heart
tissue damage due to a lack of oxygen and nutrients. Damage to myocardial
tissue limits the heart’s ability to pump blood effectively, thus affecting overall
cardiac function. As a consequence of myocardial infarction, the heart undergoes
a remodeling process consisting of an early stage followed by a late stage, and
may entail changes in myocardial size, shape, and function [19]. The early stage
of remodeling is characterized by ischemic myocardial necrosis, loss of myocar-
dial contractile force, and dilatation and thinning of the infarcted myocardium.
The process is followed by fibrosis and formation of scar tissue. This stage con-
tinues for weeks or months until the expansive outward pressure towards the
infarcted area is balanced by the tensile resilience of the newly formed collagen
scar [24]. The late phase of remodeling is characterized by structural changes
in the remaining viable myocardium, causing compromised contractile function

and ventricular dilation. The ventricular dilation increases myocardial wall stress
leading to myocardial hypertrophy of the non-infarcted myocardium [26]. Both
increased myocardial contractility, as a result from ventricular dilation, and my-
ocardial hypertrophy are compensatory mechanisms to maintain cardiac func-
tion [26]. However, this process can lead to myocardial overstretching, hence
leading to further dilatation and wall stress resulting in deteriorating cardiac
function [8]. Thus, it is crucial for clinicians to be able to quantify and describe
cardiac remodeling to assess the progression of heart disease following MI.
The standard measurements commonly used to assess cardiac morphology
and function hold limited information regarding shape of the LV and often re-
quire temporal imaging methods to assess cardiac function. In clinical practice,
these measurements are often obtained from echocardiography or magnetic reso-
nance imaging (MRI), but in many cases temporal image series are not available
and it would therefore be of interest to enable an assessment of the amount of
remodeling based on a single time frame. In recent years, Cardiac Computed
Tomography Angiography (CCTA) is becoming increasingly popular due to the
fast and low-cost acquisition along with decreasing radiation dose [12]. In this
paper, we thus focus on detecting post-MI remodeling of the LV based on the
LV geometry in CCTA images acquired at end-diastole (ED) using statistical
shape analysis.
Previous studies have demonstrated that statistical shape analysis can be
utilized to automatically detect abnormal cardiac shape and motion in differ-
ent pathological groups including hypertensive populations [3], congenital heart
disease [29] and myocardial infarction [30]. The LV Statistical Shape Challenge
held in conjunction with the 2015 Statistical Atlases and Computational Mod-
eling of the Heart (STACOM), demonstrated that it was possible to classify
MI vs. healthy subjects with high accuracy based on statistical analysis of the
LV shape at end-diastole (ED), where the myocardium is most relaxed, and
end-systole (ES), where the myocardium is maximally contracted [23]. Since the
challenge provided already registered meshes at both ED and ES, the challenge
focus was on how to extract meaningful features from the meshes and which
classification methods to use. Most of the methods used Principal Component
Analysis (PCA) to reduce the feature space directly from the vertex coordinates
[17,20,25]. They investigated different classifiers such as a Support Vector Ma-
chine, Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Linear and Quadratic Discriminant
Analysis and unsupervised clustering methods to classify based on the PCA fea-
tures. To maximize the influence of features that correlate with the classification,
Rohe et al. [15] used Partial Least Squares (PLS) regression.
To use the above-described methods, it is necessary to have point correspon-
dence between all shapes in the data set [6,18]. Such registration can be obtained
by directly registering the shape surfaces or by registering the CT images and
applying the found transformation to the segmentation or surface model. Early
approaches for deformable registration were based on B-splines [13,21], and in-
volve a time-consuming optimization of the spline parameters for each sample.
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) offer the possibility of learning the de-

information obtained from static single-time images in ED phase contrary to
previous works, which work on both ED and ES images. We present a full pipeline
consisting of template image registration and a statistical shape analysis of the
LV, exploring various methods for dimensionality reduction and classification.
We further demonstrate how the different dimensionality reduction techniques
and classification strategies utilize the shape information and expose general
trends in how global remodeling affects the LV following an MI.

Data
The data for this study consist of 3D CCTA scans, coming from two different
studies performed at anonymized site. The study was conducted according to
the Declaration of Helsinki II and was approved by The anonymized Committee
on Health Research Ethics (protocol number anonymized). Oral and written
consent were obtained from all participants.
The first group, in the following called the healthy group, consists of 81 com-
pletely healthy individuals, with scans taken in the years 2010-2011 and 2020-
2021 [2]. Their 3D scans have a varying number of axial slices, ranging from
480 to 640, with each axial slice having a dimension of 512 × 512 pixels and
an in-plane spacing of 0.43 × 0.43mm2. The spacing between slices is 0.25 mm.
The following exclusion criteria were used: 1) BMI>35, 2) diabetes, 3) hyperten-
sion, 4) hypercholesterolemia, 5) moderate or heavy smoking (>20 pack years),
6) bronchospastic lung disease (COPD or asthma) as well as 7) pregnant or
actively breastfeeding participants. Only participants with a coronary artery
calcium score of zero and no abnormal findings on the CCT were included.
The second group, the infarct group, consists of 43 individuals who have
experienced myocardial infarction, with scans taken in the years 2010-2017. The
time of infarct is unknown but myocardial scaring has been manually graded by
an experienced medical expert. Like the healthy group, their scans also have a
varying number of axial slices ranging from 480 to 640, each with a resolution

Methods
We present a full pipeline for analysis of a CT scan and identification of MI-
induced remodeling of the LV. This pipeline has several steps: registration of all
CT scans, which results in extracted LV shapes with point correspondance for
all patients, dimensionality reduction due to the large amount of points in the
LV shapes, and finally classification of MI-induced remodelling
3.1
Registration
In order to obtain point correspondence over the data sets, we register the CT
scans of all patients to a single template patient. The template patient is chosen
randomly from the healthy group. This registration process for each source pa-
tient to the template patient is threefold; first, an affine registration is calculated
between the source LV to the template LV with iterative closest point registra-
tion [5]. Second, a deformable image registration is found with implicit neural
representations (INRs). The registration from the INRs are optimized with both
the HU values from the CT scan and the values from the signed distance field
of the LV [16].
The registrations from each source patient to the template patient ensure
that all CT scans align. With the inverse transformation, the coordinates from
the template mesh can be transformed back to each of the source patients. In
this way, the same mesh is deformed and registered for each patient, resulting
in point correspondence between the LV of all patients. Finally, to ensure the
coordinate systems for the meshes with point correspondance align, a procrusters
analysis is conducted.

them [5]. In this study, we use the extended ICP approach including affine

Dimensionality reduction
Once the image registration is performed and the LV meshes are aligned with
point correspondence established, the statistical analysis can be performed to
identify a previous MI. After registration, the dataset consists of 98 extracted
LV shapes represented by 5005 points, where each point is defined in 3D space
by its x,y and z coordinate. However, the high dimensionality of each shape (i.e.,

algorithms. To address this, we perform dimensionality reduction using two tech-

regression, in addition to our experiments using the full shapes.

3.3
Classification
For the final classification task, we start by using standard classification algo-
rithms: logistic regression, support vector machine (SVM) with linear and radial
basis function(rbf) as kernels, decision tree, random forest, Naive Bayes and
multi-layer perceptron (MLP) classifier. These algorithms are run on both the
full shape dataset and the PCA components. Additionally, we test the PLS algo-
rithm using the loadings of a different number of PLS-components for outcome
prediction. When predicting from the PLS loadings, we use a threshold of 0.5.
In addition to the standard classification methods, we also explore variants
of discriminant analysis, which combine dimensionality reduction and classifica-
tion into a single method. Canonical discriminant analysis (CDA) is used to find
the best linear function to characterize and separate the input data into the two
output classes. This function seeks to maximize the ratio of inter-class to intra-
class variation. As a supervised method, CDA requires labeled data to learn the
most discriminative features, and CDA assumes the full dataset to have equal
covariance matrices. Quadratic discriminant analysis (QDA) is another variant
of discriminant analysis, which allows for quadratic decision boundaries between
the classes instead of linear. QDA also does not have the same assumption re-
garding the covariance matrices, thus each class can have its own covariance
structure. This means that the method is more flexible than CDA and can cap-
ture more complex data structures, however, it is prone to overfitting due to the
increased flexibility.

Discussion
The presented results in Table 2 demonstrated that it is possible to identify pa-
tients with a previous MI based on the shape of the LV alone, suggesting that
adverse remodeling can be detected from static images from a single cardiac
phase. We observe that especially methods with complex decision boundaries
(i.e. SVM rbf, QDA, and the multi-layer perceptron) struggle with classifying
based on the full set of input point coordinates, which is attributed to overfitting
due to the small number of training samples. The small dataset size also makes
it difficult to make strong conclusions on whether a specific classification method
is superior to others. It is, however, demonstrated that it is beneficial to consider
methods for reducing the dimensionality of the input features. We showed that
95% of the variance in the LV shapes could be described with just 39 PCA com-
ponents, underscoring that the full set of point coordinates is highly correlated.
Classifiers operating on the PCA or PLS components generally perform better
than on the full shapes, and show impressive performance for detecting infarcts.
Using the PLS decomposition allows easy interpretation of how an LV from an
infarct patient generally remodel on a global scale. This analysis provides insights
into the specific LV shape changes associated with MI and demonstrates how our
models leverage these geometric features for prediction. The findings are in line
with what is known from medical literature, saying that the LV enlarges mainly
in the width/circumference as it still has to line up with the right ventricle.

Generally, the overall size of the LV is also correlated with remodeling from the
PLS decomposition, as the infarct variants of the mesh are generally larger than
the mean shape, while the healthy are smaller (see fig. 2(bottom right)). This
is in coordinance with a common medical hypothesis, that MI leads to radial
thinning, dilation of non-infarcted areas and global remodelling [14,19].
Our investigation into the average CDA contribution across 17 segments of
infarct samples in the test set revealed no consistent pattern in terms of which
segments were most influential in the decision-making process. This lack of a
general trend can be attributed to the unique nature of each infarct, varying in
size and location, which consequently affects the remodeling process differently
for each case. Despite the absence of a global trend, the CDA method demon-
strated reasonable accuracy in classification. This suggests that the model can
effectively learn patterns based on localized features, even when a clear overar-
ching pattern is not apparent. The ability to discern such nuanced differences
highlights the potential of this approach in analyzing complex cardiac structures.
This finding opens up an intriguing avenue for future research: exploring
how statistical analysis of LV shape can be utilized to investigate the impact of
various parameters on the remodeling process. Specifically, factors such as in-
farct position, size, and time elapsed since the infarct event could be examined in
greater detail. However, it is important to note that conducting such comprehen-
sive analysis would require a substantially larger dataset with well-documented
information on these parameters.
6
Conclusion
We have shown that MI induced adverse remodeling can be detected from car-
diac CT scans using a combination of registration and statistical shape analysis.
We presented a novel use of INRs to align all LV shapes in the dataset to a
common coordinate system and utilized the point correspondence as input fea-
tures to standard statistical classification methods. We demonstrated that PLS
decomposition can be used to highlight the shape features that are generally
associated with adverse remodelling and based on just three PLS components
we can identify nearly all infarcted patients from our test set.
