Abstract
This thesis investigates the feasibility of solving a simple human inspired
task by utilizing an aﬀordable robot and standard tools. Based on sensory
inputs, the ﬂexibility of the robot is signiﬁcantly increased, and thus creating
opportunities to solve a variety of tasks. The result presented in this thesis
is speciﬁcally based on a camera- and force sensor in combination with a
manipulator from Universal Robots. A complete test setup is constructed,
consisting of a board with a valve and several spanners. By combining the
diﬀerent sensory inputs, the overall result conﬁrms that it is feasible to solve a
given required human task with an aﬀordable robot, in this case to choose a
spanner of the correct size and manipulate a valve. The result further implies
that tasks in for instance hazardous environments can be solved autonomously
to a reasonable investment.

Resumé
Dette speciale undersøger muligheder for at anvende en billig robotarm kom-
bineret med forskellige sensor data og standard værktøj til at løse en simpel
menneske baseret opgave. Ved at anvende sensorer tilføres der betydelig ﬂek-
sibilitet til systemet, dermed kan en lang række opgaver løses. Speciﬁkt er
der anvendt et camera og en kraftsensor til at løse opgaven i dette speciale.
Et test miljø er sat op bestående af en plade med hængende skruenøgler og
en monteret ventil. Resultatet som er opnået i opgaven viser at det er muligt
at løse en menneske baseret opgave autonomt, i dette tilfælde at udvælge en
skruenøgle i den rigtige størrelse, og efterfølgende dreje en ventil. Resultatet
viser dermed også at opgaver der skal løses, for eksempel steder hvor mennesker
kan tage skade, kan udføres autonomt til en rimelig investering.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The use of robots in the manufacturing industry is something that has been
on going for many years. However expanding the ﬁeld of use is of high in-
terest.
In particular the concern with various human related aspects are
essential. Even today, with the technological momentum one could expect
that humans working in hazardous environments belongs to the past, however
this is not the case. Hazardous environments implies areas with insuﬃcient
shielding of crude oil, gases, chemicals etc. The risk of situations with any
kind of malfunctions in these environments often involves humans to interfere.
The idea of having robots to aid or preferably take over the task in these
situations is ideal. In addition to the human aspect, the idea of increasing
the autonomy is also desirable. Particularly in emergencies it is generally
desired to solve the given issue rapidly and with high responsiveness. This
naturally implies that the level of autonomy must be high for a satisfying result.
Increasing the level of autonomy involves sensor based control, which is generally
divided into the two diﬀerent control paradigms described in the following.
• Perceive-and-Action: The environment is perceived before any action
is performed. The action is then performed by only considering the
perceived state. This implies that any environmental variation after the
perception is made until and during the action is performed, is not taken
into account.
1
==PAGE:16==
2

• Feedback-Servoing: Environmental perception and actual real-time
actuation are performed simultaneously, hence no prior knowledge or
state prediction are required in order to complete the objective.
The thesis will touch upon both of the mentioned control paradigms through
diﬀerent sensors, in order to obtain the objective presented in the subsequent
section.
Another signiﬁcant matter that must be addressed when dealing with industrial
robotics is the degree of specialization. In many cases well funded companies
buys setups with a high degree of specialization in order to solve a very speciﬁc
task. This means every component in the setup i.e. type of robot, stand,
sensors, gripper etc. are carefully chosen with the goal of solving that speciﬁc
task. The prominent drawback of this approach is that the setup becomes
extremely sensitive to minor changes. Modiﬁcations will aﬀect the ability of
the system to solve new tasks. Furthermore the knowledge associated with the
development of the system is narrowed down to a small group of people. The
high degree of specialization means that similar setups most likely do not exist,
and therefore in case of malfunctions, solutions are bound to the developers.
Another matter that arises from the specialized setups is the high price, which
rules out the ability of smaller companies to invest in automated solutions.
The resources needed in order to invest in these setup is simply too high for
smaller companies. The recent emerging low-cost hardware presents several
new opportunities for smaller companies to adapt robotics in their production.
Inspired by these industrial challenges, this project will investigate the feasibility
of a low-cost system based on diﬀerent sensors and standard tools to solve a
simple human inspired task. The detailed problem statement is presented in
the following section.
1.2
Project Description
The objective of this thesis originates from the robotics competition presented
in (MBZIRC 2015). The competition contains three diﬀerent sub-challenges,
the one treated in this thesis involves a mobile ground robot having to localize
and reach a panel, and subsequently manipulating a hexagonal valve stem on

the panel. This project assumes that the mobile robot has already reached
the panel, thus only the manipulation is addressed in this work. A set of six
standard spanners (ISO 7738), is likewise present on the board. The system is
required to analyze the tools and choose the appropriate one from the panel.
The valve stem is required to be rotated at-least 360 degrees. The panel and

Modularity of the solution is furthermore one of the goals. The idea is that any
of the hardware needed to realize the objective should be replaceable without
any major modiﬁcations to the solution. For instance by changing the camera,
only the driver and the raw image format would require attention. Moreover
replacing the manipulator would be even simpler. The advantage of using the
UR5 is that all manipulators from Universal Robots (UR3 and UR10) share
the same platform, implying that only the kinematical parameters would have
to be altered.
The challenge will take place in an outdoor open arena. Considering the
location of the venue, harsh lightning conditions must be expected. This taken
into account, the tests in the project will be conducted in conditions without
any speciﬁc control of the ambient lighting. Nevertheless the highest possible
success-rate is expected because the project is part of a competition.

Chapter 2
Problem Analysis
This chapter will carry out a problem analysis by examining the thesis objectives.
Various approaches will be assessed and discussed and subsequently the most
suitable will be chosen. The requirement of modularity will be addressed and
relevant system architectures will be proposed.
2.1
Manipulator
The robot utilized in the project is a 6-DOF industrial manipulator. An
in-depth description and performance analysis of this particular robot is given
in section 3.1.
The robot is selected mainly due to availability however other solutions are
possible. The manipulation tasks can be grouped into two phases. The ﬁrst
deals with the pick-and-place paradigm, i.e. after tool detection the robot
will pick up the tool and place it on the valve. The second phase deals with
the rotation of the valve.
This relatively simple task can be solved by a
2-DOF xz-Cartesian robot which only moves linearly side to side and up and
down. The approach/depth motions is solved by exploiting the fact that the
manipulator is mounted on a mobile robot. This will account for the degree of
freedom that enables the manipulator to reach the panel.
Several advantages come with a Cartesian robot where some of the notable
ones are the price and the simpliﬁcation of complicated transformations. The
disadvantage is clearly the restricted number of possible motions leading to

unpredictable situations. For instance if the detected tool hangs diﬀerently
than expected or if the valve bents and thus needs to be rotated around a
diﬀerent axis.
2.1.1
Modeling
An important decision to be made is whether to control the robot in Cartesian
or in joint space. The decision in this case depends on the project objectives.
The number of actuated joints needed in order to perform a desired motion
determine whether it is appropriate to control in Cartesian or joint space.
For instance, if one needs to perform a simple linear two dimensional motion,
Cartesian control would be suﬃcient as the number of moving joints is minimal.
However performing a rotational motion, for instance rotating a valve, all six
joints would be actuated. The ﬁrst three perform the translation, while the
last three ensure the orientation.
Nevertheless the robot will ultimately be controlled in joint space, the only
diﬀerence is whether this is done implicitly or explicitly. Thus when controlling
the robot in Cartesian space the inverse kinematics from the manufacturer are
utilized. Alternatively one could derive the inverse kinematics and transmit
directly in joint space. In this thesis an analytical inverse kinematics solution
is utilized. The one implemented by the manufacturer has major drawbacks
that would possibly aﬀect the objective. The issue is detailed in section 5.1.2.

The camera placement is another matter that also has to be addressed. Two
fundamental conﬁgurations between the robot end-eﬀector and camera exists,

• Eye-In-Hand: The camera is ﬁxed to the moving end-eﬀector and

signiﬁcantly simpliﬁed in this conﬁguration. The camera utilized in the project
is a 2D RGB camera. A brief description of it is given in section 3.2.
2.2.2
Tactile Sensor
In the domain of robot tactile sensing one must consider the issue of compliancy.
The compliancy of a manipulator can be considered as the ﬂexibility of the
arm. Force compliancy is often divided into passive- and active forms. These
are brieﬂy described in the following.
• Passive Compliance: The mechanical structural ﬂexibility due to ﬁnite
stiﬀness in the manipulator, stand, gripper etc.
• Active Compliance: Active stiﬀness control obtained through explicit
digital sensory methods.
The two types of compliancy both have their strengths and weaknesses, however
in order to determine which approach that is suitable for the main objective,

2.3
Data Processing
The data processing throughout the project is done on an external Linux
PC and the program itself is written in C++ in order to increase the real-
time performance. The image analysis is based on the second version of the
OpenCV computer vision library. The library oﬀers an extensive number
of functions, everything from video acquisition to image ﬁltering to object
detection. Relevant data, such as the diﬀerent images throughout the image
analysis is saved. Moreover, relevant manipulator data is logged, i.e. joints
positions and velocities and lastly data from the utilized force sensor.
2.4
Summary
The choice of the utilized robot has been discussed. Considering the objec-
tives of the thesis, the task can be completed with a 2-DOF Cartesian robot.
However, as presented a robot with additional DOF introduces a more reliable
system, as robustness against unexpected situations are increased.
A sensory analysis has been presented. Two sensors are utilized in this project,
a camera and a force sensor. Based on the objectives of the thesis, appropriate
control paradigms are chosen for each sensor. The vision part of the project
is based on a percept-and-action scheme, while the force part is based on
feedback-servoing. For the latter part, the aspect of active- and passive compli-
ance has been discussed. Since an actual force sensor is used, the obvious choice
is active compliance, which is proven to be convenient for control purposes.
Finally the utilized data processing method is presented. All computations
and control are performed on an external PC. Moreover, the vision library
OpenCV is used as it contains several optimized oﬀ-the-shelf functions.

UR5 Industrial Manipulator
The UR5 is an entry level low-cost 6-DOF industrial manipulator developed
by Universal Robots. One of its well known features is that the robot is ISO

cage. The weight of the robot 18.4 kg means it can be considered a lightweight
manipulator, Moreover it has a reach of 85 cm and a maximal payload of 5 kg.

Polyscope
The polyscope is a graphical user interface in form of a touchscreen. The
polyscope enables the user to program the robot by physically moving/teach-
ing the manipulator at diﬀerent waypoints and progressively constructing a
program. Moreover, the polyscope is also equipped with an USB port which
can interpret custom written programs.
URScript
URScript is a programming language developed by Universal Robots. From
an external computer a TCP socket connection can be established to one of
the client interfaces mentioned earlier. Most modern programming languages,
such as Python and C++, includes socket libraries, which makes it possible to
transmit URScript code as strings to the internal controller.
C-API
A C library, denoted C-API, has been developed by Universal Robots if one
should desire to write a custom internal controller. Controlling the robot from
this abstraction layer would result in signiﬁcantly smaller delays. However, it is
still only possible to transmit commands at a frequency of 125 Hz. The library
is currently not supported and the documentation is far from suﬃcient at this
stage. Thus, the chosen control method for this thesis is the aforementioned
URScript. Naturally when working through a network possible transmission
delays can occur, these will be analysed along with diﬀerent URScript motion
commands in the following subsection.

5.1
Kinematics
Kinematics are utilized in order to describe the motion of the manipulator
by neglecting the forces and torques that results the motion. Kinematics are
often divided into two parts, namely forward- and inverse-kinematics. Forward
are used in order to determine the position and orientation of the end-eﬀector
based on the joint conﬁguration. The main idea of the forward kinematics is
basically to assign a coordinate frame to each joint and the corresponding links.
The inverse kinematics, as the name implies, is the method of obtaining the
joint conﬁguration based on the position and orientation of the end-eﬀector.
In the following sections the kinematics for the UR5 manipulator is presented.

Inverse Kinematics
Inverse kinematics is used to pose models that specify end-eﬀector positions and
orientations in order to obtain a corresponding set of joint angles. Manipulators
are ultimately controlled in joint space, however seen from a human perspective
relating to joint angles can be cumbersome. The ﬁeld of inverse kinematics has
been researched extensively, and usually grouped into two areas, numerical-
and analytical, which brieﬂy will be outlined in the following.
Numerical
Several approaches exists for the numerical method, and many of these are
based on the manipulator Jacobian. The work (Joubert 2008) presents some
==PAGE:60==
46
CHAPTER 5. MANIPULATOR MODELING
of the relevant numerical approaches. The approaches basically entails to
solve the inverse kinematics problem iteratively by using a sequence of steps
incrementally converging to a solution.
When commanding the UR5 manipulator in Cartesian space, the robot con-
troller utilizes its own implementation. It was discovered that the inverse
kinematics implemented by Universal Robot has one very crucial shortcom-
ing. In the conﬁguration depicted in Figure 5.1 a singularity occurs. The
nature of the singularity is that the algorithm obtains an inﬁnite number of
solutions. Speciﬁcally joint four, ﬁve and six can have an inﬁnite number of
diﬀerent positions while maintaining the same end-eﬀector position. A simple
solution-selector has not been implemented by Universal Robot. The exact
structure of the algorithm is not known and information from the manufacturer
is limited. Based on the behavior of the robot it seems that a numerical method
is implemented, as the robot tries to iterate through an inﬁnite number of
solutions. In case the robot enters the singularity and the goal is close, leaving
the singularity is possible, if not the robot will jerk.

matics. In order to obtain the velocity kinematics one must revisit the forward
kinematics. As stated, the forward kinematics deﬁnes the function between the

determined by the Jacobian of this function.

Force Control
This chapter presents the force controller implemented based on the quality
of active compliance as discussed in the analysis.
Initially, the center of

6.1
Gravity Compensation
The center of gravity is naturally of great concern when working with force sen-
sors. Particularly in the setup used in this project where additional equipment
is mounted on the sensor; a gripper and a camera in this case. The weight of
the hardware mounted obviously results a displacement of the center of gravity.
One way to counter the displacement is by biasing the input data, however
this will limit the motions of the manipulator to be only linear. In order to
perform rotational motions without aﬀecting the biased data, the hardware

Reference Estimation
The orientation of the force sensor attached to the manipulator needs to be
examined. The way the sensor is mounted means that its coordinate frame
diﬀers from the frame of the last end link of the robot. The alignment of the
frames in terms of rotation is particularly important, since correct reference
forces will have to be fed to the controller, so that it is able to insert the
spanner correctly on to the valve. Ideally, if the two frames were aligned
one could determine the axis in which the reference force would be applied.
A rotation matrix can be determined in order to describe the correct rela-
tion between the two frames. This would however require that the gripper
and camera to be removed, since the weight of these disturbs the measurements.

6.3
Control Design
In most cases the ﬁrst step in designing a controller is to model the system. A
model allows the system to be simulated and thereby appropriate gain values
can be determined. However, in this thesis an empirical approach is pursued, i.e.
the controller gain values are experimentally tuned. The motivation behind this
approach is mainly due to the setup. The setup utilized in this project contains
several physical nonlinearities. The two majors ones are the board, which is
not ﬁrmly attached, i.e. the manipulator can in some circumstances move the
board. Secondly, the valve mechanism allows for a large amount of passive
compliance. The modeling of these nonlinearities would be inappropriate and
time consuming and thereby might lead to inaccurate controller parameters.
6.3.1
Proportional Control
Assuming the manipulator can be treated like an ideal integrator and with
the internal speed controller compensating for disturbances, a simple pro-
portional controller must be expected to be suﬃciently accurate to track a
constant applied reference. The pseudo-code in the following illustrates the
implementation of the controller.

6.3.2
Proportional-Integral Control
The design of the integrator-component is like the proportional controller done
empirically. In order to choose an appropriate scale for the constant one has
to consider several transient outcomes. The important detail in this case is
that overshoot should be avoided, as it would result in joint velocities which
are too large and thus large forces. Overshoot can naturally be avoided by
choosing a small gain however that would introduce a long rise-time. The
importance of the rise-time is not as crucial as the overshoot. A long rise-time
will in worst case result in the spanner being inserted at a later stage. Taken
these considerations into account a gain of Ki = −2.5 · 10−5 is chosen.
The implementation of the integral-component of the controller is based on
the following pseudo-code.
on receive
data:
F_actual = center_of_gravity (q_pos , F_raw)
F_error = F_ref - F_actual
integrator = integrator + F_error
ctrl = Kp * F_error + Ki * integrator
vw = [ctrl (0), ctrl (1), 0, 0, 0, omega]
q_vel = solve_inverse_jacobian (q_pos , vw)
send_speed(q_vel)
The error is simply integrated up at each control iteration. The accumulated
value is subsequently multiplied with the integral gain and added to the control
signal. A similar test like in the previous section is performed, that is a valve
rotation conducted in 20 seconds. The result is shown in Figure 6.8.

6.4
Summary
In this chapter, the gravity displacement caused by the mounted gripper and
camera on the force sensor has been investigated. Through rotation of the
end link perpendicular to ground, it was observed the measured forces were
acting sinusoidally. The data were modeled and thus the forces were adjusted
corresponding to the angle of the end link.
The sensor coordinate frame does not coincide with the end link frame thus
an experimental method was found in order to determine the reference forces
for the controller.
A proportional-integral controller was designed to maintain a constant force on
the valve during rotation. The manipulator itself acts as a natural integrator
and therefore a simple proportional controller was expected to be suﬃcient.
However it was observed when applying an angular velocity, the system treated
it as a disturbance, thus an additional integrator was needed.
Moreover,
the experimental reference method has proven to be a relatively reliable and
straightforward method despite not being free from errors.

The modular components of the project are now to be combined and tested as
a whole. The individual blocks have been tested throughout their respective
chapters and have proven to successfully accomplish their individual objectives.
The image analysis block, with the task of detecting the valve- and spanners

rectangles. Moreover the image depth was determined by exploiting physical
dimensions of the board.

by matrix transformations in order to relate the results in world coordinates.
An analytical inverse kinematics algorithm is applied as moving in joint space
has proven to be most suitable method with the manipulator utilized.
Finally the actual valve rotation is performed by force control. The hardware
inspection conducted revealed that optimal manipulator control is achieved
by using joint velocities. Thus the manipulator Jacobian was derived and
subsequently utilized in the force controller. The gripper and camera utilized
are mounted on the gripper, therefore it was necessary to investigate and
model the gravitational impact on the sensor. As the angular velocity is acting
as a disturbance, a PI controller was required, as stationary errors are undesired.

Chapter 8
Conclusion and Outlook
8.1
Conclusion
The work of this thesis has shown that it is feasible to solve a human in-
spired task by using a 6-DOF manipulator, combined with diﬀerent sensor
inputs. Speciﬁcally a camera and a F/T sensor are used. By exploiting the
information acquired from these sensors it was shown that standard tools
could be used in order to solve the given task. The speciﬁc task in this case
was to choose a spanner of the correct size and subsequently manipulate a valve.
A problem analysis was initially made. The purpose of the analysis was to
investigate diﬀerent solution approaches. Due to availability a 6-DOF robot
was used from Universal Robots. However other alternatives was discussed,
particularly using a Cartesian robot could be feasible. Moreover force com-
pliancy was discussed. The domain of passive- and active compliance was
presented. Based on the objective of the thesis it was decided to adapt a
solution based on active compliance, mainly due to control purposes.
The hardware utilized in the project has been inspected. Several time delays
were discovered; a 24 ms network delay between the external PC and the robot
controller and an internal delay of 150 ms in the F/T sensor. It was shown
that the size of the latter depends on the weight of the equipment mounted on
the sensor. A complete hardware and software structure was presented, with
modularity highlighted.
75
==PAGE:90==
76
CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK
An image analysis solution was developed that could locate the objects in-
cluding the depth. The solution was proven to be satisfyingly reliable as the
standard deviation based on data from ten runs was less a millimeter.
The forward- and inverse kinematics of the manipulator was presented. More-
over the internal inverse kinematics was investigated. The investigation showed
that the manipulator in some positions could enter a singularity, due to the
internal kinematics not being able to select a solution.
A force controller was designed in order to maintain a constant force on the
valve during a rotation. The natural integrator of the manipulator combined
with a simple proportional controller was shown not to be suﬃcient. An
angular velocity was applied in order to produce the rotation. As this velocity
was acting as a disturbance, an additional integrator was required. Several
tests in diﬀerent conditions were made in order to verify the performance of
the controller.
The modular components were combined and tested as a whole. The solution
produced a satisfying result, especially in terms of repeatability. The reﬂecting
nature of the spanners were initially causing inadequate detections. The issue
was solved by adding a ring light around the camera lens and thus suppressing
the reﬂections.
Thus it has been shown that a low-cost robot combined with sensory input
is capable of solving a human inspired task. The work of this thesis thereby
opens opportunities for smaller companies with less investment capacities to
adapt robotics in their businesses.
8.2
Outlook
Several interesting topics has been encountered during the work of the thesis.
However due to limited amount of time, some of these topics have not been
suﬃciently investigated and discussed. A superﬁcial description and proposal
for future work of the topics are outlined in the following.
• The project at its current state does not account for the valve size. The
user/operator will have to deﬁne the size of the valve manually. A 2-ﬁnger
==PAGE:91==
8.2. OUTLOOK
77
gripper as the one utilized in this project, however embedded ﬁnger tips
force sensors would be a useful addition. The gripper can grasp the valve
until a desired force is obtained. Then by logging the position of the
servo one can determine the valve size.
• The work of (Stegmann 2000) presents the idea of Active Appearance
Models, which is an image segmentation method. A notable advantage
of the method is that its learning-based. Knowledge about shape and
texture are learned through observations. The method has proved to
be robust and fully automated in challenging environments in terms of
object segmentation, with very high accuracy.
• The concept of modularity has in several cases been emphasized and the
solution is implemented as modularly as possible. The logical step in
the sense of increasing the modularity even more is by introducing the
Robot Operating System platform, also denoted ROS. Data sharing in
particular is signiﬁcantly simpliﬁed in a ROS system. New sensors can
constantly be added to the system without modifying the original code.

Run Code
In order to run the code two non-standard Linux libraries are needed. The
two libraries are the the GNU Scientiﬁc Library (GSL n.d.) used to solve the
inverse Jacobian, and the OpenCV (OpenCV n.d.) library used throughout
the image analysis.
Moreover, some preparation must be made in order to run a successful execution
of the code. These are listed in the following.
• Camera calibration:
The camera must be calibrated, preferably by the method presented in
section 3.2.2. The intrinsic parameters can then be updated in vision.h.
• Board orientation:
The orientation of the board relative to the robot base frame must be
deﬁned. The angles are deﬁned in mbzirc.h.
• Capturing positions:
Two approximate joint conﬁgurations needs to be deﬁned, such that the
valve and spanners are in front of the camera. These are set in main
part in mbzirc.cpp.
• Robot and force sensor IP addresses:
The IP addresses of the UR5 and force sensors are deﬁned in mbzirc.cpp
and force.cpp respectively.
