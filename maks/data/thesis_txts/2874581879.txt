Abstract
Electrical strain gauges are widely used for wind turbine load certification measurements. In this thesis will be
discussed if the use of optical strain sensors are a better choice, and advantages and disadvantages are listed.
Strain measurements are made with both traditional electrical strain gauges and optical strain sensors of the
type Fiber Bragg Grating [FBG], the optical and electrical sensors are positioned so close that they can be
compared directly. The measurements are made on a Siemens Gamesa blade in flapwise fatigue test, all
measurement points are measured individually, and are therefore being compared sensor to sensor. The mea-
surements are made at 3 different ambient temperatures, −4◦C, 0◦C, and 9◦C, the measurements show a
dependancy on temperature on the strain measurements, and the dependancy is the same for the traditional
electrical strain gauges and for the optical strain sensors, when using the same Coefficient of Thermal Ex-
pansion [CTE]. The CTE used for the electrical strain gagues are intrinsic at 10.8µm/m/◦C, using this for the
optical strain sensors, an almost exact match between the two is found. The mean strain is shifted, up to 1%
pr degree celsius.
The optical strain measurements are combined with an optical temperature measurement, also using a FBG,
due to the separation of strain and temperature measurement, an individual CTE value can be used. When the
individual CTE values are used the mean strain is shifted, only up to 0.13% pr degree celsius.
The advantages of using optical FBG strain sensors are numerous, the main advantage is that they measure
the strain directly in contrast to traditional electrical strain gauges which measure relative strain from resistance,
FBG’s can be easily individually temperature compensated, several sensors can be on the same ”wire”, even
a combination of strain and temperature can be measured on the same ”wire”. Optical FBG sensors are not
susceptible to any kind of electricity, even lightning has no influence.
Disadvantages of optical sensors are mainly that they are brittle and the fiber cannot be bend around sharp
corners. And when using more than one FBG on one fiber, the center wavelength of the sensor must be
considered, to make sure no crosstalk happens, especially when measuring high strain widths.
Strain measurements made on the V52 turbine at Risoe are also studied, this to find any dependancy on strain
measurements from ambient temperature. The measurement used are actually converted to bending moment,
but since there is a direct linear relation between the strain measured and the bending moment, this has no
influence on the conclusions here.
With the given data from the V52 turbine, it is possible to find a clear connection between temperature and
strain (actually bending moment) measurement. The dependancy is more distinct for some cross sections on
the V52 Turbine than others, and especially in Station 2 edgewise, there is a clear dependancy on all three
blades.
Actually there is a much higher dependancy on rotorspeed, and windspeed on the strain measurements, but
the mean bending moment is shifted, up to 1% pr degree celsius.

1
Introduction
Electrical Strain Gauges has been widely used for strain measurements in the wind turbine industry for decades,
and the technology is well understood by the measurement engineers, test engineers and structural designers
of wind turbines.
Electrical strain gauges are so common in the wind industry that they are mentioned in the IEC standard for
Measurement of mechanical loads IEC61400-13 Ref. [1]. They are not just mentioned, but ”strain gauges
applied to the structure are selected as the recommended type of sensor”, see chapter 7.1.1 in Ref. [1]. And
they are therefore used as reference in the following subclauses of the standard.
The standard Ref. [1] states that when using strain gauges, precautions has to be taken regarding temperature
effects, temperature compensations and cross-sensitivity
The main reason for changing to optical fiber sensors from conventional electrical sensors are, optical sstrain
sensors measure direct strain, low weight of sensor, especially the cabling, high temperature endurance, di-
electric nature, immunity to electromagnetic interference and excellent multiplexing capabilities, see Ref. [2].
The purpose of this study is to find reasons for using or not using optical strain sensors for bending moment
measurements, instead of the traditional electrical strain gauges widely used in load certifications of wind tur-
bines.
Because it is known to be an issue, that optical sensor signals are very much dependant on temperature,
different ways to handle this is introduced or discussed.
Whenever an optical strain sensor or an optical temperature sensor is mentioned, the optical sensor is always
based on Fiber Bragg Grating [FBG] technology, except if specifically otherwise mentioned.
A study is also made on measurements from a wind turbine without any optical sensors, to see if the tradi-
tional electrical strain gauges can be assumed to be correctly temperature compensated within a full-bridge
Wheatstone configuration.

Strain measured with a Wheatstone Full-Bridge
2.1
Strain gauges
Strain gauges used in the Wind Turbine industry are normally of the type metal strain gauges. The use of metal
strain gauges are based on the strain/resistance relationship discovered by Wheatstone and Thomson in the
mid 18th century (Later Thomson became Lord Kelvin) Ref. [3].
The strain gauge was invented by Arthur Claude Ruge Ref. [4]

2.2.1
Application of Strain Gauges with Wheatstone Bridges in Wind Turbines
Depending on the way the strain gauges are positioned, and the Wheatstone bridge configured, signals can
be summed for different purposes.
In Ref. [5] numerous ways of applying Strain Gauges with a Wheatstone Bridge is shown. 15 different config-
urations are described in chapter 6, and some of these are used in the Wind Turbine Industry.
Configuration 8 is normally used measuring bending in blades, configuration 11 for bending in towers, and 13,
14 or 15 for torsion in towers and on shafts.
These are all Full-Bridge configurations using all 4 resistors as active sensors, main reason for using 4 is to
eliminate temperature dependance but also to enhance the signal. All the above mentioned configurations are
measuring either bending moment or torque.
Comparing configuration 4 and 8, here configuration 8 gives twice the signal, so with little extra effort of instal-
lation much better signal is received.
Selection of any of the configurations 13, 14, and 15 is a matter of convenience while installing and the space
given for the installation.

2.3
Advantages and limitations of Electrical strain gauges
In the IEC Standard, Ref. [1] chapter 7.1.1, it is stated: ”Full strain-gauge bridge designs offer good scope for
reduction of cross-sensitivities and temperature effects and are to be preferred for most wind turbine applica-
tions”, this statement is a very big advantage for the use of electrical strain gauges in wind turbines, and the
technology has been known for many years and are fully accepted by the engineers in the need for load and
torque measurements of structures and substructures in wind turbines.
The above is a general statement, but why and how are full Wheatstone bridge strain-gauges designs good
technically?
When mounted in a Wheatstone bridge, all 4 strain gauges contributes to the measured signal in the bridge
and only the uncertainty of one measurement channel is added.
The strain values on opposite sides of a beam are subtracted giving a combined higher bending signal, and
temperature and normal forces equals out. In all the configurations used in wind turbines, temperature effects
are reduced to zero because all strain gauges are estimated to see the same temperature change, see Ref.
[5] chapter 4.

2.3.1
Temperature compensation limitations
In eq. 2.7 it is assumed that all 4 strain gauges see the exact same temperature change, but when configuration
8 from Ref. [5] is used on wind turbine blades with R1 and R3 on pressure side and R2 and R4 on suction side,
similar for leading and trailing edge, this is not necessarily the case.
The reasons it is not the same is more or less obvious, wind turbine blades have a big diameter, glass com-
posites does not transferer heat very well, and the coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) is not necessary the
same at opposite sides of the blades, see Ref. [1], Annex I.
The big diameter gives that the temperature is not the same in the two locations where R1 and R3 verses R2
and R4 are. Now all the strain gauges are located inside the blades normally, and therefore the air temperature
might be the same or almost the same, but the two surfaces are not heated from the sun in the same manner,
because they are not subject to direct sunlight at the same time. Another issue is on the suction side the
pressure is lower than on the pressure side, this is where the naming comes from, and at low pressure the
temperature is lower than at high pressure.
If glass composites were very good at heat transfer, the problem would be less, because then the temperatures
would even out quickly.

Fiber Bragg Gratings
A Fiber Bragg Grating [FBG] is an optical sensor with a periodic laterally pattern with increased refractive index
in the core compared to the fiber core refractive index, known as grating. The spacing of the lines in the pattern
is normally denoted ΛG.
The first formation of a grating was demonstrated in 1978, see Ref. [6].
The optical fibers used for FBG based sensors are normally Single Mode [SM] fibers with a round core of
approximately 9 µm covered by a cladding of about 125 µm glass, the index of the core is usually close to 1.48
and the index of the cladding is slightly less (refractive index is dimensionless), see Ref. [7]. FBG gratings
can also be written into Multi Mode [MM] fibers, but they are normally not used for sensor applications as is
described here, because they have Mode dependancies.
Bragg resonance for reflection of the incident light, happens at the wavelength where the distance in the grating,
ΛG, is the same as half the wavelength of the incomming light, divided by the effective refractive index, neff,
see Ref. [8], this wavelength is called the Bragg wavelength, λBragg, and at this wavelength the Bragg condition
is met.
The Bragg wavelength is given by:
λBragg = 2neffΛG
(3.1)
Where neff is the effective refractive index of fiber core glass.
Fiber Bragg Gratings can have different reflectiveness, depending on the length of the pattern, and the index
modulation, see Ref. [9].
The input light comes from a laser. Depending on the application the light can span a limited wavelength band
or a broader wavelength band.
Figure 3.1: Graphical display of the working principle of FBG’s, Ref. [10]
In figure 3.1, input signal, reflected signal and transmitted signal are visualized.
For low cost applications, the interrogator wavelength only covers a small wavelength range to measure only
one FBG per interrogator channel and at limited range. Higher cost application use a broader band, normally
wavelength band from 1510 to 1590nm (some manufactures can deliver and measure broad banded light up
to 1460-1620nm).
The laser light and, the reflected light from the FBG is measured with a photo diode. An Optical Interrogator
normally holds both the laser and the photo diode, and via an optic splitter inside, both the source light and
the reflected light are measured with the same photo diode. The wavelengths are measured at rates from 1Hz

to several thousand Hz, depending on the application the Optical Interrogator is made for. Different Optical
Interrogator manufactures has different approaches to make certain the light send and the light received are
measured correctly, normally they use the power reflection spectrum of the sensor, often combined with a peak
finding algorithm to follow the Bragg reflection peak. The resolution achieved is well below a picometer, see
Ref. [11], how this is exactly done, is beyond the scope of this thesis.
The none reflected wavelenghts travel to the end of the fiber, some might be reflected by another FBG which
has a different ΛG.
If the fiber-end is cut perpendicular to the fiber, the end of the fiber will act as mirror of all light at all remaning
wavelengths and it will be impossible to separate the FBG reflected light from the reflected light from the end
of the fiber. For this reason the fiber-end must be cut in an angle compared to the fiber core; normal standard
is that fiber ends are polished at an angle of 8 deg when used with FBG’s, see Ref. [12], fibers with this polish
angled, are called APC for ”Angled Physical Contact”, this will make sure any reflected light will leak out into
the cladding instead of staying in the fiber core (it can be calculated with Snell’s law how big the angle must be
to make sure no light is reflected back into the core and transmitted back to the interrogator photo diode).

3.2
Advantages and limitations of FBG sensors
The obvious advantage is, FBG offers direct absolute strain measurement, compared to conventional electrical
strain gauge sensors.
In Ref. [17] chapter 2, a long list of additional advantages in using FBG’s can be found, Here are listed some
of these, which has the biggest influence on choosing FBG’s over traditional foil strain gauges:
• FBG can measure very high strain (> 10, 000µm/m).
• FBG match very well with composite materials, as they withstand very high repeated strain levels and are
made of glass fiber similar to the glass fibers in glass fiber reinforced composites in wind turbine blades.
• FBG are immune to electromagnetic interference (even lightning strikes).
• FBG are intrinsically passive and can therefore be positioned in high voltage areas like wind turbine
generators, transformers and converters.
• Due to the insensitivity to electromagnetic interference fields of the fiber optic sensors, there is no need
to pay attention to the installation and the inductive and capacitive couplings of the electrical systems on
the wind turbine
• On a single fiber several FBG’s can be located in a row.
• Ease of installation.
Other advantages are, but not limited to:
Advantages and disadvantages of using optical strain sensors instead of traditional strain gauges for wind turbine load certification 14
==PAGE:21==
• FBG sensors are measured individually, making it possible to have a fall-back solution if one should fail.
• Bare fiber FBG sensors can be mounted in very narrow spaces, because they are slim.
• Temperature compensation sensor can be on the exact same fiber, making sure all sensor specific pa-
rameters are the same, for easy temperature compensation.
Similarly the FBG has some limitations:
• FBG sensors are all measured individually adding uncertainty for each sensor.
• Gauge factor is only k ≃0.78 due to the photo-elastic coefficient.
• Small size of strain gauges cannot be reached with FBG patches, here bare fiber solutions must be used,
which makes the installation more cumbersome.
• The stiffness of FBG sensors are much higher than electrical foil strain gauges, so they cannot be used
on ”soft” materials. This is not a problem on metal or glass and carbon reinforced composite materials
like wind turbine blades.
• The sensing fiber core is positioned at a greater distance from specimen surface (75 −125µm for bare
fibers, the same or more for fibers in patches).
• Minimum bending radius of the fiber is > 10mm, making rosettes big and cabling difficult in tight places.
• FBG strain gauges must, in most situations, be temperature compensated.
• A broken fiber is more difficult to repair than a broken cobber cable.

Bending moment on blades
Blade bending moment measurements are according to the IEC standard, Ref. [1] ch. 7. best made with
electical strain sensors in full-bridge configurations, with 2 parallel mounted strain gauges on Pressure Side
[PS] and 2 parallel mounted strain gauges on Suction Side [SS] in one full-brigde, similarly Leading Edge [LE]
and trailing Edge [TE] in another full-brigde. Strain to moment calibration is done with known loads in known
directions, enabling a crosstalk relation between strain measurements in flapwise and edgewise directions and
thereby bending moment in flapwise and edgewise directions.
Several benefits comes from using this approach, only one measurement channel is needed for flapwise and
only one measurement channel is needed for edgewise directions. Configured correct the signal is 4 times
one strain gauge signal. And axial forces pulling in the lengthwise direction is removed, because signals from
opposites sides of the blade are substrated.
The above approach is an approximation for more than one reason.
For the approach to be exact, temperature changes must have the same influence on all strain gauges in each
full-bridge. For temperature to have the same influence, the CTE values must be the same at all strain gauge
locations and the temperature must change at the same time and at the same rate. The CTE values cannot
be expected to be the same unless the structure is exactly the same, and the temperature change rate will
properbly not be the same because of the distance from PS to SS or from LE to TE, and one half of the brigde
will be exposed to direct sunlight when the other is not and vice versa. An extra temperature difference might
come from the pressure differences.
It is not possible to directly measure any strain imbalance due to temperature with a Wheatstone full-bridge,
with two sensors on one side of the blade and two at the other side, measuring all four strain gauges with one
measurement channel.
In the REf. [1] annex I. a suggested procedure to do temperature compensation is written. It is shown that a
temperature induced variation can be significantly reduced, from approximately ±100kNm to about ±20kNm,
but it is not shown if the last ±20kNm is still due to temperature variations or load/moment variations. And the
difference in temperature change rate at the two different sides of the blade makes it more complicated than
an ordinary linear regression between measured strain and induced bending moment.
And for the approach to be exact the stiffness must be the same on both sides of the blade, for real compensation
of the axial forces. This might be close to exact for PS and SS, but for LE and TE this will properbly not be the
same, because the glass layout is very different at TE compared to LE, unless measuring in the circular root
section of the blade.
To measure any difference in stiffness the Wheatstone full-brigde is not suited, individual strain measurements,
on TE and LE, and if in doubt PS and SS, must be made.
4.1
Substraction of FBG signal from opposite sensors
When using FBG sensors for bending moment measurements in blades a similar approach as with strain
gauges in full-bridge configurations should be applied, that is to substract SS signal from PS signal (similarly
for TE and LE). This will reduce or even eliminate the axial force in the combined measurement, just as for the
use of strain gauges in full-bridge configuration.
Because the FBG sensor is slightly thicker than the electrical strain gauge foil, there will be a slight difference
in the measured strain for small bending radius applications, see Ref. [20], but when substracting sensors from
opposite sites of the blade this wil average out.
In Ref. [20] it is also shown, that the uncertainty of measurement is similar for FBG’s and for electrical strain
gauges, in a direct comparison.

Several situations can arise.
• The temperature is the same on both sides and the CTE values are the same, then there is no need for
any temperature compensation. This is how it is normally handled when using traditional electrical strain
sensors.
• The temperature is the same, but different CTE values, then the difference in CTE value gives an offset
depending on the absolute temperature, this is the same issue as if traditional electrical strain gauges is
used.
• The temperatures are different, but same CTE values, then the difference in temperature gives an offset
depending on the absolute temperature, this is the same issue as if traditional electrical strain gauges is
used, but for the optical sensors the offset is bigger if the temperatures are not known and therefore not
handled, about 2 times the offset for traditional electrical strain gauges.
• Both temperature and CTE values are different, then failure can be considerable for both optical strain
sensors and for traditional electrical strain gauge, again about 2 times for the optical compared to the
electrical.
When using traditional electical strain gauges in a wheatstone full-bridge it is not easy to handle differences in
either temperature nor CTE values, due to the nature of the Wheatstone bridge and the nature of how electrical
strain gagues are made with intrinsic CTE compensation. Any temperature compensation needs to be done
with extra temperature sensors, which can be different on the two sides, and therefore not linear in relation to
the measured strain.
When using optical FBG strain sensors with optical FBG temperature sensors for temperature compensation,
the compensation is straight forward, it is even possible to asses the CTE values if not known, from equal and
known load situations at different temperatures. Reason is the independant measurements, for both temper-
ature and strain, all needed to be known are the thermo optic constansts and the centerwavelengths for the
used FBG’s.
In the same way it could be possible to make individual measurements with traditional electical strain gauges
in combination with electrical temperature sensors, but this will need many cables, and many measurement
channels, making it a cumbersome job, whereas several or all of the optical sensors can be on the same fiber,
reducing the cabling significantly.

Data source and sensors
In this Master Thesis the measured data comes from two sources.
First, the measurements including optical sensor come from a prototype blade test performed by Siemens
Gamesa Renewable Energy [SGRE] in Denmark. The blade is equiped with several optical and electrical
strain gauges. Measurement dates, for data used here, are the 9th, the 14th of December 2023, and the 18th
of January 2024.
The optical sensors are positioned 0.20m away from the electrical sensors in the longitudinal direction of the
wind turbine blade, measuring in the same direction along the blade.
Second, traditionel electrical strain gauge time series measurements are made on the V52 turbine at Risø
campus. Measurement dates are the 6th of January, 2018, the 15th of April, 2018, the 17th of June, 2018, and
the 6th of October, 2018.
And third statistical 1-minute data from the period 1th of June, 2023, to 20th of January, 2024.
For this Master Thesis, data from mainly one cross section from each source is used, where sensors are
positioned at Pressure Side [PS], Suction Side [SS], Leading Edge [LE], and Trailing Edge [TE].
5.1
Optical sensors, SGRE blade
The optical sensors are standard sensors from Luna Inc. The datasheets for the used sensors can be seen in
Appendix A.1 (Micron optics Inc. is since October 2018 a part of Luna Inc).
The optical sensors are glued to the surface with a glue selected in cooperation between Luna and SGRE,
to make sure the glue can transfer the strain and temperature from the blade surface to the FBG inside the
sensor.
Marking up the positions for installation, and cleaning the surface takes the same effort as for electical strain
gauges, whereas gluing is very fast. Since the FBG’s can be multiplexed, several sensors can be on the
same fiber along the blade, the amount of ”cable” is reduced significantly, compared to electrical strain gauges.
Installing 40 optical strain sensor and 40 optical temperature sensor, demands only 4 fiber cables if positioned
correctly, so with 4 sensor in each cross section, 10 cross sections can be covered with 4 fiber cables. The
FBG’s can be measured over very long distances, 1km is not an issue, so for mordern blades with lengths
between 100 and 200m, the distance is not a problem. The same goes for towers, which for mordern wind
turbines are above 100m tall.
To cover 10 cross sections with cobber cables for electrical strain gauges, will take minimum 10 cables with
12 cores in each, because every strain gauge needs as a minimum 3 wires. To reduce electrical noise, the 3
wires for each strain gauge, must be electrically shielded from the strain gauge to measurement device, which
could lead to 40, 3-wire shielded cables. Cobber wires have some resistance, and because of this, wires above
100m in lengths can be an issue, and it certainly is when 200m is reached. A way to overcome this, is to place
the amplifier inside the blades, some distance from root, where it is possible to power the amplifier with 230V
or similar. But having 230V cables in the blades are a big risk concerning lightning, even the wires for the
Wheatstone bridges, powered with 5V can be a risk.
Generally is the installations of FBG sensors much less time consuming than for electrical strain gauges, less
than half the time can be expected. The main reasons are, the mordern FBG patches are very easily installed,
and the number of wires(fibers) needed are reduced significantly.
Cost of optical sensors are comparable to high fatigue electrical strain gauges, and the measurement device
prize per optical sensor, including compensation sensor, is also comparable to the prize per electrical sensors.

5.2
Electrical strain gauges, SGRE blade
The electrical strain gauges are from Vishay, the electrical strain gauge foils are glued to the surface with the
glue prescribed by Vishay, for this exact type of strain gauges foil, the datasheets for these are not shared from
SGRE side.
The electrical strain sensor setup used, has a built-in temperature compensation for a structure with a Coeffi-
cient of Thermal Expansion [CTE] equal to 10.8µm/m/◦C. The electrical strain sensors are measured individ-
ually, with only one active strain gauge in a Wheatstone bridge.
When the strain gauges are measured individually, it is possible to see the individual behavior, and it is still
possible to combine the measured values, by substracting SS from PS value and TE from LE value, resembling
a full bridge setup.
The drawback of not using a full-bridge Wheastone configuration, is the need for more measuring channels, the
accumulation measurement uncertainty from this and the need for extra cabling. The benefit is, it is possible
monitor the individual measurements, at the two different locations.

7
Conclusion
Using Fiber Bragg Grating [FBG] optical fiber sensors are a better solution than traditional electrical strain
gauges, for mainly two reasons. The FBG gives a direct measurement of strain, compared to the relative
measurement of strain gauges. And the FBG can very easily be individually temperature compensated, giving
the superior possibility to use individual Coefficient of Thermal Expansion [CTE] for each location, and even to
adjust the CTE based on the measured values, to get an almost perfect temperature compensation.
Other advantages of using FBG’s are that they are non-conducting, they can be multiplexed with several sen-
sors on the same fiber, they are immune to lightning, and they can withstand very high strain values repeatedly.
Installation time is less for FBG than for electrical sensors, and the prize of sensors and measurement device
are comparable to the prize for electrical sensors.
Disadvantages of FBG’s are among other things, the gauge factor is low, the stiffness of the fibers are high,
the fibers have minimum bending radius to be considered for the light to pass, and they need temperature
compensation.
Other obsticales are that the technology is not commonly known, the fibers are brittle and more difficult to repair
than cables to electrical sensors.
Traditional strain gauges have the big advantage, they are commonly known by both technicians and engineers,
and they are recormended in the standard for certification of mechanical loads on wind turbines IEC61400-13
Ref. [1].
Some would argue that the electical strain gauges also have the advange of giving 4 times the signal when
used correctly in a Wheatstone full-bridge configuration, but it is still a relative measurement, converting an
electrical signal to strain, whereas the FBG is a direct measurement of strain. The electrical strain gauges in
full-bridge configuration cannot be easily temperature compensated, and it is not possible to adjust for different
CTE-values in a postproces. The measured strain signal is a combination of the two sides, telling nothing about
local strain levels.
From the statistical data from the V52 Turbine at Risø, it is shown that it is, to some extent, possible to find a lin-
ear relation to temperature dependance when using Wheatstone full-bridge eletrical strain gauges installations,
in a coupling between pressure side and suction side, or leading edge and trailing edge. Without individual
temperature compensation, this way of compensation demands almost equal CTE values on opposite sides
of the blade; which was shown for optical sensors, and electical sensors on the SGRE blade, to be a crude
estimation.
