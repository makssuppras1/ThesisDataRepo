Abstract
Traditionally, Fast Breeder Reactors (FBRs) are designed as large-core, high-power reactors with a high
volume-to-surface ratio, reducing neutron leakage and providing the neutron economy required for a
high breeding ratio. When scaling down to the Small Modular Reactor (SMR) range, a higher initial
enrichment of ﬁssile material is typically necessary to compensate for the increased neutron leakage.
This industry-partnered master’s thesis aims to utilize Monte Carlo-based neutronics simulation in an
initial feasibility study to assess whether a Compact Core Sodium-Cooled Fast Reactor (SFR) design,
as proposed by 92 Research, can enable what will be referred to as a Low-Enriched Uranium-started,
Breed-and-burn, Small Modular Reactor (LEU-B&B-SMR) within the U-Pu fuel cycle. (B&B: In-core
breeding for self-consumption). If proven successful, this would, to the knowledge of the author, represent
a world ﬁrst. The early-stage results presented in this report indicate that the design has the potential
to maintain criticality over a fuel cycle exceeding 60 years before becoming criticality-limited due to
ﬁssion product accumulation. A refueling strategy was developed using depleted uranium and a variable
refueling frequency to reduce the excess reactivity from breeding, rather than using, e.g., control rods,
increasing breeding performance.

Introduction
In order for nuclear power to successfully scale up and contribute substantially to the global energy
mix in the long term, the breeding of fertile into ﬁssile material will become increasingly necessary.
However, classical breeder reactors might not be ﬁt for export to developing countries due to the cost and
proliferation risk of requiring fuel recycling during the cycle. Breed-and-burn (BNB) reactors oﬀer in-core
breeding for self-consumption without reprocessing, potentially providing a more proliferation-resistant
solution.
This thesis seeks to investigate the prospects of scaling down BNB reactors into the small modular
reactors (SMR) range −from several to a few hundred MW(e) −while using low-enriched uranium (LEU)
as a starter fuel. SMRs aim to achieve shorter construction times, resulting in projects that are more
easily ﬁnanced and planned into industrial strategies, essentially making nuclear power more compatible
with free-market capitalism.
The engineering challenge of down-scaling a BNB reactor into the SMR power range comes with a
tradeoﬀbetween core volume (leakage), breeding performance, and enrichment (criticality), as depicted
in the Venn diagram in Figure 1. Decreasing the power and thus core volume lowers the volume-to-surface
ratio, thus increasing the leakage fraction. In order to ensure criticality, typically the initial enrichment is
increased, such as is the case for the TerraPower Natrium reactor −a sodium-cooled fast reactor (SFR)
−using up to 19.75% enriched high assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU) [1], labeled a HALEU-BNB-
SMR in the diagram. However, increasing enrichment lowers the breeding ratio since more neutrons are
being spent on ﬁssion events rather than breeding. This thesis provides the initial feasibility study on a
Compact SFR design concept by the thesis-industry partner, 92 Research, that uses a lower enrichment.
Speciﬁc design details are withheld, but materials are not uncommon for SFRs. If deemed feasible, this
Compact SFR design could be further investigated to serve as a LEU-BNB-SMR.

1.1. BREED-AND-BURN REACTORS
1.1
Breed-and-burn reactors
The current global ﬂeet of nuclear power plants consists almost exclusively of open uranium fuel cycle,
also known as a once-through fuel cycle, using no reprocessing of spent nuclear fuel. These reactors −
mostly Pressurized- (PWR) and Boiling Water Reactors (BWR) aka. Light Water Reactors (LWR) −
are not able to achieve net breeding of fertile material into ﬁssile, resulting in a low discharge burnup of
approximately 5% ﬁssions per initial metal atom (FIMA) [2] (U.S. annual average is below 50 MWd/kgHM
[3]), a low overall fuel eﬃciency of around 0.05% of mined uranium undergoing ﬁssion, as well as leaving
unﬁssioned actinides (with long half-lives) in the waste stream.
This is alleviated in traditional breeder reactors aiming to close the fuel cycle by having a positive
breeding gain, using reprocessing to recover the ﬁssile material. Sometimes called seed-and-blanket reac-
tors, classical breeders employ a fertile breeding blanket outside of the core to transmute fertile material
into ﬁssile material. The processes of ﬁssion and breeding are spatially separated such that the bred
blanket material is largely free of ﬁssion products, greatly reducing activity and thereby reducing the
complexity and cost of reprocessing. This fuel recycling comes with both an economic expense and a
proliferation risk if actinides are removed.
The purpose of a breed-and-burn (BNB) reactor is to provide a compromise between the two cases by
combining the increased fuel eﬃciency of a breeder reactor with the economic aﬀordability of an open
fuel cycle, without the need for fuel recycling during the fuel cycle. BNB operation refers to an open, but
self-sustainable fuel cycle, achieved via in-core breeding of fertile material into ﬁssile material for in-situ
consumption, without the use of a breeding blanket or reprocessing during the cycle, only after the end
of life.
According to [2], the idea of the BNB fuel cycle dates back at least as far as the Second United Nations
International Conference on the Peaceful Uses of Atomic Energy in 1958, where George Feinberg referred
to the practicality of not having to reprocess the spent fuel during the cycle [4]. Notable contributions to
the ﬁeld include the research by Klaus Fuchs on a homogeneous BNB reactor [5], and later, the work by
Edward Teller on a gas-cooled, thorium-fueled BNB reactor [6] for automated long-duration operation.
There are two main types of BNB reactors: (1) the traveling wave reactor (TWR) where the fuel is
static but the ﬁssion wave travels, and (2) the standing wave reactor (SWR) where the fuel is moved and
the ﬁssion wave is stationary. TWRs oﬀer simplicity in design since fuel shuﬄing need not be possible;
however, the accumulated neutron ﬂux (ﬂuence) integrated over long operational lifetimes with static fuel
elements poses technical challenges for materials. In SWRs, as high burnup fuel elements are removed
from the high ﬂux area, having become net neutron absorbers, and are relocated to lower ﬂux regions,
the ﬂuence requirements on fuel and cladding are also reduced compared to TWRs. [2]. The BNB reactor
simulated in this thesis is an SWR since refueling is simulated, and thus the fuel is not static.
Maintaining a positive breeding gain without fuel reprocessing sets stringent requirements.
Tradi-
tionally, to achieve BNB, reactors must have (1) a hard neutron spectrum to reduce parasitic neutron
capture in FPs and structural materials, (2) a large core volume to minimize neutron leakage, and (3)
an actinide-dense fuel type that enhances both of the previous factors.
Due to the ﬁnite nature of ﬁssile resources, increasing the average fuel eﬃciency of the global ﬂeet
will become increasingly important if nuclear power is to be scaled up to support a substantial portion
of global energy consumption in the long term [7][8].
The BNB reactor concept might oﬀer a more
proliferation-resistant solution for exporting breeder reactors to new nuclear countries, enabling a long-

1.2
Small modular reactors
This section will deﬁne the term Small Modular Reactor (SMR), with the main references being [9] and
[1], as well as place it in the context of this project.
Small Modular Reactor is an umbrella term that includes any type of reactor with an electrical power
output in the range of several to a few hundred MW(e). Reactors with even lower electrical outputs
are called Micro Reactors (MR), a subset of SMRs.
SMRs encompass a variety of diﬀerent reactor
technologies from both Generation III, such as advanced Pressurized Water Reactors (PWR) and Boiling
Water Reactors (BWR) and Generation IV, such as liquid metal cooled and molten salt reactors.
With their reduced size and upfront cost, SMRs aim to make low-carbon nuclear energy more broadly
available in new countries where large reactors are unsuitable.
Key drivers of SMRs include ﬂexible
generation proﬁles to accommodate renewable energy generation and higher outlet temperatures, which
improve thermal eﬃciency and enable industrial-grade steam. This opens up new industrial business
cases such as low-carbon hydrogen, steel, cement, maritime propulsion, and other hard-to-abate sec-
tors. The use of modularized construction and on-site assembly of factory-manufactured components
aims to shorten construction times and enable scaled deployment and ease of ﬁnancing. For example,
TerraPower’s Natrium reactor aims for ∼36 months from construction start to fuel load [10], while for
reactors connected to the grid between 2014 and 2023, the median construction time was 11 years [11].
According to the IAEAs Advanced Reactor Information System Database (ARIS), 68 reactor designs
are under development globally, although not all are intended as commercial products. Of these, 22 are
water-cooled reactors (WCRs) and 46 are non-WCRs: 14 land-based and 6 marine-based WCRs, 14 gas-
cooled reactors (GCRs), 11 molten salt reactors (MSRs), 10 liquid-metal fast-neutron reactors (including
SFRs), and 13 micro-reactors (<30 MW(th)).
The IAEA points out that despite advances, technical hurdles remain in areas such as control room
staﬃng and human factors engineering for multi-reactor power plants, the applicability of existing codes
and standards, supply chain and manufacturing approaches for novel components, fuel supply and enrich-
ment, as well as fuel cycle back-end solutions. While some SMR developers aim for eased regulation, such
as reduced emergency planning zones and the central operation of multiple reactors, regulatory discus-
sions remain ongoing. Although SMRs promise lower upfront unit capital costs, this comes at a trade-oﬀ
with economies of scale; thus, the overall economic competitiveness of SMRs has yet to be proven.
1.3
Enrichment
Uranium enrichment refers to the process of increasing the ratio of ﬁssile 235U to fertile and ﬁssionable
238U. While natural uranium consists of only 0.711% 235U, the existing ﬂeet of LWRs uses fuel with
up to <5% enriched uranium. Many advanced reactors aim to employ high-assay low-enriched uranium
(HALEU) which by deﬁnition is (5%−19.75% enrichment). The deﬁnition of low-enriched uranium (LEU)
is <20% enrichment of 235U.
However, as of 2024, the only facility producing HALEU on a commercial scale is located in the Russian
Federation. The USA only produces low quantities of HALEU for experimental reactor purposes, though

1.4
Sodium-cooled Fast Reactors
This section will provide a brief introduction to Sodium-cooled Fast Reactors (SFRs), with the main
reference [12]. The Sodium-cooled Fast Reactor uses liquid sodium-metal as a coolant in combination
with metallic or mixed oxide fuel, operating in the fast neutron spectrum. It enables high-power density
and higher temperature operations at low pressure. While challenges remain before deployment, the
combined global experience for SFRs boasts a total of 400 reactor years of operation among 20 reactors,
more than any other Generation IV reactor type. SFRs allow for the burning of transuranic elements
and oﬀer the potential for closing the fuel cycle, thus improving fuel utilization and reducing radioactive
waste. In Figure 2, a schematic of the Generation IV International Forum (GIF) SFR concept power
plant is shown, and the GIF reference parameters are displayed in Table 1. Relevant to this thesis is
the high power density of SFRs, which allows for compact core designs, as well as the ability of SFRs to
operate in various modes, including the "Breed-and-Burn" mode, as employed by the TerraPower atrium
reactor. SFRs date back to the EBR-1 at the Idaho National Laboratory in 1950. Today, seven SFRs are
in operation in Japan, China, Russia, and India, and another seven are being pursued globally in Japan,
France, the USA, Russia, and Canada, of which six are SMRs.

1.5
Homogenized model of the Compact SFR
A generic homogenized model was employed for the Compact SFR in the neutronics calculations per-
formed in this thesis. For fast reactors, with long mean free path of the neutrons (of several cm), such
a homogenization is decent for moddelling basic physics. Here, "homogenized" means that the average
atomic densities of the regions are preserved, but all isotopes are mixed homogeneously within larger
regions. In the core region, for example, the atomic densities of the isotopes in the cladding, fuel, and
coolant are all preserved but averaged over the entire core region. This limits the scope of the thesis
to focus on general physics rather than the speciﬁcs of the design. Actual values, such as dimensions,
materials, material densities, and initial fuel enrichment, will not be disclosed.
The geometry is plotted in Figure 3 as seen from the side and from above, with a quarter-segment
cut out to make the interior regions visible. Regions include the outward-facing radial reﬂector (brown),
the top and bottom axial reﬂectors (gray), and the core region on the inside.
What can be said of
the materials used, is that they are not uncommon for SFRs. The relevant part of the design is the
initial uranium enrichment level, and the core volume. Both are lower than, for example, the TerraPower
Natrium reactor By increasing the overall atomic density in the core, the enrichment can be lowered while
still achieving criticality. The spatial distribution of the neutron ﬂux is shown in Figures 4 & 5. Note that

2.1.2
The ﬁssion chain reaction
The emitted neutrons facilitate a chain reaction, where the released neutrons lead to new ﬁssion events.
Fissile isotopes may undergo thermal ﬁssion (induced by neutrons in thermal equilibrium with the sur-
rounding medium), whereas ﬁssionable isotopes are only capable of undergoing fast ﬁssion. Fertile isotopes
may be transmuted into ﬁssile isotopes via neutron capture. ν refers to the spectrum-averaged number
of ﬁssion neutrons emitted per ﬁssion event, averaged over a given ﬂux spectrum, and χ(E) states the
probability that the ﬁssion neutron is born with a certain energy E.
In a nuclear reactor, the eﬀective multiplication factor keff is one of several equivalent methods for
measuring the state of the ﬁssion chain reaction within the core. For a critical reactor, of the two to
three neutrons emitted from each ﬁssion event, exactly one neutron initiates a new ﬁssion event, thereby
sustaining a controlled chain reaction. If the neutron cycle represents the cycle between ﬁssion events,
the neutron gain or eﬀective multiplication factor is deﬁned,
keff = #neutrons at some point in current cycle
#neutrons at same point in previous cycle

the reactor is supercritical. If the chain reaction is sustained keff =1with a combination of prompt and

by prompt neutrons alone, the reactor is prompt critical. If keff > 1 with prompt neutrons alone, the
reactor is in an uncontrolled exponential power increase, known as a super prompt criticality accident. To
enable straightforward interpretation of reactor criticality from the sign alone, δk ≡keff −1 is utilized,

2.1.4
Neutron reactions
Being electrically neutral, neutrons do not interact through the Coulomb force and may travel through
materials hindered only atomic nuclei, via the strong nuclear force, in which case a variety of reac-
tions may occur. The most important for nuclear ﬁssion include (a) neutron absorption which includes
both radiative neutron capture denoted (n, γ) and ﬁssion reactions, (b) scattering, and (c) and neutron-
producing inelastic scattering reactions (n, xn), where an incident neutron reaction leads to the emission
of x neutrons in total. In the case of elastic scattering the energy transfer is purely kinetic, while for
inelastic scattering the target nucleus is exited to a higher energy level (or inelastic level), absorbing an
energy packet which will be released later as a γ photon. The exit angle of the secondary neutron (the
emitted neutron) for thermal scattering on hydrogen atoms and certain small nuclei is often assumed to
be isotropic (e.g., in OpenMC) while for fast neutrons and large nuclei, the exit angle is anisotropically
distributed and must be calculated as such.

The Q-value generally refers to the net energy released or absorbed during any nuclear reaction.
Exothermic reactions where energy is released have Q > 0, while endothermic reactions where energy
is required or absorbed have Q<0. In the context of neutron scattering, the Q-value shows the amount
of kinetic energy that is lost or gained by the neutron as a result of the interaction. For inelastic scat-
tering, the nucleus is excited to a higher energy state, called an inelastic level. The nucleus may have
multiple inelastic levels at speciﬁc energy levels above the ground state, each corresponding to possible
Q-values in the scattering process.

2.1.8
Depletion
Depletion or burnup refers to the change in the isotopic composition over time of a material subject
to particle or γ radiation, where isotopes can undergo nuclear reactions such as ﬁssion, transmutation,
and decay. In a nuclear reactor, neutron-induced ﬁssion, transmutation, and subsequent decay dominate
this process. These processes determine the (1) ﬁssion of ﬁssile and ﬁssionable material, (2) breeding of
fertile material into ﬁssile, (3) accumulation (and ﬁssion) of minor actinides (MA), accumulation of ﬁssion
products (FP), and (4) activation of structural material via (n, γ)-reactions (not technically depletion).
The equation used to calculate the isotopic change over time is known as the Bateman equation,

2.2
The Monte Carlo method
The Monte Carlo method (MC) is a statistical technique used to estimate the expected value of a random
parameter and its associated statistical uncertainty. In the context of reactor physics, this method is
applied to directly simulate a population of neutrons by sampling the life cycle of individual neutrons from
birth, simulating their transport and interactions throughout the reactor geometry until their termination

==PAGE:19==
2.2. THE MONTE CARLO METHOD
through absorption or leakage. Key parameters indicative of average behavior are recorded, referred to
as tallying or scoring.
This is a stochastic method for obtaining the neutron ﬂux, reaction rates, eﬀective multiplication factor,
etc., in contrast to deterministic methods solving the Boltzmann equation for the angular ﬂux and keff,
such as the discrete ordinates method or method of characteristics. The Monte Carlo method obtains the
same results by simulating a random walk of neutrons through the reactor geometry without explicitly
solving the Boltzmann equation.
Monte Carlo simulations can be either analog, where the individual neutron is simulated explicitly
without simpliﬁcations, or non-analog where various simpliﬁcations and techniques are employed to en-
hance computational eﬃciency, such as estimating the ﬂux rather than counting every single reaction,
or by improving the random walk algorithm utilizing weights to terminate the tracking and scoring of
less important neutrons. Both methods are employed in OpenMC, where the ﬁnal result is an average of
analog and non-analog results.

2.2.3
Non-analog Monte Carlo
The above describes analog Monte Carlo, where every particle and physical event is simulated as closely
to reality as possible.
Methods have been designed to minimize the number of calculations for the
same statistical certainty, called non-analog or implicit Monte Carlo. Techniques include improving the
statistics of the reaction rates when the number of reactions is low and improving the random-walk
algorithm using delta-tracking, as explained earlier, and weighting scoring neutrons more frequently the
more they contribute to the results.
The parameters estimated by the analog MC depend on directly scoring physical interactions and
counting the number of events: R is obtained by counting the number of scattering, absorption, and
ﬁssion reactions, etc.; the keff is obtained as the ratio between the number of neutrons in subsequent
generations, and the prompt neutron lifetime is obtained by scoring and averaging the time from emission
to absorption or escape. Instead, the non-analog method calculates the ﬂux, as a collision estimate, track-
length estimate, or surface and current estimates, and multiplies it by the response function, e.g. the XS
of a speciﬁed reaction (see Equation 43). All rely on using the analog method, where, instead of scoring
all sub-interactions, only total quantities are scored from which the total ﬂux is estimated.
In the collision estimate, an estimate of only the total reaction rate is obtained using the analog method,
which is then divided by the total XS to obtain the ﬂux. The ﬂux is then used to ﬁnd any speciﬁc reaction
rate by multiplying it with the speciﬁc macroscopic cross section. This is more eﬃcient than the pure
analog method.
For the track-length estimate, the total track length of the neutron through the region is recorded by
summing individual tracks created and destroyed at points of interaction or at cell boundaries, which are

then divided by the volume of the region. Since no interactions are recorded, this is more eﬃcient than
the collision-estimate and works in optically thin regions (low interaction density, Σt×d ≪1) or even in
a vacuum. It cannot be used in combination with the delta-tracking method, in which surface crossings
are not recorded.
For the surface estimate, an analog estimate of the number of surface crossings (in any direction) is
calculated for each surface and divided by its area. By applying a ﬁlter to only allow the scoring of
neutrons of certain solid angle ranges, this allows for the estimation of the net current (the diﬀerence
between the surface crossings in either direction), as well as the angular ﬂux. This method allows for
the ﬂux to be estimated for regions of inﬁnitely thin volume. Again, this method is incompatible with
delta-tracking, since it also needs the recording of surface-crossings.

2.2.7
Convergence
When a neutron is absorbed by ﬁssionable material leading to ﬁssion −using OpenMC terminology −
the information of the ﬁssion site is logged in the ﬁssion bank and will serve as the starting point for the
next generation. The neutron transport thus takes the form of an eigenvalue equation when a ﬁssionable
material is present, since the source of the neutrons will depend on the neutron ﬂux itself: the higher the
neutron ﬂux, the more ﬁssion events occur.
For the very ﬁrst simulation, the ﬁssion bank is empty, and an initial guess must be made for the spatial,
angular, and energy distributions of the neutron source, e.g., an isotropic point source of a uniform energy
distribution. No matter the initial guess, eventually, the method will converge on the same results; it
might simply take fewer inactive batches if the guess is closer to the end result.
For successive batches, the ﬁssion source distribution is now determined by the ﬁssion sites tallied in the
previous generation. This way, for each generation, the source site distribution approaches convergence
to the true result, after which the simulation can be run for as many generations as needed until the
desired statistical uncertainty is reached. The question is, how can the convergence be determined?
A popular method of determining the convergence of the ﬁssion sites is to calculate the entropy of the
spatial distribution, called the Shannon Entropy. This acts as a function that takes as input the point
distribution in Euclidean space, giving as output a scalar value, the evolution of which can be tracked
over successive generations. While the value itself is less important, its evolution over repeated neutron
generations provides the relevant information. Once the entropy has converged and is no longer changing
between generations, it can be deduced that the distribution is also unchanged between generations [24].

4.1
Case 1: Once-through depletion
Each case rely on using depletion calculations in OpenMC as described in Section 3.5, based on the
methods described in Section 2.2. The ﬁrst step employs a standard depletion calculation with a 1-year
time step, calculated for several decades. Depletion calculations allow the simulation of several decades
of reactor operation, taking into account the changes in isotopic composition over time as a result of
ﬁssion, breeding, and transmutation. This will provide a theoretical reference to what would happen if
the reactor operated for 80 years without intervention. The goal of the ﬁrst step is to see wether the
reactor goes critical with the lowered level of enrichment, and to see if there will be a positive net breeding
gain. If so, keff, when plotted as a function of time, will initially increase, until after some period of,

Were it not for the FP accumulation, the reactor could have continued operating achieving a much higher
burnup, limited only by the availability of fertile material and the amount of breeding. Both of these
eﬀects will be sought to be mitigated in the next two steps of the development of the method, in the
Cases 2 and 3, through the implementation of refueling, in the aim of keeping keff as close to unity as
possible over the operational lifetime of the reactor, which allows the estimation of the discharge burnup
and other analysis.

classmethod openmc.Material.mix_materials(). Here, a list of class openmc.Material() objects is
taken as input, along with a list of fractions summing to unity and a percentage type: "atomic/molar,"
"weight," or "volume." The refueled material is then created by mixing feed fuel and depleted fuel in a
speciﬁed ratio. From the mix_materials source code it is seen that OpenMC converts each material
to actual number densities in units of "atoms/cc" (cubic centimeter) before mixing, meaning it is not
required for the materials to have the same density type. This is relevant since the depleted materials
are of density type "sum" where the material density is given by the nuclide densities, while the feed fuel
and all manually deﬁned materials used here are of density type "g/cm3" where number densities is given
from a set material density and composition.
During refueling, each of the 28 Burnup Materials of the subdivided core (with equal volume) was
refueled using the new feed fuel and the depleted burnup-zone material from the previous depletion step,
in a volume ratio of 1/200 to 199/200. This volume ratio was found to mitigate the increase in reactivity
from breeding at the Beginning of Life (BoL).
OpenMC also supports a built-in option to add continuous removal or addition of nuclides through
a transfer rate term.
Mathematically, this adds a transfer rate term T to the Bateman equation in
Equation 14, and while not used in this thesis, it is useful for simulating liquid fuel reactors such as
molten salt reactors (MSR). Here, continuous reprocessing can be used for online refueling, increasing
the plant capacity factor on an all-else-equal basis, as well as FP removal, enhancing the neutron economy,
increasing the conversion factor, and preventing criticality-limitation from FP accumulation.
In an SFR, however, with a solid fuel, FP can only be (re)moved via refueling or reshuﬄing (not
considered here). Thus the best case scenario is to prolong the inevitable criticality-limitation from FP
accumulation by employing a variable refueling frequency, and fuel reshuﬄing which was not considered.
4.3
Case 3: Variable-frequency refueling campaign
The third and ﬁnal step in the development of the method, is to allow the refueling frequency to vary.
It would have been equivalent to vary the refueling amount. With the aim of reducing changes in the
multiplication factor over the operational lifetime, a simple algorithm was implemented ad hoc to vary
the time between refuelings. Since in Case 2, the constant refueling amount and interval were selected
as to ensure that, the initial increase in keff from breeding was counteracted by a similar-sized decrease
from refueling. As discussed, the accumulation of ﬁssion products eventually leads to a decrease in keff.
29
==PAGE:33==
4.3. CASE 3: VARIABLE-FREQUENCY REFUELING CAMPAIGN
Thus, the algorithm need only deal with increasing the refueling interval, to allow more time for breeding,
counteracting the long-term downward trend in keff. Several more complex algorithms were tested before
the following simpler approach was implemented:
1. Calculate a 3-step running average kavg of the ﬁrst step after refueling and last step before next
refueling, for the previous 3 steps, meaning a total of 6 data points.
2. Before starting the next depletion calculation, if kavg is below a predetermined limit klim, and
dropping (kavg(n) < kavg(n−1)), increase the refueling interval with some percentage fraction.
3. When eventually keff no longer increase during operation, there should be no more refuelings as
these will accelerate the decline of keff. Let the calculation run without refuelings until ρ < 500
pcm.
Here n is the latest depletion step. The running average was needed since the individual step results
show some amount of volatility and do not conform perfectly to the overall trend. Initially, only a 5%
increase in refueling interval is suﬃcient, but after 1-2 decades, a 20% increase per step was required. A
maximum time step for individual calculations was set to 2 years. Any refueling intervals larger than this
were subdivided such that no individual depletion calculation in OpenMC was longer than this limit. The
progress was monitored as shown in Figure 14, such that the algorithm could be updated when necessary.
Initially, more sophisticated algorithms were tested but were eventually deemed unpractical. These
methods included letting Tnext be a function of a) the accumulation of ﬁssion products, b) the diﬀerence
between kavg and klim, and c) the rate of descent of kavg. The issue with b) and c) is that the increase
in time step is too gradual after crossing the limit rather than a substantial sudden increase. This allows
kavg to drop too low below klim before the interval length is increased enough to make up for the drop in
k-eﬀ. This leads to an oscillatory behavior of klim over time. While a) is potentially a better candidate
for creating a general algorithm, this was not pursued due to time constraints when the simpler ad hoc
algorithm enabled the desired results.

5.2.1
Refueling noise
It was noted that the mix_materials() function introduces numerical noise in the range ∼±10−15
systematically into the isotopic atom densities of the created material; however, this occurs independently
between instances of applying the function, such that errors do not accumulate. This observation was
derived from read/write tests, where a relative change on the order ±10−16 was noted across nuclides. The
noise for all nuclides of the same material has the same sign and magnitude. From the mix_materials()
source code, it can be seen that the mixing ratios are converted to weights using the sum function on a
ﬂoating array, which introduces numerical noise with a random sign to the weight, which is then applied
to every nuclide of the material.
The implications of the results presented in this work indicate that a larger independent error is to
be expected between individual refueling steps. This error, however, is independent and should not be
expected to skew the results in a systematic sense. With 28 materials, around half exhibit a slight positive
mass change, while the other half show a slight negative mass change, in principle, canceling each other
out.
5.3
Mass deﬁcit
Ideally, the combined mass of the heavy metals and the ﬁssion products in the core is largely conserved
during depletion, since every ﬁssion reaction of an actinide will result in ﬁssion products of equal mass
being created; however, this is not entirely true, as a small mass defect is expected from both neutron
leakage and mass-to-energy conversion via E = mc2. The contribution from m/E-conversion was esti-
mated to be on the order of ∼50 [g/yr], calculated using the operating power and the speed of light c.
The contribution from n-leakage and parasitic absorption (PA) outside the core was found to be on the
order of ∼100 [g/yr]. This was determined by tallying the n-absorption reactions in non-core regions,
as well as the neutron ﬂux through the outer boundary, multiplied by the neutron mass mn. Finally,
the tallies were normalized using the power and heating score, in the method described in the OpenMC
documentation 8.3. Normalization of Tally Results. Thus, the true expected mass defect was found to
be on the order of ∼0.15 [kg/yr] for the Compact SFR.
Note, however, that using a reduced depletion chain, as discussed will result in a larger FP mass deﬁcit
as discussed in Section 3.5, since there will be ﬁssion events where no FP is created. Indeed the results
show the core losing ∼1.8 × 10−4 [fraction of the initial HM mass / year], or ∼30 times the expected
true mass loss described before. This is visible in the mass evolution plots such as in Figure 21, where the
FP mass increase is less than the HM mass decrease. It was tested that switching between the simpliﬁed
chain and a more exhaustive chain "ENDF/B-VII.1 Chain (Fast Spectrum) - version 0.12+" reduced the
mass loss to ∼160 [g/yr] which is on the order of the estimated expected value. This undercounting
of nuclides from reduction of depletion chains is recognized within the reactor physics community. One
article implements the tracking of "pseudo-nuclides" in order to preserve decay heat when simplifying the
chain [31].

5.4
Normalization mode
As stated earlier, it was found that using the normalization mode ’energy-deposition’ rather than the
default ’ﬁssion-q’ would more accurately normalize the tallies, and thus the burnup. Where the default
mode only includes the heat from ﬁssion, ’energy-deposition’ includes heating from scattering and photons.
However, due to a coding error, only the very ﬁrst depletion steps in the presented results were calculated
with the correct normalization. The conclusions should therefore keep in mind that the burnup might
be diﬀerent going forward. It is unclear how much such an error would compound over the length of a
60+ Y depletion calculation. It was found that using the correct normalization, slightly larger amount of
refueling would be necessary initially to reduce changes in keff from breeding, but it was not determined
how much longer or shorter in terms of years and burnup the reactor could be kept critical. This should
be kept in mind for future work and going forward with the design.

Three-step approach
In Section 4 is shown the iterative approach, where Cases 1 and 2 are used to develop the method of
the variable refueling campaign in Case 3; the diﬀerence between each case demonstrates the eﬀect on
criticality, breeding performance, and safety of applying the iteration. A similar approach was found in
[30][Figure 4.5], simulating the eﬀects of applying ﬁssion product removal on a 3 GWth Molten Chloride
Fast Reactor (MCFR) in the BNB mode using 10at% 235U/239Pu enrichment. First, they simulate a
reference case labeled "No treatment at all" (similar to the reference calculation in Case 1), and then,
building on that, they apply ﬁssion product removal: ﬁrst gaseous, then metallic, and ﬁnally, to reduce
the reactivity increase, they remove fuel salt (similar to the refueling campaigns in Cases 2 and 3).
Comparing the reference cases ("No treatment at all" vs. "Case 1"), the MCFR and the Compact SFR
behave very similarly. For the MCFR, the reactivity increases from about 1.00 to 1.04 in 30 estimated
full power years (EFPY) before dropping and becoming criticality-limited after about 65 EFPY. This
behavior is very similar to Case 1 in Figure 18, which hints at the validity of the results, keeping in mind
that two very diﬀerent systems are being compared.
7.2
Burnup
The MCFR is then demonstrated to achieve a much higher burnup than the Compact SFR due to the
online removal of ﬁssion products. This validates the argument that the reason the Compact SFR becomes
criticality-limited is the accumulation of ﬁssion products.
Comparing burnup to another BNB SFR,
the TerraPower Natrium reactor (a 19.75%-enriched HALEU-BNB-SFR), claims to achieve a discharge
burnup of 150 MWd/kg over 30 years [] which is about three times higher than that of WCRs. Figure
18 indicates that the Compact SFR, started on LEU, might achieve a burnup of ∼150 MWd/kgHM
over some 70 years (Natrium has higher operational power). Again, quite diﬀerent systems are being
compared.
7.3
Breeding ratio
To validate the breeding ratio and eﬀective delayed neutron fraction [32] they found a breeding ratio of
1.256 at the Beginning of Life (BoL) which is signiﬁcantly higher than that of the compact SFR at BoL.
The eﬀective delayed neutron fraction βeff = 737.8 pcm is similar to that of the Compact SFR. This
paper speciﬁcally investigated decreasing the initial enrichment from 11.9% to 9.9% by employing a more
compact UN fuel for the Sustainable Nuclear Reactor In SwEden (SUNRISE)-LFR, ﬁnding an increase
in BR of about 20%.

8.1. NORMALIZATION MODE AND TUNING ENRICHMENT FOR CRITICALITY
8.1
Normalization mode and tuning enrichment for criticality
The cases presented shows keff ≈1.012 at T = 0, rather than tuning e.g. the enrichment to achieve an
initial reactivity at the margin imposed at ρmargin =500 pcm. It should be mentioned that Case 3 was
in fact redone in exactly this way, by reducing the initial enrichment level ever so slightly. This resulted
in a slightly higher breeding ratio across the entire lifetime, but the discharge burnup and equivalently
the operational lifespan remained unchanged. Another reason for redoing the depletion simulations, was
to use the proper normalization mode −"energy-deposition" rather than "ﬁssion-q" −as discussed in
Section 3.6. Accidentally, "ﬁssion-q" was used for Cases 1 through 4, due to a coding error (the very
ﬁrst step was calculated with "energy-deposition", while all subsequent steps used "ﬁssion-q"). However
redoing Case 3 showed that the results where virtually unchanged an thus to avoid confusion, only the
original four cases was presented here, as to clearly state each step in the development of the method,
before arriving at the ﬁnal results in Case 3.
8.2
Reducing the reactivity span
8.2.1
Control rods
Further work would include simulating a reference case involving the use of control rods to manage excess
reactivity and maintain criticality during actual reactor operation, as well as its impact on breeding
performance. The results presented here indicate a reactivity span on the order of 300 pcm. This is
manageable in actual reactor operations and can be addressed, for example, via gradual insertion of
control rods during operation, counteracting the keff-increase from breeding, followed by withdrawal of
the rods after refuelings, which counteracts the keff-decrease resulting from the removal of ﬁssile isotopes
and the addition of fertile. Reducing the reliance on control rods will enhance neutron economy. This can,
in principle, be achieved with the current model by decreasing both the refueling amount and the length
of the interval, thereby reducing the reactivity span. However, merely increasing the refueling frequency
incurs signiﬁcant costs, as it reduces reactor up-time. Instead, one possible solution could involve the
use of 238U-"control rods" that may be gradually inserted, facilitating a form of online refueling, either
between refueling intervals or as an alternative to traditional refueling.
8.2.2
Fertile control rods
8.3
Heterogeneous model
This work is predicated on the homogeneous refueling of a homogenized "smeared out" model, which
preserves the true average atomic composition of the entire core material. This approach serves as a
satisfactory ﬁrst approximation for an initial feasibility study; however, in an actual reactor context,
higher-burnup pins can be targeted for removal, which contain a greater amount of ﬁssion products
(FPs) and reduced quantities of ﬁssile and fertile material. The removal of more FPs facilitates a longer
operational lifetime before reaching criticality limitations, thereby leaving more neutrons available for
breeding. The removal of a lesser amount of ﬁssile material while replenishing more fertile material will
similarly extend the operational lifetime, though it is not straightforward to predict the impact on the
breeding ratio (BR), since both the numerator and denominator are increased. One prediction could be
that since there is so much more 238U, in relative terms the ﬁssile inventory would see a higher impact
45
==PAGE:49==
than the fertile, working towards reducing the BR. This could be approximated by the homogenized
model, since it was subdivided into 28 zones, 4 radial and 7 axial. Instead of refueling the entire core,
one may target only the central column with the highest burnup for refueling, potentially in combination
with core shuﬄing. The removal of less ﬁssile material would also lead to a smaller decrease in keff for
the same volume fraction being refueled. This would allow for more refueling, either higher frequency or
larger amounts. This would in turn lead to higher replenishment of 238Uand an increase in FP removal,
both giving longer operational life. Given the results of the variable refueling-frequency campaign in Case
3, where the reactivity span is still too high for normal operation, increasing the start refueling frequency
would be beneﬁcial.
A heterogeneous model would also allow for fuel reshuﬄing, which could be deployed to further increase
the achievable discharge burnup. Combined with selected refueling of high burnup fuel pins, future work
will likely ﬁnd a higher theoretical discharge burnup for the Compact SFR than what is presented here.
9
Conclusion
This thesis provides an initial feasibility study of a design of a Compact SFR design by 92 Research, which
shows promise as a Low-Enriched Uranium-started, Small Modular Reactor, operating in the Breed and
Burn mode (LEU-BNB-SMR) in the U-Pu fuel cycle. Scaling down BNB reactor to the SMR range,
comes with a trade-oﬀ: the smaller the core, the higher the leakage, which is usually compensated for by
increasing the initial enrichment; this, in turn, decreases the breeding performance. The goal was to test
whether the initial 235U-enrichment could be lowered, by using a more compact design, and at the same
time test the impact on the breeding performance of refueling with depleted uranium (DU) based feed
fuel as a means of reducing the excess reactivity from breeding (the reactivity swing).
The initial results show that initial criticality was successfully achieved, with a reactivity swing of
∆ρ∼3500 pcm. By implementing a variable refueling-frequency scheme, the reactivity swing was reduced
to manageable levels, and the breeding ratio was increased from BR< 1.1 to BR< 1.2. A theoretical
discharge burnup of 150 MWd/kgHM was achieved, at an operational lifetime of 70 years, refueling only
with DU-based feed. This is the same burnup achieved by TerraPower’s Natrium reactor, using up to
<20% enriched HALEU fuel. These early results indicate the feasibility of the Compact SFR design in
enabling a LEU-B&B-SMR.
The ﬁndings contribute to ongoing research working towards the design of BNB-SMRs, and to the
knowledge of the author, the initial enrichment used here was lower than for any publicly released design
of a small SFR in the BNB mode. It should be noted that the ﬁndings concern only the physics, while
simply assuming that such a design could be practically manufactured; the actual feasibility of which is
outside of the scope of this work.
The initial results show great promise for the Compact SFR generic design as a LEU-BNB-SMR.
Further simulation work might include simulating control rods as a means of managing the reactivity
swing to actually measure the eﬀect of using DU-based refueling for reactivity control. This would require
extending the generic homogeneous model used in this work to a heterogeneous model of higher detail.
Experimental work in the area of fuel manufacturing is needed to test the validity of the fuel density used
in this work.
