Abstract
This thesis explores the potential benefits of implementing prefabrication in the Living Places
Copenhagen project, developed by the VELUX Group in collaboration with EFFEKT and
Artelia. After a comprehensive analysis of the building structure, the study proposes a way
forward to increase the prefabrication rate through the redesign of the facade. Following the
Double Diamond methodology, various design methods have been applied to develop a solution
that meets all the specific requirements. These methods are divided into four design phases.
The discovery phase explores prefabrication, identifying its potential benefits, challenges and
strategies, as well as Living Places project, which also led to a study of bio-based materials
and how they relate to prefabrication. In the define phase, the scope selection is the result
of the qualitative in-depth analysis of the building components and the identification of the
redesign of the facade element as a potential way to benefit from a higher level of prefabrication
in the project. In the development phase, a detailed analysis of the facade element is carried
out, looking at it from various perspectives, including structural analysis, weight assessment,
stakeholder exploration and assembly process analysis. This analysis of the current design is
followed by a concept exploration phase. Finally, the delivery phase develops the final proposal,
including the specific morphology, material definition and proposed stakeholder analysis. In
addition, the proposal is checked for consistency with Design for Disassembly (DfD), Design for
Manufacture and Assembly (DfMA) and other strategies for efficient design and construction in
prefabrication identified in earlier phases.
The final facade design has the appropriate dimensions to integrate standard elements and
reduce material wastage, which might affect the overall dimensions of the building. In analysing
the current design, it was noted that although DfD is already implemented, DfMA should also be
considered from the outset if prefabrication is to be further implemented. Thus, the proposed
solution uses simple connections that allow for easy and straightforward assembly and disas-
sembly, enhancing the economic and sustainable benefits of both initiatives. The use of hemp
as the insulation material is justified due to its identified sustainable performance, its positive
impact on the indoor climate and its availability in batts, which resulted beneficial when imple-
mented in a prefabricated design. Furthermore, the design process highlights the importance of
early identification of potential challenges and limitations through early collaboration between
all stakeholders. This collaboration should be supported by an updated BIM model containing
all relevant information about the project.
It is believed that all key lessons gathered in this report can be applied to other building
components, such as the roof panels with a similar structure, or the floor slab, which will
result in a higher level of prefabrication in Living Places Copenhagen. This will simplify the
assembly process, improve quality control and reduce on-site labour and material waste. All of
these implications are expected to reduce construction time and, potentially, the project cost.
However, there are a number of challenges that need to be taken into account when implementing
these prefabrication principles, such as the protection of the hemp insulation during transport
and installation, the increase in weight of the prefabricated element, the connection with other
building components and the complex logistics resulting from the high level of collaboration
required.

1.1
Introduction
It is well known that in the last decades human activities have been increasing carbon emis-
sions, creating a negative impact on the environment. According to various studies, the building
industry is responsible for about 40% of energy consumption, 25% to 40% of CO2 and GHG
(greenhouse gases) emissions and around 17% of water consumption worldwide [1][2]. In Den-
mark, 1/3 of the total waste volume is due to the construction and civil engineer industry [3].
However, this industry fulfills one of the basic needs of society, while contributing significantly
to global economic growth [4]. In addition, it is claimed that more than two thirds of the world
population will presumably live in urban areas by 2050, which means that construction activities
will not decrease in the following years [5]. Thus, it is of utmost importance to find alternatives
to traditional construction that do not compromise the environment and people’s well-being.
In recent years, interest around prefabricated buildings has been increasing[2][6][7], as a pos-
sible solution to the environmental issues associated with the industry. Some benefits have been
identified when comparing prefabrication with traditional constructions, such as a considerable
reduction of the construction time, higher quality control, potential cost savings [4] and better
environmental performance[8][9][10].
This thesis explores the potential benefits and limitations of prefabrication and the strategies
that should be considered when implementing this construction technique, with a strong focus
on timber structures and bio-based materials. Furthermore, it conducts an in-depth analysis
of the project known as Living Places Copenhagen[11], evaluating its current strategies and
proposing a solution to further integrate prefabrication principles and benefit from it.
1.2
Introduction to Living Places Copenhagen
Living Places Copenhagen is a project developed by EFFEKT[12], the VELUX Group[11] and
Artelia [13]. It aims to provide an alternative to conventional buildings, with the goal of con-
tributing to the fight against environmental and health issues that society is facing. In doing so,
the project proposes a living environment that benefits both the people and the planet. It has

exploring the potential benefits of increasing the prefabrication rate of the buildings for future

the project, the scope will be further defined and the redesign of the facade element of one of

of prefabrication.

Design process and methods overview
For mapping the design process and deliver a solution that aligns with the problem statement
of proposing a way forward to enable Living Places to benefit from an increased level of prefab-
rication, this thesis follows the Double Diamond framework. This methodology, that relies on
divergent and convergent thinking, provides a clear, structured and comprehensive approach to
any design challenge, allowing at the same time for flexibility and innovation. It consists of four
phases: Discover, Define, Develop and Deliver (see Figure 2.1) that allow the deep exploration of
an issue (divergent thinking and exploration of the problem space) and support the development
of solutions that meet specific requirements (converging thinking and exploration of the solution
space)[15][16].
In the case study, the Discover phase involves understanding the prefabrication context, iden-
tifying opportunities and challenges and analysing how Living Places relates to it. In the Define
phase, specific areas for improvement and clear objectives are set, finalising with a well-defined
scope within the problem definition. Subsequently, the Develop phase focuses on generating
innovative conceptual solutions and strategies to fulfill the proposed goals, followed by the De-
liver phase, in which the chosen solution is deeply explored. The methods used in each phase,
which are described below, are critical to delivering a solution that meets expectations and add
significant value to this study.

Introduction to Prefabrication
Prefabrication can be defined as an industrialized procedure characterized by the offsite produc-
tion and pre-assembly of elements in a controlled environment that subsequently are transported
to the designated construction site to be fully assembled and installed[8].
The manufacture of building components became popular during the Industrial Revolution
due to the emergence of manufacturing concepts such as mass production and standardization.
The use of prefabrication continued to grow after World War II, as it was employed to address
post-war housing needs due to its affordability[20]. However, despite is earlier adoption decades
ago[2], it has received considerable attention in the last decade. This increasing popularity,
which varies among regions, is due to their potential to be an alternative to conventional con-
structions, in order to reduce the environmental negative impact of the industry. It is believed
that prefabrication can lead to a 50% reduction in the construction waste, a decrease in resource
usage by 35,8%, a 6,6% reduction in health-related damages and a 3,5% in the overall ecosystem
damage [21]. In addition to these significant sustainable and health benefits, this construction
technique offers advantages in terms of efficiency, time saving, quality, safety, and cost, among
others. These advantages that affect developers, contractors and clients, will be discussed in
detail in this thesis (see chapter 3.1). Moreover, prefabrication building projects have been re-
cently enhanced by improvements in information management[22],technologies and equipment,
and promotion policies[2][21]. CNC (Computer Numeric Control) machines and BIM (Build-
ing Information Modeling) technologies have helped to optimize the communication between
the different stakeholders involved in a prefabrication process[9][21][23], as technical knowledge
(materials, dimensions, emissions among others) is available in the digital model[22]. This has
lead to an holistic approach of the processes and to a better integration of prefabrication in the
construction business model.
However, there are still some limitations that prefabrication has to faced to become a
widespread alternative to conventional construction worldwide. These constraints, strongly re-
lated with complex logistics and lack of general knowledge, are also shown in chapter 3.1.

As mentioned, many studies ensure that prefabricated buildings have various advantages, stand-
ing out the environmental ones [8][9][10][23][24][25].

• Materials use and waste reduction:
Compared to conventional buildings, it is a

the material usage. In addition, thanks to modeling technologies, components are designed
with a higher degree of precision, which also reduce the waste [20]. Lastly, it is easier to
disassemble and, at the end of life, to separate the different materials and components to
achieve a higher recycling rate [10] and to reduce the amount of construction waste that
end up in landfills [23].
• Reduction in the construction time: in prefabrication modules and components are
manufactured off-site and then transported to the construction site for the final assembly.
This reduces weather delays [9], and on-site construction times [8][9][24], as the components
design enable faster assembly [25] and simultaneous activities are possible.
• Increase efficiency: prefabrication facilitates the optimization of the construction sys-
tem and processes, reducing time waste and leading to mass production[10] and mass cus-
tomization techniques [26]. Moreover, a lot of potential has been identified when combining
BIM (Building Information Modeling) and prefabrication[9][21]. This digital technology
improves communication and collaboration between stakeholders and a higher productivity
can be achieved[23]. Furthermore, BIM allows to organize and visualize relevant informa-
tion for prefabrication, such as material requirements, accurate geometry representation
and behaviour, 3D models and CAD drawings, among others[23].
• Increase in safety and better working conditions:
reduction of dust, noise and
disruption on-site, while allowing safer working environment [23].
This is also related
to sustainability, which involves minimizing the environmental and social impact on the
local area [25]. In [23], it is stated that prefabrication helps to reduce work accidents
in a 80%, compared to on-site traditional construction methods. Additionally, long-term
employment is offered in the prefabrication plant, in contrast to the contractual nature of
employment seen in traditional construction projects [23].
• Improve quality: off-site manufacturing in a prefabrication plant allows achieving higher
quality and consistency in the finished components through a controlled environment,
rigorous quality control measures, including automated processes, inspections, and testing
[25]. This approach ensures higher quality and consistency in the finished components [23]
and can result in improving the building performance [8].
• Facilitate maintenance and reconfiguration: as a dry construction system (does not
need water in the construction site), prefabricated buildings offer the flexibility of partial
disassembly during their use phase. This feature facilitates maintenance and reconfigura-
tion of the building, allowing for different uses with minimal waste production [8]. This
also influences the dissasambled, recycling and reuse of components at the end of life of
the building.

• Transport constraints (due to module size/weight): transport is considered the
biggest downsides of prefabricated buildings[23]. It has associated costs, such as special

package and transport related burdens[8]. It also affects the supply chain integration [23]

• High cost: in order to get economic benefits of this construction technique, it is important

changes need to be done in the industry [9]. In case all logistics and considerations are not

uncertian [21].

higher productivity [23]. However, many considerations since the beginning to the end
of life need to be done, such as considering lifting, transportation and assembly during
the design phase. For these reasons, a prefabricated construction turns out to be a com-
plex project with high requirements of project coordination, which could create technical
barriers [8][23].
• Reduction in flexibility: this was also perceive as one of the main constraints in pre-
fabrication [23]. In prefabricated projects, the design is decided in previous phases to the
on-site construction, which reduces the possibility of making changes in later phases and
can have an impact on efficiency[23]. In addition, this lack of flexibility could end up in
constraining creativity and monotonous architecture.

3.2
Construction methods: levels of prefabrication
Depending on the complexity of the offsite manufactured building components and the project
specific conditions[29], although there is a lack of uniform definition, different construction meth-
ods, usually refereed as degrees of prefabrication or prefabricated rates[1][27], can be achieved.
Each level of prefabrication has different advantages, which are mentioned below.
• Prefabricated components: Prefabricated components are the lowest level of prefabri-
cation but allow a highest degree of customization, as the components can be assembled
based on specific needs. In addition, subdividing components into smaller prefabricated
elements can reduce the need of replacements and maintenance[30]. However, this flexi-
bility in the design increase the on-site work, where all the connections between the small
elements need to be done.
Examples of prefabricated components are stairs and roof
trusses[27].

• Prefabricated panels: Prefabricated panels are completed sections that are delivered
to the construction site to be assembled[31]. These panels are usually used as supporting
structures, such as structural walls, floors and roofs.
According to [20], two types of
prefabricated panels can be found: open structural panels that need to be fitted with
insulation (in designed cavities) and cladding on-site, and closed structural panels, that
usually include other elements such as insulation, plumbing and finishes.
It has been
noted that the integration of prefabrication panels in a construction project can reduce
the construction time around a 10-15%[20].
• Prefabricated modules: Prefabricated modules are volumetric elements in which most
of the finishes are assembled and installed at the prefabrication plant, achieving around a
90% of offsite completeness[27]. As in the closed structural panels ,prefabricated modules
usually include plumbing and other appliances [20]. The dimensions and weight of these
prefabricated modules are strongly limited by transportation restrictions, lifting capacities
and manufacturing constraints. It is worth mentioning that the thermal performance is
higher than in previous prefabricated levels due to the tighter connection tolerance [27]
and the manufacturing and assembly processes in a controlled environment, which helps
to increase the quality control.
• Hybrid prefabricated solutions: Hybrid solutions are a combination of planar and
modular prefabrication, in which structures are made of both prefabricated panels and
prefabricated modules and, again, the final assembly is done on-site [27].
• Prefabricated complete buildings: In this last degree of prefabrication, the highest
degree of completeness is achieved off-site, which means that all components are prefab-
ricated and assembled in a controlled environment. As it is the highest level of prefabri-
cation, its benefits related to efficiency, quality control and rapid project completion are
evident[20]. However, their dimensions and weight can be problematic, facing limitations
in transporting and lifting the buildings during the on-site construction.

aspects and an early identification of project limitations, reducing the need of later redesigns[32].

rative process, as provides an holistic and centralized platform for sharing updated information
among stakeholders[9][21][33]. BIM models act as digital twins[33] that provides an holistic view
of the project and enables simulation and 3D visualization of all components and their assembly.
They help to identify incompatibilities such us clashes and to plan the design and construction

for Manufacturability and Assembly(DfMA) must be explained. It is a construction approach
that consider the optimization in the design to facilitate both the manufacturing and assembly
processes, which means that it takes into consideration not only the architectural aesthetics but
also the practicalities of fabrication and assembly (see Figure 3.3)[29]. In construction, with all

its considerations, it allows to have an holistic perspective of how the building is going to be
designed and constructed.
In doing so, simplicity, standardization and the integration of modular design are considered
to enhance efficient production and streamlined assembly, which also leads to time and cost
savings, and increase in predictability[29]. Moreover, DfMA also includes the seek of material
efficiency and sustainability, simplifying on-site construction and minimizing the need of com-

limitations are also relevant in DfMA. Components must be designed ensuring that can be easily

with the available equipment and site conditions. Lastly, the coordination and integration of
building systems such as electrical and plumbing installations need to be taken into account,
which means that prefabricated components must be designed to ensure the compatibility and
accommodation of these systems[30]. Although this design approach is not inherently digital-
ized, combining it with BIM technologies can boost its effectiveness and improve the resources
utilization[29].
By incorporating all these principles into the prefabrication process, it is possible to translate
the design intent into efficiently fabricated and assembled components. This approach aligns
with the prefabrication goals of delivering high-quality, sustainable and cost-effective building
solutions, reducing the cost of labour[29].

Design for Disassembly (DfD)
Another design approach relevant in prefabrication is Design for Disassembly (DfD). In this
case, this concept is related with circular economy, the revaluation of components and materials
and extending the lifespan of the building[3]. Design for Disassembly can focus on the recycling
of materials, where the material’s value decreases, and reusing components and construction
elements, in which their value is maintained [3].
Considerations that follow the DfD approach are selecting materials that are easy to separate,
recycle or reuse, labeling components for identification during dissasembly, simplicity in shapes
and connections, and minimize the use of irreversible connections such as adhesives or other
joints that cause damage in the component[3], as it is known that component assemblies are
critical in the environmental impact of the building [30].
Despite the similarity of the concepts, DfMA is not directly proportional to DfD, since
a system that is easily assembled, such as a silicone joint, can complicate the separation of
components when looking at the disassembly and reuse of the elements. However, considering
both concepts help to create an holistic approach of how the design of components should be in

3.3.4
Standardization and modularization
As mentioned in these design approaches, to improve the prefabrication performance within a
construction project, it is recommended the use of standardized components or modules that
can be replicated and easily assembled[25][32]. This increase in efficiency is justified by consis-
tent specifications and a reduction in the variation of components and processes, which helps
to reduce the number of errors and problems identified during the design and manufacturing
processes and to increase quality control[22].
It also helps to improve and simplify collabo-
ration with suppliers[22] and to reduce the time needed for reviewing[22] and redesigning[32].
Standardization aligns with DfMA, as it simplifies the manufacturing and production and facil-
itates logistics and planning, and with DfD, as having standard elements increase the potential
for reuse in future projects. All in all, standardization enhances the construction of buildings
through automatizing and streamlining workflows.
A way of increasing standardization is by modularization[22]. In the last decades, modu-
lar design strategies in the construction industry have increased in popularity, considering the
modularization of buildings and standardization of those modules[32]. According to the Modu-
lar Building Institute[36], modular construction can be defined as an off-site process performed
in a controlled environment, in which volumetric modules are prefabricated, transported and
assembled in the building construction site. However, these modules can also refer to smaller
components such us prefabricated panels with standard dimensions.
Modularization allows
breaking down complex structures into more simple manageable units, which aligns with effi-
cient manufacture, assembly and disassembly practices.
3.3.5
Precision manufacturing and quality control
Due to the off-site manufacturing in a controlled environment inherent of prefabrication, a high
degree of precision and quality control can be achieved [25]. Precision manufacturing refers to
the used of advanced technologies such as Computer Numerical Control (CNC) machines to en-
sure that prefabricated components are crafted with a high degree of precision, which not only
affects to dimensions but also to connections and finishes[23]. Quality control ensures that the
manufactured components meet these strict requirements and specifications, so standardization
is easier to achieve. Thanks to the possibility of manufacturing under this controlled environ-
ment, errors and the need of redesigns are avoided, as well as on-site construction challenges
such as weather dependencies and variability in construction methods[32].
3.3.6
Logistics and on-site installation
Other key aspect of prefabrication processes is the planning of the project and its logistics.
Factors that need to be considered are efficient collaboration among stakeholders, scheduling
and aligning components production and assembly time, and site specific constraints, among
others. Transportation and lifting methods and limitations are also relevant to ensure the safe
and timely delivery of the prefabricated components on-site. All in all, prefabricated construction
projects involve complex logistics[8][23] and, as mentioned above (see 3.3.1), project management
and BIM technology plays a crucial role in supporting project logistics and collaboration.

Prefabrication with bio-based materials
When considering the negative impact of the construction industry, the focus should not only
be in the operation phase of the building, but also in other phases over its lifespan, such as ma-
terial selection. CO2 emissions from building materials account for around 15% of the building
emissions and this percentage can increase depending on the energy efficiency of the building
[37]. This proofs that the building material selection plays an important role in the overall envi-
ronmental impact of the building [30]. As previously mentioned, conventional building materials
are responsible for considerable Greenhouse Gases emissions, an elevated energy consumption
and waste production. In addition, most of the traditional constructions are based on mineral
and fossil based resources such as stones, bricks, concrete and other cement based materials,
which provokes the consumption of valuable natural resources[14]. For this reason, in order to
reduce emissions and other environmental impacts, the materials present in buildings must be
consciously selected and more sustainable options should be considered. In Figure 4.1, it can be
seen that building emissions should be consider in all phases.
In the last years, the interest in bio-based materials has increased, as they seem to be a
potential alternative to the problems mentioned regarding the built environment and help to
establish a circular approach within the industry. According to [38], bio-based materials are
products primarily composed of substances derived from living matter (biomass), occurring
naturally or synthesized.
This term includes materials in their original form or after being
processed utilizing biomass and they are heterogeneous in size and composition[37]. While a
strict definition includes common materials like paper and leather, in engineering it usually
refers to modern materials that have undergone more advanced processing.
Bio-based materials can be divided into two different categories: fast-growing and non fast-
growing. The main difference is that the first group refers to those materials that come from a
rapidly renewable resource, with short cultivation cycle, such as bamboo, mycelium and hemp.
On the other hand, non fast-growing materials derived from biomass with longer cultivation
cycles. Examples of this categories are wood, cotton and cellulose (see Figure 4.3).

4.3
Wood-based construction
In the last years, the use of wood in the construction industry has increase in popularity, both
in the construction of complete buildings and in smaller structures, such as walls, ceiling and
roof sections[20]. However, the use of wood in the industry has been controversial, as, although
being renewable, it is a non fast-growing bio-based material, so a considerable amount of time
is needed in order to grow it. For this reason, its carbon footprint greatly depends on the forest
growth and management and the varied byproducts generated, as well as how the material is
managed at the end of its life[41]. However, when comparing the LCA of wooden buildings with
conventional ones such as those based on concrete or steel structures, the timber ones seem to be
more sustainable [8][10]. It should be mention that in this comparison wooden based buildings
are prefabricated in some extent, which is also related with a reduction in the environmental
impact. In brief, research proves the sustainable advantages of using wood as an alternative, as
long as the forest and natural resources are managed properly and the end of life of materials
are considered[41].
4.3.1
Comparison with conventional construction materials
When comparing it with cement-based materials, wood requires only about 10% of the energy
consumption and lower carbon emissions[14]. The significant decline in consumption can be at-
tributed to the production of the wood-based components, as the processes involved in treating
this material, such as drying, cutting, or drilling consume less energy due to its properties, which
translates into a mitigation of emissions[41]. Moreover, its composition and capability to be com-
bined with other materials make this bio-based material adaptable to different type of projects
[20]. Looking at wood performance, as it is a bio-based material, its thermal conductivity is
considerably lower than other materials such as metals, so a better isolation is achieved[20].
Despite all the advantages mentioned above, potential disadvantages when using wood as a
construction material have been identified. First of all, it is widely known the inherent low fire
resistance of the material. Legislation that affect this matter needs to be reviewed and followed
during the design phase, in order to fulfill with all requirements. Fire-protection measures and
safety engineering, such as oversize of the wood structure need to be implemented to mitigate
the risk[46]. Despite of the high precision and quality control in the prefabrication of timber
elements, wood can experience dimensional changes due to fluctuation in its internal struc-
ture, which can impact in the dimensions of the wood elements and its connections with other
elements[46]. As with other bio-based materials, wood structures are not the most adequate in
those applications where extremely high load-bearing and exigent structural requirements are
needed[14] and they can be damaged in extreme weather conditions, so proper treatment and
maintenance are crucial.

versatility[20].

Living Places Analysis
This chapter provides an in-depth analysis of the Living Places Copenhagen project and its
current state, building on the theoretical foundation established in the previous section. The
entire concept is thoroughly examined to develop a comprehensive understanding of the project’s
principles and its relationship to prefabrication and other efficient design strategies. This analysis
aims to uncover specific challenges unique to the project, offering insights into both its strengths

5.1
The concept and general approach
Living Places Copenhagen is an innovative initiative led by VELUX[11] in collaboration with
EFFEKT (architectural firm)[12], Artelia (engineer consultancy) [13] and Enemærke & Pe-
tersen(constructors) [52], aiming to revolutionize the building industry by showcasing a new
approach to sustainable and healthy living environments. After the construction of the first
prototype, that consist of seven buildings, including open pavilions and full-scale homes, it has
been emphasized a threefold reduction in CO2 footprint and a considerable improvement in in-
door climate compared to average Danish single-family houses. Grounded in five key principles:
healthy, shared, simple, adaptive and scalable, Living Places challenges the notion that we must
wait for future technology to build responsibly. In doing so, the project exemplifies the great
potential of the building industry and the possibility of utilizing existing knowledge and tech-
nology to create responsible and regenerative solutions for the benefit of both people and the
planet, being affordable for the many.

5.1.1
The Compass Model
The Compass Model[11], an holistic framework developed collaboratively by VELUX and EF-
FEKT, serves as the foundation for guiding the planning, conceptualization, and design of sus-
tainable buildings within the Living Places project. This theoretical foundation considers seven
strategic drivers: flexibility, quality, environmental consciousness, health, community integra-
tion, local context, and affordability, each playing a crucial role in shaping the development
process. Flexibility encourages homes to adapt to diverse lifestyles, applying modularization
while promoting waste reduction and reuse by easily disassembly, while quality prioritizes dura-
bility through the use of high-quality materials and standard and low-maintenance solutions.
Environmental consciousness drives decisions to mitigate carbon footprint and overall environ-
mental impact, complemented by health-focused strategies that optimize indoor climates to
enhance occupants’ well-being. Community integration underscores the importance of designing
homes within larger community frameworks, while considering the local context highlights the
significance of incorporating local surroundings and contemporary design approaches. Lastly,
affordability ensures that homes meet people’s needs at accessible price points[11]. All these
strategic drivers are in-depth explained in Appendix I.
Through the integration of these drivers, an easy framework that can be followed to create
homes which holistically benefit both people and the planet is possible. Thanks to this approach,
that can serve as inspiration and can be applied, not only to this specific project, but also to
the construction industry, Living Places achieves its sustainability objectives while maintaining
alignment with overarching goals and ensuring high-quality outcomes.

5.2
Sustainability and Health in Living Places
Sustainability is a key focus of Living Places. While the building industry has traditionally
focused on the operational energy of buildings during their use phase, it is important to evaluate
all phases of a building’s lifespan to analyze its overall environmental performance. This includes
material selection, end-of-life considerations for the building and its components, and emissions
throughout the full life cycle, which have been shown to be significant[1][57].
5.2.1
Measuring approach
The approach followed by Living Places is switching the traditional perspective of how we
consider the environmental impact in CO2 emissions per square meter, to CO2 per person. The
reasoning behind this innovative idea is that measuring it following the traditional way incentives
the construction of larger and more resource-intensive buildings, as the emissions are spread out
over a larger space, resulting in a seemingly lower environmental impact. However, this approach
contributes to the climate crisis increasing the material use, and the total emissions[30][58]. On
the other hand, it is believed that measuring the CO2 emissions per person focus on the actual
impact on the individuals, encouraging a more efficient use of resources and space, as buildings
must accommodate occupants while minimizing the environmental impact per person.
The tools used to measure the sustainable performance of the building are a full Life Cycle
Assessment and the Active House Radar. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a methodology used to
evaluate the environmental performance associated with all the stages of a product life cycle[59],
considering materials and energy flows. In the case study, it considers both the embodied and
the operational energy. On the other hand, the Active House Radar[60] is a framework that
allows the integration of the principles of Comfort, Energy and Environment within a building,
providing a visual representation of its performance in the three mentioned areas. The framework
with all its criteria for the three principles can be seen in Figure 5.8. The combination of this
tools enables an holistic approach with a focus on minimizing the environmental, and optimizing
energy use and indoor climate conditions, thus contributing to the main principle of the project
of creating buildings that benefit both the people and the planet[11][12].
5.2.2
Sustainability through the whole life cycle

the materials used in the building from extraction to end-of-life, and operational energy, which
refers to the energy consumed during the building’s operational activities over its lifespan[14].

impact of the project[37][57].In the case study, the selection of materials with low LCA values
and EPD (Environmental Product Declaration) certification, which help to conduct an accuarte

5.2.5
Building systems
Within the seven buildings that make up the built prototype, there are two households, each of
them following a specific building system: Timber frame and CLT panels, being both of them
wood based constructions.
Timber framing structures, being a more traditional construction technology, consist of verti-
cal and horizontal linear elements that require incorporating stability planes and it is particularly
suitable for buildings up to four floors[55]. Compared to CLT structures with similar geometries,
the material usage is approximately three times lower (Figure 5.6) and, with identical interior
sizes, the exterior footprint is also smaller due to the fact that the insulation can be integrated
between the structural elements of the walls[8][55]. On the other hand, Cross-laminated Timber
structures are constructed using mass timber panels with perpendicular wood layers, which in-
creases their load bearing capacity. This composition allows larger spans (more than 6 meters)
without requiring intermediate supports[5]. In addition, CLT elements can be left exposed with-
out the need of an interior finish layer. However, they must be covered with insulation, resulting
in increased overall thickness and assembly time also turned out to be longer (Figure 5.6).
Both household prototypes have shown certainly strengths and disadvantages, so a combi-
nation of the two systems in the same building, combining benefits, should be explored in order
to achieve a more optimized structure and material usage with a reduced carbon footprint.

Indoor climate
People spend approximate 90% of their time indoors[64]. For this reason, it is of utmost impor-

to enhance our well-being.

the circadian rhythms. This reduce the reliance on artificial lighting, which is closely related to
well-being[30]. Due to the environmental changing conditions, the building should not be static,

5.3
Design for Disassembly in Living Places
One of the focus of Living Places is the Design for Disassembly (DfD). This holistic approach
can be applied to both conventional and bio-based construction materials and, as mentioned
before, it is usually related with modularization and simplicity in shapes and connections[3].
In order to achieve a circular strategy, it is important to identify which building components
can potentially be recycled or reused and understand its environmental and economic impact[3].
One of the main barriers for the development of design for disassembly is the lack of economical
incentives to build within a circular strategy[3]. However, Living Places is the proof of how
building sustainable can also be strongly related with building affordable[12].
5.3.1
Building and technical system separation
Within this sustainable strategy, Living Places focuses on a separation of the building system,
the structure itself, from the technical system, such as water and electricity installations. In
addition, glued or non accessible connections are avoided and the use of standard elements and
standard dimensions is promoted, as it increases the possibility of a second life. This helps to
increase the building lifespan and facilitates the separation and re-circulation of its components,
remaining part of the value chain[3].
As mentioned, design for disassembly enables to recycle material and components, achieving
an efficient and more sustainable use of resources and waste reduction. In addition to the envi-
ronmental advantages of this design approach, the fact of having the installations separated from
the rest of the building gives customers the opportunity to decide on the appliances that better
suit their needs without modifying the structure itself, which means an increase in flexibility. It
also has advantages in maintenance, as the structure has a higher lifespan than the installation.
The separation of systems allow the easy upgrade and removal of installations maintaining the
structure, aligning maintenance with product longevity[30]. In addition, it also has benefits
during the assembly, as reduces the need of coordination between stakeholders and the process
becomes more straightforward. In the Figure 5.9, it can be seen how those elements that have
the shortest lifespan are the easiest to replace.
Figure 5.9: Design for Disassembly: building components with different lifespan. Illustration
and measures developed by VELUX and EFFEKT[11][12].
In Living Places, despite being independent systems, the technical installation are hided
behind eaves for aesthetic reasons. Strategically positioning lights, sockets and ventilation in
the eaves or just below them minimize the visibility of pipes and wires. Wireless switches are
chosen to reduce even more the wires visibility[30].

5.3.2
Challenges identified when applying Design for Disassembly
However, despite all the mentioned benefits related with Design for disassembly, some challenges
have been identified. Firstly, when dealing with the separation between the building and the
technical installations, it’s important to pay close attention to aesthetics, ensuring that the
technical elements blend well with the overall building design. Due to the lack of standardization
of this design technique, good coordination between the architects, engineers and constructors
is needed in order to understand all building services and to achieve an aesthetic interior design.
Secondly, covering connections and joints for aesthetic reasons can difficult the disassembly.
Lastly, connecting living materials, like wood, with inert ones such as stone needs a carefully
planning and covers must be considered. All in all, connections between different materials need
to be studied, to improve aesthetics without compromising the disassembly[30].
In Living Places, the design for disassembly strategy and all these challenges identified con-
ditioned the interior appearance and the overall design of the buildings[30]:
• Around 80% of the building technical installation are covered and accessible through shafts
and eaves. However, some screws in plywood plates and some water pipes and wires are
still visible.
• In relation with wall elements, selecting entire plates aims to minimize material waste
generated when cutting, to speed up the installation process, and to facilitate the reuse.
• The use of elastic joints, putty in corners and sealants are reduced.
• Cover list in the edges with wood surfaces was required to cover the tolerance that the
wood requires, due to its dimensional instability.
5.3.3
Future work regarding Design for Disassembly
After having built the prototype, some lessons regarding disassembly and technical integration
can be drawn, which has helped to define future work [30]. First of all, regarding the interior
wall, it has been noted that the board edges of the tongue and groove fiber gypsum 15mm plates
are really fragile, which increases material waste due to potential damage in the transport and
installation and compromise the disassemble. The installation is specially complex in slope sur-
faces. For this reason, a more standard solution should be explored, to simplify the installation
and reduce the damage on edges. Sealants and covers for joints should be explored as potential
solutions. Secondly, a redesign of the interiors can be done according to the current overview of
all services. To continue hiding the installations, wireless switches should be considered and their
scalability must be evaluated, and a solution for covering vertical installations could be defined.
Lastly, keeping in mind aesthetics, the eaves covering technical systems should be designed as
a furniture elements and a better solution could be found to substitute the visible screws in
plywood panels.

mined depth, without concrete requirement[66]. With this system, a significant reduction in the
foundation system emissions and LCA values is achieved (see Figure 5.4 and Figure 5.10), and
it is easily prefabricated.

5.4.2
Identified benefits when applying prefabrication
It is believed that prefabrication principles can bring numerous benefits to Living Places concept.
As explained in 3.1, it can boost the scalability of the project, while increasing consistency and
quality. In addition, the cost can be reduced due to reducing labor costs, material waste, con-

identified of applying prefabrication in Living Placesafter building the prototype are explained
in more detail [30]:
• Lower environmental impact: due to the reduction of material use and waste, as
elements are cut to precise measurements. This reduction is also related with a decreasing
in emissions and water usage. In addition, prefabrication allows the disassemble, reuse
and recycle of elements and, compered with conventional techniques, it respects natural
areas surrounding the site and minimize disturbances.
• Cost and time effectiveness: increase in predictability and efficiency, as components
are manufactured in a controlled environment. Potential reduction of transportation cost
(although increase in weight should be considered) due to partially assembled elements
compared to pre-production resources. Lastly, weather and site independence, which leads
to effective and simpler assembly.
• Quality control: more consistency and repeatability due to the controlled environment of
the prefabrication plant, related with the reduction of human errors. This manufacturing
process is also related with an improvement in safety.
• Flexibility and scalability: a prefabricated design is easier to disassemble and, specially
if modular, the movement between sites is also simple. It also increases the capacity of
scaling projects quick and efficiently.

5.4.3
Identified challenges when applying prefabrication
Despite these benefits, some challenges regarding prefabrication were also identified during the
construction of the prototype:
• Due to complexity in prefabrication logistics, design and construction phases need
to be well defined and considered since the beginning of the project and a high level of
communication and coordination among all stakeholders involved is needed. Otherwise,
delays can affect the whole construction process.
• Skilled labour is required, both for the design and construction on-site, preferably with
standard elements or dimensions.
• Even that a high level of automation is achieved when manufacturing the components, the
absence of on-site automation complicates the assembly process. In addition, joints
between elements need to be carefully design, as they must accommodate various stresses
while ensuring a simple and effective on-site assembly.
• When implementing prefabrication, the initial construction costs may be elevated, due
to the change in manufacturing. To ensure the affordability, an increase in the production
volume is required.
• The degree of prefabrication of the building and the size and weight of the com-
ponents and modules limit the transportation possibilities. Local legislation regarding
transportation and damages in the process should be considered.

It has been stated that a combination between standardization and prefabrication can bring

the use of existing standardized elements proves that it is already possible to build sustainable
buildings with much lower LCA values and affordable prices [30]. Having in mind these benefits,

• Standard plywood plates used in the walls, reducing material waste.

• Joints and connections: Although most of the joints are screwed, which facilitates the

times during on-site installation or the need of storing components such as the windows

• Information flow: Prefabrication considerations and other good construction practices

Concept selection
Although both concepts present opportunities and would bring benefits to the current design,
the second option has been found to be more suitable for this project. This is because potential
logistical difficulties have been identified during the assembly of the first option. These difficulties
relate to the logistics required to slide an entire facade element, taking into account its dimensions

of connection in other projects. In contrast, hidden screws are notable for their straightforward

skilled personnel. Moreover, the number of extra screws required is estimated to be relatively
minimal compared to the first option, as in that case, it would also be necessary to screw the
vertical battens to both parts. The option with the simplest assembly was therefore chosen,
greatly simplifying the assembly process.

compromised by sustainability, but always look for the lowest LCA values when possible.
Some examples of hemp insulation with natural fire retardants can already be found in
the market, such as Hempitecture Hempwool [51], that claims to be VOC free, nontoxic,
& treated with natural fire retardant, or the product developed by Burnblock, which is a
flame retardant solution 100% made of natural substances[80].

The proposed design encompasses both DfD and DfMA practices. Considerations in these areas

9.4.1
Screw-Based Connections
It has been decided that the connections between the different layers of the prefabricated ele-
ments, as well as the connections between the structural core and the cladding system, will be
bolted. This decision is justified as this approach is in line with the principles of DfMA and
DfD. Firstly, screws are easy to handle and do not require specialised equipment for their in-
stallation, simplifying the assembly process and reducing construction time. In addition, screws
allow for consistent joints that can be accurately installed thanks to the controlled environment

of the prefabrication plant. They can also be adapted to different material and thickness re-
quirements, providing flexibility in the joints. In terms of disassembly, they are not permanent
joints, meaning that components can be disassembled without damaging the material, which is
considered crucial for their circular economy and the reduction of material waste. Finally, in the
event of maintenance, a specific component can be easily replaced without damaging the entire
prefabricated element.
However, as specified in the material selection, screws are predominantly made of steel, which
increases the carbon footprint of the building. An in-depth Life Cycle Assessment need to be

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the increase of the prefabrication rate in Living Places, with a focus
on the timber frame household. In doing so, a comprehensive analysis has been carried out in
order to understand the project’s current strategies, values, and relationship to prefabrication.
Sustainability and healthy living environments were identified as key priorities and have been
consistently integrated throughout the case study. Furthermore, while the current design of
Living Places buildings emphasizes Design for Disassembly (DfD) as an important strategy in
prefabrication projects, it overlooks Design for Manufacturing and Assembly (DfMA), which
offers numerous benefits for reducing on-site labor and construction time[29]. Additionally, it
has been noted that the project does not have a strong modular strategy. It is recommended
that both DfMA and modularization are incorporated to further enhance prefabrication in Living
Places.
The case study follows the Double Diamond framework[16], which has structured and guided
the design process. This methodology has facilitated a thorough exploration of both the current
situation and the solution space, while also emphasizing the importance of problem definition,
scope selection, and project boundary determination.
After literature research and an in-depth Living Places holistic analysis, in order to define
the project scope, the building has been divided in different building components.
After a
comprehensive analysis of their morphology and how they interact with each other, it was
consider that redesigning the facade element in order to increase its prefabrication rate would
have a positive impact on the project. The detailed analysis of the current design revealed specific
areas where prefabrication could be significantly increased, specially in the internal structure
and cladding system, in which each layer was individually installed on-site. This decision was
supported by the initial research and by challenges and future work identified by stakeholders
after building the prototype [55]. However, the methodology followed in this thesis for the facade
redesign can be applied to other building elements identified during the analysis.
In particular, it has been noted the structural similarity between the facade and the roof
panels, as both of them are based on timber frame construction, with similar components (wood
battens, vapor barrier, bio-based insulation and gypsum boards). In both cases, a cladding is
independently installed on-site. Thus, the framework developed in this thesis could be applied to
the roof panels. It is believed that the same approach of having a prefabricated structural core
and a prefabricated cladding system will reduce the on-site work during the roof construction.
Similarly, despite its structural differences, the study of the prefabrication of the floor deck is

Conclusions of the facade redesign
Looking at the facade redesign, it can be seen that the proposed solution meet with the defined
designed specifications. First of all, it aligns with both DfMA and DfD principles. For instance,
using batt insulation instead of granulated simplifies the assembly process, while an in-depth
study of the facade element assembly has led to a design that reduces on-site errors. Addition-
ally, the preference for screws over permanent connections like silicons facilitates disassembly.
However, the potential reuse of the building components have not been explored.
In terms of standardization, the dimensions of the panel have been defined to accommodate
standard elements such as the vapor and wind barrier and the internal plaster boards. Despite
considering modularization since the beginning of the process, as it was identified as an efficient
prefabrication strategy during the literature research[22][25], there have been complications to
define a standard module that allows the perpendicular assembly of the panels. Nevertheless,
through the adaptation of the structure in the ends, a viable module that allows perpendicular
connections has been defined. It should be noted that some elements (wind and OSB panel) need
to be cut in order to cover the connection, resulting in material waste. However, the proposal
considers the option in which this waste is minimized.
In terms of sustainability, materials have considered following Living Places standards. How-
ever, as the thesis mainly has a qualitative approach, it is suggested that an LCA of the redesign
is carried out, to compare both performances and identify challenges.
11.2
Conclusions of the design process
One of the key findings of the thesis is the critical importance of identifying potential challenges
and limitations early in the design phase. For example, deciding on the prefabrication rate before
the design development allowed to anticipate and address issues such as increased weight and
connections between other prefabricated elements during the facade design process. Similarly,
integrating part of the technical system into the prefabricated unit requires early planning to en-
sure proper wiring pathways and necessary openings in the timber structure, thereby minimizing
on-site problems.
This early-stage identification, which has proved to be an efficient strategy for prefabricated
construction and to reduce project complexity[32], emphasizes the need for comprehensive stake-
holder collaboration from the beginning. Each stakeholder is essential in identifying potential
limitations in its specific field and their early participation must be carefully planed. A Building
Information Model (BIM) and a correct planning of the information flow are crucial in order to
reduce collaboration complexity, and minimize errors that might arise from it[9][21][33].

measures, also related with safety[23]. Also, despite the need of cut some elements in facade
connections, the material use is expected to be reduced, as there is no need of cutting the plaster
boards and the design considers the minimization of the material waste. Hemp insulation is also
expected to bring benefits in terms of indoor climate and to simplify the assembly process and
the equipment needed. These considerations will affect the repeatability and amortization in
the manufacturing process, which can boost the scalability of the project.
All this benefits are expected to have a positive impact in the cost of the project, which is
directly related with the reduction in the construction time, and the material waste. In addition,
due to the DfD approach, the cost at the end of life should also be reduced.
Nevertheless, despite all these benefits, some considerations need to be carefully taken into
account. First of all, hemp insulation needs to be checked with the specific manufacturer, as it
has been noted that specifications change depending on the source. Also, green certifications are
required for the fire retardants added to the insulation[61][65]. Special careful when transporting
and installing the open panel (structural core) is needed, in order to protect the hemp insulation
from the weather conditions. Secondly, connections with other building components need to be
assessed, and the redesign of the panel should be considered if any limitation is identified, as
it was the case when designing the perpendicular connection. In addition, further research in
terms of transportation and installations and the identification of specific legislation should be
considered, due to the increase in terms of dimensions and weight.
All the projects logistics need to be carefully planned, as if not managed correctly, it can
result in misalignment and high costs[9][23]. Lastly, if the theoretical framework developed in
this thesis is to be implemented in other construction projects, it is strongly suggested the early
identification of all the stakeholders involved, so their input can be integrated since the beginning
of the design process.

11.4
Future work
This section outlines some recommendations for future work, based on the content of this thesis
and the identified limitations in the deign proposed.
• Research on how the increase in weight of the prefabricated element (approx.
29,1%)
would complicate the transportation and installation logistics.
• Research on how to better cover some of the visible connections (such as the screws to fix
the cladding system and to fix the gypsum board that covers the electrical layout), always
keeping in mind design for disassembly.
• Comprehensive investigation into the potential consequences of leaving the hemp insulation
in the open air for an extended period (transport and installation duration) and how the
panel could be better protected without generating material waste (as it would be the case
of a non-reusable plastic cover).
• In-depth exploration of all the building components that are in contact with the facade,
understanding the connections and how they might affect to the design. Include how the
proposed redesign could affect the overall dimensions of the building.
• Life cycle assessment of the implementation of the hemp insulation and the increase in
metal screws, to understand their impact on the building carbon footprint and research
construction systems that only use wood connections.
• Apply the theoretical framework to other building components, such as the roof panels,
and evaluate the impact in Living Places.
