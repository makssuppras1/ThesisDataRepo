Abstract
In this thesis, a numerical framework to solve a self-similar indentation problem is extended to solve a
steady-state scratch test. The numerical framework utilizes the characteristics of the indentation and scratch
test to obtain the history dependence through spatial integration instead of the traditional, Lagrangian time
integration. The spatial integration is performed through radial streamlines for the self-similar indentation
problem, and horizontal streamlines for the steady-state scratch test. Being in the self-similar framework
and displacing the indenter a sufficiently large distance horizontally, the streamlines becomes near horizon-
tal, effectively modelling a steady-state scratch test. This is verified by an ANSYS model, which utilizes the
Lagrangian time incremental method, and the two integration methods are compared. Furthermore, the case
where the indenter is in between the self-similar indentation test and a steady-state scratch test is studied.
This case is obtained when the indenter is not being displaced a sufficiently large enough distance horizon-
tally. A theoretical derivation with outset in the self-similar framework is developed for this transitional
case, showing that this corresponds to the indenter being displaced diagonally.

Introduction
Plastic problems in solid mechanics are inherently history dependent. When solving these problems
numerically through the finite element method, the constitutive equations are formulated in an incremental
form, and the history of the problem is solved for by time integration through the Lagrangian incremental
method. Some plasticity problems reach a steady-state stage in its history development, and others can be
characterized by having the property of self-similarity. For these problems, a direct connection between time
and space exist and this can be utilized to capture the history dependence. By utilizing this connection, the
classical time integration through the Lagrangian incremental method can therefore be interchanged with
spatial integration, opening up for vast possibilities in terms of precision and accuracy, that are unmatched
in a Lagrangian frame of reference. This thesis deals with a self-similar indentation test and a steady-state
scratch test, and shows that they from a numerical point of view are the same. Furthermore, the effective-
ness of the spatial integration scheme will be exploited to conduct various parameter studies, studying the
transitional phase between a self-similar indentation test and steady-state scratch test.
The spatial integration method was originally developed by Dean and Hutchinson (1980) to model
steady-state crack growth, but the general framework can be applied to any type of problem that can be
described by having the steady-state or self-similar property. The steady-state or self-similar property is
recognized in an elastic plastic problem when the stress/strain field is appearing as constant in time. In order
for the physical field to appear as constant, it has to be observed from a dynamic reference frame that adapts
to the specific problem at hand. The steady-state crack growth from Dean and Hutchinson (1980) can be
used to illustrate this; If the crack growth through the material is observed from a fixed/stationary coordi-
nate system, the stress and strain fields would appear as moving in time as the crack is propagating. On
the other hand, if the stress and strain fields where to be observed from a moving coordinate system, placed
on the crack tip, the physical fields would appear as constant in time. From this moving reference frame,
material points would appear as "flowing" horizontally towards, and past the observer, while the physical
field remains constant. Borrowing terminology from fluid mechanics, it can be said that the points flow in
horizontal "streamlines". Since the physical field remains constant while the points flow in their respective
streamlines, then, drawing analogies to fluid mechanics, the Lagrangian rate of change of any physical quan-
tity would only contain a spatial rate of change in an Eulerian sense, much like a fully developed, uniform
pipe flow in fluid mechanics. Thus by utilizing the steady-state property of the crack growth, a relation
1
==PAGE:9==
between the time rate of change of any physical quantity in a Lagrangian sense can be transformed into a
spatial rate of change in an Eulerian sense.
Taking advantage of this connection between the derivatives in time and space is the central idea behind
the spatial integration scheme developed by Dean and Hutchinson (1980). In the classical Lagrangian sense,
the constitutive equations are expressed in incremental form with regards to pseudo time, and time inte-
gration is performed to capture the history dependence. On the other hand, the spatial integration method
exploits the steady-state nature of the problem and creates a connection between time and space through the
Lagrangian and Eulerian frames of reference. This allows a transformation of the incremental constitutive
equations with regards to space instead of time, which implies that the history dependence can be captured
through space integration, where the streamline represents the path of integration. The possibilities and ad-
vantages of this method are numerous, for instance, the solution can solve the steady-state solution directly,
without having to go through the development of the crack first. By doing so, greater numerical speed is
attained, and since it does not accumulate errors during integration of this phase, it yields greater accuracy as
well. Another important advantage is that, since the physical field is stationary with regards to the dynamic
coordinate system, the mesh can be refined easily at locations of interest. This particular characteristic will
be exploited in great depth in this thesis.
The spatial integration method is not as utilized as the Lagrangian incremental method for solving plas-
ticity problems. Nevertheless, a handful of researchers have used the method to investigate various aspects
of steady-state crack growth. Some of the investigated topics have been: the effect of crack velocity on ma-
terial toughness for strain rate dependent materials Landis et al. (2000), The fracture toughness in an elastic-
plastic material joined by a laser weld Niordson (2001), 3D-modeling of ductile crack growth for thin plates
Sobotka and Dodds (2011), strain gradient effects on fracture toughness for homogeneous rate-sensitive
metals Nielsen et al. (2012), crack growth characterized by strain gradient plasticity Wei and Hutchinson
(1997).
The spatial integration method originally developed for the steady-state crack growth has in recent times
been expanded to model self-similar problems. Juul et al. (2018) has implemented the framework to model
self-similar wedge indentation of an elastic-viscoplastic single crystal, as well as a monazite single crystal
Juul et al. (2019).
The foundation for the self-similar indentation problem is, like its steady-state counterpart, to implement a
dynamic coordinate system where the physical field appears as constant. As the indenter is being vertically

Theory
In this section, the underlying principles for the steady-state and the self-similar integration techniques
are presented. Despite the fact that they are derived from two physically different load cases, namely a
scratch and an indentation test, it will be shown that their fundamental connection between an Eulerian and
Lagrangian frame of reference are almost identical. Furthermore, the constitutive law used will be presented,
which shows that by narrowing down the physics to a rate independent J2-flow model for plasticity, the two
integration techniques become identical.
4.1
Steady-state
The main objective of this subsection is to present the steady-state framework with outset in the scratch-
test. Plasticity is traditionally implemented by means of the Lagrangian incremental scheme, where the
integration is done through time to find the history dependent solution. The spatial integration method also
captures this solution, but takes advantage of the steady-state nature of the problem, which enables the time
integration to be transformed into a spatial integration. In order to do so, a transformation between temporal
derivatives into spatial ones is a necessity, and this will therefore be established in this subsection.
The spatial integration scheme, originally developed by Dean and Hutchinson (1980), was intended to
model the steady-state propagation of a crack tip. However, regardless of its original intention, what enables
the integration scheme to be implemented is that a steady-state solution can be identified for a given problem
at hand, i.e., whether it is the stationary crack tip propagation, a scratch test or a third phenomenon to be
modelled is irrelevant, as long as a steady-state stage can be identified in the problem. In this thesis a
scratch test will be modelled, and a brief overview of how the steady-state stage is reached and how it can
be identified will be presented. Figure 2 shows the three different stages of a scratch test, culminating in
a steady-state solution. Stage I illustrates when the indenter comes into contact with the scratch specimen.
The indenter can either make contact horizontally, coming from the side, or as illustrated in this figure,
vertically, effectively making this stage an indentation problem. For both cases however, a plastic zone
will develop around the indenter if the material is elastic-plastic and the indentation depth is sufficiently
large. Furthermore, the shape of the plastic zone will depend on the direction of initial contact from the
indenter. In stage II the indenter is displaced horizontally with the velocity ˙d, and the plastic zone starts to

and spatial derivatives of any physical quantity.

4.2
Self-similarity
This subsection introduces the concept of self-similarity. Just like the steady-state framework previously
described, the main idea here is to use the unique characteristics of self-similarity to enable the use of the
spatial integration scheme. This is done by establishing a connection between a stationary reference frame
where the physical field changes in time, and a self-similar reference frame where the physical field appears
as constant. Again, this connection is formally described by the streamlines and the material derivative,
connecting the temporal and spatial derivatives and thus interchanging temporal and spatial integration. The
following subsection is written with reference to, and with figures inspired from Christiansen (2022) and
Juul (2018).

4.3
Spatial integration
Having now established the connection between temporal and spatial derivatives for the steady-state
and self-similar problems, this section will show how to implement spatial integration to capture the history
dependence of the two problems. For the steady-state case, the path of integration can be described by
means of Eq. (2). This relation shows that for an observer in the moving, steady-state reference frame, a
given point (x1,x2) described in the stationary coordinate system will be flowing in the negative x′
1 direction
with the velocity ˙d, as illustrated in Fig. 6a. In Fig. 6a the location of the point relative to the steady-state
coordinate system is shown for different times, and the dotted line represent the path that the point travels,
called the streamline. As a point travels through its streamline, it goes through different stress states based on
its location on the path. Once a point is somewhere downstream on the streamline, then due to the physical
field being stationary in time, all points further upstream represents the history of that point. Therefore the
previous states/history of a point is represented by points further upstream. Doing spatial integration for
the horizontal streamlines, in the direction from upstream to downstream therefore captures the necessary
history dependence in plasticity modelling.
For the self-similar problem, the streamlines are described by Eq. (11). Here it is seen that for an
observer in the scaling, self-similar reference frame, a point (R,θ) described in the stationary reference
frame will be flowing radially and exponentially towards origo as a function of time. This is depicted in
Fig. 6b, where the flow of a material point in the self-similar coordinate system is shown for different times
at the dotted streamline. A slightly alternative way of seeing the flow of a point is through the stationary

Method
This section gives an outline of the algorithm used for the spatial integration method and how an mesh

has been implemented in a FE-model in ANSYS, in order to compare the results with the spatial integration

5.1
Spatial integration method
The part of this thesis that utilizes the spatial integration method takes outset in a FORTRAN 90 algo-
rithm written to solve a self-similar indentation problem, as part of the Ph.D thesis of Juul (2018). This
subsection will give an overview of the most important aspects of the algorithm and what have been added
in this current master thesis, especially how it is extended in order to seamlessly shift from an indentation to
a scratch test. The code can for the sake of clarity be divided into 2 sections, one outside and one inside the
iterative module, where the iterative module solves Eq. (18) iteratively by means of the spatial integration
method.
5.1.1
Outside the iterative module
Apart from the conventional material parameters suchs as Young’s modulus, poisson’s ratio etc., various
initial parameters can be defined by the analyst outside of the iterative scheme, such as the indenter depth,
angle and horizontal position. The algorithm was originally dependent on importing an external mesh file
generated in ANSYS. In this thesis the algorithm has been further developed to contain its own intrinsic
mesh routine. This is done in order to create a seamless shift between an indentation and scratch test since
the integration path, and subsequently the mesh, dictates which analysis will be performed (as well as where
the indenter is being placed). The intrinsic mesh routine furthermore enables customized features, such as
mesh refinement etc. which will be elaborated on in subsection 5.2. As mentioned in subsection 4.4, the
stiffness matrix from Eq. (18) only has to be assembled once, outside of the spatial integration scheme,

streamlines for spatial integration. Nevertheless, by enabling the possibility of mesh customization, a scratch
test can be simulated with ease and great accuracy on a radial mesh, how this is accomplished will be

5.3
ANSYS model for verification
An ANSYS FE model is created in order to solve the indentation and scratch problems by means of the
traditional Lagrangian incremental method, with the aim of comparing the results to the spatial integration
method. An outline of the FE model is shown in Fig. 10. Here a 40 × 100 mm rectangular domain is
created and meshed using 8 node quadrilateral elements with 4 Gauss points (PLANE183 in ANSYS). The
structure of the mesh is mapped, with an exponential decrease in element height as a function of the element
vertical position. The mesh has boundary conditions applied on all nodes on the two vertical and the lower
horizontal lines, with the horizontal and vertical displacements being locked. The indenter consist of a
5×10 mm rectangle and a bottom triangle with the angle φ = π/180. To minimize boundary effects during
the contact analysis, the indenter is placed 40 mm horizontally from the left domain boundary. Furthermore,
to model the indenter as a rigid body, its youngs modulus is set 10 times higher than the 40×100 mm block.
A contact pair is created between the adjacent indenter and block surfaces. The indenter is chosen as the
contact surface and the block as the target surface, this gives the indenter the ability to penetrate between
the nodes of the block surface. In addition, large deformation effect are enabled as well as plane strain in the
out of plane direction. In order to model J2-flow theory for the plasticity of the block, the material model is
chosen as a nonlinear, rate independent Von Mises plasticity model, where both the bilinear and multilinear
options will be studied, where the bilinear model refers to plasticity with constant tangent modulus, Et = k.
The multilinear model refers to a varying tangent modulus derived from an uniaxial stress strain curve. In
this thesis, a stress strain curve is imported into the model from discrete datapoints generated by Eq. (23).
ANSYS interpolates linearly between the datapoints to find the local tangent modulus, and if the simulated
stresses exceeds the maximum stress from the datapoints, it will implement ideal plasticity, i.e Et = 0. Hence
it is paramount that the stress strain curve has sufficiently large stress and strain values for the problem at
hand in order to avoid ideal plasticity. On the other hand, the stresses and strains should not be too large,
since ANSYS only can read in a 100 datapoints, and too large stresses and strains will thus compromise the
accuracy of the linearly interpolated tangent modulus.
The indentation and scratch tests are implemented by applying 2 load steps. The first load step vertically
displaces the indenter while locking the horizontal displacement of the indenter on the nodes belonging to the
top horizontal line, this effectively models an indentation problem. With outset in the vertical displacement
applied from the first load step, the second load step displaces the indenter horizontally and changes the
indenter boundary conditions from horizontal to vertical locking, ultimately modelling a scratch test. For

6.3
Concluding remarks
The above analysis has shown good agreement between the two integration methods when modelling
an indentation and a scratch test. The later is of particular importance, since it proves that the original
framework for solving a self-similar indentation problem can be extended to effortlessly solve a steady-
state scratch problem by implementing an integrated mesh routine and displacing the indenter horizontally.
Furthermore, some of the strengths of the spatial integration scheme have been brought to light. It was
shown that the stress field around the wakes for the scratch test had a significantly better resolution than its
32
==PAGE:40==
Lagrangian counterpart. In addition, the superior solution quality from the spatial integration scheme was
generated from a significantly coarser mesh, but was possible since the integrated mesh routine allows for
a local mesh refinement close the indenter tip. Implementing the same mesh refinement for the Lagrangian
method would require an immense amount of elements, or an adaptive mesh refinement, with a fine zone
moving with the indenter.

Diagonal displacement of the indenter in a self-similar framework
The previous sections have shown that the algorithm for the spatial integration scheme successfully can
model a self-similar indentation test as well as a steady-state scratch test, depending on the horizontal lo-
cation of the indenter. The indentation and scratch test are two extreme cases, where the indenter is either
located at origo or far enough away, such that the physical field is encapsulated in horizontal streamlines.
This subsection deals with the scenario where the indenter is displaced somewhere in between the two cases,
as illustrated in Fig. 17. From Fig. 17a to Fig. 17c, the indenter is being displaced further and further away,
with the same indentation depth. The red circle underneath the indenter represents the corresponding phys-
ical field. Since the radial mesh is being used, the streamlines are the sloped, dashed lines, and as with the
previous cases, points further upstream on a streamline represents the history of the solution. Fig. 17 shows
that for streamlines confined in the physical field, their slopes decreases as a function of indenter displace-
ment. The question to be answered here is which physical solution is being solved for when the indenter
is somewhere between the indentation and scratch test. Furthermore, what is the physical representation of
the decreasing streamline slopes, when the indenter is being displaced further and further away. To answer
these questions, a theoretical framework will be established, tying in some physical quantities regarding the
solution belonging to the intermediate case presented in Fig. 17. The derivation for connecting physical
quantities to the cases illustrated in Fig. 17 has a "backwards" logical structure compared to the previous
derivations for the indentation and scratch test. The derivations for the indentation and scratch test starts out
by making a connection between a stationary and a moving/scaling reference frame, and with outset in this
connection, it determines the required streamline orientation to capture the history dependence. In this case
however, the streamlines are given in the first place, as illustrated in Fig. 17, and the aim here is therefore to
develop a physical model that yields these streamlines.

horizontal distance, such that the plastic zone is confined to the positive x-axis. What ends up happening is
that each streamline is integrated from upstream to downstream, going through the streamlines in a clock-
wise direction and each streamline "updates" the physical values in the node in origo, since they all share
this node. The first streamline in the positive x-direction will have gone through the plastic zone and will
give the node in origo a stress/strain value. Then, as the last streamline in the negative x-direction is reached,
it will give the node in origo a stress/strain value that is completely different, since it has integrated through
a completely different physical field. It should be noted that all the figures besides xI = 0 mm has this kink.

8
Discussion
The validations of both the indentation and scratch test, through ANSYS, for the bilinear and power
hardening models all showed excellent results, but with small discrepancies between the ANSYS and the
steady-state method. Various plausible reasons for the discrepancies exists, one of them could stem from
the fact that the theoretical as well as numerical methods are completely different. From a theoretical
and numerical perspective, the spatial integration method solves the principle of virtual work on Eq. (18) by
assembling the stiffness matrix once, and incrementally evaluating the plastic strain contribution on the right
hand side until convergence has been reached. ANSYS, on the other hand, most likely solves a different
version for the principle of virtual work, where the plastic strain contribution is not seen as a forcing term that
is incrementally evaluated until convergence. Here, their version of the discretized principle of virtual work
equation is solved incrementally in time through the Newton Rhapson method, which stands in complete
contrast to the methodology presented for the spatial integration scheme. Thus, by having theoretically
different starting points for the spatial integration scheme and ANSYS, their respective numerical methods
will also vary, which consequently might lead to slightly different results. Furthermore, ANSYS utilizes
a different approach to the contact modelling, where a large deformation formulation is used. This is not
the case for the spatial integration scheme, which furthermore adds to the differences in their modelling
approach.
Another difference is that ANSYS models show the nodal solution, whereas the spatial integration mod-
els show the solution at the Gauss points. The Gauss points are more accurate, and this could be a contribut-

==PAGE:49==
ing factor for the small discrepancies seen between the two methods.
Everything else held equal, one could assume that the scratch-test would yield higher discrepancies than
the indentation test, since the scratch test for the spatial integration scheme did not have perfect horizontal
lines, and the domain was not a perfect 40 × 100 mm rectangle, like the ANSYS model. This however,
does not seem to be the case, as there is no significant difference observed in the discrepancies between the
indentation and scratch test. Nevertheless, the ANSYS scratch test shows a bad resolution at the wake re-
gion, despite having significantly more elements than its spatial integration scheme counterpart. This really
highlights the strength of the spatial integration scheme, which enables efficient meshing of the domain,
since no motion of the indenter occurs.
Regarding the section concerning the simultaneous displacement of the indenter in both directions, it
should be made clear that the numerical findings do not serve as a formal proof of the associated derivations.
From a logical perspective, they can only serve to falsify the theory, for instance if the numerical solutions
behave in a nonphysical way. In order to confirm the theory, it has to be validated through an ANSYS
model or experimental data. The parameter studies still hold intrinsic value, since they show the behavior of
the solution as it transitions from an indentation to a scratch, and how parameters, such as n, influence the
solution.
9
Conclusion
This thesis takes outset in the self-similar framework originally developed to model an indentation test
by Juul (2018) and extends it to efficiently model a steady-state scratch test. The self-similar and steady-
state framework utilizes the special characteristics of their physical fields to create a connection between
temporal and spatial derivatives, which enables the history dependence to be determined by spatial inte-
gration. The indentation and scratch test are both verified and compared with an ANSYS model, which
uses the traditional Lagrangian framework where the history is captured through time integration. Through
the comparison of the methods, the strengths of the spatial integration framework comes to light in terms
of computational speed, solution accuracy and the ease in which the mesh can be adapted to the specific
problem at hand.
In addition, a parameter study is conducted with the objective of studying the transition from a self-

9.1
Future work
An extension of this thesis would be to verify the solution for the case where the indenter is being dis-
placed in the horizontal and vertical direction simultaneously. This can be done with the already developed
ANSYS-model by applying the desired diagonal displacement of the indenter. If the solutions are to be com-
pared, then the domain for both the ANSYS model and spatial integration scheme should be made larger,
since the solution seems to be sensitive to boundary effects.
Another task would be to implement spatial integration method to problems in the manufacturing sector.
Here, problems such as rolling, drawing and welding are all characterized by having a steady-state solution.
The method is of particular interest here, since the computational time allows for "in time" simulations
during manufacturing processes. By furthermore extending the algorithm, such that it can load in and start
from a previous simulation, the computational time can become extremely fast when for instance performing
parameter studies, making the method even more attractive.
