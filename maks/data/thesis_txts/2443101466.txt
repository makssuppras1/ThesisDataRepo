Abstract
This report documents a project done on a booster heat pump used in a domestic hot
water substation that supplies a 15 apartment building supplying close to 60◦C water
for domestic use by utilizing district heating water at 40◦C. The booster heat pump is
an integrated part of the substation and is designed and built by Danfoss at location in
the Nordhavn district in Copenhagen. Earlier work has been done on the heat pump
including preliminary work by Danfoss on designing, dimensioning and building of the
heat pump as well as a master thesis done on building a test rig of the heat pump at DTU
along with a numerical model built in Dymola. This project was a continuation of that work.
Two separate simulations were done for steady state and dynamic analysis. In both simula-
tions the compressor size, condenser size and district heating ﬂow to the heat exchangers
was altered to investigate the heat pump performance and the start up. Simulations were
executed with the existing model with some changes needed for the dynamic model.
Simulations showed that the most eﬀective method for increasing the temperature to the
hot water tank was decreasing the ﬂow to the condenser by 10% resulting in 2◦C increase
of the water temperature in the steady state model and 3◦C in the dynamic model.
The condenser size variation was the only simulation showing diﬀerence in start up and
shut down speeds. The results showed a smaller heat exchanger with less material mass was
quicker heating up and cooling down than the base model size heat exchanger. Decreasing
the heat exchanger area by 0,33m2 decreased the ramp up of temperature to 55◦C by 23
seconds from the base model size.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
A large scale research and developmental project called EnergyLab Nordhavn was estab-
lished in the year 2015 for the Nordhavn district in Copenhagen. This project is meant to
address the challenges of integrating renewable energy sources into the energy infrastructure
and run until the year 2019. This is meant to demonstrate how focusing on diﬀerent aspects
from thermal, electricity and transportation infrastructure and down to smart controls of
smaller systems and components will improve the cost, ﬂexibility and eﬃciency in use of
renewable energy. As a lighthouse project this will spur on further research in the energy
sector and will be a model district for future development of energy infrastructure and
energy technologies in cities. A crucial factor to this project is contributing to Copenhagen’s
goal of being world’s ﬁrst CO2 neutral city by the year 2025 [1].
EnergyLab Nordhavn has divided the project into 10 diﬀerent work packages that each have
a diﬀerent focus aspect and lead by diﬀerent institutions or engineering companies. This
thesis will investigate a booster heat pump station used for supplying a multifamily building
with domestic hot water by utilizing ultra low temperature district heating(ULTDH) which
falls under the work package WP10 and is lead by Danfoss. Below is a deﬁnition of the
work package from EnergyLab Nordhavn.
Figure 1.1: Work package deﬁnition [1]
1.2
ULTDH
District heating has come a long way from its beginnings with the ﬁrst district heating
power plant built as early as 1903 in Frederiksberg Municipality. This power plant did
1
==PAGE:16==
not only rid the municipality of household refuse by burning it instead of burying but also
the waste heat could be utilized to heat diﬀerent institutions in the municipality. This
inspired diﬀerent communities in Denmark in the 20’s to follow by utilizing excess heat from
coolant water in smaller fossil-fuel powered power plants to mainly supply public buildings
with hot water. These power plants where generally centralized supplying hot water to
consumers close by which was a conﬁguration that grew in the whole of Denmark the next
couple of decades adding district heating substations for larger neighboring housing areas [2].
In short, the evolution of the district heating since the twenties has seen signiﬁcant changes.
Fuel crises in the seventies and the awareness of global warming has called for increased
share of alternative energy sources and lowering the share of fossil fuels. Technical im-
provements like more eﬃcient energy production, utilization of renewable energy sources,
insulation of piping, lower supply heat and stricter regulations on building insulation have
helped keep the district heating as a viable option both economically and environmentally
[3] [4].
Ultra low temperature district heating has been identiﬁed as a sensible evolution in district
heating and deﬁned under the 4th generation of district heating. According to the deﬁni-
tion made by Lund, H et al. (2014), several aspects have to be taken into account when
designing the district heating for the future. Getting district heating to the next stage calls
for changes in accordance to development in use of energy sources, energy infrastructure,
supply and at end user.
By lowering the supply temperature to 30-70◦C for ULTDH results in number of positives
for the district heating system. It opens up the ability to incorporate number of possibili-
ties in heat sources from renewable energy and heat recovery e.g. from industry. Lower
temperatures result in lower grid losses that helps keeping the source temperature down
making it more economically feasible. And utilization of electricity from renewable energy
sources at end user e.g. to heat up domestic hot water(DHW) can help add ﬂexibility and
sustainability to a smart grid [5].
To incorporate ULTDH in residential areas stricter rules must be applied when it comes
to energy demand of buildings. The deﬁnition of 4th generation district heat suggests
lowering energy demand to more than half of what is identiﬁed in the 3rd generation. This
can be done with stricter regulations on insulation and more eﬃcient ways in heating
e.g. ﬂoor heating and even incorporating smart controls. As for the domestic hot water
the challenge would be to heat the district hot water from as low as 40◦C to what is
acceptable in daily use. Taking user comfort and the possibility of bacterial growth into
consideration water must be heated to at least 60◦C. Certain conditions allow for water
temperatures to be lower in parts of the water installations but only for shorter periods.
From this it is clear that higher temperature production is favourable and for an example
have water boilers been used for this purpose. As water boilers are a direct method for
heating by means of converting electrical energy directly for heating a heat pump has
been identiﬁed as a more feasible option. The heat pump can greatly decrease the energy
needed to heat the water compared to the boiler. This has opened up the possibility to
utilize heat pumps in domestic hot water substations in smaller sizes for domestic hot water.
There is some interest looking at the return temperatures to the DH network from the
BHP station. Although there is not a law dictating the return temperatures a certain
2
==PAGE:17==
economical consideration is at hand. The DH sector is a non proﬁt marked where a well
balanced or self controlled (hvile i sig selv) principal protects users from paying unfair
prices for DH. In short, e.g. for a 15 apartment house, lowering the return temperatures
within a reasonable range has positive economical inﬂuence.
1.3
Motivation
In the purpose of investigating how a booster heat pump can be utilized in a ULTDH area
Danfoss has built and set up a working station in a 15 apartment multifamily house in the
Nordhavn district in Copenhagen. A test rig has also been built of a replica heat pump to
further research with initial testing done by Danfoss subsequently being donated to DTU
and set up in the research facilities. Previous work on the heat pump involves building a
numerical model of the heat pump and was also done by Danfoss. Other work involves a
masters thesis on adding systems to the test rig so that the heat pump can be run in in
a controlled testing situation as well in similar circumstances as the actual booster heat
pump in Nordhavn. The master thesis also dealt with further modeling of the base model
to incorporate these changes.
There are several diﬀerent aspects of interest when investigating a heat pump used for
speciﬁc work like a booster heat pump. The dimensioning of the heat pump components is
crucial so that it can operate correctly where incorrectly dimensioned components could
aﬀect the eﬃciency and performance. Another concern is the nature of its running which
is dictated by the hot water demand. Use of hot water in residential buildings ﬂuctuates
greatly during the day with peak loads being at early mornings and evenings. Whether
directly or indirectly, via heat exchangers or hot water storage tanks , the heat pump must
operate in an on/oﬀpattern controlled by demand throughout the day. An eﬃcient start
up could make a diﬀerence in how fast the heat pump delivers the correct temperature of
water which subsequently can eﬀect the level of comfort in delivery to user and the energy
eﬃciency.
1.4
Problem formulation
This thesis will investigate the heat pump used in a DHW sub station. The performance
aspects of the heat pump are of great interest for this project to further aﬃrm it as a
solution for ULTDH in the greater scheme of a sustainable future and used as a viable
option in a smart grid system like presented by EnergyLab Nordhavn. By investigating
the heat pump and setting up case studies to determine optimal dimensioning and control
of the heat pump it is possible to proposition possible changes for a better performance.
And by dividing the performance analysis into two, product from steady state operation
and start up speed by viewing the dynamic behavior of the heat pump these objectives
can be reached. These examinations will be aided by the use of a numerical model built
in Dymola using the Modelica mathematical language. Methods used and case studies
will be documented in this report along with the subsequent results with discussion and
conclusion of the ﬁndings.

1.5
Simulation purpose
By using a numerical model of the heat pump it is possible to investigate how the heat
pump works from a theoretical standpoint. This will also aid with further understanding
of its sub-processes and how diﬀerent aspects eﬀect its performance. This study can be
divided into three diﬀerent focus points. That is, ﬁrstly it is possible to assert the optimal
dimensions in a base model by studying results from altering components and reviewing
and comparing the results. Second, start up can have an eﬀect on the over all performance
and worth investigating. Here the numerical model can be used to view the dynamic
behavior of the heat pump to investigate how component design and external factors aﬀect
the speed of which the heat pump achieves steady state operation which is optimal for the
product. Lastly there are external factors and additional components that can produce
positive response in the heat pump. In this instance it is possible to adjust ﬂow rates of
DH water supplied to the heat pump to examine its reaction under start up. Another
option is adding relevant components to the base model to compare in both steady state
and dynamic operation.
1.6
Reading guide
Following this introduction section for the project there will be a section documenting the
methods applied in the thesis. There will be a conceptual model going over the theoretical
application of a heat pump system followed by a physical model to further clarify how
these apply to the system and its components. A mathematical and a numerical model will
describe how these methods can be implemented for the case studies. A section is dedicated
for the case studies to investigate and explain how changes to the model has an eﬀect on
the heat pump performance. A results section will discuss the most relevant ﬁndings and
emphasize for the following discussion and conclusion. A nomenclature along with an index
and list of tables and ﬁgures will be positioned in the preface of the report. All references
will be catalogued and numbered with the citation number in the bibliography after the
main body of the thesis. An appendix will be placed at the end with all relative material

2.1
Conceptual model
In this section the heat pump process will be described along with the substation. This is
done to get an overview what processes are used and their application in the domestic hot
water substation. Further more it is done to get a better grasp of the theories in play and
how they are utilized in there application.
2.1.1
Process description
A heat pump utilizes a thermodynamic vapor compression cycle that is based on the reversed
Carnot cycle which in turn is based on the ﬁrst and second laws of thermodynamics. The
ﬁrst law of thermodynamics states that in an isolated system energy is never lost or
destroyed, it can merely be transformed from one form to another. The second law of
thermodynamics states that all natural processes are irreversible and that heat in and
of itself is only transferred from a hotter body to a colder body. A Carnot heat cycle
can describe how these laws are implemented in an ideal vapor compression cycle with
reversible processes and no losses. Here the heat input to the cycle equals net energy
output and the heat transferred to the heat sink, Q1 = E + Q2 [6][7].

According to the second law of thermodynamics transferring heat from a colder to a hotter
body is only possible by introducing external work. By reversing the ideal Carnot cycle and
adding work to the process the basis for a refrigerating cycle is established. The diﬀerence
between a heat pump cycle and a refrigerating cycle is the operating temperature. This is
governed by the fact that a refrigerating cycle transfers heat to its surroundings and the
heat pump draws heat from the surroundings. In both cases the lower temperature of the
cycle is subject to a hotter body enabling it to supply heat to the cycle and transfer heat
to the heat sink at a higher temperature. This changes the premises of the Carnot cycle to
energy being delivered is equal to the external work and the heat transferred to the system
and can be described in the same form as before, Q1 = E + Q2 [6][8].

Compressor
The compressor is used for adding the work needed in the system to raise the pressure
level from the evaporating pressure to the condensing pressure and in a sense is one of the
components separating the high pressure side and the low pressure side along with the
expansion valve. Compressing the refrigerant in turn decreases its volume thus increasing
the density. Compression work in a compressor is not with out losses in friction and heat
losses to environment.
Heat exchangers
The condenser and evaporator have the objective of exchanging heat from one medium to
another with heat conduction and in this project are both counterﬂow plate heat exchangers.
They are built with multiple plates enabling the ﬂow of both water and refrigerant being
layered for better eﬀect. The main area of the heat exchangers is subject to the heat
transfer in the refrigerants phase change as it is a far more energy consuming process
than a one phase cooling or heating process. In the case of the condenser the area can
be divided up in three diﬀerent parts, cooling of the superheated refrigerant from the
compressor, two phase ﬂow where the refrigerant is in phase change from gas to liquid, and
a subcooling part where the refrigerant is cooled down past the saturation point in liquid
phase. The evaporator area can be divided in two diﬀerent areas where the refrigerant
is usually already in the two phase region after the expansion process in the expansion
valve. Here the refrigerant enters the evaporator in its gas/liquid phase where phase change
commences until reaching the saturated gas point. From there a small area heats the
refrigerant past that point to superheated gas state.
Expansion valve
The expansion valve is the other component separating the high pressure and the low
pressure sides along with the compressor. The subcooled refrigerant is expanded in a small
oriﬁce opening lowering the pressure from the condensing- to the evaporation pressure
where the refrigerant reaches the initial phase change condition i.e. only a small share of
the refrigerant has changed from gas to liquid before entering the evaporator.

2.2.1
District heating source
Flow of DH can vary in the Nordhavn area or around 35-50◦C, for simpliﬁcation of analysis
the ﬂow is assumed steady at 40◦C and will be the base value for the temperature input
to the model. A main ﬂow from the DH network is fed into the substation where it is
divided between the condenser and the evaporator. As this is an input ﬂow divided into
two diﬀerent streams the assumption is taken that the division does not entail any change
in temperature or pressure. With this assumption a mass ﬂow can be directly divided
in two diﬀerent mass ﬂows. The mass ﬂow to the evaporator and the condenser from
earlier work is 0,2005 kg/s and 0,1867 kg/s respectively. The sum of the two mass ﬂows
are divided from the source input and implemented to the model via two separate input
components. There are no heat changes immediately after the divide of ﬂow and therefore
the main ﬂow input is assumed 0,2005+0,1867=0,3872 kg/s. This ﬂow, although can vary
is assumed steady for simulation purposes. This mass ﬂow will be the base value when
simulating the heat pump model and for cases where the ﬂow distribution is changed the
sum will always add up to the base value of the DH source.
Looking at the mass ﬂow division like this is a way to simplify assumptions for control
of the heat pump. The actual BHP has more valves for controlling ﬂow to each heat
exchanger where it is possible to alter ﬂows independently from each other. Doing this
would have an eﬀect on the main DH feed to the system and due to uncertainties of how it
is controlled this is considered out of scope for the project.

Heat exchangers
The heat exchangers are of a micro-plateheatexchanger type from Danfoss. They are built
for use with R134a refrigerant on one side and water on the other. For modelling of the
system there is utilized a ﬁxed heat transfer rate input for both the condenser and the
evaporator, calculated and implemented from earlier work on the project. Although a ﬁxed
heat transfer rate does not accurately represent the inner processes of the heat exchange of
the component taking into account diﬀerent temperature diﬀerences during start up and
the initial conditions set in the model component there are some assumptions made to
ease the degree of numerical diﬃculty in the program. First of all the heat exchangers are
designed with a new dimple channel design from Danfoss that the model does not oﬀer
as an option to implement. For an example there is a heat transfer model available for
a corrugated plate design that can be implemented but due to uncertainties in how the
two correlations diﬀer the simulation results can not be assumed to be fully representative.
Another point is that after trial and error in the simulation process the implementation of
a heat transfer model is a heavy task for the program to execute if initial conditions and
assumptions are not accurate or suﬃcient for the program to simulate. Attempts made
resulted in numerical errors for the simulations on repeated attempts thus for the sake of
the projects process the ﬁxed values deemed adequate.
For the on/oﬀsimulation of the model the DH ﬂow is steady through the water side of
the heat exchangers. This results in the refrigerant in both the condenser and evaporator
are subject to the 40◦C water and when compressor is turned oﬀthe heat exchangers will
force the dormant heat pump system to seek thermodynamic balance to that temperature
in stead of ambient temperatures of e.g. facility or room the system is located in. This is
assumed to represent the heat exchangers being clad in insulation increasing the time for
cool down between operation if no water ﬂow is present.
Expansion valve
The valve used in the BHP station is a Danfoss thermostatic expansion valve TCAE068U4297
with a bulb to regulate superheat in the suction line. In this project a ﬁxed size eﬀective
ﬂow area expansion valve component is utilized as the implementation of the more complex
thermostatic expansion valve is deemed out of scope of this project. This valve component
can be viewed as similar to a capillary tube. Here some considerations must be made
if analysing the refrigerant charge or movement when the heat pump is turned oﬀ. The
reason being when a heat pump is turned oﬀa thermodynamic expansion valve will sense
no superheat in the suction line it will close with no ﬂow going through. When a system
with a capillary tube is turned oﬀthe ﬁxed opening will allow the refrigerant to ﬂow over
to the low pressure side and in most cases collected in an accumulator before reaching the
compressor.
13
==PAGE:28==
This diﬀerence in operation is assumed to eﬀect the dynamics of the heat pump in starting
up and shutting down due to the TEX self regulating properties. The TEX valve can
facilitate a faster start up where the compressor does not have to move the same amount of
refrigerant before achieving superheat in the evaporator as with a ﬁxed oriﬁce valve. This
being caused by the TEX closing when the compressor shuts oﬀholding of the migration of
refrigerant between high and low pressure sides and ﬁrst opening when sensing superheat
again in the evaporator.

Numerical model
The numerical model is built in Dymola that is based on the mathematical language of
Modelica. It has a component based user interface with predeﬁned components from the
Modelica library with add-ons from the TIL and TIL media libraries. Modelica components
are basic mathematical block components where as the TIL libraries have a more compre-
hensive thermodynamic component and media collection. This oﬀers up the possibility to
build numerical thermodynamic models with detailed components in a system like a heat
pump with deﬁned refrigerant properties [10].
A large advantage of using Dymola is the division of the modelling and the simulation
environments. All the results are stored in the model during simulation and are accessible
in the simulation window. Here it is possible to isolate parameters for data export or
in-program visualization with graphs.

the heat pump at start up/shut down. When the heat pump is not in operation the system
seeks thermodynamic balance with the ambient meaning the evaporator will be subject
to lower temperatures than the 40◦C of the DH source. As this happens a larger share
of the condensed refrigerant is moved from the high pressure side through the expansion
valve and to the evaporator as the pressure decreases from operational pressure to pressure
subject to ambient temperatures [11]. The graph shows how each cell’s decreasing vapour
quality as model reaches steady state.
At steady state it is evident that 13 of the 20 cells used to divide the evaporator are in
two phase ﬂow leaving the refrigerant in cells 14-20 in super heated state. This meaning,
as mentioned before, that 35% of the evaporator area has super heated refrigerant. As
the heat transfer of the evaporator is subject to the type of refrigerant ﬂow, with higher
heat transfer with two phase ﬂow compared to one phase gas ﬂow, an ideal evaporator
would have as small of a super heated area while securing a one phase gas ﬂow at output
to protect the compressor. Here a possibility is to adjust the oriﬁce size in the expansion
valve component with the objective to lessen the super heated area of the evaporator.

Chapter 4
Case studies
Main case studies in this project are divided into a steady state performance analysis
and an on/oﬀoperation analysis. The steady state simulations are used to look at the
performance of the heat pump by varying the sizes of the compressor, condenser and the
DH ﬂow to the condenser. This is done to understand the heat pump operation under
diﬀerent circumstances in an steady state operation. Any changes seen in these simulations
could facilitate a better understanding of underlying sub-processes and behaviour and how
to implement or realize said changes in an on/oﬀoperation of the heat pump.
To start with changes will be implemented separately, looking at steady state performance,
determining if they have a positive or a negative eﬀect on the system. From there similar
changes will be made to the model for an on/oﬀoperation to see how those changes
translate. Further more analysis will be done on the on/oﬀoperation to see how changes
eﬀect the speed of which the system starts up from stand still.

Chapter 5
Discussion
This project aimed at modelling and analysing a booster heat pump test rig situated in
DTU. As the test rig was not completed in time for experimental work to be done the
focus was shifted solely to the modelling. Numerical simulations were done in Dymola that
uses the Modelica mathematical language and the models used in this project are based on
earlier work done on the booster heat pump project. The main work was divided in two
diﬀerent aspects where the ﬁrst part was simulating and analysing how altering dimensions
of the compressor and condenser along with the DH feed to the heat exchangers would
eﬀect the steady state performance of the heat pump. The second part was applying the
same changes and simulating the model in an on/oﬀfunction to see how these changes
would translate and identifying what beneﬁts a quicker start up along with seeing if the
temperature increases to the hot water tank would translate between studies.
Working on the steady state simulations was done by establishing a base model for com-
paring diﬀerent changes to the system. Variations were done on parameters of interest and
results analysed. For the on/oﬀsimulation of the heat pump it was necessary to change a
model component from a scroll compressor type to a more simple eﬃciency type compressor
component to facilitate the oﬀperiod of the compressor needed. This being the heat
pump achieving a thermodynamic balance e.g. with pressures and wall temperatures of the
heat exchangers equalizing to their subjected heat before next start up of the compressor.
Doing this an on/oﬀsimulation was possible for analysing the dynamic behaviour of the
system during start up and shut down and the eﬀects of before mentioned changes of interest.
The steady state results showed that resizing of the compressor component had limited
eﬀects on the heat pump performance in whole. Increasing the size of the compressor
resulted in a slightly higher output temperature of water to user but at a cost of higher
power input of the compressor with negative eﬀects on the heat pump COP.
Steady state results for the condenser showed no signiﬁcant change in the heat pump
performance. A noticeable trend was observed in the start of the simulations throughout
the condenser resizing runs that showed an improvement in the system reaching steady
temperatures of the output water to the tank. The model with a smaller heat exchanger
reached steady temperatures quicker than one with the bigger component in each run.
These observations were not used or deemed applicable as the models initial start period is
subject to numerical uncertainties and diﬀerent component conditions than assumed for
the on/oﬀsimulations. Rather these observations were noted for discussion of the on/oﬀ
simulations.
33
==PAGE:48==
Some changes were needed on the base model for achieving a working on/oﬀoperation
for the dynamic modelling. New components were introduced to enable this with studies
done for comparing to the older model. These simulations showed somewhat of the same
results as the steady state simulations for the reason being that the execution can be seen
as a more elaborate way for running the heat pump from oﬀcondition to its steady state
operation. But for the condenser size variation it was possible to conﬁrm the diﬀerence in
start up time neglected in the steady state simulation due to numerical uncertainties in
the simulation start.
In both simulations the most direct way of increasing the water temperature to the water
tank was decreasing the ﬂow of DH water to the condenser with minimal eﬀect on the heat
pump COP.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Modelling of the heat pump was done in Dymola for both steady state and on/oﬀoperation
analysis. The base model was used with success although some work was identiﬁed needed
to be done on the modelling. For the dynamic simulation there was a need to replace
the compressor component with a more simple one to ease numerical diﬃculties during
simulation. Experimental work on the BHP test rig was not done as it was not ﬁnished in
time for work to commence. In both simulations it was identiﬁed that the most eﬀective
way of controlling the water output from the heat pump was altering the DH ﬂow to
the condenser. For decreasing the DH ﬂow to the condenser by 10% it was possible to
increase the output temperature to the tank by 3◦C in the dynamic simulation and 2◦C in
the steady state simulation and this diﬀerence being subject to the diﬀerent compressor
components between simulations. The only simulation having an eﬀect on the start up of
the BHP was the condenser heat exchange both noted in the steady state and conﬁrmed in
the dynamic simulations. By having a smaller heat exchanger the mass of the component is
smaller resulting in both faster heating and cool down of the component. A heat exchanger
with 0,33m2 than the base model was 23 seconds faster to 55◦C than the base size and a
0,52m2 bigger heat exchanger was a full minute slower.
6.1
Improvements in work
When working on a numerical model there are many hindrances and consideration to be
made for the model to work properly and as realistically as possible. Not only are there
ﬁner adjustments to be made on the model but most importantly a veriﬁcation and a
validation process to be done for concluding a model in general is working in the best
accordance to a physical system being simulated. For this project especially there is a
opportunity to do experimental work on a test rig designed to emulate the booster heat
pump in Nordhavn but not possible during this project due to time constraints.
The Dymola program with the TIL and TIL media packages is a powerful tool to simulate
a thermodynamic system like a heat pump and has possibilities of implementing robust
component designs to a model of that kind. For a program to simulate a complex dynamic
model a good deal of work has to be done to implement more detailed model. For the base
model in this case there was opted for the use of more simple components to achieve wanted
simulation results. In this project there is room for improvement in implementing pressure
drop and heat transfer models in the heat exchangers, there is a possibility to implement
a thermodynamic expansion valve instead of a ﬁxed oriﬁce valve and pressure losses in
pipes. Other considerations in the likes of control for the DH to the heat exchangers not
35
==PAGE:50==
only as set values as done in this project but possibly dynamic control during start up
could be interesting. These implementations could be done to establish a model of the
heat pumps already existing set up of components and focus on optimizing its performance
with control strategies. The idea here is that were there improvements to be made as per
model analysis supported by veriﬁcation of experimental analysis there would be a more
feasible solution in changing smaller components like valves and electric controls rather
than time and monetary investments of new system set up.
6.2
Further work
As the experimental analysis of the test rig was not possible to validate the numerical
model further work could be done not only on the base model but also for earlier work on
the project. This could also facilitate work to be done on the improvements on the model
both for more detailed modelling and the additional work for control strategies.

Appendix D
Varied eﬀective ﬂow area size of
expansion valve
A test was done on the oriﬁce size with preliminary simulations to establish an operational
window from where absolute superheat in the evaporator exists to a situation where no
super heat leaves the evaporator, only one phase liquid or two phase liquid/gas ﬂow. This
being purely for practical reasons for testing and investigating the model boundaries.
These simulations revealed a maximum size for only super heated refrigerant ﬂow being
0.0456·10−5 m2 and a minimum size for no super heat being 0.1514·10−5 m2. With this
information a ramp input component was introduced to the model to enable the model to
simulate the valve size increase from 0.0456·10−5 m2 to 0.1514·10−5 m2. By viewing the
vapour quality, water temperatures and heat pump performance during this simulation
could give a clue or an insight for an optimal ﬁxed eﬀective ﬂow area in the expansion
valve. It has to be noted that the model will react dynamically to the size changes and
the results will not represent steady state behaviour. Although step sizes are small and
executed over a long simulation period a concrete solution is not expected but rather a
insight to which sizes would be of interest for further investigation. The simulation showed
changes in the system as expected. Increasing the oriﬁce valve beyond the default size
decreased the amount of super heated area as well as lowering the exit temperature of water.
Further analysis showed increased evaporation and condensing temperatures but with a
lower pressure ratio. This could indicate that although the two temperatures increased,
the distance or gap between the two is lessened that results in a higher COP for the heat
pump.
Figure D.1 shows a possible optimal point in the isentropic eﬃciency of the compressor
with the changes to the expansion valve oriﬁce size over time. The local maximum eﬃciency
was found at 3960 seconds into the simulation. To verify if this is an eﬀect of the dynamic
behaviour in the simulation it is crucial to identify the valve size at the exact point in time
this occurs.
