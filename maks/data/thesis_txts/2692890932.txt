1. Abstract  
Climate changes compose a tremendous threat to marine species in the oceans. Increasing ocean 
temperatures is causing declines in dissolved oxygen levels, which challenge species physiology, 
fecundity and growth rates. Decreasing oxygen levels, together with eutrophication caused by 
agricultural activities, river runoffs of waste and sewage creates critical conditions for the benthic 
fauna, that is isolated from the more oxygenated upper water layers due to stratification of the water 
layers. Shellfish and stony corals are especially challenged due to carbon dioxide emissions to the 
ocean, as it creates acidic surroundings when it dissolves in the water, making it harder for the species 
building their shells and skeleton.  
Little focus has been on low oxygen concentrations (hypoxia) in tropical ecosystems; therefore, 
this review aims to emphasize the importance of considering hypoxic conditions as part of 
environmental changes in the oceans. Tropical ecosystems and coral reefs are particularly vulnerable 
to hypoxic conditions due to persistent warm temperatures all year round. Hypoxia is just as important 
to measure, together with the concentration of nutrients, and temperatures since hypoxia most likely 
have a massive impact on mass mortality of marine species.  
This review focus on hypoxia in tropical coral reefs, how some coral species are extremely adaptable 
to climate change and what knowledge gaps that needs to be covered to prevent future mass 
mortalities of reef biota.

2. Introduction  
2.1. Hypoxia: Global threat towards all marine organisms   
Hypoxia is a global issue in oceans in both temperate, subtropical, and tropical areas, as it disturbs 
the biodiversity and the ecosystems (Nelson, H. R. & Altieri, A. H. 2019). Hypoxia is usually 
activated by eutrophication, rising ocean temperatures and stratification of the water layers (Diaz, R. 
J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). Furthermore, it can threaten our oceans food resource if the fertilizer runoff, 
carbon dioxide emissions, and waste to the oceans is not lowered. 
Studies have primarily investigated hypoxia in the temperate regions but not so extensively in the 
tropics. The limited of focus on hypoxia in the tropical regions is problematic, since indications has 
shown that those areas are more vulnerable to the condition (Altieri, A. H. et al. 2017).  
This review aims to emphasize the importance of highlighting these regions, along with what is 
already known preliminary about hypoxia in the tropical oceans, and how to prevent more 
bleaching/mortality events from destroying coral reef ecosystems.

River runoff from fertilizers and burning of fossil fuels are increasing the eutrophication in the oceans. 
When nutrients and minerals are added to the water it creates an accumulation of particulate organic 
matter (POM) which enhances the microbial activity such as algal blooms (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg,

R. 2008). The most serious threat from eutrophication is when the microbial community starts 
consuming more dissolved oxygen in the bottom water layers, causing lower oxygen levels. 
Low oxygen levels in the bottom water layers are often referred to as hypoxia or dead zones. 
Hypoxic and dead zones have been reported from all over the world but mostly in temperate regions 
associated with large population centers (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008 and Altieri, A. H. et al. 
2017) (Fig. 1). Most of the hypoxic zones have been reported after the beginning of the industrial 
production of nitrogen fertilizer, which began in the late 1940’s and exploded in the 1970’s (Diaz, R. 
J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008 and Galloway, J. N. et al. 2008). Declines in oxygen concentration have 
been reported before the critical state of hypoxia occurs, indicating that it is possible to act through 
management strategies before the entire ecosystem is affected. To prevent this expansion of hypoxic 
and dead zones, fertilizers should be kept on land and prevented from running into the water or 
develop fertilizers that are risk-free towards the aquatic environment (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 
2008).   
Increasing ocean temperatures, decreases the oxygen concentration emerging hypoxia, while warmer 
water holds less soluble oxygen (Nelson, H. R. et al. 2019). It often leads to stratification of the water 
masses, where the thermocline separates the warmer water from the colder water. The two layers are 
prevented from mixing due to the different densities of the layers. The more oxygenated water in the 
upper layers does not circulate down to the marine organisms living in the bottom layers causing 
hypoxic and dead zones in the ocean (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020).      
2.2. Definition of hypoxia 
Hypoxia represents two states: one where the oxygen supply to the organism’s tissue is decreasing in 
a way that the organism cannot sustain its normal functioning, another is an environmental state, 
where the oxygen in a water body is decreasing. Hypoxic conditions in the ocean describes when the 
dissolved oxygen levels are low inducing a threat to aerobic organisms, as it can result in changes in 
behavioral and physiological reactions, decreasing growth rates, reduce fecundity, and finally causing 
mortality (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020).  
An often-used indicator of when a system is in a hypoxic state, is when the amount of dissolved 
oxygen is lower than 2 mg O2 L-1 (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020 and Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). 
In some cases, it is a misleading indicator since some species are more tolerant and others more 
susceptible towards low oxygen levels (Haas A. F. et al. 2014 and Altieri, A. H. et al. 2017).

The progression of hypoxia (Fig. 2): 
1) First phase: Eutrophication enhances the deposition of organic material, which creates an 
increase in microbial growth, and respiration. Increased microbial growth and respiration 
requires more oxygen which promotes a depletion of dissolved oxygen if the water column 
stratifies.  
2) Second phase: Hypoxia occurs in shorter periods along with mass mortalities of the benthic 
fauna.  
3) Third phase: After some time with more nutrients and organic material that sediments, 
hypoxia becomes seasonal or periodic. Periodic hypoxia is shown in the so called “boom-and-
bust” cycles of animal populations where the biomass increases and later decreases in cycles. 
4) Fourth phase: This phase is introduced when hypoxia occurs for years, and organic material 
and nutrients accumulate in the sediment. It creates an expansion of the hypoxic zones and as 
long as the dissolved oxygen continues to fall, hydrogen sulphide (H2S) generated by the 
microbial growth is released (anoxia). Anoxia has been documented in the Mexican Gulf, 
Chesapeake Bay and Danish waters (Kemp, W. M. et al. 2005; Turner, R. E. et al. 2008 and 
Conley, D. J. et al. 2011).  
 
Hypoxia can occur in periods, and in worst case in a permanent dead zone where the depletion of 
oxygen results in mass mortality of the benthic fauna such as corals, invertebrates, and fish (Hughes, 
D. J. et al. 2020).  
Most common form of eutrophicated hypoxia is seasonal hypoxia in temperate regions during the 
summer after the spring bloom, where the water temperature increases, and the stratification is 
strongest. When the water temperature rises there is less dissolved oxygen in the water leading to 
respiratory distress in the aquatic organisms (Altieri, A. H. et al. 2017). It means that the organisms 
have to respire more when water temperature increases (Radboud University 2016). Strong

stratification can also be a threat to the organisms since the water masses forms layers with different 
properties of temperature, salinity or oxygen in the water column, that act as barriers of mixing the 
water. It prevents the larger amount of dissolved oxygen in the least dense top waters from being 
mixed with the denser bottom waters. When the oxygenated upper water layer closets to the 
atmospheric oxygen is not mixed with the bottom water layer the benthic fauna is in risk of mortality.   
Periodic hypoxia tends to be less severe for the fauna, since it usually only lasts from days to weeks 
meaning that the benthic fauna has time to recover and recolonize (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). 
Episodic hypoxia is a state that can occur with less than one event per year meaning that, sometimes 
it can be years before a new event happens (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). Episodic hypoxia is 
usually an indicator for a system to have reached critical eutrophication, which in combination with 
high temperatures and strong stratification, can turn the system into a dead zone over time. Most 
seasonal hypoxic zones have experienced episodic hypoxia in the beginning (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, 
R. 2008).

2.3. Recovered areas from hypoxia  
There are examples of recovery of hypoxic zones (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). Areas with 
minor hypoxia recover faster than anoxic areas.  
One example was the loss of fertilizers from the former Soviet Union. A reduction of the nutrient 
loadings to the Black Sea, which were in a hypoxic state before, resulted in stabilization of the oxygen 
levels and the ecosystem started to improve with recolonization of the benthic fauna (Mee, L. et al. 
2006).   
Another case was the decrease in water column stratification which occurred in the Gulf of Finland, 
and improved the dissolved oxygen levels, since the water masses were more mixed. It caused a 
recolonization of the benthic fauna, however as the stratification increased; the previous hypoxic 
conditions returned (Karlson, K. et al. 2002).   
Final instance was the improvement of the dead zone in the northern Gulf of Mexico. Here, the dead 
zone was a result of the freshwater discharge from the Mississippi River where a massive amount of 
nutrients was transported from agricultural activities to the ocean. Once the river flow was reduced 
the hypoxic zone was limited to a smaller area (Rabalais, N. N. et al. 2007).     
Though it may take years for severe hypoxic zones to recover into a normal ecosystem, it is clear that 
management of nutrient loads to the ocean seems to be a very efficient way of regenerating hypoxic 
zones into normoxic zones.   
2.4. Hypoxia in temperate and tropical regions 
There is more extensive research about eutrophication and hypoxia in temperate regions than, in 
tropical regions (Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008 and Howarth, R. et al. 2011). Nitrogen pollution 
is the main reason for hypoxia in the temperate regions due to outlet of fertilizers from agriculture to 
the oceans. However, the impact of nitrogen might be more severe in tropical regions, since the 
ecosystems are much more vulnerable towards nitrogen pollution. Thereto an increasing nutrient 
fluxes to the coastal waters in the tropics is seen, and it is suggested that some of the largest inputs of

nutrients will occur over the next couple of years. This elucidates the importance of unraveling 
hypoxia in tropical regions (Downing, J. A. et al. 1999 and Galloway, J. N. et al. 2004).  
The enhanced vulnerability towards hypoxia in tropic regions can be explained by the exposure to 
warmer temperatures all year round, which decreases the soluble oxygen in the water and increases 
the oxygen demand by the microbes and metazoans. Microbes can generate hypoxic conditions and 
metazoans, that depend on the oxygen, can stimulate primary production that fuels microbial activity 
(Altieri, A. H. & Gedan, K. B. 2015).  
Hypoxia can also be explained by the frequent storms, like typhoons and hurricanes that can affect 
the nutrients and stratification of the water layers generating hypoxic conditions (Herbeck, L. S. et al. 
2011). The wind-driven upwelling and mixing of the water together with nutrient input from land, 
creates phytoplankton blooms and eutrophication (Shiah, F. K. et al. 2000; Lin, I. et al. 2003; Zeng, 
G. M. & Tang, D. 2007; Zhao, H. et al. 2009 and Sun, L. et al. 2010). As a result of this, stratified 
water masses are generated, and water transparency is reduced causing hypoxic conditions.  
Many of the coastal tropical countries are developing regions, where fertilizer runoff to the water is 
increasing rapidly, and where human population growth and agricultural activities exceeds the 
capacity to control sewage waste and run-off to the oceans, compared to the temperate regions (Lu, 
C., & Tian, H. 2017 and M. E. A. 2005). People are furthermore dependent on their tropical 
ecosystems in regard to food resources, tourism, and shoreline protection in order to keep up their 
well-being, income and safety of land (Barbier, E. B. et al. 2011). Less than 1% of the seafloor is 
covered by corals, yet the habitat still hosts above 25% of known marine species (Reaka-Kudla, M. 
L. 1997), indicating that it is a very important ecosystem to protect.                   
Several studies of the consequences of hypoxia towards aquatic animals, especially fish, has been 
conducted in the temperate regions. The few hypoxic studies in the tropics also focus mostly on fish 
behavior and not much on other species like corals. When fish are exposed to hypoxia, they would 
try to maintain oxygen delivery by increasing the waterflow over the gills which they can do in two 
ways. They can increase their ventilation frequency and/or increase the stroke volume (Wu, R. S. 
2002). If dissolved oxygen levels keep decreasing in the water the fish can conserve their energy by 
moving slowly, prevent burst swimming, and thereby reduce their oxygen need. Fish can detect when 
they are exposed to hypoxia and avoid it by migrating from low oxygenated water to water layers 
containing more oxygen (Pihl, L. et al. 1991). In Pihl, L. 1989, Pihl, L. et al. 1992 and Pihl, L. 1994 
it is reported that cod and whitning migrates to more oxygenated water, when oxygen concentrations 
fall below 40-25% air saturation. Dab and flounder also migrate but they are more tolerant to lower 
oxygen levels than cod and whitning, since they do not start migrating before oxygen concentrations 
fall below 15% air saturation (Gray, J. S. et al. 2002). Migration is a smart strategy when hypoxia

2.5. Acidification of the oceans  
Acidification is another great threat to both temperate, subtropical, and tropical regions. Though it 
does not directly cause hypoxia, it can contribute to reducing the organism’s tolerance to hypoxia. 
Ocean acidification is caused by CO2 emissions from the air, which can be an important risk in the 
survival of corals. PH-value has been declining causing an acidic water environment, due to the 
increase of anthropogenic CO2 emissions to the oceans over the years (Bijma, J. et al. 2013) (Fig. 4). 
When CO2 is dissolved, it reacts with the water and form carbonic acid (H2CO3). Carbonic acid easily 
breaks down to hydrons (H+) and bicarbonate (HCO3-1). Usually carbonate (CO3) binds to calcium 
(Ca2+), but when there are plenty of hydrons in the water, due to the CO2-emissions from the air, then 
the hydrons binds more easily to carbonate (CO3) than to calcium (Ca2+). Formation of calcium 
carbonate (CaCO3) is then prevented, causing a problem for the shellfish, since they need the mineral 
for building up their shells (Fig. 4). In tropical ecosystems it would have similar consequence for the 
stony corals, that needs the minerals to build up their skeleton (Howarth, R. et al. 2011). When this 
acidification happens in marine ecosystems it will, in combination with eutrophication, worsen the 
situation. Hypoxic and anoxic waters are more acidic than normoxic waters because the organism’s 
respiratory consumption of oxygen, that drives the limited concentration of oxygen in the water, is 
coupled to the production of high levels of the acid CO2.

Developing evidence proposes that when pH-values decreases in the seawater, due to anthropogenic 
carbon dioxide emissions, it could reduce the organism’s tolerance to hypoxia. In Weber, M., et al. 
2012, experimental work was carried out on the encrusting coral Montipora peltiformis showing that 
this species could survive exposures of anoxia and low pH-values for at least 4 days if the exposures 
was conducted separately. Though, when a combination of anoxia and low pH values was exposed to 
the corals it led to mortality after 24 hours. It is possible that oxygen depletion conditions occur when 
acidification occurs, since respiration from microbes and algal blooms, that generates hypoxic 
conditions, also generate the presence of carbon dioxide which lower the pH (Altieri, A. H. & Gedan, 
K. B. 2015). Whenever, experimental work similar to this, is conducted in the laboratory it is 
important to keep in mind that the thresholds established by these laboratory manipulations, most 
likely would overestimate the corals tolerance to hypoxic conditions. Laboratory experiments simply 
cannot simulate the real conditions from the oceans by varying in light, pH, diel oxygen cycle, and 
temperature during day and night.

Encrusting corals are characterized by low spreading growth forms, that usually grow from hard

Hemispherical corals are characterized by these ball-shaped structures and grows relatively slow. 
Since they have very stable profiles, hemispherical corals are rarely damaged by strong wave action, 
unless they are dislocated from their fixing point.  
Tabular corals are plate-shaped structures that can grow in a foliose flower-like structure as 
Agaricia lamarcki. Due to the folds in the structure the surface area is increased, and the space 
between the folds can provide a shelter for other marine species.     
In Altieri, A. H. et al. 2017, the hemispherical Stephanocoenia intersepta survived low oxygen levels 
of 0.5 mg O2 L-1 up to 7 days, and therefore showed a high tolerance to hypoxia compared to the 
tabular A. lamarcki, that died during the same experiment, and showed very little hypoxic tolerance. 
A correlation is also seen with the above illustration (Fig. 5) of susceptibility to hydrodynamic 
disturbance, and the morphology, where hemispherical corals are more tolerant than tabular corals. 
Corymbose and branching corals are characterized by having numerous branches, usually with 
secondary branches. Species of such morphology are more susceptible to breakage, when storms 
occur, and to decreased amount of dissolved oxygen and other nutrients to the inner part of the 
branches, where waterflow is limited.  
In Evensen, N. R. et al. 2021, the corymbose Stylophora pistillata was examined due to great thermal 
tolerance. The thermal tolerance is high likely explained by adaptation to high temperatures by 
migration from the south part of the Red Sea. The experiment neither conclude anything about the 
morphology, but it is an important study for possible future coral refuge, which is explained later in 
section 4.2.3.  
In Alderdice, R. et al. 2020, the corymbose Acropora tenuis showed greater tolerance to hypoxia than 
the corymbose Acropora selago. It was described by Acropora tenuis containing more HIF genes, 
which is more detailly explained in section 4.2.1. Since their morphologies are the same, this 
differentiation can only be explained by their inner morphology, such as the concentration of HIF 
genes.  
In Haas A. F. et al. 2014, the results showed that branching coral Acropora yongei had a relatively 
high lethal threshold to hypoxia at 4 mg O2 L-1, compared to the more conventional threshold 2 mg 
O2 L-1 (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020 and Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). Comparing this with the 
experiment conducted in Altieri, A. H. et al. 2017, a correlation is seen with the above illustration 
(Fig. 5) of susceptibility to hydrodynamic disturbance, and the morphology, where hemispherical 
corals are more tolerant than branching corals.

3.2. Diffusive boundary layer (DBL) and diffusion rate  
The rate of diffusion of dissolved substances like oxygen must occur through passive diffusion into 
and out of the coral and is dependent on the thickness of the diffusive boundary layer (DBL) (Shashar, 
N. et al. 1996; Dennison, W. C. & Barnes, D. J. 1988 and Patterson, M. R. et al. 1991). When DBL 
is thin, there would be a faster diffusion rate, and when DBL is thick the diffusion rate will be slow. 
In Shashar, N. et al. 1993, they exposed the massive coral species Favia favus to a current speed of 5 
cm s-1, that decreased the DBL from 2.44mm to 1.90mm, which increased the diffusion rate of oxygen 
into the coral. Increased water movement and high temperature decreases the thickness of DBL, and 
thereby increase the diffusion rate (Patterson, M. R. et al. 1991; Shashar, N. et al. 1993; Kühl, M. et 
al. 1995 and Jørgensen, B. B. & Revsbech, N. P. 1985). If the water flow is slow, the DBL will be 
thicker, and the rate of passive diffusion of oxygen will be slow. Calm water can be a threat for the 
corals and generate hypoxic conditions, since the diffusion rate would be very slow.  
The movement of fish in the water between the corals can help creating more turbulence, which means 
that the DBL would be thinner, and therefore allowing the rate of passive diffusion of oxygen to be 
faster (Goldshmid, R. et al. 2004). High abundance of fish at coral reefs, positively affects the corals 
in having higher photosynthetic rates, they grow faster, and their reproductive outputs are greater 
compared to coral reef without fish (Liberman, T. et al. 1995; Garcia-Herrera, N. et al. 2017).    
3.3. Diel oxygen cycle  
All corals are surrounded by three layers that controls water movement, diffusion of solutes going in 
and out of the coral, photosynthesis and respiration. 
First layer closest to the coral holobiont is the diffusive boundary layer (DBL), which usually is 
millimeters thick and is formed by diffusion of benthic metabolism like photosynthesis and 
respiration. Secund layer is called momentum boundary layer (MBL), which is a viscous layer about 
centimeters thick, and is formed by products released by benthos and the water movement friction 
over the coral. Third layer, the benthic boundary layer, is around 1m thick, and is formed by water 
turbulence and controls the interactions between the coral reef and the open seawater (Shashar, N. et 
al. 1993; 1996 and Barott, K. L. & Rohwer, F. L. 2012).  
The amount of dissolved oxygen is changing in the DBL during day and night. During day, 
oxygen production drives hyperoxic conditions and during night, oxygen consumption drives hypoxic 
conditions (Shashar, N. et al. 1993 and Silveira, C. B. et al. 2017). Corals are therefore used to 
fluctuating oxygen concentrations during a diel cycle and can tolerate these changing conditions over 
a short period of time. Corals are oxyconformers like many other sessile marine organisms, which 
==PAGE:18==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
18 
means that they cannot ventilate their external surface, and therefore enhance the exchange of 
substances with the surrounding environment (Shick, J. M. 1990 and Patterson, M. R. 1992). It is thus 
necessary for the corals to live in symbiosis with organisms, that can produce oxygen for them e.g., 
the single-celled flagellate protozoa called zooxanthellae belonging to the family, Symbiodiniaceae 
(LaJeunesse, T. C. et al. 2018). During the day, the zooxanthellae produce oxygen by photosynthesis 
due to light availability. About 78-90 % of the produced oxygen by the zooxanthellae is consumed 
by respiration of the coral, demonstrating that the symbiosis is highly important for the coral’s 
wellbeing (Kühl, M. et al. 1995; Al-Horani, F. A. et al. 2003a and 2003b). The production of oxygen 
by the zooxanthellae exceeds the amount of oxygen consumption by the coral respiration, which 
means that the extra produced oxygen is released from the coral to the surroundings during the day 
creating hyperoxic conditions (Yonge, S. C. M. et al. 1932; Finelli, C. M. et al. 2006; Al-Horani, F. 
A. et al. 2007 and Mass, T. et al. 2010). During night, photosynthesis stops due to the lack of light, 
though coral respiration continues but at a lower rate creating hypoxic conditions (Kühl, M. et al. 
1995 and Al-Horani, F. A. et al. 2003a). As the zooxanthellae during night cannot perform 
photosynthesis and produce oxygen, which the coral would consume, the coral must uptake oxygen 
from the environment during night by passive diffusion (Al-Horani, F. A. et al. 2007). If oxygen is 
depleted in the environment, the corals would be challenged to uptake enough oxygen due to hypoxic 
conditions.

When light is unavailable during the night, the respiration will continue but photosynthesis stops, 
which means that the coral needs to take up oxygen from the environment (Fig. 6, right side). The

3.4. Bleaching of corals  
Coral bleaching has been affecting many coral reef ecosystems worldwide such as Great Barrier Reef 
in Australia in 2002 (Berkelmans, R. et al. 2004), the Caribbean in 2005 (Eakin, C. M. et al. 2010), 
Malaysia in 2010 (Tan, C. H. & SF, H. 2011), Western Australia in 2011 (Moore, J. A. et al. 2012), 
northern part of Great Barrier Reef in Australia in 2016, middle part of Great Barrier Reef in Australia 
in 2017 and southern part of Great Barrier Reef in Australia in 2020 (Figueres, C. et al. 2020). 
Bleaching is a severe problem towards corals, and is often caused by environmental stressors like 
acidification, low salinity, increased solar irradiance, poison from cyanide fishing, seasonal cycle, 
pollution from heavy metals and extreme temperatures (Zhu, B. et al. 2004). These stressors are 
changing so rapidly which in turn prevents the corals from adapting (Hoegh-Guldberg, O. et al. 2007).  
The last three mass bleaching events at Great Barrier Reef in Australia happened over a period of five 
years. It is difficult for the reefs to have time enough to fully recover, since that usually would take 
15 years (Figueres, C. et al. 2020). Although, there might be a small change for the reefs to survive 
at a certain degree, because the bleaching events occurred in three different parts of the Great Barrier 
Reef. The two first bleaching events were activated by the usual human stressors like increasing 
temperatures. The third event was primarily activated by the massive bush fires that happened in 
2020, and the lack of focus on climate change’s impact on the oceans due to the Covid-19 pandemic 
(Figueres, C. et al. 2020). 
3.4.1. Definition of coral bleaching  
The term coral bleaching describes, when the coral holobiont loses its colorful tissue. Colors in the 
tissue are lost by expulsion of the dinoflagellate symbiotic algae, zooxanthellae, and the 
photosynthetic pigments under stress conditions like increasing sea surface temperature (SST) (Baird, 
A. H. et al. 2009 and Van Oppen, M. J. & Lough, J. M. 2008). Expulsion of the zooxanthellae causes 
the bleaching process, since the zooxanthellae usually would perform the photosynthesis for the coral 
host. Zooxanthellae are necessary for the corals to survive, because they absorb carbon dioxide, 
phosphate and nitrate, produced by the corals, and then transforming these substances into nutrients, 
which is required for the coral polyps (Zhu, B. et al. 2004). Bleaching is triggered by accumulation 
of reactive oxygen species (ROS), that damage the corals tissue and leads to the zooxanthellae 
==PAGE:20==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
20 
expulsion (Gardner, S. G. et al. 2017). If ROS could be neutralized in the coral host, there might be 
a possibility of preventing the expulsion of the zooxanthellae, and thereby avoiding the bleaching 
process.          
The response of losing the zooxanthellae is where the white calcium carbonate (CaCO3) skeleton 
becomes visible, because the tissue layer loses its color and develops transparency. The results of 
losing the zooxanthellae leading to the bleaching process is, that their fecundity and productivity are 
reduced, they become more susceptible towards diseases, and in the end they will not survive. When 
coral fitness is decreased the risk of degradation of coral reef ecosystems increase.  
The difference between coral bleaching and a coral disease, is unlike bleaching, where the coral 
tissue turns completely white but is still present, diseases are characterized by the absence of living 
tissue, exposing bare coral skeleton as it progresses (Fig. 7ab). Some diseases such as the “band 
diseases” are easy to recognize, as they form an “advancing colored band” on coral colonies (Fig. 
7b). Others, like “white syndrome” are much harder to recognize. Coral diseases mostly take the form 
of a narrow band of diseased tissue separating the living tissue from the exposed skeleton (Aeby, G. 
S. & Santavy, D. L. 2006). The band moves across the surface of the colony at the rate of a few 
millimeters a day, leaving behind bare skeletal material that is rapidly colonized by algae.

3.4.2. Corals adapting to bleaching  
Coral bleaching is a phenomenon, that has been known for years observed on small scale events. 
What changed since the 1980’s, is that bleaching is observed in much more large-scale mass 
bleaching events, such as the extensive bleaching and mortality event of corals on the Pacific Coast 
==PAGE:21==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
21 
of Panema (Glynn, P. W. 1983). Coral bleaching is now clearly related to anthropogenic stressors, 
like global warming, which is a wakeup call, where the actual response is seen, by corals losing their 
colorful tissues. Scientists predicts that within 20-50 years coral reefs will vanish if global warming 
continues (Hoegh-Guldberg, O. 1999). Though if the coral host somehow could adapt to the new 
warmer climate, by switching their symbiotic algae to a more thermally tolerant symbiotic algae, 
coral reef might survive the environmental changes (Buddemeier, R. W. & Fautin, D. G. 1993). In 
Buddemeier, R. W. & Fautin, D. G. 1993, they hypothesize that on behalf of available plant/animal 
symbioses data and by comparing with other symbioses (Fautin, D. G. 1991), they consider it possible 
for some taxa of hosts and algae to form stable symbioses with more than one symbiont. It is called 
the adaptive bleaching hypothesis and presupposes three processes (Douglas, A. E. 2003). First of 
all, the old symbiont has to be more inferior than the new invading symbiont. Second, the old 
population of the displaced symbionts needs to be completely emptied from the coral host. Third, the 
new population of superior symbionts needs to be available to invade the bleached coral host 
(Douglas, A. E. 2003). If this is possible, the coral host could theoretically switch between the 
symbiotic algae depending on the environmental state. 
Recent studies show that many organisms can adapt evolution relatively fast (Carroll, S. P. et al. 
2007). It would be much more convenient for a host to adapt to environmentally changes instead of 
wasting energy on searching and switching to another symbiont.       
4. Coral reefs today  
4.1. Current research about hypoxia on tropical coral reefs  
There has been some research about coral reefs, and how challenged they are due to climate change, 
but not many studies consider hypoxia in combination with temperature stress, pollution or 
eutrophication. However, one study was conducted about the effects of temperature, hypoxia, 
ammonia and nitrate on bleaching on three different coral species, Acropora nobilis, Alveopora 
verrilliana and Palythoa sp. (Zhu, B. et al. 2004). The outcome showed that these effects all result in 
bleaching of corals. By measuring the expulsion of the symbiotic algae, when the corals were exposed 
to the four effects, the results showed that Palythoa sp. in general were more tolerant towards higher 
temperatures, hypoxia and concentrations of ammonia and nitrate than the two other coral species. 
Palythoa sp. are encrusting corals, which are known to suffer the least from hypoxia, while branching 
corals like Acropora nobilis and Alveopora verrilliana suffer the most during hypoxic events 
==PAGE:22==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
22 
(Guzmán, H. M. et al. 1990; Simpson, C. J. et al. 1993; Genin, A. et al. 1995; Adjeroud, M. et al. 
2001 and Laboy-Nieves, E. N. et al. 2001).      
The symbiotic algae in corals need an alkalescent environment, where pH lies around 8.2 to 
maintain their normal metabolism. If hypoxia occurs in the seawater, a response mechanism from the 
corals is to start producing lactic acids due to anaerobic respiration. It causes an acidic environment, 
that complicates the metabolism for the symbiotic algae and thereby the survival of the coral host 
(Zhu, B. et al. 2004). Anaerobic respiration is a common strategy for many marine organisms, when 
they are exposed to hypoxic conditions (Vaquer-Sunyer, R. & Duarte, C. M. 2008). Another 
consequence when lactate acid starts to build up in the water, is that it can have lethal consequences 
towards especially smaller reef fish. When fish perform anaerobic glycolysis and produce lactate due 
to a hypoxic environment, they build up lactate in their body. It has more lethal consequences towards 
smaller fish, since they will run out of glycogen or reach the lethal levels of lactate and hydrogen 
faster than larger fish, due to their higher mass-specific metabolic rate (Nilsson, G. E. & Östlund‐
Nilsson, S. 2008).  
4.2. Adaptations and tolerances of corals to climate change   
4.2.1. Adaptation to hypoxia by possessing hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) proteins   
Some corals have adapted more to hypoxia than others, but there is still a lot of research that needs 
to be covered in that field. Fish’s adaptation to hypoxia is known, where fish have adapted by 
increasing ventilation frequency and stroke volume, reducing their oxygen need by decreasing their 
metabolism and migrating to more oxygenated water in the water column. Fish can sense if they are 
in a hypoxic environment by some specific proteins and thereby prevent cell death before it is to late 
(Mandic, M. et al. 2009 and Richards, J. G. 2011). The specific factor consisting of several proteins 
that plays a key role in cellular adaptation to hypoxia is called hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF), and 
these proteins are the sensing DNA bindings, that recognize when they are exposed to low oxygen 
concentrations (Zhu, C. D. et al. 2013). Now there are numerous fish species that possesses these 
hypoxia-inducible factors such as Atlantic croaker (Rahman, M. S. & Thomas, P. 2007), Wuchang 
bream (Shen, R. J. et al. 2010), scale-less carp (Cao, Y. B. et al. 2008), ruff, flounder, perch, 
stickleback, Russian sturgeon (Rytkönen, K. T. et al. 2007), and sea bass (Terova, G. et al. 2008). 
HIF genes are known to regulate cellular and systemic responses towards hypoxia, such as respirating 
(ventilation frequency and stroke volume), vasodilation of the blood vessels, anaerobic metabolism, 
erythropoiesis, which is the process that produces red blood cells, and angiogenesis, where new blood 
vessels are formed from existing ones (Rahman, M. S. & Thomas, P. 2007). 
==PAGE:23==

31. Maj 2021 
 
 
23 
In Alderdice, R. et al. 2021, two different coral species, Acropora tenuis and Acropora selago, 
were exposed to deoxygenation-reoxygention stress, that was supposed to simulate their natural diel 
oxygen cycle. Their morphology is characterized by corymbose clumps with thin branches (see table 
1). A. tenuis was the most tolerant species to deoxygenation-reoxygenation stress, and its inducibility 
of HIF target genes to hypoxic stress was greater than the species A. selago. A. selago showed a 
bleached phenotype response in the same experiment. Based on these results coral species containing 
more HIF target genes are more tolerant towards hypoxic conditions (Alderdice, R. et al. 2021).  
4.2.2. Adaptation to hypoxia by switching between the genera in Symbiodiniaceae 
Indications for corals adapting to climate changes are corals containing thermally tolerant 
endosymbiotic algae, which are more abundant on coral reefs that already have been affected by 
climate change. It is suggested that these corals and their symbiotic algae could have developed a 
greater resistance after surviving a thermal stress episode (Baker, A. C. et al. 2004). Corals contain 
different symbionts and therefore their sensitivity to bleaching varies (Rowan, R. et al. 1997; Little, 
A. F. et al. 2004; Baker, A. C. 2001 and Toller, W. W. et al. 2001). In Baker, A. C. et al. 2004, they 
investigated if a severe bleaching and mortality event on a coral reef can adapt the surviving corals 
to select a more thermally stable symbiont for future events. They conducted molecular examinations 
of the family Symbiodiniaceae in shallow waters from five locations all over the world, that have 
been affected by bleaching in different ways. They divided the different types of Symbiodiniaceae in 
three clades (A, C and D), to be able to distinguish such large-scale comparisons (Baker, A. C. 2001 
and 2003). Since then, the different clades of Symbiodiniaceae have been described into 7 genera on 
molecular, morphological, physiological, and ecological data (LaJeunesse, T. C. et al. 2018). In 
Baker, A. C. 2001 and 2003, they found that corals containing Durusdinium (clade D), became more 
dominant after bleaching events, while corals containing Cladocopium (clade C), were much more 
severely bleached (Glynn, P. W. et al. 2001 and LaJeunesse, T. C. et al. 2018). The results suggest 
that corals containing Durusdinium (clade D) are more dominant on coral reefs after severe bleaching 
and mortality events, which means that there has been a shift between these different genera of 
Symbiodiniaceae in the corals. Symbiodiniaceae is also known to have a short generation time, which 
means that they can mutate very rapid compared to the coral host (Van Oppen, M. J. et al. 2011). 
Therefore, a response could be that the symbionts and the coral host potentially could adapt quite fast 
to environmentally changes, which is needed in the future as anthropogenic stressors keep evolving 
(Howells, E. J. et al. 2012 and Hume, B. C. et al. 2016). 
Another possible adaptation to climate changes is the previous mentioned adaptive bleaching 
hypothesis, where the coral host switch the symbiotic algae out with another more tolerant species of

Symbiodiniaceae, which is seen in other symbioses (Buddemeier, R.W. & Fautin, D.G. 1993 and 
Douglas, A. E. 2003).  
4.2.3. Thermal tolerance of Red Sea corals  
In the northern part of the Gulf of Aqaba in the Red Sea, scientists have discovered a great thermal 
tolerance of the branching coral, Stylophora pistillata (Evensen, N. R. et al. 2021). From experiments 
in the laboratory the corals exhibited temperature increases up to 7 degrees above the local maximum 
monthly mean and was able to survive these extreme temperatures. Usually, corals undergo 
bleaching, when they are exposed to temperatures that are 1-2 degrees above their local maximum 
monthly mean (Liu, G. et al. 2014). It is hypothesized, that due to the migration of the Red Sea corals 
from the southern parts of the Red Sea, where the temperatures are much higher, it seems that they 
have retained their capacity to survive at areas with high temperatures (Fine, M. et al. 2013). The Red 
Sea corals have therefore kept their strong ability to higher thermal conditions, and surprisingly 
thrives under these conditions (Evensen, N. R. et al. 2021). This discovery is very important for coral 
reefs in the rest of the world, since they will keep being exposed to higher temperatures. Red Sea 
corals from Aqaba could therefore potentially serve as a model for restauration of other coral reefs. 
In order for these corals in Aqaba to serve as a refuge and possible model of restauration in the future, 
they need to be able to survive more stressors than rising temperatures. Corals from Aqaba also need 
to survive exposures of nutrients, heavy metals, wastewater and agricultural runoffs that corals all 
over the world are exposed to. Scientists from Aqaba tried to expose the corals to nutrients, which 
compromised the corals physiology and caused a decreasing robustness. It indicates that corals will 
have difficulty in adapting to extra nutrients, and therefore local stress like sewage and agricultural 
discharge must be removed in the potential areas to be able to protect the corals (Hall, E. R. et al. 
2018).  
4.2.4. Adaptation by dispersal of coral larvae 
Coral larvae can travel far distances in the water for approximately 30 days (Figueres, C. et al. 2020). 
Dispersal of larvae from surviving corals from a bleaching or mortality event is therefore a great 
advantage in spreading possibly more robust species and building new more tolerant coral reefs 
towards climate change. The potential way of adapting to environmental changes could appear when 
gametes or larvae are dispersed with Symbiodiniaceae from the maternal coral to the water. When 
the larvae settle in habitats that are environmentally challenged and grow to polyps, it could increase 
the possibility for the coral to establish itself into a suited coral holobiont to the environmental 
conditions. There will always be a selection of the larvae that will survive, settle and become a polyp,

K. M. et al. 2016).  
4.3. Numerical model predictions of coral reefs in the future 
Great Barrier Reef Foundation, CSIRO, The Australian Institute of Marine Science, Bureau of 
Meteorology and Queensland Government all together have formed a research project called, eReef, 
about the current status of coral reefs at Great Barrier Reef (GBR) and the future destiny of the reef 
(Baird, M. E. et al. 2018).  
The project includes the catchments, estuaries, reef lagoon and the open ocean and provides 
information on physical processes, sediment transport, biochemistry and ocean color. The eReef 
project reports enhanced monitoring, data standards, data architecture, operational modelling, 
reporting and data visualization.    
Modelling the conditions at coral reefs is an important tool to be able to describe and predict future 
scenarios for reef ecosystems e.g., temperature rise, acidic oceans or algae blooms. The eReef models 
are supposed to give numerical models, that are capable of simulation and prediction of the physical 
hydrodynamic state, sediment transport, water quality and basal ecology of the GBR lagoon and reef 
matrix. The outcome of all these models has the ability to simulate the transport and destiny of aquatic 
material of both oceanic or terrestrial origin, and its impact on the water quality at the reefs.    
As a part of the project a new coral bleaching model has been developed, which is a mechanistic 
model, considering thermal stress, irradiance and the concentrations of dissolved and particulate 
nutrients (Baird, M. E. et al. 2018). The model represents the coral host biomass and the symbiotic 
algae biomass. Furthermore, the model represents the processes of coral host and symbiotic algae 
growth and mortality and the photophysiological symbiotic algae processes of photoacclimation, 
xanthophyll pigment transformation, reaction centre inhibition, and the production of reactive oxygen 
species (ROS). Fig. 9 in appendix shows the bleaching model behavior at Davies Reef at a shallow 
water site (4.89 m) in 2012 and 2016, where it predicted the severe bleaching event in the beginning 
of 2016. The simulation showed that the bleaching model is successful in capturing the coastal 
features of the observed bleaching event at GBR. Though the model failed to capture the bleaching 
on offshore reefs since the model’s grid is unable to determine the depth of shallow platforms 
surrounded by deep water.   
Mechanistic models of coral bleaching like this one is important for management strategies, such 
as local shading (Coelho, V. R. et al. 2017), marine cloud brightening where a small fraction of 
incoming sunlight is reflected back into space in order to balance anthropogenic global warming or

(Anthony, K. et al. 2017). The eReef models have already been implemented in improving

5. Discussion 
After introducing hypoxia, the triggers of hypoxia such as eutrophication, rising temperature, 
stratification and the development and consequences of hypoxic conditions, I will now focus on what 
is important to conserve the coral reefs.  
First of all, it is necessary to discuss how different critical hypoxic thresholds needs to be considered 
to different coral species because of their varying tolerances. Second, an understanding of the coral 
morphology, corals uptake of oxygen, corals tolerance to fluctuating oxygen concentrations during a 
diel cycle and bleaching of corals is important to discuss to prevent future mass mortalities of coral 
reef ecosystems. By considering these topics, management strategies can be implemented to decrease 
human impacts on corals reefs. Third, it is essential to understand the current research on corals, and 
how some species have the ability to adapt to climate change and what can be learned from that. 
Finally, mathematical models are an important tool in management strategies to predict future 
bleaching events in the oceans, and hopefully be able to act before the bleaching/mortality events 
arise.     
5.1. Critical threshold and tolerance 
5.1.1. Hypoxic thresholds  
As previous mentioned the commonly used critical hypoxic threshold for when an ecosystem is in a 
hypoxic state, is when the dissolved oxygen levels fall below 2 mg O2 L-1 (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020 
and Diaz, R. J. & Rosenberg, R. 2008). The critical hypoxic threshold describes when marine 
organisms experience sub-lethal consequences, such as altered behavioral and physiological 
responses, reduced growth rates and reproductivity (Rabalais, N. N. et al. 2010) or lethal impacts by 
a certain concentration of dissolved oxygen in the water (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020). The conventional 
critical oxygen threshold at 2 mg O2 L-1 was originally used to describe other species like fish’s 
threshold to hypoxia (Renaud, M. L. 1986). Therefore, it can be misleading to use the same threshold 
for other marine organisms to hypoxia. Since corals usually are placed in shallow waters with 
==PAGE:27==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
27 
changing tides and a varying topography of their structure, their DBL is a lot more different, than the 
DBL on fish. Some fish also moves in other parts of the water, which means that the oxygen levels 
in the water can be very different from the oxygen levels at coral reef ecosystems. 
Not many studies have investigated sub-lethal and lethal thresholds for reef-building corals, 
except for the previous mentioned study with branching coral Acropora yongei, with a lethal threshold 
at 4 mg O2 L-1 (Haas A. F. et al. 2014), higher than the conventional hypoxia critical threshold. The 
branching coral A. yongei is one of the most dominant living contributors to reef systems in the Indo-
Pacific region. It is therefore concerning that A. yongei is more vulnerable to hypoxia than other 
corals, which are not as dominant (Hughes, D. J. et al. 2020).     
A contradicting study showed how the hemispherical Stephanocoenia intersepta can survive low 
oxygen levels of 0.5 mg O2 L-1 up to 7 days, and therefore showed a high tolerance to hypoxia, 
whereas the foliaceous coral Agaricia lamarcki died during the same experiment (Altieri, A. H. et al. 
2017). Both S. intersepta and A. lamarcki showed similar tolerances towards varying temperature 
conditions but revealed very different tolerances towards hypoxic conditions.  
The results from Haas A. F. et al. 2014 and Altieri, A. H. et al. 2017 and Fig. 5 suggest that differences 
in hypoxic threshold tolerance can be explained by differences in coral morphology, since branching 
corals seems to be more susceptible to hypoxic conditions than spherical, hemi-spherical, foliaceous 
and encrusting corals (Guzmán, H. M. et al. 1990; Adjeroud, M. et al. 2001; Laboy-Nieves, E. N. et 
al. 2001; Jiménez, C. et al. 2001; McClanahan, T. et al. 2001 and Nelson, H. R. & Altieri, A. H. 
2019).  
In the previous mentioned study Zhu, B. et al. 2004 it was also demonstrated that the encrusting coral 
Palythoa sp. suffered the least from hypoxia, whereas the branching corals Acropora nobilis and 
Alveopora verrilliana were more susceptible to hypoxic conditions. A range of different organism’s 
vulnerabilities to hypoxia with different critical oxygen thresholds is detected, which is not 
considered by the conventional oxygen threshold (table S2 in Vaquer-Sunyer, R. & Duarte, C. M. 
2008). Though, a total of almost half of the published research (43%) uses the conventional oxygen 
threshold at 2 mg O2 L-1 (table S2 in Vaquer-Sunyer, R. & Duarte, C. M. 2008).      
5.1.2. Branching corals vulnerability to hypoxia  
One reason for branching corals vulnerability to hypoxia could be explained by how the waterflows 
through the branches of a colony. When incoming water enters the coral colony, a momentum from 
the water to the colony is transferred, and this loss of energy creates a stagnant waterflow in the 
interior parts of the coral branches (Chamberlain Jr, J. A. & Graus, R. R. 1975). The lack of water 
movement and respiration between the inner branches of the coral colony can cause hyperoxia during 
==PAGE:28==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
28 
the day and severe hypoxia during the night (Nilsson, G. E. et al. 2007; Nilsson, G. E. et al. 2004 and 
Goldshmid, R. et al. 2004). During night with no waterflow the oxygen simply finds it challenging to 
perform passive diffusion through the DBL and into the coral. If waterflow increases, the water can 
enter further into the branches of the colony, but even at flow speed at 35 cm s-1, the stagnation of the 
water in the interior parts of the colony will not vanish completely (Chamberlain Jr, J. A. & Graus, 
R. R. 1975). Coral colonies with larger branches and more space between the branches experience 
more waterflow into the interior parts of their branches (Chamberlain Jr, J. A. & Graus, R. R. 1975). 
Therefore, branching corals are more challenged than spherical, hemi-spherical and encrusting corals 
when it comes to the diffusion rate of oxygen into the interior parts.    
Hypoxia has been an increasing problem since the 1980’s (Vaquer-Sunyer, R. & Duarte, C. M. 
2008). Hypoxia will continue to be a threat to the marine organisms in combination with the 
increasing eutrophication, where the production and adding of nutrients to the oceans enhance the 
oxygen demand of coastal ecosystems. Increase of the ocean temperature caused by climate change 
will also enhance the organism’s oxygen demand, reduce the amount of dissolved oxygen and cause 
stratification and thereby reduce the ventilation of the water masses (Service, R. F. 2004; Harris, L. 
A. et al. 2006; Carpenter, J. H. 1966 and Stow, C. A. et al. 2005). Especially benthic organisms are 
exposed to hypoxic conditions because they are located at the bottom of the oceans and are therefore 
further away from the upper water layers that is in contact with the atmospheric oxygen supply.  
5.2. What causes coral bleaching and mortality events? 
Coral bleaching and/or mortality events are caused by global warming, where the oceans are heated 
by fossil burning, carbon emissions and emissions of greenhouse gasses and through changes in ocean 
acidification and sea-level rise (Brierley, A. S. & Kingsford, M. J. 2009; Doney, S. C. et al. 2012 and 
Hoegh-Guldberg, O. & Bruno, J. F. 2010). Impacts like these creates a blanket around the earth 
increasing the global temperature. The oceans are capable by its massive capacity of taking up this 
heating created by global warming. If the oceans did not exist, the temperature on earth would be 
extremely high (Figueres, C. et al. 2020). On average the sea surface temperature on our planet has 
increased 0.13 Celsius degree every decade since the beginning of the 20th Century (Laffoley, D. & 
Baxter, J. M. (Eds.). 2016) (Fig. 8 in appendix). This is an average, which means that in some water 
bodies the temperature has maybe increased several Celsius degrees creating a so-called hotspot. 
Science has shown that coral reefs generally suffer in these hotspots due to the heating, therefore 
those areas have to be protected more (Figueres, C. et al. 2020). Heating of the oceans together with 
eutrophication, stratification and ocean acidification trigger hypoxic conditions, but the question is if 
it is the hypoxia that in the end causes bleaching and mortality of the corals (Nelson, H. R. & Altieri, 
==PAGE:29==
Julie Terp Jørgensen 
 
31. Maj 2021 
 
 
29 
A. H. 2019). Experimental research of corals usually has been measuring temperature and nutrient 
concentrations, without measuring the oxygen levels in water at the same time. If hypoxia is one or 
the main reason for corals dying, it is important to include those measures in the experiments. If 
increasing ocean temperature, eutrophication and stratification do not occur, the oxygen levels will 
not be affected. In other words, hypoxic conditions have not been detected in water bodies that are in 
a normal condition, indicating that hypoxia could be the potential reason for coral mass mortalities 
under such circumstances.  
Something has to trigger the hypoxia and when eutrophication, rising ocean temperatures, 
stratification or acidification happens it calls for reaction to prevent hypoxic and anoxic conditions. 
When e.g., eutrophication is detected it is possible to prevent the ecosystem from collapsing by 
decreasing fertilizers from farming, industrial waste or reducing sewage treatment (Diaz, R. J. & 
Rosenberg, R. 2008). Stratification is highly combined with rising ocean temperature (Hughes, D. J. 
et al. 2020), therefore by decreasing fossil burnings and CO2 emissions, increasing ocean temperature 
and stratification of water masses with different temperature properties, can be prevented. At the same 
time, acidification can be prohibited in the oceans by decreasing CO2 emissions from cars, airplanes 
and burning of fossils, avoiding coral bleaching and mortality. All mortality events have occurred 
when coral reefs have been in anoxic conditions for a while, but by detecting the stressors in time and 
reacting on them, these low oxygen conditions can be avoided.   
5.2.1. Protection of coral reefs 
When smaller coral reefs are hit by local stressors there are several ways of protecting them by reef 
farming or conservation (Figueres, C. et al. 2020):  
- 
Identifying the resilient reefs and find out how high/low their tolerances are to the stressors. 
- 
Harvesting slicks of robust corals when they spawn during the massive spawn events in 
November (Doropoulos, C. et al. 2019).  
- 
Building structures of concrete and deploy those in the water creating a skeleton for the corals 
to build reefs on (Abelson, A. 2006). 
- 
Breaking corals apart and transferring them into a laboratory, where they can get optimal 
conditions to grow rapidly, and then deploying them out to the water again (dela Cruz, D. W. 
2015).  
These protection strategies are more feasible at smaller coral reefs and not the entire Great Barrier 
Reef, since it is simply too diverse and too large to regenerate.

5.3. Effects on economy, tourism, fishing industry and coastal protection 
Degradation of coral reefs will affect the people living in developing countries who depend on fish 
consumption, which is around 51-94% of their total animal protein intake (Bell et al. 2009 and Teh 
et al. 2013). Large scale mass mortality events are a serious threat, not only to the ecosystems but 
also to the human population consumption. Several severe hypoxic events have already caused mass 
die-offs of fish on Ningaloo Reef in Western Australia in 1989, which resulted in over one million 
dead fish (Simpson, C. J. et al. 1993), and over 200.000 dead fish in the hypoxic lagoon between the 
South Keeling Islands in the Indian Ocean in 2012 (Hobbs, J.-P. A. & Macrae, H. 2012). Mass 
mortalities of fish can result in total extinction of some species with no change of recolonizing in the 
coral reefs.  
Large scale hypoxic events on coral reefs not only affects the human consumption but also the 
protection of shorelines, nitrogen fixation, waste assimilation and tourism (Moberg, F. & Folke, C. 
1999). Coral reefs together with seagrass beds and mangroves forests act as coastal protection towards 
storms and are therefore a very important defense shield in the coastal municipalities. Furthermore, 
coral reefs act as nitrogen fixers in nutrient poor areas, which not only is important for the local 
community but also for the productivity of other pelagic communities like fish due to their release of 
nitrogen (D’Elia, C. F. 1988; D’Elia, C. F. & Wiebe, W. J. 1990; Sorokin, Y. I. 1990 and Sorokin, Y. 
I. 1993). Coral reefs can also transform, detoxify and separate waste, which is released from land and 
therefore act as a cleansing service in the water to a certain point. Finally, coral reefs attract tourism 
which usually is economically needed in the developing countries, and if corals bleach or die there 
would be less motivation for tourist’s interest to visit the country. Hypoxic events can therefore have 
enormous social consequences for developing countries located close to the coasts besides the 
scientific consequences. 
6. Conclusion  
Based on this review it is clear that hypoxia is an important and uninvestigated subject for the marine 
environment, especially in the tropical regions. Basal knowledge about different corals species 
morphology is important to understand, to recognize their vulnerability to climate change. 
Experimental work of different species critical hypoxic thresholds is necessary to conduct, to 
distinguish how tolerant each species is to hypoxia, in order to act through the correct protection 
strategies, if corals are exposed to low oxygen levels.

Implementing marine models is an important tool in management strategies in predicting and 
reacting on future bleaching and mortality events. An understanding of the models and how to collect 
precise data is essential to get the most accurate prediction of future events. As of this moment the 
current bleaching model (Baird, M. E. et al. 2018) showed in the appendix Fig. 9 does not account 
for hypoxic conditions yet. However, it would be feasible to collect data about oxygen concentrations 
in the water and implement these parameters in the model to be able to include hypoxic conditions 
around the coral reefs.          
In the current research experimental work, where temperature and oxygen are measured at the 
same time, is yet not conducted. That is important to investigate in order to show whether if 
temperature, limiting oxygen concentration or a combination of those two effects are the cause of 
corals undergoing the bleaching and mortality process. For the future thoroughly measurements need 
to be conducted on the coral reefs and considering hypoxia as a serious threat in restauration projects.  
Corals are efficient in tolerating different oxygen levels due to the diel oxygen cycle, and some 
species are also quite capable in adapting to warmer temperatures. Therefore, other stressors have to 
be removed such as pollution, river runoffs, agricultural activities close to the coast and sewage runoff 
so that less pressure is on the corals and other marine species. Maybe then there would be a higher 
change of survival if increasing temperature and decreasing oxygen levels are the only threats towards 
the benthic fauna.  
A report from IPCC shows that as global warming continues, there is more focus on the effects 
such as acidification but also hypoxia in the oceans, “Limiting global warming to 1.5°C compared to 
2°C is projected to reduce increases in ocean temperature as well as associated increases in ocean 
acidity and decreases in ocean oxygen levels (high confidence). Consequently, limiting global 
warming to 1.5°C is projected to reduce risks to marine biodiversity, fisheries, and ecosystems, and 
their functions and services to humans, as illustrated by recent changes to Arctic Sea ice and warm-
water coral reef ecosystems (high confidence).” (IPCC, 2018). It is a step in the right direction that 
an organization like IPCC, that provide scientific information, which is relevant to recognize the 
scientific basis of the risks of anthropogenic induced climate change, creates awareness on the 
consequences of global warming in the oceans.
