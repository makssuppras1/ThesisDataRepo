ABSTRACT
1
Abstract
This paper investigates yaw-drive utilization for the IEA 15MW offshore reference wind
turbine and the implications of moving from a bottom-fixed to floating platform sub-
structure. The wind turbine is exposed to large wind direction changes based on wind
measurements from the Østerild test center site. The wind samples contain only the slow-
moving averages of the measured data, representing the main direction of the incoming
wind, that the implemented yaw controller should track.
By simulating the turbine
response for 11 one hour wind samples, the yaw-utilization and resulting loads are in-
vestigated for both fixed and floating substructure. As the transition to floating wind
turbines adds six new degrees of freedom to the system, potential instabilities are accessed
and mitigated as far as possible. The most significant differences in loads between the
fixed and floating simulations are highlighted and the level of importance is discussed.
The evaluation process of the yaw-drive utilization focuses on comparing the fixed and
floating substructure simulations with the main parameters being mean yaw error and
load statistics (mean, minimum and maximum) from relevant load channels. This study
serves as a baseline for yaw-drive utilization of floating wind turbines, an area which
has not yet been fully discovered. The simple, slow-moving winds implemented here are
very useful for preliminary analysis of the functionality of the yaw-controller, however
the addition of wind turbulence and waves are needed to obtain a greater understanding
of the wind turbine response in changing metocean conditions.

INTRODUCTION
3
Introduction
3.1
Current situation
Recent years has shown promising progress for the wind industry in spite of ongoing,
global challenges. Especially in the field of offshore wind energy, important steps forward
has both improved technology and the share of sustainable electricity production world-
wide. GWEC presented in their Global Wind Report 2022 [1] a capacity of 93.6GW new
wind installations at the end of 2021, bringing the total, global wind power capacity up
to 837GW. Hereof, 21.1GW offshore wind became grid connected and broke the previous
record in offshore wind energy. As it is shown in figure 1, China is playing a major role
in this rapidly increasing trend with Europe following up.
Due to the global energy crisis beginning in the aftermaths of the COVID-19 pandemic,
gas, coal and electricity prices have experienced large increases. Additionally, in the wake
of the Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, the European Commission released a plan to achieve
independence from Russian fossil fuels before 2030 [2].
The topicality of energy use,
security of supply and energy efficiency will most likely accelerate the installation and
development of wind turbines even further in the future. GWEC and IRENA propose
380GW of offshore wind by 2030 in their UN Energy Compact of 2021.
Figure 1: New offshore wind installations 2022, from GWEC.
The far majority of offshore wind energy comes from bottom-fixed turbines placed in
coastal areas, using either monopile, jacket, gravity base or similar as foundation. These
substructures have gained popularity and rapidly increased in numbers over the years

in order to increase the energy capture, which is the main objective when planning new

3.2
Floating wind turbine potential
Multiple factors are playing a role for the great trust in offshore wind energy. First of, the
wind resource for offshore locations is far greater than that of onshore locations. Stronger
and steadier wind will result in a higher energy yield for the wind turbine due to less
disturbance of the wind at sea. When looking at floating wind turbines, the potential is
even greater as these turbines can be located at deeper waters compared to bottom-fixed
solutions. Usually floating wind turbines become of interest at depths above 50m, as
fixed foundations become too expensive. The floating wind turbine is also less sensitive
to local variation in seabed conditions. In figure 2, a map is presented of desirable wind
resources, potential locations where floating wind farms could be deployed.
Figure 2: Floating offshore wind resource map 2021, from GWEC.
However, as floating wind turbine applications are relatively new, many things are still
unclear such as the best concept for the floating platform. Platform dynamics also be-
come an issue of great importance with possible instabilities due to changing metocean
conditions, i.e. coupled wind and wave effects. Especially wind direction changes can
have a large impact on the wind turbine response depending on the specific type of wind
loading as the offshore wind turbines are rapidly increasing in size. With increasingly
larger rotor diameters, higher loads will demand more of the blades, tower, etc. The
importance of the yaw mechanism, aligning the RNA (Rotor Nacelle Assembly) with the
incoming wind, consequently increases as well. During changing weather conditions, the

The main focal point of this report is to investigate the wind turbine behaviour of the

should of course be based on near-identical wind turbine setups and metocean conditions.
The evaluation process will concern load analysis and yaw-drive utilization. Due to the
added degrees of freedom (DOFs) for the floating platform, distinct motion from the
bottom-fixed substructure is expected and potential instabilities will be assessed.

LITERATURE REVIEW

Literature Review
4.1
Wind field generation
Modelling a realistic wind loading on the wind turbine rotor, including large direction
changes, is crucial to evaluate the yaw-drive utilization and the impact on power capture
and structural loads. Multiple approaches to describe these behaviors have been inves-
tigated in literature. In Larsén et al. [4], a spectrum of the lateral velocity component
is suggested which include mesoscale effects such as flow meandering. These low fre-
quency events become important when considering large areas, the size of offshore wind
farms. Hereby, slow-moving wind direction changes lasting for several hours, could then
be modelled and used in calculating the optimal operational strategy. Considering both
the Kaimal and Mikkelsen-Tchen model, the meso-boundary-layer model is created by su-
perimposing these microscale models with a simple mesoscale model. The result is then a
model containing the full frequency range from mesoscale (∼0.2 day−1) to the turbulence
inertial subrange (∼20 Hz). With this more general model, a better approximation of
the observed data was obtained compared to only using e.g. the Mikkelsen-Tchen model.
This was especially the case for less stationary days, i.e. days including large direction
changes (>20◦).
Hannesdóttir et al. (2019) [5] examined extreme events where the 10-minute variance of
the wind speed exceeded the Extreme Turbulence Model (ETM) level prescribed in the
International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) 61400-1 standard. These events were
based on measurements from the Høvsøre site in the westcoast of Jutland, Denmark and
where found to be caused by ramp-like increases in the wind speed. To generate turbu-
lent timeseries resembling the measured wind field, a constrained field approach was used.
The wind turbine response of this wind field was simulated using HAWC2 and showed on
average lower extreme loads compared to the IEC standard with an exception for wind
speeds in the interval of 8-16m/s. Here, a risk of high trust could occur on the rotor as
the wind speed exceeded rated wind speed leading to higher tower base fore-aft loads. In
the more recent paper by Hannesdóttir et al. (2022) [6], 50-year extreme coherent gusts
with a direction change were investigated. The results were compared the IEC standard,
showing between 20% and 74% higher load values than the IEC gusts of DLC 1.4. The
simulations also included a simple yaw controller that reduced the blade root flapwise
bending moment in the case of gusts with relatively long rise times.
Another procedure for generating a realistic wind field is proposed in Bossanyi et al. [7]
where the aim was to create a new method for tuning of yaw control strategies. The
procedure is based on long-term 10-minute site data as the low frequency input. The
data is curve fitted, thereby smoothing out the data and it becomes a representation
of the slow-moving variation in wind speed. On top, statistically matched turbulence is

4.2
Methods of yaw control
The importance of wind turbine yaw control is seen through numerous articles dealing
with the subject. And even more so for floating applications where the degrees-of-freedom,
and hereby the overall complexity, is increased on the wind turbine and floater assembly.
However, floating wind turbines are relatively new, with the "best" concept still being
unclear. The publicly available research on this area is therefore quite scarce to the au-
thor’s knowledge, thus needing more attention.
A review on the control strategy of horizontal axis wind turbines is presented in Yang
et al. [8].
Depending on the objective as a whole, three methods of yaw control are
presented. In regards to operation of a single wind turbine, one could opt for either
increasing the energy capture or reducing the fatigue loads. The third method is instead
aiming at maximizing the energy and optimizing loads in a wind farm. Here, the focus
will be on increasing the energy capture of a single wind turbine. The paper also states,
that as the inertia of the yaw system for large wind turbines causes a significant time
lag, it is impossible for the wind turbine to follow the wind direction in real time using
conventional sensors (e.g. wind vanes, anemometers). However, this is still the simplest
and a very common way of tackling yaw control. In a study by Bu et al. [9], the logic
control algorithm uses a conventional sensor, measuring the incoming wind direction at
the nacelle. It is converted with a DSP to a drive signal for the stepping motor operating
the yaw-drive. A more advanced PID controller is described by Chen and Yang [10] using
fuzzy control which is applicable for time-varying and nonlinear systems. PID control
is commonly used for pitch control of the wind turbine blades and therefore represents
proven technology. The fuzzy control logic contributes to a more robust PID control
system and adds to better tracking characteristics of the wind direction.
In order to work around the aforementioned time lag associated with conventional sensors,
wind direction estimation should be considered. Advanced measurements from LiDAR
sensors, described by Mikkelsen et al. [11], uses laser to measure upwind wind fields (axial
wind components) in a conical shape. This technology provides accurate measurements
and can measure instantaneous yaw errors in real-time. Several experiments have been
conducted with this relatively new technology showing promising results. In Kragh et
al. [12], a method for estimating the yaw error using a LiDAR device is analysed and
results from experimental data was compared with met mast data. However, some sig-
nificant shortcomings needs to be taken into account.
It was found that the LiDAR

Finally, predictions of the wind direction have also been made based on time-series models.

prevailing wind direction, results in the best predictions for wind direction compared
to the traditional-linked ARMA model. Other approaches are also available for wind
field predictions, e.g. using artificial intelligence/machine learning or spatial-temporal
models that, unlike the statistical methods, also take location and measurements from
neighbouring sites into account.
4.3
Dynamics related to floating wind turbines
With the absence of a fixed, rigid foundation, six degrees of freedom are added to the
floating wind turbine assembly. These are surge, sway, heave, roll, pitch and yaw whereof
the first three are translational motion and the three latter are rotational motion. In
order to have a stable system, the floater should be able to handle, and ideally minimize,
the motion related to turbine control and operation as well as the external metocean
conditions that the turbine experiences throughout it’s lifetime. The coupling of wind
and wave loading complicates the turbine response and may excite structural vibrations
or certain dynamics, possibly leading to instabilities. Therefore, the floater design have
to be chosen carefully.
Four main types of floaters are assessed by Bashetty and Ozcelik [14], namely a semi-
submersible platform, a barge platform, a spar bouy platform and a tension-leg-platform
(TLP). These platform makes use of either bouyancy, ballast (or a combination) or moor-
ing lines to achieve hydrostatic stability. Each concept having different advantages de-
pending on site requirements, operating conditions, construction etc. In this report, a
semisubmersible concept will be used in the simulations developed to support the IEA
15MW reference wind turbine.
Load analysis on floating wind turbines is limited in publicly available literature, however
Jonkman and Buhl [15] performed such an analysis at NREL. The aim was to characterize
the dynamic response for a 5MW turbine supported by a barge floater and identify

5.3
Data collection
The wind data used throughout this report is retrieved from two different sites in Den-
mark, namely from Risø and Østerild. At Risø, both wind data from a 70m met mast and
operational data from the neighbouring Vestas V52 (hub height at 44m) wind turbine is
available. This data is therefore used as a reference for yaw-utilization during changes in
wind direction. The wind data from Østerild, on the other hand, measures at up to 244m
and with less disturbances from the surrounding terrain. This makes the data suitable
for generating a realistic wind input for the following HAWC2 simulations.
5.3.1
Risø site
The test facilities are part of DTU Risø Campus, located on the peninsula of Risø 7km
north of Roskilde. Roskilde fjord lies to the west of the peninsula and as the dominant
wind direction in Denmark are from the west, see figure 5, the site makes for a good testing
area, with only few disturbances to the wind flow. One of these disturbances comes from
a small slope in the terrain towards the fjord. Whenever west wind is occurring, a sloped
flow will therefore be observed for both the wind turbine but also the met mast, which
is located in between the wind turbine and the fjord. Also wake effects on the met mast
will have an influence on the incoming wind to the wind turbine rotor. These effects
are nevertheless quite limited and a good correlation should still be present between the
measurements at the met mast and the wind turbine.

Similarly, the wind direction and wind turbine yaw angle is also available as 10-minute
averages. The yaw angle describes the orientation of the nacelle and thereby also the ro-
torplane which should be perpendicular to the wind direction for optimal power capture.
The yaw error is then defined as the difference between these two angles and gives insight
to how well the turbine tracks the incoming wind direction.
Additionally, 50Hz data of the yaw angle is also available of the V52 turbine, in the
same time period (2019-2023). The following procedure is used to arrive at estimations
of the number of yaw activations and total yawing time over the approximately four year
period. Figure 21 in Appendix A.2 contains a segment of the used data from the MySQL
database.
1. Extract only rows from the database with yaw angle changes from one time step to
the next, i.e. time steps with active yawing.

Implementing the wind samples directly into HAWC2 would mean interpolating linearly
between the four different heights of actual measurements. This, of course, does not give

described in the literature review, will be followed. This means removing the turbulence

the actual measurements.

5.4
Yaw controller
The yaw controller is used to correct the misalignment between the wind direction of the
horizontal wind components and the rotor direction. Multiple control strategies can be
applied to the yaw-drive, each with different complexity as it was described in the liter-
ature review. For this investigation, the yaw controller is based on an existing controller
developed by Albert M. Úrban and used in the article on extreme coherent gusts wind
direction change [6]. This controller has been further developed by Charalampos who
added an additional functionality to the controller. As his Master’s thesis ran in parallel
with this one, a continuous discussion on yaw control has assisted in creating this section.
A far more detailed description of the yaw control operation as well as analysis for the
added yaw mode is conducted in [3].

5.5
HAWC2 model
HAWC2 is an aero-servo-hydro-elastic software that models wind turbine response in
the time domain. It handles most structures, even of high complexity, through a multi-
body formulation using Timoschenko beam theory for the individual beam elements. The
bodies are connected with constraints and specified orientations in relation to adjoining
Page 22 of 40
==PAGE:25==
5.5
HAWC2 model
5
METHOD
bodies. The aerodynamics are modelled using blade element momentum (BEM) theory
with additional tools implemented to handle more complex effects. To control the tur-
bine, DLLs (Dynamic Link Library) are deployed to receive and give out new information
based on the many sensors. The hydrodynamic properties comes from the WAMIT code
and is linked to HAWC2 through the ESYSWAMIT interface.
The simulations presented in the following section are based on the IEA 15MW refer-
ence wind turbine for both fixed substructure (monopile) and floating using the UMaine
semisubmersible platform.
In both cases, identical wind blocks are added, featuring
the 10-minute moving average, linearly interpolated between the four measuring heights,
without turbulence. The yaw controller and associated yaw servo are added as DLL’s. In
order for the turbine to perform a yaw sequence, the constraint fixing the towertop to the
tower has been removed. Instead, a yaw bearing has been added through use of the sub
sub command bearing2. This constraint type allows for rotation where the angle is spec-
ified by the yaw controller. An additional force DLL is added for the floating simulations
to dampen any transient floater motion which might occur. Besides this damping force, a
transient period of 400 seconds is used to remove the initial, non-physical channel outputs
before they stabilize. The figures in the result section does not show this transient time
period - neither in the time series nor the frequency-based Power Spectral Density (PSD)
plots.

Floating platform
Moving to the floating simulations, the monopile has now been replaced by the UMaine
semisubmersible platform and also the tower has changed. In Appendix A.5, figure 24
shows statistics of the operational channels for both fixed and floating simulations where
close to identical behaviour is observed. With six additional degrees of freedom of the
floating platform, different frequencies and motions are expected in the wind turbine
response. The initial simulations with the floating wind turbine showed instabilities in
both floater motion and wind turbine loads. The first observed instability occurred in the
simulation of wind sample 9-11m/s after 1200 seconds and is displayed in figure 14. Here,
the floater surge and pitch are shown together with rotor speed, blade pitch and tower
base fore-aft moment. The large amplitude oscillations are due to pitch instability which
happens when the natural frequency of the closed-loop dynamic system for rotor speed
regulation is higher than the floater pitch natural frequency [23]. It therefore initiates as
soon as rated rotational speed is reached and dies out as the rotational speed decreases.
Pitch instability happens due to the nature of the thrust curve, where forward motion
of the floating wind turbine, into the incoming wind, will increase the relative wind ve-
locity at the hub and the blades will pitch out, decreasing the thrust. As a result, the
floating turbine will move or tilt even more forwards. When the turbine begins tilting
backwards, away from the incoming wind, the relative wind velocity will become lower
and the blades will pitch into the wind, increasing the thrust. This will help the turbine
move/tilt even more backwards and the cycle continues. In the PSDs of figure 14, a
large peak is found close to the floater pitch natural frequency (0.036Hz) also indicating
that this floater mode is excited. The remaining frequency domain is dominated by the
associated harmonics.
The floater pitch instability is present in all the wind samples above rated wind speed
and should of course be avoided as it also have a great effect on the wind turbine loads
(e.g. tower base fore-aft moment in figure 14. To minimize this problem, a different
tuning of the blade pitch controller could be used to lower its sensitivity. Aiming for a
controller natural frequency lower than the floater pitch natural frequency would make the
controller incapable of exciting the floater pitch motion. By using the aeroservoelastic tool

ferent with activations happening due to large fluctuations, either directly triggering one
of the yaw modes or having an effect on the tower and substructure response. This would
especially be interesting to investigate for the floating platform simulations. However,
the main objective of the yaw-controller is to follow the mean wind direction changes and
these slow-moving wind conditions demonstrate exactly this property. The rotor direc-
tion of the floating platform simulations are very similar to the fixed one, indicating that
this large wind turbine and floater assembly is very stable by nature for all wind fields
it has been exposed to in these simulations. With the addition of waves, the response
could very easily be different as new frequencies would be introduced and possibly would
excited the dynamical motion of the structure.
A load comparison is conducted in figure 18 based on mean, minimum and maximum
loads of the one hour wind samples.
Seven different load channels are investigated,
including the tower base fore-aft and side-side moment, out-of-plane and in-plane blade
root moment (BRM) as well as yaw-bearing pitch, roll and torsion.
The tower base
fore-aft and out-of-plane BRM are mainly driven by thrust with the absolute of the
maximum values reached around rated wind speed. The yaw-bearing pitch moment is
also related to thrust but here gravitational loads also have a large impact, giving rise
to negative moments. Therefore, as the thrust increases, the yaw-bearing pitch moment
decreases as it counteracts the gravitational loads on the RNA mass. On the other hand,
tower base side-side, in-plane BRM and yaw-bearing roll are more torque related with
the mean values stabilizing above rated wind speed where power is limited. In the case of
in-plane BRM, the range of extreme loads are high, which is expected as the blades are
experiencing gravitational pulls in both positive and negative direction for each rotation.

as being affected by the yaw-drive utilization. An immediate thought would be that these
loads are more exposed on the floating platform simulations as it has additional degrees

mean and extreme values are found for the fixed and floating cases. The main differences

DISCUSSION
7
Discussion
The 11 wind samples used throughout the project has been selected, based on extreme
wind direction changes. These weather events rarely take place as table 5 also indicate.
By counting the number of consecutive yaw manoeuvres of more than 78.5◦, 75 examples
has been found per year based on the 50 months of Risø met mast data. Compared to
the total number of yaw manoeuvres per year, these large direction changes only happen
0.14% of the time. However, as already mentioned in the result section, it is still of great
interest to examine these events to understand the functionality of the yaw controller un-
der extreme conditions which might cause unexpected behavior. Another use of the Risø
yaw-drive analysis is obtaining an estimate of the yaw-drive utilization for an operating
turbine. This then gives a rough estimate of the hourly yaw-drive utilization and thereby
what to expect from the HAWC2 simulations. With 45,280 yaw manoeuvres per year,
around 5 yaw manoeuvres would be expected on average. Figure 17 shows that up to 15
yaw manoeuvres where found and that for almost all of the 11 simulations, the number of
yaw manoeuvres exceeds 5. By selecting only wind samples with high standard deviation
in the wind direction, an increased number of yaw manoeuvres are justified and needed
to align the rotor with the incoming wind. Wind samples with low standard deviation
in the wind direction would have little to no yaw utilization and the 5 yaw manoeuvres
seems like a feasible mean value when including all wind data.
The comparison of fixed and floating simulations has been performed with caution. First
of, the difference in substructure and even turbine tower leads to different response types
with six additional DOFs for the floating platform and shifted natural frequencies. The
design of both substructures has been done with the aim of avoiding resonance issues
between the first two tower modes and the 1P and 3P harmonics. Despite of this, when
adding the yaw-drive, significant oscillations are found for especially the low wind speed
simulations which should be investigated further to make sure no resonance issues with
the yaw controller is found. Resonance issues can be critical for both extreme and fa-
tigue loads, reducing the lifetime of the turbine. Pitch instability was also observed for
the floating platform in the first simulations run, and was removed by manually tuning
the blade pitch controller. This tuning required significant changes to the controller and
resulted in very slow pitching of the blade. As the metocean conditions used in these sim-
ulations are steady and slow-moving, the slow blade pitch controller is however sufficient
to follow the same operational trends as the faster blade controller for fixed simulations,
see also figure 24 in Appendix A.5. The yaw-bearing angles and yaw-bearing loads of
figures 16 and 18 are very similar for the two substructures leaving the impression that
the yaw-drive utilization does not add critically high yaw errors or yaw loads when going
from fixed foundation to floating platform. As the bar plots in figure 17 show, both
in terms of yaw time, yaw activation and mean yaw error, a almost identical behavior
is found between fixed and floating yaw operation. Even though six DOFs are added
Page 38 of 40
==PAGE:41==
7
DISCUSSION
for the floating platform, the wind turbine, floater and mooring lines make up a quite
stable system. Caution should however be taken regarding the tower base loads where
even small floater pitch and roll angles contribute greatly as the turbine is a tall, slender
system with a large RNA mass.
The slow-moving wind conditions, without turbulence, and lack of waves are insufficient
to represent realistic metocean conditions that a offshore turbine would experience. Espe-
cially wind-driven waves should be included and increase in magnitude for increasing wind
speeds. Possible instabilities could occur by including waves and the effect of cross wind-
wave conditions could be examined as well. If more time was available for this project,
simulations with waves would have been performed and also, a method for implementing
turbulence that follows the slow-moving wind direction would have been investigated.
The quite simple wind conditions used here should, due to the previously stated reasons,
be handled with care. However, the purpose of the yaw controller is to align the wind
turbine rotor with the mean wind direction and not turbulent fluctuations that are very
random and high frequent. The analysis performed therefore still gives useful insight on
yaw utilization for large wind direction changes and can be used as baseline for further
work on the topic.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
An evaluation of yaw-drive utilization for the IEA 15MW offshore reference wind turbine
is performed both for a bottom-fixed monopile foundation and for the UMaine semisub-
mersible platform. The wind input is limited to slow-moving wind speeds and direction
changes based on met mast data from the Østerild test center site. In total, 11 one hour
wind samples are selected with large direction changes and the wind turbine response is
modelled using HAWC2. Additionally, the yaw-drive is controlled by a simple, moving-
average yaw controller, modified to also take small offsets in the yaw error into account.
Moving from fixed to floating substructure, six additional degrees of freedom is added
and an initial issue of pitch instability was found in the floating simulations. By reducing
the blade pitch controller gains significantly, this instability has been avoided and these
reductions are justified by the slow-moving wind input. In terms of yaw error and yaw-
bearing loads, the results show a very large resemblances between fixed and floating
simulations. The highest mean yaw error of 5.13◦is found at the lowest wind speed
simulation after which a decreasing trend in yaw error is found towards higher and more
steady wind speeds. The most significant differences in load statistics between fixed and
floating simulations are the tower base loads, due to small floater pitch and roll angles,
which increase the gravitational contribution to the fore-aft and side-side moments for the
floating simulations. An additional observation was found at low wind speeds where the
range between extreme loads of the fixed simulation exceeds the floating extreme loads
in tower base side-side. Potential resonance issues when activating and deactivating the
yaw-drive should be investigated in more detail by examining the natural frequencies of
the wind turbine and substructure assembly. Finally, an even larger transient time for
the simulations could be implemented to investigate the effect on outliers observed in
the yaw-bearing loads around rated wind speed. With the addition of turbulence and
wave loads, a more complex wind turbine response is expected and further investigation is
therefore needed to have a comprehensive analysis of the floating wind turbine behaviour.
