Abstract 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the intracellular metabolome of the fungus 
Aspergillus oryzae grown with acetate as the sole carbon source. To achieve this goal, 
several LC-MS methods were developed and utilized. Using the program Mzmine, MS2 
and RT libraries were generated for 33 key intracellular metabolites, facilitating high 
confidence targeted and untargeted metabolomics analysis. Subsequently, targeted and 
untargeted analyses were conducted on Aspergillus oryzae samples grown on acetate 
and glucose, allowing for a comparison of the two substrates. Finally, a quenching and 
extraction method was developed to obtain a representative state of the samples at the 
moment of harvesting, alongside the development of a quantitative triple quadrupole 
(QqQ) method for evaluating the quenching and extraction techniques as well as 
quantifying intracellular energy metabolites. 
 
This investigation yielded several promising results. It demonstrated that Mzmine could 
quickly generate spectral libraries, resulting in high-confidence spectral matches in both 
untargeted and targeted metabolomics. A QqQ method was successfully developed, 
yielding linear external calibration curves and high recovery rates of the compounds 
when spiked into a sample matrix. The quenching and extraction experiment indicated 
some degree of quenching; however, quantitative results for most energy metabolites 
were not achieved using the developed method. 
 
The findings in this thesis demonstrated several crucial initial steps to characterize and 
ultimately quantify the intracellular metabolome of Aspergillus oryzae. Nevertheless, 
further optimizations were necessary, particularly in testing additional quenching and 
extraction methods, as well as enhancing the robustness of the QqQ method.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
   According to the 2024 global report on food crisis, a total of 282 million people across 
59 countries experienced high levels of food insecurity. This is more than a 2.5-fold 
increase compared to 2016[1].  
   To combat the rising food insecurity in the world, a new, affordable and sustainable 
food source is required to combat this problem.  
   With this goal in sight, the Bill & Melinda Gates foundation along with the Novo 
Nordisk Foundation has created a DKK 200 million initiative to create a new food 
source. This food source will be created by converting CO2 into acetate using biological 
and electrochemical processes. The acetate can then be used as a carbon source in 
microbial fermentation instead of sugar. In turn, these microorganisms will produce 
proteins that can be used as a direct food source for humans. This means that this source 
of proteins will not utilize crops that take up a lot of land mass and will be an alternative 
to animal proteins that will hopefully reduce the need for meat and dairy production. 
These points along with using CO2 in the production could create a more sustainable 
source of food[1]. 
 
   Novonesis A/S is a leading biotech company specializing in the industrial production of 
enzymes and functional proteins using microorganisms. Their expertise in this field 
made them an ideal partner for the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation in the initiative to 
develop a new food source. This project, named acetate2food (A2F), aims to create a 
sustainable food source that can reduce animal protein consumption and minimize 
agricultural land use. To establish this microorganism as a viable food source for 
humans, it must produce specific compounds in precise quantities. Understanding the 
intracellular processes of these microorganisms is essential, as it provides critical 
insights into the utilization of acetate as a carbon source and the production of primary 
and secondary metabolites. This knowledge will enable strain engineering to enhance 
the yield of nutritional proteins while decreasing the production of unwanted 
byproducts.

1.3 Liquid Chromatography  
   The basic principle of chromatography is to separate compounds in a sample based on 
their different properties through interactions between the mobile phase and a 
stationary phase. This technique is fundamental in analytical chemistry and is widely 
utilized for purifying and analyzing complex mixtures[8].  
   An example of an LC system is shown in Figure 2. It consists of a mobile phase, which 
includes the solvent or solvents, and a pump that delivers the solvent to the system. The 
column contains the stationary phase and is typically placed in a thermostatic oven to 
control the temperature. The injector is where the sample enters the system. Finally, the 
detector identifies the compounds exiting the column and is connected to a computer 
with software that translates the data for analysis[8]. 
 
Figure 2: Illustration of an LC-system[8]. 
1.3.1 The stationary phase 
   The columns used for separation contain a porous material called the stationary phase. 
The separation process within the column can be described by the van Deemter 
equation, which defines the theoretical plate height H as a function of linear velocity 
u[8]. A larger plate height leads to broader peaks during detection[9].

A refers to the Eddy diffusion and flow distribution. It describes the variation in the 
paths that different compounds take as they travel through the column. This variation 
can be minimized by using smaller and more uniform particles within the column.[9]. 
   B represents the longitudinal diffusion, resulting from molecules oscillating back and 
forth due to their inherent diffusion. As this is directly linked to the diffusion coefficient 
of the compound, it remains constant and cannot be altered.[9]. However, as the 
equation shows, the B-term will have less influence on the plate height, the faster the 
linear flow rate u, meaning there will also be significant peak broadening during slow 
chromatography[9]. 
   C refers to the resistance of mass transfer, specifically the time it takes for compounds 
to reach the stationary phase, enter it, and subsequently exit via the mobile phase[9]. 
Molecules not involved in mass transfer travel faster through the column than molecules 
that are, leading to peak broadening[9]. This resistance can be minimized by using low-
viscosity mobile phases, smaller particle sizes in the stationary phase, and narrower 
column diameters[9]. 
  
   Figure 3 shows van Deemter curves plotted using different particle sizes. It shows that 
the separation efficiency is highly dependent on particle size. This makes sense since 
smaller particles reduces path variation and therefore the term A. Mass transfer will also 
be quicker, lowering the term C[8]. Smaller particle sizes also lead to higher flow rates 
representing the minima in the equation, which indicates the optimal flow rate[8]. This 
higher flowrate will result in faster separation and less peak broadening due to reducing 
the influence of the B term. However, the higher flow rates and smaller particles will 
result in significantly higher backpressure, which requires instruments capable of this. 
Systems capable of providing this are referred to as High performance liquid 
chromatography (HPLC) and the even higher performing Ultra high-pressure liquid 
chromatography (UHPLC) providing pressures up to 1500 bar[8]. In this project, UHPLC 
systems were used exclusively.

1.3.2 The mobile phase  
   The mobile phase usually consists of two liquid solvents, selected based on the 
characteristics of the analytes in question. The combination of solvents can significantly 
affect the separation process, as different solvents can enhance or inhibit the interaction 
of compounds with the stationary phase. When a sample is injected into the column, it is 
immediately followed by the mobile phase, which acts as a carrier, transporting the 
sample through the stationary phase[8]. As the mobile phase moves through the column, 
it facilitates the separation of compounds based on their affinities for the stationary 
phase. Compounds that interact more strongly with the stationary phase will move 
slower, while those with weaker interactions will travel faster. This differential 
movement results in the compounds eluting from the column at different times, which is 
called retention time (RT)[8].  
    
   It is worth noting that many different separation principles exist within 
chromatography, and the choice of solvent and column must align with the specific types 
of compounds you wish to separate. In this project, two distinct separation principles 
were applied, reverse phase chromatography (RP) and hydrophilic interaction 
chromatography (HILIC). These two methods are orthogonal, meaning they operate on 
different separation mechanisms and can complement each other effectively[10]. This 
complementary nature enhances analytical confidence, since a compound identified 
==PAGE:17==

using both methods allows for more robust conclusions to be drawn from the 
analysis[10].  
1.3.3 Reverse phase 
   Reverse phase chromatography is arguably the most common separation principle in 
liquid chromatography[8]. This principle is based on separating compounds by polarity. 
This is done by having a hydrophobic stationary phase and using polar aqueous solvents 
as the mobile phase. This makes the most hydrophilic polar compounds elute first 
whereas the hydrophobic compounds will be retained in the column for longer and elute 
the latest. This is a very powerful separation technique since many compounds vary in 
polarity and will therefore be separated with this technique.  
   The most common choice of stationary phase is the C18 column, where the stationary 
phase consists of chains of (CH2)17CH3 that highly retain non-polar compounds[8].  
   The mobile phases usually consist of a mixture of an aqueous solvent such as water, 
and an organic solvent such as methanol or acetonitrile. For instance, a gradient could 
be made, starting with a higher ratio of aqueous solvent and then slowly increase the 
amount of organic solvent, eluting compounds from lowest to highest hydrophobicity[8]. 
However, reverse phase chromatography is mainly used when a sample is believed to 
contain a mixture of both polar and apolar compounds. If on the other hand, the sample 
is believed to contain numerous compounds of high hydrophilicity that would have low 
or no retention on a C18 column, other chromatographical principles would need to be 
employed.  
1.3.4 Hydrophilic interaction chromatography  
   In hydrophilic interaction chromatography the principle is, as the name suggests, to 
separate mostly polar analytes where the non-polar column used in RP does not 
sufficiently retain polar compounds. In HILIC the stationary phase will contain polar 
groups which will retain the highly hydrophilic substances. Examples of HILIC stationary 
phases could be silica, polyamides or zwitterionic phases[8]. The mobile phases in HILIC 
should be less polar organic solvents such as acetonitrile in high concentrations (>80%). 
The gradient would be made by increasing the concentration of aqueous solvent which 
will elute the polar compounds from the column. This works since the water in the 
mobile phase will form a layer of water around the polar column material[8]. This layer 
will then release the polar compounds from the column material and be eluted with the

organic solvent[8]. This is illustrated on Figure 4. The water layer determines the 
retention of compounds; therefore, a higher concentration of water in the mobile phase 
results in less retention.

1.4 Mass Spectrometry  
   Mass spectrometry is the most powerful detector used together with 
chromatography[8]. Using mass spectrometry, compounds are detected by their mass-
to-charge ratio which means it is possible to distinguish compounds via different 
masses, even if they have similar retention times. A mass spectrometer consists, in 
general, of three main components. The ion source, the mass analyzer and the detector 
illustrated on Figure 5.  
 
Figure 5: Schematic view of an LC-MS[8]. 
   For example, when using an LC-MS system, the compounds elute from the LC system 
and enter the ionizer, where they are ionized and vaporized. This process effectively 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
removes most of the mobile phase from the sample. Compounds must possess a charge 
for the mass spectrometer to measure the mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio. In an LC-MS 
system, the most used ionization method is electrospray ionization.[8]. In this method 
samples are ionized by applying voltage to the samples. This voltage can either be 
positive or negative, where the choice depends on the compounds one wishes to detect. 
The effect of this ionization will often result in a protonated [M+H]+ ion for positive 
mode or a deprotonated [M-H]- ion in negative mode[8]. Numerous other types of 
adduct formation are also possible such as [M+Na]+,  [M+NH4]+ and [M-Cl]-[11]. This 
charge will then allow the compounds to be detected. After being ionized the 
compounds will enter the mass analyzer and detector where the compounds will be 
separated and detected based on their mass-to-charge ratio[8]. Several mass analyzers 
exist, and the ones used in this thesis were the quadrupole time-of-flight (qTOF) 
detector and the triple quadrupole mass detector (QqQ).   
1.4.1 Single Quadrupole Analyzers 
   The quadrupole is a type of mass analyzer that consists of four parallel rods to which 
voltage is applied (see Figure 6)[8]. The specific voltage applied will create different 
trajectories through the quadrupole and therefore only allow compounds with a certain 
trajectory (or masses) to reach the detector[8]. In this way, one can provide several 
different radio frequencies in the quadrupole and separate the compounds that way. 
Single quadrupoles are cheaper than their higher resolution alternatives, and good for 
analyses of only a few targeted compounds, but when you have very complex samples 
and are looking to detect as many compounds as possible, as you do in metabolomics, 
more sensitive mass analyzers are needed[8].  
 
Figure 6: Schematic of a quadrupole mass analyzer[8]. 
==PAGE:20==
9 
 
 
1.4.2 Triple Quadrupole Analyzers  
   In the triple quadrupole mass spectrometer, instead of having one quadrupole to 
separate masses you have three quadrupoles in sequence, each with their own purpose 
(see Figure 7)[8]. The first quadrupole functions the same as in the single quadrupole 
mass spectrometer by selecting the specific mass of the compound you want to detect, 
also called the precursor ion. However, instead of going directly to the detector, it enters 
a second quadrupole. Here the compounds will collide with either N2 or Ar and fragment 
into several product ions[8]. These fragments then enter the third quadrupole that will 
again be able to select and analyze the m/z of the product ions which then enters the 
detector. This instrument provides high sensitivity and specificity for targeted 
compounds and is great for targeted quantitative analysis[8]. However, for identifying as 
many unknown compounds as possible within metabolomics, this would not be the 
preferable mass analyzer.  
 
Figure 7: A triple quadrupole mass analyzer[8].

1.5 Metabolomics 
   Metabolomics is a field developed within life science that aims to characterize all 
metabolites produced in a cell, tissue or organism[12]. These include all smaller 
compounds produced by the organism, usually up to a maximum molecular weight of 
1000-1500 Daltons[12]. It is estimated that a single plant cell produces between 5000 
and 25000 metabolites, a microorganism around 1500 and animal cells around 
2500[13]. This includes a very diverse group of compounds such as carbohydrates, 
amino acids, lipids, nucleic acids and organic acids. These compounds differ greatly in 
their physiochemical properties such as molecular weight, polarity and functionality. 
This makes metabolomics stand in contrast to other “omics” fields like proteomics, 
transcriptomics and lipidomics, that classifies a singular group of compounds with a 
higher degree of similarity[14], [15], [16].  
   Metabolomics can be divided into two distinct approaches: targeted and untargeted. In 
targeted metabolomics, researchers analyze a predefined list of known metabolites. This 
hypothesis-driven approach allows for the identification of specific compounds in 
samples with a high degree of certainty. This is achieved by first acquiring and analyzing 
standards of the targeted metabolites before running the actual samples. Untargeted 
metabolomics, on the other hand, aims to identify as many compounds as possible 
within the samples. To accomplish this, it is essential to develop a comprehensive 
analysis method capable of separating and identifying compounds based on various 
physicochemical properties. This often involves utilizing previously analyzed 
==PAGE:22==
11 
 
compounds from targeted data, as well as accessing various public databases such as 
GNPS[17] and MS-DIAL[18], which contain spectral data from specific compounds 
analyzed by researchers worldwide. This will consequently require a lot of time to 
qualify the matches you are getting from the public database to not report false 
positives. 
    In general, structure confidence of a spectra can be separated into the five levels 
illustrated in Figure 9[10]. Level 1 is the confirmed structure in which MS and MS2 
spectra are available along with a reference standard of the compound in question, 
preferably on multiple orthogonal analytical methods[10]. Level 2 confidence comes 
from a spectral match using public libraries[10]. The last three levels consist of 
decreasing degrees of confidence, where only mass spectra are available without any 
matches. Gaining more confidence in these types of matches is crucial, but it requires 
thorough investigations. This project primarily focused on level 1 and level 2 matches. 
 
Figure 9: Confidence levels in mass spectrometry[10]. 
1.6 Intracellular Metabolomics 
   When performing metabolomics analysis on microorganisms, separating the 
intracellular and extracellular metabolites can be an important step to get higher quality 
information about the organism and its processes[19]. Extracellular metabolomics is 
important for identifying changes to the extracellular environment due to consumption 
of nutrients, such as the carbon and nitrogen source in the culture medium during cell 
growth, as well as excretion of various primary and secondary metabolites into the 
surrounding environment[19]. This approach is comparably easier than analyzing 
intracellular metabolites, since the need for cell quenching and lysis is eliminated and 
==PAGE:23==
12 
 
only requires separation of media and cells using filtration or centrifugation[19]. In 
contrast, intracellular metabolites provide information about metabolomic pathways 
within the cells that relate to, for instance, the rate of cell growth and rate of enzyme 
production. For these reasons, it is important to characterize intracellular metabolites as 
well as separating them from the extracellular metabolites, especially for quantitative 
targeted metabolomics. Among the intracellular metabolites are the compounds 
included in the central carbon metabolism.  
   The central carbon metabolism describes the conversion of carbon containing 
molecules into energy metabolites such as ATP and NADH as well as precursor 
compounds such as nucleic acids, amino acids and lipids used in synthesis of proteins, 
DNA and fatty acids necessary for cell growth and life[20]. The central carbon 
metabolism consists of three primary pathways, namely the Glycolysis/gluconeogenesis 
pathway[21], the pentose phosphate pathway[22] and the TCA cycle[23]. These three 
pathways are interconnected and highly regulated to meet the energy requirements as 
well as the biosynthetic demands needed (Figure 10)[20].  
==PAGE:24==
13 
 
 
Figure 10: The central carbon metabolism. Adapted from Magdenoska[24]. 
   The glycolysis pathway, this is the pathway responsible for converting glucose into 
pyruvate which produces the energy metabolites ATP and NADH in the process. From 
pyruvate, the glycolysis moves into the TCA-cycle via oxaloacetate and acetyl-CoA. The 
TCA cycle further generates molecules of ATP and NADH as well as several precursors 
for synthesis of several important biomolecules such as fatty acids, amino acids and 
nucleotides. In this project where organisms are grown on acetate as the sole carbon 
source, the pathway will move in the opposite direction from acetate to fructose-6-
phosphate. This is referred to as the gluconeogenesis pathway[25].   
   Lastly, there is the pentose phosphate pathway, that is interconnected to the glycolysis 
pathway via glucose-6-phosphate, fructose-6-phosphate and glucyceraldehyde-3-
phosphate. This pathway is primarily responsible for the generation of NADPH that is 
essential for several biosynthetic reactions as well as ribose-5-phophate that is 
important for nucleotide synthesis[20].

   All in all, when doing intracellular metabolomics, identifying and quantifying the 
metabolites contained within the central carbon metabolism provides crucial 
information that can be used to identify metabolic bottlenecks and can be used in strain 
engineering to regulate the pathways for more optimized cell growth.  
1.7 Quenching and extraction 
When conducting intracellular metabolomics, there is a risk of ongoing fermentation or 
even cell death shortly after sampling, which can lead to the degradation of high-
turnover metabolites such as ATP, which is continuously produced and degraded within 
the cell. This issue is particularly significant because the objective of intracellular 
metabolomics is acquiring a representative snapshot of the cell’s metabolome at the 
exact moment of harvesting. Therefore, it is essential to have a method to quickly 
"quench" all metabolic activity after sampling without causing degradation of any 
metabolites or compromising cell integrity. Several studies have reported on quenching 
procedures for different organisms, emphasizing that the quenching method is 
organism-dependent[24], [26], [27]. 
   Following quenching, a way to lyse the cells and extract the intracellular metabolites 
into solution is also needed. Some of the common lysis procedures include chemical 
lysis, freeze-thawing and sonication[28], [29]. Sonication was the method used in this 
project, utilizing high-frequency sound waves to disrupt cell membranes. The 
advantages of sonication are eliminating the need for chemicals that may interfere with 
analysis results, as well as its speed and efficiency when processing a large number of 
samples. However, a notable disadvantage is the heating of samples during sonication, 
which can degrade sensitive compounds. This issue can be mitigated by using ice and 
incorporating breaks between sonication cycles to allow for cooling. For extraction 
solvents, an optimal choice would be an organic solvent capable of solubilizing both 
hydrophilic and hydrophobic compounds, such as methanol or acetonitrile. 
Alternatively, extracting with the same solvent used as the mobile phase during analysis 
can also be considered.

2 Aims and objectives 
2.1 Aim 
   The overall aim of this thesis is to investigate the intracellular metabolome of the fungi 
Aspergillus oryzae using acetate as a substrate, with a focus on the gluconeogenesis and 
TCA-cycle metabolic pathways. To make these investigations, sound analytical methods 
need to be developed to determine the qualitative and quantitative intracellular 
metabolome of Aspergillus oryzae.   
2.2 Objectives 
   To meet the aim of the thesis the following experiments were done: 
1. Generation of MS2 and RT libraries using Mzmine[30]. Libraries should contain 
important intracellular metabolites with a focus on the TCA cycle and 
gluconeogenesis pathways. Generating a library using standards analyzed on the 
same instrument as the samples, will allow for higher confidence in spectral 
matches contained within the samples. 
2. Qualitative targeted and untargeted analysis of Aspergillus oryzae samples using 
Mzmine. Using the generated libraries for targeted analysis as well as various 
public libraries for untargeted analysis will allow for identification of several 
metabolites contained in the metabolic pathways as well as identification of 
wanted and potentially unwanted products. Specifically for the investigation of 
the fungi’s ability to reverse the glycolysis pathway to the gluconeogenesis 
pathway using acetate as a substrate.   
3. Development of quenching and extraction techniques for the strains of 
Aspergillus oryzae which is a crucial step to halt the metabolism and prevent the 
degradation of especially the high-turnover energy metabolites such as ATP.  
4. Development of a quantitative LC-MS method on a triple quadrupole mass 
spectrometer. Establishing a rapid targeted approach for the quantification of key 
intracellular metabolites marks the initial step in uncovering metabolic 
bottlenecks, as well as qualifying the effectiveness of quenching and extraction 
techniques.

3.2 Sample preparation of unquenched Aspergillus Oryzae 
3.2.1 Fermentation and sampling 
   All samples used in this thesis were fermented and harvested at a different location 
within the Novonesis company. The fermentations and subsequent sampling were 
conducted using a Sartorius automated microscale bioreactor (Ambr®) system[31]. The 
unquenched samples involved two fermentation campaigns.  
   Campaign 1 included 19 fermentations harvested at 72 hours and 96 hours, 
respectively. The objective of this campaign was to evaluate different strains of 
Aspergillus oryzae containing various inducers, all utilizing 40% acetate as the carbon 
source. Details of the strains are kept at a minimum, since it is less relevant to the scope 
of this thesis.  
   Campaign 2 comprised 10 fermentations, all testing the same strain. Five 
fermentations employed 15% glucose as the carbon source, while the other five utilized 
15.7% acetate. Samples from all 10 fermentations were harvested at 72, 96, and 120 
hours, resulting in a total of 69 samples across both campaigns.

Samples were collected using the automatic sampling system integrated into the 
Ambr®. A volume of 200 µL was harvested from each fermentation, placed in a 0.22 µm 
filter tube, and centrifuged at 4,000 x g for 2 minutes to separate the biomass from the 
fermentation broth. The samples were then stored at −80°𝐶 until further 
experimentation.

3.2.2 Sample preparation for LC-MS experiments 
   The biomass from all 69 fermentation samples was thawed. A sample of each biomass 
was transferred to a 2 mL Eppendorf tube, with the weight of the biomass recorded. To 
each biomass tube, 80% methanol was added, achieving a biomass concentration of 100 
mg/mL. For instance, if a sample contained 10 mg of biomass, 100 µL of 80% methanol 
was added. The samples were then vortexed and centrifuged at 16000xg for 20 minutes. 
The supernatant was diluted an additional 10 times before LC-MS analysis, resulting in a 
total concentration of 10 mg biomass/mL in each sample.

3.3 Quenching and extraction of A. Oryzae 
3.3.1 Quenching 
   A total of 36 2mL Eppendorf tubes were prepared, with 18 containing 250µL of 50% 
MeOH and 18 containing 250µL of water. The tubes containing water and MeOH were 
weighed, and the weights were recorded. From six fermentation tanks, approximately 
1.5 mL was sampled using a 5mL syringe. This 1.5 mL was distributed into six of the 
tubes, with three containing 50% methanol and three containing H2O. Around 250µL 
was added to each tube as precisely as possible using the syringe. The tubes were then 
quickly vortexed and weighed again, with the final weights recorded (see Appendix B), 
allowing for the determination of the exact amount of broth. Samples were stored 
at −80°𝐶 until further experimentation. Additionally, a sample of non-inoculated 
fermentation medium was taken for blank measurements. 
==PAGE:30==

Figure 13: Illustration of the quenching experiment. Created with BioRender.com. 
3.3.2 Extraction 
   The suspension of broth and quenching solution was thawed and kept on ice 
throughout the experiment. 100 µL of 100% methanol were added to the samples, 
followed by vortexing to solubilize additional non-polar compounds. Samples were 
sonicated for five cycles of two minutes each at 90% amplitude, with a 60-second 
cooling break on ice between each sonication step. Sonication was performed using a 
Hielscher UP200St with a VialTweeter attachment[32]. Note that this sonication 
protocol was suggested with the assistance of AI, since experiments sonicating 
Aspergillus samples using a VialTweeter could not be found in literature. Samples were 
kept on ice for at least 30 minutes before sonication and immediately placed on ice after 
sonication. Following this, samples were centrifuged at 16,100 g for 20 minutes at 4°C. 
After centrifugation, 300 µL of supernatant was extracted into 1.5 mL Eppendorf tubes. 
The supernatant was centrifuged again for 20 minutes at 16,100 g at 4°C before analysis 
by LC-MS, diluting as necessary based on the method and instrument.

4.3 Untargeted analysis of unquenched A. oryzae samples 
   Untargeted analysis was done on the unquenched samples from campaign 2, to identify 
differences between fermenting on acetate and glucose. Since campaign 1 focused on 
differences in strains, which is not the focus of this thesis, untargeted analysis was only 
performed on campaign 2. The analysis was done by searching for spectral matches in 
the public libraries GNPS[17], MoNa[35] and MS-DIAL[18]. The criteria for a spectral 
match were a cos similarity score of at least 0.700. Due to time constraints, no additional 
validation of the spectral matches was done. This makes the matches confidence level 
2[10], meaning there is some uncertainty with spectral matches even with a high 
similarity score. Especially when considering public libraries such as GNPS has no 
verification done on the uploaded spectra, meaning false positives are highly probable. 
This is why generating your own spectral libraries is important to get level 1 confidence 
on spectral matches. However, for the purposes of this thesis where there was limited 
time to acquire reference standards, level 2 data could still result in some potentially 
interesting matches that could be verified later using a reference standard. A total of 4 
feature lists were generated for HILIC POS, HILIC NEG, RP POS and RP NEG on all 
samples in campaign 2.

4.4 Development of quantitative targeted analysis method on LC-QqQ mass 
spectrometer 
   In this part of the project a triple quadrupole quantitative method was developed for 
selected intracellular metabolites. The eight metabolites chosen were ATP, ADP, AMP, 
NAD, NADH, NADP, NADPH and acetyl-CoA.  
   ATP, ADP and AMP are central products in central carbon metabolism involved in both 
the glycolysis and TCA-pathways and supplies the cell with energy via ATP’s 
hydrolyzation into ADP and AMP by breaking the phosphate bonds[47]. They are also 
used to measure the effectiveness of cell quenching via the energy charge equation[48]: 
[𝐴𝑇𝑃] + 1
2 [𝐴𝐷𝑃]
[𝐴𝑇𝑃] + [𝐴𝐷𝑃] + [𝐴𝑀𝑃] 
   NAD+/NADH and NADP+(NADPH) are important redox carriers in the cells and are 
essential for the flow of H+ and electrons in the mitochondria and is involved in ATP 
generation[49] as well as being involved in all tree pathways of the central carbon 
metabolism.  
   Acetyl-CoA was included since it is a key metabolic intermediate involved in numerous 
metabolic processes including being the sole donor of acetyl groups and being involved 
in how pyruvate enters in the TCA cycle[50].  
4.4.1 Identifying adducts, ion mode and optimizing collision energy 
   At the start of the method development, screening and collision energy optimization of 
the compounds were performed (Appendix F). This experiment was performed on single 
standards of samples in 50ppm concentrations. The adducts [M+H]+, [M+NH4]+ and 
[M+H]- were tested for. In general [M+H]+ gave good sensitivity for the three product 
ions with highest sensitivity for all eight compounds. The [2M+H]+ adduct also showed 
high sensitivity for all compounds except ATP, ADP and AMP. [M+NH4]+ did not give any 
results for the compounds and even though [M+H]- adduct were also identified, it was 
decided to only run in positive mode to avoid polarity switching in the method, that 
would result in lower scan speeds and cause sensitivity losses[51]. To summarize, the 
top 3 [M+H]+ product ions for ATP, ADP and AMP were used. For the rest of the 
compounds the top 3 ions for both [M+H]+ and [2M+H]+ were used.  
==PAGE:57==
46 
 
4.4.2 Source optimization 
   After deciding on the adducts and optimizing their collision energy, a source 
optimization experiment was performed. In this experiment different levels of capillary 
voltage, gas flow, gas temperature, nebulizer, nozzle voltage, sheath gas flow and sheet 
gas temperature were tested on a pooled sample of ATP, ADP, AMP and NADH.  
   The optimized source settings were chosen based on the highest average signal across 
all transitions of the four compounds. The results of this can be seen in Appendix G. 
   The general tendency of the results showed that the highest setting tested was also the 
most optimal one. Only nozzle voltage and sheath gas temperature showed a different 
tendency. However, it was only for the sheath gas temperature that a global maximum 
setting was found. This means that going to higher settings, or lower in case of nozzle 
voltage could give better results. However, since most of the settings have already been 
tested from highest to lowest possible, and going to higher capillary voltage could result 
in sparks in the source, it was decided to use the settings in the method recommended 
based on this experiment.

washing step was added, so it washed 20 seconds with methanol and 20 seconds with 
solvent A (90% ACN + 15mM ammonium bicarbonate, pH 9) using backflush to reduce

5 Discussion and prospects   
   The overall aim of this thesis was to investigate the intracellular metabolome of 
Aspergillus oryzae using acetate as the substrate. The first step was to generate spectral 
MS2 and RT libraries using reference standards. The choice of which reference standards 
to analyze was made primarily to cover the central carbon metabolism, however, some 
other intracellular metabolites were also added to the library.  
   The results of this were that Mzmine was able to identify most of the tested 
compounds, generating two libraries containing a total of 33 different compounds. 
These compounds covered a large part of the TCA and gluconeogenesis pathways. Most 
notably were that acetate was not successfully identified, which was unfortunate since 
the project focus were samples grown on acetate.  
   To further the goals of this project in the future, many additional reference standards 
of intracellular metabolites, should be analyzed and added to the libraries. First, the goal 
should be to cover the entirety of the central carbon metabolism. Especially the pentose 
phosphate pathway, that was scarcely covered in this project. The primary reason for 
the lack of coverage was due to delivery issues, so compounds were not received in a 
timely manner to be included in this thesis. However, they should be included in the 
future. The next step should be covering different pathways as well, since including for 
instance organic acids and various secondary metabolites such as mycotoxins is also 
important to fully characterize the intracellular metabolome of these samples. 
Additional investigations should also be done to identify the compounds already 
analyzed but not identified such as acetate and ergosterol.  
 
   The next step in the thesis was using the generated libraries as well as numerous 
public libraries to identify as many compounds as possible in the samples, as well as 
identifying differences due to changing fermentation conditions. The generated libraries 
were able to identify 10 out of 33 compounds contained in the libraries. Among the 
unidentified compounds were the energy metabolites. This indicated that these 
metabolites were either not present in sufficient concentration to be above the LOD, or 
that they had been degraded to below the LOD. Since these samples were unquenched, it 
was theorized that development of quenching and extraction protocols for these 
samples could be one method to identify these compounds.  
==PAGE:77==
66 
 
   Untargeted analysis was also done using several public libraries, that showed overall 
differences in the samples when using acetate or glucose as their sole carbon source. 
Further investigations into specific compounds showed significant differences in amino 
acids as well as some other compounds like mannitol, nicotinic acid and malic acid.  
   A notable challenge with these analyses were that the majority of the features did not 
get a spectral match, even when using numerous spectral libraries. In the future, further 
investigation into this should be done, to get larger coverage of the compounds 
contained within the samples. This could be done by testing different settings in the 
Mzmine data processing, to see if the number of spectral matches could somehow be 
improved that way. Investigations using other metabolomics software could also 
potentially reveal if the low number of spectral matches were the result of the data 
processing in Mzmine. Manual identification of compounds could also be done for 
instance by utilizing spectral networking methods and finding potential matches that 
the software did not identify automatically.  
 
    There was also an attempt to develop a quantitative targeted LC-QqQ method for the 
metabolites ATP, ADP, AMP, NAD, NADH, NADP, NADPH and acetyl-CoA. 
   The result of the method development was a 10-minute HILIC method in positive 
mode, that could successfully identify and quantify all 8 metabolites when using external 
calibration curves. A matrix spiking experiment was also conducting using a project 
samples, that in general showed high recovery rates, with a large %RSD for certain 
compounds. When using the method to analyze the quenched samples, the external 
calibration curves showed that there was significant drift throughout the run which was 
theorized to be due to compounds interacting or due to the use of silica glass vials[42].  
    
   One possible way to improve the method in the future could be using internal 
standards instead of external calibration. One way could be spiking of the references in 
the samples as shown in the matrix spiking experiments. Another way is to use stable 
isotope-labeled internal standards (SIL-ISs) alone or in combination with unlabeled 
internal standards. Several studies have shown that using SIL-ISs could be a way to 
overcome matrix interference as well as being able to distinguish the labeled internal 
standards to the compounds present naturally in the samples[24], [57], [58]. These 
standards could also be used to test the effectiveness of quenching protocols if they 
==PAGE:78==
67 
 
were added to the quenching solution before sampling. This would allow you to 
calculate the energy charge ratio even if the naturally occurring ATP and ADP were 
below the LOQ.  
 
   Lastly, a quenching and extraction experiment was done. The results showed that an 
indication of some quenching did occur by the identification of AMP and NAD in 
quenched samples that were not present in the unquenched samples. However, 6 out of 
8 metabolites from the targeted QqQ method was not found in the samples in sufficient 
amounts to measure. The reasons for this were theorized to be insufficient quenching or 
lysis of the cells. 
   It is important to note, that the original plan for this thesis was to develop and test 
several different quenching and extraction methods and compare them to each other. 
However, due to changes in plans of the department conducting the fermentations, only 
two fermentation batches were made doing the entirety of the project period. The first 
batch being sampled on the first day of the project, leaving no time for developing a 
quenching protocol for those samples, and the second batch being late in the project 
period, leaving little time for optimization. Additionally, following sampling of the 
quenched fermentation batch, a downtime of one month happened due to the LC-QqQ 
not working. This gave even less time for optimization of the QqQ method.  
   In the future, several other quenching and extraction methods should be tested and 
compared, possibly using SIL-ISs.  
 
   In summary, despite encountering several unforeseen challenges during the project 
period that led to delays and suboptimal results, significant progress toward the overall 
aim of this thesis has been made. A targeted method for quantifying energy metabolites 
was developed, and multiple targeted and untargeted protocols using Mzmine to 
identify intracellular metabolites in Aspergillus oryzae samples were established. 
Overall, the analyses presented in this thesis provide a solid foundation for qualitative 
and quantitative metabolomics of intracellular metabolites. They also highlight several 
new experiments and optimizations that can be pursued in the future, advancing the 
goal of developing a new sustainable source of food.
