Abstract
Organic chromophores with optically induced dynamics known as photoswitches provide an inter-
esting component for biological mechanical systems.
Understanding the ultrafast photo-induced
dynamics of photoswitches is key and allows for design of controllable systems. In this thesis, the
lifetimes of the photoswitch hemethioindigo with julolidine as stilbene dissolved in solvents of vary-
ing polarity is evaluated through global ﬁtting of visible spectrum transient absorption experiments.
The formation of twisted intramolecular charge transfer is found for the polar solvent acitonitrile and
conﬁrmed to be unavailable for hemethioindigo-julolidine in the apolar solvent cyclohexane. A life-
time of 2.7 ps of the twisted charge transfer state is found for hemethioindo-julilidine in acitonitrile,
and similarly an extended lifetime of 17 ps of the initially excited state for hemethioindigo-julolidine
in cyclohexane is found. An extensive analysis of multiple global ﬁtting models has been performed,
and for both solvents a resulting model of 3 sequential exponentially decaying states is ﬁtted with
R2 = 0.996 in acetonitrile and R2 = 0.991 in cyclohexane and found to be the best ﬁt. These results
provide the groundwork for further analysis of geometric transitions in hemethioindigo-julolidine in
polar solvents found through X-ray scattering and thus assist in the determination of the ultrafast
structural dynamics of the photoswitching chromophore hemethioindigo-julolidine.

1.1
Motivation for Chromophores, Photoswitches and Hemithioindigo
Chromophores are photoactive organic compounds that absorb particular wavelengths of ultraviolet
and visible light and reﬂect color as a result, but it can also only concern visible light absorbing
molecules. Everyday functions of the human body such as eye sight are perfect examples of chro-
mophores. As visible light interacts with our eyes, the optical, visible signal incident on our eyes
is converted into an electrical signal our body and brain can process [P.U. Giacomoni, 2001]. Such
chromophores are known to change structure between related species (isomers) due to light interac-
tion, which known as a photoisomerization process. These molecules are also known as photoswitches
[Pianowski et al., 2022]. Photoswitches are being studied for promising functions such as organic
solar cells [Sun et al., 2023] and to be used in a multitude of biological purposes including DNA
recombination [Kuwasaki et al., 2022]. Many classes of photoswitches exist with di↵ering proper-
ties. Such examples are azobenzenes [Sun et al., 2023], spiropyranes, dithienylethenes, phytochromes
[Kuwasaki et al., 2022] and hemithioindigos (HTIs). In recent work by Lauren E. Blackwell [2024]
discuss how photoswitches including azobenzenes and spiropyranes could function as wearable ul-
traviolet radiation dosimeters. HTIs however are of particular interest as they photoisomerize when
irradiated with photons of wavelenghts in the visible spectrum, and they are generally bistable [Kink
et al., 2017], which means lifetimes in both the cis- and trans isomerization states are long making
the states considerably stable, which is due to a high activation energy boundary between the states.
In this thesis an analysis on experimental data of a speciﬁc structure of hemithioindigo-julolidine
(HTI-J) shown in Fig.
1 dissolved in multiple solvents of varying polarity measured with time-
resolved experiments of Transient Absorption Spectroscopy (TAS) at Center for Nanoscale Materials
(CNM) and X-ray Scattering on the beamline 14-ID-BioCARS at Advanced Photon Source (APS) at
Argonne National Laboratory (ANL) in 2022 lead by Associate Professor Gianluca Levi, University
of Iceland in collaboration with the 4DMAP - Materials Physics in Time and Space group at the
Technical University of Denmark, Department of Physics, lead by Professor and Head of the Section
Martin Meedom Nielsen. The goal of the analysis is to pinpoint pico-second state transitions and the
lifetimes of the states via optical TAS and the solute-solvent cage dynamics via the X-ray scattering
experiments.

1.2
Structural Dynamics of Hemithioindigo-Julolidine
Although ﬁrst reported in 1906 by Friedl¨ander, P. according to Pianowski et al. [2022], HTIs have
yet to be fully explored. Many photoswitches have overlapping qualities, and as this thesis is on
HTI-J, mainly properties of HTIs and speciﬁcally HTI-J will be discussed. The name hemithioindigo
stems from indigo, which is a common dye with a chemical structure shown to the left in Fig. 2.
Figure 2: Chemical structure of indigo (left) and thioindigo (right). Sketch made using molview.com.
Thioindigo, which is an indigo-adjacent compound with sulfur replacing nitrogen in the chemical
structure is shown to the right in Fig.
2.
HTI is a thioindigo fragment comprising of one half
of a thioindigo molecule split by the central double bond connected to a stilbene fragment via an
additional single bond as shown in the highlighted middle structure in Fig. 1. The left structure of
Fig. 1 shows the structure of HTI-J in the cis (Z) isomerization state meaning that the stilbene and
the sulfur bonds of the thioindigo-fragment are on the same side. The right structure highlights the
julolidine stilbene constituent, where also two methyl groups are added to the para position relative
to the single bond. Switching HTI isomerization requires visible-light irradiation, blue light (around
420 nm) from Z to E and blue-green (505 nm and longer) for E to Z. By introducing methyl-groups
to the para-positions they act as donors to the acceptor carbonyl group (C = O) across the central
double bond, allowing for photoisomerization to be triggered with yellow to red light. This however
comes at the cost of lowering the energy barrier for thermal E/Z-isomerization with both methyl
donor groups and Julolidine as the stilbene fragment as in HTI-J, which results in lower E state life
times of minutes at room temperature compared to the HTI E state lifetimes of years [Pianowski
et al., 2022]. This is however not the only possible option for bond rotation in HTIs. The only
other option is to induce rotation around the single bond, which is known as the formation of a
Twisted Intramolecular Charge Transfer (TICT) state.
The TICT state is a resulting increased
dipolemoment in the molecule by now allowed transfer from donor to acceptor. This is however only
obtainable if the stilbene fragment is large enough to increase reaction time, also known as steric
hindrance, the fragment has donor groups as the methyl groups in HTI-J and if the solvent is polar,
which induces a pre-twisting state, where the equilibrium state is of HTI-J is not entirely planar, but
slightly twisted around the single bond [Pianowski et al., 2022]. A combination of the single- and
double bond twisting called the Hula twist has been measured by Gerwien et al. [2018]. Solvents
related to this thesis and other works on HTI-J by Wiedbrauk et al. [2016] have been gathered in
table 1 from Reichardt [2003] via [murov.info/orgsolvents] for reference.

1.3
Experimental Data Analysis Methods
For experimental data I will present three powerful and already known methods for data analysis
that will be used in the data analysis later. These three methods are Singular Value Decomposition
(SVD), Global Fitting and Guided Fitting. Each has their strengths and weaknesses, and together
they also provide key insights into the measured physical system.
1.3.1
Singular Value Decomposition
Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is a purely mathematical method of decomposing a matrix
via linear algebra with an impressive history traced back to revolutionary mathematicians [Stewart,
1993]. Since 1965 it has been proposed for data reduction and analysis in ﬁelds regarding biology,
chemistry and physics [Shrager, 1986][Henry and Hofrichter, 1992]. As computational power has
increased since then and is now widely available, the method can now properly be used to full
potential as a data analysis tool and has also been proposed to be a background noise reduction
method for X-ray scattering data by Haldrup [2014]. Thus for 2-dimensional data often including
a time axis, scattering or absorption axis with measured signal intensity for each time/absorption
coordinate pair, SVD has become a backbone method for data analysis. Here is a description of
the method. Given a real matrix data set A of m (rows) ⇥n (columns) it is assumed it can be
decomposed into the matrix product
A = USVT
(1.3)
where as consequence of A being real all the other matrices are assumed to be real, which is a fair
assumption except for very speciﬁc and unrealistic experimental data cases. Thus U is an m ⇥n
orthonormal matrix, S is an n ⇥n diagonal matrix and V is an n ⇥n orthonormal matrix and VT
is the transpose of the matrix V. A thorough mathematical guide of SVD for scientists has been
provided by Hendler and Shrager [1994], and a discussion on SVD vs Global Fitting is done by Henry
and Hofrichter [1992], which helped provide the context for this method. An experimental data set
A can be written in terms of the diagonal singular value matrix and either U or V as such:

Principle Component Analysis (PCA) is a related method that explains the variance of a data set

1.3.3
Guided Fitting
Guided Fitting is another ﬁtting method that takes into consideration prior knowledge of a speciﬁc
system to improve ﬁtting. This means resulting information from one experiment can be used to
guide the ﬁtting of another experiments data. Guided ﬁtting helps avoid statistical over-ﬁtting and
allows the model to better explain the underlying physical dynamics.
1.3.4
Signal- and Noise Estimation and Noise Reduction
Experimental data is not perfect, thus measurement errors are present and systemic artifacts can
be found in every scan. An interesting statistical feature to consider is the Signal to Noise Ratio
(SNR). In order for data to be considered good, the SNR must be high and as bare minimum be
greater than 5. How high it must be varies depending on the experiments and limitations. First
of all, the SNR of measured intensity is theoretically the ratio of the mean signal intensity µsignal
and the standard deviation of signal intensity corresponding to noise from any source other than the
sample interactions of interest, σnoise:
SNR = µsignal
σnoise
(1.8)
This method of SNR only works well if multiple measurements or scans of the same system are
gathered. Another method which will be used in this thesis to estimate the noise measured signal is
to take a spline of the 2-dimensional data set at a speciﬁc time, wavelength or scattering component.
This method of smoothing the data generates a more realistic and physical curvature ﬁltering out
random ﬂuctuations in the measurement. A method of increasing the SNR is to decrease the noise
by ﬁltering outliers and removing background noise.
A naive but powerful method of removing
background intensity is by averaging the intensity measured at times before time 0, under the
assumption that these ﬂuctuations and artifacts will also be present underlying the signal of interest.
As mentioned in the SVD section, SVD has proven to be a powerful tool as well to reduce/remove
background noise [Haldrup, 2014].

2
Transient Absorption Spectroscopy
HTI-J absorbs di↵erently depending on which isomerization state the molecules are in. Photoswitch-
ing molecules can simply be characterized in their ground state, S0, by Ultraviolet-Visible (UV-Vis)
Spectroscopy, which implies transmitting di↵erent wavelengths through the sample and measuring
the intensity, showing the absorption of the sample dependent on wavelength λ, A(λ). This is a
sub-category of Absorption Spectroscopy (AS). Similarly, by constantly irradiating the sample with
a pump beam at a wavelength where the sample has high absorbance, the absorption spectrum can
be measured of the excited states, also known as steady-state photomodulation. Separately the two
methods distinguish absorption properties of the di↵erent molecular electronic states, but combining
them results in Transient Absorption Spectroscopy (TAS), transient meaning lasting a short time.
A sketch of the pump-probe TAS sample interaction is shown in Fig. 5 by Berera et al. [2009]. TAS
measurements include an excitation laser pulse denoted as the ”pump”, and a white light laser pulse
that probes the excited sample with a wide range of photon wavelengths, λ. The wavelengths are
then separated by a di↵raction grating in order to measure intensity sorted by wavelengths. The
strength of TAS is to deﬁne the kinetic characteristics of samples, such as relaxation patterns of ex-
cited states down to the ground state, and determining the relaxation times/decay constants of each
transition. TAS on HTI and HTI-J in di↵erent polarity solvents have been performed by Wiedbrauk
et al. [2016] and Stallhofer et al. [2021], and the results found in this thesis will be compared to those
found in these articles.

spectroscopy is done with a white light probe delayed by ∆t after the pump. Sketch is from [Berera
et al., 2009].

2.1.1
Absorption Spectroscopy
The result of an interaction between a material and an incoming electromagnetic beam of light
di↵ers depending on the composition of the material and the wavelength and frequency of the beam.
The incident electromagnetic waves will either be transmitted through the material, reﬂected o↵the
material or absorbed/scattered by the material upon incidence and while passing through. Due to
energy conservation it can be written as:
T + R + A = 1
(2.1)
Placing a detector directly behind the material, such that both the material and detector is in the
beam, the transmitted intensity can thus be measured. If the concentration of solute/solvent liquid
compounds is low enough, the reﬂectivity is negligible, that is R ⇡0, as light interact with multiple
disordered scattering/absorption centers as opposed to a uniform surface from as example a reﬂective
metal. The absorption coeﬃcient, denoted as ↵, is a measure of power absorbed/scattered per unit
length of the medium. Denoting the axis along the beam direction z, with the intensity I(z) of
the electromagnetic beam in units power/cross-sectional area, the incremental loss of beam intensity
through the medium is
dI = −↵dz · I(z)
and thus integrating over the entire thickness of the medium results in the Beer-Lambert Law:
I(z) = I(0)e−↵z.
(2.2)
Scattering and absorption are clearly distinct phenomena, but both e↵ects result in a transmission
intensity less than the incident. Often this coeﬃcient ↵is thus named the attenuation coeﬃcient as
a combination of the absorption coeﬃcient and the product of the scattering cross-section multiplied
by the scattering centers in the medium.
The Beer-Lambert law assumes a uniform medium of
constant attenuation coeﬃcient, which is a ﬁtting assumption for a completely dissolved solute. A
more practical version of the Beer-Lambert law Eq. (2.2) is the optical density (OD):

2.1.2
Di↵erence Absorption Spectrum
Measuring the ground state, S0 absorption of a sample and comparing it to a time-delayed mea-
surement of the same sample in excited states, S1,2,3,... and T1,2,3,... is the backbone of TAS and
makes possible investigation of ultra fast molecular dynamics, often in nanoseconds, but now also
possible in picoseconds with femtosecond resolutions. The di↵erence absorption spectrum, i.e. the
comparison of Ground State Absorption (GSA) with Excited State Absorption (ESA), is then in
terms of the optical density:
∆OD(λ, ∆t) = ODES(λ, ∆t) −ODGS(λ),
(2.5)
where the ∆OD(λ, ∆t) is highly dependent on the incident wavelength, λ and the pump/probe
delay time, ∆t. The GSA is only dependent on λ as this is the steady state of the sample, and the
pump perturbation of the sample results in ODES(λ, ∆t). By measuring the ESA at varying ∆t it
is possible to resolve di↵erent molecular dynamical processes such as isomerization and molecular
relaxation times. Excitation from GS to ES results in negative signal in Eq. (2.5), and as the GS
and ES have di↵erent absorption spectra shifted in λ, the ∆OD(λ, ∆t) signal will show negative GS
bleach due to GS depletion at λ where the ES does not absorb as much as the GS or at all. Some
negative signal could be due to stimulated emission (SE) arising from a probe photon of ﬁtting λ
stimulating an already excited photon of the same λ to optically decay back to the GS, resulting in
10
==PAGE:22==
1 probe photon being measured as 2 photons on the detector. The probe signal however is weak,
and the ESA tends to mask the SE signal, and for HTIs the ﬂuorescence quantum yields have been
found to always be very small [Wiedbrauk et al., 2016]. Meanwhile a positive signal will be shown
where the ES di↵ers in absorption from the GS in terms of λ. This signal decays naturally as excited
electrons decay back to the GS via relaxation pathways.
Positive signal is also due to product
absorption corresponding to long-lived molecular states such as triplet states and isomerized states
[Berera et al., 2009].

3
Conclusion
An analysis of the ultrafast dynamics and structural changes of hemethioindigo-julolidine in acitoni-
trile and cyclohexane has been performed. Global ﬁtting of transient absorption spectroscopic exper-
imental data resulting in models of 3 sequential exponentially decaying states ﬁtted with R2 = 0.996
in acetonitrile and R2 = 0.991 in cyclohexane and are found to be the best ﬁt. An extensive in-
vestigation of global ﬁtting models and their accuracy in describing the experimental results found
has been evaluated leading to 3 sequential exponentially decaying states being the best ﬁt. For this
model a lifetime of 2.7 ps of the twisted charge transfer state is found for hemethioindo-julilidine in
the polar solvent acitonitrile, and it is conﬁrmed that for the apolar solvent cyclohexane the twisted
state is not available. An extended lifetime of the initially excited state is found to be 17 ps for heme-
thioindigo in cyclohexane. These results provide the groundwork for further analysis of geometric
transitions in hemethioindigo-julolidine in polar solvents found through X-ray scattering and thus
assist in the determination of the ultrafast structural dynamics of the photoswitching chromophore
hemethioindigo-julolidine.

Future Perspectives
The natural next step is to analyze experimental X-ray scattering data from the Biocars beamline
at APS with the found lifetime of 2.7 ps of the TICT state as constraint in guided ﬁtting. The
scattering data will provide additional information on the ultrafast structural dynamics of HTI-J in
polar solvents, and better describe how the solvent cage surrounding the HTI-J molecules shifts in
accordance to the structural changes in HTI-J.
