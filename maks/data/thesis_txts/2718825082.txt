Abstract
Exposures from the visual and thermal environment may interact and affect people’s per­
ception of the indoor environment. This study aimed at exploring the effects of different
views, types of glazing, and indoor temperatures on the overall comfort of occupants via
subjective assessments and task performance. Through experiments, 48 subjects were
split into two groups and exposed to either a warm (29°C) or a cool (19°C) temperature,
two window views, and three types of glazing.
No effects of different views were identified on users’ physiological responses, subjective
assessments, and mental performance. However, effects of types of glazing were identi­
fied on subjective assessment of the visual environment. The studies were at the border
of thermal comfort. With an operative temperature of 29°C, subjects felt more thermal
discomfort than at 19°C. The operative temperature had no association with mental per­
formance.
Thermal disposition have a significant influence on both thermal sensation and finger­
forearm skin temperature differences. No differences between physiological and subjec­
tive responses between sex were found.
Although previous studies found a possible effect of colored daylight on thermal percep­
tion, in this study, with no colored daylight, occupant’s comfort was not affected by the
different types of glazing and views. There may be potential in future research when
applying glazing with saturated colors, views with more significant variability, and/or tem­
peratures closer to thermal neutrality.

Introduction
On average, office workers in Denmark spend 160.3 hours per month in the office (Statis­
tics, 2022). This time makes up 23% of the year (not including holidays) during which
people stay at the working place. Furthermore, the current working life expectancy is
around 40 years, which continues to increase as the retirement age rises.
Since the indoor environment influences the productivity, health, and well­being of hu­
mans promoting a healthy indoor environment is of great importance (Wolkoff et al., 2021).
The indoor environment refers to the environmental domains within the built environment:
thermal, visual, acoustic and air quality. Thus, the thermal and visual environment plays
important roles in the overall occupant’s comfort. The thermal environment is well­covered
in previous studies (Fanger, 1970; Parsons, 2000; Rupp et al., 2015) and is an object of
continuous research. Furthermore, it is a central part of building regulations both in Den­
mark Danish Standard Association EN16798­1 (2019) and Danish Standards Association
DS447 (2021) and internationally ASHRAE (2020) and Bemisderfer et al. (2001). Such
regulations present the requirements for this field to be satisfactory for the occupants. In
terms of the visual environment, there are requirements for the lighting conditions to be
optimal. However, there are only recommendations for the view­out properties. These
clearly state the view­out properties needed to achieve a satisfactory work environment
in terms of visual comfort. Earlier studies have shown that view­out has an extensive im­
pact on the perception of the environment and on the ability to concentrate and perform
(Dongying & Sullivan, 2016; Hellinga, 2013; Heschong et al., 2003). The thermal and
visual environments combined shows interconnections capable of evoking responses in
the assessment of the visual environment and vice versa (te Kulve et al., 2016).
Besides providing comfortable working conditions, buildings face another challenge: en­
ergy consumption. In Denmark, buildings are responsible for nearly 40% of the combined
energy consumption (Energistyrelsen, 2023) and subject to an increased focus due to the
global climate change. Effective measures have to be implemented to decrease global
energy consumption and as stated there is considerable potential in the built environment.
The interconnections between the thermal and visual environment are possible domains
where potentials can be found. Therefore the primary purpose of this study is to inves­
tigate the interaction between the thermal and visual environment and how it affects the
occupant’s subjective assessment and performance.

About this thesis
This thesis has been done in collaboration with Natalia Giraldo Vasquéz, Post­doc at DTU
Sustain, International Centre for Indoor Environment and Energy (ICIEE) in the period of
22nd of August 2022 to the 22nd of January 2023 in the larger research project ”Wellbeing,
Health and Performance Promoted by Visual and Thermal Stimuli: Fenestration Design
to Support People’s Indoor Environment Needs”. This is supported by COWIfonden and
has a larger scope than this thesis.
The structure of this thesis is presented in 5 phases:
Phase 1: Background Study
A literature study was conducted to gain knowledge within the field of occupant comfort,
thus visual and thermal environments in relation to view­out and types of glazing. This led

2
Background
This section describes the theoretical background for the thesis and key research articles
included in a literature review. The review was built on a theoretical framework and pro­
vides a theoretical, methodological, and key definition of concepts within occupant comfort
in relation to view­out and glazing.
2.1
Thermal Environment & Comfort
The occupant’s overall satisfaction with the environment is denoted occupant comfort.
This term includes physiological factors such as thermal, visual, acoustics, and air qual­
ity as well as psychological factors capable of changing the psychophysiological signals
within the human mechanism. Combined optimally they can assist the occupants of a
building in achieving comfort.
The thermal environment encompasses heat transfer between the body and the surround­
ings and the factors that impact this. Heat transfer is generally defined as the thermal
exchange between systems. This is relevant in indoor environment studies as humans
as a mechanism require certain conditions for them to be in thermal comfort.
Thermal comfort is defined by Ashrae 55 as:
”that condition of mind which expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment and is
assessed by subjective evaluation” (ASHRAE, 2020)
The thermal properties are included in the holistic term Indoor Environment and is a com­
bination of several parameters e.g., air temperature, relative airspeed, vapor pressure,
mean radiant temperature (MRT), the thermal resistance of clothing (clo), and activity
level. With these parameters, one can calculate the optimal thermal conditions which can
be combined in multiple ways to achieve thermal comfort (Fanger, 1970).
The thermal comfort of humans is related to the total thermal balance of the body as well
as thermo­psychological influences. This is defined as the optimal balance between in­
ternal heat production (metabolism) and heat loss to the surroundings. Derived from this,
a human in perfect thermal balance has an internal heat production equal to the heat loss,
denoted thermal neutrality (Toftum, 2013).
The human thermoregulatory mechanisms ensure the balance of the internal heat pro­
duction as well as the heat loss to maintain a constant internal temperature of 37°C. This
system responds to external and internal systems and signals the human body to acti­
vate actuators capable of changing heat production or loss. Examples of actuators are
sweating and evaporation of sweat to lower the body temperature, shivering to increase
metabolism and thereby the body temperature, or relaxing blood vessels, and altering
blood flow. The heat loss to the environment is often limited by the physical properties
of the skin (wettedness, area, skin temperature) and the physical properties of the envi­
ronment (ambient and radiant temperatures, air movement, air pressure, humidity, and
vapor pressure) as well as the physical properties of the insulation that affects the human
subject.

The body as a whole needs to be in thermal balance for comfort to occur: Heating or cool­

is a constant dynamic interaction between humans and their surroundings that produce
psychological strain on the individual; this can potentially lead to discomfort, annoyance,
effects on performance, health and more (Parsons, 2000).
2.1.1
Evaluating Thermal Comfort
The thermal sensation is the subjective perception of the thermal environment (ASHRAE,
2020; Danish Standards Association ISO7730, 2006). It is usually evaluated on a con­
tinuous scale ranging from ­3 to 3 with a central point of neutrality. This scale seeks to
asses the satisfaction with the thermal environment from ”Cold” over ”Neutral” to ”Hot”
through a subjective questionnaire.
In the 1970s Fanger (1970) developed a model stating three conditions for thermal com­
fort: The human body is in heat balance, the mean skin temperature is within a required
range, and the mean sweat rate is within a required range. To evaluate the thermal com­
fort of a group of subjects the Predicted Mean Vote­model (PMV) was developed (Fanger,
1970). The model is defined as a function of the thermal load on the effector mechanisms
of the human thermoregulatory system (Toftum, 2013). In practice, the model includes
the thermal sensation and predicts a mean vote of which the Predicted Percentage Dis­
satisfied (PPD) can be calculated.
As a model of predicting and monitoring the state of thermal comfort and sensation Wang
et al. (2007) found that temperature gradients between the finger and forearm had a re­
lation to the overall thermal comfort. Wang et al. (2007) saw negative gradients up to
8.5K for discomfort due to cold and positive gradients up to 2K for warm discomfort. In
general, when the finger temperature was above 30K there was no cool comfort. Choi
and Loftness (2012) supported the conclusions of Wang et al. (2007) with the findings
of a relation between the thermal sensation vote and skin temperatures ultimately using
bio­signals to predict thermal comfort.

2.1.2
Thermal Environment & Performance
The indoor environment has been identified to have a close relationship with human per­
formance (Cui et al., 2013). Performance can be used as a method of assessment of the
indoor environment and can be assessed through a variety of mental tasks, such as Tsai­
Partington numbers test (Chinazzo et al., 2019), and memory typing (Cui et al., 2013).
The evaluation of ”real” tasks is complex as there are many variables to consider. There­
fore the tasks to evaluate performance must be of a mental form often similar to studies
conducted before to increase the level of comparability (Parsons, 2000).
Studies have shown that heat stress has a large effect on the performance of a range of
mental tasks (Ramsey, 1995; Wing, 1965). A decrement in performance was shown un­
der wet bulb globe temperature (WBGT) 30­33°C in Ramsey (1995). Wing (1965) found
a similar significant decrement in performance under 30­45°air temperature conditions
in varying exposure intervals as a supplement. On the other hand, there are a limited
number of studies that have studied the effect of cool conditions (below thermal comfort
level) on the level of performance. Lan et al. (2011) showed that subjects exposed to a
thermal environment of 0 to ­0.5 on the thermal sensation scale performed better than
when exposed to >0 on the same scale. This is partly supported by Cui et al. (2013)
which found that the optimum temperature interval for mental performance was 22°C to
26°C. Under warmer conditions, people showed a significant decrement in performance.
It has not been possible to recover studies regarding performance in the built environment
under 22°C of temperature.
2.2
Visual Environment & Comfort
The visual environment plays a prominent role in assessing overall occupant comfort.
This encompasses the external objects and conditions able to influence comfort through
psychological stimuli: light, view­out, colors, etc (Boyce, 2014).
Light is electromagnetic radiation divided into visible and non­visible by the human eye.
The electromagnetic spectrum covers γ­rays, X­rays, radio waves, and so forth. Still, this
study focuses on full­spectrum­light: UV­, visible­, and infrared light in the spectrum of 10
nm to 1.000.000 nm as illustrated in Figure 2.1. Daylight is contained in the category of
full­spectrum­light, whereas ∼380 ­ 780 nm is the visible spectrum (Boyce, 2014).
Figure 2.1: Electromagnetic spectrum of light
When the light is transmitted through a medium (e.g., glazing) with certain spectral prop­
erties the light is split into a designated spectrum that does not contain the full visible/in­
visible light. Only this spectrum is then perceivable on the exit side of the medium. By the
use of the spectral power distribution (SPD) and the color matching functions one is able
to calculate the color of the light through mediums (Boyce, 2014).

2.2.1
Effect of light on thermal comfort
When discussing the psychological effects on thermal comfort, a theory that is argued is
the Hue­Heat­Hypothesis (HHH). The hypothesis states that there is a psychophysiolog­
ical relation between visual stimuli and perceived temperature: human subjects tend to
perceive red (low correlated color temperature) objects as warmer than blue (high corre­
lated color temperature) objects and vice versa.
The perceived temperature of an object and its color showed significant relations in a
study by Ziat et al. (2016). The study used virtual reality to imitate the blue and red color
of a cup of varying temperatures from where the subject should asses the temperature
on a thermal sensation scale. There were significant differences between incongruent
objects (objects that differ in temperature and color, e.g., an object that is warm in tem­
perature with a blue color) and a significant relationship between congruent objects.
The HHH applies not only to the colors of objects but also to the color of light. The use
of LED lighting is a modern technology allowing controlled changes in CCT possible af­
fecting visual and thermal comfort. A significant relation between the thermal sensation
and CCT was found in Toftum et al. (2018) under thermally neutral conditions. Here the
thermal sensation decreased by use of cool CCT. Higher and lower temperatures derived
no associations. In contrary Bellia et al. (2021) found that the thermal sensation rose
under 20°C and 25°C while exposed to warm CCT, yet did not test at thermally neutral
conditions.
The drop of thermal sensation as found in Toftum et al. (2018) is contradicted by Brambilla
et al. (2020) that showed no associations at 21°C and 24°C, but a drop i thermal sensation
at 26°C on the edges of thermal comfort when exposed to cool CCT.
Baniya et al. (2018) tested similar temperatures and CCTs as Bellia et al. (2021) but did
not find any associations at either temperature or CCT. Baniya et al. (2018) and Bram­
billa et al. (2020) both found that subjects were most comfortable at neutral CCTs around
4000K.
Fewer studies deal with colored glazing, which affects the transmitted daylight. Chinazzo
et al. (2021) found significant relations between color of the light and thermal comfort at
22 and 26°C air temperature and not 19°C. At 22 and 26°C, the orange glazing was higher
rated than the bluish glazing. At 19°C air temperatures, the rating for the orange glaz­
ing decreased as well as the blueish glazing increased. These findings are supported by
Chinazzo et al. (2018).
In terms of visual comfort, blue glazing was considered less pleasant than orange and
neutral glazing. The neutral glazing in Chinazzo et al. (2021) was always preferred over
the colored supporting the findings of Baniya et al. (2018) and Brambilla et al. (2020) that
found neutral CCTs as the most comfortable.

2.2.2
View­out
The view­out parameter has several dimensions; such as the building­to­landscape ratio,
the possibility to see water, the distance to the view, and the general variation in the view.
There is still lacking significant models that describe this phenomenon (Danish Standards
Association ISO17037, 2018). This review describes some of the conclusions relevant
studies have made.
Hellinga (2013) investigated all these different factors with several field studies in office
buildings in the Netherlands. In the study of how window size affects people’s satisfaction,
it was found that the average window width chosen by the respondents was 2.42 meters,
which corresponded to 23% of the total wall area. Keighley (1973) did a 1:12 scale model
that showed results similar to this, concluding that window sizes between 25­30% are the
most appreciated. A third study, Butler and Biner (1989) found that there were no fixed
part of the wall, but that it was influenced by the view, size, shape, and function of the
room. All the studies agree that when the window size is below 20­25% of the wall area,
the number of occupants who found the window too small increased.
The perception of the outside view is also influenced by the shape and position of the win­
dow, and the distance from the occupant. Keighley (1973) and Markus (1967) both stated
that views should consist of three layers; a layer of ground, a layer of city or landscape,

==PAGE:24==
and a layer of sky. The authors concluded that the view should lead the design of the win­
dow shape. It was shown that different window shapes are preferred for different types of
views. Their findings were conflicting on the preferred geometry for views that consists of
all three layers as mentioned above. Markus (1967) found that narrow and tall windows
show a cross­section of the three layers. Markus (1967) also concluded that dividing the
window into smaller elements, improves the quality of the window. However, Keighley
(1973) found that the subjects preferred wide horizontal windows, especially when the
object of the view was far away. They also found that the satisfaction decreased when
the window was divided into smaller elements since it caused the view to be interrupted.
Keighley (1973) investigated the preferred sill height which depends on the elevation of
the skyline. The most satisfactory was a window sill height of 0.8 meters and a head
height of 1.9 meters for a window with a distant view. In cases where the skyline was
at a higher level, the preferred window head height is also higher so the sky is included
in the view. Keighley (1973) and Markus (1967) focused on cases with windows only
on one wall. In Hellinga (2013) an investigation of 8 different offices given a total of 558
responses concluded that the quality of view ratings, in general, are higher when the win­
dow is located right, left, or in front of the respondent, compared to when the window is
behind the respondent.
Hellinga (2013) also investigated how occupants evaluated different types of views. The
participants rated different views containing buildings, nature, water or traffic, etc. It was
seen that a distant view of nature is rated high and even higher if water is present in the
view. It was also seen that old buildings rate higher than modern buildings. According to
R. Kaplan (1993) it was not only the availability of a view but more the content of the view
that is important. Most research finds that occupants prefer a natural view over a building
view (Hellinga, 2013; R. Kaplan, 1993).
Performance & Alertness
The influence from the view on the comfort of the occupants is related to the Attention
Restoration Theory. The theory claims that people concentrate better after spending time
in nature, or even looking at scenes of nature ­ in this case from their office space (R. Ka­
plan & Kaplan, 1989). Several studies have supported this theory: Heschong et al. (2003)
found that office workers performed 10­25% better when having a view of nature versus
not having a view. R. Kaplan (1993), Leather et al. (1998), and Ulrich (1984) showed
that a view of nature positively affects people, by reducing stress at work, higher life sat­
isfaction, overall health, and even quicker recovery in hospitals. Dongying and Sullivan
(2016) investigated three different schools with identical rooms and orientations but dif­
ferent views. One without a window, one with a view of a building, and one with a view of
a green space. It was found that the pupils sitting in the classroom with a view of nature
recovered quicker from stress and mental fatigue than pupils with no view or view of a
building.
In general, all presented literature points toward view having a significant impact on hu­
mans’ well­being and performance.

Methodology
The experimental activities were carried out in a living lab consisting of two identical rooms
with different orientation in building 426 on DTU Lyngby Campus. The period of the ex­
periment was from the 3rd of October to the 7th of November. The subjects’ perceptions of
visual and thermal comfort along with their individual personal state were obtained through
questionnaires, while performance tests, and skin temperature were measured. The in­
door environment parameters such as temperature, relative humidity, local air speed,
color of light and illuminance were logged through various instruments. The temperature
in the room was partially controlled by a heat pump. In addition to this, the room had no
artificial lighting.
3.1
Independent variables
3.1.1
View­out
The rooms had two different views: Room 1 with a limited view to a courtyard with trees
and greenery and Room 2 with a view to a parking lot (Figure 3.1). The difference in
views were based partly on R. Kaplan (1993) which found that access to views containing
natural elements such as trees or other vegetation could increase the overall well­being
of office workers. This study wants to extend that conclusion and therefore differences in
the view­out could potentially have an influence on visual subjective comfort and/or the
perceived thermal comfort. R. Kaplan (1993) also stated that the subjects preferred a
natural view over a building view. View 1 is a rather unused courtyard with planting and
limited traffic and activity (Figure 3.1a). It is limited by the U­shape of the building itself.
View 2 is oriented towards a parking lot and building with a high activity in the form of
passing cars and pedestrians (Figure 3.1b). In addition to this, this view has limited view
to vegetation and is restricted by buildings directly across the parking lot.

Subjects
This section describes the recruitment of the subjects and shows a distribution of the

To define the number of test subjects for the study to have a possibility for statistical signif­

3.3.1
Recruitment
The subjects were recruited through advertisements on social media affiliated with DTU
and on DTU’s internal platform for students. These advertisements linked to an online
survey where the candidates were to fill out personal information that was not considered
sensitive. This survey contained a range of exclusion criteria to ensure that the partici­
pants chosen for the experiment would fulfill the requirements.
The suitable candidates had to:
• Have a Body Mass index (BMI) range of 19­25: Within normal weight BMI
• Be 20­55 years of age: Suitable for office work and female have not gone into the
period of menopause
• Not be colorblind: Due to color of light
• Be in good health (no mental or physical illnesses): Not to interfere with obser­
vations
• Not be using regular medication: Not to interfere with observations
• Not having night shift work: Not to interfere with observations
• Be living in Denmark for more than 1 year: Acclimatised to Danish conditions
In general, the requirements were made so unknown variables such as medication, sick­
ness, and ect. could not influence the study. In addition to this, the requirements add to

Subject performance
This section presents the methods of measuring and evaluating the subjects’ perfor­

d2­test Attention Test
To evaluate the performance of the subjects they completed d2­tests, which measures the
level of attention and the concentration performance. In addition, the test is a measure of
visual scanning speed independent of intelligence level and therefore well suited for this
experiment (Brickenkamp & Zillmer, 2017).
For the experiment, the d2­test is conducted in accordance with the instruction manual
with two d2­tests per experimental session.
(a) d2­1 sheet cut out before marking.
(b) d2­1 sheet cut out after marking
Figure 3.9: Example of a d2­test sheet before initiation and after completion with the

The d2­test consists of 14 lines of d and p with 1­4 marks above and below the letters.
The test subjects had to make a diagonal line through each of the d with two marks in total:
either below, above or combined. This was conducted for every line starting from the top
with the test supervisor saying ”next line” every 20 seconds until the test was completed.
Figure 3.9 illustrates the correct completion of the test without omission or confusion er­
rors.
The test subjects were seated in an enclosed room but since the d2 test was a timed
test, the researchers instead entered the rooms one minute prior to the scheduled time to
control the test.
Since each subject completed the d2­test 12 times across the experiment (twice under
each condition), five additional (to the original) d2 test sheets were created by random­
izing the order of the 14 lines. Thus, the first version of the d2­test was presented in the
first round of the first and fourth experimental sessions, and so on.
Tao et al. (2019) found that the test contains a learning curve, meaning that the subject
would increase in total score for every test done. To ensure that the subjects were well
trained in the test they had to perform a practise test (one line) prior to the first session.

Tsai­Partington Test
The Tsai­Partington test is an evaluation tool of subjective performance. The test consists
of 20 random placed numbers from 1 to 99 on a sheet of paper with the number ”00” in the

without making mistakes within 30 seconds (Underwood, 1961).
Tao et al. (2019) found that the test contains a learning curve, meaning that the subject
would increase in score for every test done. To ensure that the subjects were well trained
in the test they had to perform a practise test prior to the first session.

Experimental procedure
Each subject participated in the experiment for 1.5 hours in 6 sessions at the same time
slot of 09.00­10.30, 11.30­13.00, and 14.00­15.30. The fixed time slots was implemented
to ensure that daylight was available in all sessions and that the subjects’ circadian rhythm
affected the experiment as little as possible.
Before the arrival of the subjects, all indoor environment equipment were initiated, and it
was checked that the rooms had the correct glazing and temperature set point. In addi­

All subjects had been instructed to wear clothing of 0.61 clo equal to a long sleeve shirt,
underwear, trousers (jeans), ankle­high socks, and shoes. If they turned up with the wrong
clothing they were asked to wear clothes provided by the researchers.

to conduct these.

Data Analysis
The objective data includes the indoor environment, individual physiological data, and
properties of each subject (e.g. height, weight).
Initial data exploration consisted of simple plots and key value descriptive analysis to form
an image of possible relations to explore.
To obtain the responses after the subjects had fully acclimatized to the conditions the data
from Q3 were used. When analyzing the relation between the environment and the per­

For this project, a range of parameters was selected for statistical analysis since there
was no time for examining all parameters.
For the statistical analysis of the data two different models were used: the repeated mea­

for nominal and ordinal questions. To avoid clustering of the data that could corrupt the

3.8.1
Mixed Effects Model
The MEM is used to include random effects when analyzing continuous variables suitable
for scenarios with more than two observations per (UCLA Statistical Consulting Group,
2022). It takes into account the random differences of each subject and takes the generic
matrix form of:

When making the analysis the subjects are (per ID) added to have their own unique effect

(Loughin. & M., 2014). These requirements were checked before and/or after the analy­
sis. Especially the normal distribution of the residuals are subject to a post hoc analysis.
The analyzes had several independent variables that was added if they were relevant for
the particular analysis. This is done due to the fact that the dependent variable can be
affected by more than one independent variable.

5
Discussion
This thesis explores the effect of different types of glazing and views on the outside on
the occupants’ comfort. The participant’s physiological responses and perception of the
thermal and visual environment were used to assess the comfort aspects.
Additionally, the influences of human diversity were explored based on sex and thermal
disposition on view assessment, performance, and thermal comfort. This section dis­
cusses the results of the questionnaire responses, objective measurements, and perfor­
mance tests. Furthermore, the experimental design has been discussed.
5.1
Glazing types
Overall only a few relations to visual and non to thermal environment or performance were
found for the different glazing types. This conflicts with Chinazzo et al. (2018) and Chi­
nazzo et al. (2021), who found a relationship the visual and thermal environment.
There was no association between the dominant wavelength dependent on the types of
glazing and the thermal sensation for either of the scenarios ultimately not supporting
the HHH. This contradicts the findings of Chinazzo et al. (2021) that showed a relation
between the dominant wavelength and the thermal sensation at 22°C and 26°C but sup­
ports the non­relation at 19°C. The difference in the average dominant wavelength could
explain the contradicting results. Chinazzo et al. (2021) used colored­saturated glazing
that derived 488, 491, and 591nm of average dominant wavelength, whereas this ex­
periment used non­saturated­colored glazing with average dominant wavelengths of 550,
547, and 543 nm ­ a much narrower interval. The potential for supporting the HHH could
be researched at temperatures closer to thermal neutrality and wider intervals of dominant
wavelengths.
No relation was found between the glazing types when asked how clear or vivid the view
was, though Tinted got a higher number of Slightly clear­votes. The glazing type was
found to have a significant association with visual sensation. The visual sensation was
rated significantly lower in the Tinted glazing scenario than in the other types of glazing.
A relation between vertical illuminance and the visual sensation was found showing that
the illuminance did influence the subjects. The vertical illuminance was also compared to
the overall visual comfort, showing that the illuminance should be high (approx. 800­1000
lux) before the responses are Comfortable. This is almost twice the illuminance level sug­
gested for office work in Danish Standards Association DS447 (2021).
The thermal comfort is evaluated as more acceptable in the cool scenario than in the
warm scenario supported by thermal sensation votes. The declines of thermal sensation
over time do not reach stable conditions and therefore if the experiment proceeded for
a longer period than one­hour; further comparison of the differences in thermal comfort
between the two temperature scenarios could have been done.
The thermal comfort assessments shows that the negative finger­forearm temperature
differences in the cool scenario are associated with thermal discomfort due to cold as
implied by Wang et al. (2007). The temperature differences are likewise negative (≈−0.5)

in the warm scenario, when the subjects are in thermal discomfort due to heat, which
is contradicting Wang et al. (2007) that finds positive temperature differences when the
subjects are in thermal discomfort due to heat.
The measurements of the warm scenario are uncertain as the skin temperature sensors
have an accuracy of 0.5°C. This derives a potential for the finger­forearm temperature
differences of the warm scenario to be close to 0.
No overall association was found between the performance and the types of glazing. A
single significant relation was found between the glazing types and the performance in
the first Tsai­Partington test (TP1) in the warm scenario: The subjects performed better
with the Low­E glazing. As this is the only significant association further research with
other temperatures is suggested.
The subjects perform best in the warm scenario contradicting Cui et al. (2013) where the
performance is worst under warm conditions. No studies found have investigated perfor­
mance under 22°C, which could explain the contradicting results from this experiment.
The PPD for each glazing and temperature scenario was higher than the actually re­
sponded PD in the experiment showing that the subjects were more acceptable under
these conditions (including visual stimuli) than Fanger (1970) showed.
The temperature in the warm scenario was seen to have an average of 31°C and the
temperature in both scenarios is seen to have a large dispersion. This made it necessary
to consider the temperature when making statistical analyses since the difference in tem­
perature could influence the subjective responses. Due to the clustering of the operative
temperature and thermal sensation, the statistical models had to be separated into tem­
perature scenarios to fulfill the statistical requirements of the models.
A significant association was found between the thermal and visual environment and the
overall perception of the environment. Both have a high significance through thermal
environment has a slightly higher association than visual.
5.2
Outside view
No association was found between views, type of glazing, the assessment of sky condi­
tions, and the level of attractiveness.
The results contradict the findings by Hellinga (2013) where views with nature responded
significantly better than views without regarding attractiveness and performance. The re­
search shows that a view containing nature close to the window and blocking the view
would often get a low rating. Buildings with uniform facades are also known to decrease
the rating of the view. This suggests that the differences in this study between View 1 and
View 2 were not significant enough for the subjects to notice.
The experiment was conducted in the fall (October­November), which means that the
number of solar hours decreased and the weather got more cloudy. It was shown that
the sky condition affected the level of attractiveness; a potentially more significant rela­
tion could be found if the experiments were conducted in the summer when the difference
between View 1 and View 2 would have been more significant.
No overall association was found between the performance and view­out.

5.3
Human Diversity & General Responses
A relation was found between thermal disposition and thermal sensation in the cool sce­
nario. This supports the findings in Rupp et al. (2022) where a relation between thermal
disposition and the thermal sensation was found in 20°C. Rupp et al. (2022) also found
relations between the thermal disposition and the thermal sensation in 30°C, yet this is
not supported by this study, as no relation was found under the warm conditions.
This study did not have thermal disposition as a main focus, and the distribution of the
thermal disposition groups was therefore random, which could have an effect on the re­
sults.
A significant relation between thermal disposition and the difference in finger and forearm
temperature was found in the cool scenario. The relation showed that the sensitive to
cold­group had a significantly larger temperature difference between the finger and fore­
arm in comparison with the other groups.
The statistical analysis did not show an association between sex and thermal sensation in
any scenarios. In extension to this, no association was found with performance. Although
no overall relation was found between sex and the view assessment, an association was
found in View 2 but not in View 1. In View 2 the males responded to the view as being
more attractive than the females.
5.4
Sources of error
This section describes the effects different sources of error may have had on the data and
results for future studies to take this into account when designing a similar experiment.
The heat pump did not have the function of automatically switching between heating and
cooling. When exceeding 29°C the heat pump turned off instead of cooling the room. This
factor is taken into account by the statistics as an independent variable.
Operative temperatures in both scenarios were high when using the Testo station equip­
ment. This indicated direct radiation on the Testo globe deriving the high operative tem­
peratures and making it necessary to use other equipment. Instead of using the radiant
heat measurements the HOBO air temperature was inserted as a substitute as this was
not affected by direct radiation. The HOBO was less accurate and situated further from
the subjects, but the difference in stable temperature between the Testo station and the
HOBO was considered when recalculating the operative temperature. To avoid this the
Testo station should have been shielded from direct solar radiation obtaining more correct
results.
There were subjects who did not do the d2­test correctly ­ these tests have been excluded
from the study. It is unknown whether the subjects were not well instructed or the errors
indicated a lack of concentration. All subjects performed acceptably when doing the re­
hearsal d2­line for the introduction.
The questionnaires consisted of a majority of Likert scales. The responses to this type
of question can be influenced by the Central Tendency Bias describing that respondents
are likely to state their response in the center of the scale (Statology, 2022). This can be
a factor that affects the results of the subjective data.

small share of the total amount of data that it does not influence the results.

Conclusion
The effects of view­out and glazing type on occupant comfort were explored. In addition,
the influence of sex and thermal disposition on the comfort response was investigated.
Experiments were conducted in a living lab with 48 subjects exposed to two view­out
conditions with three types of glazing. The rooms were partially thermally controlled with
temperatures at the upper and lower boundaries of the comfort zone.
Considering the interrelationship of the thermal and visual environments it was found that
thermal sensation was not associated with the dominant wavelength under any tempera­
ture or glazing conditions. Yet, the thermal and visual environments influenced the overall
conditions of one another: If the thermal environment was assessed as unacceptable the
assessment of the indoor environment dropped by 88%. Opposite if the visual environ­
ment was assessed as unacceptable the overall assessment of the indoor environment
dropped by 74%.
The type of glazing had an association with the visual sensation. The Tinted glazing was
rated significantly lower than the other types of glazing. The glazing did not affect the vi­
sual comfort assessments, but the vertical illuminance did. This showed high illuminance
levels was needed to satisfy the occupants visually.
No overall association was found between view­out and mental performance for any of
the view scenarios. Neither sky conditions nor glazing types influenced the attractiveness
assessment of the view scenarios.
The thermal disposition of sensitive to cold had a significant relation to the thermal sen­
sation in 19°C with an offset of ­0.55 thermal sensation scale unit as compared with the
remaining groups. As a bio­indicator, the same thermal disposition group showed a 1.9°C
larger finger­forearm skin temperature difference than the other groups. No relations were
found in the thermal disposition of sensitive to heat in the warm scenario. There was no
relation between sex and thermal comfort, or mental performance, however a relation be­
tween sex and View 2 was found ­ none for View 1. In View 2 the males rated the view
more attractive than the females.
At 19°C subjects felt more thermally comfortable than at 29°C, independent of the view
and glazing scenario based on thermal sensation ­ when doing sedentary work. The av­
erage difference in the observed percentage dissatisfied between the two temperature
levels was 20% ­ with the warm having the highest. In addition, subjects assessed the
air quality better at 19°C than at 29°C despite lower CO2 concentration in the latter. Fur­
thermore, there was no association between the thermal environment and mental perfor­
mance.

Future work
This thesis was part of a larger research project in which this study included several pa­
rameters not used in this thesis. This was due to the limited time and resources. There­
fore as all of the parameters are expected to be implemented in the analysis of the larger
research project this section will only describe suggestions for future studies of similar
nature:
• This study gives indications of the three types of glazing not having sufficient spectral
property differences to give significant results as in Chinazzo et al., 2018; Chinazzo
et al., 2021. Though a founding idea of this study was to use glazing that was within
regular use in the construction industry and not windows with highly saturated colors
or etc. Therefore the recommendations are to increase the dominant wavelength
interval while still using glazing, which is applicable in the common building industry.
• Bellia et al. (2021), Brambilla et al. (2020), and Toftum et al. (2018) found associa­
tions between the color of artificial light and the thermal sensation at temperatures
closer to thermally neutral than in this study. Therefore future studies should con­
sider using temperatures that derive thermal neutrality and not temperatures on the
edges of thermal comfort.
• To accompany the scope of the study involving office workers, a future study should
include subjects of a broader age interval than was possible in this study.
• In terms of view­out future studies should aim at incorporating views with more signif­
icant differences in greenery and activity to create a broader base for the relationship
between view­out and the thermal environment to be explored.
• To ensure a more constant daylight illuminance that does not decrease significantly,
as well as the sky conditions, should be implemented. This could be provided by
conducting the experiment over the course of a summer ­ evenly divided over sol­
stice or only performing the experiments when there is a cloudy or clear sky. Thus,
this would generate more stable conditions for the dominant wavelength.

B
Learning curve in performance
The average scores for the Tsai­Partington and the d2 test for each session are pre­
sented. Figure B.1 and B.2 are built on data regardless of scenarios and do not show the
parameters possibly affecting the performance.
Figure B.1: The score of the Tsai­Partington test during the six different sessions.
Figure B.1 shows the regression of the performance of the Tsai­Partington test. A learning
curve is seen from the initial tests to the last as the score increases by approximately 20
pp.
Figure B.2: The score of the d2­test during the six different sessions. A tendency line is
made from the averages using a power function.
The subjects perform increasingly better in the d2 test over the course of the experiment.
In the first session, the subjects get an average score of 90%, and in the third session, it
is a score of 97%. It is seen that a power function can be approximated for the average
score with an R­squared close to 1.
