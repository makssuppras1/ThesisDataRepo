Abstract
This thesis deals with stochastic optimal control with two principles of
optimality, namely Pareto optimality and the Nash equilibrium, in the
context of fisheries.
Current Over-fishing have severe biological, social
and economical consequences and calls for optimal control. This study
aims to determine optimal harvest controls for an exploited stochastic
prey-predator system using the Pareto optimal principle and the game
theoretic Nash equilibrium principle. Specifically, the thesis investigates
the Pareto ine№гђiciency of the Nash equilibrium and its dependency on
model parameters.
The stochastic optimal control problems of the Prey-Predator model, un-
der the two optimality principles, are solved using a dynamic program-
ming approach leading to the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equations. The
optimal harvest and performance is computed by numerical solution of the
Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equations. The designed numerical algorithms
uses Markov chain approximation methods, Policy iterations and Time
marching.
Simulation of the stochastic prey-predator model under the computed
optimal controls, shows that the dynamics of the system is similar, using
both the Pareto optimal control and the Nash equilibrium. Additionally,
the simulation shows that the Pareto ine№гђiciency of the Nash equilibrium
is dependent on the predator e№гђiciency parameter ¤х. On this basis, we
conclude that the study of Pareto optimality should be included in game
theoretic studies of stochastic control of a prey-predator type fishery.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Optimal control is a useful method for analyzing controlled dynamical sys-
tems encountered in numerous branches of science and engineering. For
example, the controlled dynamical system could be a continuous stirred-
tank chemical reactor, where the objective is to control a chemical reaction
to a concentration objective of the substances by controlling the stirring
[Kir98]. Precise modelling of controlled dynamical systems is key for the
performance of the optimal control. Modelling real-life control systems
often leads to nonlinear systems, where assumptions and limitations of
the model results in disturbances. Stochastic optimal control models dis-
turbances as random noise that is added to the model, which makes the
models more robust. In many economic control problems competition is
added into the dynamical system, in order to describe strategic interac-
tions between the rational controllers of the system. For economic control
problems the definition of optimality is not unique and depends on the
modelled competition, which leads to various optimality principles, such
as Pareto optimality and Nash equilibrium.
Fishing is an example of a real-life economic control problem, which is
not currently controlled optimally, since over-fishing is reducing fish stocks
and consequently causing a long term economic loss [ADK17]. From an
optimal control perspective, the fisheries needs to be regulated such that
they are sustainable and economic optimal in the long run. Fish popula-
tion dynamics is subject to nonlinear interactions between species, envi-
ronmental fluctuations, and competition between fisheries controlling the
numerous fish populations. This makes it di№гђicult to determine an optimal
control which benefits all fisheries and is sustainable.

1.0.1
State of the art
Usually fish populations is modelled as a stochastic process, such that
fishing management concerns stochastic optimal control problems for con-
trolling the catch rates of the fish. There are several approaches to this
subject, one of them is a state-feedback model predictive control approach.
[HC13] considers a two species stochastic prey-predator model with areal
dependencies, where the Model Predictive control approach results in a
linear quadratic optimization problem, seeking to fit the states and catch
rates to a desired trajectory. This approach does not consider economical
factors and economic Model predictive control studying fisheries is not
well developed in the literature at the moment.
The main approach in the literature is dynamic programming, which leads
to the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation. In [Thy20] a Hamilton-Jacobi-
Bellman equation of a 1 species fishery model, with logistic growth of the
fish population and a square-root utility function, is derived and solved
analytically. Using 1 species model does not account for the ecological
interaction between species, which can provide misleading results that
causes sub-optimal control of the actual system [FA03].
Consequently
more advanced multiple species models are needed to understand the ac-
tual control problem. This approach is taken in [PS14; Pou+14], which
considers more complicated stochastic prey-predator models. They use a
utility function measuring profits of harvesting, which takes demand and
cost into account, providing a complicated stationary Hamilton-Jacobi-
Bellman equation. They use a numerical method for solving the Hamilton-
Jacobi-Bellman equation, which approximates a continuous time Markov
chain.
However, this approach does not consider competition between
fisheries and other optimality principles than system optimality.
Another approach to optimality is the Nash equilibrium used in the game
theoretic study of fisheries. This approach accounts for competition be-
tween fishermen, and leads to a system of Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equa-
tions. In [JY96] a 1 species controlled fishery is modelled as an N-player
stochastic differential game with profit utilities, where the Hamilton-Jacobi-
Bellman equations are solved analytically to determine the Nash equilib-
rium. More advanced models is considered in [WE10], where an N-player
stochastic prey-predator model is used. The Hamilton-Jacobi-Equations

1.0.2
Project formulation
From the the state of the art, it is clear that Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman
equations and the dynamic programming approach plays a central role in
the stochastic optimal control problem of fisheries. The Hamilton-Jacobi-
Bellman equations are studied using either an analytical approach for
simple dynamics or a numerical approach for complicated dynamics. The
Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equations can represent both system optimal-
ity and Nash equilibrium optimality. These two principles of optimality
is widely used but not often compared. Consequently the project con-
siders how to design optimal fishing strategies of an exploited stochastic
prey-predator system where the two species are harvested. The project
compares two principles for optimal harvesting: The first is based on sys-
tem optimization, which leads to Pareto optimality involving a trade-off
between the income from the two harvests. The second is based on game
theory and posits that the two exploiters identify a Nash equilibrium for
this game. The main questions this project tries to answer are:
Рђб What is the difference in performances of the stochastic prey-predator
system under Pareto optimal control and Nash equilibrium optimal
control, and what parameters influence the performance of each op-
timal control?
Рђб How is the dynamics of the stochastic prey-predator influenced by
the two optimal controls?
In the project a dynamic programming approach will be used to study
the stochastic optimal control problems of the prey-predator system. The
resulting systems of Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equations will be analyzed
using a numerical method approximating a continuous time Markov chain.
This yields the numerical method as a finite difference approximation
combined with a policy iteration.

CHAPTER 2
Game Theory
This chapter serves as a brief introduction to the general concepts of game
theory, and as a motivation for the study of the two optimal control prin-
ciples, Nash equilibrium and Pareto optimality, in the context of bioeco-
nomic problems such as fisheries. At last Stochastic differential games
will be introduced as a tool for modelling the control problems, which is
the central study of this thesis.
2.1
Games
Game theory is the study of competitive interactions between the players
of a game. A game is a set of rules or circumstances that has a result
measured in payoff, depending on the actions of the players.
A game
consists of:
1. Players: Players make actions which affects the outcome of the game.
Players are assumed to be rational decision makers seeking to max-
imize their individual payoff. The players are also assumed to have
complete knowledge about the rules of the game, such that they
know every possible actions.
2. Information structure: A games information structure determines
how much the players know about the past actions of the other play-
ers. Perfect information is where the players have full information
about the past actions of the other players, and partial information
is where the players only have partially or no information about the
past action of the other players.
3. Strategy: A complete plan which determines the actions of a player
given specific circumstances of the game.
==PAGE:11==
2.1 Games
6
4. Payoff: The payoff of a game is a value assigned to each player
depending on the outcome of the game.
Games can be used to model what is commonly known as games e.g.
Rock, Paper, Scissors, which is a two player perfect information game
with three possible actions Rock, Paper and Scissors. The payoffs could
be +1 each time a player wins and -1 each time a player loses and 0 for a
tie. Games can also be used to model more complicated phenomena such
as portfolio optimization, war strategies, and exploitation of renewable
resources [M├ў08; Vaj67; WE10].
One of the main objectives of game
theory is to determine the optimal way to play the game, that is the
optimal strategy for each player. For games where the players are playing
against each other(non-cooperative games) a definition of optimality could
be in terms of a Nash equilibrium. A Nash equilibrium is the strategies
where each player maximizes their payoff given that the other players
does the same. This leads to an equilibrium strategy (Nash equilibrium),
where every player is content with their maximal individual payoff, so
each player will not change their strategy unless another player chooses
to do so. In the case of Rock, Paper and Scissors, the Nash equilibrium is
the strategy where both players choose to play Rock, Paper and Scissors
with a probability of 1
3 each, since otherwise the players will keep changing
strategy in a loop to optimize their payoff, taking advantage of the other
playerРђЎs sub-optimal strategy.
2.1.1
PrisonerРђЎs Dilemma
The Nash equilibrium is an optimal solution in the sense of individual
rational payoff, but it turns out that it may provide lower overall payoff
in comparison with the collective optimal solution, namely the Pareto
optimal solution. The difference between the Nash equilibrium and the
Pareto optimal payoffs is referred to as Pareto ine№гђiciency of the Nash
equilibrium. The phenomena of Pareto ine№гђiciency of a Nash equilibrium
is well illustrated by the classical game the PrisonerРђЎs Dilemma.
The
prisonerРђЎs dilemma is a story of two members of a criminal gang who are
arrested and imprisoned, such that the prisoners cannot communicate with
each other. The prosecutors does not have su№гђicient evidence to convict
the prisoners for the major charges, but they have enough to convict both
prisoners on minor charges. At the same time, the prosecutors offers each

2.1.2
Tragedy of the commons
Game theory can be used to study exploitation of a limited common renew-
able resource, which could be wood harvesting, fisheries and agriculture.
For these examples the players are exploiting wood, fish and soil, seeking
to maximize their individual profit by using the common resource. The
individual profit maximizing nature of a rational decision maker can lead
to an over exploitation of the common resource leading to a depletion of
the resource causing a long term disaster for all players. A classical exam-
ple of such a situation is the tragedy of the commons [Har68]. Consider a
pasture open to all herds in some area. Every herd will try to maximise
the amount of their individual animals grazing on the common pasture
in order to maximise their payoff. The herds will encounter an issue if
the total rate of grazing becomes larger than the carrying capacity of the
common grass, leading to a depletion of the grass resource. The depletion
of the common resource will lead to a collective loss of payoff in the long
run. This shows that the individual rational decision making seeking to
maximise your own payoff can lead to a tragedy of the commons payoff.
A parallel can be drawn to fisheries where over-fishing could in principle
also cause a tragedy of the commons. The tragedy of the commons to-
gether with Pareto ine№гђiciency of a Nash equilibrium motivates the study
and comparison of the two principles of optimality, Pareto optimality and
Nash equilibrium, in the context of exploited renewable resources.
2.2
Stochastic differential games
Games such as the prisonerРђЎs dilemma and Rock, Paper, Scissors are exam-
ples of static games where the circumstances of the game is not depending
on time, and consequently the players strategies are also static. In con-
trary, for some games the actions of the players and the states (circum-
stances) of the game evolves dynamically with time, so each player can
react to the current state of the game, and the past actions of their com-
petitors, depending on the information structure of the game. A dynamic
game where time is continuous and possibly infinite is what is known as a
differential game. Uncertainty can be added to the players knowledge of
the state of the dynamic game resulting in a stochastic differential game.
Stochastic differential games can be used to model complicated dynamic

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
To begin with the basic concepts of game theory such as strategy, pay-
off, and Nash equilibrium, was briefly introduced, together with examples
of games showing Pareto ine№гђiciency of a Nash equilibrium, to motivate
the study of this project. The N-player stochastic differential game with
feedback controls, is used as a theoretical framework for defining the op-
timal harvesting problems for both the Pareto optimal and Nash equi-
librium approach. The optimal harvesting problems are solved using a
dynamical programming approach, where the value function describing
the expected future payoff is introduced, and used to setup the Hamilton-
Jacobi-Bellman equations, which describes how to determine the optimal
harvest controls.
The controlled stochastic prey-predator model with logistic prey growth,
type II functional predator response, constant predator mortality, and
state dependent noise terms, is used as the state model for the optimal
harvest control problems. For the Pareto approach the harvest control
problem is viewed as an infinite horizon 1-player stochastic differential
game, with utility function being a weighted sum of square-roots of each
species catch rate. This leads to a stationary Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman
equation that is solved numerically. The algorithm for solving the station-
ary Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation is discretizing the state space to
a rectangular grid, where the second order differentials is approximated
by a central difference scheme and the first order differentials are approx-
imated by an upwind scheme. The boundary conditions is of reflection
type on all four boundaries and the policy update is done using policy
iterations.
For the Nash equilibrium approach the harvest control problem is viewed
as an infinite horizon 2-player stochastic differential game, where each
player controls one species only. The utility function of each player is
the square-root of the catch rate of the harvested species. This formula-
tion leads to a system of two time dependent Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman
==PAGE:54==

49
equations, which is also solved numerically. The numerical algorithm dis-
cretizes the state space into a rectangular grid, where the second order
differentials is approximated by a central difference scheme and the first
order differentials are approximated by an upwind scheme. The boundary
conditions is of reflection type on all four boundaries and policy iterations
is used for the policy update. The resulting semi-discrete system is of
terminal value type and is solved by time marching backwards in time,
with a backward Euler.
The two principles for optimal harvesting was tested using two different
test scenarios where the predator e№гђiciency was varied to simulate differ-
ent uncontrolled deterministic behaviour. In the first test, the predator
e№гђiciency was set to ¤х = 0.25 representing stable dynamics towards the
coexistence critical point. In the second test, the predator e№гђiciency was
set to ¤х = 0.5 representing cyclic dynamics towards a stable limit cycle.
The test results leads to the main conclusions of this thesis which are
summarized here:
1. For the test scenario with ¤х = 0.25, the Pareto optimal principle
leads to a Pareto frontier for the harvesting performances, where
prey harvesting is optimized as much as possible and predator har-
vesting is dependent on the weighting of prey and predator util-
ity. The Pareto optimal control leads to dynamics of the controlled
stochastic prey-predator system, where the predators are harvested
significantly initially and then controlled to a small abundance where
the utility gain per predator is substantial. Under optimal Pareto
harvest control, the preys are harvested optimally in the sense that
the harvesting follows the oscillations in the prey abundance and
cannot lead to a better performance.
The Nash equilibrium principle leads to a harvesting performance
very close to the Pareto optimal performance, and consequently the
Pareto ine№гђiciency of the Nash equilibrium is insignificant.
The
dynamics of the controlled stochastic prey-predator system under
Nash equilibrium optimal harvest control is therefore indistinguish-
able from the dynamics under Pareto optimal control.
We can conclude that for ¤х = 0.25 the two optimal control principles
does not prompt significant differences in performance and dynamics
for the controlled stochastic prey-predator system.
2. For the test scenario with ¤х = 0.5, the Pareto optimal principle leads
==PAGE:55==

50
to a Pareto frontier for the harvesting performances, where it is clear
that the Pareto optimal performance is a compromise between prey
and predator performance.
The dynamics of the stochastic prey-
predator system under Pareto control, is dependent on the prey and
predator utility weighting.
If the prey has the largest weighting
then the dynamics is cyclic in the prey and predator abundances.
In contrary, if the predator has the largest weighting, the predator
abundance is controlled to a small level but the catch rates of both
species are cyclic to some extent.
The Nash equilibrium optimality principle provides a harvesting per-
formance which is distinguishable from the Pareto optimal perfor-
mance, and consequently the Pareto ine№гђiciency of the Nash equi-
librium has increased as the predator e№гђiciency has increased from
¤х = 0.25 to ¤х = 0.5. This can be caused by the cyclic relationship be-
tween prey and predator, such that the controls of each species can
impact the control of the other species negatively. The dynamics of
the stochastic prey-predator model under Nash equilibrium controls
is cyclic in the prey and predator abundances.
We can conclude that for ¤х = 0.5 the two optimal control principles
yields two different performances, where the Nash equilibrium has
a noticeable Pareto ine№гђiciency.
