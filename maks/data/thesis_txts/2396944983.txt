Abstract
Directional drilling is a method for reaching underground reservoirs of oil, gas or geothermal energy in
places inaccessible by conventional vertical drilling. With more degrees of freedom comes more uncertainty
associated with the drill-head’s true location underground which makes the capability to reliably estimate its
orientation and position absolutely critical.
This study investigates the possibilities of continuously measuring the orientation of a probe which is
lowered into the borehole is investigated using a method called directional surveying when the drill string has
been retrieved for maintenance or casing purposes. Whereas conventional methods of directional surveying
require the probe to be stationary at multiple points along the borehole path in order to estimate the hole
proﬁle, and location of the bottom of the hole, a continuous measurement approach would save expensive
time on the drill-rig.
The use of an Extended Kalman Filter(EKF) as well as an Unscented Kalman Filter(UKF) as methods of
sensor fusion and disturbance mitigation of low cost MEMS sensor measurements is proposed for orientation
tracking and hole proﬁle reconstruction. A magnetic disturbance estimation model intended to mitigate
the effects of ferromagnetic materials on downhole magnetic ﬁeld measurements is also developed and
integrated with the Kalman ﬁlters.
The proposed methods are developed and tested using accurate simulations and realistic practical
experiments. The ﬁndings of the simulations and practical experimental campaigns suggest that both types
of Kalman ﬁlter offer signiﬁcant improvements by fusing measurements from a gyroscope, accelerometer
and a magnetometer in the mitigation of both white measurement noise, as well as colored noise resulting
from vibrations similar to those experienced in directional surveying.
The Unscented Kalman Filter applied to a 10 state kinematic model proved to be superior in most aspects
when estimating orientation of downhole equipment under a combination of noise, vibration and magnetic
disturbance affecting the measurements. Unlike the EKF, the performance in mitigating effects of white
noise as well as colored noise was not signiﬁcantly affected by the addition of the magnetic disturbance
estimation model using the UKF.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis is done in collaboration with the Icelandic company Mannvit Engineering, which has been
extensively involved in the Icelandic geothermal industry for many years providing engineering consulting
for both drilling operations as well as the design of geothermal power plants. Their interest in this work is
to learn whether low-cost measurement equipment can be applied to directional drilling, and if so, which
methods of processing these measurements are effective.
In order to gain insight to the problem at hand, this section describes the machinery, strategies and
common practices in the industry that are relevant in the context of this thesis. Available literature will also
be explored to help identify technological gaps.
1.1
Attitude and Heading Reference Systems
The term "Attitude and Heading Reference System" is most commonly used in aviation, where "attitude"
refers to the pitch and roll angles, and "heading" refers to the yaw angle of an aircraft relative to some ﬁxed
global reference frame. The "System" then refers to a set of sensors, as well the computers performing the
necessary calculations to achieve the goal of such as system, that is, to determine an accurate estimate of
attitude and heading from measurements.
Although directional drilling is obviously quite different from aviation, many of the same basic principles
apply. In both ﬁelds, the need for precise knowledge of attitude and heading, or orientation as will be referred
to in this thesis, is absolutely critical for safe and effective operation of the relevant equipment. Applications
in the two ﬁelds also make full use of 3 degrees of freedom in orientation, unlike for example surface vehicles.
Therefore, The title of this thesis "Attitude and Heading Reference System for Directional Drilling" was
chosen.
1.2
Geothermal Drilling Operations
Drilling operations are very expensive both in terms of machinery(leasing, mechanical wear etc.), and
manpower. Therefore it is desired to reliably know where the drill head is located and where it is heading in
order to optimize the use of every hour.
This is done by use of specialized navigation systems utilizing sensor measurements of environmental
parameters but the exact methods implemented by manufacturers for signal processing and localization of
their machines are kept highly secret.

1.2.2
Well proﬁle
Figure 1.2: Common well proﬁles. a) Build and Hold, (b) Build-Hold and Drop(also known as an "S shape
proﬁle"), (c) Continuous Build
The general path chosen in the well design phase prior to drilling is known as the well proﬁle.
There are several different ways in which directional wells can be drilled, and each design has it’s advantages
and limitations. The deciding factors in choosing a well proﬁle are the presence of ground faults or other
known hazards, and the case-speciﬁc requirements of vertical and horizontal displacements of the target
from the drill site.
In the case at hand, which is geothermal drilling in the rough, typically non-level terrain of Iceland
where suitable energy plant surface locations are hard to ﬁnd, moderate to large horizontal as well as vertical
displacements are often required.
For this reason the most common well shape in practice is the Build and Hold proﬁle as illustrated in Fig.
1.2(a). This type well proﬁle deviates from vertical at relatively small depths (usually at a few hundred
meters) and offers a balanced capability to meet vertical and horizontal displacement requirements.
A typical Build and Hold drilling operation is done as follows:
1. The hole is ﬁrst drilled vertically for about 300m through the relatively soft ground formations.
2. The drill-head is then tilted slightly to make the turn which usually builds up at a rate of 1-1,5 degrees
of inclination per 20 meters, known as the incline buildup phase. This results in an approximately
circular arc of along-hole length ∼600m and typically a ﬁnal inclination of between 30 and 45 degrees,
depending on the operation requirements.
3. In the ﬁnal stage of the operation, incline and direction is kept constant but sometimes minor correc-
tions are made. This stage continues down to the ﬁnal depth of 2-3km.
1.2.3
Borehole casing and cementing
In order to prevent collapse of the hole after drilling has ceased and production has begun, and to prevent
contamination of the hole product the hole must be reinforced.

This is done using cylindrical steel casing which is lowered into the borehole and cemented into place.
The casing of the borehole is typically done in between each of the three drilling steps described in Sec.

1.2.4
Currently utilized strategies for drill localization and hole proﬁle estimation
There are two main approaches that may be utilized in directional drilling for localization of the drill
head and for reconstruction of the hole proﬁle.
The ﬁrst is Measurement While Drilling(MWD), in which measurement and calculations are done in real
time while the drill is in operation.
The second approach is referred to as a directional survey . This approach can be used after the drill
string has been retrieved from the hole, which is often done in between any of the three steps described in
Sec. 1.2, when the drill bit needs replacing, or at any time signiﬁcant uncertainty has built up in the MWD
system. This method is also often referred to as a wireline survey, derived from the wireline used to lower
the necessary equipment into the hole.
MWD is always utilized to some extent, but some BHA’s utilized today are less advanced than others. In
addition to that, conditions of the ground formations can also dictate which approach is more accurate.
Measurement while drilling (MWD)
For this ﬁrst method, the measurement equipment is attached to the drill within a non-magnetic section
of the drill, typically 6-10 meters behind the drill head. Measurements are done during pauses every 10-30
meters, depending on drill rig size, to connect a new section of drill pipe. The pauses are utilized to obtain
stationary measurements of gravity which are not contaminated by shock and vibration, and to update the
calibrations of the gyroscope(if one is used) by making use of zero turn-rate knowledge.
The use of magnetic direction measurements is often limited to operations where the ground formations
are known to have have negligible ferromagnetic properties, as these are known to cause distortion to the
measured magnetic ﬁeld. In the case where magnetic disturbances are present, a north seeking gyrocompass
is often utilized. More on MWD can be found at Petrowiki (2015b) and in linked articles.
Directional survey
This second method is quite different from MWD, but similar measurement principles are used. A
cylindrical capsule is used which contains sensor equipment and a microprocessor unit to collect and store the
measured data. This capsule, which will be referred to here as the Sensor Package, is lowered into the hole
using a setup similar to that illustrated in Fig. 1.3 composing of a cable drum which feeds cable over a wheel
with an encoder, measuring the speed of rotation and consequently the movement speed of the sensor package.

1.3
Literature
The vast majority of scientiﬁc research conducted, and subsequently the extent of literature available on
directional drilling is focused on the petroleum industry. This comes as no surprise considering the ﬁnancial
interests involved, and the sheer size of the petroleum industry.
Although the focus of this Thesis is on applications in geothermal drilling, the localization problem
remains in essence the same in terms of well proﬁles, depths involved, and tools utilized. The aspects
where the two operations differ are mainly the strict casing requirements in the petroleum industry in terms
of groundwater contamination risks, and the high temperatures involved in deeper sections of geothermal
boreholes .
There has been a number of publications suggesting novel methods to improve the performance of MWD
and directional survey tools throughout the years. Some focusing on the methodology of measuring and
others on the equipment. Following here is a brief summary of a few publications which have inﬂuenced the
scope of this thesis.
==PAGE:28==
6
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
In Ekseth et al. (2010), Conventional directional survey methods for quality control in drilling are
criticized and the use of an accelerometer, rate-gyroscope, magnetometer and a north-seeking gyrocompass
is suggested in order to measure attitude and heading with the equipment at rest at predeﬁned surveying
stations along the hole path. It is found that by following these proposed procedures, error growth can be
limited and uncertainty can be more accurately accounted for, when compared against conventional methods.
For MWD applications, ElGizawi et al. (2010) explored the potential use of low-cost MEMS accelerom-
eter and gyroscopic sensors placed directly behind the drill-head to continuously measure the attitude while
the drill-head penetrates the ground in oil and gas operations. In theory, it is suggested that with this approach
it is possible to continuously monitor the pitch and roll angles in between surveying stations by use of a
simple linear Kalman Filter as a means of sensor fusion. It was concluded that the best approach was to use
the accelerometer alone to determine pitch and roll angles.
Unlike the real world scenario where vibrations at the drill bit can be rather harsh, The experiments conducted
in this research utilized a smooth rotation table with minimal vibration to the gyroscope and accelerometer.
Furthermore, integration of the gyroscope bias(a phenomenon which will be discussed in Sec. 2.2 ), which
was not estimated by the Kalman ﬁlter, led to a severely degraded performance of the ﬁlter when using
measurements from the gyroscope. Despite this, this paper demonstrated that use of MEMS sensors appears
feasible for drilling applications.
Also for MWD applications, Park and Myung (2014) investigated how re-sampling the magnetic ﬁeld
and building a "magnetic map" of the Borehole can help compensate for positional drift.
This was done by installing two magnetometers two meters apart in the BHA which would sequentially
measure the magnetic ﬁeld at the same points as the drill penetrated the ground. The proposed method
was not implemented in a realistic scenario but simulations and short-duration experiments in an elevator
demonstrated a potential improvement over dead-reckoning in terms of error buildup.
1.4
Thesis Scope
In this thesis, the possibilities of continuous directional surveying using sensor measurements from
low-cost inertial sensors is investigated. This is done utilizing advanced methods of state estimation and
sensor fusion based on Kalman Filtering.
Conventional directional survey tools gather measurements while stationary at predetermined surveying
stations along the borehole path. A reliable continuous approach would remove the need to stop the surveying
probe for stationary measurements and save precious time on the drill rig.

CHAPTER 2
Measurement Equipment
Conventional long-distance navigation systems often rely on Global Navigation Satellite Systems(GNSS)
such as the Global Positioning System(GPS). Underground, however, such signals are unable to reach the
receiver which makes the navigation problem at hand signiﬁcantly more challenging and error buildup must
be minimized. For this reason, the equipment used must be very accurate and precisely calibrated. The
ﬁnal goal is to obtain an accuracy of better than 1°in any given setting which is likely to be encountered
underground.
2.1
Industry State of the Art Sensor Equipment
Underground, The are only three measurable vectors of reference for orientation, namely gravity, earth’s
rotation and earth’s magnetic ﬁeld. Where as surface systems can make use of those same vectors as well as
sun direction sensors, feature detection from camera systems etc. underground systems have rather limited
possibilities. The following information in this section is summarized from Lake (2006).
2.1.1
Gyroscopic systems
Conventional gyroscopic systems are composed of a mechanical rotor driven by an electric motor and
can be classiﬁed into two main categories:
• Free gyroscopes: Usually of the stable-platform type meaning it has two gimbals which remain
perpendicular to each other regardless of tilt, the inner gimbal remains perpendicular to the platform.
Measurements of the angular displacement of the gimbals from the zero point at survey stations can
then be used to compute orientation and subsequently reconstruct the path.
• North seeking gyrocompasses: These systems use the horizontal components of earth’s rotation,
which has a rate of 15°/hour, or approximately 7.2e−5 rad/s to align itself to true north. As the hori-
zontal component of earth’s rotation declines with the cosine of latitude, north seeking gyrocompasses
take signiﬁcantly longer to align themselves at higher latitudes and the accuracy also depreciates. The
advantage of such a system is that at latitudes below 80 °, the gyrocompass will always align itself to
north eventually, although the time it takes to reach the stable position may vary.
When used together with an accelerometer to measure inclination, these gyroscopic systems form the
basis for an Inertial Navigation System(INS).

2.1.2
Magnetic based systems
Some more primitive magnetic systems use a mechanical compass card, much like a hiking compass
which aligns itself to magnetic north. These mechanical compasses print the displacement on ﬁlm which is
read and interpreted after the unit has been retrieved.
This method is uncommon these days due to limited accuracy and temperature capability amongst other
reasons.
Electronic compasses, on the other hand, are much more common. These electronic systems employ
solid state, self contained ﬂuxgate magnetometers to measure components of earth’s magnetic ﬁeld in 3
dimensions. The theory behind using three dimensional magnetic ﬁeld measurements is discussed in more
detail in Sec. 2.2.2.
Magnetic systems are always accompanied by accelerometers to measure gravity for roll and pitch calcula-
tions.

2.2.2
Magnetometer
The magnetometer is used to measure earth’s magnetic ﬁeld. If the true magnitudes in earth coordinates
are known, the heading of the IMU along earth’s surface plane can be determined.
As magnetic ﬁeld components are dependent on geographical location, a commonly used approach to obtain
the local magnetic ﬁeld at a given location is to use static values from a global geomagnetic model such as
that from National Centers for Environmental Information (n.d.).
A common issue with magnetometer measurements is that the earth’s magnetic ﬁeld is relatively weak
and can be easily distorted, commonly by the presence of ferromagnetic materials near the IMU. Fig. 2.2
illustrates the effect of ferromagnetic objects with simple geometries affect a homogeneous magnetic ﬁeld in
two dimensions.
In places close to the magnetic poles these disturbances are particularly difﬁcult to deal with as the horizontal
components of earth’s magnetic ﬁeld, which include the information needed to determine heading, are much
weaker than they are closer to the equator leading to a larger impact on the heading estimate.
In boreholes, these disturbances are commonly present and it is impossible to know their locations and
magnitudes beforehand, and how they will affect the measurements.

where mb is the magnetic ﬁeld as acting on the sensor body frame, Dm is the magnetic disturbance vector

2.2.3
Gyroscope
The 3-axis MEMS gyroscope measures angular velocities of the IMU about it’s inertial axis in 3 dimen-
sional space.
In an ideal scenario these measurements could be directly integrated to obtain total displacement from a
known starting point of orientation in 3d rotational space. However, this is not feasible in practice due to
noise and a bias, which when integrated over time causes a rotational drift.
This bias usually has constant as well as time varying components dependent on temperature, stress etc.,
making it impossible to fully compensate for beforehand.
For this reason the bias must be estimated by some means using sensor fusion to make the gyroscope useful.
The three dimensional decomposed gyroscope vector measurement is deﬁned in Eq. 2.3 where ω, B and
vω denote the true angular velocites, gyroscope biases and gyroscope measurement noise respectively.
ωm = ω + B + vω
(2.3)
There is also a small effect from the earth’s own rotation on the order of 10−5 rad/s which is omitted here
because this is far too small for this gyroscope to measure as both the noise density and the resolution of the
ADC are an order of magnitude above this value.

Body frame
The frame in which raw measurements are obtained is the body frame.
The body frame has its origin and axes ﬁxed to the IMU as depicted in Fig. 3.1 regardless of its orientation
relative to other frames.
North-West-Up (NWU)
The North-West-Up frame, which is a Geographic frame, is used in this thesis to represent local
environmental vectors such as gravity and magnetic ﬁeld. It’s origin follows the system and it’s axes are
deﬁned relative to the system’s geographic position at any given point. This frame will also be used as
reference for angular displacements of the body frame. The z- axis points away from the interior of the earth,
perpendicular to the geodetic reference ellipsoid. The x- axis points toward true north and the y- axis toward
west.
Navigation frame
The local Geodetic frame as described in Farrell (2008) is the navigation frame used in this thesis. Its
origin in ﬁxed at an arbitrary, stationary point on the earth’s surface. In our case, it is deﬁned in the center of
the borehole opening. The axes of this frame are also, like the NWU frame, deﬁned as north, west and up, as

4.2
The Extended Kalman Filter(EKF)
As the state and measurement equations stated in Sec 3.6.1 are non-linear, a ﬁlter or algorithm must be
used that can effectively estimate the non-linear behavior.
The Extended Kalman Filter is a very commonly used solution to this problem and it has proven to be an
effective method for non-linear mean and covariance estimation. Availability of relevant literature led to the
selection of the Extended Kalman Filter, as described in Farrell (2008), as a ﬁrst choice. The abbreviation
"EKF" will be used for the Extended Kalman Filter from here on.
A classical Kalman Filter is modeled around a linear, or close to linear system. If it is applied to a
non-linear system it will only be effective in a region around a speciﬁc set of state values, or the so called
operating point.
The EKF solves this problem by recursively linearizing the system equations around the current operating
point to capture the non-linear dynamics of the system. In practice, this is done by deriving the analytical
state and measurement equation jacobian matrices, denoted F(ˆxk, uk) and H(ˆxk) respectively. At each
time step these matrices are evaluated at the current state estimates.

4.4
Underground Navigation
Figure 4.2: Block diagram illustrating how the Navigation model ﬁts into the system structure
As mentioned previously, underground navigation systems do not have the luxury of GNSS references as
surface systems do. Neither do they have a viable way of referencing their location to beacons in known
geographic locations as underwater systems commonly do.
The result of this is that a Dead Reckoning path integration approach is the only proven viable way of
estimating horizontal location, or at least the only one that is publicly known. For this reason, we must make

5.1
Magnetometer Calibration
Given the unavailability of navigational references such as global navigational satellite systems (GNSS),
the performance of sensors at our disposal must be optimized by calibration in order to minimize error
buildup.
The most critical of which is the magnetometer offset. This offset can often be relatively large compared
to the measured signal of earth’s magnetic ﬁeld, which is rather weak. Furthermore, the offset varies with
time and signiﬁcant changes can be measured in terms of days, making it necessary to re-evaluate this offset
regularly.
5.1.1
Removal of magnetometer offset
There is a rather simple way to remove this offset by calibration. First, the IMU is moved outside to a
location with a homogeneous magnetic ﬁeld.
Measurements are gathered by moving the IMU in circles, or a "ﬁgure of eight" pattern and exposing all
axes of measurement, both positive and negative, to the direction of the magnetic ﬁeld vector.
The data is then loaded into Matlab. The left plot of Fig. 5.1 shows that when the axes of measurement
are plotted relative to each other, the full range varies between the positive and negative halves of the axes
which means that there is an offset present. This offset can be removed by subtracting the maximum value
measured by each axis from the minimum value, divided by two. The result is seen on the right plot of Fig
5.1. How the circles now align is apparent.
This result can then be ﬁne tuned by making small changes to the calculated offset, and ﬁnding the
values which minimize variance of the magnetic ﬁeld magnitudes. The impact of this calibration procedure
can also be seen in Fig. 5.2, where the magnitudes are plotted before and after calibration as a function of
sample number. A more consistent magnitude indicates that most of the bias has been removed.

Notes on the development of this model
The basic idea for this model was inspired by the paper of Roetenberg et al. (2005), in which the authors
proposed a disturbance rejection model for a dual linear kalman ﬁlter to be used with indoor wearable
movement tracking systems. The proposed model therein utilized changes in magnetic dip angle and
magnetic ﬁeld magnitude to scale the variance parameter of a gauss-markov disturbance model linearly.
The main ways in which the model explained throughout this section differs from the model proposed in
the paper are the following:
• Roetenberg et al. (2005) implements the model on a classical kalman ﬁlter where as the application
here is on non-linear kalman ﬁltering (EKF and UKF)
• The variance is scaled linearly by deviations in magnetic dip and magnitude in Roetenberg et al. (2005),
but in this application it was found that exponential scaling was better suited to avoid contributions
from noise.
• The scaling of the variance parameter as contributed by deviations from the nominal magnetic ﬁeld,
σ2
δ|m| is entirely original (to the best of knowledge).
5.3
Short-Term Geomagnetic Variations
However well our model may be able to estimate a magnetic disturbance caused by crustal anomalies,
there is still another potentially limiting factor.
Time-dependent current ﬂuctuations in the Earth’s ionosphere cause distortions to Earth’s magnetic
ﬁeld. The largest contribution to these ﬂuctuations is typically caused by solar winds. The impact of
these distortions on magnetic based directional measurements is particularly pronounced in regions at high
latitudes for two main reasons:
1. At high latitudes, the horizontal component of Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld is relatively weak, making it
proportionally more sensitive to variations in Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld than in regions near the magnetic
equator.

2. The amplitude of these ﬂuctuations is high in regions within, and close the arctic. This effect stems

ﬂuctuations can be reconstructed from magnetic observatory data in distances up to 200 km north/south
of the observatory. If the drill site is at a latitude same as, or close to that of a magnetic observatory, this
distance can be up to 500km east/west.

CHAPTER 7
Practical experiments
In this chapter, the performance of the estimation methods described in Ch. 4 is analyzed and compared
using real-world experiments.
Euler angles will be used rather than quaternions to present the results and associated uncertainty in
orientation similarly to Ch. 6
The following experiments were designed only with borehole direction, or azimuth estimation in mind
as realistic scenarios including a sufﬁciently accurate reference for incline could not be put in place. Despite
this limitation to the practical experiments, the directional component is considered to typically be the most
problematic in conventional settings due to limited degrees of freedom available for measurement.
7.1
Test of Baseline Magnetometer Accuracy
The goal of this experiment is to determine how well heading can be estimated from magnetometer
measurements in the absence of other error sources. To estimate this achievable level of accuracy, A known
reference must be in place which can be compared against in a repeatable manner.
7.1.1
Setup and strategy
For the purpose of this experiment, a wooden hexagon of corner to corner diameter of 20cm is sawed out
of an MDF board. 3 holes are drilled in the hexagon to mount the XSens IMU.
A "seat" is also constructed, in which the hexagon will be placed. The seat is to ensure that the directions
of the IMU’s x- and y- axes relative to the ﬂat surface are the same each time the hexagon rests on a given
edge. The edges are numbered 0 through 5 for convenience. Care is taken that the edges of both the hexagon
and reference edge of the seat are not rounded so that placement is identical each time. The setup can be
seen in Fig. 7.1
As it is difﬁcult to get an accurate measure of absolute heading in world coordinates we will work with
incremental deviations.
First we must determine the true angles of the hexagon. Ideally the hexagon would have exactly

7.2.2
Data processing
The 10 measurement runs are recorded in a single log. As this experiment is not strictly designed to test
the ability to navigate from point A to point B, and also because the exact movement speed at any given
point proved too uncertain using GPS, we will assume the speed of movement to be a constant 1.6m/s. We
also pre-ﬁlter the accelerometer and gyroscope measurements with a low-pass ﬁlter to remove some of the
vibration effects. This procedure is the same regardless of which estimation method is subsequently used.
After feeding the data though each of the estimation methods, the estimates are then sectioned into 10
separate runs for each straight section, removing parts of the data where 180 °turns are in progress. 5 of
which will be called "forward" runs and 5 will be called "reverse" runs.
Criteria of estimated heading
Using the known reference vectors in the NWU frame for our derivations, the estimated quaternion will
represent a transformation from NWU to body and the derived heading, or yaw angle ψ, will therefore be
the absolute angular deviation from true north. In an ideal case, the difference in mean heading between the
forward and reverse paths should be 180 °, but in the presence of magnetic disturbances and vibrations this
value is likely to deviate from that.
Using the results of the previous experiment in Sec. 7.1, we can attribute up to 0.35° of the heading error
to measurement uncertainty, the remaining error will then be considered a contribution of environmental
factors.
Figure 7.3 shows a comparison of the estimated yaw angles ψ for each method It is apparent that the
Kalman ﬁlters give much more stable estimate of heading.
Looking at Tab. 7.3 for the average heading error parameter eψ we see that the effects on the mean
angular error between forward and reverse directions is essentially the same for the SVD and the Kalman
Filters if we do not include an estimate of magnetic disturbance. In the case where the magnetic disturbance
estimation is included, we see an improvement of approximately 25 % which may be contributed to improved
mitigation of the estimate to the disturbance induced by the iron weights. Differences between the EKF and
the UKF with the magnetic disturbance estimate are not large enough to be considered deﬁnitive.
Note that this result is an average of 5 sets of backward and forward runs. Considering all the data sets
individually showed an improvement when using both Kalman ﬁlters (with magnetic disturbance estimates)
over the SVD ranging from 5 % to 50 % using the EKF, and from 5 % to 40 % using the UKF.

7.3
Navigational Performance
In this experiment, the same sled setup is used as in the previous experiment of Sec. 7.2.
The purpose of this experiment is to investigate how well our proposed estimation methods are able to
track the sled’s path from a given starting point, to a pre-determined end point. For reference, the path will
be tracked by GPS simultaneously to be compared with the estimate.
As this is to be an experiment of navigation, the movement speed V must be known at all times during the
experiment. The only method found was to use the movement speed derived from the GPS reference. This
measure of movement speed does come with some uncertainty. From National Coordination Ofﬁce for Space
Based Positioning and Timing (2017) it is stated that the 95% base uncertainty is around 0.006m/s, but
other factors such as receiver properties have an impact as well, which in our case is a Samsung Galaxy S6
phone and this can be expected to add at least the same degree of uncertainty again. Despite this drawback,
we will assume the error in speed to have a zero mean over a sufﬁciently long experiment and quantify the
variance of our navigation equation as σ2
p = 10−4.
The sled is then pulled over a distance of approximately 900 meters. The magnetic ﬁeld reference used
is obtained from the observatory in Leirvogur University of Iceland (2017), which is approximately 10 km

sources of magnetic disturbance are seen near the test site, neither are any added.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The possibilities of using low cost MEMS sensors along with advanced sensor fusion algorithms for
continuous directional borehole surveys has been investigated. The investigated sensor fusion algorithms
are the Extended Kalman Filter(EKF) and the Unscented Kalman Filter(UKF). The performance of these
ﬁlters was compared against one another, as well as a direct least squares method, solved by a singular value
decomposition (SVD) approach.
Simulations and real-world experiments suggest that signiﬁcant improvements in orientation tracking
are obtainable by detecting, and compensating for the effects of magnetic disturbances in measurements by
use of the Kalman Filters’ ﬂexible modeling qualities. Furthermore, the two Kalman ﬁlters were found to
perform far better than the more primitive SVD method in terms of mitigation of vibration and noise effects,
almost canceling them out completely.
Considering the goal of sub-degree accuracy in orientation estimation, simulations suggested this to be
obtainable with largest errors being in the yaw angle, observed at a maximum of 0.8° and a root-mean-square
value of around 0.25° under a combination of noise, vibrations and magnetic disturbance. The addition of
further uncertainties in the magnetic ﬁeld reference vector, sensor offsets and installation when implemented
on a sled in the real world experiment of following a straight line, was found to increase the heading error
beyond the sub-degree limit, to 1.65°. It is believed that a large portion of these real-world issues could be
compensated for with more precise installation and calibration.
EKF compared against the UKF
The UKF was found to perform signiﬁcantly better than the EKF when the measurements were corrupted
by colored noise in the form of vibrations and the UKF also managed to cope better with the combined
effects of vibration, magnetic disturbance and measurement noise.
The disturbance estimation model derived in Sec. 5.2 could be integrated almost seamlessly with the
UKF, without sacriﬁcing performance in the absence of magnetic disturbances whereas the addition of the
disturbance model had a noticeable impact on the nominal performance of the EKF, increasing the base rms
error in orientation estimation by up to 50 %.
Furthermore, simulations showed consistent reduction of rms error in orientation estimation induced by
magnetic disturbance, when compared against the same Kalman ﬁlters without the disturbance estimation
model, of around 90-95% using the UKF with measurement noise present, and 65-87 % while the accelerom-
eter measurements are also affected by vibrations composed of colored noise with amplitudes similar to
what can be expected in real-world scenarios. The same scenarios yielded 78-93 % improvement with only

measurement noise and 63-80% improvement under vibrations when using the EKF. The absolute values
of these scenarios all favored the UKF, except for the yaw angle estimate under a combination of noise,
vibrations and magnetic disturbance which was slightly better using the EKF, more precisely 3.6%, which
perhaps cannot be considered signiﬁcant.
The UKF also demonstrated a more stable and accurate estimate of gyroscope bias as well as better
decoupling between estimations of gyroscope bias and magnetic disturbance, i.e. magnetic disturbances
caused smaller deviations of the gyro bias estimate using the UKF as compared to the EKF.
Navigation
Considering the navigational performance in tracking a path from one point to another, the improvement
using the Kalman ﬁlter derived estimates was most pronounced in regards to vibration mitigation. Whereas
the SVD method gave biased estimates of orientation, and consequently continuous error buildup due to
sensitivity issues, the Kalman ﬁlters avoided this issue with the intelligent way that sensitivity of estimates
to measurements is accounted for.
Simulations also indicated that using the Kalman Filters reduced the variance of the "random walk"
type of contribution to the path estimate, making for less uncertainty buildup in the overall estimate. This
property could not be conclusively validated in practice as the only available source of movement speed
reference at the time of experiments, the GPS receiver of a smartphone, was not sufﬁciently accurate.
Despite this, the Kalman Filters showed improvement over the SVD method in terms of magnetic
disturbance rejection, and vibration mitigation when following a straight line in a more narrowly deﬁned
real-world experiment.
Overall Feasibility of Application to Directional Drilling
Considering the performance as evaluated in this study, it does appear feasible to implement relatively low
cost MEMS measurement equipment along with advanced sensor fusion algorithms such as the Unscented
Kalman Filter in directional surveying tools and obtain accurate estimates of a borehole path by continuous
measurements. Although the sub-degree accuracy could not be achieved in practice under the combined
effects of noise, vibration and magnetic disturbance, it is likely that this is possible with further work and
more precise installation and calibration.
There are, however, still aspects outside the scope of this thesis which may hinder this such as effects of
temperature and pressure on the equipment. Furthermore, some ground formations are entirely unsuitable
for magnetic based systems as earth’s magnetic ﬁeld may be too heavily distorted over long distances or
even the whole path. Despite these potential factors which are yet to be investigated, the results provide for a
proof of concept which may be the basis for further work.
