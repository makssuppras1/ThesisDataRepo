Preface
This master thesis was conducted at the department of Applied Mathematics
and Computer Science at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of
the requirements for acquiring a M.Sc. degree in Mathematical Modelling and
Computation. The thesis corresponds to 35 ECTS.
The current thesis extends the work of Pødenphant and Truong, 2015 who
studied a version of the multiplicative mixed model where one random effect and
one fixed effect are present using a full likelihood approach. The idea here is to
examine the case where one additional fixed factor is included, giving an extra
multiplicative term. This is expected to be interesting in terms of theory where
literature on multiplicative mixed models is a bit more sparse than for general
multiplicative models, but also implementation-wise since parameter estimation
requires th use of Laplace approximation.
The code is developed in the statistical software R. Supervisors of the project
are Professor Bjarne Kjær Ersbøll, Assistant Professor Nina Munkholt Jakobsen
and PhD student Sofie Pødenphant Jensen from DTU Compute.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Nonlinear mixed models form a class of models where fixed and random effects
enter the conditional mean response function in a non linear way. These models
have been widely used in the literature for analyzing complex repeated measure
studies in different areas, especially in agricultural, biomedical and environmental
applications (Berman, Wong, Bhasin, and Ipp, 1996, Huon de Kermadec, Durand,
and Sabatier, 1997, Prajneshu, 1998, Archontoulis and Miguez, 2013).
The main advantage over their linear counterpart is that nonlinear models are
a key resource for capturing complicated real life phenomena in a more sophisti-
cated way by examining individual-specific qualities. Μodel parameters usually
incorporate theoretical features of the data allowing for a physical interpretation
of the effects and for that reason these models are often given the characterization
mechanistic (D. M. Bates and Pinheiro, 1998). Given their formulation, nonlinear
models generally require less parameters, whereas linear ones make use of high
order polynomials to achieve accurate approximations of the true relationship.
Finally, these models offer more reliable predictions for the response outside the
observed range of data since they tend to fit it better (Pinheiro and Bates, 2000).
The scientific interest for these models is so pervasive that expansions and ex-
tensions of the basic methodology are constantly developing. Owing to the ever
increasing computational power, relevant procedures and routines can handle pa-
rameter estimation with great agility and offer researchers a flexible tool with
vast applicability.
This thesis concerns a specific nonlinear model, the so called mixed multiplica-
tive. The model is introduced by P. B. Brockhoff, Schlich, and Skovgaard, 2015
as a novel approach to account for assessors heterogeneity in the analysis of sen-
sory data, a scientific discipline that applies statistical analysis to evaluate and
interpret reactions to product characteristics as perceived by human senses. The
model in its genuine form resembles a linear two-way ANOVA that contains the
panelist and product effects and their interaction. Its competitive advantage how-
ever comes from the groundbreaking partitioning of the conventional panelist-by
product interaction into a scaling and a disagreement effect.
The former em-
bodies the different use of scale among assessors that is pointed out by smaller/
larger separation of products given by some panelists. The latter stands for the

degree of agreement between assessors in regards to the rating of products. The
scaling effect enters as a panelist-dependent regression coefficient which makes
model non-linear. The profit from distinguishing between these two sources of
variation translates into improved understanding of assessor’s performance and
of their influence on the actual rating.
The case of of one fixed factor (product) and one random (panelist) effect is
fully discussed in Pødenphant and Truong, 2015. This thesis aims to extend the-
ory of the mixed multiplicative model when two fixed factors (product attributes)
and one random are present, yielding two multiplicative terms overall. Parame-
ter estimation and hypothesis testing are handled by a likelihood approach using
the Laplace method via an open source R package, the Template Model Builder
(TMB) that facilitates likelihood maximization (Kristensen, 2014). Due to lack
of established background knowledge, model behaviour is addressed via simula-
tions with definite purpose to unravel benefits and limitations of the likelihood
approach and propose practical tools that put suggested theory into practice. As
such, all applications should be seen under an investigating perspective, where
results are hoped to uncover strengths and weaknesses of the proposed model and
enlighten its ”unknown” behaviour.
The remainder of this project is organized in seven chapters: Chapter 2 famil-
iarizes reader with the basic theory of nonlinear models in general and particularly,
of the mixed multiplicative model for the simple case of one fixed factor. Chap-
ter 3 builds concepts and theory for the extended version of the multiplicative
model and covers the key points of its formulation, including identifiability and
derivation of the likelihood function. Chapter 4 discusses the Laplace method as
approximation of the marginal likelihood and how this is implemented in Tem-
plate Model Builder using a technique known as Automatic Differentiation (AD).
The model is then fit to real data from the area of sensometrics. Findings from this
application with respect to requirements and model performance are addressed
with more detail right after on Chapter 5 in the context of an extensive simu-
lation study. In the same sense, significance of model terms is assessed via the
commonly accepted Likelihood Ratio Tests (LRT). Once again, simulations pro-
vide a useful tool to examine behaviour of these tests and to set them up as an
adequately convenient method for checking terms in the nonlinear multiplicative
setting. Chapter 6 is split in two parts; in the first, the model is fit to a real
data set that satisfies prerequisites with respect to the optimum setup so that the
model is able to distinguish between scaling effect and disagreement. The second
part applies the classic three-way ANOVA model on real and simulated data and
compares to results of the multiplicative model. Finally, Chapter 7 concludes
with some discussion on findings and proposals for future work.

CHAPTER 2
The Multiplicative Mixed Model
The following chapter gives a brief introduction on the general nonlinear setup
and proceeds on description of the multiplicative mixed model that belongs to the
general class of non-linear mixed effects models. The simple case with one fixed
and one random term has been thoroughly examined in Pødenphant and Truong,
2015. The key features of the simple model are presented here in short, since they
provide background knowledge for the extended version that is developed in the
current thesis and will be introduced in Chapter 3.
2.1
Non-Linear Mixed Effects Model
The basic theory of mixed models considers two sources of variation for data clas-
sified in groups based on one or more factors: a) within-subject variability (intra-
individual) which can be non-constant, autocorrelated or of another form and b)
between-subject variability (inter-individual) which can be random or related to
specific characteristics of the individuals. Bearing this in mind and combined
to Lindstrom and Bates, 1990, nonlinear mixed effects model for a single level
of grouping can be written as a generalization of both linear mixed effects and
nonlinear fixed effects models in the following two-stage hierarchy:
Stage 1 (Intra-individual):
yij = f(ϕi, xij) + ϵij,
ϵij ∼N(0, Λi))
Stage 2 (Inter-individual):
ϕi = Aiθ + Uiwi,
wi ∼N(0, Gi))
where yij is the jth observation on the ith group, f is a nonlinear function of a pa-
rameter vector ϕi and a predictor vector xij that forms the relationship between
response and predictors, and ϵij is an independently and normally distributed
error term.
Parameter vector is group-specific, i.e. it is allowed to vary from group to
group; this is exactly what expression in the second formula accounts for. Ai
and Ui are design matrices of size r × p and r × q for the fixed effect θ and
random effect wi respectively, where wi is normally distributed with zero mean
and variance-covariance matrix Gi, independent of ϵij.

Usually Λ = σ2I (constant error variance), but in general it is possible to ac-
commodate any other variance-covariance structure. The single-level model can
be extended to cover cases where data are grouped based on multiple nested fac-
tors (Pinheiro and Bates, 2000). The so called multi-level model is derived by
modifying the model for random effects in Stage 2.
As a main benefit, Lindstrom and Bates, 1990 argue that the above two-
stage representation simplifies model specification no matter how complex f is.
They also underline its importance from a computational perspective; given its
formulation, expression in Stage 2 enables calculation of derivatives of f with
respect to θ and wi that are necessary for optimizing the model function.

properties of the nonlinear counterpart. Focus is placed on the analysis of data
from sensory evaluation experiments, where assessors rate consumer products a
number of times (replicated data).
2.2
Multiplicative Mixed Models
The word ”multiplicative” characterizes a model that includes terms whose mag-
nitude appears as the product effect of other terms. Multiplicative models are
very common in a wide range of scientific disciplines (medicine, biology, physics),
and especially multiplicative mixed-effects when interaction between a fixed and
a random effect is present. Interpretation is similar to fixed effects case, yet es-
timation of parameters changes, since a fixed-random multiplicative interaction
affects the structure of covariance matrix, while interaction between fixed factors
affects expectation of response (Oman, 1991). These models provide not only pre-
cise inference, but also a useful way for checking the structure of random effects
in the (non)linear mixed effect context (Pødenphant, Kristensen, and Brockhoff,
2018).
P. B. Brockhoff and Skovgaard, 1994 introduce a multiplicative approach to
handle individual differences between assessors in sensory profiling. As stated in
the article, what happens in reality, members of a panel vary in their ability to
distinguish products. This is reflected in the fact that assessors use the rating
scale differently by separating products with more units, what is known as the
scaling effect. Assessors also differ in terms of individual variances, which, if not
systematic, they cannot say much about the spread of products. The authors
proposed the model given below, which stems from the usual two-way ANOVA

Therefore, let the likelihood of a model and a set of parameters. Sometimes, it

value of ζ and then replaces δ by its maximum likelihood estimate. Repeating
this process across a range of (constant) values for ζ, in sufficiently small steps,
gives the profile likelihood mentioned above.
Model assumptions can be checked via Likelihood Ratio Tests (LRT) built
upon either the (log)likelihood or the profile likelihood, depending on the situa-

The R Package: Template Model Builder
As has been discussed in Chapter3, parameter estimation in the multiplicative
mixed model requires the use of Hessian matrix involved in Laplace approxima-
tion of the marginal log-likelihood. A further manipulation of the Hessian matrix
( eH) results to the modified Laplace method. If not for the case where random
effects enter the model linearly and thus approximation is exact ( eH = H), the
modified method is inevitably preferable. With the cost of some loss of informa-
tion, it avoids numerical issues that would otherwise occur during maximization
of the Laplace function. Looking deeper into the method, one can see that evalua-
tion of Laplace function ends up to a classical optimization problem that involves
two steps: first an approximate Hessian is computed at each iteration of the al-
gorithm and then it is inserted to the Taylor expansion of the log-likelihood in
order to obtain a set of maximized parameter estimates (cf. Pødenphant and
Truong, 2015 for more details). Evidently, the whole process remains cumber-
some and computationally expensive to operate within the context of successive
optimizations. Given the above limitations, it is reasonable to seek an alternative
numerical technique for evaluating derivatives and Automatic Differentiation is
designated for such calculations.
Relying on a fairly simple principle, Automatic Differentiation (AD) extracts
accurate derivatives and prevents researcher from ”writing, testing and maintain-
ing derivative code” by himself (Kristensen, Nielsen, Berg, Skaug, and Bell, 2016).
Owing to its convenience, AD gradually gains ground into statistical software that
incorporate C++ templates. The R package Template Model Builder (TMB) com-
prises a very flexible tool of this sort that implements complex random effects
models very fast and bypasses the computational cost underlined before. Devel-
oped by Kristensen, 2014, TMB facilitates calculation of first order derivatives of
the negative log-likelihood, written in C++, via AD. The likelihood function along
with its derivatives enter a conventional optimization routine (e.g. nlminb) to
get the desired parameter estimates that maximize the Laplace function (Kris-
tensen, 2015). The package carries exactness of the Hessian approximation off,
and produces satisfactorily accurate estimates.

4.1.1
Automatic Differentiation
Automatic Differentiation (AD) is a well known technique that enables calculation
of exact derivatives for a function given by a computer program. AD exploits the
fact that every program can be broken down to a sequence of simple mathematical
operations and functions (+, −, ·, /, √, exp, log, sin, cos, . . .) regardless of its
complexity. More precisely, the program itself records all operations performed
along the evaluation of the function and at the same time builds a representation
for it, referred to as a computational graph or tape ( Kristensen et al., 2016). The
tape consists of nodes with labels indicating the simple operations, a numbering
that denotes the order in which operations are evaluated and arrows that assign
operation arguments to results (Kristensen et al., 2016). Initial nodes correspond
to the independent variables of the function and the final node to the function
value as such. Following the flow of the tape, the program utilizes the chain rule
appropriately to update the derivatives one operation at a time.
Decomposition of differentials given by the chain rule can be carried out in two
ways, by running the tape either forward (forward mode) or backwards (reverse
mode). In forward mode the program begins with the independent variables and
computes derivatives in the direction where arrows point at. On the contrary,
reverse mode starts with the function and conducts calculations in the opposite
direction.

4.1.2
Software Implementation
TMB is built around the principle of calculating high order derivatives using
AD and is designed to operate well on high dimensional latent variable problems
containing up to 106 random effects and 103 parameters (Kristensen et al., 2016).
Steps below outline how TMB functions and offer an overview of its main features:
a) The user defines the (negative) joint log-likelihood of data as a function of
fixed and random effects, h(·, w), in a C++ template (all other operations are
performed R). This is advantageous in that it not only treats variables of multi-
ple types simultaneously, but also checks whether they are correctly specified
during compiling. All factors and model parameters should be precisely de-
scribed in the code. Expressions, such as vectors, matrices or arrays, can be
reported back to R together with their derivatives using a compile function.
b) The package evaluates and maximizes the Laplace approximation of the marginal
likelihood, LLA, or equivalently it minimizes −lLA, where random effects are
integrated out. In order for the latter to be done, derivatives of lLA up to third
degree are required; these are obtained via successive application of AD on the
joint log-likelihood with respect to random effects. In particular, the gradient
(1st order) of h(·, w) is used to optimize the joint likelihood with respect to
w, i.e. to find h(θ, σ, Ψ, ew; y). The Hessian, H(·, w) (2nd order) is necessary
for evaluating lLA and for Laplace approximation itself. Finally, the 3rd order
derivative is used to compute the gradient of lLA for its optimization. The
above quantities are then given as input to a chosen optimizer (nlminb) that
returns the desired maximum likelihood estimates for the model parameters.
c) Apart from the estimates, the package computes a measure of uncertainty
(standard deviation) for both fixed and random effects (including non linear
expressions of random effects that have been stated in (a)). More precisely,
once the Hessian matrix with respect to fixed effects is calculated, the program
estimates the parameter covariance matrix as:

The user calls function compile to compile the template in R and loads it to the
environment via dynlib. In order to construct the objective function, s/he uses
function MakeADFun that takes as input the TVbo data and a list of parameters
which are given initial values. Argument random is a character vector containing
the names of parameters that must be considered as random; the remaining pa-
rameters are considered fixed by default. TMB automatically checks for spelling
errors and names that either have not been defined correctly in MakeADFun or
do not exist in the template. Once checking is completed, the function returns
an object with the Laplace log-likelihood and its derivatives, which in turn are
passed into nlminb for optimization. After that, sdreport calculates standard
deviations, including anti-log versions of the variance components.

CHAPTER 5
Simulation Study
Just like any relevant approach, theory around the extended multiplicative model
should provide a methodology for constructing product contrasts and testing
model terms. In order for the latter to lie on a stable basis, it is important to en-
lighten more on model’s behaviour under varying conditions. A simulation study
is a very promising tool to accomplish this, however it should be developed with
caution to encounter the various issues emerging along the process in the most ef-
ficient way (non-positive definiteness of the Hessian, singularity/ non-convergence
of the optimization, initial values of variance components not to close to 0). The
simulation study that is described in the following adopts a comprehensive strat-
egy that accounts the above issues and aims to uncover strengths and weaknesses
of the model towards an overall assessment point of view.
5.1
Model Performance
The principle behind the study is to carry out several simulations, each relying
on a different setup or on slightly modified parameter values. To accomplish this,
300 data sets are generated under each simulation based upon either the setup
of raw data or of a replicated version of the data that includes combinations of
the subsequent characteristics: a) extra replicates (L > 2), b) extra assessors
(I > 8), c) extra TVset and Picture levels (J > 3, K > 4).
The generation
process resembles that one described in section 4.2, yet the theoretical parameter
values that are used to compute the response variable are allowed to vary (e.g.
σb = 1 and all others set to 2 or all sigmas set to 5,...). Once data is ready,
the (negative) joint log-likelihood is defined in a template and optimization is
performed for every data set separately.1
After implementation, simulation is
evaluated according to certain criteria that summarize model performance and
convergence of the optimization algorithm.
In particular, criteria refer to the
number of cases (i.e. number of data sets out of 300) for which:

5.2
Likelihood Ratio Test
In linear models testing model terms is usually conducted via F-tests that are
formed by Mean Square Errors. In non-linear models however this approach is
set aside due to lack of normality. Likelihood Ratio Tests (LRT) emerge as the
best-suited alternative for checking significance of random and fixed terms via
comparison of nested models. In the current version of the multiplicative mixed
model, although there is no established theory, LRT are judged to be the most
convenient tool to proceed in hypothesis testing.
Thus, the scope here is to
construct likelihood-based tests for the significance of the five random and two
fixed effects individually and see whether these tests are reliable enough to rely
inference on. For this purpose, the section ends with a simulation study that
is hoped to feature the distribution of tests and bring out issues that should be
taken into account when decision on model terms has to be taken.
For the multiplicative model the method considers the following sequence of
models:
M1 : yijkl = µ + ai + νj + γk + biνj + ciγk + dij + lik + ϵijkl
M2 : yijkl = µ + ai + νj + γk + biνj + ciγk + dij + ϵijkl
M3 : yijkl = µ + ai + νj + γk + biνj + ciγk + lik + ϵijkl
M4 : yijkl = µ + ai + νj + γk + ciγk + dij + lik + ϵijkl
M5 : yijkl = µ + ai + νj + γk + biνj + dij + lik + ϵijkl
M6 : yijkl = µ + νj + γk + biνj + ciγk + dij + lik + ϵijkl
M7 : yijkl = µ + νj + ϵijkl
M8 : yijkl = µ + γk + ϵijkl
M9 : yijkl = µ + ϵijkl
and performs in two equivalent ways: either by adding or removing terms. In
the first case, the method begins with the constant model (M9) and proceeds
by comparing it with models including higher order terms. In the second case
it does exactly the opposite; starting with the full model (M1) it removes the
higher order terms to conclude on the significance of a simpler lower order model.
No matter which way is used, LRT relies on calculation of the likelihood null
(L0) and alternative (L1) hypothesis, which then plugs into χ2 = −2log
(
L0
L1
)
(or equivalently χ2 = −2 (log(l0) −log(l1)) for the log-likelihood) to derive the
so-called χ2 statistic. Under null hypothesis and if all regularity conditions are
met, this quantity is χ2 distributed with degrees of freedom df = df1 −df0.
Tests for random terms are obtained comparing M1 with each of the models
M2 - M6. Every time the tested model is reduced by one term, hence according
to theory LRT should follow a an asymptotic χ2
1. Note however, that testing a
==PAGE:62==
52
5 Simulation Study
variance parameter equal to zero forces it to the boundary of the parameter region
(always positive); LRT should be adjusted accordingly by halving the degree of
freedom (P. Brockhoff, 2017b).
Things change when testing fixed terms; in fact removing main effect from the
M1 still leaves ν (or γ) parameters inside the model as regressors of the scaling
part. This double consideration of the the main effect leads to technical issues on
parameter estimation. As such, model comparison should be done alternatively
and here three different approaches are presented. The first one (a) considers the
intercept model M9 and the simple one-way ANOVA models M7, M8 for TVset
and Picture respectively. The second approach (b) is based on the rational that
when excluding main effect, scaling also disappears owing to its nature. Therefore,
assessor by product interaction in the reduced model accounts for only one source
of variation, the disagreement effect (personal preference). This demands extra
models to be considered for TVset and Picture:
M10 : yijkl = µ + ai + γk + ciγk + dij + lik + ϵijkl
M11 : yijkl = µ + ai + νj + biνj + dij + lik + ϵijkl
The nonlinear part that corresponds to the factor being tested disappears, but
since the second factor is still present the reduced model remains nonlinear. M10,
M11 can be compared directly to M1 and the LRT is computed as ususally. Lastly,
third approach (c) considers a nested pair that checks directly scaling effect via
comparison of M4 - M10 and M5 - M11.

each null hypothesis H0 in Table 5.8 via a simulation study.

5.2.1
Power estimation
The chapter closes with an overview of the power of tests. Recall that the power
measures a test’s True Positive ability which is the proportion of times null hy-
pothesis is correctly rejected at α significance level.
To put it in the current
context, power for a test here means how possible it is that the latter detects
any difference in the parameter of a specified magnitude (effect size). This itself
is a hard computation on its own as it requires knowledge about the sampling
distribution of the test statistic under the alternative hypothesis, which in general
is not readily available. Again, simulation is the tool for exploring the behaviour
of tests as was done before.
To exemplify this with attribute effects, let the null hypothesis formulated as
H0 : ν1 = ν2 = ν3 for TVset and H0 : γ1 = γ2 = γ3 = γ4 for Picture. Then the
alternative is defined as H1 : ∃m, n|νm ̸= νn and H1 : ∃m, n|γmγνn respectively.
The question rises upon how big the difference should be in order for each of the
tests to detect it.4. To investigate this, 1000 simulations are conducted under
each alternative hypothesis and then probability of rejecting the null is estimated
by using the empirical proportion of simulations that reject it.5 The process is
repeated for varying effect sizes.
Results are summarized in Tables E.7 - E.14 of Appendix E. As seen from
the tables and the ensuing figures, a σl or σd value equal to 1 is enough to raise
power at 75% or even higher, whereas larger values are required for the remaining
variance components. Regarding fixed effects, both approaches (a) and (c) give
power close to 5% for zero effect size, that is the value of correctly rejecting by
chance. However, (b) and (c) that include comparison of multiplicative models
demand larger TVset differences than Picture to reach a specific amount of power.
4From theory, there are four well-known parameters affecting power: 1) sample size (n), 2)
error type I, 3) error type II and 4) effect size, that is the change in the outcome (Y) brought
about by a change in an independent variable (X).
5Following the same rationale as was done in the simulation study for the distribution of
LRT, simulations for which optimization converged with no problems are only selected.

6.1
The Car Dataset
The car data comes from Bang & Olufsen company and consists of sensory eval-
uations on sound performance in cars.
The raw data is structured under 29
characteristics, including demographic information and general questions about
personal preferences on music and reaches a total number of observations equal
to 1620.
A short description of the variables is given in Table 6.1. In particular, 10 male
assessors of different ages rate 8 attributes with respect to the stimuli of sound
systems (continuous variables lying within (0, 1)). A sound system is determined
by the car, track and SPL attributes that appear at 6, 5 and 3 types respectively
yielding overall 90 different sound systems products. Each assessor rates every
single attribute twice, apart from assessors 1 and 6 who rate only one (n =
8 × 180 + 2 × 90), hence there are missing measurements.
In the following, the attribute associated with continuous noise is selected to
be analyzed with the extended multiplicative model. The aim is to detect possible
differences between the sound systems of the six cars.
6.1.1
Exploratory analysis
Assessors differ in their scoring pattern both in terms of average and variance
(Figure 6.1). The revealed pattern supports the presence of assessor main effect
and varying behaviour which however cannot discriminate assessors into clearly
separated categories. The high variability among participants may be attributable
both to individual differences and different use of scale.
Similarly for Car and SPL factors. Car 3 collects the highest average noise
rates, yet comprises the most unstable category with the largest variance among
all six. SPL 1 is also prone to higher levels of noise compared to the other two
which rate almost equal.
Quite on the contrary, it seems that Track cannot
separate products, while equality of variances across the five Tracks implies that
scaling effect is not that evident.

7.1.2

The multiplicative mixed model in (3.1) considers assessors as random and prod-

model changes significantly. Smith, Cullis, Brockhoff, and Thompson, 2003 sug-

product and error variance when product is a random factor. Their model can be

7.2
Conclusion
The scope of the current thesis was to consider an extension of the non linear
mixed multiplicative model with emphasis in the analysis of sensory data that are
grouped based on two factors. The main objective was to formulate the statistical
framework of the model and examine parameter estimation and hypothesis testing
from a likelihood approximation approach. Due to the lack of established theory,
simulations studies were conducted throughout the process and model behaviour
was evaluated accordingly.
Philosophy behind the model is the same to the simple one-factor case, that
is to distinguish between sources of variation in settings when products fall into
more than two categories. Implementation however, revealed additional require-
ments for the extended version that stem from the nature of the model as such.
Therefore, it was shown that the fit is better in situations where factor levels are
more than six and the number of assessors is adequate large.
The very fact that parameter estimation incorporates successive optimization
of the Laplace likelihood, release several convergence and numerical issues, which
are mostly due to the Hessian matrix that is used to compute standard errors of
model parameters. Such issues are prone to appear when variance components
lie close to the boundary of the parameter space.
In general, the model yields unbiased estimates for fixed effects. Scaling vari-
ance components however tend to be overestimated, resulting in higher uncer-
tainty when product contrasts become large.
The latter affects in turn asym-
metry of confidence intervals, which was shown to become less prominent for
moderate contrasts and more intense for smaller and larger ones. Despite asym-
metry, coverage probabilities remain adequately high even for small contrasts
(ranging between 93% −96%). This topic needs further investigation.
Likelihood Ratio Tests seem a reliable tool to test model terms as seen from
the power analysis. Tests exhibit an asymptotic χ2 behaviour with more degrees
of freedom than what is supported from theory.
