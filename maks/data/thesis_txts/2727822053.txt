Abstract
This thesis explores the inﬂuence of preprocessing and the reproducibility of functional
connectivity in resting-state rodent functional magnetic resonance imaging. Rodent fMRI is
of interest due to the wide availability of disease models and general translational applications.
For human subjects, preprocessing pipelines for extracting functional connectivity from fMRI
data are quite well-established, but that is not the case for rodents. Moreover, reproducibility
and stability of human functional connectivity are fairly well-understood but also a limitation
for rodents. Thus, in this thesis, we ﬁrst attempted to build an automated pipeline for both
rats and mice, consisting of distortion correction, motion correction, brain extraction, slice-
timing correction, registration, band-pass ﬁltering, spatial smoothing, and nuisance regression.
We found that brain extraction and normalization required manual tuning of parameters in
order to get satisfactory results. We evaluated the pipeline’s eﬀect for single-subject rat and
mouse data and multi-subject rat data. We did so by analyzing the corresponding results of
4 functional connectivity estimation methods: seed-based analysis, region-to-region analysis,
network analysis, and independent component analysis. The results showed that most steps
of the pipeline greatly impacted the resulting functional connectivity for the methods. The
eﬀect of smoothing and band-pass ﬁltering was further analyzed, where we found smoothing
to have a larger impact, as measured by functional connectivity, than band-pass ﬁltering. We
then addressed the stability and reproducibility of the functional connectivity by looking at
multi-subject data. Seed-based analysis and region-to-region analysis looked relatively stable
from visual inspection, and network analysis was also stable. The multi-subject extension
Canonical ICA was used for ICA, where its stability was analyzed quantiﬁably. We found the
stability to be lower for rodents than for humans. Ultimately, we managed to preprocess the
data, estimate functional connectivity, and analyze the stability of functional connectivity in
rodents.

Chapter 1. Introduction
Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis concerns functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), a neuroimaging technique
that measures brain function through local hemodynamic responses to neural demand. It is
measured by the blood oxygen-dependent level (BOLD) contrast and is the signal we analyze
in fMRI[1]. More speciﬁcally, we will investigate various aspects of preprocessing such data
in rodents, along with the reproducibility and stability of the resulting brain connectivity
analysis.
Resting-state is a particular mode of fMRI where the subject does not perform a task in
the scanner, allowing us to gain insight into intrinsic brain function, during development,
disorders, ageing, psychiatric conditions, etc.[2]. Resting-state fMRI (rs-fMRI) is of growing
interest, particularly within pre-clinical neuroimaging research, where animals (often rodents)
are scanned. Due to the similarity between human and rodent brains, translational research
and animal models are promising areas of inquiry. Rodent resting-state fMRI (rrs-fMRI) is
especially powerful due to the laxed ethical constraints compared to human scanning. A prime
example is transgenic animal models, which allow us to help understand the development of
brain diseases and potential treatments, which we hope to translate to clinical settings[3].
Resting-state fMRI analysis aims to determine brain connectivity[2]. It builds on the as-
sumption that spatially distant brain areas collaborate to bring about brain function. In order
to characterize brain connectivity using fMRI, we can use functional connectivity (FC), which
is the idea that two brain regions are functionally connected if there is a statistical relationship
between them. This is most often measured through correlation.
In order to model functional connectivity (whether in humans or rodents), it is necessary
to preprocess the data from the scanner since the BOLD signal is generally disturbed by con-
founds and artefacts that can lead to spurious correlation and hence false positives[1]. Motion
of the subject is one such problem, which we correct by using motion correction and nuisance
regression of motion parameters. Physiological noise (e.g., heartbeat and respiration) is an-

other problem we can take care of with nuisance regression. There are also inhomogeneities in
the scanner’s magnetic ﬁeld, which we try to correct with distortion correction. We also need
to account for the fact that brain slices from the scanner are not acquired simultaneously.
This is taken care of using slice-timing correction. The brain slices often include non-brain
matter, which the brain extraction method removes. We also seek to improve the signal-to-
noise ratio using spatial smoothing. Often, one also wishes to scan several subjects in a study
and generalize across them. In order to do so, we bring the scans from the individual subjects
into a common space, which is referred to as spatial normalization. A complete treatment
of the details of the preprocessing steps is outside the scope of the current work, but further
information can be found in Poldrack, Mumford, and Nichols[1].
For human subjects, preprocessing pipelines carrying out these corrections are reason-
ably well-established and standardized[4]. However, those pipelines are not suited for rodent
data[5], where consensus and standardization of pipelines have yet to be developed[6]. The lack
of consensus means that the pipelines vary signiﬁcantly from lab to lab and even from project
to project[7][3]. Thus, analysis and reproducibility of connectivity are much less established
for rodents. These are some of the issues that this thesis speciﬁcally aims to address.
The ﬁrst aim is to attempt to build an automated preprocessing pipeline for rodents. We
will investigate if it is possible to harmonize the pipeline across mice and rats. Once we have
built the pipeline, functional connectivity will be estimated and analyzed. The third aim is
to investigate how preprocessing choices aﬀect rodent functional connectivity. The fourth and
ﬁnal aim is to investigate the stability of the functional connectivity estimation methods when
applied to rodents. The goal is to inch closer to standardization and reproducibility of fMRI
analysis in rodents, which is much more established for humans.

1.1
Research Questions
We can put the scope of the thesis into a more concrete form by formulating research questions:
1. Is building an automated preprocessing pipeline to analyze resting-state fMRI data in
rodents feasible?
2. Can pipelines be harmonized across mice and rats, or are speciﬁc adaptations needed
according to the species?
3. How do preprocessing choices inﬂuence functional connectivity for rodents?
4. How stable are the functional connectivity estimation methods for rodents?
We will investigate the ﬁrst research question in the single- and multi-subject experiments in
section 6 and 7, respectively. Research questions 3 and 4 relate to the stability of functional
connectivity. In order to investigate these questions, we will use multi-subject data, which
will therefore be investigated in section 7.

Chapter 2. Literature Review
Chapter 2
Literature Review
The ﬁrst step of this thesis is to build a preprocessing pipeline for rodent resting-state fMRI
(rrsfMRI). In the rodent fMRI ﬁeld, a signiﬁcant problem[8] is the lack of a standardized
and robust pipeline akin to the fMRIprep pipeline for humans [4]. Therefore, we conduct a
literature review of the various pipelines that others have employed in this area. The review
aims to determine similarities and diﬀerences between pipelines, which we can use to create
our own. We will also highlight some general points they make for preprocessing and rodent
fMRI that will be useful for our experiments and results. There are, of course, a vast number
of pipelines in the literature, but one of the aims here was to look at papers that explained
their pipeline in detail.
The paper by Grandjean et al.[8] is an excellent place to start. Their paper investigates
whether networks identiﬁed by mouse rrs-fMRI are stable across centers. As mentioned, pre-
processing pipelines are not standardized, but the same problem is also present for equipment,
scan conditions, sedation protocols, etc. They build one pipeline (see Fig. 2.1), which they
then use on the data from all the diﬀerent centers, which have diﬀerent setups. They con-
clude that, despite cross-center diﬀerences, they identify reproducible resting state networks
(RSNs). Crucially, for our thesis, it suggests that it, to some extent, is possible to build a
standardized pipeline. They also emphasize that a clear limitation of their paper is that they
only experiment with one preprocessing pipeline. In another paper by Grandjean et al.[9],
they argue that speciﬁc preprocessing steps might favor speciﬁc protocols and acquisitions.
This suggests that a standardized pipeline might be challenging. Another limitation they
mention in [8] is that there is a lack of consensus on how to determine the quality of FC. In
part, we hope to shed some light on this with the stability analysis later.

Another paper that describes their pipeline in detail and justiﬁes its steps is Bajic et al.[7].
The paper also has a detailed description of how to think about and apply ICA for rodent
fMRI. They base their pipeline on FMRIB Software Library (FSL)[10], where a particular
trick is worth highlighting. FSL is optimized for human-sized brains, and they argue that it
makes sense to scale the rodent brains to human size. Another general preprocessing point
they make is that since resting-state FC is determined from correlation, preprocessing is even
more critical since artifacts like motion will impact the correlation. If we do not account
for that, there will be a lot of false positives, which inﬂates the correlation. Simply put,
correlation can be due to motion (or another artifact/nuisance regressor). The paper also has
some good suggestions on debugging preprocessing steps, as this is generally not trivial.
Figure 2.2: Pipeline used by Bajic et al.[7]
The paper by Pan W. et al. [3] highlights both protocols and preprocessing steps. They
also aﬃrm that preprocessing is generally far less standardized for rodents than for humans.
Another critical point is that rrs-fMRI can suﬀer from false positives, as argued in [7]. For
preprocessing in general, they say: "fMRI preprocessing involves a ﬁne balance of keeping the

Pan et al. [12] goes over some general considerations one should make when creating rodent
fMRI preprocessing pipelines. They also highlight some of the diﬀerences between rodent and
human pipelines. For motion correction, they mention that the rodent is often restrained in
a stereotaxic device. They argue that this, along with anesthetics, means motion correction
is less important[12]. They agree with [3] that MC should not be done blindly due to the
potential introduction of artefactual correlation. Further, they argue that MC realignment
is harder for scans that cover only part of the brain, implying that that is more often the
case for rodents than for humans.
They generally recommend slice-timing correction but
point out that it can propagate issues from one slice to the rest, so one should be careful.
For segmentation, they mention that it is not as easy for rodents since there is a much
lower amount of white matter, which will usually be the diﬀerentiator for the segmentation.
Regarding registration, they argue that using templates is less valuable than for humans
since anatomical variability is smaller among rodents. Instead, one could create a within-
experiment, average brain that can be used as a substitute template. For ﬁltering, Lee et
al.[13], among others, show that higher frequencies of BOLD ﬂuctuations have signiﬁcant
contributions to functional connectivity. Many of the other pipelines in this literature review

It is also generally common to do distortion correction when preprocessing fMRI data in
humans[15]. Hong et al.[16] showed that the method can be eﬀective for rodents.
Brain extraction is also a common preprocessing step. We can see that this is included in
most of the pipelines in this literature review. However, an issue is that most pipelines use
manual delineation to extract the brains. For humans, automatic tools[17] exist, making an
otherwise long and tedious process very easy. Since the tools are optimized for human brains,
they seldom work to a satisfactory level for rodents[18].
Another essential aspect of fMRI preprocessing is nuisance regression. The use of nuisance
regression in rodent fMRI is investigated in the paper by Chuang K. et al. [19]. First and
foremost, they conclude that nuisance regression methods for humans might not be suitable
for rodents. They caution that some of the regular nuisance regression methods are at best
ineﬀective and at worst introduce false positives and lower the sensitivity. They also ﬁnd
that using the global signal might increase the speciﬁcity but at the cost of decreasing the
sensitivity, so one should be careful. They propose using the signal from non-brain tissue and
motion regressors as a possible alternative method of doing nuisance regression in rodents.
The paper also explains the general preprocessing pipeline they use, which can be seen in
Fig. 2.5. They also scale the rodent brains to roughly human size to make the tools perform
better. We will elaborate on this in section 4.

3.1
Nuisance Regression
Nuisance regression is the process of removing confounding eﬀects like the global signal and
motion-related artifacts.
The procedure aims to increase the signal-to-noise (SNR) ratio.
A matrix of confounders is created, where each column is a confounder, and each row is a
timestep corresponding to a volume in the functional data. We then ﬁt a linear model for
each regressor, resulting in as many regression coeﬃcients. Each coeﬃcient then represents
the contribution of that nuisance regressor to the fMRI time-series.
Formally, we apply a QR decomposition to the confounder matrix, C:

3.4
Independent Component Analysis
Independent component analysis (or simply ICA) is another way to characterize functional
connectivity[2].
Whereas seed-based analysis is a hypothesis-driven method, ICA is data-
driven. ICA aims to decompose the signal into its internal/latent representation. The method
assumes that the underlying sources of the signal are non-Gaussian, independent, and that the
underlying independent components mix linearly. The main objective will then be to decide
on a measure of non-Gaussianity and ﬁnd a way to maximize it. Formally, the ICA model is
deﬁned as:
B = MA
(3.12)
where rows of B are the mixed/observed signals, and the rows of A are the source/latent
signals/components we seek to ﬁnd. M is the mixing matrix that combines the components
A into the observations B. Since both A and M are unknown, additional assumptions are
needed to identify the components, in this case that rows of A are independent and non-
Gaussian. The implementation we will use in this thesis is based on ﬁxed-point interations
[29], also referred to as fastICA. It uses a ﬁxed-point iteration scheme that maximizes an
approximation of the negative entropy. Most implementations also prewhiten, resulting in the
new mixing matrix being orthogonal. This, in turn, means that the number of parameters to
be estimated by ICA is reduced, as the only thing left after prewhitening is the rotation[30].
In the context of fMRI, the ﬁrst step is to reshape the data into a 2D matrix such that each
column corresponds to the time course of one voxel, which will then be our mixed signals,
B. The mixing matrix, M, will then consist of the time courses for the components, and our
latent/desired signal, A, will be the spatial maps. In practice, however, one often applies
ICA to a subset of the data due to noise. PCA is the most common choice to carry out the
data-reduction, and ICA is then used on the components determined by PCA. We will use
the fastICA implementation from Nilearn[31], which does precisely this. Fig. 3.2 shows the
reshaping and decomposition.

Canonical ICA
In order to do multi-subject ICA, we will use Canonical ICA (CanICA)[33]. It is a generative
model that views the subject-speciﬁc observations as a mixture of global (inter-subject) inde-

Building Pipeline
Based on the literature review, we will now build our pipeline. In terms of software choice,
there are a few considerations to make. Firstly, the tool needs to be open-source. Secondly,
the tool must be widely used, making the pipeline easier to build since examples, tutorials,
and documentation will be more readily available. Thirdly, the pipeline is easier to build if
the tools support a command line interface (CLI) instead of just a graphical user interface
(GUI). As mentioned in section 1, one of the motivating factors of the pipeline is to build an
automated solution, which can be a considerable challenge, and a CLI might make development
faster. FSL[10] is a tool that meets these criteria and will, therefore, be our pipeline’s basis.
A CLI-based approach (instead of a GUI) also enables the pipeline to integrate tools from
other packages if desired. Currently, no such tool has, at the time of writing, to the best of
our knowledge, been fully developed.

4.1.1
BIDS Format
Part 1 of this step is to convert the data to BIDS format[36], which stands for Brain Imaging
Data Structure. As the name suggests, the aim is to store the data in a systematized and
standardized way, making collaboration easier and faster. BIDS speciﬁes that images must be
in the NIFTI format[37]. The data used in this project was originally in DICOM format[38], so
conversion is necessary. A tool such as dcm2niix[39] can be used. BIDS speciﬁes a particular
folder structure, which is most often created manually. Semi-automatic alternatives exist[40],
but using them requires that the user creates conﬁguration ﬁles, which makes the process just
as cumbersome. Thus, this part requires some manual eﬀort.
4.1.2

The second part is to ensure that the orientation of the images is correct. The pragmatic way
to do this is to import the images in a viewing tool such as Freeview or FSLeyes. Here, it will

The qform matrix (which transforms voxel coordinates to real-world/anatomical coordinates)

4.1.3
Resizing
As argued by [7], resizing rodent brains to human size can be beneﬁcial when using FSL.
In the FSL documentation[10], they conﬁrm that quite a few parameters are ﬁxed in the
implementation. Some of these are crucial for our uses. For example, in MCFLIRT, the param-
eter current_scale determines the scaling factor applied to the reference volume during the
diﬀerent correction stages. It determines the voxel size in the resampled reference volume.
The parameter is set to 8mm in stage 1, then 4mm in stage 2, which would not make sense
for rodents’ brains where the voxels are often of size 0.1 to 0.3mm. It would mean that the
reference volume is much larger than the volume we seek to correct, leading to poor results.
Another problematic set of parameters in mcflirt are the search tolerances, which are also
hard-coded into the implementation and deﬁned in terms of millimeters. It is set to 0.8mm. If
the rodent brain was not resized, the tolerance of 0.8mm would be too slack, resulting in the
algorithm stopping before motion had been adequately corrected. Similar arguments hold for
the registration tool flirt. Consequently, we scale the rodent brains to human size. However,
when results are shown, it will be done in the original brain size for two reasons. Firstly, this
is more natural as it represents the actual anatomical size of the brain. Secondly, it makes it
easier to compare with other studies.
4.2
Distortion Correction
We have now arrived at the "actual" preprocessing methods. The ﬁrst step is to correct for
inhomogeneities in the main magnetic ﬁeld of the scanner, which is called the B0 ﬁeld. Inho-
mogeneity of the B0 ﬁeld mainly arises from tissue-air boundaries, which cause disturbance
of the magnetic ﬁeld due to the diﬀerence in magnetic susceptibility. The inhomogeneities
result in spatial distortions in the functional data due to the long read-out of the echo-planar
imaging technique. We can then use a (B0) ﬁeld map provided by the scanner to somewhat
correct for it[1]. There is often noise in the ﬁeld map, so it is common to regularize it ﬁrst,
as it would otherwise introduce noise to the functional data. This could, for example, be
Gaussian smoothing. We will implement distortion correction using the FSL tool FUGUE.

4.3
Motion Correction
The next step is to correct for motion, which is mostly agreed upon in the literature, but
even in humans, there is no consensus on how to go about it exactly[41]. Due to constraints
on time for the project and lack of expert knowledge on our end, we aim to do this entirely
automatically, as opposed to doing manual inspection as proposed in [3]. Instead, we will
use the FSL tool MCFLIRT[21]. We compute the mean volume of the functional data and
then register to that volume using a rigid body 6-parameter aﬃne transformation with the
normalized correlation cost function. This follows the suggestions of Bajic et al.[7]. The mean
volume will also be the basis for later registration to structural and template data.
Additionally, the motion correction gives us the motion transformation parameters, which
record how each volume was transformed to the reference volume, here the mean volume. The
parameters are translation and rotation in x, y, and z. These are used for nuisance regression
(see section 4.9). They are also used internally in MCFLIRT to compute absolute displacement.
We also use them to compute the framewise displacement.
Lastly, motion scrubbing is another method one can apply to reduce motion, but it is
outside the scope of this thesis. Scrubbing refers to completely removing the eﬀect of volumes
displaying too high motion levels. This can either be done manually or automatically through
some heuristic. The omission of the step is discussed further in section 8.

Discussion
We will now discuss the results of the experiments. The discussion is structured in terms of

8.1
Feasibility of an automated preprocessing pipeline for
rodent resting-state fMRI
This section will discuss the various aspects of building an automated preprocessing pipeline
for rodents. The critical point is that it was not possible to automate the pipeline with the
tools used here fully. The primary factor is the registration from structural to template space.
That became evident for the multi-subject rat data. There, the parameters of FSL flirt[10]
had to be manually chosen for each subject to attain satisfactory registration. It meant altering
the parameters and then visually inspecting the results. Speciﬁcally, the degrees of freedom
had to be varied. Another issue was whether or not to do brain extraction for normalization,
which will be discussed below. This might indicate that FSL flirt is unsuitable for rodent
data registration/normalization. Of course, this is limited to one dataset: in another dataset,
we cannot be sure if flirt might work perfectly well. As a point of future work, it might
be interesting to experiment with other registration tools like, for example, AFNI[52] or even
build a rodent-speciﬁc registration tool.
Another issue was that when using flirt for registration from anatomical to template, it
was sometimes necessary to do brain extraction. However, it was impossible to conﬁgure the
parameters of FSL BET so that all non-brain matter was removed, and it was necessary to
choose the parameters for each brain manually. Speciﬁcally, the fractional intensity threshold
had to be varied. For this reason, several papers do the extraction manually[7][18]. This is thus
an area where it is interesting to experiment with other tools. In particular, tools designed with
the rodent brain’s shape (and size) in mind (pun intended) will be of interest. Importantly,
it is worth noting that brain extraction sometimes led to worse registration of anatomical
to template space. This further shows that the FSL toolbox has clear limitations for rodent
67 of 90
==PAGE:75==

data. Currently, the development of a rodent extension of fmriprep, fmriprep-rodents[5],
has started. However, at the time of writing, the tool is far from developed, and judging from
the GitHub repository commit history, one might suspect that development has stopped[5].
Another point is that it is not easy to quantify if a preprocessing pipeline is successful
or not systematically. As all levels of analysis show, one cannot simply conclude that more
correlation is better, and it can thus not be a measure of success. That is also why the section
visually inspecting the results from each step (section 6.1) was included. One might be able
to build an automated pipeline, but it will probably still be necessary to inspect the results
visually and compare them with the literature.
8.1.1
Scrubbing
The pipeline could be improved by using automated scrubbing, i.e., eliminating the eﬀect of
volumes that exceed some particular bound on motion, e.g., the frame-wise displacement[7].
Bajic et al.[7] additionally suggest that entire subjects should be excluded if they show too
high levels of motion, which will introduce spurious correlation throughout the whole time-
series. Excluding subjects with an average frame-wise displacement over a certain threshold
could automate this. There are, however, both pros and cons of the method. The method’s
beneﬁt is that volumes with too much motion to be corrected with MC (as described above)
are removed to ensure that they do not contaminate the rest of the time-series and, thus,
ultimately, the results. One of the method’s cons is introducing bias since data is removed.
The loss of data itself is a con. It also disrupts the temporal continuity of the time-series, but
that is something we can take care of. Lastly, there is a lack of consensus and standardization
of scrubbing[53]. If time had permitted, it would have been interesting to look at the eﬀects
of automated scrubbing heuristics for rodents, for example investigating the eﬀect of varying
the threshold used to exclude volumes. Experimenting with other, perhaps more advanced,
heuristics/rules is also possible.
8.1.2
"Perfect" registration
The idea of "perfect" registration has been referenced several times throughout this thesis. It
is important to elaborate on what perfect means and whether it is desirable. For rodents, as
for humans, there is anatomical variability[54]. It is unclear to which extent these variabilities
should be smoothed out in normalization, as it can introduce bias. This point becomes more
critical when the registration becomes more complex, i.e., it has more degrees of freedom. For
translation (and perhaps rotation), distances between points are preserved, and the brain’s
68 of 90
==PAGE:76==

anatomy is not fundamentally altered. In the complex case, like a non-linear transformation,
the individual rodent brain might be registered very acurately to the template, but the brain
might be altered in undesireable ways.
8.2
Harmonization of preprocessing pipelines across mice
and rats
The discussion of harmonization across mice and rats should be prefaced by acknowledging the
data limitation: we only have access to one mouse. The multi-subject analysis showed large
between-subject diﬀerences in the resulting analysis, at least for rats. The literature suggests
that this is also the case for mice[8], which means that we should be careful making any
conclusions about mice. In particular, the spatial distortions seen in the mouse’s functional
scan further hinder our ability to use the data to make general remarks.
As discussed above, it was not possible to automate the pipeline even for just rats, making
harmonization across species impossible to know about. A suﬃcient brain extraction and
normalization tool that works well for rats might be extendable to mice, but we can not be
sure.
The steps carrying out slice-timing correction, motion correction, band-pass ﬁltering, and
spatial smoothing worked relatively well across species, as well as registration from functional
to anatomical.
Generally, the functional and structural data analyzed in this thesis were
almost aligned beforehand, perhaps making the step easier. The question remains whether
brain extraction and normalization methods can be harmonized across mice and rats if one
ﬁnds an automated method that works for at least one species.
Another general point to consider for harmonization is anesthesia, which is still not a settled
issue[14]. If too high doses are used, or scanning happens shortly after injection, we risk
observing no signal. We encountered this in our preliminary analyses of the single-subject rat
from DRCMR. One obvious option is to use no anesthetic and instead completely physically
fasten the subject, as was done for the mouse, which is of growing interest in the literature[55].
The mouse’s motion was comparable to the rat’s, indicating that scanning awake rodents is
possible.

8.2.1
Stripes in single-subject mouse ICA
For the mouse data, stripes were observed for the ICA analysis, which makes generalization
across species even harder. It is diﬃcult to determine what the reason for the issue might be.
One possibility is that the problem comes from the scanner, for example due to instability.
There were also issues with overheating for the scanner used for the mouse. It is also possible
that it is due to physiological noise. It is not immediately recoverable, as the preprocessing
did not alleviate the problem.
8.3
Inﬂuence of preprocessing choices
The ﬁrst part of investigating the inﬂuence of preprocessing choices was by looking at the
single-subject mouse and rat data. It was done by using 4 progressive levels of preprocessing.
First and foremost, we found that all levels of preprocessing had a large eﬀect on both the rat
and mouse for most methods.
We saw that scenario 2 greatly aﬀected both the mouse and the rat. The visual inspection of
the preprocessing steps (section 6.1) showed that there was much motion, so this makes sense.
In general, slice-timing correction is not expected to have a large eﬀect when repetition times
are low[1]. Distortion correction (for the rat only) mostly aﬀected the anterior part of the
brain, which is not where most of the signal was located. In general, scenario 2 decreased the
correlation since a lot of it was probably motion. Large amounts of the removed correlation
were located at the brain’s edges.
Adding band-pass ﬁltering in scenario 3 generally increased the correlation compared to
scenario 2 in the single-subject analysis. Ideally, this could be because band-pass ﬁltering
removes non-neuronal signal. We initially just used a band-pass ﬁlter of 0.01-0.1Hz, since
Bajic et al.[7] showed that rodent and human resting-state frequencies are similar, and that
is the range commonly used for humans[1][4]. However, Boubela et al.[50] argued that neural
signal is present at higher frequencies.
Hence, we experimented with varying the low-pass value in the band-pass ﬁlter when we
looked at the multi-subject data. In order to evaluate the results quantiﬁably, we looked at
the corresponding network metrics. What we found in section 7.3.3 was that diﬀerences are
generally not that large, which in part might be explained by the fact that thresholding of
the R2R correlation matrix to get the network was done at the 90th percentile. However, we
70 of 90
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 8. Discussion
still did see some diﬀerences. The clustering coeﬃcient decreased when the band-pass range
was increased (i.e., the allowed range of frequencies is e.g. increased from 0.0-0.1Hz to 0.01-
0.2Hz). Since the clustering coeﬃcient measures the level of local connectedness, the results
suggest that including a wider range of frequencies reduces that local connectedness. It is vital
to remember that the results are only based on 10 subjects. For the clustering coeﬃcient,
the inter-quartile range for one band-pass ﬁlter covers the median for another (see Fig. 7.8).
This indicates that the diﬀerences in (median) clustering coeﬃcient are small relative to the
uncertainty. We also looked at how the global eﬃciency varied with the band-pass ﬁlter.
Including a band-pass ﬁlter led to a slightly higher global eﬃciency, but increasing the range
of the ﬁlter did not further increase the metric. The same was observed for local eﬃciency.
Generally, the results point towards band-pass ﬁltering having a small but noticeable eﬀect
on functional connectivity when network analysis models the brain. For future work within
this particular part, it would be interesting to analyze diﬀerent graph thresholds and observe
how that inﬂuences functional connectivity.
In the last scenario, we added spatial smoothing. The single-subject results show what we
would expect: smoothing leads to more correlation, which is not that insightful. The exciting
question is what happens when the level of smoothing is varied. That was investigated by
once again evaluating how the level of smoothing inﬂuenced the network metrics for the multi-
subject data. One could also have done this for SBA or R2R, but we would simply observe that
the correlation to the seed would be smoothed out more, which is, again, not that interesting.
We did not see enormous changes in the network metrics when varying the smoothing. The
clustering coeﬃcient initially decreased when smoothing was applied and then increased for
high levels of smoothing (2.0mm), but the changes were small. For global eﬃciency, we saw
larger diﬀerences, which decreased as the smoothing increased. Part of the explanation might
be that increased smoothing means that more regions are connected, and as a result, a higher
number of edges need to be traversed to get from spatially distant nodes. Another part of
the explanation might be that when more smoothing is applied, the threshold still can only
keep connections that exceed the 90th percentile. Since smoothing leads to higher correlation,
the correlations that the threshold removes become rather arbitrary, potentially making it
even harder to travel between two nodes. For local eﬃciency, the values become higher for
the same reason that the clustering coeﬃcient becomes higher. Smoothing essentially just
increases local connectivity by blurring regions/nodes together.
Generally, the results point towards smoothing having a larger eﬀect on functional connec-
tivity (as measured by network analysis) than band-pass ﬁltering.

To further look at the eﬀects of preprocessing, we also looked at diﬀerent nuisance regression
tests for the single-subject rat and mouse data. We found the global signal to be the most
dominant regressor for both species. Adding motion-related parameters (here measured by
DVARS) or the CSF signal made little additional diﬀerence and had a modest impact on their
own. This was the case for the SBA and R2R results. The network metrics generally changed
very little with the diﬀerent tests. In general, we have seen that preprocessing choices did not
have as large an eﬀect on network analysis as measured by the metrics. Here, it is important
to stress that global network metrics, by nature, are reductionist as they represent the entire
brain by one value, averaging out a lot of potentially useful information.
For ICA, preprocessing did not have as noticeable an impact on the results in the single-
subject analysis. If we consider e.g. motion, this might partly be explained by the fact that
motion-related eﬀects will ideally be captured in its own component (or several components).
Thus, a component that might be related to the DMN will be less corrupted by motion when
no motion correction is done compared to e.g. SBA. However, we did see that a component
looking like the DMN became more visible for the rat after preprocessing. The components
were generally also quite noisy, even after preprocessing.
8.4
Stability of estimation methods
We will now discuss the last research question. It is worth noting that the stability of the
functional connectivity that came from the 4 estimation methods (SBA, R2R, network, ICA)
was analyzed at various levels of depth.
8.4.1
Seed-based analysis
For SBA, we ﬁrst visually inspected the subject-level results of the multicenter data. We
saw that there is much between-subject variation. That can partly be explained by poor
registration. For the analysis, we chose a seed in template space. Since registration was not
equally successful, the seed coordinates will refer to slightly diﬀerent places in the functional
data.
This is somewhat alleviated using a seed region instead of just a seed.
Naturally,
we can not rule out that we would still see a lot of diﬀerence between subjects if we had
perfect registration. The "true" underlying signal may be simply that diﬀerent, which is a
general issue with the interpretation of fMRI results. However, we still found that several
subjects showed a similar correlation structure, which was captured nicely when taking the
average of the brains. The standard deviation of the SBAs showed a lot of diﬀerence between

==PAGE:80==
Chapter 8. Discussion
the subjects. One could analyze the SBA results further by computing how correlated the
individual SBA was to the average SBA. We suspect from visual inspection that a few outlier
subjects drive the standard deviation.
8.4.2
Region-to-region analysis
In the R2R analysis, there was also a lot of between-subject variability in intensity at the
individual-level analysis, but the structure of the correlation matrices was quite similar. This
structure was then captured when the individual matrices were averaged to get a group-level
correlation matrix. The standard deviation matrix shows that there was a lot of variability.
One of the method’s limitations is, again, the registration quality. If registration to the
template is poor, regions deﬁned by the atlas will not overlap properly with the functional
data. Also, in our case, the templates, particularly the SIGMA template used for the rats,
were very granular, dividing the brain into roughly 250 regions. Some of the regions of the
atlas are then very small, which ampliﬁes the problems due to poor registration. One way to
take care of the problem is by manually changing the granularity of the atlas. That is what
we did in the single-subject rat DMN analysis. It is a time-consuming procedure, and a more
appropriate solution might be to ﬁnd another template.
In the multi-subject results section, the focus was not on SBA and R2R, as the network
analysis and ICA results were more interesting as there was a more quantiﬁable way to char-
acterize stability.
8.4.3
Network analysis
For the network analysis, it was more convenient to dive deeper into stability-related questions
as the method enables us to represent a brain with one value, which also has its downsides.
Such a metric naturally abstracts away information. Also, the extent to which the metrics
have a biological interpretation is still being determined. For example, global eﬃciency is said
to measure the eﬃciency of the ﬂow of information in the brain (at a global level)[28]. It is
unclear if this meaningfully translates to the neuronal ﬂow of information, additionally since
the BOLD signal itself is an imperfect measure of neuronal activity[1].
From the boxplots for the diﬀerent tests for the network metrics, we generally see that the
interquartile range, which is a proxy for uncertainty, was relatively low. At a glance, this
could also just suggest that the between-subject variation is small. However, if we assume

that the between-subject variability is small, then the uncertainty could still be large if the
method itself was unstable. Since we see small levels of variation/uncertainty, the method is
relatively stable.
8.4.4
Canonical ICA
When CanICA is applied, the results are much less noisy, analogous to what we observed for
SBA and R2R. Components that might be suspected to be true RSNs emerge. To some extent,
this indicates that in order to use ICA to identify networks of interest correctly, multi-subject
data is likely required.
In the stability analysis for the method, the subspace stability for the experiment variyng
the number of components for the rodents suggested that the optimal number of components
was between 10 and 16. Interestingly, this is also what visual inspection of CanICA compo-
nents suggested. At some level, this indicates that the subspace stability tells us something
meaningful. For the multi-subject human data, the subspace stability vs. number of compo-
nents could be argued to suggest that roughly 40 components were optimal. In the CanICA
paper[33], they ﬁnd 42 components optimal for their resting-state analysis using methods
other than varying the number of components and looking at the subspace stability. Thus, it
is tempting to conclude that the subspace stability vs. number of components plot can assist
in choosing the number of components. However, it is more complex than that, which will be
explained later in this section.
The subspace stability vs.
smoothing plots were not as interesting.
They showed that
increasing the smoothing leads to higher subspace stability, which makes sense. The interesting
part, however, was that the subspace stability improvement when increasing the smoothing
was not linear.
For low levels of smoothing, an increase leads to a large improvement in
stability, but when the smoothing levels get higher, the improvement diminishes slowly. The
largest improvements are seen around 6mm for humans, which is also what is commonly
used[56]. For the rodent data, the improvement does not diminish as fast.
When comparing to the results from Varoquaux et al.[33], they get a subspace stability of
0.71, while we get 0.55 for rodents (using 16 components and smoothing of 0.6mm), while our
human data analysis gave 0.65 (with 40 components and smoothing of 6mm). It indicates
that the rodent components are less stable than the human components. The smoothing for
the rat data would have to be increased to roughly 1.8mm to get a similar subspace stability

8.5
Interpretation of Canonical ICA
As argued in the CanICA paper by Varoquaux et al.[33], CanICA should be interpreted with
care and has limitations. One of the model’s assumptions is that the images are spatially
aligned, i.e.
registered properly to the template.
As we have argued, this is one of the
limitations of the pipeline in this thesis, and they mention that this is problematic for the
model. Along the same lines, the model assumes that the brains are anatomically alike. This
limitation might be less severe for rodents who are generally less anatomically diverse[54].
However, this is mitigated (in fMRI modeling in general) by including spatial smoothing.
Another limitation of the model and the stability analysis, they argue, is that it does not
distinguish between artefactual and "true" signal. An artefactual component is, of course,
not necessarily a negative thing, as it can be used for regression, but it is important to keep it
in mind when interpreting the stability results (here, as measured by subspace stability, e). A
natural extension for the algorithm might be to have a mechanism that automatically detects
if a component consists of artefactual signal, and then regresses it out.
Another observation for CanICA, as implemented in Nilearn[31], is that fastICA, which
is the work-horse of the method, did not always converge. We tried altering the number of
maximum iterations and tolerances of fastICA, but it did not make a diﬀerence. This might
also partly explain why the subspace stability was low relative to the results in [33]. In general,
there is no paper using the stability analysis methodology of the paper on rodents, so it is
impossible to make direct comparisons with the literature.
The results for CanICA also heavily depend on the way thresholding is done. Varoquaux et
al. threshold diﬀerently from the Nilearn implementation[31], even though they migrated their
original implementation to Nilearn themselves. Therefore, we analyzed the non-thresholded
results, which allowed comparison with the paper.
Despite the stability analysis method’s limitations, it oﬀers value and is a framework worth
applying in multi-subject rodent fMRI ICA in general.

8.6
Future Work
We have now discussed the results and their relation to the research questions. It now makes
sense to discuss possible avenues for future work within the general research area of prepro-
cessing and reproducibility of rodent resting-state fMRI. For preprocessing, it would make
sense to look into building rodent-speciﬁc preprocessing tools. It would be particularly useful
for tools that did obviously not work properly, like brain extraction and registration from
structural to template. For brain extraction, a possible avenue is to investigate deep-learning-
based methods, for example the method proposed by Pontes-Filho et al.[57]. For many of the
FSL tools, we showed (in section 4) that many of the tools have parameters hard-coded into
the implementation. A starting point for these algorithms would be to expose these variables
to the user and see if they can be optimized for rodents. Building registration tools from
scratch is naturally also a possibility. For [5], they are building a rodent pipeline, but the
actual tools of the pipeline are mostly just the tools optimized for human fMRI data. Thus,
a general suggestion is to focus more on rodent-speciﬁc preprocessing methods.
Another remark is that templates in their current form are limited compared to humans.
For human data, functional templates based on clustering can improve the reproducibility and
accuracy of results[58][59].
Additionally, CanICA and the corresponding stability analysis is a promising framework
with many applications. One such application could be to compare the stability of sick and
healthy rodents.

Chapter 9. Conclusion
Chapter 9
Conclusion
In this thesis, we characterized functional connectivity in rodents using functional MRI. We
will now make conclusions about the research questions deﬁned in section 1.1.
Is it feasible to build an automated preprocessing pipeline for analysis of resting-state fmri data
in rodents?
Building a fully automated pipeline with the tools used in this thesis was impossible. The
brain extraction and registration from structural to template space required manually changing
the parameters to suit the individual subjects. With the manual intervention, the data was
preprocessed to a satisfactory level, as evaluated by visual inspection of the diﬀerent steps
and resulting FC results.
Can pipelines be harmonized across mice and rats or are speciﬁc adaptations needed according
to the species?
It follows from the conclusion of research question 1 that this was impossible in this thesis.
It is possible that pipelines can be automated with diﬀerent tools for brain extraction and
registration from structural to template space. Due to between-subject variation, a multi-
subject mouse dataset would also be necessary to answer the question properly.
How do preprocessing choices inﬂuence functional connectivity for rodents?
For the single-subject analyses, we found that each of the 4 scenarios generally had a large
impact on the FC for both the mouse and rat for SBA and R2R. We found that the diﬀerences
were not as stark for ICA and network analysis (to the extent that we can compare between
methods). Applying slice-timing correction and motion correction generally removed a lot of
correlation. Band-pass ﬁltering increased correlation and smoothing naturally increased FC.
For the nuisance regression, we found that the global signal made the biggest diﬀerence for
the FC and that nuisance regression generally does not heavily inﬂuence the network analysis
results, which might be due to the thresholding.
The multi-subject results before and after preprocessing lead to mostly the same conclusions
77 of 90
==PAGE:85==
Chapter 9. Conclusion
as the single-subject analysis. We varied the smoothing and band-pass ﬁlter for the network
analysis. For the smoothing, we found that more smoothing leads to lower global eﬃciency
while it leads to higher local eﬃciency and a higher clustering coeﬃcient. For the band-pass
ﬁlter results, we found that the clustering coeﬃcient decreases when the band-pass ﬁlter range
is increased. The uncertainty across subjects was decreased for the global eﬃciency when the
ﬁlter was applied. The local eﬃciency increased. In general, despite ﬁnding diﬀerences, they
were relatively small.
How stable are the functional connectivity estimation methods for rodents?
SBA results highly depend on good registration when the seed is in atlas space. Suboptimal
registration leads to unstable SBA results at the individual level. However, by taking the mean,
the group-level analysis smoothes out a lot of the variability. Additionally, small changes in
seed coordinates change the results a lot.
For R2R, there were large diﬀerences in correlation intensity, but the correlation structure
mostly remained across subjects. If registration is suboptimal, one should be careful using a
very granular atlas.
In the network analysis, the global metrics generally showed relatively stable behavior. The
between-subject variation of the metrics was quite low, and the diﬀerences when varying the
preprocessing parameters, as mentioned earlier, were also modest.
For CanICA, we could quantify the stability using the subspace stability, e. We found that
rodent ICA components (e = 0.55) are less stable than human components (e = 0.65 in our
analysis, and e = 0.71 in the analysis by Varoquaux et al.[33]). However, we found that the
stability heavily depends on the number of components and the smoothing. Lastly, we saw
that the plot of the stability vs. number of components can indicate the optimal number of
components.
Ultimately, it was possible to preprocess, albeit not automatically, the resting-state rodent
fMRI data, and we could analyze the functional connectivity and its stability and reproducibil-
ity.
Suggestions and future work
The preprocessing tools used in this thesis were unstable for rodents, and an avenue for future
work is to develop and apply rodent-speciﬁc tools that work out of the box. One such tool
78 of 90
==PAGE:86==
Chapter 9. Conclusion
could be the deep learning-based brain extraction tool from Pontes-Filho et al.[57]. As the
amount of pre-clinical fMRI data grows, such tools are of increasing potenial and interest.
We also observed that preprocessing choices lead to vast diﬀerences in the resulting functional
connectivity, and it is thus important to do the step with care. We suggest generally using the
stability framework built around CanICA for reproducibility, as it allows for quantiﬁcation
and comparison between studies. A particular use case could be to compare the stability of
sick and healthy rodents. Developing clustering-based functional templates for rodents could
also be of interest, as has been done for human data[58][59].
