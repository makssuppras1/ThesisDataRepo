Abstract
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has become a serious threat to the health of humans
and animals worldwide. It has already been declared from the World Health Organi-
zation (WHO) in 2014, the existence of this problem and the necessity to interfere [1].
Furthermore, to date, the major problem is antibacterial resistance which results in
ineﬀective medications and persistence of infections in the body, due to the high rates
of resistance observed in bacteria [2]. The lack of new strategies lead to look for new
targets in microorganisms, such as targeting bacterial central metabolism [5]. In the face
of these issues, there is an urgent need to investigate more on metabolic interactions
between bacteria and the host cells, since they are still poorly understood. Cell culture
are an essential tool to investigate metabolic interactions and despite new technologies
of 2D (two-dimensional) models are expanding, nowadays science research is shifting to
3D (three-dimensional) culture. Therefore, this study will contribute to elaborate on
discovering the interactions between bacteria and host cells, testing the feasibility of a 3D
infection model that allows a non-invasive metabolic study with NMR (Nuclear Magnetic
Resonance).

Aim of the project
The aim of this project is to transfer a 2D cellular infection model established in the
laboratory into a 3D Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) compatible version that allows
non-invasive metabolic studies. This work will involve the following objectives.
4.1
Objectives
1. Choice of the 3D model based on literature research to anchor adhering cells under
conditions used for NMR.
New technologies of two-dimensional (2D) models are expanding and show a big
improvement on the cell culture, however, nowadays science research is shifting to
three-dimensional (3D) models, which can overcome limitations of traditional 2D
cell culture and establish a new concept of infection model.
2. Establish a protocol in a 3D model for adhering host cells compatible with the NMR
experiment.
Recently a scaﬀold material for 3D cell culturing was used in NMR metabolic
studies of adhering endothelial progenitor cells [1]. This material is therefore chosen
as anchoring material for mammalian host cells in this project, since it has been
demonstrated to be compatible with the NMR technique. Therefore, a new protocol
for growing mammalian cells on a 3D scaﬀold will be established and validated.
3. Application of the MTT assay to measure cell viability
MTT is known as a quantitative and sensitive detection of cells proliferation. The
assay has been widely used to asses cell viability, therefore it will be helpful to
validate the cell growth on the 3D scaﬀold and their viability compared to the 2D
model.
4. Perform a non-invasive metabolic measurements using the 3D model
Once the scaﬀold material for 3D cell culturing will be established and a proof of
growing cells will be validated, a non-invasive metabolic measurement using 3D
scaﬀold might be conducted with NMR.

background has been created in order to provide an overview of the research behind the
project, see Figure 1.
Figure 1: Flowchart provides an overview of the background in this section.
5.1
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR)
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a serious threat to the health of humans and animals
worldwide. In April 2014 the World Health Organization (WHO) collected data from
national and international surveillance networks, demonstrating the existence of this
problem worldwide and the necessity of interventions in order to contain the resistance [2].
AMR occurs when microorganisms such as bacteria, fungi, viruses and parasites resist the
eﬀects of medication that was supposed to treat the microbe itself. These microorganisms
are sometimes deﬁned as “superbugs” [3]. It takes place naturally over time, usually due to
genetic mutations or exchange of DNA among bacteria. However, the main reason which
is accelerating this problem it is the overuse of antibiotics or sometimes given without
professional oversight. To date, ABR (antibacterial resistance) is the major problem,
because of the high rates of resistance observed in bacteria and for its consequences to
humans and animals [3]. As a result of this issue, the medications become ineﬀective
and infections persist in the body, leading scientists to develop and research for new
drugs against those microbes. Nowadays, the available methods to understand the ability
of these microorganisms to evolve strategies for inactivating antibiotics and to evade
chemotherapy are rudimental and we are now facing with substantial infectious-disease
challenges. In the face of antimicrobial resistance and the emerging of new infectious
organisms, there is a need to investigate more the technology of anti-infective drugs
and the mechanism of infectious disease, based on a deeper understanding of microbial

physiology and antibiotic resistance, and the discovery of novel antimicrobial molecules
[4].
The lack of new strategies and development of novel antimicrobial lead to look for
new and diﬀerent targets in microorganisms. Several recent reports encourage targeting
bacterial central metabolic pathways as a new strategy for antimicrobial drug discovery
based on lessons learned from oncology and virus infection [5]. For example, in infectious
diseases, a recent research showed how metabolic ﬂux induced by enveloped viruses
revealed numerous metabolic processes important for viral replication in vivo as targets
for antiviral therapy [6]. Using fatty acid biosynthesis inhibitors, Munger et al.(2008)
chemically validated the suppresion of viral replication during human cytomegalovirus
(HCMV). The question is posed whether central metabolism can be the bacteria’s Achilles
heel as has been seen in cancer cells and during viral replication. During the history,
central metabolism has never been taken into account for dveloping antimicrobials because
of the lack of selectivity, since most metabolic enzymes are conserved from bacteria to
human. Furtheremore, most of the clinically used antibiotics have as a target: DNA
replication, protein synthesis, and cell wall biosynthesis. Therefore, with the worldwide
threat of AMR, there is the need to investigate of alternate essential cellular processes, as
a target against antimicrobial resistant.
5.2
Cellular infection model
Nowadays, 2D cells models have been the most common resource to investigate the
metabolic interaction between bacteria and host cells. Despite their inexpensive price and
the fact that they are easy to handle and fast, Di Shi et al. [7], deﬁne them as a failed model
since they don’t include important environmental parameters and, therefore may lack
eﬀective predictive power. The real issue with 2D models is the inaccurate representation
of the cellular environment and the lack of imitating the in vivo architecture, which lead
to inaccurate results on drug development and metabolism analysis. For this reason, many
antimicrobial drugs fail when testing their eﬃciency from the bench to bedside. The
lack of new strategies and the encouragement of targeting the central metabolism, lead
researchers to look for new models to investigate antimicrobial resistance. Therefore, this
project aim to suggest an alternative infection model, transferring a 2D infection model
into a 3D model compatible to the NMR tube which will allow a non-invasive metabolic
studies. The strength of this model is shown in Figure 2, as it can be seen: host cells
when seeded on a 3D model, will be able to maintain themselves through a ﬂow system
for continuous nutrient supply, mimicking a real time environment. The advantages of
3D models are shown in their ability to enable cell-cell and cell-environment interactions
and mostly, real-time monitoring and high-resolution imaging.

5.3
Cell culture: indispensable tool
For testing the capability of novel antimicrobial drugs and to understand the metabolic,
biophysical and biomolecular mechanisms of cells and how their function becomes disrupted
in disease: cell culture is now widely used as a model in biotechnology and biomedical
research to investigate their functions. For over a century, culture were mostly carried
under adherent condition (2D monolayer cell culture) in science research, however recent
research has shifted toward culture using three dimensional (3D) structures [9]. In the
following subsections, it will be given an overview of the current 2D and 3D cell culture
and, a comparison of them.
5.3.1
Current 2D cell culture methods
Basic 2D cell culture depend on adherence to a ﬂat surface such as petri dish of glass or
polystyrene, which provide mechanical support for the cells. Cell growth in 2D monolayers
results in homogenous growth and prolifereration due to a similar amount of nutrients and
==PAGE:12==

growth factors present in the medium [9]. Althought 2D methods are really simple and
eﬃcient, most of them do not provide control of the cell shape causing biophysical and
bioactivity diﬀerences in vivo. To overcome this issue, micro-patterned substrates such as
microwells [10] and micropillars [11] have been used to customize the 2D shape, however,
these substrates induce an apical-basal polarity which alter the function of native cells [9].
The eﬀect of apical-basal polarity can be mitigated using a sandwich culture, furthermore
modeling physiological events can be challenging for many cell lines, therefore alternative
methods to traditional 2D cell culture were developed, such as: micro-patterning and
altering of substrate stiﬀness. A list of the most current common used 2D cell culture are
listed below.
1. Sandwich culture: consist of placing cells between two layers comprised of ECM
(extracellular matrix), polycrylamide or collagene. This method was proven to
reproduce morphology and function of hepatocyte cells more accurately, as in vivo
behaviour [12]. For a better structure understanding see Figure 3.
2. Micro-patterning : consist of modifying the microenvironment of 2D models aﬀecting
the microtopography of the plate. Cells are cultured in a substrate patterned with
adherent micrometer-sized domains of predeﬁned geometric features. When cultured
on this, cells adhere speciﬁcally to the patterned area, acquiring for example, the
geometry of the pattern [13], (see Figure 3).
3. Altering substrate stiﬀness is another way of modifying 2D models. For example,
Engler et al. [14] demonstrated that the diﬀerentiation of mesenchymal stem cells
(MSCs), can be regulated based on the stiﬀness of the substrate. Hence, growing
cells on a soft or on a stiﬀsubstrate, change the expression in the cells of speciﬁc
markers.

5.3.2
Current 3D cell culture methods
Limitations of traditional 2D culture such as imitating the in vivo architecture and the
microenvironments, lead the emergence of developing 3D cell cultures systems. Nowadays,
3D cell culture systems are considered an in vitro platform for cancer and stem cell
research, which hold a great potential as a tool for drug discovery and disease modeling
[17]. Thank to the recent advances in cell biology and tissue engineering, a wide range of
3D cell culture platforms were developed, such as: spheroid culture, biopolymer scaﬀolds
and hydrogels. Referring to Dr. Comley [18], the list below will described 3D cell culture
techniques that have shown most promise. In Figure 4 are shown detailed pictures and
structures of how the 3D models are composed.
1. Hydrogels are water-swollen networks of natural or synthetic polymers. Compared
to natural ECM extracts, synthetic gels are highly customizable, as they can be
modiﬁed for speciﬁc cultures by incorporation of diﬀerent types of proteins, peptides
and other molecules. In this way, it is possible to incorporate speciﬁc cues to
inﬂuence cellular function and behavior.
2. ECM-sheet: a variety of 3D matrices are available. Such 3D matrix gels often
feature a soft, tissue-like stiﬀness and mimic the ECM that is naturally present in
tissues. Using 3D matrices for cell culture has several advantages, as it promotes
cell adhesion and enables proper transport of gases, nutrients, and growth factors
to the cells. This promotes cell survival, proliferation, and diﬀerentiation.
3. Spheroid culture consist of three-dimensional spherical cellular aggregates and
Spheroids are useful in the study of changing physiological characteristics of cells,[28]
the diﬀerence in the structure of healthy cells and tumor cells, and the changes cells
undergo when forming a tumor.
4. Collagene tissue construct is the main structural protein in the extracellular space
in various connective tissues, as it is the main component of natural ECM. It is
therefore often used as a matrix in 3D tissue culture.
5. Prefabricate scaﬀolds are usually printed by 3D printer and are materials that
have been engineered to cause desirable cellular interactions to contribute to the
formation of new functional tissues for medical purposes. Cells are seeded into these
structures in order to support three-dimensional tissue formation.
6. Cell sheets is an approach to engineering organs and to make multiple layers of cell
sheets without using a prefabricate scaﬀold [9]. Sheets can be made of layers of
various cells and they can be used for example for kidney or liver application.

Referring to Dr. John Comley in 3D cell culture easier said than done [18], the ﬁrst
most successfully assay type with 3D cell culture was cell viability. Therefore, in order
to determine the cell viability on 3D InsertTM, MTT (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-

method to measure viable cell number in multi-well plates.
This assay will play a
foundamental role in this project. There are a lot of existing methodologies that can
perform such a function, however the MTT assay is viewed as a convinient, reliable,
fast and less labor intendive compared to other methodologies [28]. Diﬀerent types
of colorimetric tetrazolium compounds are used to detect viable cells, but the most
commonly used are MTT, MTS (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-5-(3-carboxymethoxyphenyl)-
2-(4-sulfophenyl)-2H-tetrazolium), XTT (2,3-bis-(2-methoxy-4-nitro-5-sulfophenyl)-2H-

compounds are divided in two categories: MTT which is positively charged and easily
penetrate into eukaryotic cells, instead the second one includes MTS, XTT and WST-1
which are negatively charged and do not penetrate cells, that’s why they are usually
associated with an electron acceptor that can transfer electrons from the cytoplasm or
plasma membrane to facilitate the reduction of the tetrazolium [29]. In this project, MTT
was chosen as a tetrazolium compound to assess the cell viability. NAD(P)H-dependent
cellular oxidoreductase enzymes (see Figure 7, [30]), which play a main role in many
central metabolic pathways in the cells, are capable of reducing the tetrazolium dye MTT
to its insoluble formazan, which has a purple colour.

5.7.1
Parameters
As mentioned above, the reduction of MTT into formazan is not well understood yet and

a common MTT assay protocol has been established and used for Caco-2 and Hela cells

the establishment of the protocol will be crucial to analyse, interpret and discuss the ﬁnal
results.

5.8.1
NMR principles
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance is a qualitative tool to characterizes the chemical structure
of organic compounds in a solution, based on the signal they return at a speciﬁc frequency
when pulsed with a radio wave. NMR is able to detect Carbon-Hydrogen conformation in
space. The detection happens only for atoms with an odd Mass number, such as H1, C13,
N15 or F 19, which have a nuclear spin and the nucleus behave as a magnet. For atoms
with an even mass number, therefore an equal number of protons and neutrons cannot be
detected by NMR since their magnetic ﬁelds cancel each other. Referring to odd atoms,
they generally prefer a lower energy level with a random orientation in space, however
when a magnetic ﬁeld is applied (H0), the nuclei will align or oppose their spin to the
magnetic ﬁeld (Figure 9). If align with H0, their energy state is deﬁned as a low energy
level alpha, whereas if oppose against H0 they will be in a higher energy level called Beta.
Electromagnetic radiation is then applied, ﬂipping the nuclei from a low energy state to
a higher one and, in this state, they are deﬁned as being in resonance. The Percentage
change in energy between the alpha level and the Beta level is deﬁned as chemical shift.
Figure 9: On the left side an example of nuclei being in space with a random orientation, hence a low
energy level. On the right side, it can be seen that nuclei acquire diﬀerent orientations when subjected to
a magnetic ﬁeld (H0). In blue same orientation as the magnetic ﬁeld and in red the opposite. Picture
was taken by NMR Theory, [33].
NMR is also a versatile tool, which can detect diﬀerent type of metabolites and a
quantitative analysis of them can be conducted. The choice of this tool in this project,
it was because of its availability to conduct and perform a real-time analysis of the
metabolites produced during the interaction between the hosting cells and bacteria. Since
the focus of the project is to perform a non-invasive metabolism studies, NMR represent
the most suitable tool to conduct this analysis. As it is mentioned in Table 5, the data
extraction doesn’t require a lot of time, hence it is fast and can detect many type of

5.8.2
1H NMR
Protonon nuclear magnetic resonance is deﬁned as 1H NMR. 1H NMR measures the
resonance frequency of all protons within molecules of a substance, to determine their
structure. The resonant frequency of a nucleus relative to a standard in a magnetic ﬁeld
is called chemical shift and is the position on the ppm scale where the peaks occur. The
type of protons detected is indicated by the chemical shift of each group. Since the area
of a peak is proportional to the number of protons that the peak represent, when using
the integral which measure the area of the peak, each integral relate to the concentration
of each compound. Hence, the integral gives the relative ratio of the number of H for
each peak [34]. The Proton NMR gives details about the:
1. the number of diﬀerent types of hydrogens present in a compound
2. the electronic environment of the diﬀerent types of hydrogens
3. the number of hydrogen "neighbours" a hydrogen has

6.9.2
MTT assay analysis
The absorbance values obtained from the plate reader were reported on an excel sheet.
The plate reader determines absorbance by sending light with known intensity through
the sample and detecting the intensity behind it. Light that didn’t make it through to the
detector was either absorbed or scattered. The scattered part is determined by measuring
the blank. Therefore, the blank value was subtracted to each other absorbance value to
obtain pure absorbance of the cell lysis, and then plot as a value of the graph.
6.9.3
MrNOVA
The FID ﬁle of the NMR spectra was opened in MestReNova (MNova) version 14.1.1-24571
(2020). The active spectrum was chosen and the spectra were prepared before analysed by
manual phase correction and baseline correction (equal to 0). The internal reference (DSS)
at 0.000 ppm, was set and the peaks were manual integrated relative on that reference.
The manual integration function allows to compute the area of each spectra peak from
the integral start to the integral end point based on metabolites chemical shifts. Integral
values for each peak will be collected as an output of the integration function. An example
of how integration values from Glucose are obtained, it can been seen in the Figure 20
below. Integral values were then reported on an excel sheet. Since to each spectrum was
reported a reference (DSS), once overlaid, it was integrated, and each metabolite integral
was divided by the corresponding DSS integral values. This is a way to compensate for
diﬀerent shimming in the diﬀerent samples.

8
Discussion
Antimicrobial resistance has become a serious threat to the health of humans and animals
worldwide. It has already been declared from the World Health Organization in 2014, the
existence of this problem and the necessity to interfere [1]. Furthermore, to date, the major
problem is antibacterial resistance which results in ineﬀective medications and persistence
of infections in the body, due to the high rates of resistance observed in bacteria [2]. In the
face of these issues, there is an urgent need to investigate more on metabolic interactions
between bacteria and the host cells, since they are still poorly understood. Therefore,
this study will contribute to elaborate on discovering the interactions between bacteria
and host cells, testing the feasibility of a 3D infection model that allows a non-invasive
metabolic study with NMR.
8.1
MTT standard curve in 2D model
The MTT assay has been known to be one of fastest and more practical method to measure
cell viability [28]. Handling Caco-2 and Hela cells to plot a standard curve required
training and precision. As it can be seen in Figure 23, handling a high concentration of
cells to plot a standard curve is diﬃcult and if not precise when handling cells, it will create
a lot of variability among the same samples. Since it was a high concentration (50x106
cells/mL) the suspension of pellet within a small volume of medium was challenging,
therefore cells were prone to create aggregates leading to a not homogeneous stock solution.
Therefore, plotting an MTT standard curve with a low cell concentration results easier
and, more accurate to use as a reference for comparing the cell viability between the 2D
and 3D model (see Figure 22). Furthermore, conducting the cell counting before and after
preparing the plate for the MTT assay was beneﬁcial in terms of identifying if there was a
loss of cells while seeding cells. Performing the cell count more than once, revealed a low
accuracy of the tool. Indeed, the values shown in Table 9 are an average of four counts.
8.2
Cell seeding test on 3D model
Even though the MTT standard curve made with a high cell concentration was excluded,
handling cells in high concentration was kept to seed the 3D model. The choice of keeping
it, was due to the fact that it would be seeded a higher amount of cells with a small
volume (10µl with 500.000 cells), since the 3D model had a diameter of 4,2 mm.
8.2.1
Critical steps on cell seeding test
Seeding cells on the scaﬀold was the most critical step. When seeding a volume higher
than 10µl, the pressure of the pipette and high volume would have overturned the 3D

model, causing a loss of seeded cells. Results shown in the Appendix in Figure 38 and 40,
support this argument. Therefore, after a few testing a needle was introduced to keep
the 3D model down, facilitating the seeding. Another phenomenon noticed during to the
seeding procedure was the inability of cells to stick on the 3D model, which it was visible
at the bottom of the plate. Instead of seeding the cell volume and being absorbed by
the model, part of seeded volume was going trough seeding at the bottom of the plate.
Furthermore, stamping out the 3D models with a diameter of 4,2 required carefulness.
If pressing too hard with the tool, it might cause a damage on the model. This was
visible from small ﬁbers of polystyrene breaking away from the structure. As mentioned
in the 3D Biotek protocol regarding cell seeding, after seeding cells the 3D model is
supposed to be incubated three hours without any medium. Adding a volume of 1,5 mL
in each well containing the 3D model seeded with cells, lead the cells to be subjected to
a strong pressure causing some of them to detach from the model and ﬂoat into the medium.
8.2.2
3D standard model and DMEM test
Despite successful results shown in the Paper X and Y, using the 3D insert to seed cells
as a an alternative to the 2D model is still challenging and deﬁned parameters need to
be found. When testing the 3D standard Insert (8 mm of diameter), it showed a low
performance compared to the expected output compute with the protocol provided by
3D Biotek. In Figure 25 can be observed that the model reached an amount of 100.000
cells compared to the MTT standard curve. Various factors might be involved in the
low performance of the model, such as a low tendency of cells to stick and seed on the
3D model or the waiting time of 3 hours before adding the medium might aﬀect their
viability. Because tendency was one of the suggested argument, in the next experiment
a comparison of washing the 3D models with ethanol and DMEM was performed, but
this time stamping out the models with 4,2 mm of diameter. In Figure 26, results didn’t
show a substantial diﬀerence, therefore washing it with growth medium didn’t improve
the eﬃciency. Another factor was evaluated, the last experiment of the cell seeding test
shown in Figure 27 refers to a comparison of the 3D model seeded with two cell stocks.
However, the results show a non substantial diﬀerence also in this experiment.

when removing the medium and during the washing step. This latter one was a critical
step, since the tendency of the cells to attach to the model was low, the washing step lead
to another strong pressure on them, detaching the cells from the model. The MTT assay
includes four washing steps with PBS, therefore lead to a certain loss of cells seeding on

models. As it can be seen in Figure 43 in the Appendix, it was noticed that 3D models
with few cells attached shown a "yellow" pattern, due to the light of the microscope
lighting up all the spaces within the model. Whereas, for 3D models with most of the
cells attached shown a grey layer, ﬁlling up all the spaces among the polystyrene ﬁbers.

may be due to the wrong handling or few training; whereas, Caco-2 cells seem to have a
lower variability compared to Hela cells. It was noticed while performing the MTT asssay
that Hela cells, after being seeded on the well, tend to grow one up to each other without
spreading in the well. This phenomenon may have caused a high variability in the 2D
model (purple squares).

Conclusion
In conclusion, establishing and testing new protocols to give foundations to a 3D cell
infection model for a non-invasive metabolic studies was successfully produced and gave
promising results for a new initial research area.
The feasibility of using the MTT standard curve to measure the cell viability on the 3D
model was demonstrated and approved. Handling a low cell concentration and perform
an MTT standard curve with it, it results with a low variability. The MTT assay was
a fast and practical method to measure cell viability. Using the 3D Insert provided by
3D Biotek to seed cells is still challenging and required some training time and, deﬁned
parameters which may improve its performance.
The established protocols involved in this project resulted well designed and eﬀective,
however some improvements in the cell seeding procedure and the non-invasive metabolism
studies are needed, such as redeﬁning some intermediate steps which might improve the
eﬃcacy of the 3D model and the whole protocol design.
Non-invasive metabolism studies need to be more elaborated and investigations. Results
obtained from the lactate analysis in comparison of 2D and 3D model, showed already a
good path in which it may be investigate and research more, in order to elucidate and
conﬁrm this phenomenon. Further data are needed, in order to conﬁrm and statistically
demonstrate that cells on the 3D model are in a better environment compared to the 2D
one.
3D models are the new path that science research is looking at and the perspectives which
can oﬀer are wide and they may change and aﬀect drug development deeply. Even though
handling mammalian cells on 3D models resulted challenging and diﬃcult, this project
already oﬀer a well established overview on how they may be involve in future research
and simple experiments which may oﬀer great outcome simply changing the environment
on where the cells grow.

3D InsertTM Cell Seeding Protocol 
 
Application 
 
This is the protocol for cell seeding cells on 3D InsertTM scaffolds in a non-treated cell culture plate/dish. It is 
critical to follow this 3D cell seeding protocol. Following this protocol will allow the cell suspension to 
be completely held inside the scaffold by the capillary force so that the cells will attach to the 
scaffolds. 
 
 
Materials and Equipment 
 
Sterile non-treated cell culture plate/dish 
Sterile forceps 
Pipette  
 
 
 
 
Sterile pipette tips 
Vacuum 
 
 
 
 
Biosafety hood 
Cell culture incubator 
 
 
Procedure 
 
1. Calculate the appropriate cell concentration for 3D cell seeding. Refer to Tables III and IV for the 3D 
scaffold’s cell growth areas. We suggest using the same cell seeding density (number of cells/cm2) as you 
normally use with 2D cell culture. See the Appendix for an example. 
 
2. Remove cells of interest from culture and resuspend in culture medium at an appropriate concentration. 
 
3. Slowly pipette the correct volume (Table I) of this cell suspension onto the top surface of each 3D InsertTM 
scaffold, covering 80-90% of the scaffold’s surface. Note: Do not allow the cell suspension to contact 
the sides of the wells. This will result in a reduced seeding efficiency.

APPENDIX 
 
 
        An Example of How to Calculate The Cell Suspension Concentration for 3D Scaffolds Seeding 
 
We suggest using the same cell seeding density (number of cells/cm2) as you normally use with 2D cell 
culture. However, because the 3D InsertTM provides a much higher surface area (Tables III-IV) than a 
traditional 2D well, you will need to seed more cells in each scaffold than you normally would in a 2D well.  
Below is a 3D cell seeding example for seeding cells on a total of 24 scaffolds in a 96-well package of 3D 
InsertTM–PS152096-24. 
 
a. 
Assume you will need to seed 1,000 cells in each well of a 96-well plate when you perform 2D cell 
culture. When conducting your experiment using the 3D InsertTM–PS152096-24, you will need to seed 
1,000 x 4.2 = 4,200 cells in each 3D scaffold, because the surface area of the 96-well 3D InsertTM–
PS152096-24 is 4.2 times greater (the factor in column 5 of Table III) than the surface area of a 
traditional 2D, 96-sized well. NOTE:  Be sure to use the appropriate ratio listed in Tables III and IV 
for your experiment. 
b. 
These 4,200 cells will be in a medium volume of 15 µl (the seeding volume for a 96-well 3D InsertTM–
PS152096-24). Thus, the cell suspension concentration you need to prepare will be 4,200/0.015 ml = 
280,000 cells/ml.  
c. 
A normal 3D InsertTM–PS152096-24 package contains 24 scaffolds. For an experiment using all of 
these scaffolds at a concentration of 280,000 cells/ml, you will need a total cell suspension of 24 x 
0.015 ml = 0.36 ml to seed all 24 scaffolds. Be sure to prepare more cell suspension in order to 
compensate for possible pipette loss. We typically prepare an extra volume for two extra scaffolds. In 
this case, the extra volume is 2 x 0.015 ml = 0.03 ml. Therefore, the total cell suspension volume for 
seeding is 0.36 ml + 0.03 ml = 0.39 ml. 
d. 
Using your original/stock cell suspension, make a total 0.39 ml of 280,000 cells/ml cell suspension. This 
is the working volume and concentration necessary to seed 24 3D InsertTM–PS152096-24 scaffolds.
