Abstract 
In recent years, overheating in buildings is a developing problem. Climate change, more use of 
electronic equipment, intensive insulation increase the risk. 
A majority of the buildings in Copenhagen, especially in the central area has been constructed 
between 1850-1930. Usually, these old residential buildings are subject to energy efficient 
renovations that can lead to overheating in certain parts of the year. Sustained exposure to high 
temperatures can cause discomfort and unhealthy indoor climate, and negatively affects 
occupants’ wellbeing and productivity.  
Several potential efforts have been done to eliminate this risk. Solar control systems as renovation 
strategy aims to improve the indoor environment and reduce vulnerability of the building to 
overheating. Previous work has shown that shading systems could extremely reduce the unwanted 
solar gains. However, research question is the effect of human interactions on the applied shading 
systems and their overall performance in terms of overheating. This question is particularly 
relevant to such residential buildings as use of automated solutions is not common due to higher 
price and installation constrains. Moreover, the number of effective solar shadings for application 
in such residential buildings are limited for several reasons e.g. aesthetical reasons, applicability 
etc.  
To provide more information on human interaction with shadings, a sensitivity analysis has been 
performed on a carefully selected solar shading devices from the current market.  As a first step, 
the shading devices were explored with respect to their influence on the overheating of two 
buildings constructed the mentioned period.  Based on a simple single parameter experimental 
design, simulations were implemented in IDA ICE and the impact of six shading devices on the 
overheating performance of the buildings was investigated. Purpose of this investigation was to 
find energy efficient solutions which reduce the vulnerability of the building.  
 Subsequently, affective parameters such as surrounding density were simulated and evaluated 
for the two buildings. 
Finally, in both buildings, comparisons between the performance of the applied devices and of 
different scenarios regarding occupants’ behaviour have been reported and discussed. The 
occupant behavior scenarios were based on solar irradiance (direct solar radiation on the window 
and vertical interior illuminance at the façade), indoor air temperature and daylight at workplace. 
Based on these simple models, two different shading systems were considered. 
 
==PAGE:6==
v 
 
Results have shown a number of shading systems provide better overheating protection while 
they contain the daylight penetration. Based on these results a classification of the selected 
shading systems was made.   The study on occupant behavior and human interaction with shading 
was concluded in a series of recommendation which suggests considering indoor temperature, 
when internal shadings need to be activated and direct solar radiation when external shadings 
need to be controlled.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Residential buildings constructed between 1850-1930, characterize many large Danish cities. The 
central area of Copenhagen is highly densely populated with historical residential, multi-storey 
buildings from this era. Buildings with heavy masonry brick construction and small windows or 
buildings with brick or concrete external wall with larger glazing areas can be seen in this period. 
In both façade typologies, when constructed, no standards for thermal insulation were required 
by building regulations. Therefore, high heat loss through transmission or infiltration can cause 
energy consumption problems. To improve the energy performance of these buildings, intensive 
renovations are adopted. The most immediate and common energy efficient renovation strategy 
is to improve the insulation level the building’s envelope. 
However, these buildings become prone to overheating risk when insulated for thermal reasons, 
which leads to overheating in summer. Occupants of such buildings are facing uncomfortable 
indoor climate, poor indoor air quality and high temperatures. Residents’ health, wellbeing and 
productivity are negatively influenced, especially vulnerable groups like elderly and young 
children.  
Avoiding overheating conditions in buildings is a great challenge for the project. However, 
eliminating the risk of overheating, maintaining a healthy indoor climate and reduce the need for 
cooling options prove to be a challenge.  
In this study the focus will be on shading systems as a strategy for overheating reduction in 
residentials. Given that shading systems as renovation strategies could extremely reduce the 
unwanted solar gains, their performance in relation to overheating is being investigated at the first 
step. A few solar shading devices relevant to the specific building types are selected for 
evaluation. Architectural aesthetics, availability in market, operation, use and maintenance are 
the main factors for this choice. At the final step, occupancy schedules are being compared for 
these shading systems. The idea is to compare and evaluate the role of occupant through 
predefined interactive schedules with shadings. The ultimate goal is to to find energy efficient 
solutions that can reduce the vulnerability of the building to overheating and provide 
suggestions/guidelines for manual control to solar control users. Guidelines for building users in 
residential buildings where automated shading systems are less appreciated, if followed by 
building users, would help with appropriate use of shading devices in residential.  
Before the sensitivity analysis, some main meanings and definitions used in the project are 
explained in order to understand the background of this study. Thereafter, the simulation strategy 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
is described in detail and every simulating case is specified. In IDA ICE, evaluations of the 
overheating situation of the buildings in different scenarios are performed.  
Overheating elimination in buildings is a priority to this project, however in residential buildings, 
daylighting cannot be neglected. Daylight is an important aspect when occupants are in the house 
and usually necessitates contradicting requirements in relation to design solutions for overheating 
protection.  A balanced usage of shading systems where the daylight quality is maintained is 
another challenge when selecting the appropriate shading system and the correct occupant 
interaction with shading systems.  Thus, an investigation for solutions that prevent overheating 
but still ensure the daylight levels is also performed.  
Most of the results are presented in graphs and tables in order to be more easily comparable for 
discussion. Additional information, more graphs and tables that provide a wider view of the 
research can be found in Appendices.

2 BACKGROUND 
Extreme weather conditions are caused by the effects of climate change, for instance hot and dry 
summers and milder and wetted winters. Occupants in renovated older buildings, but also in some 
newer highly insulated buildings are facing uncomfortable indoor climate and high temperatures 
[1]. 
Usually, old residential buildings are subject to intensive renovations. Adopting energy efficient 
renovations strategies, heat loss through transmission or infiltration can be reduced or even 
avoided. On the down side, this can cause poor indoor air quality and overheating in certain parts 
of the year with negative effect on occupant’s health, wellbeing and productivity especially 
among vulnerable groups such as children and elderly.  
In this section, overheating risk is explained and several preventive strategies are described. One 
effective preventive strategy is adoption of shading devices. External awning blinds and 
marquisolettes provide some protection, preventing the sunlight to enter the building, as do 
venetian blinds and rolling shutters. In addition to the external devices, internal protection can be 
provided by internal roller blinds, curtains or internal shutters.  
With observation of occupancy behaviour in relation to shading systems in focus, in this section 
an overview of available devices and their specifications as well as importance of occupants’ 
behaviour is given. Moreover, the building typologies that are common to the era between 1850 
and 1930 are presented.  
2.1 Overheating 
Generally, occupants use the term overheating in buildings to describe a discomfort situation 
resulted from the accumulation of warmth within the building. It is still a growing problem due 
to climate change, more use of electronic equipment, intensive insulation, and other factors. There 
are no defined conditions or international bounds under which can be said that overheating is 
happening [1]. 
However, for building that occupants can open windows to provide ventilation to the indoor 
environment, Danish Building Regulations [2] state the provision that overheating is caused when 
more than 100 hours per year the indoor temperature exceeds 27°C and more than 25 hours per 
year the temperature exceeds 28°C.  
In addition to high temperatures, there are other factors related to overheating that affect the 
thermal comfort of the occupants, such as the sustained exposure to high temperatures or even the 
lack of air movement [1]. 
==PAGE:13==
4 
 
2.1.1 Principles of overheating 
As mentioned above, there are several factors that can cause overheating. Both internal and 
external heat gains along with inefficient ventilation in the building should be the main concerns 
when a renovation strategy is occurring.  
External solar gains can pass through the glass of the window and heat the internal spaces of the 
building. Indoors, surfaces and objects absorb these gains and emit them in the form of heat to 
the area. As the phenomenon referred the ‘greenhouse effect’, the heat remains trapped within the 
building’s envelope [1]. Even double-glazed windows can maintain the heat in the room to some 
extent, especially in newer buildings with low-e coating, where the heat is not able to enter 
through highly insulated fabric [3]. 
Internal gains are the result of occupants and their activities inside the building. Active people 
within a building emit heat according to the level of their activity [4]. Furthermore, electrical 
appliances, such as fridges, televisions, laptops and dishwashers, washing machines, etc., release 
heat when in use but also even in stand-by mode. Lighting and building services such as 
mechanical ventilation systems can also be a source of unwanted heat, especially in the summer 
[1]. 
Purge ventilation is the most appropriate ventilation strategy in order to remove build-up heat. By 
providing high levels of ventilation to the building, not only pollutants can be removed, but also 
to fresh air is supplied replacing the existing warm one. This method is a possible way to eliminate 
overheating [1].  
2.1.2 Building characteristics that increase the risk of overheating 
The main sources of heat in a building are the unwanted solar gains that enter through the building 
fabric or through the windows and high ambient air temperature, especially when it is higher than 
the indoor air temperature. Moreover, internal gains such as building services, electrical 
appliances, lighting, equipment and even the human body are significant sources of heat 
production inside a building [4]. In addition, the building itself can be the reason for overheating 
problems. The location and the orientation of the building, the construction, thermal mass and 
ventilation are the contributing factors which are explained in this section. 
Site context – Location 
There are both positive and negative impacts of the location of the building referring to 
overheating in a building. Site surroundings, location in a large urban area (Urban heat island 
effect) or even the direction and the floor level of the apartment are factors that cause different 
thermal conditions to the buildings [1]. In residential buildings at noisy areas, such as near railway 
==PAGE:14==
5 
 
lines and airports, or near industrial areas, such as industrial plants, occupants are unwilling to 
open the windows. In addition, when the apartment is located at the ground or first floor with easy 
access to a balcony or windows, opening windows can usually be an issue in terms of security. 
Therefore, means of ventilations are not used properly either during the day, or overnight [4], 
which can enhance overheating problems.  
Orientation 
One factor that affect overheating in dwellings is the orientation of the construction. Appropriate 
orientation, in summer can protect the indoor space from unwanted solar gains that cause 
overheating, and in winter, reduce the heating loads by utilizing the solar gains. It has been proved 
that the same house type behave differently, when being rotated to different orientations [1]. 
Furthermore, in case of apartment blocks, flats might have more unpleasant internal environments 
on one side of the façade compared to those on another side. A house with its main windows 
facing southwest is more prone to receive solar heat gains, which in this case, are caused mainly 
from low-level sun from mid-afternoon towards evening when the external temperature is at its 
highest [4]. Elevations facing south experience more direct sunlight during the day and for that 
reason the rooms on this side may have an overheating internal environment due to higher levels 
of heat gains [3], [5]. 
Building design and construction 
Higher standards of thermal mass, insulation and airtightness have been introduced by the 
Building Regulations with the purpose of improving the energy efficiency. However, these 
improved thermal standards along with the warming climate are the main factors that increase the 
risk of overheating in dwellings. In order to protect buildings from overheating and to retain a 
pleasant environment indoors, appropriate measure should be included to the design or have the 
prospect to be effectively retrofitted in a later stage. Regarding the new buildings, passive solar 
design strategies are suggested, for example optimizing the orientation by constructing south-
facing rooms [6]. A main concern is the older buildings, which represent the majority of the 
residential houses in Copenhagen. These houses were built with little or no insulation at all and 
that makes them energy inefficient and difficult to be heated [4]. Nowadays, old pre-1960 
buildings are subject to intensive energy efficient renovations (see paragraph 2.2) that can 
provide higher protection from the negative impacts of heat gains.  
Thermal mass  
The ability of construction materials to absorb and emit heat is called thermal mass. Since, higher 
thermal mass material (heavier materials) can store more heat compared to lighter materials [1], 
they can contribute to overheating problems. Nevertheless, the effects of  high temperatures can 
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
be moderated due to thermal storage, which means that the heat stored through the daytime, is 
able to be ejected at night [7]. Furthermore, a high thermal mass can actually be beneficial at 
preventing overheating if specific measures are taken, such as shading and ventilation [4]. 
Restricted ventilation 
As noted above, adequate ventilation is effective to eliminate heat from dwellings. Opening the 
windows, results in high air change and limitation of the pollutants [1]. This is harder to achieve 
in practice because of security reasons at lower floors. However, occupants should be aware that 
opening windows when the ambient outdoor temperature is higher than the indoor temperature 
(or even equal) enhance the overheating situation [6]. Equally important is night-time ventilation 
in summer time when the weather is hot and when the security of the house in not in danger. 
Nevertheless, in urban areas night-time ventilation is less effective to the indoor air quality due 
to the slight difference of ambient temperatures between day and night [4]. 
2.2 Overheating prevention renovation strategies 
One of the main challenges in the building sector, is the decrease of the energy consumption and 
the improvement of the energy performance of the buildings. Global energy consumption is 
caused in a significant level by buildings. In Denmark, as in central Europe, most of the residential 
buildings do not fulfill specific energy requirements. Therefore, these buildings become subject 
of energy renovations in order to upgrade their energy performance [8]. 
The most common energy renovation strategy aims to improve the level of insulation of the 
building’s envelope. In this section, the effects of thermal insulation and ventilation systems to 
the performance of the building are described. Moreover, the influence of the replacement of the 
window and the installation of shading devices as renovation strategies are explained. 
2.2.1 Thermal insulation 
No insulation  or low levels of insulation lead to heat losses through the building skin [1]. One of 
the most beneficial ways to prevent solar gains to pass through the envelop s the application of 
additional thermal insulation to the envelope of the building [4]. 
Buildings constructed according to recent energy standards, are insulated and airtight properly in 
order to limit heat losses in wintertime. However, older buildings, like most buildings in the 
central area of Copenhagen, were constructed with no insulation at all. These buildings are usually 
the targets of energy renovations by adding insulation.  
A problem often seen by adding high-insulation or excessive air-tightness to the building, is that 
more heat can retain within the house and solar gain can be blocked from entering the building. 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
In fact, this is a common problem, as reduction of the infiltration lead to poor indoor air quality 
and increase overheating problems.  
Poorly insulated structures and around thermal bridges, where there is intensive heat flow, can 
cause lower temperatures in the spaces. On the other hand, higher temperatures can easily occur 
in houses with better insulation and careful design to avoid thermal bridges [6]. 
A Major concern is old buildings, especially built during the period 1850-1890, because energy 
renovation can cause problems to the appearance of the building (when adding external insulation) 
or moisture damages (when adding internal insulation) [9]. 
2.2.2 Ventilation 
Ventilation systems are another mean to provide proper indoor air quality but not to cool indoor 
air. Mainly because air-conditioning is not considered as an energy efficient solution [10] and can 
lead to increased dwelling ‘running costs’, it is a none preferable alternative for occupants on a 
low income [4]. 
Even though ventilation is required for indoor air quality, opening the windows when indoor 
temperature is equal or below the outdoor temperature is harmful and may increase overheating 
problems. However, high air change can be achieved with purge ventilation in order to remove 
pollutants and to replace the existing warm air with fresh air [4]. Therefore, natural ventilation is 
beneficial only when the outdoor temperature is lower than the indoor [6]. Moreover, night 
ventilation in order to replace warn indoor air with fresh cooler air is useful to the residents, even 
to their sleep.  
Although this may be applicable to upper floors, in reality, in ground floor and first floor 
apartments or other apartments easily accessible by possible intruders, it is not secure to open the 
windows. Mechanical ventilation is a common system in such dwellings. If it sized and installed 
appropriately, this system can significantly reduce heating loads in the winter period and 
guarantee the indoor air quality. The principle of operation in such systems, is to recover heat 
from the exhaust air and return it to the room by increasing the temperature of the supply air [11]. 
2.2.3 Replacement of windows 
One common renovation strategy for old dwellings is the replacement of the old windows with 
newer windows that inherit more energy efficient properties.  
In addition to the size, window glazing is a window property that influences heat transmission. In 
fact, a smaller size window can reduce heat gain or heat losses [12]. However, among the several 
different glazing types on the market, glazing with high U-value are preferable concerning 
overheating risks. Such glazings in summer time reflect a large portion of solar radiation to the 
==PAGE:17==
8 
 
outside avoiding the risk of overheating the room. Nevertheless, during winter time, a high U-
value results in a large amount of heat loss.  
An effective solution in relation to overheating is a low visible transmittance glazing, which can 
control the glare in the room. However, this solution creates an unpleasant environment to the 
occupants, since the view to the outside is partly obstructed. An obstructed view interferes with 
occupant´s visual comfort and consequently their level of productiveness and motivation [13], 
[14]. 
2.2.4 Solar shading systems 
Finally, another effective and potential renovation technique to buildings is the installation and 
use of solar-shading systems. Solar control systems can effectively limit the unwanted solar gains 
to the interior, decreasing the risk to overheating and the need for cooling options. Apart from 
overheating prevention, solar shadings are usually a preferred part of renovation adaptions in 
order to protect the indoor environment from visual discomfort in terms of daylight and to 
maintain occupants’ privacy [15]. 
In old residential buildings that need renovation, daylighting is an important aspect of design but 
usually it requires contradicting approaches in relation to overheating. The contradicting approach 
rises due to the window size in these building and lack of daylight throughout the year. Therefore, 
it is a real challenge to choose the proper solar control systems in order to prevent overheating 
but still to ensure enough daylight when the tenants are in the house.  
As previously mentioned, beside overheating prevention, using shading systems reduces glare 
and provides privacy. The use of some types of solar devices can also increase the U-value of the 
window. In that way, a large amount of solar radiation can be reflected back outside through the 
window. Therefore, reduced heat losses can result in lower energy consumption for heating in 
winter time [16]. 
On the contrary, if a solar control system is not correctly selected or not properly operated, there 
are also negative effects that can lead to high energy consumption or to unpleasant indoor climate 
for the occupants. Occupants’ preferences and interactions with shadings have been studied to a 
larger extend in office spaces and commercial buildings. These studies have shown that in office 
buildings, a good indoor climate is important for employees to be more productive, engaged and 
satisfied. So, discomfort glare at their desks is an unpleasant situation, where occupants most of 
the times, lower the shades to reduce high levels of illuminance from the façade. In addition, at a 
residence when the luminance level at a working plane is higher than usual and the reflections are 
disturbing the vision, occupants are likely to activate the shadings in order to decrease the amount 
of daylight transmittance through the windows [17]. 
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
Furthermore, the main drawback is their operation can block an amount of needed daylight and 
prevent the view to the outside. For instance, when the shading device is fully activated, 
exploitation of daylighting potential is blocked. Consequently, daylight levels are reduced inside 
the room, causing dissatisfaction to the occupants and the tendency to use artificial lighting [18]. 
Therefore, energy consumption for lighting can be raised [12]. 
The other aspect to worry about, is to what extent the solar shading device prevents the view to 
the outside environment. It is important for the occupants to know the weather conditions and 
identify the changes and of course, the time of the day by looking out of the window.  
2.3 Description of the buildings types 
Jesper Engelmark supports that everyone who works with renovation of Danish buildings should 
be familiar with the architectural, construction and material history of every building that is being 
renovated. Only in this way the buildings can be well kept as heritage and delivered for posterity. 
In his book “DANSK BYGGESKIK - ETAGEBYGGERIET GENNEM 150 ÅR” [19], it is stated 
that there are about 375.000 preserved buildings in Denmark and he introduces five different types 
of them. Therefore, this section contains information on building technology and materials for all 
types according to the book. Details about the construction of such residential houses are 
described. Moreover, for each Type similarities and differences from the previous are mentioned 
and figures from a perspective view are shown.  
2.3.1 Type 1  
The first building Type that was selected as a case study, is building Type 1, one of the historical, 
residential, multi-storey buildings with basement. Such buildings are located in the central area 
of Copenhagen, which is highly densely populated since the 18th century. Solid masonry and 
bricks are the main characteristics of the construction, especially at the outer walls. One of the 
main challenges with buildings in this period is the diverse thickness of the outer walls, which 
decreases at each floor as the building rises. The ground floor has a thickness of three stones and 
the top floor has the thinnest external wall, one stone. In principle, the thickness of the wall is 
reduced by half of the block width at each floor, corresponding to 120 mm walls. Inner walls are 
mainly timber walls and in some cases, are made as double brick walls. Floors and staircases are 
made of wood and the roofing material is brick, slate or metal carried by a wooden structure. The 
lower walls, are the foundation masonry walls using local natural stone. Figure 2.1 illustrates the 
characteristic façade of buildings Type 1, front view (a) and back view (b). These buildings 
comprise parts of a rectangular arrangement of similar buildings with the back side facing to the 
courtyard in the middle. A common plan layout for buildings and the outline of the courtyard is 
shown in Figure 2.2. The plan layout is identical to all floors [19].

2.3.2 Type 2 
The second Type of buildings also contains solid masonry at the outer walls. The difference from 
the first Type (Type 1) is that they also have massive masonry at the main partitions walls and 
walls that the staircases are surrounded of. This trend started in Copenhagen by legal requirements 
in 1889 and then it continued in most of the cities in Denmark. Internal walls are double brick 
walls and floors are replaced with iron beams in minor extend, required for structural reasons. 
Iron layers, for instance, were often used for protection against moisture (used at the bathroom 
floor for water tightness) or fire (used at the floor over the basement). Stairs and roofing materials 
and construction are as in Type 1. Lastly, fundaments and possibly basement walls were made of 
concrete cast on site. A characteristic perspective view of the façade of buildings Type 2 is shown 
in Figure 2.3 This type is the most common type until the early 1930s [19].

2.3.3 Type 3 
Type 3 was widely used in the 1930s. Similarly, as in Type 2, Type 3 contains solid masonry 
outer walls and double brick inner walls. However, the windows appear larger than before and 
they are also placed in new positions, such as in and around corners, which are clearly shown in 
Figure 2.4. For that reason, more iron is used for the structure of the outer walls. Bay – windows 
and balconies are also introduced to the buildings. In addition, in this type floors are beams with 
iron in larger extend due to these new elements. Staircases are made of prefabricated steps and 
cast in-situ concrete slabs. As a roofing material, cardboard and cement-based sheets are used, 
still on a wooden structure but usually with a lower roof slope. Furthermore, foundations and 
basement exterior walls are constructed with cast in-situ concrete [19].

2.3.4 Type 4 
The following building (Type 4) have a lot in common with Type 3, with the main difference that 
floor separations are made either with concrete. In particular, external walls are massive brick 
walls, once more with large windows and more concrete elements are introduced. Staircases of 
concrete with prefabricated runs and landings were presented and entered the building with the 
use of a crane, in the late 1940s. Roof and foundations were constructed using the same way and 
with the same material as in Type 3. Type 4, which gradually replaced Type 3 due to the lack of 
iron material, began to flourish in the 1930s and became commonly used in the 1950s [19].

2.3.5 Type 5 
The last type, Type 5, is chosen as the one of the prefabricated element building alternatives and 
has proven to be the preferred one. The interior of the building (the main house) is built entirely

a smaller part of the longitudinal direction wall in order to be stabilized. Inner walls are plates of

Transverse external walls are the load-bearing and stabilizing walls which are made either as 
“sandwich construction” or as the corresponding internal walls that are coated outside. In addition,

2.4 Solar shading devices 
A wide variety of solar control shading systems is available on the market. In this section, an 
attempt of classification of such systems is presented and each subcategory is explained. 
Moreover, the characteristic shading devices that are selected for further investigation are 
described.  
2.4.1 Classification of solar shading systems 
A wide variety of solar control shading systems is available on the market. Although the aspects 
to be considered are plenty, an attempt of categorization-classification has been introduced in the 
study ‘An overview on solar shading system for buildings’ [20], presented in Diagram 1. 
 
Diagram 1 Solar shading systems for buildings: a possible classification based on [20] 
The first subcategories that solar shadings are divided into, are related to the principle of the 
operation of such systems. Thus, there are fixed shading systems, movable shading systems, and 
other shading systems, for instance a combination of the previous types. 
Several factors, such as simplicity, durability, low installation and low maintenance cost, make 
fixed solar shading systems more attractive compared to adjustable ones [18]. Despite the fact 
that they cannot be adjusted according to the weather conditions, they perform well in summer 
by blocking the sun radiation and in winter period by letting the low angled solar radiation to 
enter [3]. Although diffuse and reflected daylight is prevented, fixed solar shadings are not able 
to generate an optimized thermal and daylight efficient environment.  
Movable systems can either be manually controlled by the users or automatically controlled 
according to a sensor or a schedule. They are flexible, whereas their position can be fully or 
partially activated. Occupants can adjust them according to their needs, the moving of the sun, 
weather conditions, therefore, they control the solar radiation, daylight and glare as they desire. 
For instance, in cloudy days with low temperature, residents could let more solar radiation to enter 
the room, optimizing daylight conditions in the room, the occupants’ comfort and energy savings 
[21], [18]. However, not everyone is aware of the proper operation of manually control solar

As shown in Diagram 1, another factor of classification is the location of the shading device in

the window (on the inner side of the wall, ceiling or even on the window itself). 
With external shading devices, the largest amount of the heat in solar radiation is interrupted

Mid-pane shading systems are installed between the panes for example of a double glazing, but 
if combined with effective ventilation, they have the benefits of both the external and internal 
shadings. The main advantage is that the shades are always protected from the weather conditions, 
and due to their location, they dissipate heat gains to the outdoor environment, controlling glare 
in an effective way [16], [3]. Nevertheless, sun radiation is being reflected only after its 
transmission through the window pane, so, mid-pane shading devices can be less efficient 
compared to the external shades.  
Most of the times, internal shadings are movable and can easily be adjusted by the residents. 
People prefer internal shadings due to the low cost and the simplicity of the installation, whereas 
they control effectively the diffuse and reflected daylight that can cause glare issues [17]. Internal 
solar control systems are of little benefit in terms of overheating, therefore, once the heat is 
released into the building, it should be dissipated by mechanical cooling or ventilation [3]. But 
since there are not exposed to the outdoor conditions, less maintenance is needed.

Since shading devices consist of components that affect the architectural appearance of the 
building or the interior design of the room, they should be carefully selected before the 
installation. A different parameter to be considered is the view to the outdoor environment. 
Maintaining a good view and contacting to the outside world is of great importance for occupants’ 
well-being and satisfaction [22]. The proper selection of solar control systems is not only 
important to assure thermal comfort or a good indoor climate in relation to overheating and 
daylighting, but also has positive impact to tenants’ health and productivity [23]. 
2.4.2 Characteristic devices of movable solar shading systems 
For this project, movable solar shading devices are selected which can be manually controlled, in 
order to examine the human factor in relation to them. The external awning blinds, marquisolette 
and venetian blinds, whereas the internal venetian blinds, roller blinds and drapes are described. 
2.4.2.1 Awning blinds 
Awning blinds can be fixed or movable, but it should be mentioned that only movable systems 
are recommended to this project, in order to investigate human behaviour in relation to them, in 
terms of overheating. Thus, movable – retractable awnings allow the occupants to control the 
indoor climate and the daylight passage on their own terms. In both residential and public used 
buildings, awnings can be considered as a common and classic shading system. Awnings usually 
cover only the top half of the window, and therefore, they provide good visibility. Thus, visual 
contact can be maintained with the outdoors, even when the sunshades are activated. As Figure 
2.7 shows, awning blinds are designed to prevent excessive solar radiation from entering the 
room.  
 
Figure 2.7 Awning blinds' principle of operation 
Awnings can be mounted on all facades and can also be hanged to the sides of the window frame, 
or to the wall on the sides of the window. The window can still be opened when the shadings are 
drawn.  Generally, the design is simple, and awnings are available in market with different fabrics, 
qualities and colors, in order to able to fit the design with the building's architecture [22]. 
==PAGE:25==
16 
 
Moreover, awning blinds are divided into two sub-categories, depending on the type of the arm 
for the movement and the resulting solution of the movement; folding-arm awnings and drop-arm 
awnings. 
In both subcategories, the device is attached to the exterior surface of the building and the movable 
arms of the awning can be tilted having a stop position in a specific angle. In the first option, the 
folding-arm is self-supporting and is the only component responsible for the operation, while in 
the second solution there is also an upper part, usually parallel to the window that cannot be 
entirely tilted at all.  
In addition, drop-arm awning blinds have a subcategory called sliding-arm awning blinds which 
is known as marquisolette. Marquisolette is composed of an upper part with a shading fabric 
screen that cannot be tilted. The lower part is the tilted element with the projection effect. 
Marquisolette is a combination of roller blinds and drop arm awning shading systems, and 
therefore it includes the impacts and the advantages of both. The upper vertical part prevents 
excessive solar radiation to enter the room, regardless the angle, and at the same time, the lower 
part blocks the high angle solar radiation and not the view to the outside.   
All types of awning blinds usually cover the upper half of the window providing clear visibility 
with the surrounding environment even when the shadings are activated. In most cases, the design 
is simple and classic, and many different types of fabric, qualities and colors are available in the 
market in order to apply a design that fits exactly the building's architecture [22]. 
2.4.2.2 Venetian blinds 
Another common retractable shading system is the venetian blinds. They can be positioned either 
at the inner or at the external part of the window. However, there are also windows with integrated 
mid-pane venetian blinds. Venetian blinds are one optimum form of sun protection as they can be 
easily adjusted to control the entering solar radiation. They are constructed with horizontal slats, 
which can be tilted in order to allow the daylight to penetrate the building. This principle of 
venetian blinds operation is illustrated in Figure 2.8, where the direct and the reflected from the 
ground solar radiation are either blocked or allowed to penetrate in the room. The angle can be 
decided by the occupants according to the current indoor temperature, the desired lighting 
conditions and the wishes for visibility to the outdoors as well. Since this shading device is very 
flexible, it can be a suitable solution for most building types and for every façade orientation [22]. 
External venetian blinds are wider and thicker compared to the internal ones. The reason for that 
is that the external shadings should be more resistant to the weather conditions. Movability of the 
system is one of the biggest advantages. The moving slats can be stopped with an angle that the 
direct solar radiation that causes high illuminances on the work plane and excessive solar heat 
==PAGE:26==
17 
 
gain can be blocked. Furthermore, in this way, the diffusion and the reflection of the rest solar 
radiation can create a good daylight in the room, avoiding glare.  
 
Figure 2.8 Venetian blinds' principle of operation 
Many decisions should be taken before installing venetian blind as a shading system. There are a 
lot of different options for this device which also have an impact at the performance of the 
venetian blinds. Geometrical properties, such as the width of slats, material properties, such as 
thermal and optical properties of the material of the slats or even the tilted angle can be important 
factors that influence the performance and should be considered before the application. 
2.4.2.3 Roller blinds 
Similarly, like the venetian blinds, roller blinds are a retractable shading system that can be 
installed at the internal or at the external side of the window or even between the glazing (mid-
pane). In general, it is a flat screen fabric applied to reflect the solar radiation. External roller 
blinds can also provide protection against excessive solar heat gain more effectively than the 
interior ones. But both types are efficient to provide comfort and a good indoor environment [22]. 
The screen fabric can also be transparent in order to allow visual contact with the outdoor 
environment when the device is activated. On the other hand, if the fabric is not transparent, the 
device can be rolled up in order not to obstruct the view to the outside, when there is no direct 
solar radiation or when the solar radiation is needed, like in winter. Their operation is shown in 
Figure 2.9. 
Roller blinds are quite popular devices due to their simplicity. They can be mounted directly on 
the wall or to a window part. The selection should be done carefully because material and color 
of the roller blinds can influence its transmittance, reflectance and absorbance and therefore their 
performance. Roller blinds can vary to their fabric or to their color, but the operation principle is 
always the same.

2.4.2.4 Drapes 
Curtain panels or drapes are an internal shading system that consists of a fabric which is hanged 
from a rail on the wall at the top of a window with the intention to block the solar radiation (Figure 
2.10). Drapes are one of the inexpensive and easy to control solution of shading device. The 
occupants are able to adjust the drapes according to their desire for lighting conditions, indoor 
comfort or visibility to the outside. Drapes are very flexible, and they can be fully drawn, half 
drawn or fully activated blocking the solar radiation and the view to the outdoor environment, 
like roller blinds in Figure 2.9 (b). Since they are internal, they do not affect the building design 
but similarly as with roller blinds, the material and the colors should be considered carefully.

3 OBJECTIVES 
The objective of this study is to evaluate solar control systems for façade renovation strategies in 
different building typologies between 1850-1930 in Copenhagen. The overheating situation in 
such buildings is expected to be unfavorable and the indoor conditions unpleasant for the 
occupants. The installation of high insulation as an energy renovation technique increases this 
problem, especially during specific periods of the year. Knowing that shading systems could 
extremely limit the unwanted solar gains, the impact of selected solar control devices in terms of 
overheating was examined.  
The case study buildings, in which the performance of these shadings was tested, are old 
residential buildings. In these buildings, as any other buildings, daylight is appreciated [24]. One 
challenge of the present study was to include daylight for the occupants when overheating is 
investigated, which most of the times requires contradicting demands. The daylight potential of 
the selected shading devices was thus evaluated using daylight factor and daylight autonomy 
measures.  
Another objective was to observe the effect of human interaction and solar control systems and 
their effect on the overall performance of the systems in relation to overheating. A sensitivity 
analysis was done in order to assess the performance of such devices by simulating combined 
scenarios with different occupancy behaviour.  
The aim was to approach a guideline solution for manual control for solar control systems in 
residential buildings, as part of the main goal which contains elimination of the risk of 
overheating, maintaining a healthy indoor environment and less need for cooling options.

4 METHODOLOGY 
 
4.1 Main procedure 
The methodological approach for investigating the overheating problem is descripted with an 
overall comprehensive view in the following steps. In particular, the aim was to analyze and 
compare different kind of shading devices and their performance with a focus on different control 
strategies.  The ultimate goal was to end up with a guideline for manual control of solar control 
users for the examined type buildings. To this end, a simulation-based process was carefully 
determined.  In this project, IDA Indoor Climate and Energy software and DIVA for Rhino were 
used as simulation tools.  
• 
First, the models of the case study buildings were build and evaluated. So, the current 
overheating situation was recorded.  
• 
The second step was a selection, through a sensitivity analysis, of the method of insulation 
strategy as the energy renovation technic that was applied to the buildings. Therefore, 
performance assessments for three different insulation strategies was done, but only one 
was chosen for both building types. 
• 
Thereafter, in both building typologies, manual shading devices were implemented and 
their effect on the overheating condition of the building were observed. 
• 
As the final step, user behaviour models for manual control of the shadings were built in 
IDA ICE in order to assess their effect.  
4.2 IDA ICE simulations 
For the simulations in this project, the most suitable software seemed to be IDA Indoor Climate 
and Energy (IDA ICE). This software makes it possible to create advanced indoor climate, energy 
analyses and daylight calculations, which can be analyzed in order to determine the performance 
of the building. The user interface of IDA ICE is designed to simplify the way of building and 
simulating both simple and advanced cases, in which whole year detailed and dynamic multi-zone 
simulations are available. The simple process of reporting the results, makes it easy and efficient 
to compare them in different systems and options, that is very useful in a project of sensitivity 
analysis. One, great advantage of using IDA ICE is the capability that of occurring multiple zone 
simulations with additional options such as user behaviour details, variable set points for energy 
systems and also for rooms. So, these features make it possible to see results of multiple zones 
and that makes the analysis on an entire building to be more complex. Although it provides a 
detailed model for simulating and analyzing the energy condition of a building, the calculations 
that IDA ICE does for daylight analysis are not fully developed. However, focus is placed in 
overheating problems, and that is mainly the reason of using IDA ICE as a simulating tool. So, in 
==PAGE:30==
21 
 
order to estimate the actual overheating situation combined with daylight conditions, Diva for 
Rhino was used for the daylight results which is a radiance-based software that uses the physical 
properties of the light.

4.5 Description of the case study buildings 
Among the five different building types introduced in section 2.3, Type 1 and Type 4 of this 
categorization, are selected for the investigation. All building types descripted are residential 
buildings with different façade typologies and were built between 1850-1930. Mainly two façade 
categories can be identified in this era; buildings with heavy masonry brick constructions with 
small window areas and buildings with brick or concrete external walls with much larger glazing 
areas. Buildings in this era, are subject to intensive renovation, which in case of residential 
buildings mostly leads to overheating problems in certain periods of the year.  
So, the reason for choosing the two types for evaluation, was that buildings Type 1 belongs to the 
heavy construction façade category (first mentioned) and building Type 4 to the lighter one 
(second mentioned) with larger glazing areas.  Therefore, assessments of overheating, daylight 
performance and indoor climate were done in buildings of both these two wider categories stated.  
4.6 Case study buildings modeling 
Both case study buildings were created and simulated in IDA ICE software and the results were 
exported to excel for editing. The models were based on the construction details of building types 
that are mentioned; building Type 1 also modeled with thickness variations at each floor.  
In order to have a generic and representative geometrical model, two representative case studies 
were picked and the models in the project were built based these two cases. The basis for the 
geometry, the dimensions of the model, the distribution of rooms and the sizes of the windows 
where set based on these two existing buildings, located at Ahornsgade, 2200 København N 
(building Type 1) and at Novembervej, 2730 Herlev (building Type 4) in Copenhagen.  
On each floor of the both case study buildings, there are more than one apartments located. All 
apartments consist of living room, bedroom and kitchen, but only one critical room from one 
apartment was chosen for the performance assessments; and that was the living room with the 
largest window area compared to the other rooms.  
For the simulations the focus was on the most typical apartment layout at ground floor, first floor 
and the pre-last floor in each apartment, from where a south-facing room was selected to serve 
out simulation experimental design. The purpose of a simple and not heavy model in IDA ICE is 
not only to reduce modelling time, but also simulation time, and that is practical for more and 
faster simulations at a given time. Also, the specific method allowed this project to assess more 
variables and conditions for the two building types.

4.7 Insulation selection 
In IDA ICE, preliminary simulations were done to compare three different kinds of insulation 
strategies. The purpose of this investigation was to compare the overheating situation of the 
building, after the implementation of the most common renovation strategies in Denmark. The 
investigation was performed in building Type 1, but for the selection both types were considered, 
since the same form of insulation was decided to be applied in order for the final results to be 
comparable. Insulation strategies with different applications and thicknesses are chosen for the 
analysis.  
The specific renovation technics that are shown at the following figures were applied to the case 
study according to the recommendations for construction and thicknesses given in the “Store 
bygninger, Klimaskærm og installationer” [26]. 
In IDA ICE, simulations in all three floors were done to assess how each renovation technic can 
affect the overheating situation and the daylight levels of the building, that each renovation 
technic can cause. 
4.7.1 Internal additional insulation 
In option No. 1, an additional internal insulation of the external massive brick wall is modeled. It 
is important to be aware that the interior additional insulation is the second optimum solution for 
an outer wall renovation because moisture problems can be caused, whereas the space of the room 
is reduced. However, these buildings in the central area of Copenhagen, often have a great 
architectural value and represent an important part the Danish heritage, that any kind of additional 
renovation which can alter the appearance of the façade is not under discussion. 
Videncenter for Energibesparelser recommends the additional insulation to be up to 100 mm for 
moisture – sensitive reasons. Together with the insulation thickness, the wall will not meet the 
requirements of BR15. Even so, the approach will still be attractive in terms of reducing energy 
consumption and counteracting cold radiation and cold drops from cold wall surfaces [26]. 
==PAGE:39==
30 
 
So, in the first option, interior additional insulation with 95 mm thickness was chosen and Figure 
4.3 shows its application to the external wall of building Type 1.

it prevents thermal bridges and eventual mold. Since most of the buildings were not constructed 
by following any building regulations, they have high heat losses through the outer walls. For this 
reason, requirements and properties for external thermal insulation are recommended, always 
considering architectural and constructive conditions [26].

4.7.3 Rebuild façade with additional insulation 
Option No. 3 is a rebuild façade, which is hanged to the existing construction together with the 
insulation layer and that does not alter the appearance of the façade.  This energy solution is 
especially applicable to buildings with few facade details, typically masonry buildings from 1940 
to 1970. It can be applied to all masonry surfaces as an alternative to exterior insulation with 
plaster or cladding. The rebuild façade is constructed with a new layer of insulation, if there is an 
existing one, it is taken down and recycled, and a new layer of bricks that is composing the final 
form of the façade [26]. 
In this strategy, the thickness of the insulation varies from floor to floor due to the thickness of

4.9 Modeling of occupants’ behaviour 
In general, occupants are motivated to control the shading devices based on the current weather 
situation rather than a future forecast [38]. Particularly, the occupants are more influenced from 
the interior conditions of a room instead the exterior ones. For that reason, it is important for this 
project to investigate these physical parameters, such as  the transmitted solar radiation to the 
inside or the temperature [38]. Besides sunlight on the façade and operative temperature, daylight 
level at the workplane is a significant reason for closing the shading devices in order to prevent 
glare, any brightness on work surfaces and other forms of visual discomfort [39]. 
4.9.1 Irradiance and blind use 
Although solar irradiance is an important factor, it lacks measurement specifications for the most 
optimum result. For instance, it is unknown where is better to be measured, what the threshold 
should be or even if an absolute threshold is sufficient [39]. 
Therefore, two scenarios about the solar irradiance were examined. The first scenario of 
occupants’ behaviour depends on the direct solar radiation on the window. The occupants change 
the occlusion according to the exterior irradiance and the penetration depth. World Meteorological 
Organization defined as the threshold value of direct solar irradiance at 120 W/m² [40]. So, in this 
project, when solar radiation increases over 120 W/m², blinds are manually activated, and it falls 
below this value, blinds are retracted [41]. In IDA ICE, the default setpoint for lower the shades 
is at 100 W/m2 of global radiation at the outer side of the window. But the shading control limit 
used in all the simulations that have been done in this project was 120 W/m², even when the 
relation of irradiance with the shading systems is not a reference factor.  
The second scenario is about the vertical interior illuminance on façade. If vertical interior 
illuminance is over a certain trigger limit, solar shadings are manually lowered. The assumption 
for this limit is based on a previous research of algorithms that describe the manual control of 
window blinds, and concludes with a threshold of 233 W/m² [42]. 
4.9.2 Air temperature and blind use 
Probably the best predictor that occupants are likely to manually activate the shadings, is the 
ambient indoor temperature and occupants’ tolerance to overheating. Thus, in scenario No.3, the 
shadings are manually drawn when the mean indoor temperatures is above 25oC [43] over the 
past 24 h or as soon as it exceeds the value of 26◦C [44], in order to provide good comfort 
conditions. 
==PAGE:55==
46 
 
4.9.3 Daylight at workplace and blind use 
In occupants’ behaviour scenario No.4, the amount of daylight is the determinant factor of 
controlling the shading systems. The occupants fully lower the solar shadings, when the incoming 
direct solar irradiance exceeds an acceptable value, with the intention to reduce glare and 
brightness on work surfaces [41]. Reviews of published papers on occupants’ behaviour and 
preferences conclude that effective daylight illuminances fluctuate between 100 – 500 lux and 
desirable or at least tolerable ones are within a range of 500 – 2000 lux [45].  
The objective of this scenario was to build an algorithm that uses the interior horizontal 
illuminance at the workplane to control the solar shadings. And the assumption for a control set 
point in the daylighting was at 500 lux  an acceptable illuminance value for moderately difficult 
visual tasks and color judgment that might be required [46], [24],.

5.1 Metrics for overheating condition assessment  
The main objective of the project is the evaluation of different shading systems and their impact 
to the overall performance of the case study building, in terms of overheating. According to 
Danish Building Regulations 2015 (BR15) [2], the indoor temperature should not exceed 27°C 
for more than 100 hours per year and 28°C for more than 25 hours per year, in order to avoid 
overheating and maintain a pleasant indoor climate.  
The results collected from the simulations are mainly the Overheating Hours, meaning the hours 
that the temperature in a room is higher than 27oC and 28oC during a yearly simulation. Both 
results were collected, edited, checked with the limit values and compared with each other. From 
the graphs, it was noted that the results from both temperature thresholds followed the same trends 
and had peaks and low values at the same point. So, it was decided to analyse the values that 
resulted when the temperature exceed 27oC compared with the limit of 100 hours, in order to 
avoid obtaining the same information and redundant graphs. Therefore, the abbreviation “OH” 
(Overheating Hours) is referred to the hours that the indoor temperature exceeds the 27oC 
threshold. Other related graphs can also be found in Appendices. 
==PAGE:60==
51 
 
Besides overheating, which is the main aspect of this study, another interesting point of view to 
estimate the performance of the shading devices is daylighting. As mentioned in section 4, IDA 
ICE was used for the Daylight Factor calculations at the insulation strategies assessment. Even 
though this software is a not fully developed for daylight analysis, a general overview of the 
daylight conditions can still be achieved. In this section, where accuracy is needed for the 
evaluation of the selected solar control systems, daylight results are taken from Diva for Rhino 
which uses the physical properties of the light for the simulations. In order to show the daylight 
performance of the shading devices daylight autonomy (DA) was used. DA is an annual climate-
based metric, which describes the percentage of the floor area that receives sufficient daylight for 
at least 50% of the occupied hours on a yearly basis. The daylight performance was evaluated 
using an existing dataset on Building Type 1 [28]. This building has the worse daylight condition 
due to is small window size and think exterior walls

5.7 Summary of results  
The general finding for all solar shading systems is that by their installation, as a renovation 
technique to older buildings, can definitely improve the previous overheating situation. The only 
exemption which increased the risk of overheating more than the Reference Case was when Roller 
blinds with black fabric (S5.2) were applied. Their low reflectance value prevented the heat gains 
to leave the building.  
A major observation about the external shading devices is that by their activation, overheating 
hours were diminished, preventing the problem to be caused again. At the same time, in three 
internal cases of building Type 1, the overheating hours on the ground floor are less than 100, but

6 CONCLUCIONS 
Energy simulations were performed for the different building typologies between 1850-1930 in 
Copenhagen. In order to eliminate the risk of overheating, three insulation strategies, six solar 
shading systems, four scenarios of occupants’ behaviour and surrounding environment were taken 
into account as the experimental parameters. Several combinations of these external and internal 
conditions were examined with the intention to reduce vulnerability to overheating in both 
buildings. Daylight performance was also included in some cases, which is a crucial factor for 
wellbeing, visual comfort and a appropriate indoor environment.  
The aim was to approach a guideline solution for manual control for solar control systems in 
residential buildings, as part of the main goal, which contains elimination of the risk of 
overheating, maintaining a healthy indoor environment and less need for cooling options. 
6.1 Conclusions: Overheating and daylight - Impacts of solar control 
shadings 
Trying to find a balance between desirable indoor climate related to temperature and visual 
comfort for the occupants can become increasingly difficult. Analyzing the results showing the 
relation between overheating conditions and daylighting in the rooms, proved hard to find some 
renovation strategies that improves both situations. The installation of solar control shadings, 
internal or external, made the daylight conditions worse on every floor. However, Awning blinds, 
despite being an external system, was the optimum solution among the selected shading devices.  
With regard to the results related to the performance of the shading systems, the investigation 
showed that external shading devices provide approximately the same daylight compared to the 
internal ones, but more significant overheating protection.  
On the contrary, regarding the internal shading systems, Drapes and Venetian blinds slightly 
improved the overheating situation of the Reference Case, but not enough to eliminate the risk. 
Even when surrounding building were present and the overheating was already reduced by a 
significant percentage, overheating could not be prevented at the upper floor. 
Moreover, an interesting finding about shadings is that the overheating conditions can also be 
influenced by the reflectance of their material. In fact, more reflective materials result in less 
overheating hours.  
 
==PAGE:87==
78 
 
6.2 Overheating in different building types 
A consequence of comparing the results between the two building types is the better performance 
of building Type 1. A significant difference at their constructions is the thicker external wall of 
building Type 1, especially on the ground floor. Although heavier material, such as concrete, are 
able to store more thermal heat [1] by applying specific strategies, the high thermal mass can 
improve the overheating situation [4]. Therefore, based on the percentages of the Reference Cases 
and when shadings were installed in both building types, the higher thermal mass of building 
Type 1 proved to be more beneficial in terms of overheating, as indicated in [4]. 
6.3 Overheating and insulation as a renovation technique   
The investigation of different insulation strategies showed that the three examined techniques 
enhanced the risk of overheating and decreased the daylight levels of the building.  In general, 
internal insulation performed better, either by reducing the overheating hours or by improving the 
amount of daylight compared to the case study building. Additionally, similar effects were 
observed to the selected external insulation. 
6.4 Overheating and occupants’ behaviour on shading devices   
The four occupant behaviour models that were studied show that manual interaction with shadings 
have an effect on the OH results.  This effect is small, however, a guideline to building occupants 
derived from the results, could raise awareness and help with reduction of the overheating hours. 
Such approach is not costly but effective. Based on the analysis of the effect of human interaction 
on solar control systems, indoor temperature (when internal shadings are applied) and direct solar 
radiation on the window (when external shadings are applied) were the experimental parameters 
that resulted in less overheating hours. These findings of this study support the idea that current 
weather conditions and current indoor environment are the factors that motivate people to control 
the shadings [38].  
Besides direct solar radiation, occupants can rely on the daylight on workplace to control external 
shading devices as an efficient solution to prevent overheating conditions.  
The study on the two different building types showed that the overheating situation of the building 
the is not affected by different occupant behaviour. In other words, same occupant behaviour 
decreased the overheating hours for both buildings. Knowing that the building types are 
independent from different occupant behaviour, an extended study and research into occupant 
schedule and interaction with shadings that can be used for all building types proves to be 
beneficial.

7 FUTURE WORK 
Finding the optimal shading control strategy is a very complex task and the six presented in this 
thesis only serve as starting points for further simulations. Evaluating a larger sample of shading 
systems, including mid-pane devices, would help for a broader view.  Evaluating the performance 
of a wider range of shadings would make it possible to choose the proper one in case of a 
renovation.  
Usually overheating solutions and daylight require contradicting approaches, thus it is 
recommended that daylight simulations should be performed.  Future work should focus on 
enhancing the quality of the indoor environment. More affecting internal and external factors, e.g. 
indoor materials, indoor furniture, vegetation and surrounding buildings, building shapes, etc., 
should be considered and evaluated.   Certainly, further work needs to be done regarding other 
orientations. investigation of south-west rooms would be carried out would lead to interesting 
findings.  
In this study, we defined the Base Case to have a constant ventilation. By doing so, we eliminated 
the effect of ventilation systems. This can be considered as a limitation despite being an 
experimental design strategy. Therefore, further simulation where ventilation systems are 
considered in the simulation can enhance our understanding for wise selection of renovation 
strategies for the study buildings.  
Another limitation of the study caused by the experimental design is exclusion of, of the attic and 
the basement in calculations could lead to more accurate results. The complex behavior of the two 
spaces, was the reason for this exclusion. However, this decision allowed us to have a comparative 
study on the performance of the shading systems which serves the purpose of the current study. 
Hence, future work could be considered for more accurate simulation with consideration of these 
spaces.  
Finally, the results the study on occupant behavior and interaction with shading systems, however 
simplified, but are encouraging to examine further scenarios in order to reach a more conclusive, 
generic and detailed set of guidelines for building users in residential spaces. A focus on humans, 
as an unpredictable behavior, is recommended where more occupant models can be included in 
the study. Such alternative could be extended to real world cases with the values estimated by the 
current residents of the apartments and considering some influencing parameters that are usually 
simplified during simulations. Comparable data on existing buildings with different shading 
systems would lead to more realistic results.
