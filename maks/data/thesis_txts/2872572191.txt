Abstract
This thesis investigates whether flexible machine learning models can improve over cur­
rent best regression models giving estimations of 2­meter air temperature (T2m) over Arctic
sea ice. Infrared satellites can derive surface temperatures (Tskin) by measuring thermal
radiation emitted by the surface, providing broad spatial coverage. Building upon previous
work using linear regression to estimate T2m from satellite­derived Tskin, this thesis evalu­
ates three machine learning models, Elastic Net, XGBoost, and Multi­layer Perceptron, to
estimate T2m from satellite data and auxiliary inputs. The models use Tskin records from
the gap­free satellite dataset of combined sea and sea ice surface temperatures available
through Copernic Marine Service. Accurate estimation of T2m over the Arctic is critical due
to rapid climate change and Arctic Amplification. Current reanalysis products like ERA5,
combining observations with numerical models, exhibit warm biases in the Arctic due to
sparse in situ observations. Data was chronologically split into training (70%) and test sets
(30%). Additional features engineered include polynomial terms of Tskin, seasonal Fourier
harmonics, and cloud cover masks. A baseline ridge regression model, re­implemented
from prior work, using only Tskin and first harmonic seasonality, achieved a test RMSE of
3.05°C. Each machine learning model improved upon this baseline by approximately 9%,
with XGBoost reaching the lowest test RMSE of 2.77 °C and an R2 score of 0.947. The
summer months showed particularly significant improvements, reducing RMSE by up to
50% in some cases. Feature importance analyses found Tskin and its quadratic interac­
tion with the first seasonal harmonic as key predictors. Overall, this thesis showed that
data driven machine learning models can contribute to reducing uncertainty in Arctic T2m
estimations, as a reliable alternative to reanalysis products.

1
Introduction and Motivation
1.1
Problem and Significance
The Arctic regions are experiencing significant climate changes, and at a much faster
rate than the rest of the globe [1, 2, 3]. This phenomenon is known as Arctic amplifi­
cation, and contributes to the melting of the Greenland Ice Sheet (GrIS) and rising sea
levels. Therefore, accurate monitoring of climate warming in the Arctic is very impor­
tant, as underestimating the rate of warming can lead to severe consequences for global
ecosystems and human health [4, 5].
The air temperature at a height of 2 meters (T2m) is considered one of the key indicators
to assess global climate changes [6, 7]. Currently, estimates of T2m rely on so­called
reanalysis products, such as the common standard ECMWF Reanalysis v5 (ERA5)[8]
and JRA­55 [9], or observational gridded datasets like Berkeley Earth [10].
Reanalysis products combine in situ observations with numerical weather prediction mod­
els to produce a consistent dataset in a gridded format. ERA5 have been shown to exhibit
a persistent warm bias over the Arctic, where in situ observations are sparse due to the
extreme weather conditions, and T2m estimates have to lean heavily on model physics
[11, 12, 13].
Given the sparse in situ observations, remote sensing offers a practical alternative for
obtaining more accurate estimates of T2m over the Arctic [5]. In contrast to the sparse
in situ observations from automatic weather stations (AWS) and drifting buoys, infrared
satellites can offer a much greater spatial coverage.
This comes with a challenge as satellites do not directly measure T2m, but instead mea­
sures the thermal radiation emitted by the surface and converts that signal to surface
temperature at the highest layer of the surface, known as skin temperature (Tskin). Addi­
tionally, cloud cover blocks the sensors, which leaves large gaps in observations.
Tskin and T2m have been shown to be strongly correlated, but not identical [5], with the gap
between these temperatures changing with various weather conditions. Building on that
finding, Nielsen­Englyst et al. [14] fitted a simple linear regression model that predicts
daily mean T2m from clear­sky Tskin and some auxiliary meterological information. When
validated against in situ data, the satellite­derived T2m was able to outperform ERA5 in
most cases.
Nielsen­Englyst et al. [6] has since produced a Tskin dataset providing daily fields of sea
surface temperature (SST) and ice surface temperature (IST) north of 58° latitude at a
spatial resolution of 0.05° × 0.05° (hereafter referred to as the L4 SST/IST dataset).
This thesis moves beyond the work of Nielsen­Englyst et al. [6, 15] by exploring flexible
machine learning models to more accurately estimate the link between Tskin and T2m over
Arctic sea ice. With access to the recent L4 Arctic SST/IST dataset, the machine learning
models have a significantly greater quantity of data to learn from.

1.2
Overall Goal
The goal is to implement, evaluate and compare statistical machine learning models capa­
ble of providing robust estimates of 2­meter air temperatures (T2 m) from satellite­derived
skin temperature (Tskin) and selected auxiliary predictors over Arctic sea ice.
The quality of a model is evaluated based on three main criteria:
Predictive error: Prediction errors should be reduced as much as possible, and be lower
relative to current methods existing over Arctic sea ice.
Reliability The model should deliver consistent performance across the entire problem
domain, so that its estimates can be trusted under varying conditions.
Parsimony: The ideal model includes exactly the complexity necessary to capture the
underlying relationship for interpretability and simplicity. In other words, the increase
in complexity is only worth it if model generalization is improved.
Predictive error and reliability are considered the most important criteria, while parsimony
is viewed as a desirable but not strictly necessary aspect. In practice, this means that the
best model may be more complex than the simplest model, as long performance justifies
the added complexity. These criteria therefore serve as a balanced checklist for model
comparison. A secondary objective is to learn about the reliability of the available data
in supporting robust and unbiased predictions. This involves investigating which features
carry the greatest influence on predictions and potential limitations of the data.
1.3
Thesis Structure
This thesis is structured as follows: Section 2 documents the literature review, giving an
overview of previous relevant methodologies and studies. Section 3 presents the theo­
retical foundations and methodology used. Section 4 gives a descriptive overview of data
and the preprocessing steps performed. Section 5 explores relationships and patterns in
the data through exploratory analysis. Section 6 details the conducted experiments and
presents their results. Section 7 discusses the significance and limitations of the find­
ings and recommendations for future work. Finally, Section 8 concludes with a concise
summary of the main findings and contributions of the thesis.

2
Literature Review
2.1
Review Strategy and Criteria
A purposive literature review was carried out to evaluate methods for estimating T2m pri­
marily under Arctic conditions and in other spatial domains, due to limited literature cover­
ing the Arctic. Without being completely exhaustive, this review aims to analyze a broad
range of recent work to obtain an understanding of the field, identify gaps, and position
this thesis within the literature.
The review includes peer­reviewed scientific articles and generally excludes preprints with
the exception of some well­established methods (e.g. XGBoost [16]) which are mainly
used as references in the methodology in subsequent sections within this thesis. The
initial literature search queries were restricted to papers published within the last 5 years,
but including earlier papers was not an explicit restriction. This review will still contain
older articles based on high relevance (e.g. Nielsen­Englyst 2019, [5]).
The reviewed literature on estimating T2m falls into two main categories. First, many
studies address a point­wise prediction task, using meteorological variables as input to
regression models, to predict T2m in locations where direct observations exist. This is
the most directly relevant category that closely relates to the problem. Second, the work
focuses on generating complete spatial fields of T2m using methods for interpolation, to
reconstruct T2m in a gridded format over a broad domain, often evaluating their results
against ERA5 data. Although the interpolation problem is distinct in its formulation, they
both share the common end goal of reconstructing T2m. These two problems are often
tackled together, and therefore both are valuable to review.
2.2
Results
2.2.1
Arctic Domain
Nielsen­Englyst et al. [6] fitted ridge regression models for land ice and sea ice to convert
clear­sky satellite observations of Tskin to T2m. Over sea ice, they used daily average in
situ observations of T2m from Automatic Weather Station (AWS) and drifting buoys (from
various campaigns) covering the period 2003­2009, matched up with satellite observa­
tions of Tskin.
The best model achieved an RMSE of 3.20°C over sea ice, and a correlation coefficient
of 96.5 %. While showing a great fit to the data, the model is very simple, using only Tskin
and two additional linear terms that capture seasonality. After training, the model was
applied to a larger field to produce a consistent field of T2m over the Arctic. They found
that it validates as well or better than ERA5, further emphasizing the value of accurately
predicting T2m from satellite data (This is the baseline model described in Section 3.4.1).
Ma et al. [17] combine T2m observations from ERA5 ship reports, drifting ice stations
of Russian North Pole (NP) and International Arctic Buoy Programme (IABP) buoys to
reconstruct T2m air temperature on a consistent grid over the Arctic using a deep learning
convolutional network. Their model was trained with T2m information from ERA5 and ERA­
Interim reanalysis datasets. For validation over the Arctic Ocean, they used drifting ice
stations, which were explicitly excluded from their training and reconstruction process,

2.2.2
Other Domains
Although models used in other domains are not directly comparable in terms of prediction
error metrics, they illustrate a variety of modeling approaches and techniques that may
be adapted to T2m over Arctic sea ice.
1. Linear modelling: Linear regression models provide transparent and interpretable
baselines, but are usually surpassed by nonlinear methods. Some region specific
examples of studies using linear model approaches include Moroccan and Viet­
namese mountains [18, 19], Mediterranean parts of Spain [20, 21] and Chinese
terrain [22]. Every study here uses linear regression as a comparison for various
other approaches, similarly to the structure of this thesis, and each of them find it to
be outperformed by nonlinear methods
2. Tree­based ensembles: Random Forest, XGBoost and related tree gradient boost
methods consistently show the lowest prediction errors in comparative analysis, with
XGBoost in particular standing out. This was the outcome of a number of studies
covering the GrIS, Moroccan and Vietnamese mountains [18, 19], the Antarctic [23],
Mediterranean parts of Spain [20], parts of China [24, 22] and Poland [25]. Strong
performance is attributed to their ability capture to nonlinear interactions, with mixed­
scales and sparse data.
3. Neural Networks / Deep learning: Fully connected neural networks (NN) and tem­
poral Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) or Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM)
variants has been shown to yield additional gains where large amounts of data
is available.
Examples of success include a neural network marginally beating
Random Forest (RF) on the GrIS [26] and a temporal CNN [4] in which several
temporal models were applied. The temporal aspect means models take the history
of observations into account rather than making only point­wise predictions. The
complexity in their architecture and training process varies widely, but even sim­
ple architectures of fully connected neural networks have been shown to perform
well against tree­based methods. Che. et al. [26] found a shallow NN as the best
performer among a variety of models, and Pang et al. found it to be on par with
tree­based metods [23].
4. Kriging: Several studies also feature so called kriging methods such as Ordinary
Kriging (OK) and Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) [26, 20, 25]. Kriging is a
geostatistical method that uses spatial correlation (or spatio­temporal [27]) to inter­
polate values at unobserved locations based on observed data. However, they are
often outperformed by machine learning methods, as was the case for Hassani et

2.3
Thesis Positioning
In summary, the studies above are often implementing several methods in parallel, com­
paring their performance on the given domain, in which XGBoost and RF often show the
lowest prediction errors, with XGBoost often standing out [21, 25, 22]. Deep learning can
be competitive when data is plentiful, but simpler architectures have also shown to work
well [26, 23]. However, the exact outcomes of each study is very domain­specific, and
with the high complexity of many of these models, the most straightforward way to dis­
cover which model is the best fit, is to implement them and compare their performance
on the given domain.
Despite the broad use of reanalysis products that reconstruct T2m, and their biases over
the Arctic, there is a lack of research targeting the correction of this bias using satelitte
data. Only two such studies could be found (Nielsen­Englyst et al. [6] and Ma et al. [17]).
In summary, Nielsen­Englyst et al. [6] showed that a transparent linear model can perform
well, but is a simple architecture that had been shown to be outperformed by more com­
plex models in other domains. Ma et al. [17] used a deep learning model to reconstruct
T2m over the Arctic aiming to improve over ERA5 reanalysis, but with a limited validation
sample size, whether the outcome is a significant improvement in this region remains
unclear.
This thesis thus contributes to the knowledge gap by exploring new methods for recon­
structing T2m over Arctic sea ice more accurately, with access to a larger and more diverse
set of in situ observations through the L4 SST/IST dataset. The selection of models in
this thesis is explained and discussed in more detail in Sections 3 and 7.

3
Theory and Methods
3.1
Data Collection
Learning a model for estimating T2m requires paired values of Tskin and T2m (hereafter
referred to as matchups).
Data collection has been performed by the Danish Meterological Institute (DMI), who pro­
vided access to the data used in this thesis. This section gives a summary of the data
sources and matchup procedure, as described by Nielsen­Englyst et al. [15].
1. Satellite skin temperature (Tskin) Polar­orbiting infrared satelittes measure the ra­
diance emitted by the surface under clear­sky conditions, from which skin temper­
ature Tskin is derived. The skin temperature is defined as the temperature of the
topmost layer of the surface, whether over open water, sea ice or snow. The L4
SST/IST dataset then combines multiple clear­sky observations through optimal in­
terpolation and bias corrections to generate daily fields of gap­free Tskin estimates
representative of all­sky conditions.
2. In situ 2­meter air temperature (T2m). In situ T2m observations assembled numer­
ous campaigns, using mass balance buoys (SIMB deployed by CRREL), drifting
buoys (deployed by ECMWF) and drifting ice stations (Russian “North Pole” sta­
tion (NP), Russian “North Pole” stations). These measure air temperature at a height
of approximately two meters above the surface. To ensure consistency with the daily
satellite product, these high resolution in situ observations are quality controlled and
then averaged to daily means.
While the daily fields of Tskin exist on a regular grid, the in situ T2m observations are point­
based observations that do not fit in with the satellite grid cells. Therefore, satellite data
and in situ observations have been collocated for training machine learning models. The
matchup procedure involves aligning the daily fields of gap­free L4 SST/IST dataset with
daily means of in­situ T2m observations. For each day, in situ observations are matched to
the nearest satellite Tskin grid cell as long as the distance between the in situ observation
and the grid cell center is within a distance criterion.
Winds from ERA5 have also been collocated with in situ observations, in a similar fashion,
though ERA5 has a coarser spatial resolution of 0.25° compared to the 0.05° of the L4
SST/IST dataset.
For each matchup, the difference between Tskin and T2m is defined and used throughout
this thesis:

3.2.2
Overfitting and Regularization
Overfitting is a very common problem in statistics and machine learning, which typically
happens when a model is too complex. The model “memorizes” the random noise fluctu­
ations in its training data, failing to learn a generalization of the underlying relationships.
When the model performs well on the training data and poorly on new unseen observa­
tions, it is typically a sign of overfitting.
Avoiding overfitting comes down to balancing the so­called bias­variance trade­off:
1. Bias is the error due to the models overly simplistic assumptions about the problem.
A high bias will cause an algorithm to miss the relevant relations between features
and target outputs (underfitting).
2. Variance is the error due to excessive sensitivity to small fluctuations in the training
set. High variance can cause an algorithm to model the random noise in the training
data rather than the intended outputs (overfitting).
The goal is to find a model that minimizes both bias and variance errors.

Regularization is a technique that penalizes complexity, forcing the model to make simpler
assumptions about the data. Exact implementation of regularization is different depending
on the model. Standard regularization terms are ridge L2 which prevents coefficients
from growing too large, and rasso L1 which filters out irrelevant features by setting their
coefficients to zero. Regularization is one way to reduce variance, but it can also increase
bias. Thus, the challenge lies in finding the right balance between the two. Another way
of preventing overfitting is to simply use a less complex model, which is often preferred.
3.2.3
Hyperparameter Tuning
Each model addressed is configured by a set of adjustable hyperparameters which define
how the model will learn. Hyperparameters depend largely on the specific models, but
typically regularization strength is adjusted through hyperparameters. Because these set­
tings directly influence the bias­variance trade­off, they must be chosen carefully, through
hyperparameter tuning.
All models go through a similar procedure of inner cross­validation (CV) on the training
set:
1. Split the training data into 6 folds.
2. For each candidate hyperparameter configuration:
(a) Train the model on 5/6 folds.
(b) Validate on the remaining fold.
(c) Repeat against every combination of fold, holding out a different fold for vali­
dation each time (6 total).
3. Compute the average validation error across all 6 folds.
4. Select the configuration with the lowest average error.
The aim is to optimize generalization by choosing the parameters that perform best across
a wide range of data, rather than one particular subset. Six folds were chose based on
the number of available data points, to ensure that each fold has enough data to provide
a reliable estimate of the model performance.
The model­specific differences are in the way candidates get selected. The specific algo­
rithms for tuning are covered in their respective theory sections.
3.2.4
Modeling Pipeline
Building on this fundamental setup, a common end­to­end procedure is defined for every
implementation of every model, to ensure fairness in comparison:
1. Feature matrix construction: Predictor variables for all rows are gathered into the
feature matrix X
2. Data split: The dataset is divided into training set and test set.
3. Standardization: Each predictor is centred to zero mean and scaled to unit vari­
ance using statistics computed on the training set only. This helps prevent any
single feature from dominating during training.
4. Hyperparameter tuning and model selection: Hyperparameter tuning is performed
according to the procedure defined above.

3.4.3
eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost)
XGBoost [16] is a tree boosting method known for its strong predictive performance on
tabular data. Several studies in the literature review have found it to outperform other
approaches in similar domains (e.g.[18, 19, 23]).
Although often considered a “black box” model (which compromises parsimony), the
demonstrated high performance makes it a relevant choice since quality of prediction
was the main priority. As noted in the research goals, parsimony was secondary to over­
all reliability and performance. Integrating XGBoost into the modeling pipeline is relatively
straightforward thanks to the scikit-learn implementation.
In a nutshell
XGBoost builds an ensemble of shallow regression trees. Boosting adds
trees one by one so that every new tree reduces the remaining residuals of the previous
trees. The final prediction is the sum of outputs from all trees. See Appendix C.3 for the
specified objective function.
Hyperparameter tuning
The tuning was performed using the optimization framework
Optuna [30], which automatically searches for good hyperparameters by minimizing RMSE,
based on a defined search space over a number of so­called trials (i.e. candidate hyper­
parameter configurations). Each trial is evaluated by training the model on the training set
and validating it on the inner CV folds, as described in Section 3.2.4. The search space
was defined for some key hyperparameters, focusing on important settings that control
tree size, learning dynamics, and regularization strength. This is defined in Section 6.
The model was configured to balance the fit with complexity by including three penalty
terms in the objective: a cost γ for each additional leaf (favoring simpler trees), and reg­
ularization of the leaf weights L2 (λ2) and L1 (α). Both L1 and L2 may be unnecessary,
but like previously mentioned, the search procedure should learn to ignore one if it is not
needed, while still allowing the model to benefit from the other.
Tuned hyperparameters
The following hyperparameters were tuned:
• nestimators: Total number of trees in the ensemble. More trees can improve fit, but
also increase the risk of overfitting.
• max_depth: Maximum depth of each tree. Deeper trees can capture more complex
relationships, but also risk overfitting.
• Learning rate η: Scales the contribution of every new tree.
• subsample: Fraction of training rows sampled for each tree. Introduces some ran­
domness which acts as regularization.
• colsample_bytree: Fraction of predictor columns sampled per tree. Similarly intro­
duces randomness as regularization.
• γ: Minimum loss reduction required to make a split. This is a way to control tree
complexity, as it will only allow splits that that reduce the loss significantly.
• α (L1 penalty): L1 regularization on leaf weights.

Data Description
4.1
Introduction to Dataset
The L4 SST/IST dataset is provided by DMI through Copernicus Marine Environment
Monitoring Service (CMEMS), providing gridded daily fields of Tskin at a spatial resolution
of 0.05° [15]. The term “L4”, meaning “level 4”, refers to it being a gridded gap­free analysis
product, derived in the process described in Section 3.1. Thus, it does not provide the
actual observations but rather a corrected and interpolated field with the best estimate of
Tskin at each grid point.
In contrast to L4, Level 3 (L3) products consist of gridded averages of individual clear­sky
satellite observations, which inherently contain gaps where observations were obscured
by clouds. The L3 data serves as the input for generating the gap­free L4 fields through
optimal interpolation.
In total the dataset consists of 94126 mathchups, covering the time period. 1982 ­ Dec.
2022. In contrast, Nielsen­Englyst et al. [14], used a subset of these matchups (L3 data)
to train and validate their ridge regression model, for a total of 31146 matchups over the
period 2000­2009. Thus, roughly 3 times the amount is covered here.

for a summary and the general distribution of features discussed here.

4.3
Pre­processing
Because the L4 SST/IST dataset is a reanalysis, it has gone through extensive process­
ing steps to ensure its quality and consistency. For this reason, data cleaning was kept
minimal to preserve the authenticity of the data. However, outliers may still occur, within
the reanalysis itself, the in situ T2m observations or introduced through the matchups. The
preprocessing applied in this study is summarized below:
1. Extreme values of Tdiff: Discarded
|Tdiff| > 3 × σTdiff, where σTdiff was computed
from training data only. This set the threshold value at 11.63C.
2. Several calendar years with incomplete in situ observation coverage, across sea­
sons or no data at all were discarded to keep high quality representation of annual
cycles. This covered years 1991­1995 and 2016­2019.
In total, these steps reduced the dataset from 94126 matchups to 89301 matchups.
Based on observed in situ observed relationships, Nielsen­Englyst et al. specified the
range of observed Tdiff over sea ice and found that Tskin and T2m rarely differ by more
than 3°C [5]. Thus, with a threshold of 11.63°C for extreme values it is assumed with
confidence that these were actual outliers.

5
Exploratory Data Analysis
This section presents the Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) performed on the dataset to
investigate relationships between the features and the target variable, guiding the choice
of features and understanding the problem. All exploratory analysis is performed exclu­
sively on the training data to avoid leakage of information from the test set in modeling
design, respecting the purpose of the test set as completely unseen data.
5.1
Domain Knowledge
Because Tskin is already known to closely correlate with T2m, this section focuses on in­
vestigating patterns in Tdiff, which would help in bridging the remaining gap that simple
linear regression could not. The Arctic climate exhibits a significant level of natural vari­
ability, which can be observed in Tdiff like in the works of Nielsen­Englyst et al. [5]. This
study also included sites not used in this thesis, including land weather stations on the
GrIS. Some of their key findings on the principal relationships between Tskin and T2m can
be summarized as follows:
• Tskin was colder than T2m roughly 85% of the time, 0.65 ­ 2.65 °C at all test sites.
• Tdiff is largest in mid­winter and summer during ice melt periods, and smallest in
summer in non­melting periods. Melt periods are not fixed, but occurs when Tskin
around 0 °C, while T2m may continue to rise.
• Cloud cover contributes to warming the surface, decreasing the mean Tdiff.
• Tdiff decreases steadily with wind speed, as a result of warmer air mixing with the
surface.
These form a premise for the EDA. Because their study was based on in situ data, in­
cluding regions regions not covered here, it is desirable to see investigate if similar rela­
tionships can be observed in the L4 SST/IST dataset.
5.2
Temperature Relationship
Figure 5.1 presents the mean Tdiff as a function of Tskin. Between roughly ­40°C and ­8°C,
the mean Tdiff drops steadily, reaching a minimum of around ­5°C. Beyond this point, as
Tskin approaches 0, Tdiff increases again significantly reaching a maximum around 4°C.
The standard deviation is stable across the range of Tskin, with only slight variations near
the higher temperatures.
This indiciates a rather distinct non­monotonic relationship between Tskin and Tdiff, sug­
gesting that the relationship is more complex than a simple linear regression could cap­
ture.

7
Discussion and Future Work
7.1
Key Results
Recall from the goal of this thesis, that the evaluation criteria for the models were accuracy,
reliability, and parsimony.
Predictive error and reliability
Every new model outperformed the baseline, achieving
RMSE of around 2.78°C on the test set, while the baseline got an RMSE of 3.05 °C, with
very similar uncertainty estimates on their metrics. Nonlinear models achieved R2 scores
of 0.946­0.947 vs R2 0.935 for the baseline, and their predictions were shown to correlate
strongly with the actual values (See Figure 6.5).
While a 9% reduction could seem modest, significant reductions were especially achieved
in summer months, with test RMSE being reduced by up to 50% (See Figure 6.6), and
the spread of errors being significantly smaller as observed by the dip in standard devia­
tion in Figure 6.8. These figures showed that the residuals in every new model showed
homoscedasticity, with some systematic bias still remaining with T2m values above 0 °C,
and very low temperature regimes.
CRPS were also reduced with particularly the ENet and XGBoost achieving reductions to
1.55 °C and 1.53 °C respectively, compared to the baseline of 1.72 °C.
Parsimony
Every new model showed agreement of mainly Tskin and its quadratic term
explaining the majority of the variance in T2m, where they were consistently considered
significantly more important in driving predictions relative to all other features. Including
higher seasonal harmonics also showed a performance improvement, and their interac­
tions with Tskin were also consistently ranked high in importance analysis. Using just these
few features is likely to explain the majority of the variance with a linear model, at least
given the current state of the test data, which implies a simple approach is ideal.
Recall that the baseline features were kept fixed, with just three features compared to the
new models using 31 features.
Comparison to Literature
The prediction errors here are still higher than those ob­
served in the work of Ma et al. [17] (between 2.39°C and 2.51°C). However, they used
just three in situ NP platforms for validation, which is a small sample size relative to that
of this thesis. The reliability of this estimate is further compromised by the variance be­
tween in situ platforms observed in Section 6.5. These in situ platforms come from only
one project, which can make the prediction error overly optimistic by avoiding the variance
between observations of the platform.
This solidifies the reductions achieved in this thesis as a more robust and generalizable
estimate by taking various data sources into account and using a bigger sample size.
7.2
Interpretation of Results
Summer Corrections
Nielsen­Englyst et al. [5] explain that during ice melting condi­
tions, Tskin will tend to reach an upper limit of 0 °C, acting as a thermal buffer, while T2m
can rise above. In the results of this thesis, a different but similar effect was observed in
Machine learning approaches for estimating air temperatures over Arctic sea ice
51
==PAGE:60==
summer months. The mean Tdiff reached −6 to −4° C in the summer months on average,
which is a significant difference from the expected 0° C. Figure 5.2 also demonstrated
an example of Tskin and T2m following closely except between days 150­250, where Tskin
stays somewhat consistently around −5°C.
The reductions in RMSE, specifically in summer months, is likely a result of new models
capturing this gap with the quadratic term of Tskin showing high importance. Polynomial
terms of Tskin allows the model to capture the curvature of the relationship, letting the
prediction follow Tskin closely in non­summer months, and then bend upward in summer
months. This effect can be observed in Figure 6.9, with models learning a closer repre­
sentation of the true curve of T2m from June to September.
The observation that Tdiff behaves differently from in situ observed relationships could
be explained by two things. First, it could be an indicator that the thermal buffer effect
during melt conditions is present, with a systematic bias in the satellite data. Second,
it could be an artifact of interpolation process of the L4 SST/IST dataset, given that the
cloud cover mask heavily overlapped with summer months. Recall that this mask was
computed based on the assumption of missing values from level 3 satellite data.
Spatial Corrections
Figure 6.13 showed that, the biggest RMSE reductions were ob­
served in regions that also contained the least training data overall. This is a counterin­
tuitive finding by itself. However, the residuals of the baseline are also most pronounced
in these same regions, suggesting that the new models are correcting for the biggest
weaknesses of the baseline.
Cloud Cover
Table 6.10 showed that cloud cover biases were reduced across all mod­
els, particularly under cloudy conditions in which a baseline bias of −0.38 was reduced
to the lowest of −0.08 with the ENet model, which is encouraging. However, it is unclear
whether this correction is explicitly handled by the inclusion of cloud cover as a feature, or
implicitly captured through another. For example, it was observed that cloudy conditions
largely coincided with summer months, which is where the biggest RMSE reductions were
observed, meaning this correction in bias could be a reflection of that.
XGBoost actually shows the worst bias of new models under cloud cover, which could be
a sign of overfitting to the training data. This is also supported by the observation that
XGBoost had the lowest training score with a significant gap between training and test
scores.
Sea Ice Concentration
Figure F.2 showed that every new model clearly correct a sys­
tematic bias in residuals under low SIC conditions. However, the step­wise feature se­
lection procedure did not include it as an explicit feature, suggesting that this correction
is implicitly captured in another way. Given the direct linear relationship with Tdiff and SIC
that was observed in 5.7, SIC is likely still an important aspect, particularly if intending to
extrapolate to lower latitudes in future works.
Case Study
Each prediction field in the case study shows looks very similar across
the newer models over Arctic sea ice. Though the overall results suggest a robust for the
region of interest, one should be cautious if extending to broader regions. When looking at
open water regions toward lower latitudes, the predictions fields tend to become smoother
and converge around 0 °C and this effect gets stronger with increasingly complex models.
52
Machine learning approaches for estimating air temperatures over Arctic sea ice
==PAGE:61==
This is a fair outcome given the models are optimized for Arctic sea ice conditions and
will likely overfit if extended beyond domains with different climate dynamics. It does not
diminish their robustness on this domain, but it emphasizes the need for domain specific
training data if expecting it to extrapolate well to other regions. This aligns with the model
showing the best fit to the training data, exhibiting this smoothing effect the most.
Notably, all models show high uncertainty right along the edge of the ice pack. A likely
explanation is these regions are very sparse (See Figure E.1b), and with less information
in these areas, the ensemble spread is also likely to widen more. The baseline, ENet
and XGBoost have very similar uncertainty fields and show surprisingly low uncertainty in
open­water regions.
XGBoost and MLP are non­deterministic methods. This is likely the reason why MLP
in particular shows higher uncertainty relative to other models, as a result of retraining
multiple times. However, the same cannot be said for XGBoost, which is more stable in
its predictions. Using the same approximate uncertainty method against vastly different
types of models is likely not the best and most comparable approach. And given that open
water regions were not the focus, one cannot discredit MLP, or any other model, based
on the results observed with the case study.
With more time, the next step might be to implement probabilistic models. One such ex­
ample is a Mean­Variance Estimation (MVE) neural network [33], which outputs a mean
and variance estimate simultaneously for each prediction and better captures the uncer­
tainty in the predictions.
ERA5 Winds
ERA5 winds were seemingly insignificant predictors, showing only mini­
mal impact through latitude interactions from the SHAP analysis. A potential signal be­
tween the ERA5 winds and Tskin is likely lost due to the coarseness of ERA5. A spatial
resolution of 0.25° vs. the L4 SST/IST datasets 0.05°, the result could be that any subtle
interactions are smoothed out.
Convergence of Results
Every new model exhibit similar patterns in the residuals,
suggesting that they mostly correct for the same systematic biases in the data, rather than
discovering new information with increased complexity. This can especially be observed
temporally (See Figure 6.8), spatially (See Figure 6.13) and against SIC (See F.2). The
optimization of the MLP tells the same story, as the best hyperparameters resulted in a
single hidden layer, which is very simple for a more complex type of model. XGBoost
does not add measurable accuracy beyond this despite the lower RMSE training.
Learning curves for every method converge after roughly 20,000 training samples (Figure
6.7), and the validation loss for the MLP converges after only a handful of epochs (Figure
6.3). The ENet is almost the same the baseline, but with added features and an extra
regularization term. Combined with the similar performance across models, it is likely
that improvements are a result of better feature engineering rather than the models.
XGBoost and MLP have many more possibilities of adjusting their architecture and finding
optimal hyperparameters, but this is likely excessive complexity that likely yields dimin­
ishing returns. All together, this is substantive evidence that a performance ceiling set by
the current feature set and problem framing rather than by model capacity or data volume,
with models converging on the roughly best result of 2.78 °C.

7.3
Spatio­Temporal Framing and Experimental Constraints
T2m has been observed to vary rather smoothly with space and time (see for example
Figure 6.9). Current approaches make predictions in an independent point­wise manner,
not taking advantage of the spatial and temporal coherence of the data.
There are many alternative temporal or spatio­temporal approaches that are able to utilize
these kind of relationships. For example the UNet­like structure used by Vedrí et al. [21]
and LSTM, TCN and N­BEATS by Lee et al. [4] (both from literature review).
Spatio­temporal modeling was considered, but based on the results discussed so far,
believed to be infeasible for effective use with the L4 SST/IST dataset due to two major
data­centric constraints:
1. Sparse, drifting targets With the current matchups, each in situ observational plat­
form provides at most a single daily average. Additionally, in situ platforms drift from
their original positions over time, making it difficult to leverage spatial and temporal
relationships, particularly with methods expecting a fixed grid of targets. This is fur­
ther complicated by the temporal dimension, increasing the size of the state space
enormously. Thus, in situ platforms drifting close to each other simultaneously will
be a rare occurrence.
2. Processed predictors The L4 SST/IST dataset fields already apply optimal inter­
polation in space and time, and then match those with in situ observations of T2m.
Thus Tskin data is no longer reflecting the actual observations, but a smoothed field
of consistent Tskin values. With each layer of processing comes increased uncer­
tainty and risk of the data losing its original signal. Thus, feeding those into a model
that is supposed to discover spatial coherence is unlikely to be accurate.
Constraints of processing
During exploratory data analysis, the hypotheses based
on in situ observed relationships [5] were not able to be replicated with analysis. E.g.
the cloud over bias had an opposite of what was expected in Tdiff, and winds showed no
meaningful relationship with Tdiff. A strong seasonal cycle was observed, but even here
the expected melting conditions were not observed with Tskin rarely reaching 0 °C.
This is probably another result of the processing steps and straying further from direct
observations in addition to the daily averaging smoothing out any diurnal effects, while
the study by Nielsen­Englyst et al. was based on a higher resolution of data.
7.4
Methodological Reflections
Data Partitioning and Preprocessing
The spatial similarity between training data and
test data instills confidence that the results on performance is a good reflection of in­
domain predictive skill. However, a different approach to data partitioning may be bene­
ficial if aiming to evaluate the ability to extrapolate beyond this region. Holding out entire
areas from the split could have better demonstrated the ability of a model to make predic­
tions within an area it has never seen.
The choice of partitioning the data by time was to ensure the performance on the test set
simulating a real­world scenario. This may have been an unnecessary constraint given
the current approach not distinguishing years from each other, and that models do no
leverage temporal relationships. For the same reason, filtering out years with incomplete
data was not necessary, but would likely not affect the results significantly given the small

number of samples removed. On the other hand, better preprocessing would have been
to filter out matchups with SIC = 0%, as the intention was to focus on Arctic sea ice.
The similar residual patterns are likely also partially a result of the test set. Adding more
variation to the test approach may reveal weaknesses in the models that are not ob­
servable here, and reveal potentially whether more complex models may overfit in other
regions. Another way would be partitioning such that data coverage is spread evenly
domain Arctic Ocean, ensuring to get more representation of edge cases where overall
density of data is low.
Baseline Comparison
Recall that baseline features were kept fixed to keep comparison
relative to prior work. Then new models can be compared against the baseline, and
against each other. However, in retrospect it would have been best to keep features
constant across all models, as this would have made it clearer whether the improvements
in RMSE are a result of better model architecture or better features. As discussed, there
is some indication that the improvements are a result of better features, but this approach
would help confirm it.
RMSE Confidence Intervals
The confidence intervals for performance metrics were
computed using a bootstrap approach on the test set. Bootstrapping only the test set can
underestimate the uncertainty because it treats the fitted model and the train test split as
fixed, so it misses the variability in the model itself, which can lead to overly optimistic
confidence intervals.
Feature Importance Analysis
While the standardized ENet coefficients help provide
some understanding of which features drive the predictions, they remain influenced by
the penalty structure and potentially some feature collinearity, obscuring direct interpre­
tation of their improtance. In hindsight, supplementing this ranking with the SHAP analyis
applied on subsequent models would have given a more reliable ranking of feature impor­
tance. Nonetheless, the outcome highly overlaps with the SHAP analysis of the MLP and
XGBoost models, suggesting that the interpretation of importance based on coefficients
is still valid.
Feature Selection
The step­wise feature selection procedure here was a simple solu­
tion to reducing the feature set to only features given some kind of proof of importance,
and was included at a later phase during the thesis. This was probably not the best way
of using likelihood in feature selection. In hindsight, this should have been used earlier
to include a simple Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) model and compare the inclusion of
important features against the baseline, supporting a more ideal approach of starting with
a simple model and working up to more complex models.
7.5
Implications and Future Work
Practical Implications
In summary, there is substantive evidence that new models are
a significant improvement over the baseline, with clearly visible bias corrections across
temporal and spatial dimension, under cloud cover and by SIC. The even spread of resid­
uals and high correlation with actual values suggest that the models are reliable and con­
sistent predictors of T2m over the Arctic Ocean. That said, residuals can surpass 10°C in
rare cases, with a 90th percentile of 5°C (See Table 6.9). This is important to be aware of
if high precision is a concern.

It is also conclusive that MLP and XGBoost are excessive in complexity relative to the
current state of the data, yielding diminishing returns in performance. In practice, the best
bet is likely using ENet for parsimony, or simplify even further, with using nonlinear terms
of Tskin seasonal harmonics with OLS.
Spatio­temporal modeling
A natural next step would be to explore spatio­temporal
modeling approaches. This will require addressing the two issues of sparse, drifting tar­
gets and the uncertainty of the input data. Using level 4 data, which went through layers
of processing and optimal interpolation, could be problematic due to the propagation of
uncertainties from its original processing.
A more direct way to address the uncertainty could be to merge clear­sky L3 Tskin data
with the sparse in situ T2m observations, in a multi­variate optimal interpolation model
[34]. The resulting gridded field of paired Tskin and T2m values would give the fixed spatial
support that many spatio­temporal models require. In principle, a model trained on such
a grid might outperform the current pointwise prediction approach, but this will require
future studies to explore.
Temporal modeling
Time series modeling may be a feasible approach given the current
data, under the assumption that the in situ observational platforms are drifting slow enough
to take advantage of temporal coherences on the short­term. Series of observations from
individual in situ observational platforms can be treated as a time series, and the temporal
coherence can be leveraged to improve predictions. Over longer time scales it will likely
be difficult to distinguish temporal relationships from spatial ones.
Lee et al. [4] showed how incorporating the temporal context could be very effective in pre­
dicting air temperatures from satelitte derived land surface temperatures, outperforming
non­temporal models. A straightforward way of exploring this without complex modeling
could be adding lagged features to existing models. Future studies are encouraged to
explore this further as it shows promise in further improving predictions.
Additional features
Additionally, exploring the inclusion of additional features may im­
prove predictions further. For example, Zampieri et al. [35] included, sea ice thickness,
snow thickness on sea ice, and reanalysis surface downward longwave radiation as ad­
ditional input to skin temperature.
Reconstructing T2m over the Arctic Ocean
This thesis took a narrative of a compar­
ative analaysis of various machine learning approaches which has led to positive results
and insights which can help future studies. Given the context of the converging results,
a more ideal approach might be to focus on optimizing a single model, setting specific
targets for parsimony and operational use.
The case study from this thesis was meant as a proof­of­concept, with the limitation that
it has no T2m field to compare against. Thus, it is not currently possible to say directly
whether the predictions are better or worse than existing reanalysis products, thought
seeing improvements over current best similar solutions is a step forward. The natu­
ral next step would be to compare predictive fields with existing reanalysis products and
investigate whether they can eliminate systematic biases over the Arctic Ocean. Further­
more, it would be far more meaningful to obtain results over a longer period, as using just
a single day cannot be representative of its general performance.

8
Conclusion
This thesis set out to determine whether flexible machine learning models could improve
current best estimations of 2­meter air temperature (T2m) over Arctic sea ice using infrared
satellite observations of skin temperature (Tskin) and auxiliary meteorological predictors.
A baseline was established by reimplementing the linear regression model from Nielsen­
Englyst et al. [6], and three models implemented and tuned to compare against the base­
line, namely ENet, XGBoost and MLP. All new models lowered the baseline RMSE from
3.05 °C to about 2.78 °C, which is a reduction of approximately 9%. XGBoost achieved
the lowest RMSE of 2.77 °C. Improvements were greater in summer months, showing
reductions of about 30% to 50% between June and August.
Through exploratory data analysis, relationships between Tskin and T2m were investigated
and a number of features were engineered based on the results observed. These included
polynomial terms of Tskin, seasonal Fourier harmonics, interactions with geographical lo­
cation, cloud cover, wind speed and more. A quadratic term of Tskin and some Fourier
harmonics based on the day­of­the­year were consistently found to explain most of the
variance in T2m across feature importance analyses of each model. This suggests that
nonlinear relationships between Tskin and T2m are important.
The three models also improved R2 scores from the baseline of 0.935 to 0.946 for ENet
and MLP, and 0.947 for XGBoost. The high R2 scores indicate that the models explain
a majority of the variance in T2m. Residuals are showing clear homoscedasticity, though
with a remaining systematic bias particularly in T2m values above 0°C.
It was shown that approximately 18% of variance in the data could be attributed to dif­
ferences between the various types in situ observational platforms. This emphasizes the
importance of validating models across a variety of in situ data sources to get a general­
izable estimate of performance, solidifying the reliability of the reported RMSE results in
this thesis.
The observation that each new model hit a similar performance ceiling, indicates that
further complexity yield diminishing returns with a pointwise prediction approach on the
current state of the data. Fast convergence of learning curves indicate that additional
training data could not help reduce RMSE even further. A new and different approach is
likely needed to show a significant break through in performance. A natural next step for
future studies would be to explore spatio­temporal modeling approaches, and addressing
the two issues of sparse in situ observational platforms drifting over time and the uncer­
tainty of the input data.
Overall, this thesis has shown that modest expansion of model flexibility and feature en­
gineering delivers a significant step toward bias­free estimates of 2­meter air temperature
over Arctic sea ice.

C.1
Why residuals are not predicted directly
Given that Tskin is already known to be a strong proxy for T2m, an alternative approach is
to predict the residual between the two and use this as a correction term to obtain T2m.
Formally, using this approach implies T2m can be reconstructed as:
T2m = Tskin + f(features including Tskin)
(C.1)
Where f is a learned function intended to correct for the residuals under varying environ­
mental conditions.
However, if Tskin is included both as a base term and as a feature in f, you risk overcor­
recting or introducing a bias, as the influence of Tskin is effectively counted twice.
One way to resolve this is by excluding Tskin from the set of features and relying solely
on the base term. However, this prevents the model from learning potential interaction
effects between Tskin and other features. These may be critical in determining when and
why Tskin deviates from T2m.
