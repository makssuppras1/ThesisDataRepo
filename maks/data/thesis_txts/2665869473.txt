Abstract 
Parkinson’s disease (PD) presents a large and increasing personal, societal and economic 
burden. Preventative or curative PD treatments are absent, and the exact etiology is still poorly 
understood. Therefore, there is a large need for a better understanding of PD. In vitro human 
neural stem cell (hNSC) disease models hold promise as they differentiate to dopaminergic 
neurons relatively well, but there are few genetic variations of such cell lines available. In this 
project, we attempted to generate three novel variations of the established hNSC line called 
hVM1, which is derived from fetal ventral mesencephalon. Specifically, these variations were 
a parkin knockout (KO), the p.A53T point mutation in SNCA and the p.E326K point mutation 
in GBA1.  
CRISPR-Cas9 was used to for gene-editing, limiting dilution was used as the single-cell 
isolation method, and PCR, restriction enzyme digestion, and Sanger sequencing were used for 
screening. Although these mutations were observed in polyclonal cell populations, we were 
unsuccessful in obtaining these clones: the mutations consistently no longer showed for the 
daughter cell populations in subsequent screening. The polyclonal cell populations with the 
mutations were however cryopreserved.  
This may have been because we used too high cell densities in the limiting dilutions, which led 
to a high likelihood of overgrowth of the interesting candidates by uninteresting candidates due 
to evolutionary forces. While the intended cell lines were not obtained, there are several 
promising but (likely) multiclonal cell populations cryopreserved for all three of the cell lines. 
Therefore, if these cell lines are still desired, using these samples as a starting point and using 
the proposed recommendations, development of these cell lines is likely to be fruitful.

1. Introduction 
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease and is widely prevalent. 
While it is generally known for its movement-related symptoms such as tremors and 
bradykinesia, it may also include cognitive, psychiatric and other physiological symptoms. 
Already, the disease presents a significant personal, social and economic burden to society 
(Poewe et al., 2017). Since PD incidence rises sharply with age (Pringsheim et al., 2014) and 
populations worldwide are ageing due to increased life expectancy and lower fertility (ONU, 
2015), this burden is only expected to rise.   
While we know that loss of dopaminergic neurons (DAn) in the substantia nigra pars compacta 
underlies the disease, the exact cause of this neuronal death is still unknown. This incomplete 
understanding hampers the development of curative or preventative treatments, and currently 
treatments for PD are only symptomatic and with significant side effects (Poewe et al., 2017). 
Given the already big and increasing burden on society, there is an urgent need for a better 
understanding of PD.  
An accurate and reliable research model will aid in this. While animal models may be able 
match the complex and interconnected neuronal environment relatively well, developmental, 
genetic and life-span differences limit translatability to humans (Anderson et al., 2017; 
Cummings et al., 2014; Dawson et al., 2018; Pfeuffer et al., 2016). On the other hand, human 
cell-based in vitro models may be genetically similar but will miss a lot of the complex 
environmental interactions. Nevertheless, the relative simplicity of cell cultures and ease with 
which genetic and environmental factors can be manipulated or controlled for, makes in vitro 
human cell models a valuable resource. In addition, there are promising developments in ways 
to mimic relevant in vivo complexities in in vitro models, e.g. through 3D and organoid cell 
culturing (Fitzgerald et al., 2015; Smits et al., 2019). 
While primary dopaminergic cell cultures may be a suitable in vitro PD model since they reflect 
the dopaminergic cell identity well, they have scalability, reproducibility and ethics issues (X. 
M. Zhang et al., 2014). Immortalized cell lines such as the neuroblastoma cell line SH-SY5Y 
have also been a valuable model but they do not differentiate to authentic dopaminergic 
neurons,  have low differentiation yield and their cancer origin may be undesirable (Pahlman et 
al., 1990; Schüle et al., 2009). Recent developments in stem cell research and fate 
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
reprogramming have enabled stem cell-based models as a promising alternative for PD 
research. 
For example, somatic cells such as fibroblasts can be used to generate pluripotent stem cell 
(iPSC) lines (Takahashi & Yamanaka, 2006). This opens ways to more closely match 
(epi)genomic backgrounds in disease research, for example by generating iPSCs from PD 
patient cells. An alternative to iPSCs are neural stem cells (NSCs). For PD specifically, human 
NSCs of ventral mesencephalic origin may also hold promise: they differentiate into functional 
and mature dopaminergic neurons well, and possibly more efficiently so than iPSCs (Courtois 
et al., 2010; Martínez-Serrano et al., 2011; Villa et al., 2009). 
Regardless of the model, genetic manipulation has long been a staple for disease research. For 
example, disease-related mutations can be introduced in healthy cells as well as the opposite: 
mutations of e.g. patient-specific cells could be restored. Such cell line variations have become 
increasingly accessible due to developments with CRISPR-Cas9 (Hsu et al., 2014). Indeed, 
iPSC lines are available with PD-related genotypes (reviewed in Ke et al., 2019), but similar 
variations are mostly absent for human NSCs.  
1.1 Project aim 
In this project we aimed to develop three of such cell line variations for the human ventral 
mesencephalon-derived NSC line hVM1 using the CRISPR-Cas9 system. The variations are 
the homozygous knock-out of the parkin gene (PRKN, PRKN-KO), the heterozygous or 
homozygous p.A53T point-mutation in the α-synuclein gene (SNCA, SNCA-A53T), and the 
homozygous p.E326K point-mutation in the β-glucosylceramidase gene (GBA1, GBA1-
E326K).  
Practically, this would be done in two phases. First, monoclonal cell lines with these mutations 
would be created using CRISPR-Cas9 at the Danish biotechnology company Bioneer A/S. 
Then, these cell lines would be moved to DTU Bioengineering (formerly Nanotech) where they 
would be characterized and analyzed for PD phenotypes using assays such as neurite outgrowth, 
neural arborization and dopamine detection amongst others.  
Practically, it proved to be more difficult and time-consuming than expected to obtain the 
desired monoclonal cell lines and only the first phase could be attempted. In this paper, first 
relevant theory underlying the project is described, followed by the methods and results, and

finally a discussion of results, reflection on the project execution, and finally recommendations 
for future endeavors of this kind.  
2. Theory 
2.1 Neuropathology and -genesis of Parkinson’s disease 
Parkinson’s disease’s main pathological feature is neurodegeneration of dopaminergic neurons 
in the substantia nigra pars compacta. Specifically, neuronal cell death is especially prevalent 
for the dopaminergic cell group A9, a group of nigrostriatal neuromelanin-containing DAn 
(Kouli et al., 2018) . Quantitatively, at motor symptom onset there may have already been a 
30% decrease in striatal dopamine levels, which may increase to up to 98% for advanced PD 
(Kordower et al., 2013). While other non-dopaminergic systems are also be impacted, and 
possible even precede the degeneration of the nigrostriatal pathway (Chaudhuri & Sauerbier, 
2016; Giguère et al., 2018; Kalia et al., 2013), the focus of this text will mainly be on the 
pathology of DAn. 
Intracellular, a common feature of PD (but also several other neurodegenerative diseases) is 
abnormal accumulation of misfolded and phosphorylated α-synuclein in aggregates called 
Lewy bodies (Wakabayashi et al., 2013). However, while the presence of Lewy bodies is a 
prominent feature of PD, there have been reported cases without Lewy bodies (Gaig et al., 2007; 
Johansen et al., 2018). Still, these protein aggregates, of which α-synuclein is the main 
constituent, are cytotoxic and capable of disrupting, amongst others, mitochondrial, lysosomal 
and synaptic functioning (G. Zhang et al., 2018). However, it may also be the other way around, 
where dysfunction of these systems could trigger and stimulate Lewy body formation, and that 
Lewy bodies themselves aren’t directly causative for PD (Chartier & Duyckaerts, 2018; 
Shahmoradian et al., 2019). To further complicate things, more recent evidence may actually 
point to transneuronal propagation rather than intracellular origin, where Lewy bodies spread 
through the nervous system (possibly even having a gut origin) and trigger further Lewy body 
formation (Brundin & Melki, 2017; Sangjune Kim et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2016; Uemura et al., 
2018). 
While the exact etiology of PD is unclear, it is clear that both genetic and environmental risk 
factors underlie the disease: around 10-15% of PD diagnoses are familial (with 5% following 
mendelian inheritance patterns) and the remaining 85-90% cases being idiopathic (Deng et al., 
2018). While multifactorial etiology underlies most PD cases, a better understanding of how 
genetic risk factors contribute to PD risk will improve overall understanding of PD 
==PAGE:13==
4 
 
pathogenesis. Amongst others, significant risk factors are found in the genes PRKN, SNCA and 
GBA1 (Heras-Garvin & Stefanova, 2020; Santos et al., 2019; Stoker et al., 2018). In specific, 
this project focuses on loss-of-function mutations in PRKN, the p.A53T point-mutation in 
SNCA and the p.E326K point-mutation in GBA1.  
2.1.1 PRKN 
The PRKN gene encodes for parkin, a RING-in-Between-RING (RBR) E3 ubiquitin ligase 
involved in protein degradation and mitophagy. Loss-of-function (homozygous or compound 
heterozygous) mutations in PRKN are responsible for 50% of autosomal recessive early-onset 
PD and are the most common cause of autosomal recessive PD in general (Ge et al., 2020; 
Kitada et al., 1998). In addition, mutations in PTEN-induced putative kinase 1 (PINK1), which 
regulates parkin activity through phosphorylation, are also a significant source of familial early-
onset PD (Valente et al., 2004), further pointing to the significant role of parkin in PD.  
Ubiquitination is a key part of the ubiquitin-proteasome system, one of two major protein 
clearance systems (the other being the autophagy-lysosome system). In short, this system 
conjugates undesired proteins, e.g. because they are no longer needed/are dysfunctional, with a 
specific conformation and number of ubiquitin molecules. This specific ubiquitin tag then 
determines further processing, for example protein degradation by the 26S proteasome. Beside 
degradation, ubiquitination  is also involved in other (non-degradative) functions such as 
deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) repair, endocytosis, mitophagy and the immune response 
(Brinkmann et al., 2015).  
As a E3 ubiquitin ligase, parkin plays a role during the conjugation step. Specifically, it binds 
to the substrate protein and to an E2 ubiquitin conjugase, thereby catalyzing the ubiquitination 
reaction. This gives E3 ubiquitin ligases a critical role in substrate specificity and ubiquitin 
chain composition (Hristova et al., 2020). Parkin also has complex autoregulatory activity: 
when in the active confirmation, its RBR domains (Figure 1) can ubiquitinate parkin itself 
(autoubiquitination). However, another domain at the N-terminal of the protein, the ubiquitin-
like domain (UbI), generally conforms the protein into an inhibited conformation which 
prevents autoubiquitination (Chaugule et al., 2011).  
==PAGE:14==
5 
 
 
Figure 1. Domain structure of parkin, the ubiquitin-like domain (Ubl) and RING0 domains are specific to parkin. The numbers 
correspond to residue numbering. Figure and description adapted from Spratt et al. (2013). 
 
There are over 200 identified mutations that are associated with increased PD-risk, ranging 
from deletions, insertions and missense/nonsense mutations to larger exonic rearrangements 
(Santos et al., 2019). Most of these mutations involve the Ubl and RING2 domains (Nuytemans 
et al., 2010; Tang et al., 2009). Mutations in the RING2 domain disrupt ligase activity whereas 
mutations in the Ubl prevent conformation into the inhibited state (Spratt et al., 2013). This in 
turn is thought to lead the parkin to persistently and pervasively autoubiquitinate, leading to 
degradation by the proteasome. Practically, these Ubl mutations also present itself as loss-of-
function as no functional parkin remains. Experimental data supports this: Ubl-mutated parkin 
is unstable and undetectable in cells, but does become detectable in the presence of proteasome 
inhibitors (Chaugule et al., 2011; Spratt et al., 2013). This is also in line with the recessive 
nature of parkin mutations, since autoubiquitination only occurs in cis and not in trans (Spratt 
et al., 2013).  
Decreased parkin activity has various outcomes. First, as a result of ubiquitin-proteasome 
dysfunction, defective proteins accumulate, amongst others misfolded α-synuclein (reviewed 
in Ge et al., 2020). However, this effect on α-synuclein accumulation is not major as the main 
α-synuclein degradation pathway is the autophagy-lysosome pathway (H. J. Lee et al., 2004). 
Second, parkin plays a major role in mitochondrial quality control pathways. For example, 
parkin mediates mitophagy, and its inactivity may therefore lead to accumulation of defective 
mitochondria (Vincow et al., 2013). However, total mitochondrial content actually appears to 
decrease in a Parkin knockout (parkin-KO) background (Stevens et al., 2015; Sung et al., 2016), 
and a role for parkin specifically in mitophagy in differentiated neurons (rather than 
undifferentiated in vitro (HeLa/fibroblast) cells) is also controversial (reviewed in Ge et al., 
2020).  
Another way that decreased parkin activity may play a role in mitochondrial dysfunction is 
through mitochondrial fusion and fission. Parkin inhibits mitochondrial fusion (and thus 
==PAGE:15==
6 
 
stimulates fission) through degradation of the fusion-promoting factor Mitofusion (Poole et al., 
2010). Mitochondrial fission is hypothesized to be a defense mechanism against severe (but not 
mild) stress as it allows defective mitochondrial components to be isolated from healthy 
mitochondrial components followed by targeted degradation, thereby limiting the extent of 
overall mitochondrial damage (Ge et al., 2020).  
Parkin’s role in mitochondrial-derived vesicles (MDV) functioning may further extend the 
possible role of decreased parkin activity leading to mitochondrial dysfunction, as parkin is 
necessary for MDV formation (although only for specific cargo). Drosophila parkin-KO 
mutants indeed displayed increased mitochondrial concentration of components typically 
removed through MDVs (Vincow et al., 2013), but recent evidence contradicts and indicates 
Parkin’s role in MDV formation to be limited and highly specific to MDV cargo (Roberts et 
al., 2020).  
Besides removal of dysfunctional mitochondrial components, there is preliminary evidence that 
parkin plays a role in mitochondrial biogenesis and mitochondrial repair mechanisms (reviewed 
in Ge et al., 2020). In addition,  a recently published study convincingly proposed that parkin’s 
critical role in mitochondria-mediated apoptosis (through polyubiquitinating VDAC1) but not 
parkin’s role in mitophagy (through monoubiquitinating VDAC1) is relevant for PD 
pathogenesis (Ham et al., 2020). Finally, it is hypothesized that besides genetic parkin 
inactivation, it may also be inactivated by α-syn aggregation (but through indirect mechanisms), 
thereby also giving parkin a central role in sporadic PD pathogenesis (Ge et al., 2020). 
Evidently, parkin is an exciting and contemporarily interesting subject for PD research. Still, 
many questions remain and contradicting results between different research models are 
frequent. Therefore, establishing a parkin-KO human neural stem cells (hNSC) line that 
differentiates well into (relatively) authentic DAn is a worthwhile endeavor.  
2.1.2 SNCA 
The SNCA gene encodes α-synuclein. Despite it having been at the center of attention due to its 
aggregation playing a central role in numerous neurodegenerative diseases (synucleinopathies), 
the exact function of largely remains unknown. It is, however, involved in a variety of processes 
such as neurotransmitter release, mitochondrial functioning, gene regulation and vesicle 
transport (Heras-Garvin & Stefanova, 2020). Besides being abundant throughout the nervous 
system, α-syn is also present in blood cells and (heart) muscle, although its role there is also not 
known (Barbour et al., 2008). 
==PAGE:16==
7 
 
The native state of α-syn is unclear although it is thought to be in an unfolded monomer-state 
with no clear tertiary protein structure, or in a α-helical and tetramer formation, or a mix of both 
(Dettmer et al., 2015). Its conformation is flexible and complex: it can form α-helical structure 
and β-sheet formations; it can be a monomer and it can oligomerize into spherical, chain-like, 
annular and tubular shapes; and it can undergo numerous  posttranslational modifications 
(Heras-Garvin & Stefanova, 2020; Kouli et al., 2018). 
In relation to PD, the missense mutations p.A53T, p.A30P, p.E46K and p.H50Q as well as 
amplification mutations are linked to autosomal-dominant early-onset PD, with additional 
single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) being a PD risk factor (Campêlo & Silva, 2017). 
Functionally, these mutations appear to promote aggregation and oligomerization leading to 
dysfunction of many cellular processes. These include impaired lysosome and proteasome 
activity leading to reduced axonal transport, increased mitochondrial and DNA damage through 
promoting ROS generation and pathological membrane composition leading to altered endo- 
and exocytosis (reviewed in Heras-Garvin & Stefanova, 2020).  
p.A53T was the first mutation discovered to cause familial early-onset PD (Kitada et al., 1998). 
The mutation appears to cancel long-range interactions between different protein domains, 
thereby increasing β-sheet formation which in turn increases protein stability and 
fibrilization/aggregation rate (Coskuner & Wise-Scira, 2013; Meade et al., 2019). In rodents, 
the p.A53T mutation leads to decreased dopamine signaling followed by  increased dopamine 
toxicity and subsequent DAn loss compared to wild-type (WT) (Kurz et al., 2010; Lu et al., 
2015; Nemani et al., 2010; Yoon et al., 2020) . The decrease in dopamine signaling may be 
explained by the p.A53T mutation enhancing the presynaptic suppressive effect that WT α-syn 
already has, and/or through inhibiting postsynaptic vesicle reclustering leading to postsynaptic 
deficits (Nemani et al., 2010; Teravskis et al., 2018).  
Alternatively, p.A53T may increase neuroinflammation through overactivation of pro-
inflammatory cascades in microglia (Hoenen et al., 2016), and by, possibly in parallel, also 
losing the protective effect that WT α-syn has on oxidative stress (Khan et al., 2019). Both the 
increase in inflammation and oxidative stress may explain why mitochondrial respiration is 
impaired for neurons with the p.A53T mutation (L. Li et al., 2013). In addition, p.A53T 
accumulation promotes parkin deactivation through activation of its inhibitor p38 MAPK, 
thereby providing a pathway for p.A53T to indirectly cause mitochondrial dysfunction through 
mechanisms described in the 2.1.1 PRKN section above (J. Chen et al., 2018; Gui et al., 2020).

2.1.3 GBA1 
GBA1 encodes for β-glucosylceramidase (GCase), a lysosomal enzyme which hydrolyzes 
glucosylceramide and glucosylsphingosine into ceramide and glucose and sphingosine and 
glucose respectively. It is mainly associated with Gaucher disease, which is the most common 
lysosomal storage disorder and caused by GCase deficiency, which in turn leads to the toxic 
accumulation of glucosylceramide and glucosylsphingosine. There are various degrees of 
severity, with compounded or homozygous impactful mutations typically leading to more 
severe forms which include neurodegeneration, compared to heterozygosity for e.g. a point 
mutation leading to a milder disease phenotype without neurodegeneration (Weiss et al., 2015). 
Besides Gaucher disease, GBA1 mutations are also associated with PD. Although they do not 
cause familial PD, they are the most important genetic PD risk factor, with between seven to 
twelve percent of PD patients carrying a GBA1 mutation (Stoker et al., 2018). Interestingly, 
heterozygous and homozygous GBA1 mutation carriers have a similar PD risk (Alcalay et al., 
2014). In the substantia nigra, a decrease in GCase activity correlates with an increase in α-syn 
concentration, possibly (partly) mediated through buildup of glycosylsphingosine (Gündner et 
al., 2019). In addition, buildup of  glucosylceramide and glycosylsphingosine appear to stabilize 
and trigger α-syn oligomerization respectively (Mazzulli et al., 2011; Taguchi et al., 2017).  
However, there is contradicting evidence on whether PD-related GBA1 mutations lead to this 
GCase substrate accumulation (Arkadir et al., 2019; Gegg et al., 2015; Mazzulli et al., 2011; 
Taguchi et al., 2017). Theoretically, there could also be a link between GCase and α-syn for 
sporadic PD without GBA1 mutations, as α-syn appears to inhibit neuronal GCase activity 
(Mazzulli et al., 2011). However, as of yet there is no consensus on whether GCase activity is 
indeed lowered for sporadic PD (Alcalay et al., 2015; Huh et al., 2020).  
While most PD-associated GBA1 mutations cause Gaucher disease when in homozygosity, 
p.E326K (the most prevalent PD-associated GBA1 mutation), does not (Duran et al., 2013). 
This may be because p.E326K does not decrease GCase activity significantly, most likely 
because E326 is a non-conserved residue and predicted to be far away from the GCase active 
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
site (Alcalay et al., 2015; Duran et al., 2013; Dvir et al., 2003; Horowitz et al., 2011; Lwin et 
al., 2004). Indeed, only a modest decrease in GCase activity is observed for p.E326K-carriers 
compared to Gaucher disease-causing mutations (Alcalay et al., 2015; Duran et al., 2013; 
Horowitz et al., 2011; Lwin et al., 2004).  
Unfortunately for PD research, this may indicate that the pathological mechanism through 
which this specific mutation increases PD risk may be separate from GBA1 mutations 
abolishing GCase activity significantly (Stoker et al., 2018).  GCase activity is decreased in 
idiopathic PD, and better understanding why this is may also shed light on why p.E326K is 
increasing PD risk (Chiasserini et al., 2015; Parnetti et al., 2017). Because p.E326K does 
significantly reduce GCase activity in combination with another GBA1 mutation on that allele 
(Horowitz et al., 2011), it may be plausible that p.E326K does not inhibit GCase activity by 
itself, but instead increases sensitivity to other inhibiting factors, e.g. by reducing protein 
stability.  
Given the high prevalence of the p.E326K mutation  and that it is distinct  from other PD-related 
GBA1 mutations in that it does not cause Gaucher disease in homozygosity, it is interesting to 
better understand how it affects DAn. A p.E326K hNSC line will aid in this but does not exist 
yet. 
2.2 CRISPR/Cas9 
While a long-standing element of the adaptive immune systems of bacteria and archaea against 
viral pathogens, CRISPR (clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats) and Cas(9) 
(CRISPR associated protein 9) have been the center of attention in the biology field ever since 
the seminal paper by Jinek et al. (2012). This paper first demonstrated that the CRISPR/Cas9 
system could be used for RNA-programmable (ribonucleic acid) DNA targeting and editing. In 
the future the CRISPR system may also be used to regulate and investigate gene expression and 
epigenetics through variations on the CIRSPR/Cas9 system (Dort et al., 2020). 
Gene editing with CRISPR/Cas9 typically uses the type II CRSPR/Cas system from S. 
pyogenes. Originally, this system was an adaptive defense against bacteriophages. In this 
system, a fragment of the foreign DNA from the invader (which includes a PAM-site (5′-NGG-
3′ for Cas9)) is first recognized and integrated into the CRISPR array. Then, through 
transcription of the CRISPR array into pre-crRNA and subsequent processing mature crRNAs 
are formed. These crRNAs have the spacer sequence from a previous invader which is flanked 
by repeats through which it can base pair with the trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA) and guide 
==PAGE:19==
10 
 
the crRNP (crRNA ribonucleoprotein) to the complementary protospacer sequence after which 
the Cas9 protein cuts 3-4 base pairs (bp) upstream of the PAM-site (Figure 2) (Dort et al., 2020).  
For gene-editing, this system is slightly altered as the tracrRNA and crRNA are combined into 
one RNA molecule called the single guide RNA (sgRNA) (see comparison Figure 2). This 
sgRNA has a constant part corresponding to the would-be tracrRNA and a variable part 
corresponding to the would-be crRNA and the target sequence. It is important that this target 
sequence contains a PAM-site, as without it Cas9 won’t cleave the DNA (Cui et al., 2018). 
However, near-PAMless CRISPR-Cas9 variants may make this requirement redundant in the 
future (Walton et al., 2020). 
 
Figure 2. Simplified overview of the native CRISPR/Cas9 of S. pyrogenes (B) and the modified version CRISPR/Cas9 gene 
editing system using a sgRNA as described by (Jinek et al., 2012). Image A adapted from (IDT DNA, n.d.) , image B adapted 
from (Cui et al., 2018). 
 In general, there are three main delivery methods for in vitro delivery of the CRISPR/Cas9 
components. Firstly, there is the plasmid-based approach where the components are encoded in 
cassettes on the plasmid (Figure 3). However, entry of the plasmid into the nucleus is generally 
difficult. In addition, because transcription and subsequent translation is needed before gene 
edits can take place, it is more time consuming compared to alternative methods. It also tends 
to produce more off-target effects compared to alternatives, which is undesirable (C. Liu et al., 
2017). The second method is delivery of the Cas9 mRNA and sgRNA into the cell. The Cas9 
mRNA is translated into Cas9 protein, which will then bind with the sgRNA to form the crRNP. 
The advantages of this are that it only needs to be introduced into the cytoplasm and that gene-
editing occurs faster, for a shorter duration and with fewer off-target effects compared to the 
plasmid method (C. Liu et al., 2017). Lastly, preformed crRNP can be introduced directly into 
the cell. This is the most commonly used method (also used in this thesis), as it is relatively 
==PAGE:20==
11 
 
fast, efficient and has reduced off-target effects and toxicity to the cell (Sojung Kim et al., 
2014).  
 
Figure 3 Three methods to introduce CRISPR/Cas9 components for in vitro models. Strategy 1 uses a plasmid that expresses 
the necessary components; Strategy 2 is RNA-based where Cas9 mRNA and sgRNA are injected into the cytoplasm and the 
crRNP forms subsequent to Cas9 translation; Strategy 3 already pre-forms the crRNP ex vitro and is injected as one 
component. Figure and description adapted from Liu et al. (2017).  
Once the crRNP is bound to the target sequence and a PAM-site is present, Cas9 creates a blunt-
end double-strand break (DSB). The cell can then repair this DSB through either non-
homologous end-joining (NHEJ) or through homology-directed repair (HDR) (Figure 4). NHEJ 
occurs most often and does not require a donor template, but it is also error prone and often 
creates random insertions or deletions. HDR on the other hand does require a donor template 
and is more precise (Ran et al., 2013). HDR can be used for nucleotide-specific gene-editing. 
For this, a single-stranded donor oligonucleotide (ssODN) designed to have desired 
modification in the middle with homologous overhangs on each side, will be introduced into 
==PAGE:21==
12 
 
the cell along with the CRISPR/Cas9 components. Then, after the DSB is made the ssODN can 
act as the donor template and the desired modification will be copied into the genome.

Singapore (2017). 
3. Methods 
3.1 Experimental setup 
The human ventral mesencephalic cell line hVM1 was used (Villa et al., 2009). 
First, cells were nucleofected through electropermeabilization to introduce the gene editing 
components into the cell. Then, limiting dilutions were made to obtain monoclonal cell 
populations. After growing to an appropriate confluence, DNA was isolated and screened for 
the gene modification of interest through gel electrophoresis of polymerase chain reaction 
(PCR) samples, restriction enzyme digestion and Sanger sequencing. This cycle of limiting 
dilution and screening was repeated for interesting cell populations until we could either 
conclude that the desired gene edit was present but was heterozygous or, ideally, that it was 
homozygous and that the cell population was monoclonal.

3.4.2.1.1 PRKN-KO 
To screen cell populations for the deletion through PCR, two primer pairs were used. The first

the designed deletion area, but the corresponding sequence of primer PRKN_abs_FW lies 
within the designed deletion area. Therefore, for this PCR, a PCR product of 229 bp would 
indicate the presence of the deletion area in the cell population (i.e. WT), and when there are 
amplified PCR products this indicates that the deletion area is completely absent in the cell 
population (or that the PCR did not work).

3.4.5 Sequencing 
If PCR screening indicated an interesting candidate, it was Sanger sequenced. First, 5 µL of 
PCR product was cleaned of excess primers and nucleotides by mixing it with 1 µL of ExoSAP-
IT™ PCR Product Cleanup Reagent (ThermoFisher Scientific). This was incubated in the 
thermal cycler for 15 minutes at 37 °C and subsequently for another 15 minutes at 80 °C.  
Next, a chain termination PCR was done using the BigDye™ Terminator v3.1 Cycle 
Sequencing Kit (Applied Biosystems). For this, primers PRKN_E2del_seq, SNCA_A53T_seq 
and GBA1_E326K_seq were used, which were around 80-100 bp prior to the desired gene-edit 
(appendix B). The following master mix was prepared (volume per well, reagents completely 
thawed):  
• 4.65 µL PCR-grade H2O; 
• 1.65 µL of buffer (included with kit); 
• 1.00 µL of (sequencing) primer (diluted 1:10); 
• 0.70 µL of BigDye enzyme. 
2 µL of the ExoSAP-treated DNA was added and mixed. The thermal cycler was set to:  
a) 1 minutes at 96 °C; 
b) 10 seconds at 95 °C; 
c) 5 seconds at 50 °C; 
d) 1.5 minutes at 60 °C; 
e) Repeat from step b for 25 times; 
f) Cool at 4 °C until taken out. 
Next, the PCR product was precipitated with 8 µL of sodium acetate (NaAc) mixed with 200 
µL of 96% ethanol (EtOH), which was added to the BigDye sample (but not mixed). This was 
centrifuged for 3 minutes at 12.000 g and immediately after, the supernatant was discarded. 200 
µL of 70% EtOH was added and centrifuged for 3 minutes at 12.000 g, and the supernatant was 
again discarded. Any remaining moisture was evaporated by heating the sample at 37 °C for 
two minutes. The DNA was resuspended in 15 µL of formamide, transferred to a sequencing 
tube closed with a silicone septa cap, and sequenced with the PRISM® 310 Genetic Analyzer 
==PAGE:32==
23 
 
(Applied Biosystems). Sequencing results were analyzed with Pregap4 and Gap (Staden, 
Windows version 1.6-r and v4.11.2-r respectively).

5. Discussion 
This project attempted to introduce Parkinson’s disease mutations in hNSCs. Specifically, the 
CRISPR/Cas9 system was used to create cell lines for homozygous knockout of PRKN, SNCA-
A53T and GBA1-E326K.  
While we were unsuccessful in obtaining the desired cell lines, some interesting polyclonal cell 
populations were cryopreserved (and stored at Bioneer A/S). If these cell lines are still desired, 
these may enable a quickened way to obtain the desired cell lines. On the other hand, if 
screening methods are established properly and single-cell isolation methods improved, then 
starting over with a WT population may not be that more time-consuming but will decrease risk 
of genetic aberrations and/or detrimental growth characteristics (due to senescence/genetic drift 
as passage number was high) significantly. Amongst others, there is:  
• A likely-to-be monoclonal but heterozygous (and therefore probably incomplete) 
PRKN-KO cell line (candidate 8 from Figure 8);  
• A polyclonal cell population which contains the p.A53T SNCA mutation and may either 
be homo- or heterozygous (candidate 1 from Figure 9);  
• A likely-to-be heterozygous and monoclonal GBA-E326K cell line (panel C Figure 13);  
• A likely-to-be monoclonal and possibly homozygous GBA1-KO (p.L324HTer61) cell 
line (candidates 1 and 2 from Figure 14) (acquired unintentionally).  
Reflecting back on the project, I think there were two issues which significantly decreased the 
success rate of the project: 1) single-cell isolation method causing low likelihood of desired 
mutations persisting in polyclonal cell populations, and 2) poor PCR establishment and 
problem-solving causing screening issues to persist thereby decreasing screening rate. These 
factors will now be elaborated upon, concluding with an overview of how these factors are 
interlinked and what lessons were learned.  
==PAGE:49==
40 
 
5.1 Single-cell isolation method 
Out of the various methods to isolate single cells for cell line development, there were two 
viable options in terms of required resources, throughput and downstream cell viability: 1) 
limiting dilution and 2) manual cell picking (Gross et al., 2015). Because hVM1 cells grow in 
an interconnected web-like fashion, we assumed that seeding on a large well followed by 
manual cell picking would be difficult since the distinct colonies may grow towards each other 
and become indistinguishable. Therefore, we chose the limiting dilution method. 
In hindsight, this assumption should have been tested before deciding on doing limiting 
dilutions, as I believe that doing manual cell picking would have been more effective. 
Especially since manual cell picking is the standard method of single-cell isolation for (iPSC) 
cell line development at Bioneer, whereas there was no to limited experience with doing 
limiting dilutions. In fact, towards the end of the project we tried manual cell picking which 
worked well (Figure 13, panel C). Even if the cell line did have the tendency to rapidly grow 
towards each other, we could have used cloning rings to create a physical barrier between 
colonies. 
5.1.2 Ignorance towards statistical probability of limiting dilution 
While the choice of using limiting dilution over manual cell picking was unideal, success would 
have still been achievable. However, me not understanding the underlying probability theory 
that limiting dilution is based on strongly reduced the odds of success.  
A key parameter while doing limiting dilutions is the dilution used: too high and most wells 
will be filled with more than one cell per well, leading to an inefficient process of ongoing 
subcloning and increased risk of losing interesting candidates due to overgrowth; too low and 
many of the wells will be empty resulting in having to screen more wells and having to waste 
resources on the empty wells. The probability of either happening, i.e. to obtain X number of 
cells in a well, can roughly be estimated by the Poisson’s distribution (assuming a homogenous 
cell mixture) (Figure 17) (Moon et al., 2011; Staszewski, 1984). Although deviations may occur 
dependent on cell line characteristics and true seeding densities (i.e. through miscounting, cell 
clumping, non-homogeneous mixture), and despite reports of over- and underestimating true 
monoclonality (Staszewski, 1984; Zhou et al., 2018), it still gives an indication of appropriate 
dilutions to use and the subsequent number of empty wells to expect.

5.1.3 Evolutionary forces 
When subclones of interesting candidates were analyzed, they often didn’t show the interesting 
trait anymore (e.g. Figure 9, Figure 13). Especially for PRKN-KO, we observed many times 
that the lower PCR band from PRKN_E2del would no longer be visible in subclones (e.g. 
Figure 7, Figure 8). In the beginning, we attributed this to having made a mistake, e.g. by mixing 
up the plate orientation at some point during the screening process and selecting the wrong well. 
Therefore, careful attention was paid at each step and well counting would be double checked 
by others. Frustratingly, it kept happening. 
Now knowing that most subclones were likely to be polyclonal (due to the high seeding 
density), a more plausible explanation is that (in most cases) these traits were lost due to genetic 
drift, possibly in combination with natural selection.  
5.1.2.1 Genetic drift 
Genetic drift is the ‘random change of allele frequencies in populations of finite size’. If given 
enough generations and without recurrent mutations occurring, genetic drift will eventually lead 
to an allele becoming either fixed or lost. Logically, the higher the allele frequency, the higher 
the likelihood of that allele becoming fixed and vice versa. In addition, genetic drift has a larger 
influence on smaller populations and speeds up the allele fixation process (Nielsen & Slatkin, 
2013).  
Effectively, the limiting dilution method creates many small populations. Normally, genetic 
drift wouldn’t be so relevant here as, if you do it correctly, most wells will have one cell per 
==PAGE:52==
43 
 
well. However, as Table 4 shows, when using a high seeding density like 5 or 25 cells per well, 
it is probably that there is more than one cell per well. Given that the frequency of the allele of 
interest is likely to be low (e.g. homo/heterozygous in one cell of the population) and because 
population size is low, genetic drift will relatively often lose that allele in that population within 
relatively few generations. Because the generation time for hNSC cultures is within the order 
of magnitude of hours, it is feasible that loss of the allele of interest could happen within 
days/weeks. In support of this also the fact that for the interesting candidates, the original cell 
populations were also maintained as a backup after creating limiting dilution plates from them. 
These populations also consistently did not show the interesting phenotype when screened later 
(Figure 7, panel D; Figure 9, panel D).  
In addition, cell culture splitting essentially represents a population bottleneck which speeds up 
loss or fixation of alleles (Armbruster & Pfenninger, 2003). This is the underlying principle of 
limiting dilution, where the desired outcome is instant fixation of the allele of interest in zero 
generations because there is only one founder for that population. However, it is a counter-
productive force when splitting cell cultures for anything other than limiting dilution, e.g. to 
isolate DNA or to prevent overgrowth. In fact, loss of interesting cells due to overgrowth by 
non-interesting cells is a notorious problem in hybridoma cloning, which is why protocols urge 
you to clone as soon as interesting candidates are identified (Anne Underwood & Bean, 1988; 
Fuller et al., 1988; Staszewski, 1984). In our case, this was not always possible due to screening 
delays, variable growth rates and time constraints. 
Besides a high seeding density, contamination with cells from nearby wells may have also 
happened and thereby enabled or sped up allele fixation. The strongest argument for this is that 
in Figure 8, candidate 3 and its subclones showed a band at around 200 bp similarly to candidate 
2. However, candidate 1, the progenitor cell population of candidate 3, showed no indication of 
having this band. It is unlikely that this is a contamination at the PCR level as it persists 
throughout limiting dilutions and because the sequencing confirms the presence of such a 
deletion (Figure 8, panel B, C, E). In addition, the two p.L324Hter61 cell populations from 
distinct wells points to possible contamination (Figure 14). The probability of this specific 
mutation happening twice with the used CRISPR design is unknown, but in general the 
probability of two rare events happening independently and concurrently of each other is 
negligible.  However, if contamination happened, it should have only happened within the 
==PAGE:53==
44 
 
sterile environment of the laminar flow hood (LAF) bench, as mycoplasma testing returned 
negative twice.9  
5.1.2.2 Natural selection 
Besides genetic drift, natural selection may have also facilitated loss of the interesting alleles. 
For it to be plausible that natural selection played a role, e.g. our mutations of interest having 
an impact on proliferation, there are three requirements to be satisfied: 1) the gene would need 
to be expressed in the undifferentiated NSC state, 2) the mutation needs to have a negative 
impact on proliferation/fitness of the undifferentiated NSC, and 3) the mutation should still 
allow proliferation in undifferentiated iPSCs as all iPSC lines exist for all three mutations 
(Ambasudhan et al., 2013; Chung et al., 2013; Imaizumi et al., 2012; Schöndorf et al., 2014).  
5.1.2.2.1 PRKN-KO 
Although there is no direct research on whether parkin is expressed in undifferentiated hVM1 
cells, there is some evidence of expression in adult rat hippocampal NSCs (H. Park et al., 2019), 
as well as widespread expression during mouse brain and CNS development (specifically high 
expression in the brainstem of which the midbrain is a part) (Choi et al., 2016; Kühn et al., 
2004; Stichel et al., 2000). Furthermore, fibroblasts and iPSC and induced neural stem cells 
derived from these fibroblasts also express parkin (Pacelli et al., 2019). However, while 
spatially (i.e. high expression in the developing brainstem) and temporally (i.e. expression 
already in early brain development) the literature supports parkin expression in hNSCs, some 
cell types like HeLa do not express parkin (Husnjak & Dikic, 2006). Therefore, expression 
would ideally be verified experimentally through e.g. western blotting. 
If parkin is expressed in undifferentiated hVM1-cells, then one mechanism of action through 
which PRKN-KO could lead to decreased proliferation is accumulation of the cyclin-dependent 
kinase inhibitor p21. p21 is a neuronal cell cycle inhibitor and overexpression in cell lines 
results in cell cycle arrest (Marqués-Torrejón et al., 2013; M. H. Park et al., 2017). Parkin is 
ubiquitinates p21, which induces proteasomal degradation. Supporting this, p21 protein was 
highly accumulated in NSCs of PRKN-KO mice (M. H. Park et al., 2017). These NSCs also 
experienced a loss in self-renewal (M. H. Park et al., 2017). Therefore, it is feasible that

monoubiquitination of epidermal growth factor receptor pathway substrate 15 (Eps15) (Fallon

growth factor receptor (EGFR) from the plasma membrane. Overexpression of Eps15, which 
PRKN-KO may mimic, substantially reduces cell proliferation, likely through inhibition of the 
EGFR recycling pathway and the associated detrimental mitogenic effects (Chi et al., 2011).

neuronal survival, possibly through impaired recruitment of DNA repair factors (Lim et al., 
2012; P. Liu et al., 2018).  
Collectively, it is plausible but not certain that PRKN-KO had a negative effect on proliferation 
in our experiment. While p21 accumulation, EGFR downregulation and impaired DNA repaired 
through decreased parkin activity lead to substantial detrimental effects, it is unclear whether 
parkin is expressed in hNSC and hVM1 in specific.  If desired, parkin expression can be 
determined through western blotting. If expressed, an inducible PRKN-KO model would allow 
to test whether growth rates decline when parkin is knocked out. However, if such a model is 
unavailable then a knockdown experiment could also be done, e.g. through RNA interference 
(RNAi). If this is observed, the hypotheses on the underlying mechanisms of action could be 
investigated, e.g. whether p21 accumulates, if EGFR is downregulated and/or if the DNA repair 
pathway is inhibited.

5.1.2.2.2 SNCA-A53T 
A-syn is endogenously expressed in (embryonic and adult) NSC (Flierl et al., 2014; J. Li et al., 
2002; Winner et al., 2004). Overexpression has a detrimental effects on growth, viability 
(around half of WT) and stress resistance (Flierl et al., 2014). . Although the effect of p.A53T 
on undifferentiated hNSC is not known, it does have a detrimental effect on neurogenesis and 
proliferation of neural progenitor cells (Crews et al., 2008; Winner et al., 2008).  
Mechanisms underlying a possible decrease from p.A53T in hNSC proliferation were 
previously covered in the theory section, i.e. impaired lysosome and proteasome activity, 
increased mitochondrial and DNA damage through promoting ROS generation and 
pathological membrane composition leading to altered endo- and exocytosis (reviewed in 
Heras-Garvin & Stefanova, 2020). WT SNCA also has anti-apoptotic effect in non-
dopaminergic neurons (already differentiated, i.e. not stem cells) by, amongst others, inhibiting 
P53 expression, but p.A53T abolishes P53 inhibition thereby promoting apoptosis (Alves Da 
Costa et al., 2002; Da Costa et al., 2000). This may also be the case in hNSC.  
5.1.2.2.3 GBA1 
Neural stem cells express GCase and decreased GBA1 activity is associated with decreased 
proliferation and self-renewal (Awad et al., 2017; H. Lee et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2015). The 
mechanisms previously in the theory section could underlie this decrease in proliferation. In 
addition, the involvement of GCase in the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway may be highly 
relevant in the context of neural stem cells renewal (Awad et al., 2017).  
Wnt/β-catenin signaling is crucial for NSC renewal, expansion, cell division and neural 
differentiation (Bengoa-Vergniory & Kypta, 2015; Chavali et al., 2018; Wexler et al., 2009). 
In GBA1-/- neural progenitor cells the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway was significantly 
downregulated compared to WT,  likely due to increased proteasomal degradation and 
destabilization of β-catenin, which in turn may be caused by dysfunctional endolysosomal 
compartmentalization due to the absence of GCase (Awad et al., 2017). 
 However, it is unclear if p.E326K would result in a similar phenotype, especially given the 
mildness of this mutation compared to Gaucher-disease related mutations which most of these 
studies are based on. Nevertheless, GCase is implicated in NSC-specific self-renewal, and it is 
plausible that p.E326K (mildly) decreases proliferation rate compared to WT, which would 
allow for increased rate and likelihood of p.E326K allele loss in the cell population.

5.1.3 Recommendations 
One clear lesson I have learned is to validate assumptions. With regards to the single-cell 
isolation method, the assumption of not being able to do manual cell picking due to hVM1 
growth characteristics should have been tested. In addition, the assumption that achieving 
single-cell isolation through limiting dilution with 5 cells per well would be likely should have 
been validated, e.g. by seeking to understand the underlying probability distribution. If this 
would have been done, then we would have concluded that the empty wells we were seeing for 
the 1 cell per well plates were expected and necessary, and we would not have increased the 
seeding density to 5 or 25 cells per well. This would have ultimately increased the chance of 
success and saved a lot of resources and frustration.  
In addition, I would recommend to not do limiting dilution for single cell isolation at a small 
scale, and instead do manual cell picking or do dilution plating but with a visual check directly 
after seeding. For manual cell picking, you seed a large dish with a similarly low cell 
concentration and then mark spots where you can see single cells through microscopy. These 
are then allowed to expand to an appropriate size (large enough for inoculating into a separate 
dish/well). The biggest benefit of manual cell picking is the increased chance of monoclonality, 
which leads to decreased risk of interesting candidates being overgrown by uninteresting 
candidates. 
If your cell line does not grow in dense distinct colonies like iPSCs tend to do, but instead tend 
to grow outwards quickly, you can still use manual cell picking. After identifying single cells 
after seeding, you could use cloning rings around single cells to create a physical barrier. Using 
cloning rings would also allow for cell disassociation liquids like trypsin to be used instead of 
having to manually and mechanically inoculate which may be inaccurate and detrimental to cell 
health. Furthermore, doing manual cell picking has the added benefit of allowing slower 
growing colonies to grow to a size suitable for DNA isolation and splitting. This is important, 
as, especially for disease modeling such as was done in this project, it may be plausible that the 
intended gene edit has a negative effect on proliferation. Also, for cell line creation with the 
aim of molecule production, increased yield is often associated with slower growth (Fuller et 
al., 1988).  
However, manual cell picking can be too slow for high-throughput screening. In such a case, I 
recommend doing a variation on limiting dilution proposed by Anne Underwood & Bean 
(1988). Here, a similarly low cell concentration is used (i.e. 0.5 to 0.9 cells per well) but after 
==PAGE:57==
48 
 
seeding you visually check if cells are present in the wells, mark those that are empty and reseed 
them. This does lead to a higher upfront time investment at the seeding stage but will save you 
time in analysis and cell culturing due to the decreased volume of wells that need to be screened. 
In the ideal case where all wells without cells are reseeded until there are no more empty wells, 
the probability of a well having one cell increases from 30% to 77% (Table 5). To increase 
efficiency in obtaining a monoclonal clone, you may also mark wells with more than one cell 
after seeding. Then, if an interesting candidate corresponds to a well that had more than one 
cell, you will have to at least do two limiting dilutions. If that well had only one cell, it is 
recommended to do a subsequent round of visually-confirmed single cell plating (recommended 
due to probability of incorrect cell counting, e.g. through cell clumping (C. Chen et al., 2020; 
Greenfield, 2019)). 
Lastly, if single-cell survival is low for your cell line, you may still be able to do the above 
techniques by using specific media supplements for increased single cell survival or by 
supplementing growth media with 25-50% conditioned media.

5.2 PCR establishment and problem-solving 
Although the single-cell isolation likely was the main culprit in obtaining the desired cell lines, 
analysis of the DNA samples with PCR and sequencing was also less than optimal. Mainly for 
SNCA and GBA1 the PCR reaction was often unsuccessful: SNCA_A53T_1 and 
GBA1_E326K_long did not lead to amplification, and later SNCA_A53T_3 consistently 
showed a smear instead of a specific PCR product band. Even though the exact reasons for 
these issues were never found, we eventually were able to move passed them by using different 
primers and changing from prepGEM to the DNeasy for DNA isolation. However, I believe 
that if we had used more extensive (positive) controls once problems arose in addition to a more 
==PAGE:58==
49 
 
structured problem-solving approach, then we would have been able to home in on and solve 
the problem much faster.  
In the beginning, no controls were included for the PCRs on 96-well plates. Only when 
problems arose, a positive (DNA sample that worked well in previous PCR) and a negative 
control (DNA replaced with DNase-free water). However, when none of the lanes on the gel 
display a band, you can only deduce that the PCR itself didn’t work (rather than that the PCR 
would have worked but that the DNA samples were faulty). They also do not give an indication 
on why the PCR did not work. Given my inexperience, the positive control also not working 
may indicate faulty PCR preparation, and so we often had to resort to trying the same thing 
again (often with the same result). If that gel would also be empty, we would test one specific 
parameter without first determining at what step the issue occurs. Looking back, I think having 
information on at which stage of the screen process the issue occurs would have led to more 
fruitful problem-solving, thereby saving resources. 
One way to see at what stage in the screening process the issue occurs, is by including more 
controls. Specifically, I think the following controls would have been informative yet realistic: 
1. A standard positive control (‘positive control’, PC) as was used, i.e. similar DNA 
sample (DNA isolated the same way by the same person for the same cell line cultured 
with the same setup) that has worked well in a previous (similar) PCR. 
2. A control using a high-quality DNA sample (‘high-quality positive control’, HQ PC), 
i.e. in this case rather than a DNA sample isolated using prepGEM and diluted to an 
unknown concentration, a DNA sample isolated using the DNeasy kit and diluted to a 
known concentration (and worked well in a previous PCR). 
3.  A ‘primer positive control’ (primer PC) made up from a mix of the DNA samples to be 
tested (e.g. 1 µl from each well of a 96-well plate row mixed together), but a different 
primer pair (of which its template DNA is also present in the sample) is used. Ideally 
this primer pair is one that robustly works and is similar in amplicon length and in primer 
melting temperature to avoid having to use two thermal cyclers. 
Together, these three controls will give you an indication at what stage the issue occurs. For 
example, if there are no PCR products visible on your gel you can consult the decision tree in 
Figure 18 and Table 6 to help you pinpoint and solve the problem. A practical example of how 
this may be useful is that when the PCR didn’t work, we would often resort to new DNA 
isolation amongst others. However, later we tried the more robust PRKN_E2del PCR on the 
==PAGE:59==
50 
 
DNA samples that wouldn’t work (e.g. Figure 11, panel B), and it would result in clear and 
clean bands from which you can deduce that the DNA sample was likely not the culprit. If we 
would have included the primer PC, we would have been able to deduce this immediately. 
In hindsight, we should have also had appropriate controls for the restriction digestion (one 
positive control, i.e. PCR product sample with restriction enzyme cutting site, and one negative 
control, i.e. a PCR product sample without a cutting site) and Sanger sequencing (positive 
control, i.e. a sample that was successfully sequenced previously, and one negative control, i.e. 
a sample where DNA is replaced with DNase-free water).

5.3 Interconnectedness 
In this discussion, two main problem areas were discussed: 1) inappropriate cell seeding 
densities for limiting dilutions, and 2) poor PCR establishment and subsequent problem-
solving. In this section, the interconnected component of these issues is discussed (visualized 
in Figure 20). While these issues would have been surmountable on their own, together they 
decreased the odds of success significantly. 
While the base risk of interesting alleles being lost in the cell population due to overgrowth was 
set by the seeding densities for the limiting dilutions, the delays caused by being unable to 
screen candidates due to PCR issues exacerbated this risk. This is because interesting candidates 
spent more time/generations in polyclonal cell populations, which increases the probability of 
allele loss due to genetic drift.  
In addition, the screening issues increased workload. Firstly, because the screening would need 
to be repeated (at least once), but secondly because it bloated cell culture work as uninteresting 
cultures couldn’t be discarded. This bloating became significant, as each screened 96-well plate 
would lead to a net increase in cell cultures to maintain:  
• Additional 96-well plates for the limiting dilution of the most interesting candidates; 
• Additional wells (on 6/12-well plates) for the other candidates that might be promising 
(as a back-up); 
==PAGE:63==
54 
 
• An additional well for the original interesting candidate as a back-up. 
Since the issues investigated in Figure 11, Figure 12 and Figure 16 arose towards the end, there 
were sometimes already 3-4 cycles of limiting dilution done per initial interesting candidate 
(e.g. see Figure 8), so there were a lot of cell cultures to maintain. Because working time was 
limited (as the laboratory would close after working hours), there was less time for screening 
and problem solving, leading to further delays in identifying interesting candidates which then 
fueled this feedback loop further. Poor usage of PCR controls further enhanced this detrimental 
feedback loop, as PCR issues persisted for longer than needed. Additionally, increased cell 
culture work increases the risk of human error due to having to do an increased number of 
actions but also due to increased complexity and time pressure.  
While the project was unsuccessful, valuable experience was gained in gene editing and cell 
culturing. In addition, it has demonstrated the importance of proper experiment design, of 
validating assumptions, of proper documentation and of good stakeholder management.

6. Conclusion 
There is a large need for a better understanding of PD, as its burden is significant and increasing 
yet preventative or curative treatments are absent. In vitro models with hNSC hold promise, but 
cell line availability is limited. Therefore, we aimed to generate hVM1 cell lines with PD-
related mutations using CRISPR-Cas9. These mutations were PRKN-KO, SNCA-A53T and 
GBA1-E326K. Although these mutations were observed during screening, we failed in 
establishing monoclonal cell lines: the mutations seemingly but consistently disappeared in the 
daughter cell populations in subsequent screening.  
While the desired cell lines were not obtained, there are several cryopreserved and promising 
but (likely) multiclonal cell populations for each of the desired gene-edits. Therefore, if these 
cell lines are still desired, using these samples as a starting point may expedite the cell line 
development process.  
Looking back, it is likely that too high cell concentrations were used for limiting dilution, which 
significantly increased the likelihood of the cell populations staying polyclonal. In turn, alleles 
with the mutation of interest faced a higher probability of loss due to genetic drift. Additionally, 
this probability of loss may have further been enhanced due natural selection, since the desired 
mutations may have negatively affecting evolutionary fitness. Furthermore, screening delays 
extended the time (and thus generation number) that these interesting alleles spent in polyclonal 
populations, multiplying the probability of allele loss.
