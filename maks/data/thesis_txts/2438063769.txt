Abstract 
In this project, a framework has been developed for optimization-based product-process design using 
group contribution methods. The framework proposes to help the user in the decision making. The 
framework presents a methodology of solving an integrated problem, and subsequently ranking a list 
of solutions such that flexibility is incorporated into the sequence of optimal solutions. In this way, 
individual needs can be meet. The solution is then subject to a product-process analysis to determine 
missing process data. Subsequently, an economic analysis was conducted to assess the final solution(s) 
obtained from the framework.  
The framework has been applied to two cases: Case-study 1 is the integrated product-process 
design of an acetic acid-water extraction process. The aim was to intensify the process and find better 
alternatives. Using the framework, isobutyl formate was found to be a better solvent than the industrial 
standard, which is ethyl acetate. The improvement showed to be more economical as the profit was 
increased with 46 %.  Furthermore, the application also resulted in a reduction of directly emitted CO2 
with 53 %, which was the result of reduced utilities. 
Furthermore, Case-study 2 was also conducted. This was on the product design of switchable 
water for improved dehydration of THF by optimizing the molecular structure of the amine, which is 
an additive in switchable water. The THF recovery was predicted using a group contribution approach 
by fitting experimental data to first-order parameters. A process flowsheet was suggested for the 
switchable water process and the amine additive was tried to be optimized. From a list of solutions, 
Dimethyl-N-propylamine was found to be the most optimal for the formulated problem. Based on a 
TRL and NPV assessment, the switchable water was assessed, but did not show to be a better alternative 
than current existing processes, such as pressure swing and extractive distillation.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background and historical review 
Our society today needs different products that are produced in the chemical and biochemical industry. 
The demand of these products is highly subject to the free market forces and societal needs, and the 
demand is a function of the price and what the customers prefer. It is in the interest of the industry to 
act upon a demand and provide a solution to earn a profit. To increase revenue, we would like to make 
the product as profitable as possible, or better performing to increase customer preferability over 
competitor products. This gives a reason for the engineer to better understand each step in the product 
design process from an identified need to the shelf. According to (Cussler & Wei) the methodolog [1] 
of product design as seen in Figure 1 can be divided into four major steps: 1) Need, 2) Idea, 3) Selection, 
and 4) Manufacture. Each major step then have different methodologies and approaches that pushes the 
product closer to the customer who wants it. 
Figure 1. The four steps in product design. 
Our role as engineers could be to facilitate and make the product available to the society, whether it is 
a single molecule, or formulated product e.g. a detergent, a perfume, or a soft drink. This can include: 
the identification of a need at a customer segment, generation of different ideas to solve a given need, 
the selection of the best solution to a problem, and the manufacturing of this product that essentially 
fulfils a given need. 
A few examples of products that society needs can be process fluids, plastics, fuels, detergents, 
and cosmetics. These products can consist of a pure component or a mixture of different chemical 
substances in varying concentrations. The product essentially has to fulfil one or more objectives in 
terms of functionality e.g. heat-transfer fluid’s ability to transfer energy, a solvent’s ability to solubilize 
a solute, or a fuel’s combustion and flammability properties. The design of these products can be centred 
on specific properties and better alternatives can be found by identifying the solutions that have 
enhanced properties, for example better environmental properties due to lower human toxicity. 
Currently, the approach to designing products in the chemical and biochemical industry is based 
on heuristics and by trial-and-error. It might provide a feasible solution, or a good initial first guess to 
a problem but does not guarantee that the best possible solution is achieved, which also is referred to as 
the global optimal solution. This means, better opportunities in terms of design, economics, and 
sustainability. By combining systematic models, algorithms, and computational methods, the value of 
an objective function, which is a pre-defined quantitative measure, can be maximized or minimized by 
varying different process and/or product variables. In this way, the global optimal solution can be

determined for a given problem. Also, a list of optimal solutions can be determined for further

By including models that can associate pure component and/or mixture properties, one can 
identify a molecule or a mixture, which is the best for a given problem. This approach is called 
computer-aided molecular design (CAMD). However, this can be difficult as the different interactions 
between all components often are of non-linear nature, which makes it very hard to predict precisely 
the properties. Thus, it is important to know the capabilities and limitations of the models used for the 
prediction, and the right model should be identified for a given use. The results obtained from CAMD 
is only as good as the prediction capabilities, and simply wrong predictions will result in untrustworthy 
results. Furthermore, a model containing parameters for predicting specific properties must also be in 
place and the lack of the same makes it hard to apply the method. More advanced group contribution 
(GC) models can be applied to increase the accuracy of the prediction. However, this will decrease the 
simplicity, and the application of more advanced models might result in a more constrained problem. 
CAMD has been used for several cases [2]–[4], and even has the potential to be applied in more areas, 
such as process development. 
The method could be used as a decision and design tool for selection of the right solution based 
on guidelines, which can help the user identify the right product in a more efficient, less random, and 
less time-consuming way compared to what is done traditionally. Also, by providing more accurate 
parameters, and models for predicting more pure component and mixture properties, the more useful 
and applicable will the method be. 
Two notable articles [2], [5] exist that can be helpful in making a decent overview of the 
different parts and aspects of CAMD. The review article by (Austin, Sahinidis, & Trahan, 2016) and 
then the book chapter by (Gani, Zhang, Kalakul, & Cignitti, 2017).  
The first paper provides a thorough overview of the material available on CAMD from a more 
engineering point of view as it tries to explain the different tools, applications, and ways to solve the 
problems, as wells as discusses the different approaches to a problem. Different techniques and 
mathematical methods are described to solve these problems. Lastly, the application of these tools to 
CAMD are explained and discussed for different applications and situations. 
The second paper introduces CAMD and property prediction of diverse kinds of applications. 
In the chapter, different solution frameworks and solving methods are described. Subsequently, 
different case studies follow such as refrigerant design, and surfactant design. Everything is wrapped 
up by a discussion of the author’s view of the areas of future development for CAMD.  
Additionally, the book chapter by (Abildskov et al., 2004) introduces the modelling and use of 
the semi-empirical UNIFAC model and its different modifications. The progress and development of 
the model is described as well as the different thoughts and considerations that had been made when 
developing the different modifications. This is useful when considering when and how to use it when 
considering the prediction of non-ideal mixture properties, which can be applied to CAMD as a tool for 
prediction of new solvents.

1.2 Problem statement 
A framework should be in place, which uses CAMD for integrated product-process design, which also 
takes economic and environmental considerations into account. However, such a framework is not fully 
investigated and put in place. 
Currently, the heuristic approach to product-process design gives feasible solutions but 
opportunities of better alternatives might be missed out. This approach includes rule of thumb, giving 
an educated guess, use of intuitive judgement, and guess estimation. However, they do not guarantee to 
give a global optimal solution or even an optimal solution.  
By providing a methodology that describes the use of an optimization-based integrated product-
process design framework, using GC methods for product-process design, a list of optimal solution 
candidates can be obtained. At the same time other consideration such as economics and sustainability 
will be taken into account. This is done by developing a methodology that will include these 
considerations and applying it to two case-studies as a proof-of-concept. 
 Work to be done 
First, the framework will be applied to a case-study on single-stage extraction of acetic acid-water to 
show that an optimal solution can be obtained when doing integrated product-process design. Thus, 
obtaining the full potential whether it is maximum sustainability, or highest profit. 
Another case-study will be conducted on switchable water in parallel to the case with the acetic 
acid-water. The study will be about product design, which in this case will be the design of amines 
using GC methods to predict the recovery of THF from water, and to find a suitable amine for the 
switchable water process. 
 Future outlook as result of framework 
Using this framework, one could find solutions in which the economic and environmental aspects are 
considered in a more systematic way such the end result also takes these considerations into account. 
This will not just ease the way of finding more optimal solutions, but hopefully it will make it possible 
to obtain improved results in a more non-heuristic way.

Task 1: Design of solvent candidates for acetic acid-water system using sequential approach for

Using the general algebraic modelling system (GAMS), the optimization problems will be solved. In 
parallel to the sequential optimization-problem a program will be used to determine the most optimal 
solution using integrated product-process design. A list of solvents is generated according to the 
objective function. The solvents are then ranked according to availability, economics, and sustainability 
and will be screened for the most optimal solvent. 
Task 2: Rigorous process simulation of solvent candidates for case 1 
After the global optimal solutions have been identified, ternary LLE data is obtained from the process 
simulation program ASPEN Plus using the UNIFAC model. Rigorous simulation is done using the 
process simulation tool and process equipment is sized and costed. 
Task 3: Detailed economic analysis of process design of case 1 
The net-present value (NPV) will be calculated based on Guthrie’s method. Furthermore, another 
economic evaluation might be conducted, which assesses the uncertainty of the process based on the 
inputs and outputs of the process. The overall costs will be compared between the simulation cases that 
has been obtained using the sequential method. 
Task 4: Data acquisition of switchable water (SW) for case 2 
Data on the recovery of tetrahydrofuran (THF) from water using different amines in carbonized water 
after reaction must be collected. A collaboration partner from North Carolina State University (NCSU) 
is included and preferably relatively simple tertiary amines will be experimentally investigated to obtain 
solubility data. 
Task 5: Parameter estimation for case 2, solvent design and selection for SW 
The recovery data obtained using different amines in carbonized water will be analysed and GC 
parameters will be obtained by fitting first-order parameters to experimental data, that later can be 
applied to the design and selection of other amines for the same application. From the obtained first-
order GC parameters for the different molecular groups, the data is planned to be validated against other 
experimental values to investigate the accuracy of the prediction of the THF recovery using amines in 
carbonized water. The validation might also include the use of a solver to determine an optimal or a list 
of optimal candidates of amines to find better solvents for the switchable water technology. 
Task 6: Writing of report 
The writing of the report will be carried out continuously throughout the whole project. However, 
several partial reports will be submitted. The first partial report will be submitted as an introduction to

water case. The second report will contain the finished SW case. The last report will be submitted in 
the form of a master thesis, which sums up the work of the whole project.

1.4 Computer-aided molecular design 
In CAMD, a molecule or a mixture/blend is designed such that a given set of property requirements are 
met and/or the product fulfils a given function. Using models that can associate pure component, or 
mixture properties to the molecular structure or composition, the structure can be determined based on 
requirements to product, or process properties. The method might include mathematical optimization 
which includes an objective function to find one or more optimal solutions. In this way, thousands of 
candidates can be screened, which otherwise would have been investigated using heuristics, and the 
number of possibilities can be narrowed down to a few manageable candidates. 
CAMD could provide a way to optimize current processes such as liquid-liquid extraction by 
identifying solvents, which have better attributes e.g. solvents that at least have the same performance 
as the current but less toxic. The idea is, CAMD should be a tool for scientists and engineers to make 
the selection and decision-process easier in the development of new products and chemical processes. 
By providing one or more good initial guesses, one could provide a list of compounds which could be 
taken to further investigation. However, it must be emphasized that CAMD is not considered to be a 
final decision tool by itself. 
 Quantitative structure-property relationship [5] 
Within CAMD different approaches exist to relate the molecular structure to specific properties. The 
most used method is by semi-empirical quantitative structure-property relationships (QSPRs). Different 
modelling methods exist to make a QSPR, such as the mentioned semi-empirical, molecular 
mechanical, and ab initio. These methods can by describing molecules in different levels, predict with 
different levels of accuracy. The semi-empirical method is the most used due to its simplicity. 
Furthermore, different classes of QSPRs exist, such as GC methods, topological indices, and signature 
descriptors. 
Group contribution 
This type of QSPR is the most used because of its simplicity. It is also easily translated into a 
mathematical formula such that it can be included in e.g. optimization problems. It associates a pre-
defined sub-structure or part of a molecule and the number of occurrences of the sub-structure to 
estimate a specific property. One weakness is that the method hardly recognises different isomers based 
on the GC groups. Thus, the predictive ability of the model is drastically reduced. However, methods 
such as the one developed by (Marrero & Gani, 2001, 2002) can distinguish between different isomers, 
because it includes larger groups. Another weakness is the low level of consistency in definition of the 
groups. This has no effect on the property prediction but might cause trouble when setting up the 
mathematical formulation for the CAMD problem. Furthermore, there is a lack of consistency in the 
estimated parameters as the estimation might result in different outcomes depending on which groups 
are used. 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
Topological indices 
It is the use of chemical graph theory to do QSPR. Using the method, molecules can be described as 
nodes and edges in a graph as seen in Figure 2. In this way, various aspects of the graph such as number 
of atoms, and the length of paths between atoms, also called topological indices, can be used to relate 
the structure to properties. 
 
Figure 2. Example of topological indices [5]. 
The properties of the graph can be correlated to coefficients, and like the GC method be used to predict 
properties. One of the strengths of the method is that it in some cases can distinguish between isomers. 
Contrary to the GC method, the contribution to a property is not independently contributed by each 
molecular sub-fragment but a function of the whole graph. Furthermore, it has been reported that the 
quality and amount of literature on topological indices is very high. Even though the method seems 
simple, and lots of data exist, then it has not been used a lot in CAMD. This is because the method often 
is restricted to a certain sub-class of molecules, thus reducing the possible search-space drastically. 
Lastly, the method is harder to implement in CAMD, and they often face combinatorial problems. Thus, 
resulting in only the design of smaller molecules. 
Signature descriptors 
In this method a combination of the concepts from topological indices and GC are used. While 
considering specific sub-sets of a molecule, it also relies on graph theory to capture properties. In Figure 
3, a single atom is considered, and different levels of description exist from that atom. Thus, by 
increasing the height of description, the level of accuracy can also be increased. 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
Figure 3. Example of signature descriptors for a single carbon atom (purple) [5]. 
An advantage of the signature descriptors is that it can be used to describe a molecule with low 
inaccuracy, compared to the other methods. The only thing that must be defined is how the different 
atoms are distinguished, such as colouring. Since every atom is included in the signature, in principle 
no limit exists on the size of the designed molecular structure. Furthermore, since the signature 
descriptor has a high equivalence to the GC method and the topological indices method, then it can be 
used directly with these QSPR models, thus increasing the available data library for the signature 
descriptor method. A disadvantage of using the method is that since all atoms are included in creating 
an atomic signature up to a certain level, then the accuracy may suffer, because not enough data has 
been provided to give a good enough fit. 
 Group contribution methods 
Pure component property prediction 
GC methods can predict the properties of molecules or mixtures based on the contribution of specific 
molecular groups and the number of times they occur [8], [10], [12]. A simple example is given for the 
acetaldehyde molecule which is considered in Figure 4. The molecule consists of two first-order groups: 
CH3 and CHO. By fitting parameters to a collection of molecules the contribution of the each individual 
group can be estimated. 
 
Figure 4.  The molecular structure of acetaldehyde and its first-order groups (blue and green). 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
These specific groups can be represented in different degrees of order as the need of accuracy for the 
prediction and the complexity of the molecule increases. In Figure 5, a more complex molecule can be 
seen. It can be described in more detail by considering first, second [10], and third-order groups [12]. 
In this case, the molecule will consist of the following: first (aC-NH2, aC-NH, aC, aCH), second (p-
aromatic ring), and third-order (aC-NH-aC), where a is indicating an aromatic group, C, H, and N 
represents carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen atoms, respectively. The p is indicating that the aromatic ring 
is para-substituted. 
 
Figure 5. Molecular structure of N-phenyl-1,4-benzenediamine. 
Properties such as boiling point, critical temperature and pressure, specific heat capacity, and heat of 
vaporization can be predicted based on the molecular structure. This is useful when data is not available 
for the pure component and mixture. 
Properties can be categorized differently according to how they are predicted or calculated [20]: 
 
Primary: Pure component properties that are only dependent on the molecular structure, and 
are often of a single value such as the critical temperature and pressure. 
 
Secondary: Pure component properties that are dependent on other properties such as the 
boiling point Tb(P), and enthalpy of vaporization ΔHvap (Tb). 
 
Functional: Pure component properties that are dependent on either temperature, or pressure 
such as the vapour pressure, and heat of vaporization. 
 
Mixture (Two types) 
o Bulk: Averaged properties of a mixture, and the phases it consists of where 
temperature, pressure, and composition are specified, such as the saturation pressure 
or density. 
o Phase equilibrium: Properties related to the phase equilibrium, where composition, 
temperature, and pressure are specified.

Mixture-design 
When doing mixture-design an activity coefficient model is needed to consider the non-ideal behaviour. 
Mostly three models are used in CAMD: UNIFAC, SAFT, or COSMO-based. 
UNIFAC is possibly the most widely used model due to its simplicity, and that many interaction 
parameters already exist. It is simple and is reported to often give good accuracy [6]. The original model 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
might lack parameters outside the intended range. However, as earlier mentioned then additional 
parameters have been supplied as an extension. 
SAFT [25] is a theoretically derived model and an equation of state, which is based on 
perturbation theory. Many versions of the SAFT model exist, but to calculate activity coefficients the 
SAFT-γ [26] was developed to predict binary mixture data. The model uses pure component data to 
obtain parameters for mixture property prediction. Furthermore, the method shows to have a good 
agreement with experimental data in a broad pressure range. 
The COSMO-based [27] approach only requires molecular volumes and molecule-specific 
charged density distributions to predict mixture properties [28]. These can again be estimated using GC 
methods. Also, the method enables the combination of highly accurate molecular information to be 
estimated using ab initio quantum chemistry for mixture property prediction. 
The UNIFAC, and SAFT-γ require binary data, which have been obtained experimentally, 
whereas the COSMO-based approach does not require any. However, it has been shown that pure 
component data might be used in the estimation of binary interaction parameters for SAFT-γ, which 
eases the estimation. Furthermore, SAFT-γ has shown to be applicable in a much broader pressure range 
than UNIFAC. Nevertheless, UNIFAC until now has continued to be the most used model for mixtures 
in the scientific literature. Possibly because it is simple, widely applied in CAMD, and many binary 
parameters already exist. 
UNIFAC 
The most used GC method currently is the UNIFAC model [29], developed by (Fredenslund et al., 
1975), which is a thermodynamic semi-empirical activity coefficient model for estimation of phase 
equilibrium data. It is also a further development of the UNIQUAC model. Using UNIFAC, the liquid 
phase activity coefficient is calculated as the sum of all the contributions of the groups present in a 
mixture of different components. In the model, the activity coefficient is the combined contribution 
from a combinatorial and a residual part. The combinatorial part is the contribution from the difference 
in molecular sizes, shape of molecules, and are based on parameters that account for the surface area of 
groups and volumes of the same. The residual part is the contribution from energetic differences 
between molecular groups, which is accounted for by interaction parameters.

The UNIFAC model comes in a variety of modifications such as the KT-UNIFAC, UNIFAC-LLE, and 
linear-UNIFAC, which accommodates different needs and situations. This together with the method 
being dependent on the temperature and the composition makes it useful when one must predict the 
properties of a non-ideal mixture and no data is existing. 
The model is a versatile tool to estimate phase equilibrium data when no data is available. This 
is due to its nature, being only dependent on molecular groups, composition, and temperature. The 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
temperature ranges that the model can be applied to depend on the type of UNIFAC model, and the 
temperature range the parameters have been estimated at. Temperature ranges that the model is applied 
to typically are 0-150 °C  for the original model, and 10-40 °C for the UNIFAC-LLE [31]. Furthermore, 
the components that are included in the prediction must be condensable close to ambient conditions.  
The accuracy of the model highly depends on the groups, mixture, and the binary interaction 
parameters available. The size parameters can be calculated by theory, and the interaction parameters 
can be estimated using experimental phase equilibrium data. A lot of parameters [29] are listed in tables 
in the original paper, but it is not complete. The table of parameters have been extended and revised 
afterwards along with the model equation Work by others have resulted in additional parameters since 
the original paper came out in 1975. It has been reported that satisfying prediction was not obtained 
using parameters fitted to vapour-liquid equilibrium (VLE) data [31]. This resulted in a separate model 
called the UNIFAC-LLE, which is based on parameters fitted to liquid-liquid equilibrium (LLE) data. 
This was later partially proven [32], but this seems not always the case as the predictions are accurate 
enough in some cases using VLE parameters for LLE. 
The use of more complex versions of the model reduces the simplicity, thus might require the 
change of model for a more reliable prediction. Second [21] or higher-order [12] groups can also be 
used to make the estimations better, but this decreases the simplicity. It should also be mentioned that 
the model does not distinguish between different structural isomers of a molecule, but this can be done 
using second-order or higher-order group parameters with modified versions of the UNIFAC model, 
such as the KT-UNIFAC. Furthermore, to use second or higher-order group parameters, then these 
should be available as this seems not always to be the case as parameters are still missing for some 
groups. 
 CAMD solution methods 
When solving a CAMD problem or an integrated product-process design problem, different approaches 
can be used in finding an optimal solution. Most of the methods can be categorized as either: sequential, 
simultaneous, or continuous molecular targeting. The most used ones are the sequential, and the 
simultaneous. In the sequential approach, a set of solutions are first generated and subsequently “tried” 
on a process. Using enumeration the solutions can then be ranked and a list of optimal solutions 
according to their objective function, or a specific attribute value can be obtained. In CAMD, the 
objective function is generally subject to constraints imposed on the molecular structure (𝑔1), pure 
component properties (𝑔2), and/or mixture properties (𝑔3). In the case of an integrated product-process 
optimization problem one or more process models (𝑔4), which sets the boundaries for the feasible 
regions are also included. A problem can have a multitude of solutions, which all are optimal solutions, 
except the one that gives the global optimal solution. Depending on the CAMD solution method used, 
the approach to achieve the global optimal solution can be different. 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
Simultaneous approach 
Simultaneous optimization is the solving of a problem simultaneously with the objective function being 
either maximized or minimized. 
This kind of optimization problem consists of an objective function, which either is maximized 
or minimized by systematically changing the variables to obtain the best possible outcome within the 
allowed domain. This allowed domain is restricted by constraints, which sets the boundaries for the 
feasible solution of the objective function. Furthermore, an objective function can have several feasible 
solutions or optima. Thus, when the search space is getting bigger, then it might take longer time to 
compute all the solutions to find the global optimal solution. 
CAMD problems will often be solved as mixed-integer non-linear programs (MINLPs). Thus, 
different methods exist to solve these efficiently. In general outer-approximation [9], [33], has been 
used over the years, but in cases where the method fails to provide a solution, metaheuristic optimization 
strategies are used [34]. Stochastic optimization such as efficient ant colony optimization (EACO) [18], 
[34], and simulated annealing (SA) [15] have been suggested in cases where the simpler gradient-based 
methods do not give a solution. However, it should be mentioned that these methods does not 
necessarily result in optimality, and the performance of a specific solving method depends on the nature 
of the problem.

Continuous molecular targeting [17] 
The method uses simultaneous optimization with a process-based objective function. The process 
objective function calculates the optimal pure component or mixture properties based on optimal 
process conditions needed to achieve the global optimal solution. Based on the target product properties 
the pure component or mixture can be generated using a GC method. The application of this method is 
existing in the case when the thermodynamic model used to predict the product properties is complex, 
such as the PC-SAFT. However, the method has the drawback of that the finding of an exact fit of target 
molecules can be hard with the variable properties. This leads to a relaxation of the product properties 
for a more efficient solution. 
 Application areas 
Different applications of CAMD can be mentioned, such as design of refrigerants [39], gasoline and 
lubricant blends [40], solvents [41], [35], [32], and process fluids for organic Rankine cycles [42]. This 
list is not limiting and CAMD can in principle be applied to all products and processes, which depend 
on the pure component or mixture properties. These properties must of course also be predictable using 
models. 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
An example of application that can be further outlined is the design of more environmentally 
friendly refrigerants. The current refrigerants are depleting the ozone layer and by applying GC methods 
one can predict the ozone depletion potential based on the molecular structure. Other properties should 
also be determined such as the specific heat capacity and enthalpy of vaporization. Furthermore, by also 
including a process model the coefficient of performance (COP), which is a measure of energy 
efficiency of a refrigeration unit can be determined and the overall efficiency of the compound can be 
determined. Using CAMD, potential candidates can be determined for replacement of the current 
refrigerants that otherwise would not have been discovered. 
Another example of application using CAMD is the design of novel solvents for liquid-liquid 
extraction. When extracting solutes from a mixture, it is desirable to have a solvent which is as 
immiscible with the feed and can extract as much solute as possible. Often constraints such as flash 
point and boiling point are put to the solvent to make sure that different requirements to the process 
performance and safety is met.

The framework developed in this work can be seen in Figure 10. In the following sections the major 
steps of the framework is further described in details. It should be noted that in the framework by 
(Cignitti et al., 2018) suggests a simultaneous solution method in Step 1, whereas in this project a

The main area of development of the framework is the addition of Step 2 to 5. Nothing similar to a 
ranking of the solutions was found in the literature. The solutions obtained from the ranking are then 
subsequently investigated and then subject to an economic analysis. 
The main argument to adding a step for ranking is that attributes such as environmental 
properties e.g. toxicity, and potential impacts, cannot be readily be associated directly to cost in an 
objective function, except if the emissions are considered as variable costs related to direct emissions 
in the form of taxation. Pricing of the product is also an issue because in the literature no GC method 
has been found where price is associated to the structure. Also, the price cannot be included in an 
economic objective function, when it is not in a database. Pricing is obviously not simple because e.g. 
variations might occur due to seasonal and geographical differences. These complexities makes it useful 
to incorporate a way of flexibility to adapt the solution to the need of the user after the optimization. In 
Figure 1, a need is followed by idea generation. However, the need of one user might not be the same 
as the need of another, thus the advantage of the incorporated flexibility of the ranking is justified. Over 
the past, several optimal solutions have been claimed for e.g. solvents for liquid-liquid extraction [14], 
[36], [43]. However, the need for an optimal solution might be more complex than just the performance 
in e.g. solvent’s ability to extract a solute. Thus, a method should be in place such that other 
consideration also are taken into account when deciding which solution(s) to further investigate.

2.5 Step 3: Product-process analysis 
Step 3.1: Analysis. The best alternative(s) are analysed either by literature and/or supplier search, 
experimental work if data is missing, and rigorous process simulations. The literature and/or supplier 
search could include known data from already existing rigorous process simulations or data obtained

work, unknown properties can be determined, or predicted properties can be accurately determined to 
obtain a more reliable process simulation and economic analysis. For the rigorous process simulation 
mass and energy balances, chemical equilibrium, reaction kinetics, and thermodynamics are included 
to get a very detailed overview of the process design metrics. Based on these metrics, sized equipment 
and materials needed to manufacture the given product can be costed such that the process subsequently 
can be subject to an economic analysis.

3 Application of Framework 
In the following, two case studies are presented in which the described framework has been applied. 
The first application is on the intensification of the liquid-liquid extraction process of acetic acid-water. 
The second application deals with the dehydration of tetrahydrofuran (THF). 
3.1 Case-study 1 - Liquid-liquid extraction of acetic acid-water 
In this case study, a binary mixture of dilute acetic acid (CH3COOH), and water (H2O) must be 
separated using a solvent for single-stage liquid-liquid extraction as seen in Figure 13. The extract is 
acetic acid rich, and the raffinate is water rich. The reason why such a case is relevant is that acetic acid 
is an inhibitor in the production of certain biofuels and it can be present in small amounts. Thus, it 
would be desirable to remove this impurity in an economically and environmentally acceptable way. 
 Step 1: Formulation and solving 
Step 1.1: Define product target 
(i): The type of product is a mixture of acetic acid, water, and a solvent. The target is to design a solvent 
such that acetic acid can be separated from an aqueous solution. The product constraints are defined 
using upper and lower bounds as seen in Table 8. The main structural constraints imposed to the solvent 
are the total number of groups (NT), the total number of functional groups (NF), and the total number of 
groups of the same type (NS). All groups except CH3, CH2, CH, and C are defined as functional groups. 
Due to the accuracy of the prediction, and environmental reasons, only acyclic molecules are considered 
containing C, H, and O atoms for group set n, thus q in equals 1. Also, it is assumed that more simple 
molecules are cheaper, thus by excluding cyclical components the solvent is expected to be cheaper. 
The groups considered in the problem are i ∈ {CH3, CH2, CH, C, OH, CH3OH, CHO, CH3COO, 
CH2COO, HCOO, CH3CO, CH2O, HCO, COOH, COO, H2O}. The structural requirements to the 
solvent are not to be too complex as it is assumed that more simple molecules are cheaper and more 
volatile, giving a lower operational cost with regards to solvent recovery. Thus, the component must be 
a liquid at ambient conditions and have a maximum melting point of 270 K. It must also be easy to 
recover from the product, therefore having a minimum boiling point of 323 K. 
(ii): The search space from where the molecule for the solvent is generated from is by using CAMD 
using GCs in the form of the UNIFAC model.

Background on switchable water 
Switchable water is a concept belonging to switchable solvent. A switchable solvent is a liquid, which 
can reversibly change between two states so that properties such as solubility, polarity, or hydrophilicity 
changes. This is achieved by adding an additive in the form of a nitrogenous base (B) in the form of an 
amine, or an amide to a liquid. The process is “turned on” by reacting it with CO2, thus resulting in 
protonation of the additive as seen in Equation (3.2.1).

Few applications [53] have been suggested by (Lestari & Abolhasani, 2014) such as desalination of 
water, and dehydration of organic solvents. The desalination is done by utilizing switchable water in 
forward osmosis. The ionic strength of the switchable water solution is increased beyond the ionic 
strength of the saline water by turning on the switchable water. The water will diffuse over a membrane 
from the saltwater into the switchable water solution. In this way, fresh-water can be produced without 
applying high-pressures, which usually is 6-8 MPa for reverse osmosis of seawater, and is an expensive 
process. It has been suggested to remove the amine additive as a gas by heating up the solution. An 
alternative solution to remove the amine could be to use pervaporation. This can be done both on dilute 
and concentrated solutions. However, currently no membranes have been reported in the scientific 
literature to selectively separate amines from an aqueous solution. Also, a membrane will be very 
dependent on the composition, and temperature of the feed. So the process has to be very well 
understood in order to make the implementation successful, and more preferable over distillation as it 
is one of the most used separation processes in the industry.

subsequent desalination of the water is energy intensive and therefore expensive.

4 Discussion 
In both case studies, optimal solutions were obtained, which based on the estimated properties seemed 
to be optimal for the given need. This need is highly dependent on the weights put on each attribute 
during the ranking of the list of solutions. Since no obvious way of weighting exists, then it seems to 
be the intuition of the decision maker to decide this. This can greatly be made easier if constraints are 
put on pure component and/or mixture properties such that the number of decision variables could be 
reduced. Furthermore, a simple way of weighting was suggested, such that adequate weight are put to 
the objective function such that the top values of the objective function are near to be the highest ranked. 
One of the strengths of the framework is that it can incorporate flexibility into the final solution. Others 
have been claiming optimal solutions e.g. solvents for liquid extraction. However, the need of one user 
might not be the same as for another. Thus, a way of weighting different properties could be a very 
useful tool. 
As it is now, then it is not known whether the framework actually provides an actual optimal 
solution from either case-study 1 or 2. Validation must be done in order to get trustworthy solutions. In 
this project, experimental work was not conducted to show the validity of for example the prediction of 
the solvent for acetic acid-water extraction. 
The optimal solution depends on the accuracy, or the relative accuracy of the GC method used to 
predict properties. For example, the UNIFAC model which is used in the first case to predict liquid-
liquid equilibrium data is good at predicting properties of simple molecules with first-order groups. 
These molecules do not contain aromatics and do not have a high degree of branching. However, for 
the prediction of for example pure component properties, such as LD50, ODP, THF recovery, or Tm then 
the accuracy varies for some of the other GC models.  
The prediction happens using parameters, which has been estimated based on experimental data. 
When the number of data included in the parameter estimation is relatively small, then this might 
propagate to the accuracy of the prediction. The GC methods are often used when data is not available 
for specific compounds. However, the lack of data also impacts the prediction of properties as GCs 
must be fitted to enough data in order to have an adequate accuracy. This can for example be seen on 
the standard deviation of the prediction of THF recovery, which is ± 14.2 percentage points for 5 
experimental data points and with an average error of 16 %. As a comparison from the method by 
(Joback & Reid) the average error is 3.6 % and 11.2 % for Tb and Tm, respectively and have many more 
data points, 438 and 388, respectively. Higher-order groups could also be considered to make the 
prediction more accurate. However, the complexity of the prediction method used is then increased. 
Another consideration is that for some properties, for example GWP or ODP, data does not exist for 
many first-order groups. This makes it very unreliable to use and the methods will not have an as broad 
application range. Thus, the optimality may not necessarily be the true optimal solution. 
In this work, the chemical stability and reactivity have not been considered. It has for example 
before been claimed that butane-2,3-diyl diformate [41] is an optimal solvent for the extraction of acetic 
acid-water. However, from investigation in the scientific literature and especially on (SciFinder.com), 
==PAGE:65==

no evidence of synthesis was found. Since esters are produced in acidic aqueous solutions, then this 
could strongly indicate that this is not done as the compound is highly reactive or not stable in water. 
In this way, solutions obtained using the framework would not really be optimal solutions as the product 
would not be stable.

5 Conclusion 
In this project, development of a framework has been done to do optimization-based integrated product-
process design. The framework included the solving of a problem using mathematical optimization, 
ranking, product-process analysis, economic analysis, and subsequent solution assessment. The process 
could iterate by re-formulating the optimization problem. The framework has been backed up by 
applying it to two different cases. In the first case, a solvent for the liquid-liquid extraction of acetic 
acid-water was determined and it showed that process intensification was achieved by obtaining an 
optimal solvent. This solvent was found to be isobutyl formate, which was designed, selected, and 
subsequently investigated as a potential candidate to replace ethyl acetate. This assessment was done 
based on rigorous process simulation, which subsequently was subject to an NPV-analysis. The 
intensified case showed that the environmental metrics in terms of direct CO2-emissions were improved 
with 53 %. The reduction in CO2 was indirectly caused by the reduction in energy utilities by 53 %. 
However, the toxicity was worsened as the predicted toxicity was 405 % higher than the base case. The 
economical metrics had improved as the annual profit had increased with 46 %. The capital investment 
was 36 % higher but the NPV after 10 years had increased with 8.5 %. Thus, actual improvements could 
be achieved using the framework. However, it cannot be concluded whether the framework actually can 
suggest a feasible solution as the solution has not been experimentally tested to validate the modelled 
results. In the second case, an amine was found for the dehydration of THF. First, based on experimental 
data from the literature parameters for the GC method were estimated for the prediction of the THF 
recovery. Using the framework an amine, which was dimethyl-N-propylamine, was chosen to fit the 
need of the process. However, from the economic analysis, then it was decided that the optimization 
did not influence the “fitness” of the amine for full-scale implementation to a significant extent. This 
was because the technology was not mature enough to compete with other already existing solutions 
such as extractive and pressure swing distillation as it was decided from an NPV-analysis. Thus, actual 
optimization is hard to do on the case as it would be very difficult to make it more competitive with 
existing and well developed technologies without more research to be put into the field.

6 Future work 
To make the framework more trustworthy, case studies must be done from which the solutions are 
subsequently validated to show that the prediction and subsequent ranking holds what it promises. 
 
The possibility of some pure components or mixtures being unstable is realistic. Thus, a 
measure of chemical stability and/or reactivity could be implemented using Gibbs energy to avoid that 
products decompose or react with their surroundings immediately. By doing so, it could be avoided that 
false optimal solutions were obtained. 
 
The accuracy of especially some of the pure component properties e.g. ODP, GWP, and AP, are not 
very trustworthy as data is missing for some groups. Thus, more data must be included in the parameter 
estimation to make the prediction of especially these environmental properties more reliable. 
For the switchable water case, it could be very advantageous to make use of a more advanced 
activity model e-KT-UNIFAC, which considers the interaction between different electrolyte species. 
This require that interaction parameters are estimated for amines at different concentrations and 
conditions, such that it can be predicted for different types of amines. Again, this leads to the missing 
of enough parameter data. Furthermore, it could be investigated whether it could be an advantage to 
add higher-order group parameters when finding optimal solutions. Thus, a comparative study of 
solutions could be done comparing results from first and second-order group parameters using the 
suggested framework.

The separation of acetic acid and the recovery of the solvent is designed using the driving force method 
[70]. The driving force method utilizes the maximum difference in chemical/physical properties 
between two co-existing phases to obtain the best possible separation as seen in Figure 34.  
A driving force diagram was generated for the components in the extract phase as seen in Figure 
33. Since a lot more solvent exists in the extract compared to acetic acid, then it is assessed that the best 
choice will be to design the column using the acetic acid-isobutyl formate split, even though the driving 
force between the acetic acid-water is greater, DFy of 0.23 compared to acetic acid-isobutyl formate 
with a DFy of 0.15. Furthermore, the top product will be an azeotrope between acetic acid-water as on 
a solvent free-basis the stream will have a molar composition of 0.935. However, having a ternary 
mixture the azeotropic points will vary.

9.3  Costing 
The costing of process equipment is done using the method developed by (Guthrie, 1969), which 
includes empirical parameters for factors such as pressure, and materials, and is obtained in the book 
[71]. The method has been reported to provide an error in accuracy of <40 % for preliminary designs, 
which is not very accurate. However, the method is mentioned to be very efficient in predicting the 
relative cost between different conceptual designs. Thus, the cheapest design can be identified using the 
method. The prices obtained using the method is given in 1969 money, thus it has to be converted into 
modern cost by using an update factor called “chemical engineering plant cost index”, which is 
abbreviated CEPCI. The index value used for the update of cost is 560 (2017-value) 4, which is an 
approximate number. The update factor is multiplied with the cost to convert it into modern cost as in 
Equation (9.3.1).

the conversion efficiencies, which is assumed to be 0.75, and 0.7 for low pressure steam (Steam LP, 
125 oC), and medium pressure steam (steam MP, 175 oC), respectively.
