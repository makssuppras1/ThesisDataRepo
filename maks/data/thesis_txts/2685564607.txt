PROJECT DESCRIPTION
2
Project Description
Te aim of the project is to investigate the restoration properties of three thick, rolled tungsten plates, as well as
a thin potassium doped tungsten plate, for the application of shielding the plasma facing component in fusion
reactors. Various isothermal annealings are made for the thicker tungsten plates, in the temperature range of
1125 °C-1225 °C.
Investigation of the specimens are done through the use of light optical microscopy, X-ray diﬀraction, elec-
tron backscater diﬀraction as well as hardness analysis. Using these experimental techniques require adequate
preparation through grinding, polishing, etching and electropolishing procedures.
Te kinetics of the restoration processes are investigated using established models by D. Kuhlmann for recovery,
and Johnson, Mehl, Avrami and Kolmogorov for recrystallization, with the aquired data.
All the results are evaluated and investigated, to evaluate the usability within the fusion reactor.

BACKGROUND
5
Background
Te dream of clean unlimited energy sounds too good to be true, however fusion energy brings the power of the
sun and stars to earth. Utilizing this energy will help the world face the increasing energy demand in a clean,
low-cost and steady manner. It may thus provide another path which decouples us from fossil fuels to be more
environmentally friendly, and is an alternative to renewable energies which require fossil fuel as backup power
source due to their weather-dependent nature. By the year 2040 the world population will reach nine billion
people, which requires generation of 29 trillion kWh of energy. Fusion energy has the potential as an always
available, environmentally friendly, and steady energy source to take over and generate those enormous amounts
of energy. Te economic growth and technological evolution is heavily connected to power generation. [1] With
5G, autonomous cars and industry 4.0 on the horizon, the missing piece is energy. For that reason, engineers
and scientists from around the globe work hand in hand on an international joint experiment in fusion energy
called ITER. Te design of the International Termonuclear Experimental Reactor was ﬁrst launched in 1985
and 35 years later 35 nations collaborate to advance in fusion science and to build the world’s largest tokamak
reactor. Similar to conventional power plants running on fossil fuels, where a turbine is powered by steam
which transforms rotational movement into electricity, the heat source for the steam in a tokamak reactor is the
fusion of atoms. Te heart of the reactor is a doughnut-shaped vacuum chamber that is under extreme heat and
pressure to create a plasma. Massive magnetic coils around the vessel shape and control the plasma that emits
kinetic engery to the vessel wall to produce steam and then electricity by driving turbines and generators. [2]
Te combined interest is to build and operate the ITER experimental device as a research and testing facility,
which will serve as the basis for the fusion reactors built in the future. Te gained knowledge builds the scientiﬁc
base to design a demonstration fusion reactor, call DEMO by the year 2050. Many milestones have already been
passed, but there is still a long way to go before the fusion power plants of tomorrow become a reality. In
order to complete the project, the research is divided among various universities, research companies and other
specialists which are responsible for designated challenges. Te research done on the thermal stability of the
tungsten plates meant for the plasma facing components (PFC) are split between DTU , CEA and KIT. DTUs
department of materials science in particular tackles the issues these materials have to overcome in regard to
annealing to be considered as suitable PFCs. PFCs have to withstand extreme heat loads and heat bursts from
the plasma, while maintaining their strength and should not break. An interesting and promising material is
tungsten, as it has the highest melting point of all metals, good thermal conductivity, high strength at extreme
temperatures and high sputering threshold. Tis projects focus is not solely on heavily deformed pure tungsten,
but also partially on tungsten doped with 80 ppm potassium. Te idea of doping tungsten with potassium is to
improve the resistance to recrystallization and grain growth, as uniformly distributed potassium bubbles have
an interlocking eﬀect on the grain boundaries in the material, thus decreasing annealing eﬀects and increasing
creep resistance. Tis pinning eﬀect forces any recrystallization front or grain growth to walk around potassium
bubbles so that the grains are locked and boundaries have to walk around them. In order to gain insight into the
occurring processes, annealings at various temperatures are done, and subsequently analysed through hardness
tests, XRD, EBSD, and LOM.

BACKGROUND
It is important to avoid recrystallization of tungsten in the fusion reactor, mainly because this may cause embrit-
tlement, but also because of loss of mechanical properties. Terefore, as tungsten is a candidate for the plasma
facing components, it is important that it can function at the expected service temperature. Mapping the kinet-
ics of the candidates is thus important when considering the materials. Instabilities of the plasma in the fusion
reactor can cause thermal shocks, which may cause recrystallization regardless, but with a britle material, this
could cause massive damage, as opposed to a ductile one. [3]

History
Investigation of tungsten as the shielding material for plasma-facing part in fusion reactors, has been a project

its main stakeholder from DTU, which is Professor Wolfgang Pantleon, who has supervised all of the projects,

On this scale, this project is merely a small drop in a rather large pond, but is nonetheless moving the overall
project forward.
Te project is mainly a continuation of the work done by Umberto Ciucani (PhD. student), Ditlev Madsen (Master
thesis) and Benjamin Larsen (Master course), who have obtained data which can be used either directly, or as a
starting point.

6.1
Fusion reactor
Te energy source of the sun and stars is a promising alternative to fossil fuels and renewable energies that could
function as an unlimited energy source. Based on the fusion reaction process in the core of the sun and stars,
fusion energy follows the same principle. In order to produce fusion energy in the future in a controlled and
eﬃcient manner, ITER is under construction to function as a testing facility for researchers. Late in 2025 ITER
will reach the state of ﬁrst burning plasma where the ﬁrst ”small star” will be created. Tis will be the starting
point of the experimental campaign to gather data and prepare the path for future fusion power plants. [4]
Te project is carried out by 35 nations and count China, the European Union including EEA nations, Switzerland
and the UK, India, Japan, Korea, Russia and the United States as members. Te idea for an international joint
experiment in fusion emerged in 1985 and in the year 2006, the ITER agreement was signed. Te ITER fusion
reactor is built on the TOKAMAK concept. Heat, pressure and gaseous hydrogen fuel create a plasma in which
hydrogen nuclei collide and fuse into helium atoms which releases astounding amounts of energy. [5] [4]

by a magnetic ﬁeld produced by massive magnetic coils, that are mounted around the doughnut-shaped vessel

the material. Tese stresses are useful in certain applications. For instance, compressive stresses are for some
applications wanted, in order to close cracks at the surface of the material, and tensile stresses if the material
structure carries a compressive load. Tensile stress can change to compressive stress or vice versa by cross rolling.

Because dislocations play such a large role in the material properties of the materials worked with in this project,
as well as how they react to the annealing process, it is relevant to look into the theory behind what dislocations
actually are.
Dislocations exists within the crystal structure of metallic materials, and are a type of defect in the crystal
structure. Generally speaking, plastic deformation is carried by the movement of dislocations. Dislocations
create stress ﬁelds in a material. Tis is beter understood visually through the two diﬀerent types of dislocations
which exist: edge and screw dislocations. [20] Tese can be seen in Figure 5.

Te latice distortion due to the dislocation is determined by the Burgers vector. Te Burgers vector represents
the magnitude and direction of the displacement, and is indicated by the ⊥symbol for edge dislocations and //
for screw dislocations in the crystal structure. Burgers vector is important with regard to modelling the recovery,
as this process concerns itself mainly with dislocations.
Plastic deformation happens through dislocation movement, and in order to increase the strength, dislocation
movement has to be hindered. Dislocations in a material act as obstacles for the dislocation propagation, which
means that increasing the amount of dislocations (dislocation density) will increase the strength of the material.
Tis can be achieved through cold work, where the material is deformed at low temperatures such as rolling,
which is described in section 6.3. Dislocations are generated by the Frank-Read Source mechanism [22]. Tis
mechanism is best described by considering two pinning points, A and B, which may consist of diﬀerent types
of defect. In the case of this project, the pinning points will likely be dislocation nodes. When a shearing force
is exerted parallel to the dislocation line, the dislocation line will start to bow out, as the points A and B are
pinned. Te dislocation line will bow out around the two points, until they come together and detach from the
original points, which can start to form new dislocation lines.
Te tungsten which is worked with in this project has been deformed heavily in order to reach the strength
and ductility required for the ITER fusion reactor project, and will therefore contain a high amount of dislo-
cations. Te increase in ductility through deformation comes from the fact that tungsten is a BCC material,
and deformation makes it possible to decrease the britle-to-ductile transformation (BDT) temperature (which
is characteristic for BCC materials). Tis was proven by Bonnekoh et al [23]. who showed that annealing heav-

6.4
Annealing and Related Phenomena
Annealing is a process in which a material is heat treated at a temperature, which changes some of the properties
of the material. Te change usually involves a decrease in hardness and strength, but an increase in ductility,
though this is not the case for tungsten, as it is a BCC material.
From a metallurgical point of view, an annealing treatment allows a deformed material to reach a state closer to
equilibrium through diﬀusion of the atoms or collective motion of dislocations. When a material is deformed, to
any extent, it is moved away from its equilibrium state. Tis may, for instance, be in the form of edge or screw
dislocations, or simply in the form of grain boundaries. A plastically deformed metal, containing dislocation
is thermodynamically unstable, and one could be convinced that the defects should disappear spontaneously.
However, this is not the case, because the atomistics jumps which are necessary for the removal of these defects
are slow at lower temperatures. Terefore, many of these defects continue to exist at room temperature, and
only start to move towards an equilibrium state when heated.
Annealing consist of three processes: Recovery, recrystallization and grain growth. Tese will be explained in
the following sections. It should be noted that the theory presented in this section is applicable for single phase
materials.
6.5
Recovery
Recovery is the ﬁrst part of the three processes, and should be looked at from a dislocation structure perspective.
Te grain boundaries remain where they are, and the changes happen primarily within the grains. Recovery
reduces the strength of the material through changes in the dislocation structure.
Because recovery happens at a latice structural level, it is very diﬃcult to see the changes through optical
microscopy. Instead, the changes become evident when measuring a physical property such as the hardness,
which is what is done in this project.
In essence, recovery is the annihilation and rearrangement of dislocations within the crystal structure. Tere are
two types of dislocations, as shown in Figure 5: Edge and screw dislocations. Dislocations can be characterized
as defects in the crystal structure [24], representing a change in the atomic arrangement. Tey travel along slip
planes in a crystal structure, and depending on the structure, diﬀerent slip systems are available. Slip systems,
deﬁned by slip direction and slip plane, can be thermally activated, which means heating of the material will
allow for more slip systems.
Dislocations are typically induced through cold working of material, where the dislocations are caused by the
deformation. Te dislocations strain the material, and result in internally stored energy, which is what recovery

Some dislocations will cancel each other at low temperatures, while others require higher temperatures. Tis all

6.6
Recrystallization
As touched upon in the previous section regarding recovery, an annealing treatment reduces the internally stored
energy of the material. Recrystallization achieves this, by nucleating new, stress-free grains, which consume the
deformed ones. It is a fully thermally activated process, which can be split into two parts: Nucleation and
growth. When a deformed material is heated, it will at some point start to form nuclei at grain boundaries.
However, these nuclei are not stable, and will dissolve. A certain temperature is necessary for the nuclei to
achieve a critical radius, at which they will grow into grains. Te temperature can however be lowered through
a higher degree of cold working [28], or through alloying. Once the stress-free grains are starting to grow,
they consume the deformed grains, and grow until they reach another stress-free grain. Tis happens through
boundary migration. Troughout the material, nucleation and growth of grains are happening simultaneously.
Deformed areas nucleate faster.
Unlike recovery, the recrystallization is not necessarily a continuous process, as it has a starting point, and will
slow down drastically once most of the material has nucleated and grown new grains. Anything in between
a non-recrystallized structure and a fully recrystallized one can be characterized as a partially recrystallized
structure. Tis is usually deﬁned by ”volume fraction of material recrystallized”. According to Humphreys et al.

6.7
Grain Growth
Grain growth usually proceeds afer recrystallization has occurred, but it is not a prerequisite. An undeformed
material, with equiaxed grain structure, produced through casting will not experience recovery or recrystalliza-
tion, but will instead directly show grain growth when annealed [31]. In the case of tungsten, which is worked
with in this project, it is always deformed to a high degree through rolling, and therefore preceded by recrystal-
lization.
Tere is no longer a driving force coming from the stored energy from deformation. Instead, the driving force
now comes from the high angle grain boundaries [32]. In its most simpliﬁed form, grain growth seeks to reduce
the overall stored energy of the material by reducing the amount of grain boundaries. Just like recovery and
recrystallization, grain growth lowers mechanical properties such as yield strength and hardness, but has an
advantage in also reducing creep [34]. Single crystal materials are far more creep resistant than their polycrystal
counterparts, and are used for example in turbine blades, where the high centrifugal forces would otherwise
induce creep [33].
Having gone through the two previous processes, the driving force is already severely reduced. Te grain bound-
ary velocity is lower than for the recrystallization (typically in the magnitude of two times lower). Te conse-
quence of this is, that the grain boundary migration is much more aﬀected by pinning from inclusions or solutes.
Tis is relevant in the case of the WK80, which is doped with potassium.
Grain growth can occur in two variations: Normal and abnormal grain growth.
Normal grain growth is uniform grain growth throughout the material in a spatially homogeneous manner.
Looking at the distribution of grain sizes, it represents a rather narrow range. Eventually, at a constant anneal-
ing temperature, the driving force is lowered to the point at which the grain development will reach a state,
where practically no change occurs.
Te second variation, abnormal grain growth, represents an uneven growth of the grains. Some grains will grow
signiﬁcantly faster than the surrounding ones, and consume them in the process, while the smaller grains ex-
perience no growth at all [35]. In this way, a bimodal size distribution arises, which means that as opposed to
the narrow single distribution of continuous grain growth, there are now two distinctly diﬀerent peaks when
looking at the distribution of grain sizes. Tis typically happens in the presence of second phase particles, or
anisotropy in the material.
As mentioned previously, the driving force at this stage, compared to recovery and recrystallization is low. In
order for serious grain growth to occur, the temperature has to be increased signiﬁcantly [36].
In the case of a severely rolled material, the texture should also be considered. A highly textured material con-
tains a lot of low-angle boundaries with low energy, and thus low driving force for grain growth, combined with
the fact that low angle grain boundaries are not mobile. [37]
Te combination of the extremely high melting point and lots of low-angle grain boundaries indicate that the
rolled tungsten plates, which are the subject of this project will not see signiﬁcant grain growth right away.
One must however also consider that the orientation may change with recrystallization, to more grain growth
favorable high angle grain boundaries.

6.8.1
Estimation of annealing times for new temperatures
While it is not possible to accurately predict the annealing time to reach a certain state in the restoration process
at a new, unexplored temperature, it is possible to make an estimation. Te estimated annealing time is based

Electron Backscater Diﬀraction (EBSD) is an analysis method, which can be used to gain quantitative informa-

obtain, as individual grains, depending on their orientation, have slightly diﬀerent hardness, and will thus be

7.6
Grinding
Te ﬁrst preparation step the specimens undergo afer being uncased from the ampoules, regardless of the sub-
sequent experiments, is grinding. Grinding creates a moderately smooth and uniform surface through abrasion.
Grit paper is used for this purpose, which has a rough surface with abrasive particles on it, such as silicon carbide
or aluminium oxide. Tis is therefore a case of 2-body abrasion, where one surface contains partially embedded
”grains”, as opposed to 3-body abrasion (polishing), in which case the ”grains” are loose between the two sur-
faces, see Figure 30. Initially, rougher paper is used, as there may be a large diﬀerence between the peaks and
valleys of the surface. Te rough paper removes a lot of material, and will therefore ensure that enough mate-
rial is grinded oﬀ, for the surface to become more uniform. Hereafer, progressively ﬁner grit papers are used,
which lower the surface roughness. When a surface is grinded it is deformed simultaneously. Tis is unwanted,
as this will not accurately represent the deformation structure throughout the material, and will thus have to
be removed. Rough paper induces the highest amount of deformation, while ﬁne paper will induce the least.
Terefore, deformation afer the ﬁnal grit paper should kept minimal.

7.7
Polishing
Polishing is the continuation process of grinding that is introduced to ﬁnalize the specimens surface by levelling
the topography and reducing the surface roughness even further. Polishing results in a smooth and plane parallel
surface that has none or very few small scratches or microscopic peaks, that could distort electron beams from
entering as well as exiting the top-surface. In the case of EBSD and XRD, an uneven or rough surface can distort
the backscatered signal or beam and result in a poor quality measurement, as there will be a lack of measurable
area leading to wrong or inconsistent measurement results. Terefore, the purpose of polishing is to enhance
the measurement quality by decreasing the uncertainty and enabling more consistent standardized results.

DP-U2. Compared to grinding the speed was severely decreased to approximately 1 to 1.5 RPM, which was also
manually adjusted and estimated by counting the rotation of a certain spot on the disk. In order to gain an

7.9
Hardness
As mentioned previously, a material parameter has to be tracked in order to map the changes of the microstruc-
ture introduced by annealing. Terefore, the investigation of this project is based on indentation hardness
measurements. Tese provide information about recovery, recrystallization and grain growth, when several
measurements are ploted over time. Te initial deformed tungsten specimens have a hardness value of 430 to
440 HV, see section 7.1. Tis value will decrease as the material is exposed to the heat in the furnace and maps
the restoration of the material.
Tere are several methods for testing the hardness of a material, including Brinell, Rockwell and Knoop. For
this project, Vickers hardness indentation is chosen, as it is a very versatile hardness test, suitable for a variety
of materials which can be performed on both the macro and micro scale. Te Vickers hardness testing machine
used for this project is the Struers DuraScan, see Figure 40a which is situated in the basement of DTU building
425. In order to estimate the hardness of the material, a diamond at the tip of an indenter creates a square-based
pyramidal indent on the top surface of the specimen. Te angle between the opposing sides of the pyramid
is 136°. [61] Te resulting marks are measured by a microscope, providing insight into the material. Te two
diagonals of the diamond-shaped mark are measured and averaged, and the hardness can be calculated based on
them. Depending on the material and the annealing time, observations were made which show that the materials
microstructure evolves towards or generally consists of extremely large grains. Tese grains can in some cases
cover the area of an entire indent. In order to prevent taking measurements of only a single grain, a weight of
10 kg is selected, to increase the indentation area. Te Vickers Hardness Number (HV) is calculated using the
formula seen in equation 18. [61]
HV = 1.8544F[Kgf]
D2[µm]
(18)
Where F is the applied load, and D2 is the projected area of the indent (with D being the averaged diagonal
length).
An additional factor, which has been mentioned previously in Section 7.6, is the plane-parallelism of the specimen
surface. Plane parallelism is important, as a crooked surface or extremely rounded edges can cause the indenter
to create so called ”dragons”, see Figure 31b. With the intention of preventing measurement errors, hiting angled
surfaces can be detrimental. Some rounding of the edges is impossible to avoid, and in order to account for this,
the valid space to take measurements is decreased as can be seen in Figure 40b by the green area. A border of
600 µm to the edges provides a buﬀer zone, in which the surface is allowed to be declining. Additionally a gap
between the indents of 600 µm is selected to prevent them from hiting the plastic deformation zone of each
other. In regards to calculating the plastic deformation zone and hence the necessary distance between indents
to avoid interaction, the ASTM E384 standard is used. Referring to the standard, the distance should be at least
2.5 times the diagonal of the indent. Tis shows that a distance of 600 µm is suﬃcient.

Etching
Implementing etching to the preparation process enables the examiner to see the microstructure of the tungsten
in the light optical microscope (LOM). Te specimens are etched afer the grinding and polishing steps. If the pol-
ished specimens are put under the optical microscope without etching, the grain structure itself will be diﬃcult
to observe. Te chemical etching reveal the grains by developing a contrast in the structure [62], thus making
it possible to examine them. Seen with the naked eye, the specimen may have a grey surface afer etching, and
will always require repolishing before EBSD or XRD. For etching of metals, the solution can be either acidic or
alkaline, and aqueous or alcoholic. In this case, it is an acidic aqueous solution, as it consist of 25 wt% hydrogen
peroxide (15 ml) with the remaining being demineralized water (45 ml) to complete the composition. Te setup,
including the equipment needed can be seen in Figure 41.

7.12.1
Electropolishing issues
Tough the electropolishing parameters given have proven eﬀective, the practical operational setup regarding
the placement of the sample, and achieving the correct voltage and ampere has been diﬃcult. Te setup was
done as described, with a preset voltage of 12 V and a current of 2 A. When the setup was initially tested, nothing
happened to the specimen. Terefore, the electropolishing time was increases whereas a diﬀerent approach was
to increase the voltage in smaller increments to 16 V which changed the surface treatment to etching. Afer only
5 s the surface became grayish and mat with some parts of the surface being over etched, see Figure 43.
Looking into the theory regarding electropolishing, it can be seen from Figure 46 that ﬁnding a suitable ratio is
important to achieve the desired outcome. Te material is not considered in the schematic ﬁgure which can shif
the curves.

Te ﬁgure shows how a potential which is set too low will etch the specimen instead of polish, and how a too

the idea of increasing the potential arises.
Afer some investigation and experimentation, it was discovered, that the initial instructions given for operation
of the equipment was correct. Te operational instructions were to preset the voltage and amperes to 12 V and
2 A, and to only lower the surface into the solution. However, this does not necessarily mean that these are the
values the specimen will experience. Afer tweaking the interface on the power supply, it was discovered that
the voltage and current the specimens experienced was lower, and were in fact in the range of 10 V and 1.7 A.
Note that these values are for fully immersed samples, and that the values will be even lower if just the specimen
surface is immersed. Tis happens because the anodic area in the process is reduced, and thus the reaction will
not be driven as fast.
In conclusion the most important parameters are the correct presetings, to immerse the specimen into centre of
the beaker so that the voltage and amperes result to be 12 V and 2 A and to check that the parameters are kept
close to the set parameters during the electropolishing process, otherwise either the setings or the degree of
immersing the specimen can be changed.

X-ray diﬀraction
An alternative to EBSD is X-ray diﬀraction (XRD), which in this case is one of the last experiments done on
the material (WK80), as previous analysis outside of this project has already been done. XRD should provide
information regarding the eﬀects of annealings, in regards to the crystal size and latice strain. As already
explained in section 6.9.1, it can provide information regarding the phases present inside the material, but in
this case, where the material is ”pure” heavily rolled tungsten, the resulting peaks should all belong to the
{200} plane-family. XRD experiments were conducted in two diﬀerent modes (in- and ex situ). Te used X-ray
diﬀractometer is the BRUKER D8 DISCOVER and BRUKER D8 Advance in the basement of building 425, which
are both parallel beam diﬀractometers. Te setup and procedure is described in the sections below.

situ experiments is the reduced risk of specimen to specimen variation.

8.1
Restoration evolution
Hardness measurements form one of the fundamental data bases of this project, and are used to evaluate the
progression of the materials annealing. It is a fast, standardized method to gain insight and serves as basis for
further analysis which is discussed in this section of the report. For the purpose of structuring and creating an
overview over the annealing behavior of each material, the corresponding isothermal graphs are presented in
the beginning of each materials section.
Isothermal annealings at diﬀerent temperatures ranging from 1150 °C to 1225 °C, in steps of 25 °C are ploted in
Figure 63, 64 and 65 below. Each temperature series is based on hardness measurements done on specimens
annealed at diﬀerent annealing times. Generally speaking, an overall tendency indicating the dependence be-
tween temperature and annealing time is evident. In the upcoming sections, isothermal annealings of various
series are compared to each other, in order to analyse the evolution of annealings. Annealing comprises three
diﬀerent phenomena: recovery, recrystallization and grain growth where one process overlaps with the other.
Terefore, it is diﬃcult to distinguish and separate them from each other, but there are indications and expecta-
tions that allow to make a good assumption. Te ﬁrst stage in the restoration process is ofen recovery, which is
indicated by a slow decrease in hardness, as some of the internal strains in material are relieved. At one point,
very much dependent on the thermal energy provided, recrystallization will start, and becomes more dominant
with time (and temperature), until it becomes the most impactful process happening. Finally grain growth may
occur, afer the recrystallization process is ﬁnished at one or more grains. Tis is investigated throughout the
upcoming sections. Due to the correlation between hardness and microstructure, and the time and temperature
dependency, this method provides a great representation of the current material state. Finally, to guarantee that
the specimens have experienced the exact same conditions, all three types of tungsten are annealed in the same
ampule, furnace and under the same parameters.

8.5.4
Pseudo-Voigt function
As explained in section 6.9.1, the Voigt proﬁle describes a distribution by superposition of a Gaussian and
Lorentzian distributions. It is possible to distinguish the fraction of each, and the distribution in this case can be
seen in Figure 106.
Figure 106: Lorentzian Fraction
Clearly there is a discrepancy between the distribution of Lorentzian and Gaussian between the 400 peak and
200 peak. Tis show that in general the 400 peak has a larger fraction of Lorentzian, while the 200 peak has an
even split.
Over time, as the annealing process progresses, it was expected that the Gaussian fraction would decrease, and
thus the distribution would become more Lorentzian. Tis expectation was based on previous work, which did
not show recrystallization at any point, even at much longer annealing times, which would indicate that the
grain size should stay the same (no change to Lorentzian fraction), while the microstrains should be reduced
(Gaussian fraction should go down) due to recovery. However, as Figure 106 clearly shows, the distribution
remains almost constant throughout the annealing.
Tis may be explained by the formation of subgrains, the concept of which was explained in detail in section
6.5.1, and shortly explained here as well. Subgrains look like normal grains, but at a much smaller scale. While
dislocations annihilate if possible, the remaining which have the same sign, can form walls, to reduce the overall
energy. Tese walls then become boundaries of subgrains, which have a very low angle of misorientation. From
the perspective of the diﬀractometer however, even though the actual grains do change, this may be considered
grain growth within the grains, and could thus increase the Lorentzian fraction. Terefore, even though the
fraction of Lorentzian and Gaussian stay the same, it does not mean that nothing is happening. Afer all, the
graphs of the FWHM and integrated intensity clearly show that a reaction is taking place in the material. It is
theorized that while the strain does indeed decrease, and thus the Gaussian fraction should do the same, it is
countered by the formation of subgrains, which contributes to a reduction of the Lorentzian fraction as well.
Tis theory is consistent with the progression of the fraction distribution seen in Figure 106.

Background noise
While the background noise has been subtracted from the previously presented data for the in-situ XRD, it is
presented how it progresses over time in this section. A graph of background noise over time can be seen in
Figure 107.
Figure 107: Background noise
It is clear that the noise is increasing over time for both the 200 and 400 peak, though the 400 peak seems
more aﬀected. Tere are many things which may impact the background noise level, such as surface quality,
smoothness of sample, or in general from the equipment. Adding in a dome with cooling and heating capabilities
increases the complexity of the experiment, and the amount of equipment which may have an impact on the
background noise.
Te most likely theory behind the increase in background noise comes from the annealing process itself. When
the material is heated, it expands due to increasing vibration of the atoms. Tis vibration lowers the intensity of
the peaks, by resulting in less constructive interference, but also increases the background noise due to varying
constructive and destructive interference (also known as the Debye-Waller theory [88]). Tis does however not
explain why the background noise keeps increasing over time. When the annealing temperature of 1100°C is
reached, the background noise should stabilize. Terefore, some of the increase in background noise must come
from another source.

8.7
CLW large grain theory
As is clear from all of the experimental results presented, CLW behaves in a signiﬁcantly diﬀerent manner to
that of ITER and CHW. From the hardness results, see section 8.1.3, it can be seen how it takes a long time for
the recrystallization to start compared to the two other materials, but when it ﬁnally does start, it has a tendency
to proceed very fast. Tis is of course problematic, as a rapid change in material properties may cause material
to fail without warning, thus endangering the application in which it is implemented.
Te size of the grains of CLW are signiﬁcantly larger than for both ITER and CHW, when recrystallization has
begun. Te AR condition have similar or slightly larger grains than the other materials, but once recrystallization
starts, the size of the grains are much larger. Tis can be seen in the LOM results section 8.3.3, where both the
partially recrystallized as well as the fully recrystallized show massive grains. To quantify this beter, the analysis
of the grain size and grain boundary evolution and distribution from the EBSD may be considered (section 8.4.4).
Here the area fraction show how, completely opposite to CHW and ITER, the material consists of very few grains.
Te grains of CLW even grow to the extent that they cannot be accurately characterized in the magniﬁcation
which the EBSD is done, because all they continue out of the image.

While no absolute reason has been found as to why CLW behaves this way, a theory have been considered.
Te cause for this large discrepancy with the other materials may come from the material composition. A

require more reheating in order to be rolled. Te extended heating of the plate between the rolling steps, see

which is lower than for the other materials, with larger grains. Te hardness of CLW in the as received condition
is lower than for the two other materials, see appendix 11.1, thus supporting this theory, though it is not more
than in the range of 5-10 HV. If one considers a true stress vs strain curve, like the one seen in Figure 118, it
can be seen that the increase in stress with increasing strain becomes lower at high strain (as is the case with
these tungsten plates). As illustrated in the ﬁgure, a diﬀerence from 90 % to 70 % eﬀective plastic strain may not

9.1
ITER, CHW and CLW
Investigation of hardness for the three materials showed large similarities between ITER and CHW, with CLW
proving to be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent. Overall, CLW shows the most homogeneity in regards to hardness, whereas
ITER and CHW show some heterogeneity. ITER has the largest diﬀerence between edge and core, with the core
restoring faster than the edge. While CHW show a similar behaviour, it does so to a lesser extent. It recrystallizes
faster than CLW, and has lower max hardness compared with ITER (at half hardness loss). Te progression of
the restoration process happens at a steady pace for ITER and CHW, which acts according to an Arrhenius like
behaviour, where the reaction rate increases with increasing temperature. CLW has the best endurance of the
three materials, as it has the best resistance to recrystallization. However, when recrystallization ﬁnally starts,
it moves extremely fast, which can be seen at the 1175 °C 288 h data point, where the annealing process was
stopped at a time where the material had completely recrystallized 600 µm from the edge, but were almost in
as received condition 1200 µm from the edge. Tis was ﬁrst discovered when considering the hardness results,
which showed a large standard deviation at this point. Tis was signiﬁcantly reduced when the three rows of
indents were split. LOM images conﬁrmed this behaviour, and can be seen in Figure 67.
Once the hardness reaches a value of 360-370 HV, the material can be considered fully recrystallized.
LOM images has been taken at three stages in the restoration process: As received condition, partially recrystal-
lized (≈400 HV) and fully recrystallized, for both edge and core. Generally, an increase in grain size can be seen
for all three materials throughout the annealing treatment, as well as a move towards more equiaxed grains, but
to diﬀerent degrees. Even afer recrystallization, an elongated behaviour may still be present within the grain
structure. Te same trend which was seen in the investigation of hardness continues in the LOM, as ITER and
CHW behave in a relatively similar manner, while CLW is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent. Te as received condition of
CLW has larger and more elongated grains when comparing it to the other as received materials. Te grains
in CLW grow very large and very fast, ofen in excess of 200 µm, whereas the change for ITER and CHW is
much smaller. ITER and CHW do show a diﬀerence in the grain structure between as received and partially
recrystallized condition, but there seem to be a rather large diﬀerence between the partially recrystallized edge
and core, as the core show larger more equiaxed grains (but similar hardness).
Te analysis of EBSD data showed that afer rolling, both ITER, CHW and CLW contain primarily low angle
boundaries, as conﬁrmed by the misorientation angle distributions. Tis is consistent to what would be ex-
pected from rolling, as the grains are reoriented during the process. From the as received condition to partially
recrystallized, CLW experience the most dramatic change, with the grain size at this stage being so large, that is

9.2
WK80
Previous analysis of WK80 preceding this project showed no signs of recrystallization of the material at 1100 °C,
and indicated that recovery may be the mechanism taking place at this temperature. Because of the low annealing
temperature (considering its tungsten), it was possible to perform the annealing in a beryllium dome in the XRD
machine, while performing the in-situ measurements. Tis means, that is has been possible to extract a lot of
information from one sample. Diﬀractograms were continuously produced over 24 hours.
While the diﬀractograms provide plenty of data, which have been analysed, the most interesting is the full
width half max (FWHM) values ploted with time. Te width describes how the microstructure is aﬀected by the
annealing treatment, which in this case show a strong logarithmic dependence. As recovery is suspected, the
Kuhlmann regression has been applied to the data. Te regression has a good ﬁt with the data, strengthening
the belief in recovery as the primary restoration mechanism at this temperature.
By applying a Pseudo-Voigt function to the diﬀractograms, it has been possible to investigate how much of the
peak broadening comes from grain size reﬁnement (Lorentzian), and how much comes from strain (Gaussian).
Te ratio between Lorentzian and Gausian in the Pseudo-Voigt function remain almost constant throughout the
annealing treatment. Te FWHM indicate that recovery is the mechanism taking place, and thus the Gaussian
fraction should be going down. As this does not happen, it could mean that formation of subgrains occur.
A theory was formed, to explain unexpected results from the background noise and peak position, which indicate
that the WK80 could be sensitive to creep at high temperatures. As the material is doped with potassium to pin
the grain boundaries, this is highly unexpected, and may be problematic if this is the chosen material for the
fusion reactor. It should however be considered, that the thickness of the WK80 is much lower than the other
materials, and the load was applied in the weakest direction of the cross-section.

10
Future work
Tough the project is ﬁnished, there is more work with these materials which can be done, to further complete
the analysis, and there are certain things which can be optimized.
Te LOM as well as the EBSD orientation maps showed a large diﬀerence between the grain structure of edge and
core at the same partially recrystallized state (≈400 HV). Te reason behind this has not been found, but one must
consider that the surface on which the hardness was measured was not the same used for the images. Terefore,
it is suggested that the areas shown in the images are hardness tested speciﬁcally with micro indentation, to
accurately describe the hardness at these points, and compare them.
It was not possible to recrystallize CLW at 1150 °C, but it may be possible if enough time is given. Long time
annealings for CLW at this temperature could be interesting to explore, also in regard to the fast recrystallization,
to see if this would continue at lower temperatures.
More isothermal annealings could be done at diﬀerent temperatures, for instance at 1100 °C and 1125 °C. Tese
series were initially atempted, but failed. Annealings at higher temperatures may be interesting as well.
It is possible to identify subgrains through EBSD data, if the correct parameters are applied. Unfortunately, a
step size which was too large has been used in this project. If the subgrains are to be investigated, one would
have to redo the scans with an adequate step size.
When performing the in-situ XRD experiment on WK80, it had to be aborted prematurely because of concerns
for failure of the turbo pump. It could be interesting to examine what the data would have looked like, if the
planned 3 d annealing was actually done. Furthermore, in-situ annealings at higher temperatures could provide
information about recrystallization.
Finally, the procedure for etching of the surfaces for LOM should be optimized. Te LOM pictures presented in
this report are too inconsistent, and this comes down to the etching. Tough the test setup is consistently the
same, the specimens are lowered into the solution manually, which introduces a source of human error. If this
was avoided, it is believed that the etched surfaces could be more consistent.
