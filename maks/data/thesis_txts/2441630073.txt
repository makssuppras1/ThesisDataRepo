Abstract 
 
Foodborne diseases are growing public health problem, causing a large burden of morbidity and 
mortality globally. In Denmark and other European countries, the consumption of contaminated 
food products and exposure to a variety of health hazards lead to a wide range of foodborne 
diseases (FBD).  The concept of burden of disease is used to quantify the impact of disease in 
population health by looking at the effect of the morbidity, mortality. Burden of disease studies 
allow comparison of different diseases and their risk factors and facilitated public health policy 
making and resource allocation. This study estimated the burden of Hepatitis A virus in Denmark in 
terms Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs), considering gender and age groups using Burden of 
Communicable Disease toolkit (BCoDE. It is estimated that the burden of HAV is low in Denmark 
compare to other viruses, bacteria and chemicals. This study suggests the need to consider specific 
prevention and control measures, to further decrease of HAV burden in Denmark. The conclusion is 
that Denmark is considered to be a region with a very low hepatitis A Virus (HAV) endemicity.

1. Introduction 
1. 1 Foodborne disease 
The consumption of contaminated food products and exposure to a variety of health hazards lead to 
a wide spectrum of foodborne diseases (FBD). Foodborne diseases  are a growing public health 
problem and cause a significant burden of morbidity and mortality worldwide (World Health 
Organization, 2014). Researchers have identified more than 250 foodborne diseases that cause 
illnesses in humans via consumption of contaminated foods or water (Bijkerk, 2014). Foods are 
contaminated by hazards (causative agents) such as bacteria, viruses, parasites, chemicals, toxins, 
and allergens. The hazards or risk factors can cause a wide range of conditions from relatively mild 
(e.g., gastroenteritis) to severe chronic diseases (e.g., cancer) (Gibb et al., 2015a). Therefore the 
food - associated diseases have a substantial impact on public health as well as on social and 
economic losses globally (Kirk et al., 2015). To protect people from unsafe food and improve 
public health as well as limit economic losses, food safety strategies need to be made by the 
policymakers. It is only possible to improve food safety and prevent foodborne illnesses when we 
have knowledge and understanding of the burden of foodborne diseases (World Health 
Organization, 2006). 
In order to obtain this knowledge and the range of health dimensions of the diseases, it is not 
enough only to compare the health impacts or looking at the incidences and mortality of the 
foodborne diseases alone. In order to prioritize the significance of the health dimensions of diseases,  
scientific approach is required  (Kirk et al., 2015). There are several ways to measure the impact of 
food-associated diseases on society and the different comparing methods that lead to different 
ranking order. The hazards can be ranked either by their public health impact or by the economic 
burden caused by the diseases. Both approaches can provide important evidence that can help 
policy-makers in preparing various food-safety strategies (Van Lier et al., 2016).

1.2 Global Burden of Foodborne Diseases 
 
The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) study is a complete regional and global research program of 
disease burden that measures mortality (death) and morbidity (disability) caused by diseases, 
injuries and risk factors (WHO, 2007). Moreover, the first study on GBD diseases was made in 
1990, where the health effects of more than 100 diseases and injuries for eight regions of the world 
was quantified (WHO, 2007). The study produced comprehensive estimates of mortality and 
morbidity by taking the age groups into account, gender, and region. Additionally, the study 
presented a new metric called the Disability-Adjusted Life Year (DALY), as a single measure for 
quantifying the burden of disease, injuries, and risk factors. The DALY is primarily based on years 
of lost lives due to early death and years of lived life in less than full health (Devleesschauwer et al., 
2015). 
Furthermore, the world health organization (WHO) established a Food Disease Burden 
Epidemiology Reference Group (FERG) in 2007. The World Health Organization (WHO) made an 
update of the first GBD study for the years 2000-2002, where a broader analysis of the mortality 
and burden of disease was included. For the year 2004, this analysis contained mortality and burden 
of diseases linked to 26 risk factors known as the Comparative Risk Factor Assessment (CRA) 
(WHO, 2007). The burden of diseases is an estimation of health impacts of the diseases in a 
population when taking into consideration mortality, morbidity, and disability. The different 
matrices are developed to find the total burden of diseases or to compare the burden across the 
diseases (Kirk et al., 2015). 
Subsequently, one of the widely used metrics are the method described above, called the Disability 
Adjusted Life Years (DALYs), developed by WHO organization for comparing different diseases, 
risk factors and their health outcomes (Gibb et al., 2015b). People are exposed to illnesses from 
either microbiological or chemical contamination by consuming unsafe food. This is one of the 
most important reasons why there is a need for having uncontaminated food because 
uncontaminated food saves lives. The FERG group was established to assemble, appraise and report 
on the burden of foodborne disease, which is estimated both on global and regional burden 
(Devleesschauwer et al., 2015).  
==PAGE:13==
5 
 
Consequently, the FERG group estimated that the burden of 31 foodborne hazards is based on 
incidence, mortality and disease burden. They reported that the global burden of disease is similar 
to infectious diseases such as HIV/AIDS, malaria and tuberculosis (Gibb et al., 2015b; World 
Health Organization, 2007). The FERG group estimations showed that diarrheal disease agents 
globally the cause of most foodborne disease. They found that the agents that cause foodborne 
illnesses frequently are norovirus and Campylobacter spp. (Kirk et al., 2015). Their findings also 
showed that the majority of deaths due to foodborne Disease (FBD) were caused by non-typhoidal 
Salmonella enterica, as well as Salmonella Typhi, Taenia solium and hepatitis A virus (Havelaar et 
al., 2015). 
Accordingly, the FERG group reported that the FBD mostly affected children under 5 year of age. 
They make up 9 % of the global population, but they suffer from 38 % of all foodborne illnesses 
and succumb to 30% of foodborne deaths which results to bear 40% of global foodborne DALYs. 
According to their reports, people who live in the most deprived area make up almost 41 % of the 
world population. The same people suffer from 53 % of all foodborne illnesses, yield to 75% of 
foodborne deaths, and they result to bear 72% of global foodborne DALYs (Devleesschauwer et al., 
2014; Mangen et al., 2013). 
 
1.3 Public health surveillance of Foodborne Disease in Denmark 
 
Food-associated diseases are a threat to Denmark as well as for other countries. Each year 
thousands of Danes are exposed to foodborne illnesses by consuming contaminated foods, caused 
by bacteria, viruses, and parasites, prionic or chemicals. Chemical exposure and unhealthy diets can 
lead to chronic diseases, and foodborne infections can lead mostly to short-term diarrhea, vomiting, 
and fever. The symptoms and severity of the disease depend on the patients, as different people may 
experience different complications due to their physical situation. The complications might be so 
severe for some people that they need to get hospitalized and some experience health issues several 
years after the infection (Statens Serum Institute, 2017). The National Public surveillance in 
Denmark collects and reports all the cases of foodborne diseases. Although they try to collect all the 
cases, only a small part is identified and reported by the health surveillance.  
 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
Moreover, to identify and collect all the cases of foodborne diseases, several steps are required both 
by the health sectors and patients. Before a case is identified and reported, multiple steps must be 
taken both from the physicians and patients. The patients must go to the doctor to seek medical 
care, the doctor has to take a sample and send it to the laboratory, and finally, the result must be 
reported to the public health surveillance system. Therefore, most foodborne illnesses are 
underdiagnosed and underreported, and because of that, the health surveillance can only estimate a 
fraction of cases. Since it is a long process even though the cases are modifiable, unfortunately, due 
to a passive surveillance system, cases are underestimated (Pires, 2014).  
 
Additionally, to measure public health and the economic impact of foodborne diseases, it is 
necessary to know the number of people suffering from a foodborne illness each year and the 
economic impact of the illnesses. Also, it is essential to know about the agents (microbial and 
chemical), causing the illness and the foods responsible for the illnesses. Consequently, there are 
two ways to rank diseases. It can either be done on their occurrence (incidence) or the number of 
deaths (mortality) caused by the infection. Since these two ways of ranking are the simple ones, 
multiple things should be taken into consideration.   
For example, it is necessary to know about the severity of the illness, the duration of it, how to 
compare the diseases and rank them by public health relevance. The burden of disease studies is 
used to quantify the health impact of diseases in a population in terms of incidence, mortality, 
morbidity, and disability (Devleesschauwer et al., 2014; Pires, 2014). Therefore, the burden of 
disease metrics provides us with opportunities to compare different diseases and risk factors, as well 
as, considering all the outcomes of a specific disease. Furthermore, these methods help policy-
makers to quantify and rank different foodborne diseases both in terms of the population and in 
terms of the individual level. Thus, the purpose of estimating the health impact of the foodborne 
disease is to help the industries to implement effective intervention to improve food safety. It can 
only be done by knowing the primary food sources causing the illnesses. When the reasons and the 
sources of illnesses are identified, whether they are related to specific foods or animals, first then it 
is possible to prevent food contamination and improve food safety (Pires et al., 2009). Until now the 
project has been explaining the general concept of foodborne diseases which is leading us to 
elaborate further on a specific topic in this case Hepatitis Virus (HV). Therefore, it is natural to 
proceed on to the following about Hepatitis A Virus.

Chapter 2 
 
Hepatitis A virus (HAV) is a food and water-borne virus, and the specific scope of this thesis is the 
Hepatitis A virus. Foodborne viruses have become a significant cause of food associated with 
outbreaks and HAV are one of these pathogens. Many food-associated outbreaks of HAV have 
resulted from consumption of shellfish. Shellfish have a high concentration of the virus when they 
are grown in contaminated waters. Contamination of the water (both fresh and seawater) can occur 
from untreated or improperly treated sewage run-off. The other high-risk food products associated 
with outbreaks of HAV include foods, which are minimally processed before consumption. Many 
outbreaks of HAV have been associated with fresh products and other foods sold in restaurant 
settings or at the market. These include baked goods, dried products, raw beef, liver pate, ice cream, 
and dried fruits (Fiore, 2004) 
2.1 Background of Hepatitis A 
 
The word hepatitis comes from Latin (Hepa refers to the liver, titis means inflammation), which 
means inflammation or irritation of the liver. The inflammation can cause conditions such as 
swelling and tenderness that can cause permanent damage of the liver. Viral hepatitis is one of the 
most important health problems worldwide that affects 120 million people annually worldwide  
(Carvalho et al., 2012).  
 
2.2 Viral Hepatitis Made Simple - Pathology, Clinical features & Classifications 
 
There are five different types of Hepatitis viruses: Hepatitis A Virus (HAV), Hepatitis B Virus 
(HBA), Hepatitis C Virus (HCV),  Hepatitis D  Virus (HDV) and Hepatitis E Virus (HEV) 
(Robertson & Kuhnert, 2003). 
 
2.3 Hepatitis A - Pathology and Clinical features 
 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
This report only focuses on and is limited to the Hepatitis A Virus, which is an acute short-term 
disease. Hepatitis A is also known as infectious hepatitis that affects children and young adults. It is 
most common in the developing countries due to poor hygiene, outbreaks often occur due to 
unsanitary condition such as contamination of the food and water. Hepatitis A virus has been 
recognized as the most common cause of foodborne diseases in developed countries, linked to 
contamination of the following three priority food groups: fresh produce, seafood, and ready-to-eat 
foods and semi-dried food (e.g., sun-dried tomatoes, dates, dried apricots, raisins, etc.) (Phan & 
Hollinger, 2013). The fecal-oral route transmits hepatitis A. It spreads by close personal contact 
with fecally contaminated water supplies, fruits, and vegetables. 
Consequently, poor personal hygiene increases the spread of the virus. Furthermore, raw or partially 
cooked clams from contaminated food could also be the cause of transmission. The virus is also 
transmitted sexually primarily among homosexual men. This virus is not a chronic infection but 
self-limiting (Organization & Alliance, 2010). The virus is characterized as a single-stranded RNA, 
and it has a non-enveloped property. The virus could be present in the liver, bile stool and blood of 
the patient. The symptoms could be mild, moderate and severe. It always starts with mild and vague 
symptoms. The symptoms appear from approximately two weeks to six months after exposure to 
the virus. In the beginning, the patient experiences a mild case of flu and are feeling tired. The 
symptoms become worse after some days, and the patient begins to feel a loss of appetite and 
nausea with a low-grade fever (Kwon et al., 2014).  
 
Consequently, due to the enlargement of the liver, the patient can also have pain and discomfort 
above the liver. After that, the patient experiences darker urine than usual and Jaundice (yellowish 
of the skin) appears. This indicates that the patient's liver is not functioning correctly in removing a 
reddish-yellow pigment called bilirubin from the blood (Fiore, 2004). Likewise, in most cases of 
Hepatitis A virus, previously healthy people with a strong immunity can fully recover within two to 
six weeks. Whereas the recovery period is longer for elderly people or the people with severe 
underlying disorders, such as heart failure, anemia, diabetes, cancer, etc., and they might develop 
severe or fulminant hepatitis with the low diagnosis.  However, the overall mortality rate of HAV is 
less than one percent (Mackinney-Novelo et al., 2012). 
 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
Hepatitis A virus is a member of Hepatovirus genus that belongs to the family Picornaviridiae, 
which includes the human enteroviruses and rhinoviruses. Hepatitis A Virus is a non-enveloped 
virus of 27-28 nm diameter of single-stranded RNA virus (Koff, 1992).  It replicates (copies or 
reproduces) in liver cells and affects the liver function, causing an immune response which leads to 
liver inflammation. There are characterized six genotypes of HAV (I–VI). Genotype I–III is 
associated with human infection and genotype IV-VI is related to simian infection. Moreover, the 
three human genotypes are further divided into two sub-genotypes, known as subgenotype A and 
sub-genotype B, which show a nucleotide difference of approximately 7.5 %. Additionally, a new 
sub-genotype called IC has been proposed (D’Andrea et al., 2015). There are four major patterns of 
HAV infections worldwide, and they are distributed into areas of high, intermediate, low, or very 
low occurrence (Wasley, Fiore, & Bell, 2006).

Hepatitis A virus is primarily transmitted from person to person via the fecal-oral route and through 
ingestion of contaminated water and food such as shellfish and uncooked vegetables or fruit 
prepared by infected food handlers in the endemic area (see figure 1).  
 
Moreover, the most common sources of foodborne HAV contamination are oysters, mussels, fruits, 
and vegetables. Fresh produced foods as, such as salad, strawberries, raspberries, blueberries, and 
vegetables, have increasingly been implicated in foodborne outbreaks of HAV. The person who is 
infected with HAV can excrete the virus in their stool for several weeks before the symptoms 
appear. Consequently, the infected person can have a high concentration of the virus in the stool in 
the period between the appearance of the initial symptoms and the full development of the 
symptoms (prodromal phase), whereas the concentration of the viral is usually very low by the time 
jaundice appears and the viral concentration is undetectable before symptoms are resolves (Fiore, 
2004; Mackinney-Novelo et al., 2012).

2.4 Risk Factors 
 
The risk factor for HAV is found to be connected to several different things, such as having low 
household socioeconomic status (low income, wealth, and/or educational level), a larger household 
size and crowding, residence in a rural area, membership in certain ethnic groups, limited access to 
clean water sources, and limited access sanitation facilities.  Several additional high-risk population 
groups have been identified, including those who travel or emigrate from a non-endemic region to 
an endemic region and those who work in certain high-risk occupations (such as some daycare 
employees) (Franco, Giambi, Ialacci, Coppola, & Zanetti, 2003) 
2.3 Clinical Characteristics 
 
Hepatitis A infection has four clinical phases, although these do not occur in all patients (Ruiz, 
Zumbrun, & Nalca, 2013) which are as follows: 
1. The first stage is an incubation period ranging from 15 to 50 days (mean 28-30 days). 
This stage is asymptomatic, but the infected person may be actively shedding the virus in the 
stool. 
2. The second stage is a pre-icteric (pre-jaundice) period of several days to weeks that may 
precede the onset of jaundice. This prodromal period is characterized by nonspecific 
symptoms followed by gastrointestinal symptoms such as loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, 
abdominal pain, fatigue, malaise, and fever. Other symptoms at this stage may include 
myalgia (muscle pain), arthralgia (joint pain), cough, pharyngitis, constipation, diarrhea, 
pruritus (itchiness), and urticarial (rashes). Dark urine caused by elevated bilirubin levels 
usually occurs before the onset of jaundice. 
3. In the third stage, the characteristic yellowing of the skin and eyes of jaundice appear, and 
most symptoms subside, although clinical signs such as hepatomegaly and hepatic 
tenderness are found in about half of patients. There is no treatment for HAV infection. 
Jaundice usually resolves within a few weeks. 
4. The final stage is a recovering period. 
2.5 Age-dependency  
 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
Hepatitis A virus is highly age-dependent. Young children with an acute HAV virus are usually 
considered to be asymptomatic or nonspecific, and they are not diagnosed with HAV, due to the 
lack of jaundice symptoms. However, children are most at risk for infection, and are often carriers 
of hepatitis A. They are at increased risk for acquiring and transmitting fecal-oral pathogens such as 
hepatitis A, because young children are often still in diapers, do not know what hygiene is or 
entails, and explore their environment with their mouths as well as their hands. It is estimated that 
80-95 % of young children less than five years old have asymptomatic infections of HAV. They 
play a predominant role in disease transmission (Stanaway et al., n.d.). 
Additionally, in the case of adults with an acute infection they can either have a mild form of the 
disease or they may develop a serious complication. Subsequently, the severity of the disease 
increases with age (Phan & Hollinger, 2013). So, all age groups are at risk, but the disease is milder 
in young children under 6 years than older children and adults. Case-fatality risk increases with age, 
so risks are higher for unexposed older people.  The adults that are most at risk of infection with 
hepatitis A are the once that travels to countries where hepatitis A occurs, the household contacts 
and sexual contacts of infected individuals, adults living in US counties with increased rates of 
hepatitis A, men who have sex with men (Fiore, 2004). 
2.6 Complications 
 
The majority of hepatitis A patients make a full recovery, and the case fatality rate is low. The 
estimated mortality rate is 0.1% for children less than 15 years old, 0.3% for adults ages 15 to 39, 
and 2.1% for adults ages 40 and older [Hollinger, 1996]. Several complications may occur. About 
15% of patients experience prolonged jaundice and relapses over several months. Some develop 
cholestasis hepatitis, in which the bile duct leading from the liver to the intestine becomes blocked. 
A few suffer from fulminant (acute) liver failure that may require a transplant or cause death. 
Although liver failure is more likely to occur in patients suffering from chronic liver disease before 
the onset of hepatitis A, it can occur in anyone with HAV infection. Hepatitis A does not cause 
chronic liver disease (Fiore, 2004). 
2.7 Detection 
 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
Hepatitis A is usually diagnosed through the detection of HAV-specific antibodies in serum. 
Antibodies are present during the early stages of infection, and HAV-specific immunoglobulin M 
(IgM) is detectable for 45-60 days after the onset of symptoms. HAV-specific immunoglobulin G 
(IgG) is detectable for many years, and recovery from infection is associated with lifelong 
immunity. Hepatitis A virus infection can be diagnosed by genome detection using RT-PCR. 
Viruses can be detected in blood and feces for a similar period after acute illness (Koff, 1992). 
 
2.8 Vaccination 
Treatment 
Since Hepatitis A is an acute infectious disease and has no sequalae and it is self-limiting. There is 
no specific treatment for HAV except supportive treatment. Only the patients that develop severe 
disease such as fulminant and get hospitalized, doctors will perform liver transplantation. In order to 
protect and prevent the occurrence of HAV in Denmark it is very important to the following: There 
have been developed effective vaccines against hepatitis A Virus. Currently, there are two kinds of 
HAV vaccination available in the market that can be applied on all ages both children and adults. 
Chapter 3 
 
This chapter focuses on the objectives of the project and the methods used to estimate the burden of 
Hepatitis A in Denmark in terms of Disability Adjusted Life (DALYs). The advantage of the DALY 
metric is that makes it enable to compare the disease burden of all the different diseases caused by 
the foodborne hazards (bacteria, virus, parasites, and chemicals). 
 
3.1. Objectives 
 
The overall aim of this project was to estimate the burden of disease of HAV in Denmark in the 
years 2013, 2016 and 2017, in terms of DALYs taking the gender and age groups into account.  
Furthermore, the project includes an overview of routes of transmission and finally a discussion of 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
the public health impact of HAV in Denmark, when compared to other foodborne diseases, and of 
the potential public health strategies to reduce this burden. 
To accomplish the aim, specific objectives were defined as follows: 
1. Adapting a previously developed model to estimate the incidence of HAV in the Danish 
population. 
2. Collecting available data for quantifying HAV in the Danish population 
3. Identifying all potential health outcomes associated with HAV in the Danish population. 
4. Constructing or developing health outcome trees for HAV using the information collected 
from the previous step 
5. Calculating, YLDs (Years lived with disability), and YLLs (years of life lost) and lastly 
DALYs.

Hepatitis A virus was selected for this study due to its public health significance and the outbreaks

3.3 The concept of disease burden 
 
The primary purpose of the public health policy is to protect and promote the health of the 
population. The burden of disease (BoD) gives information on the health conditions of the 
population. This paper outlines some important aspects of this term. The information on the BoD is 
important for the process of decision making within the health sector. Furthermore, the 
policymakers have to make relevant decisions and set appropriate priorities, and for this purpose 
they need to know about the size of health problems in the population, the groups that are at risk 
and the trends in the state of health over time (Gibb et al., 2015a). Additionally, the estimation of 
the population health condition is useful to determine the expected health care for prioritizing 
effective interventions. Subsequently, there are some indicators related to the population health, 
==PAGE:23==
15 
 
including life expectancy, mortality rates, and numbers of new and existing cases of the specific 
disease (World Health Organization, 2014).  
 
However, all these indicators highlight only one facet of public health in terms of either mortality or 
morbidity. Nevertheless, for combining the morbidity and mortality, important methodological 
advances have been developed in so-called summary measures of the population (SMH). 
Furthermore, SMH is divided into two parts, such as health expectancies and health gaps. Both 
metrics from the different parts combine morbidity and mortality into a single figure. The health 
expectancy-based metrics, such as Disability-Free Life Expectancy (DFLE), Healthy Life Years 
(HLY), and Disability-Adjusted Life Expectancy (DALE), translate or convert these indicators into 
a health-adjusted life expectancy; health gap metrics, such as the Disability-Adjusted Life Year 
(DALY), translate these indicators into a number of YLL due to ill health and mortality. Therefore, 
the report will focus on the DALY as the main metric for quantifying the BoD.

3.9 Foodborne disease model (outcome-tree) 
 
The outcome tree for a disease shows the sequence or sequence of the disease to help as a guide for 
the further process of the study. Generally, risk factors increase the risk of disease, either directly or 
indirectly through facilitating exposure to biological, chemical or physical hazards.  The course of 
the disease is described by different health states (e.g., acute or chronic phases, short-term or long-
term sequelae), possibly having different severity levels. A disease model is a schematic 
representation of the different health states associated with the concerned cause of disease burden, 
and the possible transitions between these states. There are three different approaches or disease 
models for calculating the DALYs depending on the area of interest. 
1. Outcome-based approach (Acute illness SequelaeFatal): The outcome-based disease 
models show different health states of the disease regardless of etiologies. For instance, an 
outcome-based disease model for diarrhea might describe different severity states of the 
disease, such as mild, moderate and severe, that contributes, death, YLD, and YLL. 
 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
2. Hazard-based approach (Not affected Affected): The second disease model which is a 
hazard-based model, describes all the health outcomes associated with an agent caused by 
the exposure to biological or chemical hazards. It is a natural choice approach, which 
defines the burden of a specific foodborne disease resulting from health states, such as 
symptoms and sequelae including death at different time scales with different severity 
levels. 
 
3. Risk factor-based approach: (Risk factor 1  Risk factor 2 Risk factor 3): The third 
disease model applied for estimating the burden of foodborne diseases associated with risk 
factors for different health states that can affect exposure to contributing agents of 
foodborne illness. 
 
 
3.10 Disability weights 
 
The Disability Weight (DW) is a weight factor that reflects the severity of the disease on a scale 
from zero (perfect health) to one (worst possible health state or death). Consequently, DW is one of 
the most important components of the DALY calculation as they allow comparing morbidity and 
mortality.

3.13 Health Metric Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) 
 
The Disability-Adjusted Life Year (DALY) currently is the most commonly used metric in the field 
of public health and health impact assessment (HIA) to estimate the burden of disease (BoD) 
(WHO, 2015). This concept was used to estimate the BoD by the World Bank in 1993 and achieved 
more importance in 1996 after the GBD study by the WHO (Pires, 2014). DALY provides 
information on health-gap measures, which calculates health losses based on that gap.  It combines 
morbidity, mortality, and disability in one metrics that makes it possible to compare and rank 
between diseases in all regions of the world (Murray, n.d.). 
 
3.14 The DALY components 
 
DALYs are composed of two components, Years Lived with Disability (YLDs) and Years of Life 
Lost due to premature mortality (YLLs). YLD, the morbidity component of the DALYs, is 
calculated as follows: 
 
YLD = Number of cases × Duration till remission or death × Disability Weight 
 
However, the Disability Weight (DW) is an important component of the DALY calculation, as they 
translate morbidity into healthy life years lost, to compare morbidity and mortality. A DW, scaled 
from zero (perfect health) to one (worst possible health state), can be interpreted as the proportional 
reduction in good health due to an adverse health state. In the DALY concept, this is equal to losing 
the same proportion of healthy life years over the course of the disability.

3.16 Burden of Hepatitis A in Denmark and Europe 
 
Denmark was part of the first Nordic foodborne outbreak of HAV infection in the year 2013. In 
February 2013, a higher than the usual number of notified cases of hepatitis A virus (HAV) 
infection who had no travel history were registered in Denmark. The Danish case-control study 
reported that frozen berries (strawberries in smoothies) were the source of the outbreak. Most of the 
affected people were children and teenagers as they were the biggest consumers of the berries.  
Furthermore, HAV typing from these patients showed that several patients had been infected with 
the same genotype 1B and that several had the same sequence, which indicated a shared source of 
infection.  At the same time, Finland, Norway, and Sweden also reported an increase in the number 
of patients with HAV infection, who had no history of travelling. A comparison of HAV sequences 
showed that several of the cases in the three other countries had the same genotype and sequence as 
the Danish cases. It was there for established that it was a single Nordic outbreak. The three 
remaining countries also conducted case-control studies, and a combined analysis for all four 
countries indicated that the source of the outbreak was frozen strawberries.  
The incidence data that is reported for the year 20013 on Hepatitis A cases showed, that in the 
year 2013, a total number of 108 cases of Hepatitis A were notified. Among those 108 cases, 51 
were female patients, and 75 were males. Out of 51 female patients, 44 of them were infected

3.18 The Burden of Communicable Diseases in Europe BCoDE (toolkit) 
 
The Burden of Communicable Diseases in Europe (BCoDE) toolkit was developed by ECDC in 
2015 to enable the calculation of DALYs from incidence-based data for 32 different infectious 
diseases and six healthcare-associated infections in Europe. The purpose of developing BCoDE 
toolkit was to use this software in computing the analyses the Burden of Communicable Diseases in 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
Europe. BCoDE toolkit software is a full top Dow ranking risk ranking tool. It provides significant 
outcomes such as DALY, DALYs Per case, and DALYs per 100.000, YLD and YLL/ 100000.  
It is based on input parameters such as age group and gender-specific number of cases in countries. 
The BCoDE toolkit runs Monte Carlo simulations of disease progression model and gives disease-
specific results, including 95% uncertainty intervals, and permits comparisons between the different 
diseases models entered. After that, the results are shown as mean and median overall DALYs, 
DALYs per 100,000 population, and DALYs related to mortality vs. disability. Visualization 
options summarize complex epidemiological data, with the goal of improving communication and 
knowledge transfer for decision making. The toolkit uses a disease progression pathway that 
describes the progression of the disease over time, from acute infection to death, resolution or long-
term sequelae. Thereafter, the disease progression pathway is shown in a disease outcome tree   
The transition probabilities, durations of health outcomes, number of cases associated to a specific 
health state in each health outcome, such as number of severe cases of acute symptomatic infection 
over the total number of acute symptomatic cases in used in BCoDe toolkit were derived from 
literature reviews for each of the 32 diseases and 6 healthcare-associated infections included in the 
tool approved by the disease experts. Results of BCoDE do not include age-weighting, and are 
optionally displayed with and without time discounting. There are two types of transitions 
probabilities including lifetime transition probabilities (LTP) which represents the probability that 
can be applied once to all cases exiting the health outcome. The second type of probability is known 
as the annual transition probabilities (ATP) which can be applied annually to all cases in the health 
outcome for the duration of latter. 
The disability weight used in the toolkit were obtained from European studies specified for the 
European population   using same methodology that was applied by the Global Burden of Disease 
2015 study. The results are shown both as a form of detailed results and aggregated results, pie 
chart and bar charts in order to show different impact of acute disease. In detailed results summary 
tables for total and health outcomes specific results are displayed.  
The detailed results table shows notified or estimated number of cases, deaths incidence per 
100,000 population, years of life lost due to disability (YLD), years of life lost due to premature 
death (YLL), DALYs, and DALYs per case as well as YLD per 100,000 population, YLL per 
100,000 population, and DALYs per 100,000 population.  DALYs per case shows the average 
==PAGE:35==
27 
 
burden of a disease experienced by each infected symptomatic case and it can be understood as a 
measure of average severity apart from those disease models where some of the burden comes also 
from asymptomatic infections (e.g., hepatitis B).  Averages, medians and age- and sex-specific 2.5 
and 97.5 percentiles are displayed for DALYs, DALY per case, DALY per 100,000 total 
populations and DALY per 100,000 stratum specific populations.

4.3 Conclusion 
 
This study estimated the burden of foodborne disease of hepatitis A virus as well as the 
transmission routes by which the virus spreads to humans. The three most important transmission 
routes are food, water and direct person to person contact. 
The virus can be also caused by the Sexual transmission (MSM).  Foods that are associated to the 
transmission of HAV found to be liver pate, oyster, semidried tomatoes, raw seafood, bakery 
products, and pomegranate seeds and especially frozen strawberries or frozen barries. Some of the 
cases in the outbreak in 2013 was caused by imported frozen strawberries either from east Europe, 
Africa or China. These strawberries were contaminated during the packaging process. The 
incidence numbers are higher in 2013 compared to 2016 and 2017, where  some  of  the  cases of 
HAV were caused by men who have sex with men (MSM). Addiotnally, the estimated number of 
death,  YLD/Year, YLL/Year and DALYs/year, DALYs/case  are higher in the year 2013 compared 
to the year 2016 and 2017. The DALY estimates YLL and YLD which are utilized to compare the 
diseases.  We compared the total DAILY with other pathogens such as bacteria (e.g. salmonella, 
camplyobactor) and among chemicals we compared with acrylamide.   
The conclusion is that Denmark is considered to be the country with a low HAV endemicity. 
Although the burden of Hepatitis A is lower in Denmark compared to other pathogens such as 
bacteria and chemicals (Figure). This might be due to that many cases of HAV have an 
asymptomatic course of infection, and the asymptomatic course of infection might give positive in

This study suggests that the need to be considered with specific prevention and control measures in

processing and clean water and direct person to person contamination.
