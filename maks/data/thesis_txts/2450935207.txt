Abstract
Motivation and Purpose
Worldwide one billion people are estimated to suﬀer from neuro-
logical disorders where numerous feature an increased intracranial pressure (ICP) [31]. The ICP
is maintained within a normal range by compensatory mechanisms in the brain. Failure to do so
results in raised ICP that is associated with poor patient outcome. For this reason, monitoring
these pressure changes is of importance as it assists in diagnosing and treating patients. Despite
the widespread use, pivotal ICP questions remain unanswered.
Using an experimental paradigm, this project investigates the ICP in the ﬁrst ICP-related
physical head phantom model to obtain an improved understanding of the generation of the
wave morphology in ICP signals.
Methods
To mimic the complex physiology within the human head, a head phantom was
developed. The skull was segmented by the use of a human head CT scan, remodelled, and
lastly 3D-printed. This was comprised of a brain tissue mimicking material and a water-ﬁlled
balloon associated with the constructed pressure generators. Experiments were to elucidate the
inﬂuence of water pressure, postural elevation, height, and temperature on the measured ICP.
Moreover, to acquire ICP data at two depths and a comparison of diﬀerent conﬁgurations for
generating the input pressure. The relationship between the measured ICP during pressure
application and the associated tissue displacement about the pressure sensor was also explored.
Both clinical ICP sensors and diagnostic ultrasound (US) were utilised.
Results
Analysis of the measured ICP demonstrates the usefulness of the constructed phys-
ical head phantom model in which measurements at ventricular and parenchymal depths were
performed. The ICP obtained at changing postural elevation indicated that the higher the ele-
vation, the lower the ICP. Contrary to the expected, the height did inﬂuence the ICP. Moreover,
the mean ICP was highest at low temperature (i.e. 4°C), though, the lowest variations in mean
ICP were at 21°C. The ICP waveform did not perfectly resemble the clinical ICP morphology,
however, the construction of the pressure generators showed to have a great inﬂuence. Further-
more, the analysis of the speckle patterns, identiﬁed by the use of US during pressure application,
enabled visualisation of tissue displacement about the pressure sensor. A comparison with the
measured ICP signals revealed that small patterns were not distinct in the displacement images.
Conclusion Based on the results, it can be concluded that the physical head phantom model
enabled ICP tests as well as visualisation of the brain tissue displacement. The model lacks
robustness and complexity before conclusions about the generation of the ICP wave morphology
can be drawn. Despite this, this project has made a foundation for future investigation of the
ICP and the factors inﬂuencing it, with the inclusion of encountered experimental challenges
and recommended future work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Neurological disorders have a great impact on society due to both their economic cost and
human suﬀering. This substantial impact does not only aﬀects the patient but also his/her ac-
quaintances. Examples of neurological disorders include traumatic brain injury, hydrocephalus,
and idiopathic intracranial hypertension. Neurological disorders account for 6.8 million deaths
worldwide each year [31]. The total number of people in the world suﬀering from these diseases
is estimated to one billion [31]. To reduce these economic and human costs, prevention along
with early detection and a better understanding of their pathology are necessary.
Most of these disorders inﬂuence the intracranial pressure (ICP). ICP contributors are the
cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF), the blood, and the relatively ﬁxed and uncompressible brain tissue.
All being within the rigid skull.
A volume increase in one compartment (or from an addi-
tional pathological volume) is compensated by a decrease in one or more of the other ICP
contributors. This compensatory mechanism maintainw the ICP within a normal range. When
compensation is not possible, the ICP rise, which is associated with poor patient outcome.[1, 41]
The ICP is the most commonly collected brain-speciﬁc parameter regarding neurocritical care
[1]. It assists in diagnosing, treating, and predicting the prognosis of a neurological disorder
[41]. Gold standard ICP monitoring is performed invasively through penetrating the skull and
inserting a pressure sensor into the brain, where it should ideally stay for up to 48 hours. De-
spite the widespread usage of ICP monitoring, some pivotal questions remain unanswered. For
instance, there is no agreement on what a normal ICP range is and neither a complete knowledge
of the physiological sources generating the ICP morphology [1, 30, 41]. This is mainly because
ICP monitoring is invasive that entail ethical considerations since ICP cannot be monitored in
healthy individuals.
Scientiﬁc work shows the presence of non-invasive ICP monitoring methods such as transcranial
Doppler ultrasonography and optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD). The latter has among the
non-invasive ICP methods shown to be the best. However, compared to the invasive standard
monitoring method it is less accurate and less reliable.[5, 8]
One way to better understand the ICP abnormalities and their relation to neurological disorders
is to consider the wave patterns in the ICP signal along with the study of possible sources
inﬂuencing the behaviour of the signal. The waves can either be examined on a micro scale
as the ICP waveform or as macro patterns lasting seconds to minutes. Various computerised
mathematical models have been developed to simulate the ICP for this purpose [20, 30, 36].
Various aspects hamper the mathematical simulation of a more realistic and general simulated

 By imaging the physical head phantom with ultrasound, the relationship between
applied pressure and tissue displacement were explored.

Chapter 2
Background
This Thesis focuses on the intracranial pressure (ICP) signal and its relation to arterial pulsa-
tions. This chapter provides the physiological background concerning the brain and the ICP
required to understand the theoretical reasoning behind the aforementioned relationship. It
also includes a description of the techniques used to study this relationship: ICP monitoring
and interpretation as well as diagnostic ultrasound (US).
2.1
The Brain
The main organs of the human central nervous system (CNS) are the brain and the spinal cord.
The brain consists of the cerebrum, the cerebellum, the brainstem, and diencephalon, which are
all contained within a protective non-expandable skull. The cerebrum is divided into the right
and left hemispheres each having frontal-, parietal-, occipital-, and temporal lobes. The last
mentioned is separated from the others by the lateral ﬁssure (Figure 2.1). The brain consists of
the nervous tissue grey matter on the surface and the white matter located deeper within the
brain. Grey matter is composed of neuron cell bodies and their dendrites, while white matter
is axons with myelin sheath.[4]
The brain and spinal cord are surrounded by the cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF) and the meninges
that function as protective layers. The meninges consist of three tissue membranes: dura-,
arachnoid-, and pia mater. The thick dura mater adheres to the skull and keeps the brain ﬁxed.
Underneath the dura mater is the thin arachnoid- and pia mater with the subarachnoid space
in between. CSF lies within the subarachnoid space together with blood vessels and in the
ventricles. The main production (80% - 90%) of the CSF takes place in the lateral ventricles in
each hemisphere.[4]
The blood reaches the brain through the internal carotid arteries and the vertebral arteries.
The vertebral arteries join into the basilar artery at pons, and further into the posterior cerebral
arteries that supply the cerebrum. The internal carotid arteries ascend through the carotid
canals where they branch to the middle cerebral arteries and anterior cerebral arteries. These
supply the lateral cerebral cortex and the frontal lobes, respectively (Figure 2.2). The venous
sinuses in dura mater transport the venous blood and CSF away from the brain.[4]

2.2
Intracranial Pressure
Multiple mechanism aﬀects pressure. This section ﬁrstly covers general inﬂuences on pressure
followed by more detailed biological descriptions on the ICP.
The ICP is the gauge intracranial pressure, that is, the diﬀerence between the absolute pressure
within the skull and the surrounding atmospheric pressure.[14]
As an example, the absolute pressure in a water column located at height, h, have a pressure

The volume distribution within the rigid skull is approximately 10% CSF, 10% blood, and 80%
parenchymal brain tissue [33]. The ICP arises from the contribution of the partial volume of
these three components, that can be modelled as compartments. The Monro-Kellie hypothesis
states that a volume increase of one compartment or an introduction of an additional (patholog-
ical) volume is (by the regulatory mechanism of the brain) compensated with an equal volume
decrease of one or more of the other compartments. This is to keep the intracranial volume
constant [41]. The relatively restricted brain tissue has the poorest ability to change, where the
CSF and blood are better at compensating for volume changes. In particular, the blood com-
pensates by venous compression and the CSF compensates by increased absorption, decreased
production, and relocation to the spinal subarachnoid space.
The overall ability of the brain to maintain the ICP under changing intracranial volume,
∆V , is deﬁned as the compliance, C = ∆V / ∆ICP. The pressure-volume relation (Figure

To analyse the ICP signals monitored with the sensors, both ICP patterns and amplitude are
used. An elevated ICP is associated with poor outcome and can for example manifest as in-
adequate tissue perfusion, cellular hypoxia, and neural death. No ﬁnite deﬁnition regarding
the ’normal’ range of the ICP exists. Though in general, literature agrees that ICP in the
supine position is normal when the it is lower than 15 mmHg [1, 28, 33, 41]. Treatment should
be initiated when the ICP is 20 mmHg or higher. The range from 25 mmHg to 40 mmHg is
considered to be severely elevated [1, 3, 41]. However, these categorisations between ’normal’
and ’abnormal’ ICP ranges are much debated. As an example, the reference ranges are usually
deﬁned for the supine position, which can be relevant for subjects in a coma, but nonrelevant
for active patients taking various body positions during the ICP monitoring [26, 27].
When looking at ICP signals from a micro-pattern scale (Figure 2.7a), a single ICP waveform
is comprised of three peaks. These peaks are from highest to lowest amplitude: P1, P2, and P3
(Figure 2.7b). All peaks are a result of the cardiac cycle. P1 is the percussion wave that reﬂects
the arterial pressure caused by the transmission of blood through the choroid plexus. P2 is the
tidal wave, which is associated with brain compliance, and P3 is the dicrotic wave reﬂecting
the aortic valve closure.
The amplitude of the peaks reveals physiological and pathological
conditions, as an elevated ICP results in higher peak amplitude. As an example, an increased
P2 indicates poor compliance with a depleted cerebrospinal compensatory reserve.[6, 41]

min, and is caused by diminishing brain compliance. B waves are associated with respiratory
changes and appear as slow waves having pressure 10 mmHg - 20 mmHg. C waves are related to

2.3
Diagnostic Ultrasound
Diagnostic ultrasound (US) is an imaging modality based on the propagation of sound above
the human audible range in diﬀerent media. The modality makes use of the various acoustic
properties of the media when visualising materials such as biological components.
Generation of an ultrasonic wave occurs within a transducer that is responsible for both
transmission and reception of the US signal.
2.3.1
Ultrasound Interactions
The transmitted US wave is a beam-shaped wave pattern that during propagation will interact
with the media. The types of interactions are reﬂection, scattering, refraction, and absorp-
tion (Figure 2.8). Reﬂection and scattering happen as a transmitted wave strikes an interface
between two media having diﬀerent speciﬁc acoustic impedances. Speciﬁc acoustic impedance
is the density multiplied by the acoustic propagation velocity within the medium. Reﬂection
occurs at large interfaces relative to the wavelength and scattering at small interfaces relative
to the wavelength. Only the part of the US wave reﬂected and/or scattered back towards the
transducer is received and used for imaging. This is called the echo. The part of a wave that
8

continues the forward propagation along a new direction has been refracted. Lastly, absorption
is when mechanical energy is transformed into heat. All interaction types result in the intensity
loss of the wave where the wave will be debilitated and eventually cease. This is referred to as
attenuation.[18]

2.3.2
Ultrasound Imaging
The received echo carrying information about the intensity and the time between transmissions
is used to generate an US image. The longer the time between transmission and reception, the
deeper is the structure - which gives rise to an echo - located. Likewise, the higher the intensity,
the larger is the speciﬁc acoustic impedance diﬀerence between interfaces resulting in a stronger
echo.[18]
An US image is constructed by multiple amplitude-mode (A-mode) lines that combined gives
the greyscale brightness-mode (B-mode) image. An A-mode line is an envelope-detecting signal
that carries information about the presence of a boundary at a certain depth extracted from
the media’s speciﬁc acoustic impedance. The information is obtained from the received radio
frequency (RF) echo along a single stationary line in the beam direction. The received RF echo
is usually time-gain compensated and log compressed. This is to improve intensity strength of
structures located far from the transducer as well as to get a better greyscale interpretation for
the human eye. Lastly, the envelope of the received RF echo is computed (Figure 2.9).[18]
Basically, spatial information regarding the extent of the boundary requires multiple A-mode
lines. A collection of parallel A-mode lines that are slightly displaced gives the B-mode image.
Other improved beamforming methods such as spatial compounding methods exist but will not
be covered.[2, 18]
A commercial US scanner is used in this thesis. This means that the knowledge related to
the speciﬁc beamforming, image construction, added ﬁlters and more are unknown as it is an
intellectual property right. The already processed US data means that the output is not the
raw acquired RF signals but rather the envelope-detected US image data.

2.3.3
Tissue Displacement
Speckles are a spacial distribution of greyscales that arise within an US image as dotted-hash
of varying intensities (Figure 2.9). They occur due to constructive and destructive interference
between echoes emerging from closely spaced scatters [18]. Constructive interference increases
whereas destructive interference reduce the amplitude signal. The received US echo is thereby a
result of the interference pattern of the imaged region. The internal arrangement of the scatters,
which generate the speckle patterns, is unchanged. This means that the same speckle pattern
will be visualised under a stationary setting, where no displacement is present. On the other
hand, if scatters are moving, the associated speckle pattern will likewise move. As a result,
the displacement of the scatters are tracked by identifying that speciﬁc speckle pattern (Figure
2.10).
Figure 2.10:
Displacement of speckle pattern between time t0 (blue dots) and t1 (red
dots). The speciﬁc speckle pattern has displaced (∆x, ∆z). Arrows indicate the displacement
direction.
Speckle and structure displacements are tracked between multiple subsequent B-mode US im-
ages from a ﬁxed transducer location. This can, as an example, be done during a period of
applied pressure or a heart cycle. The position with the lowest diﬀerence between an observed
speckle pattern and a chosen kernel in the neighbouring B-mode image indicates where the
speckle pattern has displaced to. The magnitude and direction of the displacement are there-
fore estimated. As mentioned, only envelope-detected US image data is available and not the

RF signals. As a result, only the intensity of the signal (i.e. the amplitude information) can be
used for speckle tracking.[15]
The speed of the movement of speckles should not exceed the US frame rate. A too low
frame rate leads to insuﬃcient speckle tracking especially in situations with large tissue defor-
mations. The speckle tracking and thereby the determination of tissue displacement can further
be inﬂuenced by factors such as poor spatial resolution, poor signal-to-noise ratio, image arte-
facts, and large displacements. The spatial resolution is a measure of the average speckle size.
A large speckle size impairs the change of ﬁnding the exact displacement location. Next, the
better a signal-to-noise, the better a speckle tracking. However, artefacts as shadowing and re-

tracking performance. Lastly, tracking performance of large displacements is highly aﬀected by

tracking (as it consists of many characteristic speckle patterns) but spacial resolution impair.

After pre-processing, two diﬀerent visualisations were made.
Firstly, an emphasising of the connection between scan location and associated US data was
made by visualising one frame of the zoomed US data on top of the physical head phantom
model. This included the modelled skull, the blood-supplying balloon, and the US- and the
ICP probes.
Secondly, the US data were used to investigate the displacement of the brain tissue substi-
tute when applying pressure. The tissue displacement was found between one frame and its
neighbouring frame, i.e. the displacement between frame 1 and 2, frame 2 and 3, frame 3 and 4,
and so forth. To ﬁnd the displacements, a 2.5 cm × 2.5 cm window segment was initially deﬁned
along with a region of interest (ROI) of size 5 cm × 5 cm. The window segment having data
from frame 1, seg1, was placed at the same location in frame 2. A window segment with data
from frame 2, seg2, was moved around within the deﬁned ROI (Figure 3.15, i1). The center of
seg1 and the ROI were identical. For each location of seg2, the diﬀerence between seg1 and seg2
was summed and stored in the center of seg2 (Figure 3.15, ii1). This was repeated until seg2 had
covered the ROI such that all summed diﬀerences within the ROI were calculated (Figure 3.15,
iiend). The minimum of all the summed diﬀerences within the ROI was identiﬁed and stored at
the center of seg1 in the displacement matrix (Figure 3.15, iii). The minimum diﬀerence reveals
where frame 1 and frame 2 were most alike and thereby where the tissue was displaced to as the
internal arrangement of scatters were unchanged. A new seg1, displaced one pixel from the ﬁrst
seg1, was now placed in frame 2 together with the associated ROI. The previously described
steps in Figure 3.15, i1, to Figure 3.15, iii, were repeated. This entail that a new minimum
displacement between the summed diﬀerences was found and placed in the displacement matrix
next to the ﬁrst calculated minimum. All this was repeated until all minimum displacements
between frame 1 and frame 2 were calculated and placed in the displacement matrix (Figure
3.15, iiii). Finally, the displacements were normalised to range between 0 and 1. The same
procedure was performed for all 110 frames, resulting in 109 displacement frames.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The purpose of this Master’s Thesis was to obtain a better understanding of how the wave
morphology within intracranial pressure (ICP) signals are generated.
This project demonstrates the development and construction of a physical head phantom
model mimicking the complex physiology. Within the physical model, multiple conducted exper-
iments explored diﬀerent factors inﬂuencing the ICP signals. Both ICP equipment and diagnos-
tic ultrasound (US) were used in the experiments. Results conﬁrm that the tissue displacement
occurring about the ICP sensors can be elucidated using US. Furthermore, monitoring of ICP
at both parenchymal and ventricular depths was possible and showed that the ICP morphology
was greatly inﬂuenced by pressure generator.
This chapter discusses the obtained results and ﬁndings (Chapter 4) regarding the objectives,
the existing literature, and limitations and strengths. Additionally, clinical relevance will be
covered.
5.1
Evaluation of Experimental Intracranial Pressure
The evaluation of the ICP results from experiment one and two will be covered within this
section. Firstly, results related to the usefulness of the model are covered followed by a discussion
on the ICP results related to the ICP morphology.
5.1.1
Usefulness of the Model
The test of the three pressure sensors in water (Experiment 1.0) showed that Neurovent-P is the
most accurate and Neurovent-P-tel is the least accurate. The Neurovent-P-tel had measured ap-
proximately 3 mmHg lower than the applied water pressure. A drift was expected as the sensor
had been in use for more than 29 days and the company claimed that the typical long term drift
is ±2 mmHg / 29 days (Table 4.1, Chapter 4) [35]. The Neurovent-P-tel was inconvenient to
use during measurements in the upside down positioned phantom since the reader TDT1 readP
needed to be ﬁxated with tape or similar such that it was close to the Neurovent-P-tel (Figure
A.1b, Appendix A). Additionally, zero-calibration of the Neurovent-P-tel was performed by the
producer, the other sensors were zero-calibrated by the user. This imposed the drift leading to
a less accurate ICP measurement by the Neurovent-P-tel. Despite this, all sensors function and
measure within a trustworthy ICP range.
Experiment 1.2 to Experiment 1.4 tested the inﬂuence of elevation, height, and temperature on
the ICP signals in the parenchymal depth.

The head phantom was in all experiments, except 1.2, tested in the upside down position
(i.e. −80°) to ensure full contact between the pressure sensors and the tissue substitute. In a
clinical setting, this makes limited sense. At −80° the largest mean ICP was obtained, whereas
the lowest mean ICP was measured in the supine position (i.e. 0°). This indicates that elevation
inﬂuences the ICP such that a one-to-one comparison with literature cannot fully be made.
Contrary to the expected, results showed that the higher the setup was placed the larger the
ICP. If this was the case, clinical data would suﬀer when patients as an example climb stairs.
Results thereby imply that other things inﬂuence the experiment such as unattended rotation,
sensor displacement, or movement of the tissue substitute. A small sensor displacement may
occur during patient monitoring. Patients are monitored up to 48 h in which they continue
their everyday life, whereas this experiment only applies pressure for 30 s. The inﬂuence of the
sensor movement is therefore expected bigger within these results. Furthermore, a sensor path
was made for each sensor as the brain tissue substitute was moulded into the skull with the
sensors already in place. Air can have been trapped and tissue displaced during movement so
that the tightness of the tissue substitute around the pressure sensor varied.
Theoretically, temperature aﬀects pressure.
The higher the temperature, the larger the
volume expansion and thereby the larger the pressure within the rigid skull (Equation 2.3).
Experiment 1.4 showed the opposite. Here the largest ICP was measured at the lowest temper-
ature (i.e. 4°C) and the lowest ICP was monitored at the highest temperature (i.e. 30°C). The
largest mean ICP spread was obtained at these temperatures. The smallest spread was at 21°C
hence future experiments are suggested to be conducted at 21°C. A plausible explanation for the
variations at 4°C and 30°C is that the 3D-printed skull is not completely sealed. As a result,
the tissue substitute expands through the openings of the skull instead of being compressed
within the skull, which would have entailed an increased ICP. In a real head, these are not
directly exposed to air but covered with for instance eyes and muscles. To mimic these their
elasticity should be known. The challenge between the eye area and US image at the current
probe location within the phantom should too be solved. Further, movements of the skull and
sensor displacement may have inﬂuenced the results as the sensor was reinserted between ICP
measurements at diﬀerent temperatures. The reason being the method of heating involving vast
amounts of movements of the setup.
5.1.2
ICP Morphology
The wave morphology of the measured ICP did not match the clinical ICP waveform that is
comprised of three peaks (i.e. percussion wave, tidal wave, and dicrotic wave). Looking at the
results, ICP generated with the ﬁrst pressure generator had one peak followed by rapid small
oscillations that debilitated about the baseline. The ICP generated with the second pressure
generator generally had one peak followed by a slow pressure decrease, which often contained
a small secondary peak. Despite the lack of similarity, this conﬁrms that the measured ICP is
inﬂuenced by one or more pulsating mechanisms as theory foretells. The model indicates that the
pressure generator needs further improvements. Recommended improvements are elaborated
and discussed in Future Work (Chapter 7).
The secondary small peak within the ICP, occurring when pressure was applied with the
second pressure generator, was not present using the Neurovent-P-tel. This is likely due to the
low sampling frequency (i.e. 5 Hz) whereas the Neuroent-P and Neurovent sample with 100 Hz.
Generally, diﬀerences in ICP baseline before and after pressure application were seen.
The
baseline level before and after pressure application was not constant in all ICP recordings. A
reason may be that the tissue substitute did not move back to its start position resulting in a

change in baseline pressure. Presumably, the use of a ﬂuid brain tissue substitute would prevent
the deformation. Clinicians often make use of mean ICP in proportion to the baseline when

On the other hand, the pressure application produced by manually compressing the heart

Diagnostic ultrasound data were obtained during pressure application in Experiment 1.1 and

5.3
Comparison to State of the Art
Findings within this Thesis are compared with existing relevant literature and discussed in re-
gard to what new insights and possible implications these ﬁndings can provide. The comparisons
are divided into the categories: phantom materials, ICP morphology, and postural eﬀects on
ICP. No similar studies use US to elucidate the tissue displacement about the pressure sensors.
For this reason, no comparisons with US literature were made.
5.3.1
Phantom Materials
Literature describes various materials used for head phantom constructions. The utilised brain
tissue substitute was an agar-based mixture consisting of agar, silica dioxide, evaporated milk,
alcohol, and demineralised water [7, 12, 13]. This agar-based mixture was selected due to its
ﬂexibility, easy production, ability to withstand US compression without cracking, low cost,
availability, acoustic properties, and because it is disposable and non-toxic [12, 13]. Literature
showed that inclusion of evaporated milk in the mixture entailed a more realistic brain tissue
substitute by introducing an acoustic absorption and increasing the attenuation [7, 12, 29].
However, evaporated milk facilitates the growth of bacteria within the material, which limits its
lifespan [7]. Generally, agar-based phantoms are mostly used as soft tissue substitutes because
they are easy to produce and ﬂexible in regard to changing the acoustic properties by additives
like evaporated milk [29].
Other materials have been proposed as suitable soft tissue-mimicking substitutes. These can
be divided into water-based, oil-based, and silicone-based phantoms. The latter is irrelevant

Water-

5.3.2
ICP Morphology
The value of intracranial pressure monitoring has long been known. ICP plays an important role
in the neurocritical care in regard to diagnosis, treatment, and prognosis of patients’ disorders
[1, 41]. Back in 1965, a pioneer study by Lungberg identiﬁed the macro-patterns of the ICP
signals. As mentioned, an identical morphology of the clinical and measured ICP waveform was
not seen.
Literature covers various aspects related to ICP. However, no investigation of the ICP has, ac-
cording to literature, been made within a physical head phantom model. No direct comparisons
can thereby be made between this physical head phantom model and the ICP models based on
computerised mathematical simulations.
A study by Wang et al. (2018) [20] estimated the ICP by combining a theory-based physical
model (containing modelled intracranial dynamics) with a data-based model with ICP-related
non-invasive measurements, including arterial blood pressure (ABP) and cerebral blood ﬂow
volume (CBFV). The modelled intracranial dynamics included the major arterial territories
where the vascular size varied with volume changes.
Further, the total volume within the
skull was assumed constant and the maintenance of the cerebral blood ﬂow modelled as a low-
pass ﬁlter. The study showed a more accurate ICP prediction when incorporating CBFV and
ABP data, although results varied between major arterial territories. Overall, incorporation
of intracranial dynamics has shown to improve the ICP estimation even though it was limited
by how well the physiology was mimicked. Furthermore, the model was limited by the sparse
training data, which usually were measured from patients with one speciﬁc neuropathology.
Another study by Unnerb¨ack et al. (2019) [30] constructed a mathematical model with
the inclusion of physical features of the ﬂuid compartments. These features (measured in 10
patients) were intracranial arterial blood inﬂow, venous blood outﬂow, and cerebrospinal ﬂuid
(CSF) ﬂow over foramen magnum. The estimated patient-speciﬁc ICP was compared with the
measured ICP. Similarities between the estimated and the measured ICP morphology was seen,

which indicated that peaks were inﬂuenced by the arterial blood inﬂow. However, the model
lacked generality as parameters were tuned for each subject and the amount of training data
were limited.
To summarise, the computerised mathematical ICP simulations and the physical head phan-
tom model approaches are diﬀerent methods used to obtain improved knowledge regarding the
ICP. Both approaches enable tests under controlled conditions and exploration of various physi-
ological aspects related to the ICP. However, decisions made when constructing a computerised
mathematical ICP model may to a greater extent inﬂuence the estimated ICP by imposing a
bias. Finally, the lack of suﬃcient training data within the mathematical models diminishes the
usefulness of the model.
5.3.3
Postural Eﬀects on ICP
Postural eﬀects on the ICP have been studied both via modelling and clinical evaluation. A
study by Schwarz et al. (2001) [39] investigated the ICP at the tilt angles 0°(supine position),
15°, and 30°. All 43 monitoring sessions performed on 18 patients, generally showed that the
higher the elevation, the lower the ICP. Though, some of the measurements showed small or no
diﬀerence.
Qvarlander et al. (2013) [38] investigated the postural eﬀects on ICP at seven tilt angles
(0°, 12°, 27°, 37°, 47°, 57°, 71°). Three mathematical models investigated ICP changes due to
hydrostatic pressure gradients in the CSF system, and the relation between ICP and venous
pressure. Results demonstrated a collapse of the jugular veins at high tilt angles, which lead
to a division of the hydrostatic system.
As a result, a lower ICP was measured as it only
corresponded to the hydrostatic pressure gradient from that site. All three models showed that
the larger a tilt angle, the lower the mean ICP.
A similar behaviour was demonstrated by Petersen et al. (2016) [23], who evaluated the
ICP at -20°, -10 °, 0°, 10°, 20°, and 90°.
Furthermore, Andresen et al. (2014) [26] studied the ICP in upright standing, sitting in a
chair, supine, and lateral recumbent lumbar puncture position in a total of 31 patients. The
lowest median ICP was monitored when the patient was either standing or sitting in a chair
(i.e. 90°). Generally, the highest median ICP was measured in the lateral recumbent lumbar
puncture position and followed by the supine position (i.e. 0°).
For recapitulation, the literature agrees upon the higher the tilt angle the lower is the
measured ICP. This too is the case in this relevant study (i.e. Experiment 1.2) carried out
in this Thesis. In addition, based on this, the ’normal’ ICP range cannot be evaluated in the
present physical head phantom model.
5.4
Further Limitations & Strengths
The investigation of ICP within a physical head phantom model based on CT scans on human
patients is, as far as known, the ﬁrst of a kind. No similar literature exists making this project
and its content unique. Compromises for simplicity and enablement were to be made due to
the time limitation. Both further limitations and strengths are now covered.
The reliability of the recorded data is inﬂuenced by the fact that the brain tissue substitute
consists of a biodegradable content (i.e. evaporated milk), whose physical properties change
over time, speciﬁcally in the latest experiments. The evaporated milk was condensed March

5.5
Clinical Relevance
There is a great interest in deriving a complete knowledge about the ICP and the factors inﬂu-
encing this pressure. This would provide knowledge about a normal ICP as well as the disease
inﬂuenced ICP. There is still some way to go before this is achieved and can be used in a clinical
setting.
The ICP classiﬁcation made by Lundberg (1965) was made on critically ill patients.
This
classiﬁcation can be seen in ICP monitoring in the intensive care unit. However, nowadays a
large proportion of patients that undergo ICP monitoring are less critically ill, meaning that
their pathological wave patterns are not as accentuated.
Ph.D. student Isabel Martinez Tejada is currently working on a classiﬁcation of the ICP pat-
terns to elucidate whether the same patterns are present in near-to-normal subjects. The aim
is to automate macro-pattern identiﬁcation in ICP signals. This is to study possible relations
between disease states and ICP signal characteristics. Furthermore, it is desired to be combined
with additional patient data (e.g. blood pressure). The source of the ICP signal is not fully
understood, which is an issue when considering a more general description of macro-patterns
in the ICP signal and their quantiﬁcation.
This makes the identiﬁcation of macro-patterns
challenging for diagnosis and treatment of the diﬀerent disorders. The investigation of the ICP
within the physical head phantom model is intended to assist the onward work related to Is-
abel’s Ph.D., and in the future by giving insight about how diﬀerent disease entities begin to
diverge from a ”normal” ICP signal and to enable identiﬁcations on early-stage deviations for
use in clinical practice.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This project aimed to obtain an improved understanding of the generation of the intracranial
pressure (ICP) signals arising within a rigid skull. No earlier documented research has explored
the ICP under controlled conditions by use of a head phantom mimicking the complex physiol-
ogy. For this reason, a physical head phantom model was developed and constructed. The head
phantom was constructed by use of a 3D-printed remodelled CT origin skull containing an agar-
based brain tissue substitute and pressure generators. Various factors inﬂuencing the ICP were
investigated by use of three diﬀerent pressure sensors and diagnostic ultrasound (US) equipment.
Based on the analysis of the US data, it can be concluded that the displacement of the brain
tissue substitute during pressure application can be visualised. The eﬀect of smaller ICP changes
occurring after the maximum ICP peak is not distinct.
This research demonstrates that the creation of a simple physical head phantom model en-
ables investigations of the ICP. The ICP was measured at both parenchymal and ventricular
depths and was clearly inﬂuenced by the construction of the pressure generators. It can be con-
cluded that elevation inﬂuence the measured ICP, while the results related to the temperature
and height limits the generalisability of the results. Despite this, it is suggested to conduct
future experiments at 21°C since the smallest mean ICP spread was seen here. Furthermore,
experiments at diﬀerent heights should be obtained where the whole model is ﬁxated such that
for instance rotation or movement of the construction is impossible. Generally, the physical
head phantom model lack robustness and complexity before further conclusions about the ICP
can be drawn.
To better understand the implications of the results, the future focus should be on developing
a pressure generator in which the same force is applied instead on being manually applied by
hand. The skull should likewise be remodelled and reprinted, in which it is recommended to
enable an upright positioning of the physical head phantom. Further details and other recom-
mended improvements are presented in Future Work (Chapter 7).

7.1
Anatomical Modiﬁcations
The current physical head phantom model consists of the skull comprised of one agar-based
compartment mimicking the brain. As shortly outlined in the Background (Chapter 2) the
complex brain consists of multiple anatomical compartments such as the spinal cord, ventricles,
meninges, eyes, arterial tree, and so forth. The incorporation of these and more would possibly
entail a more realistic head phantom in which testing of the pathological models could be
improved.
All need to be non-toxic.
Examples on how to mimic some of the anatomical
compartments and the value of these improvements are listed below:
 The spinal cord is connected to the skull at the foramen magnum. Both the brain and the
spinal cord are surrounded by the meninges and furthermore, the ventricles are continuous
with the central canal of the spinal cord [4].
As the intracranial pressure (ICP) rises
within the skull, the pressure in the spinal cord is likewise aﬀected. Here, both pressure
changes in the vascular system and pulsatile movements of the dura mater, the spinal
cord, and the CSF are present, and may all inﬂuence the ICP [32]. The incorporation of
the spinal cord would render investigations of these phenomena possible. Spine phantoms
have been constructed in various ways for use in medical imaging. As an example, by
3D-printing a spine having a spinal cord made of a rubber-like material, the intervertebral
discs of platinum-core silicone gel, the intervertebral disc of polymide, and the soft tissue
of gelatine [34]. For simplicity, it is suggested to start with a tube having the same length
as a real spinal cord.
Additionally, Andresen et al. (2014) [26] showed that the highest median ICP was obtained
in the lateral recumbent lumbar puncture position.
This seems to be caused by the
compression of the spinal cord [32]. For the physical model to take this into account it
should be ﬂexible and compressible when bent. This incorporation would improve the
comparisons with literature.
 The ventricles are ﬁlled with cerebrospinal ﬂuid (CSF) and are divided into the lateral (lo-
cated in each hemisphere), the third ventricle (located in center of diencephalon), and the

of the ventricles would enable a distinction between ICP measurements at parenchymal

for instance a raised ICP is elucidated. This requires a remodelling of the skull such that it is
airtight.

balloon used as the blood-supplying part. A simple arterial tree could be constructed using
various blood-supplying balloons, tubes, or similar expandable/compressible materials in var-
ious shapes and sizes. For instance, this could be mimicked using a glove whereupon smaller
tubes are connected at the ﬁngertips to inﬂict higher resistance in the small arteries. The use of
other balloons that can withstand a higher pressure should be considered. Further, the utilised
balloons also tended to expand outside the head phantom instead of only being expanded inside
the skull. This should be avoided in the future model.
Furthermore, one might measure the ICP using multiple identical pressure sensors. This way a
fault in one sensor is eliminated.

7.2.2
Skull
An improved 3D-modelled and printed version of the skull is needed. The construction of it
varies depending on the used pressure generator. However, generally, the following should be
considered:
I The current skull was tripartite to ease the 3D-printing as the printer caused problems.
The new skull should be printed as one compartment to eliminate openings. Furthermore,
large openings are present between the incorporated ﬂat bottom-plate (at which the US
transducer is placed upon) and the rest of the skull. A remodelling should ensure that the
bottom-plate is placed such that no holes/openings are present between it and the rest of
the skull. Additionally, the nose should be sealed, as the brain tissue substitute and the

the leakage.
II The skull is recommended to be 3D-printed in ABS material such as the Z-ULTRAT
material.
III The US transducer holder should be rotated 90°. This way, it does not cover the foramen
magnum and the US images are not in conﬂict with the eye area. As a result, the spine
can be incorporated. Additionally, the rotation of the transducer will enable more room
such that the hole for the pressure generator at the lower occipital bone can be moved
closer to the foramen magnum.
Additional, depending on the pressure generator, the following changes should be made:
IV If the eyes are not desired to be incorporated, the eyes should be completely blinded
already in 3DSlicer software, such that it does not contain any gaps.
V If a pressure generator were to keep the baseline ICP at a certain level, the bottom-plate (at
which the US transducer holder is ﬁxated) could be modelled as a turn-and-lock. For this
to function, the bottom-plate compartment (that would be 3D-printed separately) should
be tight with the remaining skull. The turn-and-lock solution leads to the following:
i Enablement of implementations of various bottom-plate compartments, which would
reduce the 3D-printing (as the skull could be reused). Examples of diﬀerent bottom-
plate compartments could be with/ without spine or diﬀerent constructions depend-
ing on the design of the blood-supplying balloon(s). It is of importance to integrate a
part where the brain tissue substitute can be poured into the skull. Furthermore, in
the current, only one model could be tested at a time. Having diﬀerent bottom-plates,
each with diﬀerent complexity, would make it easier to apply and omit modiﬁcations
such that tests on old versions are possible.
ii The hole at the lower occipital bone in the current 3D-printed skull was made at a
time where no tripartite was planned and where a meninges substitute was intended
to be included. The size of the hole, therefore, ﬁts with a small hand. This hole
rendered the upright position of the skull impossible. If the hole was ceiled, the
remodelled bottom-plate would enable the physical head phantom to be placed in
the upright position. Here, the gravitation force will no longer drive the brain tissue
down and out of the skull.
A custom hole for the pressure generator should be
made on the new bottom-plate. Furthermore, it is important to incorporate a strong
clamp/holder for the stand. The current 3D-modelled hollow cylinder functioning as
a stand holder cannot handle the weight of the phantom.
7.2.3
Experiments
Tests of the inﬂuence of the elevation and height on the ICP need custom stands. The stands
need to ensure that the physical head phantom is ﬁxated and that the experiments can be
repeated at the exact position with no rotation involved.
The stand needed for the height
experiments needs to move the whole setup to diﬀerent altitudes without the model being
rotated and without the equipment (e.g. the pressure sensor) being moved.
In relation to postural eﬀects, Qvarlander et al. (2013) [38] and Petersen et al. (2016) [23]
agreed that venous pressure aﬀects ICP. At high tilt angles (i.e. upright position) the jugular
veins collapse leading to a separation of the hydrostatic pressure system such that only the
change in hydrostatic pressure from the location of the collapse inﬂuences the ICP. As a result,
