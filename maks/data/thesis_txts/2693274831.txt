Abstract
Wind turbine blades are constructed with an array of different ﬁbre reinforced poly-
mers, including multi-axial laminate skins, sandwich structures, and a unidirectional
spar cap. As such, these structures contain many interfaces where material defects and
inhomogeneities often reside. These defects can form during manufacturing, and can
lead to rapid fracture of the structure. When considering interfaces, adhesive joints
or sandwich structures, critical failure modes are delamination and debonding. This
report details the fracture analysis of a bimaterial three-point bend test specimen. The
specimen analysed is a cross-section of a wind turbine blade, in which a pre-infused
spar cap is joined either side to a polymeric foam sandwich structure. A ﬁnite ele-
ment model has been applied in conjunction with a linear elastic fracture mechanics
(LEFM) approach to determine the energy release rates and mode mixity of a number
of pre-cracked specimen conditions.

Introduction
Many modern structures are formed by layered materials, including thin ﬁlms on sub-
strates, adhesive joints, as well as laminated composites and sandwich structures. In-
terfaces, residual stresses, thermal cycles and elastic discontinuities all play an impor-
tant role in inﬂuencing the lifespan of these structures, and the manner in which they
fail under given loading conditions. Indeed, face/core debonding and skin delamina-
tion are critical failure modes of sandwich structures, in which interfaces commonly
act as nucleation points for material or manufacturing-induced defects to propagate.
In liquid resin processing, defects can form if there is insufﬁcient wetting of the mat-
ing surfaces of the spar cap and core interface. These defects are often unavoidable
and it is common practice for manufacturers to design structures with the presump-
tion that defects are present. It is therefore important to develop and apply models
to understand these failure mechanisms and predict the conditions for which this oc-
curs. Delamination is often analysed within the framework of linear elastic fracture
mechanics (LEFM) of interface cracks lying between two elastic bodies. When con-
sidering crack plane problems, there are three conventional modes of crack tip stress
ﬁelds. Mode I refers to crack faces opening in the normal direction to the crack plane,
while mode II refers to sliding or tangential face opening displacements. Mode III
refers to out-of-plane tearing, which will not be considered in this study.
One of the most common parameters used to characterise fracture behaviour is the
stress intensity factor K which can be described for each mode of crack tip stress ﬁeld
as KI, KII, and KIII. It is a measure of the effect of the singular stress ﬁeld at the crack
tip on the stress state of the rest of the specimen. Another parameter commonly em-
ployed is the energy release rate G, which expresses the energy released per unit area
at fracture as a crack propagates. A similar parameter to the energy release rate is the
J-integral, which is a path independent contour integral. When considering elasticity,
it can be considered analogous G. For cracks under mixed mode conditions, the mode
mixity ψ describes degree of the mode of loading at the crack tip between modes 1 and
2. It measures the proportion shear to normal stresses within the K-dominated region
of a crack tip.
It is well described in literature that cracks in isotropic elastic solid tend to propa-
gate straight ahead under mode I conditions. This condition is applicable for fatigue
crack growth, corrosion cracking and monotonic crack advance [16]. Cracks at an inter-
face between two dissimilar elastic isotropic materials experience mixed mode loading,
where a combination of normal and shear stresses develop at the crack tip. It has been
shown experimentally that the mode mixity inﬂuences the fracture behaviour [24]. The
stress ﬁeld at an interface crack between two dissimilar isotropic elastic solids, is in the
form of an oscillating singularity as shown by Williams (1959) [36]. This ﬁeld can be
expressed by a complex stress intensity factor K = K1 + iK2 [36, 25, 26]. Where K1
and iK2 are the real and imaginary components of the complex stress intensity factor.
Interface cracks inherently experience mixed mode loading due to the asymmetry in
their elastic moduli [16]. Unlike cracks in homogeneous isotropic materials, interface
cracks cannot be separated into pure modes.
This report will review some of the important aspects of mechanics of interface cracks
relevant to the procedure used in this study, and will present a method for constructing

Literature Review
3.1
Development of an LEFM approach to characterise interface cracks
This section will review some of the most important concepts in LEFM-based char-
acterisation of mixed mode fracture behaviour and interface cracks. Some relevant
specimens and material fracture data are also brieﬂy discussed.
Some of the earliest work in describing the stress ﬁeld of interface cracks is based
upon solutions to the oscillatory singular stress ﬁeld ﬁrst described by Williams. The
Williams ﬁeld initially drew critique within the discipline due to the ﬁeld predicting
interpenetration of the crack faces for some instances. Nevertheless, solutions for spe-
ciﬁc interface crack problems with more rigidly deﬁned geometry and loading, have
been presented by Cherepanov (1962) [3], Erdogan (1965) [10] and England (1965) [8].
The oscillating nature of the normal and shear stress ﬁelds as the singular crack tip is
approached is shown in ﬁg. 8 of Erdogan (1965) [10] Rice and Sih (1965) [25] described
solutions applicable to both a semi-inﬁnite crack subject to point loads, and a ﬁnite
crack in an inﬁnite plate. Rice’s description is comparative to a full expansion of the
Williams ﬁeld in which the near tip stress ﬁeld is derived by analytic functions in terms
of stress and displacement ﬁelds [26]. Rice details how the complex stress intensity fac-
tor has real and imaginary components that are intrinsically inseparable. Hutchinson
& Suo (1992) [16] showed that for materials with very little elastic mismatch (ε ≈0), K1
and K2, can be considered to serve the same function as their counterparts for LEFM of
homogeneous cracked bodies. In this case, K1 is represented by the amplitude of the
singular normal stresses, and K2 is governed by shear stresses.
Another interpretation of the non-linear behaviour and interpenetrating surfaces pre-
dicted by the Williams ﬁeld is given by Comninou (1977) [4]. Here, a solution is de-
scribed in which the oscillating ﬁelds predicted by Williams are eliminated by assum-
ing a very small zone of friction-less contact near the crack tip. Rice considered that
the contact area for which interpenetration was predicted occurred in a such a small
area within the K-ﬁeld, and gave an approximate estimation of it’s size. This condition
is termed small-scale yielding [26].
J.W Hutchinson and Z. Suo (1992) [16] have collated and developed upon much of the
literature above, presenting a more general approach to solving bimaterial interface
crack problems. Their solution can be applied to specimens with more complicated
geometry and loading conditions, thus offering an ideal framework for the analysis
in this report. Interface cracks of bi-materials with both similar and dissimilar elastic
properties have been dealt with, as well as anisotropic materials, where more detailed
solutions are found in the work of Wang & Suo [35] The fracture criterion of mixed
mode crack propagation has been described as well as the case of crack kinking. Using
the Dundurs’ parameters [7] for elastic mismatch at interfaces, solutions which revert
to the standard conventions for notation of cracks in homogeneous isotropic materials
have been developed for materials with minor and signiﬁcant mismatch. The interface
toughness for nonzero values of β has been considered, with the possibility of con-
tact at the crack faces near the K-dominated annulus/ﬁeld. Using a similar approach
and notation to Rice (1988) [26], they deﬁne the mode mixity by suggesting a refer-
ence length corresponding to a distance r from the crack tip within the K-ﬁeld at the
interface.

3.2
Test specimens and Sandwich Structures
One of the ﬁrst and most simple fracture test sandwich specimens used to characterise
mixed mode fracture is the Brazil nut sandwich (BNS) test specimen. Constructed by
cutting a Brazil nut in half, inserting a notch or crack of known length a, and gluing
the two halves together with an adhesive. By attaching the specimen to a test jig which
allows for the compression angle to be changed, the degree of loading from pure mode
I to pure mode II can be varied. This specimen was used by Wang Suo to obtain
fractured data of a plexiglass/epoxy interface, and is capable of measurements along
most range of mode mixities [35] Other such specimens found in the literature are the
single edge-notch beam (SENB), three-point bend test and centre notch ﬂexure (CNF)
specimen and the end notched ﬂexure (ENF) specimen, and the DCB.
Various modiﬁcations have been applied to DCBs, with a useful conﬁguration de-
scribed in Sørensen et al. (2006)[32] for which the energy release rate can be deter-
mined across the full range of mode mixities. This specimen known as the DCB-UBM
(uneven bending moments) yields steady state crack growth under displacement con-
trol and can be controlled by altering the ratio of the bending moments. By applying
uneven bending moments to the DCB, the degree of mode mixty can be altered.
3.3
Material fracture data
Fracture data of various polymeric foams has been examined by Marsavina and Linu
[22]. Analytical and numerical micro-mechanical models have been compared with
experimental evaluations. Here measured values of fracture toughness are in the range
of 240 - 380 J/m2 for PES, while the measured value for PVC was 1530 J/m2. The
fracture toughness of PET reported by Jishi et al (2016) [18] in Skin-Core is 675 J/m2. A
value of mode I toughness of CFRP was found to be 1040 J/m2 [23] These values can
be used in assigning a failure criterion which can assess the tendency of crack advance
through the interface versus crack kinking into the adjoining materials.

7.0.1
Comparison of VCCT and Contour Integral Method
From the validation study in section 5, both VCCT and the contour integral method
give a measurement of the energy release rate that is fairly consistent with the analyti-
cal solution for a bimaterial DCB specimen. This suggests that both methods should be
valid for determining the energy release rate of an mixed mode interface crack. When
choosing a method, an engineer must factor in the computational time and thus re-
sources required for these calculations. The processing time is critical and so a method
with a lower computational time has an advantage. Comparing both methods, the
Contour integral method has a quicker processing time than the VCCT method, which
makes this approach more favourable. The VCCT method also requires a very ﬁne
mesh size at the crack tip, which further increases processing time. Moreover, con-
vergence problems were encountered when modelling two cracks using the VCCT
method, which ultimately was it’s limiting factor. Both methods use a different calcu-
lations from the raw output data to determine the energy release rate and mode mixity
of each crack. For the Contour integral method, the complex units of the complex stress
intensity factor have the unfortunate consequence that the values do not scale easily
with units complex units of N/m2.m−iε. Therefore it is highly recommended that a rig-
orous SI unit convention is used when deciding upon a consistent units when building
the model in abaqus. This model uses dimensions in metres and modulus in Pascals,
rather than millimetres and mega-Pascals. This is particularly consequential for the
calculation of mode mixity that depends on terms of involving the natural logarithm
of a length scale. This is something that does not impact the results from the VCCT
method.
From ﬁgure 52, it is clear that the VCCT method and contour integral method do not
yield consistent results for energy release rate. From ﬁgure 53, this is also the case
with mode mixity. Finer meshes should be applied to the VCCT method for a better
comparison.

Conclusions
A three-point bend specimen consisting of a pre-infused composite spar and a sand-
wich structure has been modelled and analysed within the framework of linear elastic
fracture mechanics. The contour integral method has been used and compared with
the virtual crack closure technique. Although both methods give quite accurate results
