ABSTRACT
In this thesis, an energy analysis of a Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) system is performed
with the aim of determining how the energy is being used during a cleaning procedure
performed by the system.
Moreover, Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and
optimization potentials of the system will be deﬁned. The system to be studied is
located in a milk process plant of a Danish dairy company.
Measured data of the system is used as an input to develop the energy and mass
balances for two lines of the CIP unit analyzed in order to identify and quantify the
sources and sinks of mass and thermal energy. The energy balance yielded a total of
68,96 MWh of steam consumed during a period of 50 days, from which 76% of them
are dedicated to heat up the base solution used to clean the equipment. Besides, a
total amount of 20.19 MWh have been drained between the two lines, from which
99% are related to the draining of the water. These results correspond to two of the
six lines that compose the CIP unit.
A 1D thermo-ﬂuid model of the system is developed to recreate the evolution of
the ﬂuid’s temperature and compute the heat that has been consumed during the
cleaning process of diﬀerent equipment. The results obtained from the model’s
simulations were validated with the measured data to corroborate that the model is
able to estimate the heat consumed by a cleaning procedure with a maximum error
of 13.78%.
Diﬀerent types of KPIs have been deﬁned to represent the performance of the CIP
system. From them, it has been calculated that the CIP unit consumes 12% of the
natural gas consumed by the plant, 21% of the water consumed by the plant and 1.3%
of the electricity consumption of the plant. Besides, a benchmark of cleaning times
for diﬀerent equipment is reported for further studies or performance comparison.
Optimization opportunities of the system were analyzed with the developed model in
order to asses the most eﬀective way to reduce the energy consumption of the system.
The potential solutions were the reduction of the cleaning times of the equipment,
the reduction of the cleaning temperature targets and the use of surplus heat or heat
recovery to preheat the water phases of the cleaning procedures.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
The actual world population of 7.6 billion people is expected to grow to 9.8 billion
by 2050 [1]. This will represent an increment in the demand for basic services and
needs like water supply, health care, food, etc. Though these multiple demands
can be fulﬁlled, all the methods or solutions depend mainly on one single driven
factor, energy. According to Smalley [2], energy demand would be the ﬁrst problem
of humanity in the upcoming years. As a matter of fact, some studies reveal that
energy demand will increase one-third of the current consumption by 2040, with the
industrial sector accounting for half of this increment [3].
Among the many human activities, the use of energy represents the largest source
of greenhouse gases, from which CO2 emissions account for the largest share [4].
Greenhouse emissions are the main contributor to climate change which has been
the main Earth’s issue in the last decades. An agreement was reached during the
COP21, where 195 countries agreed to reduce the CO2 emissions. In the same way,
some countries like Denmark or UK have established their own goals to reduce CO2
emissions. One way to achieve this reduction is supplying the energy demand in a
less carbon intensive manner, which can be achieved in 3 diﬀerent ways: a change to
renewable energy, use of carbon capture and storage technologies or improving the
energy eﬃciency. According to Oluleye et al. [5], the last option has been identiﬁed
as the most cost-eﬀective in a short-medium term.
In Denmark, industry and agriculture account for more than 20% of the energy
consumed [6]. From this, the dairy industry is a major sector and represents one of
the most energy-intensive industries within the food sector. However, it accounts
for more than 20% of the agricultural exports with a total of 4.7 billion kg of raw
milk that are processed, and revenues of 1.8 billion Euros annually. The Danish
dairy industry is composed mainly of Arla foods, which covers more than 90% of the
production [7]. Besides, is the fourth largest dairy company in the world in terms of
milk intake and processing [8]. Therefore, the dairy industry is not only important
for Danish economy, but it is also an industry that needs to reduce its environmental
impact improving eﬃciency in their processes.
Processing milk at a dairy site consists of diﬀerent thermal steps like pasteurization,
sterilization, concentration, and drying. Being the last two the most energy-intensive
processes. Besides the milk processing, the equipment, needs to be regularly cleaned
to guarantee a hygienic production. As explained by Bylund [9], the cleaning process

is complex because the plant operators must deal with milk remaining in pipelines,
heat-precipitated milk constituents on heat exchangers, and other residues like fat
or hard water salts. Old or small dairy facilities use manual cleaning procedures
where production is stopped and the equipment needs to be disassembled to be
cleaned. This procedure turns into a reduction in production time and proﬁt for the
company as well as a risk for the operators that are exposed to harmful chemicals.
Nonetheless, modern dairy facilities use an automated cleaning procedure, called
Cleaning-in-Place (CIP).
The CIP systems allowed dairy facilities to clean process equipment cost eﬀectively
without the need for any dis-assembly and re-assembly. Therefore, they provide
fast, productive, consistent and reproducible cleaning of all the equipment in contact
with the product [10]. However, one of its main disadvantages is that these types of
systems and cleaning routines are built speciﬁcally for each facility. Hence, detailed
information on the energy use of the CIP procedures is often not available. The
project aims to ﬁll this knowledge gap and create methods to determine energy use in
CIP system based on available information and also to deﬁne some Key Performance
Indicators (KPIs) for this type of systems.
1.2
CIP System description
In principle, the cleaning procedure performed by a CIP system works by circulating
chemical solutions at temperatures from 65 °C to 80 °C and rinsing or disinfection
water through the production equipment like pipes, valves, heat exchangers, etc.
Moreover, in case of tanks, Tamime [11] explains that the solutions should be sprayed
or jetted directly to the product contact surfaces to create conditions of increased
turbulence and high ﬂow velocity.
The main functions of the cleaning solutions throughout the cleaning procedure
can be summarized as follows: First, the displacement of organic and inorganic
soils from the equipment using chemical reactions and mechanical processes. Then,
the dispersion of the soils into the cleaning medium. Finally, prevention of soil
re-deposition on the equipment surface.
The CIP systems make use of two types of chemicals for the solutions, which
are detergents and disinfectants. The detergents available in the market can be
divided into Alkaline, Neutral or Acid. Nonetheless, each industry should select the
detergent that will provide the best results for the type of soil that they are treating.
Furthermore, disinfectants are also important to reduce microorganisms that might
be present in the equipment after the cleaning process.
Depending on the industry and the manufacturer, there are many types of CIP
systems as shown by Moerman et al. [10]. However, they can be classiﬁed into three
main categories. The ﬁrst category considers the heating method used to increase the
temperature of the cleaning solutions. In this category there are two types which are
2

the most common among diﬀerent industries: the direct heating of the storage tanks
and the heating in line systems. The approach of the direct heating of storage tank
is to keep the tanks at a temperature suitable for cleaning by means of a heating
device into the tank. Also, a re-circulation system connected to a heat exchanger
can be used. On the other hand, the heating in line conﬁguration increases the
temperature of the solutions by means of a heat exchanger located on the supply
line to the equipment. The heating process is only performed when the solutions are
going to be used for cleaning purposes.
The second category considers the way that the cleaning solutions are used. In the
single-use type, the solutions are sent to the equipment to perform the cleaning
procedure and then they are directly drained. In contrast, in the re-use type, the
solutions go back to the system. However, in the return line the solutions are
examined to check its level of cleanliness. If they are within the acceptable range
they are sent to the storage tanks, otherwise, they are sent to drain. The third
category is based on the distribution of the system into the facilities. If there is only
one single system to clean all the equipment in the facilities the system is known as
centralized. If the system has been divided into units to clean diﬀerent equipment of
the plant is known as decentralized.
For the development of the current project, the CIP system to be analyzed is located
in one of the Arla’s pasteurization plants in Denmark. In this case, it is a heating in
line, decentralized and re-use CIP system. This project will be focused on analyzing
the unit number 3 that consists of 7 bulk tanks as can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1. The
tank 2100 contains the water for the ﬂushing and rinse processes. The tanks 2200,
2300 and 2500 contain the chemical aqueous solutions used in the cleaning process
whose speciﬁcations are presented in table 1.1. The tanks 2600 and 2700 work as
collectors of the ﬂushing processes for milk and fermented products, respectively.

The tanks previously mentioned are connected to six process lines. Each line is in
charge of transporting the ﬂuids from the tanks to the diﬀerent equipment of the
facilities in order to clean them. Depending on the line, the current CIP unit has 5
to 27 equipment that can be cleaned per line. The solutions coming from the tanks
need to be heated to a certain cleaning temperature. To achieve this, each line has a
shell and tube heat exchanger where the ﬂuids are heated with steam coming from
a boiler at 6.5 - 7 bar. At the end of the heat exchange process, the steam leaves
the heat exchanger as condensate water at 100 °C. The CIP system has numerous
sensors to register and control the cleaning processes performed. On the supply side,
each line has a ﬂow meter before the heat exchanger and a temperature and pressure
sensor after the heat exchanger. On the return side, each line has a ﬂow meter,
temperature, pH, turbidity and concentration sensors. Both supply and return line
with its respective components are presented in ﬁgure 1.2.
A standard and most common cleaning cycle performed by the analyzed unit can be
summarized in the following steps.
1. Initial purge using water from tank 2100 to recover milk or fermented product
into tanks 2700 and 2600 respectively.
2. First rinse using water of tank 2500, the water in the tank comes from the
recovered ﬁnal ﬂush of previous CIP cycle and is used to remove gross soil.
3. Caustic wash using solution from tank 2400.
4. Intermediate rinse to clean caustic from the system with water of tank 2100.
5. Disinfection or sterilization solution from tank 2200.
6. Final rinse with water from tank 2100, the water from this rinse will be
recovered to perform initial rinse of the next process.
7. Drying or ﬂushing process-using air.

1.3
Energy consumption of a CIP system
In the previous section, the diﬀerent types of CIP systems were brieﬂy introduced
altogether with a description of the system that is going to be used in the development
of the project. In practice, the fact that there is not a standard way to build these
systems represents a challenge for the manufacturers and consumers when estimating
the energy consumption. Therefore, for most of the users, it is not possible to
calculate how much energy the system is consuming until is fully functional. Some
studies have reported the amount of energy demand by the CIP systems compared to
the total energy demand of the dairy facilities in order to provide a guide for future
dairy projects. In case of milk process plants, the amount of energy consumed has
been estimated in 9%. However, in cheese or butter plants this value can increase up
to 19 to 26 respectively [12]. Other authors have estimated this consumption based
on the energy cost of the system, providing values around 14% for milk production
plants [13].
The previous facts represent a guideline, but the only information exposed is the
energy demand of the system without any knowledge of how the system is using
this energy and how eﬀective or eﬃcient it is. Throughout the literature available
on CIP design and constraints, the energy consumption of this type of systems is
divided into electricity and thermal consumption as shown by Sillett [14]. Electricity
is easy to calculate, being the circulation pumps the main consumers. In case of
the thermal consumption, authors like [15] have clearly stated that the thermal
energy consumption of the system is the energy used to bring the solutions to
the desired cleaning temperature. However, this concept has been misunderstood
by manufacturers and developers of energy management software as the energy
dissipated by the equipment that the system is cleaning.
In terms of CIP optimization literature, authors like Jude and Lemaire [16] only
focused their attention on the optimization of the cleaning procedures by means of
new devices and advanced control strategies of the system. Whereas, the energy
optimization perspective is barely explained with basic concepts like increasing
the temperature of the solutions or the cleaning time will increase the energy
consumption. Furthermore, some studies have proved that at temperatures above
75°C, the eﬀectiveness of the cleaning procedure does not increase and actually
working at higher temperatures is not suitable for the surface of the equipment as

appropriate energy analysis of the system it is not possible to quantify how beneﬁcial
this integration could be for the system and the plant.
1.3.1
Thesis statement
From the previous sections, it can be established that CIP systems are essential for
the proper operation of dairy facilities but they are a "black box" in terms of energy
consumption. To address this lack of knowledge, the aim of this thesis is to develop
a complete energy study of a CIP system. From the results of this analysis, the
following questions are sought to be answered:
• How the energy is being used during a cleaning procedure performed by a CIP
system? Which one should be the right approach to perform an energy balance
of this type of systems?
• It is possible to predict the energy that is going to be consumed by the CIP to
perform a cleaning procedure of an speciﬁc equipment?
• Which parameters can be used to evaluate the performance of a CIP system?
• How can be the consumption of thermal energy of the CIP reduced?
To answer the fore-mentioned questions, an energy mapping of the real system will
be performed in order to disclose the diﬀerent stages of thermal consumption during
a cleaning routine. To perform this analysis, two temperature transmitters before
the heat exchanger of two diﬀerent lines were installed as can be seen in ﬁgure 1.2
enclosed by a red dashed circle. With the addition of these sensors, the heat being
added to the solutions can be properly computed. The sensors are located in line 4,
which is the line with more elements connected to the unit for cleaning operations,
and line 1, which is the line in charge to clean the milk coolers (which are a special
element of the facilities). Thus, more data can be gathered to have a better energy
mapping of the CIP System in a shorter time.
Aside from the energy mapping, a thermo-ﬂuid model of the system will be developed.
The model will be validated with the information gathered from the system in order

DATA ANALYSIS OF THE SYSTEM
The aim of this chapter is to show the path that goes from the individual cleanings
data, which is recorder and saved by the software 3D TRASAR, to the mass and
energy balances of the CIP. These balances have been done for lines 1 and 4 of
the CIP unit analyzed, as those lines are the ones that had the new temperature
transmitters installed, which were needed to complete the balances. The ﬁnal goal of
these balances is to identify and quantify the sources and sinks of mass and thermal
energy of the mentioned lines. Particularly, and with regard to the energy balance,
it was of interest to analyze the approach that the software 3D TRASAR employs
to estimate the thermal energy consumption and compare it with a newly proposed
method.
2.1
Introduction
The main tool that translates the data from the valves and measuring equipment
that intervene during the cleaning process is the software 3D TRASAR. The data
used in the PLC control system is sent to 3D TRASAR and an excel ﬁle containing
it is generated. For those variables with a continuous physical meaning, such as ﬂow
and temperature, 3D TRASAR stores the time at which the variable is measured
and its value. The time interval within measurements is not constant, being the
smallest resolution of 1 second. By contrast, as the position of the valves can be
represented by a discrete variable (1 for open state, 0 for closed), 3D TRASAR stores
the value at the initial position when the cleaning started and each time there is a
switch on its state. altogether with the time at which this switch happened.
Table 2.1 gathers the direct measurements that 3D TRASAR stores. Some of these
measurements are treated in order to calculate the steam heat consumption and the
volume sent of each solution. The so-called code stands for the numerical ID that
identiﬁes the measurement tool that measures its respective variable on ﬁgures 1.1
and 1.2.
Figure 2.1 shows a graphical example of the wash data stored by 3D TRASAR in
the fore-mentioned excel ﬁles. The upper part of the ﬁgure, where the horizontal
straight lines are, is where the data regarding the valves is presented, whereas the
rest of the space is used to plot the set of continuous variables: temperatures, ﬂows,
pressure, etc.

Even though other variables measured at the return line such as pH, turbidity,
conductivity, etc. play an important role on the control of the return valves of each
solution’s tanks, they have not been used on the mass and energy balances. Besides,
the data from the ﬂow meter placed at the return line has not been used on the
balances due to the poor reliability of its readings, mainly caused by the air bubbles
created by the air trapped in the object.
With respect to the valve type variables, the following have been used during the
data analysis and are involved on the mass and energy balances of the system:
• The valve that represents the forward pump state (code 2810). When its value
is 1 means that the forward pump is active and pumping the solution the
cleaning routine dictates.
• The forward valve of each of the solutions (code 2X00.01). When its value is 1
means that its respective solution is being pumped forward from its tank and
to the equipment, in accordance to the cleaning routine.
• The return valve of each of the solutions (code 2X00.11). When its value is 1
means that its respective solution has arrived to the return line and thus is
redirected to its respective storage tank.
Finally, the fore-mentioned selection of information has been extracted from the
excel ﬁles by using a Matlab code that created a data base for the wash with all
these variables, from which all the calculations involved in the mass and energy
balances have been done. The outcomes of these balances are the volumes sent/back
of each solution and the values for the heat terms that are detailed later.

2.2
Method
With the data from the variables described on the Introduction of this chapter, ﬁrst,
a mass balance has been carried out. It serves to estimate the mass sent and the
mass back for each of the solutions involved on each wash. With this estimation, the
energy balance can be completed, and the sources/sinks of thermal energy can be
quantiﬁed. In consequence, this section is dedicated to explain the methods employed
to perform the mass balance ﬁrst, followed by the description of the methods used
for the energy balance.
2.2.1
Mass balance
Figure 2.2 contains a simple schematic of the base solution tank. Each time an
object is cleaned, a certain amount this solution is sent to the object and a certain
amount is received back, which can diﬀer from each other. The diﬀerence between
the amount sent and the amount back of all the washes gives an estimation of the
amount of solution drained, which is equal to the amount that needs to be re-ﬁlled
in order to maintain the system stable.
Figure 2.2: Wash mass balance for the base solution
As all the solutions are composed by water in its majority, it has been assumed that
the solution’s density is the density of the water at normal conditions (997 kg/m3),
and that it remains constant within the temperature’s range at which the system
operates, thus allowing the use of volume when referring to mass.
2.2.1.1
Volume sent
Figure 2.3 shows the evolution of the forward ﬂow rate for the wash example presented
in 2.1, along with the evolution of the forward valve position of the base solution
(coded 2400.01 in ﬁgure 1.1).

It must be remarked that when the base forward valve closes, the fresh water tank
forward valve opens, even though is not represented on the ﬁgure, as it is the next
phase of the recipe. From this data, the volume of base solution sent has been
calculated as follows:
1. For each of the intervals where the forward valve is active, the time-weighted-
average value of the forward ﬂow rate is calculated.
2. The duration on active state for each of the intervals is obtained.
3. The volume sent on each of the intervals is calculated by multiplying its
respective values for the ﬂow rate and the duration.
4. The volume sent on the speciﬁc wash is obtained by the sum of the volume
sent on each interval.
This procedure is applied to calculate the volume sent of each of the solutions, on
each wash. Once all the washes for the corresponding lines have been analyzed, the
total volume sent of each solution for each line is calculated by doing its summation.

2.2.2
Energy Balance
With the data from the variables presented on the Introduction section of this chapter
and once the mass balance of the wash has been performed, the energy balance of
the individual washes can be carried out, and then, by summation of the results, the
energy balance for the line is completed. Figure 2.5 presents a simple sketch of the
CIP unit in which the points where heat is introduced and lost have been depicted.
The following are the terms identiﬁed:
• Steam heat input (Qin): this term represents the heat that is added to the
solution through the steam heat exchanger.
• Heat lost to the equipment (Qlost,equipment): this term represents the heat that
the solution losses to heat up the equipment that is cleaning, including the
losses to the environment.
• Heat lost to the tanks (Qlost,tanks): this term represents the heat that the tanks
loose to keep their respective temperature.
• Heat drained (Qdrain): this term represents the heat that is wasted through
the drain.

2.2.2.2
Heat to lost to the equipment
This term of the heat balance seeks to provide information regarding the heat that
the solution transfers to the equipment that is cleaning and the inherent losses to
the environment that occur during the cleaning. For example, the base solution is
sent at a high temperature to the equipment that initially was at a cold temperature.
Thus, the volume sent during the forward base phase is in charge of increasing the
equipment’s temperature, whilst it also losses heat to the environment due to its
temperature diﬀerence. Therefore, in order to evaluate this term it is assumed that
the whole volume sent ﬂows through the equipment and arrives to the return point
where the temperature transmitter measures its temperature, which means that
there are no leaks or other kind of losses along its path. The temperature at which
the solution arrives is then calculated as the time-weighted-average value of the
return temperature measurements when the return phase of the respective solution is
active. Figure 2.7 shows the time frame where the return phase of the phase solution

2.4
Discussion
The discussion of the results is structured in a such way that the wash example
results are treated ﬁrst followed by the discussion of the line results.
2.4.1
Wash example
From the mass balance of the wash one can observe that it is very unlikely that
the volume sent matches exactly the volume back. This was expected, and can be
explained by the fact that on the return there are several transmitters measuring
special parameters such as pH, conductivity, turbidity etc.
which are used to
distinguish between solutions and thus open the adequate tank’s return valve. When
a volume that is mixed arrives and can not be classiﬁed in any of the solutions it
is usually drained. The solution that presents the highest ratio of draining is the
fresh water (27% in this wash). The fresh water is usually pumped in between two
warm and chemically active phases (between the base and the disinfection solutions
in this case), and when its received back may present high temperature and diﬀerent
values of the fore-mentioned variables that do not allow its classiﬁcation as pure
water. Hence the reason why the fresh water is the solution with the largest ratio
of draining, whilst the drained proportion for the rest of the solutions is relatively
low (around 1-2%). Besides, it is important to remark that the fresh water does
not return to its original tank, but returns to the pre-rinse water tank (the recycled
water tank). The volume sent of pre-rinse water during the ﬁrst part of the cleaning
is drained in its totality.
The term from the energy balance that arises more attention is the steam heat input,
since it is the one for which there are two estimation methods. Focusing on the base
solution, 3D TRASAR yields a value for the steam consumption larger than twice
the value estimated by the newly proposed method. Since the volume sent and the
forward temperature are used in both methods, one can state that the diﬀerence

comes from the second temperature used to calculate the temperature diﬀerence. 3D
TRASAR uses the average value of the return temperature when the forward phase
of the base solution is active, which means that the cold ﬂuid that is ﬂowing out
of the system during the ﬁrst seconds of the phase is being measured on the return
and thus lowering the average value. This can be observed on the ﬁrst and second
intervals of table 2.5, but also if the value for the return temperature of the third
interval is compared to the return temperature of the base solution measured when
its return valve is active presented on table 2.8. Besides, one can ﬁnd surprising
the fact that 3D TRASAR yields a negative value for the steam consumption of the
disinfection phase, whose magnitude is signiﬁcant. Again, this can be explained by
the fact that during the ﬁrst seconds of the disinfection’s forward phase the solution
that is ﬂowing out of the system is the base solution, which is at a high temperature,
thus contributing to increase the average value of the return when the disinfection’s
forward phase is active up to a value that exceeds the average temperature at which
is sent and, causing the 3D TRASAR steam consumption estimation to be negative.
Since the purpose of this term is to quantify the thermal energy added to the solution,
by means of the steam in order to increase its temperature from a certain one up
to a target temperature, the utilization of the return temperature to estimate it is
believed to lead to misleading conclusions.
When analyzing the methodology and the results of the newly proposed method
presented in table 2.3, it is clear that it overcomes the problem described above
as it uses the supply temperature directly. Therefore, the newly proposed method
estimation captures the physical meaning of the steam heat input term in a
straightforward way. The results for the base and disinfection solution are found to
be reasonable and in accordance with the expectations. However, the curious result
of this method is that the wash consumption also includes the steam heat input
consumption estimated for the fresh water, even though steam is not directly applied
within this phase. Moreover, the magnitude of the estimated value is signiﬁcant,
being in this case way larger than the steam heat input from the disinfection phase.
This can be explained by the fact that the fresh water is pumped after the base
and disinfection phases, thus ﬂowing through the hot steam heat exchanger and
increasing its temperature considerably (from 19,85 °C to 27,87 °, on average). Even
though steam is not added actively while the fresh water is being pumped, it is the
steam that leaves the heat exchanger at a high temperature and, as a consequence,
increases the fresh water temperature. Hence the reason why this term has been
included when calculating the steam consumption of the wash.
With regard to the heat lost to the equipment term, its value can be estimated as
negative, as it is the case of the disinfection solution shown in table 2.8. From a
physical point of view this means that the solution has returned at higher temperature
than the one at which it was sent, thus absorbing some heat from the equipment,
which was still at a high temperature from the previous hot phase (the base phase
in this case). One would expect the same from the fresh water as it is pumped

2.4.2
Line results
Once the analysis of a single wash is done, the understanding of the line mass and
energy balances becomes easier. From the mass balance results of ﬁgures 2.9 and
2.10 one can point to several important facts. The ﬁrst one is that it is clear that the
solution most frequently pumped is the base solution, followed by the disinfection.
Secondly, for all the solutions, the volume that returns back is less than the volume
sent, meaning that an amount of the solutions is drained and must be reﬁlled in
order to keep the system stable. The solution that is most commonly drained is the
fresh water, as it was expected. Also, one can notice that the volume that should
be re-ﬁlled of rinse water is negative, in both lines. This can be explained by the
following fact: the fresh water does not return to the fresh water tank, but to the
recycled water tank. For example, in line 1 494 m3 of fresh water were sent to
clean the equipment whilst 294 m3 were received back in the recycled water tank.
However, only 122 m3 of rinse-water have been sent, which means that 172 m3 of the
fresh water received had to be ﬂushed out of the tank as it was full. This fact was
witnessed during the visits to the dairy site, as in several occasions when the fresh
water was returning to the recycled water tank the system had to ﬂush water out of
it in order to not overload the tank. Finally, the magnitude of all the values for line
4 is considerably larger compared to line 1, on the grounds that line 4 accounts for
the cleaning of 26 objects whereas line 1 is in charge of 11 objects only.
With respect to the energy balances, the ﬁrst point to be addressed is the diﬀerence
between the 3D TRASAR and the newly proposed methods for estimating the steam
heat input term of the lines. When analyzing the steam consumption for each
of the solutions, one can see from table 2.11 that the base phase consumption is
about 3 times larger for the 3D TRASAR method than for the newly proposed one,
whereas the values for the acid and disinfection phases estimated by 3D TRASAR
are negative. These negative values balance the oﬀset between the 3D TRASAR
method and the newly proposed when doing the total estimation. This oﬀset ends up
being of 25% for line 1 and 32% for line 4. Furthermore, focusing on the results for
the steam heat input estimation using the newly proposed method one can observe
that the values estimated for the fresh water are of a considerable magnitude, even
comparable to the values of the disinfection solution (sterilization water). As stated
on the wash example subsection of the discussion, these values have been included
when computing the steam consumption of the lines due to the same reasons as the
exposed previously. The base phase accounts for the 76% of the heat input estimated
for the period of analysis, followed by the fresh water (13%) and the disinfection
solution (10%).

2.5
Conclusion
From the above discussion of the results presented in this chapter, the following
conclusions regarding the mass and energy balances and its procedures have been
reached.
First, and referring to the mass balance’s results and procedures, it is certain that
the strategy followed by 3D TRASAR, and used in the mass balance conducted
throughout this project, to estimate the volume sent of each solution is correct and
yields reasonable results. However, when it comes to the estimation of the volume
that returns back to the tank, the lack of a trustworthy ﬂow rate measurement on the
return line forces the use the ﬂow rate measured on the forward line on its estimation.
Besides, the interruptions on the forward pumping cause a decrease on the ﬂow rate
at the return. Thus, in order to decrease the forward ﬂow rate so the return ﬂow
32
==PAGE:48==

rate becomes more realistic, a factor that takes into account the intermittent pattern
of the forward pumping is proposed. The results that have been obtained with it
have been assessed as reasonable, even though a comparison with real data from the
solution’s consumption would have been beneﬁcial in order to conﬁrm the results.
With regard to the energy balance, the ﬁrst conclusion that has been reached is
that the steam consumption estimation done by 3D TRASAR is not appropriate if
considering the physical implications of adding steam in the heat exchanger. In order
to capture this implication, the new method used throughout this project proposes to
use the supply and forward temperatures, which are the temperatures measured right
before and right after the heat exchangers, thus reﬂecting in its formula the purpose
of adding steam to the heat exchanger. The usage of the return temperature that
3D TRASAR does would be be meaningful if the term to be estimated is the heat
lost to the equipment. However, in order to do it properly, the return temperature
used should be the one measured when the return valve of the respective solution is
active and the solution returns to its tank. This approach is the one followed when
calculating the heat lost to the equipment term of the energy balance shown in the
methodology section.
From the above paragraph one can state that the estimations for the steam heat
input term and the heat lost to the equipment can be trusted in the sense that
all the variables that intervene on its estimation come from reliable measurements.
When it comes to the heat lost to the tank term, it has been seen that it depends on
the estimation of the volume that returns back to the tank, a value whose reliability
is not 100% due to the facts detailed previously. Besides, using the global average
temperature of the tank can yield to individual wash balances whose results are a
not plausible, as seen on the discussion of the individual wash results, but that are
reasonable when analyzing the line as a whole.
Finally, the fact described on the above paragraph inﬂuences the estimation done
for the heat drained, as it is calculated by completing the energy balance shown
in the methodology. However, it has been concluded that the values estimated are
reasonable and that are a good approximation of the heat drained in reality.

MODEL OF THE CIP SYSTEM
This chapter describes the background and development of the 1D thermo-ﬂuid model
of the CIP system that is used to recreate the evolution of the ﬂuid’s temperature
during the cleaning process.
3.1
Introduction
In the warm phases of the cleaning process, the CIP system needs to heat up the
equipment until a desired temperature before start registering the conﬁgured cleaning
time. The only way for the system to asses that the equipment has reach the required
temperature is to measure the temperature of the ﬂow once is back to the system. In
order to estimate the heat consumption of the CIP system to perform the cleaning
process of the equipment, a thermo-ﬂuid model that resembles this operation is
developed.
The aim of the model is to calculate the temporal evolution of the ﬂuid’s temperature
while is going through the system until reaches the outlet where, in practice, the
temperature could be measured. The model accounts for the fact that diﬀerent
solutions can be found at the same time inside the equipment. This occurs since, in
most of the cases, the volume of ﬂuid that the equipment can hold is larger than the
volume sent of a single solution. Hence, during some periods of time, the presence of
two warm cleaning solutions with a cold phase of water in between is common.
A schematic of a pipe, which is the simplest equipment cleaned by the CIP, is
presented in ﬁgure 3.1 in order to explain variables used as inputs in the model.
First, the most representative geometrical variables of the equipment like the length
L, diameter D and thickness th are needed. Then, the ﬂow conditions required to
clean the equipment like pressure P and ﬂow rate ˙V (velocity Uf and mass ﬂow
˙m could be used instead of ﬂow-rate). Next, the type of recipe, which is going to
deﬁne the cleaning times for the diﬀerent cleaning solutions, is required by the model.
Finally, the working temperatures of the system that can be divided into: the target
temperature (Ttarget), which is the desired temperature to sent the solutions from
the CIP to the equipment; the acceptable return temperatures at which the cleaning
process of each warm phase starts being considered as valid (Tacceptable); and the
supply temperature of the ﬂuid coming from the tank to the steam heat exchanger
(Tsupply).

3.2
Method
3.2.1
The one-dimensional pipe model
In order to start the model of the CIP system, the simplest geometry, which in this
case is the pipe, is used as a starting point. The model is composed by 3 actors: the
ﬂuid, the pipe and the ambient. Since there is only one main ﬂow direction, it is
assumed that the ﬂow can be considered one-dimensional. Besides, it is considered
that the ﬂow is steady and that there is no mixing between the solutions inside the
pipe. The governing equations that ensure conservation of energy are solved using
the ﬁnite diﬀerence method. Thus, as can be seen in ﬁgure 3.2, the pipe is discretized
into a number control points surrounded by ﬁnite volume cells, and then the ﬁnite
diﬀerence scheme is applied to the governing equations.

3.2.4
Steam heat exchanger model
This type of heat exchanger is a shell and tube condenser that works with steam as
a heating medium. All the geometry and basic speciﬁcations of the heat exchanger
are summarized in appendix D. In order to model this equipment, is important to
deﬁned some boundaries and assumptions. First the model is composed by the ﬂuid
through the tube bundle, the pipes, and the steam. Since the outer surface of the
shell is isolated, no heat exchange between the shell and the ambient is considered.
The velocity of the steam that is coming to the heat exchanger (≈20 m/s) is much
larger than the average velocity of the ﬂuids going through the heat exchanger tubes
(≈1.5 m/s). Besides, taking into account the length of the heat exchanger, it can be
assumed that by the time the ﬂuid is entering to the heat exchanger, the steam has
already covered all the shell side as represented in ﬁgure 3.9. Hence, the ﬂuid in the
pipes will exchange heat directly with the steam since the initial time step.
Since only condensation of the steam is assumed to take place in the heat exchanger,
the temperature of the steam through the shell is assumed to be constant due to
only change in phase occurs. On the pipe side, due to the thickness of the pipe is
very small, the heat stored in the walls would be negligible and therefore only direct
heat transfer between the ﬂuid and the steam is considered. Moreover, uniform
distribution of the ﬂuid going into the pipes is assumed and only one main ﬂow
direction. With these appropriate constraints deﬁned, the governing equations to
ensure energy conservation are solved using the ﬁnite diﬀerence method as in the
previous models, and the discretized model can be seen in ﬁgure 3.10.

3.2.7
Control Algorithm
As stated on the previous sections, the model needs to know which phase of the
cleaning process is active, since for each phase one target temperature is deﬁned
on the speciﬁcations for each recipe routine. Besides, if the aim is to simulate the
whole routine, the model must be able to recognize when one phase is completed
and it is time to start the next one. In order to meet these requirements, a control
algorithm has been developed and its simpliﬁed ﬂow chart is shown in ﬁgure 3.12.
It must be mentioned that this ﬂow diagram summarizes the control process of a
warm phase, which means that the analyzed phase corresponds to a solution sent at
a high temperature and that has certain requirements on the return temperature to
consider the time of cleaning as valid.
The algorithm uses the input data that follows:
• t0: corresponds to the time when the phase started.
• T_target_phase: corresponds to the target temperature of the solution that is
being pumped in the present phase.
• dt: corresponds to the time-step used throughout the model’s simulation.
• transportation_time: corresponds to the time that takes to the ﬂuid to travel
from the forward point to the return point. As the length of the individual
objects that are coupled to form the model of the equipment are known, as well
as the velocity at which the solution ﬂows through them, the transportation
times of the individual objects can be estimated and by summing these so can
be the transportation time of the equipment.
• T_acceptable: corresponds to the acceptable return temperature at which the
cleaning time of the phase starts being considered as valid.
• Target_cleaning_time: corresponds to the time set by the recipes for the phase
(e.g the base phase target cleaning time in a standard recipe 1 is 480s).
• T_supply: corresponds to the temperature at which the solution of the studied
phase is sent (its tank temperature).

Discussion
The discussion of the results is structured in a such way that the veriﬁcation results
of the individual models are treated ﬁrst followed by the discussion of the validation
of the complete coupled models with the control algorithm implemented.
3.4.1
Veriﬁcation
First, the discussion of the results obtained from the veriﬁcation of the pipe model
will be presented. After that, the results obtained from the simulations of the
diﬀerent equipment will be discussed.
3.4.1.1
One dimensional pipe model
As can be seen in the ﬁgure 3.13, all the alternatives yield to same steady state
temperature. However, slightly diﬀerent behaviour is observed on the transient zone.
The curve resulting from the Fluent simulations seems to be in better agreement
with the element method at the low Courant number.
Whereas, the response
obtained with the developed model and the one obtained using the element method
at C = 1 are basically identical, presenting in both cases a sharp increase at the
outlet temperature when the transportation time (tt) is reached. On the other hand,
[28] explains that the shape of the curve corresponding the element method is due to
numerical artiﬁcial diﬀusion, particularly on the increase on the temperature before
the transportation time has been reached, produced by the fact of using a small
∆t (and a small Courant number, consequently). The Courant number set for the
Fluent simulation was less than 1, thus the numerical artiﬁcial diﬀusion eﬀect is also
present in its solution.

==PAGE:89==
3. Model of the CIP System
The results for the second case are shown in ﬁgure 3.14. In this ﬁgure the eﬀect
of increasing the length of the pipe is clearly depicted in both methods. Being
the transportation time of 100 s (L = 200 m and Uf = 2 m/s), the developed
model shows that exactly at that time the ﬁrst increase in temperature is obtained.
However, due to the larger losses throughout the 200 m pipe and the increased
thermal inertia of the longer pipe, the temperature is much lower compared to the
temperature reached after the transportation time of the ﬁrst case. Finally, one can
notice the eﬀects of the numerical diﬀusion that goes along with the element method
at low Courant numbers (C = 0.1 in this case) on the temperature increase before
the transportation time has been reached.
From the previous simulations, it can be deduced that the developed model is able
to predict the temperature evolution of the ﬂuid going through a pipe. This also
implies that the approach used to discretize the geometry and the equations of energy
conservation can be further applied and developed in the modeling process of the
remaining equipment.
3.4.1.2
Equipment models
The result of the shell and tube heat exchanger simulation presented in ﬁgure 3.15
yielded what was expected. In this case, it is clear that by the time the ﬂuid reaches
the outlet of the heat exchanger its temperature will have been increased up to the
desired value. The abrupt temperature increment without no appreciable dynamic
behaviour on the ﬂuid is due to the assumption of the steam initial conditions. As
explained in the methodology, the required mass ﬂow of steam needed to increase
the ﬂuids temperature to the desired value is assumed to be occupying all the
shell side by the time the ﬂuid starts ﬂowing. Therefore, thermal steady state
conditions are imposed since the beginning of the simulation on the steam side, being
the transportation time of the ﬂuid the cause of delaying the abrupt temperature
increment. In practice, the heat exchanger has a control ﬂow system that regulates
the mass ﬂow of steam through the heat exchanger. This mechanically controlled
system only regulates the ﬂow once it measures the temperature at the outlet. As
can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2, which is the wash example used for the data analysis, there
is an abrupt increment in the inlet temperature until is regulated and stabilized
to a steady state condition. Even tough this does not happen in all the washes as
there is a conﬁgured initial mass ﬂow for each equipment, it can be assumed that
the conditions imposed for the heat exchanger are still a fair representation of the
real process.
In case of the comparison between the pipe model with the plate heat exchanger
and the tank models, ﬁgure 3.16 shows the results of the three models normalized
by its respective transportation time. The green line which represents the inlet
temperature remains constant since it is the temperature of the ﬂuid coming from
the already explain shell and tube heat exchanger. In case of the pipe, the behaviour
is as expected, once the ﬂuid reaches the outlet at tt = 1, there is a sharp increase in
74
==PAGE:90==

temperature and, after a few seconds, a complete steady state condition is reached.
For the plate heat exchanger, it can be seen that the increment in the outlet
temperature is not as sharp as in the pipe at tt = 1. This is mainly due to the fact
that the solution that is going through the plate heat exchanger needs to heat up
the water trapped in the plates. Since the heat capacity of the water trapped in
the channels is higher than the heat capacity (thermal inertia) of the pipe material,
the ﬂuid through the heat exchanger looses more energy in the heating process of
the equipment before it reaches a steady state condition. Even though the result
was expected, the fact that the convective heat transfer coeﬃcient of the cleaning
solution’s side and the one of the trapped water’s side are similar in magnitude leads
to believe that there is a misalignment between them. Since the correlations used for
the ﬂow going through the plates were developed and tested for this type of geometry,
the value of this convective heat transfer coeﬃcient is assumed to be appropriate.
In case of the heat transfer coeﬃcient for the trapped water, it is believed that the
correlation tends to over-predict the coeﬃcient since it is very similar in quantity to
the value obtained for the ﬂow going through the heat exchanger, which in practice
should be higher since is forced convection. When other correlations proposed by
Martynenko and Khramtsov [25] and NKhalifa [30] were tested, values obtained for
the coeﬃcients were more or less the same or even higher. However, this correlations
are for vertical rectangular enclosures that is still not a condition that represents
exactly the plate geometry of the current study. Hence, further improvements in the
model could take place if more accurate correlations are developed.
For the tank simulation also presented in ﬁgure 3.16, the ﬂuid going out of the tank
does not present a sharp increment in temperature as in the pipe, or a relatively
quick reach of the steady state condition if it is compared with the plate heat
exchanger. Actually, even that at 1 tt the outlet temperature of the ﬂuid going
out of the tank and the ﬂuid going out of the plate heat exchanger have a similar
value, in the tank model, the ﬂuid needs an equivalent time of 26 tt to reach suitable
cleaning temperature of 70◦and several tt′s to reach a steady state condition. In
practice, the result could be expected since the tank is bigger in size than the plate
heat exchanger or the pipe. However, if the heat capacities of the wall and the air
considered in the tank model are compared with the heat capacity of the water
trapped in the plate heat exchanger, they are around the same order of magnitude,
thus a behaviour similar to the plate heat exchanger should be expected. The only
quantity in the model that could aﬀect the temperature evolution in this way is the
heat transfer coeﬃcient for the falling ﬁlm. Since there is not literature available
regarding discrepancies between correlations for falling ﬁlms speciﬁcally applied for a
circular geometry of the size of the tank, it is not possible to quantify how misaligned
is the calculated value. However, it is believed that the value has been over predicted
so, in the model, more heat is transferred to the walls of the tank. Therefore, the
time needed for the ﬂuid to start increasing the temperature of the tank and to
reach a steady state condition in the outlet is much longer than for the other two
cases. Other correlations proposed by Am. [31] and Padmanaban [32] were tested in

results was achieved.
3.4.2
Validation
First, the simulation of the cleaning procedure for the pipe R_Ring1 with a recipe
R_SO_1 (Appendix C) is presented in ﬁgure 3.17 to be analyzed. The procedure
starts with a rinse cycle of fresh-water at a temperature of ≈14◦C and after that
a second rinse with recycled water at a temperature ≈21◦C. In the return line,
the initial temperature of 5 ◦C will remain almost constant and no considerable
increment on the return temperature before 1 tt caused by the initial rinse cycles
with fresh water and recycled water will be appreciated, as expected. After 1 tt the
return temperature starts increasing, but at a temperature of ≈20◦C will reach a
steady state condition that will remain for a few seconds.
In the forward line, after those rinse cycles, the base solution starts being pumped
to the system at 0.4tt but no inﬂuence in the return temperature is captured until
the respective transportation time has passed. As expected from the previous model
simulations, once the base arrives to the outlet of the pipe the temperature sharply
increases until a steady state condition is reached. Even though there is a transient
increment in the return temperature, this happens in a period of time relatively
shorter than the duration of the whole base phase and therefore it looks like a
straight vertical line. However, when a detailed measurement is performed, it takes
40 seconds for the basic solution to reach the temperature of 70 ◦C in the return,
which for the base phase is the temperature needed to start considering the cleaning
process as valid. When the previous condition is fulﬁlled, the counter starts and the
base will still be pumped other 480 seconds. In practice the total time that the base
will have been sent to the system is equal to 480 s + tt + heating time. Once the
counter has reached the required time, the pumping of basic solution will stop and
then the 60 seconds of fresh water will be sent to the system. As in the previous
phases, the water will not be reﬂected in the return temperature until the tt has
passed. It can be seen that the temperature of the water in the return goes down
until almost the same temperature that was sent to the system, meaning that the
the cycle of water is able to cool down the pipe until the water temperature for the
period of time that was inside the tank.
After the 60 s of water are ﬁnished, the disinfection solution starts being pumped
into the system. At this point is where two diﬀerent solutions (base and disinfection)
and the fresh water, are ﬂowing in the same pipe but properly distributed in the
longitudinal direction since the ﬂow is assumed to be one dimensional and no mixture
is considered. Once the base solution and water left the pipe and considering that
the water cycle was long enough to cool down the pipe, the disinfection solution will
start being noticed in the return when its respective tt has been accomplished and
the solution starts heating up the pipe again. At the temperature of 25 ◦C , the

in the estimation of the average forward temperature will be analyzed. As can be
seen in tables 3.12, 3.13 and 3.14 for the pipe, pipe with plate heat exchanger and
pipe with tank respectively. There is not a considerable error between the estimation
of the model and the real values of the washes for any of the equipment. Besides,
from all the errors presented, it is clear that even the error is small in value, it is
always positive. This means that the estimation of the temperature is above the real
value, thus the heat consumption also should be always above the real value which
is not reﬂected in the error of the heat consumption.
The error for the times consumed in each phase of each recipe are presented in
tables 3.15, 3.16 and 3.17 for the pipe, pipe with plate heat exchanger and pipe
with tank respectively. In contrast with the errors presented for the average forward
temperature, these errors are in agreement with the errors of the heat consumption
in terms of magnitude and also in tendency to predict either below or above the real
value. Therefore, it can be assumed that the main source of error within the models
is the time that the simulations estimate for each cleaning phase compared with the
real time of the washes. A more in deep analysis of the source for this mismatch in
time turns out in the following reasons: ﬁrst, the pipe geometry introduced in the
models. In case of the pipes, since they have an intricate path along the facilities,
their length was estimated considering the time diﬀerence between the time in which
the supply valve opens and the time in which the return valve opens. From this time
diﬀerence, which in practice is the transportation time, and considering the ﬂow-rate
used to clean the pipe and a standard pipe diameter of 3 inches, the length was
calculated. In the real life, this pipe diameter is not always constant since along the
facilities the pipes can have multiple diameters that will change the speed along the
cleaning process. The same approach was used to calculate the length of the piping
coupled to the plate heat exchangers and tanks, but the time that takes to the ﬂuid
to ﬂow through the heat exchangers/tank is subtracted from the value obtained from
valve’s timing. Then, a standard pipe diameter of 3 inches was assumed to calculate
the pipe length, which in practice is not always full ﬁlled since the connections of
the pipe to the equipment have a lot of variations in geometry. The second reason
is the already discussed variation in the time needed for the return temperature to
reach the cleaning temperature in the plate heat exchangers and pipes. From the
simulations, it was demonstrated that the models under estimate this time, thus
the cleaning procedures are faster and less energy is consumed. Finally, in case
82

of the tanks, an extra source of error is the assumption of the ﬂow velocity in the
return. As explained in the methodology, the velocity in the return pipe of the tank
is assumed to be the same as in the supply. In the real system, there is a pump in
the outlet of the tank that is in charge to pump the solutions from the tank to the
system. The working conditions of this pump are not similar to the supply pump
and no information regarding the ﬂow-rate or pressure of it were known by the plant
owner. Furthermore, as already introduced in the second chapter, the measurement
of the return ﬂow is not accurate, thus, it was not possible to estimate the ﬂow-rate
of the ﬂuid that is coming back. In practice this will also introduce an error in the
time of the real recipe versus the time the model estimates.
3.5
Conclusion
From the above discussion of the results presented in this chapter, the following
conclusions regarding the procedure to develop the thermo-ﬂuid models of the CIP
system have been reached.
First, regarding the procedure to develop the model. The ﬁnite diﬀerence method
used to solve the equations that deﬁne each system or equipment analyzed, yield
acceptable results in terms of predicting the evolution of the ﬂuid temperature
through the equipment. Even some discrepancies were encountered in the dynamic
behaviour of the ﬂuids until reach a steady state condition, it was clear that the
assumptions and correlations used to calculate some of the heat transfer coeﬃcients
were the source of these discrepancies.
As a matter of fact, in the plate heat
exchangers, there is not literature available to account for the coeﬃcient when the
equipment is partially loaded with water. The same applies for the falling ﬁlm of
the tanks, the literature available for falling ﬁlms in circular geometries is limited to
small geometries, and the correlations proposed for geometries of the same magnitude
as the tank are modiﬁcations of falling ﬁlm in vertical plates. Moreover, in the
numerical implementation, the assumption that the ﬁrst volume cell in the initial
time step i = 1 is already occupied by the ﬂuid, also accounts for an error in the ﬁnal
results. However, the error can be minimized if a small ∆t is used for the simulations
Regarding the complete models of the equipment, the simulations yield acceptable
results in the prediction of the temperature evolution though the coupled equipment.
Besides, the developed control loop was accurate enough to change and maintain
the phase of the cleaning procedures during the simulations. Moreover, the model in
conjunction with the algorithm was capable to manage multiple solutions phases
though the same equipment at diﬀerent positions. However, the assumption that no
mixing between ﬂuids occurs within the equipment cleaning procedure will introduce
error in the calculation of the temperature evolution since in real life there will be
a mixing between the warm phases and the water phases that will decrease the
temperature of the warm phases within the mixing zone. Hence, if a more accurate
result of the simulation is desired, the mathematical formulation of the models should
83
==PAGE:99==
3. Model of the CIP System
be able to account for this mixing. Moreover, if more accurate information of the
return ﬂow of the tanks could be gathered, the coupled models and the algorithm
would need to be improved to properly account for the eﬀects of the change in
velocity in the return pipe.
Finally, the models are able to calculate the heat consumed in each phase as well as
the total heat consumed in a cleaning procedure. However, the reported error of the
models should be considered when further analysis or decisions are developed form
these results. Nonetheless, the models are able to properly capture the tendency
of the equipment’s cleaning procedure. Thus, they can also be used to study the
variations in time, temperature and heat consumption if diﬀerent conﬁgurations of
the cleaning recipes want to be tested.

KEY PERFORMANCE INDICATORS
4.1
Introduction
Within the industry sector, the companies need to measure and estimate how good is
the outcome of their processes considering some initial inputs. From these procedure,
some indicators that can be either quantitative or qualitative can be obtained. If
this concept is focused to the dairy industry companies, which is where the current
system is being used, their main indicators would be how much ﬁnal product can be
produced considering the initial takeover, how good is the quality of the product,
how many resources are needed to produce the product, etc. This set of measurable
values that can be compared against each other will deﬁne the Key Performance
Indicators (KPIs) of the company and its facilities.
Companies with large facilities normally deﬁne multiple levels to evaluate their KPIs.
The high-level ones are focused on the overall performance of the facility, which in
case of dairies could be related with the amount of energy, water or other utilities
used to process the milk. Normally, this KPIs are well know for the companies
since production and delivery of milk is well monitored [12] and all the utility’s bills
are registered [33]. Therefore, allows the facility managers to know in general how
eﬀective is their plant is and to spot the areas with improvement potential. On the
other hand, the low-level KPIs are focused on processes within diﬀerent stages of
the production which depending on the systems, it is diﬃcult to deﬁne which KPIs
should be tracked to enable the improvement of their performance. Moreover, for
some of the processes, exists an impossibility to collect suﬃcient data to accurately
measure the proposed KPIs.
Throughout the literature, some methods to deﬁne KPIs for diﬀerent equipment
and processes have been developed by diﬀerent authors. For instance, some basic
energy cost oriented KPIs presented by [33] are relevant if a simple breakdown of
the high-level energy KPI is performed. Moreover, authors like [34] have developed
more complex approaches to account for diﬀerent energy states to produce energy
eﬃciency KPIs in manufacturing companies. However, for the development of the
current KPIs, publications like [35] or [36] which are more relevant within the dairy
industry are used as an inspiration for the development of this chapter. Hence, the
following section will explain the KPIs that have been proposed and that intend to
represent the performance of the CIP system and the approach used to develop them.
All of the KPIs rely on data regarding consumption and production parameters that
have been provided by Arla Food and that correspond to the Slagelse factory site.

4.2
Method
Though many methods can be used as a starting point to develop the KPIs of the CIP
system, it is important to deﬁne some constraints in order to avoid overproducing
vague indicators and more importantly, to maintain the indicators within the scope
of the project. Since the project developed so far is mainly oriented to perform the
energy analysis of the system, the indicators related with the quality of the cleaning
process, safety, control performance, maintenance, etc. will not be considered. In
case of the quality of cleaning, it is a biological condition that is not analyzed in
the current project. The rest of the fore-mentioned indicators, even that the can
inﬂuence some how the energy consumption, they would require extra measurements
of values that are out of scope. Furthermore, the system will be considered as one
entity that consumes a certain amount of resource to perform its intended operation.
Therefore, no breakdown analysis of its individual components e.g. pumps or air
fans to purge the lines, etc. will take place for the indicators.
The development of the following KPIs is mainly based in the approach introduced
by [37], which is a general guideline to proposed KPIs to track diﬀerent types of
performance. Besides, to corroborate the previous approach, the indicators proposed
by [36], [35] and [12] will also be considered as a reference. Therefore, considering
the fore-mentioned constraints and references, the energy KPIs will be divided into
thermal energy consumption and electricity consumption. For the raw materials
KPIs, since there is not an accurate measurement of how much chemicals are used in
the system, only water is considered. Finally, the time that takes for the system to
clean the equipment can be quantiﬁed into a performance indicator. The development
of the introduced KPIs will be explain in this section.

Time KPI
The performance KPI presented in this project proceeds from a well-known and more
complex KPI within the operation KPIs category, which is the Overall Equipment
Eﬀectiveness (OEE). In general this KPI measures performance against some arbitrary
benchmarks like availability, the performance and the quality of the system. The
90

4. Key Performance Indicators
availability of the system could be an ambiguous and overproduced indicator since
depends in the operation time over an speciﬁc period, schedules, uptime of schedule
time, etc. In practice a CIP system is intended to work on demand after the diﬀerent
processes in the facilities are ﬁnished, which means that it always needs to be
available and more importantly to operate even at schedules out of the plant shifts.
On the other hand, it was already stated that the quality of cleaning is not within
the scope of the project. Therefore, from the OEE, the indicator that it would be
useful to determine the functionality of the system is the performance indicator. In
general, the performance indicator compares the actual or current time that it takes
for a process to perform its intended operation versus a standard period that the
process should last. In order to avoid confusion between terminologies, this indicator
is going to be named as Time KPI.
To develop this KPI, the time that the system has spent performing each cleaning
cycle of each equipment considering the diﬀerent types of recipes is known from the
data analyzed in Chapter 2. From these data, the average time that the system
should use to perform each cleaning cycle of each recipe can be calculated for each
equipment. Besides, in order to assess that this time presented as benchmark is
accurate enough to qualify future cleaning procedures, the standard deviation of
the data-set used to compute each average time was also calculated to check how
much the duration of the cleaning procedures tends to vary. For simplicity, only

4.4.0.1
Thermal KPIs
In case of the team based KPIs presented in table 4.1, the uniﬁcation represents
and eﬀective and realistic approach since in real life each tank is connected with
it respective cooler (plate heat exchanger), and even though they are cleaned per
separate, both need to be cleaned in order to use the tank. Some of the tanks with
lower capacities can be connected to the same cooler. Thus, they were not considered
in the development of the current KPIs since the connection between small tanks
with one single cooler is not a process that can be repeated in the same way in other
facilities. Thus, the KPIs of this conﬁgurations would not be applicable for other
studies or facilities.
From the results presented in table 4.1, it can be seen that the tanks used for the
development of the KPIs have similar number of washes and also a similar steam
consumption within the 50 days analyzed. Moreover, the KPIs obtained from the
yearly estimation of steam consumption and milk stored shows that on average the
cleaning procedure of a tank with a capacity of 50 square meters will consume around
2 kWh per ton of stored milk. It is important to remark that for this analysis, it
was assumed that the tanks go from full to empty state. In real facilities, the tanks
are not always ﬁlled until reach maximum capacity, actually the tanks are ﬁlled
depending on the type of milk or product that are intended to produce and the
amount of raw milk destined to that speciﬁc product. However, the tanks are going
to be cleaned in the same way no matter how much milk would have been stored.
With regard to the estimation of the heat input for those lines that do not have the
new temperature transmitters installed, which allows to perform the calculation of
the heat input using the newly proposed method, one must point that the approach
does not consider diﬀerences within recipes, as an unique value for the consumption
per wash is used. This is considered to be overcome by the fact that the vast majority
of the washes correspond to recipe 1. A more detailed estimation could be done
if bench-marking the type of equipment that the rest of the lines clean with the
ones from line 1 and 4. However, the estimation that has been obtained with the
current approach has yielded a value that has been assessed as reasonable and thus
used for the development of the KPIs. A yearly estimation of 1201 MWh for the
CIP has been obtained, which by using the boiler’s eﬃciency of the Slagelse factory
site implies a natural gas consumption of 1414 MWh. Compared to the natural gas
consumption of the plan, KPI1 indicates that 11.58% of it its being used on the CIP.
Besides, KPI2 yields a value of 6.28 kWh/ton (consumption of natural gas per ton of
milk treated), which lies within the recommendations of National Dairy Council of
Canada [36], thus proving that the estimation approach is valid enough.

5
OPTIMIZATION OF THE CIP SYSTEM
Throughout this chapter a set of optimization ideas that intend to improve the
eﬃciency of the CIP are discussed and evaluated in terms of thermal consumption
and, in some cases, of the total time that takes to complete the cleaning of the
equipment.
5.1
Introduction
As has been seen on the previous chapter, the CIP unit accounts for a signiﬁcant
amount of the thermal energy consumption of a dairy factory site, hence the reason
why attention should be put into improving its eﬃciency. The present chapter is
dedicated to the proposal of optimization ideas and its evaluation in terms of savings.
A brief description of the optimization ideas that arose throughout the development
of this project is the following:
• Cleaning time reduction: the aim of this optimization proposal is to reduce the
cleaning time set for the base, acid and disinfection phases in order to reduce
the steam consumption of the CIP.
• Target temperature reduction: this optimization proposal seeks to achieve
a reduction on the thermal energy consumption by means of decreasing the
target temperatures of the base and acid phases. Since the return temperature
is always larger than the minimum value required to ensure proper cleaning
in both base and acid phases, it is believed that a reduction on the target
temperature while maintaining the lower temperature limit to ensure proper
cleaning could result on a lower steam consumption. However, the option of
reducing this lower limit is also analyzed in this proposal.
• Increase the temperature of the initial and intermediate cold phases: in a
normal cleaning procedure, there are some initial and intermediate steps were
cold water (either fresh or recycled water) is sent to the system to remove traces
of milk or cleaning solutions. These solutions cool down the equipment, thus
increasing the thermal energy needed to heat up the equipment in the base,
acid or disinfection cleaning phases that follow this cold phase. A temperature
increase of this cold ﬂuids could be beneﬁcial for the system since less thermal
energy will be required to heat up the equipment during the consecutive warm
phase.

5. Optimization of the CIP System
5.2
Method
The optimization proposals presented in this section have been assessed by using the
models introduced on chapter 3. The validation procedure of this optimization ideas
has been applied to the two objects of each type among all the equipment analyzed
through the 3D TRASAR data analysis that present the highest thermal energy
consumption, which are the following:
• Pipes: R_Ring1 and R_Ring7.
• Pipes + Cooler: TR_Yogh and TR_SKM.
• Pipes + Tank: T401 and T521.
On Appendix A a description of the equipment altogether with its speciﬁcations can
be found.
5.2.1
Cleaning time reduction
In order to analyze this proposal, a reduction on the cleaning time of the warm
phases for each recipe has been applied. The reductions considered in this study
correspond to 5%, 10%, 15% and 20% of the original cleaning time set for the warm
phases (base, acid and disinfection phases). On the developed models, these new
times were used as an input to run a simulation of the cleaning routines for each
of the equipment previously mentioned. From the simulations, the new thermal
energy consumption of the system, with this new cleaning times, is computed. The
evaluation method for this proposal assumes that the temperature of the solution’s
tanks (the supply temperature) will be unaﬀected by the reduction of the cleaning
times.
5.2.2
Target temperature reduction
As a reduction of the target temperature also causes a reduction of the return
temperatures, which in consequence causes a reduction of the solution’s tank
temperature, an estimation of this new tank’s temperature has been done in order
to take this fact into consideration. The following approach has been applied.
1. With the data from 3D TRASAR the average tank temperature of the base
and acid solutions has been obtained. Also, the average return temperature at
which the base and acid solutions return to its respective tanks has also been
calculated. With these two, a temperature diﬀerence between the return and
tank’s temperature has been obtained. Finally, this temperature diﬀerence has
been assumed to be constant, thus allowing the estimation of the new tank’s
temperature once the new return temperature is known.
2. The model simulates the new cleaning routines with the reduced target
temperatures set as the forward temperatures and not including the tank
temperatures (supply) on the simulations.
The result of each of these
101
==PAGE:117==
5. Optimization of the CIP System
simulations is the return temperature proﬁle, from which the average return
temperature for the base and acid solutions is obtained. By applying the
temperature diﬀerence explained in the ﬁrst option, the new tank’s temperature
for the base and acid solutions is calculated.
3. With the new tank’s temperature and using the new target temperatures, the
model is run again and, as this time the supply temperature is available, the
steam consumption of each of the phases can be estimated. In order to check
the eﬀect of an overestimation of the new tank’s temperature, extra runs with
a reduction on the tank’s temperature estimation done in the second point
have been done.
Finally, two options regarding the constraints of the cleaning procedure were
considered in the analysis of this optimization proposal. The ﬁrst option is to
maintain the actual lower acceptable return temperature of 70°C, 50°C and 25°C
for base, acid and disinfection solutions, respectively. The so-called second option
relaxes this constraint by keeping the original oﬀset of 15°C with respect to the
target cleaning temperature.
5.2.3
Increase the cold phases forward temperature
In order to achieve this temperature increase, two options have been identiﬁed as
possible: the use of a surplus heat or waste heat source and using the thermal energy
available on the volume that is drained at a high temperature (referred as heat
recovery).
For the analyzed equipment, the ﬂow rate at which the cold ﬂuids are sent and the
amount of time that are being sent are known. Besides, from the energy and mass
balances presented on chapter 2, the average temperature of the water going to the
system is known: 20°C. Therefore, using these facts, the thermal energy needed to
heat up the water from this 20°C up to a desired temperature has been calculated
and used to evaluate the two options mentioned above.
Besides of analyzing if the temperature of the water can be increased until the desired
value, it is important to estimate the energy savings of this idea. With help of the
developed models, the cleaning routines were simulated introducing the new water
temperature that could be achieved. Therefore, a new thermal consumption for the
cleaning procedures can be calculated assuming that the water’s temperature could
be increased until that temperature. The results of this simulations are compared
with the reference case in which the water has an average temperature of 20 °C.
In this way, it is possible to quantify how beneﬁcial this proposal could be for the
current conﬁguration of the system in terms of energy savings. The new temperatures
tested for the cold phases are the following: 25 °C, 30 °C, 35 °C and 40 °C.

5.2.3.1
Use of surplus or waste heat
During normal operation of the plant, there are some sources of heat that are
produced as a byproduct of another processes that use energy. This heat, normally
considered as waste heat o surplus heat, is dissipated to the ambient while still has
some potential. The possibility to use this heat to increase the temperature of the
water exists, and has been considered.
As stated above, once the energy that is needed to increase the temperature of the
cold phases up to a new one and the beneﬁts in terms of thermal energy consumption
that this increase produces, one can compute the ratio between the beneﬁts and the
surplus/waste heat extra that was needed in order to evaluate this option.
5.2.3.2
Heat recovery
This option focuses on the fact that during the cleaning procedure, if the cleaning
solutions do not fulﬁll the desired speciﬁcations of concentration and turbidity on
the return side, they are drained. Since this solutions are still at high temperature
when they are drained, they could be used to pre-heat some of the cold ﬂuids (fresh
water or rinse water). In this way the heat that would be drained can be recovered
to keep providing energy to the system, avoiding its waste.
From the energy and mass balances presented in chapter 2, the number of solution
drains from each cleaning procedure in line 1 and 4 is known. More precisely, the
temperature and the volume drained are known. Thus, the heat that can be recovered
from these drains can be estimated. Assuming a constant heat exchanger geometry
for all the cases, the heat that is needed to heat up the water can be matched with
the heat that the drains can provide. In this way, it is possible to estimate how
many drains are available to heat up the water in the cleaning procedure of each
equipment until the desired temperature. The exact procedure followed to count the
number of available drains is the following:
1. From the data analysis presented on chapter 2, the number of drains, as well
as its temperature and volume, have been obtained.
2. As the heat transfer mode would be ﬂuid to ﬂuid, a plate heat exchanger
in counter current ﬂow has been assessed as the most convenient type. For
this type of heat exchangers, a value of 2000 W/m2K has been considered as
reasonable, as proposed by [22]. Then, an eﬀective heat transfer area of 15 m2
has been assumed as reasonable. Therefore, a UA value of 30 kW/K has been
used as a reference.
3. As the new temperature of the cold phase is known (Tnew), a ﬁrst ﬁlter that
discarded all the drains whose temperature is lower than Tnew + 10◦C has been
applied.
4. Then, equation (5.1) is used to calculate the outlet temperature once the
drained volume passes the heat exchange. A new ﬁlter in charge of discarding

5.4.1
Cleaning times reduction
As it was expected, the trend that can be observed from table 5.1 is that a reduction
of the cleaning times set for the warm phases yields a decrease on the thermal energy
consumption. The eﬀect per second reduced on each warm phase out stands more
for recipe 2, as it includes the three warm phases, being followed by recipe 1 (which
includes 2 two warm phases), and ﬁnalizing with recipe 3 (which only includes one
warm phase). Further studies regarding the correctness of the unaﬀected tank’s
temperature should be conducted, as the reduction of the cleaning times implies a
reduction of the volume that is heated and that returns to the tank. However, this
is believed to be compensated by the increase of the cleaning frequency that can be
achieved by reducing the washing times.

5.4.2
Target temperature reduction
Figures 5.3 and 5.4 show a clear trend for the return temperature and the phase
duration, which is repeated on the rest of the equipment and also for the acid phase, for
the respective proposed options. As it was expected, the return temperature decreases
if the target temperature is reduced. The diﬀerences appear when comparing the
phase duration between the two proposed options. Option 1, as it is the one whose
constrain on the acceptable return temperature is more restrictive, presents a clear
increasing trend on the phase duration when decreasing the target temperature;
whereas in option 2 the opposite is observed. As in option 1 the constraint on the
return temperature is kept as in the original case, a reduction of the solution’s target
temperature implies an increase on the time needed to reach the constraining return
temperature. On the other side, the relaxation on this constraint causes a reduction
also on the phase duration. This fact is also appreciable when analyzing the results
of the "Time KPI’s" presented in tables 5.2 and 5.3, since option 1 presents positive
KPI’s whereas option 2 negatives ones. One last comment to make about these
"Time KPI" is that they have been obtained by getting the slope of the linear ﬁt of
the set of points, even though the shape that the points seem to draw corresponds
more to an exponential one, in the case of option 1.
With regard to the Heat KPI’s, the trend is clear for both proposed options: the
thermal energy consumption is reduced when reducing the target temperature. Also,
the reduction on the base target temperature presents higher saving potentials than
the reduction on the acid target temperature. When comparing options, it is also
seen that option 2 presents better KPI´s, as it was expected due to the relaxation
on the return temperature constraint for counting the time as valid.
Finally, from ﬁgure 5.6 one can see the strong dependency between the thermal
energy consumption and the supply temperature, which was of course expected.
However, the interesting outcome of this graph is the following: if an horizontal line
is drawn from the point whose supply temperature is the furthest to the right on the
curve corresponding to the original target temperature of 85 °C, it would cross the
rest of the curves (which correspond to the rest of target temperatures tested). If
from these crossing points a vertical reading of the horizontal axis is done, one could
read the lowest supply temperature that could be obtained if savings with respect to
the original point are to be obtained.

Cold phases temperature increase
For a given equipment, its KPI_1 value varies within tables 5.4 and 5.5 as a
consequence of the recipe’s conﬁguration. More precisely, recipe 2 presents the
highest value for this KPI as this recipe is the one that includes the most intermediate
cold phases, followed by recipe 1 and ending up with recipe 3. When comparing
the values of KPI_2, one can see how recipe 2 presents the best potential savings
for the thermal energy consumption per °C increased, followed closely by recipe 3
and ending up with recipe 1. Thus, it can be stated that the major part of the
savings derive from the increase of the intermediate cold phase temperature that goes
before the acid and disinfection phases. If the values for the KPI_3 are contrasted
within tables, it can be seen that recipe 2 presents the largest reduction on the wash
duration, followed by recipe 1 and ending up with recipe 3. Finally, by comparing
the values of KPI_4, one can see how recipe 3 presents the best values for this KPI,
which means that it is for this recipe where the extra heat is added in a more useful
way.
With regard to the results presented on tables 5.7 and 5.8, which cover the heat
recovery possibility, the following has been concluded: even though it is very unlikely
that the same line could provide with a drain to the fresh water phase of a cleaning
routine that is taking place in the same line, there are other 4 other lines that could
(one of them with the same amount of equipment that the current two lines). Thus,
it is believed that with the draining of the lines it is possible to provide with enough
heat to increase the temperature of the freshwater and rinse water, if comparing the
amount of available drains and the water/rinse phases. More precisely, for the object
types pipe and pipe+tank, a ratio greater than 3 times more of drains available than
the number of cold phases sent has been estimated if the new temperature is set to
40 °C, whereas for the object types pipes+cooler this ratio could only be achieved if
the new temperature of the cold phases is set up to 35 °C (mainly due to the high
ﬂow rate used in the wash).
5.5
Conclusion
Once the big picture from the optimization proposals has been described on the
previous section, the following remarks have been concluded.
First, and with regard to the cleaning times reduction option, it must be mentioned
that this proposal’s feasibility relies on the possibility to include a device on the
return line that measures the cleanliness of the base/acid/disinfection solution once
it returns. This will allow to reduce the duration of the phases since, the way the
system is currently conﬁgured, the values set as target duration are over-sized on
purpose to ensure proper cleanliness within a considerable security margin. Thus,
the mainly costs related with this optimization proposal are those of the installation
of this measuring device and the setting up the new control system.
115
==PAGE:131==
5. Optimization of the CIP System
With respect to the reduction of the target temperature proposal, its implementation
only depends on the feasibility to reduce the target temperature while maintaining
the cleaning standards. The so-called option 1 of this proposal does not reduce the
return temperature acceptable limit for each solution. It is thus believed that if from
this temperature and upwards the cleaning is assessed as valid, the target temperature
can be reduced as long as the same return temperature can be achieved. This has
been proved to be possible and yields a reduction on the thermal energy consumption,
even though an increase of the wash duration has to be accepted on return. By
contrast, option 2 presents a decrease on both thermal energy consumption and wash
duration. However, in order to implement it further studies regarding the en-sureness
of the cleaning standards when reducing the acceptable return temperature should
be conducted.
Finally, with regard to the optimization proposal that focuses on increasing the
temperature of the cold phases, several diﬃculties make the implementation of this
proposal unpractical. The ﬁrst one refers to the implementation on an already built
CIP unit, as the case of the one analyzed. Usually the CIP units are built as compact
as possible, thus hindering the installation of the new heat exchangers that would
be needed (one per line). Besides, if the option of recovering the heat from the
drains is considered, it should also address the challenge of implementing a control
system (which includes a set of valves and temperature transmitters) that would
rule over the drain. Thus, for CIP units that are already build, it is not only the
cost associated with the equipment that this option requires, but the lack of physical
space to actually include the equipment that precludes the implementation of this
proposal. However, if a new CIP unit is to be designed, a cost analysis to decide
whether the investment on the extra equipment is worthy or not should be carried
out to help the decision making process. Further more, a positive outcome from this
proposal, and that could be included on the cost analysis, is that due to the decrease
of the temperature diﬀerence between the warm and cold solutions that ﬂow through
the cleaned equipment (tanks, coolers, pipes, etc) the thermal stress at which the
equipment’s material is exposed can be reduced signiﬁcantly, thus contributing to
increase the equipment’s useful life.
As a global conclusion, it can be said that the reduction of the cleaning times and
the reduction of the temperatures are optimization proposals whose implementation
is very simple and that present substantial savings, whilst option 3 can be discarded
for already built CIP units, even though it could results on beneﬁts if a new CIP
unit is to be built.

CONCLUSION
The answers to the questions that arose during the thesis statement section have
been addressed throughout this project as follows:
• Chapter 2 has been dedicated to deﬁne a detailed approach that allows the
energy mapping of the unit and identify the terms that are involved on the
energy balance of the system.
• Throughout Chapter 3 a theoretical model has been developed, whose purpose
is to recreate the evolution of a wash cycle that allows the estimation of the
thermal energy consumption of a cleaning procedure performed by the CIP
system.
• In Chapter 4 diﬀerent Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) have been proposed
that sought to evaluate the performance of the CIP system
• Chapter 5 analyzes diﬀerent optimization proposals whose purpose is to reduce
the thermal energy consumption of the CIP system, but also analyses its
implications with respect to the wash duration.
The conclusions of the previously mentioned chapters are summarized through the
following lines:
Data analysis of the system With the measurement from the new temperature
transmitters installed on lines 1 and 4 the development of an energy balance was
enabled for those lines. The term of the energy balance that arises more attention is
the heat input (steam consumption). The energy balance yielded a total of 68,96
MWh of steam consumed during a period of 50 days, from which 76% of them
are dedicated to heat up the base solution. Besides, as the software 3D TRASAR
proposes a diﬀerent method to estimate this heat input, a comparison has been
done in order to contrast the results. The software 3D TRASAR turns out to be
overestimating this term by 30% with respect to the result obtained throughout the
energy balance. Finally, an important term that also derives from the energy balance
is being drained by the system. For the 50 days analyzed, a total amount of 20.19
MWh have been drained between the two lines, from which 99% are related to the
draining of the water that is sent in between the warm phases.
Model of the CIP System The models to predict the temperature evolution of
a ﬂuid going through a pipe, a plate heat exchanger, a storage tank and a shell
and tube heat exchanger were developed. The veriﬁcation of the models showed
that the approach for the development of the models is valid, and the models are
able to properly predict the evolution of the ﬂuid temperature. However, further

improvements in the procedure to calculate the heat transfers coeﬃcients will yield
more accurate results. The individual models were coupled to create a complete
model of an equipment sequence that intervene in the cleaning procedure performed
by the CIP System. In conjunction with the coupled models, an algorithm in charge
to deﬁne and control the diﬀerent cleaning phases, their temperatures and the time
that the diﬀerent ﬂuids would ﬂow through the system was developed. With this
coupled models and algorithm, the diﬀerent cleaning procedures that the CIP system
is able to perform were simulated and the heat consumed in the procedures was
estimated. The results of this simulations were compared with the data of the real
washes through a relative error analysis. In case of a pipe cleaning simulation, the
largest reported error is -4.02%, for the plate heat exchangers -6% and for the tanks
-13.78%. From the error analysis can be concluded that the models estimate a lower
heat consumption than the real one. The main source of error of this calculation
comes from the estimation of the transportation times of the equipment due to
inaccurate measurements of the pipe dimensions. Besides, in the case of the tank,
an extra source of error was the ﬂow-rate at which the ﬂuid is pumped back to the
system which also inﬂuence the transportation time.
Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) Diﬀerent types of KPIs have been deﬁned
that seek to provide information regarding the performance of the CIP unit. The
ﬁrst type have been deﬁned as the thermal KPIs, as they are calculated using a
yearly estimation of the thermal energy consumption. It has been calculated that
the CIP unit consumes 12% of the natural gas consumed by the plant and that
6.28 kWh of natural gas have been used in the CIP per each ton of milk treated
in the dairy. Secondly, the set of KPIs developed to assess the performance of the
CIP in terms of water consumption have shown that the CIP accounts for 21% of
the water consumed by the dairy, which implies a usage of 0.15 liters of water per
kg of milk processed. Besides, from the water that is destinated to the CIP, 76%
of it ends up being drained. Then, a third KPI that accounted for the electricity
consumption of the CIP showed that only 1.3% of the electricity consumption of the
dairy is associated with the CIP system. Finally, several KPIs per equipment were
calculated, being each one of them the average and the standard deviation of the
duration of each equipment.
Optimization of the CIP System Throughout this chapter it was proved that the
optimization ideas which rely on the tuning of some of the parameters that control
the wash cycle are better suited for CIP units that are already built. Particularly,
and for the equipment studied, the savings per second of target cleaning time reduced
ranged between 0.039 and 0.076 kWh per wash of recipe 1 done whilst the savings that
would be obtained if the target temperature of the base or acid target temperatures
are reduced by 1 °C range between 0.24 kWh - 0.47 kWh and 0.15 kWh - 0.19 kWh,
respectively. Then, the option that considered the addition of a heat exchanger to
the current conﬁguration in order to allow heat recovery or the use of surplus heat
yielded savings that range between 0.027 kWh and 0.072 kWh per 1 °degree increase

6.1
Future work and recommendations
The development of the mass and energy balances of the CIP system could be
improved by the use of a proper ﬂow measurement on the return line, meaning that
the air bubbles will not inﬂuence the measurements. This will allow an estimation of
the volume that returns back to the system that relies only on the accuracy of the
measurements, and not on the underlying assumptions that the usage of the forward
ﬂow meter implies could be used. Focusing on the energy balance, further analysis
whether a diﬀerent approach to calculating the heat lost to the tanks and the heat
drained term could be done. Particularly, and with regard to this last term, as the
volume of the drains could be better estimated and using the return temperature, a
diﬀerent approach could be adopted when estimating it.
When it comes to the model developed, several ideas regarding further improvements
can be applied. For example, the governing equations could include the continuity
one if the pressure drop of the system is to be estimated. Also, the eﬀect of using
diﬀerent thermodynamic properties for each of the solutions could also be considered.
Besides, the correlations used to calculate all the convective heat transfer coeﬃcients
assume steady state ﬂow conditions for the temperature and ﬂow, which is not the
case of the problem solved, hence opening the door to further research on this topic.
Moreover, a model that includes the dynamic behavior of the steam side of the heat
exchanger could also be included, even though the signiﬁcant complexity that it is
involved in this particular problem.
Finally, and with the purpose to broaden the scope of this project, an expansion of the
equipment to be cleaned could be done by developing its corresponding theoretical
models of the problem to be solved. Also, the theoretical models for diﬀerent kind
of CIP conﬁgurations (for instance the CIP units in which the tanks are heated
directly) could be developed and coupled with the equipment that has been modeled.
