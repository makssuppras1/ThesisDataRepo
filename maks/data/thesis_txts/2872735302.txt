Abstract
This thesis investigates whether electroencephalography can reveal brain responses to
external stimuli in such patients using unsupervised analysis methods. Two datasets
were analyzed: one involving auditory stimuli (familiar and medical voices) and another
involving pharmacological stimulation followed by mental arithmetic tasks. A three­stage
preprocessing pipeline was developed and refined to suppress artifacts while preserving
signal quality, with a final version achieving high data retention across both datasets.
For the pharmacological dataset, low frequency EEG features, delta and theta band
power, were extracted from fronto central channels. For the auditory dataset, a broader
set of features was computed, including spectral, complexity, and connectivity measures.
In both datasets, Principal Component Analysis and Gaussian Mixture Models were used
to identify latent brain states. Clustering was performed per patient, and brain state dy­
namics were evaluated by tracking cluster membership over time.
Results showed that a subset of patients exhibited detectable EEG changes during cog­
nitive tasks, particularly during placebo or baseline sessions, with no consistent enhance­
ment from active drugs. In the auditory dataset, no systematic differences were found
between voice types; however, some patients did show signs of neural reactivity during
auditory stimulation, indicating a possible response to the voices regardless of their type.

1
Introduction
Electroencephalography, as a non invasive and temporally precise tool, offers a promising
avenue for detecting residual brain activity that may indicate preserved cognitive function.
This thesis investigates whether unsupervised machine learning methods applied to EEG
data can uncover neural responses to external stimulation in DoC patients. Two dis­
tinct datasets were analyzed: a pharmacological stimulation dataset, in which patients
were administered a drug or placebo followed by mental arithmetic tasks and an audi­
tory dataset, in which patients were exposed to familiar and medical voices. The central
hypothesis is that even without behavioral responses, EEG recordings may reveal latent
structure or shifts in brain state during task periods.
To address this, a EEG preprocessing pipeline was developed and iteratively refined to
clean clinical EEG recordings while maximizing data retention. Extensive manual inspec­
tion of raw data and intermediate outputs was conducted during pipeline development to
identify potential sources of noise unrelated to muscle activity or patient movement, such
as external or environmental interference. For the pharmacological dataset, analysis fo­
cused on low frequency activity, specifically delta and theta band power extracted from
fronto central channels. For the auditory dataset, a broader set of spectral, complexity,
and connectivity features was computed to better capture varied brain dynamics. In both
cases, dimensionality was reduced using Principal Component Analysis, and Gaussian
Mixture Models were applied to detect clusters of similar brain activity patterns.
Cluster transitions and principal component trajectories were tracked over time to deter­
mine whether changes aligned with stimulus or task periods. The aim of this work is not
to draw clinical conclusions, but rather to demonstrate that unsupervised EEG analysis,
supported by careful data quality control, can reveal subtle task related brain responses
in DoC patients that may otherwise go unnoticed with traditional approaches.

2.1
Signals
2.1.1
Electroencephalography (EEG)
EEG is a technique used to measure the brain’s electrical activity. In scalp EEG, between
10 and 256 electrodes are placed on the scalp to detect voltage fluctuations generated
by neuronal activity [1]. This signal, called an electroencephalogram, is linked to various
mental processes [2].
EEG is non­invasive, relatively low­cost, portable, and offers high temporal resolution
(typically between 250 Hz and 2 kHz). However, its spatial resolution is limited, making
it difficult to assess activity in deeper brain structures [3]. Abnormal EEG patterns can
indicate conditions such as epilepsy, brain injury, or altered states of consciousness [4].
Signals are interpreted through montages—systems that define how voltage differences
between electrodes are calculated. Common methods include sequential, referential, and
average montages [5]. The international 10–20 system is a widely adopted standard for
electrode placement.
2.2
Auditory EEG Recordings Dataset
2.2.1
Experimental Design
This dataset contains EEG recordings collected at Rigshospitalet in Copenhagen, Den­
mark, from 80 patients diagnosed with disorders of consciousness (DoC). Each patient
was recorded using a 32­channel EEG setup, including 25 standard EEG electrodes and
7 auxiliary channels, such as electrocardiography (ECG) for monitoring heart activity.
During the recording sessions, patients were exposed to auditory stimuli designed to as­
sess residual cognitive function. Specifically, each patient was presented with their own
name 10 times using a medical voice and 10 times using a familiar voice. These stimuli
were chosen to probe the patient’s recognition and attention capabilities.
To compare neural responses, three resting periods were also recorded: one before any
stimulation, one after the first 10 stimuli, and one at the end of the session. Each resting
block lasted at least 10 minutes. The environment was kept quiet to minimize interference.
An overview of this timeline is shown in Figure 2.1.

2.3
Pharmacological Stimulation Dataset
2.3.1
Experimental Design
This dataset includes EEG, fNIRS, and pupillometry recordings from 50 patients diag­
nosed with DoC. Each patient underwent up to three recording sessions. In each session,
recordings were taken at three timepoints:
• T0: Before drug administration
• T15: 15 minutes post­administration
• T60: 60 minutes post­administration
In this research, we focus exclusively on the EEG recordings. The fNIRS and pupillometry
data were not analyzed as part of this thesis.
Patients were administered one of three substances—methylphenidate, apomorphine,
or placebo (saline)—in a double­blind setup. The substance given in each session was
randomly selected, ensuring that the experimenters could not anticipate the condition.
This randomization prevents bias and avoids the influence of expectations on the results.
An overview of the administered substances is provided in Table 2.2. EEG and fNIRS
were recorded using a 32­electrode headcap, and pupillometry was recorded using a
handheld device. A standardized audio stimulus was presented via headphones.
Audio paradigms
Each session included a standardized audio recording, identical across all patients and
sessions. The recording consisted of two parts:
• Passive paradigm: Alarm sounds and music were played for 10 seconds, followed
by 20 seconds of rest. This cycle was repeated three times. This part is considered
”passive” because a person in a normal conscious state is expected to respond
automatically to such stimuli [gomez2004emotion].
• Active paradigm: Patients were instructed via audio to perform mental arithmetic
tasks, five of moderate and five of higher difficulty. Each task lasted 25 seconds,
followed by a 20 second rest period. This is called the ”active” paradigm, as it
engages the patient’s cognitive abilities.
==PAGE:13==
Figure 2.2: Overview of a potential experimental design.
Scope of analysis
Although the pharmacological stimulation dataset (Section 2.3) includes multimodal recordings—
EEG, fNIRS, and pupillometry—as well as two types of auditory paradigms (passive and
active), this thesis focuses on a specific subset of the data. Only the EEG recordings are
analyzed; fNIRS and pupillometry signals are excluded from the analysis. Additionally,
only the arithmetic tasks from the active paradigm are considered. The passive audi­
tory stimuli are not analyzed but may still appear in plots to indicate the timing of audio
presentation. Figure 2.2 illustrates the experimental setup used in this thesis, focusing
exclusively on the EEG modality and the arithmetic task paradigm.
2.4
Data Considerations
Because of variability in patient responsiveness and occasional deviations from the stan­
dard experimental procedure, not all recordings followed the full protocol. For consistency
and comparability, only those sessions that included the complete experimental paradigm
were retained for analysis in this thesis.
Another key consideration is class imbalance. In both datasets, resting­state segments
make up a substantially larger portion of the data compared to stimulus­related activity.
This is largely due to the longer duration and easier acquisition of resting periods. Care
must be taken to mitigate this imbalance when training models or drawing statistical in­
ferences, so as not to bias results toward the majority class.

3
Preprocessing methods and results
3.1
Preprocessing Pipeline – First Iteration
3.1.1
Goals
The primary goal of this initial phase was to validate and understand the EEG preprocess­
ing pipeline inherited from previous study. This was essential to confirm that the pipeline
operated as documented and provided a robust foundation for subsequent improvements.
The approach taken was directly based on previous thesis, representing an effort to re­
produce and closely match the original implementation as accurately as possible.
3.1.2
Overview of the Initial Pipeline
The original pipeline was based on a set of standard EEG preprocessing steps imple­
mented in MNE­Python [7] and PyPREP [8]. This preprocessing approach, directly inher­
ited from previous thesis [9], served as the starting point for this project. The following
preprocessing steps were applied:
• Bad channel detection: Noisy channels were identified using PyPREP functions
find_bad_by_deviation and find_bad_by_hfnoise, targeting abnormally high am­
plitudes and excessive high­frequency noise.
• Interpolation: Marked channels were interpolated using spherical splines to pre­
serve channel count and maintain spatial consistency.
• Re­referencing: The data were re­referenced using an average reference mon­
tage.
• Filtering: A band­pass filter from 1 to 70 Hz was applied to retain neural signals of
interest, followed by a notch filter at 50 Hz to remove line noise.
• Epoching and baseline correction: The recordings were cropped around 13 task­
related events and segmented into 35­second epochs. A baseline correction was
applied by subtracting the mean signal from the 10 seconds preceding each epoch.
• Epoch rejection: The autoreject [10] library was used to automatically detect and
reject noisy epochs. Any epoch with at least one noisy channel was discarded.
3.1.3
Data Quality Assessment Strategy
To evaluate the effectiveness of the preprocessing pipeline, a systematic quality control
process was implemented. The goal was to identify remaining noise or artifacts that could
affect downstream analysis. The approach combined automated feature analysis with
manual signal inspection:
• For each epoch, the average delta and theta power across all channels was com­
puted and log­transformed. These bands were selected because the analysis fo­
cuses on low­frequency features, as a consistent noise pattern was found above
10 Hz in several recordings—making higher­frequency bands unreliable(See Sec­
tion 3.5.1).
• Violin plots were generated to visualize the distribution of power values across ses­
sions and identify potential outliers.
• Outlier values were detected using the interquartile range method.

3.1.4
Initial Observations
The first iteration of the quality control process revealed several anomalies. As shown
in Figure 3.1, the violin plots of delta and theta power exhibited extreme values across
multiple sessions and patients, indicating that some residual artifacts remained in the data
despite automated preprocessing.
To investigate further, representative epochs corresponding to outlier power values were
manually inspected. In these cases, the EEG signals showed non­physiological charac­
teristics such as abrupt amplitude spikes, broadband noise, and unusually flat or distorted
spectral patterns. These issues were not always detected by the initial artifact rejection
steps.
Figure 3.2 illustrates two such examples—each showing a pair of 35­second epochs from
Patient 35 and Patient 42. For each patient, the top panel shows a baseline (non­outlier)
epoch from the same time window, while the bottom panel displays the corresponding
outlier epoch that was flagged during the quality control process. These pairs allow direct
comparison between typical signal activity and potential contamination.
In the example from Patient 35, several EEG channels in the outlier epoch display am­
plitudes exceeding 500µV—well above expected limits. In the case of Patient 42, signal
amplitudes reach beyond 10,000µV in the outlier epoch, confirming the presence of ex­
treme artifacts. The colored traces represent individual EEG channels, with their spatial
locations indicated in the accompanying head map legend. This comparison highlights
how visually inspecting flagged segments can help differentiate clean brain activity from
noisy or corrupted signals that may persist after automated preprocessing.
3.1.5
Data Quality Summary
A summary of preprocessing outcomes is provided in Table 3.1. On average, the pipeline
interpolated about 4 bad channels and rejected over 4 epochs per file. However, 89 out
of 304 recordings were excluded entirely due to poor data quality, with an average data
retention rate of just over 67%.
3.1.6
Limitations Identified
This first iteration of preprocessing revealed several key limitations:
• Residual noise and muscle artifacts were still present in some recordings, as ev­
idenced by extreme outlier values in low frequency features and abnormal EEG
amplitudes during manual inspection.

• The approach of rejecting entire epochs and bad channels sometimes led to exces­
sive data loss, reducing the number of usable segments for analysis.
These findings motivated the development of a more robust preprocessing pipeline. The
initial version was based on a previous thesis project [9] and served as the starting point
for this analysis. After applying it to the current dataset, initial quality checks using violin
plots of delta and theta power revealed unusually high values in some sessions.
To investigate these anomalies, a backward analysis was conducted: the extreme val­
ues were flagged as outliers, traced back to their corresponding epochs, and visually
inspected in the raw EEG recordings. This inspection confirmed that several of these
outlier epochs exhibited severe artifacts—such as amplitudes exceeding 500 µV or even
10,000 µV—that had not been removed by the initial pipeline. These observations high­
lighted its limitations in detecting subtle or non­muscle­related noise.
As a result, a second preprocessing iteration was developed (Section 3.2) that introduced
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) for improved artifact rejection, enhanced bad
channel detection using PyPREP methods, and refined filtering parameters. The goal
of these changes was to better suppress residual artifacts while maximizing the retention
of clean, analyzable EEG data.
3.2
Preprocessing Pipeline – Second Iteration
3.2.1
Goals
Based on the limitations identified in the initial pipeline, the goal of the second iteration
was to improve noise and artifact rejection while preserving more usable EEG data. This
version introduced several new steps, such as Independent Component Analysis based
artifact removal and stricter bad channel handling and re­evaluated thresholds to better
balance data quality and retention.
3.2.2
Pipeline Enhancements
Filtering and Initial Signal Cleaning
The pipeline began with a notch filter at 50Hz to suppress power line noise, followed by
a band­pass filter between 0.5–60Hz. The high pass cutoff at 0.5Hz was used to remove
slow drifts and DC offsets, while the low pass cutoff at 60Hz was chosen because fre­
quencies above this range primarily contain muscle artifacts and do not carry task related
EEG information relevant to this study. This filtering range was selected to retain neural
signals of interest while reducing high­frequency noise.
Artifact Removal with ICA and ICLabel
ICA was introduced to separate the EEG signal into statistically independent sources. We
used 20 ICA components, and muscle­related artifacts were automatically detected using
MNE’s find_bads_muscle function (z­score threshold = 4.5).
Components were further classified using ICLabel [11], which labels sources into six cat­
egories:
• Eye blinks
• Muscle artifacts
• Cardiac artifacts
• Line noise
• Brain signals
==PAGE:20==
• Other non­brain activity

and 150 µV. Thresholds of 250–500 µV provided the best balance between data retention

3.3
Preprocessing Pipeline – Third Iteration
3.3.1
Goals
The third iteration of the preprocessing pipeline aimed to preserve the improvements
made in second iteration 3.2 while addressing its remaining limitations. Specifically, the
third iteration was designed to:
• Reduce computational time by removing unnecessary steps.
• Improve data retention by optimizing epoch segmentation and rejection criteria.
• Avoid over interpolation in noisy recordings.
• Maintain effective artifact removal despite low movement in the clinical EEG dataset.
Several experimental runs were conducted to tune hyperparameters, and the resulting
refinements provided a better trade off between signal quality and data availability.
3.3.2
Pipeline Refinements
Removal of ICA
This decision was based on that ICA flagged very few components in this dataset, which
consisted entirely of patients in coma. Removing ICA significantly reduced computation
time without compromising artifact suppression.
Adjusted Filtering Parameters
The low pass filter cutoff was lowered from 60 Hz to 40 Hz to suppress more high fre­
quency noise such as muscle and hardware related artifacts [13], while retaining relevant
neural activity.
Revised Bad Channel Interpolation Strategy
The statistical detection methods for identifying bad channels (e.g., NaN values, low cor­
relation, RANSAC) remained unchanged. However, the policy for interpolation was up­
dated:
• Interpolation was applied only if fewer than 30% of channels were marked as bad.
• If more than 30% of channels were bad, no interpolation was performed to avoid
reinforcing poor quality signals.
This rule was based on that extensive interpolation can degrade data quality if too many
channels are missing.
Epoching Redesign
A major change in the third iteration was the shift from 35­second epochs to direct seg­
mentation into 1­second epochs during preprocessing. In the previous version, artifacts
in a small portion of a long epoch often caused the entire 35­second window to be re­
jected. By moving to 1­second epochs, this issue was avoided, dramatically increasing
data retention.
Adaptive Amplitude­Based Rejection
Initially, manual thresholds of 125–150 µV were explored. To improve robustness across
recordings, the autoreject package was introduced again to determine amplitude thresh­
olds automatically on a per file basis. This resulted in more adaptive and consistent epoch
rejection across the dataset.

3.5.1
Manual Inspection of Harmonic Noise in the Pharmacological EEG
Dataset
During preliminary analysis of the pharmacological EEG dataset, we noticed unusual pat­
terns in the power spectral density (PSD) plots of many recordings. These patterns re­
sembled strong, repetitive frequency peaks. After discussing these observations with
my supervisor, it was hypothesized that the recordings might contain sharp, harmonic­
like noise components centered around approximately 10.5 Hz, 21.0 Hz, 31.5 Hz, and
42.0 Hz. These peaks appeared regularly across multiple sessions and patients.
This raised concerns about external or equipment­related contamination. Since many
downstream features rely on frequency bands below 40 Hz, it was essential to assess
the nature and extent of this spectral noise in order to make appropriate preprocessing
decisions.
Definition of Harmonic­Like Noise
Harmonic noise typically appears at evenly spaced frequencies (e.g., 5 Hz or 10 Hz inter­
vals). However, in this dataset, the observed peaks were only approximately harmonic,
such as at 10.3 Hz, 20.3 Hz, 30.5 Hz, and 40.8 Hz. While not mathematically precise
harmonics, their regular spacing and amplitude were sufficient to consider them a form
of harmonic­like noise. In signal processing, such near­periodic frequency components
are often treated equivalently to harmonic noise due to their practical impact on spectral
features.

Limitations
This method failed in practice due to three key reasons:
1. Imperfect harmonics: The actual noise peaks were not exact harmonics (e.g.,
10.3, 20.3, 30.5, 40.8 Hz), causing them to fall outside the detection bands.
2. Uneven amplitude: High power at low frequencies (e.g., 10 Hz) could exceed the
threshold, while higher harmonics often remained below it, leading to false nega­
tives.
3. Strict matching: To enable a fully automated detection process, the method was
configured to flag a file as noisy only if peaks were present in all expected harmonic
bands. This strict requirement was necessary to avoid false positives from isolated
peaks, but it led to false negatives when one or more harmonics were weak or
missing, even in otherwise clearly contaminated files. Striking the right balance
between sensitivity and specificity proved difficult under this constraint.
Because of these limitations, this method was not used for final noise detection.

4.2
Analysis Methods Applied to Pharmacological Stimulus
Dataset
This section describes the unsupervised clustering analysis performed on the EEG fea­
tures extracted from the pharmacological stimulus dataset(See Section 2.3). The goal
was to identify patterns of neural activity across different drug conditions and timepoints
by grouping similar epochs based on their low­frequency power features.
4.2.1
Dimensionality Reduction with PCA
For each unique combination of patient, session (drug), and timepoint, PCA was applied
to reduce feature dimensionality. The number of components retained was determined
automatically to explain at least 95% of the total variance. The first principal component
was also stored separately for further analysis, as it captures the largest source of variation
in the data.
4.2.2
Clustering with Gaussian Mixture Models
To cluster the PCA­reduced feature space, Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) were used.
A GMM assumes that the data are generated from a mixture of several multivariate Gaus­
sian distributions. Each cluster is represented by one Gaussian component, defined by
its own mean vector and covariance matrix.
Formally, given a dataset X = {x1, x2, . . . , xN} ∈Rd, the GMM models the probability
density function as:
p(x) =
K
∑
k=1
πk N(x | µk, Σk),
where:
• K is the number of components (clusters),
• πk is the mixing coefficient for component k, with ∑K
k=1 πk = 1,
• µk is the mean vector of component k,
• Σk is the covariance matrix of component k,
• N(x | µk, Σk) is the multivariate normal density.
The model parameters {πk, µk, Σk}K
k=1 are estimated using the Expectation­Maximization
(EM) algorithm, which iteratively maximizes the log­likelihood of the data given the model.
GMMs were chosen for clustering because they offer several advantages in the context
of EEG feature analysis. Unlike hard clustering methods such as k­means, GMMs model
each cluster as a probabilistic distribution, allowing for soft cluster assignments. This flexi­
bility is particularly useful when the boundaries between states (e.g., resting vs. stimulus­
related activity) are not sharply defined. Moreover, GMMs can model elliptical cluster
shapes through their covariance structure, which better captures the underlying feature
distributions observed after PCA.

s(i) =
b(i) −a(i)
max{a(i), b(i)},
where:
• a(i) is the average distance between i and all other points in the same cluster,
• b(i) is the lowest average distance from i to all points in the next nearest cluster.
The model with the highest silhouette score across all points in the group was selected
as the best clustering solution.
Clustering per File
To respect individual variability, clustering was performed separately for each combination
of patient, session, and timepoint. This ensured that the clusters reflected differences in
neural activity within each condition, rather than being biased by inter­subject differences.

These values were used to identify the most influential channels and frequency bands per
patient and session (see Figure 4.2).

For each group, the PC1 values were plotted over time with cluster labels overlaid. Stim­

terpretation of potential brain state transitions. An example is shown in Figure 4.3. This
visualization was essential for identifying when transitions between clusters occurred in
relation to the stimulus, which could indicate a brain reaction. The PC1 over time plot was
particularly important, as it allowed us to pinpoint when and if a shift between clusters oc­
curred. When such a shift was observed, the corresponding PC1 feature loadings were

Signal Processing Approach
The features were computed from preprocessed EEG data using established algorithms
implemented in Python. All features were extracted on an epoch­wise basis using 1­
second windows, and stored in a structured format for analysis.
In this thesis, the theoretical background behind these features is not explained in detail,

4.3.3
Feature Categories
The dataset includes the following categories of features:
• Spectral Features: These include absolute and relative power in standard EEG
frequency bands—delta (0.5–4Hz), theta (4–8Hz), alpha (8–13Hz), beta (13–30Hz),
and gamma (30–45Hz), which reflect different aspects of brain activity. Additional
features such as the FOOOF­derived spectral slope quantify the background “1/f”
trend of the power spectrum [15], while spectral edge and median frequency capture
shifts in the overall frequency distribution.
• Complexity Measures: Kolmogorov Complexity estimates how compressible the
EEG signal is, serving as a proxy for signal randomness. Permutation Entropy mea­
sures the temporal unpredictability or irregularity of the signal, providing insight into
neural complexity.
• Connectivity Measure: Weighted Symbolic Mutual Information (wSMI) estimates
the degree of nonlinear information sharing between EEG channels, reflecting func­
tional connectivity in the brain.

4.3.6
Labeling Strategy for Resting and Auditory Epochs
In a previous thesis by Rasmus [16], task related epochs were defined based on auditory
stimulation periods, and an equal number of resting epochs were included, although the
selection strategy for resting data was not specified.
==PAGE:44==
In this thesis, each patient recording was divided into two segments. Within each seg­
ment, the patient was exposed to alternating auditory stimuli: five Familiar voice and
five Medical voice blocks, each separated by short gaps. These gaps are marked as
Unlabeled in the original event annotations and were redefined in this work as resting­
state epochs. Each gap typically lasted between one and two minutes, providing a sub­
stantial period of non­stimulus activity.
Thus, each segment consists of a structured sequence:
Familiar voice →Resting →Medical voice →Resting →. . .
Each stimulus block was used to define task­related epochs, and each gap between them
served as a source of resting­state data.
To capture possible delayed neural responses after auditory processing, task related la­
bels were extended into the beginning of the subsequent resting period. Specifically, the
final task label in each pair was extended to include the first half of the following resting
segment. This labeling strategy ensures that subtle post­stimulus effects are not missed,
while maintaining a balance between task and rest conditions.
4.3.7
Conclusion
The computed features in this dataset capture a broad range of signal properties, from
spectral distribution and complexity to inter­channel communication. These features pro­
vide a comprehensive representation of brain dynamics and form the basis for the unsu­
pervised analyses described in later chapters.

Methods Applied to Auditory Dataset
4.4.1
Overview
This section describes the unsupervised clustering analysis performed on EEG features
extracted from the auditory dataset ( 2.2). The goal was to uncover latent brain states
during different auditory conditions by grouping similar epochs within segments.
4.4.2
Dimensionality Reduction and Clustering
The same PCA and Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) clustering method described in sec­
tion ?? was applied to the auditory EEG dataset (Dataset 1). Briefly, PCA was used to
reduce feature dimensionality per EEG segment, and the resulting low dimensional repre­
sentation was clustered using GMMs with automatic model selection based on silhouette
score.
Each segment was treated independently. The optimal number of GMM clusters (rang­
ing from 1 to 3) was selected per segment by maximizing the average silhouette score,
consistent with the procedure described previously.

features, such as specific channels or frequency bands, most strongly influenced the clus­
tering and variance structure.

6
Discussion
6.1
Overview of Datasets and Pipelines
This thesis analyzed EEG data from two distinct datasets, each targeting different re­
search objectives and involving customized preprocessing and feature extraction pipelines.
The auditory dataset consisted of EEG recordings from 80 patients with disorders of con­
sciousness exposed to an auditory paradigm. The primary aim was to determine whether
clustering analyses could distinguish between patients who exhibit signs of brain reactivity
(responders) and those who do not. A secondary objective was to explore whether EEG
responses to familiar voice and medical voice stimuli resulted in distinct neural activity
patterns.
The pharmacological stimulus dataset focused on EEG recordings collected while pa­
tients were exposed to different drug conditions and performed mental arithmetic tasks
of varying difficulty. Each patient underwent EEG monitoring during baseline and after
administration of one of three agents methylphenidate, apomorphine, or placebo. The
analysis aimed to identify patients showing measurable brain responses to moderate and
hard mental arithmetic tasks. Further, the study investigated whether different drug con­
ditions modulated the brain’s response patterns differently.
In both datasets, EEG recordings was the only tool used to study brain activity. Although
the goals were different, auditory responses in one case and drug related task responses
in the other, the analysis followed a similar process. This included reducing the number
of overall features (dimensionality reduction), grouping similar brain patterns (clustering),
looking at how these patterns changed over time (PC1 trajectory), and checking which
features were most important.
6.2
Auditory Dataset
6.2.1
Interpretation of PC1 Over Time Shifts
In a few patients we observed shifts in PC1 values during auditory stimulus periods. These
changes suggest that the clustering and dimensionality reduction approach may be able
to capture some level of brain reactivity. However, these shifts were not consistently
stronger or different for familiar voices compared to medical voices, making it difficult to
conclude whether the brain is specifically reacting to one stimulus type over another.
6.2.2
Feature Contributions
We found that the features contributing to PC1 varied a lot between patients. In many
cases, spectral edge frequency features (SEF90, SEF95), gamma band power, or beta
band power were among the most important. However, other features like foof slope
mean, foof slope std, or smi weighted had very low influence on PC1 in most patients.
This shows that while the method captures something, not all engineered features con­
tribute meaningfully to brain state separation in this context.
6.2.3
Limitations
A major limitation was the exclusion of many EEG segments due to noise, artifacts, or
incomplete recordings. Since our analysis relies entirely on EEG data, any missing or
corrupted segments directly impacted the ability to analyze individual sessions or com­
pare across segments. Without access to behavioral or clinical information, we were also

unable to validate whether observed PC1 shifts reflect meaningful neural responses or
random fluctuations.
6.2.4
Challenges Encountered
One major difficulty was the replication of existing code from a previous thesis. Many of the
original scripts were written using older versions of Python libraries, which are no longer
maintained or compatible with recent environments. As a result, significant time was spent
identifying compatible library versions, fixing deprecated functions, and restoring broken
functionality to ensure the original logic could be executed properly.
Another limitation related to this was the inability to compute heart rate variability HRV
features. Although ECG data were available, the associated code for HRV extraction was
incompatible with the updated software stack, and thus six ECG features could not be
included in this analysis.
Computation time was also a significant challenge. Early versions of the pipeline required
over two days to extract features for a single run, without full certainty that the outputs
were correct. Even after optimization and the use of basic parallelization techniques in
Python, the total runtime for the auditory dataset remained approximately 16 hours. These
long processing times slowed down testing and debugging and placed limits on iterative
refinement of the pipeline.
Overall, both software compatibility issues and limited computational resources impacted
the efficiency and scalability of the analysis.
6.2.5
Future Work and Methodological Improvements
If more time were available, several improvements could be made to the pipeline. First,
the preprocessing steps could be optimized, for example, checking further and in depth
whether every cleaning step is actually needed or if some steps remove too much or too
little information. Ensuring that noise removal does not affect meaningful brain signals is
essential for improving signal quality and analysis reliability.
Second, the pipeline could benefit from parallelization to reduce runtime, as the full pre­
processing of the auditory dataset currently takes over 16 hours.
Third, we suggest a more selective feature extraction approach. Many features had low
impact on PC1, adding unnecessary complexity. Reducing the number of features would
make the analysis easier to interpret. It would also help in identifying which types of
features are truly relevant for distinguishing brain responses in this population.
Finally, future work should include a more focused backward analysis. If a subset of
patients, such as 3, 4 or 10 individuals, show consistent PC1 shifts and potential re­
sponsiveness, then deeper investigation into those segments is warranted. Specifically,
analysis should target the time windows and epochs where these shifts occurred to de­
termine whether the change reflects true neural activity or noise that passed through the
preprocessing pipeline.
By analyzing the PC1 feature contributions within those specific epochs and responders, it
may be possible to gain a clearer understanding of what these shifts represent. This could
lead to more targeted identification of brain reactivity markers and improved interpretation
of unsupervised clustering outputs.

6.3
Pharmacological Dataset
6.3.1
Interpretation of PC1 Over Time Shifts
In a subset of patients, shifts in PC1 values were observed during mental arithmetic tasks,
particularly during the hard condition. These shifts tended to be more noticeable during
baseline or placebo sessions rather than after drug administration, suggesting that the
observed changes may reflect underlying brain responsiveness rather than direct drug
effects.
Overall, these findings indicate that the method is capable of capturing meaningful time­
dependent changes in brain activity in some patients, often aligned with periods of cog­
nitive engagement. In nearly all cases where PC1 shifts occurred, the top contributing
features were dominated by delta band activity, highlighting the role of low­frequency
EEG dynamics in capturing state transitions in this population.
6.3.2
Feature Contributions
In this dataset, the feature contributions were more interpretable and directly linked to
the cognitive task. Since the analysis focused only on delta and theta frequency bands,
the interpretation of results was clearer. It is generally understood that a decrease in
delta activity may reflect increased alertness, while increases in theta activity are often
associated with memory and cognitive engagement.
As expected, delta band features were dominant in the majority of patients, especially
during periods where state transitions were observed. In some patients, the top 10 con­
tributing features included a mix of both delta and theta metrics, aligning with the mental
effort involved in the arithmetic tasks. This more focused feature space made it easier to
relate clustering results to plausible brain dynamics.
6.3.3
Limitations
A major limitation of this analysis was the quality and completeness of the EEG data.
Due to noise, artifacts, or missing segments, not all sessions could be included in the
final analysis. In several cases, patients were missing one or more sessions, or certain
sessions were too noisy to reliably extract features or observe consistent brain dynamics.
As a result, comparisons both within and across patients were limited.
Additionally, a specific form of noise resembling harmonic interference was observed in
many recordings, particularly in frequencies above 10 Hz. This noise contaminated large
parts of the spectrum, forcing the analysis to focus solely on the delta and theta bands.
While these bands are relevant to consciousness and cognitive processing, this restriction
may have caused the loss of potentially valuable information in the alpha, beta, or gamma
ranges.
6.3.4
Challenges Encountered
Several technical challenges were encountered during the analysis. First, replicating and
adapting code from earlier theses proved difficult due to changes in Python package ver­
sions. Many libraries used in prior work were outdated or deprecated, requiring manual
adjustments and compatibility fixes to make the code functional in a modern environment.
Second, preprocessing the EEG data for this dataset was time consuming. Each patient
had multiple recordings across several sessions, resulting in a larger number of files com­
pared to the auditory dataset. This significantly increased the computational load during
preprocessing.
However, the feature extraction step in this dataset was considerably faster, as it focused
only on delta and theta frequency bands. As a result, the total processing time ended up
==PAGE:82==
being roughly equivalent to that of the auditory dataset.
6.3.5
Future Work and Methodological Improvements
Future work could focus on narrowing the analysis to theta band features, which were the
most informative across patients in this dataset. By reducing the feature space to only the
most relevant components, such as theta power and related metrics, the analysis would
become more targeted to the mental arithmetic task and easier to interpret.
Additionally, a backward analysis approach should be implemented to further investigate
specific time windows where PC1 fluctuations were observed. This would help confirm
whether the detected brain activity is truly related to the experimental task or caused by
unrelated signal fluctuations or residual noise. Performing feature contribution analysis
within these specific task relevant epochs could help clarify the neural basis of the ob­
served shifts.
Finally, integrating statistical methods would complement the unsupervised clustering ap­
proach. These methods could validate or further explain clustering results, offering a more
complete picture of brain responses and improving the robustness of interpretations.

7
Conclusion
This thesis explored whether unsupervised clustering of EEG features can reveal resid­
ual brain responses in patients with disorders of consciousness. Two complementary
datasets were analyzed: one involving pharmacological stimulation followed by men­
tal arithmetic tasks, and another involving auditory stimulation with familiar and medical
voices. The goal was to detect changes in brain activity over time that might reflect task
engagement—even in the absence of overt behavioral responses.
In the pharmacological dataset, analysis focused on delta and theta band power extracted
from fronto central electrodes. In the auditory dataset, a broader set of spectral, complex­
ity, and connectivity features was used. For both datasets, dimensionality reduction with
PCA and clustering with Gaussian Mixture Models enabled unsupervised detection of po­
tential brain state transitions. These transitions were then examined over time to assess
alignment with task or stimulus periods.
Results showed that a small subset of patients exhibited EEG changes suggestive of
stimulus related neural activity. In the pharmacological dataset, such shifts were most
often observed during the hard arithmetic task, and frequently occurred during baseline or
placebo sessions rather than following active drug administration. In the auditory dataset,
while no systematic preference was found between familiar and medical voices, several
patients showed signs of reactivity to auditory stimulation.
These findings highlight the potential of unsupervised EEG analysis to detect subtle neural
dynamics in patients with DoC. Although not yet suitable for clinical use, this approach
lays the groundwork for future tools that could contribute to bedside monitoring or the
detection of covert awareness in severely brain injured populations.
