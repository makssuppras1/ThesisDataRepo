Abstract
Efficient utilization of earthãs limited resources requires effective waste management systems.
Material flow analysis (MFA) is a valuable tool in identifying inefficiencies in waste management
processes to increase the ability to reuse resources.
This study introduces pyMFA, a PythonôÙbased tool that provides a programmatic model tailored
specifically for dynamic MFA within circular resource systems. Applied in a case study adapted
from Eriksen et al. (2020), it explores four scenarios concerning polyethylene terephthalate
(PET) packaging and textiles in Europe. Assessing the modelãs performance involved replicating
results from Eriksen et al. (2020). While the presented scenarios are theoretical and aimed at
evaluating the modelãs performance, the results show that it requires a joint effort to reach
recycling targets, in line with current implementation of new regulations. It will not suffice to rely
entirely on consumer behavioral change or implementation of new recycling facilities, a
combination of multiple strategies needs to be employed. Additionally, the results suggest that
prioritizing closedôÙloop recycling may not always lead to the most efficient use of resources.
The model seeks to streamline MFA calculations efficiently compared to other tools like Excel
and STAN. Its emphasis lies in ensuring simplicity and userôÙfriendliness, with compatibility for
ExcelôÙbased data input and output. The tool can generate interactive sankey diagrams that also
support export of static png image files. Moreover, pyMFA has been created as an openôÙsource
project on GitHub which allows users with Python skills to add new functionalities, or only use
parts of the model.
The findings suggest promising capabilities of the pyMFA tool in visualizing and analyzing
material flows within circular resource systems. However, the current version possesses certain
limitations, laying a foundation for further development. Suggestions for enhancements of the
model, including additional functionalities are proposed in this thesis.

Introduction
By 2030, projections indicate an increase in the global population, from 7.8 billion in
2020 to an estimated 8.5 billion individuals, as highlighted by The World Bank (2023).
As a consequence, resource consumption heightens as coupled with the standard of
living. Therefore, the limited resources on earth are steadily depleting. The practice of
utilizing nonôÙrenewable resources to create products that are discarded after a single
use is highly inefficient and wasteful and is referred to as a linear takeôÙmakeôÙwaste
economy (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2019). The prevention of overuse and depletion
of resources can be supported by shifting from a linear consumption model to a circular
approach. Thus, effective resource and waste management systems are needed.
Dynamic material flow analysis (dynamic MFA) is used to enable improved
comprehension of complex resource dynamics within a system, and to track material
mass balance over time by accounting for changes in stocks, flows, and
transformations. It plays a crucial role in waste management analysis, aiding
decisionôÙmaking by pinpointing areas of inefficient material usage (Brunner and
H. Rechberger, 2016ëƒ Moriguchi and Hashimoto, 2015).
Materials entering production, use, global import and export as well as system
distribution, pose challenges for their recovery into a new life cycle. Understanding how
resources enter, circulate, and exit systems is essential, facilitating improved recycling
strategies and system enhancements. Dynamic MFA can be used as a decisionôÙmaking
tool for policymakers, as it can give an overview of what results can be obtained by
following a certain resource management path. While it can provide value on its own,
dynamic MFA often benefits from the combination with other analysis methods, such as
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) (Turner et al., 2016).
The European Union (EU), sets targets for its member stateãs resource consumption, in
the effort to address the growing environmental and social issues with increasing
consumption of resources. For example, one of the defined targets is to recycle 55% of
all plastic packaging waste by the end of 2030 (European Parliament, 2018). Targets
serve as a valuable tool for measuring progress towards a circular economy and
promote more sustainable waste management practices.
However, setting targets is not enough to solve the complex challenges concerning
resource consumption and should be seen as part of a wider strategy for addressing
issues with waste and minimizing material use. To achieve real results, recycling targets
need to be supported by additional measures and policies, such as extended producer
responsibility, waste reduction, prevention campaigns and investment in research to
develop new technologies and materials (European Parliament, 2019). Furthermore,
the success of recycling targets is highly dependent on the availability and quality of
recycling infrastructure as well as the willingness of businesses and individuals to adopt
more sustainable practices (Kahlert and Bening, 2022).
In line with the high focus on recycling in plans and targets set by policymakers,
dynamic MFA allows for assessing the effectiveness of recycling initiatives. This
emphasis on recycling aligns with the broader aim of achieving a circular economy,
where materials are kept in productive use for as long as possible, minimizing the need
for extraction of virgin resources and reducing environmental impacts from production,
incineration and landfilling (Roland Berger, 2023).

2.1
Material Flow Analysis
MFA is a method that quantifies the movements of materials within a system over a
specific time period. Materials are tracked in intermediate and final destinations and
sinks, providing an overview of the material journey in the system. By adhering to the
law of mass conservation, the inputs, stocks, and outputs within a process, must always
be balanced, so nothing is lost (Brunner and H. Rechberger, 2016). The net flow of a
process without a stock should therefore be zero (Mû¥ller et al., 2014). The net flow of a
system process is presented in Equation 2.1, where t is the timestep.
Net flowprocess(t) = Inflowprocess(t) ãOutflowprocess(t)
(2.1)
A notable use of MFA is as a decisionôÙmaking tool in resource management. It offers a
comprehensive analysis of material flows and stocks within a system, often visualized in
sankey diagrams to emphasize larger and smaller mass flows. By detecting material
stock depletion and accumulation it allows for making changes and strategies for better
utilization of materials. Even subtle changes that might potentially lead to longôÙterm
damage become apparent through MFA (Brunner and H. Rechberger, 2016).
As anthropogenic systems enclose more than mass flows and stocks, MFA is often
coupled with the analysis of energy, economy, urban planning and environmental
impacts if is should be used for interpretation and use of the results for decisionôÙmaking
(Brunner and H. Rechberger, 2016).
In order to increase transparency and comparison of MFA results across different
studies, a common language of terms and methodologies was established by Brunner
and H. Rechberger (2016). The term material is used as an umbrella term for
substances, goods and products. These can be represented in material flows in an
MFA, but refer to different things. A substance is a material made of a single element or
chemical compound (for example carbon, or carbon dioxide), whereas goods or
products can be a mix of substances, with economic value based on the market (for
example a plastic bottle). In MFA the term good, is only used for material goods, and
does not include energy, services or information (Brunner and H. Rechberger, 2016). A
process defines the transformation, transport or storage of materials, and is visualized
as square boxes, linked by flows or fluxes, as seen in Figure 2.1.
Flows, visualized as arrows, define the mass flow rate in units mass per time, for
example ton/year, or flux rates in units of mass per cross section per time, for example
kg/capita/year. A system consists of a defined space and time boundary containing
interconnected material flows, stocks and processes.
Employing MFA over multiple timesteps, termed as dynamic, can provide additional
insights. This is described further in the following section.

2.1.1
Dynamic Material Flow Analysis
MFA can be both static and dynamic. Static MFA considers a system at one given time,
providing a snapshot in time. Whereas in a dynamic MFA, the system behavior is
evaluated over multiple timesteps, where results from previous timesteps effect the
following timesteps (Mû¥ller et al., 2014). Dynamic MFA are appropriate to account for
the retention of materials over time in a system process (stocks) and/or a flow of
materials from one timestep to the next (recycling) (Allesch and Brunner, 2017).
As a material stock is released over time, it influences the output of the given process,
based on the input to the process in previous timesteps. This often occurs for the
useôÙphase of the material life cycle, as materials remain inôÙuse for the duration of their
product durability or lifetime. In this regard it is known as the inôÙuse stock (Elshkaki
et al., 2005ëƒ Mû¥ller et al., 2014). In other parts of the material life cycle, like production,
manufacturing and EndôÙofôÙLife (EoL) management, the MFA is often static, meaning
that the net flow (inputôÙoutput) from these system processes in each timestep will be
zero (Mû¥ller et al., 2014).
A dynamic MFA can be both retrospective and prospective. A retrospective MFA only
considers historical data. A retrospective and prospective MFA considers both historical
data and the extrapolation thereof, whereas a solely prospective MFA only considers
scenarios for the future, without historical data input (Mû¥ller et al., 2014). The modeling
approach employed in this thesis is both retrospective and prospective.
2.1.2
Dynamic Stock Modeling
Dynamic stock modeling serves as a crucial aspect within dynamic MFA, allowing for
the assessment of material flows and stocks over time. It aids in understanding the
dynamics of resource utilization, accumulation, and potential environmental impacts
within a system (Elshkaki et al., 2005). Two prevalent modeling approaches, namely
topôÙdown and bottomôÙup, offer distinct methodologies for analyzing and forecasting
material stocks and flows (Mû¥ller et al., 2014).
A topôÙdown modeling approach within dynamic stock modeling involves analyzing the
aggregate material flow data at a higher level. It considers overarching data and
statistical trends to estimate material stocks and flows within a system. This method
typically relies on macroôÙlevel data, extrapolating and inferring trends based on
comprehensive data sets and statistical analyses. It allows for a broad overview of
material movements and is particularly useful when detailed, fineôÙgrained data is
unavailable. However, the topôÙdown approach might lack specificity regarding individual
processes or components within the system (Mû¥ller et al., 2014).

A bottomôÙup modeling approach involves a more granular analysis, focusing on
individual components or processes within the system. It integrates detailed data
specific to each component or process, offering a more comprehensive and detailed
view of material flows and stocks. The bottomôÙup approach requires data at a lower
level of granularity, allowing for a more accurate representation of individual processes.
This method is advantageous in capturing nuances and intricacies within the system but
might pose challenges in aggregating diverse, detailed data into a comprehensive
systemôÙlevel view (Mû¥ller et al., 2014).
The choice between topôÙdown and bottomôÙup modeling approaches depends on the
available data, the level of detail required, and the specific objectives of the analysis.
While the topôÙdown approach provides a broad overview and is suitable for systems
with limited detailed data, the bottomôÙup approach offers a more detailed,
processôÙspecific understanding but requires extensive, detailed data collection. In
practice, a combination of both approaches might be employed to leverage their
respective strengths and compensate for their limitations, enabling a more robust and
accurate dynamic stock modeling within Material Flow Analysis.
2.1.3
Extrapolating Data to Estimate Material Production
Estimating material production, often based on historical data, is a critical aspect of
prospective MFA. Different growth types, such as exponential growth, are used to model
material consumption patterns over time. The production of material is often estimated
based on the production in previous years (Eriksen et al., 2020). Choosing the growth
type that fits the development from previous years the best.
Modern societyãs increase in material consumption, can most often be described by
exponential growth, where consumption increases with a certain percentage each year.
The equation for exponential growth is shown in Equation 2.2.
P(t) = p0(1 + r)t
(2.2)
Where P(t) is the production in timestep t. p0 indicate the production amount in
timestep 0, ie. the timestep with known production amount, prior to the estimated
timestep that follow and r is the growth rate (ie. the percentage of growth).
The equation for exponential growth can also cover static or negative growth if the
growth rate, r, becomes zero or negative.

2.1.5
Circularity
In resource systems, the principle of circularity asserts that resources should never be
lost but rather perpetually circulated within a material loop (Ellen Macarthur Foundation,
2019). This concept highlights the importance of sustainable practices, where materials
are continuously recycled or repurposed, thereby minimizing waste and maximizing
resource utilization.
The principle of circularity emphasizes the perpetual circulation of resources within a
material loop, highlighting the importance of sustainable practices and minimizing
waste.
2.1.6
Indicators
Indicators play a crucial role in quantifying and assessing the efficiency and
sustainability of material flows and stocks, offering valuable insights into resource usage
and potential areas for improvement within a system. Indicators are used to assess
specific aspects of a material system in regard to circularity or resource efficiency. It is
considered a simple way of evaluating system performance, witch is less dataôÙintense,
compared to life cycle assessment for example (Saadûˋ et al., 2022).
An example of evaluation indicators are the recycling rate (RR), indicating the amount of
material in a system that is recycled. The circular material use rate (CMUR), the amount
of material entering the production which comes from recycling of materials. The closed
loop circularity rate (CLCR), which defines how much of the production is recycled into
materials of the same type they originated from (Eriksen et al., 2020). These indicators
are used to evaluate the results of the case study of PET packaging and textiles in
Europe, and is therefore elaborated further in section 5.1.
2.2
Plastics
Plastics are ubiquitous in modern society, serving as versatile materials in various
sectors, from packaging and construction to healthcare and electronics. Their
widespread use stems from their durability, versatility, and costôÙeffectiveness. However,
the exponential growth in plastic production has led to an escalating environmental
concern due to mismanaged plastic waste, especially from the packaging industry
(Nisticûý, 2020). Improper disposal of plastics results in extensive pollution, adversely
impacting ecosystems, wildlife, and human health. As plastics persist in the
environment for hundreds of years, implementing proper waste management strategies
becomes imperative to mitigate their detrimental effects, reduce pollution, and
safeguard the planet for future generations.
2.2.1
Polyethylene Terephthalate
Plastics, in particular polyethylene terephthalate (PET), is a compelling subject for MFA
and has been selected for the case study in this thesis due to several critical aspects
that will be outlined in this section. Among plastic polymers PET stands out due to its
high recyclability potential (Nisticûý, 2020), making it an intriguing material to investigate
to enable increasingly sustainable practices for its use. Approximately 14% of the global
polyester market consists of recycled polyester, primarily derived from PET bottles,
indicating a viable opportunity for increased recycling (Zero Waste Europe, 2022).
PETãs prevalence in food packaging significantly contributes to the overall demand for

2.3.3
From Structured to ObjectôÙoriented Programming
ObjectôÙoriented programming (OOP) is a programming paradigm that revolves around
the concept of objects, which are described by classes. Objects are created in the
memory of a computer when a program is run, and usually represent concepts and
things that exist in reality. If multiple objects share the same structure, behavioral
pattern, and attributes in a system, they are described by a single class inside the
developed software. A class is written as code inside a program or script, and is like a
recipe that can then be used to create as many instances of objects as desired ôÙ each
with their own unique values assigned to their shared attributes. The strength of this
approach is the ability to model and simulate complex systems that follow the dynamics
and structures of realôÙlife systems and therefore allow easier understanding and storage
of contextual information (Mathiassen et al., 2000).
OOP emphasizes the organization of software as a collection of objects that contain
both data and methods. It employs principles like abstraction to enhance code
reusability, maintainability, and flexibility (Asagba and Ogheneovo, 2008). It facilitates
the development of software in modules, where each module should be mostly
independent from other modules. To make use and testing routines for individual model
blocks easier (Pauliuk and Heeren, 2020).
Structured or procedural programming is another programming paradigm that focuses
on breaking down a programãs functionality into smaller, manageable sections or
modules. It emphasizes the use of control structures like sequences, selections (ifôÙelse
statements), and iterations (loops) to streamline the flow of control within a program.
This paradigm avoids the use of unrestricted branching to enhance code readability,
ease of maintenance, and debugging (Asagba and Ogheneovo, 2008).

independently managed by the programmer. It handles data, logic, and application
rules. The view contains the functionality that is responsible for representing information
visually, which can include charts, diagrams, or tables. It communicates primarily with
the controller, which acts as an intermediary between the view and model. The
controller takes user inputs and translates them into commands for either the model or
view. The interactions among these components are defined in the design (Mufid et al.,
2019).
Specifically, the user inputs data in the controller, which then validates it, and sends a
request to the model. The model receives input from the controller and sends a
response back to the controller. The controller then passes the information to the view
and updates figures and table with the relevant information, which then is visible to the
user.
The implementation of the MVC pattern can be adapted to fit specific system
requirements, as the communication pattern between the three layers can be adjusted
to fulfill the technical and functional requirements of the solution. An example of the
paths between the three layers used in an MVC pattern could look like it is illustrated in
Figure 2.2. The figure compares this structure with an example of a single layer code
pattern, where all the model functions are entangled and dependent on each other, also

2.5
Optimization Models
Another approach for evaluating scenarios within a MFA, is by coupling it with
mathematical optimization. Mathematical optimization involves the systematic process
of maximizing or minimizing an objective function while adhering to a defined set of
constraints. The aim is to find the most optimal solution or configuration within the given
parameters, whether itãs maximizing recycled content, or minimizing costs or
environmental impact of a given system. It is about achieving the best possible outcome
within the specified limitations.
Typically when constructing a prospective dynamic MFA analysis, results are
considered for a limited set of preôÙdefined scenarios. Alternative could be to develop a
tool to systematically optimize scenarios, by allowing a optimization tool to choose the
prefered path under a set of conditions (Vadenbo et al., 2014a).
The following section describes some available optimization models related to resource
systems. The current version of the pyMFA model presented in the following chapters of
this thesis does not apply optimization, but these models present possibilities for further
development of pyMFA. This will be followed up on in chapter 6.
2.5.1
Waste InputôÙoutput Linear Programming Model (WIOôÙLP)
The waste inputôÙoutput linear programming (WIOôÙLP) model applies linear programming
techniques to waste management systems, focusing on inputôÙoutput analysis. The
linear programming model is an extension of the waste inputôÙoutput (WIO) model
presented by Nakamura and Kondo (2002), which offers a structured framework to
select the most suitable waste management and recycling strategy from a range of
feasible alternatives (Kondo and Nakamura, 2005).
It aims to optimize resource utilization and waste management by considering the flows
of materials within an economy. The model typically incorporates constraints related to
material flows, resource availability, and waste generation to find optimal solutions. Its is
used within waste management, environmental economics, and resource allocation
studies (Kondo and Nakamura, 2005).
2.5.2
Dynamic Optimization Model for Solid Waste Recycling
This model is a dynamic decision model, that optimize the logistics of solid waste
recycling operations in real time. For example considering changes in citizens behavior,
that affects the amount of solid waste produced. The objective function of the
optimization model is minimizing the system costs. The model is integrated in a
GISôÙbased Decision Support System (DSS), which estimates the waste generation
(Anghinolfi et al., 2013).
2.5.3
MultiôÙobjective MixedôÙinteger Linear Programming (MILP)
This optimization model is aimed as a decision support tool for waste and resource
management in industrial networks. By combining MFA, LCA, specific process models
and mathematical optimization in one it aims at optimizing both the economic and
environmental performance of a given system simultaneously. The model consist of four

main blocks of equations. These are the functional unit and reference flows, mass
balances, operational constraints and objective function (Vadenbo et al., 2014b).
2.5.4
Solid Waste Optimization LifeôÙcycle Framework in Python (SwolfPy)
Solid waste optimization lifeôÙcycle framework in Python (SwolfPy) is an openôÙsource
framework developed by Sardarmehni et al. (2022) for optimizing solid waste
management systems. The framework is designed to address the challenges of solid
waste management and recycling by providing a comprehensive and dynamic
optimization model. SwolfPy integrates various aspects of solid waste management,
including waste collection, recycling, treatment, and disposal, and aims to optimize the
entire lifecycle of solid waste. The framework leverages Python programming language
to provide a flexible and customizable platform for modeling and optimizing solid waste
management strategies. SwolfPy is designed to support multiôÙobjective optimization,
enabling decisionôÙmakers to consider various factors such as environmental impact,
costôÙeffectiveness, and resource efficiency in the optimization process (Sardarmehni
et al., 2022).

3.2
Development Approach
The development of the software pyMFA initially relied on procedural matrices for
computation, which followed a structured programming paradigm. This code resembled
the structure of the mathematical calculations behind the designed model but made it
almost impossible to add additional functionalities. Therefore, different programming
paradigms and software architectures were considered for the further development.
Transitioning from the more engineerôÙoriented, mathematically approach to a more
objectôÙoriented programming style, also affected the structure of the model. While the
use of objectôÙoriented programming is common in software development, procedural
methods are typically used in data science and can be easier to develop initially when
starting out from mathematical calculations. Some of pyMFAãs methods still use
procedural elements, but the code slowly shifted towards an objectôÙoriented approach to
overcome limitations associated with matrixôÙbased procedures. Specific challenges
emerged with the dynamic stock model and recycling pathways for various materials
within the structured procedural paradigm. ObjectôÙoriented programming, often abstract
and less intuitive to nonôÙsoftware developers due to its reliance on ãobjectsã for data
storage, facilitated a more sophisticated and streamlined model, accommodating
complexity while disentangling functionalities.
To improve the structure of the code and the model, a classic MVC architecture was
chosen as a guideline for further development. A lot of effort was invested in starting the
refactoring of the existing project to separate functionalities belonging to either the
model, view, or controller components into independent methods. pyMFA does not yet
fully apply the desired MVC architecture and objectôÙoriented programming paradigm.
However, the current version has certain advantages. As pyMFA has been developed
with engineers and data scientists in mind, the combination of procedural and
objectôÙoriented elements and primary focus on usage of tabular structures eases the
comprehension for a user group that is used to working in Excel. The tool still
accomplishes the desired tasks, and the current structure is flexible to allow adding of
new functionalities.

Model Calibration
Sometimes there might be incongruities between the data input to the use process in
historic years, versus the data input to the use process in modeling years. In this case
the dynamic stock model might overestimate or underestimate the mass flows entering
the inôÙuse stock in historic years. In the case of overestimation, the mass flows in
historic years into use will be higher than in modeling years, and ãstock values might
be negative in the first few modeling years, even though consumption is growing. This
depends on the lifetime of the materials in question. For a material with a short lifetime,
this has a higher effect on the results, as a higher percentage of the material is released
quicker, providing a peak in the results. Whereas a material with a longer lifetime,
provides a smoother transitioning from historic years to modeling years, as the release
of material is spread out over more years.
Therefore it is recommended to start the model at least one year prior to the intended
model period, to avoid overôÙ or underestimation.

6.1.1
Case Study Results
When developing pyMFA it was valuable to have a relevant case to reproduce results
from, in order to make sure the model followed standards within the field, and produced
the expected output which could then be verified against the specific data of the existing
study. The background for choosing the case study scenarios relied on various
assumptions. These scenarios emerged to address specific aspects of the evaluated
resource system. It is essential to note that these scenarios are based on data from
2016, and are considered mainly for model performance purposes. They suggest
potential outcomes from a range of specific system changes. This section elaborates on
the results from these scenarios.
The reference scenario (S0) was chosen because Eriksen et al. (2020) was an
interesting case to investigate, and since the study was conducted at DTU data was
highly available. As also outlined in chapter 2, plastic is an interesting case to
investigate, specifically PET, as it has a high potential for increased recycling, which due
to current waste management and consumption patterns remains untapped.
When comparing the indicator results in Eriksen et al. (2020), the study found a
recycling rate (RR) around 16%, a circular material use rate (CMUR) of around 15%
and closedôÙloop circularity rate (CLCR) around 9% for PET in the baseline scenario.
These numbers corresponds to the indicator results from pyMFA of 17% for RR, 15% for
CMUR, 9% for CLCR in S0. This indicates that the pyMFA model satisfactorily replicates
the results from Eriksen et al. (2020). The slight difference can be explained by the
following differences: Firstly, the numeric data from Eriksen et al. (2020) is read from a
graph, since the actual numbers have not been listed in a data table. Secondly, the
study considered additional sectors in their scenario (agriculture, automobile and other).
These sectors where left out of the scenario data for this project, as they only accounted
for around 1.5% of the total amount of PET.
PET plastic products, especially in the packaging sector, are often singleôÙuse products,
which is especially problematic in a system with a low recycling rate of materials.
Considering the limited resources available, they should be regarded of having higher

The second alternative scenario (S2: Closed loop) explores a situation where, in

not necessarily represent the most efficient use of resources. Conversely, the findings
suggest that concentrating on recycling materials at the bottom of the cascade could

6.2
Limitations and Future Model Improvements
The current version of the pyMFA exhibits several limitations, highlighting the fact that
this is the first publicly released version of this openôÙsource tool.
6.2.1
Lack of Uncertainty Handling and Automated Checks
One prominent drawback is the absence of automated mass balance checks. Without
such checks, potential errors in input data may lead to inaccurate results without any
warning to users. Furthermore, the model lacks functionalities to handle data
uncertainties, leaving uncertainty propagation and sensitivity analysis to be manually
conducted in external tools like Excel.

Uncertainties in input data propagate through the model, amplifying uncertainties in
output data. Sensitivity analysis is crucial to identify which sections of the model are
most sensitive to changes in input data, thereby highlighting the need for highôÙquality
data in those areas.
Future iterations should include a feature that automatically alerts users if mass balance
conditions, such as transfer coefficients and partitioning coefficients, are not met.
6.2.2
Full ModelôÙViewôÙController Architecture
Transitioning to a full modelôÙviewôÙcontroller (MVC) architecture involves refactoring
remaining procedural code into objectôÙoriented structures. This restructuring promises
improved model flow and allows an easier implementation of additional features, such
as commandôÙline argument support or additional visualization customization options
(e.g. sizing and coloring of sankey charts). The main outstanding tasks to complete for
a transition to a more compartmentalized MVC architecture are:
ãÂ Moving all functionalities related to the visualization, such as the sankey and Excel
generating methods into a separate View class.
ãÂ Moving the currently classôÙless method to retrieve the configuration file as well as
some of the input data parsing from the Model class initialization into a separate
Controller class.
6.2.3
Constraints and Indicators
Integration of constraints such as limits on recycled material in new products,
consideration of purity decrease with increased collection, and processôÙspecific mass
capacities would enhance the modelãs accuracy. Additionally, automatic calculation of
specific evaluation indicators, though variable across studies, could be a valuable
addition.
6.2.4
Optimization Integration
Integrating optimization methodologies within the model represents a significant
enhancement. The implementation could leverage various Python libraries supporting
mathematical optimization. Optimization offers a broader exploration of scenarios,
suggesting pathways potentially overlooked in specific chosen scenarios.
The possibility of integrating pyMFA with optimization tools presents a noteworthy
advancement. Tools like Waste InputôÙOutput Linear Programming (WIOôÙLP), Dynamic
Optimization Model for Solid Waste Recycling, MultiôÙobjective MixedôÙInteger Linear
Programming (MILP) Optimization Model, and Solid Waste Optimization LifeôÙCycle
Framework in Python (SwolfPy) enable multiôÙobjective optimization in complex systems.
The integration with optimization tools could significantly contribute to rethinking
scenario analysis, by allowing the tool to suggest the preferable path. However, these
tools face challenges related to resource efficiency and circular economy tradeôÙoffs.
They need to address limitations of linear economic systems and finite resources,
considering tradeôÙoffs between circularity and resource efficiency. Careful consideration
of tradeôÙoffs and limitations remains pivotal in leveraging these tools effectively for
advancing resource management and circularity objectives.
6.2.5
Calibration in Dynamic Stock Model
Calibration of models often occurs in the transition from a historic period, with realôÙlife
data to a model period that estimates values into the future. The pyMFA model needs to
calibrate in the dynamic stock model. This calibration depends on the available input
data. The input data into the calculation of stock release from historic years, might not
41
==PAGE:46==

correspond to the input data into calculation of stock release in modeling years. In the
case study used in this thesis, only the production data was available for historic years,
which was used to calculate release from stock, as Eriksen et al. (2020) also did.
Whereas in the modeling years, release from stock was calculates from materials
entering the useôÙphase after manufacturing. Hence the manufacturing loss is not
accounted for in historic years. This meant that even though production was increased,
the inôÙuse stock decreased in the first modeling timesteps, for the packaging materials,
due to their short lifetime in use. This provided an misleading results in the first
modeling timestep. This issue was addressed by starting the model a year earlier than
planned, in year 2019. This means the that variables in year 2019 was modeled instead
of using the historic data.
Another way to address this could be to assume a manufacturing loss in all the historic
timesteps, corresponding to the manufacturing loss in the modeling years. This was not
the selected approach in the conducted case study, as it would lead to other issues:
Firstly, in terms of accounting for the loss of material elsewhere in the system, secondly,
because implementing a loss would add extra uncertainty to the results, as the
assumed loss might be further away from what happened in reality.
6.3
Complexity Feeds Complexity
In the period of this project, it was discovered that adding new attributes to the model
could have several impacts. The change might require an output or data format that
was not necessary for previous model attributes. Furthermore, it could negatively affect
the ability to add needed features, if it is found that the data structure needs to be
refactored to support them. This occurred during the development on the initial version
which was more heavily utilizing the Python library NumPy, but it lacked certain
functionalities for restructuring and handling data to be included in the dynamic stock
model. Here, the Python package pandas proved more suitable for the efficient
development of desired features.
This emphasizes the need for inôÙdepth planning in advance, when wishing to develop a
new model. It is a good idea to visualize desired model aspects and what inputs and
outputs are needed to complete this. However, in a case where the learning curve has
been as steep as in this project, with somewhat limited programming skills prior to this
project, it is important to consider, that trying something, and failing, is just as essential
as planning ahead. Therefore, the planning and development phase of the project, was
done concurrently. With a mix between considering what the desired output should be,
and what actually succeeded.
It should also be taken into account that adding more complexity to the model will add
complexity for the user. It would have limited relevance to create a model, that will
potentially not be used. Either because it is to hard to understand for new users, or
because it is too simple, so that it does not add value and does not save time for users,
as opposed to doing the same study in Excel for example. If the use of the model is too
challenging, this could as a minimum lead to mistrust in the model results, and that
users might not benefit from the full potential of the model. Another possibility, is that
users give up before they even get any results from the model.
This is why a large focus in this study, has been to find a simple format for the input
data, and to limit the amount of programming skills needed, just to run the model.

6.4
Model Usage
As mentioned in chapter 1 setting recycling targets is a tool often used in policyôÙmaking.
Targets work as a tool for tracking efforts towards a certain goal, but do not accomplish
the goal by itself (Kahlert and Bening, 2022). Targets need to be supported by
additional measures, and in order to identify the extend of the measures needed, an
MFA tool like pyMFA can be relevant, to identify the effectiveness of individual or
combined measures.
This project only evaluated simple system changes in each of the scenarios. This was
due to the fact, that the scenario analysis was used to highlight the performance of
pyMFA, and not to suggest a way to reach a specific target. When used for
policyôÙmaking multiple scenarios should be investigated, to shows the effect of a system
change across a range of different values. For example, illustrating the effect of
increased collection with multiple collection schemes. Furthermore, combined scenarios
should also be explored, as the results from the case study emphasize.
Another inspiration for the development of this MFA tool stemmed from practical
experience within a consultancy setting. One of the critical challenges encountered in
such an environment involves optimizing the time and resources allocated to each
project. There is a need to streamline processes to enhance efficiency, but this requires
competences for creation of tools designed to minimize redundant efforts.
Consultancy work often involves tasks that overlap or share similarities between
different projects. Therefore, developing a versatile tool capable of handling repetitive
tasks efficiently was seen as pivotal to optimizing productivity. By implementing a
PythonôÙbased solution, tailored functionalities can be programmed to automate routine
processes, drastically reducing the time and effort expended on these recurrent tasks.
Moreover, within the consultancy landscape and among their pertinent clients, Excel
remains a cornerstone for data analysis and reporting. Recognizing this, the necessity
arose for a tool that seamlessly integrates with Excel. The ability of the tool to import
data from spreadsheets, perform complex calculations, and generate outputs in the
form of ExcelôÙcompatible sheets significantly enhances its utility and accessibility within
professional settings. By providing a tool that aligns with the prevailing practices and
tools of the industry the intention is to enhance workflow efficiency, minimize
redundancy, and ultimately elevate the overall quality and speed of project deliverables.
In scientific research, the utilization of tools capable of handling complex data
processing, statistical analysis, and visualization is paramount. Pythonãs extensive
libraries and capabilities make it an ideal platform for scientific computations and
analysis. Within scientific research, a toolãs ability to calculate and visualize results from
multiple scenarios faster, aids researchers in adding valuable insights efficiently.
Furthermore, the reproducability and automation capabilities benefit scientific research,
where there is a high emphasis on transparency and replicability.
While both consultancy and scientific research could benefit from the efficiency,
automation, and compatibility with a generic tool, there could by some differences in the
functionalities required. In scientific research, a toolãs focus should by on empowering
researchers with robust computational and analytical capabilities, fostering
reproducability, and facilitating customized solutions for diverse research needs. While
consultancies benefit more from optimizing efficiency and reducing repetition.

6.5
Relevance of pyMFA for Future Research Projects
One notable discovery in this project is the absence of a widely used, generic tool
designed for executing dynamic MFA in Python, within the scientific community
dedicated to resource management.
When reviewing available MFA tools, it became evident that a limited number of publicly
downloadable tools are established and actively used, and STAN seems to be the only
commonly used software. However, STAN has its limitations, for example in regard to
its static nature. Another tool, ODYM (Pauliuk and Heeren, 2020), takes an approach
exhibiting complexity and potential limitations in accessibility to a broader user base. A
lesson of this project has been that it is indeed challenging to find a balance between
userôÙfriendliness and the complexity and depth of outputs, when creating a model.
When publishing a tool for a larger user base in an openôÙsource format on e.g. GitHub,
the success of the project also depends on the existence of a developer and
collaboration network behind the project to broaden its use. It is challenging to establish
new software in a research community, but this is required to have a successful
openôÙsource project which highly depends on the interaction and coôÙdevelopment of
multiple peers. As long as this is not given, many researchers will return to developing
their own tool, either because existing software does not meet their unique
requirements or because existing projects are too hard to comprehend and further
develop for their research. There is a need for a MFA tool that supports a more generic
model, which is userôÙfriendly, and can be easily adapted and customized.
This thesis project introduces a tool for dynamic MFA, named pyMFA, which was
developed by the author in Python and has been publicly and independently released
under an openôÙsource license on GitHub. This allows the community to access and
further develop the tool or collaborate on the addition of functionalities. Furthermore,
Python provides a good platform for creating a generic MFA tool, because it has many
libraries and packages available for performing calculations and visualizations required
for this kind of model. Once data is collected and a system has been set up, it provides
a quick way to calculate and visualize results from multiple scenarios.
It was found that a more objectôÙoriented approach provides more flexibility in the model
functionalities. Especially regarding the visualization of sankey charts, which require
more manual inputs from the user, compared to the other parts of pyMFA.
The case study that was used to evaluate the tool was based on a relatively small data
set, but the use of a Python model as opposed to Excel or STAN becomes increasingly
more effective when data amounts become larger.
The introduced model and tool should however be considered as a proofôÙofôÙconcept,
and the author acknowledges that current version of the tool does not yet provide a fully
generic approach. Even though pyMFA has not reached this goal of a generic model yet,
this is the vision behind the tool and the basic architecture provides the needed
foundation. Future development can help to advance this project further towards this
goal and enable its use across various MFAôÙbased research initiatives.
To prevent a situation where every individual recognizing the necessity for such a tool
independently develops their own version, collaborative efforts among multiple research
institutions are essential. These collaborations would be crucial for the continued
development and expansion of pyMFA.

Conclusion
This thesis introduces pyMFA, an openôÙsource PythonôÙbased tool that provides a
programmatic model designed for dynamic Material Flow Analysis (MFA) of circular
resource systems. The primary goal was to implement a model in Python which would
be capable of conducting dynamic MFA. This has been successfully realized. This
achievement highlights the authors advancement in programming skills through the
development of this model. This process has not only expanded technical skills but has
also provided a platform for continued learning and development in programming.
Furthermore, the evaluation of the modelãs performance, particularly in its application to
the case study involving PET packaging and textiles in Europe from 2020 to 2030, has
showcased its application. The modelãs ability to analyze and illustrate the flow of
materials within this specific context demonstrates its practical utility and relevance in
realôÙworld scenarios.
In regard to the results from the case study, it was shown that it requires a joint effort to
reach recycling targets, in line with current implementation of new regulations. It will not
suffice to rely entirely on consumer behavioral change or implementation of new
recycling facilities, a combination of multiple strategies needs to be employed.
Additionally, the results suggest that prioritizing closedôÙloop recycling may not always
lead to the most efficient use of resources.
The investigation conducted to identify potential approaches for further development of
the model and pyMFA has yielded valuable insights. These insights will serve as a
foundation for future enhancements and refinements, thereby ensuring the modelãs
adaptability and effectiveness in addressing evolving challenges within circular resource
systems.
In summary, this thesis has successfully achieved its objectives of creating a functional
PythonôÙbased dynamic MFA model, enhancing proficiency in Python programming,
evaluating the modelãs performance through a case study, and outlining potential
avenues for its future development.
