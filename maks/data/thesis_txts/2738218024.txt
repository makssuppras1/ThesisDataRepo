housing 4.0.

Introduction
1.1
Background
According to Tutam (2022), Industry 4.0 significantly improves production efficiency and
flexibility through the adoption of advanced technologies such as the Internet of Things
(IoT), big data analytics, artificial intelligence (AI), cloud computing, 5G communication
technology, blockchain, and automation equipment. As a result, it sets higher demands
for the intelligence of logistics. To match efficient production, logistics systems need to
achieve synchronized efficiency, enhance supply chain flexibility, increase transparency
and traceability, optimize resource utilization, support fully automated production proôÙ
cesses, and meet personalized customer needs.
Logistics 4.0 is the inevitable outcome of the development of Industry 4.0. By means of
realôÙtime monitoring, dynamic adjustments, automated operations, and data integration,
Logistics 4.0 ensures high efficiency, flexibility, and intelligence throughout the supply
chain, comprehensively enhancing the competitiveness and operational efficiency of enôÙ
terprises. Consequently, Logistics 4.0 necessitates warehouse systems to be mobile, auôÙ
tonomous, compact, or collaborative to improve system or workforce productivity, thereby
reducing the continuously rising labor costs. This has led to the development of WareôÙ
housing 4.0.
Warehouse 4.0 includes intelligent, automated, and interconnected systems, transforming
the interaction between humans and robots within the warehouse. Robots and humans
work together in a safer, more flexible, and more efficient warehouse environment. The
comprehensive automation in Warehousing 4.0 heavily relies on the extensive application
of HumanôÙRobot Collaboration (HRC).
In Warehousing 4.0, HRC enhances safety greatly. Through task sharing, robots handle
repetitive, heavy, or dangerous tasks, reducing the physical burden on human workers
and improving the work environment. Robots equipped with advanced sensors and safety
protocols monitor the workspace and provide realôÙtime alerts, reducing accident risks. AdôÙ
ditionally, robots help lift heavy items, further reducing the risk of injuries. The integration
of machine learning enables robots to learn from human actions and feedback, gradually
improving safety measures.
Meanwhile, implementing HRC in warehouses presents significant safety challenges.
Traditional safety approaches often rely on physical barriers and separation between huôÙ
mans and robots. ISO 3691ôÙ4:2023 specifies that protective devices should comply with
ISO 12100:2010 and ISO 14120:2015 standards. However, in the dynamic and interactive
settings of Warehouse 4.0, where humanôÙrobot coexistence, synchronized cooperation,
and especially collaboration occur, regular frameworks and current practices, such as
using barriers, are no longer suitable.
Despite some safety requirements outlined in ISO 3691ôÙ4:2023 for driverless industrial
trucks and ISO/TS 15066:2016 for collaborative robots, these standards have many shortôÙ
comings. Specifically, ISO 3691ôÙ4:2023 lacks provisions for other types of collaborative
robots and fails to consider human factors and psychological safety in HRC. Meanwhile,
ISO/TS 15066:2016, being a nonôÙmandatory technical specification, does not fully adôÙ
dress the complexity of all HRC scenarios and lacks detailed guidelines for ensuring opôÙ
erator psychological safety and comfort.(Bjoern et al., 2016)

In conclusion, traditional safety approaches do not work anymore, and existing safety

1.2
Research objectives
The purpose of this project is to apply a functional hazard identification method to find
the limitations of existing relevant standards in Warehousing 4.0 and to identify functional
requirements to improve the safety of HRC in warehouses. These identified functional reôÙ
quirements will be validated and refined through simulation, aiming to reduce risks during
the design phase.
1.3
Research scope
For achieving the research objectives, this project will follow the below steps.
First, the literature review will identify suitable methods for safety verification in humanôÙ
robot collaboration within Warehouse 4.0. This review will examine relevant standards reôÙ
lated to humanôÙrobot collaboration and humanôÙrobot collisions to ensure compliance with
EU and Danish regulations. Following this, a hypothetical automated warehouse setting
will be defined, followed by hazard identification and proposed functional requirements.
Specific scenarios will then be chosen based on riskôÙbased criteria. Next, simulation softôÙ
ware, such as Isaac Sim, CoppeliaSim, and Webots, will be evaluated and used. Based
on relevant standards, the tolerant navigating speed of the robot when colliding with a
human will be calculated by determining the maximum permissible pressure for different
parts of the human body. Finally, simulation verification of the calculated results will be
performed.

2.1
Regulatory framework and current practices
The development and implementation of safety standards are essential for ensuring the
safe design, manufacturing, and operation of machinery and industrial systems. Various
international standards provide comprehensive guidelines to enhance machinery safety.
ISO 12100:2010 establishes the foundation for machinery safety, emphasizing risk asôÙ
sessment and mitigation strategies. IEC 61508:2000 offers a framework for the functional
safety of electrical and electronic systems, ensuring their reliability. ISO 13849ôÙ1:2015
and IEC 62061:2005 focus on the safety of machinery control systems, defining perforôÙ
mance requirements and implementation principles. ISO 13855:2010 defines safe disôÙ
tances from machinery, while ISO 13850:2015 specifies emergency stop functions. ISO
13851:2019 sets requirements for safeguards and interlocks. For industrial robotics, ISO
10218ôÙ1:2011 and ISO 10218ôÙ2:2011 provide guidelines for safe design and collaborative
operations.
Besides the general standards mentioned, ISO/TS 15066:2016 supplements the requireôÙ
ments for collaborative robot operations defined in ISO 10218, offering detailed guidelines
for designing and implementing collaborative robots. It specifies four primary collaborative
modesãSafetyôÙRated Monitored Stop, Hand Guiding, Speed and Separation Monitoring,
and Power and Force Limitingãto facilitate safe HRC.
DS/EN ISO 3691ôÙ4:2023 addresses the safety requirements and verification for driverless
industrial trucks, such as AGVs and autonomous mobile robots (AMRs). This standard
includes requirements for braking systems, speed control, load handling, and protective
devices to ensure safe operation in warehouse environments, reflecting the increasing
adoption of automation in industrial settings.
Although ISO/TS 15066:2016 provides guidelines for the design and scaling of collaboraôÙ
tive robot applications, extensive validation work is still required during actual deployment.
More experience needs to be accumulated, particularly in measuring or calculating the
physical characteristics of contact between robots and humans. This means that while
ISO/TS 15066:2016 offers theoretical guidance, the effectiveness and reliability of these
guidelines in practical applications still need further validation and refinement.(Bjoern et
al., 2016)
Aayush et al.(2022) found additional challenges in the application of HRC beyond those
already mentioned. They found that higher productivity often results in less control over
operator safety in HRC systems. They also noted that current HRC systems are limited
in their application within complex industrial environments. Furthermore, the terminology
used to describe collaboration is not standardized, which makes the design of these sysôÙ
tems challenging. Additionally, there is a lack of application knowledge and reference
cases for implementing HRC. As collaboration levels increase, the likelihood of unintenôÙ
tional contact also rises, necessitating validation through biomechanical tests to establish
force and pressure limits during collisions.
Overall, even if relevant standards are followed, risks such as insufficient safety controls,
lack of clear guidance, and technical challenges persist. Therefore, it is crucial to conôÙ
duct further theoretical research to address these gaps. Enhanced understanding and

3.2.2
FTA
By using FôÙCHIA to identify the severe hazard event, the next step is to visualize and
refine the results of FôÙCHIA through FTA.
1. Define the Top Event: Identify the system and its critical functions, defining the priôÙ
mary manifestation of system failure (top event).
2. Construct the Fault Tree: Start with the top event, break it down into direct causes
(intermediate events) using logic gates (AND, OR), until reaching basic events (comôÙ
ponent failures or external factors).
3. Qualitative Analysis: Examine the fault tree structure to identify weak points, critical
paths, and key events in the system.
4. Quantitative Analysis: Perform a qualitative analysis to identify the minimal cut sets,
collect fault data for basic events, calculate the probability of the top event, and use
probabilistic methods to analyze the contributions of each event to the top event.
5. Evaluation and Improvement: Assess the systemãs fault risks and propose mitigation
measures, such as adding redundancy, replacing components with higher reliability,
and improving maintenance strategies.
Using FTA allows for systematic analysis and understanding of fault mechanisms within
a system, aiding in the development of effective risk management strategies to enhance
system safety and reliability.
3.2.3
Summary of hazard identification methods
By using FôÙCHIA, potential risks in HRC within the warehouse are functionally identified.
FTA is then utilized to visually represent the identified top event, breaking it down into
hardware, software, and human factors to illustrate the logical relationships between failôÙ
ure events. Qualitative analysis in FTA identifies and prioritizes critical failure points and
paths, while quantitative analysis, through minimal cut sets, pinpoints the smallest combiôÙ
nations of failures leading to the top event, calculates the probability and impact of failures,
and provides numerical data on the severity of risks.

Conclusion
This project develops a functionôÙbased safety analysis for HRC in Warehousing 4.0 enôÙ
vironments. The primary objective was to apply a robust hazard identification method
that reduces the gaps in existing standards and current applications. This conclusion
summarizes the key findings and implications of the work.
This project enhanced Hazard Identification. By employing the FôÙCHIA with FTA, systemôÙ
atically and functionally identified potential hazards in HRC scenarios. This method alôÙ
lowed for a comprehensive functional deviation analysis, ensuring that all possible sources
of risk were considered and addressed.
This project excuted a simulationôÙbased verification: The use of simulation tools, such
as Webots, provided valuable insights into the realôÙtime interactions between humans
and robots. The simulations confirmed that the calculated collision forces and operational
speeds were within acceptable limits for human safety under specified conditions. HowôÙ
ever, limitations in accurately replicating human body inertia highlighted areas for further
improvement of human body models.
This project found that current safety standards, such as ISO/TS 15066 and ISO 3691ôÙ4,
have significant shortcomings in addressing the complexities of HRC in dynamic environôÙ
ments. For example, using the force formula described in ISO/TS 15066:2016 to estimate
pressure is insufficient. They are not considering the contact area and uneven distribuôÙ
tion of force during impact. Therefore, the proposed functional safety requirements aim
to bridge these gaps, ensuring more effective and comprehensive safety measures. The
functional requirements are as follows:
1. Localization
ãÂ Set up redundant sensors and install additional positioning cameras in the
warehouse to keep the map error within an acceptable range and ensure there
are no visual blind spots.
2. Navigate to goods pickup shelf
ãÂ Develop a userôÙfriendly interface that reduces the likelihood of incorrect comôÙ
mand inputs by providing clear options, predictive text, and errorôÙchecking
functionalities.
ãÂ Provide workers with special uniforms that protect against serious injury upon
collision, do not hinder task performance, and are easily detectable by sensors.
ãÂ Use multiple obstacle avoidance algorithms in parallel to detect obstacles.
ãÂ Define worker permissions.
3. Navigate to the charging station to charge
ãÂ Set a reasonable battery threshold and issue timely alerts.
ãÂ Install sufficient charging stations to ensure efficiency.
ãÂ Implement multiple charging methods to ensure MiR100 always has access to
power.

ãÂ Set predefined speed limits for different areas within the operating environment,

ãÂ Set height restrictions for items on the rack to ensure they do not fall out due

6. Load goods
ãÂ Monitor the gripper pressure to ensure it is functioning properly, and set up an
