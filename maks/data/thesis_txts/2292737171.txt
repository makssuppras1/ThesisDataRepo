Wind energy today is a mature and viable option for energy production 
and a better estimate of the true wind power potential for the whole 
world will help utilizing it. The Global Wind Atlas (GWA) is a detailed 
wind resource assessment from DTU covering all land masses (except 
Antarctica) and a 30km offshore zone. It will give a consistent wind 
resources estimation using publicly accessible data for validation.  
In this study, the validated Wind Atlas for South Africa was used to 
suggest different ways to use grid data towards validation and 
uncertainty estimation of the GWA. Approaches to compare and 
visualize grid data with scatter plots take different views on ranking and 
aggregation and show differences in power density for the windiest 
areas due to lower mesoscale resolution. Spectral comparison of the 
wind speeds show some agreement on scales below 3km and missing 
variance on scales above 20km.  
To test the ability of the GWA to identify regions of interest for wind 
energy, we introduced a threshold in the comparison. The result is a 
more qualitative approach using contingency maps and performance 
parameters that leads to reduction of complexity, offering powerful 
tools due to their simplicity. Furthermore, the prospective of uncertainty 
estimation from grid data is explored in a prestudy using WAsP and 
found not to be directly applicable to the GWA. We suggest a more 
qualitative approach using uncertainty flags. 
In summary, our study found that high resolution grid data is found to 
be a valid source for comparison for wind atlas studies. The large 
number points in the comparison of the resource grids can be reduced 
to focus on a certain aspects based on a more aggregated view.

Abstract
Wind energy today is a mature and viable option for energy production and
a better estimate of the true wind power potential for the whole world will
help utilizing it. The Global Wind Atlas (GWA) is a detailed wind resource
assessment from DTU covering all land masses (except Antarctica) and a 30km
oﬀshore zone. It will give a consistent wind resources estimation using publicly
accessible data for validation.
In this study, the validated Wind Atlas for South Africa was used to suggest
diﬀerent ways to use grid data towards validation and uncertainty estimation of
the GWA. Approaches to compare and visualize grid data with scatter plots take
diﬀerent views on ranking and aggregation and show diﬀerences in power density
for the windiest areas due to lower mesoscale resolution. Spectral comparison of
the wind speeds show some agreement on scales below 3km and missing variance
on scales above 20km.
To test the ability of the GWA to identify regions of interest for wind energy,
we introduced a threshold in the comparison. The result is a more qualitative
approach using contingency maps and performance parameters that leads to
reduction of complexity, oﬀering powerful tools due to their simplicity. Further-
more, the prospective of uncertainty estimation from grid data is explored in a
prestudy using WAsP and found not to be directly applicable to the GWA. We
suggest a more qualitative approach using uncertainty ﬂags.
In summary, our study found that high resolution grid data is found to be
a valid source for comparison for wind atlas studies. The large number points
in the comparison of the resource grids can be reduced to focus on a certain
aspects based on a more aggregated view.

1
Introduction
Over the last decades wind energy has matured to a viable option for energy
production but to fully exploit wind energy resources worldwide a better esti-
mate of the true wind power potential for the whole world is necessary. To date,
only coarse data is available, cf. Figure 1.1. This data is not appropriate to esti-
mate the true potential of wind energy. A wide range of methods are applied to
estimate the wind energy potential. The report on wind energy from the Inter-
governmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) estimates a global wind enrgy
potential in the range from 19400TWh to 125000TWh [23]. This number de-
pends on many assumptions about technology, wind data and other constraints.
Therefore, ”there is no single ’correct’ estimate of technical potential.” [23].

ground level. The data has a resolution of 2.5◦× 2.5◦

The Global Wind Atlas (GWA) is a new data set especially designed for
wind energy purposes.
Its dedicated goal is giving a better estimate of the
true wind power potential of the world. Such a publicly available data set like
this is highly needed for numerous aspects of wind energy promotion. Global
reanalysis data is used together with the FrogFoot microscale model to produce
a consistent data set, enabling comparability of wind resources over the whole
world. Section 2.1 shows an introduction to the GWA project.
In this thesis I will investigate how to compare the GWA with spatially
resolved data to towards providing an estimation of validity and uncertainties.
The GWA wind data will be a unique data set of wind resources over the whole
world. The goal of this thesis is to give new ideas about how to approach the
validation and uncertainty estimation of wind atlases using grid data.
1.1
Motivation
The Global Wind Atlas is a model output, as with every model it needs valida-
tion to add conﬁdence that its results are reliable: ”Validation is the assessment
of the accuracy of a computational simulation by comparison with experimental
data” [20]. A procedure to do this validation is necessary to use the GWA data.
Detailed national wind atlases are available for some regions of the world.
A lot of these wind atlases are numerical wind atlases modelled for example
with WRF or the KAMM/WAsP method [3]. Examples are the Finnish wind
atlas [16] or Wind Atlas for South Africa [15]. These national wind atlas projects
are validated with measurements from meteorological masts. Because they take
regional particularities into account they oﬀer a good estimations of regional
wind resources, cf. Section 2.6. The global scope of the GWA is too big to
have the same level of details. Since national wind atlases are adjusted for their
region and validated they could oﬀer a good source for comparison.
The Global Wind Atlas is intended to estimate the wind resources on an
aggregated scale. The end user will need information on the validity of these
estimates.
The uncertainty will vary with the location and the aggregation
scale. We need an approach that will give spatial estimates of the uncertainty
for diﬀerent aggregation levels. Thus, suggesting which aggregation scale can
be regarded as accurate within a region. This information enables better uncer-
tainty estimates for results derived from the GWA data and thus increases its
value for wind energy planning.
Resource maps from the GWA and other National Wind Atlases (NWAs) are
grid data. Validation is normally done for point measurements, cf Section 2.2
and 2.6. To use national wind atlas data we need methods to compare resource
grids designed for the validation of the GWA project. The documentation of
the GWA together with the validation procedure will be published to make it
transparent.

1.2
Aim of study
This thesis aims to investigate new approaches towards understanding and com-
paring grid data and demonstrates theses ideas alongside examples. It is in-
tended to take ﬁrst steps towards developing a method to use high resolution
grid data for the validation and uncertainty estimation for the Global Wind At-
las. This will be approached by comparing GWA grids with grids from the Wind
Atlas for South Africa. Ranking and aggregation of resource data will empha-
size diﬀerent aspects of the comparison. We will choose examples to show them
and discuss their advantages and disadvantages. By using spectra to investigate
diﬀerences between the data sets we aim at emphasizing diﬀerences between the
data sets and link them to their underlying methodology and input data.
We aim to map uncertainties of a resource assessment through the resource
grid of a more detailed study. A simple approach to uncertainty estimation will
be used to model grid uncertainties from a reference grid. A prestudy using a
WAsP environment will be used with diﬀerent inputs to generate resource grids
with characteristic diﬀerences in the input.
Taking into account the intended use of the GWA, we approach its valida-

Background and data
This thesis is embedded in the Global Wind Atlas project (GWA). For the GWA
approach diﬀerent sources of input data are needed. This chapter will introduce
the data used throughout the thesis and some important concept and ideas.
2.1
The GWA project
The global wind atlas will give a publicly available data set to estimate the
global wind resources. Its data together with the methods and validation will
be published. The project aims to close the gap between coarse global reanalysis
based wind data and regional studies of wind resources.
The Global Wind Atlas is a numerical wind atlas approach. Its approach
is similar to some national numerical wind atlases, but instead of running a
mesoscale model it uses reanalysis products for the entire world, cf. Section 2.4.
The reanalysis is generalized using global topography data. FrogFoot calculates
the small scale variation in the wind from generalized wind climates together
with topographical inputs. Thus, capturing wind resources from spatial and
temporal variance, cf. Sectction 1.3.
The goal of the Global Wind Atlas is similar to a national wind atlas only
for a global scale. The project intends to promote the use of wind as a source of
power and help energy planners to estimate the world’s wind resources better.
High quality of the data will be ensured through a transparent method and
validation process. It can serve to estimate wind resources in countries without
detailed national wind data. But it will also make wind resource estimation
comparable over the entire world [4].
Three diﬀerent data sets are needed for the creation of the Global wind atlas:
Reanalysis data, elevation map and roughness maps. Here is a short overview
of the data used in the calculation of GWA data examples for this thesis.
• Elevation data comes from the viewﬁnder project 1 from the SRTM
(Shuttle Radar Topography Mission).
• Roughness maps are available through global land cover maps from
GlobCover [25]. Land cover is converted to roughness lengths at 300m
resolution.
• Generalized reanalysis data can be derived from diﬀerent sources, for
example CFDDA, MERRA or ERA-Interim.

FrogFoot gives a calculation for the wind resources every 250m, giving a ﬁne

2.2
Validation
Before we start to talk about data and comparing data, clariﬁcation on the
meaning of validation is needed. The following quote and general considerations
on this are taken from [20]. Validation is: ”The process of determining the
degree to which a model is an accurate representation of the real world from
the perspective of the intended uses of the model”. Our best estimate of the
real world are measurements. Therefore, validation must connect the model to
measurements. Modelling always includes a simpliﬁcation of reality. We need
to take the use of our model into account when considering validations. The
model needs to be accurate within its intended use.
The term uncertainty is used with diﬀerent meanings in science.”Epistemic
uncertainty as a cause of nondeterministic behavior derives from some level of
ignorance or lack of knowledge about the system or the environment.” [20]. This
deﬁnition will not be used in a too strict term since there is also a contribution
of intrinsic uncertainty of wind resource assessment. The term error refers to
diﬀerences with no further speciﬁcation on their sources. We will use epistemic
uncertainty for a uncertainty model in Chapter 3.
Data like wind resources have a value for a given location. The most con-
ventional way to get the wind resources at one speciﬁc location and height is
to measure the wind speed at this point. If we make a model to predict the
wind resources, the best way to check would be a measurement at the point of
prediction. Resource grids give the wind resources for an entire area – thus,
comparing measurements at each point is not possible. Comparison at single
points aims to show the validity for the entire grid but the number of observa-
tions is often very limited. This is the validation normally used for wind atlas
projects, for example in Section 2.6.
The data used for the GWA project has been validated. The models used
for creating the data are veriﬁed (”The process of determining that a model
implementation accurately represents the developer’s conceptual description of
the model and the solution to the model.” [20]). Our concern is to make sure
that the input data and modelling gives an accurate description of reality.
2.3
Measurements and grid data
Measurement of wind is most frequently done with meteorological masts and
anemometers. They measure the wind speed over a long time period thus giving
the wind climate at this point. Measurements with met masts represent the wind
climate at a site very accurately when done correctly but they are very costly.
13
==PAGE:18==
A resource grid gives the wind resources in mean wind speed, power density or
annual energy production at a speciﬁc height. FrogFoot and WAsP resource are
calculated for points and grid cells are spaced around this point.
Resources from wind atlases calculated with FrogFoot have a lot of grid
cells. There are about O(1, 000, 000) grid cells in the resource grid of the WASA
project compared to only 10 measurements in the area. Using grid data for
validation purposes yields many more points to compare at while the data of
a measurement is very reliable. The diﬀerence in the number of data points
becomes important when we want to consider using grid data for validation.
Wind resource grids (over land) can not be directly measured. There are
a number of studies in form of national wind atlases that use mesoscale and
microscale modelling to estimate the wind resources in form of resource grids.
Over sea SAR data is available giving spatial resolved wind measurement.
2.4
Reanalysis data
We will not focus too much on the calculation of the reanalysis data sets them-
selves but since they are an important input to the GWA we will give a brief
introduction. Reanalysis data has been calculated in meteorology for the last
decades. With increasing computational power, the resolution of reanalysis data
has increased. Reanalysis data uses the similar but coarser resolution models as
weather forecasting systems (e.g. WRF). The models cover the synoptic scale
and part of the mesoscale. It is a model of the past weather coherent with ob-
servations and physical laws. ”Reanalysis data provide a multivariate, spatially
complete, and coherent record of the global atmospheric circulation” [10]. The
reanalysis is kept consistent with weather observations and give out time series
of atmospheric properties. Diﬀerent reanalysis data sets could be used for the
GWA but it will most likely be the CFDDA data with 40km horizontal grid
spacing covering the period from 1985 to 2005.
FrogFoot needs generalized wind climates for the ﬂow modelling. The time
series from the reanalysis data is generalized using the WAsP methodology. To-
pography maps for the generalization are consistent with the reanalysis data.
The eﬀect of the elevation is quite small and was neglected during the general-
ization of the CFDDA data.
2.5
FrogFoot
FrogFoot is a program that enables the use of multiple generalized wind climates
for a microscale ﬂow model. It uses a WAsP engine to compute the microscale
eﬀects of the terrain on the ﬂow. Therefore, limitations are linked to the WAsP
part of the calculation.
The generalized wind climates are interpolated and
given to the WAsP worker [2]. The interpolation of the generalized wind en-
ables FrogFoot to calculate the wind resources over a large area with changing
atmospheric forcing.
WAsP uses a linearised ﬂow model. This causes problems in complex terrain
since WAsP always assumes attached ﬂow [18]. This limitation translates to
FrogFoot as well. Stability in WAsP is modelled through an average heat ﬂux
and an RMS value. FrogFoot does not oﬀer changing stability over the resource
14
==PAGE:19==
grid. Until now only standard stability conditions are implemented. This adds
uncertainty to the FrogFoot calculation since stability greatly inﬂuences the
surface boundary layer [29].
2.6
The WASA project
National wind atlases are regional maps of wind resources. They can be based
purely on observations as an observational wind atlas or based on a mesoscale
simulation as a numerical wind atlas. Examples of observational based wind
atlases are the European wind atlas and for numerical wind atlases the Wind
Atlas for South Africa (WASA). The number of numerical wind atlases has
increased in the past years.
In recent years an increasing number of countries have undertaken studies of
their wind resources. They are used to identify the wind potential on a national
level. They help the energy authorities and the grid operators to plan the future
integration of wind power. Their accuracy for a speciﬁc site is not high enough
to use them to plan wind parks directly. They can be used for prefeasibility
studies in wind farm development before a site speciﬁc study.
The WASA project uses two diﬀerent mesoscale approaches [13]: 1) A WRF
model run with 3km horizontal resolution for a period of 8 years and creat-
ing generalized wind climates from these time series. 2) Statistical dynamical
downscaling using the KAMM/WAsP method where the wind climate is deter-
mined according to classes of geostrophic wind, wind direction and stability [11].
The result is detailed wind resource maps derived from numerical simulations.
For this study uses wind resource maps derived from the WRF run with 3km
horizontal resolution.
We want to support the validation process of the Global Wind Atlas. Valida-
tion ties the model to reality, cf. Section 2.2. Measurements of the wind speed
can be compared to the results of the numerical wind atlas giving an estimate
of the uncertainty. National wind atlases are generally validated with in situ
measurements. The extent of this validation depends on the available data.
The following information about the validation can be found in the docu-
mentation of the WASA project [13, 14]. The data from the WRF model is
compared at 10 locations to data from meteorological masts. The validation
is done by comparing generalized wind climates from the numerical model to
generalized wind climates from observations. The wind roses and distributions
are compared visually, and the absolute error of the mean generalized wind is
4.75% for a 3 year period.
At a meteorological mast, more information is available than only the mean
wind speed. The temperature gradient and the wind shear are also compared.
They give information about the correct modelling of stability since they are
related to the gradient Richardson number [29]. Time series of the WRF derived
wind speed are checked against seasonal and diurnal cycles from the measure-
ments. More detailed information on validation and sensitivity studies of the
WASA project can be found in the mesoscale documentation of the project [13].
The data from the WASA project is validated well. Even though uncertain-
ties arise from the mesoscale and microscale modelling, we can consider them
a good representation for the wind resources in South Africa. One constraint
from the microscale model will be complex terrain.

A WAsP prestudy
One of the goals in the GWA project is to give ”comprehensive uncertainty
estimates” [4]. Ideally, we would like maps of uncertainties alongside the wind
resource maps. The idea is to link sensitivities, resource grid and topography
parameters to uncertainties. An attempt to map uncertainties was presented
in the example of north-eatern China [4]. ”A number of physical phenomena
provide mechanisms for creating areas with a locally high sensitivity, or con-
versely, areas with locally low sensitivity to adjustment in the model system.
Here, these sensitivities, as well as horizontal gradient of mean wind speed and
measures of topographical complexity, are used to estimate an uncertainty map
of the wind resource calculation.” [7].
We will introduce their approach here and try to adapt it to grid resource
data. A linear relationship between sensitivities and uncertainties is assumed.
We will not use sensitivities but map properties like the horizontal mean wind
gradient and topography complexities that we think are related to model un-

Discussion on the WAsP prestudy
The adaption of uncertainty estimation using a linear regression model as in
[7] can be expanded to grid comparisons of WAsP resource grids calculated
with a modiﬁed generalized wind climate Inﬂuences from rotations and reduced
prevailing frequencies in the generalized wind climate can be predicted with the
simple uncertainty model.
Uncertainty modelling introduces some averaging
eﬀects and cannot predict diﬀerences between the grids on a small scale. This
25
==PAGE:30==
uncertainty model assumes a linear dependency from the uncertainty estimators
to uncertainties which is a considerable simpliﬁcation.
The examples were chosen to resemble generic errors between numerically
derived generalized wind climates. Changes applied here seem reasonable but
have not been deducted from systematically comparing observational and mod-
elled generalized wind climates.
The two examples showed that uncertainty
prediction is possible if we accept that the model will include averaging eﬀects.
This approach can also be useful to examine sensitivities of the WAsP model
towards generalized wind climates.
For this example, input and reference grid use the same WAsP model. WAsP
uses a single generalized wind climate to force the ﬂow model. It is a linearised
ﬂow model and blind to any mesoscale eﬀects not present in the generalized
wind climate. A direct application to uncertainty predictions between GWA and
WASA resource grids using the same uncertainty predictors did not give good
results. The diﬀerences in these models is larger and this simpliﬁed uncertainty
estimation does not yield good results. Application of uncertainty prediction
will need a more complete understanding of diﬀerences between the modelling
of GWA and WASA data.

4.2.3
Aggregated ranked scatter plots
We want to introduce aggregation into the ranked scatter plots. We are inter-
ested in the performance of the GWA. Therefore, we rank the GWA data and
compare the resources to the same locations of the WASA grid. The data is
aggregated in bins of 1% of the entire map size. Ranking only one data set will
introduce biases on the low and high end. The position of the lowest ranked
GWA resources will generally not correspond to the lowest WASA resource po-
sition. Vice versa, the windiest GWA resources are not the windiest WASA
locations.
Figure 4.7a shows the wind speed. All points are close to the diagonal with
a slight oﬀset towards higher resources in the GWA. All data points are within
±25% of the diagonal. The power density in Figure 4.7b shows again, tendencies
for higher resources in WASA,. Even though the highest GWA wind resources
are not compared to the highest WASA resources, but to the resources at the
same location. This shows that the power density of the GWA will be a more
conservative estimate, especially for higher wind resources.

be very diﬀerent [6].

show good agreement for high wind resources. Again the WASA power density
is higher especially for the windiest sites. This is consistent with the ranked
scatter plots in Figure 4.6, since the windiest percentiles are integrals of the
ranked plots from high towards low wind resources.

4.4
RIX dependent Plots
Both WASA and GWA use FrogFoot and result in complex terrain will have
uncertainties due to ﬂow detachment. Additionally, the generalization process
will also be aﬀected by terrain complexity. Generally, it is not recommended to
use WAsP’s linear ﬂow model in complex terrain [18]. Complex terrain will be
a source of uncertainty for both resource grids. Unfortunately, areas with high
wind resources often lie in complex terrain. Here, we ﬁlter the data according
to the RIX value and exclude areas where the complexity of the terrain is too
high. For this example, grid cells are chosen with RIX ≥0.05 in accordance
with the recommendation for the WASA data.

4.5
Rigidly aggregated scatter plot
We want to preserve information on wind resources from the dense grid spacing
whilst still aggregating the data. One way of aggregating is a rigid aggregation
grid with squares of an edge length n over the entire map. This divids the maps
into a set of submaps with n2 grid cells each. Then we can ﬁnd a representative
value for the submap. During this thesis I investigated three diﬀerent meth-
ods to represent the submaps: mean, maximum and windiest percentile.
This aggregation method still compares submaps at the same location but loses
information about the distribution of the wind resource within the submap.
The maximum can characterize the submap. We are interested in the high
wind resource areas. But as the aggregation area grows, this value becomes less
representative for the submap. For example, a submap of O(10km×10km) with
a ridge of a similar length ridge would not be characterized well by the maximum
value. The maximum on the ridge would overestimate the usable resources in
this area. For large aggregation areas, the maximum will overestimate the wind
resources, but this value can be representative for small aggregation areas.
The mean can also characterize the submap. The additional wind speed
from the higher resolution is preserved since the spatial variance is included
in the mean value [4]. If we take the example of the O(10km × 10km) ridge,
again the mean would include all the area around with no speed up eﬀect, thus
underestimating the wind resources here. With an increasing aggregation area,
the variance over the aggregated map will decrease, and the means will approach
the mean of the entire map.
The maximum would be too optimistic and the mean would be too conser-
vative. We can deﬁne a windiest percentile method in between, similar to
Figure 4.8. Instead of the mean of the entire submap, we can use a mean of
the highest x percentile within this map. The example ridge would be better
represented since an average of the highest wind resource is considered. This
corresponds better to the way wind turbines can exploit the wind resources
within an area.

The Fourier transformation assumes periodicity of the signal. Discontinuities

In the last section we have looked at the quantitative diﬀerences between GWA
calculations and the WASA resource grids.
These approaches are useful to

5.2
Regions of interest
The FSS is a measure how accurate a prediction is considering a threshold and
an allowable misplacement. The idea of the GWA is to ﬁnd ”regions of interest”
for wind energy. Some studies to assess ”technical and economical potential” [8]
from wind atlases have been done. There is no simple way to jump from the
wind speed or power density directly to an energy production, without more
detailed information on the Weibull parameters and an associated power curve.
We will not go into estimations of energy yields and remain comparing wind
speed and power densities to identify regions of interest.
We will approach this by using binary maps with a threshold. Every grid
cell with resources larger than the threshold is 1 and every grid cell under it
is 0. Information on the exact amount of wind resources for this grid cell are
neglected. The introduction of the threshold is a shift from a quantitative view
on the results of the GWA to a qualitative assessment. We can answer what
diﬀerences arise between using WASA and the GWA resource grid in ﬂagging
potential locations for wind energy. We will introduce this alongside the same
example area as in Chapter 4, see Figure 4.1 to 4.4.

Discussion on a contingency approach
The introduction of a threshold and performance parameters is a good way of
interpreting the GWA in a diﬀerent way. Aggregation before the contingency
calculation seems reasonable to capture areas of high wind resources. Again,
the comparison is only showing diﬀerences to the WASA data. The qualitative
approach is emphasising the limitations of this comparison since it does not give
an exact diﬀerence of the wind resources.
The threshold is a characteristic value for the contingency map. Maps like
Figures 5.2 and 5.3 give a simpliﬁed picture of the real wind resources. Their
interpretation is much faster than looking at a map of diﬀerences.
This is
their value for validation purposes – they are a more qualitative approach and
powerful in their simplicity.
Performance parameter, cf. Figure 5.5, 5.7 and the cumulative contingen-
cies, cf.
Figure 5.2, 5.3 compare the resource grids for diﬀerent thresholds.
Contingency maps for thresholds with a high sensitivity should be interpreted
with care. The performance parameter can be useful to assess the quality of
the prediction for a certain threshold. The Heidke Skill Score for wind speed
and power density is zero for low wind resources and increases within a few a
steps of the threshold, cf. Figure 5.5 and 5.7. This indicates from where on the
prediction becomes useful in a sense that it starts to exclude areas. It shows
how much better the GWA forecasts are doing compared to random forecasts.
For the test cases here the HSS was almost constant – especially for the power

Outlook
The GWA will be validated for some chosen areas. Application of the rank-
ing and aggregation methods shown in this thesis can support the validation
here. Expanding comparison to measured wind ﬁelds from SAR data works
with the same methods. The spectra of resource grids show the interaction of
mesoscal and microscale models. Especially in the region between mesoscale and
microscale modelling further investigation could help improving wind resource
assessment.
The contingency approach in Chapter 5 has taken a more qualitative ap-
proach to diﬀerences and validation. In Chapter 3 we tried to map uncertain-
ties from uncertainty predictors. This approach worked for the simple WAsP
case but uncertainty estimation for the GWA was not possible with the same
set up. We know some of the limitations of the GWA approach. The idea of
uncertainty predictors could be used in a more qualitative way by introducing
uncertainty ﬂags. Uncertainties could be characterized by the number of ﬂags
indicating the level of uncertainty. This would also give hints towards the rea-
sons for uncertainty in a region. A more detailed investigation of limitations
and parameters inﬂuencing the uncertainty will be necessary. I would like to
suggest some possibilities for this uncertainty approach.
RIX
WAsP/FrogFoot cannot model the ﬂow in complex terrain correctly. The
RIX value will deﬁnitely be an indicator for uncertainties here. It is a well known
indication for uncertainty in the WAsP model [18].
Stability
Stability determines the surface boundary layer of the atmosphere
[29]. So far FrogFoot uses constant stability over the entire map. The stabil-
ity conditions will vary especially since the GWA is used for the entire world.
Information about stability and where it departs severely from the FrogFoot
assumption would help to estimate uncertainties better.
Measure for missing terrain variance
The reanalysis data uses coarse ter-
rain data for the mesoscale model. The terrain slope spectra can be linked to the
power density [7]. FrogFoot reintroduces speed up eﬀects from the topography
but does not inﬂuence the generalized wind climates. The Era-Interim reanaly-
sis data [10] gives an estimate for the missing terrain variance. This could be a
parameter closing the gap between terrain eﬀects on the coarse reanalysis scale
and on the microscale. Properties and the estimation of this parameter would
need to be investigated and assessed whether it can be used to characterize un-
58
==PAGE:63==
certainties. It is not necessary representative for the missing variance of other
reanalysis data but could be a path to investigate further.
Roughness/elevation complexity
Roughness and elevation complexity as
used in Chapter 3 seems to work for a simple WAsP case and mesoscale mod-
els [4]. Together with RIX they could work well for uncertainty estimations of
the microscale part of the GWA approach.
Sea/land change
The coastal region has change has a big inﬂuence on the
wind resources since they can introduce a drastic change in roughness and sta-
bility. The reanalysis data will for example not display eﬀects like sea breezes
correctly.
Possible gap ﬂows
The shape of the valley, the direction of the geostrophic
wind and the stability determine if and what kind of valley ﬂow appears [9,27].
Identifying areas that have a shape that can cause such eﬀects could be a good
indication. One idea is to use 2D elevation spectra and determine if there is a
directional preference indicating straight and parallel ridges. This idea would
need more thorough investigation in order to indicate possibility of valley ﬂows.
Dependency to reanalysis data
The wind resource maps calculated with
the GWA approach depend on the reanalysis data as input for the generalized
wind climates.
The sensitivity to the choice of reanalysis data could be an
indication of uncertainty. Its interpretation requires detailed knowledge on the
diﬀerences in the generation of the reanalysis data.
Libﬁle interpolation
FrogFoot uses an interpolation between generalized
wind climates as part of the calculation of wind resources. This interpolation
will introduce uncertainties and can also causes visible edges in the resource
grids. Interpolation in combination with the sea/land change and elevation and
roughness complexity could give information about the uncertainty.
All of these suggestions will need further research to assess their viability for
uncertainty estimation. Methods shown in this thesis could help investigating
them towards a more comprehensive understanding of uncertainties associated
with the GWA approach.

7
Conclusions
We have shown that resource grids from a reference calculation like the WASA
are useful to compare to GWA data. From a simple point by-point-comparison
we have gone to a more aggregated comparison emphasizing the windiest areas
of the map. Ranked aggregation takes the grid apart and the plots become
representative for the whole map.
Ranking makes patterns of the resource
distribution more visible in the scatter plots.
Aggregation with a rigid grid
and the windiest percentile within each submap keep the grid character intact
and are also applicable for a single grid. These methods were shown for one
example in South Africa but could be applied for other types of grid data as
well.
The WASA data uses a mesoscale model with a higher resolution than the
reanalysis data. Features from better resolution are present and show, for ex-
ample areas of higher wind resources. Gap ﬂows and other mesoscale features
below the reanalysis resolution will be missed in the GWA. Spectra of the mean
wind ﬁelds have shown fundamental diﬀerences in the distribution of variance
on the mesoscale. The spectral behaviour between mesoscale and microscale
is uncharted terrain in ﬂow modelling.
Regardless of the diﬀerences on the
mesoscale, the spectrum on the microscala is very similar for both data sets.
Filtering for regions of interest for wind energy and comparing through a
contingency approach changes the view towards a more qualitative approach.
We have shown how to introduce contingency maps from a resource grid with a
threshold. For comparisons this oﬀers an easier visual perception of diﬀerence
between the grids by. This tool is simple and can be useful towards validation
of the GWA from an end user’s perspective.
Uncertainty prediction in a WAsP prestudy showed that for a simple case
like this, uncertainty modelling is possible, but the complex inﬂuence from the
mesoscale on the microscale in the GWA and WASA approaches makes uncer-
tainty prediction more challenging. A qualitative approach using uncertainty
ﬂags could be useful in giving an uncertainty estimation.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
