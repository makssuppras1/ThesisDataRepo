Abstract
The purpose of this master thesis thesis is to model an offshore ﬂoating wind turbine
for control perspective, and investigate the towers inﬂuence into the model. Thereby design
a model predictive controller with integral control and softconstriants on the outputs, for
the system without the tower included. To compare and evaluate the control performances
when the controller is applied to the full model. From simulation with the nonlinear
models, it is seen that there is not any sufﬁcient difference between the models, only in
the mooring lines is seen a difference. The evaluation of the controller shows that the
MPC with integral action and softconstraints performed best, and there was no signiﬁcant
difference when applied to the linearisation of each of the nonlinear systems.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Wind energy has been known for thousands of years, back to Persia and Babylon
were wind turbines were placed in the desert or other suitable places for ﬂour production
etc. Moving forward to today huge wind turbines with the capacity of producing up to
10MW wind turbines are possible and are a signiﬁcant source of power. With the increased
demand for electrical energy, and a rapidly growing interest for renewable energy to replace
conventional power sources, wind energy is a major player to become our new main power
source. Wind turbines are mostly land based which are called onshore: few turbines are
places in the ocean, these are called offshore wind turbines. Usually these are limited to
areas with swallow waters since the tower is founded to the seabed, which on deep water
are too expensive to install. Thus other technologies have to be developed to harvest the
energy in the wind where these is deep water. Figure 1.1 shows different types of offshore
wind turbines platforms. Another positive aspect of moving the wind turbines to deep
water is that the wind ﬁeld has less turbulence, thus more power is produced and there is
less strain on the wind turbine supports. Further, in an article in [8] it is described how the
leading wind turbine operators are beginning to look into this technology. A test project is
going on in Norway as a collaboration including among others NTNU. In Scotland a pilot
wind farm with ﬂoating offshore wind turbines is under construction and it is estimated
that the Northsea would be able to produce four times the electricity demand of all of
Europe.

1.0.2
Control of a Wind Turbine
For a HAWT wind turbine the control method changes depending on where on the
power curve the turbine is operating. A constant power control curve for a wind turbine is
shown in Figure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: This ﬁgure shows the different working zones for a wind turbine.

The zones shown in Figure 1.2 are named I, II and III. Zone I is called the partial
load zone, which covers the wind speeds from cut-in to the transaction zone, II. In zone I
the controller is designed to keep the tip speed ratio constant, the pitch is set to zero and
the controller uses the torque to control omegar for the generator. This is done to keep
the highest possible CP value. Zone two, II, in Figure 1.2 shows the transaction zone for
where the control strategy is changed from constant TSR control to keeping ωr constant.
In zone three, III, the control strategy is purely pitch control in order track the reference
for ωr, here the generator torque is kept ﬁxed, thus from standard electrical machinery we
have the following relationship between torque and power as shown below.
Pe = ωrNgTe
(1.5)
In Equation (1.5) Pe is the electric power, Ng is gear ratio and Te is the electric torque,
thus it is seen that the power generated and electric torque are proportional to each other
and with the gear and rotational speed, for a ﬁxed generator torque.

rotating subsystem and a structure subsystem, which are connected through the relative
wind speed. A principal schematic drawing is shown in Figure 3.1.

Freedom (DOF) of the structure given in surge, heave and pitch. It is assumed that the
wind and waves enter in the same direction. The selected reference coordinate system ξ, η
and α represents the displacement of the mass centre according to the normal coordinate

the platform to its mass centre and l0 is the length of the mooring lines at rest.

CHAPTER 4
Controller Design
4.1
Model Predictive Control
As described in Section 1.0.1 the goal for the controller is to stabilize the system and
track of the power produced and rotational speed of the wind turbine. The controller
architecture is chosen to be a Model Predictive Controller (MPC) which is an advanced
optimal controller design, where a quadratic control problem is solved for each sampling
event. The advantage of MPC is that for every sample the optimal solution is derived,
together with ′N ′ samples ahead, which is known as the prediction horizon. The forward
prediction of the solution is required in order to guarantee a convergent and stable solution.
The disadvantage of MPC is that the algorithms that are used to solve the quadratic problem
quickly become heavy and cumbersome.

speed at 15/[m/s]. Waves are included as disturbance in the system and is modelled as a
single sinusoid. The wave hight is chosen to four meters with a frequency ωw = 0.71, thus
the wave is given on the form below.
ηw(t) = 2 ∗sin(0.71 ∗t)

to be 0 for simplicity, since it only depends on when the wave is started, thus the wave
equation can be reduced to the one shown in Equation (6.1). The simulations is carried
out for the MPC with integral action and for the MPC with integral action and constraints.

CHAPTER 7
Final remarks and conclusion
It can be concluded that the the nonlinear model given in [2] has been succesfully
implemented and augmented with a tower model, the two nonlinear models are compared
in Chapter 3, and shows identically behaviour. The nonlinear platform models are further
augmented to include a more realistic hydrodynamic model for low water velocity. The
linearisation of the nonlinear model without the tower dynamic and the corresponding
nonlinear model are validated by simulations in Chapter 3.
In Chapter 4 and 5, a MPC is successfully designed together with a Kalman ﬁlter and
augmented to include integral control action and softconstraints on the outputs. In chapter 6
the controller with integral action and the controller with integral action and softconstraints
are tested against each other, here it can be concluded from Tables 6.1, 6.2, 6.4 and 6.4 that
the controller including the softconstraints performed better than the controller without
the softconstraints. Too it can be concluded that the controller with integral action and
softconstraints performed equally when used on both models.
