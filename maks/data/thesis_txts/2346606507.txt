Abstract
Wind-tunnel experiments were made by the National Maritime Institute (NMI) in the
1970’s of model-scale lattice masts composed of circular members, with the objective of
determining the drag coeﬃcient. They found the drag coeﬃcient in smooth ﬂow to be less
than in turbulent ﬂow. A repetition study performed by H. H Koss and R. G. Srouji [1]
concluded quite the opposite, that the drag coeﬃcient in smooth ﬂow was greater than in
turbulent ﬂow.
The wind-tunnel tests of model-scale lattice masts conducted by NMI allegedly helped
shape current codiﬁed procedures, which in turn should allow accessible reproducibility,
although this was not the case. The aforementioned lattice structures comprised of circu-
lar cylinders why this project set to investigate the predictive accuracy of current codiﬁed
procedures (ESDU) with respect to single circular cylinders. The roughness parameter ε/D
was manipulated in that regard by two approaches, the ﬁrst being to vary the surface rough-
ness (ε) while maintaining the diameter, and the second being to increase the diameter (D)
while maintaining the surface roughness, both of which inﬂuence the roughness parameter.
Experiments were conducted in smooth and turbulent ﬂow in two independent wind-
tunnels for repetitious reasons.
ESDU was found to consistently overestimate the drag
coeﬃcients of circular cylinders with increasing surface roughness with 8-10% in the sub-
critical Reynolds regime, and at times up to 40% in the transition from the critical to the
supercritical Reynolds regime.
However, ESDU’s predictive accuracy for circular cylinders with increasing diameter
was found to be more accurate and at times close to identical. The generality of ﬁndings
agree well with previous studies on drag coeﬃcients and reach similar conclusions for in-
creasing surface roughness and turbulence intensity - that is, increased surface roughness
and turbulence intensity both decreaase the critical Reynolds number [2],[3].
Finally, the obtained drag coeﬃcients showed unambiguous indications that turbulent
ﬂow yielded less drag than smooth ﬂow, which ultimately questions the ﬁndings from NMI.

1 Introduction
Slender structures such as chimneys, cables of cable-stayed bridges and lattice masts all
experience eﬀects of wind. Common to all are their circular cross-section, a focal point for
several studies throughout history in particular with respect to the drag forces. Wind loads
on high-rise structures are an essential part of the design process and wind-tunnel testing
provides knowledge thereof.
The National Maritime Institute (NMI) conducted wind-tunnel experiments of lattice
masts with circular members in the 1970’s, some of which facilitated current codiﬁed pro-
cedures including the detailed items from the Engineering Science Data Unit (ESDU).
However, circular cylindrical bodies are aﬀected by scaling due to the geometrically
dependent Reynolds number and full-scale studies of lattice masts composed of circular
members obtained signiﬁcantly lower drag coeﬃcients compared with codiﬁed procedures,
the reason presumably being that their predictions are based on model-scale structures
rather than full-scale experiments [13].
If the proposition is sound that wind-tunnel tests on lattice structures with circular
members conducted by NMI laid the foundation of current codiﬁed procedures, the repro-
ducibility should be achievable.
A repetition study in that regard was conducted by H. Koss and R. G. Srouji [1] to
verify the validity of both current codiﬁed procedures and the original tests from NMI in
the particular case of lattice masts with circular tubes. Interestingly, the results showed
fundamental inconsistencies with respect to the drag coeﬃcient to a degree that ultimately
questions the soundness of the study from NMI.
The drag coeﬃcient obtained by NMI for lattice masts with circular members at 25
m/s was lower in smooth ﬂow than in turbulent ﬂow, quite the opposite than what was
concluded by the repetition study.
This consequently founded the present project with the main objective of investigating
the accuracy of current literature in determining the drag coeﬃcient of a single circular
cylinder.
The project primarily investigates the accuracy of drag approximations from
ESDU under the assumption that tests from NMI contributed to its current practice.
A systematic validity review of ESDUs approximative accuracy of drag coeﬃcients of
circular cylinders was performed in the present study by manipulating the inﬂuential rough-
ness parameter ε/D where ε expresses the roughness type and D the circular diameter.
The conceptual approaches was to vary one component while maintaining the other,
which consequently lead to circular cylinders with distinguishable roughness types (ε) while
maintaining the circular diameter and diﬀerent circular diameters (D) with ﬁxed roughness
types.

The experiments conducted in the present study comprise of testing in both smooth and
turbulent ﬂows, along with a time-dependent statistical analysis for reasons of both experi-
mental veriﬁcation and possible identiﬁcation of various inﬂuential irregularities. Moreover,

Wind-tunnel tests of circular cylinders executed in the present project comprise of:
- Four types of increasing roughness with ﬁxed diameter and,
- Seven steps of increasing circular diameter with ﬁxed roughness type.
The varying roughness of the surfaces range from lacquered surfaces to a grain-sized
texture and the increasing diameter ranges from thirty millimetres to one-hundred-and-

Additionally, corresponding wind proﬁles in all cases were examined with the purpose of

Wind-tunnel experiments for circular cylinders with altering roughness types were con-

(DTU) and at FORCE Technology both in closed test sections, whereas cylinders with in-
creasing diameter required a tolerant test section to prevent inaccurate measurements due
to blockage eﬀects.
The report initiates with a literature review describing previous ﬁndings in the same
area of interest, followed by a theoretical elaboration of the aerodynamics regarding ﬂow
around circular cylinders and more. Thereafter the experimental approach is presented and
subsequently the results with discussion. Lastly, a conclusive summary and suggestions for
future research.

2 Brief Review of Literature
Next are presented the literature reviewed during the present project with the main purpose
of acquiring relevant knowledge in the ﬁeld of wind-tunnel testing. Note that some is purely
informative.
Wind-tunnel experiments can deliver high-accuracy and high-frequency data that nu-
merical simulation will not reach for decades. Wind-tunnel testing with respect to civil
engineering is often on structural models where full-scale testing is not feasible and has
proven essential in the design process.
History has shown us that wind-tunnel testing has a broad spectrum of applications
and has been and still is a dominant tool in aerodynamic research such as wind-induced
vibrations, drag on structures or dynamic responses of structures as mentioned by G. Solari
[14, p.137]. Also, interdisciplinary application of wind-tunnel tests and numerical methods
are described by [14, p.159] in which pressure tabs were distributed on the structural surface
of the model and subsequently applied to ﬁnite element software, which calculated the
structural dynamic response. Another approach is to measure base reactions of a model, as
explained by J. Holmes [15, p.184], with a high-frequency base balance (HFBB) followed by
a power spectral analysis, from which one may interpret model properties such as vortex
shedding frequency, drag or damping ratios.
The characteristics of the ﬂow around circular cylinders is a benchmark problem in
ﬂuid dynamics which has been studied for more than a hundred years usually as a function
the ﬂuid mechanically dependent and dimensionless Reynolds number. Reynolds number
is usually parted in four stages with regard to ﬂow past circular cylinders; subcritical,
critical, supercritical and transcritical, going from low to high Reynolds numbers, and the
aerodynamic eﬀects such as drag and lift forces and vortex shedding are well-documented.
The drag force experience a sudden decrease at the critical Reynolds number ﬁrst discovered
by G. Eiﬀel [16] and later conﬁrmed by E. Achenbach and E. Heinecke [5] and G. Schewe
[17] and others.
They additionally found that the vortex shedding frequency experiences a similar shift
in the critical Reynolds regime, although both the drag coeﬃcient and vortex shedding
frequency level out eventually. The mechanism of drag reduction is thought to be caused
by the transition from laminar to turbulent in the near ﬂow of the circular cylinder [2].

2.2
Drag coeﬃcient in smooth/turbulent ﬂows
Drag coeﬃcient of circular cylinders obtain a lower critical Reynolds number in turbulent
ﬂows compared with smooth ﬂows, a well-documented postulate made by, among others,
J.D Holmes [15, p.86] and H. Schlichting [4, p.573]. The ﬂow characteristics that tend to
contribute to a lower critical Reynolds number for circular cylinders was suggested by M.
Arie et al. [20] to primarily be turbulence intensity and scale of turbulence. This fact is
evident on physical grounds since high turbulence intensity moves the transition point to
lower Reynolds numbers so that the point of separation is shifted downstream, consequently
decreasing the wake stream, and this, in turn, reduces drag [4, p.573].
Surprising results were obtained by C. B. Millikan and A. L. Klein [21], as elaborated by H.
Schlichting [4, p. 573], where free-ﬂight measurements on a sphere were conducted in the
free atmosphere and acted seemingly independent of the structure of turbulence. Free-ﬂight
measurements gave a critical Reynolds number of Recrit = 3.85 × 105 which is larger than
for most wind-tunnel tests in turbulent ﬂow, although low-turbulence tunnels approach the
critical Reynolds number as in free-ﬂight. This may be explained by the circumstance that

2.4
Blockage
Wind-tunnel models are limited in size due to blockage eﬀects, which is the ratio between
the projected area of the test body looking downstream, and the cross-sectional area of
the wind-tunnel, further elaborated in Sec. 3.3.
If the model is too large it introduces
pressure interference, which inﬂuence the drag coeﬃcient. The blockage eﬀect is generally
not appreciative with respect drag coeﬃcient and Strouhal number (a measure of vortex
shedding) if the blockage ratio is less than 6% as concluded by G. West and C. Apelt [22],
however, this is in direct conﬂict with the fact that wind-tunnel tests try to compensate
the Reynolds number through larger sized models. A blockage correction procedure was
developed by H. J. Allen and W. G. Vicenti and appreciated by several ﬁeld experts including
A. Roshko and E. Achenbach. The correction procedure includes adjustments based on the
additional pressure generated between the test body and the wall including the change
of pressure between the wake and the wall.
Although well suited, a small change was
introduced by C. Dalton [23] due to insuﬃcient blockage corrections at spacing ratios greater
than 0.1. Another blockage correction procedure introduced by ESDU [24] was reviewed,
which depends on two main factors, λ1 and λ2, derived from the cross-sectional shape of
the wind-tunnel and the test body dimensions.

2.8
Reaching Similarity
The primary purpose of wind-tunnel testing is to achieve similarity between model-scale
and full-scale structures, a challenge regocnized by H. Dryden and G. Hill [26] in the early
days of wind-tunnel testing.
The drag coeﬃcient of circular cylinders is as mentioned
Reynolds dependent and the Reynolds number comprises a geometrical component, that is
the diameter D which consequently troubles the scaling of circular cylinders. This became
known as the Reynolds number scaling problem as explained by H. Schlichting [4, p.71-72].
G. Eiﬀel (1832-1923) understood this problem and concluded, since a whole airplane could
not ﬁt inside the wind-tunnel, that he instead had to manipulate the ﬂow ﬁeld in the test
section. G. Eiﬀel suggested several ways to increase the Reynolds range e.g. increasing the
velocity u or increasing the density ρ. He also suggested decreasing the dynamic viscosity µ
by cooling the air, hence an increase in air density ρ. A lower µ and higher ρ lead to higher
Reynolds numbers and many of the high-range Reynolds number facilities existing today

3.3
Blockage
The blockage eﬀect is generally not appreciative for the drag coeﬃcient or Strouhal number
if the blockage ratio is less than 6% suggested by G. West and C. Apelt [22], however, this
is in direct conﬂict with the fact that wind-tunnel test try to compensate the air velocity
through larger sized models in order to achieve adequate Reynolds numbers.
The relation between the projected area of the circular cylinder relative to the wall cross-
section may give rise to pressure interference, which inﬂuence the measurements. Blockage
correction was developed by H. J. Allen and W. G. Vicenti as elaborated by C. Dalton [23]
and appreciated by several ﬁeld experts including A. Roshko and E. Achenbach. The cor-
rection procedure includes adjustment of additional pressure generated by the interference
between the circular cylinder and the wall including the change of pressure between the
wake and the wall. Though well suited a small change was introduced by C. Dalton [23]
due to insuﬃcient blockage corrections at spacing ratios greater than 0.1. Another blockage
correction procedure from ESDU [24] depends on two main factors, λ1 and λ2, which is
based on the cross-sectional shape of the wind-tunnel and the test body as presented next.
3.3.1
Blockage Correction
Wind-tunnel testing of circular cylinders and curv-shaped bodies is limited by the ratio
between the projected area of the test body and the cross sectional area of the wind-
tunnel, the so called blockage ratio. Fundamentally, the measurements will be aﬀected by
the increased pressure generated by the ﬂow between the circular cylinder and the wall as
explained in Sec. 3.3. The measurement are likely to be disturbed when exceeding a blockage
ratio of 6% though lower level blockage ratios are recommended to also undergo blockage
correction. Blockage correction has proven an indispensable analytic tool in wind-tunnel
testing and have made higher Reynolds numbers accessible through increasing diameter.
This thesis only consider two blockage correction procedures presented by [24] and [23] both
of which consider two-dimensional ﬂow.

3.4.1
Data Acquisition
Wind-tunnel testing produce huge amounts of data usually of a random nature and gath-
ering data from a random process are best treated as a time-dependent stochastic process.
Although the instrumentations produce accurate measurements, the digital transition from
nature is to some extent very rough due to digital limitations. Firstly, the whole transi-
tions from nature to computer has to pass through a decent amount of instrumentations
and devices before reaching the computer. This large chain of components is referred to as
the acquisition chain, illustrated in Fig. 3.10, which is a chain that includes all instruments
and devices from nature to the computer. Wind loads are a inﬁnite continuous system of
Fig. 3.10: Acquisition chain - the transition from nature to accessible data [10, p. 3].
random nature which looses consistency during data transition due to digital limitations.
Because of modiﬁcation and conversion along the way the ﬁnal output provide a discrete
representation of the natural phenomenon. The signal conversion from analogue to digital is
referred to as an A/D converter and doing so initiates the signal discretization. The resolu-
tion of discretization is known as n-bits and describe the extent of measuring accuracy (not
to be confused with the time-series), i.e. the number of n-bits essentially determines the
accuracy of the force measurements. This means, that since the A/D converter is a 16-bits
module the resolution of the force spectrum, or force precision, is 216 = 65536 i.e. the force
spectrum is a discrete system represented by 216 elements. It should be mentioned, that the
equipments entire force range is ±5 Volts which, compared with the range of measurements
from 0.004-0.06 Volts (determined through a quick range analysis of the unconverted Volt
data) leads to an even rougher discrete system with a measurement resolution of 52 and
786 elements respectively depending on the velocity2. Aliasing would occur if the force
spectrum is suﬃciently narrow together with a relative small resolution which is an eﬀect of
insuﬃcient signal processing, and consequently make the signal appear diﬀerent from what
it actually is.

3.4.3.1
PSD using pwelch
The built-in function pwelch(x) is used to generate the power spectral density (PSD)
Pxx of an input signal x with corresponding frequency f using Welch’s method. Welch’s
method divide the data into overlapping segments, compute-modiﬁed periodograms of the
overlapping segments, followed by an averaged value of the segments to produce the spectral
density estimate. As a default, the signal x is segmented into eight sections, each with ﬁfty
percent overlap. The window option controls the segment length. The routine uses N
number of Fast Fourier Transformations (NFFT) and the signal x determines the length
Pxx including the range of corresponding frequencies. The pwelch function used for data
treatment requires four input values; X which is the signal, window controlling the segment
length, nfft the number of Fourier transformations and ﬁnally the sample frequency in
Hertz fs.
Additionally, the overlapping is default as 50% speciﬁed as [] in pwelch.

A closed circuit wind-tunnel (CCWT, Fig. 4.1) is among the most popular low-speed (¡130

Advantages
- Consistent and uniform ﬂow quality.
- Low operation costs. The fan only needs to overcome friction losses ones the air
has reached the desired velocity.
- Several possibilities with a changeable test section.
Disadvantages
- Relatively large construction.
- Higher building costs due vanes and ducting.
- Require active cooling due to increasingly running temperatures.
- Diﬃcult to clean after experiments with particles.

turbulent ﬂow naturally, due to pressure loss from turbulence grid, have a lower maximum

temperatures though this was not deemed problematic since the temperature was measured
continuously and accounted for throughout the present project, besides the temperature

velocity ﬂuctuations and a conﬁguration for force measurements, both with corresponding
NI-modules.

Prandtl tube). The velocity measurements therefore required conversion for two outputs,

4.6
Experimental Observations
The Cobra probe vibrated during velocity testing though this was not thought to aﬀect
the measurements signiﬁcantly. The calibration procedure showed much larger oﬀsets than
expected though consistent for both test sessions.
The large deviations was found solely for the horizontal force components Fx and Fy as
these components were the governing components prospectively. The reason for the large
oﬀsets have not become clear though the ampliﬁers seemingly play a key role. This is in
need of further investigation but the present project introduced a calibration factor Tcor to
compensate for the large oﬀsets.
It was also noted that the second test session was conducted immediately after a large
number of erosion tests which had ﬁlled the wind-tunnel with particles. The cleaning was
good two-days work and yet a few particles some times broke free during testing which may
aﬀect the results.
The test section had a slightly smaller cross-sectional area (475x450 compared to 480x450)
which increases the velocity in the test section considering the equation of continuity (see
Eq. (3.8)) though this eﬀect was not assessed to inﬂuence the measurements.
4.7
Retrospective Considerations
A couple of retrospective considerations should be mentioned to enlighten experimental
work in future similar studies.
- The BLR consist of a bottom and top plate separated by a NACA airfoil as mentioned
in Sec. 4.1.5. The initial design were made with the aim of removing the boundary
layer induced by the top and bottom surface roughness of the closed test section,
although thinner metal plates instead of wood would be preferable due to mainly two
things; the possibility of polishing the surface thus minimizing the surface roughness
and hence the boundary layer eﬀects, and two, the edge should have been sharper
thus slicing the velocity proﬁle more suﬃciently. Additionally, a more slender design
would equal lesser blockage which is also preferable.
- The new turbulence grid design should have been extended and reached the wind-
tunnel walls.
- The initial intention with the two roughest cylinders to simulate a random surface
roughness may be diﬃcult to reproduce, unless one can purchase those exact sprays,
which is in direct conﬂict with the scientiﬁc requirement of experimental reproducibil-
ity.

5.7
Data Analysis
The purpose of the statistical analysis was primarily to identify irregularities or potential
pitfalls that may prove signiﬁcant in wind-tunnel testing. The time-history analysis was
executed in accordance with the procedure elaborated in Sec. 3.4.1 and the following will
present the relevant irregularities found during the data analysis for both A-cylinders and
B-cylinders.
As mentioned previously, force measurements from wind-tunnel experiments conducted
at FORCE did not undergo similar statistical analysis due to an error in the conversion
procedure from the exclusive format .GDF to a readable ASCII-format.
Force measurements were parted in drag (FD) and lift (FL) forces, longitudinal and
lateral direction respectively, and examined separately. The statistical analysis was made
primarily for the drag force FD though the lift force FL was used in the power spectrum
for easier identiﬁcation of the vortex shedding frequencies.
The sample time was 120 sec for the force measurements with a sample frequency of
2048 Hz. The sub-series was subdivided into 30 sub-sets and the histogram also 30 sub-
divisions. Initially is should be mentioned that the limitations of data acquisition chain
(DAQ) became apparent at lower velocities due to the narrow spectrum of forces occurring
at those air speeds. An example is shown in Fig. 5.14 where aliasing (see Sec. 3.4.1) occurs
as a consequence of insuﬃcient resolution. The apparent eﬀects of aliasing faded out at
higher velocities in step with force increase and thus a broader force spectrum.
The time-dependent drag and lift forces were treated with the MATLAB routine THA.m,
producing the time-series with corresponding histogram, sub-series, and power spectrum,
which taken together provide a satisfactory statistical depiction of the forces over time.
The mechanical eigenfrequencies were identiﬁed by identifying the stationary values in the
power spectrum as explained in Sec. 3.4.3.

5.8
Drag Coeﬃcient
The previous have estimated the aerodynamic parameters corresponding to each wind-
tunnel test as required by the detailed items from ESDU [19].
Two blockage correction procedures were presented earlier, one formulated by C. Dalton
[23] and the other by ESDU [24]. The diﬀerence between the two procedures was found to be
very small (±1%) considering the present experimental setup for A-cylinders in the closed
test section. Blockage correction was accounted for in accordance with ESDU 80024 [24] as
elaborated in Sec. 3.3.1 for both wind-tunnel test at both DTU and FORCE Technology.
The wind-tunnel tests conducted with the tolerant section did not undergo similar block-
age correction procedure since the blockage ratio for all B-cylinders did not exceed the
blockage ratio limit of 43.4% as concluded by M. Zdravkovich [36] for circular cylinders.
Additionally, the boundary layers were estimated for each velocity proﬁle and accounted
for by reducing the projected area as mentioned in Sec. 4.1.6; however this procedure solely
acts as an approximative consideration.
The following will present the established drag coeﬃcients in accordance with the exper-
imental setups listed in Tab. 5.1 together with the drag predictions from ESDU. Note that
drag formulation from ESDU comprises of a relative drag coeﬃcient and a relative Reynolds
number however the mathematical basis of these terms are not further elaborated due to
copyright infringement though they may be found in [19]. The relative drag coeﬃcients are
shown for relative Reynolds number Ree > 104 since values below are generally of a too
disrupted nature. The relative drag coeﬃcient CD/(1 + 2ε/D) and the relative Reynolds
number Ree = λRλT Re are referred to as simply the drag coeﬃcient CD and Reynolds
number Ree in the following.

6 Discussion
The following will comprise discussion of the ﬁndings of the present project and discuss
their potential inﬂuence on the fundamental diﬀerences found between the wind-tunnel
experiments conducted at NMI for lattice masts with circular members and the repetition
study performed by R. G. Srouji [1].
6.1
Flow Field Geometry
The initial turbulence grid was found to generate an inhomogeneous ﬂow ﬁeld geometry,
possibly due to frayed edges and skew dimensions. Such inhomogeneity in turbulent ﬂow
generates high and low pressure zones, which may yield diﬀerent drag forces depending on
the test position in the ﬂow ﬁeld.
The repetition study was executed in inhomogeneous turbulent ﬂow ([39, p.83] and may
have been subject to such pressure diﬀerences, however it was not speciﬁed whether the
original wind-tunnel experiment at NMI was conducted in homogeneous turbulent ﬂow or
not.
Nevertheless, even though homogeneous turbulent ﬂow was not found to conﬁdently
inﬂuence the drag coeﬃcients, the potential inﬂuence of an inhomogeneous ﬂow ﬁeld would
still be eliminated in a homogeneous ﬂow ﬁeld, which was shown to be achievable with a
turbulence grid made with sharp edges and production precision.
6.1.1
Integral Length Scale
ESDU has embedded in its procedure the integral length scale as a property of turbulence.
The inhomogeneous ﬂow ﬁeld was found to aﬀect the integral length scale depending on the
position of measurement, which consequently changes the drag approximation from ESDU.
Deviations of the integral length scale over height in inhomogeneous turbulence were found
up to 25%, which in turn aﬀect the relative Reynolds number up to 6%.
6.1.2
Boundary Layer Considerations
Although not necessarily the case, the preliminary assumption of the boundary layer consid-
eration procedure was a constant boundary layer thickness with increasing velocity. Bound-
ary layer thickness may vary with velocity and such knowledge was not taken into account
during measurements, which consequently may provide a less precise reduction of the pro-
jected area. Retrospectively, this should have been considered .

6.2
Surface Roughness
The surface roughness for each cylinder was measured as thoroughly as possible with the
available apparatus. Even so, ESDUs predictions were consistently in disagreement with
the drag coeﬃcients obtained.
This stresses the sensitivity of the drag formulation presented by ESDU with respect
to surface roughness of circular cylinders. Additionally, such cautious surface roughness
considerations were not taken in the repetition study [1], an element that may too inﬂuence
the drag comparison.
6.3
Lattice Model
A rather banal suggestion to the inconsistencies found between NMI and [1] is the model
ﬁnish. The joints in which the legs and bracings are connected were welded together leaving
relatively large weld lumps, essentially increasing the projected area. However this notion
is strictly speculative.
6.4
Size Matters
ESDUs predictive abilities were found to be more accurate for circular cylinders with larger
diameters for reasons not fully understood. However, this indicates that circular cylinders
with small diameters are increasingly diﬃcult to predict compared with larger diameters.
6.5
General Considerations
The detailed item from ESDU was found only to predict the drag coeﬃcient one time out
of twenty-two experiments to a satisfactory degree. This was for cylinder 7:B, the cylinder
with the largest circular diameter. This suggests, that although comprehensive, the degree
of detail from ESDU proves insuﬃcient in predicting the aerodynamic forces involved in the
ﬂow past circular cylinders.
A general comparison for A-cylinders was made in smooth and turbulent ﬂow, which
indicated unambiguously that turbulent ﬂow decreases the critical Reynolds number. All
drag coeﬃcients were found to obtain greater values in smooth ﬂow than in turbulent ﬂow
in air speeds above 15 m/s, which ultimately questions the ﬁndings from the NMI study.
The study conducted by NMI in the 1970’s came to the conclusion that drag coeﬃcients
in smooth ﬂow are lower than in turbulent ﬂow, a conclusion that completely contradicts the
ﬁndings of the present study and the literature reviewed in general. Only two articles were
found in which the same situation occurred, though at much higher Reynolds numbers than
in the NMI study. The case being that the drag coeﬃcient in critical regime in smooth ﬂow
decreases, while drag coeﬃcient in the supercritical regime in the turbulent ﬂow increases,
both with the same Reynolds number at the point at which they overlap [40], [41].

7 Conclusion
Flow Field Properties
Turbulence homogeneity was found to be inﬂuenced by the craftsmanship of the turbulence
grid, with special attention to sharp corners of the grid beams including precise production.
However, grid beam thickness may also be an inﬂuential parameter in that regard.
Wind-tunnel experiments were conducted in two diﬀerent turbulent ﬂow ﬁelds, both of
which were inhomogeneous. Less comparable drag coeﬃcients were obtained in velocities
below 10 m/s, although it was not apparent if inhomogeneity was the cause. Measurements
from the two wind-tunnels at velocities above 10 m/s were in good agreement indicating no
signiﬁcant inﬂuence of turbulence inhomogeneity.
However, inhomogeneous turbulence was found to aﬀect the scale of turbulence depend-
ing on the test position in the wind-tunnel, which lead to diﬀerences of up to 25% of the
integral length scale.
Data Analysis
Irregularities of cylinder 3:A and 4:A were found during the statistical analysis of force
measurement. The irregularities presumably occurred due to resonance of vortex shedding
frequencies and mechanical eigenmodes, although their inﬂuence on the time-averaged force
measurements were deemed insigniﬁcant. Additionally, an unusual peak was observed dur-
ing the data analysis of B-cylinders, although this was assessed to be a unique incidence and
therefore eﬀectively having no signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the time-averaged force measurement.
Increasing Roughness
Wind-tunnel experiments were conducted for four circular cylinders with increasing surface
roughness while maintaining the diameter (A-cylinders), with roughness parameter ε/D as
indicated in Tab. 7.1, in both smooth and turbulent ﬂow. The primary objective was to
assess the predictive accuracy of the detailed items from ESDU [19] of the drag coeﬃcient.
The ﬁndings of the present project agree well with previous studies on drag coeﬃcients
and reach similar conclusions for increasing surface roughness and turbulence intensity, that
is, that increased surface roughness and turbulence intensity both decreases the critical
Reynolds number [2],[3].
However, the predictive capabilities of ESDU for circular cylinders with increasing sur-
face roughness were found to overestimate the drag coeﬃcient for the vast majority, with

8 Future Research
One subject in particular ought to receive more experimental attention, which is homogene-
ity in turbulent ﬂow and its inﬂuence on wind-tunnel experiments with respect to drag coeﬃ-
cients.
Inhomogeneous turbulent ﬂow was observed in the present project in two independent wind-
tunnels. The inﬂuence of inhomogeneity on drag coeﬃcients was not apparent, although
this may not always be the case. Inhomogeneity generates high- and low-pressure zones and
the drag coeﬃcients may depend on the speciﬁc test position. Such pressure zones may be
eliminated through homogeneity, which was found achievable with a turbulence grid with
sharp edges and precise dimensions. It would be interesting to the ﬁeld of wind-tunnel
testing to clarify the diﬀerence between inhomogeneous and homogeneous turbulent ﬂow
with respect to drag.

MC5 Force and Torque Transducer 
 
DESCRIPTION 
The MC5 force and torque transducer measures loads in the range 
between the capacities of AMTI’s popular MC3 and MC8 transducers. It’s 
available with vertical force capacities of 5,560N to 45,000N (1,250lb to 
10,000lb) and corresponding horizontal force and moment capacities. 
These sensors are available with one to six outputs coresponding to Fx, 
Fy, Fz, Mx, My & Mz. Models with custom capacities and layouts are 
available for special applications. 
 
 
Applications.   
The MC5 force and torque transducer is particularly suitable for 
applications requiring simultaneous measurement of several forces and 
moments, or measurement of forces that change direction and position over time. Applications for this transducer 
include research and development in machining, robotics ocean engineering and aerospace. It’s commonly used 
in test frames and orthopaedic simulators. 
 
 
AMPLIFICATION 
The MC5 force and torque sensor incorporates strain gauges mounted on four precision strain elements in a 
patented design to measure forces and moments. As with most conventional strain gauge transducers, bridge 
excitation and signal amplification is required.  
AMTI’s product line includes one analogue strain gauge amplifier, the MSA-6. There is also digital signal  
amplifier, the Gen 5. All these amplifiers are high gain devices which provide excitation and amplification for 
multiple channels in one convenient package   
 
 
Calibration  
Each platform is inspected and tested is AMTI’s calibration facility. The calibration procedure provides a 
detailed sensitivity matrix and a complete test of all systems components, including the amplifier and 
connecting cable.  
 
Custom 
AMTI also offers special multi-axis transducers to meet your specific requirements. Units are available that 
are water proof, pressure compensated, non-magnetic, non- conductive and transparent. Capacities from 
1lb (4.5N) to 3 million lbs (13.3Mn) can be made.
