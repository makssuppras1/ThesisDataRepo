ABSTRACT (ENGLISH)
Introduction: To utilize rHDL nanoparticles for anti-cancer drug delivery purpose the
biodistribution must be improved. In vivo and ex vivo optical imaging using nanopar-
ticles labelled with a uorophore has recently become a popular technique to determine
biodistribution because it is cheap, fast, and use non-ionizing radiation. However the
technique is only considered qualitative. Therefore the aim is to quantify the biodistri-
bution of uorescent labelled rHDL. Furthermore, the quantication is used to explore if
PEG modication of rHDL increases uptake in the tumor compared to the liver.
Methods: rHDL particles were formulated using ApoA-I puried from human blood
and labelled with two uorophores. The biodistribution of rHDL and PEG-rHDL was
investigated in mouse tumor models in three steps. First step was to acquire in vivo NIR
optical images. Second step was to assess tissue distribution from ex vivo optical images.
Third step was to quantify the biodistribution in %ID and %ID/g by interpolation of bulk
uorescence measurements from tissue homogenates to a standard curve.
Results: Biodistribution measured from uorophores outside the NIR window veried
that the measurements are compromised by autouorescence. Moreover, the ex vivo based
biodistribution is dissimilar to the biodistribution determined from bulk uorescence from
DiR in tissue homogenates. It was found that the tumor/liver ratio based on %ID/g de-
creased from 0.82 ± 0.09 to 0.55 ± 0.15 with PEG-modied rHDL.
Conclusion: The biodistribution of nanoparticles based on uorescence should be quan-
tied by homogenizing harvested tissues and measure bulk uorescence. However, the
particles should be labelled with uorophores emitting with wavelengths in the NIR range.
PEG-modication of the rHDL particles did not improve the biodistribution because tu-
mor uptake decreased compared to the liver.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION
High-Density Lipoprotein (HDL) are endogenous particles that consist of apolipoproteins
and lipids and have a size of 7-12 nm. The main function of HDL is to transport lipids
from the peripheral tissues to the liver by sequestering cholesterol into their core. The
small size and ability to contain hydrophobic cargo make HDL strong candidates for drug
delivery vehicles. In addition, the particles are endogenous, which make them biocompat-
ible, biodegradable, and non-toxic [1, 2, 3].
reconstituted High-Density Lipoprotein (rHDL) are particles that mimic the discoidal
nascent HDL. It can be utilized to deliver cancer therapy because it is recognized by the
Scavenger Receptor class B type 1 (SR-B1) receptor, which is largely over-expressed by
many cancerous tissues due to high cholesterol metabolism [1, 3]. However, the SR-B1
receptor is also found in the liver where it is most abundant, followed by macrophages, the
adrenal gland and female reproductive system [1, 4]. Targeting the rHDL via the SR-B1
is therefore problematic. Nonetheless, rHDL have a long biological half life, which can
result in accumulation of the particle in the tumor due to the Enhanced Permeability and
Retention (EPR) eect. The EPR eect is the accumulation of particles with size below
200 nm in cancerous tissue because of defects in the vascular wall that allow particles to
permeate into the tissue where drainage is poor because it lacks lymphatic vessels [5, 6].
Currently, several research groups have studied the biodistribution of particles that mimic
the endogenous HDL. The approach has mainly been focused on in vivo imaging and/or
ex vivo experiments tracking the particle labelled with either a uorophore for optical
imaging [3, 7] or a radioactive isotope for gamma counting and/or Positron Emission
Tomography (PET)-imaging [8, 9]. Similar for the biodistribution studies using optical
imaging, is that the liver uptake was largest, followed by the tumor. Both studies were
conducted in a xenograft mouse model with particles made from synthetic Apolipoprotein
A1 (ApoA-I)-mimicking peptides. Dierent from the optical imaging biodistribution, the
studies using radionuclides report high kidney uptake which is followed by the liver and

tumor respectively [8, 9].
There are a variety of techiques to determine biodistribution of nanoparticles: histology,
electron microscopy, liquid scintillation, optical imaging, nuclear medicine imaging, CT,
and MRI. Each approach has its own advantages and disadvantages [10]. Optical imag-
ing is considered to be a qualitative technique for assessing biodistribution of uorescent
labelled nanoparticles, however a novel method for quantifying the tissue distribution of
uorescent antibody probes has been proposed [11]. Here, the harvested tissues of mice
are homogenized for bulk uorescence measurements and from standard curves of the
injected probe, the %Injected Dose per Gram of tissue (ID/g) of tissues could be quanti-
ed. The results are compared to the more accepted gamma ray quantication in order
to evaluate the method. The approach has not yet been used to asses the biodistribution
of nanoparticles, nor has it been used to evaluate biodistribution determined from 2D ex
vivo images.
To alter the pharmacokinetics of the rHDL, Tang et al. [3] investigated the eect of PE-
Gylating the lipid layer of their particle and they reported reduced uptake in tumors and
liver. However, Murphy et al. [12] showed in hypocholesterolemic mice (an atherosclero-
sis study) that PEGylating the human ApoA-I in rHDL increased its circulating half life.
However, this has not yet been investigated in a mouse tumor model for an anti-cancer
drug delivery purpose.
The feasibility of using rHDL as drug delivery vehicle for anti-cancer therapeutics de-
pends heavily on its biodistribution. Current advances report large liver uptake of HDL-
mimicking nanoparticles and a method for limiting this uptake must be developed to
create an ecient delivery vehicle. Also, to evaluate the ability of utilizing the nanoparti-
cle for delivery of cancer immune therapy, the biodistribution is investigated in a syngeneic
mouse model with intact immune system.

BACKGROUND
2.1
High-Density Lipoproteins
HDL are nanoparticles of 7-12 nm found in human blood. They are reered to as "good
cholesterol" because their main function is to remove excess cholesterol from peripheral
tissues. HDL blood levels are moreover known to have an inverse relationship with the
risk of developing cardiovascluar disease [13, 1, 14, 2].
HDL consists of phospolipid bilayer and apolipoproteins. ApoA-I of approximately 28
kDa is the main protein component in HDL particles. ApoA-I determines the shape and
size of the HDL. It has eight amphiphatic α-helical domains of 22 amino acids and is syn-
thesized in the small intestine and the liver from where it is secreted into the bloodstream
in a lipid-free form. It quickly associates with free cholesterol and phospolipids to form
nascent HDL.
Cholesterol is transported back to the liver by the HDL via the RCT pathway which
is illustrated in Figure 2.1. The endogenous HDL is heterogeneous in size and density
because the shape of the complex depends on its maturation state. The nascent HDL is
discoidal in shape whereas it becomes spherical when it takes up cholesterol by LCAT
mediated conversion of free cholesterol to cholesterol esters. It can also interact with the
LDLs and VLDLs. Cholesterol esters are lastly delivered to the liver [1, 14, 2, 15]. HDL
bind to the SR-B1 receptor which is most abundant in the liver, but is also found in other
tissues. SR-B1 is a cell surface receptor that mediates the inux and eux of cholesterol.
It recognize the ApoA-I in the HDL, and binds to the termini of the protein [16].

2.1.1
rHDL as drug delivery vehicle
Recently, there has become an increasing interest in utilizing HDL as a drug delivery
vehicle. It possess many features that make it a strong cadidate. It is rst and foremost
associated with being good cholesterol as described earlier. Furthermore, it can trans-
port hydrophobic cargo within the core and hydrophilic cargo in its outer layer. The
formation of discoidal nanoparticles that mimics the native HDL particle (α4HDL from
Figure 2.1) is referred to as reconstituted High-Density Lipoprotein (rHDL) and can be
seen in Figure 2.2(a). They can be formulated using ApoA-I and phospolipids. It is not
conrmed how the discoidal rHDL-complex is structured, but most researches assume that
the ApoA-Is encircle the phospolipid bilayer as an anti parallel belt. The two proteins go
opposite way around the lipids, and the two N-terminals are in close proximity to each
other [17]. This is also assumed to be the structure of the rHDL in this thesis.
A lot of research have been put into the development of liposomes as drug delivery ve-
hicles [18]. However, because rHDLs is much smaller, it may in some cases be superior.
Moreover the rHDL is biocompatible, biodegradable and non-toxic. The rHDL have rela-
tively long circulation time, compared to other nanoparticle carriers, because it does not
get eliminated by the immune system. A half-life of approximately 12 hours have been
reported [14]. rHDL can be used to target cancerous tissue because ApoA-I is recog-
nized by the SR-B1 receptor which is overexpressed in cancer cells because of their high
metabolism. This is further elaborated in Section 2.2. However, the SR-B1 receptor is
also found in the liver, adrenal gland and female reproductive organs [1], which make
targeting of rHDLs to cancer cells troublesome.
The most important features of a drug delivery vehicle is that it is relatively stable when
entering the biological system and it delivers the drugs to the target tissue selectively.
However rHDLs are not currently ready to full these requirements since they accumulate
to a high degree in the liver [19, 3, 20, 7]. One approach to overcome this problem can be
to PEGylate the rHDL.

2.2. CANCER BIOLOGY
PEGylation
PEGylating is the process in which a Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) polymer is attached to
e.g. a molecule and nanoparticle. PEG is a hydrophilic polymer that it is widely used
to optimize pharmacokinetics of liposomes by covalent attachment of it [18]. Food and
Drug Administration (FDA) has approved it for clinal use [22]. It is commonly used to
increase circulation time by giving nanoparticles stealth-like properties that reduce their
elimation by the immunesystem [23, 24]. The latter is not neccesarily the inteded eect
of PEGylating the rHDL in this thesis. The circulating half-life is still intended to be
increased by PEG modication, however, the hypothesis is that the PEG will shield the
ApoA-I binding-site which is recognized by the SR-B1 receptor. Murphy et al. [12] have
shown that PEGylation of the N-terminal of ApoA-I in rHDLs noticeably increases the
biological half life of rHDL. On the other hand, one must take into account that the length
and density of the PEG increases size and may change toxicity of the nanoparticle [2]. Our
hypothesis is that the increased circulation time will result in an increased accumulation
of rHDLs in tumor tissue. The biological mechanism is the EPR eect which is explained
in Section 2.2.1. Eventually, rHDL will be recognized by the SR-B1 receptor of the cancer
cells.
rHDL can be used to deliver anti-cancer drugs to tumors. Knowledge of the biological
mechanisms of cancer is evident to design a proper delivery vehicle. Therefore, cancer
biology is briey described in the following.
2.2
Cancer biology
Cancer is one of the leading causes of death in many parts of the world [6, 13]. It is a
disease that can hit people of any age, and can originate in most organs. The prognosis
depends on the type of cancer. Some cancers are mostly cured whereas lung cancer for
example is resistant to treatment [13].
Cancer is characterized by cells that dier from normal tissue by means of dierentiation
and growth. Cancer cells are uncontrolled and self-governing due to decit in normal reg-
ulatory controls of growth and dierentiation. Proliferation rates are excessive in tumors
and the cells are poorly dierentiated. The tumor is more undientiated and show higher
proliferation rates the more malignant it is. The cells merely operate to ensure their own
survival. High proliferation rates can partly be explained by avoidance of apoptosis (pro-
grammed cell death) which gives cancer cells unlimited life span. Moreover, malignant
tumors grow by invasion, meaning that they invade and inltrate local tissue, making the
boundary between healthy and cancerous tissue indistinct. Malignant tumors also have
the ability to metastasise through blood vessels and lymph channels to create secondary
tumors [13].
Currently, cancer is most often treated by surgical removal, radiation, and/or chemother-
apy.
In most cases, the treatment methods are used in a combination in a so called
multimodal treatment plan. Chemotherapeutics is the broad name for drugs which seeks

2.3.1
HPLC
HPLC is a rened column system that can separate molecules according to certain char-
acteristics. It uses simple column techniques to separate the molecules, but it can be
neccesary to use pressure to ensure adequate ow rates through the more ner column
material. Columns separate proteins according to characteristics by containing beads with
certain functions [28].
In Size Exclusion Chromotography (SEC), the column consist of porous beads. Here,
small molecules can enter, whereas the large ones cannot. Because the small molecules
enter the beads, they will travel slowly through the medium, and in contrast, the large
molecules will quickly go through. Hence, large particles are eluted rst, following smaller
molecules, see Figure 2.4(a) [28].
In ion-exchange chromatography, the column contains beads that are either positive or
negative and binds proteins of the opposite charge see Figure 2.4(b). When using anionic
beads, positive moieties of the protein will bind, and negatively charged proteins will be
removed. Hence, a molecule of specic charge can be separated from molecules of opposite
charge. The protein of interest can be eluted from the column by increasing the sodium
chloride concentration in the elution buer. The protein with the smallest net charge will
tend to be released rst [28].

5.4
Further discussions
There are several techniques for investigating biodistribution of nanoparticles in vivo.
Optical imaging was the technique pursued in this thesis despite limitations such as low
spatial resolution and relatively low detection depth within the tissue due to scattering
and absorption of the light [10, 31]. The instrument used in this thesis creates 2D images.
Image intensities are inuenced by thickness of object as a consequence of collapsing the
third dimension. Moreover, is the method only qualitative and inuenced by autouores-
cence. However, despite the above mentioned limitations, optical imaging is advantageous
because it is cheap, fast, uorophores are accessible and it does not use ionizing radiation.
If uorophores emitting light in the NIR-region is used, autouorescence is minimal [10].
Also, much eort was in this thesis made to overcome the limitations of the technique. Ex
vivo images of the organs were performed to better distinguish the relative distribution
between organs. Bulk uorescence measurements of tissue homogenates were sought to
evaluate the usefulness of ex vivo organ NIR images and to pursue quantication.
Another popular technique used for assessing biodistribution of nanoparticles in vivo is
PET. This technique relies of the labelling of the nanoparticles with radionuclides that
emit positrons. PET-imaging detects the two gamma photons emitted in opposite direc-
tion as a consequence of the annihilation of the positron and an electron. The attenuation
of the photons within the tissue is corrected in the reconstruction through attenuation
maps often provided by Computed Tomography (CT) by the combination of PET and CT
[48]. This technique is therefore very sensitive, quantitative and not restricted by tissue
depth. Images are tomographic, enabling temporal quantication of biodistribution by
drawing ROIs. PET is a functional imaging technique lacking anatomical reference, how-
ever this is often provided by CT images that are aligned the PET images. The downsides
of using PET for biodistribution studies is exposure to ionizing radiation which make the
process laborious, also, the radionuclides decay. The decay can be corrected for by sim-
ple math, however, PET radionuclides have relatively short half live and the duration of
biodistribution must therefore be taken into account. Moreover, the technique is expen-
sive and image acquisition is slow [10].
The biodistribution of the rHDL particles were only determined at a single time point,
however it could have been interesting to investigate the temporal biodistribution. This
is possible using uorophore labelled particles, however, not from the in vivo images pro-
vided by the IVIS®. More mice should have been used, so that groups of mice could be
sacried at dierent time points. If the ROI analysis of ex vivo images had been satisfac-
tory, this would have been easier to conduct. However, it appears that homogenizing the
organs and performing bulk uorescence measurements are more accurate, but it would
be time consuming to homogenize, create dilution series and measure uorescence from
tumor and organs of many mice. Temporal biodistribtion could have been sought with
PET/CT imaging as it is possible from these to quantify the organ accumulation by the
use of ROIs drawn on CT encircling the tumor and organs as done in Huda et al. [9].
The number of mice would be minimal for this method however the disadvantages are as
mentioned previously.
Both aforementioned techniques rely on the detection of photons emitted from either
uorophores or radionuclides attached to the nanoparticle. A major concern is the possi-
bility of disassociation of the ourophore [35] or the radionuclide from the particles, which

give faulty information and incorrect biodistribution images. Careful considerations must
therefore be put into the labelling strategy and high in vivo stability must be ensured.
Pérez-Medina et al. [8] tested the attachment of 89Zr on both the lipid layer and the
protein component of rHDL particles for assessing their biodistribution with PET. Both
labelling strategies showed high accumulation in tumor and liver likewise the results from
this thesis. However they also reported a high uptake in the kidneys for both labelling
strategies perhaps indicating dissociation of the radionuclide which then is removed by
renal clearence. High kidney uptake was signicantly higher for protein-labelling than the
lipid-labelling. This might suggest that the labelling strategy might impair the stability
of the particle (likewise the PEG molecule) and/or reduce the ApoA-Is ability to take up
new lipids. Therefore it can be cleared in the kidneys due to its small size (<5 nm [6]).
Huda et al. [9] went for another radiolabel strategy where 64Cu is bound to a chelator
DOTA which was conjugated to the protein component of engineered human HDLs. The
uptake was high in the liver and tumor as expected, but the uptake in the kidneys are
approximately double compared to the liver. This could be explained by disassociation
of the 64Cu from DOTA, or inability of the particle to reassemble after the release of
lipids. Both studies [8, 9] claim that their labelling strategy is stable enough in vivo for
satisfactory biodistribution studies. The stability of the particles used in this thesis have
not been investigated. Partly because the biodistribution of the cargo has been of focus.
The cargo is the uorophore which should portray the drug to be delivered preferentially
to the tumor. However, the stability of the particle should be investigated in the future.
The uptake in the kidneys are not high in the studies performed in this thesis. This can
be explained by the uorophore labelling strategy. Here, the DiR is mostly expected to
be cleared through the hepatic pathway because it is lipophilic and anchored into the
lipid layer.
Previous studies using lipid-anchored DiR [24, 3, 7] show similar level of
accumulation of the uorophore in the kidneys as this study.
Photobleaching is when uorophores stop to uoresce and must be considered and taken
into account when measuring uorescence. However it is dicult to take into account
because the level of photobleaching is unknown. Photobleaching was investigated briey
during the thesis in order to examine if it was an issue for the measurements. However
the photobleaching was dicult to detect as the uorescence varied from measurement
to measurement. Photobleaching was attempted to be studied for both the NIR-imaging
acquisitions and for the bulk uorescence measurements. Photobleaching by NIR images
was sought by taking an image immediately after another image. Looking at ROI mea-
surements made from the same area of the two images could reveal a decrease in radiance
which likely could be due to photobleaching. However in three out of six mice the total
ux increased in second image. Hence, the photobleaching does not seem detectable from
image to image. Possibly the level of photobleaching could be investigated, by acquir-
ing more photos one after another and doing this for many mice, nonetheless this was
not pursued in this thesis. It was also investigated if photobleaching was an issue for
the bulk uorescence by measuring the same well plate three times. From rst to third
measurement, the uorescence intensities increased on average by 6.5%. Hence, the photo-
bleaching is not an issue for bulk uorescence measurements, at least not when measuring
the same plate 3 times. From the well plate that was measured 3 times, the variation
between measurement was also estimated. Tumor and liver, which showed high uores-
cence intensity, could vary approximately 1-2% from measurement to measurement. For

the kidney which showed lower uorescence, measurements varied approximately 5% from
measurement to measurement. Variations in measurements can be caused by background
and is amplied when increasing gain. It is therefore important to have high uorescence
however while being in the linear region [11, 44].
When examining the biodistribution of particles, it is important that the amount of
material injected is in the correct window. There must be enough injected material to
saturate the blood cells while there still are particles enough to accumulate in the tissues.
In addition, high amount of material may also be toxic to the receiver. The amount
of material to be injected was based on biodistribution studies on sHDL by Tang et al.
[3] and Liadaki et al. [24] where a dose of 200 µg/kg and 100 µg/kg DiR respectively
was used. From the molar ratio between DiR and ApoA-I used in this thesis, it was
found that injecting with a dose of approximately 3 mg/kg ApoA-I would lie in between
the doses indicated by the aforementioned studies. The two rst pilot studies showed
satisfactory accumulation of the uorophore in tissues when approximating the dose to 3
mg/kg ApoA-I and therefore this dose was also used for the nal experiment.
In this chapter the ndings from the experiments have been stated and interpreted. The
limitations of the reported results were also presented as well as a comparison of the
ndings to similar studies.

CONCLUSION
In this thesis the biodistribution of rHDL particles to be used as drug delivery vehicles
as investigated in xenograft and syngeneic mouse models in pilot studies. The biodistri-
bution seemed similar despite mouse model and because the syngeneic mouse model is
advantageous because it is more cheap and a better model when exploring the rHDL for
its ability to also deliver immune therapy. The syngeneic mouse tumor model was used
in the nal full-scale biodistribution study.
From the two pilot studies, an appropriate homogenization method was established in
order to perform bulk uorescence measurements and in future, ELISA measurements of
homogenates. Homogenization method consisted of creating tissue powder by fracturing
the tumor or organ after submersing it in LN2. The tissue powder was hereafter resus-
pended in Irvine lysis buer with protease inhibitor cocktail and incubated for 1 hour.
Samples were centrifuged at 10.000 rpm for 15 minutes at 4°C and supernatant was col-
lected for measurements. Bulk uorescence was measured from homogenate with known
tissue concentration and a dilution series thereof conrmed linearity of measurements,
which is a necessity to use uorescence quantitatively.
The particles were labelled with two uorophores which enabled the possibility to measure
the biodistribution based on the two uorophores. However tissue homogenates of control
mice showed high levels of uorescence at 484 nm and 550 nm corresponding to the exci-
tation wavelengths of DiO and DiI respectively. Control mice did not show uorescence
at 750 nm which is the excitation wavelength of DiR. Despite taking the autouorescence
into account in the bulk uorescence measurements, the biodistribution measured from
DiR and DiO or DiI was not identical nor completely dierent. However, the biodistribu-

tion should be determined from uorophores in the NIR window where autouorescence
and absorbance is minimal. In this thesis, the DiR uorophore was use with success.
The biodistribution was assessed by three steps: 1) in vivo imaging, 2) ex vivo imaging
and 3) bulk uorescence measurements from tissue homogenates. From rst step the ac-
cumulation of the uorophore in tumor over time and signal drop due to clearance and
degradation was seen. The distribution between tissues became visible in step 2 in the ex
vivo images of the excised tumor and organs. From here it was also possible to extract the
total ux and the average radiance that could be used to attain the relative dierence in
distribution between tissues. The nal step allowed quantication of the biodistribution
expressed as %ID and %ID/g. Hence, each step was a step closer to asses a more precise
distribution and to quantify the biodistribution of the DiR loaded rHDL particles. Bland-
Altman plots of results obtained from step 2 ex vivo imaging and step 3 bulk uorescence
showed a discrepancy which is thought to be caused by geometric and secondary eects
in 2D optical images.
The biodistribution was investigated for the rHDL particles and a PEG-modied version
of it. The hypothesis was that attaching a PEG polymer to the N-terminal of one of the
ApoA-Is of the rHDL would decrease liver uptake and possibly increase tumor uptake.
However, the %ID/g from the rHDL uptake in tumor was 12.94 ± 0.75 which was higher
than 10.72 ± 0.42 %ID/g of the PEG-rHDL. The uptake in the liver was similar for
both formulations: 15.88 ± 1.83 and 16.95 ± 0.87 %ID/g for rHDL and PEG-rHDL
respectively. The eect was quantied as a tumor to liver ratio based on the %ID/g. A
higher tumor/liver ratio is desired to improve drug delivery eciency of the rHDL however,
the tumor/liver ratio decreased from 0.82 ± 0.09 to 0.55 ± 0.15 when PEG-modifying the
rHDL particle. Thus, the PEG-modication had an eect on the biodistribution, however
not what was hypothesized nor desired.

Future perspectives
A limitation to this study was the small amount of ApoA-I which limited the number
of mice. Also for future drug delivery purposes, the supply of ApoA-I must be sucient
and therefore the purication of ApoA-I must be optimized. PEG-NHS-20K is not as
N-terminal specic as the PEG-ALD-20K normally used and therefore the N-terminal
binding of PEG-NHS-20K on rHDL should be veried. A possible attachment of PEG to
the lysines of ApoA-I could explain why results do not show what was hypothesized.
Moreover, if the size of the particle after PEG-modication causes the decreased tumor
uptake, the PEG-modication of rHDL with smaller PEG polymers of for example 5 kDa
could be investigated. Also, the stability of the particles should be investigated to see if
PEG-modication changes the dynamics of the particle resulting in rapid dissociation of
lipids and uorophores.
ELISA measurements of tissue homogenates can reveal ApoA-I distribution and be com-
pared with the uorophore distribution to gain insight into the dynamics of the rHDL
