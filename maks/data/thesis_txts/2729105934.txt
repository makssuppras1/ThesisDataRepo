Summary  
 
 
 
CO2 methanation is pivotal for sustainability, capturing diverse CO2 emissions and converting them 
into environmentally friendly methane. It mitigates greenhouse gas effects and provides versatile 
energy for heating, power, and industry. Methane also serves as a precursor for chemicals and 
materials, fostering a circular economy. 
 
Methanation is the conversion of CO and CO2 to CH4 through hydrogenation.  To overcome kinetic 
limitations in CO2 methanation, it is crucial to employ a catalyst that enables high conversion of 
CO2 and selectivity towards CH4. Among the extensively researched catalysts for this process, Ni-
based catalysts are particularly favored due to their exceptional activity and cost-effectiveness. 
According to the literature, the most widely accepted mechanism of the methanation reaction is the 
combination of an endothermic reversed water gas shift (RWGS) reaction and an exothermic CO 
methanation, respectively: 𝐻2 + 𝐶𝑂2 → 𝐶𝑂 + 𝐻2𝑂 ∆𝐻 = 41 𝑘𝐽/𝑚𝑜𝑙 and 3𝐻2 + 𝐶𝑂 → 𝐶𝐻4 + 𝐻2𝑂, 
∆𝐻 = −206 𝑘𝐽/𝑚𝑜𝑙 CH4. 
 
CeO2 stands out as one of the most catalytically active materials for reduction/oxidation reactions, 
thanks to its remarkable capacity to transition between Ce3+ and Ce4+ states. This unique ability 
contributes to its exceptional performance in various catalytic processes. Compared to other oxide 
supports, CeO2 also enhances the performance of noble metal catalysts in various oxidation 
reactions. Oxygen vacancies, always companied by Ce3+, play a crucial role in oxidation reactions 
over CeO2 and its vastly mentioned in the literature.  
 
The study aimed to prepare and assess experimentally Ni catalysts supported on CeO2 for CO2 
methanation. Different Ni concentrations (5% Ni/CeO2, 0.02% Ni/CeO2, and CeO2) were used, with 
a focus on vacancies and a small Ni concentration. A range of comprehensive characterization 
techniques was employed to investigate both the CeO2 support and the as-prepared catalysts, such 
as EPR, Raman, TPR, and TPSR of CO2.

Preface  
 
 
 
This thesis unveils contributions to the field of catalysis (ceria as support for Ni) for CO2 
methanation through a combination of laboratory experiments and a review of relevant literature. 
 
The experimental work was carried out at the National Institute of Technology (INT) in Rio de 
Janeiro, Brazil, under the guidance of researcher Priscilla Zonetti (INT), during January and 
February of 2019, and October of 2022. The overall supervision of the project was provided by 
Professor Susanne Mossin from DTU Chemistry.  
 
The thesis encompasses four comprehensive chapters. Chapter 1 introduces the methanation 
reaction, catalyst development, the chemistry of cerium oxide, and the significance of oxygen 
vacancies. Chapter 2 outlines the experimental methodology, with a specific emphasis on catalyst 
preparation, characterization techniques, and equipment description. Chapter 3 presents the results 
and engages in an in-depth discussion, highlighting the criticality of oxygen vacancies in the study. 
Finally, Chapter 4 concludes the thesis, offering recommendations for future investigations, 
including the potential integration of density functional theory in exploring this catalyst further. 
 
It is my sincere hope that this thesis contributes to the existing knowledge and sparks further 
research in the field of CO2 methanation catalysis. May it serve as a steppingstone for future 
investigations and advancements, ultimately contributing to the sustainable utilization of carbon 
dioxide.

Chapter 1 
 
Introduction  
 
 
1.1 
Methanation 
 
Fossil fuels are still one of the primary sources of energy and their use has led to an emission of 
massive amounts of the greenhouse gas CO2. Since the industrial revolution, annual CO2 emissions 
from the combustion of fuel have intensely increased from near zero to over 33.8 Gt CO2 in 2014 
[1]. In addition, CO2 is considered the main cause of climate change and has led to a record increase 
in the atmosphere exceeding 402.3ppm on global average in 2016. If CO2 continues to increase 
substantially, it will lead to further increase the global average temperature [1]. 
 
Among different forms of fossil fuels, natural gas that consists primarily of methane is ideal, owing 
to its ready availability, high energy density and conversion efficiency, and smoke- and slag-free 
composition [2]. 
 
The most studied technology to reduce CO2 emissions is Carbon Capture and Sequestration (CCS), 
which consists of CO2 capture, transportation, and underground storage. Alternatively, the captured 
CO2 could be utilized and converted into fuels and chemicals, such as dry reforming of methane for 
synthesis gas production, or CO2 hydrogenation to CH4, methanol or higher alcohols [3]. 
 
The development of renewable energy projects is increasing in number which results in the 
production of excess electrical energy that cannot be sent to the electrical grid due to either the 
longer distance from the existing electrical grid or excess production at the time when the demand 
is less. This excess renewable electricity can therefore be used to produce hydrogen via water 
electrolysis, which is considered a renewable electricity storage route [1]. If such practices are 
applied and enable to produce renewable H2, this excess hydrogen can be used as a reactant to 
convert CO2 into fuels based on the power-to-fuel process, or into other products like methane, 
methanol, ethanol, formic acid, and dimethyl carbonate.  
 
Figure 1 summarizes the possible routes of the CO2 conversion combined with renewable energy 
in sustainable energy and production [1]. The renewable energy is used either directly or indirectly, 
according to two main possibilities: (i) production of renewable H2 or (ii) generation of electrons, 
or electrons/protons (by photooxidation of water) used in the electrochemical paths.  
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
 
11 
 
 
Figure 1: Schematic overview of possible routes of the CO2 conversion to incorporate renewable 
energy in the chemical and energy chains. Reprinted from [1]  
 
Recently, the CO2 methanation has gained renewed interest due to its application in the so-called 
power-to-gas technology as well as biogas upgrading. In power-to-gas technology, H2 produced 
from excess renewable energy is reacted with CO2 and chemically transformed to methane, which 
can be stored and transported through the well-developed natural gas infrastructure already in place 
[3]. The utilization of CH4 produced from CO2 through the utilization of H2 derived from renewable 
sources is an innovative approach and offers a promising and environmentally friendly pathway. 
 
The CO2 methanation reaction is exothermic, characterized by ΔH298K = -165.4 kJ/mol and ΔG298K 
= -130.8 kJ/mol. In this reaction, H2 and CO2 combine to form CH4 and H2O. Converting fully 
oxidized carbon to CH₄ requires an eight-electron reduction, which has significant kinetic barriers. 
Thus, a catalyst is essential for achieving the desired reaction rates and selectivity for CH4 
production. Under ambient pressure, maintaining a temperature below 300°C is crucial to achieve a 
conversion rate greater than 90% with high selectivity towards methane [1]. 
 
The reaction stoichiometry is shown in equation 1.

The most widely accepted mechanism of the methanation reaction is the combination of an 
endothermic reversed water gas shift (RWGS) reaction and an exothermic CO methanation, as 
shown in equation 2 and equation 3, respectively [3].

Several other thermodynamic studies can be found in literature as described [1]. Also, during 
methanation, some side-reactions may occur which affects the purity of the natural gas product [2]. 
 
Still according to [1], CO2 methanation is favored at a lower temperature than expected due to the 
exothermic nature. At ambient pressure, the temperature should be lower than 300°C to maintain a 
conversion > 90% and high selectivity to methane. The feasible temperature range enlarges when 
the pressure increases (e.g., T < 500 °C at 30 bar). If the temperature increases further the reversed 
water-gas shift is more pronounced and the selectivity to CH4 drops quickly. The carbon formation 
is not expected at a H2/CO2 ratio of 4. Lower ratios, in contrast, show significant carbon formation, 
which is undesirable. On the other hand, ratios of H2/CO2 significantly greater than 4 are beneficial 
in terms of suppressing carbon formation but valuable hydrogen is left. The addition of steam to the 
feed lowers CO2 conversion slightly due to Le Chatelier’s principle because H2O is one of the 
products formed, but it can prevent carbon formation in CO2 as well as CO methanation. However, 
the presence of excess H2O in the catalytic bed can facilitate the oxidation of reduced metal particles 
which, in turn, result in faster deactivation of the catalyst [1].  
 
The mechanisms in general, can be divided into two categories: an associative scheme and a 
dissociative scheme [1]. In an associative scheme, CO2 adsorbs associatively and reacts with 
adsorbed hydrogen atoms to form oxygenates which are hydrogenated to methane in subsequent 
steps. The distinctive feature is that no CO intermediate is formed in this sequence. On the contrary, 
in a dissociative scheme, CO2 dissociates first into carbonyl (CO) and an oxygen atom. The carbonyl 
is then hydrogenated step by step in the same mechanism as CO methanation proceeds to form 
finally methane. At this point, one can anticipate that no consensus has been reached on which 
mechanism is exact so far. 
 
The authors in reference [45] also provided insights into the two mechanisms for CO2 methanation. 
One mechanism involves CO2 absorbing H atoms to form oxygenated intermediates, which are 
subsequently hydrogenated to CH4 (associative pathway). The other mechanism involves CO2 
dissociating into COad and adsorbed O, followed by COad hydrogenation to CH4 (dissociative 
pathway). The possible formation mechanism of methane is displayed in the Figure 2.

[47] introduces an innovative microkinetic model that provides a comprehensive description of 
surface kinetics involved in CO and CO2 methanation over nickel catalysts. Through calibration 
against diverse datasets, the model captures a wide range of conditions and catalyst compositions, 
shedding light on the significance of surface carbide and the preference for the formate pathway. It 
adeptly simulates the methanation of CO, CO2, and their co-existence, thereby facilitating research 
and optimizing reactor design. Regarding CO2 methanation, the authors discuss the challenges 
arising from differing experimental conditions, which result in varying suggestions regarding the 
precise reaction pathways. Adsorbed CO2 may undergo either dissociation, leading to the formation 
of CO(s), subsequently following the CO methanation mechanism via a surface carbide species, or 
it may react directly with hydrogen, forming oxygenated species like carboxyl, COOH(s), or 
formate, HCOO(s), which then undergo dissociation to form CO(s) or further hydrogenation to 
methane (direct hydrogenation path). Furthermore, it’s mentioned that the reaction pathway may 
vary based on the support material of Ni catalysts. This divergence could stem from catalyst 
structural aspects such as particle size and crystal face exposure, along with the adsorption and 
desorption characteristics of the support material, influencing the predominant reaction pathways. 
While inert supports tend to favor direct CO2 dissociation and formate involvement, more basic 
materials propose CO2 adsorption and carbonate species formation directly on the support.

1.2 
Catalyst development 
 
The CH4 product gas, here obtained from CO2 methanation, is versatile since it could be either used 
as synthetic natural gas (SNG) for direct supply to the existing natural gas networks or employed as 
intermediates for production of higher carbon chemicals [4]. Research and development have been 
focusing on (i) development of highly active/selective catalysts and (ii) construction of efficient 
system for handling a large amount of CO2. 
 
Catalyst evaluation is mostly conducted at atmospheric pressure. However, pressure is of great 
importance in terms of optimizing the process. Temperatures above 500°C can cause sintering and 
increase carbon deposition, leading to catalyst deactivation [3].  
 
According to [1], the methanation reaction is limited by thermodynamics. So, it is necessary to 
design catalysts of suitable activity to reach conversions close to equilibrium at moderate reaction 
temperatures (< 350 °C) and high-space velocities.   
 
Both supported noble and nonnoble metals are known to be used as catalysts for CO2 methanation 
[1], the activity of the metals can be arranged as follow: Ru>Fe>Ni>Co>Rh>Pd> Pt. As pointed out 
in [1], Ni and Ru based catalysts produce almost exclusively CH4, while less reactive metal catalysts 
of Pd, Pt, Rh, Mo, Re and Au simultaneously produce CH4, CH3OH and CO (by RWGS) in CO2 
methanation. Cu and Ag catalyze mainly CH3OH. Supported Ni catalyst are the most promising 
catalysts for COx (x = 1, 2) methanation due to their good catalytic performance and relatively low 
price.  
 
Oxides such as Al2O3, TiO2, ZrO2 and CeO2 are the most used supports for nickel catalysts. Among 
them, Ni/SiO2 and Ni/Al2O3 have been widely studied due to their good initial activities, which 
however often suffers from sintering and serious carbon deposition at high temperatures. 
Additionally, Ru based catalysts are also offered for the methanation processes. It was reported that 
the methanation catalyst MCR-2X from Haldor Topsøe could be operated at high temperatures with 
high reaction rates to produce high-quality steam thus making the process more energy efficient [2]. 
 
According to [3], recently, most of the attention on catalyst development for CO2 methanation has 
been support and promoter improvement for Ni based catalyst. Composite support consists of two 
or more typical oxides has been found to improve catalytic properties such as reducibility, active 
metal dispersion and stability. On the other hand, nickel supported on different metal oxides (mainly 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
 
16 
 
on γ-Al2O3) is the most common catalyst used for the CO2 methanation [1]. It possesses high 
activity, high CH4 selectivity, and a low cost. 
 
In [1] is highlighted that different supports have been investigated for Ni catalysts because it is well 
known that the catalytic performance strongly depends on the nature and properties of the support. 
The supports investigated include SiO2, TiO2, CeO2, ZrO2, hydrotalcites, zeolites, and CNTs. 
Another point emphasized is that although Ni on γ-Al2O3 catalysts synthesized using different 
methods are preferred in catalytic methanation, some challenges remain, such as carbon deposition, 
sintering, Ni (CO)4 formation, and sulfur poisoning during CO2 methanation. Therefore, the stability 
of the methanation catalyst is of great importance besides activity and selectivity.  
 
In the work presented by [5], Ni/CeO2 was determined to be the most active for CO and CO2 
methanation among Ni catalysts supported on different supports such as: Al2O3, SiO2, TiO2, CeO2, 
and ZrO2. These catalytic activities increased with increasing surface area of CeO2. The high Ni 
dispersion and an intimate contact between Ni and ceria appeared to be responsible for the high 
catalytic activity for CO methanation. Furthermore, strong CO2 adsorption on CeO2 is beneficial for 
CO2 methanation. A further increase in the specific catalytic activity for CO and CO2 methanation 
can be made using Ni-CeO2 catalyst prepared by co-precipitation method. The best catalyst, 
Ni0.8Ce0.2Ox, has the high Ni content and small Ni particles. This catalyst can be applied to the low-
temperature CO or CO2 methanation reactor to achieve high single-pass conversions of CO and 
CO2. 
 
CeO2 supported Ni catalysts seems to be more active and selective towards CH4 formation when 
compared to other oxides such as TiO2, MgO, and Al2O3 during CO2 methanation [6]. The greatest 
distinguishing attribute of CeO2 is OVs and oxygen storage capacity, which set it apart from other 
metal oxides. Surface oxygen vacancies (OVs) on metal oxides is thought to be constitute active 
centers for CO2 activation [7][8]. These vacancies can act as binding sites for surface-adsorbed 
species or traps for excited electronic charges. 
 
1.3 
Cerium oxides  
 
The relative ease of switching between the 4+ and 3+ oxidation states and the high oxygen storage 
capacity are key factors in the catalytic activity of CeO2. To develop active, selective, and stable 
catalysts for the removal and conversion of acid gases, a prerequisite is to gain a fundamental 
understanding of how these gases interact with, and convert, at the surfaces of catalysts [9].  
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
 
17 
 
Cerium oxides have attracted progressive attention as very efficient catalyst for water splitting, solar 
cells, pollutant degradation, and oxidation-reduction transformations [10]. Owing to the unique 
ability of shuttling between Ce3+ and Ce4+ states, CeO2 is among the most catalytically active 
materials for oxidation reactions [11]. Relative to other oxide supports, CeO2 also enhances the 
performance of noble metal catalysts (Pt, Pd, Rh, Au, Ag, etc.) in a variety of oxidation reactions 
[11]. Furthermore, the strong intrinsic redox property of this material has been only recently 
discovered [10].  
 
The oxygen vacancies (OVs), always companied by Ce3+, play crucial roles for oxidation reactions 
over CeO2. For example, when an adsorbate is oxidized at the surface, the oxidant is often a surface 
lattice oxygen atom, thus creating a surface oxygen vacancy (the Mars-van Krevelen mechanism). 
Such a mechanism explained the CO oxidation process over CeO2-based catalysts well. The Ce3+-
OV species can store electrons, activate oxygen, and are therefore identified as important active 
phases involving in the Mars-van Krevelen cycle of CO oxidation [11].  
 
It is not easy to distinguish the roles of Ox (surface active oxygen) and OVs (oxygen vacancies) over 
CeO2 in reactions separately. This is because Ox- is always accompanied with Ce3+- OV, while the 
latter varies with the Ce3+/Ce4+ dynamic shuttling during reaction. Besides oxygen vacancies and 
Ox-, hydroxyl species were also reportedly involved in various oxidation reactions. For example, 
when treated at elevated temperatures, -OH on ceria can react with CO via water–gas shift type 
reactions: CO + OH → 0.5H2 + CO2 [11]. 
 
Ceria is regarded to be stabilized in several bulk phases with stoichiometric ranging from fully 
oxidized state (CeO2) to totally reduced state (Ce2O3). As pointed out by [10], though the oxygen 
vacancy (OV) is expected to significantly affect the various properties of ceria, a detailed study of 
the relationship between the OV and the performance of Ceria is still lacking. A systematic study 
of the role of the OV on the modulation of properties in ceria should lay a foundation for developing 
various applications of ceria controllably introducing OVs. 
 
The methods used for the catalyst preparation can affect the crystal structure, catalytic performance, 
metal dispersion, and possible amount of metal loading [9]. Different techniques have been used for 
the preparation of catalysts for CO2 methanation. The commonly used methods include incipient 
wetness impregnation, co-precipitation, sol-gel, microemulsion, and the double impregnation 
method.

Different synthesis procedure can lead to differences in the surface properties of the resulting ceria, 
such as the amount and nature of defect sites. Consequently, the interaction with CO2 can be 
different for ceria synthesized by different procedures [9].  
 
In addition, the interactions of CO2 can vary depending on the synthesis methods employed. As 
discussed in reference [9], two distinct approaches were employed to synthesize nanostructured 
CeO2 powders: (i) precipitation from a basic solution and (ii) a hydrolysis method facilitated by 
microwave-assisted heating. CeO2 particles prepared via precipitation exhibited lower surface areas 
(41 m2/g) and broader particle size distributions (8-15 nm) compared to those synthesized using the 
microwave-assisted method, which resulted in smaller particles (3.3-4 nm) and a higher degree of 
reduction. The enhanced reduction in CeO2 from the microwave-assisted method can be attributed 
to the reduced particle size. Because the interaction of acid gases (such as CO2) with CeO2 starts 
with adsorption of the gas on the surface of CeO2, the surface structure of CeO2 is deemed to be 
vital in determining catalytic activity.

In the case of CeO2, the facets (111), (100), and (110) represent specific crystallographic planes. 
These facets can have different surface properties and reactivity due to variations in atomic 
arrangement and exposed atoms on the surface. The specific properties and reactivity of these facets 
play a crucial role in various applications, including catalysis, as different facets can exhibit different 
catalytic behaviors and selectivities.

Surface cations (Ce4+) and anions (O2-) exhibit varying coordination states on different surfaces. 
Bulk ceria has Ce and O coordination numbers of 8 and 4, respectively. On ceria surfaces (111), 
(100), and (110), the coordination numbers of Ce4+ cations are 7, 6, and 6, while O2- anions have 
coordination numbers of 3, 2, and 3, respectively. These differences in surface atom arrangements 
and coordinative unsaturation affect oxygen-vacancy formation energies, surface-adsorbate 
interactions, and redox and acid-base properties in ceria forms with defined surface planes. It is 
generally believed that the (111), (110), and (100) facets dominate the surfaces of ceria nanocrystals, 
with morphologies of octahedra, rods, and cubes, respectively [9].  
 
In [46], researchers studied the XRD patterns of raw nanoceria and different ceria powders under 
varying treatment conditions to examine changes in crystal size. They found that increasing the 
treatment temperature resulted in larger crystal sizes. Treating ceria with hydrogen led to an 
expansion of the lattice parameter due to the reduction of Ce4+ to Ce3+ ions, causing lattice 
expansion. This was because the ionic radius of Ce4+ (0.970 Å) is smaller than that of Ce3+ (1.143 
Å), leading to an expansion of the lattice during reduction. Conversely, treating ceria with 
atmospheric air decreased the lattice parameter as the treatment temperature increased. This 
reduction was due to the oxidation of Ce3+ back to Ce4+ ions, leading to a contraction of the lattice. 
Overall, they found that the changes in the ceria lattice parameter with treatment temperature were 
influenced by the interplay between reduction and oxidation processes, impacting the balance of 
Ce3+ and Ce4+ ions in the lattice structure. The observed lattice expansion and contraction were 
influenced by the differences in ionic radii between Ce3+ and Ce4+ ions. 
 
According to [12], formic acid adsorbs on CeOX (111) surfaces, undergoing decomposition at 
different temperatures. On oxidized CeO2 (111), formate decomposes to H2 and CO2 at 77 °C, 
shifting to predominantly CO production above 400 K. On reduced CeOX (111), only CO production 
is observed at higher temperatures. The presence of oxygen vacancies influences the decomposition 
process, with formate adsorbed in low coordination sites atop Ce cations decomposing at lower 
temperatures. In contrast, formate adsorbed in higher coordination oxygen vacancy sites leads to 
higher-temperature decomposition. These findings highlight the role of adsorption sites in 
determining the reaction pathways and temperatures during formic acid decomposition on CeOX 
(111) surfaces.

1.4 
Oxygen Vacancies (OVs) 
 
Oxygen vacancies (OVs) in cerium oxide catalysts play a crucial role in their catalytic activity. 
These vacancies are defects in the crystal lattice where oxygen atoms are missing, resulting in 
localized electron density variations. The presence of oxygen vacancies creates redox-active sites, 
enabling the catalyst to participate in oxygen transfer reactions. These vacancies enhance the oxygen 
storage capacity and facilitate the redox cycles required for many catalytic processes.  
 
Surface defects, particularly oxygen vacancies (OVs), act as active sites for catalysis and have a 
significant impact on heterogeneous catalysis. When a reactant molecule is oxidized, an oxygen 
atom is removed from CeO2 lattice which leads to the development of an oxygen vacancy. This 
oxygen vacancy can then be rejuvenated by an oxygen atom present in the reactant molecule, 
thereby completing the stoichiometric catalytic cycle. An oxygen vacancy on the surface of the 
CeO2 could serve as a singular “active site” under these circumstances. This “active site” is 
dynamically produced and destroyed depending on the conditions of the reaction. Surface oxygen 
vacancy is regarded as a type of defect that can be discovered on CeO2 and other reducible oxides 
[13]. 
 
The vacant oxygen sites may facilitate the separation of reactants during the hydrogenation process, 
while H2 binds to trace metal species on a CeO2 catalyst. Electron-rich OVs promote hydrogenation 
in this scenario, enforcing metal support interactions [14]. 
 
The exposed crystal planes and the concentration of their surface OVs are strongly influenced by 
the morphology of the CeO2 catalyst. In recent years, several scientists have successfully produced 
CeO2 in a variety of morphologies, including nano octahedra, nano cubes, and nanorods. Since the 
nanorods are confined by their (110) and (100) crystal planes, the surface of the rods has the largest 
concentration of oxygen vacancy sites [15].  
 
The structural, electronic/redox properties, reducibility, OVs, surface area, and basicity of the 
catalysts work together to promote the activity of the catalysts. It is well recognized that 
physicochemical characteristics such as the population of basic sites, particle size, dispersion of the 
active sites, and the interaction between metal and support have a major impact on improved 
catalytic activity and selectivity. CeO2 has excellent chemical stability, and a stable Ce3+/Ce4+ redox 
pair, which may aid in the formation of abundant oxygen vacancies. Since CeO2 contains a high 
number of oxygen vacancies, it is capable of significantly boosting the catalytic activity for CO2 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
21 
 
methanation. CeO2's ability to boost catalytic performance due to the presence of moderately basic 
sites and its redox properties has been the subject of a significant amount of research and has been 
widely reported [16]. 
 
The presence of oxygen vacancies (OVs) plays a key role in the interactions of CO2 with CeO2-
based catalysts. The CO2 capture capacity of CeO2 catalysts can be enhanced by doping with metal 
cations, which increases the number of O vacancies. It has been reported that amine-based sorbents 
adsorb more CO2 in the presence of water vapor. The increase in the CO2 capture capacity might be 
due to the enhanced diffusion rates of physically bonded amine−water−CO2 moieties [9].  
 
Cerium has an electron structure of [Xe] 4f1 5d1 6s2, which is responsible for the excellent redox 
ability of Ce3+/Ce4+ cerium ions pair. Oxygen vacancies in ceria are formed on the surface and 
subsurface of cerium dioxide and enhance various properties of ceria. In pure stoichiometric CeO2, 
one cerium atom donates four electrons to fill the p orbitals of oxygen, but oxygen vacancies are 
easily formed due to the redox reaction between Ce4+ and Ce3+. Ceria was reported to maintain a 
fluorite structure even with 14% oxygen vacancy concentration [17]. 
 
When an oxygen vacancy is formed, the two electrons previously occupying the p orbital of the 
departed oxygen would be left and redistribute in the solid. CeO2 has a good oxygen storage capacity 
since oxygen vacancies form rapidly and are easily eliminated. The oxygen vacancies in ceria can 
enhance the oxygen storage capacity [17]. 
 
The CeO2's strong redox property and large oxygen storage capacity are principally correlated with 
the ease of oxygen release and absorption. When a reactant molecule is oxidized, an oxygen atom 
is removed from CeO2 lattice which leads to the development of an oxygen vacancy. This oxygen 
vacancy can then be rejuvenated by an oxygen atom present in the reactant molecule, thereby 
completing the stoichiometric catalytic cycle. An oxygen vacancy on the surface of the CeO2 could 
serve as a singular “active site” under these circumstances. This “active site” is dynamically 
produced and destroyed depending on the conditions of the reaction. Surface oxygen vacancy is 
regarded as a type of defect that can be discovered on CeO2 and other reducible oxides. By 
interacting with reactants and reaction intermediates, this type of defect has potential to play a 
substantial role in heterogeneous catalysis [18].  
 
In [19], a range of Ni-xCeO2/MCM-41 catalysts with a nickel content of 20 wt% CO2 methanation 
were synthetized. It was discovered that all CeO2-supported catalysts displayed more catalytic 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
 
22 
 
activity than Ni/MCM-41. At 380oC and atmospheric pressure, the catalyst modified with 20 wt % 
CeO2 exhibited the greatest catalytic performance.  
 
In reference [20], the impact of support morphology and oxygen vacancy on the catalytic 
performance of Ni-based catalysts for CO2 methanation at low temperatures was examined. It was 
found that the nanowire like Ni/CeO2 catalyst (Ni/W) showed the highest CO2 conversion. The 
dispersion of metallic Ni species on the CeO2 support was aided by oxygen vacancies, which also 
improved the metal-support contact and improved the catalytic activity.  
 
The study described by [21] aimed to investigate the role of oxygen vacancies in the methanation 
reaction using ZrO2 and Ni-based catalysts.  Ni/ZrO2 catalysts doped with Ca and Ni/ZrO2 were 
studied for CO2 methanation. Characterization techniques including XPS, XRD, EPR, and surface 
reaction analysis were employed. According to this study, the addition of Ca to Ni/ZrO2 did not alter 
the metallic surface area, but significantly increased the CO2 consumption rate. XRD, XPS, and 
EPR analyses indicated the presence of Ca2+ and Ni2+ ions on the ZrO2 surface lattice, forming pairs 
with oxygen vacancies and coordinatively unsaturated sites (cus). Increasing the number of these 
pairs enhanced the CO2 methanation rate and the number of active sites involved in the rate-limiting 
step, suggesting vacancies-cus sites pairs as the reaction sites. 
 
An approach is presented in the study conducted by [22] to enhance the efficiency of low-
temperature CO2 methanation using a Ni-based catalyst. By adjusting the surface oxygen vacancy 
and medium-strength basic sites of the CeO2 support through the introduction of La species and 
subsequent calcination at 600°C (CeO2-La-600), they observed the formation of a structure 
comprising a La-Ce-O solid solution with a thin La2O2CO3 layer.   
 
To investigate the influence of surface oxygen vacancies (OVs) on the performance of CO2 
methanation catalysts, the researchers [22] developed a unique flowerlike hollow structure of CeO2 
microspheres as a support for Ni-based catalysts, incorporating La as a dopant. Among all the 
catalysts tested, the La-doped CeO2 catalyst exhibited the highest catalytic activity in CO2 
methanation. This superior performance can be attributed to the increased number of OVs, which 
play a crucial role in methane formation. In the process, CO2 molecules are first adsorbed onto the 
surface of Ni/CeO2-La-600, where they interact with the oxygen vacancies (active O* sites) present 
at the interface of Ni, CeO2, and La. This interaction leads to the formation of OCO2* species. This 
reaction cycle continues until the desired quantity of OCO2* is generated. Subsequently, the 
dissociated H* species react with the newly formed OCO2* to form hydrogenated carbonates, which

then undergo further hydrogenation steps involving various intermediate species to produce CH4 
(methane). 
 
CO2 methanation using Ni/CeO2 catalysts prepared with different nickel loadings in the research

2.1 
Preparation of the support and catalysts 
 
Cerium oxide (CeO2) was prepared by the precipitation method using [(NH4)2Ce (NO3)4] as 
precursor (Figure 8A). The nitrate was dissolved in distilled water to obtain a concentration of 0.4 
molL-1 of Ce4+ ions. Then, a solution of NH4OH (27%) was slowly added to the solution under 
vigorous stirring (Figure 8B). Once the precipitate was obtained (Figure 8C), the suspension was 
filtered under vacuum and washed up until pH 7 (Figure 8D). The solid was calcined at 500°C for 
1 h with a heating rate of 10°Cmin-1 in air. In Figure 9 is shown the solid aspect after the washing 
and calcination steps. 
 
The 5% Ni/CeO2 catalyst was synthesized by the wet impregnation method using Ni (NO3)2.6H2O 
as precursor (Aldrich). The Ni concentration was 5% by weight. After impregnation, the catalyst 
was dried up overnight at 100°C and then calcined at 500°C for 5 h with heating rate of 3°Cmin-1 in 
air (30 mLmin-1). 
 
The catalyst 0.02% Ni/CeO2 was synthesized by the wet impregnation method using Ni 
(NO3)2.6H2O as precursor (Aldrich). The Ni concentration was 0.02% by weight. The solid after 
impregnation was calcined at 500°C for 20 h with heating rate of 3°Cmin-1in air (30 mLmin-1).  
 
The Ni/SiO2 (reference) was prepared with Ni 10 % by weight and Ni (NO3)2.6H2O as precursor 
(Aldrich). The Ni content used has been chosen to obtain similar metallic area of the Ni/CeO2 
system.

2.2.2 Temperature Programmed Reduction (TPR) 
 
The temperature programmed reduction (TPR) analyses were carried out employing an AutoChem 
II Micromeritcs equipment, at first, catalysts were dried at 500°C under N2 flow (30 mLmin-1) for 
30 min. After that, they were reduced at 500°C during 30 min with 10%H2/N2 flow, purged for 15 
min under N2 flow and then calcined at 500°C for 30 min under 30 mLmin-1 of synthetic air flow 
rate. The analyses were conducted using 50 mLmin−1 of 10%H2/N2 mixture, from 30 to 1000°C 
(10°Cmin-1) and remaining at 1000ºC for 30 min. 
 
2.2.3 Temperature-programmed desorption of CO2 (TPD of CO2)

(200 mg) were cooled under 30 mLmin-1 of flow rate of He until it reaches at room temperature. 
The samples exposed to CO2 (20 mLmin-1) for 30 min at room temperature. After that, the TPD of 
CO2 measurements were carried out heating the samples at 20°Cmin-1 up to 500°C, under He flow 
(80 mLmin-1). The fragment m/z = 44 was assigned to CO2 was continuously monitored.

occurs in two steps, CO2 is reduced to CO in the surface oxygen vacancies. Then, the spillover of H 
(H2 is dissociated in the Ni0) from the Ni0 to the support occurs and reacts with the oxygen of the 
vacancy producing water and ending the cycle.

Chapter 4 
 
Conclusion and Recommendations 
 
The choice of catalyst support significantly influences the CO2 methanation chemical reaction, as 
demonstrated by the catalytic test outcomes of Ni/SiO2 and Ni/CeO2 catalysts. Notably, a physical 
mixture of 5%Ni/CeO2 and 0.02%Ni/CeO2 catalysts enhances the rate of the methanation reaction. 
The inclusion of 0.02%Ni/CeO2 catalyst increases the number of active sites on the 5%Ni/CeO2 
catalyst. These findings are likely linked to an augmentation in the abundance of surface oxygen 
vacancies (OVs), as confirmed by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR). The catalytic test results, 
CO2 temperature-programmed surface reaction (TPSR), and characterization collectively indicate 
that the addition of 0.02%Ni/CeO2 to 5%Ni/CeO2 creates a system with heightened redox properties 
and oxygen vacancy content. 
 
To continue this study would be highly recommended to investigate the formation energies of 
oxygen vacancies in ceria oxide catalysts using DFT calculations. Comparing the calculated 
energies with experimental values will help validate the accuracy of the theoretical model and 
provide insights into the stability of these vacancies. 
 
Furthermore, using DFT to investigate various oxygen vacancy configurations in ceria oxide will 
allow to understand how they affect the catalyst's electronic and geometric properties. This analysis 
involves studying the band structure, density of states, and lattice parameters of the catalyst when 
vacancies are present. 
 
The integration of experimental part with DFT calculations in a systematic study will provide a 
deeper understanding of the role of oxygen vacancies in the Ni/CeO2 catalyst for CO2 methanation. 
This knowledge will contribute to the development of more efficient and sustainable catalytic 
systems for CO2 conversion.
