Abstract
Menstrual cycle challenges disrupt the daily lives of many women, often causing them
physical pain and various psychological symptoms. Current clinical solutions are often
insufficient and not used widely enough to address these issues. Therefore, there is
a growing need for accessible, non-medical coping strategies to improve women’s well-
being. Mindfulness-based activities, such as guided meditation or breathwork, have been
shown to alleviate menstrual symptoms effectively.
To address this need, a mHealth application, BreatheThrough, was developed to help
women manage menstrual symptoms using mindfulness-based activities, specifically breath-
work. The application was designed by following a user-centered design methodology,
actively involving users in the process to ensure the solution met their needs.
A resulting application was evaluated in a final validation to assess its feasibility. Users
evaluated the application positively, highlighting its ease of use, and experienced an av-
erage 15% reduction in menstrual symptom intensity after breathwork sessions. How-
ever, some users expressed doubts about the perceived usefulness of the application
and showed a tendency to use it less frequently over time.

Introduction
Menstrual symptoms are a common challenge for women of reproductive age, often dis-
rupting daily routines and significantly impacting overall well-being. Studies indicate that
about one in three women experience symptoms severe enough to interfere with work or
everyday activities [1]. Research has also shown that the brain activity of women with
severe psychological symptoms before menstruation resembles that of individuals with
depression [2]. However, even in severe cases, it is often difficult for women to have their
symptoms diagnosed as disorders, as the diagnostic process is frequently marked by
misdiagnoses and requires significant self-advocacy [3]. This is further challenged by the
perception among many women that their symptoms are a natural part of life. As a result,
despite the notable impact of these symptoms, women often rely on self-care strategies
rather than seeking medical intervention [4]. These challenges highlight the need for ef-
fective and accessible non-medical strategies to better manage menstrual symptoms and
improve women’s quality of life.
Addressing this need, this thesis project was developed in collaboration with Samphire
Neuroscience, a company specializing in innovative approaches to managing menstrual
symptoms by exploring the brain-menstrual cycle connection. Their main product, Nettle,
is a headband that stimulates specific brain regions to help soothe menstrual symptoms.
To complement Nettle, Samphire has developed a menstrual health-tracking app. Cur-
rently, Samphire aims to include features that support users with mild-to-moderate symp-
toms who may not require a clinical device. Building on this foundation, this thesis project
aims to expand the Samphire app’s offering by developing an accessible mobile health
(mHealth) application that integrates simple coping techniques for managing menstrual
cycle symptoms.
mHealth is a promising approach in healthcare due to its accessibility, convenience, effi-
ciency, user-centered design, and capacity to deliver valuable data insights [5]. However,
most mobile apps for female health primarily focus on menstrual cycle tracking rather
than offering active symptom management strategies. Trépanier et al. (2023) found that
apps incorporating symptom management techniques often struggle with user engage-
ment and providing high-quality information. Furthermore, few apps include mindfulness-
based interventions for symptom management despite their proven success in managing
non-menstrual pain in other contexts [6]. Therefore, to address this gap, mindfulness
techniques, proven to reduce pain, anxiety, and depression [7, 8], have emerged as the
primary approach for integration into a mHealth application to help manage menstrual
cycle symptoms.
Mindfulness-based practices provide an effective approach to managing menstrual symp-
toms. Programs such as Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) and Mindfulness-
Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) have demonstrated positive effects on reducing phys-
ical and psychological symptoms related to the menstrual cycle [9, 10, 11]. Addition-
ally, guided meditation has been shown to alleviate menstrual-related stress and improve
well-being [12, 13, 14]. Breathwork practices, including slow diaphragmatic breathing
and yoga-based breathing techniques, have effectively reduced menstrual pain [15, 16].
Moreover, journaling and affirmations show potential for reducing stress and anxiety,
which are common in the pre-menstrual period [17, 18, 19, 20]. Therefore, while various
mindfulness practices could be integrated into a mHealth tool to help manage menstrual
symptoms, determining the best design to support women remains a key challenge.

1.1
Research Question
The research question of this thesis is as follows:
What is the design and feasibility of a mobile application that uses mindfulness-based
activities to improve menstrual cycle symptoms?
To help resolve the research question, several sub-questions will be addressed:
• Which mindfulness-based activities have strong scientific evidence supporting their
effectiveness in managing menstrual cycle symptoms?
• What is the design and implementation of a mobile application that includes mindfulness-
based activities for managing menstrual cycle symptoms?
• Will users be willing to use this mobile tool, and will it be useful for them in relieving
their menstrual symptoms?
1.2
Research Goals and Methods
The goals of this thesis project are as follows:
• Review existing work on mindfulness-based activities and their application for man-
aging menstrual symptoms.
• Design a mobile application that aims to reduce menstrual symptoms with mindfulness-
based practices.
• Implement the design as a mHealth application prototype.
• Run a feasibility study to evaluate the usability and usefulness of the app.
The research on mindfulness-based activities was conducted by identifying and analyz-
ing relevant literature, including review articles, scientific experiment studies, and book
chapters. Key insights from these studies were summarized to provide an overview of
the effects of mindfulness practices on menstrual symptoms.
Additionally, menstrual
symptom management applications were reviewed to assess if and how they incorpo-
rate mindfulness-based activities.
User-Centered Design (UCD) methodology is applied throughout the mobile application
design process. Firstly, user interviews are conducted to identify the main user needs,
which inform the creation of user personas and the user requirement specification docu-
ment. Following that, design with the user sessions are held while creating the first design
prototype with the Figma tool [21]. The created design prototype is evaluated using the
design walk-through and think-aloud interview techniques for gathering user feedback.
The design is implemented as a high-fidelity mHealth application prototype using the Re-
act Native framework [22]. React Native enables the development of cross-platform ap-
plications that operate on both Android and iOS mobile systems. Various React Native
libraries implement specific application features, enhancing development efficiency.
The feasibility of the application was evaluated through a two-week final validation study
using the developed mHealth application prototype. The study design followed the Copen-
hagen Center for Health Technology (CACHET) Unified Methodology for Assessment of
Clinical Feasibility [23]. The main aspects evaluated during the study include usage adop-
tion, health efficacy, and perceived usefulness and usability.

Background and Related Work
This chapter reviews key literature and related work, beginning with an overview of men-
strual cycle phases, common symptoms, and their classification as disorders. It then
explores the benefits of breathwork for managing menstrual symptoms and evaluates
popular mobile applications for symptom management and breathwork. Lastly, it exam-
ines existing solutions for improving user engagement in these exercises.
2.1
Menstrual Cycle Symptoms, Disorders, and Treatment
Before exploring the various symptoms women may experience during their menstrual
cycle, it is helpful to review its main stages. As illustrated in fig. 2.1, the menstrual cycle
is divided into two main phases: the follicular and luteal phases, separated by ovulation.
The follicular phase lasts an average of 14 days and begins with menstruation (bleeding),
typically lasting 4–6 days. This phase is characterized by rising levels of estrogen and
follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which stimulate the development of a follicle in the
ovaries. The follicle contains an egg released during ovulation, ready to be fertilized.
Ovulation is triggered by a luteinizing hormone (LH) surge, usually around the 14th day
of the cycle. Following ovulation, the luteal phase begins and typically lasts for 14 days.
This phase is marked by increased progesterone, which prepares the body for potential
pregnancy. If the egg is not fertilized, progesterone and estrogen levels drop sharply,
signaling the end of the cycle and the start of menstruation [24].
The most distressing menstrual cycle symptoms typically occur during the luteal phase,
lasting 1–2 weeks before menstruation and often continuing into the initial days of the
menstrual period. Physical symptoms may include abdominal pain, headaches, fatigue,
acne, insomnia, nausea, and vomiting. In addition to these, emotional symptoms such
as mood swings, irritability, anger, social isolation, anxiety, and lack of focus may also
arise [25, 24] Many women experience only mild versions of these symptoms. The term
Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS) is often used as an umbrella term to describe these symp-
toms, although there is no universal consensus on what constitutes a clinically significant
level of PMS [26]. In more moderate to severe cases, the condition may be diagnosed
as Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD) [27]. Additionally, intense abdominal pain,
particularly common among adolescent girls, is often classified as Dysmenorrhea [28].
Studies report varying estimates regarding how common intense premenstrual symptoms
are among women. A review of multiple studies found that approximately 25–50% of
adult women and around 75% of teenagers experience some degree of menstrual pain,
with 5–20% reporting severe pain that could be classified as dysmenorrhea [29]. More
recent research indicates that 50–80% of women experience mild PMS symptoms, 30–
40% report more severe symptoms, and 3–8% suffer from PMDD [27]. Although there
is no agreement on the exact number of women affected, it is clear these symptoms
significantly impact the lives of many. For instance, it is estimated that one in three women
must cancel daily activities due to the severity of their symptoms, with pain, psychological
distress, and fatigue being the most commonly reported issues [1]. As such, PMS is a
serious condition that requires proper care and treatment to improve the quality of life for
a large number of women.
Currently, the available treatments for women dealing with menstrual cycle symptoms are
insufficient, especially when considering the risks associated with long-term use. The

quality of life.
2.2
Mindfulness-Based Activities
Mindfulness-based interventions can offer a non-medical option for helping women man-
age their menstrual cycle symptoms. Various mindfulness-based therapies, meditation,
affirmation, and breathwork practices and their potential impact on female health-related
symptoms are summarized below.
2.2.1
Mindfulness-Based Therapies
Various mindfulness-based therapies can help alleviate menstrual symptoms. Two com-
monly cited clinical practices in this field are the Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction
(MBSR) program and Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT). Both are 8-week
group programs that teach techniques such as guided meditation, with the primary dis-
tinction being that MBCT incorporates elements of cognitive behavioral therapy [32]. Re-
search has demonstrated that MBCT positively impacts the physical and psychological

symptoms of PMS [9, 10]. Similarly, studies have shown promising results for MBSR in
alleviating symptoms of PMDD [11]. These longer-term therapeutic programs, therefore,
hold significant potential for women experiencing severe premenstrual symptoms.
2.2.2
Meditation
Meditation is an Eastern practice that helps people calm their minds by redirecting focus
from everyday worries to a deeper state of concentration [33]. The term ”meditation” is
broad and encompasses various practices, such as yoga, Tai Chi, Transcendental Med-
itation, Mindfulness Meditation, Zen Buddhist Meditation, and many others. Due to the
vast diversity of these practices, it is nearly impossible to identify elements of meditation
that are universally applicable to all meditation forms [34].
Researchers have studied the relationship between various forms of guided meditation
and PMS. The findings suggest that guided meditation can help reduce PMS-related
stress, fatigue, and headache symptoms [12, 13, 14]. Practices that demonstrated a pos-
itive effect on PMS symptoms often included slow breathing, focused attention on bodily
sensations, and the use of pleasant mental imagery throughout the session.
2.2.3
Journaling
Journaling is a widely used tool for mental health management, often involving writing
down thoughts and feelings or listing things one is grateful for [35]. Research has shown
that journaling can be effective in reducing stress, mental distress, and anxiety [19, 20].
Notably, journaling interventions are particularly beneficial for women in alleviating anxi-
ety [35]. Therefore, journaling could potentially help reduce anxiety and stress related to
PMS symptoms. However, no studies directly exploring this possibility have been found.
2.2.4
Affirmations
Affirmations are positive self-statements that, when repeated, are believed to improve
mood and self-esteem during challenging situations [36]. Studies suggest that repeating
self-affirmations aligned with personal values (statements that reflect what individuals care
most about) can activate the brain’s reward centers and reduce stress [17, 18]. However,
it has also been found that while people with high self-esteem benefit from affirmations,
those with low self-esteem may experience a negative effect [36]. Therefore, affirmations
may not be a universally effective practice for reducing stress. Furthermore, no known
research has explored the impact of repeating self-affirmations on menstrual cycle symp-
toms.
2.3
Breathwork
Breathwork is one of the most researched mindfulness activities, gaining significant atten-
tion from both scientists and the general public in recent years. Since breathwork activities
are the main focus of the application design during this thesis project, a broader literature
review was performed that highlights four specific breathwork practices: box breathing,
diaphragmatic breathing, 4-7-8 breathing, and cyclic sighing.
Breathwork involves structured breathing patterns, often combining varying durations of
inhalation and exhalation with periods of breath holding. Structured breathing techniques
have been shown to activate the parasympathetic nervous system, as evidenced by in-
creased heart rate variability (HRV), and to influence central nervous system activity, such
as elevated EEG alpha power, which reflects calm and focused brain activity [37]. These
physiological changes are associated with enhanced relaxation, psychological well-being,
and improved mood regulation. Research has revealed that just five minutes of structured
breathwork can bring a positive emotional effect [38].

Given these benefits, breathwork holds promise for managing symptoms related to the

2.3.1
Box Breathing
Box breathing is a breathwork technique, also referred to as Tactical breathing or Com-
bat breathing, which can reduce stress and enhance mental clarity [39]. The military
reportedly uses this technique to manage highly stressful and dangerous situations [40].
As illustrated in fig. 2.2, it is performed in four stages: inhale for four seconds, hold the
breath for four seconds, exhale for four seconds, and hold again for four seconds.
Box breathing is effective because of its specific pattern, which distracts the mind from
stressors, and the breath-holding phase, which triggers the diving response [39]. The div-
ing response refers to the body’s reaction to breath-holding, which activates the parasym-
pathetic nervous system, thereby reducing heart rate [41]. Additionally, it has been shown
that box breathing is beneficial for mothers in managing pain levels and mood after la-
bor [42, 43]. Therefore, box breathing may be especially helpful in managing symptoms
associated with the menstrual cycle, such as mood swings, anxiety, stress, and loss of
focus.
2.3.2
Diaphragmatic Breathing
Diaphragmatic breathing, also known as abdominal or deep breathing, is a breathwork
technique that emphasizes the use of the diaphragm muscle. As illustrated in fig. 2.3, it
consists of deep inhalations that expand the abdomen rather than the chest, followed by
slow exhalations [44].
A study on frontline nurses in Wuhan, China, during the COVID-19 pandemic concluded
that abdominal breathing improved their sleep quality and decreased their anxiety lev-
els [45]. Furthermore, research indicates that consistent practice of this technique can
reduce blood cortisol levels, effectively lowering stress and self-reported anxiety levels,
and reduce migraines [44, 15, 46]. Emerging research suggests that abdominal breathing
exercises can alleviate menstrual pain, even in severe cases such as dysmenorrhea [16,
47]. Therefore, these findings can be applied to managing menstrual cycle symptoms, in-
cluding abdominal cramps, lower back pain, migraines, anxiety, and insomnia, providing
both physical and emotional relief.

2.3.3
4-7-8 Breathing
The 4-7-8 breathing technique only emerged in research over the last five years. It is
performed by inhaling for four seconds, holding the breath for seven seconds, and finally
exhaling for eight seconds. The breathing phases of 4-7-8 breathwork are illustrated
in fig. 2.4.
This breathwork pattern has been shown to reduce stress hormones, which in turn slows
heart and respiratory rates [48]. The 4-7-8 technique can also reduce depression levels
and is more effective at relieving anxiety than deep breathing [48, 49]. Additionally, studies
indicate it can improve insomnia and help manage pain; however, deep breathing has
shown slightly better outcomes in those areas [50, 51]. Given its ability to lower stress
hormones and improve sleep, the 4-7-8 breathing technique holds potential for addressing
common menstrual cycle symptoms such as fatigue, irritability, and anxiety.
2.3.4
Cyclic Sighing
Cyclic sighing is a breathwork technique that includes prolonged sighing. As illustrated
in fig. 2.5, it consists of a four-second inhale followed by a short top-up inhale to completely
fill the lungs. Then, the air is exhaled with a long sigh [38].
This technique incorporates a sigh (or ”augmented breath”), a spontaneous respiratory
action. It was found that mice with a genetic mutation that prevented them from sighing
experienced lung collapse and severe respiratory issues, ultimately leading to death [52].
This suggests that sighing is a vital respiratory function.
The purpose of sighing extends beyond lung ventilation. Sighs signal the release of strong
negative emotions such as anxiety, fear, panic, fatigue, and more [53, 54]. They also act

2.4
Current Technologies for Menstrual Symptom
Management and Breathwork Practices
This section reviews menstrual symptom management apps and their interventions, fol-
lowed by an analysis of popular breathwork apps to evaluate their features. Finally, exist-
ing solutions for providing user feedback and enhancing engagement during breathwork
exercises are explored.
2.4.1
Menstrual Symptom Management Applications
Various mobile applications are available for menstruation tracking, but only a few incor-
porate interventions to relieve menstrual symptoms. A recent study by Trépanier et al.
(2023) reviewed 119 applications from the App Store and found that they mostly focus
on tracking features and educational content. There is a notable absence of interven-
tions that help users manage their symptoms. Only six apps included mindfulness and
meditation-based components, and none focused on developing pain management skills.
No scientific publications related to the reviewed apps were identified, suggesting a lack of
involvement from researchers [6]. This section includes an analysis of applications high-

Methodology
This chapter outlines all the design activities undertaken throughout the course of this
thesis project, along with an explanation of the methods and techniques that guided them.
These activities are presented in the timeline shown in fig. 3.1.
In addition to the activities shown in the timeline, bi-weekly meetings were held with the
thesis supervisor and separately with the Samphire Neuroscience team to leverage their

3.1
User Centered Design
User experience (UX) is the overall impact a user feels before, during, and after interacting
with a product or system [61]. Figure 3.2 illustrates a design process that reflects the
principles of the main activities of UX design used for this project. This design process
has five main groups of activities:
• Understand user needs and the problem domain to inform the design process.
• Define system requirements that establish concrete goals for the design.
• Design solutions that address the researched needs.
• Create prototype candidates to illustrate the possible design solutions.
• Evaluate UX by verifying and reshaping the design prototypes based on user feed-
back.
This process is iterative, requiring continuous evaluation of previous steps and repeating
the previous stages as needed. For example, during the course of this project, there
were two cycles of building and evaluating prototypes. This chart reflects the core of
the User-Centered Design (UCD) methodology - an integral part of UX. UCD is a design
methodology that emphasizes involving users in the process to shape the final design
outcome. This approach allows for various levels of user participation throughout the
design process [62].

3.2
Participant Recruitment Methods
Since the project followed the UCD methodology, human participants were an integral part
of the project’s success. The participant inclusion and exclusion criteria were selected af-
ter considering what target population would benefit from the designed technology the
most and after discussing the targeted users with the Samphire Neuroscience team. It
was decided to focus on the women who menstruate and experience mild to moderate
menstrual cycle symptoms, are between 18 and 36 years old, and reside in Western
countries (primarily the US and Europe). On the other hand, the exclusion criteria in-
cluded diagnosed menstrual disorders, such as PMDD or dysmenorrhea, or an absence
of menstrual symptoms, for example, due to contraceptive use. The decision to exclude
women with the most severe symptoms was taken because Samphire Neuroscience’s
existing clinical solution already addresses their needs.
The participants were recruited primarily from my personal network, with me contacting
them privately. In addition, the women I contacted suggested other women who fit the
recruitment criteria and would potentially be interested in the project, thereby expanding
the participants’ group. All participants have been fully informed about the project and
given their written consent before participation.
3.3
Understand User Needs
Understanding user needs is a critical first step in the design process. A common pitfall
many designers encounter is developing a product based on their assumptions about what
users want. Instead, thorough user research must be conducted before ideating potential
solutions. The process of identifying user needs is known as requirements gathering [63].
In this project, user research was conducted using the following tools and methods: user
interviews, user personas, and user requirement specifications.
3.3.1
Interview Design and Methodology
User needs were gathered through semi-structured interviews, ensuring each session is
unique and uncovers new insights about the topic [64]. In semi-structured interviews, the
20

==PAGE:29==
discussion is less formally structured, and the interviewer is not required to follow a spe-
cific order of questions. However, the researcher must plan the key topics to be covered
and guide the conversation back to those topics if the participant strays too far. The in-
terviewer can pose questions spontaneously, often opting for open-ended questions. If
yes/no questions are used, they should be followed by open-ended questions to encour-
age the participant to elaborate, providing more depth and insight to the discussion [65].
During the interview design process, the main themes were developed based on the
project’s objectives. The primary goal of these interviews was to explore how women
experience various menstrual symptoms and how they manage them. Additionally, par-
ticipants’ initial perceptions of mindfulness-based activities, such as breathwork, affirma-
tions, and mindfulness practices, were assessed. The aim was to understand users’
awareness of these activities, their perceived benefits, whether they currently engage in
them, and their reasons for doing so. Furthermore, the interviews were a tool to identify
the challenges participants face in maintaining these practices consistently.
3.3.2
User Personas
The information gathered during the user interviews was used to create User Personas.
User Personas are hypothetical representations of users that capture their key charac-
teristics, important narratives, and goals. It is recommended to create multiple personas,
each represented as a specific individual with an assigned name, personality, and, most
importantly, a detailed description of how they relate to the problem domain [61]. The
primary benefit of using personas as part of UCD is their ability to narrow the focus on
specific user groups and their main goals. This, in turn, facilitates the prioritization of user
and product requirements [66].
3.3.3
User Requirement Specification
Functional and non-functional requirements were developed based on insights from user
interviews.
These requirements are concrete statements that reflect user needs, ex-
pressed clearly and accompanied by a rationale explaining their importance. They should
be regarded as the primary design goals of the system rather than constraints, allowing
flexibility in how they are achieved [61]. Functional requirements refer to the features
that address user needs from a functionality perspective—focusing on which features are
most useful for meeting user expectations. Non-functional requirements define specific
system qualities, such as security, scalability, and more, that support the user experience
from an operational perspective. The created user requirements were reviewed and con-
firmed by the Samphire Neuroscience company team to ensure they aligned with their
expectations.
3.4
Create Design Concepts
The Create Design Concepts phase of the UX design lifecycle (fig. 3.2) focuses on coming
up with initial ideas for solutions. Various ideation and conceptualization activities are
conducted during this stage, building on the user research completed in the previous
phase.
Users are included in the form of ideas or more tangible contributions, such as sketches.
For example, allowing users to sketch specific parts of the user interface, like the home-
page, has been shown to enhance the overall design. However, this approach becomes
less effective when users are required to sketch complex systems [61].
For this project, I applied the UCD methodology by conducting one-on-one design with
the user sessions to create a section of the app that presented scientific information. The

task involved providing users with a scientific text and asking them to share their ideas on
how they would like to receive this information within the app. They were also asked to

3.5
Build Design Prototypes
Prototyping is a crucial activity in the design lifecycle, enabling the realization and explo-
ration of different design solutions. It often overlaps with the design phase and is typically
carried out in parallel with it [61]. A design prototype is an illustration of the final design
or some aspect of it. The most common goal of building prototypes is to verify and refine
the system UX [67]. The design prototypes can range from being as simple as a sketch
on a piece of paper to being as difficult as a fully coded technology. The choice of what
prototypes should be built for verifying the system features depends on each individual
project. For this project, two prototypes were developed to validate the technology. The
first prototype was a medium-fidelity prototype developed in Figma that included all of
the app’s features. The second prototype was developed as a fully functioning mobile
application. Both of these prototypes served as a tool to evaluate the system UX.
3.6
Evaluate UX
The evaluation stage of the UX design process focuses on activities that validate the
design and identify necessary adjustments to better meet user needs. Consistent with
the UCD methodology, these validation activities are conducted with user participants
using a variety of methods. These methods are generally categorized into two broader
types: formative and summative evaluation.
Formative evaluation involves activities aimed at improving the design, while summative
evaluation is used to assess the overall success of the UX design [61]. Both methodolo-
gies were applied in this project. Design walk-throughs and think-aloud interviews served
as formative evaluation sessions, while the final validation study of the high-fidelity proto-
type was an example of summative evaluation. This section provides a detailed explana-
tion of these validation methods.
3.6.1
Design Walk-Throughs
Design Walk-Through is an evaluation technique used in the early stages of design to
gather user input at the outset. Since the design at this stage might lack interactive el-
ements, the designer takes a more active role during the session by presenting and ex-
plaining the design artifact [61]. These sessions allow for gathering user opinions through
direct questions and discussions that arise while sharing the designs with the users. It is
an informal and flexible evaluation method adaptable to the specific use case.
In this project, design walk-throughs were used to evaluate the early stages of the design,
particularly when a choice between different design options was needed. This method
allowed for selecting the best design option without investing significant time in creating
elaborate prototypes for all possibilities.
3.6.2
Think-Aloud Interviews
The think-aloud technique is a simple and effective method for data collection. It involves
a session with a user participant who verbally expresses their thoughts while interacting
with the design being tested. This technique is especially useful for evaluating design
prototypes. By allowing the user to navigate various prototype features independently,
the designer can observe their interactions, note any struggles, and gain insights from
the user’s verbalized thoughts, which help better understand the design’s impact [61].

==PAGE:31==
Traditionally, this technique discourages interrupting the user during the session to avoid
introducing bias or leading questions that could corrupt the gathered insights. However,
designers intervene in certain situations in practice to ensure the session yields more
comprehensive or relevant insights [68]. For instance, if a user is less vocal or becomes
stuck on a particular feature, minor interventions can be beneficial. These could involve
reminding users to share their thoughts or offering subtle hints to help them navigate the
prototype. Ultimately, the think-aloud technique relies on active user participation, with
strategic interruptions from the designer to maximize the value of the collected insights.
This project used the think-aloud technique to evaluate the medium-fidelity Figma proto-
type. Four user sessions were conducted to identify issues and gather user reactions to
the main app features before implementing the prototype in code. This evaluation aimed
to detect as many errors as possible before investing time in developing a higher-quality
prototype.
The think-aloud interviews followed this structure:
• The concept of think-aloud was explained to the users.
• The users received a brief introduction to the app.
• The users were presented with the Figma prototype and asked to click through it
while verbally sharing their thoughts (but not their actions).
• The users were observed throughout the process and were only interrupted when
necessary.
This method of gathering user feedback provided insight beyond user-verbalized thoughts.
It additionally showed what issues arise when the users navigate the system indepen-
dently, as well as the emotional impact that the prototype created.
3.6.3
Final Validation Study
The final validation study served as a summative evaluation method to assess the final
prototype’s UX. Its primary objective was to evaluate the feasibility of the BreatheThrough
application for women experiencing menstrual symptoms. Feasibility was measured across
three key aspects: usage adoption, perceived usefulness and usability, and health effi-
cacy [23].
Usage adoption focuses on determining whether users actively engage with the new tech-
nology. Perceived usefulness and usability evaluate the likelihood of the technology’s suc-
cess and acceptance among users. Finally, health efficacy measures the actual health
benefits provided by the application, addressing the critical question of whether it effec-
tively addresses the health issues it claims to target. Thus, the final validation study was
designed to address all three feasibility aspects.
In the final validation study of this project, users were asked to use the BreatheThrough
application for two weeks. Users were instructed to open the app daily and perform one
breathwork exercise. Additionally, users were encouraged to explore other app features
at their own pace.
The application was designed to collect specific usage data to evaluate two aspects of
feasibility: usage adoption and health efficacy. Usage adoption was assessed by tracking
whether users completed the daily exercises as instructed. The app also recorded how
many users canceled an exercise after starting it without completing it. Furthermore,
all app screens were monitored to measure how frequently users viewed and clicked
through them. Health efficacy was evaluated through the built-in functionality of tracking
Mobile Health Application with Mindfulness-Based Activities for Managing Menstrual Cycle Symptoms
23
==PAGE:32==
users’ symptoms. Users selected their symptoms and rated their intensity before and after
completing the recommended breathwork exercise. This data was analyzed to identify
trends in symptom improvement experienced by the users.
The perceived usefulness and usability aspect of feasibility was assessed using the CA-
CHET Unified Methodology for Assessment of Clinical Feasibility (CUMACF) Question-
naire [23]. This questionnaire was designed to evaluate users’ acceptance of the tech-
nology and willingness to continue using it. The questionnaire is divided into five main
sections:
• Health Expectancy (HE): evaluates the health benefits users would hope to achieve
by using the technology.
• Effort Expectancy (EE): measures how easy users would find the technology to use.
• Social Influence (SI): assesses whether the user’s social environment would en-
courage using the technology.
• Facilitating Conditions (FC): determines whether users feel the system would be
technically and organizationally supported.
• Behavioural Intention (BI): determines the likelihood of users intending to use the
system in the future.
Lastly, throughout the study, some of the most engaged users voluntarily provided open-
form feedback without formal prompting. This feedback offered valuable insights into the
issues they faced and the features of BreatheThrough they found beneficial. Additionally,
the CUMACF questionnaire featured a dedicated field for optional notes. Many partici-
pants used this field to share their additional open-form feedback. The collected open-
form feedback provided qualitative data that complemented the quantitative findings and
aided in interpreting the results.
This setup and methodology formed the foundation of the validation study, allowing for
the evaluation of the feasibility of the BreatheThrough application.

4.1
Participant Recruitment
A total of 11 women were recruited to participate in the user interviews during the initial
research phase. These women ranged in age from 26 to 30 and were all educated to
at least a bachelor’s degree level. Their professional backgrounds ranged from software
engineering to design, and they represented four different nationalities. This allowed for
more diversity in the participant’s perspective.
The same participants were also involved in subsequent activities later in the project lifecy-

involved in each subsequent design activity, such as the design with the user’s sessions
and think-aloud interviews. This approach allowed for a more efficient and quick design

4.2
User Interview Results
The user interviews carried out at the start of the thesis project provided a solid basis on
which all the following project ideas were built. Therefore, it is important to summarize
the main findings of these discussions.
4.2.1
Summary of User Responses
1. What are the most common menstrual cycle symptoms you experience? What symp-
toms interfere with your daily routine the most?
Participants commonly reported both physical and psychological symptoms during their
menstrual cycles. Lower abdominal pain was the most frequently mentioned physical
symptom, with nine out of eleven users experiencing it in varying intensities and durations.
Headaches were also a concern for 6 out of the 11 participants.
On the psychological side, mood swings, sadness, and irritability were prevalent, each
affecting nine out of eleven users. Anxiety was also a significant issue mentioned by
eight participants. Both physical discomfort and emotional disturbances were noted to
interfere with daily routines, impacting productivity and overall well-being.
2. How do you currently manage menstrual cycle symptoms?
When it comes to managing physical symptoms, most users rely on painkillers, with 10
out of 11 participants stating they use them to alleviate discomfort. However, many try
to delay taking medication unless absolutely necessary, such as when they have work or
other responsibilities. When at home, some users prefer to explore alternative methods
or endure the pain rather than immediately turning to medication. A few participants,
specifically two out of 11, mentioned using breathwork techniques, like diaphragmatic or
box breathing, to manage their cramps.
For psychological symptoms, users mentioned a variety of coping mechanisms. Four
participants try to regulate their emotions by going for walks or engaging in physical ac-

25
==PAGE:34==
tivity. Eating comfort food, particularly snacks, fast food, or chocolate, was another com-
mon coping strategy, with seven out of eleven users resorting to this method. Other
approaches mentioned by one to three users included slow yoga, massages, watching
movies or listening to podcasts, and drinking more water. Interestingly, users who track
their cycles reported that being aware of upcoming PMS symptoms helped them man-
age their emotions better, while those who don’t track often realized the cause of their
emotional fluctuations only after their periods started.
3. What do you think about mindfulness-based activities?
All participants had positive opinions about mindfulness, acknowledging its benefits for
managing anxiety, controlling thoughts, and clearing the mind. However, only two out
of eleven users practiced mindfulness consistently, at least three times per week. The
main barriers to regular mindfulness practice included a lack of education about how it
works and what it achieves, a lack of discipline or motivation to maintain the practice,
and difficulty focusing due to distractions or a wandering mind. Additionally, two users
were skeptical about the practice, questioning its scientific validity and expressing con-
cerns about its commercialization, wondering if it was simply an industry profiting from
vulnerable individuals.
4. What are your thoughts on Meditation?
Most participants had tried some form of meditation, typically guided sessions through
mobile apps. However, they often found it difficult to focus during meditation sessions,
especially when not tied to breathwork. Users reported that meditation was one of the
most challenging practices to concentrate on, describing it as somewhat boring and ad-
mitting that their minds often wandered during sessions. The difficulty of sitting still made
this practice less appealing to most users.
5. What are your thoughts on Breathwork?
Breathwork, on the other hand, was a more familiar and accepted practice among the
users, with eight out of eleven users mentioning that they used it, sometimes intuitively,
to manage pain, reduce stress, prevent anxiety, or even help them fall asleep. While one
user thought it looked odd when observed from the outside, and another felt awkward
doing it in a yoga session, the overall sentiment toward breathwork was positive.
6. What are your thoughts on Affirmations?
Opinions on affirmations were more divided. While most users believed that affirmations
could improve their mindset and help them feel better, the practice was also met with
some skepticism. Some participants used affirmations regularly and felt that they helped
cultivate positivity. Others believed that affirmations could be ineffective if underlying is-
sues were left unaddressed. These users felt personal problems needed to be resolved
before affirmations could make a real impact. Additionally, this was the practice that users
were least likely to associate with scientific backing; one user even mentioned that if she
saw affirmations suggested in an app, she would like to see supporting research for it.
7. What menstrual tracking apps do you use? What is your primary goal when using
them?
Nearly all participants (10 out of 11) used menstrual tracking apps, with Flo being the
most popular. Clue and Period Calendar were also mentioned. Users primarily relied
on these apps to track their cycles and sometimes their symptoms. However, none of
the participants reported using apps specifically designed to help them cope with their

4.2.2
Design Decisions Guided by User Interviews
The data collected from the user interviews played a crucial role in shaping key decisions
for the application design. Breathwork was chosen as the most suitable mindfulness-
based activity for implementation in the designed application. The main reason for this
choice is that users identified breathwork as the most trusted and intuitively performed
exercise for alleviating their symptoms during initial interviews. Many users reported prior
experience with breathwork and expressed confidence in its effectiveness. In contrast,
users were more skeptical about affirmations, often questioning their scientific validity
and personally felt usefulness. Additionally, breathwork is absent in current menstrual
symptom management applications. Even apps that offer diverse interventions, such
as guided meditation and physical exercises, rarely include breathwork. This omission
highlighted a gap in the field, especially given the growing body of scientific evidence
supporting its effectiveness ( section 2.3). Finally, breathwork is particularly well-suited for
seamless integration into a mobile application. Other mindfulness-based activities pose
significant challenges: mindfulness-based therapies often require group workshops, and
journaling raises concerns about protecting sensitive user data.
These interviews also informed the creation of user personas and user requirement spec-
ifications. These tools were used throughout the solution design and prototyping activities
as the main guidance, ensuring the final system was aligned with the needs and prefer-
ences of the target audience.

Functional Requirements

Breathwork exercises should include interactive elements to keep users engaged.

fulness practices is losing focus and experiencing a wandering mind. This issue
was identified as a primary reason why the women I interviewed do not engage in
breathwork consistently and often feel discouraged from continuing the activity. An
exercise incorporating interactive elements throughout the practice could help keep
participants engaged and focused, enhancing their overall experience.
• Real-Time Feedback

The exercises should detect when users are getting distracted and provide real-time

Similar to the rationale for the previous requirement, this user need focuses on help-

active approach. The system should include a mechanism to detect when users
lose focus and guide them back to the exercise by providing real-time feedback.
This feedback mechanism must not distract the users further; therefore, it should

• Science-Backed Explanations
Each exercise must have a science-backed explanation of its purpose and effects.

recent research.

Users should be able to view statistics from their past exercises and understand the

Users shared that a significant barrier to continuing mindfulness activities over the
long term is a lack of motivation and the inability to see tangible results. To ad-
dress this, the system should allow users to access detailed information about their
progress. This includes tracking the number and type of exercises they have per-
formed, as well as the impact these exercises have had on their symptoms. Such
functionality would offer users clear insights into the results they can expect from
breathwork activities and the benefits of incorporating this practice into their routine.

Breathwork Exercises

The user distraction detection and feedback mechanism was an important part of the
breathwork exercise. This mechanism was adapted to fit breathwork exercises from the
Pause interactive meditation application developed by Niksirat et al. (2017), which is
discussed in section 2.4.2 [60]. During the whole exercise, users had to trace the shape

long and did not start the exhale phase), it was considered an indication of the user getting
distracted. Then, the user received onscreen visual feedback on doing the exercise too
slowly or fast to nudge them back to awareness. Figure 5.2 showcases this feedback
mechanism.

5.2.1
Data Management
The data layer refers to the parts of the application that define the storage and manage-
ment of the app’s data. The BreatheThrough application uses persistent data storage,
allowing users to close or reinstall the app and still retrieve their logged data. A rela-
tional PostgreSQL database provided by Supabase [71] was chosen for this application.
It contains tables for users, user-logged symptoms, and user-completed or canceled ex-
ercises. These tables are connected through a unique ID assigned to each user, ensuring
that each data point is correctly linked to the appropriate person.
In terms of fetching the data from the database and saving it to it, this process was aided
by the API automatically created by the Supabase solution. Therefore, the data can be
queried by making API calls. React Query library was used in the project to define the
API calls for inserting and fetching the symptom improvement and breathwork exercise
data.
5.2.2
Symptom Quiz Functionality
The exercise recommendation function is one of the most important calculations of the
Symptoms Quiz (fig. 5.3) logic. The most appropriate exercise is selected based on the
user’s chosen symptoms. Each breathwork exercise is tied to a list of symptoms it could

alleviate based on research. A simple function is applied to the user-selected symptoms
to count which exercise matches most of these symptoms. If two or more exercises score
equally, one is randomly chosen from the best-suited options.
Another integral part of the Symptoms Quiz logic was using Context, a React Native fea-
ture designed to store information locally and share it as the user navigates through dif-
ferent screens. When the user goes through the Symptoms Quiz, the selected symptoms
information is saved to the Context at every step and only sent to the database after

call design to insert the symptoms data into the external database table for user-logged
symptoms.

Analytics Section Functionality
The data processing functionality for the Analytics Section ( fig. 5.4) is managed within the
application logic layer. Functions responsible for data processing, such as aggregating
completed exercise numbers and calculating symptom improvements, are intentionally
separated in the code from the UI logic. Data is retrieved by executing API calls to fetch
the relevant user-specific records from external database tables. All information related
to exercises and logged symptoms is stored in the database. Therefore, when a user
navigates to the Analytics section, the required data is fetched and processed within the
logic layer before being displayed in the Analytics Section UI.
5.2.4
Breathwork Exercises Functionality
The breathwork exercises leverage two key animation libraries: React Native Reani-
mated [72] and React Native Gesture Handler [73]. Together, these libraries created
an interactive experience where users trace the shape presented on the screen with their
finger fig. 5.1.
A crucial logical component of these exercises is the Exercise Feedback Functionality and
Timing. Each breathing phase is timed using Timers implemented with React Hooks to
ensure precise phase countdowns. Additionally, the exercises provide real-time feedback
to users based on their finger movement speed. If a user moves their finger to the next
breathing phase position too late, a ”Too slow” message appears on the screen; if they
move too early, a ”Too fast” message is displayed. React Hooks are also employed to
manage and update these feedback messages effectively.

Discussion
7.1
Revisiting Research Question
This thesis project addressed the research question posed at its start (section 1.1). The
following discussion examines key aspects contributing to the answer: an evaluation of
related work, the design and implementation process, and an assessment of the final
design.
7.1.1
Related Work
One of the goals for this project was to look into research on mindfulness-based activities
to determine which ones are effective in managing menstrual cycle symptoms. Therefore,
to achieve this goal, studies on the following activities were reviewed: mindfulness-based
therapies, meditation, affirmations, journaling, and breathwork were examined.
First, I reviewed literature directly exploring the relationship between mindfulness-based
activities and menstrual cycle symptoms. However, this research was sparse and did
not cover all the mindfulness-based activities I was interested in. As a result, studies
on the effects of these activities on individual symptoms associated with the menstrual
cycle were also included. For instance, breathwork has been shown to effectively manage
migraines [46], a common premenstrual symptom, making it relevant for addressing PMS-
related challenges.
Most studies that found a positive impact on PMS and PMDD symptoms directly were
conducted on mindfulness-based therapies [9, 10, 11] and meditation [12, 13, 14]. In
addition, breathwork has been shown to alleviate menstrual pain [16, 47], and various
other symptoms related to the menstrual cycle, such as migraines, anxiety, stress, sad-
ness, and more (all discussed in section 2.3). In contrast, journaling and affirmations do
not appear to have published studies directly linking them to menstrual cycle symptoms.
However, they have recorded benefits for stress and anxiety [19, 20, 18], which are com-
mon in PMS. Despite this, their impact on a wider range of PMS symptoms is limited.
Therefore, based on current research, mindfulness-based therapies, guided meditation,
and breathwork show the most potential for effectively managing menstrual symptoms.
Another aspect of this analysis involved exploring whether these mindfulness-based ac-
tivities are already incorporated into mobile apps for menstrual cycle symptom manage-
ment. It was found that guided meditation is the most commonly implemented activity,
while other mindfulness-based activities are absent. This highlights a gap in the mobile
application landscape that needs to be addressed by including a more diverse range of
activities that support symptom management.
7.1.2
Design and Implementation
Another aim of this project was to use user-centered design methods to design and im-
plement a mHealth application that includes mindfulness-based activities for managing
menstrual symptoms. Since the mindfulness activity space is very crowded, I decided
to focus on breathwork as the sole activity implemented in the app. This decision was
primarily based on user preferences and their positive perception of breathwork, the sci-
entific evidence supporting its effectiveness (as detailed in section 2.3), and its current
absence from existing menstrual symptom management apps.
One of the key insights from initial user interviews was that users often lack tangible results
from mindfulness activities, leading to a loss of motivation. This highlighted the need for
Mobile Health Application with Mindfulness-Based Activities for Managing Menstrual Cycle Symptoms
49
==PAGE:58==
the app to track the impact of breathwork on user well-being. Research also identified
this as a gap in current menstrual pain management apps [6]. To address this issue, the
Symptoms Quiz and the Analytics section were designed to let users see the impact they
get from engaging with the app. During the validation study, the screen visits (fig. 6.3)
revealed that some users visited the Analytics section daily. However, only about half of
the users who opened the app navigated to this section, indicating that not all users found
this feature relevant for daily use. Despite this, it is worth noting that the impact-tracking
functionality received only positive feedback from users, with some stating that it provided
additional motivation to continue using the app.
Another important challenge highlighted by users was difficulty maintaining focus during
mindfulness practices, often due to their minds wandering. To address this, breathwork
activities were designed to detect distraction and provide feedback to refocus attention.
Similarly, Niksirat et al. (2017) highlighted this issue and proposed two mechanisms for
mHealth apps to address it: user finger movement detection and feedback [60]. This
framework guided the development of breathwork exercises in the BreatheThrough ap-
plication, where users kept their fingers on the screen, and the app provided feedback
if they became distracted. Overall, validation study participants regarded this approach
positively. However, technical issues with 4-7-8 breathing and cyclic sighing occasionally
disrupted their focus and practice. Improving the animations for these exercises would
likely enhance user engagement.
Additionally, it became evident during the design process that users lacked clear and
accurate information on how breathwork could help them. Research supported this ob-
servation, as the lack of trusted, research-based information was identified as a significant
flaw in existing menstrual symptom management apps [6]. I relied heavily on user input
to design the scientific information page to address this. Users preferred a chat-based
interface with interactive elements, leading to the development of the Information Chats
section. However, as shown in fig. 6.3, user interest in the Information Chats section
quickly waned, with visits declining significantly by the second week of the study. This
decline is expected, as users may only need to review the information once, but it still
reflects low overall engagement with the feature.
7.1.3
Validation Study
The final main goal of the project was to evaluate the feasibility of the created mHealth
application prototype. This goal was achieved during the final validation study, which
assessed three key aspects of feasibility: usage adoption, health efficacy, and perceived
usefulness and usability. The findings, previously presented in chapter 6, will be analyzed
and interpreted in this section.
The overall usage adoption rate calculated after the validation study was 64.3%. This
reflects the percentage of time users fully completed one breathwork exercise per day,
as instructed in the study. However, a breakdown of usage per user (fig. 6.1) reveals
that most participants missed more usage days during the second week. This suggests
a gradual decline in interest as the study progressed. It is worth noting that the second
week coincided with the Christmas holiday period, during which users were likely out of
their usual routines, potentially affecting their engagement with the app.
To add, User 12 (table 6.1) only engaged with the application during the first two days
of the trial, citing personal events and emotional distress as reasons for discontinuation.
Similarly, User 8 (table 6.1) stopped using the app early after experiencing a stressful life
change that demanded her full attention. Therefore, while designed to support emotional
well-being, the BreatheThrough app was not an intuitive tool for users during stressful

==PAGE:59==
moments.
The second key feature evaluated was health efficacy, which assessed whether the Brea-
theThrough application impacts menstrual cycle symptom intensity.
The average im-
provement for insomnia was 0%, exposing a design flaw in the study: insomnia improve-
ments cannot be effectively evaluated immediately after a breathwork exercise. Other
symptoms showed average improvements ranging from 11% to 28%, with an overall
average of 15%. Anxiety demonstrated the highest improvement at 28%, aligning with
prior research that consistently mentions anxiety as a symptom significantly improved by
breathwork [15, 48, 49, 51].
Abdominal cramps showed significant improvement (24%), but with only 10 logged in-
stances, the sample size is too small for definitive conclusions. Additionally, one extreme
outlier reported a 130% increase in cramp intensity. Headaches showed a 15% overall
improvement but included instances of 0% improvement and one negative result, sug-
gesting that physical symptoms like headaches and cramps may require longer or more
consistent breathwork sessions for significant benefits. For instance, studies found posi-
tive results for abdominal cramps after 30 minutes of daily practice [47] and for migraines
after six months of consistent breathwork [46]. Further data on symptom management
over an extended period is needed for more confident conclusions.
The Perceived Usefulness and Usability were evaluated using the CUMACF Question-
naire, yielding mostly positive results across all measured aspects (fig. 6.7). Effort Ex-
pectancy and Facilitating Conditions showed the highest scores, indicating that users
found the system easy to use, appreciated its features, and felt confident in their ability
to use it. These findings were reinforced by free-form feedback, where users praised the
intuitive and enjoyable design of BreatheThrough.
However, responses regarding Health Expectancy were less positive, suggesting that
users had doubts about the system’s effectiveness in addressing their menstrual cycle
needs. This skepticism was also reflected in the Behavioral Intention results, where nearly
50% of responses were neutral or negative, indicating doubts about future usage. One
possible explanation is that users might not use the app if they were symptom-free. There-
fore, the results might be different if they were not instructed to use the app daily.
The overall feedback collected during the validation study indicated positive trends across
all aspects of feasibility. While additional data is needed for a more confident assessment,
the findings suggest that women view the BreatheThrough application as a potentially
useful and effective tool for managing menstrual cycle symptoms.
7.2
Limitations
This thesis project was subject to a few significant limitations, which are important to
consider while discussing its main findings:
• The validation study duration was only two weeks. This is a very short period for
a user study, particularly for technology targeting female health. Firstly, it was not
possible to ensure that all participants experienced their luteal and menstrual phases
during the study. Hence, not all participants used the application at the stage of
their cycle where its benefits would be most apparent. In addition, observing women
across at least two cycles is recommended to gain valid and meaningful insights [77].
• Symptom intensity measures could be further improved to get more valuable in-
formation about breathwork impact. Currently, users use a slider to approximately
self-evaluate their symptom intensity. However, different users could interpret their

bring more clarity.
• As mentioned, the BreatheThrough application had a few technical issues. Most no-
tably - the animations of 4-7-8 breathing and cyclic sighing exercises were lagging,
interrupting breathwork sessions.
• It is also important to mention that all participants were recruited from my personal
network, which may have introduced biases due to the limited diversity in their pro-
files.

8
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, I aimed to answer the question of what features and functionalities
a tool incorporating mindfulness-based activities for managing menstrual cycle symptoms
should include. In addition, I wanted to assess whether such a tool would be used by
women, whether it would be effective in helping them cope with their symptom intensity,
and whether women would perceive it as both useful and usable.
To address this, I explored research and existing solutions related to mindfulness-based
activities and their effects on the menstrual cycle, aiming to identify those most suitable for
such a technology. The findings revealed that mindfulness-based therapies, meditation,
and breathwork had the strongest scientific evidence supporting their effectiveness in this
context. Among these, breathwork was selected as the primary activity for implementation
due to users’ preference for it and its notable absence in existing menstrual symptom
management tools.
Additionally, I have used a user-centered design methodology to design the application
and its main functionality. The main features defined throughout this process were en-
gaging in breathwork activities that give real-time feedback to the users, the ability to track
the impact of these activities on user symptoms, and reliable information on why and how
these sessions might help women. All of these features were implemented in code as a
BreatheThrough mobile application.
After implementing the application, I conducted a two-week validation study to assess its
feasibility. The study showed that users engaged with the app as instructed, though usage
declined slightly over time, indicating a potential loss of interest. On average, symptom
intensity improved by 15% after breathwork sessions, suggesting the app’s potential to
help women. Users found the application easy and straightforward to use, though some
expressed doubts about its effectiveness and future use. Despite these doubts, the overall
positive evaluations across all measured feasibility aspects highlight the app’s promise as
a menstrual cycle management tool.
However, it is important to consider several aspects that might have influenced the val-
idation study results. First, a study that runs longer and includes a more diverse group
of women would need to be conducted to gather more reliable data for evaluating female
health. In addition, the intensity of the symptoms measuring scale could be improved, as
the current measure might leave too much for users to interpret. There were also a few
technical issues with the breathwork exercises that negatively affected users’ symptom
intensity.
Overall, this thesis represents an important step toward developing alternative solutions to
help women manage challenging symptoms associated with their menstrual cycle. While
further data is needed to draw definitive conclusions, the design of the BreatheThrough
application shows promise as a practical tool for effectively reducing symptom intensity.
Preliminary findings also suggest that women would find the app intuitive, easy to use,
and beneficial for using effective breathwork techniques for menstrual cycle symptom
management.
