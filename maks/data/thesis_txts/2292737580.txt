Resume 
Der findes op til flere forskellige hæmoglobinvarianter i røde blodlegemer, her i blandt føtal 
hæmoglobin (HbF). I nyfødte er mængden af HbF meget høj, men dette bør ændre sig til voksent 
hæmoglobin i løbet af de første seks måneder af livet. Ved forskellige sygdomme kan niveauet af 
HbF og andre hæmoglobinvarianter øges. Det er derfor vigtigt at være i stand til, at detektere og 
beregne fraktionen af HbF i forhold til den totale hæmoglobinmængde. 
Målet med dette projekt er at udvikle en ny referencemetode til bestemmelse af HbF. Denne metode 
vil blive brugt som referencemetode i forhold til HbF målinger på Radiometer Medicals ABL735 
analyser. Indtil for nyligt har Radiometer Medical brugt referencemetoden fra Herlev Hospital. 
Denne metode er baseret på en HPLC metode, hvor de forskellige hæmoglobinvarianter kan 
adskilles og detekteres i forhold til den samlede hæmoglobin mængde i en blodprøve. Den nye 
referencemetode hos Radiometer Medical er derved også baseret på en HPLC metode. HPLC 
metoden fra Herlev Hospital antages at give den sande værdi af mængde af HbF i en prøve. 
I forhold til Herlev Hospital blev en PolyCAT A kolonne valgt som den foretrukne kolonne for den 
nye referencemetode. For at finde den bedste og mest præcise metode til HPLC‟en blev en 
metodeudvikling udført. Gennem denne udvikling, viste en metode (metode B34) det bedste og 
mest præcise kromatogram for både Bio-Rad level 1 og 2 kontrolprøver og for en føtal blodprøve. 
Det var således den foretrukne metode til videre undersøgelse. 
Sammenligning tests over 3 dage med 1) Radiometer Medicals ABL735 analyser og 2) HPLC 
metoden på Herlev Hospital blev herefter udført. Forskellige statistiske beregninger blev udført for, 
at angive præcision og nøjagtighed af den nyudviklede HPLC metode, i forhold til den sande værdi 
fra Herlev Hospital, og i forhold til at skulle anvendes som nye referencemetode til ABL735. 
De statistiske beregninger beviste, at den nye referencemetode på HPLC‟en viste god præcision, 
korrekthed og nøjagtighed i forhold til den antagede sande værdi fra Herlev Hospital. Den vil 
således kunne blive foretrukket som ny referencemetode til ABL735. Dette kan blive præciseret i 
relation til, at den viste samme præcision og nøjagtighed som referencemetode fra Herlev Hospital.

Abstract 
Foetal haemoglobin (HbF) is one of several haemoglobin derivatives which can be found in red 
blood cells. The level of HbF is high in new-borns, but should convert to adult haemoglobin during 
the first 6 months of life. Under different diseases the level of HbF can increase along with other 
haemoglobin derivatives. It is therefore important to be able to detect and calculate the fraction of 
HbF of total haemoglobin.  
The goal of this study is to develop a new reference method to determine the fraction of HbF. This 
will be used as the reference method for Radiometer Medicals ABL735 analysers. Until recently 
Radiometer Medical has used the reference method present at Herlev Hospital. This method is 
based on a HPLC method in which the different haemoglobin derivatives can be separated and 
detected in relation to the total haemoglobin in a blood sample. Due to this the new reference 
method at Radiometer Medical is also based on a HPLC. The HPLC method from Herlev Hospital 
is assumed to give the true value of the fraction of HbF in a sample.  
In relation to Herlev Hospital a PolyCAT A column was selected as the preferred column for the 
new reference method. To find the best and most precise method for the HPLC, a method 
development was performed. One method (method B34) presented the best and most precise 
chromatogram for both Bio-Rad level 1 and 2 control samples and for a foetal blood sample. It was 
thus the method of choice for further investigation. 
During further investigation comparison tests over 3 days with 1) Radiometer Medicals ABL735 
analyser and 2) the HPLC method at Herlev Hospital were performed. Different statistical 
calculations were carried out to specify the precision and accuracy of the new developed method in 
relation to the assumed true value at Herlev Hospital and in relation to be the new reference method 
of preference to the ABL735.  
The statistical calculations prove that the new reference method on the HPLC shows good 
precision, trueness and accuracy in relation to the assumed true value from Herlev Hospital. It is 
thus preferred as the new reference method for the ABL735. In relation to this it shows the same 
precision and accuracy as the reference method from Herlev Hospital.

1 Introduction 
The human body contains approximately one-eleventh of blood of the total weight. Blood is 
consisting of different cell types within an extracellular matrix that function together. It is a unique 
tissue and because the extracellular matrix is a liquid it is the easiest tissue from which to isolate 
components. 
Blood consists of a liquid component, the plasma (55%), and formed elements (45%), which are 
called the solid components. The formed elements can be divided into blood cells (erythrocytes and 
leukocytes) and platelets (thrombocytes). A cubic millimetre of whole blood consists of 5 million 
erythrocytes (red blood cells), 5000 to 10,000 leukocytes (white blood cells) and 200,000 to 
300,000 thrombocytes. [1] Thrombocytes are important for the blood clotting process. The 
leukocyte protects the body against infection when occurring. They are larger than erythrocytes. 
The erythrocyte carries oxygen in the blood from the respiratory organs to the rest of the body and 
facilitates the return transport of CO2. The erythrocytes consist of haemoglobins.   
Today many people are living with a critical blood disease, which will be further discussed in 
section 1.2.1 Diseases related to foetal haemoglobin, caused by different mutations in e.g. the 
haemoglobins. Some of these diseases are related to different kinds of haemoglobins in the red 
blood cells. Foetal haemoglobin is increased in several of these diseases (this will be further 
discussed in section 1.2 Foetal haemoglobin). Measuring the level of different haemoglobins is thus 
important to the human health and safety. At Radiometer Medical, they have a conventional method 
(ABL735 (Acid Base Laboratory no. 735)) to measure the level of foetal haemoglobin.  
The department of metrology is responsible for reference methods and for making reference 
samples that can be used as quality controls to the ABL735 analyser. One of their goals is to make a 
reference method to detect foetal haemoglobin in a blood sample using preferable method based on 
the HPLC. The method development and comparison with the ABL735 analyser and the HPLC 
reference method at Herlev Hospital, which is assumed to be the true value for measuring the 
fraction of foetal haemoglobin, will be the main focus in the current project.

1.1 Haemoglobin 
Haemoglobin is the main protein in the red blood cells in humans. Each red blood cell contains 
approximately 280 million haemoglobin molecules. Haemoglobin is an iron-containing 
metalloprotein. It is an important factor in the red blood cells (1 Introduction). In human beings, 
maternal (adult) haemoglobin (HbA) is the most common. It represents over 95% of all 
haemoglobins in the body. HbA can be divided into several different HbA‟s: e.g. HbA1A, HbA1B 
and HbA1C. In a healthy person, it is also possible to detect some amount of HbA2 (3-5%) and HbF 
(foetal haemoglobin) (< 1%). [2]  
Haemoglobin A is build-up of four polypeptides two α and two β chains. Each residue contains one 
iron (Fe), bound to a porphyrin group (heme group) (Figure 1.1). Fe can exist in both ferrous (Fe2+) 
and ferric (Fe3+) state in the heme group. It is only Fe2+ that is capable of binding oxygen. [3] 
 
Figure 1.1: The porphyrin group in haemoglobin, with ferrous bound  
During oxygen binding, haemoglobin undergoes a change. This transition is characterised by the 
oxygenated (R) state and the deoxygenated (T) state. Figure 1.2 shows the T-state (left) and R-state 
(right). The heme group is represented in each subunit of both the R- and T-state. The subunits are 
closer to each other in the R-state than in the T-state, as oxygen have been bounded to the heme 
groups in the R-state [4].

1.2 Foetal haemoglobin (HbF) 
Foetal haemoglobin (HbF) represents 50 – 95% of all haemoglobins in a foetus. As the HbA 
synthesis is activated, HbF declines after birth. When the infant is approximately 6 months the level 
of HbF should be < 1%. HbF consists of two α and two γ chains. The γ chain is 72% identical to the 
β chain in HbA. Figure 1.3 shows the differences of deoxy - HbA (T) (left) and deoxy - HbF (right).

In addition to HbA, HbF does not have the same affinity to oxygen. This is due to the differences 
between the γ and the β chain. This cause higher oxygen-binding affinity for HbF compared to HbA 
(Figure 1.4). The difference allows oxygen to be effectively transferred from maternal to foetal red 
blood cells, so the mother can give the foetus oxygen during pregnancy [3].  
 
Figure 1.4: Oxygen affinity of foetal haemoglobin compared to the oxygen affinity of adult haemoglobin [3] 
 
1.2.1 Diseases related to foetal haemoglobin  
If the HbA synthesis is not properly activated, the γ chain does not change to the β chain. This 
inactivation can cause different blood diseases. Measuring foetal haemoglobin in infants after birth 
is thus important to make sure that HbF in the infant has changed to HbA. 
The two main blood diseases, in which a significant level of HbF is observed, are sickle-cell anemia 
and thalassemia. Sickle-cell anemia is caused by a mutation of a single amino acid substitution on 
both β chains. This mutation is called haemoglobin S (HbS). Most people with sickle-cell anemia, 
has increased level of HbF [8].  
Thalassemia can be divided into two types, α-thalassemia and β-thalassemia. In α-thalassemia, the α 
chain is not produced in sufficient quantity.  This results in haemoglobin tetramers, called HbH, 
which only contains β chains. Due to this, the release of oxygen in the body is poor. In β-
thalassemia, the β chain is not produced in sufficient quantity. Therefore, the α chain form insoluble 
aggregates which precipitate inside incomplete red blood cells. This results in anemia. Many 
different genetic variations are associated with both α- and β-thalassemia, which can cause a wide 
range of clinical severity. A child with thalassemia or sickle-cell anemia will require regular 
lifelong blood transfusion, due to the low amount of functional haemoglobins and a decreased 
production of red blood cells. [3]

1.3 Methods to measure HbF used worldwide 
Since 1951, it has been possible clinically to distinguish between HbF and HbA. The first method 
used was the alkali denaturation method. This is a well-known spectrophotometric method for 
measuring HbF. The uncertainty of this method can be significant, since it underestimates the 
amount of HbF at high levels. Also a significant disadvantage with the alkali denaturation method 
is, that it is not only HbF which may convert into alkali hematin at the specific time [12–15]. 
Besides the uncertainties, it is still used as a reference to most of the newer HbF methods, including 
Radiometer Medical‟s ABL735 analyser. 
Today, there are many different methods where the different haemoglobins including HbF can be 
measured. Of these, there are for example capillary zone electrophoresis (CZE) [16, 17], capillary 
isoelectric focussing (cIEF) [17–20], radial immunoassay [21, 22] and High Performance Liquid 
Chromatography (HPLC) [23–31]. 
Table 1.2 lists some of the recommended methods used to measure foetal haemoglobin. Of these, 
the HPLC is today the most used worldwide. This is due to the reliability of the method in 
separation of the different haemoglobins and the precision of the per cent level of a specific 
haemoglobin compared to the total per cent level of haemoglobins in a blood sample.

1.4 Determining the fraction of HbF (%HbF) by High Performance Liquid 
Chromatography (HPLC) 
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) uses high pressure to force solvent through a 
column with fine particles, which gives high resolution separation. The HPLC system used in this 
study consists of an autosampler, a solvent delivery system, a sample injection valve, a high-
pressure chromatography column, a photodiode absorbance detector and a computer to handle the 
program and display the results from the HPLC. The column is placed in an oven where it can be 
kept at a constant temperature. [33] 
For qualitative information, the retention time of a component has to be constant under identical 
chromatographic conditions. For quantitative information both the area and the peak height have to 
be proportional to the amount of a compound injected [34]. From the total peak area of all 
haemoglobins, the fraction of HbF (%HbF or FHbF) can be calculated.

To be clinically useful, the results of the HbF quantification have to be expressed as a percentage of 
the total haemoglobin content. Both precision and accuracy are extremely important since an 
increased HbF level in a sample could be caused by one of the mentioned diseases (1.2.1 Diseases 
related to HbF) or since some interference in the apparatus could cause an overestimation.

1.6.2.3 Two- and three-way analysis of variance  
To make quantitative assurance of the tests made, the analysis of variance (ANOVA) is used. The 
purpose of using ANOVA is to compare averages in more than two populations. The principle of 
the ANOVA is like the F- and t-test, which means calculating a suitable test size on the basis of 
some test samples and compare these to a table value or a test probability. To make an ANOVA, all 
populations have to be normalised and the hypothesis that the variance of the results, which should 
be tested is equal should be accomplished. [44–46] 
To make a two-way analysis of variance, two qualitative factors have to be used. For the two-way 
ANOVA there can be row effect, Ri, column effect, Cj, and interaction     , for i rows, and j

1.7 Project description  
As mentioned in 1. Introduction, foetal haemoglobin is one of several haemoglobin derivatives 
which can be found in red blood cells. The level of HbF is high in new-borns, but during the first 6 
month of life it should change to adult haemoglobin. The HbF level can increase under different 
diseases in which levels of other haemoglobin derivatives are increased as well. Due to these 
different parameters in which the level of HbF can increase, it is important to measure the fraction 
of HbF (%HbF) and also other haemoglobins (this will not be considered here, but should be 
considered during further validation) of total haemoglobin. Today, there are several different 
methods to measure the haemoglobin derivatives where some are more precise than others. 
The goal for Radiometer Medical is to have a reference method to determine the HbF level in 
comparison to their ABL735 apparatus and the HPLC reference method at Herlev Hospital, which 
they assume to give the true value of %HbF. Thus this method is chosen to be established on a 
HPLC. As the HPLC is known as a reliable reference method (Herlev Hospital) for measuring the 
fraction of different haemoglobins and since this apparatus is available at Radiometer Medical. 
Before the experimental work could be done, some problems had to be considered. First of all, a 
column which could separate haemoglobins had to be found. Second of all, to find control samples, 
that could be used as a kind of reference to the blood samples. This to be sure that the peak chosen 
as HbF was the correct one.    
To be able to perform a new reference method on the HPLC, a method development had to be 
carried out. This method development should be performed on control samples and foetal blood 
samples to give a precise and reliable chromatogram. Where the peak of foetal haemoglobin can be 
distinguished and the fraction of HbF of the total haemoglobin can be calculated.

2.4 High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) 
Before measuring on the HPLC some changes had to be made. First of all, none of the mobile 
phases may be degassed before running into the column. This is stated from PolyLC Inc.:  
“Because haemoglobin tetramers exchange dimers in solution in a dynamic fashion. In the absence 
of oxygen, this process is much slower.”  
PolyLC Inc. [49]. 
This was done by removing the tubes from the degasser and put them together. Afterwards the 
system had to be checked for air bubbles. Also the mounting of the computer program had to be 
changed in correlation with the method development. This will be described further in section 3 
Results and 4 Discussion.      
2.4.1 The column chosen 
The column used is a PolyCAT ATM column from PolyLC Inc.. The column is a silica-based 
material with a bonded coating of polyaspartic acid. It is a weak cation-exchange (WCX) material. 
Detail information is given in [49].  
2.4.2 Mobile phases 
From the protocol from PolyLC, two mobile phases (A and B) were presented. Mobile phase A 
consists of 20mM Bis-tris buffer + 2mM KCN, pH = 6.96. Mobile phase B consists of 20mM Bis-
tris buffer + 2mM KCN + 200mM NaCl, pH = 6.55. The pH in both mobile phases was adjusted 
with 1M HCl under continuous measuring of pH with a pH meter. Mobile phase A was also used as 
dilution agent for both the control- and blood samples. [49]

4 Discussion 
In this project a new reference method based on HPLC to measure the fraction of HbF in a blood 
sample has been developed. The new reference method has been developed in order to fulfil a need 
by Radiometer Medical to have a reliable and accurate reference method to determine the fraction 
of HbF to compare to the ABL735 analyser. Previously UHPLC method from Herlev Hospital has 
been used as a reference and was assumed to be the true value. To make the new reference method 
based on the HPLC, method development was carried out.   
A haemolysis method was reported in the protocol from PolyLC Inc. this haemolysis had to be done 
before method development could be performed [49]. An adult blood sample was haemolysed using 
the method described in the PolyLC protocol [49]. This haemolysis was incomplete. A haemolysis 
test was thus carried out, to find the best method to haemolyse a blood sample before measurement 
on the HPLC. These tests were carried out since it was stated from PolyLC that a blood sample 
should be haemolysed before entering the column. Herlev Hospital used the same column for their 
measurements as the column used for the measurements at Radiometer Medical, thus their 
haemolysis method was investigated. The procedure from Herlev Hospital showed that they did not 
remove the plasma phase before haemolysis and it was chosen not to use this method. Since only 
one column was available a precise haemolysis method was desired, so it was certain that nothing 
could clog the column and destroy it.  
To find the best haemolysis method, a literature search was carried out, to investigate different 
methods [25, 50–53] (Appendix B). Some of these methods showed good results, but none of the 
literature methods showed a precise haemolysis with a clear pinkish haemolysate. It was then 
chosen to combine some of the methods, which in the end gave the desired haemolysis that could be 
used for further work (see 2.4.1 Haemolysing blood for the chosen method). The chosen method for 
haemolysis was used on all foetal blood samples for the measurements during the method 
development and the comparison tests.              
The accuracy, trueness, reliability and precision of the method, both through the method 
development and the comparison with 1) ABL735, and 2) Herlev Hospital (true value) will be 
investigated. A comparison with the literature will also be considered, to see how reliable and 
precise the new HPLC method at Radiometer Medical is, compared to HPLC methods which are 
used worldwide. Also a small comparison of other methods will be considered.

4.1 Method development  
The wavelength was chosen to be 415 nm for the detector on the HPLC, since this lies within the 
soret band, which is within the absorption band of all porphyrins at approximately 400 nm [38]. 
Different wavelengths have been used in different articles [20, 25, 31, 59]. The reason why the 
wavelength was not investigated, is due to the ICSH report [9] where the wavelength of 415 nm is 
stated to be the optimal wavelength to use for haemoglobin measurements on a HPLC.    
Since Herlev Hospital use a PolyCAT A column from PolyLC Inc. for their HPLC method, this 
column was chosen for measurements carried out on the HPLC at Radiometer Medical. This 
column showed good reliability both for the results from Herlev Hospital and from experimental 
data reported in the literature [51, 60]. The method, which was stated in the protocol for the 
PolyCAT A column, provided by PolyLC showed poor results on the HPLC at Radiometer 
Medical. Therefore, several methods were investigated in order to find the most suitable and precise 
method for both Bio-Rad control- and blood samples. Overall 28 different methods were 
investigated to find the most suitable method for haemoglobin measurements on the HPLC. Each 
method was run several times to be sure of the choice of the appropriate method.  
For the method development with Bio-Rad level 1 and 2 control samples, flow rate and gradient 
composition were changed whereas the start gradient was kept at 10 (%B). This showed poor 
separation (Appendix D) compared to the chromatograms given from Herlev Hospital (Figure 4.1) 
of the two Bio-Rad control samples [61]. The gradient composition of the HPLC from Herlev 
Hospital is not known.

overestimation was shown, method 8 was selected as the best method for the Bio-Rad control

obtained (Table 3.4). The resolution at 0.00 indicates that a manual separation of two peaks was

were carried out with method 17, the peak separation were again not well defined (Figure 3.5). 
After further method development where the different parameters (e.g. flow rate, Table 4.2) were 
investigated an appropriated method (method B34) with good separation and precision for both Bio-
Rad control- and blood sample was found (Figure 3.6). The parameters for this method were a flow 
rate of 1.5 mL/min and a gradient composition of 5 %B increasing to 20 %B over 35 min, to 100

reason for this could be that the fraction of HbF in Bio-Rad level 1 was so small, that the HPLC 
program could not properly distinguish the HbF peak from the other peaks. Thus it was necessary to 
make a peak separation manually. Method B34 did not display the best theoretical plates or 
resolution for the foetal blood sample, but it presented the best separation of the peaks for both 
foetal blood- and Bio-Rad level 2 samples (Table 3.5). Due to the good separation (Figure 3.6) and 
that the resolution of Bio-Rad level 2 was 1.08, method B34 was selected in comparison to the rest 
of the methods.  
The LOD and LOQ value for the HPLC method selected were 0.03 %HbF and 0.08 %HbF, 
respectively. The detection limit of the instrument, LOD, indicates that the quantity of the used 
HPLC with the used column is precise and good for detecting the fraction of HbF. The detection 
limit associated with the method, LOQ, indicates that the quantity of the method for detecting the 
fraction of HbF is precise and good. Even though these two detection limits were so small for the 
HPLC and the used method, it could be difficult to distinguish this small amount of %HbF. The 
reason for this could be that when %HbF was below 1% the peak could interfere with some of the 
other peaks and cause uncertain detection. A level of %HbF below 1% does not cause any clinically 
critical condition. This means that when measuring %HbF at the HPLC and other apparatus these 
small amounts of %HbF should not be considered.

4.2 Comparison  
The comparison test was carried out over three days. Each day a portion of foetal blood from 
Hvidovre Hospital was delivered. The blood was prepared according to 2.4.3 Test blood with 
variation of ctHb to get the three different ctHb levels. The pH and sO2 had to be adjusted before 
any measurements on the ABL735 could be performed. The pH had to be adjusted since EDTA, 
which lowers the pH, was used as anti-coagulant. Due to this, different parameters could interfere 
with the result on the ABL735. These uncertainties could be caused by the amount of 1 M NaOH 
that was added and that the blood sample had to be aerated every time more NaOH was added. 
These uncertainties will be discussed later where a foetal blood sample with heparin was measured 
and the results compared to the results from a foetal blood sample with EDTA (both blood bags 
were filled from the same donor, 5 ABL735 analysers used for this comparison). After the 
measurements had been carried out, they were investigated with the use of different statistical 
analyse. In this section a discussion of these results will be carried out, to get the impression of the 
precision, trueness and accuracy of the used methods (HPLC(RMED), ABL735 and HPLC(Herlev) 
in comparison to each other.        
As mentioned one of the five ABL735 analysers showed an error message due to some oxy 
mismatch (apparatus 2). The apparatus was sent to maintenance after the three days of 
measurements. The results from this apparatus were thus excluded from further data processing. 
The exclusion of the results was done according to of Radiometer Medical guidelines for the 
ABL735.  
Before any comparison tests could be carried out, a two-way ANOVA was completed on the results 
from each method to see if there was any dependency of 1) ctHb or 2) day. For the results from 
ABL735 a three-way ANOVA was also completed to see if there was any dependency in 1) ctHb, 
2) day or 3) apparatus (analysis made on 4 apparatus). As mentioned in section 3.2.1 Analysis of 
variance, the interaction does not have any relevance for further assessment in this case and was 
therefore not considered.

ANOVA showed that there was ctHb dependency in the results from both HPLC(RMED) and 
ABL735. This was indicated with an F-ratio at 29.2 and an α-value of < 0.0001, and an F-ratio at 
19.5 and an α-value of < 0.0001 for ctHb in the results from HPLC(RMED) and ABL735,

molar extinction coefficients were not considered as %HbF is determined by the percentage of the

5 Conclusion  
Through literature and experimental investigation, a reference method to the ABL735, to detect the 
fraction of HbF has been developed. This reference method was developed on the HPLC apparatus 
in the department of Metrology through a method development. From the literature and 
conversation with Herlev Hospital, the PolyCAT A column from PolyLC Inc. was chosen to be the 
column of preference for the new reference method at Radiometer Medical.  
Through the method development, which implies change in flow rate, gradient composition and 
time of gradient, a lot of methods were investigated, to find a suitable method where both foetal 
blood- and Bio-Rad control samples could be measured. Of all the investigated methods, method 
B34 with a theoretical plates value at 239 and 136 for foetal blood and Bio-Rad level 2, 
respectively. A resolution at 0.00 (the resolution at 0.00 indicates that a manual separation of two 
peaks was done, and thus the program is not able to calculate a resolution) and 1.08 for foetal blood 
and Bio-Rad level 2, respectively, was the method of choice for measuring the fraction of HbF in 
both Bio-Rad control- and foetal blood samples.  
Quality assurance calculations showed that it was possible to detect a small amount of %HbF from 
an adult blood sample, in which it was known that the %HbF level was low, on the HPLC at 
Radiometer Medical. The LOD and LOQ, based on the method from Shimadzu [35] were found to 
be 0.03% and 0.08%, respectively. These limits may be considered as a qualitative indication of the 
performance of the HPLC and the chosen method (method B34). Even though the LOQ value 
indicated this small fraction of HbF to be detectable, it was not possible to measure this small 
amount of %HbF in an adult blood sample.             
The two-way ANOVA showed ctHb dependency for the results on both HPLC(RMED) and 
ABL735, thus for further comparison the results were divided into the three ctHb levels. The three-
way ANOVA showed apparatus dependency for the ABL735, this dependency was not further 
considered since the result from the ABL735 had to be an average of several apparatus in this 
project.  
For the comparison tests between results from HPLC(RMED) and HPLC(Herlev), which is 
assumed to be the true value. The paired t-test showed that with a hypothesised mean difference at 
0.5, it could not be rejected that the two methods were similar and thus the results from

5.1 Perspective 
The statistical results in this study have showed good reliability, even though small number of 
measurements and time limit of the HPLC at Radiometer Medical. For further developing and 
validation, the number of measurements from each method should be appreciably larger. An 
agreement with Herlev Hospital should be made, so the number of measurements performed will be 
the same for the two HPLC methods. This to give a more precise and accurate HPLC method at 
Radiometer Medical compared to the assumed true value from Herlev Hospital.  
As for the chosen wavelength (415 nm), the clinical agreement from the ICSH report and that 
Herlev Hospital uses this wavelength approves this chose. In further study this could be 
investigated, but as the new HPLC reference method at Radiometer Medical should be used instead 
of the HPLC method at Herlev Hospital. The wavelength at the HPLC method at Radiometer 
Medical should be the same as the HPLC method at Herlev Hospital.
