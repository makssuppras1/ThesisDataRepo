Abstract
This comprehensive project is inspired by assessing anxiety in children during hospital vis­
its, mainly in emergency rooms. A pivotal aspect of this initiative involves the creation of
eye­tracking glasses explicitly tailored for young children aged 4­7. Taking as inspiration
the versatile ”Core” eye­tracking glasses from Pupil Labs, the primary objective is to de­
sign other frames that comfortably fit the smallest heads and prioritize user­centric design
principles. The project encompasses the development of functional prototypes, subject
to rigorous testing, encompassing evaluations for eye­tracking precision and qualitative
assessments for the glasses’ usability and child­friendly attributes.
The project benefits from crucial resources such as access to Pupil Labs’ ”Core,” utilization
of DTU’s 3D printing facilities, and valuable collaboration with healthcare professionals at
Herlev Hospital. This thesis aims to conceptualize frames that resonate with children,
conducting extensive analyses to determine the optimal shape, appearance, and fit for
the glasses. Beyond design considerations, this work carries significant potential to en­
hance pediatric healthcare solutions, reshaping the landscape of child care by attuning to
their emotional needs and ultimately contributing to a more compassionate and practical
healthcare experience.

1
Introduction
Children arriving at the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED) often face high anxiety
stemming from the unfamiliar clinical environment, acute pain and fear of the unknown.
While Herlev Hospital PED in Denmark seeks a positive patient experience, peak times
may result in untreated anxiety, carrying potential consequences like delayed medical
treatments or increased reliance on faster calming methods, like sedatives. Effectively
addressing pediatric anxiety in the PED is crucial for improving patient experience, treat­
ment quality, and efficiency. However, accurately measuring this subjective feeling poses
a significant challenge, particularly in children aged 4­7, who may lack the cognitive ability
to express anxiety and other distressed emotions verbally.
This study focuses on the emotional well­being of children aged 4­7 in the PED waiting
area, taking advantage of the eye tracking technology, seeking to answer the problem
statement:
“How might we design a pair of eye­tracking glasses that are children­friendly and
useful in hospital settings?”
The principal objective of this study is to develop and design child­friendly eye­tracking
glasses intended for use in pediatric hospital settings. These glasses will be tailored to
engage children effectively, ensuring their willingness to wear and interact with the device.
Beyond the scope of the current project, the ultimate goal is to use these glasses to
measure children’s anxiety levels during their hospital stay. This will be done by collecting
biometric data, focusing on eye parameters such as pupil dilation, fixation and blink rate.
The aim is to understand better how anxiety manifests itself in children in the Paediatric
Emergency Department (PED) setting.

1.1
Motivation
This study is motivated by young children’s challenges in medical environments, espe­
cially in emergency departments. The ultimate wish is to reduce the anxiety experienced
by them during hospital visits, given that conventional anxiety measurement methods,
such as self­reporting or observational methods, are intrinsically subjective. Therefore,
the objective is to shift the paradigm towards objective data collection using advanced
eye­tracking glasses, which can provide customised interventions and enhanced emo­
tional support for children. More details about the observational methods will be explained
in the following sections.
Furthermore, this project aims to redefine the healthcare experience for children by com­
bining empathetic design principles with advanced technology. The convergence of these
principles prioritises understanding, comfort, and positive outcomes that can improve chil­
dren’s emotional well­being. Addressing children’s emotional well­being optimises imme­
diate medical care and contributes to long­term resilience and trust in healthcare systems.
This study is driven by the potential to revolutionise pediatric healthcare, providing a more
precise, real­time understanding of children’s emotions and fostering the development of
compassionate, child­centric healthcare solutions.
The study aims to assist in this quest by providing a better tool for young children, focusing
on their preferences and needs.

1.2
Problem Statement and Research Questions
In pediatric healthcare, Herlev Hospital recognizes the importance of reducing anxiety in
young patients during hospital visits. The hospital takes a proactive approach by imple­
menting various techniques, such as providing games in the waiting area, toys, stuffed
animals, and bubble­blowers in the triage area and scheduling regular clown visits. How­
ever, nurses have expressed concerns about accurately assessing children’s anxiety lev­
els, as the current evaluation methods often rely on subjective measures.
This concern highlights the critical need for a more objective and reliable tool to mea­
sure anxiety levels in children. Developing child­centric eye­tracking glasses could be
a potential solution to this challenge. These glasses aim to measure anxiety objectively
and provide a deeper understanding of children’s emotional states during medical visits.
The challenge of this project lies in designing eye­tracking glasses tailored explicitly for
children, prioritizing child­friendly attributes and serving as valuable tools in hospital en­
vironments, which represents a transformative step. It seeks to integrate cutting­edge
technology with empathetic design principles to enhance the healthcare experience for
the little ones.
In this manner, this project will try to answer the following question:
“How might we design a pair of eye­tracking glasses that are children­friendly and
useful in hospital settings?”
As well as the sub­questions:
1. ”How can an eye tracking device aid in measuring children’s anxiety in hospital wait­
ing areas?”
2. ”How can we integrate playful and engaging elements into the design to make the
glasses more attractive to children?”
3. ”How can we adapt the existing technology to better suit children’s unique needs
and preferences in a hospital setting?”
To gain a deeper understanding of a project’s significance, expansive questions that go
beyond its scope can be asked. For instance, exploring how eye­tracking glasses can be
used to monitor anxiety levels in young children and how biometric data can improve pedi­
atric care can lead to identifying innovative solutions that align with the goal of advancing
pediatric care and promoting wellness.
In this way, the scope of the project is the following:
The target audience for this project is children aged between 4 to 7 years old. The objec­
tive is to develop a pair of eye­tracking glasses designed using industrial design methods,
focusing on meeting the users’ specific needs, especially ensuring that the glasses are
comfortable for children in this age group and, hopefully, they are willing to wear them.
While theoretical research has been made to explore methods to use biometrics to as­
sess anxiety and understand anxiety in young children, the steps to collect, analyze, and
visualize biometric data using eye­tracking devices are not within this project’s scope.

Background and Related Work
This chapter aims to deepen our understanding of anxiety. Throughout this section, vari­
ous dimensions of anxiety will be explored, covering its identification, its relationship with
fear and pain, and the complications that can result in medical interventions in the context
of Paediatric Emergency Departments. In addition, this concept will be linked to its mani­
festation in the eyes, delving into the field of eye­tracking technology, its market presence
and applicability in the hospital setting. All of these aspects contribute to establishing a
theoretical framework to support this project.
2.1
Understanding Anxiety
Understanding anxiety is crucial to addressing its harmful consequences. Anxiety is a
complex state that can involve psychological, physiological, and behavioral aspects in­
duced by the perception of threats to well­being or survival [1]. In response to such threats,
the body activates the sympathetic branch of the autonomic nervous system (ANS), initi­
ating the ”fight or flight” response. Physiologically, this involves an increased heart rate,
increased oxygen consumption and an intensification of the senses, preparing the body
to overcome immediate threats [2].
In contrast, the parasympathetic nervous system facilitates ”rest and digest” [3], promoting
calmness, slower heart rate and muscle relaxation. Emotionally, anxiety is often described
using a model with a linear combination of two fundamental neurophysiological systems
[4], comparing the dimensions of valence (pleasant­unpleasant) and arousal (activation­
deactivation). It means valence describes a sensation’s pleasantness. At the same time,
arousal is related to the intensity of a cognitive stimulus, being passive or active. The
Circumplex model in Figure 2.1 below helps to categorize and understand emotions, em­
phasizing the need for a nuanced view, especially when dealing with anxious children.
Figure 2.1: The Circumplex model with arousal as the vertical axis and valence as the
horizontal axis.[5]

In this model, fear and anxiety are predominantly situated in the space of low valence,
high arousal, and avoidance motivation, which highlights the fact that these emotional
states are very complex and nuanced in the context of pediatric healthcare. Therefore,
the circumplex model is insufficient to reflect, for example, the behaviour of an anxious
child. In that case, the self­assessment manikin (SAM) [6] is often used, which adds a
third dimension related to dominance to distinguish between anger and fear, which are in
the same category in the figure above but are concepts that need to be treated differently.
It can be seen in Figure 2.2 below.
Figure 2.2: The Self­Assessment Manikin (SAM). [7]
The valence dimension corresponds to the happy­unhappy scale and the arousal with the
excited­to­calm scale. Hence, the dominance dimension distinguishes between feeling
controlled or in control of the situation. This third dimension underlines the need for a
more diverse and qualitative view of emotions. This concern will be addressed during this
study.
Anxiety can manifest in various forms, as the psychologist Charles D. Spielberg differen­
tiates between two types: trait anxiety and state anxiety [8] [9]. Distinguishing the two
of them is crucial. State anxiety is situational and temporary, often triggered by specific
events, such as a hospital visit, a job interview, or fear­triggering scenarios. Trait anxiety,
on the other hand, reflects a person’s baseline susceptibility to anxiety. In conclusion,
people with higher trait anxiety will be more sensitive and, therefore, more likely to expe­
rience state anxiety [10]. This project exclusively focuses on state anxiety, aligning with
its emphasis on children’s situational distress in the critical PED setting.
Given the project’s emphasis on the momentary distress experienced by children, the term
”anxiety” will specifically denote state anxiety. Furthermore, anxiety, fear, and distress are
used interchangeably in this study due to their closely related physiological implications.

2.2
Methods for Measuring Anxiety
This section is intended to provide an overview of the different methods available for
measuring and quantifying anxiety, fear, and stress. The first part will present the chal­
lenging task of measuring this psychological state, listing the different aspects to consider
when identifying it. Secondly, a review of the different methods available, both at an ob­
servational level conducted by third parties and the self­analysis of the situation, will be
presented. This will be followed by a discussion of the use of biometric data for this type
of measure, its potential, and the research conducted using this technology.
2.2.1
Quantifying Anxiety: The Challenge
Measuring anxiety is difficult due to the inherent subjectivity associated with the mea­
surement of emotional states, particularly in young children. They often lack the verbal
and cognitive abilities to articulate their emotions explicitly, relying on non­verbal signs
and different behaviours as their primary mode of communication. Consequently, relying
solely on self­report measures is somewhat impractical, and alternative approaches are
needed to understand anxiety in this age range. Research recognizes the complexity of
measuring anxiety in young children, emphasizing the need for a multi­method assess­
ment approach that considers a variety of sources of information, including behavioural
observations, physiological indicators, and caregiver reports [11]. Behavioural observa­
tions, such as changes in facial expressions, body language, and activity levels, serve as
valuable indicators of anxiety in non­verbal children [12].
In addition, physiological measures, such as heart rate and cortisol levels, provide objec­
tive information about the child’s stress response [13]. The subjective nature of anxiety
assessment is enhanced by the fact that children may express their distress differently,
ranging from withdrawal and avoidance to increased attachment or irritability [14], making
it difficult to establish a universal set of criteria for identifying anxious behaviours [15].
As indicated, the concept of anxiety is inherently subjective and may initially appear chal­
lenging to quantify. Nevertheless, numerous studies and methodologies have explored
this possibility. Anxiety measurements commonly fall within two categories: biometrics
and subjective human assessment [16]. The bio­metric category measures human phys­
iological responses captured through sensors [17]. On the other hand, the subjective
measures are based on human assessments, including:
• Patients Direct Assessment: Patients report their anxiety levels themselves.
• Indirect Assessment: Another individual, typically a parent/caregiver or nurse, re­
ports the patient’s anxiety on their behalf.
• Observational reporting: external individuals, often trained experts or researchers,
report the patient’s anxiety.
The biometric method relies on the activation of the ANS, but it is mainly involuntary, mak­
ing it challenging to control easily [17]. However, this report holds onto the potential of
the biometric assessment of anxiety, which offers the possibility of mitigating biases intro­
duced by social factors and cultural norms compared to measurements involving human
interactions [18].
In this following section, the different methods to measure anxiety will be presented, as
well as a deeper look into the potential of biometric data and its presence in hospital
environments.

2.2.2
Self­reporting methods
Although nurses and doctors are highly skilled in healthcare, patients also play a vital
role in ensuring that care procedures run smoothly. With the help of autonomous mea­
surement methods, medical procedures can be conducted with empathy and a deep un­
derstanding of the patient’s emotions. This is particularly important when treating small
children who may have difficulty expressing their feelings in words. By utilizing visual aids,
healthcare professionals can effectively communicate with young patients, making them
feel more comfortable during stressful situations in pediatric emergency rooms.
In the ”Understanding Anxiety” section of this chapter, we discussed the Self­Assessment
Manikin (SAM) method, which is a very visual way of identifying a patient’s emotions.
However, there are other techniques available, such as the ones listed below:
• STAIC
The State­Trait Anxiety Inventory­Children (STAI­C) is a commonly used self­report
measurement of state anxiety for children. It is referred to as the “gold standard” [19]
and is used and mentioned as a validated method in many studies [20][21][22][23].
Its value has also reached different borders, translated into Turkish, Italian, and
Chinese. There are also versions of this method, the STAI­P intended for parents’
assessment of children’s status.
It consists of 40 items that measure two separate aspects of anxiety: state anxiety,
a person’s current level of anxiety, and trait anxiety, which is a person’s overall level
of anxiety. It consists of answering all the questions in the questionnaire, asking for
ratings of agreement on a 3­point scale using the stem “I feel…” where one must
choose an answer out of 3, depending on the intensity with which you experience
anxiety, that moment. Then, all the scores are added up; the higher the score, the
higher the anxiety level.
However, the STAI­C is lengthy, unsuitable for use in the PED, and cannot be used
in younger and non­verbal children [24][25]. Therefore, it has limited use in the fast­
paced PED where interventions should be initiated quickly, and on top of all, it has
not been used in children younger than five years old [26], so part of our target (4­
7) is discarded due to them not being able to read the questions correctly, and not
understanding the task altogether. Although a reduced version has been created,
with only six items (STAIC­SF), it still needs to be revised for children who cannot
read since the objective of the self­assessment is for the child to be able to evaluate
his state.
• STOA
The STOA method, a measurement tool targeting surgery­linked anxiety, comprises
20 questions, with 10 assessing trait anxiety and the rest evaluating immediate anxi­
ety levels [27]. Each of the ten questions addressing state anxiety involves affective
and cognitive dimensions, utilizing a four­point scale ranging from 0 (”not at all”) to
3 (”very much so”). A cumulative score ranging from 0 to 30 portrays the intensity
of state anxiety, with higher scores indicating elevated anxiety levels.
Several studies have confirmed that the STOA questionnaire is a reliable and valid
tool for measuring children’s state anxiety [28]. It has been considered a trustworthy
questionnaire for assessing surgery­related anxiety because of its consistency and
ability to measure constructs [27].

However, the STOA questionnaire is primarily focused on surgical fears, which could
limit its usefulness in the specific context of the preoperative setting at Herlev Hos­
pital PED. Additionally, some concerns have been raised about children’s ability to
understand certain items on the questionnaire, particularly concepts like ”restriction
of freedom” or ”being at the mercy of others.” Moreover, since our target audience
lacks the cognitive development to complete the questionnaire properly, it is not
suitable for them.
• CPMAS
The Children’s Perioperative Multidimensional Anxiety Scale (CPMAS) employs a
five­item visual Likert scale; each question has a self­reported VAS measurement
where a child selects a point on a 100 mm line (from “not at all worried” to “very
worried”) (see Figure 2.3). Participants respond to these Likert­scale questions, and
the cumulative score, ranging from 0 to 500, indicates the child’s state of anxiety,
with higher scores reflecting elevated anxiety levels.
Figure 2.3: The CPMAS. [20]
The collaborative effort of developmental personality psychologists, anesthesiolo­
gists, psychiatrists, and clinical psychologists led to the creation of CPMAS. Unlike
conventional one­item Likert rankings, this multidimensional scale addresses the
complexity of a child’s anxiety by incorporating multiple questions, aiming for en­
hanced depth [20].
While recognizing the time constraints in a bustling hospital environment, the scale
emphasizes the importance of facilitating concise yet accurate communication of
children’s feelings. While the scale demonstrates robust internal consistency, mod­
erate test­retest reliability, and convergent validity, it is essential to note that its

2.2.5
Related work of evaluating anxiety with biometric systems
The significance of recognizing anxiety within clinical environments is pivotal for ensuring
the highest standard of care, with biometric technologies providing crucial insights into
patients’ emotional conditions. A blend of neurophysiological and physiological indicators
forms a robust basis for analyzing anxiety levels.
Neurophysiological Indicators
• Skin Conductance Activity (SCA)
SCA assesses variations in skin conductance linked to sweat gland activity, indica­
tive of the sympathetic nervous system’s operation. It acts as a portal to gauge the
arousal aspect of emotions, facilitating the evaluation of autonomic reactions. This
method tracks the skin’s electrical resistance alterations triggered by sweat secre­
tion, known as the electrodermal response. It is also known under the term gal­
vanic skin response (GSR), which includes skin conductance level and response as
metrics. SCA mirrors the influence of sympathetic neural activity on sweat glands,
marking its utility in psychological investigations as a marker for stress and anxi­
ety. Despite its effectiveness in reflecting emotional arousal and stress responses,
SCA’s sensitivity varies with age, showing limited efficacy in children due to their
lower sweat production rates [60]. Research indicates insignificant SCA variation in
children with and without psychological conditions like autism under certain stimuli,
challenging its applicability for anxiety detection in younger populations.

• Heart Rate (HR) and Heart Rate Variability (HRV)
Techniques such as ECG and Photoplethysmography (PPG) extract HR and HRV

2.3
Anxiety in the Pediatric Emergency Department (PED)
In pediatric emergency departments (PEDs), the reported prevalence of anxiety and fear
among children is remarkably high. According to a 2009 study, approximately 90% of
children aged 4­6 years showed fear related to various aspects of the hospital environment
[67]. These fears were mainly centred on concerns related to nursing interventions, fear of
assuming the role of a patient, and anxieties derived from the child’s developmental stage.
Contributing factors included the unfamiliar hospital environment and lack of information,
and children with previous hospital experiences showed higher levels of fear.
The implications of children’s anxious behaviour in the PED are considerable. They can
have significant consequences for the patient and the surrounding environment. Anxiety
episodes can delay crucial medical treatments, decreasing patient satisfaction [68]. Due
to children’s limited cognitive development level, communication often manifests through
behaviour rather than verbal expression. Consequently, treatment of an anxious child may
evoke withdrawal, regression, uncooperativeness or even aggression [69] [70], including
actions such as kicking or pushing to resist medical procedures. Unfortunately, treating
a child in such a state may prolong the duration of medical procedures, subsequently
delaying the treatment of other patients in the PED [71].
In cases where a child cannot be tranquillised for medical procedures, nurses may ad­
minister pre­operative sedatives. A study in Korea found that high levels of anxiety and
prolonged waiting times contributed to the need to administer pre­operative sedatives to
children [72]. This practice introduces a loop of prolonged waiting times and traumatic
experiences for the patient.
Moreover, as one can read in the following section, anxiety is directly connected to the
ability to feel pain on a higher or lower scale, altering the actual perception of it. Research
indicates that children with elevated levels of state anxiety tend to form more negative
memories of pain [73]. In turn, this can lead to distorted or exaggerated memories of
painful experiences, which influences the child’s future coping mechanisms in potentially
painful situations. These negative experiences may persist into adulthood, leading the
child to avoid similar medical procedures or situations.
In the worst case, there is not enough time to create a comfortable environment for the
child. In such cases, healthcare professionals may have to restrain the child and perform
the procedure against his or her will, which may cause trauma and complicate future treat­
ments. Therefore, mitigating children’s anxiety in the PED is a fundamental imperative to
improve and ensure a satisfactory patient experience, treatment quality and efficacy.

2.5.1
Eye Tracking and Anxiety
According to the research, eye­tracking technology has been identified as a helpful tool
for investigating cognitive and emotional brain activation [78]. It offers insights into atten­
tion, cognitive processing, and emotional arousal [79]. Significant clues can be obtained
regarding an individual’s anxiety, fear, or stress levels by examining pupil size, eye move­
ment patterns, and blink frequency.
Pupillary dilation is associated with the body’s alert state, activating the ”fight” response
as part of our survival mechanism [79]. However, unique chemical configurations in indi­
viduals or specific medications may alter pupil behaviour, causing contractions instead of
dilations in some instances.
Eye tracking is considered crucial in measuring anxiety levels by examining the vigilance
hypothesis and the maintenance hypothesis [80]. These hypotheses explain how indi­
viduals with anxiety tend to orient their attention towards threats, which influences their
emotional distress regulation and involuntary processing. The vigilance hypothesis sug­
gests that those with anxiety disorders can easily detect threats and, thus, tend to focus
on them frequently. In contrast, the maintenance hypothesis focuses on intentional at­
tention and suggests that people can focus on specific things based on their experience.
Unlike the vigilance hypothesis, which predicts increased attention to threatening stim­
uli, the maintenance hypothesis predicts that individuals with anxiety may have difficulty
shifting their attention away from threats once detected. This means that individuals with
anxiety may struggle to redirect their focus from something perceived as threatening [81].
Recent doubts about the reliability of methods like the dot­probe task have led to increased
reliance on eye­tracking tools [82]. Eye tracking, with its continuous recordings, provides a
more direct measure of attention, less influenced by confounding processes. Eye tracking
not only focuses on usability but also on measuring anxiety levels effectively [82]. Its less
expensive nature than other methods like fMRI and electrophysiology makes it a potent yet

2.5.2
Exploring Emotional Spectrum: Uncovering Anxiety and Fear
through Eye Tracking
This project is primarily about leveraging eye­tracking technology to understand and mea­
sure emotional responses to anxiety triggers, which also lead to other emotions such as
sadness, anger or fear. Although we are not directly focused on analysing the differences
between fear and anxiety, it is vital to recognise and understand these distinctions, as
they provide valuable context for our research findings.
Given the potential of eye tracking to measure emotional responses, an intriguing question
arises:
Can we distinguish fear from anxiety in the eyes?
According to theoretical definitions, anxiety is an emotional response that anticipates un­
pleasant sensations, activates the sympathetic nervous system, and triggers physical
stress reactions [89]. On the contrary, fear is externally triggered and linked to an ad­
verse stimulus’s presence or imminent arrival. While both are alarm signals, they prepare
the body for distinct responses. Anxiety is a more prolonged state, resulting from a sus­
tained expectation that the adverse event is likely to occur [90].
Although anxiety and fear represent potentially distinct emotional states, there may be
some overlap in the underlying brain and behavioural mechanisms. In the quest to try
to separate these two emotions, it has been found that both fear and anxiety are used
interchangeably when addressing this issue. Some argue that anxiety may be a more
intricate form of fear, providing individuals with a greater capacity for adaptation and future
planning. Furthermore, both activate the amygdala. The amygdala is a region of the
brain involved in emotional response and forming emotional memories, especially those
related to fear. When we perceive a threat or fearful stimulus, the amygdala is activated,
triggering physiological and emotional responses, such as the ”fight or flight” response, to
prepare the body for action [91]. Another consequence of activating the ”fight” mode is the
release of cortisol, the stress hormone. This hormonal and neural cascade also influences
pupil dilation, highlighting the intricate connection between our emotional responses, brain
chemistry and physiological functions [92].
To answer the question posed at the beginning of this section, it is not entirely clear
whether it is necessary to differentiate between fear and anxiety because they have practi­
cally the same effect on the brain at chemical levels and, therefore, in the eye behaviour.
However, in this study, the aim is to develop a child­friendly tool to quantify the nega­
tive emotions experienced by children in emergency rooms, including fear, anxiety, and
stress. To achieve our goal of improving the hospital experience for children, we will refer

To better understand eye­tracking technology, it is essential to know the different types of

• Remote eye tracking systems: These utilise cameras to track eye movement from
a distance without requiring direct eye contact.
• Head­mounted eye tracking systems: These systems are worn on the head and

• Wearable eye tracking systems: These are integrated into devices like glasses or

• Desk­mounted eye tracking systems: These are typically connected to a com­

• Mobile eye­tracking systems: These portable solutions track eye movements in

Remote eye trackers
Eye­tracking systems that operate remotely employ a camera to track eye movements
without requiring the participant to have direct contact. The camera captures eye move­
ments from a distance and adjusts to compensate for head movements. Such systems
evaluate eye position and head orientation by analyzing the pupil center and cornea re­
flection. Typically, the camera is situated beneath the computer screen, which can pre­
cisely map eye movements onto the calibration plane. The head box is a well­defined
operational region, and any deviation from it can disrupt the tracking process. Remote
experiments are frequently conducted, where gaze data is solely collected for the com­
puter screen.
Remote eye­tracking systems have several advantages, including:
1. Participants can work with a computer seamlessly while the eye tracking system op­
erates. That is useful in usability testing, human behaviour psychology experiments,
vision studies, and screen­based market research.
2. Remote systems are non­intrusive and the only option for investigating infant or
neuro­compromised populations. This technology also enables individuals with quadriple­
gia or locked­in syndrome to communicate through eye movements.
3. Remote systems work well with other research technologies, including EEG, NIRs,
and biosignals. These systems are mainly binocular, with some capable of measur­
ing or accommodating vergence, enhancing accuracy across varying depths.

differentiate between past and future and engage in make­believe. However, their think­
ing is still intuitive and not entirely logical. Complex concepts like cause and effect, time,
and comparison remain challenging for them to grasp.

The foundation of a child­friendly design lies in its ergonomics and comfort. Children

7.1
Has the problem statement been answered?
Assessing emotions, especially in children, is a complex task marked by innate biases
and varying perspectives among nurses, parents, and the children themselves. This com­
plexity extends to physiological evaluation methods and algorithms, often influenced by
subjective self­assessments, societal norms, and measurement content. While portable
biometric systems, as reviewed, exhibit accuracies above 70%, contextual factors in stud­

Scientific support garnered during the research phase validates the feasibility of leverag­
ing eye­tracking technology as a complementary tool for measuring anxiety, fear, or stress
in children. However, introducing a novel element into the delicate hospital environment,
particularly one unfamiliar to children, presents significant challenges. Success hinges
on collaborative efforts between patients and healthcare professionals, underscoring the
need for an attractively designed tool for children.
The guiding problem statement, ”How might we design a pair of eye­tracking glasses
that are children­friendly and useful in hospital settings?” prompts a comprehensive
exploration. Commencing with an understanding of the root problem—children’s anxiety
in hospital environments—the project aimed to construct a solution rooted in empathy and
user­centric design principles.
Empathy, gained through insights into the emotions prevalent in emergency rooms, laid
the groundwork for a child­friendly design. Ideation principles shaped the success criteria,
including safety, comfort, emotional resonance, and positive interaction. The resulting
design, as showcased in the ideation phase, met these requirements, integrating playful
elements and innovative aesthetics.
Testing prototypes at Herlev Hospital provided valuable user feedback, revealing positive
responses across aesthetic appeal, ergonomics, comfort, safety, and engaging experi­
ences. Children’s varied initial reactions converged into widespread acceptance, with
some even forming strong connections with the product.
Reflecting on the initial problem statement, the meticulous design process, attention to
user needs, functional and ergonomic requirements, and behavioural observations pre­
dominantly addressed the question. The potential for introducing eye­tracking technology
into hospitals as a tool to alleviate children’s anxiety is quite evident and feasible. Despite
the need for further development, if eye­tracking technology elements are incorporated
into the glasses, they can become more than just child­friendly elements and transform

Limitations
Although the project mainly yielded positive results, some limitations need to be ad­

1. Sample Size:The project’s testing phase involved a relatively small number of par­

cameras. Future iterations should address this limitation by incorporating these
elements. Holistically evaluating the glasses’ functionality, including their technical
aspects, is essential for a comprehensive assessment of their potential impact in
real­world hospital settings.

Conclusion
The project was focused on addressing anxiety in children during hospital visits, particu­
larly in the emergency room. The goal was to develop eye­tracking glasses designed for
kids aged 4 to 7. These glasses were inspired by Pupil Labs’ ”Core” eye­tracking glasses
but were redesigned to make them more appealing to children and more effective at cap­
turing their attention.
The project involved creating functional prototypes and undergoing rigorous eye­tracking
precision and usability testing. Cutting­edge technology, 3D printing facilities at DTU, and
collaboration with healthcare professionals at Herlev Hospital provided a solid foundation
for this initiative.
The primary objective was to design frames that not only met technical specifications but
also appealed to children. Through extensive analysis, the glasses’ best shape, appear­
ance, and fit were determined to make them child­friendly. The prototypes were tested in
real­life scenarios, directly in the emergency room at Herlev Hospital, with actual patients
undergoing medical situations. The results were fruitful, which showed that a successful
user­oriented design process was carried out, listening well to the needs and wishes of
the users.
This project went beyond conventional design endeavours. It was about understanding
the emotional needs of young patients and crafting an experience that met those needs.
While it is true that the project focused on redesigning glass frames, its core objective was
to create functional frames and redefine the narrative of pediatric healthcare. The project
also wishes to contribute to a paradigm shift in pediatric healthcare solutions, creating an
empathetic and practical environment to reduce anxiety in the long run.
