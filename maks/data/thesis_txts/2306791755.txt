ABSTRACT  
 
European eels has suffered a drastic decline in population, with an estimated~90% reduction 
since the 1970s. Global warming and other anthropogenic affects have played a large role, along 
with wide consumer demand. If demand is to be sated in a sustainable manner, it is necessary 
for the industry to close the life cycle and, ultimately farm the European eel. Eel early life stages 
(eggs and larvae) are characterised by low hatching success and high mortality. It is thought 
that rearing them far from their natural environment, where they are presented with unnatural 
environmental and biological conditions, is the primary cause of this high mortality. To 
determine the effects of some of these conditions on rearing success, the effects of temperature 
and rearing regime were investigated during the early life history of laboratory raised European 
eels. 
To obtain the optimal temperature range for rearing European eel larvae, the egg quality and, 
egg viability as well as hatching success were evaluated at five temperatures (16, 18, 20, 22 and 
24°C). Time to hatch was shortened at higher temperatures; > 32 hours at 22°C and > 64 hours 
at 16°C. The occurrence of larval deformities was also evaluated, but at four temperatures (16, 
18, 20, and 22°C) as no egg hatched when incubated at 24°C. Interestingly, hatching success 
did not reveal any significant difference among 22°C and 24°C. Furthermore, there was no 
difference among the lower and intermediate temperatures, 16, 18 and 20°C. Egg viability was 
the same among 16, 18 20 and 22, until 24hpf (hours post fertilization), then eggs at 22°C 
showed lower viability count. The higher temperatures, 22°C to 24°C can be considered 
suboptimal, due to their negative impact on egg viability, hatching success, and larval 
deformities. Some deformities were observed at all temperatures, where edema of the 
pericardial cavity was frequent (< 98.15 ± 0.96%) at all temperatures, while abnormal 
neurocranium (12.57<X<31.98 ± 10.92%) and jaw deformity (25.62<X<40.61 ± 9.43%), were 
moderately low in frequency suggesting that other influences were at fault. Four out of seven 
deformities were temperature dependent: spinal cord deformity, brachymorphic, tail deformity, 
and emaciation, which increased significantly at 22°C, while 16°C and 18°C had the lowest 
frequency of larvae deformities. 
 
 
To investigate the effects of disinfection regimes on hatching success, mortality and larval 
survival, ten disinfection regimes were applied in triplicate. Two concentrations of hydrogen 
peroxide (0.6% and 0.8%) were used to disinfect embryos at 15 hpf, 30 hpf and 15 + 30 hpf. 
Results indicated that hatching success and larval survival were positively related to disinfectant 
==PAGE:4==
 
3 
 
concertation and application rate (0.6% at both 15 and 30 hpf), where best results were achieved 
with 0.8% concentration applied at both 15 and 30hpf. Furthermore larval survival was better 
when disinfection was done later, i.e. at 30hpf with 0.8% concentration. To determine whether 
handling affected mortality or hatching success, a zero group was applied. Here eggs received 
the same treatment as disinfected eggs but with no disinfectant, i.e. same water as the incubation 
water. Results on both mortality and hatching success indicate that handling was not a 
dependent factor.  
Overall, thesis studies indicate that incubation and rearing temperature should be kept within 
16 to 20°C for best hatching success and lowest larvae deformity and disinfection should 
incorporated as part of good farming practises to improve hatching success and larval survival.

1. INTRODUCTION: EUROPEAN EEL  
 
The European eel (Anguilla anguilla) is a catadromous teleost species, meaning they spawn in 
seawater but live most of their life in freshwater. The European eel is one of the most diverse 
fish species with respect to their habitat preference, as they can be found in both marine and 
freshwater ecosystems, including lakes, ponds, marshes, rivers and estuaries, over most of 
Europe, as well as in the Atlantic Ocean and Mediterranean Sea (Bouchereau et al., 2009). 
Figure 1 represents their habitat in red, and spawning grounds are noted in the Sargasso Sea, 
with larvae size in blue numbers.  
 
Figure 1. Spawning site of the European Eel (Anguilla anguilla) is shown in the Sargasso Sea, the blue lines represent the 
oceanic drift of the larvae, and the numbers, its size. The red area is its continental distribution (Curd, 2010) 
 
1.1.IMPORTANCE: AQUACULTURE AND CONSERVATION 
The European eel is considered a tasty dish by many, but due to its wide range of habitat 
preferences, and high trophic level it is also important for ecosystems. Eels have been 
documented countless times in ancient literature and still today they are important enough to be 
written about. Recently, the Guardian published a news coverage about the European eel and 
reasons for its decline (Bryce, 2016). Eels remain a popular food product for many, especially 
in Asia, despite EU regulations banning the export of European caught eels. The European eel 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
 
is illegally traded on black-markets, and kilo of glass eels (~3000 individuals) can be sold for a 
hefty price or 1300 euros (Bryce, 2016). European eel farmers rely on wild caught eels, which 
like most species collected from nature can be highly unpredictable. The life cycle for European 
eel is far from being optimised in captivity especially when compared to other farmed species, 
such as salmon (salmo salar) or Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua). Over 2 million tonnes of farmed 
Atlantic salmon were produced worldwide (2014), where advanced rearing techniques and 
genetic manipulation are part of the efficiency and effectiveness of today’s salmon farming 
(FAO, 2004).  
 
Figure 2. Total production of European eel in thousands of tonnes as reported by the FAO, 1950-2010 (wikipedia contributor, 
2016). 
On the contrary, European eel farming relies on wild-caught glass eels, which is not only 
unreliable but also unsustainable (Horne and Birnie 2001). Furthermore, their early life stages 
are characterised by high mortality rate, where routine use of antibiotics further challenges the 
road to sustainable production (Sørensen et al., 2014a).  The lack of knowledge regarding the 
natural early life history of the eel makes it difficult for farmers to imitate their natural 
environment. Eels are reared under suboptimal environmental conditions where low hatching 
success, high mortalities and high deformities are common during early life stage (Kurokawa 
et al, 2008). Morphological abnormalities during early life stages can be a big obstacle and a 
bottleneck for aquaculture hatcheries, where high percentages of produced juveniles have 
skeletal deformities. Skeletal deformities not only affect the efficiency of the production, where 
abnormalities affect larval survival, but they can also be a quality issue (Georgakopoulou et al., 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
2010; Ahn et al., 2012). Moreover, the preferred dietary regime for eel larvae (leptocephalus 
larvae) is still under debate and the fertilization protocol was just recently standardized (Butts 
el al., 2014) and early ontogeny described (Sørensen et al., 2016). Despite these obstacles silver 
eels are still in high demand due to their nutritious meat, with a high fat content, up to 28%, 
giving them a high calorie value, and tasty texture (Horne and Birnie 2001). Silver eels are 
caught with baited traps, nets etc., but today more are farmed than caught from the wild. Today 
it is more common to catch eels during the glass eel stage and raise them in farms. Glass eels 
move upstream where they settle after a long journey in the sea, as larvae and finally glass eels. 
Global wild caught production in 2013 was around 3800 tonnes compared to the farmed, 
approximately 4400 tonnes (Horne and Birnie 2001). Figure 2 shows a clear picture of this 
development, where farming overthrew the wild caught eels in the 1990’s and has been steadily 
increasing since then. Hopefully very soon the eel industry will not have to rely on wild caught 
eels, and a team of scientist are bringing this hope to reality. The Japanese eel (Anguilla 
japonica) is a close relative to the European Eel, where research and farming facilities in Asia 
have had substantially more success in farming practices, as the life cycle has been closed in 
captivity for this species (Tanaka, 2015). Moreover, in 2010 a second generation was 
successfully bred in captivity, and this was achieved with induced maturation (hormone 
injections), and artificial insemination (Tomkiewicz, 2012; reviewed by Okamura et al., 2013; 
Tanaka, 2015).  
Eels are not only important for economical gain and farming, intrinsic values in relation to 
biodiversity can arguably be held at a higher level in the long run. Eels are positioned at high 
tropic levels, living mainly as a carnivorous species feeding on smaller fishes, crustaceans, 
molluscs and amphipods. They can alter their diet to the food sources that are readily available. 
Often this is directly related to seasonal regimes and/or geographical area. Due to their high 
tropic level they are used as a bio indicator for ecosystem health (Bouchereau et al., 2009).  
Governments and non-governmental organizations have acknowledged the importance of the 
European eel and today there are numerous conservation plans being applied (Jacoby & 
Gollock, 2014; Bryce, 2016).

1.2.THREATS 
Climate change challenges life on earth from many different perspectives, where species are 
forced to adapt to new and constantly changing environments. Marine fauna is especially 
vulnerable due to its specialised thermal dependency. Ectothermal organisms are even more 
affected by temperature fluctuations as their biological and physiological processes are directly 
affected by water temperature (Pörtner et al., 2006, Pörtner and Peck, 2014). Many species are 
already living at the extremes of their thermal window. Furthermore, oceanic temperatures 
affect numerous water parameters, due to interactions between temperature and carbon dioxide 
acidification, hypoxia, and pH (among others). Driving fish species to higher and lower 
latitudes as a consequence of warming and cooling of the ocean, ultimately results in 
distributional changes on large geographical scales (Pörtner and Peck, 2014). The European eel 
is no exception. In 2008 an extensive study was published, which shows high correlation 
between oceanic temperature shifts and glass eel recruitment (Bonhommeau, et al., 2008). The 
study concluded that warming temperatures have a negative effect on eel recruitment, while 
cold phases are linked to higher recruitment. Bonhommeau et al. (2008) searched databases on 
eel recruitment stretching back to 1970s, alongside with data regarding food availability in 
relation to temperature fluctuations (from the Bermuda Biological Station for Research). At 
first their results were not significant, meaning that high food availability did not affect eel 
recruitment, but when a 3-year lag time is applied, a significant correlation is seen. Suggesting 
that the transatlantic migration, metamorphosis and freshwater arrival can take the larvae years. 
These results further highlight the early life history of the eel, e.g. its long term effects due to 
temperature changes (Bonhommeau, et al., 2008).  
Unfortunately, climate change is not their only threat, as habitat destruction and human 
pollution might have a bigger impact. For example, parasites, virus infections, and diseases 
have been found in eels all over Europe, some more threatening than others (Münderle, Sures, 
Taraschewski, 2004). The nematode Anguillicola crassus is thought to be one major reason for 
the collapse of the European Eel worldwide during the 1980s; a parasite which resides 
specifically in eels swim bladder and has been thought to have detrimental impacts on silver 
eels. Heavily infested eels are thought to expend more energy, causing them to arrive late or 
not at the spawning site (Münderle, Sures, Taraschewski, 2004).  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
 
As a consequence, the European eel is listed as a critically endangered species on IUCN Red 
List. Population collapse and further decline can also be partly explained through the synergy 
of some obvious reasons, such as anthropogenic pollution, habitat destruction, and overfishing. 
More detailed knowledge is required to fully understand the interplay of the diverse reasons for 
its decline. The IUCN Red List criteria is also difficult to apply to the European eel, as it has 
only one reproductive event in its entire life, where details regarding its many life stages still 
remain unknown. Ideally the IUCN Red List is applied to one or two life stages for the discussed 
species, commonly staged are early life stages and juvenile stage, but due lack of knowledge 
regarding each life stage of the European Eel, several stages fall under the IUCN Red List 
criteria (Jacoby and Gollock, 2014).

1.3.LIFE CYCLE 
 
Figure 3. Life cycle of the European eel (Anguilla anguilla) (Maes and Volckaert, 2007) 
 
The European eel has a complicated life cycle, travelling long distances at both the beginning 
of their life and the end (Durif et al., 2009). Ultimately, the eel’s life begins and ends in the 
Sargasso Sea, in middle of the Atlantic Ocean, with the fringes of the Bermuda triangle at the 
western edge of the sea (Stibane, 1983). This geographical location is somewhat appropriate as 
mystical stories have been associated with the Bermuda triangle for centuries, and the same can 
be said about the European eel; as no one has witnessed spawning and maturing eels in nature 
(Thillart et al., 2009). Eels hatch as yolk-sac larvae, and become leptocephalus larvae. 
Typically, they stay in this planktonic life phase longer than most fish larvae, and grow larger, 
particularly in body length (Bishop et al., 2000). During this unique stage they can be described 
as translucent and leaf-like, drifting with the ocean currents until they reach the continental 
shelf to undergo metamorphosis. Metamorphosis from leptocephalus larvae to glass eel occurs 
approximately 250 days after hatching, although they can be in the larvae phase for one to three 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
 
years (Horne and Birnie, 2001). Transformation to glass eel allows them to migrate from 
seawater to freshwater (Lafont et al., 2009). Glass eels are vulnerable to overfishing, as all eel 
farms rely on them for production, capturing them in coastlines, and rivers in Europe. Glass 
eels migrate from seawater to freshwater, developing into yellow benthic eels, and settle in 
freshwater until the onset of sexual maturity (Durif et al., 2009). Silvering is the second 
metamorphoses, silvering process is induced by lowering temperatures commonly during 
autumn, where maturating eels head towards sea for spawning. At this point they stop feeding 
and prepare for mitigation to sea. Swimming 6000 km for 6+ months, arriving late winter/early 
spring. Food abundance is also temperature dependant, where lower latitude larvae, such as 
those hatching in the Sargasso Sea, face a greater chance of starvation than those who hatch in 
higher latitudes (Houde, 1989). Lower latitudes have higher temperatures, and are often 
characterised by frequent spawning, as fishes try to make sure their offspring are hatching 
during an optimal window that is rich in available food resources with fewer competing rivals; 
as low latitude larvae have to consume up to three times more food than those at higher latitude 
(Houde, 1989). Paradoxically, the European eel has one reproductive episode during its lifetime 
with the hope of food abundance to ensure the viability of its offspring. This counterattacks 
starvation if the timing is good, but it still not characteristic of low latitude spawning fishes.

1.3.1. GENES 
Genetic structure of the European Eel has received some attention, through allozyme, 
genotyping and mitochondrial DNA markers (Dannewitz et al., 2009; Wirth and Bernatchez, 
2001; Maes and Volckaert, 2002; Pujolar et al., 2011). Reproduction of the European eel in 
nature has still not been witnessed, and the European eel has long been a paradigm for a 
panmixia species. Panmixia is characterised by random mating. For fishes this entails many 
females laying eggs at the same location, followed by many males releasing sperm over the 
eggs. Panmixia reproduction does not assume there is mate choice, and each individual within 
the whole breeding population is a possible mate, suggesting the whole European Eel 
population comes from the same breeding population. Three separate studies have been 
published regarding this subject and the conclusion is not unanimous. Dannewitz et al. (2009) 
conducted a comprehensive study which confirms the panmixia hypothesis. While another 
study shows increasing genetic differentiation with geographical distance, same study further 
concluded restricted gene flow, refuting non-random mating. The third study was conducted by 
Maes and Volckaert (2002), where they sampled at seven different locations and identified 
genetic differentiation within geographical areas. Overall, different research methods were 
applied, hence different results may be anticipated and are apparent. Independent of previously 
mentioned studies genetic difference is adequate, and the alleles are diverse, and some appear 
to be rare (Pujolar et al., 2011), giving hope that with current and more progressive conservation 
and quota management, the species will recover. Furthermore, survival of the fittest ensures 
natural selection, where the most genetically competent individuals survive.  Manis and 
Claussen (1985) were able to identify inter-population differences in relation to thermal 
tolerance. Environmental factors, such as geographical location that have different temperatures 
can affect the genetics. Meaning a population in the north hemisphere is more genetically 
competent to live in colder climate than more southern populations. Manis and Claussen (1985) 
studied Wood frog (Rana sylvatica), and if applied to the European eel with the panmixia 
hypothesis refuted, they might have lower chance of survival, because the leptocephali larvae 
drifts with the ocean currents, arriving at a random location. On the contrary, assuming the 
panmixa hypothesis is accurate, genetic incompatibility ensures the species survival as it is 
thought to be an influential factor for fertilization, and offspring survival. Suggesting that eggs 
from one female are more likely to be fertilized by a genetic compatible mate rather than 
incompatible (see, Nordeide, 2007 review). This is often referred to as “Sire-dam interactions”, 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 
where maternal and paternal haplotypes conflict with each other. High embryonic loss has been 
linked to egg and sperm carrying identical haplotypes (see, Nordeide, 2007 review).  
Laboratories and farms have only a sample of species population, and these individuals are not 
necessarily genetically compatible or with the best genes. They might be ill fitted to the set 
environment, i.e. better suited at lower/higher temperatures and, genetically incompatible. From 
these assumptions we can expect lower fertility, higher mortality rates and overall a bad 
production. These three concepts are all too familiar for European eel farmers, thus genetics 
and suboptimal rearing conditions should be investigated further to eradicate all bottlenecks.

1.4.EARLY LIFE STAGES  
Water temperature, microorganisms and other environmental parameters affect fish to a higher 
degree during early life stages (i.e. include eggs, larvae, and young juveniles) than later, where 
they face the highest natural mortality rates (Kroll et al., 2014). Alongside environmental 
factors the survival of an embryo is generally presumed to rely on genetic and non-genetic 
influences from parents, where contributing paternal and maternal factors are not necessarily 
evenly divided (Dahlke, 2011). 
1.4.1. SPAWNING AND FERTILIZATION 
It can be assumed that the female can lay 2 million to 10 million eggs at a time, or 3 million 
eggs per kilo of bodyweight (see Deelder review). It is presumed that females spawn eggs, then 
the males release sperm over the eggs. Activation of eel eggs requires a sperm cell and seawater, 
with fertilization dependent upon the salinity (Sørensen et al., 2015. Once activated the zygote 
starts to develop, through mitotic divisions. Whereas, unfertilized eggs might be at arrest in the 
metaphase (second step of meiosis), or a sperm did not find its way to the egg (Kinsey et al., 
2007). Sperm motility is a huge limiting factor for fertilization success, as the motility can vary 
from few seconds to couple of minutes (Penaranda et al., 2009). By mixing sperm from several 
male eels, and having a high sperm to egg ratio there is a greater chance of high fertilization 
(Butts et al., 2013; Nordeide, 2007). Furthermore, like most fish eggs they are poikilothermic, 
meaning hatching time is dependent upon water temperature (Pauly and Pullin, 1988). Here, 
increasing water temperature has been shown to have accelerating effects on embryonic 
development. This results in early hatching, which is followed by smaller larvae that, have 
lower survival chance due to predators, and decreased size also limits otherwise bigger food 
options (Bengtson et al., 1987).  The larvae are thought to hatch a couple of hundreds of meters 
above the spawning area, at warmer and more food abundant water layer. As fertilized eggs 
swell up and float up the water column (Sebert et al., 2009), found at a temperature stable water 
layer from 100-400 m deep, where temperature is 18-19°C (Haenen et al., 2009). High amount 
of spawned eggs is the species survival strategy to counterattack low fertility rate, high 
predation, and harsh environment during the early life stages. Low percentage will actually 
survive till hatch and even fewer through early life stages (Kroll et al., 2014).

1.4.3. EGG QUALITY, GENES & ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS 
Egg quality is the corner-stone for species survival, where environmental factors play a role 
alongside parental effects. Janhunen et al. (2010) investigated these interplaying factors for the 
salmonid species Arctic charr (Salvelinus alpinus), where they specifically investigated whether 
genetic traits have the ability to modify their response when under high levels of environmental 
stress. Here, they documented an increase in water temperature as a stressful time for 
developing embryos, and they further concluded that temperature can alter which genetic traits 
are predominant during early developmental stages. Optimal mate choice can affect the 
offspring survival significantly. For instance Dahlke (2011) showed that paternal effects were 
more important at unfavourable temperatures. While maternal effects are presumed to be 
predominant during early life stages (Rideout et al., 2004; Politis et al., 2014b), Janhunen et al. 
(2010) documented lower maternal genetic traits in larvae at higher temperatures. Maternal 
characteristics are also related to offspring survival, and these include age, spawning 
experience, and physiological conditions (Marsh-Matthews, 2005). Maternal size correlates to 
egg size (Ganias et al., 2015) and other physiological traits of farmed fish are highly dependent 
on feed, which can further translate to the biological composition of the egg (Furuita et al., 
2003). Long lasting and short-term stress on broodstock can also interfere with offspring 
survival (Mileva at al., 2011). The mother by natural instinct, always tries to ensure its offspring 
survival however nature is unpredictable, which might lead to bad quality eggs, therefore a high 
number of eggs are necessary to ensure the survival of some.  Eggs and larvae face many 
obstacles early on, and here maternally developed passive immunity can play a big role in their 
survival (Hansen and Olafsen, 1999). 
1.4.4. BACTERIA  
Eggs are not only affected by genes and temperature, their characteristic mucosal surface serves 
as good substrate for microbial colonization (Keskin and Rosenthal, 1993). There is a universal 
acceptance within the scientific community that a commensal relationship thrives between the 
indigenous microflora and opportunistic pathogens found on fish egg (Hansen and Olafsen, 
1989). This relationship can be disrupted in hatcheries, where the intensive commercial farming 
requires eggs in high densities which further promote a rapid increase in microorganism 
population (Keskin and Rosenthal, 1993). This new ratio of microflora can be a lot denser than 
the eggs natural environment, making their immune system weak in comparison (El-Dakour et 
al., 2013). Embryonic development and larvae survival can be affected by microflora, bacteria, 
==PAGE:21==
 
20 
 
and pathogenic viruses. Microorganisms can cause physical damage, and disrupt the chorion, 
while others such as Leucothrix mucor which is a common bacterium within the aquaculture 
industry, induces hypoxia with its hairy growth and ultimately hinders permeability of the 
chorion (Hansen and Olafsen, 1989). Low oxygen or hypoxia are known to cause precocious 
hatching (Czerkies et al., 2001). Bacteria does not only affect the eggs as they can also infect 
the larvae post-hatch. Larvae do no start feeding immediately although they start drinking prior 
to first feeding, ingesting substantial amounts of bacteria by drinking, thus affecting their 
digestive tract long before it is fully developed (Bergh et al., 1992). This is why it is important 
to rear larvae independently from eggs. Other counteracting measures are ensured with good 
farming practises such as ensuring good water quality through filtration, UV light and ozone 
(review Hansen and Olafsen, 1999). Disinfection of eggs is also practised, with use of various 
chemicals (El-Dakour et al., 2013). Commonly used chemicals include: glutaraldehyde, 
iodophor, hydrogen peroxide, formalin (Overton et al., 2010; El-Dakour et al., 2013). Early 
embryonic stage requires less oxygen, as some oxygen storage is found within the pervitelline 
fluid and in the yolk (review Hansen and Olafsen, 1999).  This suggests that disinfection is 
more necessary during later embryonic stages. Disinfection success can be measured with 
various methods, including; hatching success (Sørensen et al, 2014a), bacterial activity, and 
survival of larvae  (El-Dakour et al., 2013). These three measurements will all be applied in this 
study, with the addition of mortality rate, to evalute if mortality is linked to handling and/or 
disinfection. It is known that disinfection can lead to substantially better hatching success, 
where Sørensen et al. (2014a) reported almost double hatching success of the European eel, 
with the use of antibiotics, they further concluded that hydrogen peroxide effectively removes 
microorganisms. Hydrogen peroxide is commonly used for water disinfection in aquaculture 
and laboratory facilities (Bownik and Stepniewska, 2015). When used it should be kept at the 
lowest possible concentration as it is highly toxic to various organisms and is known to cause 
oxidative stress when introduced to invertebrates (i.e. Daphna magna, Bownik and 
Stepniewska, 2015). Concentration is also addressed here, where 0.6% and 0.8% concentration 
is used to investigate the lowest possible concentration for disinfection of European eel.

1.4.5. DETERMINING EGG QUALITY 
Egg quality can be determined through various tests and observations, where todays methods 
include: buoyancy, fertilization rate, hatching success, biochemical composition, blastomere 
morphology, distribution of oil droplets (Kjorvik et al., 1990; Mazorra et al., 2003; Furuita et 
al., 2003; Shields et al, 1997; Mansour et al., 2007). No method is applicable to all species, and 
none have gained universal acceptance, although some have gained more popularity throughout 
time, due to simplicity and concurrent technology. Egg size has been heavily investigated, and 
is thought to be a contributing factor for the overall survival of the egg and larvae (Rideout et 
al, 2004; Wotton, 1994; Pauly and Pullin, 1989). Bigger eggs develop more slowly, which 
prolongs a sensitive stage (Pauly and Pullin, 1989), but on the other hand they face fewer 
predators (Wotton, 1994). Buoyancy and egg size are easily studied and do not require 
expensive equipment, but with today’s technological advances scientists are linking cheap and 
easy techniques to more complex and expensive studies. For instance, a team of Japanese 
scientist (Seoka et al., 2003) have shown a correlation between buoyancy and chemical 
composition, for the Japanese Eel. Buoyancy or the pelagic nature of the eel eggs is also a 
positive characteristic for farmers, as the dead ones sink to the bottom and are easily removable 
from the incubators. Another positive characteristic of a fish egg is their transparency, 
 
which allows scientist/ farmers, to inexpensively observe their development and assess 
quality. To be able to determine egg quality as soon as possible is a huge economical gain for 
farmers cultivating fish (Avery et al., 2006) as it can be time consuming and expensive to 
incubate eggs, especially if the eggs are non-viable from the beginning. Blastomeres are the 
result of cellular division, which has gained increased attention, as research suggests that 
mitotic division is a good indicator for hatching success (Effer et al., 2013; Avery et al., 2008; 
Shields et al, 1997). Blastomeres or cell mitosis are visible with a microscope early on, for the 
European eel 2 hours after fertilization at 20°C. There are a number of studies which specifically 
focus on the early life stages, for instance blastomeres of the Atlantic salmon (S. salar) have 
been categorised by Effer et al. (2012) as follows:  
(1) Normal blastomeres 
(2) Blastomeres with dispersed margins 
(3) Unequal blastomeres 
(4) ‘pie-shaped’ blastomeres 
(5) Amorphous blastomeres 
(6) Three similar and one different blasomere 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
 
(7) Blastomere with eccentric cleavage 
These 7 categories are for the morphometric of two and four cell stage. Effer et al. (2012) study 
was isolated to morphometric of the blastomeres, e.g. length and width of the blastomeres. 
Results did not show any difference in mean length and width between the six abnormal groups. 
Furthermore they concluded that the asymmetry was not only due to size, but the positioning 
of the blastomere in the blastodisc. The qualitative factor; cell positioning, had previously been 
categorised by Shields et al. (1997), and later by Sørensen et al. (2016). Shields et al. (1997) 
result showed five blastomere morphology categories for the Atlantic Halibut, using eggs at 8 
cell stage. Categories are: asymmetrical cell positioning, unequal blastomeres, incomplete inter-
cell adhesion, cell margins poorly defined (blastomeres with dispersed margins), vacuolar 
inclusions between cells. An even more simplified categorisation has been published for the 
European eel. Here, Sørensen et al. (2016) examined cell cleavage pattern and salinity types 
and concluded that there are three types of cleavage patterns e.g.; regular and symmetric, mostly 
regular with minor irregularities, and irregular (Sørensen et al., 2016). Effer et al. (2012) and 
Shields et al. (1997) results do not show any significant difference between different types of 
blastomere abnormalities. Sørensen et al. (2016) study concludes that the number of irregular 
cleavage patterned eggs rises in contrast to salinity, and that “mostly regular cleavage pattern” 
where affected by salt types. Together, this suggests that this simple categorisation is enough 
as there is a documented difference between groups. Overall abnormal blastomeres are 
considered bad when estimating egg quality (Sørensen et al., 2016; Effer et al., 2012; Shields 
et al., 1997).

1.4.6. LARVAE 
The European eel larval stage is categorized into three main stages. Firstly, the larvae hatch as 
yolk-sac larvae that are completely dependent upon energy from the egg. In captivity the larvae 
stay in the yolk-sac stage for 12 to 20 days, before developing into pre-leptocephalus and finally 
metamorphosis into leptocephalus.  The first larvae stage, (i.e. yolk sac stage) is not dependent 
upon food, although its resources can be drained quickly as the yolk sac disappears with 
increasing activity. Simultaneously physiological changes are occurring, which include (among 
others): teeth forming, skull development, and jaws formation. At this stage they are classified 
as pre-leptocephali larvae and soon after they commence feeding (Bouilliart, 2015).  
 
Figure 5. Larvae at start feeding stage, or 10 days old, reared at 20°C. 
 
Finally, leptocephali larvae start feeding and in nature they move vertically into water layers, 
to more food abundant areas during day time and migrate downwards as the sun sets. (Haenen 
et al review, 2009). Some eels stay in the leptocephali stage for years, before they 
metamorphose into glass eels (Bonhommeau et al., 2008). Environmental factors affect species 
growth and survival, especially during embryogenesis and early life stages. All larvae stages 
can be affected by temperature, although the younger larvae are more vulnerable (Kurokawa et 
al., 2013). Larvae activity also entails sensitivity to oxygen uptake in contrast to temperature, 
as physiological stress from high temperatures increases metabolic processes, which demands 
higher rates of food consumption (Pörtner et al., 2006, Gadomsky and Cadell, 1991). Most 
aquatic species and vertebrates are highly sensitive to ambient temperatures, which makes them 
vulnerable to simple and complex body anomalies (Napiórkowska et al., 2016). For fish the 
most suitable water temperature is not necessarily the same for all life stages, and can vary from 
one developmental stage to the next (Kurokawa et al., 2008). Furthermore, water temperature 
not only affects embryonic development, but can also effect larvae growth rate and development 
(Georgakopoulou et al., 2010). Temperatures outside the thermal window, whether they are too 
low or too high are at risk to higher frequencies of deformities as shown for the Japanese eel 
(Kurokawa et al., 2008). Optimal water temperature and thermal tolerance for early life stages 
of eels have not been documented to its fullest. Studies show high number of farmed eel larvae 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
 
suffering from some kind of morphological deformity, or up to 50% (Kurokawa et al, 2008), 
although not all deformities appear to be fatal. Some have suggested by administrating 
hormones to induce maturation, there is a greater chance larvae will become deformed 
(Kurokawa et al, 2008; Okamura et al., 2007). The Japanese eel is vulnerable to pericardial 
edema at lower/higher temperatures, but it does not seem to hinder their chance of survival, as 
the larvae with this deformity were able to feed normally (Okamura et al., 2007).  A 20°C 
temperature regime has been used for both egg incubation and during yolk sac stage 
(Sørensen et al., 2016; Sørensen et al., 2015; Tomkiewicz, 2012).  A study by Politis et al. 
(2014b) further recommends light regimes: as continues darkness or low intensity light regimes.  
1.5.OBJECTIVES 
1.5.1. TEMPERATURE AND ITS EFFECT ON EARLY LIFE STAGES  
For this study, five incubation and rearing temperatures were chosen to investigate the effect of 
temperature on early life stages of the European eel. Eggs from three different families were 
used, and applied in statistical analysis. European eels have been documented to withhold rare 
alleles and high genetic comp 
lexity (Pujolar et al., 2011), which can be assumed to 
ensure countless future generations a place on earth. This is especially important as eels face 
many obstacles, where global warming is among them (Bonhommeau, et al., 2008). Global 
warming is causing fluctuations in oceanic temperatures, which affects ectothermal species 
tremendously (Pörtner and Peck, 2014). Furthermore early life stages are presumed to be more 
vulnerable to temperature changes than later stages (Kroll et al., 2014). Provoking interest to 
analyse temperature effect on the early life stages of the European Eel. This study evaluates the 
effect of temperature on egg quality, and larvae deformity, fertilization rate, hatching success.

2.1.1. BROODSTOCK MANAGEMENT AND GAMETE PRODUCTION 
Female and male eels were kept at the Danish Technical University facilities (55.407444 N: 
9.403414E) in 300 L tanks equipped with a re-circulation system (Tomkiewicz, 2012). Females

light (~20 lux), 12 h day/ 12 h night photoperiod, salinity of ~36 ppt, and temperature of 20°C. 
Acclimatization to seawater took place over 14 days. Before experimentation, eels were 
anaesthetized (ethyl p-aminobenzoate, 20 mg L-1; Sigma–Aldrich Chemie, Steinheim, 
Germany) and tagged with a passive integrated transponder (PIT tag).  
Females used for experiments (n = 4; hereafter f1-f4) had a mean (±SEM) standard length 
and body weight of 65 ± 4 cm and 486 ± 90 g, respectively. Females received weekly injections 
of salmon pituitary extract (Argent Chemical Laboratories, Washington, USA) at 18.75mg kg-

Within 12-14 h, females were stripped, and anaesthetized (with the same anesthesia as above) 
to tranquilize specimens.  
Male eels used in the experiment (n = 11) had a mean (±SEM) standard length and body 
weight of 40 ± 3 cm and 135 ± 25 g, respectively. Males were injected weekly with recombinant 
human chorionic gonadotropin (Ovitrelle, Madrid, Spain) at 1.5 IU g-1 fish (Gallego et al., 
2012). Before mixing of gametes, an additional injection was given and milt was obtained ~12 
h after administration of hormone. Milt samples were pipetted into an immobilizing medium, 
P1 (see Peñaranda et al., 2010) and used for fertilization within 4 h of collection (Butts et al., 
2014; Asturiano et al. 2016).

The experiment was conducted in 2015. Eggs from a single female were “crossed” with a sperm

2.4. STATISTICAL ANALYSES 
Raw data and tables were created, alongside with some basic processing, in the program 
Microsoft Excel 2013 for PC. Graph design was done in SigmaPlot 12.5 (Copyright © 2014 
Systat Software Inc). Statistical analysis were done using Statistical Analysis System (SAS 
Institute Inc. 2011. Base SAS® 9.3). All data were tested for normality (Shapiro-Wilk test) and 
homogenous variances (PROC g-plot). Alpha was set at 0.05 and Tukeys test was used to 
compare means.  
 
TEMPERATURE AND ITS EFFECT ON EARLY LIFE STAGES 
To infer the effects of temperature on viability, fertilization, blastomere morphology, and 
hatching success Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was applied to datasets. ANOVA detects if 
there’s a significant difference between measured factors at different temperatures.  
One-way ANOVA was applied to each measured trait; the initial egg quality (blastomere 
morphology), hatching success, and larval deformities at the first-feeding stage. Temperature 
treatment means were contrasted using the Tukey-Kramer method. 
 
DISINFECTION REGIMES AND ITS EFFECT ON MORTALITY RATE 
HATCHING SUCCESS, AND LARVAL SURVIVAL 
The disinfection experiment had more factors and interaction terms to examine. There were 9 
disinfection regimes, and one control group, or 10 treatments. Measured factors here are: 
mortality rate, hatching success and larval survival.  
 
Mortality rate 
 
A three-way repeated measures ANOVA model was first used to examine the effects of 
disinfection regime on mortality rate. The model included the model terms: Concentration, 
Handling Time, Concentration × Handling Time, Time (repeated factor), Time × 
Concentration, Time × Handling Time, Time × Concentration × Handling Time. If an 
interaction was detected the model was analysed using a lower-order ANOVA model, however 
of the interaction terms were non-significant the Time, Concentration, Handling Time effects 
were interpreted. To include the control group, a two-way ANOVA model was generated with 
==PAGE:36==
 
35 
 
the Disinfection Treatment (10 in total), Time and Time × Concentration interaction term; 
effects were interpreted as above. 
 
Hatch success 
 
A two-way repeated measures ANOVA model was first run to examine hatch success. The 
model included: Time, Concentration, and the Time × Concentration interaction. Moreover, a 
one-way ANOVA model, including the experimental control, was run to include the 
experimental control. 
  
Larval Survival 
 
For larval survival a two-way ANOVA model was run which included the effects of Treatment, 
Time, and the Treatment × Time interaction.

DISCUSSION 
Population decline of the European eel is mainly affected by two things: environmental affects 
and human activities. Both affects can be minimized through farming, but as of today early life 
stages of the European eel are characterized by low hatching success and high mortality (Kroll 
et al., 2014), making conservation and demand for food product difficult, as both rely on wild 
caught eel.  Seeking further knowledge regarding the early life stages of the European eel, to 
enhance it production with the aim of fully closing its life cycle in vitro. Present study was 
conducted to analyse the effect of water temperature on embryonic development and early life 
stages of the European eel. Here investigated factors were egg quality, egg viability, hatching 
success and larvae deformity at five temperatures.  
It is apparent from this study that temperature has various affects on early life stages of the 
European eel. Temperature affects, egg viability, hatching success and frequency of larval 
deformity. Controversially egg quality cf. blastomere cleavage symmetry was not a temperature 
dependant factor, most eggs were categorised as category 1a, which is the best egg quality. 
Proportionally, few eggs were categorised differently therefore statistical correlation was not 
revealed. Previously reviewed literature has not been able to effectively demonstrate the success 
of blastomere morphology categorisation other than abnormal cell cleavages are followed by 
low hatching success (Effer et al., 2013; Avery et al., 2008; Shields et al, 1997). Hatching time 
for the European eel is short, ~45-48 hours(Sørensen et al. 2016), whereas incubation time for 
Atlantic cod is ~ 2-4 weeks, and salmonoid species can be weeks up to months, depending on 
temperature (Geffen et al., 2006; Hendry and Cragg-Hine, 2003). Therefore, the timeframe for 
each developmental stage of the European eel is more likely to overlap with other eggs in the 
same batch, as asynchronous development is rarely the case (Politis et al., 2014a). The eels 
relatively short incubation time makes the labour intensive process of blastomeres 
categorisation nearly impossible to become cost-effective. 
Temperature had a significant effect on egg viability, although they were similar from 
fertilization till 16hpf. Thereafter egg viability differed among incubation temperatures, which 
was visible through interaction term Time×Temperature, meaning that with time egg viability 
varied significantly depending on incubation temperature. Temperature had an accelerating 
effect on hatching time which is visible through the interaction between Time×Temperature, as 
eggs hatch or die off.  Hatching time is highly influenced by temperature, i.e. shortened with 
increasing temperature. Here our, present study documented eggs incubated at 22°C hatching 
==PAGE:52==
 
51 
 
after 64 hours and eggs at 16°C hatched after 32 hours. These results are in contrast with other 
studies and previously assumed effects of temperature on egg development (Pauly and Pullin, 
1988; Georgakopoulou et al., 2010; Ahn et al., 2012). There was no significant difference on 
viability rate among temperatures 16, 18, and 20°C until hatch. It can therefore be assumed that 
the optimal thermal window for the European eel is on this interval, 16-20°C. This correlates 
well with previous studies (Sørensen et al., 2016; Sørensen et al., 2015; Tomkiewicz, 2012), 
and on oceanic temperature at the Sargasso Sea (Haenen et al., 2009). Here temperature can go 
as low as 16°C at 500 meter depth and rise up to 19°C at 100 m and keeps on increasing as 
measurements are closer to the surface (~30°C) (Haenen et al., 2009). 
Hatching success was significantly lower at the two highest temperatures (22°C and 24°C), 
where they showed no significant difference among each other when compared to all 
temperature treatments. This is especially interesting as the eggs incubated at 24°C did not hatch 
and the fact that there was no statistical difference among 22°C and 24°C, demonstrates that 
drastic effects of oceanic warming can be expected to have on the European eel in nature. These 
results are in correlation with viability rate, where lower and intermediate temperature (16, 18 
and 20°C) did not reveal any significant difference among each other. These results further 
emphasize that 16 to 20°C is the natural thermal window for the European eel (Haenen et al., 
2009). 
Frequency of larval deformity was highest at 22°C and lowest at 16 and 18°C. Larval 
development was affected by rearing temperature, with faster development occurring at higher 
temperatures, with larvae at 22°C reaching the first-feeding stage after 8 days, compared to 
larvae at 16°C which took 16 days (Table 2). Some larval deformities were significantly 
affected by temperature, while others showed no correlation to incubation and rearing 
temperatures, these are: edema of the pericardial cavity, abnormal neurocranium and jaw 
deformity. Controversally Kurokawa et al. (2008), concluded a significant relationship between 
edema of the pericardium of Japanese eel larvae and water temperatures, although it was a 
common deformity among all the temperatures. Present study also documented edema of the 
pericardium as a frequent deformity, although it was not temperature dependant. A study on 
Eleginus navaga documented high frequency of edema of the pericardium cavity in association 
with high salinity (Doroshev and Aronovich, 1974). Further studies are needed to find the cause 
for these persistent and temperature independent deformities. 
==PAGE:53==
 
52 
 
My present study revealed several deformities that appear to be associated with high rearing 
temperatures, these are brachymorphic, spinal cord deformity, emaciation and tail deformity. 
Here, brachymorphic larvae were highly temperature dependent, they were most frequent at 
22°C and barely visible at the other temperatures. Controversially Kurokawa et al., (2008) study 
did not document temperature effect on the occurrence of brachymorphic larvae of the Japanese 
eel. Furthermore they documented the occurrence of emaication frequent but independent upon 
temperature, while my study revealed it to be temperature dependent, but highly frequent. 
Moreover there was only significant differences documented at 22°C, and larvae with 
emaciation were almost dominating at all temperature, with the lowest percentage at 18°C. It is 
important to clarify reasons for this high frequency, as it is relatively high even at optimal 
rearing temperature (20°C). 
The occurrence of tail- and spinal cord deformity increased markedly with temperature, where 
lowest frequency was at 16 and 18°C and highest at 22°C. This correlates with the overall 
acceptance that deformities increase with increasing temperature (Okamure et al., 2007). 
Although tail deformities are often categorised in same category as spinal cord deformity 
(Kurokawa et al., 2008). Furthermore a study conducted on gilthead seabream larvae, revealed 
similar results as the present study, i.e. lower and higher temperature induces frequency of 
spinal curvature (Polo et al., 1991, Kurokawa et al, 2008).  
Intensive farming of the European eel would be highly beneficial for conservation and 
management of this endangered species, as well as for food production. Microflora, 
opportunistic pathogens and parasites have been known to negatively impact early life stages 
of fish, where egg and larvae are especially vulnerable (Hansen and Olafsen, 1989; Sørensen et 
al., 2014b). High egg and larvae mortality has often been denoted to microbial activity, this is 
especially apparent in the aquaculture industry due to high egg density. Eels might be at higher 
risk, leptocephalus larvae of the Japanese eel has developmentally delayed immune system, 
which is presumed to make them especially sensitive to microorganisms (Suzuki and Otake, 
2000; Sorensen et al., 2014). Because of the close relation to the Japanese eel, and high early 
life stage mortality, we fear that the European eel might also possess a weak immune system. 
Furthermore, Sørensen et al. (2014a), documented the presence of Ichthyodinium parasite at 
15hpf during incubation of European eel eggs. As 36 ppt salinity at 20°C are good conditions 
for parasitics growth, disinfection treatments during or shorty after this time period could show 
a reduction of parasites.  
==PAGE:54==
 
53 
 
Therefore this disinfection experiment was conducted, with 10 disinfection regimes where 
successfulness of each treatments was assessed by mortality rate, hatching success and larval 
survival. Moreover adverse effects associated with bacterial colonization and fish eggs have 
been documented for many species, provoking growing interest in the characterization and the 
understanding of their modes of action. Hence a pilot study was run to assess microorganism is 
relation to egg incubation, which give further recommendations on future disinfection studies.  
 
Figure 20. Temperature measurements during disinfection experiment 
 
The present study provided an examination of key concepts regarding disinfection, where our 
data revealed that mortality rate was not affected by any of the disinfection treatments, only 
time was significant. The extent of high mortality during early life stages has not been 
documented numerically or graphically, but is often stated (Kroll et al., 2014). Therefore we 
don’t know at what time high mortality is expected, or if it is distributed through the whole 
stage. Although, here mortality rate was expected to be high as the inlet water temperature was 
22°C, which is suboptimal (see above) during egg incubation and the yolk sac stage of the 
European eel (Sørensen et al., 2016; Sørensen et al., 2015; Tomkiewicz, 2012). Mortality rate 
was highest at 20 hpf, but 8 hours before a spike in incubation temperature was measured 
(Figure 20). Furthermore, the temperature experiment revealed that 22°C incubation 
temperature had significantly lower egg viability from 24 hpf and until hatch, compared to 16, 
==PAGE:55==
 
54 
 
18 and 20°C, hence we can assume that the temperature spike can be the cause for the high 
mortality at 20 hpf.  
In order to further test the hypothesis that disinfection is a successful way to improve farming 
practises, hatching success was investigated. Our results are consistent with published reports 
showing that disinfection regime improves hatching success (Hansen and Olafssen, 1999). 
Furthermore, our data imply that handling during the disinfection of eggs did not affect the 
hatching success, same as with mortality rate. Moreover all hydrogen peroxide disinfection 
regimes, when compared to control and handling group improved hatching success and larval 
survival, therefore we can assume that microbial activity is at fault.  Here the highest number 
of hatched larvae was acquired with the higher concentration i.e. 0.8%, applied twice at both 
15 and 30hpf. These results correlate with data from the larval survival, which implies that 0.6% 
concentration (applied once) might not be enough, but the amplification effect of disinfecting 
twice at both 15 and 30hpf is better.  
Larval survival data was more revealing, i.e. it showed more significance between the 10 
treatments. Studies on microbiota associated with eggs and fish larvae have demonstrated the 
importance of separate rearing tanks, as the fish larvae start to drink before eating, meaning 
their undeveloped digestive tract might come in contact with unnatural microflora at a 
vulnerable time (Bergh et al., 1992). Here good water quality with proper water flow is 
important, as the larvae do not all hatch at the same time, and they are forced to be in the 
incubation tank for some time. Here a later disinfection is more beneficial, as everything is 
cleaner when the larvae hatch. This correlates well with our data, as it reveals that disinfection 
regime where 0.8% concentration is only applied once, later i.e. at 30hpf is better than earlier 
(15hpf, Figure 18) has larvae surviving for longer period of time. Previous studies have shown 
that colonization of microorganisms is greater during later periods of the incubation time 
(Hansen and Olafsen, 1989). Therefore disinfection of higher concentration at the latter part of 
incubation period is recommended, best case scenario would also be once to minimize labour 
cost.

PERSPECTIVES 
Data clearly shows that temperature had an effect on the early life stages, although it can be 
assumed that there were some other negative factors. Further research should be on other water 
parameters and genetic compatibility, as there was no correlation between temperature and 
several larval deformities. Here, deformities were apparent and some were frequent at all 
temperatures thus the aim should be to investigate what impacts this high frequency of larval 
deformities. Deformities independent of temperature can be due to various suboptimal rearing 
parameters, such as light regimes (Politis et al., 2014b), and or water quality.  The larvae were 
reared in water containing antibiotics (rifampicin and ampicillin), and this is a highly unnatural 
environment for the larvae, and furthermore the influences of antibiotics have not been studied. 
Same can be said about the hormonally induced maturation, as some researchers 
have hypothesised that high frequency of larvae deformities and incomplete maturation of 
gametes are all connected (Kurokawa et al., 2008).  Additional explanations besides 
environmental parameters that affect the frequency of larval deformity can be genes or genetic 
incompatibly (Politis et al, 2014a).  
Disinfection clearly has a positive effect on the farming of the European eel. Therefore, further 
research should aim to find the lowest possible concentration of disinfectant, and the best 
possible timing of the disinfection. I suggest investigating later than earlier, as that would make 
it more likely that one time disinfection would be adequate, as it can be labour intensive. Further 
research should also focus on the bacteria and microorganisms and their effect on hatching 
success and larval survival. Specify which the genre of microorganism, as the agar plate pilot 
study, clearly demonstrates the slowing effect disinfection has on bacterial growth (Figure 19).  
From there different chemicals can be tested, targeting specific microbiota.  
CONCLUSION 
Temperature had an effect on early life stages of the European eel, where suboptimal 
temperatures can be considered to be 22°C and 24°C. As they were characterized by negative 
impacts which were visual through low hatching success, and high frequency of larval 
deformities. Based on the findings it appears that 16 to 20°C is within the thermal window, with 
provides good hatching success and significantly lower larval deformities. Disinfection should 
also be applied to eggs, to increase hatching and larval survival.
