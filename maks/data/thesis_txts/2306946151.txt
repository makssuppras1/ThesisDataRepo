Abstract
Sound plays an important role in life. The hearing sense allows the communication among
people and the spatial localisation of sound sources. Whether we are conscious or not of our
surroundings, the environment affects our perception of the sound around us. As the distance
between a talker and a listener increases, the energy and audibility of the speech typically decreases.
Within an enclosed environment, such as a classroom or a concert hall the listener does not receive
only the direct speech signal, but also the reﬂections of the signal coming from the boundaries.
The sound perception in enclosed spaces is dominated by the room acoustics properties of the
enclosures. Today it is known that reverberation challenges hearing-impaired people, even with
hearing aids, and several studies have been conducted to address this problem . However, so far,
the focus has been mainly on the effects of reverberation on speech intelligibility. Relatively little
is known about how various signal processing blocks within hearing aids affects the reverberation
content of the speech signals.
The objective of this project was to investigate and quantify the effects of wide dynamic range
compression on the reverberation content of speech signals. The focus was put on the parameters of
the gain model which could interact and modify the reverberation content of the signals processed.
The study aimed at ﬁnding appropriate objective metrics which can detect potential changes in the
reverberation content of speech signals. Such metrics should be robust to the presence of other
distortions, such as noise, and should also correlate with the listeners’ subjective perception such
that they could be used as a predictor for the perceived changes with regard to reverberation. Both
subjective and objective methods were employed to investigate the topic. In order to detect changes
in the reverberation content, several objective metrics which correlate with reverberation were
applied on the speech signals before and after the compression. A listening test with fourteen normal
hearing participants was performed to assess whether the changes in the reverberant components of
the compressed signals were perceivable. The correlation between the objective metrics and the
perceptual results of the listening test was then investigated. It was found, from the results, that the
gain model changed the perception of reverberation content of the speech signals tested. All the
metrics concurred that the compressed signals had a stronger reverberant component compared to
the corresponding uncompressed signals. The amount of changes in the reverberation depended
on the particular settings of the gain model. The reverberant component increased with lower

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Sound plays an important role in life. The hearing sense allows the communication among
people and the spatial localisation of sound sources. Whether we are conscious or not of our
surroundings, the environment affects our perception of the sound around us. As the distance
between a talker and a listener increases, the energy and audibility of the speech typically decreases.
Within an enclosed environment, such as a classroom or a concert hall the listener does not receive
only the direct speech signal, but also the reﬂections of the signal coming from the boundaries.
The sound perception in enclosed spaces is dominated by the room acoustics properties of the
enclosures. Reverberation deteriorates speech intelligibility (Boothroyd 2002) as the energy of
the direct speech is masked by the energy of the reﬂections. Today it is known that reverberation
challenges hearing-impaired people, even with hearing aids, and several studies have been con-
ducted to address this problem (e.g. Smith, 2003; George et al., 2010; Kates, 2001; Reinhart et al.,
2016). So far, the focus has been mainly on the effects of reverberation on speech intelligibility.
Relatively little is known about how various signal processing blocks within hearing aids (e.g. the
gain models) affect the reverberation content and perception of speech signals. The aim of this
thesis is to investigate and quantify the effects of wide dynamic range compression with respect to
reverberation on speech signals.
When a conversation takes place in open spaces, the distance is the main factor which affects
the transmission of speech sounds. In free-ﬁeld conditions, the energy of the sound generated at
the mouth of the talker follows the "6 dB rule" (Boothroyd, 2002). That is, the average speech
level decreases by 6 dB per doubling of the distance from the lips of the talker. The dynamics of
the sound transmission are different if the conversation takes place in a enclosed space, such as
a room. Walls and boundaries generate secondary sound sources as they reﬂect the sound waves
generated by the talker. The listener does not perceive any more the sound only from the talker
but also the reﬂected version of it due to the room. The relation between the level of the direct
and the reverberant sound depends on the geometry, the size and the material of the room. When
considering the effects of reverberation with respect to speech intelligibility it is of interest to
distinguish the early and late reverberation components. The early reverberation component, which
corresponds to the early reﬂections, arrives at the ear of the listener after the direct sound and

it is known that it enhances audibility and intelligibility (Boothroyd, 2002). In contrast, the late
reverberation arrives later at the listener than the direct sound. The late reverberation cannot be
integrated with the direct sound and furthermore it interferes with the recognition of the upcoming
sounds. Boothroyd (2002) showed how the intelligibility of reverberant speech is limited by its
self-masking properties. The presence of reverberation was proven to be detrimental in speech
recognition. The effects are even more accentuated for impaired listeners (Finitzo-Hieber and
Tillman, 1978; Duquesnoy and R.Plomp, 1983; Humes et al., 1986). Nowadays, most of the
conversations takes place in rooms or enclosed spaces. Speech recognition becomes particularly
challenging when the hearing devices are used in a reverberant environment. A few experiments
have been conducted to investigate the effects of reverberation on syllables or consonant recognition
with hearing aids. However, only a few studies focused on in which way the processing blocks
within hearing aids (e.g. gain model) modify the reverberation content of speech signals (Smith,
2003) while most of them focused on the effects of the gain model’s parameters with respect to
the perceived sound quality and speech intelligibility (Moore et al., 2004; Nabelek and Robinette,
1977; Hansen, 2002; Gatehouse et al., 2006; Hansen, 2002; Schmidt, 2006).
The objective of this project was to investigate and quantify the effects of wide dynamic range
compression on the reverberation content of speech signals. The focus was put on the parameters
of the gain model which could interact and modify the reverberation content of the speech signals.
The study aimed at ﬁnding appropriate objective metrics which can detect potential changes in the
reverberation content of speech signals. Such metrics should be robust to the presence of other
distortions, such as noise and should also correlate with the listener’s subjective perception such
that they could be used as a predictor for the perceived changes with regard to reverberation. This
project focus on thCRCRquestions: In which way the gain model change the reverberation content
of speech signals? Are the changes perceivable by normal hearing listeners? Are the changes
predictable using objective metrics? Is it possible to have a better ﬁtting procedure which takes
into account the reverberation content of the signals? The hypothesis was that the gain model used
in compression-based hearing aids modiﬁes the dynamic range of the speech signals which results
in a different perception of reverberation in the compressed signals. Figure 1.1 shows the time
domain envelopes of a reverberant speech signal and its compressed version. The envelopes of the
signals are indicated as the solid curves in the ﬁgure. The yellow curve represent the envelope of
the reverberant envelope while the purple curve is the envelope of the compressed envelope. The
two envelopes show differences in amplitude range and slopes. The reverberant envelope has larger
dynamic range than the compressed one. The purple curve has a shallower slope than the yellow
one. It was investigated here whether this difference in the dynamics of the signals is perceived as
a difference in the reverberation content.
In current hearing aids, reverberation is not always taken into account in the ﬁtting process.
Some devices have a de-reverberation algorithm. A clear understanding of whether a given gain
model actually changes the reverberation content of speech could improve the ﬁtting process in the
future. It would be then possible to design novel de-reverberation algorithms that take the effects of

2.1
Room acoustic properties
Room acoustics describe the behaviour of sound in enclosures. The assumption is that enclo-
sures behave as linear and time-invariant (LTI) system and so the properties of the enclosures can
be thoroughly described by its transfer function.

whit a sound input only using its impulse response. The Room Impulse Response (RIR) contains
all the acoustic informations for any source-receiver position. When the room is excited by an

2.3
Hearing aid compression
The human auditory system has a wide dynamic range which ranges over 12 orders of magni-
tudes. In listeners with hearing loss this dynamic range is reduced. The most common complaint
associated with hearing loss is the inability to hear; speciﬁcally, the inability to hear soft sounds.
On the other hand the loud sounds are still perceived as loud and the mid-level sounds are still
perceived as comfortable. To compensate for the hearing loss the hearing aids provide a gain to the
input signal in order to deliver an ampliﬁed version of the signal into the ear canal of the wearer.

2.3.1
Basics of compression
One of the most important issue for people with hearing loss is the inability to hear. To
solve the problem, one approach would be to make the sound louder providing a linear gain
application. However with this approach sounds like speech may be perceived loud and intense
and sounds could be ampliﬁed beyond the limit of comfort. To make the soft sound louder without
making the loud sound uncomfortable, a non-linear ampliﬁcation is necessary. With the linear
ampliﬁcation approach, the gain is an independent function of the input level. For a non-linear
ampliﬁcation strategy the gain applied must be level-dependent. The characteristics of a non-linear
ampliﬁcation/compression model are basically deﬁned by its static and dynamic parameters. The
static parameters are the compression threshold and compression ratio and deﬁne the behaviour of
the compressor in response to steady input signal. The dynamic parameter such as the attack and
the release time describe the amount of time required by the compressor to react to a change in the
input signal.
2.3.2
Compression threshold
Compression threshold (CT) is deﬁned as the input SPL which, when applied to the hearing
aid, gives a reduction in the gain of 2 (±0.5) dB with respect to the gain in the linear mode (IEC,
1983b). It is the point in the I/O function, where the output is two dB lower than it would be if
no compression was applied. The point at which the slope of the I/O function changes is referred
to as the threshold knee-point (TK). Because the CT and TK are so close to each other, the two
terms are often used interchangeably, as throughout this thesis. Figure 2.8 shows the compression
threshold and the knee-point for a given I/O function. The choice of the TK depends on the purpose
of the compression model. A high TK (usually > 60 dB) is used to limit the output of the hearing
aid to prevent loudness discomfort. A low TK (usually < 60 dB) is used to improve the audibility
of the soft part of speech or to restore loudness perception. Finally, some hearing aids provide a
combination of the two possibilities with a lower and higher threshold knee-point.
2.3.3
Compression ratio
When the input level exceeds the threshold knee-point the compression ratio (CR) determines
how much the signal will be compressed. The CR relates the change in input to the change in
output:

Objective methods

3.4.1
Percentile difference [P]
The percentiles of the signals were computed by the ISMADHA tool (ISMADHA, 2014)
implemented in Matlab which analyse the time domain signal in frequency bands and returns the
percentile values desired in dB. The function is optimised to analyse the dynamics of the signal
when hearing aid compression is applied. This percentile function is often used by audiologist
analysis/comparison of pre-calculation of HI gain of different HIs or ﬁtting formulae. In this study
the 99th and the 30th percentiles were used (99p, 30p). More speciﬁcally the difference of the two
values was employed:
P = 99p −30p
(3.1)
This difference is well related to the dynamic range of the signal since the 99p percentile
represents the onset of the signal and the 30p represents the low level part of the signal. From
theory it is known that a more reverberant signal presents a reduced dynamic range compared
to a drier signal as the reverberation content increases the low level components of sounds. To
investigate the changes introduces by the gain model on the difference of the P values before and
after the gain model is considered:
∆P = Pi −Po.
(3.2)
In the equation, Pi are the P values of the reverberant input signal and Po are the P values of the
compressed output signal.
Limitations
The 99th −30th percentile difference is a metric which could evaluate changes
in the reverberation content on speech signal after compression. However, this approach presents
some limitations as the metric is sensitive to other parameter besides the reverberation content.
The metric is particularly sensitive to noise. An increase of the noise level will move the 30p to
higher values and consequently the percentile difference will be reduced even if the reverberation
content was untouched. Within the limitations the percentile metric is a useful tool to investigate
the changes in reverberation content caused by compression on speech signal.
3.4.2
Blind estimation of reverberation time [T60]
The blind estimation of the reverberation time is based on a statistical model for the sound
decay which allows to estimate the reverberation time T60 using the maximum-likelihood (ML)
estimator (Lollmann et al. (2010)). The room reverberation causes a sound decay which is modelled
as a discrete random process. The model analyses this sound decay and ﬁnds a relation between
the decay rate and the reverberation time.
The speech signal is ﬁrst down-sampled to reduce the computational complexity. The down-
sampling factor chosen depends on the sampling rate. Since the model is based on the energy decay
==PAGE:40==
22
CHAPTER 3. METHODS
of the sound, the down-sample step does not affect the results since the energy decay is not affected
reducing the number the samples. The algorithm implemented in this model uses an adaptable
time-frame length to detect the real sound decay and it is robust to noise and overestimation of the
T60.
To investigate the changes introduced by the gain model on the reverberation time the difference
of T60 before and after the gain model is considered:
∆T60 = T60,i −T60,o.
(3.3)
In the formula T60,i is the reverberation time of the reverberant input signal and T60,o is the
reverberation time the compressed output signal.
Limitations
A blind estimation of the reverberation time would be a powerful metric to
investigate and in particular quantify the changes in reverberation content caused by compression
on speech signals. However, the algorithm presents limitation which makes this method unusable
is some conditions, as the algorithm allows to estimate the T60 within a range of 0.2s to 1.2s and
assumes that source and receiver are not within the critical distance. A de-noising is not performed
by this function and has to be done in advance.
Keeping in mind the limitations inherent to this metric it is possible to use this blind estimator
as a tool to quantify the effect of compression with respect to reverberation on speech signals.
3.4.3
Signal to reverberation ratio [SRR]
A well known metric that correlates with the reverberation content of sounds is the signal-to-
reverberation ratio (SRR). The SRR was computed by a function implemented in Matlab which
analyses the input signal over time-frames and returns the estimated SRR in dB.
The two input signals are buffered in frames and time-windowed. The length of the Hanning
window was set to 1 sec. The energy power is computed in each frame. Next, the ratio of the
energy of the direct component over the reverberant is computed frame by frame. The values are
converted in dB and returned as output. The metric was applied on both reverberant and compressed
signals. When applied on reverberant signals, the procedure to obtain the direct and the reverberant
components was the following:
The direct part of the signal is obtained as the convolution between the anechoic speech signal
(s(t)) and the direct part of the room impulse response (hd(t)) (see Figure 2.5).
The reverberant component of the signal was obtained as the subtraction of the direct component
deﬁned before from the whole signal (which is obtained as the convolution of the anechoic speech
signal and the whole room impulse response). If y(t) is the reverberant signal and yd(t) is the
direct component, the reverberant component yr(t) is obtained as:
yr(t) = y(t) −yd(t)

3.6
Listening test
The research question was investigated also from a perceptual point of view. A listening test
was performed to assess whether the changes in reverberation that the compressor may introduce
are actually perceivable. In this project, the listening test was performed only with normal hearing
subjects. The test was designed based on the MUSHRA listening test (EBU, 2000). MUSHRA
stands for MUltiple Stimuli with Hidden Reference and Anchor and is a methodology for subjective
evaluation of audio quality, to evaluate the perceived quality of the output from lossy audio
compression algorithms. Here, the MUSHRA method was adapted for the purpose of the thesis.
The test presents multiple stimuli with hidden reference but there is no anchor, in this sense the
listening test performed cannot be called properly MUSHRA test but only MUSHRA based test.
The test was structured in two different parts, the sound stimuli (items) and the reference were
speech signals of four second duration normalised to RMS value of 65 dB SPL. The listener could
listen to the reference and the stimuli as many times as needed. However in the test guide, it was
suggested to judge on the ﬁrst impression and not to focus too long on the same item to avoid
fatigue. In the bottom of the screen a "loop" and "keep position" buttons were provided. In both
parts of the test the references was the reverberant speech signal artiﬁcially recorded in room 4 at
the source-to-receiver distance of one meter. The items presented, except for the hidden reference,
are compressed versions of the reverberant reference obtained with the gain model. The listening
test was focused on evaluating the effect of different knee-point and compression ratio on the
compressed signals while keeping all the other parameters constant. Table 3.3 shows the setting for
the gain model used for the listening test.

CHAPTER 5
General discussion
In this chapter, the metrics presented in Chapter 3 and the corresponding results presented in
Chapter 4 are analysed and discussed. In Section 5.1, the results obtained by applying the objective
metrics are discussed. In Section 5.2, the results from the listening test are discussed. In Section 5.3,
the correlation between the objective and subjective metrics is discussed.
5.1
Objective metrics
In this section, the objective metrics and the results obtained by their application are discussed.
The reverberant signals are here referred as "input signals" or "uncompressed signals" and the
signals sent through the gain model are referred as "output signals" or "compressed signals".
5.1.1
Percentile difference
The percentile difference (P) is a metric based on the amplitude difference of the onsets
and the offsets of a sound signal. Signals recorded within the same room, but with different
source-receiver distance, differ mainly in their reverberation content. More precisely, longer sound-
receiver distances imply higher reverberation content in the recorded signals and it is expected that
reverberation affects the dynamic range of the signals (Tsilﬁdis, 2001). This changes in dynamic
range can be detected by the percentile difference analysis which, in this sense, is a metric sensitive
to reverberation content of sound signals. Figure 4.1a shows the P values measured on reverberant
speech signals recorded at the source-receiver distances of one, two and three meters. The P
values decreases with increasing source-receiver distance for the signals. Figure 4.1b shows the
P values measured on the compressed speech signals. The most reverberant signals, even when
compressed, show a lower P value. However, a general decrease in the P values is visible for all
the three recording distances. Figure 4.1c shows the difference of the P values measured between
the uncompressed and the compressed signals. There is no signiﬁcant difference in the way the
gain model affects the signals based on their recording distance. Based on Figure E.2, which
shows the ∆P value for all the rooms, it can be seen that, in general, the most reverberant signals
present a smaller change in the dynamic range after the gain model is applied (even if this is not
the case for Figure 4.1c). A possible explanation for this can be based on the differences in the
45
==PAGE:64==
46
CHAPTER 5. GENERAL DISCUSSION
power spectrum of a drier signal compared to the spectrum of a more reverberant one. Signals
with high reverberation have more peaks and dips respect to signals with low reverberation content.
Moreover, the magnitude spectrum values in the high frequency range are usually lower for dry
signals (see Appendix, Figure D.2). Signals with different power spectra are going to be affected
differently by the gain model. If no gain (linear) is applied and the gain model is only compressing
(non linear), then signals with higher magnitude values should be more affected than signals with
lower spectrum values. In addition, the gain model behaves differently on a spectrum with peaks
and dips respect to a smooth spectrum. The lower parts of the dips are less likely to be compressed
and therefore bigger portion of the signals could be preserved at the output. Based on these two
considerations, it can be argued that the signal recorded at three meter is less affected than the
signal recorded at one meter. Therefore, the dynamic range is less reduced for signals with high
reverberation content. Figure 4.1d shows the dependency of the P values for the compressed signal
on the number of channels. Increasing the number of channels, the dynamic range of the signals
decrease. This trend can be explained considering the differences in the signal processing of the
gain model when different number of channels are employed. When one channel is used, the
gain model is not actually performing a frequency analysis as all the samples are processed in the
same way regardless their actual frequency content. The one-channel based gain model performs
a time-analysis rather than a frequency analysis. When increasing the number of channels, the
samples are processed based on their actual frequency content. As explained in Chapter 3, the gain
model sets the knee-point within each band used based on the LTASS. Therefore, increasing the
number of channels, the signals are more affected by compression. Figures 4.1e and f shows how
the P values are affected by different knee-points and compression ratios. Lower values for the
knee-point correspond to more compression in the gain model. Therefore, the dynamic range of
the output signals decreases as the knee-point decreases. For similar reasons, higher value for the
compression ratio lead to higher reduction of the dynamic range of the signals. It is not possible to
estimate from the P values how the onsets and the offsets of the signal are modiﬁed by the gain
model. It is reasonable to think that the onsets of the signal are more affected than the offsets. If
this is the case, then, a reduction of the dynamic range can be associated with an increase in the
reverberation perception as the direct parts, which are mainly represented by the onsets, have been
more reduced than the low parts of the signal.
5.1.2
Blind estimation of reverberation time
So far, several algorithms have been developed for the blind estimation of the reverberation
time to make the estimation as close as possible to the real measured quantities. The method used
in this study has been proved to be time-efﬁcient and reliable (Lollmann et al., 2010). The main
limitation is the restricted range of possible estimated reverberation time in the range from 0.2 to
1.2 seconds. Figure 4.2 shows the results obtained by applying the algorithm on signals before
and after the gain model. The reverberation time, estimated from an impulse response or blindly
from a reverberant signal, is a metric which is position- and signal-dependent. The reference
value given for an enclosure usually refers to the average of the measured quantities in different
positions. Therefore, also for the estimated quantities at one, two and three meters, the mean
==PAGE:65==
5.1. OBJECTIVE METRICS
47
value should be considered. Figure 4.2c shows the difference between the estimated values for the
input and the output signals. The gain model affected the signals in such a way that the algorithm
estimated higher values for the compressed signals. This results could have been predicted based
on Figure 1.1 which shows the slopes of the time-domain envelopes for an uncompressed and
a compressed signal. The gain model reduced the dynamic range of the compressed signal and
modiﬁes its slope. Signals recorded in enclosures with high reverberation time show a shallower
slope, in time-domain, than signals recorded in dry enclosures. Based on this and Figure 1.1, the
results obtained with this metric can be explained. If it is assumed that more compression leads
to lees steep slopes and therefore higher estimated values for the reverberation time, then also the
results shown in Figures 4.2c, d and f can be explained. Indeed, higher values for the reverberation
time are found for more aggressive compression settings. In Figures 4.2d it is clearly visible the
effect of the limited range of the estimator. The reverberation time saturates at 1.2 s for the 8 and
16 channels.
5.1.3
Signal to reverberation ratio
The signal-to-reverberation ratio is a well known metric which correlates with the reverberation
content of sound signals. The metric considers the energy of the direct part of the signal over the
energy of the reverberant part. For signals recorded in the same room at different sound-receiver
position the metric will give lower value for the longer recording distance. The speciﬁc values
obtained for the SRR depend on the equation and parameters used (e.g. time-window). Therefore,
it is more relevant to focus on the relation between the results at different recording distances and
not their absolute values.
Figure 4.3a shows the results for the input signals. The three meters recording, which has the
highest reverberation content, shows the lower value for the SRR compared to the other recordings.
Figure 4.3b shows the results for the SRR measured on the compressed signals. Figure 4.3c
shows the difference between the uncompressed and the compressed values. It is clear that the
gain model affected the signals differently. The SRR of the driest signal (one meter recording)
was more reduced than the other two signals. This trend was found among all the tested signals.
Such a ﬁnding could be explained as discussed in Section 5.1.1 with the different behaviour of
the gain model depending on the spectrum of the input signals. If more reverberant signals are
less compressed, then the reverberant component is more reduced for drier signals than for more
reverberant ones. The effect of the number of channels, of the knee-point and the compression ratio
shown in Figures 4.3d, f and e is explained based on the previous considerations.
5.1.4
Perceptual envelope difference index
The EDI is a metric based on the differences in the time-domain envelopes of two signals.
As explained in Chapter 2 an amplitude normalisation is performed on both signal’s envelopes.
The normalisation avoids the metric to be affected by possible amplitudes differences between
the signals (e.g. before and after the gain model). Therefore, the metric considers mainly the
differences in the modulation of the two envelopes.
==PAGE:66==
48
CHAPTER 5. GENERAL DISCUSSION
The Perceptual based EDI (PEDI) has the advantage, respect to the original EDI, to analyse the
envelope differences over frequency bands. The bands were chosen based on a gammatone ﬁlter-
bank to make the metric better correlate with the human perception. Figure D.3, in Appendix D,
shows the PEDI measured at one, two and three meters between uncompressed and compressed
signals. The PEDI values are not constant over frequencies. The difference in the envelopes
increases as the frequency increases, regardless the recording distances of the signals. To verify
whether this increasing difference in the high frequency range was due to the signal processing
in the gain model, a signal (room 4 , one-meter recording) was sent through the gain model with
compression ratio equal to one and knee-point value higher than the maximum signal spectrum
amplitude. Such a setting should not modify the input signal which, then, should be only processed
by the signal processing block of the gain model. The PEDI was measured between the input and
the output. Figure D.4 shows the results of this test. It can be seen that the PEDI is basically 0 or,
very low across all frequencies. This preliminary test ensured that the difference in the envelopes
of the compressed signals did not come from the signal processing performed by the gain model
but exclusively by the compressor block.
Figure 4.4a shows the results for the PEDI computed between input and output signals. A
common trend that can be seen from this ﬁgure and Figure E.5 is that the PEDI decreases as the
source-receiver distance for the recording increases. One possible explanation for this trend could
be, again, that the more reverberant signals are less affected by the gain model and, therefore, the
time-domain envelope is less modiﬁed at the output. This explanation justiﬁes also the results
shown in Figures 4.4b, d and f.
5.1.5
Results from hearing aid recordings
For the recordings, it was not possible to investigate the effects of each parameter in a controlled
way as they were set based on the ﬁtting procedure of the device. The reverberant signals played
with the loudspeaker had RMS values equal to 75 dB. The knee-point was set to 65 dB SPL based
on the LTASS. The recordings were used to investigate whether the results obtained with a real
hearing device and the results obtained with the gain model showed a similar trend.
Figure E.6a shows the ∆P difference between the input signals and the recorded compressed
signals. The amplitude difference between the signals sent trough the gain model and the ones
sent through the hearing aid does not play a role since the metric considers the difference of two
quantities and not the absolute values. Therefore, the ∆P values measured on the recordings were
affected only by the particular settings of the device and not the amplitude of the input signals. The
trend is the same as for the results obtained with the gain model. The more reverberant signals are
less affected by the hearing aid.
Figure 4.5b shows the reverberation time measured on the compressed signals. The mean
value among the three recording distances was 0.9878 seconds while the mean value estimated on
the input signals was 0.7068 seconds. Therefore, also for the hearing aid the metric indicates an
increase of the reverberation content in the output signals.
Figure 4.5c shows the SRR computed on the compressed signals. As for the gain model, the
metric shows that drier signals are most affected by the hearing device.

Figure 4.5d shows the PEDI computed on the recordings. The results obtained with this metric
do not agreed with the results obtained with the gain model. In this case the more reverberant

5.2
Listening test
In this section the results obtained by the listening test are discussed. The compressed signals
presented during the test are here referred as "items" while the reverberant uncompressed signal is
the "reference". The items and the reference presented were normalise to the same RMS value.
All the results submitted by the listeners were analysed with the e-Gauge method (Appendix H).
The methods returns four plots which represent in order: the reliability, the discrimination and the
agreement distributions and the scatterplot of reliability versus discrimination. The scatterplot is
very useful to immediately understand how the listeners are distributed for both discrimination and
reliability. However, it is necessary but not sufﬁcient to be only above the thresholds. The results
of an individual listener should be, with some extent, similar to the results of the other listeners.
This is what agreement distribution stands for. The perceptual results presented are the individual
and mean results of the listeners above the threshold in the agreement panel.
5.2.1
Perceived sound quality dependency on the knee-point
Figure 4.6 shows the individual and mean perceptual results. For this test, two conditions were
investigated. The compression ratio was set to 2 and 5 and the knee-point was varied among the
items presented. By inspection of the ﬁgure it can be seen that most of the results were negative
which indicates a general lower perceived sound quality of the items compared to the reference.
Moreover, the slope of the mean curve for the condition with CR = 5 is steeper than the curve of
the condition with CR = 2. The results indicate that lower values for the knee-point and higher
values for the compression ratio leaded to lower scores for the perceived sound quality.
This test was performed to investigate if there is a relation between the perceived sound quality
and the perceived reverberation. It is clear that there is a connection between a decrease in the
perceived sound quality and an increase in perceived reverberation. In the test, there was no
previous agreement with the listener on the exact deﬁnition of sound quality. The listener was asked
to judge the sound quality based on his/her internal deﬁnition. However most of the participants
focused on the noise component in the items. One of the most common comments was in general,
in the whole listening test, that the items were in some cases very noisy. These cases they referred to
were the conditions with strongest compression. As mentioned before, the items and the reference
==PAGE:68==
50

were normalised to the same RMS value. After the normalisation, the noise level was increased
for the compressed signals, as their amplitudes was lower compared to the reference. Therefore,
the more the signals were compressed the more the normalisation increased the noise component.
In everyday life it is common to associate the noise and the low deﬁnition of a sound with the
presence of reverberation coming from the surrounding. It would be then interesting to investigate
more deeply how the noise perception affected the given scores for the perceived reverberation and
if there is any direct relation between the two components.
5.2.2
Perceived reverberation dependency on the knee-point
Figure 4.7 shows the individual and the mean perceptual results from the investigation of the
effects of the knee-point on the perceived reverberation. The results were obtained by keeping the

The knee-point tested were 30, 40, 50 and 65 dB SPL. The ﬁgure shows the individual scores given

across the individual results. By inspection of the ﬁgure it can be seen that all the results were

Moreover, lower values for the knee-point leaded to higher scores for the perceived reverberation.
It can be noticed that the slope of the mean curve is not the same in all the conditions. More
speciﬁcally, the conditions with the highest compression ratio shows the steepest slope. It can be
argued, then, that also the knee-point parameter is connected to the compression ratio used by the
gain model in terms of perceived reverberation.

the effects of the compression ratio on the perceived reverberation. The results were obtained
by keeping the knee-point constant and varying the compression ratio among the items in each

considered (30, 40, 50 and 65 dB SPL). Within each condition the items were compressed with
compression ratio of 2, 3, 4 and 5 respectively. In the ﬁgure the grey lines are the individual scores
given by the listeners and the corresponding standard deviations (vertical bars). Each individual

slope of the mean curve in the condition with the lowest knee-point is much ﬂatter compared to
the other conditions. It could be argued that the effect of the compression ratio on the perceived

5.3
Correlation between metrics and perceptual results
Each objective metric was applied on the items presented in the listening test. The results
are shown in Figure 4.10. In the ﬁgure, the result of each metric is presented in such a way that
higher values indicates more estimated reverberation. It can be noticed that the blind estimation
of the reverberation time saturated at the combination TK = 50 dB SPL and CR = 3. The other
three metrics seems to show a similar trend compared to the perceptual results (Figure 4.9). To
investigate the correlation between the objective and the perceptual results, the objective data of
each metric was plotted versus the subjective data and the curve was ﬁtted with a second order
polynomial function. Figure 4.11 shows the correlations between each objective metric and the
perceptual results.
A positive relation between the percentile difference and the perceptual data was found (Fig-
ure 4.11a), with an Adjusted R-squared of 0.9804. No relation between the reverberation time and
the perceptual data was found (Figure 4.11b), with an Adjusted R-squared of 0.2799. A slight
trend between the signal-to-reverberation ratio difference and the perceptual results was found
(Figure 4.11c), with an Adjusted R-squared of 0.8910. Also, a positive relation between the PEDI
and the perceptual data was found (Figure 4.11d), with an Adjusted R-squared of 0.9668. Based on
the Adjusted R-squared values, it can be presumed that the PEDI and the percentile difference are
the metrics which best correlate with the subjective perception. Both of them perform an analysis
in frequency bands and are easily implementable in hearing device. The blind estimation of the
reverberation time was implemented with an efﬁcient algorithm, however, the method cannot be
used on signals with reverberation time higher 1.2 seconds. Therefore, the method was found not
to be suitable for the compressed signals. The signal-to-reverberation ratio, as implemented in this
study, is not suitable for implementation in hearing devices as it requires a previous knowledge of
the direct component of the signal.

CHAPTER 5. GENERAL DISCUSSION
In this study, the focus was mainly on the knee-point, the compression ratio and the number of
channels of the gain model. The effects of the time constants, the linear gain and the noise were
not investigated. However, a preliminary analysis on the effects of these parameters was conducted
for few signals. Some of the results can be found in the Appendix F.
==PAGE:71==
CHAPTER 6
Summary and conclusion
The work presented in this thesis investigated the effects of wide dynamic range compression
on speech signals with respect to reverberation. A database with uncompressed and compressed
signals was created and several objective metrics were used on both type of signals to analyse their
reverberation content. A MUSHRA-based listening test was performed on fourteen normal-hearing
listeners, where the reference was an uncompressed signal and the items were signals compressed
with different settings for the gain model. The focus was on the effects of knee-point, compression
ratio and number of channels on the reverberation content of the compressed signals. The results
from the objective metrics were compared to the perceptual results and their correlation was
investigated. Some recordings from a hearing aid were also analysed with the objective metrics to
verify whether the results showed a similar trend compared to the results from the gain models.
From the results, it was found that the gain model changed the perception of reverberation
content of the speech signals tested. All the metrics showed that the compressed signals had a
stronger reverberant component compared to the input signals. The amount of changes in the
reverberation content depended on the particular settings of the gain model. The reverberant
component increased with lower knee-point, higher compression ratio and higher number of
channels. A common trend identiﬁed among the results was that the more reverberant signals,
those recorded at the source-receiver distance of three meters, were less affected by the gain
model. The listening test conﬁrmed that the changes in the reverberation content are perceivable
by normal-hearing listeners. The subjective results indicated that the increase in the reverberation
perception is related to increases in the compression ratio and decreases of the knee-point. All
the metrics applied predicted an increase in the reverberation content of the signals. However,
the percentile difference and the PEDI were the metric which best correlated with the subjective
ﬁndings and could, then, be used as predictors. The blind estimation of the reverberation did
not correlate with the perceptual results probably due to the limitations of the algorithm which
the metric is based on. The signal-to-reverberation ratio showed only a common trend with the
perceptual results. However, the gain model behaves based on the power spectrum of the input
signals and has no information about the recording distances or the actual reverberation content.
Therefore, the best approach to actually understand and predict the changes in reverberation, is by
characterising the power spectrum of the signals based on their reverberation content. Based on
53
==PAGE:72==
54
CHAPTER 6. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
these ﬁndings, it seems it is possible to improve the current ﬁtting procedure of the hearing aid
devices in order to take into account the reverberation content of the signals. For example, the
de-reverberator processing block could be improved in such a way that the changes introduced by
the gain model settings are taken into account when the parameters to de-reverberate the signals
are deﬁned. In previous studies (Smith, 2003) it was pointed out the importance to ensure that
the signal-processing blocks within the hearing aids do not affect the reverberation content of the
signals. From the ﬁndings of this study, it appeared that the gain model within hearing aids is
actually changing and increasing the reverberation content of the speech signals. The study showed
that normal-hearing listeners can perceive the changes in the reverberation content. It would be,
however, interesting to investigate how these changes relate to the perception of hearing-impaired
listeners. For future investigations it would be interesting to test the effects on reverberation of the
linear gain and the time constants of the gain model. A more complete knowledge of the effects
of each parameter and their interconnections could be the base for a possible reverberation model
which can predict the changes in reverberation based only on the settings of the hearing aid. If
such a model could be found, the algorithms for de-reverberation could be improved based on the
predictions of the model and, therefore, clearer and more understandable signals could be delivered
to the listeners.

APPENDIX H
eGauge method
The eGauge method establishes an absolute metric for assessor performance within a MUSHRA
type listening experiment. The expertise of an assessor is deﬁned as being able to repeatedly
discriminate differences in a consensual manner within an experiment. For this reason, eGauge
calculation requires at least two replicates (i.e. repetitions of assessments for each stimulus) per
assessor.
eGauge calculation is an ANOVA-based approach, aiming at qualifying the expertise of
assessors in terms of their discrimination and reliability skills and their overall agreement with
other assessors. Therefore, the eGauge method analyses the differences in variability, presence of
disagreement, differences in scaling and differences in sensitivity.
Three metrics are derived by the eGauge calculation: Reliability, discrimination and agreement.
Reliability measures the ability of an assessor to repeat the same evaluation scores for a given
listening stimulus. Discrimination is deﬁned as the signal to noise ratio (SNR) of the repeated
scores for a stimuli set by an assessor. Finally, agreement measures the closeness of scores between
an assessor and the mean score of the assessors group. Three different graphs are obtained from
the eGauge calculation: reliability, discrimination and agreement bar plots. Each bar plot presents
the absolute value per assessor on each different metric. Two lines are added to each graph to
present the 95% of conﬁdence interval. Those intervals are calculated based on a permutation test.
Two different random permutations are performed to the scores of the assessors. The ﬁrst random
permutation is applied to all scores of an assessor to break the relationship between systems.
An ANOVA calculation is performed to obtain values for the agreement threshold. The second
permutation is applied to the scores of an assessor per trial. The ANOVA function is calculated
again to obtain the reliability and the discrimination thresholds. These permutations and the
ANOVA calculations are repeated a large number of times to obtain the chance levels. The 95th
percentile is calculated and it is saved in an array in every repetition. At the end of the repetitions,
the conﬁdence intervals are obtained using a t-test (with a probability=95%) on the percentile
array data. The lower conﬁdence interval can be interpreted as the level of signiﬁcance of the
measure. This level is referred to as the rejection threshold of assessors. Additionally, a scatterplot
is obtained from the eGauge calculation. The x-axis of this scatterplot refers to the reliability and
the y-axis refers to the discrimination values. This scatterplot is useful to separate "good" from
81
==PAGE:100==
82
APPENDIX H. EGAUGE METHOD
"bad" assessors according to these metrics (reliability and discrimination). The rejection thresholds
(conﬁdence intervals) of each metric are also plotted in the same graph. In this way, the graph is
divided in four different zones. The location of the not rejected assessors is on the top-right zone,
where both the reliability and discrimination values are high. The intersection zone could be also
deﬁned as zone for good assessors. Therefore, the rejection threshold is where either the reliability
or discrimination values are low.
