Abstract 
Transportation accounts for about a quarter of Denmark’s total greenhouse gas (GHG) emis-
sions and plays a key role in the country’s climate strategy, which targets a 70% reduction by 
2030 and climate neutrality by 2050 compared with 1990. With Denmark’s strong cycling cul-
ture, it offers a valuable context for studying sustainable mobility. 
This project examines the environmental and health impacts of individual transportation behav-
ior, drawing on data from the Danish National Travel Survey (DTU, 2025) and Ecoinvent 
(2025). It first analyzes travel patterns—distance, mode, and purpose—then applies regression 
analysis to identify factors influencing mode choice. Results show that walking and cycling 
dominate trips under 15 km, while cars are used more for longer distances. 
A life cycle assessment evaluates carbon footprints, 17 midpoint, and 2 endpoint environmental 
indicators across transport modes. The study also quantifies health benefits from physical ac-
tivity and compares them with environmental health losses. While car travel typically leads to 
net health losses, walking and cycling often provide a net health gain of up to 1 avoided DALH 
per day. 
Finally, an absolute environmental sustainability assessment was conducted by benchmarking 
daily multimodal transportation against planetary boundaries, using both grandfathering and 
economic allocation approaches. Survey data were scaled to the national level using weight 
factors, revealing that over half of the population exceeds the most lenient sustainability thresh-
old (1 kg CO₂-eq/day), underscoring the urgency of shifting toward more sustainable transport 
options.

1. Introduction 
Denmark is responsible for approximately 8.4‰ of 
global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, while ac-
counting for 7.4‰ of the world’s population (Our 
World in Data, 2024a). Although this emission 
level is not significantly above the global average, 
it contributes to the broader challenge of global en-
vironmental degradation. According to the latest 
planetary boundaries assessment, climate change 
boundaries have already been transgressed, indicat-
ing that humanity is operating well beyond the 
Earth’s safe environmental limits (Richardson et al., 
2023). 
In response, the Danish government has adopted a 
strong and proactive stance on climate action, po-
sitioning itself as a global leader in the green tran-
sition. Denmark has ensured ambitious climate ac-
tion by passing the 2020 Climate Act into law, 
which sets a target to reduce Denmark's greenhouse 
gas emissions by 70% in 2030 compared to 1990 
levels and a long-term objective of climate neutral-
ity by 2050 at the latest (Danish Council on Climate 
Change, 2023). 
The transportation sector plays a pivotal role in this 
context, contributing roughly one-quarter of Den-
mark’s total GHG emissions (Our World in Data, 
2024b). To achieve sustainable development in the 
field of transport, the Danish government aims to 
decouple the environmental and health impacts of 
transportation from economic growth. This vision 
dates back to the 1996 CO₂ reduction plan issued 
by the Ministry of Transport, which advocated for 
improved energy efficiency, reduced transport de-
mand, and a greater reliance on bicycles and public 
transit (Danish Ministry of Transport, 1996). 
However, most existing life cycle assessment 
(LCA) studies in the transportation field focus on 
specific transport modes in isolation. For example, 
recent research has evaluated electric vehicles (Xia 
et al., 2022), autonomous electric vehicles 
(Heiberg et al., 2023), and fast track transportation 
(Li et al., 2019). Eriksson et al. (1996) conducted 
one of the earliest LCAs of the road transport sector 
nearly three decades ago. Regarding the link be-
tween transportation and public health, Lawrence 
D. Frank (2010) investigated how travel behavior 
influences energy expenditure (kcal) and GHG 
emissions.  
Overall, most studies in this area rely on aggregated 
or mode-specific data, without capturing the full 
complexity of multimodal, real-world travel pat-
terns. Meanwhile, there is still a notable research 
gap in assessing the environmental and health im-
pacts of transportation based on realistic, multi-
modal individual travel behavior.  
This thesis addresses this gap by using detailed in-
dividual-level data from the Danish National 
Travel Survey (DTU, 2025) to analyze both envi-
ronmental burdens and associated health outcomes 
in the Danish context. 
More specifically, this study aims to: 
• 
Perform a statistical analysis of travel distances 
and transportation mode choices to understand 
individual mobility patterns in Denmark. 
• 
Carry out a comprehensive life cycle assess-
ment of climate change and other environmen-
tal impacts at both midpoint and endpoint lev-
els, across various transport modes and travel 
distances. 
• 
Evaluate the transportation-related physical ac-
tivity and its health benefits, including a com-
parison between health gains from physical 
movement and health losses caused by 
transport-related environmental impacts. 
• 
Perform an absolute environmental sustaina-
bility assessment of daily multimodal transpor-
tation, benchmarked against planetary bounda-
ries using both the grandfathering and eco-
nomic allocation approaches.

2. Methods 
2.1 
Transportation data and statistical 
analysis 
2.1.1 Data Description 
The transportation data used in this report is de-
rived from the Danish National Travel Survey (ab-
breviated as TU data), an annual survey that cap-
tures travel behavior among residents of Denmark 
aged 6 and above, excluding those in Greenland 
and the Faroe Islands (DTU, 2025). The survey fol-
lows a consistent design, where each participant is 
interviewed about their travel activities over a 24-
hour period, spanning from 3:00 AM on the inter-
view day to 3:00 AM the following day. Respond-
ents are randomly selected throughout the year, and 
while there is a small chance of the same person 
being selected in multiple years, this occurrence is 
rare. The most recent dataset covers the period 
from 2006 to 2024, with this thesis primarily focus-
ing on data from the year 2024. 
The TU data records travel behavior at multiple 
levels. At the Session ID level, it captures individ-
ual demographic information and a summary of 
daily travel behavior. Each session can include 
multiple journeys, where a journey is defined as a 
round trip that starts and ends at home. Within each 
journey, there may be several trips, each represent-
ing travel between two points with a specific pur-
pose (e.g., commuting, education). Furthermore, 
each trip may consist of multiple stages, with each 
stage involving a single type of transportation 
mode. (The structure of survey data levels is illus-
trated in Supporting Information Figure 26.) 
For this analysis, data is primarily drawn from the 
trip level and session ID level. These levels provide 
key information such as transportation modes, 
travel distances, and trip purposes (from the trip 
level), as well as demographic and contextual data 
like income, parking availability, and distance to 
the nearest station (from the session ID level). 
It is worth noting that while the TU dataset is rich 
and detailed in its coverage of domestic travel, it 
offers limited information on international travel—
such as air or cruise journeys—which may result in 
the underrepresentation of long-distance travel 
emissions in the analysis. 
2.1.2 Statistical analysis of transportation 
data  
Travel length 
In 2024, a total of 11,686 individuals participated 
in the TU data. Among them, 9,707 interviewees 
recorded non-zero travel distances, indicating that 
they made at least one trip during the 24-hour sur-
vey period. The remaining 1,979 interviewees did 
not report any travel, suggesting they did not leave 
their residence on the day of the interview. 
In this section, we analyze each interviewee’s mul-
timodal travel distance, as well as their travel dis-
tances categorized by purpose, to gain insights into 
transportation mode usage. The total travel dis-
tance for an individual is calculated as the sum of 
distances traveled using all transportation modes 
throughout the day. Similarly, the travel distance 
for a specific purpose represents the sum of dis-
tances using all applicable transportation modes as-
sociated with that particular purpose (e.g., com-
muting, education, leisure). 
By visualizing these travel patterns, we aim to bet-
ter understand the distribution of travel behavior 
across different purposes and modes, and how in-
dividuals utilize various transport tools throughout 
a typical day. 
Relevant variables for transportation mode 
choice 
Furthermore, we aim to explore the factors that in-
fluence individuals' choice of transportation modes 
using multiple linear regression models. In this 
analysis, travel modes are grouped into three main 
categories based on the initial classifications used 
in the TU data (Christiansen, 2024):  
• 
non-motorized modes, 
• 
private motorized modes, 
• 
public motorized modes.

Certain sociodemographic and household charac-
teristics are known to influence travel behavior, as 
noted by Frank et al. (2010). Building on this, we 
incorporate additional potentially relevant varia-
bles to provide a broader perspective on the factors 
that may affect individuals’ transportation mode 
choices.  
In total, 16 variables are selected to investigate the 
factors influencing travel mode categories. These 
variables are grouped into five thematic aspects: 
personal 
attributes, 
travel-related 
conditions, 
household characteristics, work-related conditions, 
and day type. 
• 
Personal attributes includes: sex, age, income 
level, and occupation. 
• 
Travel-related conditions includes: distance 
from home to the nearest transit station, 
straight-line distance to destination, and own-
ership of a bicycle, travel card, or driver’s li-
cense.

• 
Household characteristics includes: number 
of cars, car ownership status, household size, 
and availability of home parking. 
• 
Work-related conditions include: availability 
of parking at the workplace and the number of 
commuting days per week. 
• 
Interview day type refers to the type of day on 
which the interview was conducted (e.g., 
weekday, Saturday, or public holiday). 
To ensure consistency and data completeness, only 
respondents with valid records for all 16 variables 
are included in the analysis. Data from the 2023 
and 2024 TU dataset are combined to increase the 
sample size, resulting in a final dataset of 7,148 in-
terviewees who meet the inclusion criteria. 
Two approaches are used to construct multiple lin-
ear regression models. The first is a full model ap-
proach, which includes all variables in the initial 
regression. The second is a forward selection 
method, which incrementally adds relevant varia-
bles one at a time, helping to reduce the risk of 
overfitting. 
By applying both methods, we are able to cross-
validate the results, ensuring that the findings are 
consistent and robust. This dual-approach strategy 
strengthens the reliability of the conclusions drawn 
from the analysis. 
Prior to statistical analysis, some variables were 
converted into dummy variables because they rep-
resent categorical data that do not have inherent nu-
merical meaning and cannot directly explain the re-
lationship with travel distance or with each other 
(Grotenhuis & Thijs, n.d.). These dummy variables 
were coded with values of 0 and 1. Variables such 
as sex, bicycle ownership, travel card status, 
driver’s license status, and whether parking is paid 
were all transformed into binary dummy variables. 
Other variables—such as income level, home park-
ing situation, workplace parking availability, and 
day type—were converted into multiple dummy 
variables due to having more than two categories. 
The reference (baseline) categories used were: in-
come below 100,000DKK, parking on the street at 
home, rarely available parking at the workplace, 
and workdays. 
In statistical analysis, a p-value less than 0.05 indi-
cates statistical significance, meaning there is a low 
probability that the observed relationship occurred 
by chance. The symbol β represents the regression 
coefficient of each independent variable, reflecting 
the strength and direction of its relationship with 
the dependent variable. To assess the relative im-
portance of each predictor, we use ΔR², which rep-
resents the squared semi-partial correlation. Specif-
ically, ΔR² is the change in the model’s R² value 
when a single predictor is removed from a model 
that includes all significant predictors. A larger ΔR² 
indicates a greater unique contribution of that vari-
able to explaining the variance in the dependent 
variable (SPSS Inc.,2008). 
This measure helps in comparing the relative im-
portance of different independent variables in pre-
dicting the outcome.

Environmental impact of transporta-
tion  
2.2.1 Match TU data with Ecoinvent data-
base 
The Ecoinvent database (Ecoinvent, 2025) is used 
to link the TU data to corresponding environmental 
impacts, as it provides comprehensive life cycle in-
ventory data across various sectors. This study ap-
plies the latest version of the database, Ecoinvent 
3.11 using the cut-off method which indicates re-
cycling materials are burden free in terms of envi-
ronmental impact (Ecoinvent Support, 2025). The 
mapping between transportation modes and the 
Ecoinvent database is presented in Table 9 of Sup-
porting Information. 
In addition to travel distance, this study also con-
siders the fuel type, model year of vehicles, and the 
presence of co-passengers in the same vehicle. The 
model year is used to align each vehicle with the 
appropriate Euro emissions standard (Wikipedia, 
2025), while fuel type helps capture variations in 
environmental impact across fuel categories. For

all electric modes, the electricity supply chain is re-
placed with the corresponding Danish dataset from 
the Ecoinvent database.  
The Ecoinvent database reports data for bicycles 
and public transportation in person-kilometers, 
while data for cars, vans, and lorries are provided 
in vehicle-kilometers. Since this study focuses on 
individual travel patterns, it is necessary to attribute 
the environmental impacts at the personal level. In 
the TU documentation, there is information on "fel-
lows", which is defined using a purpose term rather 
than the exact number of passengers in the vehicle 
(Christiansen, 2024). To more accurately reflect 
real-world vehicle occupancy—where cars often 
carry more than one person—we interpret "fel-
lows" as additional passengers beyond the inter-
viewee, with most records showing fewer than four 
passengers. This adjustment helps improve the re-
alism of emissions calculations for private vehicle 
use.  
Besides, the following assumptions were also ap-
plied: 
• 
Since the Ecoinvent database only includes 
Euro 3, 4, and 5, vehicles with a model year 
prior to 2006 (the introduction of Euro 4) are 
assigned Euro 3 standards. Vehicles with a 
model year later than 2011 (the introduction of 
Euro 5) are assumed to comply with Euro 5. 
• 
Certain transportation modes, such as walking, 
skateboarding, and horseback riding, are as-
sumed to have no environmental impact, and 
are therefore excluded from Ecoinvent match-
ing. 
• 
Passenger cars are assumed to be mid-sized by 
default, and vehicles with missing fuel type in-
formation are treated as petrol fueled. 
• 
For interviewees who own more than one ve-
hicle, the vehicle with the most recent model 
year is selected.

2.3 
Health benefit of transportation  
Regular physical activity is well-known for its role 
in preventing and managing noncommunicable dis-
eases. A significant portion of this activity comes 
from transportation-related movement. It is im-
portant to study the relationship between health 
benefits and transportation activities, especially in 
the case of Denmark, which is known for embrac-
ing cycling both as a recreational activity and a 
practical means of transportation. 
Transportation physical activity  
The absolute intensity of physical activity refers to 
the amount of energy expended during an activity, 
regardless of an individual's cardiovascular fitness. 
It is measured in Metabolic Equivalent of Task 
(MET), which is the objective measure of the ratio 
of the rate at which a person expends energy. One 
MET corresponds to the energy or oxygen used 
while sitting quietly (Wikipedia, 2024). The inten-
sity of transportation-related physical activity is 
measured using marginal MET (mMET), which 
represents the energy expenditure above resting 
levels by subtracting the energy used while sitting. 
Since there is a difference in MET consumption be-
tween drivers and passengers of motorized modes, 
a classification is needed when this information is 
missing. For individuals without specific records 
indicating whether they were driving or riding as 
passengers, we use driver's license status as a ref-
erence. If the individual holds a driver's license, 
they are assumed to be the driver; if not, they are 
assumed to be a passenger.

3.1.1  Travel distance 
Analysis of multimodal travel patterns 
Among the 9,707 interviewees who reported travel 
activity on their interview day, a clear pattern 
emerges in the relationship between travel distance 
and mode choice. As travel distance increases, 
there is a noticeable shift in preferred transporta-
tion modes: from walking to cycling, followed by 
a transition to public transport and to private cars 
or long-distance trains, as shown in Figure 1. 
According to Figure 2, 90% of total travel distances 
fall within the range of 1.6 to 172.3 km. Among 
these, the most common travel distances lie be-
tween 7.4 and 54.2 km, accounting for 50% of 
travel activity among interviewees with recorded 
trips. Only 5% of respondents reported daily travel 
distances exceeding 172.3 km. Additionally, ap-
proximately 25% of interviewees traveled less than 
7.4 km per day.  
Referring to both Figure 1 and Figure 2, the top 5% 
of interviewees with the longest travel distances—
those exceeding 172.3 km—primarily rely on cars 
and long-distance trains. In the moderately long 
range of 54.2 to 172.3 km, which accounts for 20% 
of the traveling population, cars remain the domi-
nant mode of transportation. Although public trans-
portation is also used within this range, its fre-
quency is lower than vehicles. Among the 50% of 
interviewees whose daily travel distances fall be-
tween 7.4 and 54.2 km—the most densely popu-
lated range in terms of travel activity—the greatest 
diversity in transportation modes is observed. 
To further explore modal preferences at shorter dis-
tances, the right subfigure of Figure 1 provides a 
closer view of travel distances below 40 km. Ap-
proximately 25% of interviewees travel less than 
7.4 km, with walking and cycling being the most 
commonly adopted modes in this range. Bicycle 
usage extends up to around 15 km, but becomes 
much less frequent beyond 20 km. The widespread 
use of bicycles highlights the success of Denmark’s 
cycling infrastructure, supported by strong envi-
ronmental awareness and a health-oriented culture.

yield similar coefficient of determination (R² val-
ues) across the non-motorized, private motorized 
and public motorized modes with values of 0.097, 
0.040 (or 0.041), and 0.148 (or 0.149), respectively. 
These results are comparable to those reported by 
Frank et al. (2010), who found R² values of 0.09 for 
walking and 0.16 for motorized transport when 
modeling and energy consumption of different 
transportation modes across travel distance. 
One possible explanation for the lower R² value ob-
served in public motorized modes is the limited 
representation of such trips in the dataset—fewer 
than 10% of interviewees recorded public transport 
usage in the analyzed sample (as indicated by N* 
in Table 6). This smaller sample size likely reduces 
the explanatory power of the model for this cate-
gory. 
The table presents only those variables with a p-
value below 0.05, listed in the order determined by 
forward selection. Across the three travel mode cat-
egories, three variables consistently appear as sig-
nificant: straight-line distance to destination, travel 
card status, and number of household cars.

3.2.1 Carbon footprint 
Figure 5 presents the environmental burdens asso-
ciated with various transportation modes. Walking 
and running are assumed to have no associated car-
bon footprint or environmental impact. The figure 
displays the carbon footprint per person-kilometer 
or per vehicle-kilometer, depending on the trans-
portation mode. The data are based on the Ecoin-
vent database, which applies the cut-off method—
excluding the environmental burden of recycled 
materials. 
• 
Transportation modes are arranged in ascend-
ing order of emissions. Bicycles, S-trains or 
metros, trams, and other trains all show per per-
son-kilometer emissions below 0.05 kg CO₂-
equivalent. Among these, regular bicycles of-
fer the most evident environmental advantage, 
with emissions primarily stemming from em-
bodied processes. For electric bicycles, both 
embodied processes and maintenance contrib-
ute significantly to their overall impact. 
• 
In the case of S-trains (or metros) and other 
trains, the dominant contributor to their carbon 
footprint is track construction and maintenance. 
For trams, however, road construction and 
maintenance account for a relatively larger 
share of emissions compared to other rail-
based modes. 
• 
Buses and motorcycles exhibit similar perfor-
mance in terms of total carbon footprint, with 
values around 0.11 kg CO₂-eq per person-kilo-
meter. Their emissions are mainly driven by di-
rect use-phase emissions and the energy supply 
chain, likely due to their reliance on fossil fuels 
(motorcycles typically use petrol, while buses 
operate on diesel). 
• 
For cars and vans, the emissions are reported 
per vehicle-kilometer. Electric vehicles (EVs) 
demonstrate a significant environmental ad-
vantage, with a carbon footprint of approxi-
mately 0.15 kg CO₂-eq per vehicle-kilome-
ter—roughly half that of fossil fuel-based cars, 
mainly due to the absence of direct tailpipe 
emissions. Additionally, EVs incur lower 
emissions from road construction and mainte-
nance, as they tend to be lighter than fossil-
fueled models in the Ecoinvent database. Their 
embodied and maintenance-related emissions 
are relatively balanced. Interestingly, the car-
bon footprint of the energy supply chain for 
EVs is comparable to that of natural gas vehi-
cles under Denmark's electricity generation 
mix. 
• 
For fossil-fueled cars with different Euro emis-
sion standards, fuel type appears to have a 
greater influence on emissions than the emis-
sion standard itself. Carbon footprints follow 
an ascending order from natural gas to diesel to 
petrol vehicles. These differences are primarily 
attributed to variations in direct emissions and 
the energy supply chain. Petrol cars, in partic-
ular, exhibit relatively higher emissions in both 
categories compared to diesel and natural gas 
vehicles. 
• 
Vans are modeled as large petrol cars in the 
Ecoinvent dataset and consequently exhibit 
higher emissions across all contributing com-
ponents compared to medium-sized cars. 
Overall, bicycles and track-based transportation 
modes (such as S-trains, metros, and trams) 
demonstrate a significant environmental advantage 
over other transport options. On average, the per-
person emissions from a fossil fuel car with at least 
two occupants are comparable to those from diesel 
buses, motorcycles, or single-occupant electric ve-
hicles (EVs). However, these values are still ap-
proximately three times higher than those of track-
based transport and electric bicycles—even when 
the latter are fully loaded with five passengers. In 
contrast, the per-person emissions of a fully occu-
pied EV (five persons) are roughly equivalent to

cycles, highlighting the efficiency gains from vehi-

3.2.2 Midpoint environmental impact  
The main purpose of this analysis is to compare the 
environmental impacts of different transportation 
modes. The data used represents a subset of the full 
dataset, selected to better highlight the differences 
between modes while still including most common 
forms of transportation. Lorries are excluded from 
the comparison due to their significantly higher en-
vironmental impact, which would otherwise distort 
the scale and hinder meaningful comparison with 
other modes. 
The midpoint environmental impact assessment 
considers a range of intermediate impact categories, 
rather than focusing solely on global warming po-
tential. A single mode of transportation may per-
form well in one category while being a major con-
tributor to pollution in another. Midpoint analysis 
offers a more comprehensive perspective and pro-
vides a more balanced basis for evaluating the sus-
tainability of various transportation modes. Addi-
tionally, the environmental impact intensity can 
vary depending on the energy type used by vehicles.

After calculating multimodal health benefits, the 
results can be compared with the human health

As shown in Figure 22, individual data points are 
arranged in ascending order of net health benefit, 
which is calculated as the difference between 
health benefits and health losses based on daily 
transportation activity. The following conclusions 
can be drawn from Figure 22. 
• 
 
 
The figure illustrates that approximately 40% 
of interviewees experience health benefits that 
are entirely offset by health losses caused by 
environmental burdens from transportation. 
Only the remaining 60% achieve a positive net 
health benefit. Cars, vans, and lorries generally 
result in negative net health benefits. This is 
due to the relatively low health gains from 
physical activity during driving or riding, com-
bined with significant health losses from envi-
ronmental pollution. Notably, as travel dis-
tance increases, the health benefits from these 
modes grow much more slowly than the asso-
ciated health losses—often resulting in health 
losses that are tens of times greater than the 
gains. This trend is particularly evident in the 
left portion of the figure.
• 
Public transportation modes on their own pro-
vide only modest health benefits. Their net im-
pact largely depends on how they are combined 
with other travel modes: when used alongside 
cars or vans, the overall net health benefit tends 
to be negative. However, when public transport 
is combined with walking or cycling, the net 
health effect approaches zero or can even be-
come positive. 
• 
Modes dominated by walking or cycling can 
achieve a positive net health benefit, meaning 
that the health gains from physical activity out-
weigh the health losses caused by environmen-
tal pollution. In most cases, the net health ben-
efit ranges from 0 to 1 avoided DALH per day

4. Conclusion   
This study is based on individual travel data from 
the 2024 Danish National Travel Survey, which in-
cluded 11,686 participants. Among them, 9,707 re-
ported non-zero travel distances, while 1,979 did 
not travel on the interview day. 
The analysis first examined individual transporta-
tion behaviors in terms of travel distance, mode 
choice, and trip purpose. It found that walking and 
cycling are the most common modes for trips under 
15 km, reflecting Denmark’s strong cycling culture. 
Bicycle usage is particularly prominent for short 
trips, supporting both environmental sustainability 
and public health. For longer distances (over 
15 km), private cars become the predominant mode 
of transportation, while public transport plays only 
a limited role as an alternative. Mode choice also 
varies by purpose: public transportation is fre-
quently used for education-related trips, walking is 
common for education, errands, and leisure, while 
passenger cars are used across most trip purposes 
except education. 
To understand what influences mode choice, mul-
tiple linear regression analysis was applied. The re-
sults showed that bicycle ownership is the strongest 
predictor of non-motorized mode use, travel card 
possession is the key factor for choosing public 
transport, and straight-line distance to the destina-
tion is the most decisive factor for choosing pas-
senger cars. 
Building on the travel data, environmental impacts 
were assessed using life cycle data from Ecoinvent 
(2025) and methods of IPCC and Impact World+. 
Carbon footprints and 17 midpoint and 2 endpoint 
indicators were evaluated. While electric cars per-
form better on climate change, they may have 
worse impacts than fossil-fueled cars in other cate-
gories such as freshwater ecotoxicity (FEC), hu-
man toxicity-cancer (HTC), ionizing radiation (IR), 
and land and marine impacts. Track-based modes, 
although low in climate impact, show elevated im-
pacts in biodiversity-related categories like land 
occupation (biodiversity) (LO-BIO) and freshwater 
ecotoxicity (FEC). Car-bon footprint roughly 
demonstrates effect intensity on 2 endpoints, eco-
system quality and human health. 
The study also evaluated health benefits from 
transport-related physical activity, comparing them 
with health losses from environmental pollution. 
Results indicate that while motorized modes typi-
cally result in net health losses, walking and cy-
cling can yield positive net health benefits—typi-
cally under 1 avoided DALH per day. 
Finally, an absolute environmental sustainability 
assessment was conducted by benchmarking daily 
transportation emissions against planetary bounda-
ries, using both grandfathering and economic allo-
cation methods. Survey data were scaled up to rep-
resent the national population using weight factors. 
The findings reveal that 20% of individuals are re-
sponsible for approximately 80% of the total car-
bon footprint. , with 54.5% exceeding even the 
most lenient sustainability threshold of 1 kg CO₂-
eq/day. Notably, this estimate may be underesti-
mated, as approximately 17% of the weighted pop-
ulation without travel record on the interview day 
take in consideration. This underscores the urgent 
need for more sustainable transportation practices.

5. Limitation  
There are several limitations to this study that 
should be acknowledged. 
• 
The TU data primarily covers domestic road 
transportation. Aviation and sea transport are 
rarely recorded, and thus excluded from the 
analysis. This may lead to an underestimation 
of the total transportation-related environmen-
tal and health impacts for individuals who fre-
quently engage in aviation or ship travel. 
• 
The calculation of health benefits is based on 
weekly transportation-related physical activity. 
However, since the TU dataset only includes a 
single day of travel records for each participant, 
weekly activity is estimated by simply multi-
plying the daily value by seven. This approach 
can introduce significant bias, especially for in-
dividuals with atypically short or long travel 
distances on the recorded day. 
• 
In the Absolute Environmental Sustainability 
Assessment (AESA), individual-level data are 
scaled by population-based weight factors. 
However, including individuals with no rec-
orded travel on the interview day may lead to 
an underestimation of the proportion of the 
population that exceeds sustainability thresh-
olds. 
• 
Additional limitations arise from the Ecoinvent 
database. For instance, it does not yet include 
updated emission data for Euro 6 standard cars, 
and it only provides data for diesel-powered 
buses—potentially skewing results for electric 
bus emissions. 
• 
Car and van emissions in Ecoinvent are re-
ported per vehicle-kilometer, not per person. 
To estimate individual-level environmental 
impacts, this study accounts for co-occupants 
by interpreting “fellows” as additional passen-
gers. However, in the TU data, “fellows” are 
defined by trip purpose rather than the exact 
number of passengers (Christiansen, 2024), 
which may introduce uncertainty and potential 
bias in the individual-level emission estimates. 
• 
Moreover, the baseline physical activity used 
in this study is based on the global average 
level of physical activity and the average trans-
portation-related activity among interviewees. 
Ideally, it should be calculated using national-
level data that reflects Denmark’s average 
physical activity and typical transportation be-
havior. While applying a baseline helps reduce 
estimation errors for individuals with low 
transportation activity, it may lead to overesti-
mation for those with higher levels of transpor-
tation-related activity. However, this overesti-
mation is relatively minor, as the slope of the 
health benefit function increases more gradu-
ally at higher activity levels.
