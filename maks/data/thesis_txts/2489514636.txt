Abstract
The common carotid artery (CCA) branches into the internal carotid artery (ICA)
and the external carotid artery (ECA). The bifurcation site is a particularly exposed
location for formation of atherosclerosis and occlusions, which affect the blood flow.
Atherosclerosis affects the arterial tissue, which results in stiffening and thickening
of the arterial wall [41]. The pulse wave velocity (PWV) measurement describes the
velocity of the pulse wave propagation along the arterial system.
Complex arterial branching systems can be simulated in finite element models,
which investigate flow patterns, pressure changes and boundary displacements.
This thesis investigates the effect of wall material properties on pulse wave velocity
and propagation. Linear-elastic and different hyper-elastic models are investigated
along with varying stiffness of the solid material. The aim of this investigation is
to demonstrate a correlation between material stiffness and PWV. The simulation
results are validated with experimental ultrasound measurements based on Polyvinyl
alcohol (PVA) cryogel phantoms. The phantoms mimic the surrounding tissue of the
CCA and are fabricated with the geometry and dimensions of the in vivo arterial
lumen.
The presented results conclude that the PWV increases with increasing stiffness
and decreasing wall distension. The PWV is estimated locally using the Bramwell-
Hill equation, although it omits to incorporate how the pulse wave propagates along
the lumen.
The finite element models of fluid-structure interaction (FSI) systems have been
proven to simulate results comparable to observed experimental ultrasound measured
data. Limitations related to the experimental setup and applied boundary conditions
should be reconsidered in related future work.

Resumé
Halspulsåren er lokaliseret på hver sin side af halsen og forgrenes til to mindre ar-
terier, der forsyner henholdsvis ansigtet og hjernen med blod. Åreforgreningen er et
specielt eksponeret område for åreforkalkning og indsnævring af karret, hvilket forår-
sager nedsat blodtilstrømning. Åreforkalkning øger stivhed og tykkelse af karvæggen
[41]. Puls bølge hastigheden er et estimat der beskriver hastigheden af puls bølge
udbredelse langs blodkar.
Komplekse in vivo karforgreningssystemer kan simuleres som elementmetode mod-
eller, hvor gennemstrømningsmønstre, trykændringer og materialeforskydninger un-
dersøges.
Dette speciale projekt undersøger effekten af karvæggens egenskaber på puls bølge
hastigheden og udbredelsen. Lineærelastiske og hyperelastiske modeller undersøges
med varierende stivhed af faststoffet. Formålet er at demonstrere en sammenhæng
mellem materielstivhed og puls bølge hastigheden. Simuleringsresultaterne valideres
med eksperimentelle ultralydsmålinger baseret på polyvinyl alcohol cryogel fantomer.
Fantomerne efterligner det omkringliggende væv ved halspulsåren. De er fabrikeret
med patient-specifik lumen geometri og dimensioner, der svarer til in vivo strukturer.
De præsenterede resultater konkluderer at puls bølge hastigheden stiger med sti-
gende materialestivhed og faldende karudvidelse. Puls bølge hastigheden bestemmes
lokalt ved brug af Bramwell-Hill ligningen, der dog undlader at inkorporere den præ-
cise puls bølge udbredelse langs karvæggen.
Elementmetode modeller har bevist at simuleringsresultaterne er sammenlignelige
med observerede eksperimentelle ultralydsmålinger. Der opstår dog begrænsninger
i forbindelse med den eksperimentelle opstilling og de anvendte randbetingelser, der
burde genovervejes i relateret fremtidigt arbejde.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Cardiovascular related diseases are a common cause of death [45]. A chronic increase
in blood pressure can lead to hypertension, which can lead to geometrical adaptation
with thickening of the arterial wall [45]. Structural adaptation characterized by ar-
terial stiffening is related to atherosclerosis, which occur due to formation of fibrous
plaque [63]. Narrowing of arteries can result in occlusions and obstructed blood flow
[45]. The common carotid artery (CCA) is a particularly exposed location for the for-
mation of atherosclerosis and occlusions, where the vascular branching of the artery
disturb the blood flow.
Flow patterns and arterial wall behaviour can be accessed and investigated through
non-invasive diagnostic methods. Ultrasound is an easy accessible, non-invasive, and
non-ionizing diagnostic tool. Detection of abnormal flow patterns and wall distension
can contribute to early diagnosis of cardiovascular diseases.
Fluid-structure interaction (FSI) simulation models can be used to investigate flow
patterns and material response in complex arterial branching mimicking systems.
1.1
Motivation
Simulation models mimicking in vivo arterial systems can contribute to realistic mod-
els, where the system response can be studied. The relation between pulse wave ve-
locity (PWV) and material property characteristics, influenced by changes of material
stiffness, is investigated.
The effect of arterial wall material properties and parameters in finite element
(FE) fluid-structure interaction (FSI) simulation models is investigated, with the
aim of comparing simulation results with ultrasound measurements. The effect of the
material parameter Young’s modulus is investigated, in order to study the effect on
pulse wave velocity and propagation in simple and patient-specific phantoms.
The hypothesis states that it is possible to detect distension differences with vary-
ing stiffness of the CCA though FSI simulation models, where stiffness will affect the
PWV and propagation. It is expected to find an increase in PWV with increasing
stiffness, which will result in a decrease in wall distension. Stiffening of an artery
affects the elasticity of the arterial tissue [45].

2.1
Clinical background
The cardiovascular system consists of the heart, blood vessels and blood, which trans-
ports and distributes oxygen and nutrients to the various tissues of the body [45, 61].
The blood flow in the systemic circulatory system depends on a pressure difference,
which enables a force needed to move the blood through the system and a blood
volume that is sufficient to fill the blood vessels [45]. The pressure difference in mean
arterial pressure between the arteries and the veins facilitates the flow of blood [45].
The cardiac cycle characterizes the repetitive rhythmic pumping of the heart,
which provides the cardiovascular system with blood. The cardiac cycle is divided
into the systole and diastole. The heart ventricles contract during systole and relaxes
during diastole, where they are refilled with blood [45]. Pressure changes in the heart
chambers, which occur during the cardiac cycle, and the opening and closing of the
valves, determine the direction of the blood movement [61].
2.1.1
Common carotid arteries
The common carotid arteries (CCA) are branches originating from the ascending aorta
and are located on the right and left side of the neck respectively. The CCA bifurcates
into the internal carotid artery (ICA) and the external carotid artery (ECA). The
CCA supplies the cerebral circulation with blood, where the internal carotid artery
supplies the brain, and the external carotid artery supplies the scull and facial muscles
[32]. The vascular branching of the CCA is illustrated on Figure 2.1.
Formation of plague and development of atherosclerosis can contribute to occlu-
sion of arteries, which is often seen near the bifurcation of the CCA. Occlusions can
cause disturbed, turbulent, and reduced blood flow [26, 45]. Ruptured vulnerable
plague can lead to a thrombotic response with blood clots in the cerebral circulation
system, which can lead to embolic strokes and ischemia of brain tissue [32, 45].

A healthy CCA has a diameter between 6-8 mm with a mean wall thickness
between 0.5-0.7 mm [32]. The CCA is an elastic artery, which consist of the three
layers; tunica intima, tunica media and tunica adventitia [61]. Figure 2.2 illustrates
the structure of an elastic artery. The arterial walls in elastic arteries are flexible,
where the pulsation of the blood results in a expansion and contraction of the arterial
wall [24].
The intima is the inner layer closest to the arterial lumen, which consists of en-
dothelial cells, a basement membrane and a thin layer of connective tissue [61]. The
intima layer is in healthy subjects very thin, but can occur with increased thickness
due to aging and diseases [22].
The tunica media is the middle layer of the arterial wall (0.3-0.4 mm) consisting
of elastin, collagen fibers and vascular smooth muscle cells (vSMC). The structure of
the media layer is characterized by an elastin-dominated matrix with fiber-reinforced
layers, where the collagen fibers are arranged in symmetrical spirals along the axial
direction of the artery [22, 32]. This composition of the media layer enables elasticity,
strength, and the ability to resist loads in both the axial and circumferential direction
[14, 22]. The passive mechanical behaviour of the arterial wall is governed by the
elastin and collagen fibers.
The degree of activation of the vSMC contributes to
the active mechanical behaviour of the wall, and are responsible for vasodilation and
vasoconstriction when regulating the vessel diameter in respect to the pulsating flow
[22, 32].
The tunica adventitia is the outer layer and is composed of connective tissue with
a dense network of collagen fibers and fibroblasts, which produces and supplies the
media with collagen and blood [22]. The layer prevents the artery from over-dilating.

Disturbances of the normal pulsating flow of blood in the arterial system can be

Atherosclerosis is a cardiovascular

intima layer of arteries and may spread into the media layer, but often extends into
the arterial lumen [41]. The disease is characterized by the formation of fatty streaks,
which are often accompanied by inflammation, fibrin, calcium and collagen fibers,
which can lead to disturbance of arterial distensibility, due to the decreased cross-

increasing wall thickness, which results in stiffening of the arterial wall [41].
The mechanical properties and behavioural response of the arterial wall mate-
rial are expressed by constitutive relations, which depend on the micro-structure of
the material [56]. The arterial wall can be assigned different characteristic material
properties, which determine the behavior and response of the wall, when a time- and
spatial dependent pulsating flow is applied to the arterial lumen.
These material
properties can be characterised as being linear-elastic and hyper-elastic. The me-
chanical properties of strength and elasticity in arterial wall tissue is most precisely
described as anisotropic, where the properties are directional dependent [14].
The relation between the mechanical behavior and the micro-structure of the arte-
rial wall is fundamental, when predicting the biomechanical properties of a biological
system [14]. The relation can be investigated through experiments with the aim of
mimicking the mechanical behavior found in vivo [14]. These experiments can be
simulated, where the contribution from elastin and collagen fibers in the wall can be
implemented to improve the model to more accurately mimic the in vivo environment.

The flow of blood in the human body is generated by the pulsating behaviour of
the heart, where pressure waves are generated during the cardiac cycle. Blood flow is
represented by the cardiac output [45]. The blood velocity profiles are dependent on
time and space [24]. Flow depends on the pressure difference between the proximal
and distal end of a vessel, or a bundle of vessels, and the resistance the blood has
to exceed as it moves along the vessel [45]. Differences in blood pressure keeps the
blood flowing from areas with high pressure to areas with lower pressure. The max-

the peripheral system [61]. Blood flow decreases with increasing resistance and vice
versa. The resistance depends on fluid viscosity, lumen diameter and vessel length

blood flow through the vessel will decrease, whereas an increase in vessel diameter
decreases resistance and increases the blood flow [61].

2.1.3
Pulse wave velocity
The blood pressure propagates as a pulsating wave through the arterial system, which
causes the wall to dilate and contract respectively. The pulse wave velocity (PWV)
describes the velocity of the pulse wave propagation along the arterial system. Blood
velocity relates to displacement of mass through incompressible blood, while PWV
relates to the transmission of energy through the arterial wall [31]. The PWV measure
depends on arterial geometry and material characteristic properties [32]. Figure 2.7
illustrates the propagation of the pulse wave through an artery, where the wall dilates
during systole and contracts during diastole.

When the arterial wall is stiffened due to cardiovascular diseases or ageing, the
magnitude of wall distension decreases. A decrease in distension of the arterial wall
can therefore indicate an increase in stiffness and a decrease in elastic properties of
the arterial wall material. A decrease in wall distension is expected to result in an
increase of the PWV measure.
A decrease in distension can be a consequence of
increased arterial wall thickness and/or stiffness, which can be located globally or
locally [62].
An increase in thickness and stiffness of arteries is reported for cardiovascular dis-
eases such as atherosclerosis and hypertension, where a rise in systolic blood pressure
is thought to be related to an increase in stiffness of larger arteries [33, 45].
The PWV can be estimated locally or globally by investigating a small arterial
segment or a longer arterial segment, for example along the aorta. The PWV can be
estimated using different approaches. These measurement and estimation variations
result in a fairly wide range of reported PWV values, which varies according to age
and pulse pressure between 4-12 m/s [1, 29, 31].
PWV estimations are useful when estimating the degree of atherosclerosis or hy-
pertension due to the relation between PWV and arterial stiffness of arteries, where
arterial wall distensibility will decrease with increasing wall stiffness [49]. It is there-
fore interesting to investigate the appearance of a pulse wave propagation and the
accuracy of the PWV measure. This is investigated in this thesis by varying the stiff-
ness of solid materials in different models with simple and complex patient-specific
arterial geometries.
It is believed that the PWV measure has the potential to detect early cardiovas-
cular diseases based on non-invasive measurements [2, 41, 62].

ment approaches within the application of the pulse wave velocity measure.

2.3
Fluid-structure interaction (FSI)
Fluid-structure interaction (FSI) systems relate fluid and solid mechanics. FSI sys-
tems consist of a fluid domain solver and a solid domain solver, where the interaction
between the fluid and solid domains can be considered time-dependent fully coupled
[62]. Complex fluid and solid systems can be solved as computer simulated systems
of for example blood flow through a vessel system.
Complex FSI systems often include freely moving structures with relatively large
deformations [27]. The partial differential equations for the fluid and solid domain
respectively are fully coupled on the moving interfaces, which separate the fluid and
solid domain [27]. FSI simulations enable an investigation of the effect of changing
and optimizing individual material parameters. Fluid dynamics can be studied in a
FSI simulated model, where the system response depends on the material properties
such as pressure, velocities and flow patterns [12].
2.3.1
Finite element method (FEM)
Spatial and time dependent problems for fluid and solid domains can be expressed
in terms of partial differential equations [40]. The finite element method (FEM) is
a computer-aided technique, which solves the system equations using approximate
numerical solutions of for example FSI systems [27]. FEM can be used to numerically
solve the differential equations and approximate the real solution [40]. In vivo systems
are often composed of complex and irregular geometry systems. It is therefore an
advantage to use FEM when modeling human in vivo structures and systems.
The structures in a system is meshed by dividing the surfaces into small elements,
which can have different structure characteristics such as tetrahedral, pyramidal or
hexahedral shaped elements [40]. The accuracy of a simulated solution depends on
the mesh element size.
The software COMSOL Multiphysics can be used as a FEM modelling tool, where
the simulation aims at mimicking the behaviour of a real in vivo system or an exper-
imental setup as accurately as possible.

partly reflected and scattered as it propagates through tissues with different densities
and velocities [24]. The pulses are received as echoes by the transducer. Ultrasound
enables an easy study of tissue motion [24]. Figure 2.8 shows a linear array transducer

A simple estimate of the pulse wave velocity (PWV) measure includes the distance

This relatively simple estimation approach lacks accuracy, where the measure-

Alternative measurement approaches for estimating the PWV have been presented
and investigated in previous work, where various models predict an increase in PWV
with increasing arterial wall stiffness. A selection of previous research is presented and
summarized in this section. The numerous suggested PWV estimation approaches,
show limitations due to model assumptions and simplifications, which are discussed
within this section.
The relation between PWV and elastic material properties of the arterial wall was
introduced in 1920 by Moens and Korteweg, who introduced the Moens-Korteweg
equation
PWV =
√

2 · r · ρ

where E is the Young’s modulus in [Pa], h is the arterial wall thickness in [mm],
r is the lumen radius in [mm] and ρ is the density of blood in [kg/m3] [63]. This
formulation of the PWV measure is independent of the actual propagation of the
pulse wave in terms of wall distension, pressure changes, and fluid velocity.
The Moens-Korteweg equation has several underlying assumptions, where the
thickness of the arterial wall is assumed so thin (i.e. h/(2 · r) << 1) that there is
no noticeable change in the wall thickness during loading [41]. The geometry of the
artery assumes a mechanical and geometrical homogeneous infinitely long straight
tube. The lumen is assumed to contain an ideal homogeneous incompressible invis-
cous fluid, where inviscous indicates a fluid flowing along a solid wall without slowing
down due to friction [16, 47]. It is assumed that the arterial wall is isotropic and
experiences isovolumetric changes with varying pulse pressure [42]. The pulse wave
velocity in the tube is assumed without influence from reflective waves generated by
the arterial system [63].

determine the specific contribution from each parameter affecting the arterial wall

Materials and methods
The results obtained from the developed COMSOL simulation models are compared
to measured data collected through an experimental ultrasound setup. The experi-
ments include Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) cryogel phantoms, which mimic the geomet-

common carotid arteries. This chapter presents the fabrication materials and meth-

propagation results obtained from simple uniform structural geometries and patient-

and results, which the simulation models are compared to. The measurement results
give an idea of the expected outcome of the simulations. The models are optimized

CHAPTER 4
Model implementation
The pulse wave propagation through the fabricated phantoms is simulated in a con-
trolled environment with COMSOL fluid-structure interaction (FSI) models, with the
aim of obtaining results similar to the measured US results. The development and
implementation of the straight phantom model and the patient-specific phantom002
model is described in this chapter. The wall-less straight phantom model is modified
to include a wall domain, with the purpose of investigating the effect of different
arterial wall material property models. The contribution and influence of an arte-
rial wall domain is investigated by introducing constitutive modeling, where the wall
domain is given different material property characteristics. These properties include
hyper-elasticity using the Neo-Hookean model and the 2-Fiber-Family (2FF) model,
which includes contribution from the active and passive elements of the wall.
The COMSOL simulations are based on simplified models of the anatomic and
physiologic in vivo human system of the CCA. The computation time depends on
model complexity, which increases with increasing number of components. The COM-
SOL FSI models are developed based on a compromise, where the models aim at im-
itating the in vivo environment, while only the most important boundary conditions
and assumptions contribute to the simplified simulations. Figure 4.1 illustrates how
the phantom imitates the in vivo system of arteries. Simple validation experiments
are conducted using the phantom, which creates a system that resembles simple in
vivo measurements. The FSI models simulate the controlled environment created
with the phantoms. The simulation models are modified to investigate how changing
material parameters affect the system.

4.2
Fluid-structure interaction model implementation
The computersimulations are performed in the software COMSOL Multiphysics ver-
sion 5.3a and 5.4. The simulations are implemented as fluid-structure interaction
systems (FSI), where the fluid domain (laminar flow) models the dynamics of the
blood-mimicking fluid. The solid domain (solid mechanics) models the tissue mim-
icking block and the arterial wall domain. The concept of FSI applies to systems,
where a fluid exerts forces on a solid structure, which deforms the solid and results in
stress in the material due to the loading on the surface between the fluid and solid [36].
The CAD geometry models are imported to COMSOL as .STEP-files. Figure 4.4
shows the imported models in COMSOL of the straight phantom with and without
the wall domain along with the patient-specific phantom002. The dimensions are kept
in [m] and the coordinate system corresponds to the SolidWorks setting with origo in
the center of the lumen at the inlet plane. The fluid domain is indicated with blue
on the top left figure and on the bottom figure, whereas the wall domain is indicated
with blue on the top right figure. The tissue solid domain is gray on all figures. The
fluid and solid domains are fully coupled on the moving interfaces, which separates
the fluid and solid domain, in the fluid-structure interaction setting. The arterial
wall domain is simulated as a one-layer structure, which has been shown to provide
sufficient accuracy for the behaviour of the wall [30]. The radius of the arterial wall
domain is set to 2.0 mm. It has previously been shown experimentally at the CFU
laboratory that this wall thickness is the thinnest possible when fabricating a separate
wall in the phantoms. A normal CCA is expected to have a wall thickness within the

The COMSOL simulation models are characterized as time dependent models,
when applying a spatial and time-dependent velocity field to the inlet of the models.
COMSOL solves the Navier-Stokes equation for the fluid domain, as introduced in
Section 2.2. The blood-mimicking fluid is assumed incompressible [36]. Physiological
pressure changes do not significantly change the density of blood, which lead to the
assumption that the fluid density is constant (or nearly constant) in time and space.
This assumption in combination with the assumption of an isothermal system lead
to the assumption of the fluid being incompressible [36, 53].
In COMSOL the reference pressure level is by default chosen to be pref = 1[atm]
and the reference temperature is set to Tref = 293.15[K]. Blood is in vivo a non-
Newtonian fluid, but the blood-mimicking fluid in the COMSOL models is assumed
Newtonian. A Newtonian fluid is characterized by a linear relationship between the
shear rate and shear stress, which implies that the coefficient of viscosity is constant
at all rates of shear [42, 52].

Solid mechanics solver
The solid mechanics solver is applied to the defined solid domains in the models.
Different material property characteristics are assigned to the solid domains, to in-
vestigate the contribution and influence from the solid domains on the fluid domain.
This is investigated through changes in wall displacement, pulse pressure and pulse
wave propagation in the models.
The solid domains include the tissue-mimicking domain and the arterial wall do-
main. Linear-elastic and hyper-elastic properties are assigned as material character-
istics to the solid domains. The models are compared in order to investigate the
influence of assigning different material properties. The models are compared to the
measured US data obtained from the phantoms to study which model approximates
the US measurements most accurately.

4.2.5
Material properties for solid mechanics solver
The solid domains are assigned different material characteristic properties, which de-
termine the behaviour and response of the material. Respectively linear-elastic and
hyper-elastic characteristic properties are implemented for the solid material in order
to investigate whether or not this influences the response when the fluid acts on the
solid.
The isotropic linear-elastic model is the simplest material model for structural
mechanics [48]. A linear-elastic material shows deformations in the range of 3-5%
[54]. Arteries in vivo have hyper-elastic properties with physiologic distension and
deformations in the range of 10-17% [1]. A deformation of 10% for a lumen with
radius 4 mm would result in 0.4 mm displacement. These small magnitude displace-
ments might therefore make it difficult to distinguish between the linear-elastic and
hyper-elastic models.

4.2.6
2-Fiber-Family implementation
The Neo-Hookean model has limitations, where the model omits the contribution
from the histological details of the arterial wall tissue. The wall consists of collagen,
elastin and vascular smooth muscle cells (vSMC), which contribute to the mechanical
behaviour of the material. The 2-Fiber-Family (2FF) model includes contributions
from the collagen fibers, which are helically wound around the longitudinal direction
of the artery [22]. The 2FF model presented by G. A. Holzapfel in [22] is a detailed
non-linear elastic model, which can withstand stretches up to 30% [22]. The 2FF
model incorporates two layers of collagen fibers, which leads to an anisotropic re-
sponse under loading [22, 57].

4.3
Mesh convergence study
The solution of a finite element model is influenced by the element size and struc-
ture of the chosen mesh. The meshes assigned to the COMSOL simulation models
have tetrahedral element mesh structures. COMSOL has predefined sets of physics-
controlled mesh element sizes.
An appropriate mesh size ensures results with good accuracy with an acceptable
error. It is investigated whether the solution is independent of the mesh size.
An enhancement of the mesh element size increases the accuracy of the model,
which simultaneously increases the computation time. The simulation models are
constructed using a mesh element size, which is a compromise between accuracy and
acceptable computation time.
The mesh convergence study is conducted to investigate the influence of the ap-
plied mesh element size in the straight wall-less phantom model. The varying mesh
sizes are chosen according to the predefined options in COMSOL. The following six
different sizes are applied to the model; extra coarse, coarser, coarse, normal, fine,
finer. Figure 4.6 shows the straight wall-less phantom with the different mesh element

5.1.6
Pulse wave velocity estimations for the straight phantom
The presented results obtained through the US measurements and COMSOL simula-
tions show a non in vivo like propagation of the pulse wave through the PVA phantom
and simulation models. When using the US measurement data processing approach
[19], the dilation of the wall in the phantoms, turned out to happen simultaneously.
It was not possible to track a delay in the displacement between the proximal and
distal point at the wall [19]. The propagation of the pulse wave through the COMSOL
models is restricted due to the outlet boundary condition. Even though the propa-
gation of the pulse wave did not turn out as expected, when compared to human in
vivo data [19], the pulse wave velocity can be estimated locally within a point along
the wall [5]. A local measure of PWV depends on the wall displacement and pres-
sure change between peak systole and peak diastole along the flow cycles in one point.
The results presented showed a clear correlation between increasing stiffness and
decreasing wall displacement. The radial displacement results for different values of
Young’s modulus and the resulting pressure changes are investigated in order to es-
timate the pulse wave velocity.
The displacement of the wall depends on the pressure in the phantom during the
pulsating flow cycles. The change in pressure between peak systole and peak diastole
found in the simulation models is included in Table 5.2. The pressure values obtained
in the simulations are lower than expected, where the maximum obtained change in
pressure (∆P) is 849.7 Pa, for E = 1 · 104 Pa, which corresponds to a pulse pres-
sure of 6.4 mmHg. The pulse pressure is in vivo expected to be approximately 40
mmHg, which corresponds to 5300 Pa. These pressure results might explain why the

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This chapter presents a discussion of the obtained measurement and simulation results
along with the used materials and methods. The limitations regarding the experi-
mental US setup and results, due to assumptions and simplifications, are discussed
along with thoughts on potential future work.
6.1
Experimental US setup limitations
The pulse wave velocity measure depends on the chosen approach. The PWV is a
sensitive measure, where minor changes can have a significant influence on the PWV
estimate. The local PWV measure based on the Bramwell-Hill equation, which de-
pends on local wall displacement and pressure changes has limitations. This approach
depends on a few points selected along the lumen, where a variation in distension can
occur especially in complex geometrical structures. Local measures does not incor-
porate how the pulse wave propagates along the lumen.
The global PWV measure (2.15), which incorporates the distance traveled between
two reference points and the transition time, is limited by excluding considerations
of elastic material properties and complex geometrical lumen structures. This global
PWV measure is in vivo disturbed by reflective waves, which can affect the accuracy
of the PWV when investigating the distension wave form. Reflective waves arise due
to geometrical changes along the arterial system, where branching, irregularities, and
lumen narrowing occur. The forward progressing pressure wave is affected by the
reflective wave, which travels back through the aortic system. The reflective wave
can influence the amplitude of the pulse pressure, due to an overlap between the two
waves [31]. The bifurcation of the CCA creates a reflection site, which affects the
CCA proximal and distal measuring sites.
The US measurements in the experimental setup showed no distinguishable time
delay between the proximal and distal reference points [19]. The PWV will therefore
theoretically be infinitely large, when the displacements occur simultaneously. The
distance between the reference points is relatively small (1-2 cm) due to the size of
the transducer. Limitations in the experimental setup could contribute to the indis-
tinguishable time delay between the two reference sites. Reflective waves generated
within the phantom in the experimental setup could possibly explain the results.
In the phantoms, the outlet tube provides a change in geometry and material, from

78

where it is expected to see reflective waves. The bifurcation site in the patient-specific
phantom002 is also a reflection site. An example of another reflection influencing the
wave propagation in the phantom setup is signal noise generated from the sides of
the container. The posterior wall is close to the bottom side of the container, where
the free deformation of the phantom is restricted. Noise generated from this side of
the container might influence the displacement measurements.
In an ideal experimental setup, the phantom would float in a big water container,
which would allow the outer phantom surfaces to deform freely. The challenge in
such a setup is to ensure that the transducer is held fixed at the same location during
the measurements to obtain consistent data.
The presence of air bubbles in the experimental setup resulted in significant dis-
turbances of especially the straight phantom measurements. The homogeneous lumen
geometry of the straight phantom, with a low angle difference of the wall along the
length of the phantom, increased the accumulation of air bubbles.
The irregular
lumen geometry, which mimics the in vivo structure more correctly, showed less in-
fluence from air bubbles.
In the experimental setup the fluid flows through the inlet tube, the phantom,
and then the outlet tube. The tubes differ in material and size from the phantoms.
The flow is accelerated through the inlet of the phantom, which is expected to affect
the developed fluid flow profile. The length of the phantom is insufficient for the flow
to fully develop into a parabolic profile. The entrance length is not reached within
the length of the phantom. The simulations accounts for this, by reconstructing the
velocity profile using the Womersley’s theorem. This might partly explain the small
differences between the measured and simulated results.
The position of the transducer in the experiment is very important for the out-
come of the measurements. The transducer must be placed exactly at the maximum
diameter of the lumen. If the transducer is slightly shifted from the maximum di-
ameter, the peak velocity in the measured data will be correspondingly lower when
assuming a parabolic velocity profile. This will affect the reconstruction inlet velocity
field, which will then show a lower peak velocity.
A small shift in transducer position will also influence the magnitude of the dis-
placement, which is largest perpendicular to the displacement of the wall. Figure
6.1 illustrates the scenario of scanning at the maximum diameter and scanning with
a shift in transducer position.
The figure shows that the maximum displacement
component is found perpendicular to the maximum lumen diameter. When the scan
plane is shifted from the maximum diameter, the displacement measured is not the
maximum component perpendicular to the displacement movement, but perpendic-
ular to the scan plane. This is illustrated with the red arrow in Figure 6.1, which
indicates how the magnitude of the detected displacement is decreased for a shift in
scan plane.

In the ideal US experiment, the length of the phantom should be significantly
longer in order to develop a fully parabolic flow profile. The transducer should be
significantly longer, in order to measure the delay in displacement of a larger segment.
The transducer should be held fixed exactly at the maximum diameter during all

The phantom should be free to deform in all directions and the
contribution from noise within the system should be limited.
6.2
COMSOL simulation limitations
The simulated models are developed and implemented based on numerous assump-
tions and simplifications, which restrict the models from being identical to the realistic
in vivo human environment.
The tissue mimicking material is assumed isotropic and homogeneous, which is a
simplified assumption of the human tissue properties. The actual in vivo CCA sur-
rounding tissue consists of different tissue types varying in stiffness between 1 kPa to
1 MPa [15]. In vivo the internal jugular vein (IJV) lies parallel up against the length
of the carotid artery. The influence IJV, other arteries, muscles, bones and other soft
tissues should be taken into account for an ideal phantom and simulation model.
The PVA phantom material has previously been tested in a compression test setup
at Risø. The outcome of the test estimated the Young’s modulus to be approximately
3 · 105 Pa (300 kPa) for three freeze-thaw cycles [9]. The accuracy and circumstances
of this test should be considered. The test was only performed once, where the test
samples were all taken from the same batch of PVA material.
==PAGE:102==
80
6 Discussion
The Young’s modulus estimation test should be performed as a tensile test on
several phantom material samples, in order to estimate the actual PVA material elas-
tic properties. In addition, the influence of varying the amount of acoustic scatterers
(Graphite) could be considered. An increase in the percentage of acoustic scatterers
in the PVA solution has been shown to increase the Young’s modulus of the PVA
material [15].
When simulating the models with E = 3·105 Pa, the wall displacements were sig-
nificantly lower than the measurements with deformations in the interval of 0-0.5%.
The simulation displacements corresponding to what was found in the US measure-
ments, were obtained when optimizing the models by setting the Young’s modulus
to E = 1 · 104 Pa. These simulation results with a very small displacement when
applying E = 3 · 105 Pa are consistent with similar simulation models developed
at CFU [54]. J. Fromageau et al. [15] estimated in 2007 the Young’s modulus for
PVA material with three freeze-thaw cycles to be approximately half the parameter
value found by CFU, where they estimated E = 1.5 · 105 Pa [15]. This indicates that
the Young’s modulus for PVA material can vary significantly within different batches.
The relatively low Young’s modulus for the optimized simulation models is most
likely linked to the small pulse pressure results obtained with the simulations. It is
expected to find the pulse pressure approximately corresponding to in vivo conditions.
The pulse pressure close to the inlet is expected to be slightly higher than the pulse
pressure close to the outlet. This small pulse pressure drop occurs in vivo and ensures
the propagation and flow of blood.
The low pulse pressure most likely occurs in the model due to the boundary
condition of applying 0 Pa to the outlet of the models. This boundary condition
enables the fluid to flow steady out of the tube but limits the expansion of the outlet
in order to adapt to the deforming wall.
The simulated models mimic a segment of an arterial vessel surrounded by a
tissue-mimicking material. The cardiovascular system including arteries, veins, and
capillaries is in vivo a closed volume preserved system. The outlet of the simulation
models is a necessary component of the model, but does not resemble in vivo condi-
tions. The propagation of the pulse wave along the lumen is clearly interrupted, when
0 Pa is applied. In order to compensate for the 0 Pa at the outlet, the Windkessel
model should be implemented in the simulation models at the outlet boundary. This
will enable the outlet to follow the pulsating movement of the fluid along the length
of the lumen. It is expected to see a propagation of the displacement along the lumen
when this model is included. The outlet needs to depend on the fluid properties and
movement in order to allow pressures within the expected interval as found in vivo.

6.3 Future work
81
6.3
Future work
The FSI models showed promising displacement results when validated with the US
measurement results.
Future work should include a comprehensive study investigating multiple patient-
specific geometry phantoms.
The PVA material should be tested in a tensile ex-
periment in order to determine the exact Young’s modulus for the given batch. The
actual Poisson’s ratio and material density should also be tested. The patient-specific
phantom should be fabricated and molded with a separate wall domain, in order to
compare compare the simulation results with the 2FF model with US measurement
results of the wall behaviour. It could be interesting to investigate the behavior of
different tissue mimicking materials, for example agar.
The length of the lumen core in the phantoms should be reconsidered. The core of
the straight phantom should ideally be 3D printed according to the entrance length
of the fluid, in order to obtain a fully developed velocity flow profile.
The pulse pressure results, found in the simulations, should be validated against
pressure measurements in the experimental US setup. The change in pressure could
be measured as a part of the US measurements, where a pressure catheter should be
included at the inlet and outlet of the phantoms. The pressure catheter can provide
the experiment with pressure measurements, which could reveal whether the expected
pulse pressure of approximately 40 mmHg occurs in the phantoms or if the pressure
changes are closer to the values obtained with the simulations.
It is a challenge to introduce pressure catheters in phantoms. Local conditions
such as flow, geometry, and velocity profile of the fluid is affected by the invasive
pressure catheter [34].
The observed pressure differences in the experimental setup, when introducing a
pressure catheter, could be implemented in the simulation models. The pulse pres-
sure could be applied as a boundary load to the inner lumen wall. The stresses and
displacements within the arterial wall domain during the applied loads could be in-
vestigated.
The boundary condition for the outlet of the simulation models should be recon-
sidered, where the Windkessel model should be implemented in order to obtain results
with a realistic propagation of the wall displacement along the length of the lumen.
It could be interesting to remodel the geometry of the phantoms, for example by
introducing an occlusion of the lumen, in order to investigate the variation in wall
displacement and pulse pressure. A small region along the lumen could also be given
stiffer material properties, where the difference in displacement and pressure from
regions with different stiffness should be investigated.
An ideal optimized experiment would include data from the different tissue types
surrounding the CCA in the human neck region. The PVA material assumes isotropic
==PAGE:104==

and homogeneous structure of the tissue, which does not resemble in vivo conditions.
A CT (Computed Tomography) scan covering the entire CCA area should be per-
formed in order to segment and identify the surrounding tissues. The different tissue
material characteristics and varying stiffness should be considered and contribute to
the implementation of an ideal simulation model and phantom. It should be incorpo-
rated that the CCA is located very close to the skin surface compared to the location
of the lumen in the phantoms. The influence from surrounding tissue structures and
material characteristics should be considered e.g.
the influence from the internal
jugular vein (IJV). The propagation of the pulse wave in vivo might be influenced by
the IJV, where the wall displacement might differ depending on whether the IJV lies
close to the outer arterial wall surface.
Important in vivo factors, which determine and regulate the behaviour of the
pulse wave and blood propagation through the cardiovascular system, include function
of the left ventricle, endothelium properties, smooth muscle tone (sympathetic and
parasympathetic properties) and neural control mechanisms.
Simulation models are simple systems, which neglect to implement these neural
regulatory control mechanisms. It is challenging to implement these contributions
found in vivo, using simple parameters, and this is yet to be done in simulation mod-
els [2]. An ideal model would incorporate these contributions.
Factors affecting the mechanical response of in vivo arterial tissue include in-
evitable factors such as gender, genetics, and age.
Environmental factors include
smoking and obesity. These factors are also difficult to incorporate in models, but
should be considered during scans and PWV estimations of human subjects.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The development and implementation of FSI models have been shown to provide
simplified systems of in vivo arterial environment. The results obtained with the sim-
ulation models have been successfully validated and compared to experimental US
measurements with good correspondence when optimizing the simulation models.
The purpose of the study was to investigate the effect of wall material proper-
ties on PWV. The phantoms and simulated models show a wall distension within the
expected interval of arterial tissue distension of 10-17% [1]. The CCA mimicking sim-
ulation models developed in this study showed, as expected, a dependent relationship
between material stiffness and PWV. The results presented conclude that the PWV
increases with increasing stiffness and decreasing distension. The PWV is estimated
locally using the Bramwell-Hill equation, which includes a distensibility coefficient
depending on wall displacements and pressure changes. This PWV estimate omits to
incorporate how the pulse wave propagates along the lumen.
The patient-specific phantom and simulation model emphasize the importance of
lumen geometry when estimating PWV non-invasively. The patient-specific study
concludes that the flow and velocity profile at the bifurcation depends on the lumen
geometry, which affects the PWV measure when considering wall displacement mea-
surements.
The FEM models need further refinement in order to fully resemble realistic in
vivo environments.
Further research should be performed before clinical use of PWV measurements
is possible as an early detection of cardiovascular diseases indicated by stiffness of
arterial tissue.

Initial Project plan
This initial project plan describes the objective and justification of my Master’s the-
sis. The thesis accounts for the final assignment for a graduate student enrolled in the
MSc Eng programme Master of Science in Biomedical Engineering. This project is
written at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) at the Department of Health
Technology in cooperation with the Center for Fast Ultrasound Imaging (CFU). The
project accounts for 30 ECTS points, which accounts for a project duration of 5
months during the fall semester of 2019 from August 1, 2019 to January 1, 2020.
Supervisors include Associate Professor Marie Sand Traberg and Professor Jørgen
Arendt Jensen from the Department of Health Technology at DTU.
The main project tasks and the learning objectives are described along with an
initiate time plan for the project.
Project objective
This project investigates the stiffness of the common carotid artery (CCA) wall
through finite element (FE) fluid-structure interaction (FSI) models, which study
the effect of wall material properties on pulse wave velocity (PWV) and propagation
in carotid bifurcation phantoms. The hypothesis states that it is possible to detect
varying stiffness of carotid artery (CCA) walls through FSI models, where stiffness
will influence the PWV and propagation. Atherosclerosis can cause stiffening of the
arterial wall, which is indicated by an increase in pulse wave velocity and propaga-
tion[35].
The effect of different material parameters is investigated through finite element
fluid-structure interaction simulated COMSOL models with and without the arterial
wall in a straight vessel phantom model and in a common carotid bifurcation phan-
tom model. The straight vessel COMSOL model is based on a 3D printed phantom
developed within this thesis. The relation between pulse wave velocity and mate-

rial characteristics influenced by changes of stiffness in wall material properties is
investigated. The simulated models are validated against ultrasound imaging in an
experimental setup using ultrasound imaging and through relevant literature studies.
The motivation for doing this study is to contribute with simulation models and
results of the effect of including artery wall characteristics on the frequently used
carotid bifurcation phantoms at the research unit CFU at DTU. Another motivation
for doing this study is to ideally contribute to the ongoing research of measuring
and predicting the degree of atherosclerosis and other cardiovascular diseases based
on PWV measurements and knowledge about the effects of wall material properties
regarding stiffness in arteries and especially CCA. Previous studies have investigated
stiffness of arteries using different approaches, models, assumptions and boundary
conditions along with arteries, mainly the aorta, from different species, mainly from
animals, to investigate the pulse wave velocity.
This study is relevant to obtain
knowledge about the effects of the wall material properties on the pulse wave velocity
for multiple human CCA. The models developed in this project investigate the effect
of different material properties, which most precisely mimics the in-vivo environment
and conditions for human carotid arterial tissue. These parameters and boundary
conditions are chosen based on previous literature findings and approaches.
Data
The carotid bifurcation phantoms, used in this project, are made in-house.
The
arterial wall and surrounding tissue geometry is constructed in the solid modeling
computer-aided design (CAD) software SolidWorks. This model geometry is used in
the simulation, which is performed in COMSOL Multiphysics version 5.3a.
Project tasks
Simple COMSOL models
Online tutorials and manuals have been studied in order to become familiar with
COMSOL modeling and simulation. Simple fluid-structure interaction models of a 3D
straight vessel with and without arterial wall and surrounding tissue are constructed
in SolidWorks (CAD). The model without wall is 3D printed based on the geometry
constructed in SolidWorks. The models are imported to COMSOL along with an
inlet flow data file, which depends on the geometry of the inlet. The propagation of
the flow is simulated in COMSOL, where the velocity fields and wall displacement
are investigated. These models are investigated prior to the simulation of the arterial
wall in the CCA phantoms.

COMSOL model of CCA
A fluid-structure interaction simulated model of the carotid bifurcation phantom using
fluid flow data is constructed in COMSOL. It is investigated how changes of chosen
arterial wall parameters, properties and boundary conditions influence the stiffness of
the arterial wall. This is done through analysis of changes of the pulse wave velocity
and propagation. The simulated velocity field data obtained in the simulated models
are extracted to construct a simulated ultrasound flow image of the model.
Validation of simulated models
The results obtained with the simulated COMSOL models are validated using ultra-
sound vector flow imaging. The validation is used to investigate the relation between
PWV and artery wall material characteristics.
