Abstract 
 
Background: The adaption to a circular model is crucial for reversing the increasing generation 
of E-waste. Lifetime extension is an effective strategy for reducing waste generation and reduce 
the high environmental impacts from the production of consumer electronics. Increasing repair 
is an important step in achieving this, yet it is found that current expenses for repair of consumer 
electronics for an average Danish household are minimal. Scope: This project focusses on users 
lacking knowledge about repairability of consumer electronics and seeks to help the user in 
estimating repair price and repair feasibility at available repair service providers. Methodology: 
This thesis uses a combination of literature review and qualitative empirical research, consisting 
of interviews with users of consumer electronics and repair technicians. Project: This project 
analyses the current state of repair of consumer electronics in Denmark. The analysis leads to the 
development of a support tool for estimating repair feasibility of laptops, targeted to be used by 
the average user of consumer electronics in order to improve the decision-making process when 
considering a repair. Conclusion: Trough the project a prototype is developed and tested against 
estimates from repair technicians. Though further development and testing are suggested, the 
general approach for repairability estimation shows potential as a simple, scalable solution. 
 
Keywords: Circular economy, repairability, repairability assessment framework, repair 
feasibility estimation, consumer electronics, ICT products, E-waste, WEEE,

1 Introduction 
Technological development undoubtedly has shaped the history of humanity and will do so 
in the future. Simultaneously it shapes the face of this planet, with alarming speed, as increasing 
amounts of electronic waste (E-Waste) are generated. E-Waste is now considered to be the fastest 
growing waste stream, with 44.7 Mt generated worldwide in 2016 and is projected to pass 50 Mt 
generated in 2020 (Balde, Forti, Gray, Kuehr, & Stegmann, 2017). The global system is not geared 
to handle the generated waste, and in 2016 only 20% of the generated E-waste was documented 
to be collected, while the rest was likely dumbed, illegally exported or recycled under inferior 
conditions (Balde et al., 2017). Europe is leading the way with the highest collection rates of E-
waste (35% in 2016) and has introduced regulations to bring member countries to a collection 
rate of 65% already in 2019. Though a market leader in the collection of E-waste, a European 
citizen on average generates 16.6 kg pr. Year, while the world average is 6.1 kg pr. Year (Balde et 
al., 2017). The growth is fueled by increasing access to an ever-expanding market of electronic 
products and devices, decreasing prices and a generally shortened lifespan.  
The circular economy, as an alternative to the traditional linear growth, throw-away society, 
have gained an increasing popularity. The circular economy fosters strategies that keep products, 
components and materials in a closed loop, minimizing the generation of waste and maximizing 
the utility of products. The circular approach to consumer electronics is promising with an 
estimated raw material worth of global E-waste of 55 Billion Euros, and even higher returns are 
expected from increased reuse, refurbishment and repair of products and parts (Balde et al., 
2017). To facilitate the transition towards a circular economy and releasing the economic 
potential, there is a need to increase focus on strategies for increasing material efficiency of 
consumer electronics such as recyclability, upgradeability and repairability.  
The European directive on Waste electrical and electronic equipment (WEEE), first introduced 
in 2003 (European Parliament, 2003), was partly aimed at encouraging the design of Electrical 
and Electronic Products (EEE) with increased repairability and upgradability. However, the 
positive effect it has induced has been debated (Cole, Gnanapragasam, Cooper, & Singh, 2019). 
Today there is a growing dissatisfaction among users of consumer electronics, since their 
products are becoming less repairable, and electronics manufacturers are accused of increasingly 
excluding privates and independent repair technicians from fixing their products (The Economist, 
2017). In the USA the Right to repair movement, is mobilizing many users and professional repair 
technicians, is lobbying for companies to change policies and local governments to enforce 
regulation. Currently legislation to enforce laws on repairability has been proposed in 28 of the 
50 states (Ackermann, Mugge, & Schoormans, 2018). Organizations like iFixit and the french 
electronics review channel Labo Fnac are working on scoring repairability of selected consumer 
electronics to make their repair more accessible (iFixit, 2019; Labo Fnac, 2019). The European 
Commision is currently working on developing a possible framework for assessing and labeling 
the repairability of electronic products (Cordella, Alfieri, & Sanfelix, 2019). Yet, most products are 
not currently being scored, and the knowledge of repairability of electronic products among the 
average user is limited.  
This thesis focuses on the challenges the user of consumer electronics faces when taking a 
decision to perform a repair on a defective product, and it seeks to develop a tool that helps the 
average user in estimating the feasibility of repairing their product based on the different repair 
options available. This involves considering the factors defining if a product can be repaired at a 
certain repair option, and how these factors can be estimated with limited knowledge, time and 
tools available for an average user of consumer electronics.

1.1 Methodology  
The project was planned in an agreement between the authors of this thesis and the 
supervisor under the title: “Increased repairability of consumer electronics within the circular 
economy” with the goal to investigate the possibilities in prolonging lifetimes of consumer 
electronics, through increasing the possibility to repair. 
The Design Research Method (DRM) was used as a guide to structure the thesis, and four work 
packages (WP) were formulated based on four stages described in DRM; Research and 
Clarification (RC), Descriptive study 1 (DS1), Prescriptive study (PS) and Descriptive study 2 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). For every WP a set of research questions were formulated: 
 
WP 1: Understand the current flows and challenges of repair of consumer electronics.  
• 
What is the current state of consumer electronics in Denmark today, in relation to the 
circular economy, and what options for repair of consumer electronics are currently 
available?  
• 
What is the potential for extending product lifetimes of consumer electronics through 
increasing repair rates? 
 
WP 2: Define the key factors relevant for increasing repair of consumer electronics. 
• 
What key factors are influencing the user’s decision to repair, and how it is related to the 
available repair options.  
 
• 
What key factors are influencing repairability of consumer electronics, and how they relate 
to challenges faced by repair service providers. 
 
WP 3: Develop and test a support tool for increasing repair of consumer electronics. 
• 
What current methods are available for assessing repairability of consumer electronics, 
and what users are they targeting? 
• 
How can a new support tool be developed for helping users of consumer electronics in 
the decision process to perform a repair? 
 
WP 4: Apply the support tool in a case study and evaluate the application and impact. 
• 
What is the potential impact of applying the support tool in the case study? 
• 
What can be learned from applying the support tool and how can it be improved further? 
 
To answer the research questions for each WP a range of research methods was carried out. 
A selection of methodologies was applied or developed and is described in the relevant chapters 
through the report.  
A set of learning objectives and a project plan was formulated based on the project goal. For 
every WP a milestone presentation was planned with a set of relevant deliveries. The overall 
project plan was developed using a Gannt chart and was continually updated as the project 
evolved. The initial and the latest version of the project plan as well as the authors reflection on 
learning objectives and the working process can be found in Appendix A.

1.3 Defining the Product Scope 
In preparation for the thesis a pre-study was conducted: “Mapping repair of consumer electronics 
in Denmark” (Haslund-Gjerrild, 2019). The purpose of the study was to define categories of 
consumer goods, map the repair rates for these product groups and estimate their potential for 
repair. A total of 14 categories were defined, and their repair potential was estimated based on 
technical, environmental and economic criteria. Of the 14 categories 10 of these were estimated 
to have a medium or high repair potential. To find a single product category the following criteria 
were set up to evaluate the 10 categories: 
 
• 
Must contain high quality products  
• 
Have relatively high product prices 
• 
Have environmental benefit from repair rather than replacement 
• 
Have products widely available 
• 
Repair services should be available 
• 
Have relatively slow devaluation of product (loss of functionality/value over time)  
• 
Include wear in use (Repair/maintenance in necessary)  
 
The parameters were used as basis for a qualitative selection process, narrowing down the 
selection to six categories. In addition, with the consideration of literature availability, the authors 
decided to focus on the consumer electronics as the product category of the project and more 
specifically on three types of products: laptops, smartphones and TVs.  
The category “consumer electronics” is defined as follows: “Electronic products bought for 
personal non-commercial use” (Cambridge Dictionary, 2019). Consumer electronics include a 
long range of electronic product types. However, in this thesis the term will be used to describe 
the three product types. Laptops, smartphones and TV’s are all considered to be high complexity 
products with a relatively high retail value, and the products are common among Danish 
consumers. According to Statistics Denmark 88% of Danish households own a smartphone, 86% 
a Laptop and 61% Smart-TV (Danmarks statistik, 2019a). For all the abovementioned there are 
currently repair service providers (RSP) available. The three types differ in the way they are being 
used. TVs are stationary where the two other types are transportable, and thus the way the 
products are used changes and are susceptible to wear in use. The authors found that the three 
product types fulfilled all the mentioned criteria to some extent. Furthermore, it appeared that an 
enough research on the relevant topics based on the product types was available.

3.2 Introduction to Circular Economy 
The concept of Circular Economy (CE) has been rising in popularity since the late 2000’s, and have 
gained a broad appeal, both in society and among policy makers (Charter, 2018). Also, in the 
academic world an increasing number of articles on the topic have been published (Kirchherr, 
Reike, & Hekkert, 2017). The concept is connected with sustainable development and responsible 
material consumption; however, a universal definition of CE is yet to be defined (Kirchherr et al., 
2017). The goal of CE is to move from a linear way of consuming materials and products towards 
a circular approach, where materials stay in a closed loop. A popular model within CE is presented 
by the Ellen MacArthur foundation which describes the flow in several circles within the 
biosphere and technosphere. Since the focus of the thesis is restricted to consumer electronics 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
 
 
[17] 
 
only the technosphere is considered as the use of organic materials in consumer electronics are 
limited. The model is depicted in Figure 2 
In Figure 2 the circular arrows illustrate possible flows of a product. The four circles cover 
several actions for creating a closed loop. Recycle, Refurbish/remanufacture, Reuse/redistribute 
and Maintain/prolong. The closer the circle the higher the value of the original product is 
maintained. This principle is described as “The power of the inner circle” (Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, 2013). Keeping materials in the loops longer and slowly moving outwards will in 
theory increase the material efficiency but also possibly reduce demand for new products and 
thus decrease the total flow of materials in the system. 
CE have gained traction among policy makers, and in 2015 the European Union adopted the 
“Circular Economy Action Plan” with the goal to empower the European transition towards a 
circular economy. The purpose of the strategy is however not only to ensure a sustainable 
consumption and preserve natural capital, but also to boost competitiveness, foster economic 
growth and create jobs (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2015). The expectation for economic 
growth through a transition towards a circular economy might be one of the main motivators for 
policy makers, and the potential is great. In a study by The Ellen MacArthur Foundation it is 
estimated that Europe will be able to increase the resource productivity by 3% annually, resulting 
in up to 7 percent points increase of GDP growth relative to current growth estimations (Ellen 
MacArthur Foundation, 2015).

3.3 Material Efficiency 
CE provides the overall framework for leading the way towards a circular system. However, many 
separate strategies for empowering the transition can be identified. Many of these strategies can 
be categorized under the umbrella material efficiency. Material efficiency means decreasing the 
total need for materials or maximizing the utility of materials in providing the same product 
(Tecchio, McAlister, Mathieux, & Ardente, 2017). Material efficiency is key in transitioning to a 
circular economy, and according to Tecchio, McAlister, Mathieux, & Ardente (2017) these can be 
described within three policy goals: “Reduced embodied impacts”, “Extended lifetime” and 
“Reduced residual waste” which all enables increased efficiency within the four value circles as 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
 
 
[18] 
 
described by Ellen MacArthur Foundation (2013). “Reduced embodied impacts” covers the 
production stage and with a focus on reducing material in amount and reducing the use of critical 
and hazardous materials (Relevant material content) and ensuring the use of recycled and reused 
content in new products (Recycled content, reused content). “Extended lifetime” covers the use 
phase with strategies as re-manufacturability, reparability, durability and upgradability. Finally, 
“Reduced residual waste” covers strategies for increasing the efficiency of reusing, recycling and 
recovering materials at the “End of Life” (EoL) phase as illustrated in Figure 3. All these 
approaches can be implemented and will be necessary for a successful transition to a circular 
economy. For electronic products specifically strategies for lifetime extension have been found to 
be especially effective in reaching material efficiency, due to the high impact of the materials used 
in the production (Prakash, Ran, Schischke, & Stobbe, 2012).

3.4 Lifecycle Impacts and the Potentials in Life-Time Extension  
To determine the exact environmental benefits from extending lifetimes of consumer electronics 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is the most comprehensible method. Several such studies have been 
carried out, some with the purpose of determining the breakeven point between improvements 
of energy efficiency and impacts from the production of the product. Two studies were found 
estimating the breakeven point for laptops. However, the results vary. In a study from 2014 it was 
found that the optimal replacement time was 7 years (Bakker, Wang, Huisman, & Den Hollander, 
2014), while in another study the breakeven point, when considering a 10% energy efficiency, 
was found to be between 33-88 years depending on different scenarios (Prakash, Liu, Schischke, 
& Stobbe, 2012). While the results varied the study concluded that the environmental impact of 
the production of a laptop was too high to be compensated only by means of an increased energy 
efficiency within in a realistic lifetime of a laptop. 
In a comprehensible study from the German research and consultancy organization, Oeko-
Institut, a comparative LCA for short- and long-life variants of washing machines, laptops and TV’s 
were carried out (Prakash, Dehoust, Gsell, Schleicher, & Stamminger, 2016). The study found that 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
 
[19] 
 
the long-life variants for both TV’s and laptops showed lower impacts overall impact categories 
covered, when compared to the short-life variant. This was true even though long-life variants

3.5 Consumer Perspective on Product Life-Time 
The consumer or user of consumer electronics will also typically be the owner of the product; 
thus, they have a significant influence on the fate of the product. This human perspective, 
influencing a product’s lifetime, is equally significant as the technical perspective and should be 
considered in order to extend the product lifetime (Sabbaghi & Behdad, 2018). Consumers with 
the pressure of external influences from their socioeconomic environment, are constantly making 
decisions that determine the lifetime of the product. These actions can be seen in different stages 
of a product's life.  
 
3.5.1 
Pre-purchase stage  
Consumer choice of a product is the first step that defines the product's lifetime. Products can be 
found with different price ranges, designs, functions and services. It requires an effort during the 
purchasing stage in order to ensure that the product characteristics are fulfilling the consumers’ 
needs and expectation. When a product is not fulfilling the consumer's need any more or new 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
 
 
[21] 
 
needs arise, it becomes more sensitive to be outdated, come out of use or replaced. This process 
is called obsolescence.  
 
3.5.2 
Use period 
Consumers can have different reasons to retire a currently used device. Based on the status 
of the device four types of obsolescence can be used to describe the reasons: material, functional, 
economic and psychological (Proske, Winzer, Marwede, Nissen, & Lang, 2017). All types of 
obsolescence can overlap and influence each other in numerous ways. 
 
Material obsolescence: Happens when at product becomes obsolete due to the failure of the 
materials, either optically if the visual expression of the materials is not appealing e.g. due to wear, 
or because it is broken and the function is lost (Proske et al., 2017). 
 
Functional obsolescence: It happens when there is a change in technical and functional 
requirements for products (software and hardware). High end products as laptops, TVs and 
smartphones, with a fast-technological development, tend to change rapidly and with it the 
needed functionalities. A high technological sophistication of a device might delay the 
replacement decision of the user, and therefore, increases the useful service-life of it (Riikonen, 
Smura, & Töyli, 2016).  
 
Economic obsolescence: It happens when there is a loss of functionality due to the high cost 
of maintenance, repair and from comparable low costs for new products. Economic obsolescence 
describes when the user is not willing to invest more money on this specific product and 
therefore, it becomes obsolete.  
 
Psychological obsolescence: It appears when the user loses the emotional connection to the 
product. This could happen because of new fashion trends, technical trends or for personal 
reasons. Consumers tend to make new purchases, although existing products are still functioning, 
thus causing a high resource consumption (Prakash et al., 2016). 
 
Besides the four types of obsolescence, Proske et al. (2017) defines three additional reasons 
of obsolescence:  
 
Planned obsolescence: When there is a deliberate and active shortening of the product 
lifetime by the manufacturer. This can be seen through weak points in the design that are known 
to break, or when products loose compatibility when new products or software is launched. 
 
Accepted obsolescence: When the manufacturers decisions on materials, design and build 
quality, due to strategic reasons, are restricting the quality standards to the minimum acceptable, 
resulting in the reduction of the products lifetime. 
 
Obligatory obsolescence: When regulations require replacement of security-relevant 
components in fixed time frames.  
 
Planned obsolescence even though it is a constantly discussed topic as it affects the life time 
of the product, it is not part of the scope. The reason is that from the perspective of the user, 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
 
 
[22] 
 
planned obsolescence is expressed as either functional or material obsolescence, regardless the 
reasons of appearance. 
As accepted obsolescence is part of the manufacturer design decisions about the product 
qualities, the appearance of it appear from the perspective of the user, as functional or material 
obsolescence. Therefore, the accepted obsolescence in not part of the projects scope.  
Lastly, obligatory obsolescence as it occurs by regulation influence, repair in order to extend 
the lifetime of the product does not apply.  
 
3.5.3 
Storage period 
According to Victoria Perez (Pérez-Belis, Braulio-Gonzalo, Juan, & Bovea, 2017) storing 
obsolete of EEE is also a common practice of consumer behaviour that affects product lifetime. 
According to the Danish Ministry of Environment and Food, Miljø og Fødevareministeriet, an 
average Danish family has a stock of 45 kg electronics, equivalent of 11% of their total owned 
consumer electronics, which are not in use anymore (Fødevareministeriet, 2015). Storing of used 
products at home reduces the time they are in use also called: the “useful service-life”. This is 
problematic as the products stay out of the reuse phase. In many cases the products stay out of 
use long for so long that functional obsolescence occurs and lose the reuse potential completely 
(Milovantseva & Saphores, 2013). 
The Waste and Resources Action Programme (WRAP) is a UK based charity organization 
conducting research on waste issues. In a study from 2012 on reuse of products, it was found that 
after 2-3 years, consumer electronics still contain one third of its original value, highlighting that 
not only they can provide use value, but also provide a high profit if they are resailed in this time 
period. (Riley, 2012) 
 
3.5.4 
Repair/ reuse/ dispose 
 The last stage is when a device has reached its end of life due to one of the types of obsolescence 
and therefore, is not fulfilling the user’s needs anymore. At that point, the user decision will in 
part determine what flow the product will follow. By choosing repair and reuse the product will 
return to use, thereby extending the useful service-life. Disposing the product will lead to the 
recycling or dumping of the product based on the waste system of the specific country.  
 
3.5.5 
Conclusion  
Understanding all the different ways a product can become obsolete is important for a successful 
strategy for extending the product life-time. Repair of a product addresses primarily the material 
obsolescence, by restoring material to an original or functional state. In the case of an upgrade of 
a product, it might also address functional obsolescence by adding feature or improving 
performance. While a repair less likely will address psychological an economic, a consumer 
electronic product will be vulnerable too all types of obsolescence, thus product life-time 
extension will likely not be achieved through exclusively focusing on repair.

4 The Flow of Consumer Electronics in Denmark 
To understand the scale of the problem with E-waste in a Danish context the authors decided to 
map the flow of consumer electronics in Denmark. The mapping is structured according to the 
four value circles presented by Ellen MacArthur (2013) and covers the total inflow of products 
and the flow through maintenance/repair, reuse, refurbishment and the end of life (EoL) with 
recycling and incineration.  
4.1 Methodology 
The map of the material flow was based on publicly available data from official sources, academic 
literature and material from major Danish companies. The findings were organized and analyzed 
based on three categories: recycle, refurbish/remanufacture and reuse. 
The database of the Danish environmental protection agency, Miljøstyrelsen, was searched for 
all material relevant to the topic, using specific search terms such as; “circular economy”, “design 
for repair”, “consumer electronics”, “WEEE”, “EEE”, both in English and Danish. This led to Danish 
Producer Responsibility System (DPA-System, 2019a), that provides official data for the collected 
Danish WEEE as well EEE put on the market. The free database from DPA-system was then used 
to find the latest figures of WEEE in Denmark. 
Statistics Denmark (Danmarks Statistik), was used as a major source of data. The online 
platform (Danmarks statistik, 2019b)was used as a source for relevant data using the built in

4.2 Recycle 
With the EU directive “2002/96/EU on Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment” (WEEE), 
producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) became responsible for the collection and 
handling of the products at the end of life (European Parliament, 2003). As part of the regulation, 
producers must account for new products placed on the market and products collected as WEEE. 
In 2018 Denmark was estimated to generate almost 25kg of WEEE pr. capita, the 3rd highest 
among the EU-28 + Norway and Switzerland, only surpassed by Norway and the United Kingdom 
(Urban mine platform, 2019). The collection goal set in the WEEE directive dictates that 45% of 
marketed products needs to be collected in 2016, and 65% in 2019, the Danish national goal is 
more ambitious: A total collection of 65% in 2018, and 75% for households specifically (Neidel et 
al., 2016). In 2013 the collection rate was 60%. However, a relatively large portion of products 
are still not accounted for, or handled incorrectly (Neidel et al., 2016). Miljøstyrelsen lists several 
causes of this mismatch including theft of materials of high worth and missing information of 
WEEE classification leading to WEEE sorted in wrong fractions (Neidel et al., 2016).

5 The Concept of Repair 
While the popularity of the repair acts, like the Right to repair movement, is starting to get the 
necessary critical mass of supporters, a need for a better understanding of the repair concept has 
arisen. For that purpose, this section of the report documents what are the general steps of a 
repair process, the different types of repair that a device can go through, the available repair 
options of a user and the stakeholders involved in each repair option.  
5.1 Methodology 
A semi structured review of current available solutions and stakeholders within “circular 
economy and consumer electronics” was conducted. The review was based on a collection of 
solutions already known to the authors, solutions found through literature as part of the first 
review, and general online searches on google.  
A matrix was created to organize the different solutions and was based on the 4 value circles: 
recycle, reuse, refurbish and repair/maintenance. The Matrix can be found in Appendix B.  
The research later focused on repair and maintenance solutions, these were explained 
further, and a stakeholder map was created, demonstrating the interactions happening between 
them. The stakeholder map and description were edited iteratively through the project based on 
knowledge gained from interviews and research, which will be presented later in the report.

5.3 Types of Repairs 
Repair on consumer electronics is taking place in both hardware and software of a device. The 
spectrum of repair includes repair types with different areas of interference, as it is presented in 
Figure 10. Each repair type is described in the section below.  
 
 
Figure 10 Diagram with the overview of different types of repairs for Laptops, TVs and smartphones. The relevant types of 
repair for each product is indicated with colored dots as indicated in the legend. 
Maintenance of parts: Maintenance is the first and most cost-effective way to increase the 
longevity of a device. Maintenance in the short term can prevent a device from a series of defects 
that might have an impact on the functionality. In the long term, maintenance prevents the 
appearance of defects that might require different types of repairs. As an example, in the case of 
laptops, a monthly fan dedusting process, prevents the device from overheating, which in turn 
slow the polymerization of the thermal paste and eventually the appearance of unexpected shuts 
down (T. Meleounis, Personal communication, November 26, 2019).  
 
Check or replacement of interfaces, connections, wires: Check or replacements of 
interfaces include simple operations that do not require a high level of experience to return some 
of the lost functionalities. An example of this type of repair could be reconnecting a wire that has 
been dislocated or replacing a broken wire.  
 
Upgrade of parts (enhance performance): Includes changes in the products hardware 
improving the performance. Upgrades might be needed due to changes in the users’ needs or 
because the device has become slower over time.  
 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
 
 
[32] 
 
Replacement of defective part: The defective part is replaced with a new or refurbished 
functioning part. The replaced part will in some cases be refurbished or sent to recycling (C. 
Schmidt, Personal communication, September 17, 2019). This repair type is preferred when the 
defective part cannot be fixed with another more cost and time effective method. 
 
Repair of part/ change of component: In this type of repair, the defect is detected to the 
component level of the defective part and is replaced with a new component with the same 
properties. It requires high levels of experience and skills as the repair technician needs to detect 
the faulty component and replace it. In some cases, like an integrated graphical processing unit 
(GPU) in the motherboard of a laptop, components are so small and the part so complex that the 
process can be extremely time consuming and need highly specialized and expensive equipment. 
From an interview with the RSP Service Center Gruppen, it was stated that such equipment could 
cost up to 200.000 DKK and would never pay to invest in (C. Schmidt, Personal communication, 
September 17, 2019).  
 
Software maintenance: Software maintenance is a regular process that is necessary to 
ensure that the device operates optimally. It can be done automatically by specialized software 
or manually and can include erasing unnecessary files, deleting malware, applying the necessary 
settings and adjusting the requirements of operating software.  
 
Upgrade of software: Software is constantly evolving; thus, devices need to stay updated in 
order to have the optimal functionality. Updates eliminate detected errors from previous versions 
of the software and introduce new futures or enable compatibility with new technologies and 
software’s. An updated device can avoid defects that might affect hardware or the overall 
experience of the user. New versions of operating systems are programmed with a focus on newer 
devices, which in turn might put older devices to their limits if the software is updated. Thus, the 
appropriate software needs to be installed in each device to achieve the optimal operational 
performance (Farman, 2017).  
 
Interconnection between hardware and software: In electronic devices, hardware and 
software are inextricably linked together. The operation of the system is based on the hardware 
architecture of the device, and how well the software takes advantage of this hardware. Based on 
that, an occurrence of a defect can have origin in both hardware and software. While hardware is 
static, meaning it cannot change unless it gets replaced, defects can be fixed with interventions in 
the software. When the software cannot overcome a defect automatically, a manual process can 
give the solution, by adding the appropriate software, e.g. installing the right driver for a part to 
make it compatible with software or other parts. Software can also protect the devices from 
defects that might occur in hardware as a response to a certain use, an example could be 
overheating the GPU through extensive use. Software might also be used to detect certain 
malfunctions or defects in hardware.  
5.3.1 
Scope of types of repairs 
Software repairs will not be analyzed further in this thesis. Even though software part of the 
repair is considered an important element of a product’s life extension, it introduces a whole new 
dimension in defining repairability, therefore, it was decided to focus on hardware repairs. 
Furthermore, it was chosen to focus on the time where the defect has already appeared and there 
is a need of action from the user, therefore, maintenance of parts was excluded. Finally, as repairs 
on component level was identified to be highly complex for the three product types in focus, and 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
 
 
[33] 
 
few of the interviewed RSP’s were able to do this type of repairs, only the “upgrade of parts” and 
the “replacement of defective part” are investigated in depth. 
5.4 Repair Options and Stakeholders  
 
5.4.1 
Authorized repair service providers 
Authorized repair service providers (ARSPs) are qualified to service products under warranty. 
They conduct an agreement with one or multiple manufacturers, in order to provide repair 
service in a specific area if they meet the manufacturer standards and obligations. In most cases 
an ARSP requires to pass a certification exam in order to arrange or to extend the agreement. 
ARSPs benefit from an authorization agreement by getting access to perform warranty repairs 
and access to manufacturer support and technical materials. Some manufacturers give exclusive 
rights for a single ARSP and service conducted by anyone service provider typically voids the 
warranty (Rouse, 2017). 
 
Besides the businesses that provide authorized repair services, other stakeholders are 
involved in the sphere of the repair option. Stakeholders are explained underneath and are 
illustrated in Figure 11: 
 
• 
Manufacturer: The three terms: manufacturer, OEM and vendor overlap in multinational 
companies, the term in this report is defined as: The company designing and producing a 
specific product. 
• 
OEM: The company making the original parts used by manufacturers. Parts are sold only 
to the manufacturer for the period of the OEM agreement. (“OEM Agreement | UpCounsel 
2019,” n.d.) 
• 
Retailers: The retailer, or sales channel for consumer electronics. The user might choose 
to contact the retailer when a product is still covered by warranty. Retailers operate as 
mediator between the users and the ARSP as part of their customer service.  
 
5.4.2 
Unauthorized repair service providers:  
Unauthorized repair service providers (URSPs) operate autonomously, outside of the influence 
sphere of the manufacturer and therefore, they may have their own methods to repair and own 
quality standards. The repair technicians can be certified or non-certified, meaning that they have 
qualifications that meet a stated level of training and work experience (Electronics Technicians 
Association, 2019). Repair services with certified repair technicians can form agreements with 
manufacturers, that allows them to repair limited products under warranty or to have access to 
spare parts. 
 
Besides URSP’s, other stakeholders are involved in this repair option: 
 
• 
Spare part supplier: Are all the possible agents that can provide spare parts to service 
providers or individuals. The origin of the parts they supply, vary based on the product 
and the manufacturer. In cases where the manufacturer makes OEM spare parts publicly 
available, everyone can purchase them from official channels. In other cases, service 
providers use other sources of parts like reuse or refurbished parts, discarded OEM parts 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
 
 
[34] 
 
that did not pass the quality control of the manufacturer, or 3rd party producers’ parts 
(M. Nielsen, Personal communication, November 18, 2019).  
• 
Side job repair technicians: Individual repair technicians providing repair services, 
however it is not their primary source of income and they usually operate locally within 
their personal cycle.  
• 
Repair service platform: Connects users and repair service providers. The user can reach 
RSPs or side job repair technicians and negotiate the nature of the repair. As an example, 
the service platform Make It Good Again, gives the opportunity for repair technicians to 
bid on repair jobs posted by users (“Make it good again,” 2019). This approach supports 
the user’s decision process by removing hazzle to find the cheapest repair service 
provider 
 
5.4.3 
Repair events: 
Repair events are organized by and for local residents usually under the initiative and assistance 
of an organized community, well known concepts are Repair cafe (Repair Cafe Danmark, 2019) 
or iFixit clinic (“Fixit Clinic,” 2019). Repair events are held at fixed locations and pre-scheduled 
monthly time periods. The main objectives are to reduce waste, to enhance repair skills and to 
create social spaces. For this thesis repair events organized by Repair Cafe Denmark will be 
covered in more detail, here the term repair cafe will be used.   
Besides the event organizations, other stakeholders are involved in this repair option: 
 
• 
Fixers: Volunteers who participate in the repair events. Their task is to perform repairs 
of their expertise and inform, guide and assist the users with repairing their own 
products. 
• 
Facility providers/Coffee shops: They are the hosts of the events without being the 
facilitators. Besides cafes other public areas like coworking spaces, fablabs or 
multipurpose event spaces can host events.  
 
5.4.4 
DIY Repair 
In Do it yourself repairs (DIY) the user repairs his/her device without the direct aid of experts or 
professionals. People involved in DIY are often excited to get involved in different repairs, even if 
they are not owners of the product or they do not have any economic profit. The success of the 
attempt is affected by the device design, the experience of the user, the access to tools and the 
available spare parts. Products designed to enhance repairability like Fairphone (“Fairphone,” 
2019) with a modular design give incentives for users to attempt a DIY repair. Other stakeholders 
in the DIY sphere are: 
 
• 
Online communities, blogs, tutorials: Since DIY is based on the user’s skills and the 
available information, several communities and platforms have emerged, providing walk-
through tutorials and repair solutions. An example is iFixit (iFixit, 2019), an online 
platform where the community publishes repair guides and troubleshoots for different 
kinds of electronic devices.

5.5 Repairability of Consumer Electronics 
In the previous section of consumer perspective on product’s lifetime” the four kinds of 
obsolescence that potential can end the useful lifespan was described. Some of these were defined 
mainly by the consumer and the influence from society, while others were primarily determined 
by the external factors. Important external factors are the product design, service options 
available availability of spare parts which in combination determines the feasibility to perform a 
successful repair. The term Repairability or Reparability is used in several studies for defining 
these factors in a single term, (Bracquene et al., 2018; Cordella et al., 2019; Flipsen, Bakker, & Van 
Bohemen, 2017)  however a single definition have not been defined. Some definitions include 
aspects such as time and price, that influences the consumer decision to perform the repair, while 
others strictly focus on the technical aspect: 
 
• 
“The ability to bring a product back to working condition after failure in a reasonable 
amount of time and for a reasonable price” (Flipsen et al., 2017) 
 
• 
“the ability to restore the functionality of a product after the occurrence of a fault.” (Cordella 
et al., 2019) 
 
To include all the main elements defining the feasibility of a repair including time and price the 
following definition was formed: 
 
• 
“The characteristic of a product that allows all or some of it is parts to be brought back to 
working condition after failure in a reasonable amount of time for a reasonable price 
without having to replace the entire product” (Bracquene et al., 2018) 
 
The focus of the thesis is in between the design factors and the factors influencing the consumer 
behavior, thus the authors found the broad definition as proposed by Bracquene et al. (2018) to 
be most descriptive.  
5.6 Conclusion and Goal Definition  
Repair/maintenance of products is the preferred cycle in the circular economy, however rates of 
repair in Denmark are low and within consumer electronics, only around 1-2% of the expenses 
for new products. The strategy of lifetime extension for consumer electronics was found to lead 
to lower generation of WEEE and a lower GWP. From the analyzed studies it was estimated that 
optimal replacement time of a laptop considering energy savings were between 7 and up to 88 
years. Estimates of laptop average lifetimes of 5.1 years indicates that a reduction of GWP can be 
achieved through lifetime extension.  
There are potentials for improving the situation in repair, refurbishment and reuse of 
consumer electronics in Denmark. Furthermore, the authors found that the limited use of repair 
services in Denmark today, implies that special attention needs to be given to this area.  
When it comes to repair of consumer electronics the user is the central stakeholder. The 
decision to repair is taken by the user, with many factors to influence their decision such as 
availability of repair options, the product’s repairability and ability factors such as required 
repair cost and time. 
The connection between the two stakeholders, user and the repair service provider, was 
identified as key in understanding the current state of repair of consumer electronics, and how

repair rates can possibly be increased. The authors found a more in-depth literature review into 
the factors influencing the consumers decision to perform a repair and factors influencing 
repairability to be necessary. The research goals for the following project stage was formed as

“Identify key factors influencing the user’s decision to repair and how it is related to the

• 
“Identify key factors influencing repairability of consumer electronics, and how they relate

7.2 Factors Influencing Consumer Behavior to Repair  
The following section defines and explains several factors influencing consumer behavior in 
relation to product care, repair and refurbished products, with a focus on consumer electronics. 
Some of the analyzed studies found through the review were based on qualitative interviews 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
 
 
 
[41] 
 
(Ackermann et al., 2018; Weelden, Mugge, & Bakker, 2016) while others combined qualitative 
interviews and a quantitative survey (Wieser & Tröger, 2016). 
 
7.2.1 
Environmental awareness 
In a study on consumer reactions to refurbished products by Weelden, Mugge, & Bakker (2016) 
it was found that most buyers of refurbished products were not aware of the environmental 
benefits, however new buyers saw an environmental benefit as a motivation for buying a 
refurbished product. The environmental benefit is however rarely the main motivation for a 
purchase (Weelden et al. 2016). In the case of reused products, DBA found in their yearly report 
“GenbrugsIndekset” (The reuse index) that 31% of the participants in 2019 chose to reuse for 
environmental reasons, compared to 21% in 2018 (DBA & Radius, 2019). This could imply that 
the cultural acceptance towards repair of consumer electronics could also be increasing.  
 
7.2.2 
Perceived risk 
In the same study perceived risk played an important role in the user’s considerations for buying 
a refurbished product (Weelden et al. ,2016). In the study four risk categories were identified as:  
 
• 
Performance risks - the refurbished product will not perform as well as a new product, 
• 
Financial risks - that the refurbished product will not be a good monetary investment,  
• 
Time risk - the risk that the bought product will not be available for a longer time 
• 
Obsolescence risk - that a refurbished product will be outdated faster.  
  
The same risks can likely be applied for repairs of products that the user already owns, as many 
considerations will be corresponding. Similar findings were found in the study by Wieser & 
Tröger (2016), who mentions that the perceived obsoleteness of a mobile phone in some cases 
keep the respondents from attempting a repair.  
 
7.2.3 
Price of repair:  
The price of a repair is an important determinant for choosing a repair option over a new product. 
In the study by Weelden et al. (2016), it was found that for new buyers of refurbished products, 
the financial motivations were considered as the most important factor. Similarly, the majority of 
the subjects would choose a new product over a refurbished one, if the price was considered to 
be the same (Weelden et al. 2016). This indicates that in order to reduce the financial risk of a 
consumer, a repair should not surpass the price of buying a new product.  
The willingness of consumers to repair is motivated by the value that the product contains. 
Wieser & Tröger (2016) found that Apple iPhones, even though the failure rates for these devices 
were generally low, were some of the most commonly repaired smartphones, suggesting that the 
higher upfront cost and resale value a product has, the more likely a user will choose to repair it. 
Taking the above findings into account, it is clear that the repair cost needs to be part of a general 
strategy, in order to engage the consumers. This is also mentioned in a similar study done on 
“consumers perspective on product care”, where it is proposed that companies should find a 
balance between a high selling price and moderate prices on product care activities, in order to 
increase user’s motivation (Ackermann, L., Mugge, R., & Schoormans, J, 2018). 
  
 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
 
 
 
[42] 
 
7.2.4 
Time investment and product down time 
A relevant part of accessibility is time, as described by Ackermann et al. (2018) where 
respondents mentioned the time investment to hand in a product for service. Another aspect is 
the down time where the product is not available for the user because of the repair process. 
Weelden et al. (2016) found this to be a barrier for some respondents to buy a refurbished 
product, because of the concern that it might break more easily and therefore, the product might 
be unavailable for longer time periods. Wieser & Tröger (2016) found that financial cost and time 
for repair, was considered the most important factors for choosing to replace a product rather 
than to repair it. 
 
7.2.5 
Cognitive effort 
The knowledge of how or where to repair/maintain a product is also described as an important 
factor. Especially for electronic products, where the high complexity behaves as a barrier to a lot 
of users, when it comes to take a decision to repair. Ackermann et al. (2018) found that 
respondents had fears of ruining their product or hurt themselves, if they would try to repair the 
product, without the proper knowledge.  
 
7.2.6 
Personal connection - emotional value 
In the survey by Wieser & Tröger (2016), the researchers found that 57% of the respondents 
reported that their previous phone only served a functional purpose and did not hold any 
emotional value to the users. The researchers concluded that a phone will be likely replaced if it 
loses important functionality, without a concern to return the functionality through a repair as 
the perceived loss experienced when a product is replaced, can be offset by the perceived benefits 
of a substitute that offers additional features (Okada, 2006).  
 
7.2.7 
Expectations of repair quality 
In the survey by Wieser & Tröger (2016) two thirds of the respondents did not attempt to repair 
their broken phone, with many of them expressing scepticism towards the quality of the repair 
and the service in general and that the manufacturers likely did not make spare parts available.

8 Interviews 
The following chapter presents the methodology and results of a series of interviews among 
relevant stakeholders. The purpose was to supplement the knowledge gained from the literature 
review, and to identify if the same factors could be identified in a Danish context with a focus on 
consumer electronics. Interviews were carried out with repair service providers and users of 
consumer electronics.  
8.1 Methodology 
8.1.1 
Interviews: 
The studies were generally conducted as semi-structured, face-to-face interviews. The 
respondents were encouraged to express their opinions and explain their answers in further 
detail. This approach was taken to ensure the relevant questions were answered while still 
allowing new questions. For all interviews, the respondents were introduced to the purpose of 
the study and the estimated duration of the interview. All interviews were recorded for later 
review by the researchers, this was only done after a consent was given by the respondent. 
8.1.1.1 
Users of consumer electronics 
The interviews were held between two different groups of actors considered relevant for the 
study:  
Group 1. High involvement users of electronic products. The participants were assumed to be 
involved in repair practice, either through DIY or by using repair services (N= 6).  
Group 2. Low involvement users of electronic products. The participants were assumed to 
have a low involvement or no involvement in repair (N=6). Each respondent was given a code as 
an identifier, the codes (H1-H6) – High involvement users and (L1-L6) Low involvement users.  
The two user groups were distinguished based on the involvement of the repair act in order 
to study the differences in the motivations, concerns and level of experience. In order to collect 
the right sample for each category the following assumption was made: People that are 
participating in repair events, are more aware of repairing, compared to people that do not 
participate in such events.  
For the high involvement users, interviews were conducted at two repair cafes in 
Copenhagen. The organizers of the repair cafes were contacted beforehand with a brief 
explanation of the project, whereas the respondents were approached at the spot. The interviews

For self-repair specifically the learning experience was found as a motivation, while personal

the respondent from some or all forms of self-repair of consumer electronics.  
A set of ability factors were also discovered, factors that are important for a user to perform 
a repair. Most of these were related to knowledge about the product and the repair process. Many 
of these were mentioned in relation to their lacking capability to do a self-repair of consumer 
electronics, however the lack of knowledge also influenced the ability to repair at a RSP. The 
factor “Finding the right RSP” was mentioned as a factor to be able to choose to repair.  
The authors found that the general limited knowledge about consumer electronics and the 
repair process could be a key factor influencing the concerns, and thereby indirectly the decision 
to repair. Many of the concerns appeared to be connected with the product, as price, time and 
reliability are connected with the product and repairability of that product. The authors will argue 
that concerns arise when the user lack the ability to estimate where and when a product can be 
repaired, and what a potential repair implies for price, time and the product after the repair.  
Based on this finding, it was decided to investigate the available options for increasing the 
knowledge about repairability, which is presented underneath.  
 
8.3.1 
Ability to estimate repairability  
A user can have several ways to achieve more knowledge about the product and the defect, in 
order to reduce the concerns. From the interviews three main options were mentioned: To ask a 
RSP or a friend with expert knowledge for an estimation or by conducting own research online. 
However, some barriers were also mentioned in relation to these options.  
 
Online research: Since most respondents had little knowledge about the product, and repair, 
it is believed that online research is only restricted to a few users. Two respondents mentioned 
that they did some online research and discovered that the problem would be within their 
capabilities to fix. Primarily for users capable of self-repair.  
 
Ask RSP: The risk of paying an inspection fee for getting an estimation of the repairability 
was mentioned as a barrier to involve in a repair. One respondent had tried contacting several 
RSP’s, but all places were giving him a decline. However, he was not convinced that a repair could 
not be done, thus an estimate from an RSP is not always trusted.   
 
Ask a friend: One respondent had a successful experience with asking a friend with 
knowledge about the product for advice. This option is however relying on the availability of a 
friend with the needed knowledge.  
 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
 
 
[52] 
 
All the three options have a time risk involved in the process, that might be a barrier for some 
users, either stopping the process before a successful repair is achieved or before it begins. 
8.3.2 
User types 
Another take away from the conducted interviews was that the difference between the two 
groups “High involvement” and “Low involvement” in repair were not as obvious as it was first 
assumed by the authors. There are differences in the results. However, most of them are linked 
to the nature of the service provider or the product characteristics and not related to the 
responders.  
One main indicator of this observation is that both groups, rated their own abilities to perform 
a repair to be low, with only a few exceptions. Both groups did engage in DIY repair, which was 
assumed to be connected only to high involvement users. Furthermore, both of the groups had 
limited understanding of the technical aspect of their products, but they could easily describe the 
appeared defects, especially in cases where the defect could be visually detected. Motivations to 
repair were primarily similar in both groups, with learning experience and life view point to be 
mentioned in both groups, something that was expected to be related only to high involvement 
users.  
All the responders in Group1 were at the repair cafe for the first time, and several mentioned 
that they just recently heard about the concept. This implies that the authors first assumption 
that people attending repair events would be more involved in repair was not true.  
The products brought by people in Group 1 were quite different compared to Group 2. In Group 
1 many products were old or even antique, while the products covered in Group 2 were primarily 
newer consumer electronics. Since repair cafes are promoted as solution to bring old products 
back to life and extend the product lifetime (Repair Cafe Danmark, 2019), and is free of use, it can 
be expected that the type of products that will be repaired at a repair cafe is different than at other 
RSPs. 
The comparison of the two groups mainly highlight the similarities of the responders instead 
of emphasizing the differences. Out of these results, the classification of two types of users based 
on the involvement level was reconsidered and shifted towards a new classification of three user 
types, based on motivations, concerns and skills. 
 
Users interested in the learning experience from repair, tend to have more experience with 
DIY and with a high evaluation of own skills (H5, H6, L3). Following users with an intrinsic 
motivation to repair from a life viewpoint or environmental concern, but who do not necessarily 
have knowledge about consumer electronics and how to repair, in order to engage in DIY (H4, 
L1). Finally, for a large group of respondents the main motivation to engage in a repair, was the 
economic reasons and to retrieve a wanted functionality. For this group the potential new 
functionalities of a new device over the old one, can also function as a barrier when it comes to 
repair the former one. This group would mainly choose to repair, when it is considered to be 
convenient for them, or if external factors like limited monetary resources force them to do so. 
(H1, H2, H3, L2, L4, L5, L6).

8.6 Conclusion Repair Service Providers  
The interviews with repair service providers were done to increase the understanding of the 
work processes behind a repair, and the challenges experienced by different repair service 
providers. Based on the learnings from the repair processes a general flow chart was created and 
will be presented in the following section. The interviews only represent a single representative 
company for every RSP, thus the results might be different at other companies. With this in mind, 
the following conclusions were made.  
 
Challenges observed for all RSP’s: 
• 
New product design fosters changing of parts rather than changing individual 
components.  
• 
Price of parts are generally high as the number of parts are fewer and parts become more 
complex. 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
 
 
[58] 
 
• 
The availability of spare parts is a main factor for repairability, and is dependent on what 
spare parts suppliers are available, which differs depending on the RSP. 
• 
Low standardization of parts means that it generally does not pay off to keep parts in 
stock. 
• 
Low prices of new products, and the introduction of new smart features makes repairs 
less competitive. 
 
Challenges observed for individual RSP’s: 
• 
Only Repair Cafes mention access to tools as a challenge.  
• 
The type and quality of information on products for repair, is different for each RSP. 
ARSPs have access to information, the other service providers will not get access to.  
• 
The customer interaction varies between the RSP. Repair Cafes are the only service that 
values community feeling and learning experience. 
• 
IRSP’s and repair cafes relies primarily on the experience of the individual repair 
technician.  
 
Based on the findings it was concluded that the feasibility to perform a successful repair, will 
differentiate between different types of RSP’s, based on the product (product complexity) and the 
age of the product (availability of spare parts). Levels of skills and available work environment 
also changes depending on the Type of RSP.

9 Analysis of the Repair Process 
9.1 Methodology 
A set of personal requirements were made based on the categories of concerns identified and 
presented in Table 8. And a set of Repair requirements were generated based on the interviews 
with RSP’s.  
  
Based on the interviews with users a flow chart was created to visualize the general repair 
system. The flowchart demonstrates the flow of actions and processes through the repair system, 
from a defect occurs until the repair is terminated (successfully or unsuccessfully). The flowchart 
was created using standard symbols used in most flowcharts (“Flowchart guide,” n.d.). The three 
different user types described in chapter 8.3 were illustrated in the flowchart, according to how 
the three different types were defined by the authors.  
Another flowchart was made of the repair process from the perspective of the RSP’s. This 
flowchart demonstrates the path a technician follows during the repair process, based on the 
service descriptions generated from the interview with RSP’s. 
Based on the key factor identified “Lacking knowledge about consumer electronics and the 
repair process”, and with input from the interviews with users and RSP, challenges were 
identified and mapped on the process flow. Additionally, a set of barriers terminating the repair 
process, as well as loops in the system were identified by the authors and mapped in the 
flowchart.   
9.2 Requirements of Repair  
As discussed previously a repair can be performed if the repair is technically feasible and if the 
product user (the owner) decides to perform the repair with a certain repair option. Continuing 
from this understanding the authors suggest that two sets of requirements need to be fulfilled 
before a successful repair can take place: repair and personal requirements 
 
9.2.1 
Repair Requirements 
The repair requirements are set by the RSP (The user in case of a self-repair) and are to a large 
extent influenced by the repairability factors. Based on factors like spare part cost, ease of 
disassembly etc. the repair technician will set the requirements. Requirements can be different 
depending on the RSP:  
 
• 
Repair feasibility - If the product can be fixed at the RSP 
• 
Expected repair time - How long will the repair take from it is received until it can be 
returned to the user. 
• 
Expected repair price - What will the price of the repair be, including expenses for spare 
parts, and revenue for the RSP  
• 
Expected quality of repair - How long will the repair last and will there be a warranty on 
repair or the product. 
 
9.2.2 
Personal requirements 
The user of the product will similarly have a set of personal requirements defining under which 
circumstances they are willing to repair. These requirements can change over time depending on 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
 
 
[60] 
 
circumstances, such as economic or social situations. The authors would argue that the 
requirements are influenced by the user type, e.g. a DIY repairer might be willing to invest more 
time in a repair process compared to the two other user types. Likewise, the product by itself 
influences the requirements, e.g. the “irreplaceability” of the product will likely affect how much 
time and money someone is willing to spend on a repair, or how the functional value of a product 
will influence the requirements for product downtime.  Two groups of requirements were 
identified: Related to the user and related to the product. All requirements are based on the 
concerns in Table 8:  
 
Related to the user: 
• 
Time - How much time the user is willing to invest in the repair process 
• 
Repair cost - How much money the user willing to invest in the repair process 
• 
Repair skills - What is the skill level of the user in relation to perform a self-repair 
• 
Repair service - What level of service the user expects of a RSP.  
 
Related to the product: 
• 
Product downtime - How long will the user accept to be without the product 
• 
Repair quality - The expectations for the extended lifetime of the product after a repair  
• 
Product functionality - How much of the functionality of the product is restored after a 
repair  
 
As mentioned, it is believed the actual requirements will change depending on the user. 
However, they will also likely be interdependent, thus the requirements to time invested or repair 
quality might be reduced, if the cost of a repair get reduced as well. 
The authors would argue that a successful repair is more likely if there is a match between 
the technical and personal requirements, e.g. that the price, time and quality estimation set by the 
repair technician matches how willing the user is to invest, and it matches the expectations for 
quality and downtime.  
Ideally a user can make a decision based on how well technical and personal requirements 
match, however other factors and uncertainties about the repair requirements might hinder a 
well evaluated decision to take place.

9.3.1 
Processes  
Even though all three user types had the same starting- and end points in the system, none of 
them followed exactly the same processes. Each path contained a different number of steps that 
were translated to different time, effort and cost invested in the process. The more steps a user 
needs to go through in order to repair a product the more “exposed” he/she is to challenges and 
barriers that might lead to the exit of the repair system without a result.  
The DIY repairer, illustrated with the green arrow in Figure 16, can potentially follow all 
processes, since it is the user type who spends most of the time and effort in the repair, when 
engaging in DIY solutions. On the other side the convenient repairer follows the least steps 
between the defect appearance and the end of the process. That is achieved by delegating the 
process to a repair technician and thus limiting the processes done by the user.   
The convenient repairer follows fewest steps, as he/she will not go through the phase to 
increase their knowledge about repair, but rather go straight to an available RSP. Likewise, the 
convenient repairer will not try another RSP, if the first does not lead to a successful repair. The 
conservative repairer might try to increase their knowledge but will not do a self-repair. Both the 
convenient repairer and the conservative repairer are generally not able to asses repair 
requirements on their own.  
 
9.3.2 
Process Loops 
All the users go through process loops, at some point of the repair process. Each one of them 
increases the knowledge level of the user on the topic, however this comes with a higher 
investment of time, cognitive effort and potentially cost. Every loop also makes the user deal with 
more challenges and barriers that potentially can lead to the end of the process.

9.3.3 
Barriers 
In the chart, there are two decision nodes that can lead to a termination of a repair. In the first 
node a comparison between repair requirements and personal requirements can lead to a 
termination. An uncertain estimation of the repair requirements can function as a barrier, since 
the user won't continue the repair process. The second barrier comes from the RSP when the 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
 
 
 
[63] 
 
product is considered unfixable, affecting directly the convenient repairer and indirectly the other 
types, as it eliminates potential repair options and resets the repair requirements in a more 
pragmatic base. 
 
9.3.4 
Challenges 
Challenges are marked in Figure 16 as numbers on top of the process steps that were identified. 
This section describes how they occur and the effect they have on the repair process 
  
1) In most of the cases seen in the interviews chapter 8.2, responders could identify the 
product had a failure and describe when and or how it occurred, in several cases it was 
considered easy to identify. In cases where there is no clear indication, or the defect is not 
a consequence of a specific event, the user might not be able to identify the problem. As a 
result, the user can develop uncertainties that can increase the difficulty of selecting a 
repair option or how to proceed with the repair process (H2, Personal communication, 
September 09, 2019).    
 
2) Even though most of the interviewed users were able to identify the defects, only a few 
were able to assess the defect and estimate the repair requirements for the repair. When 
there is a lack of knowledge, the users tend to see an electronic device as a “magical item” 
that no one besides the manufacturer can fix, or that it might not be fixable at all (A. 
Nissen, Personal communication, September 12, 2019). This might make the users 
uncertain about whether to search for a repair service option or to proceed into the repair 
in general. 
 
3) When users are faced with the dilemma to choose the fate of a device, there is a complex 
decision process that can be summarized as the comparison between the personal 
requirements of the user with the repair requirements. When the repair requirements 
meet the personal requirements, the user will proceed with a repair, otherwise a repair 
is not a valid option. If a user does not have an estimation of the repair requirements or 
the estimation comes from unfounded assumptions, can create uncertainties that might 
lead to termination on the repair process.  
 
4) When users do not have a clear understanding of the nature of the defect and repair 
requirements, it is challenging to estimate what repair option is the most suitable to refer 
to. As a result, users develop uncertainties about the process, that might eventually 
prevent them from repairing. Alternatively, users need to spend time and effort 
researching for the available and most suitable repair option, a process that can be 
discouraging for a lot of the users (L2, Personal communication, September 20, 2019). 
Furthermore, in the event of referring to the wrong repair service provider, users can 
spend time, effort and potentially get charged without having a result, an event that can 
demotivate them to continue any further repair attempts (L5, Personal communication, 
September 20, 2019). The process of looking for a new RSP can be described as a loop in 
the repair system, that is illustrated with the red arrow in Figure 18.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
 
 
[64] 
 
5) Each repair service provider defines a set of repair requirements, that are often not 
known to the user until the time an offer is given. At that moment, the user might have 
invested a lot in the process as he/ she has gone through several steps. If the repair 
requirements do not meet the personal requirements or the RSP cannot perform the 
repair, the user must go through the same process again and refer to another RSP. This 
process is time consuming, but might also include inspection fees, which will prevent the 
user from engaging multiple times, and maybe from approaching an RSP in the first place.  
 
6) In a DIY repair process a user can go through different steps up until he/she starts the 
repair process, only to realize that his/her skills and knowledge do not match the 
products repair requirements. As a result, the user invests time, effort and potential 
money in a process that they could not undertake, leading them either to go through the 
same process again in order to increase the skills and knowledge level or leading them to 
be demotivated and make them follow different repair options  
9.4 Summary of Challenges in the Repair Process 
The flowchart as a visualization of the user interviews and the repair technicians’ interviews, 
demonstrates the whole process and summarizes the findings in a structured way.  
Starting from the process length, a reduction of the steps a user needs to go through is needed, 
in order to minimize the exposure to challenges that bring uncertainties and avoid barriers. If the 
user is able to know in advance what processes to follow, then it is more likely that a repair will 
be the outcome. Furthermore, the earlier a barrier is detected, the more efficient the process can 
become. If a user like a convenient repairer can find a suitable repair option from the beginning, a 
successful repair is more likely. Lastly several concerns creating uncertainties emerges from the 
lack of an accurate assessment of repair requirements. A main contributor appears to be the lack 
of knowledge on consumer electronics and the repair process in general among many users.  
 
9.5 Conclusion and Revision of Research Goal DS1 
Based on a literature review and 12 interviews among users of consumer electronics, a set of 
motivations, concerns and ability factors were identified. The factors were grouped as 
motivations and/or concerns related to money, time, the product, the repair service, beliefs and 
self-repair. Both in literature and among interview respondents’ monetary concerns appeared to 
be considered most important. Among ability factors, lacking knowledge about consumer 
electronics and the repair process in general was apparent among most respondents, and it was 
concluded to be a key factor influencing the decision to repair.  
Based on the interviews three different user types were suggested: The DIY repairer, he 
conservative repairer and the convenient repairer, representing different motivations, concerns 
and capability related to repair.  
Through a literature review of four relevant repairability assessment tools a list of 21 
repairability factors for consumer electronics were identified. Through interviews with three 
different RSPs a shorter list of similar factors was identified, and it was found that work process 
and repairability factors considered important varies between different types of RSPs.  
The terms “personal requirements” representing a user’s expectations for a repair based on 
factors such as price, product downtime and repair quality, and the term “repair requirements” 
representing the repair technicians’ expectations of price, feasibility and repair time was 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
 
 
 
[65] 
 
suggested by the authors. Matching personal and repair requirements are necessary for a 
successful repair.  
Based on findings from interviews and literature, a flowchart mapping the repair process 
from the user’s perspective was created. It was found that the stages a user requires to go through, 
while being in the repair process, needs to be reduced, in order to minimize the exposure to 
challenges and barriers and eventually to minimize the user concerns. The authors concluded that 
providing the user with accurate repair requirement early in the repair process, likely will allow 
the user to make better estimations based on personal requirements and therefore will lead 
him/her to make better decisions for his/her benefit. 
Based on the findings the authors concluded that there is a need for a method to estimate the 
repair requirements in the beginning of the repair process to reduce the concerns of the user. The 
authors suggested to develop a tool to support user in estimating feasibility of repairing 
consumer electronics. To develop the suggested support tool the authors found that a review of 
the current available repairability assessment framework was needed, to ensure there was a gap, 
and to use as inspiration in the development. With this goal the following research questions were 
defined: 
 
• 
What current frameworks are available for assessing repairability of consumer electronics? 
○ What scoring methods are used in current frameworks?  
○ What is the targeted user of these frameworks? 
○ What are the goals of these frameworks? 
○ What is the needed input and the final output of these frameworks?  
 
• 
How can a new support tool be developed for helping users of consumer electronics in the 
decision process to perform a repair?

11 Prescriptive Study 
Prescriptive Study (PS) following the Design Research Method aims to answer the research 
questions of (DS1). Based on the findings from the previous stages the goals and requirements of 
the support tool is defined, and the support tool is developed through an iterative design process. 
The support tool was developed to an extent where it could be tested and evaluated within the 
time frame of the project. Blessing & Chakrabarti (2009) describes different ways of approaching 
the PS phase. The present study follows a Review based PS, as the support tool will be developed 
after a review of currently available repairability assessment frameworks. 
11.1 Methodology 
Through the Prescriptive study a review and an analysis of four frameworks for assessing 
repairability of consumer electronics were performed. Based on the goal defined in DS1, the 
missing gaps in the current assessment frameworks were identified, leading to the development 
of the goals and requirements for the support tool. 
The collected repairability factors were evaluated based on whether or not they were relevant 
to the goal criteria, and a new set of factors with measurable values were defined. A general 
structure and a calculation method were developed to incorporate the tools factors. A simple 
Excel based prototype of the tool was developed for the first implementations, with more 
elements to be added gradually. The prototype was further developed to match the research 
findings and assumptions. For parts related to technicians, an evaluation with a professional 
technician was conducted and further improvements were done to reflect the repair technician’s 
input. Finally, the prototype was tested together with two professional repair technicians 
representing different repair options. The collected results were analyzed and used to drive 
conclusions and recommendations for improvements.

11.2.3 Analysis and evaluation 
The purpose of the first part of the analysis, was to define the general description of repairability

target user, products covered, input, scoring system, weighting and output. The analyzed 
frameworks were evaluated according to the goal defined in the end of DS1.  
11.2.4 Repairability assessment frameworks 
A repairability assessment framework, provides the basis on which the repairability of a product 
can be evaluated. The framework consists of a set of factors, defining one aspect of the 
repairability of a product. Typically, each factor is defined with two or more scoring criteria, that 
describe under which conditions a product should receive a certain score. Scoring criteria are 
defined to such a detail level, that the user of the framework can apply a score, without the need 
of an opinion. An example of the factor “Availability of spare parts” is given in Table 9.

product type (Bracquene et al., 2018; Cordella et al., 2019). The framework by Labo Fnac is 
focused only on laptops, and the assessment framework suggested by Flipsen et al. (2017) covers

not described in the framework.  
 
Inputs: All the analysed frameworks are based on a comprehensive analysis of a specific 
product. To supply the relevant input the user needs to perform research on available information 
provided by the product manufacturer e.g. for supply of spare parts and available product 
information. It also relies on input that needs to be obtained from disassembling and 
reassembling the device such as number of steps, time needed, and number of required tools. The 
JRC and Benelux reports additionally refer to the the eDIM Method developed by Vanegas et al. 
(2018) to be applied. In general, the given input needs to be product specific.  
 
Scoring: The JRC framework score includes the only pass/Fail criteria. A failed criterion 
returns a score of 0 and other criteria are scored in the range of 0.25-1. Later on, each factor, 
containing several criteria, a weight is applied relative to its importance in the final score. The 
frameworks by Benelux (0-10) and Labo-Fnac (0-10) are weighted in the score base by 
differentiating the maximum score given to a criterion, and the repairability index by Flipsen et 
al. (2017) is not weighted.  
 
Outputs: All frameworks return a single score as the output of the tool, taking the overall 
repairability of the product into consideration. In the JRC and Benelux studies, a final output of 
the framework is not defined. Instead JRC suggests options such as score scale (1-10), 
classification (A-F) or score stars (1-5) (Cordella et al., 2019). Labo-Fnac uses a 1-10 score, and 
Flipsen et al. (2017) suggest using a 1-10 scale as well. The repairability score supplied by all 
frameworks describes the repairability to the entire product and thus it is not related to a specific 
defect.

12 Development of Support Tool 
12.1.1 Motivation for development of new support tool 
The goal defined in DS1 was to develop a support tool to assist users of consumer electronics to 
estimate the repair feasibility of a defective device, based on the challenges that were identified 
in the current repair process. Through analysis of current repairability assessment frameworks 
it was found that they are intended to be used by experts, policy makers, designers or self-repair 
enthusiast, however, none include users with a low involvement in repair, as the intended user of 
the support tool. Similarly, the input used in current tools are extensive, and require both time 
and skills to obtain, with some information potentially inaccessible for the end user. The authors 
believed that there is a need for a “simple to use” tool, targeted to users of consumer electronics, 
in order to assist the decision process to perform a repair, at the time a defect occurs. A result 
based on the specific context can benefit users of consumer electronics in the decision process, as 
repairability might be dependent on the specific factors as available RSPs, the age of the product 
when the defect occurs, or the nature of the defect.  
12.2 Process of Tool Development 
As the understanding over the problem area was gradually increased, the implementation plan of 
the solution was gradually developed. Therefore, the planning of the tool development was done 
iterative, alongside the progression of the project. Overall the tool’s development can be divided 
into three main stages: Preparation, Conceptualization and Test. And overview of the stages and 
the input can be seen in

In the Preparation stage the scope and the requirements were defined, providing a solid frame 
for the conceptualization stage. In conceptualizations the first step was to evaluate influencing 
factors of repairability found in literature, based on criteria related to the tool’s scope. The 
selected factors were transformed to meet the defined tool requirements. The overview of the 
tool was developed, along with the dependencies of the selected factors. The next step was to 
define the system inputs, ensuring that they will be or can be transformed into measurable values. 
The values related to system input were collected through a survey and market research, creating 
a dataset to be used in the prototype of the support tool. The structure of the support tool was 
developed to incorporate the defined elements, based on which the scoring system was 
developed together with the different weights for the factors. 
Having all the building elements, a prototype was developed in a spreadsheet platform 
(Google Sheets) for the first iterations and the evaluation with a professional repair technician. 
The relevant building elements along with the structure were updated, leading to an improved 
version of the tool.  
The last stage of the development involved the prototype test with two technicians, each 
representing a different repair option. The results were analyzed, allowing to draw conclusions 
for the success of the implementation, but also to recommendations for future improvements. 
 
12.3 Scope of Support Tool 
 
Goal of tool: Translate repairability parameters into understandable information that aids 
the user to make the decision to repair a defective product or not. 
 
Supported product types: It was decided to limit the scope to one product type, as many 
factors were product type dependent, and including several product types will require increased 
research and the need to involve more repair technicians with different specialty during the 
evaluation process. The product type of “laptops” was chosen, as they generally represent a high 
value product, have a relatively long average lifetime, and generally are considered as 
upgradeable. Furthermore, laptop repair services were widely available.  
 
Priority parts: Each product type is associated with at set of parts that are generally found 
in the product. Cordella et al. (2019) and Bracquene et al. (2018) suggest the use of priority parts, 
in order to limit the total amount of parts covered for each product type. The list of parts is defined 
based on how prone a part is to be replaced, repaired, or upgraded and its functional importance 
for the product (Cordella et al.,2019). Based on the mentioned methodology Cordella et al. (2019) 
suggest a list of priority parts for laptops that was adopted for the support tool, and later edited 
based on input from interviews with repair technicians. A final list can be found in Appendix M. 
 
Target user: The suggested target users are users of consumer electronics (TV’s, 
Smartphones and laptops). The tool will focus on all user types as described previously, however 
the focus will be the “Conservative repairer” and the “Convenience repairer”. These two groups 
are the most relevant as their current knowledge on repair of consumer electronics is limited. 
The tool could also be relevant for DIY repairers who consider attempting at a DIY repair. 
 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
 
 
 
[73] 
 
Input: The user input used in the support tool should be obtainable by the target user of the 
tool with no need to do extensive online research, preferably input already known to the user. 
This ensures that the support tool can be used by non-experts, and within a short time span.  
Data (system input) used in the tool, should be widely available, and obtainable by the authors 
within the timeframe of the project, in order for the working prototype to be developed.  
 
Time of application: The support tool should be used at the time a product owner 
experiences a defect, thus the estimates should be based on the age of the product and the specific 
defect.  
 
Supported defects 
• 
The focus of the project was on hardware errors and thus software errors and solutions 
were not covered, as explained in chapter 5.3 .  
 
• 
The tool will focus only on the replacement of priority parts, either with a similar part or 
with an upgrade. This limitation was based on the scope of the project explained in 
chapter 5.3 
12.3.1 Output of support tool 
 
Repair options: The support tool should refer to the most common available repair solutions 
including self-repair, authorized and unauthorized RSP’s, repair cafes and repairs covered by a 
warranty.  
 
Repair requirements: The output will be displayed as estimations of repair requirements. 
Several repair requirements could be specified, however, due to the timeframe of the project it 
was decided to focus on the requirements that create most of the uncertainties to majority of the 
users. 
 
• 
Feasibility to repair: Estimated feasibility of a successful repair relative to each repair 
option. It summarizes all the identified technical elements that the user has difficulties to 
grasp. This estimation should allow the user of the support tool to know, if the repair is 
technically feasible or not, and what repair option is best suited.  
 
• 
Repair cost estimation: An estimation on the cost related to the repair displayed as a 
range. Price was mentioned by all interview responders as a main concern in performing 
a repair.  
 
Repair time estimation: Repair time was another repair requirement expressed as a 
concern by the users, but it was excluded from the tool. Initially it was assumed that repair time 
was related to factors like type of defect, magnitude of the damage and difficulty to replace a 
priority part. Through the interviews with the repair technicians, it became clear that the time 
estimation was based on the standards, the operation, and the capabilities of the repair service 
provider and not on the time a repair requires. The estimation from the technicians was given in 
a scale of days and it could deviate due to non-repair related factors. It was also found that the 
more organized a service provider was, the more accurate the time estimation was, as it was 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
 
 
 
[74] 
 
based on the actual capabilities of the provider. Based on the above findings, it was considered 
impossible to incorporate the time estimation to the tool in a such a general level.  
 
Success criteria: Providing an accurate estimate is crucial in guiding the user of the support 
tool to make the right decision, however, since it is an estimate based on general considerations, 
a 100% accuracy cannot be reached. Therefore, the following success criteria have been set, to be 
able to test the accuracy of the support tool. 
 
• 
Repair feasibility estimation score should be less than 1 value of difference with the 
estimation of the repair service providers, on a scale from 1-5. 
• 
Price estimates should be within a 25% accuracy, relative to the repair technicians 
estimate, or within a 200 DKK difference.  
• 
The price range should reflect the price range provided by the RSP.

13 Conceptualization 
In the conceptualizations the methodology for developing the support tool framework was 
developed, defining all the building elements of the tool. 
13.1.1 Defining relevant factors for a consumer-focused support tool 
Having defined the two outputs of the tool, as repair feasibility and repair price, a set of 
influencing factors needed also to be developed along with the correlations between them. The 
support tool’s factors were based on the factors found in DS1 and presented in chapter 7.3 from 
the four repairability assessment frameworks. The assessment frameworks were developed to 
provide repairability estimation on individuals energy related products, with information 
provided by professionals, based on characteristics they inherit at the time they get on the 
market. The support tool gives a simplified assessment in a more general level, at the time the 
defect appears, with information that include the defect characteristics and provided by the user. 
Therefore, factors relevant to the support tool needed to be developed to match with the 
requirements of the tool. To decide which factors can contribute to the tool development, an 
assessment of the collected factors took place, based on the following four criteria. 
 
• 
Can the factor be used as an indicator for the repair feasibility?  
• 
Can the factor be used as an indicator for the repair price estimation?  
• 
Can the factor be used as an indicator for the available repair options?  
• 
Can the factor be used to describe a barrier or a limitation? 
  
Furthermore, the factors need to provide a valuable input, based on the limitations of the user 
and system input, defined in the criteria. 
 
• 
The factor should be a user input that lies within the support requirements 
• 
And/or a system input data that can be generalized on a product type level.  
• 
And/or resulting from the combination of two factors fulfilling the above criteria. 
• 
The factors need to provide a measurable contribution to the outputs of the tool 
 
Based on the above, a new collection of factors was formed, as it can be seen in Appendix N. 
The assessment was done as an iterative process throughout the development of the tool. Some 
factors which were first included were later deemed to be of insignificant influence, or it was 
found that the required input was not realistic to obtain within the timeframe of the project.  
A factor that do not follow entirely the set of criteria is the priority parts identification. 
Priority parts as an input were an essential part for the development of the tool, as they were 
used to calculate different scores or to retrieve values related to the product. A well-known 
problem to the authors, was the limited capability of the users to identify which priority part is 
the defective one and needs to be replaced. A defect identification process dedicated to laptops 
could narrow down the focus to the potential defect priority parts Methods as IRIS codes (“IRIS: 
International Repair Information System - DIGITALEUROPE,” 2020) exist but they are targeted to 
==PAGE:80==
 
 
 
 
 
[76] 
 
professional technicians. Within the project, the defect identification process was out of scope 
and it is expected to be part of an additional external assessment process.

13.2 Defining the Support Tool Structure 
13.2.1 Introduction 
In the next part, the building elements of the support tool are described, along with the logic 
behind them. The development of the tool took inspiration from the structure of the assessment 
frameworks (Cordella et al., 2019) and (Bracquene et al., 2018). Both frameworks use multiple 
criteria decision methods (MCDM) in order to produce a score. The elements and the structure 
are borrowed and adjusted to fit the support tool’s scope.  
Building elements are considered the system input, score matrices, weights, and filters. A 
thorough description of the tools structure and of each score can be found in the Appendix N. 
13.2.2 Inputs  
The tool takes advantage of two different sets of inputs. The user input and the system input. 
The user inputs are given by the user, from the interface page of the support tool and they consist 
==PAGE:84==
 
 
 
 
 
[80] 
 
of either numeric, or non-numeric data. The full list of User input is found in Table 12. Since the 
outputs are given as numerical variables the non-numerals inputs, required to get converted. For 
the conversion, scoring matrices were used.  
For the system input the tool uses data that have been collected from by authors to create a 
general database related to product characteristics. Some of the values arose from literature and 
others from interviews, like part compatibility/ upgradability. Others came from conducting an 
online survey and by conducting manual data collection. 
 
Online Survey: For the collection of “replaceability rates”, “replaceability difficulty” and 
“spare part replacement time” values, the involvement of professional technicians was required. 
For that reason, an online survey was conducted, asking technicians to give an estimation about 
the values. Along with the numeral responds, participants had the opportunity to place comments 
over the questions they were asked to answer. These comments were taken into consideration 
during the development of the tool. The average of the collected values was later used as a system 
input. The survey questions and responses can be found in Appendix O. The survey was shared in 
the repair forum Gratis Computer Hjælp (“Computerhjælp (gratis),” n.d.) and iRepair (“iRepair,” 
n.d.) a chain repair store in Greece with 33 stores. Furthermore, it was sent pr. Email to all 
relevant RSP’s contacted throughout the project. In total 19 responses from professional repair 
technicians were collected. 
 
Price collection: To define a system input for spare part costs, a manual online data 
collection was conducted. The price of spare parts was given in a range. The minimum and 
maximum prices of each priority part per brand was collected from a variety of online resellers 
of spare parts. The collected data were processed, to provide the price ranges of each priority 
part. The database can be found in Appendix P.

14 Evaluation of the Support Tool. 
14.1 Methodology 
The evaluation was conducted through a semi-structured phone interview with a professional 
technician, with extensive knowledge on laptop repair. Through the interview, three parts were 
aimed to be evaluated, the structure of the scoring factors, the scoring criteria used in the matrices 
and the scoring weights. Each part of the tool was considered as an assumption needed to be 
evaluated. Therefore, each part was converted into a comparison statement that the participant 
needed to answer based on a four-scale score. An assumption can have: 
 
• 
Strong connection: The assumption is confirmed 
• 
Under circumstances connection: The assumption can be confirmed in some cases.  
• 
Weak connection: The assumption cannot be confirmed in a significant degree. 
• 
No connection: The assumption cannot be confirmed 
 
During the whole process, the participant was encouraged to express his thoughts, leading to 
additional information related to the evaluated part.  
 
14.1.1 Result overview 
A new factor, “Laptop size” was introduced in the replaceability of priority part score. 
Furthermore, updates were made to the score criteria, aligned with the collected results of the 
evaluation. Also, a number of score calculations were adjusted. More specifically the cause of the 
defect was added in the calculation of defect identification effort score and the defect 
identification cost was based on defect identification effort. Lastly the two priority parts, GPU and 
CPU, were excluded as separate priority parts and were considered as an irreplaceable part of the 
motherboard as in most of the cases a defect in these parts, requires the replacement of the whole 
motherboard. The filled evaluation templates with the additional comments and the changes on 
the tool can be found in Appendix Q.  
The changes in the criteria scores the weights and the new scoring calculations were not 
further evaluated. The authors implemented the changes and went to the testing phase, to follow 
the limited time schedule of the project. It is worth mentioning that for future work an additional 
evaluation needs to take place. 
15 Test of the Support Tool 
A final test of the tool was designed with the purpose to test the accuracy of the estimation 
provided by the support tool. To ensure that the test reflected a real scenario it was decided to 
use three real life cases, with as much knowledge about the case available as possible. For each 
case estimates from the repair technician, and the support tool was collected and compared to 
the known results of each case. Secondly every repair technician was asked to mention a repair 
case and compare the results of the support tool with his/her own experience. 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
 
 
 
[86] 
 
15.1 Methodology 
15.1.1 Structure of test 
The test was structured in 3 phases.  
1st phase: The repair technician was introduced to the project and was given a quick 
introduction to the support tool. 
2nd phase: The repair technician was introduced to the cases and was asked to give estimates 
based on the case described. Estimates collected were: 6 repair feasibility scores were collected, 
the overall score, 4 sub scores which and a calculated feasibility score found by weighting the sub 
scores (1-5 score, 5 = product is very feasible to repair). Weights were also applied by the 
respondent. 3 price estimations were collected, and estimate, and as well as the minimum and 
maximum price range. An estimation of detection time and replacement time was also collected.  
3rd phase: In the last phase the repair technician was asked to describe a repair case from his 
or her own experience, the repair technician used the support tool to define the needed input for 
the specific case. The result of the support tool was compared to the repair technicians 
experience. 
4th phase: The repair technician was asked to give comments or input for the functionality of 
the support tool.  
 
15.1.2 Test responders 
To perform the test 3 test subjects, one representing each Repair option for laptops, were needed. 
It was not possible to find a volunteer representing an authorized RSP. Thus a total of 2 test 
responses were collected. The DIY option was not included in the test, as the participant requires 
to have similar past experience on the presented repair cases, in order to be able to provide a 
repair feasibility estimation.  
 
15.1.3 Definition of repair cases 
Three cases were developed for conducting the test. The cases were found through two different 
online forums for sharing knowledge and post questions of computer repair (“Computerhjælp 
(gratis),” n.d.; “Tom’s Hardware Forum,” n.d.). Cases were chosen to cover different inputs of the 
tool, including different product age, brand, defective priority part etc. Cases including all the 
needed input, as well as the result of the repair (successful, non-successful) and repair costs were 
preferred. When all input was not available, the authors provided an estimated input. For two 
cases, the original text was translated from Danish to English, and a copy of the original posts, and 
the full description of the cases can be found in Appendix R.  
 
15.1.4 Documentation of interview responses 
To record all the replies from the interviewed repair technicians and to guide the interview 
an interactive response sheet was developed. The response sheet included the case description, 
the input used in the support tool, and fields for recording the repair technician’s estimation of 
repair feasibility, feasibility sub scores, weights and price and time estimations. All the template 
sheet and all the collected results can be found in Appendix S. 
All comments and details from personal cases from the repair technicians was noted under 
the interview and the full summaries can be found in Appendix T. With permission from the test

[87] 
 
subjects, the interviews were recorded for later reference. Every test was planned to take 60 min

15.1.5 Analysis of test results 
Estimations of repair feasibility, price, detection time and replacement time was collected and for 
every entry the difference between the repair technician’s estimation and the tools estimation 
was calculated. For every score an average difference was calculated based on the three cases, the 
average difference was used to determine the accuracy of the tool estimation for the given RSP.  
Due to a limited amount of data available, the focus of the analysis was to understand possible 
flaws in the functionality, thus it was decided to identify situations when the difference in the 
repair technician’s estimation and the support tool’s estimation were particularly high, based on 
the success criteria defined in chapter 12.3.  
 
• 
The difference on the repair feasibility should be 1 value or less, relative to the 
average estimate provided by RSPs.  
• 
Price estimates should be within a 25% accuracy relative to the repair technicians 
estimate or within a 200 DKK difference.  
• 
The price range provided by the support tool should reflect the price range provided 
by the RSP.  
 
For the feasibility score differences of more than 1, was marked, to visualize where important 
differences occurred and based on the repair technicians’ comments possible factors causing the 
differences was identified. Same approach was taken for estimation of price. 
The presented results were calculated as the difference between the repair technicians 
estimate (RTFeasibility and RTPrice) and the support tools estimates (STFeasibility and STPrice). 
The difference is denoted as STFeasibility and STPrice. Negative values mean the support tool 
underestimate relative to the repair technician. The calculations are shown in the following

15.4 Discussion 
 
Test methodology: The chosen test method for the support tool provided a lot of valuable 
information for further improvements of the support tool, as combination of qualitative 
information from the collected comments and quantitative data on estimates. Due to the time-
consuming process of conducting the test and limits in access to test subjects, the amount of data 
collected was restricted.  
The three cases defined, only covers parts of the available input, and thus the test method will 
only be able to test a limited part of the tool’s output. Finally, the personal cases presented by the 
test subjects, did not involve the feasibility sub scores, thus the results of personal cases and the 
3 pre-defined cases were not directly comparable. 
 
Discussion of test results: Due to the difference in different repair technicians’ level of 
experience and skills, it must be expected that relatively large differences in the provided 
estimations can occur. This increases the total number of tests needed with subjects representing 
the different RSPs.  
The repair technician representing URSP was operating from Greece rather than Denmark. 
Though, feasibility estimates can be assumed to be similar, price estimates will most likely be 
lower.  
The conducted test did not include test subjects representing self-repair or authorized RSPs. 
The repair technician representing repair cafe, had also experience as a professional, thus some 
of his personal cases reflected his experience from a professional perspective.

15.5.2 Suggested improvements of the support tool - long term 
 
Defect identification support: Both repair technicians mentioned the need for 
incorporating a defect identification support, as the intended user of the tool, will likely not be 
able to determine the appropriate spare part as input. Some digital diagnostics tools are already 
available, that might be used for detecting issues at the storage drive, however they are generally 
focused on software related problems. Thus, this type of support might only cover a small portion 
of potential defects. Through the repair technicians interviews it was mentioned that error 
detection in many cases is the most difficult part of the repair process, and that in many cases 
they will need to open the product, to understand the actual cause of a problem.  
 
Incorporation of laptop series as input: The more available knowledge about a specific 
product, the more accurate estimations can be made. However, it comes with the trade of, that 
more data will be needed as system input. During the test a repair technician proposed to increase 
the accuracy of the support tool by using laptop series as an input. Most brands produce laptops 
in different series, focusing on specific markets, e.g. for business, gaming or study, and it was 
mentioned that different series usually have different levels of repairability. Using laptop series 
as input rather than specific models could be a way to increase accuracy, without the need for 
data related to the specific model. 
 
Success criteria for support tool: The success criteria that were used in the test, were 
defined as what the authors found “realistic” with the current prototype stage. The final success 
criteria should be defined depending on the expectations of the quality of the result, based on 
what will be acceptable for the user of the tool. One of the interviewed repair technicians, 
expressed the concern that customers might be disappointed or angry if they first hear one price, 
and later learn that the repair will be more expensive (M. Nielsen, Personal communication, 
November 18, 2019). A survey conducted among potential users of the support tool, could be a 
method to get a general understanding on the users’ expectations on the accuracy of price and 
feasibility estimates.  
 
Range in types of repairs: Due to the time frame of the project, a strict focus on replacement 
and upgrade of parts was needed. To increase the width of the tool it should be considered to 
integrate other types of repair and maintenance of both hardware and software. This could 
increase the usability of the tool as the intended user of the support tool will likely not be capable 
of differentiating between what type of repair/maintenance is needed for a certain problem. 
Increasing the range in types of repairs will however be time consuming as a new set of factors 
and input will be needed for software problems.  
15.5.3 Identified limitations of the support tool design 
 
Updating system data: The support tool relies on system data including spare part prices 
and brand repairability. This data will need to be updated over time, as prices or products likely 
change over time. Similarly, technological innovations in repair methods can change repairability 
of certain parts. 
 
Estimation of repairability: The purpose of the support tool is to estimate repair feasibility 
and repair costs. This approach ultimately limits the possible accuracy that can be obtained. In 
==PAGE:102==
 
 
 
 
 
[98] 
 
the development of the support tool repair technicians were used as the reference for the 
accuracy of the estimation. If this approach is taken, it should be expected that the support tools 
estimation cannot become more accurate than the accuracy of the average repair technician.  
15.6 Conclusion on Prescriptive Study  
The goal of PS was, as defined in the conclusion of DS1, to propose a support tool for assisting 
in estimating the feasibility to repair a consumer electronics device and develop and test a 
prototype. 
First four repairability assessment frameworks were analyzed and evaluated based on their 
goals, target users, type of products, scoring systems, inputs and outputs. The authors identified 
a set of gaps in the analyzed assessments frameworks and found that none targeted the average 
user of consumer electronics based on the intended user and input needed, which lead to the 
decision that a new support tool should be developed.  
A scope and requirements for the new support tool was set, and it was decided to cover the 
product type of laptops, input needed to be easily obtainable by the user, and the output should 
be a repair feasibility and price estimate.  
The repairability factors found in DS1 were evaluated based on criteria matching the 
requirement, leading to a final list of 13 dependent and 11 independent factors that were  
implemented in the support tool. The support tool was conceptualized in 5 steps taken in several 
iterations. A new scoring and weighting system were proposed, a database with system input was 
generated based on an online survey among repair technicians, a prototype was developed in 
Microsoft Excel, and scores and weights were evaluated with a repair technician. 
The finished prototype of the support tool was tested on 5 repair cases and was conducted 
with two repair technicians, each representing one of the four identified repair options. Test 
results showed that the feasibility estimation was within the success criteria with an average 
differentiation of 0.7 in a 1-5 score. The Repair price estimate was within 25% or 200 DKK 
difference in 7 of 12 estimates. It was concluded that both estimates require further improvement 
and a set of short term and long-term improvements was suggested, with more tests needed to 
be carried out, to prove the accuracy of the support tool.  
Based on the test results it was decided that the support tool was premature for a final 
evaluation in Descriptive study 2, and that the time frame of the project did not allow for further 
improvements. Instead it was decided that the potential use of the support tool should be 
evaluated through an interview with a case company. With the new goal a set of research 
questions were formulated: 
 
• 
What is the potential impact of applying the support tool in the case study? 
• 
What can be learned from applying the support tool and how can it be improved further?

16 Case-Study 
The thesis was done in a collaboration with the start-up Make It Good Again (MIGA), an online 
platform interconnecting repair technicians and users with products in need of repair. The 
collaboration with the company, provided useful insights from the operation of the platform, 
along with the challenges experienced with the service. Τhe following section describes the 
purpose of the collaboration, and an estimation of advantages, disadvantages and action needed 
for a potential implementation of support tool in MIGA’s service. 
16.1 Methodology 
The collaboration with the company was established prior to the initiation of the project, 
through mail communication, by presenting the purpose of the thesis. In total two meetings took 
place between the authors and two founders of the service. The first meeting’s purpose was to 
gain a general understanding of the operation of the platform and to document the main 
challenges they are trying to overcome. The second meeting took place during the support’s tool 
test phase. A summary of the thesis progress was made, where the challenges of the project in 
relation to the support tool were presented, as well as the factors that are related to user’s 
motivation and concerns. The final prototype of the tool was shared with stakeholders, leading to 
an open conversation about the performance and potential of the support tool in a service like 
MIGA. The interview was recorded, with the consent of the stakeholders and notes were taken, 
and can be found in Appendix U. 
The interview material was used to conclude the main challenges, identified by MIGA’s 
founders. Finally, an implementation of the support tool was proposed, along with the needed 
actions for such an application. The advantages and disadvantages were described and the 
potential to resolve the platform’s challenges were explored. The implementation in the case 
study was based on the experiences through developing and testing the support tool, but was not 
backed up with any tests, and thus it should be seen as an reflection on the potentials that might 
works as inspiration for further work.  
 
 
16.2 Description of MIGA  
16.2.1.1 Make It Good Again 
The company has a strong focus on sustainability, by trying to answer the problem of 
disposing products that can still become functional, in favor of new products. 
MIGA operates in the general area of Copenhagen, with the goal to expand to the rest of 
Denmark. The online platform connects repair technicians from the entire repair industry with 
users, making it easy and convenient to search for a suitable repair option.  
The platform is divided in the user site and the repair technicians’ site. In the user’s site, the 
owner of the defective product registers the case with a description and optionally pictures of the 
product and the defect. When the case is registered, it is available to all repair technicians where 
they can estimate the defect and evaluate the potentials of a repair. The technicians that are 
interested in the case, can bid with a price for the repair, that is visible to the user but also to other 
repair technicians who can bid under the current bid. When the user feels that a repair option 
satisfies the requirements, he/she can close the deal. The user can base the decision on the final 
==PAGE:104==
 
 
 
 
 
[100] 
 
bid price, but also by evaluating other factors as the rating of the repair service provider, the 
location and the quality level of the proposed repair. 
The platform does not make any differentiation between repair service providers, allowing 
all the registered members to bid and have an opportunity to arrange a deal with the user.  
 
16.2.2 Challenges 
 
1. Defect identification online: The biggest problem the platform faces is in the product 
defect identification. Users have their own understanding of the defect of the product that 
might not align with reality, therefore, a good product and defect description is important. 
On the other side, a repair technician is required to provide a bid on the user’s description 
of the defect, something that most of the technicians are hesitating to do. A common 
statement from the repair technicians is: “If I can't see the product, then I can't fix it” (J. 
Britze, Personal communication, December 12, 2019). It was mentioned, because of the 
Danish “offer law”, when an offer is given to a customer, there is a legal obligation for this 
offer to be fulfilled. Consequently, repair technicians are often skeptical about online 
offers as they are bonded to the statement. To overcome the obligation with the offer, a 
statement is given besides the offer related to what it includes. As an example, a statement 
like” I am going to fix exactly this cable” instead of “I am going to fix the product” is given. 
A more structural defect identification method can improve the communication between 
users and repair technicians, relieving the latter from unneeded concerns.  
 
2. Linking repair technicians to suitable cases: The company operates with many 
different product types, including white goods, consumer electronics, furniture, bikes and 
more. Repair technicians typically only repair a single product type, and sometimes only 
certain brands or subtypes. In the current setup repair technicians receive notifications 
about every offer not only repairs relevant to them. This approach makes it more time 
consuming for repair technicians possibly leading to relevant repairs not being seen. 
MIGA hopes to address this problem in future updates of the platform.  
 
3. Reflecting quality in bids: Because the platform operates based on auctions, the lowest 
bid is getting selected by the user. The low price might come with the cost of a lower 
quality of the repair. At the same time other RSPs with similar bids but with better quality 
are left out from the auction. MIGA considers applying alternative ways to bid, including 
quality and time.  
 
4. Attracting repair technicians for atypical products: Some product categories are more 
attractive to repair technicians as they have a higher profit margin. This is especially 
present for consumer electronics, as the repair market is relatively large and fast moving. 
For others, less common product types, users might struggle to find a repair option, and 
when they do, they are willing to agree to higher bids. MIGA’s intention is to attract more 
general repair technicians to make the platform more attractive for users with different 
kinds of products. 
 
5. Ensuring data safety: The data stored in the devices is typically valuable for the user, 
therefore, through the repair process the user expects either to retrieve them, or to ensure 
that nothing is going to be erased. MIGA recognizes data protection as challenge and 
==PAGE:105==
 
 
 
 
 
[101] 
 
works alongside with the repair technicians for that purpose. MIGA’s intentions are to 
incorporate automated advice for the users what to do in order to preserve their data 
before a repair and inform them for the potential complications. 
16.2.3 Applying support at MIGA 
Currently the platform contains a registration platform where the user can add a description and 
photos of the product and the experienced. The support tool interface could be added in the 
registration page, giving more options to add structured information related to the product and 
the defect. The support tool will provide an output both for the user of the service and repair 
technicians. The application is illustrated in Figure 27. A further addition, is to create a feedback 
loop that will give the opportunity to update the database based on the solved repair cases, 
coming from the repair technicians. The added functions of the tool are intended to complement 
already existing functions, without altering the structure or the operation of the platform.

16.3 Potentials of Implementation 
An estimation of the potentials that the tool can have for the involvement stakeholders are 
described in the next section. The identified areas where the tool is expected to have an impact 
are marked with a number in Figure 27.  
 
16.3.1 1) User input  
 
Advantages: Users have the option to add information about the defect and the product in a 
more structured way, besides the description in a text form. In this way, the description report is 
more likely to contain all the necessary information needed for a repair technician to make an 
estimation, making them less cautious to bid on a case. Furthermore, the provided information 
will be stored structured and not in a text form, as the user will be asked to fill a well-defined 
form, in some cases with fixed input options. This will allow for better data collection. and can 
help addressing the 2nd challenge, by using the information collected to notify specific repairers 
relevant for the case. This could e.g. be by, product type or brand.  
Disadvantages: The user is required to manually add the information, a process that might 
prevent a user from completing the registration.  
==PAGE:106==
 
 
 
 
 
[102] 
 
Implementation actions: The tool’s interface needs to be added as part of the defect 
registration. The solution relies on the quality of the input, so the user needs to be helped through 
the process by information or pictures.  
 
16.3.2 2) User estimations 
 
Advantages: With the implementation of the tool on the platform, a user can get instant 
feedback on the repairability of their product, to know what to expect before the repair technician 
returns a bid. This might help user to understand if no offer is received. The 3rd challenge could 
potentially be addressed by making the user aware of the severity of their defect. If a product has 
a low repair feasibility it could be used as a motivation for choosing an experienced repairer that 
ensures a certain quality. The 5th challenge can be addressed by informing the user about keeping 
his/her data safe based on the specific repair. E.g. the user could get the advice to backup data, if 
the repair involves the storage drive.  
Disadvantages: The estimations give the user expectation of what to expect, if the repair 
technicians bid does not match the user might be frustrated, be less inclined to accept a bid or 
give up on a repair before the receive an offer. 
Implementation actions: The user estimation relies on a high accuracy of the support tool,  
thus, further improvement of the tool needs to be done. 
 
16.3.3 3) Estimation given to repair technicians 
 
Advantages: The repair estimation can be a help for repair technicians when doing their own 
estimation, especially for less experienced repairers. The estimation might give the repair 
technician a quick overview of which repair cases are suitable for his skill level, and thereby in 
part addressing the 2nd challenge.  
Disadvantages: Repair technicians might be skeptical about a repair estimation provided by 
the support tool, making them inclined to ignore it. If the support tool wrongly underestimates 
the feasibility of a case, it might reduce the amount of offers.  
Implementation actions: The user estimation relies on a high accuracy of the support tool; 
thus, further improvement of the tool needs to be done. 
 
16.3.4 4) User input given to repair technicians 
 
Advantages: A more structured description of a repair case might make it easier for the 
repair technician to get an overview and place an offer reducing the time he spends. It might also 
ensure that more information is given, so the repair technician do not need to contact the user. In 
general, it will address the 1st challenge, by increasing the possibility to identify the defect. 
Disadvantages: If the input is not correct the repairer can be misguided, potentially causing 
him/her to place an inaccurate bid. The input might be irrelevant for the repair technician.  
Implementation actions: The input should be relevant for the repair technician. From the 
test of the support tool it was learned that the tool did not always use the same logic as the repair 
technician, thus the input given should be tested with repair technicians. This could be done with 
co creating the wanted input together with repair technicians. 
 
==PAGE:107==
 
 
 
 
 
[103] 
 
16.3.5 5) Feedback on repair cases 
 
Advantages: Feedback can be used to generate a database of repair cases, that again can 
improve the estimates generated by the support tool. Data can be shared with any relevant 
stakeholders, repair technicians on the platform or possibly external stakeholders. The 4th 
challenge could be addressed by storing data of repair cases with no bids. The collected 
information could be used to attract repair technicians outside of the platform, if enough cases 
with a certain profile appears. 
Disadvantages: Any errors in the collected data, either from the given inputs or the false 
connections between the two can pollute the data. Repair technicians might not want to share 
information about the finished repair cases such as costs, time etc.  
Implementation actions: To generate a valuable database connection between input and the 
result of a case must be clear. An input form for repair technicians should be developed, with 
information repair technicians want to share. It could be by rating different factors such as 
difficulty to repair, or by providing an estimated repair time, cost etc.  
 
16.3.5.1 Conclusion of case-study 
Based on the case-study several suggestions were made for the possible implementation of 
the support tool in MIGA’s service. The tool shows some promising advantages, by improving the 
current description formula, for the benefit of the user and repair technicians, however more 
work needs to be done in adapting to ensure the relevant input is given. Repair feasibility 
estimates can provide both users and repair technicians with useable output, put the quality 
needs to be ensured and the output format should be evaluated with both repair technicians and 
user to ensure it is used correctly. A more structured input can possibly give repair technicians a 
better overview of the repair cases, reducing the time they need to invest per repair case. Finally 
collecting data with a structured input from both users and repair technicians holds a great 
potential for improving the service, yet a high effort will be needed to ensure quality of the 
collected data and how it is analyzed. Potentially all the 5 challenges given by MIGA can be 
addressed by applying a solution like the support tool on the platform, yet further work needs to 
be done to test the assumptions.

18 Final Discussion 
The thesis started from a concern about the global problem with increasing generation of E-waste 
and the environmental consequences of this and sought to find a tiny part of the problem that 
could be addressed, within the limitations of the project. With regards to EU’s high consumption 
of consumer electronics, product life extension strategies and specifically the repairability of 
products was chosen as the angle of the problem that the thesis would take.  
 
To narrow the scope the focus was set on Denmark, and the first work package of the project 
sought to “Understand the current flows and challenges of repair of consumer electronics in 
Denmark.”:  
  
• 
What is the current state of consumer electronics in Denmark today, in relation to the 
circular economy, and what options for repair of consumer electronics are currently 
available?  
 
It was found that several studies have been done on quantifying E-waste, and the flows of 
these in Denmark (sources), yet none of the found studies covered the repair rates specifically. 
With sample data from Statistics Denmark (Danmarks statistik, 2019b), it was found that an 
average Danish household spends 93 DKK/year on repair of consumer electronics which is 1-2% 
relative what is spent on new consumer electronics. The findings were used as a strong indicator 
that repair rates are low, and preferably should be higher. No effort was done in defining at what 
rate the relation between spending on new products and repair of products ideally should be, and 
other product categories show similar relations (Haslund-Gjerrild, 2019), thus further work 
would need to be done in defining the current rates of repair in Denmark. Furthermore, the use 
of sample data should be supplemented with sales data to increase the validity.  
Four available repair options were defined by the Authors, Self-repair, Repair Cafes, 
unauthorized RSP’s and authorized RSP’s. This categorization was necessary for later use in the 
support tool and based on interviews with RSP’s it was possible to make distinction between the 
repair options. However, individual RSP’s in the same category can differentiate and thus possibly 
further categorization could be done for increasing accuracy of the support tool. It was also found 
that the difference between ARSP’s and URSP’s varies between product brands, a distinction that 
was not made in the report or support tool.  
 
• 
What is the potential for extending product lifetimes of consumer electronics through 
increasing repair rates? 
 
To answer this research question, it was decided to search available studies based on a “Life 
cycle assessment” of laptops, as this method was considered to be state of the art. A systematic 
review was not conducted, but two comprehensible studies were chosen. The results showed a 
large difference between the results of the different studies, and different conclusion had been 
made. With the time limitations of the project the authors decided to focus on the general 
conclusion that lifetime extension is the preferred approach for reducing environmental impacts 
from consumer electronics (Prakash, Liu, Schischke, & Stobbe, 2012), rather than deciding on a 
single optimal replacement year.  
The scope of the thesis was from the beginning repair of consumer electronics, yet a holistic 
approach was taken in the beginning covering all the flows of potential pathways of the products. 
==PAGE:109==
 
 
 
 
 
[105] 
 
Though the holistic approach provided a good overview, a more focused study could have brought 
more depth. While the overview of the problem worked as a great perspective, the project never 
came to a stage where the potential for implementing the support tool could be estimated, as too 
many uncertainties have yet to be addressed.  
The second work package aimed to “Define the key factors relevant for increasing repair of 
consumer electronics.” Two stakeholders were found to be central in understanding the factors, 
the user of consumer electronics taking the decision to repair, and the RSP making the repair 
possible. Originally the author's intended to strictly focus on motivational factors for the user 
decision process, however it became clear to the authors that technical factors influencing the 
repairability of a product could not be omitted. The relatively late discovery meant a change in 
the scope, and that some parts covered in work package 2 were of lesser relevance.  
 
• 
What key factors are influencing the user’s decision to repair and how it is related to the 
available repair options? 
 
To answer the research, question a literature review of 3 studies and 12 interviews among 
users of consumer electronics was conducted. A list of motivations, concerns and ability factors 
were identified. The factors were grouped as motivations and/or concerns related to money, 
time, the product, the repair service, beliefs and self-repair. The 3 identified studies were all 
relevant to consumer behavior, however they covered several product categories and 
maintenance and refurbishment. A systematic review could have been done to cover a wider 
range of studies, and potentially only studies of repair of consumer electronics could be included.  
The authors found that the findings from interviews generally reflected the findings from 
literature though the factors collected through interviews were more specific. Both in literature 
and among interview respondents’ monetary concerns appeared to be considered most 
important. The authors concluded that a rating of the importance of the factors was not beneficial 
as they will likely change based on the connection with the specific product, and due to personal 
beliefs among the users. 
 
The authors concluded that lacking knowledge about consumer electronics among the users 
should be considered a key factor, influencing the concerns of the user and thereby their decision 
to repair. A similar conclusion was not directly found in the reviewed literature, however others 
also found that the complexity of consumer electronics might keep users from doing self-repair 
(Ackermann, L., Mugge, R., & Schoormans, J, 2018). Several of the interviewed repair technicians 
mentioned that customers often did not understand the defect of the product, and how that 
influenced the repairability and the repair price. Though there could be a connection between 
knowledge about the product, and the decision to repair, this would need to be further 
investigated, also to find out whether this effect is more apparent for consumer electronics over 
other products.  
 
• 
What key factors are influencing repairability of consumer electronics, and how they 
relate to challenges faced by repair service providers? 
 
A list of 21 repairability factors were identified, all of which were found in literature. Through 
interviews with RSP’s several of the same factors were also identified. However, these were used 
to put the factors in the context of the specific repair option. The connection between repairability 
factors and different repair options were based on three interviews and might not be 
==PAGE:110==
 
 
 
 
 
[106] 
 
representative of the repair option in general or to other product types. A literature review of this 
connection was not carried out, but should have been done to confirm the findings, if any relevant 
literature can be found.  
  
The factors identified in literature were described with different terminology and in 
different levels of details, though duplicates of factors were left out, a clear definition the detail 
level factors should be described was not defined, thus the number of identified factors could be 
reduced or expanded upon. The literature review was based on 4 repairability assessment 
frameworks, that was found relevant of the total 8 studies found. The literature review was 
strictly focused on repairability assessment frameworks, and while these generally was found to 
cover a wide range of factors, it cannot be excluded that other relevant factors could have been 
found if other literature was included.  
 
The key factor identified was the lack of knowledge about consumer electronics and the 
repairability among most users. The assumptions we based on findings from user interviews, and 
backed up by literature, however this focused on the relation to DIY repairs (Ackermann et al., 
2018). Further research could be done to find a stronger relation between lacking knowledge and 
the decision to repair.  
A support tool to help estimate the repair feasibility was proposed. The solution was found to 
be a good fit based on the defined challenges, however weighing the challenges and identifying 
other possible solutions in a structured way could have guided the decision process and 
strengthened the argument for developing a support tool.  
 
Work Package 3 covered the Development and test of the support tool for increasing repair 
of consumer electronics. 
 
• 
What current methods are available for assessing repairability of consumer electronics, 
and what users are they targeting? 
 
The same four repair assessment frameworks previously identified were analyzed 
documenting the goal, target user, repair phases covered, products covered, scoring and 
weighting system and the output. The authors found that the goal of the intended support was 
significantly different than what was found in literature. The literature review conducted focused 
on assessment tools covered in the literature, however less attention was given to tools that could 
be available through other sources. As the intended user of the tool is an average user of consumer 
electronics, it would be relevant to spend more time to do a systematic online review, through 
tools, on places available to a normal user. Such a review could either give input for the developed 
tool or serve as an argument for the novelty of the support tool. 
 
• 
How can a new support tool be developed for helping users of consumer electronics in 
the decision process to perform a repair? 
 
A new repairability assessment framework was developed, and a prototype of the Repair 
Feasibility Tool was developed. Based on simple input about a defective laptop, the tool generates 
an estimate of feasibility for repair  
The tool was developed as an iterative process. While the analyzed assessment frameworks 
provided a theoretical background for building the tool, it was found that a high level of 
adaptation was needed to ensure that lay people will be able to provide the input. 
==PAGE:111==
 
 
 
 
 
[107] 
 
Interviews with repair technicians was used as the main input in the adoption process of 
repairability factors and for defining scores. Using RSP’s knowledge was most suitable as the 
support tool simulates the estimation of a repair technician, however the number of experts were 
restricted, thus the results are representing a smaller group of repair technicians. The authors 
believe that the final tool is reflecting a more general truth however a more quantitative approach 
to collect inputs from repairers should be considered. 
The support tool was tested against the estimates of professional repair technicians. It was 
found that the feasibility score was with-in the success criteria, while the repair price estimate, 
was within the success criteria in about 50% of the cases. Due to a limited sample of test subjects 
(2), further testing is needed to provide a solid result, however the results implied that seemingly 
reasonable estimates could be achieved with the general approach that was taken. The authors 
will argue that with a further finetuning of the prototype, and the introduction of improved 
system data, the developed tool can prove to provide usable estimates for feasibility and repair 
price.  
 
The main purpose of the support tool was to target lay people, only using input obtainable by 
an average user of consumer electronics. While this was achieved for most input, the decision was 
made to keep “Defective priority part” as a main output, as it was considered crucial for 
determining repairability based on the specific defect. Determining the defective priority part 
based on simple input, relies on other factors than what was covered in this thesis, and might 
need a new study to obtain, thus it was not included. Usability of the Repair Feasibility tool, 
related to the input and output was not tested with the intended user, this decision had to be 
made as proving the functionality was prioritized higher.    
 
19 Final Conclusion 
Circular economy is a promising framework for closing loops and reducing waste, while allowing 
for new business opportunities to arise, so that both environmental and economic benefits will 
be seen. As one of many strategies within a circular thinking, this thesis seeks to understand and 
develop a solution for increasing repair of consumer electronics in Denmark. 
Through the review of two studies it was found that a reduction in the environmental impact 
can be expected by extending product lifetimes of laptops from 5 to at least 7 years. Repair is an 
effective method to prolong the lifetime of a product, and a central strategy to extend product 
lifetimes of consumer electronics. The scale at which repair of consumer electronics is taking 
place in Denmark was found to be minimal, as the average Danish household currently invests 1-
2% in repairs, compared to their annual expenses for consumer electronics, or equivalent to 96 
DKK a year.  
The user of consumer electronics is the central stakeholder for increasing repair as he or she 
makes the final decision to repair or not, however, the design of a product influences the 
feasibility for how, and by whom it can be repaired. The thesis investigated the factors influencing 
user decisions in relation to repair, and the factors defining the repairability of consumer 
electronics. 
The thesis covers literature reviews and interviews, with users of consumer electronics 
investigating the motivations, concerns, and the ability factors for users to decide to repair. The 
identified factors were related to money, time, the product, personal beliefs, the repair service 
and skills, and make up the personal requirements a user will have in order to decide to repair. 
==PAGE:112==
 
 
 
 
 
[108] 
 
The thesis identifies that there is a lack of knowledge about the repairability of consumer 
electronics among interviewed users, and that it limits their ability to estimate who is able, and 
under what requirements to perform a repair. Based on this understanding a support tool was 
developed, to assist the user in estimating the repairability of a laptop and take a grounded repair 
decision. Based on a review of 4 repairability assessment frameworks, it was found that none of 
them had the average user of consumer electronics in the center of interest, and that the general 
repairability score, was detached from the appearance of the defect and the context that frames 
the repair process. 
The support tool was developed based on knowledge gained through a literature review of 4 
repairability assessment frameworks and through interviews with repair service providers, were 
a general list of factors defining the repairability of consumer electronics and how the factors are 
experienced for 3 different repair options were identified.  
 A prototype of the support tool was evaluated and improved with input from repair 
technicians representing different repair options. The developed support tool uses simple input 
from a user, as the product age, new product price and brand. The tool estimates the repair price 
and feasibility of repairing the product at different repair options, with a score from 1 to 5. The 
final version of the support tool was tested with two repair technicians representing different 
options, covering different cases of defective laptops. 
The tests showed promising results when comparing estimates generated by the support tool, 
with estimates from repair technicians. The feasibility score showed an average difference of 0.7 
in a 1-5 scale reaching the success criteria of the thesis. The price estimates were accurate in 7 of 
12 cases indicating the need for improvements. It was concluded that further tests would need to 
be done, to ensure the validity of the results, and a set of recommendations for further 
improvement of the support tool were proposed.   
 
The support tool was developed as the authors identified that the uncertainties of users of 
consumer electronics can be reduced, by providing a quick estimate about if, and for what price, 
a laptop can be repaired, potentially increasing the number of people choosing to repair their 
broken laptop. While much work is being done on developing assessment frameworks for 
repairability of products, the authors will argue that more work can be done to define 
repairability based on the specific context of the user including: defect, available repair options 
and cost of labor. While the authors believe that the tool with further improvements can deliver 
a valuable output, much further work needs to be done before the potential impact of such a 
support tool can be evaluated.  
20 Recommendations for Further Work 
In this thesis the development of a support tool for a simple estimation of repairability has been 
presented. The prototype that was tested showed some potential providing an initial assessment 
of repairability and repair price, yet several improvements of the current prototype and additions 
in the functionality must be implemented and tested before a final version could potentially be 
released. The authors found that 4 major challenges have yet to be tackled before such a support 
tool could be successfully implemented.  
First the functionality of the support tool should be improved through further iterations of 
the prototype of the tool, focusing on the internal logic and connections between factors. The list 
of possible short-term and long-term improvements was proposed by the authors in chapter 15.5, 
could serve as a starting point. Furthermore, a list of long-term improvements was proposed for 
==PAGE:113==
 
 
 
 
 
[109] 
 
improving usability and reach of the support tool. Of these the authors stress the importance of 
developing and integrating a support for defect identification. This feature will aid the intended 
user of the tool in deciding the appropriate priority part and might prove to be a key factor in 
ensuring a reliable result. From interviews with several repair technicians this was mentioned as 
an absolute necessity for such a tool, but also a great challenge to overcome.  
Secondly further work needs to be done in testing the potential in developing and releasing 
the proposed support tool. Due to the time frame of the project, a test of the support tool was not 
carried out with the intended user, as the focus was to develop a functional prototype. The goal 
should be to test the ability of the tool in guiding the decision process among the users and 
indicate that a significant effect on users choosing a repair option over buying a new product can 
be expected if the tool is implemented. The authors would argue that this challenge should be the 
first to be addressed in any further work.  
Thirdly, further work should be done in finding a suitable business model for implementing 
the tool, with the wanted exposure to the intended user. Through the project several repair 
technicians expressed their interest in the support tool, if a certain reliability of the tool could be 
ensured, thus a collaboration with repair service providers could be a promising market looking 
into. Potentially a version of the tool can be customized for the service of a specific RSP through. 
This would allow for an estimation build on the specific repair technicians capability, but also 
increase the needed accuracy of the support tool. Finding a business model for the support tool, 
as a separate entity without affiliation with specific RSP’s should also be considered, however the 
authors stresses that keeping the support tool as a free service for the user is a necessity for 
ensuring the highest possible impact.  
Finally, work should be done on increasing the quality of the system input by expanding and 
improving the database. The authors believe that there could be great potential in further 
integrating results from repairability assessment frameworks. The scores from existing 
organizations like iFixit or Labo Fnac could be better integrated by getting access to sub scores 
for specific products. With the work being done by the EU on developing a standardized 
assessment framework, and potentially implementing a repairability labelling system on a 
European level, it should be investigated if this data could be integrated in a future version of the 
support tool. It could give an extra dimension of the labelling system by putting the general score 
into context of time, available repair options, and repair price.
