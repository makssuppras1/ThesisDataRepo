Abstract 
In recent years, it has become more frequent to see multi-storey buildings with timber as the load-bearing 
structural element being constructed. The world record for highest multi-storey timber buildings has been 
broken several times, most recently in Bergen, Norway, where a 14-storey timber building was constructed. 
More multi-storey timber buildings are planned to be constructed in the coming years, where even taller 
buildings will be constructed.  
One of the main reasons for the increasing numbers of multi-storey timber buildings, is the implementation 
of performance based codes in the fire regulations. Nowadays, most countries, including Denmark and 
Greenland, are still using prescriptive fire codes, that do not allow timber buildings to be higher than a 
certain height above the ground. Another reason for increasing in multi-storey timber buildings is a new 
building material in the construction industry called called cross laminated timber (CLT). CLT is a mass 
timber product that is produced by stacking and gluing timber panels perpendicular to one another. CLT-
panels are plates with widths up to 0.5m and lengths up to 20m. This new material has replaced the role of 
concrete and has been an eye opener for new way of building multi-storey buildings.  
In this thesis, the possibilities of using timber as load-bearing structural element in a multi-storey building in 
Nuuk, Greenland, was investigated. The investigation of the timber building, analysed in the present thesis, 
was performed from a structural engineer’s point of view. The building analysed in the present thesis is 
based on an existing concrete builing in Nuuk, where the concrete was replaced with timber. The building is 
27m high and has 8 storeys.  
During the design process, CLT-panels from different manufacturers were analysed, that all had their 
weaknesses and strengths. Some of them had insufficient stiffness properties, which led to high horizontal 
deflections of the building and some of them had insufficient tensile capacities. The main challenge of 
designing the timber building, turned out to be the basic wind velocity of Nuuk and the light weight of the 
building. The wind effect in Nuuk, on buildings with a height over 20m, was more than twice as high as in 
Denmark and in Norway. This gave problems in terms of wind induced vibrations of the building. The 
comfort criteria in ISO 10137 was used to evaluate the wind induced vibrations. To satisfy the comfort 
criteria, extra shear walls were needed to increase the stiffness and moment inertia of the building.  
The design of the load-bearing structural elements was performed by using structural analysis program called 
‘FEM-Design’, developed by the company ‘StruSoft AB’. 
Another challenge was to verify the fire safety of the load-bearing structural elements. This was solved by 
embedding the steel connections in the timber elements. Fire insulations in terms of gypsum boards were 
applied to all load-bearing timber elements.  
The final design of the building was drawn in 3D in Autodesk Revit. The drawing was used to extract 
quantities of materials. The quantities were used in the calculations of assessing the cost of the load-bearing 
structural elements of the building. The estimated cost was compared to the original concrete building. The 
estimated cost of the timber version of the building turned out to be 14% more expensive compared to the 
original concrete building.  
The main objective of the thesis was whether it is possible to use timber as the load-bearing structural 
element in a multi-storey timber building and was concluded to be possible.

1   Introduction 
In the past decade the number of multi-storey buildings with timber as load-bearing structure has increased. 
The wooden tall buildings have reached new heights as never seen before; the world record for highest 
timber building has been set multiple times in the past decade, most recently in Norway, where a 14-storey 
tall timber building was constructed. Having the title of the world record for tallest timber building will not 
last long – new taller buildings are planned to be constructed in the coming years and other taller buildings 
are on the drawing boards. It is countries such as Norway, Sweden, Germany, Austria, England, Australia, 
Canada and a number of other countries that has built multi-storey timber buildings (MSTB) one after the 
other in the recent years.  
The main advantages of using wood rather than other materials such as concrete and steel, are sustainability 
and environmental aspects of wood. Even though the performance of materials as concrete and steel are 
greatly appreciated, their contribution for greenhouse gas emission is high, and with the world striving to 
decrease the carbon footprint, wood as a construction material becomes desirable in the construction 
industry. Trees are known to absorb carbon dioxide (CO2) and release oxygen (O2) into the atmosphere. 
When trees are cut down and timber is manufactured from the trees, the CO2 stays in the wood. Trees release 
the CO2 in the process of decaying and dying or when the wood is burnt, so it gives an advantage of storing 
the CO2 to cut down the tree before the process of decaying and dying has taken a hold and gives room for 
another tree to grow.  
Development of a building material, called cross laminated timber (CLT), has revolutionised new way of 
building multi-storey timber buildings. CLT is a mass timber element that consists of layers of lumber 
boards that are glued and stacked together crosswise in perpendicular to one another, as illustrated on Figure 
1-1. With large strength and stiffness properties, it has replaced the role of concrete and has been an eye 
opener for new way of building multi-storey timber buildings in combination with other mass timber 
materials, such as glued laminated timber (GLT), laminated veneer lumber (LVL) etc. In some multi-storey 
timber buildings CLT has been used to take on the horizontal forces and has been used as the core of the 
building. Using prefabricated CLT elements has reduced the construction time of a building and has reduced 
the complexity of assembling a building.  
 
Figure 1-1 CLT configuration (left)  and a CLT wall assembly (right). (FPInnovations, 2013) 
Another reason for increase in numbers of timber buildings higher than 4 storeys is innovation of traditional 
national building codes. In most countries the traditional building codes do not allow timber buildings to be 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
10 
more than 4 stories high due to the fire safety regulations. Today, countries such as Norway, England, 
Sweden, Canada etc. has developed and made changes to their fire safety standards. Taking Norway as an 
example, their fire safety design approach is performance based, which means that they can build as high as 
the timber construction can stand and as long as the fire safety of the building can be verified through 
calculation methods. Other countries are still approaching and progressing to implement performance based 
fire safety codes. Denmark is one of the countries that has yet to make progress in developing its fire safety 
regulations, but has shown interests in multi-storey timber buildings. The Danish building regulations for fire 
safety limit to build timber buildings no taller than 4 stories. (Erhvers- og Byggestyrelsen, 2010). Denmark is 
a country with ambitions to reduce the carbon footprint and it is highly probable that actions will be taken in 
the coming years to use timber in multi-storey buildings. (Danmarks træportal, 2015).  
Use of mass timber in buildings has reduced the challenges of fire proving multi-storey timber buildings, as 
their fire performance has been proved to be safer than of light wood-framed buildings. With predictable fire 
behaviour, it has been proven through fire tests that mass timbers can be designed to withstand 2 hours of 
fire, which is the performance requirement of a high-rise / multi-storey building in the fire regulations. 
(Energistyrelsen, 2012) (FPInnovations, 2013).   
Due to the light weight of the multi-storey timber buildings, compared to conventional concrete and steel 
buildings, one of the main challenges has been the response to the dynamic load actions. In some buildings, 
this has been solved by increasing the mass of the building, by adding concrete slabs in some floors. Taking 
the 14-storey timber building in Norway as an example, concrete slabs have been added to every 5th floor to 
increase the mass of the building. Combining wood with other materials has made it possible for some multi-
storey timber buildings to be built higher and overcome forces of the nature.  
1.1   Hypothesis and thesis statement 
In Greenland, single-family houses and 1-2 storey buildings are almost exclusively made of light wood-
frame construction. It is not common to see single-family houses made of other materials such as concrete, 
steel or brick. Wood is a material that has lower thermal conductivity than concrete, steel and brick, making 
it the preferable material to use in constructions in Greenland in terms of keeping buildings well insulated for 
the harsh, cold winters. Unfortunately, the traditional way of using wood in buildings has not been used for 
buildings with 2+ storeys. Concrete along with steel reinforcement is the common construction material for 
2+ storey buildings, as it is the case in other countries.  
There are several reasons wood is not used in multi-storey buildings, one of which is for fire safety reasons. 
Building codes in Greenland have virtually been copied from the Danish building codes with some 
exceptions and the fire safety regulations in Greenland are the same as in the Danish Building Regulations. 
(Erhvers- og Byggestyrelsen, 2010) (Grønlands hjemmestyre, 2006). Even though the regulations block the 
use of timber construction in buildings with 4+ floors, there is one other major reason why 4+ storey 
buildings of timber do not exist.  
One major reason why timber has not been used in 2+ storey buildings is the same reason as in any other 
countries as Denmark – its properties. Light wood-frame buildings have lower stiffness than concrete and 
steel. Wood expands when the moisture content increases and shrinks when the moisture content decreases. 
Due to the low stiffness and high expandability when subjected to moisture change, the subsidence of light 
wood-frame buildings are more extensive than of steel and concrete structures. Development of the mass 
timber material, CLT, has eliminated weaknesses of the light wood-frame such as weak fire performance, 
moisture content problems, subsidence etc. and has opened new opportunities. One of the main reasons why

material in the market and limitations from outdated prescriptive fire regulations.  
Norway has recently changed their fire safety regulations to performance based design, which gave the 
opportunity to build higher with timber. To this day, multi-storey timber buildings has already been 
constructed, namely in Bergen (14 storeys), Oslo (8 stories) and one is under construction in Trondheim (10 
storeys). Norway has similar weather conditions as Greenland.

1.2   Procedure and objective 
The main objective of this thesis is to select an existing multi-storey building in Nuuk, Greenland, and 
design it with timber as the load-bearing structure instead of the original material that was used in the actual 
building – concrete. A 3D structural analysis software will be used for the design purpose. The design will 
clarify if it is possible and profitable to build a multi-storey timber building in Greenland. The procedure of 
this thesis will be as follow: 
•  
Description and analysis of other high rise wood buildings around the world 
•  
Identification of challenges in using wood as a structural material 
•  
Selection of an existing multi-storey building in Nuuk to be designed in wood 
•  
Choice of structural analysis software to be used in designing the building 
•  
Design of the selected building 
•  
Cost assessment of the building 
•  
Assessment of structural analysis software 
•  
Discussion and overall assessment of using wood in multi-storey buildings in Greenland 
1.3   Scope 
This thesis will focus mainly on the structural part, to confirm whether or not it is possible to design a multi-
storey timber building in Nuuk, Greenland. Other subjects such as mould will be addressed, but no further 
investigations or calculations will be conducted. Moreover, used structural software programs will be 
assessed and evaluated for suitability of designing wood structures and for suitability to be implemented in 
Greenland. Pre-fabrication, shipping and estimated cost of the multi-storey timber building will also be 
assessed and compared to the original concrete building.  
Structural analysis software will mainly be used during the design of the structural system, to reduce the time 
spent on calculations. Furthermore, not every single detail e.g. connections will be calculated, but the most 
important / critical details will be assessed and calculated. The structural geotechnical parts will not be 
looked into or designed.

2   Tall timber buildings through history 
Throughout the history of the mankind, wood has always been available to create shelter against wind, rain 
and cold. Wood is the oldest building material and has been a useful material in providing necessary tools to 
the mankind. Through archaeological findings timber framed longhouses can be dated back to 4500 – 3000 
BC, that were built by farmers and did not usually stand more than twenty years. (Kuklik, 2008). 
Reconstruction of the longhouses can be seen in Figure 2-1.  
 
Figure 2-1 (left): Longhouse 4500 BC. (right): Longhouse 3000 BC. (Kuklik, 2008). 
Historical multi-storey or tall timber buildings from 800 – 1400 years ago that are still standing are to be 
found in countries China, Japan, Norway etc. The most common tall timber buildings are temples called 
pagoda. These are the traditional temples that have been built throughout Asia for centuries. Those that are 
still standing have resisted the forces of the nature for centuries, forces like seismic, wind, humid 
environments etc. One of the oldest pagoda temples still standing is in Japan (Horyu-ji temple) that is 5 
storey and 32 metres high and was built in 7th century. The structure is supported by a centrally placed 
wooden pillar, as shown on the left side of Figure 2-2. (Green & Karsh, 2012). One of the tallest wooden 
pagoda temple is in China (Yingxian temple) that has 9 storeys and is 67 metres high and was built in 11th 
century, see right side of Figure 2-2. The building is supported by circular arranged timber columns and the 
building is made entirely of wood. (Gerard, 2014). 
 
Figure 2-2 (left): Pagoda temple in Japan from 7th century. (Green & Karsh, 2012).  
(right): Pagoda temple in China from 11th century. (Gerard, 2014). 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
15 
Other known historical tall timber buildings are stave churches in Scandinavia; they were built entirely of 
heavy wood. Their load bearing structures usually consisted of post, beams and arches. Two of the oldest and 
well preserved stave churches can be seen on Figure 2-3. They were built in 12th century and are still 
standing despite being exposed to forces of nature for centuries. (Thelandersson & Larsen, 2003) (Brooke, 
2002-2011).  
 
Figure 2-3 (left): Urnes stave church from 12th century in Norway. (Green & Karsh, 2012).  
(right): Borgund stave church from 12th century in Norway. (Brooke, 2002-2011). 
Some more modern tall timber structures that were built around a century ago are 6-storey office and 
industrial building, Leckie Building, built in 1908 in Vancouver, Canada, and 7-storey (later expanded to 8 
storey) business and storage building, Perry House, built in 1913 in Brisbane, Australia, both shown on 
Figure 2-4. The structural system of the two buildings, are based on brick-timber construction, where the 
beams and columns are of heavy timbers and the facades are of brick. The technique brick-timber 
construction was popular back in the early 20th century and it allowed construction of multi-storey industrial 
and office buildings with large open spaces. (Gerard, 2014). 
 
Figure 2-4 (left): 7-storey (now 8) Perry House in Brisbane, Australia. (Gerard, 2014). 
(right): 6-storey Leckie Building in Vancouver, Canada. (Gerard, 2014). 
Historical timber buildings that are shown above were built before prescriptive codes for fire limit the 
heights, maximum storeys and open area of timber buildings. Due to the large city fires throughout the 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
16 
history, the prescriptive codes that limit the height, maximums storeys and open areas of the buildings 
containing combustible materials, such as timber, were created and applied. In other words, timber was 
officially replaced in multi-storey / high-rise buildings by other non-combustible materials such as concrete 
and steel. In the next century, in most countries timber was only used for 1-, 2-, 3- and in rare cases 4-storey 
buildings. Tall timber buildings that became more visible were sports halls, where a number of them were 
made of arched heavy / mass timber members and trusses in terms of glued laminated timber (GLT).  
Performance-based codes were first introduced in the late 20th century, where United Kingdom was the first 
to take a step into applying performance-based codes in their building codes. The performance oriented 
codes were modified in 1985, but were later revised in 1991. A year later in 1992, New Zealand applied 
performance-based codes in their building code and years later in the late 1990’s Norway followed by 
implying performance-based codes in their building code. (Meacham, 1996). Later in the past 2 decades a 
number of other countries have also revised their fire safety regulations and made it possible to build higher 
with wood. This has resulted in significantly increase of multi-storey timber buildings in the recent decade. 
In combination with new implemented buildings codes, the world strive to reduce the carbon footprint and 
new developments of mass timber products, the multi-storey / tall timber buildings are becoming more 
popular. The world record for tallest multi-storey timber building has been broken multiple times, most 
recently ‘Treet’ in Bergen, Norway, where a 14-storey, 49-meter-high high building with timber as the load-
bearing structure was constructed and opened in 2015. Now the runner-up for the world record is 10-storey 
and 32.2-meter-high apartment ‘Forte’ in Melbourne, Australia, constructed in 2013.  
‘Treet’ is an apartment and was built in combination of cross laminated timber (CLT) and GLT. The CLT is 
not part of the load-bearing structure of the building – the elevator shaft, internal walls, ceilings, floors and 
balconies are of CLT. The frame-structure, which is the load-bearing structure of the building, is of GLT, see 
the building on Figure 2-5. The ground floor which is the base of the building is of concrete. 
The runner-up for the tallest timber building ‘Forte’ was entirely made of CLT, except the ground floor is of 
concrete. The building can be seen on Figure 2-5. More detailed in terms of structural descriptions can be 
seen in chapter 3.  
 
Figure 2-5 (left): ‘Treet’ in Bergen, Norway. (Malo & Abrahamsen, 2014). 
(right): ‘Forte’ in Melbourne, Australia. (Lend Lease, 2012).  
Having the title of world record for tallest multi-storey timber building won’t last long, other taller buildings 
are planned to be constructed in the coming years. Another residence building that is planned to be 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
17 
constructed in 2017 is ‘Tall Wood Residence’ in Vancouver, Canada. It will be 18-storey and 53-meter-high 
multi-storey timber building and it will be the new world record holder. Just like ‘Treet’ and ‘Forte’ the base 
of the building (ground floor) will be of concrete – the 2 staircases and elevator shaft will as well be of 
concrete. Rest of the building will consist of mass timber construction, which will be a combination of CLT 
and GLT-elements. See Figure 2-6. (Danmarks træportal, 2015). 
‘Tall wood residence’ won’t hold the world record for long – two towers in Vienna are planned to be 
constructed in the same year, in 2017. They will be called ‘HoHo Wien’, they will consist of 24 floors and 
will be 84-metres high. The structural system of the buildings will be a so called hybrid-construction, which 
is a combination of concrete and timber. The buildings will primarily consist of timber and it is expected that 
around 76% of the materials will be of timber. Use of concrete will increase the mass of the building, which 
will increase the buildings resistance to horizontal forces and improve its dynamic response. See Figure 2-6. 
(Danmarks træportal, 2015).  
 
Figure 2-6 (left): ‘Tall wood residence’ in Vancouver, Canada. (Danmarks træportal, 2015). 
(right): ‘HoHo Wien’ in Vienna, Austria. (Danmarks træportal, 2015). 
The history of using wood in tall buildings will continue in the next decades and it is exciting to see how tall 
the timber buildings will get and how impressive they will get. Development of new mass timber products 
and innovation of building codes has opened new opportunities to build higher with timber and the history of 
using wood in tall timber buildings can continue and will be remained.

3   Multi-storey timber buildings and their structural systems 
In the following subchapters some of the multi-storey timber buildings that have been built in the past 
decade will be presented, the main focus is to present the different structural system methods that have been 
used to these buildings. It will be shown how they have solved and constructed their structural systems in 
order to overcome the forces of the mother nature e.g. wind forces. It will also be presented and described 
how the fire safety of the buildings has been ensured and verified against fire. At last the challenges using 
wood in these buildings will be presented and compared with other buildings.  
3.1   ‘Treet’ in Bergen, Norway 
The tallest multi-storey timber building in Bergen, Norway, is named ‘Treet’ which means ‘The Tree’. It has 
14-storeys and is 49-meter high. 13 storeys of the 14 consist of entirely mass timbers and the ground floor, 
which is the base of the building, consists of concrete. The building is an apartment and was completed in 
2015. The building consists of prefabricated modules of CLT and trusses that holds the building together are

4   Timber as a structural material 
One of the oldest known building materials is timber. All timber comes from trees in forests. Botanical tree 
types used for solid timber can be categorized into softwood and hardwood. Softwood trees are evergreen, 
cone-bearing trees with needle-like leaves, whereas hardwood trees are broad-leaved trees that lose their 
leaves at the end of each growing season. Hardwood has higher density, higher strength, is more durable and 
more expensive than softwood. Out of the two types of trees, softwood is almost exclusively used as 
construction timber in Denmark. In addition, CLT is manufactured almost exclusively from softwood.  
An idealized, flawless, basic structure of a tree is illustrated on the left side of Figure 4-1, and as shown 
consists of (from the inner most part) a pith, heartwood, sapwood, cambium and inner and outer bark. The 
annual rings are seen as a difference in color, which is due to the fact the grains grow differently during the 
different seasons.  
 
Figure 4-1 (left): Basic structure of a tree. (right): Softwood’s microstructure, where EW stands for early-wood, LW for late-wood. 
(Ormarsson, 2013). 
A softwood’s grain structure is less complex than of hardwood’s, as 90 – 95 % of it consists of tracheids, 
which are 2 – 5 mm long, 10 – 50 𝜇m wide cells with a hole in the middle called lumen. As mentioned 
already, there is a difference between grains grown on different seasons. The cell wall is thicker on grains 
grown on autumn (latewood, denoted LW on right side in Figure 4-1) than the cell walls grown on spring 
(early-wood, denoted EW on right side in Figure 4-1). As the cell wall is the solid part of the tree, the 
latewood has higher density and higher strength than early-wood. The density of latewood can be 3 times 
higher than of early-wood’s (Rump, 2008).  
A flawless tree has three main directions – longitudinal, tangential and radial direction, denoted L, T and R 
respectively on Figure 4-2. The longitudinal direction is also called the grain direction.  
==PAGE:30==
 
 
29 
 
Figure 4-2 Three main directions in a flawless tree structure – L: longitudinal, T: tangential, R: radial. (Christensen, 2011). 
A tree’s different physical structure in each direction makes it an orthotropic material, which means that it 
has different properties in different directions and therefore the material response depends on the load’s 
direction. The strength, stiffness and other properties are not the same for different directions. Even though 
there are three main directions in a flawless tree, there is distinguished between only two main directions 
after the conversion to timber – grain direction and perpendicular to the grain direction. The strength as well 
as its stiffness is higher in the grain direction than perpendicular to the grain direction.  
A tree is sawn along the longitudinal direction when converted into timber. Examples of sawing patterns are 
shown on Figure 4-3. 
 
Figure 4-3 Examples of sawing patterns. (Porteous & Kermani, 2013). 
Once the tree has been sawn and converted into timber, it is sorted and classified according to the strength. 
The strength is graded either by visual grading or machine grading, which either way is done according to 
standards. Visual grading is done by an approved grader, who manually examines each one of the timbers for 
the size and frequency of physical characteristics and defects. The grader checks the knots, wanes, distortion 
and slope of grains among other things to classify the timber. Machine grading of timber can be done in 
number of different ways, one of which is to exert pressure to the timber, measure the deflection and 
compare it with the criteria set by the standards, which leads to a classification of the timber (Porteous & 
Kermani, 2013). 
4.1   Mass timber  
One framing style in timber construction is mass timber construction, which refers to the use of large pieces 
of wood-based products in a construction. Mass timber products include glued-laminated timber (GLT), nail 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
30 
laminated timber (NLT) and cross laminated timber (CLT) among others. Mass timber can be applied in 
some construction buildings instead of concrete, steel and masonry.   
GLT, also called glulam, consists of timber boards (laminates), glued together and arranged such that the 
grains of all laminates are parallel to the longitudinal axis of the timber. This is done in order to achieve a 
high strength and stiffness in one direction, to be used as columns or beams in large constructions.  
4.1.1   Cross Laminated Timber 
CLT, on the other hand, is a solid timber panel that consists of minimum three orthogonally bonded layers of 
laminates. A CLT panel is symmetrical around the middle layer, and thus must have an odd number of 
layers. Unlike glulam, the layers are glued perpendicular to one another and therefore have high strength and 
stiffness in both the longitudinal and transverse directions. Other advantages on using CLT include increased 
robustness and splitting resistance, high axial load capacity for walls and the much lower carbon footprint 
compared to other construction materials. The market provides panels with a thickness up to 500 mm and a 
length up to 24 m, but limiting factors as transport and crane lifting of the panels must be considered when 
designing constructions of CLT. The outer layers of the panels can be oriented in either direction when 
manufactured, but it should be designed in such a way that the outer layers are vertically oriented for walls, 
and that the outer layers are oriented in the length direction for floor and roofs. 
Each CLT manufacturer has their own build-up laminates, width, length, strength and stiffness properties of 
CLT-panels and most of the manufacturers uses planks that are graded between C16 – C24. Under and after 
the production, the planks are kept within a constant moisture content of about 10 ± 2%. This is because the 
physical properties of wood changes according to its moisture content. When the moisture content of wood 
increases the volume of wood increases as well and the opposite happens when its moisture content 
decreases. Changes in moisture content can lead to cracks, twisting, bowing etc. in the wood which will 
deteriorate the mechanical properties and quality of the wood product. This is also one of the main reasons 
why concrete bases are used in multi-storey timber buildings, which is to avoid direct exposure to the ground 
and weather conditions. This means also that CLT-panels can only be used in service class 1 and 2 and 
should not be used in service class 3 (direct exposure to weather changes).  
4.1.1.1  
CLT as a composite material 
Due to the crosswise lamination, CLT can be treated as a composite material. The strength and stiffness 
properties of CLT-panels are depended on the build-up of the laminates. Since CLT has not been 
implemented in buildings codes / standards and because the manufacturers have their own dimensions and 
strength and stiffness properties, the composite theory can be used as a guidance to determine the strength 
and stiffness properties of CLT.  
The composite theory takes into account the lamination build-up of the panel and if the panel is loaded in or 
perpendicular to the plane and if it is loaded parallel or perpendicular to the outer grain direction. From this a 
composition factor, 𝑘(, is calculated. The composition factor is then multiplied with the strength and stiffness 
value of the outer layer to get the effective strength and stiffness properties of the panel. Examples of 
determinations of composite factors, 𝑘(, can be seen in Fejl! Henvisningskilden blev ikke fundet.. (Blass & 
Fellmoser, Undated).

The Greenlandic national annexes have been made to make the Eurocodes applicable to the Greenlandic

states namely in Ultimate Limit State (ULS) and in Service Limit State (SLS). In ULS the structural 
components are designed for their ultimate bearing capacities and in SLS they are designed according to 
their deflections.

7   Structural analysis software 
The building will be modelled in a structural analysis software program called ‘FEM-Design’, which is a 
software that was developed from company ‘StruSoft AB’. The program is an advanced finite element 
method (FEM) analysis software. FEM-Design features a selection of several templates. The template that 
will be used in this project is called ‘3D Structure’. This template can analyse a mixture of 3d-models of 
shells, frames, soil, etc. whereas other templates as ‘3D Frame’ can only analyse 3d frame models. FEM-
Design performs design of concrete-, steel- and timber-structures according to Eurocodes.  
When the program starts, it gives a selection of different Eurocodes that are to be used in designing the 
model. In this project the Danish National Annex to Eurocode will be used, because the partial safety factors 
for timber and concrete are the same as in Greenlandic National Annex to Eurocode. Even if the partial 
safety factors or other factors are not the same, the user of the program has the choice to make changes, so it 
complies with the desired building code.  
The designer in FEM-Design has also the choice to auto-generate snow and wind loads on the model, but in 
this project the snow and wind loads will be manually applied to the model, by using snow loads and wind 
loads from Greenlandic National Annex to Eurocodes.  
The program will be used to design the structural members of the building and to analyse global and local 
deflections of the building. In addition, connection forces or other forces acting on the structural elements 
that the program has calculated will be used in further design calculations, for example, when designing the 
connections, which will be done by hand. 
In the next chapter ‘8 Design of multi-storey timber building’, the creation of the building model in FEM-
Design will be described.  
The FEM-Design program is a BIM-based program, which means that it can import other architectural or 
structural models into the program, such as models from Autodesk Revit. However, if the imported model 
should be ready to be analysed in FEM-Design, the models has to feature an analytical model and not only 
an architectural model, otherwise the imported model won’t be complete or won’t work. 
The final designed building in this project will also be modelled in Autodesk Revit. The model will include 
all the analytical models, so it can be imported to other structural analysis software programs. The model can 
be seen further in the report. 
No other FEM-based programs will be used in this project.

8.1   Design considerations 
The original concrete building of ‘Suloraq’ consists as earlier described entirely of concrete walls and slabs, 
no beams or columns were used. From a structural engineer point of view, it is a solid way of constructing a 
building, where bearing walls are everywhere to resist the lateral forces of nature as wind. This way of 
constructing a building has also its downsides, because having bearing structural elements everywhere in the 
building reduces the flexibility of the building, meaning that the bearing structures can not be taken down in 
future. This limits the possibilities for future renovations or changes to the building that might be needed. 
The holes for windows and doors are fixed to the walls and walls can’t just be removed to join rooms.  
When designing the building with wood, one option is that, the concrete walls in the original building can 
just be replaced by the CLT-plates and see if the building can hold, but it is more interesting to see and 
explore how flexible the building can be made with CLT. This means that the criterions such as minimum 
usage of CLT-walls and minimum usage of diagonal bars should be aimed when designing the building. Of 
course the comfort criterions such as deflections, vibrations and dynamic response of the building should be 
maintained and should be kept in an acceptable level. The flexibility of the building can be categorized into 
two categories: 
1.   Flexibility in facades 
a.   If no bearing walls are used in facades and only post and beams are used instead, this creates 
a flexibility in the facades for future renovations that might be needed. In nowadays it is 
common to see old buildings undergo renovations for aesthetic reasons or to improve the 
sustainable performance of the building to fulfil new energy and comfort requirements in the 
present building codes. When buildings undergo renovations, in some cases more windows 
might be needed in the facades to increase and utilize the sunlight. 
2.   Flexibility inside the building 
a.   Flexibility inside the building can also be beneficial in the future. There might come in the 
future a necessity to remove some walls to join 1 or 2 rooms, this can be due to the change 
of building usage, e.g. from apartment to office. 
It will be great if these two flexibilities can be met, if not, flexibility in the facades will be prioritized, 
because when building codes get updated, they usually get updated to stricter requirements, especially with 
the sustainability.  
First off, the core of the building which will consist of CLT-panels, should be kept centred in the building 
and should be symmetrical to avoid torsions of the building. If the core can be kept centred in the building, a 
high flexibility in the facades and in the apartments can be achieved, and this way the structural bearing 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
57 
walls won’t stand in a way to have the opportunity for future renovations or changes that might come to the 
building. If the horizontal deflections are too high, the thickness of the core walls can be increased or extra 
walls can be added in the building.  
When designing the building with CLT-panels, the thicknesses will be kept under or equal to the concrete 
wall thickness of 180mm in the original building, and if possible smaller thicknesses will be used. The 
reason is to avoid too large total wall thicknesses, because the CLT-panels might need extra insulation for 
fire safety reasons and for sound insulation, because of the poorer sound performance of CLT-panels.  
As described in chapter 4.1.1 CLT, the CLT-panels should not be categorized to service class 3, this means 
that they cannot be directly exposed to weather conditions as rain or other forms of moisture. They need to 
be lifted away from the ground to avoid direct exposure. In chapter ‘3 Multi-storey timber buildings and their 
structural systems’, four different multi-storey timber buildings around the world were described and they all 
have been lifted off from the ground and are standing on top of a concrete base. So, the first step here is to 
keep the concrete elements in the base and ground floor of the original building to use them as a base for 
CLT-panels, see Figure 8-1. The reason for keeping the ground floor concrete elements and raising the CLT-
panels to on top of the ground floor ceiling, is to avoid the risk of being exposed to accumulated snow 
around the building, where snow accumulations around the buildings in Greenland is a common feature.

8.1.1   Static system 
The static system of the building can be in many ways. In steel structures diagonal steel bars are usually used 
to take on the lateral forces, in concrete structures the concrete walls are usually used to resist the lateral 
forces and in timber framed buildings the lateral forces are either taken by diagonal steel bars or by diagonal 
timber members or by timber panels e.g. plywood.  
The CLT-walls will be used to resist the lateral forces and they act in similar way as concrete walls, but with 
different strength and stiffness parameters. As earlier described the core of the building will be kept centred 
in the building and it should rather be symmetrical to avoid torsions of the building. One example of a 
common static system is having core in the middle, which surrounds stairway and elevator, and where beams 
and columns are used in the exterior of the building, see Figure 8-2. This static system is common in 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
58 
concrete buildings. This method was also used in one of the described multi-storey timber buildings in 
chapter 3.3 and chapter 3.4.  
One idea behind this system is that all the lateral forces from the wind should be carried from the core of the 
building, which will act as a cantilever column. The columns, beams and slabs will only take the vertical 
forces. The vertical forces will go through the core and columns to the foundation. When the wind hits the 
façade of the building, the columns and beams will pass on the lateral forces through the slab and into the 
core of the building. In this system, the connections between beams, columns and slabs will be hinged. 
The other idea is to activate the beams, which means to have fixed connections in the beams and between 
beams and columns, so that they also can take some of the lateral forces. This method can be used if the 
lateral deflections in the previous method are too large or if the core can’t itself resist the lateral forces. By 
activating the beams will mean greater forces in the beams, which will also result in bigger dimensions of the 
beams.  
The third idea can also be, increase the thicknesses of the core walls or add shear walls inside the building.  
 
Figure 8-2 Core in the middle and frame at exterior. 
The static system in Figure 8-2 has this advantage of being flexible both in the facades and inside the 
building. If the building should undergo renovations or changes, no bearing walls will stand in a way. This 
method will also be the first one to be investigated.  
The connections between beams, columns and slabs and between beams and the core will be hinged. This 
means that all the lateral forces will be carried by the core.

8.3   Material choice

described and included in Eurocodes.

8.7.3   Wind-induced vibrations

and steel. For this matter, the timber buildings will more likely to create low frequency motions when 
subjected to dynamic loads as wind. In this section the serviceability comfort criteria of the building will be 
investigated, a graph that gives recommended design criteria from ISO 10137 will be used to evaluate if the 
vibrations and accelerations of the building are within the comfort criteria. The graph is used for a 1-year 
return period of the wind and first mode of natural frequency of a building. (International Organization for 
Standardization, 2007). 
As described in chapter ‘3 Multi-storey timber buildings and their structural systems’, the ‘Treet’ building in 
Norway, has three floors consisting of concrete, which has the purpose to increase the mass of the building 
and improve the dynamic response of the building.  
The multi-storey timber building that is designed in this project has been made extra stiff and it resulted in 
horizontal deflections of only 20mm. The main reason for making the building extra stiff, is to keep within 
the comfort criteria for serviceability in wind induced vibrations.

8.9   Structural fire safety 
One of the main reasons for Greenland, Denmark and other countries for not be able to build multi-storey 
timber buildings is, because the fire safety regulations do not permit buildings where the topmost floor is 
above 9.6𝑚 to have materials that have lower fire performance than A2-s1,d0. (Energistyrelsen, 2012). This 
fire performance class means (Bengtsson, 2014): 
•  
A2-s1: no significant contribution to fire growth, limited smoke production 
•  
d0: no droplets within 600 s 
Wood materials are mostly classified as D-s2, which means that they contribute to the fire and flashover. The 
Stora Enso CLT-panels that are used in this project are classified as D-s2,d0. This means that they contribute 
to the fire and has limited smoke production. According to the fire regulations, the topmost floors of timber 
buildings are allowed to have a maximum height of 9.6𝑚, but only if automatic sprinklers are applied to the 
building or fire insulations with performance class of K2 60 A2-s1,d0, are used as sheathings. See also Figure 
8-42.  
The load-bearing structural elements of buildings with the topmost floor higher than 9.6𝑚 and less than 12𝑚 
should be able to resist the fire at least for 60 minutes and should be classified at least with A2-s1,d0.  
The load-bearing structural elements for buildings where the topmost floor is above 12𝑚 should be able to 
resist the fire for at least 120 minutes and should be classified at least with A2-s1,d0. See also Figure 8-42. It 
means that the load-bearing structural elements in the building that is designed in this project should be 
designed for a 120 minutes of fire.  
 
Figure 8-42 Fire performance requirements of load-bearing structural elements. (Energistyrelsen, 2012).

11  Discussion 
Cross laminated timber is still new to the construction / engineering market, which has made the project 
challenging. Information about the product is to be found in the internet or in the manufacturer’s webpages, 
but more detailed information about the product is still limited. For instance, the available data on the 
strength and stiffness properties of CLT are limited, the manufacturers often require request to get the 
desired information.  
The designing process of this project started off with the most accessible CLT-data, which is the built-in data 
in the design program software’s library. This was used most of the time in the designing process. As the 
designing progressed it was apparent that the building had horizontal deflection problems – the vertical 
deflections were not the problem. The problem lies in the materials stiffness parameter, which is the shear 
modulus of the walls. The shear modulus in the program was determined to be 108 MPa, which is more than 
5 times lower than other wood products. To satisfy the recommended deflection limit of 𝑙/500, the building 
ended up having shear walls in every module-lines inside the building, plus some shear walls in the facades. 
The thickness of shear walls was at 174mm, which is relatively thick, because with some sound and fire 
protection added to them, it will take up quite amount of space in the building. It was clear that other CLT-
products needed to be analysed. The CLT manufacturers use wide range of wood lumber classes when 
producing their products, ranging between C14 to C30, and in addition, each manufacturer has its own 
lamination build-up configurations. The products that were available at that time of the designing process 
had either missing information about the shear modulus of walls or the strength properties were not sufficient 
in terms of tensile strength. When subjected to horizontal forces, the building, with such a light weight, 
creates tension forces at the shear walls, so CLT-walls with small tensile strengths had load-bearing capacity 
problems. Finally, CLT-panels from Stora Enso were chosen to be used. Their CLT-products had high 
strength capacities, as high classes of wood lumbers are used in their products and had the right 
configurations of lamination build-ups. The final design of the building was made with these products. With 
load-bearing capacities verified and with deflections within the maximum limits, the next problem was the 
wind induced vibration problems of the building.  
One of the most challenging when designing buildings with height above 20m in Greenland is the high wind 
velocities. In Nuuk, the basic wind velocity is 40	  𝑚/𝑠. In Denmark, the basic wind velocity is 24	  𝑚/𝑠 and 
27	  𝑚/𝑠 for coastal areas in the west. The multi-storey timber building ‘Treet’ in Norway was designed for a 
basic wind velocity of 26	  𝑚/𝑠. (Bjertnæs & Malo, 2014). 
The challenge of having a basic wind velocity of 40	  𝑚/𝑠 lies in calculating the wind velocity to wind 
velocity pressure, where the basic wind velocity is power of 2 in the equation:

another.  
One major challenge was to satisfy the comfort criteria in ISO 10137 for wind induced vibrations, due to the 
high wind effect on the building and light weight of the building.  
The wind pressures are established for each town and village in Greenland for buildings with height lower 
than 20m. The wind pressure for Nuuk is 1.6	  𝑘𝑁/𝑚S, which in comparison to a building with a height above

With the current building codes, it will be challenging for multi-storey timber buildings with height over 
20m to be designed in Greenland, not that the reason is that it is impossible, but simply because more shear 
walls or some sort of damping of a building will be needed. For instance, in ‘Treet’, three reinforced 
concrete slabs were added to the building, to increase the weight of the building. By increasing the weight of 
the building the comfort criteria in ISO 10137 for wind induced vibrations was satisfied. (Bjertnæs & Malo, 
2014). This method can also be investigated for multi-storey timber buildings in Greenland.

Due to the light weight of the building and high wind pressures, the hold-down connections were essential. 
The connections in the CLT-panels were chosen and designed according to the recommended connections in 
a CLT book. (FPInnovations, 2013). Even though the calculations verified the load-bearing capacities of the 
connections, the choice of type of connections can be further discussed; will the connections loosen up after 
the building has come into use and has settled? The connections that are used in this project do not have the 
option to be tightened further. For that reason, further investigations on the connections are needed to verify 
whether it will be necessary to tighten the connections further after the building has settled.   
During the design of the connections in the CLT-panels, it was clear that information about CLT is still 
limited. For instance, further research on the embedment strength and splitting capacity of CLT-panels was 
necessary. For the embedment strength, an equation developed through empirical tests, was found. (Uibel & 
Blass, 2012). On the splitting capacity, no formal method or equation was found. In all the findings on 
splitting capacity of the CLT-panels, it was insisted that CLT-panels have high splitting capacities due to 
their crosswise build-ups and no further explanation or equation was given. Instead, the equation for splitting 
==PAGE:108==

capacity for an ordinary timber was used in the design, and was found to be sufficient. The splitting capacity 
of CLT-panels is one of the things that can be investigated further through empirical tests.  
The connections were chosen and designed by incorporating the fire safety of the load-bearing structural 
elements into considerations. The main reason that all the connections are concealed by the timber members 
is to protect steel parts against fire. The reduced cross section calculations for 120 minutes of fire showed 
that the connections in beams will still be concealed after 120 minutes of fire. In the columns, 4 of the 8 bolts 
will fall off during the fire and the remaining 4 bolts will still be intact and concealed by the column, this 
was assessed to be sufficient, since the bolts are not connected and are independent of one another. The bolts 
in CLT-panels will, on the other hand, be exposed to fire. This is one of the things that can be further 
investigated. The further investigations can include whether or not extra insulation is needed in CLT-panels, 
if coating the steel parts with intumescent fire safety paint would be sufficient, and if sprinklers should be 
applied in the building. The fourth option is to reconsider and evaluate the fire safety regulations.  
According to the current fire safety regulations, the topmost floor of timber buildings must be lower than 
9.6m, which means the building that is designed in the present thesis will not be allowed to be built in 
Greenland or in Denmark. The fire safety regulations in Greenland and Denmark are prescriptive codes, but 
Denmark has shown interests to re-evaluate their fire safety regulations and implement performance based 
fire codes in the near future.  
The building was designed for a fire duration of 120 minutes. In order to verify the load-bearing capacities of 
structural elements during the fire, it has been necessary to add fire insulations in terms of gypsum boards. In 
both sides of the walls 2-layers of gypsum boards, each with a thickness of 18mm, were added, resulting in a 
40% increase of the CLT-wall thickness from 180mm to 252mm.  
In other multi-storey timber buildings, the structural elements have been insulated against fire by adding 
plasterboards, fire painting, oversizing structural elements or applying sprinkler systems into the building. It 
was the building ‘Forte’ in Australia that used the plasterboards (also called gypsum boards). One layer of 
plasterboard with a thickness of 13mm on both sides of the walls was applied, which was sufficient to verify 
the load-bearing capacity.  
In all the examined multi-storey timber buildings in this report, the load-bearing structural elements were 
verified for 90 minutes of fire and performance based codes were applied in designing of the buildings.  
There is a big difference in designing structural elements for 90 minutes of fire and for 120 minutes of fire. 
The charring process of an insulated wall during 120 minutes of fire is shown on Figure 11-2. The figure was

11.1  FEM-Design 3D Structure 
Use of 3D modelling in Greenlandic construction industry is almost no existent. The architectural, structural, 
mechanical, electrical and plumbing drawings are drawn in separate 2D AutoCAD files, without any further 
interaction between them. When a structural engineer has to run structural analysis through a program, the 
person concerned has to draw structural drawings from the scratch into the 2D structural analysis program.   
In the recent years, considerations and discussions in implementation of building information modelling 
(BIM) programs has been present among architectural and consulting engineering companies. 3D programs 
such as Autodesk Revit, Autodesk Robot Structural Analysis software (RSA), FEM-Design etc. have been 
under considerations and discussions.  
The question is whether the Greenlandic building codes can be customised into any structural analysis 
software programs. Regarding the ‘FEM-Design 3D structure’ program, the answer is yes, but is it simple? 
Not quite.  
When the program starts, the user gets to choose between different Eurocodes that are applicable for 
different countries, one of the options is Danish National Annex to Eurocode, which is the closest to the 
Greenlandic National Annex to Eurocodes. All the different codes that can be chosen, has their own material 
libraries. For instance, one of the options is ‘Code Independent’, but the downside of this is that the material 
libraries are empty.  
If any Eurocode is chosen, the user has the option to change the different coefficients in the materials so that 
they are in accordance to the desired building code. This is not quite simple, because if another material is to 
be used, it requires to repeat the process, where the user goes into the specific material and change its 
coefficients. In RSA program it is simpler, since everything can be changed in one place, see also the right 
side of the Figure 11-3. In RSA, the coefficients for different materials are listed in one place, for instance in 
the right side of Figure 11-3, the coefficients for timber materials are all listed in one place and the user can 
change the coefficients and save them as user-defined for later use. On the left side of the figure, material 
coefficients in FEM-Design for timber C24 are shown, in this window the coefficients can be changed, but 
one by one for each material.  
==PAGE:110==
 
 
109 
 
Figure 11-3 (left): Material parameters in FEM-Design. (right): Material paramers in RSA. 
In terms of designing timber materials, the FEM-Design program has built-in timber products in terms of 
timber bars and timber panels in its library, and it treats the timber products as orthotropic materials. If a 
desired timber panel is not in the program, which is the case with plywood, an existing timber panel can be 
customized. The customized / user defined materials can be saved and be reused in future projects. Not all 
the structural analysis software programs have timber panels in their libraries and for instance in RSA, the 
timber panels have to be self-customized which can get advanced in terms of user friendliness.  
At last the question is, whether FEM-Design as it is, is suitable to be implemented in a consulting 
engineering company in Greenland? The answer is yes, the material coefficients, snow loads, wind loads etc. 
can be customized so that they comply with the values in Greenlandic building codes. It is just matter of 
personal and company’s preferences.

12  Conclusion 
The main objective of this thesis was to clarify whether it is possible to build a multi-storey building with 
timber as the load-bearing structural element, in terms of load-bearing capacity of structural elements, the 
deflections, dynamic response and fire safety of the building. The short answer to this is: yes, it is possible. 
However, according to the Greenlandic and Danish fire regulations it is not permitted to use timber as the 
load-bearing element in buildings where the topmost floor is above 9.6m.  
An existing 8-storey concrete building in Nuuk, Greenland, was used as the basis building to be designed 
with wood as the load-bearing element. Mass timber products were used in 7 out of 8 storeys as the load-
bearing structural elements, where CLT-walls were used as shear walls to resist the lateral forces. The first 
step of the designing process was to find an optimal shear wall configuration, in other words, the amount of 
shear walls in the building was to be kept minimal. The reason behind it was to give the best possible 
flexibility to the building, in such a way that prospective future renovations or changes to the building can be 
made without too many obstacles in terms of load bearing structural elements.  
The most challenging part of designing the multi-storey timber building in Nuuk, turned out to be the 
extreme wind forces. When the basic wind velocities are converted into wind pressures, the wind effect to 
the buildings was more than twice as high than in Denmark and in Norway.  
Different CLT-products from different manufacturers were analysed. It gave problems with horizontal 
deflections and dynamic response of the building using the CLT-products that are built in the FEM-Design 
program. CLT-products from Martinssons were also analyzed, but due to their low tensile capacities 
compared to other CLT-products, the acting tensile forces were too large for them to bear. The CLT-products 
that gave the most satisfying results were from Stora Enso and therefore were chosen in the final design. Due 
to the large wind speeds and light weight of the building, it was a necessity for the building to have a high 
moment of inertia in order to satisfy the comfort criteria in ISO 10137 for wind induced vibrations.  The final 
design of the building ended up having horizontal deflections of 20mm which is about 3 times under the 
recommended limit of 𝑙/500 = 54𝑚𝑚, but this is what is needed to keep within the comfort criteria in ISO 
10137 for wind induced vibrations.  
Another challenge was to verify the fire safety of the load-bearing structural elements. Due to the fire safety, 
all steel parts of the connections were concealed inside the timber elements. The reduced cross section 
method after 120 minutes of fire showed that the dowel connections between CLT-walls and CLT-floors will 
be exposed during the fire. To minimize the risk of collapse in the slabs due to the failure of the connections, 
the slabs are resting on top of CLT-walls, so that even without the dowel connections, the slabs will still be 
supported by the walls. This connection method was shown in Figure 8-45 and Figure 8-52. The load-
bearing structural elements were also verified for 120 minutes of fire, by the help of gypsum boards that act 
as fire insulation.  
One of the problem statements concerns the cost of the timber building compared to the original concrete 
building. According to the cost assessment of the load-bearing structural elements, it will be higher than the 
original building, that stands erected in Nuuk. The cost assessment showed that the timber building will be 
14% more expensive than the original concrete building and 9% more expensive by excluding the shipment 
charges.

13  Further work  
In this project, all the main topics of the structural elements have been covered, but due to the limited time 
all details could not be analysed. One of the topics that could be investigated is the effect of the slip modulus 
of the joints. Further investigation could include how the connections affect the global deflections and 
vibrations of the building. Regarding the connections, it will be interesting to see whether it is necessary to 
have connections that can be tightened after the building has come into use.  
In the verification of the fire safety of the load-bearing structural elements, the gypsum boards were used as 
fire insulations, but it will be interesting to investigate the use of intumescent paint to see if they could be 
used instead. Another thing that could be investigated is how the connections that will be exposed to fire will 
hold.  
One of the biggest issues in wooden buildings in Greenland is prevalence of mould. Nowadays, because of 
the excessive occurrence of mould in Greenlandic timber buildings, the Greenlandic authorities has begun to 
dissociate from using organic materials such as wood in residential buildings. As a part of the investigation, 
48 residential buildings without organic materials in them have been constructed. The question is whether 
use of mass timber can prevent prevalence of mould. This is one of the problems that is necessary to consider 
and it would be interesting to investigate how other countries such as Norway, Canada, etc. tackle, solve or 
prevent the prevalence of mould in timber buildings.  
This project is a demonstration work, to show whether it is possible to build multi-storey timber buildings in 
Greenland and should be used as a starting point in investigation of using timber as the load-bearing 
structural element in multi-storey buildings in Greenland.
