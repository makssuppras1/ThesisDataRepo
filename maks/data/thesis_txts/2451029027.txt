Abstract
While pavement systems are critical for the global economy and societal equality, structural
condition evaluation techniques have not progressed in a long time. The current techniques are
based on stationary devices that interfere with the traﬃc ﬂow and provide point measurements.
Recently, a new class of devices is emerging, which oﬀers the possibility for condition evaluation
while moving at traﬃc speeds. One of these moving measuring platforms is the Dynatest Raptor,
which is the topic of this thesis. Speciﬁcally, the work focused on viscoelastic pavement modeling
to be used with the Dynatest Raptor. For this purpose, an ultra-eﬃcient mechanistic pavement
model was formulated and coded to serve as kernel for performing inverse analysis of pavement
properties, utilizing Raptor’s measurements.
The work included a literature review of existing pavement modeling approaches that involve
layered half-space and a review of moving measuring platforms. Then, a computationally eﬃcient
formulation was oﬀered for a three-layered system, wherein the top layer is viscoelastic and the
other two are elastic, loaded by moving and ﬂuctuating stress magnitudes operating over circular
areas.
Afterwards, the formulation was applied in an inverse way to analyze measurements
provided by the Raptor.
It is found that for a three-layered elastic system, the solution based on an analytic matrix inversion
is remarkably faster than numerical inversion. Regarding the analysis, the loading from all Raptor
tires should be considered for a proper analysis of measurements. Forcing an elastic solution
on the results obtained by a viscoelastic pavement model can produce bias in the derived elastic
properties of all layers, while the load ﬂuctuation of the Raptor can inﬂuence the deﬂection basin
for a speciﬁc point in time. Finally, the code developed is suﬃciently fast, accurate and workable,
capable of addressing moving and ﬂuctuating loads.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Pavement systems are considered to be a key element for ensuring societal well-being and
economic growth. By connecting people, markets and services, these systems enable the eﬃcient
function of society. Besides, trustworthy transportation, in terms of safety and speed, is crucial
in today’s economically competitive world. To that end, the physical transportation infrastructure
is a top expenditure of public funds.
The European road network alone has a 5.5 million km span and is worth trillions of euros
[1].
Even if the need for new infrastructure in developed countries is limited, networks of
that scale require systematic maintenance and restoration, as traﬃc load and time can degrade
pavements severely. This is supported by studies revealing that inadequate maintenance can lead
to catastrophic deterioration of the pavement network and subsequent need for replacement or
extensive repair of high cost and severe ﬁnancial impact on the economy. For that reason, the
trend in modern societies shifts towards pavement condition monitoring and assessment.
As far as the structural condition of pavements is concerned, the respective engineering ﬁeld
has long been interested in the non-destructive assessment of pavement condition. This can
be achieved by measuring deﬂections, which is a term used in the pavement arena to refer to
the vertical displacements due to an external load, disregarding any lateral displacements. The
most widespread technique of deﬂection measurement so far is the Falling Weight Deﬂectometer
(FWD). With this method the mechanical properties of a pavement are backcalculated by way
of inverse analysis, which consists of a multi-objective optimization problem where the material
properties are modiﬁed until the calculated deﬂections ideally match the measured ones. However,
FWD is a stationary test, meaning that it requires disruption of the traﬃc during the testing. This
inconvenience of hindering the circulation generates a need for new methods.

1.2. Motivation
Introduction
1.2
Motivation
In recent years, the pavement engineering community is increasingly interested in developing
Moving Measuring Platforms (MMP) which provoke signiﬁcantly less disturbance in the traﬃc,
compared to the FWDs. This thesis aims to contribute to the progress of MMPs by focusing on a
new promising device, the Dynatest Raptor. The Raptor consists of a beam instrumented with a
set of distance lasers and motion tracking sensors. This setup allows, by means of image analysis,
to track a point on the pavement surface and measure displacements while driving with traﬃc
speed.
This project will create an ultra-eﬃcient mechanistic model for forward-simulating asphalt pave-
ment deﬂections, which combined with the Raptor’s measurements, can accurately determine
the pavement layer properties by inverse analysis. In spite of the fact that pavement modeling
so far has been conducted by means of layered elastic theory, viscoelasticity is considered to
be the next step in pavement analysis. By employing the linear viscoelastic theory, the time-
and temperature-dependent response of asphalt-concrete is represented. Herein, the asphalt layer
of the pavement model will be modeled as viscoelastic, to address the eﬀects of driving speed
and temperature. Moreover, the impact of possible load ﬂuctuation will be investigated and
accordingly included in the model.

2.1
Introduction
This chapter reviews literature about pavement mechanics and moving deﬂection measuring de-
vices. Section 2.2 focuses on layered elastic theory which is the most commonly used mechanical
model for practical asphalt pavement design and analysis. Section 2.3 deals with viscoelastic
pavement modeling which is forthcoming in asphalt pavement design and analysis. Finally, Sec-
tion 2.4 addresses the current state of deﬂection measuring devices while moving with special
attention to the new Raptor device.
2.2
Elastic pavement modeling
Asphalt pavements are commonly modeled within a continuum mechanics framework by means
of layered elastic theory [2],[3],[4]. This theory analytically resolves the mechanical responses
in a layered half-space due to a vertical loading applied at the upper boundary over a circular
area. The typical modeling assumptions include: (i) linear elastic, isotropic, weightless, and
homogenous layers; (ii) constant thickness of all layers; and (iii) no body forces acting in the
system.

1 is the top layer while layer n is the half-space. This system is loaded by a uniform stress of
magnitude q spread over a circular area with radius a (representing tire-pavement contact for a
single wheel). The arrangement is shown in Figure 2.1 wherein hi is thickness, Ei is Young’s

2.2.2
Numerical considerations
The evaluation of the displacements and stresses as given in Equations (2.9) - (2.14) must be
done numerically, because analytic solution does not exist. The semi-inﬁnite integrals contain
integrands that are highly oscillatory and decay slowly towards zero. These require non standard
integration techniques and special numerical considerations for balancing accuracy and com-
putational eﬃciency. A major computational cost is the need to invert a 4n −2 matrix for the
estimation of Ai(m), Bi(m), Ci(m), Di(m) for every integrand evaluation.
The most straightforward technique for numerical evaluation of semi-inﬁnite integrals with os-
cillatory integrands consists of three steps: i) determination of the ﬁrst Kz integrand roots
(zero-crossings); ii) computation of the deﬁnite integrals between each pair of successive zero-
crossings; iii) addition of the Kz −1 deﬁnite integrals to arrive at an estimation for the original
semi-inﬁnite integral. One way to evaluate the choice of Kz is to repeat the calculation with
a larger Kz and compare. The last step was a matter of discussion in several papers [7, 8, 9],
where instead of the simple summation of the deﬁnite integrals, several acceleration techniques
were introduced to increase the eﬃciency. The main assumption in order to use the proposed
acceleration techniques is based on the fact that the integrand oscillates in a way that, every
deﬁnite integral between two successive zero-crossings is bigger than the next one in absolute
terms.

2.3
Viscoelastic pavement modeling
Asphalt concrete layers exhibit time-dependent mechanical properties.
As a result, asphalt
pavements systems also exhibit time-dependent behavior. This time-dependency can be described
by the theory of linear viscoelasticity. The theory is based on the convolution or hereditary or
Boltzmann’s superposition integral and can apply to both material elements and entire systems.
In what follows the basics of the theory are reviewed and then applied to a layered half-space.
2.3.1
Formulation
According to Boltzmann’s theory, any response R (e.g. internal stress or displacement) in a
viscoelastic body is aﬀected by the entire ‘input’ history I (e.g. boundary force or displacement).
This is represented using the curly brackets around I to denote that the value of R is a function of
the entire history of I [15, 16].
R = R{I}
(2.22)
Assuming that the response is linearly related to the input, the principle of superposition applies:
R{I1 + I2} = R{I1} + R{I2}
(2.23)
where I1, I2 are diﬀerent input histories.
The time-dependent viscoelastic response Rve(t) of a linear body, due to a time-dependent input

2.4.1
History
One of the ﬁrst and main contributors to the target of designing a non-contact, non-destructive
method of evaluating pavements was the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA). The quantiﬁ-
cation of the aging stage of a pavement ,was rather important from 1977 and still remains. M. E.
Harr and N.T. Ng-A-Qui in [30] states the main reasons for the establishment of such a technique.
The main reasons are today’s pavement length, the increase of vehicles’ weight, especially in avi-
ation, and lastly the time and coverage of more conventional techniques like the Falling Weight
Deﬂectometer (FWD) method. Moreover, in the above mentioned paper one machine with two
diﬀerent conﬁgurations is proposed to measure displacement basins of a moving load. Both
are using a long beam equipped with diﬀerent measuring sensors. The ﬁrst one, operates with
the use of light emitting diodes (LED) while the second one, adopts linear variable diﬀerential
transformers (LVDT) to perform measurements.
Holt, F.B. and Eckrose, R.A. in 1989 in [31] present two new technologies that are mounted in a
moving van and are non-destructive. The ﬁrst non-destructive method is the ground penetrating
radar which delivers an electromagnetic pulse and through the time diﬀerential, til the return
of the signal, the layer thickness can be quantiﬁed. The second non-destructive method is the
infrared thermography which can detect diﬀerences as small as 0.1 °C. The usefulness of that
equipment lays on the fact that deteriorated areas of the pavement heat more during daytime so
detection of those areas through infrared thermography is possible.

Current devices
The latest moving measuring platforms can be categorized in two main categories.
Firstly,
the Rolling Wheel Deﬂectometer (RWD) and secondly, the Traﬃc Speed Deﬂectometer (TSD).
Three devices are the main competitors in today’s market. Those are, namely, the Greenwood
deﬂectometer (TSD), the ARA deﬂectometer (RWD) and the Dynasest Raptor (RWD). A small
introduction to the devices used in the Greenwood TSD and the ARA RWD will be attempted,

The Greenwood TSD [33, 34] is a custom-built truck that carries a stiﬀbeam which is equipped
with several Doppler lasers. Those sensors estimate the velocity of the surface in the pointing
direction. The measured velocity has two components, one of the desired displacement velocity
and a second, attributed to the velocity of the moving platform. In order to estimate the second
part so that can be subtracted later, a reference laser is considered in the furthest position of
the back wheel. The biggest challenge in the above procedure is the accurate estimation of the
vehicle’s velocity.
The ARA deﬂectometer [35] , is a custom-built semi-trailer mounted with triangulation lasers
underneath. The machine is able to measure deﬂections due to a 8.164 kg of axle load. The
lasers are positioned in a way that both deformed and undeformed pavement can be estimated, as
the vehicle moves forward. Through the comparison of the two values, deﬂected and undeﬂected
pavement at the same point, the deﬂection basin is formed. The main concern in the above
procedure is the assumption of measuring the undeﬂected pavement in a distance relatively close
to the moving load.
2.4.3

The Raptor moving platform consists of a heavy wheeled semi-trailer truck with the two trailer
wheels having each one its own suspension system. A long stiﬀbeam is installed in the trailer
along the movement direction. The beam is equipped with a number of laser sensors, rigidly
attached on it, which measure self-distance to the pavement surface. These measurements are
combined to provide the Raptor Displacement Indices (RDI), which are used to obtain the
properties of the pavement.

Chapter 3
Objective and Methodology
The objective of this Thesis is to develop an ultra-eﬃcient mechanistic model for forward-
simulating pavement surface displacements due to a moving Raptor. The project involves the
mathematical formulation, coding and application of the model to perform inverse analysis of
pavement layer properties with Raptor-measured data as input. The pavement system will be
considered as a three-layered stratiﬁed half-space containing linear, homogenous, isotropic and
weightless layers that are fully bonded at the interfaces. The top layer will be assumed viscoelastic,
representing the time- and temperature-dependent response of asphalt-concrete. The two other
layers will be assumed elastic, representing the resilient (time-independent) response of base,
sub-base, sub-grade and deeper soil layers.
The developed code has to meet two main demands: accuracy and computational eﬃciency.
Accuracy refers to the determination of the displacement basin. The accuracy level goal of the
deﬂections calculated on the elastic system is set to 0.01µm. Moreover, the code has to be
computationally eﬃcient in terms of calculation speed, in order for the inverse analysis to be
executed quickly.
The selection of the three-layered system is based on the fact that in Raptor 1 laser conﬁguration
the minimum distance between lasers is 300 mm. That creates a barrier in the inverse analysis
and searching for properties of thin layers is unrealistic. Moreover, it is computationally more
eﬃcient to invert analytically the parameters Ai(m), Bi(m), Ci(m), Di(m), which is possible for
a three-layered system as shown by Burmister in a series of papers [2, 3, 4]. The analytical
solution of the elastic layered system will serve as a kernel function for the desired viscoelastic
solution. The implementation of a moving load on a viscoelastic pavement will be based on
Boltzmann’s superposition principle, while the Laplace transform inversion required will be held
by Schapery’s ‘direct’ method, as it oﬀers high computational eﬃciency. To use the model with
the Raptor data, it will be adapted to Raptor 1 conﬁguration, considering the tires’ and laser
sensors’ positions.
The adapted algorithm will receive as input 10 RDI measurements from the Raptor device and
will compute by inverse analysis the moduli of Layers 2, 3 and part of the viscoelastic creep

4.2.2
Numerical implementation
The formulation above, was implemented by coding it in Matlab. The objective of the program is
the forward calculation of surface displacements under a moving load. The layered elastic code
described in Subsection 4.1, was the core function of the viscoelastic code.
The inputs required are the evaluation point’s y−coordinate (ye [mm]) and the properties of
the moving load and the layered system. Speciﬁcally for the moving load, the distributed load
magnitude q [MPa], the radius of the loaded area a [mm], the speed of the load V[km/h] and
the starting point x0 [mm] (absolute value | −x0|). For the layered system, the Poisson’s ratios of
the three layers, the Young’s moduli of Layers 2 and 3 E1, E2 [MPa], the thicknesses of Layers
1 and 2 h1, h2 [mm], and the four viscoelastic parameters D0, D∞, nD, τD composing the creep
compliance D(t). In case of ﬂuctuating load, instead of q, a vector q(t) is required as input.
The following describe the procedure of calculation.
Step 1: Elastic calculation
First is calculated the elastic surface deﬂections, for a number nx of selected radial distances
(knots) spread in the range [0, x0]. The closer to the evaluation point the smaller the interval
between the knots.
The response is also calculated for nt time-durations of the loading, hence diﬀerent D values
logarithmically spaced in the range [D0, Dend], where Dend = D(ω · ∆τ).
A matrix nx × nt was generated from the above results.
Step 2: Interpolation
To calculate all the required in-between points in order to create a more dense and uniform mesh
with constant increment of the desired ∆x, an interpolation is done between the calculated points,
both in terms of location (nx-direction) and time duration (nt-direction) of the load. First, is held
a cubic interpolation in the nx-direction and then a linear interpolation in logarithmic scale for
the nt-direction.

Validation
The validation of the Viscoelastic code was carried out in two ways. First, the code can be
internally validated, by simulating an elastic problem. This can be achieved by setting the speed
of the vehicle to be inﬁnite, so that the loads are applied instantaneously. In that case, the response
should be identical for a moving load on an elastic system with Layer 1 Young’s modulus equal to
the instantaneous creep compliance, i.e. E1 = D0. Indeed, the response of the evaluation point for
a moving load in the viscoelastic system described in Table 4.6 for inﬁnite speed is approximately
equal to the response of the equivalent elastic system with E1 = 25000 MPa, having a maximum
deﬂection equal to 0.3882mm.
Second, the code is compared to the ELLVA [40] Excel modeling tool.
In Figure 4.9, the
viscoelastic deﬂections calculated by the two programs for the previously mentioned case are
plotted together. As can be seen, the two lines are identical, as number ω was set to have the
same accuracy level as ELLVA.

used to reach the desired step ∆x. To determine the loss of accuracy, the code is executed for
nx = ω/2 + 1, meaning that all ω + 1 deﬂections are calculated by the elastic code instead by
interpolation. Compared to the solution including interpolation (for the case of Table 4.6), the
maximum diﬀerence was found to be 2.31 · 10−5 mm, which can be considered minor compared
to the saved computational expense.

Chapter 5

5.1
Introduction
In this chapter, the viscoelastic pavement model will be adapted to the Raptor 1 conﬁguration.
In Section 5.2, the load conﬁguration of the Raptor and the resulting Viscoelastic-Raptor code
will be presented. In Section 5.3 the measuring principle of the Raptor as well as the concept
of RDI will be introduced. In Section 5.4 the connection between the RDIs measured by the
machine and the ones calculated by the code will be demonstrated as well as the Error Function
that compares the two. Afterwards, an inverse analysis procedure is proposed to determine the
pavement properties, by minimizing the Error Function through Raptor −VEPA. Finally, the
veriﬁcation of Raptor −VEPA and the eﬀect of the load ﬂuctuation will be presented.
5.2
Raptor modeling
In this section, the viscoelastic code described in Section 4.2 is adapted to Raptor’s conﬁguration.

5.5.2
Procedure
For the inverse analysis procedure the MATLAB code Raptor −VEPA is created. This code
accepts as input 10 RDIm measured from Raptor 1. The thicknesses and the Poisson’s ratios of the
three layers are also considered known. Raptor −VEPA is an optimization code that performs
the inverse analysis by calculating the set of 4 variables E2, E3, nD, τD that minimize the Error
function which is presented in Equation (5.19).

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary, ﬁndings and recommendations
In this project, an ultra-eﬃcient mechanistic model was developed and validated for forward
simulating pavement surface displacements caused by a moving Raptor device (see Section 5.2).
The established model includes three linear layers and assumes viscoelastic properties to the top
layer of the pavement and elastic properties for the other two. In addition, the eﬀect of ﬂuctuating
load due to vehicle dynamics in combination with pavement imperfections was included in the
formulation (see Section 5.7). As the Raptor device provides RDIms, the code was adapted to
provide RDIcs (refer to Section 5.4). Ultimately, best matching the two (see Section 5.5) was
guiding an inverse analysis to ﬁnd the model pavement properties, namely: Young’s moduli of
Layers 2 and 3 (E2, E3) and part of the viscoelastic creep compliance for Layer 1 (nD, τD).
Synthetic cases were generated and analyzed to demonstrate the functionality of the code for the
Raptor (see Subsection 5.5.3).
The main ﬁndings of the work are:
(i) For a three-layered elastic system, the derivation proposed in this work, which is based on
a purely analytic matrix inversion, is almost 20 times faster compared with a traditional
solution approach based on a numerical matrix inversion (see Section 4.1).
(ii) The loading from all Raptor tires should be taken into consideration when analyzing
measurements (see Figure 5.4).
(iii) An error function based on the absolute diﬀerences between measured and calculated RDIs
is rather smooth in the search-parameter space (i.e., does not include a lot of local minima)
and is convex in the vicinity of the optimal solution.
(iv) Forcing an elastic solution on results generated by a viscoelastic pavement model can
produce bias in the derived elastic properties of all layers; the bias is case speciﬁc.

6.2. Suggestions for further studies
Conclusion
(v) For a given point in time, load intensity ﬂuctuation can largely inﬂuence the RDIs - both in
magnitude and in sign.
(vi) The Raptor −VEPA code is capable to converge to the correct solution in synthetic cases.
This is a ﬁrst and necessary test before error analysis, and before application to actual ﬁeld
measurements.
Based on the above-mentioned ﬁndings, the following list of recommendations is oﬀered: (i)
Consider replacing elastic pavement modeling for Raptor interpretation with viscoelastic analysis.
The code developed herein is suﬃciently fast and workable, capable of addressing moving and
ﬂuctuating loads, therefore addressing (also) the eﬀects of driving speed and temperature, and
(ii) Load ﬂuctuation should be considered for Raptor analysis. One option is to assume the
pavement system is uniform over a certain length, average the measurements to eliminate bias,
and utilize static loads for the inverse analysis.
In this connection, it appears that the low-
frequency ﬂuctuations are governing the length over which averaging is needed. Another option
is to include the ﬂuctuation in the forward simulation - as the code herein in capable of doing.
6.2
Suggestions for further studies
Raptor −VEPA is a newly developed code. As such, it has to undergo rigorous validation prior
to adaptation. Thus, a task for future investigation is deep examination of the code performance
across all relevant cases. Also, it may be worth to consider improving the elastic solution by
further reﬁning the integration procedure. The viscoelastic code’s accuracy-eﬃciency balance
may be investigated further, considering the number of path segments (see Subsection 4.2.2) and
the interpolation techniques used.
The chosen Error Function introduced in Raptor −VEPA for guiding the inverse analysis was
of a simple form. Further study is needed to assess the possibility of further improving the
formulation. One idea is to introduce weighting factors to the diﬀerent RDI values based on, for
example, the associated laser spacings, (Table 5.1), or the relative deﬂections in a homogenous
half-space under the Raptor wheel-loading arrangement.
The entire optimization procedure
introduced by Raptor −VEPA can be investigated to oﬀer a better balance between accuracy
and eﬃciency. For this reason, all the algorithm optimization parameters could be re-examined.
The code should be next applied to ﬁeld-measured data where the tested pavement is also
instrumented to provide an independent validation for the results. If load ﬂuctuation is not directly
considered in the forward modeling, then average measurements should be analyzed (with static
Raptor loads). In this case, determination of the minimal length over which measurements are
averaged requires further investigation.
