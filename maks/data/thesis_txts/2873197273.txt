Abstract
Escherichia coli is one of the most widely used microbial hosts for recombinant protein
production in industry. Among the various strategies used to control gene expression in
E. coli, lactose has been widely explored as a cost-effective alternative to IPTG, func-
tioning as a metabolizable inducer that enables tunable expression levels. The E. coli
strain HMS174(DE3) is capable of fully metabolizing lactose without causing galactose
accumulation, a common issue in the widely used BL21(DE3) strain.
This study aims to characterize and model recombinant green fluorescent protein (GFP)
production in HMS174(DE3) using a two-feed system with glucose and lactose. Five fed-
batch cultivation experiments were carried out with varying glucose and lactose feeding
profiles. The experimental data were used to develop a mechanistic model describing the
relationship between substrate uptake and productivity. Model development followed an
iterative workflow involving parameter estimation, sensitivity analysis, and identifiability
analysis. The model structure was selected based on biological interpretability and quality
of fit.
The experiments demonstrated that both lactose and glucose uptake influence GFP ex-
pression. Moreover, simulations showed that optimal productivity could be achieved with
moderate feeding of both sugars. The model also revealed an interaction between glucose
and lactose uptake.
When compared to experimental data, the model exhibited good
predictive capability, with normalized root-mean-square errors (NRMSE) below 10% for
biomass, glucose, and GFP, and below 20% for lactose.
In summary, this work provides a framework for understanding and predicting productivity
outcomes under dual-substrate feeding in HMS174(DE3). The model holds potential for
future applications in process optimization and control, such as designing optimal feeding
strategies or enabling predictive and real-time process control.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Escherichia coli as a host for recombinant protein production
Recombinant protein production plays a crucial role in modern biotechnology, enabling the
synthesis of a wide range of high-value biomolecules. These include industrial enzymes, an-
tibody fragments, RNA-based therapeutics, and functional proteins that support emerging
applications in sectors such as artificial meat and dairy production. Among the available
host systems, Escherichia coli (E. coli) has emerged as one of the most extensively used
microbial platforms for the expression of heterologous proteins [1].
In 2009, it was estimated that approximately 30% of approved biopharmaceutical products
were manufactured using E. coli-based systems [2], underscoring the organism’s signifi-
cant industrial relevance. Several factors contribute to its popularity. As one of the best-
characterized prokaryotic model organisms, E. coli offers a well-established and versatile
platform for genetic engineering, both at laboratory and industrial scales [3]. The organ-
ism’s ability to grow rapidly in inexpensive, chemically defined media provides substantial
economic advantages compared to more complex eukaryotic hosts [4, 5]. Moreover, E.
coli is favored for recombinant protein production due to its ease of genetic manipulation
and the wide availability of compatible biotechnological tools[6, 7]. In addition to these
intrinsic advantages, E. coli benefits from a deep mechanistic understanding of its tran-
scriptional and translational machinery, enabling precise regulation of gene expression for
scalable protein production [8].
Among various strains, E. coli BL21(DE3) is particularly prominent due to its ability to
produce high yields of recombinant proteins using the T7 promoter system [9, 10, 11].
This strain is optimized for protein expression, with reduced protease activity and com-
patibility with tightly regulated induction protocols, making it an ideal host for industrial
applications [11, 12]. alternative E. coli strains such as HMS174(DE3), a K12 derivative,
are also employed for recombinant protein expression due to their more stable maintenance
of certain plasmids [13].
Induction Strategies in E. coli
In typical expression systems, the T7 promoter used in E. coli BL21(DE3) is induced by iso-
propyl ￿-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG), allowing strong and controlled transcription
[14]. While widely used, E. coli also presents several limitations. Its endotoxin-producing
nature raises safety concerns, particularly for the production of therapeutic proteins [15,
16]. In addition, the system often leads to the formation of inclusion bodies, which are
associated to high metabolic burden during the induction [17, 18].
As a cost-effective alternative to IPTG, lactose has been explored as an inducer for recom-
binant protein production in E. coli BL21(DE3). Unlike IPTG, lactose is metabolizable,

allowing for gradual and tunable induction. Previous studies have shown that the forma-
tion of inclusion bodies, as well as overall productivity, can be modulated by physiological
parameters—specifically, the specific uptake rates of glucose and lactose [19, 20, 21].
However, BL21(DE3) lacks key enzymes of the Leloir pathwaywhich renders the strain
incapable of metabolising galactose [22]. As a result, continuous induction with lactose can
lead to the accumulation of galactose in the medium. This accumulation may interfere with
downstream metabolism or induction eﬀiciency, especially in prolonged cultivations [23].
In contrast, strains such as HMS174(DE3), which retain an intact galactose metabolism,
are able to fully utilise lactose [24]. This metabolic capability makes HMS174(DE3) a
suitable host for lactose-based induction strategies [22].
Bioprocess design considerations for lactose-inducible systems
The use of lactose as an inducer, especially in strains capable of fully metabolising it,
presents opportunities for more controlled and physiologically compatible induction. How-
ever, achieving consistent and effective protein expression under lactose-inducible systems
requires more than just strain selection. The dynamics of lactose availability and its inter-
action with other carbon sources, such as glucose must be carefully managed throughout
the cultivation process [20, 22].
To support both optimal cell growth and eﬀicient induction, recombinant protein produc-
tion processes are commonly implemented as two-phase cultivations. The initial growth
phase focuses on biomass accumulation under non-inducing conditions, typically supported
by glucose as the primary carbon source. In the second phase, lactose is introduced to
initiate protein expression [25]. This transition must be precisely timed and quantitatively
controlled, as excessive glucose can lead to repression of lactose uptake, and uncontrolled
lactose feeding can lead to byproduct accumulation [26, 22].
To address these challenges, two-feed systems are employed, in which glucose and lactose
are supplied independently and dynamically adjusted according to the physiological needs
of the culture. Several studies have highlighted the role of specific uptake rates of the
main carbon source and lactose in modulating both growth and protein production. For
example, Kopp et al.
[21] and Kittler et al.
[23] investigated mixed glycerol/lactose
feed strategies using both BL21(DE3) and HMS174(DE3) strains.
Wurm et al.
[20]
further developed a mechanistic correlation between glucose and lactose uptake rates in
BL21(DE3), which was subsequently leveraged by Ulonska et al. [27] to implement model
predictive control for optimized feeding.
1.2
Objectives and Scope of the Study
Previous studies on lactose-based induction in recombinant protein production, such as
those by Ulonska et al.
[27], Wurm et al [20].
and Kopp et al.
[21], have primarily
employed E. coli BL21(DE3) strains, which lack key enzymes of the Leloir pathway and
are thus unable to metabolize galactose. As a result, lactose is only partially utilized,
leading to the accumulation of galactose and potential prolonged induction effects—an

effect sometimes referred to as the ”Lazarus effect” [23].
This limits the tunability of
lactose-based induction and can complicate the control of recombinant protein expression.
In contrast, the HMS174(DE3) strain used in this study retains full galactose metabolism.
This enables complete consumption of lactose, offering an additional degree of freedom in
the design and control of induction strategies. As such, this study seeks to fill a gap in the
current literature by investigating induction and feeding dynamics in a host that is fully
capable of metabolizing both the carbon source (glucose) and the inducer (lactose).
The overall aim of this thesis is the characterization and modelling of recombinant GFP
production in 2-feed system using E. coli HMS174(DE3) strain.
To achieve this, the
following specific objectives are defined:
• To design and carry out fed-batch cultivations with different glucose and lactose feed
profiles using E. coli HMS174(DE3) expressing GFP;
• To select model structures for describing biomass growth, substrate uptake, and
product formation kinetics;
• To estimate model parameters from experimental data and validate model perfor-
mance;
• To use the model to explore productivity landscapes and identify optimal feed re-
gions.

Materials and Methods
2.1
Strain
In this study, the strain used was E. coli HMS174 (DE3). A His-tagged green fluorescent
protein (GFP) was expressed from the pET28a(+) plasmid.
The strain was stored in
cryovials at –80 °C.
2.2
Media
Luria-Bertani (LB) medium was used for overnight cultures to revive the strain from
cryovials. The pre-culture and batch cultivation in the bioreactor were performed using
modified DeLisa minimal medium [28], with the following composition per liter: 13.3 g
KH2PO4, 4 g (NH4)2HPO4, 1.7 g citric acid, 1.2 g MgSO4·7H2O, 0.1 g Fe(III) citrate,
8.4 mg EDTA, 1.3 mg Zn(CH3COO)2·2H2O, 4.5 mg thiamine·HCl, 50 mg kanamycin,
and 5 mL of a trace element solution. The trace element solution contained (per liter):
2.5 mg CoCl2·6H2O, 15 mg MnCl2·4H2O, 1.5 mg CuCl2·4H2O, 3 mg H3BO3, and 2.5 mg
Na2MoO4·2H2O. The initial concentrations of substrates used in the batch cultivations

2.4
Bioreactor Cultivation
For each experiment, preculture was transferred into batch medium with a final volume of
1 L in a glass stirred-tank bioreactor system (Biostream International, The Netherlands)
with a maximum working volume of 2 L. The fermentation temperature was maintained at
37°C by circulating heated water through the bioreactor jacket. The pH was continuously
monitored using a pH probe (Hamilton, Switzerland) and controlled at 6.75 via automated
addition of acid or base solutions. Dissolved oxygen (DO) was monitored using a DO
probe (Hamilton, Switzerland), and maintained above 30% by controlling the agitation
speed (400–1200 rpm) and aeration rate (0.5–5 vvm).
During the fed-batch phase, substrates and inducers were supplied using calibrated peri-
staltic pumps through tubing with diameters of 1 mm and 2 mm. The amount of substrate
added and the total medium volume inside the reactor were monitored in real time using a
connected digital balance. Off-gas composition was continuously analyzed using an online
mass spectrometer (Prima Pro, Thermo Scientific, USA), enabling real-time monitoring of
oxygen uptake and carbon dioxide production. Process control and data acquisition were
performed using the Lucullus Process Information Management System (PIMS, Securecell
AG, Switzerland). A schematic overview of the fermentation setup is shown in Figure 2.1.

2.6
Sample Analysis
Samples were collected automatically every 1 to 2 hours using an autosampler (Gilson
Inc., USA), with a sampling volume of approximately 10 mL per time point. The samples
were stored at 4°C until further analysis.
2.6.1
Biomass Determination
Dry cell weight (DCW) was determined gravimetrically. A total of 1.8 mL of fermentation
broth sample was transferred into pre-weighed, oven-dried 2 mL microcentrifuge tubes.
Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 10,000 rcf for 5 minutes at 4 °C. The supernatant
was carefully decanted and stored at −20 °C for subsequent analysis of substrates and
metabolites.
The cell pellet was washed once by resuspension in 0.9% (w/v) NaCl in
deionized water (DI), followed by a second centrifugation under the same conditions.
After removing the wash solution, the pellet was dried in an oven at 105 °C for at least
72 hours. The dried tube was then weighed, and DCW was calculated by subtracting the
pre-weighed empty tube mass.

2.6.2
Substrate and Metabolite Concentrations
Glucose concentration was measured using the Megazyme assay based on glucose oxidase
and peroxidase enzymes (Megazyme International Ireland Ltd., Ireland). Cell-free samples
were appropriately diluted with deionised water, and standard solutions (0.05–1 g/L) and
blanks were prepared. A volume of 60 µL of each sample, standard, or blank was pipetted
into a clear 96-well microplate, followed by the addition of 180 µL of reagent. The reaction
was incubated at 40°C for 20 minutes, before absorbance measurement at 510 nm using a
microplate reader (Tecan, Switzerland).
Lactose, galactose, acetate, and ethanol concentrations were quantified by high-performance
liquid chromatography (HPLC) equipped with a refractive index detector (Shimadzu,
Japan). Separation was carried out using an Aminex HPX-87H column (Bio-Rad, USA).
Samples were diluted with 12 mM sulphuric acid and injected at a volume of 40 µL.
Isocratic elution was performed at 63 °C and 0.6 mL/min for 25 minutes.
2.6.3
GFP Concentration
The concentration of green fluorescent protein (GFP) was quantified based on fluorescence
measurements. A volume of 100 µL of sample, diluted to fall within the linear range of
the fluorescence assay, was transferred into wells of a black flat-bottom 96-well microplate.
Fluorescence intensity was measured using a microplate reader (Tecan, Switzerland) at an
excitation wavelength of 485±10 nm and an emission wavelength of 530±10 nm. A stan-
dard curve was prepared using a GFP quantification kit (Abcam, Cambridge, UK), and
used to convert fluorescence intensity into GFP concentration. All samples and standards
were measured in triplicate.

Results and Discussion
In this study, a total of five fed-batch cultivation experiments (E1-E5) with each up to
3 different feeding regimes (01-03) were conducted. The experimental data served as the
foundation for developing a mathematical model describing recombinant protein produc-
tion in E. coli based on substrate uptake rates.
The effects of glucose (qSGlu) and lactose (qSLac) uptake rates on recombinant GFP produc-
tion by E. coli HMS174(DE3) were explored within a design space. The experimental set-
points for glucose and lactose feed trajectories, as summarized in Table 2.2, are illustrated
in Figure 3.1. Since multiple setpoints were applied sequentially within each experiment,
the feeding strategy followed a dynamic trajectory. As such, cellular responses observed
at each setpoint may not solely reflect the immediate conditions, but can also carry over
influences from earlier phases. These time-dependent dynamics were acknowledged in the
analysis and incorporated into the modeling framework.

3.1.1
Planned Versus Observed Uptake Rates
This section presents an analysis of the actual substrate uptake rates observed during the
fed-batch cultivations in comparison to the predefined setpoints. The glucose and lactose
uptake setpoints were calculated based on the expected biomass concentration at the start
of the fed-batch phase, aiming to maintain specific substrate uptake rates throughout the
process. However, for data analysis and modeling, the actual uptake rates were determined
retrospectively based on physical measurements. This approach accounts for inevitable
deviations between planned and observed uptake rates. Such discrepancies are common
in feedforward feeding strategies, which do not adapt to disturbances [34].
Figure 3.2 shows a comparison between the glucose uptake rate setpoints and the observed
uptake rates over time for all five experiments.
While the general trends are similar,
noticeable discrepancies exist, particularly at the onset of the fed-batch phase.
These

relied on biomass concentration estimates. These estimates were obtained either through
offline OD600 measurements or through stoichiometric mass balance approximations. In
addition, pump accuracy also has contribution to the offset, especially at very low flow
rates, where mechanical tolerance of the peristaltic pumps introduced measurable error.
A more substantial deviation was observed during the late fed-batch phase of Experiments
1 and 2 (indicated by the shaded purple regions), which coincided with a reduction in

3.1.3
Correlation Between Substrate Uptake Rates and GFP
Productivity
Building upon the previous analysis of biomass growth, GFP expression, and substrate
utilization, this subsection investigates the correlation between substrate uptake rates and
recombinant protein productivity. The patterns observed suggest that both glucose and
lactose uptake could influence the eﬀiciency of GFP expression, either synergistically or
antagonistically depending on the regime.
Figure 3.6 illustrates the correlation between GFP productivity (qP ) and lactose uptake
rate (qSLac). The observed trend indicates a clear increase in productivity with increasing
lactose uptake, supporting the role of lactose as an inducer in the system. The relationship
follows a Monod-like saturation behavior, where qP increases asymptotically as qSLac rises.
Moreover, the slope and maximum level of this Monod-like curve appear to be influenced
by the glucose uptake rate. For instance, experiments conducted at higher qSGlu setpoints
(e.g., Experiment 3 versus Experiment 2, and Experiment 4 versus Experiment 5) resulted
in steeper and elevated productivity responses, suggesting that glucose may synergistically
enhance the induction effect of lactose under certain conditions.
Figure 3.7 shows the relationship between qSGlu and qP .
When evaluating individual
experiments, a clear trend is not immediately apparent. However, when data from all
experiments are combined, qP initially increases with qSGlu, reaching a peak at intermediate
uptake rates, followed by a gradual decline at higher qSGlu values. The low qP values at
low glucose uptake rates may be attributed to insuﬀicient energy and carbon supply for
recombinant protein synthesis, in line with the findings of Wurm et al. [19]. Conversely, at
high qSGlu, the decreased productivity might result from the repression of lactose uptake,
which limits induction eﬀiciency.
Overall, the data reveal a clear positive correlation between lactose uptake and GFP
productivity, exhibiting a Monod-like response. On the other hand, glucose has a dual
effect, where moderate qSGlu levels promoting productivity, but higher levels tend to inhibit
it. These observed relationships, together with the good quality of the experimental data
across biomass, product, and substrate, provide a solid basis for developing a mechanistic
model capable of capturing these dynamics.

Model Verification
Having selected the most suitable model structures for substrate uptake, biomass growth,
and specific productivity in the previous section, this section focuses on verifying the pre-
dictive capability of the model. Model verification was performed using two independent
experiments (Experiment 4 and 5) that were not used during the model calibration pro-
cess. The purpose of this step is to evaluate whether the proposed model can generalize
well beyond the training data.
To begin with, the selected model was simulated across all available experiments, including
both the calibration and verification datasets, and the model predictions were compared
against the experimental measurements.
3.3.1
Model Prediction Against All Experimental Data
The simulation results of the selected model structure across all five experiments are shown
in fig. 3.13. Overall, the model was able to capture the dynamics of biomass growth, glucose
consumption, and protein production reasonably well, particularly within the calibration
set (Experiments 1–3). The model predictions also extended fairly well to the verification
set, although with some notable deviations in specific variables.
In Experiment 4, the model successfully reproduced the trends of biomass growth and
glucose depletion. The prediction of GFP production (P) slightly overestimated the actual
data at certain time points but still followed the overall trajectory of the experimental
profile. The largest deviation was observed in the lactose concentration, where the model
failed to capture the transient accumulation of lactose that occurred during the cultivation.
This deviation is likely due to the model that was calibrated using data from experiments
with limited exploration of the high-lactose, low-glucose regime. As a result, it may lack
the flexibility to extrapolate accurately to conditions with sustained lactose excess and
minimal glucose repression, such as those applied in Experiment 4.
In Experiment 5, the model again accurately predicted the biomass and glucose trends,
suggesting that the core uptake and growth mechanisms remain robust across a wide
range of glucose feeding strategies. However, the simulation of GFP production was less
accurate. While the model was able to match the experimental data up to around 40
hours, it significantly overestimated the GFP accumulation toward the end of the process.
Similarly, predicted lactose profiles did not capture the observed accumulation, consistent
with previous observations in Experiment 4.
The mismatch observed in GFP production during the later stages of Experiment 5 may
result from plasmid loss or the emergence of subpopulations with reduced recombinant
protein expression, phenomena not accounted for in the model and not observed in the
calibration experiments. Additionally, physiological limitations such as metabolic burden
or protein misfolding, which have been reported to suppress recombinant protein expres-
sion during prolonged induction periods [37, 38], may further contribute to the observed
deviation between model predictions and experimental data in late-stage GFP production

Summary and Outlook
This study set out to characterize and model the recombinant GFP production in dual-
substrate feeding system using E. coli HMS174(DE3), a strain capable of metabolizing
both glucose and lactose. Through a combination of carefully designed fed-batch experi-
ments and mechanistic modeling, a predictive framework was developed to link substrate
uptake dynamics to biomass growth and recombinant protein productivity.
A series of five bioreactor cultivations, each employing distinct glucose and lactose feeding
profiles, were conducted to explore the effects of different regimes. These experiments
demonstrated that the uptake rates of both glucose and lactose play essential roles in
determining biomass accumulation and GFP expression. In particular, conditions char-
acterized by moderate glucose availability and sustained lactose presence were associated
with enhanced productivity, suggesting that balancing catabolite repression and induction
strength is key to process optimization. While glucose uptake was found to suppress lactose
utilization under certain conditions, it could also contribute synergistically to improved
specific productivity when appropriately managed.
From a biological standpoint, this study confirmed E. coli HMS174(DE3) as a promising
and tunable host for recombinant protein production under dual-substrate feeding. Induc-
tion was shown to be directly linked to lactose uptake, with productivity enhanced under
moderate feeding of lactose and glucose.
The mechanistic model developed in this work successfuly described the dynamic behavior
of substrate uptake, biomass growth, and productivity.
The selected model structure
showed good predictive performance across both calibration and verification datasets,
achieving normalized root-mean-square errors (NRMSE) below 10% for biomass, glucose,
and GFP, and below 20% for lactose. The model also captured a nonlinear interaction
between glucose and lactose uptake, highlighting the complexity of co-substrate dynamics.
From a process engineering perspective, the model provides a powerful tool for feed strat-
egy design. Simulations of specific productivity (qP ) across a range of uptake rates revealed
an optimal feeding window of approximately 0.5 to 0.6 g/g/h for glucose and 0.1 to 0.15
g/g/h for lactose, where GFP productivity is maximized without triggering metabolic
repression or burden.
These insights enable the development of rational feeding strategies and support the imple-
mentation of model-based control. The model can be used to evaluate feed combinations
in silico prior to experimentation, helping to minimize trial-and-error and accelerate pro-
cess development. Furthermore, it lays the foundation for future integration with real-time
control systems such as model predictive control (MPC), which could dynamically adjust
feeding rates based on real-time predictions of process behavior.

In conclusion, the study offers a useful starting point for characterizing and modeling the

The insights gained provide a foundation for further optimization and scale-up efforts, and
