resistance and its aggression towards the host. However, an important mechanism in the evolution

shown, not only a correlation between the switch of O-antigens as an aid in the emergence of a 
specific MDR strain, but also the observation of a deficient CRISPR-Cas system in some HiRiCs5,6.  
The focus of this project was therefore to classify and identify possible phenotypical similarities 
between MDR P. aeruginosa strains with the O-antigen classes 3, 4, 5, 6, 11 and 12, with three of 
them being MDR serotypes. To study the virulence, an assay based upon a protocol from 2014, was 
modified to fit the material available in the lab used for conductance of all experiments. Motility 
was assessed by testing swimming, swarming and twitching and biofilm was measured using 
absorbance. Serum sensitivity was tested using human serum of different percentages and to 
evaluate the ability to uptake foreign DNA in each of the O-antigen, a conjugation assay was 
performed using a simple gentamycin resistance plasmid.   
Although results to some degree were lacking due to assay-sensitivity and time, it was clear that

of the biological mechanisms underlying the particular high-risk MDR clones to emerge.

1 Introduction   
There is a noticeable increase in Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) infections causing 
health-care associated infections (HAIs) in hospitalized patients worldwide8. Hospital data illustrates 
that P. aeruginosa is highly associated with healthcare-associated pneumonia, hospital-acquired 
pneumonia as well as ventilator-associated pneumonia and it often arises in ICU patients within four 
days9–11. P. aeruginosa holds a high versatility due to its large group of regulatory genes encoded in 
its sizeable genome surpassing 6MB, which, combined with its significant repertoire of virulence 
factors and its capacity to evade antimicrobial treatments, makes this bacterium a highly feared 
bacterial pathogen1. A report shows that from year 1994 to 2001, activity of 11 common antibiotics 
against P. aeruginosa decreased and an emergence of antibiotic resistance resulted in severe 
adverse clinical outcome, which has limited future therapeutic choices12. 
Multiple reports warned of the increasing prevalence of multidrug-resistant (MDR) P. aeruginosa 
in hospitals worldwide and, more recently, new reports inform on the presence of global MDR 
clones which are linked with epidemic high-risk clones (HiRiCs)1–4. Increasing rates of MDR/XDR P. 
aeruginosa in HAIs is a major public health problem and is associated with poor outcomes including 
increased resource utilization and costs, morbidity, and mortality13. This highlights the need for 
better early detection. 
 
As MDR strains of P. aeruginosa solely appears clinically in hospitals, little data have been 
documented and, although these strains are associated with deprived clinical outcomes and 
contains a large number of resistance determinants, the biological mechanisms that enable specific 
clone types to emerge as HiRiCs remain unknown. When examining the samples at hospitals, there 
are two main interests; they look at what species the bacteria is without going any further as well 
as its drug resistance. Very rarely do they examine the sample further or evaluate if the same clone 
has been seen in multiple patients.  
Therefore, there are no data showing the correlation between its resistance and aggressiveness 
towards the host. However, it is known that an important mechanism in the evolution of pathogens 
is the acquisition of antibiotic resistance as well as virulence. Some results have shown not only a 
correlation between the switch of O-antigens as an aid in the emergence of a specific MDR strain, 
but also the observation of a deficient CRISPR-Cas system in some HiRiCs5,6.  
==PAGE:5==
 
Page 4 of 46 
 
 
 
The focus of this project was therefore to classify and identify possible phenotypical similarities 
between MDR P. aeruginosa strains with the O-antigen classes 3, 4, 5, 6, 11 and 12, with three of 
them being HiRiC MDR serotypes. To strengthen the hypothesis that genetic variation in the CRISPR-
Cas defense system and a certain switch in the O-antigen gene cluster underlie the emergence of 
high-risk MDR/XDR P. aeruginosa clones, virulence, serum sensitivity and the ability to uptake 
foreign DNA was tested in different O-antigen specific P. aeruginosa. Other phenotypical tests such 
as motility and biofilm creation were also tested. 
To study the virulence, an assay based upon the protocol by Koch G. et al. (2014)7, was modified 
to fit the material available in the lab used for conductance of all experiments. Motility was assessed 
by testing swimming, swarming and twitching and biofilm was measured using absorbance. Serum 
sensitivity was tested using human serum of different percentages and to evaluate the ability to 
uptake foreign DNA in each of the O-antigen, a conjugation assay was performed using a simple 
gentamycin resistance plasmid.   
Although results to some degree were lacking due to assay-sensitivity and time, it is clear that 
there are certain phenotypical differences internally between the studied serotypes. Albeit not all 
assays showed a pattern between strains with similar O-antigen, it was illustrated for serum 
sensitivity and conjugation and the data presented highlights the need for more in-depth analysis 
of the biological mechanisms underlying the emergence of particular high-risk MDR clones. 
 
1.1 Pseudomonas aeruginosa and its pathogenic lifestyle  
As P. aeruginosa can adapt to conditions of partial or total oxygen depletion, it is a facultative 
anaerobe. Through access to nitrate or nitrite, it can achieve growth and when these are absent, it 
can ferment arginine and pyruvate14–16.  
P. aeruginosa is an opportunistic pathogen that, as an environmental Gram-negative bacillus, is 
able to colonize multiple environmental niches, including the human body17. Infections related to P. 
aeruginosa is associated with a compromise of host defense and are therefore often seen in patients 
with AIDS, cystic fibrosis (CF), severe burn victims or in patients hooked to a respiratory system18,19. 
Hospital infections mainly manifest as acute lung infections in patients who are in intensive care 
units (ICU)20. During lung infection, a thick layer of mucus can limit diffusion of oxygen, where P. 
==PAGE:6==
 
Page 5 of 46 
 
 
aeruginosa still can thrive due to its facultative lifestyle and the motile P. aeruginosa penetrates 
into the hypoxic mucus.  This growth is most often asymptomatic until the bacteria form a biofilm 
in the mucus layer. Despite a dynamic influx of neutrophils into infected airways, accompanied by a 
high production of specific antibodies, P. aeruginosa infections become chronic (in CF patients), 
airways are destroyed and lung function declines14. 
An advance patient treatment from hospitals allows for a prolonged period of compromised 
state for the patient. This, together with the use of a high variety of drugs, inadvertently promote 
infection and pathogenesis caused by different bacilli, hereof P. aeruginosa. Under these 
circumstances P. aeruginosa takes advantage of a compromised and susceptible health and 
colonizes tissue, spreads to organs and attack host cells and initiate an overwhelming host 
inflammatory response contributing to morbidity21. A type III secretion system, as well as different 
virulence factors such as proteases, flagella, pili, and the LPS O sidechains (O-antigen), have been 
highlighted as important for P. aeruginosa infections17,22. It also holds a high resistance to many 
antibiotics due to a reduced permeability of its outer membrane1. Even though P. aeruginosa 
achieves pathogenicity through a secretion system that relies on a bacteria-host cell binding, very 
little data indicates that it binds for more than a transient binding23–25.  
 
For the type III secretion system, exotoxin U has been seen to increase virulence in animal 
studies, but is only produced in a minority of isolates and shows no significant difference in 
pathology compared to those not producing it26,27. P. aeruginosa also uses the virulence factor 
exotoxin A to inactivate eukaryotic elongation factor 2. Without elongation factor 2, eukaryotic cells 
cannot synthesize proteins and necrotize. The release of intracellular contents induces an 
immunologic response in patients. Exotoxin A appears to mediate both local and systemic disease 
processes caused by P. aeruginosa28. In addition, P. aeruginosa uses an exoenzyme, ExoU, which 
degrades the plasma membrane of eukaryotic cells, leading to lysis. P. aeruginosa has also been 
found to use the exoenzyme S (ExoS) that is produced by growing bacteria in burned tissue and 
seems to be detected in the blood before the bacteria is present there. This has led to the suggestion 
that this specific exoenzyme acts to impair the function of phagocytic cells as preparation for 
invasion of P. aeruginosa29.  
 
==PAGE:7==
 
Page 6 of 46 
 
 
It has long been known that P. aeruginosa is found in highest numbers trapped within the mucus 
layer that lines the epithelium, allowing it to consistently have transient connections with host cells 
and almost all studies regarding normal and CF mice illustrate this23,30–32. Primary adhesion for P. 
aeruginosa in respiratory mucus is the flagella cap protein, FliD, but it has also been seen to adhere 
by the flagellin itself33,34. However, many strains from long-term CF patients do not produce flagella 
and adhesion in the long run must be based upon different factors. Especially in infections of burn 
victims, where flagella and pili have been found to be important for virulence28. For the bacteria to 
disseminate throughout the host, different proteases and elastase is essential35. Elastase is able to 
inhibit chemotaxis, which affects the early clearance of the bacterium from wound sites by 
phagocytosis as well as presentation of bacterial antigens to the host immune system36. An earlier 
report37 stating that Fc receptor (bind to antibodies attached onto infected cells or pathogens 
stimulating phagocytic or cytotoxic cells) expression is decreased post injury in burn victims 
supports the possibility of the local deficiency of antibody-mediated immunity. The gene lasR 
controls multiple factors critical for P. aeruginosa pathogenesis in burn victims here among initiation 
of the quorum sensing response involved in virulence factor production and biofilm formation38. An 
important factor in burn wound infections is definitely the loss of the skin’s barrier, but it does not 
hold enough strength to explain the somewhat narrow range of bacterial pathogens able to culture 
in the wounds39. As such, it is most likely that there are some compromises in host defense 
mechanisms in severe burns.   
 
1.2 The role of Crispr/Cas  
Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) are transcribed as a single 
large transcript that is processed within identical repeat sequences to form small CRISPR RNAs 
(crRNAs) by their associated genes (cas)40. The ability of mature CRISPR/Cas systems to incorporate 
foreign DNA sequences and subsequently resist phages or plasmids has led these systems to be 
referred to as bacterial adaptive immune systems41. In 2011, Cady et al.42 investigated the P. 
aeruginosa CRISPR/Cas systems of 122 human clinical P. aeruginosa isolates. They found that many 
of the CRISPR spacers found within the clinical strains were 100% identical to integrating P. 
aeruginosa, such as phages and transposons, suggesting that the systems interact with these 
elements in a manner similar to that of other characterized CRISPR/Cas systems42. It was then later 
demonstrated, by Sampson et al. (2013)43 that the specific Cas protein, Cas9, of the Gram-negative 
==PAGE:8==
 
Page 7 of 46 
 
 
bacterium Francisella novicida, used CRISPR/Cas-associated RNA to repress endogenous transcript 
encoding a bacterial lipoprotein. As a proinflammatory innate immune response is triggered by 
bacterial lipoproteins, this illustrates the system to be important for promotion of virulence in the 
pathogen. Since Cas9 proteins are highly enriched in pathogenic bacteria, their work indicates that 
CRISPR/Cas-mediated gene regulation contributes to the regulation of endogenous bacterial genes, 
particularly during the interaction with eukaryotic hosts43. A recent study by Pawluk et al. (2014)44 
illustrates that it is not necessarily a lacking CRISPR-Cas system that is the reason for accepted DNA 
in a P. aeruginosa, but can also be the cause of up to five different anti-CRISPR proteins inhibiting 
the CRISPR-Cas. Their genes are located at the same genomic position of a group of closely related 
Pseudomonas phages. For this project the focus of the CRISPR/Cas system lies in its ability to protect 
the bacteria from foreign DNA and how it plays an important role in the shaping of accessory 
genomes of globally distributed P. aeruginosa isolates6. Because of the illustrated theory it is, for 
conclusions to be possible in this project, assumed that a high conjugation-frequency is due to a 
lacking CRISPR/Cas system and other possible explanations are as such disregarded until further 
research is possible. 
 
1.3 LPS and the O-specific antigen 
In the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria is the lipopolysaccharide 
(LPS) that consists of three parts; lipid A, comprised of a number of fatty acids 
(either five or six fatty acids in P. aeruginosa) anchoring the LPS to the bacteria, 
a core and a O-specific antigen (O-antigen), which is a target for recognition by 
host antibodies as well as a determent for the penetrability of certain 
antibiotics. The LPS of P. aeruginosa is furthermore split between smooth, O-
bearing, and rough, without an O-antigen. P. aeruginosa is capable of producing 
two types of LPS, A-band or B-band LPS. The A-band contains a conserved O-
antigen, while the B-band O-antigen structure varies45. The O-antigen consists 
of two to eight repeating oligosaccharide units with a great variety in sugar 
types present, linkages between the units and their arrangement. Its variability 
provides the basis for serotyping schemes of most Gram-negative bacteria, here among P. 
aeruginosa, as well as being an important virulence factor46. The chain making up the O-antigen is a 
highly variable surface antigen. If the bacterium experiences any immune system intervention or

1.3.1 Genetic variation in the O-antigen: the 20 serotypes 
An O-antigen cluster is present at the chromosome, varying in size from <15kb to >25kb each, 
and genetic variation in this cluster is the basis for O-antigen diversity. Genes involved in O-antigen 
synthesis are classified as O-unit processing genes, nucleotide sugar synthesis genes and sugar 
transferase genes46,48. 
For P. aeruginosa, at least 20 different O-antigen serotypes have been discovered49. For these 20 
serotypes, not many studies revolve around a comparison of the 20 serotypes and of those that are 
none compare their pathogenic role. Raymond et al.49 discovered in 2002 that within the high 
diversity of the serotypes, certain groupings based on serological relation as well as chemical 
structures and genes are possible. The largest group contained strains of serotypes O2, O5, O16, 
O18, and O20. The rest of the groupings included O3 and O15, O7 and O8, O10 and O19, O11 and 
O17, and O13 and O14, which can be seen in Figure 2.

1.3.2 Immunological responses induced by the LPS and O-antigen 
The LPS of P. aeruginosa is one of the multiple pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) 
that a gram-negative has that leads to transcription of different triggering factors in our system to 
give rise to the innate immune response. Once a microbe breaches an outer physical barrier, the 
structure of these PAMPs are recognized by certain toll-like receptors (TLRs) and other pattern 
recognition receptors (PRRs) expressed on sentinel cells such as dendritic cells and macrophage. LPS 
is recognized by the TLR-4 as illustrated in Figure 3.  The serum protein, the LPS-binding protein 
(LBP), retrieves LPS from aggregates or intact bacteria and transfers it to the surface-bound protein 
CD14 (not shown in Figure 3) that presents it to the cell-surface protein MD2 that associates with 
TLR-4. All but one of the LPS lipid A fatty acid chains bind to the hydrophobic pocket within the 
MD2/TLR-4 complex. The remainder of the LPS and the last lipid chain creates contact with the 
convex surface of another TLR-4 monomer inducing a dimer, which activates intracellular signaling 
pathways, inducing secretion of cytokines such as IL-8 (stimulates phagocytosis and induces 
chemotaxis), IL-6 (mediator of fever by initiating synthesis of a prostaglandin) and IL-10 (enhances 
B-cell survival and aids in cytokine regulation)51,52.  
 
Figure 3 After the LBP attaches the LPS to the CD14, five fatty acids of the LPS lipid A binds to the MD-2/TLR-4 complex. The last acid 
chain and remainder of the LPS binds to the convex surface of another TLR-4 domain to induce a dimer, activating intracellular 
response51. 
However, in contrast to lipid A, which is known to be crucial for the endotoxic action of LPS in 
human macrophages, a clear role of O-antigen in endotoxicity is not described. Though, it seems 
that it does not aid in the innate response mediation of the host, but rather it aids in an evasive 
strategy by interfering with the recognition of the fatty acids53. This hypothesis was tested in 2003 
by Bäckhed et al.53 and they illustrated that smooth LPS was 100-fold less potent at inducing IL-8 
(interleukin produced by the macrophage as a response in the innate immune response) and LPS 
==PAGE:12==
 
Page 11 of 46 
 
 
isolated from P. aeruginosa was a poor agonist for human TLR-4 where production of IL-8 by E. coli 
was inhibited up to 70%53. 
It is worth mentioning that the article does not specify which serotype P. aeruginosa is being 
evaluated. They do in their article describe how the response is by penta-acylated lipid A P. 
aeruginosa, which is found environmentally whereas the one pathogenic and described in 
immunological books51 as well as also found in CF-affected patients, is hexa-acylated54. Hajjar et al. 
(2002)54 illustrated that hexa-acylated LPS is better at initiating IL-8 production, nonetheless, they 
on the other hand classifies in their methods section that the tested P. aeruginosa LPS have had 
their O-antigen removed and which serotype it initially was is also not stated. It therefore seems 
that not only the play between O-antigen and lipid A affects the production of proinflammatory 
mediators, but also the acylation state of lipid A55. 
 
Similar results to reinforce the role of O-antigen in the TLR-4 response was illustrated in a study 
from 200956 done on a wild-type and O-antigen knock-out Salmonella enterica (S. enterica) where 
the knock-out lead to a significantly enhanced recognition. They further tested the effect using a 
smooth LPS as well as a rough and the interrupted recognition of the S. enterica was caused by the 
O-antigen inhibiting the LPS recognition by TLR-4 rather than an O-antigen-mediated suppression. 
They also found that the presence of an O-antigen influenced the intraepithelial survival rather than 
the epithelial invasion and their results as such further identify the O-antigen expression as an 
innate immune evasion strategy. Data, although sparse, shows that a similar conclusion may be 
done on P. aeruginosa, or at least on serotype O-11 where a serum sensitivity assay demonstrated 
that differing lengths of the O-antigen, obtained by mutation,  exhibited greater defects in virulence 
when the O-antigen was longer57. Both length58 and the presence59 of the O-antigen have also been 
proven necessary for the virulence of Shigella flexneri.  
 
1.3.3 Serum sensitivity - activation of the complement system 
Since the end of the 19th century, it has been recognized that certain Gram-negative bacteria are 
resistant or sensitive to the lytic role of serum and the presence of a smooth LPS is the characteristic 
most associated with serum resistance60. Serum-killing of these bacteria require the participation 
of the complement system and its components51. The complement system is essential for prompt 
==PAGE:13==
 
Page 12 of 46 
 
 
elimination of bacteria that has passed the physical line of defense and results in clearing of 
pathogenic bacteria via large pore-forming complexes as well as the rapid bacterial killing by 
immune cells61.  There are three differently timed ways in which the complement system eliminates 
the intruding bacteria (direct killing, labeling of the bacteria for phagocytic killing and stimulation of 
adaptive immune cells)61. This project will focus on the immediate response with direct killing 
involving the activation due to recognition of the LPS and the O-antigen. 
 
C3 and C5 are the main proteins of the complement system62,63. When the antigen of the 
bacterium bound by an antibody of the host is recognized by C1, a C3 convertase enzyme is 
prompted on the surface of the bacterium. This cleaves the C3 protein C3 into C3b. C3b holds a 
reactive thioester that can attach itself to the hydroxyl groups of carbohydrates on a bacterial 
surface which then can trigger phagocytosis by immune cells64,65. C3b is also recognized by 
complement receptors that enhance engulfment and as such, intracellular killing. Another highly 
important role of C3b molecules is to alter the specificity of the C3 convertase, due to a high density 
on the surface, to a C5 convertase that takes C5 as substrate and converts it to C5a and C5b. Here, 
C5b triggers the assembly of a membrane attack complex (MAC), but only in Gram-negative bacteria 
as Gram-positive with their thick peptidoglycan membrane are immune to this assembly66. The 
complement protein C3 can also react directly with the lipid A to generate the MAC in the absence 
of antibodies67. C5b binds to C6 and C7 creating a complex that binds to membrane C7. This allows 
for a C8 to bind to the complex and insert itself into the membrane attracting 10-16 C9 molecules 
that binds and polymerizes forming a pore51. For a schematic illustration of this process, see Figure 
4.

The aforementioned description of the complement system activation seems to be the most 
often described nowadays, however, through narrowed research it is clear that there seems to be 
some divergent explanations regarding the step before activation of the C3 convertase. Some article 
seems to think that it is in fact not the O-antigen that is recognized, but rather the lipid A68. Morrison 
et al. (1977)68 believed that it was a pathway in which the antibody did not recognize the antigen 
that was the classical pathway and used multiple articles as argument69–71. Due to the classical 
mechanism described in Figure 4 being seemingly simpler in its description and yet still mentioning 
the fact of the MAC only being possible in Gram-negative, it must be assumed that there seems to 
be some sort of knowledge gap. Of course, it is also to be taken into consideration how the articles 
used in this section spans all the way from the 70’s to the 21-century, meaning this gap might 
revolve around a decreasing interest in the precise mechanism due to a shift in research focus.  
==PAGE:15==
 
Page 14 of 46 
 
 
Following the presumption that it is the lipid A in which the activation occurs, there seems to be 
some articles describing the O-antigens role. When testing mutants vs wildtypes, it seems that 
rough mutants are serum sensitive and O-antigen as such is thought to block the access of 
complement to the lipid A67. The case in which the O-antigen is protecting the cell from serum killing 
has been seen in other species e.g. Klebsiella pneumoniae where the thickness of the O-antigen 
layer correlated with the protective effect72. In another old study an increase in O-antigen chain 
length in Vibrio anguillarum correlated with an increased resistance to fish-sera when grown on 
blood73. In the study by Grossman et al. (1987)74, the amount of O-antigen produced was regulated 
to modify the chain-length. They illustrated that if the LPS had above 14 or between 4-5 units, then 
complementation was possible and led to cell death. They then further argued how O-antigen 
activates complement away from the cell surface and so the MAC decays before causing any damage 
and so a reduction in chain length lets the MAC reach the outer membrane and kill the cell. Their 
statement then implies that the MAC, the C5b6-9-complex, can create the pores without the 
membrane surface and that they to some degree agree with the antigen playing a role in activation 
of the complement system. Another explanation given is that the reduced O-antigen chain allows 
the complement to actually reach the cell surface and trigger the cascade om the lipid A. However, 
resistance to complement killing is an attribute of most smooth Gram-negative bacteria, illustrated 
as not a failure of efficient complement activation, but instead failure of the assembled C5b6-9-
complex to insert into the outer membrane of the cell75. The somehow conflicting arguments for 
how complement precisely functions seem to color how complement resistance is believed to work 
and even though it was known that the O-antigen presence and length had an effect on the serum 
sensitivity and resistance, the precise requirements and mechanisms were and still seem unknown. 
 
A fairly recent study conducted on S. enterica showed for the first time in this species that not 
only the presence of the O-antigen held a significant role, but also the length of the O-antigen 
affected invasion and uptake of macrophages76. A length of the O-antigen varying between 16 to 35 
repeating units was found more efficiently at activating the complement system than other lengths 
and the length also illustrated an effect on the complement resistance. This study seems to be 
validated by a newer study by Bravo et al. (2008)77. Therefore, it seems that a consensus can be 
==PAGE:16==
 
Page 15 of 46 
 
 
found between some older studies and more recent, regarding the role of the O-antigen length, 
even though there are still some blind spots regarding the complete mechanism.  
 
1.4 Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) methods of P. aeruginosa 
As a highly pathogenic microorganism, P. aeruginosa holds multiple methods in which it can 
survive inside the host. Other than its inbuilt biological methods to avoid the innate and adaptive 
immune response of the host, as described in earlier sections, P. aeruginosa is as mentioned a 
highly-known MDR and as such holds multiple mechanisms in which it can avoid antimicrobial 
treatment. 
 Activity of 11 common antibiotics against P. aeruginosa decreased in just 7 years and an 
emergence of antibiotic resistance have resulted in severe adverse clinical outcome12. The major 
methods of which P. aeruginosa avoids antibiotics can be classified as adaptive, acquired or 
intrinsic78. The adaptive involves the creation of a biofilm in the lungs to limit antibiotic access into 
the cells. It is inside this biofilm that multidrug-tolerant cells, able to survive an antimicrobial attack, 
can form and be responsible for a prolonged and recurrent infection in CF patients79. Even though 
bactericidal action of b-lactams is found to be significantly affected by a slow growth rate induced 
by a biofilm80, it does not explain a 100-fold increase of resistance to specific antibiotics. This means 
more mechanisms holds a role in the AMR of P. aeruginosa81. The acquired resistance can be 
achieved by horizontal transfer of certain genes or mutational changes as was found with the switch 
in the O-antigen gene cluster in a specific MDR P. aeruginosa clone5,6. The intrinsic resistance of P. 
aeruginosa refers to its innate aptitude to reduce efficacy of certain antibiotics through structural 
or functional characteristics82. This includes a low outer membrane permeability, it being 12- to 100-
fold lower than that of E. coli78, expression of efflux pumps that pump antibiotics out of the cell, and 
production of antibiotic-inactivating enzymes83. Overproduction of the chromosomal enzyme AmpC 
b-lactamase and a reduction of permeability of the outer membrane porin-protein OprD, that 
contains a b-lactam binding site (leads to an increased efflux pump), are two well-known resistance 
mechanisms of P. aeruginosa84. These mutations have especially been taken notice of in MDR 
serotype O11 and O4, as well as O12 which is enriched with multiple b-lactamases85. It is therefore 
the selection of strains for this project have been focused around these mutations, rather than the 
individual  b-lactamases (see 2.1 Bacterial strains).

1.5 High-risk epidemical P. aeruginosa MDR clones 
Identifying possible risk factors predicting acquisition of MDR P. aeruginosa is a crucial step in 
diagnostic initiatives and can be crucial for appropriate antibiotic therapy. MDR P. aeruginosa are 
able to contaminate floors, hospital sinks, beds as well as the hands of nurses, causing nosocomial 
infections. However, the most often case seen is the bacterial carried into a hospital by a patient in 
which the person itself is the source of infection after injury17,86,87. Studies also show that prior use 
of antibiotics and hospital or ICU stay is one of the most significant risk factors in patients being 
hospitalized13. 
The increasing prevalence of infections caused by MDR P. aeruginosa strains compromises the 
selection of possible treatments and is associated with a high rate of morbidity and mortality. Its 
ability to further develop resistance to available antibiotics through selection of mutations in 
chromosomal genes, as well as having an increased prevalence of transferable resistance 
determinants, results in its growing threat1.  
It is not known why and how the clones have emerged, but the predominant epidemic MDR 
phenotype is associated with serotype O12, followed by O11, O4 and O65,88–90. Thrane et al.5 
illustrated in 2015, that the switch from the O4 to the O12 gene cluster in the antibiotic resistant 
sequence type (ST) ST111 was an important part in the advancement of MDR ST111 isolates. While 
certain serotypes are closely linked to the core genome phylogeny, the study observed horizontal 
exchange of O-antigen biosynthesis genes among phylogenetically distinct strains. They saw that 
the five identified isolates with serotype O12 clustered together structure-wise with a strain 
belonging to the O4 serotype5. This was even though they reacted to the monoclonal antibody 
specific to O12 and not O4 and all of them were of the sequence type ST1115.  
Interestingly, in accordance with aforementioned findings, it seems that P. aeruginosa strains 
from clinical specimens, holds a higher phenotypical and genotypical variation compared to strains 
isolated elsewhere90.  
 
At a high frequency, a large number of unrelated genotypes are recombining in P. aeruginosa, 
which means it holds a population composed of a limited number of widespread clones providing 
evidence of the existence of global HiRiCs MDR, disseminated in hospitals worldwide making them 
epidemic1,2. Epidemiological surveys have illustrated that environmental or non-MDR CF recovered 
==PAGE:18==
 
Page 17 of 46 
 
 
P. aeruginosa reveal a high clonal diversity. However, newer analysis illustrates that this is not the 
case for MDR strains and most isolates belongs to just three clones: ST111, ST175 and ST2353,4,91. 
Interestingly, these clones have shown significantly increased biofilm formation and mutant 
frequencies with a significantly reduced motility compared to regular P. aeruginosa clones further 
aiding them in regard to lung-infection4. Moreover, the association between these high-risk clones 
and transferable resistance with close to 100 horizontally-acquired resistance elements is 
remarkable. ST111 is as mentioned associated with serotype O12 with a world-wide spread, ST235 
is associated with serotype O11, also with a world-wide spread, and ST175 is associated with 
serotype O4, widely distributed in European countries and Japan1 (see Figure 5).

1.5.2 Factors driving the success of P. aeruginosa HiRiCs 
It is remarkable how vast the association between the HiRiCs and their transferable resistance 
mechanisms is, especially for the horizontally-acquired b-lactamases1. Unlike the underlying 
mechanisms for becoming an MDR HiRiC, the mechanisms for their resistance are well documented. 
For the widespread ST175 the mechanisms underlying is as mentioned earlier mostly mutational 
including an overexpression of AmpC, an inactivation of the OprD porin and an overexpression of at 
least three quinolone-resistance determining regions (QRDR)3,92,93. The immense majority of 
extended-spectrum b-lactamases (ESBL) producing P. aeruginosa isolates belong to few sequence 
types with ST235 being the most frequent, followed by ST111. ST235 has been found with up to 39 
different ESBL from class A93–96, with PER-1 being the most widespread and GES the most diverse, 
and classes B93,97,98 and D93,99. Also noteworthy among the ST235 isolates is the class A 
carbapenemase KPC-2100,101 and the more frequently seen B carbapemases, IMP and VIM, with VIM-
==PAGE:19==
 
Page 18 of 46 
 
 
2 being the most widespread99,102. Although ST111 have, with 14 found, fewer b-lactamases than 
ST235, multiple resistance genes from all three classes of ESBL have also been detected. Here is the 
KPC-2101 detected and the most dispersed acquired b-lactamase is again the VIM-299,102. For ST175, 
even though their resistance profile is frequently caused by aforementioned mutations IMP-
producing isolates have been detected in Japan103 and VIM-2 producing isolates have been 
identified in Europe104,105. The geographical distribution and diversity of documented resistance 
elements in HiRiCs suggest that distribution of these clones is global, but the acquisition of the 
resistance elements is frequently local106. Therefore, the presence of local features has to be 
considered in order to understand and implement appropriate control strategies.  
 
Beyond its antimicrobial resistance and obvious virulence, a subject to clarify are other possible 
factors driving the clonal success of the HiRiCs. Even though resistance plays a vital role by itself, 
additional factors are necessary to exist as not all MDR isolates become widespread HiRiCs, as have 
been illustrated with O6. A study from 20134 collected 100 isolates including ST111, ST175 and 
ST235 from diverse sources to evaluate biological parameters that could potentially be involved in 
their success. They tested here among swimming, swarming and twitching, the capacity to form 
biofilm and their spontaneous mutation rates. Interestingly, the three major clone isolates were 
found to be defective in all three motility types and showed an increased biofilm formation as well 
as spontaneous mutant frequencies4. These phenotypes have also been documented for HiRiCs in 
another study from 2013107, and they are therefore presenting certain similarities worth examining 
closer to step closer to understanding the biological mechanisms required for an isolate to become 
a HiRiC.  
 
1.5.2 Laboratory detection of P. aeruginosa HiRiCs 
Detection of HiRiCs in clinical environments holds a major relevance in public health and 
infection control. Currently, multi-locus sequence typing (MLST) is the most widely accepted 
standard method which, by characterizing isolates of the species using its DNA sequences of 
multiple housekeeping genes, defining it ST. However, other cheaper and faster methods, such as 
multiple-locus variable number tandem repeat analysis (MLVA), have illustrated comparable results 
in the detection of international HiRiCs108. Moreover, O-antigen serotyping is a simple and fast 
==PAGE:20==
 
Page 19 of 46 
 
 
screening technique for the detecting the high-risk clones ST111 (O12), ST235 (O11) and ST 175 
(O4). A recent work was able to identify several P. aeruginosa HiRiCs using MALDI-TOF mass 
spectrometry109. Approaching with whole genome sequencing (WGS) provides further information 
regarding outbreak investigations, especially for transmission dynamics. A good example is a study 
by Turton et al. (2015) 110 that evaluated over 80 ST111 isolates where WGS helped to identify 
outbreaks as well as the transmission inside the hospital of this clone. In this study, no methods 
were directly used as strains were given already identified, but strains were given identified using 
serotyping and MLST. 
   
1.5.3 HiRiC’s among other species 
It is to note, that P. aeruginosa by far is not the only epidemic HiRiC known to be seen frequently 
in hospitals worldwide111. Extra-intestinal pathogenic Escherichia coli (ExPEC) is the most common 
pathogen causing urinary tract infections and bloodstream infections in both developing and 
developed countries112. Until 2014, ExPEC was thought to be responsive to first-line antibiotics, but 
after a World Health Organization report, it was acknowledged as an epidemic MDR high-risk 
pathogen with special focus on a single clone, ST131113. Klebsiella pneumoniae, a Gram-negative 
causing urinary and respiratory infections, has also emerged as an MDR HiRiC with its strain ST258 
and have, together with ExPEC been seen as prime examples of HiRiCs worldwide114. However, not 
all MDR HiRiCs are at a level in which it is still unknown how to properly screen and treat it. A well-
known pathogen, MDR mycobacterium tuberculosis (MDR-TB), is known to be treatable, albeit be 
extensive, even in the severe cases where second-line antibiotics does not work115,116. 
As for P. aeruginosa HiRiCs, it is important to consider that, at least at local levels, the HiRiCs are 
associated with defined MDR profiles and as such detection of the MDR HiRiCs directly from clinical 
samples could be an important step for early appropriate targeted therapy. Despite the amount of 
documentation in regard to the uptake of resistance genes, serotype association and presence of 
integrons among its isolates, information regarding genetic environment, transference mechanisms 
and any possible correlation between resistance and host aggressiveness, is sparse1. Nonetheless, 
it is known that an important mechanism in the evolution of pathogens is the acquisition of 
antibiotic resistance as well as virulence and some results have shown, not only a correlation 
==PAGE:21==
 
Page 20 of 46 
 
 
between the switch between O-antigens, through conjugation, as an aid in the emergence of a 
specific MDR strain, but also the observation of a lacking CRISPR-Cas system in certain HiRiCs5,6.

2.3 Conjugation of psEVA658::ssr 
The conjugation protocol used for this study was created by Ph.d. student Morten Lindqvist 
Hansen from the Center for Microbial Secondary Metabolites at DTU Lyngby. It was then adjusted 
for this particular experiment. The plasmid mentioned and used in this protocol was a broad host 
range plasmid. 
 
Procedure was divided into four days with the first day being preparation in form of streaking of 
E. coli DH5α/pSEVA658::ssr (donor strain) on LB plates with gentamycin (10 μg/ml), E. coli 
HB101/pRK600 (helper strain) on LB plates with chloramphenicol (10 μg/ml) and the six chosen 
strains of P. aeruginosa on regular LB plates O/N at 37 degrees Celsius. The next day, a single colony 
of the donor strain and helper strain was inoculated in 5 ml LB with gentamycin with a 10 μg/ml 
concentration and chloramphenicol with the same concentration, respectively followed by 
incubation at shaking at 37 degrees Celsius. P. aeruginosa was inoculated in 5 ml LB 37 degrees 
Celsius at shaking at 250rpm as well. 
On the third day, P. aeruginosa was inoculated in a 1:5 dilution in LB at 42 degrees Celsius for 
four hours without shaking as to kill the toxins that is able to kill the E. coli. At the same time the 
helper and the donor strain were also inoculated in a 1:5 dilution in LB for four hours. This allows 
the bacteria to be in the exponential phase before proceeding with the conjugation. Afterward, 
triparental mating was done with a thorough washing to remove antibiotics. 1 ml of each O/N 
culture was added to 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes in which they were centrifuged at 13,000xg for 2 
minutes and then re-suspended as well as washed in LB media. A 10 times dilution was prepared of 
each for measurement of OD600 to calculate and prepare 1 ml of each at OD600 = 1.0. A 1:1:1 
conjugation mixture was then formed by combining 333 μl of each culture. The cells were 
centrifuged at 13,000 g for 2 minutes, decanted and re-suspended in the remaining liquid. 20 μl of 
the concentrated conjugation mix was spotted onto sterile 0.22 μm Nanopore filter paper where-
after the agar plate was incubated O/N at 37 degrees Celsius. 
==PAGE:24==
 
Page 23 of 46 
 
 
On day four, the conjugation mix was flushed from the Nanopore filter paper in 1 ml LB media 
and then re-suspended by vortexing. A 101 to a 105 times dilution row was prepared where the 104 
and 105 diluted conjugation mix was spread on two PIA agar plates with gentamycin (30μg/ml) as 
well as on two PIA plates without any antibiotics. To verify the ability for each strain to uptake 
foreign DNA, colonies on the plates where counted to resolve the growth frequency on the fifth day.   
 
2.4 Motility: twitching, swimming and swarming 
For the testing of motility, three different assays were performed, and all assays were performed 
in classical LB agar. For the swimming, swarming and twitching motility assays the agar 
concentration used was 0.3%, 0. 5% and 1.5%, respectively. For the swimming assay, approximately 
5 µl liquid culture was with a pipette stabbed halfway through the agar. The plate was left to 
incubate upright at 30oC for one to two days where outward growth was measured with a ruler. To 
assay swarming motility, 5 µl of an overnight culture was used to inoculate in the center on top of 
the agar and left to incubate upright at 30oC for two nights for biggest measurable radius to be 
observed. For the twitching assay, again, approximately 5 µl liquid culture was with a pipette 
stabbed all the way through the agar and wiggled to make sure all bacteria were added. This was 
then left to inoculate at 37oC for one to two days and the radius of the interstitial colony was 
measured. For clarification, this is illustrated in the table below.

Biofilm production of the strains was investigated using a crystal violet assay. A 96-microtiter

4 Discussion 
In this section, results from previous passage will be analyzed and discussed. Additionally, 
probable sources of error from experiments will be mentioned and possible improvements on 
assays will be considered together with potential further studies. 
 
In the present study, the phenotypical correlation between different MDR HiRiC P. aeruginosa 
O-antigen was evaluated as to examine whether an O-antigen shift was the cause for the 
transformation to an MDR clone. Although the data was not able to fully illustrate the relationship 
between a specific O-antigen and phenotypical abilities, certain areas were evidently highlighted 
through the specific assays performed in this study. 
Unfortunately, data from the virulence assay was too unclear to let any deductions be based on 
the different O-antigens. There were a lot of issues with this assay from the beginning and it was 
not possible to properly troubleshoot them in the timespan of this study. All larvae died too fast and 
differences were not noticeable as such. In the attempt to try and correct the assay to fit this 
particular case, trials for better concentrations were done and even ideas regarding heat- or night-
cameras were discussed. However, as the larvae needed to be in an already warm room, that we 
did not succeed in learning their normal heat temperature as baseline and that most cameras were 
not able to either send live feed or save more than 6 hours of data to a computer where the cable 
also left an issue, these ideas were shot down rather quickly. From the project done prior to this 
study, it is evident that the MDR strains holds a much higher virulence than normal P. aeruginosa 
with the same O-antigen. However, as this is evident in it being MDR’s and it not giving any clarity 
in the role of specific O-antigens, there was not much more we could achieve from this assay. This 
assay may be more appropriate for drug testing in a semi-in vivo state for screening, but for the 
purpose of testing virulence in more aggressive bacteria, it seems insufficient in its sensitivity. 
 
The data that most clearly illustrated what had been hypothesised was the conjugation assay. 
Not only did we see a pattern across different strains based on their O-antigen, but we also saw that 
strains containing O4, which had been found as the acceptor of an O12 in an MDR5, had the highest 
conjugation frequency. For O12, there was an interesting dip in one of the clones’ frequency. At first 
it was thought to be a single deviant result, but the three replicates of this held the lowest deviance 
==PAGE:31==
 
Page 30 of 46 
 
 
between all of the strains. However, for O12 there are, phylogenetically, two main groups where 
one is far away from the others and more so resembles PA7 (see Figure 12) and as such, this could 
be a reason for this different O12. Another reason could be that this particular strain holds a certain 
unknown mutation lowering its conjugation frequency.

A prior project to this illustrated in non-isogenic P. aeruginosa that the conjugation frequencies 
among the MDR-known O-antigen was from highest to lowest O4, O12 and then O11. Although the 
prior strains were non-isogenic and that we see a rather large deviation for the O11 in the present 
study, it was still clear that there was a replicable pattern for this phenotype that could be linked to 
the CRISPR/Cas system. Nonetheless, the direct role of the CRISPR/Cas was not tested here due to 
the time frame, so it is only due to the role that the system holds we can hypothetically conclude 
that this tendency is related to a lacking CRISPR/Cas system where in fact there may be other 
variables alone or in combination with the system. It was especially here that the lacking data from

the virulence assay would have been helpful. It would have been interesting to see if the known 
MDR O4 that had switched to an O12 already had its host aggression from O4 or if this was acquired 
at the uptake of the O12, if it is a specific cocktail of the two together or if O12 brought something 
still yet unknown. 
 
Two assays that wasn’t directly correlated with the O-antigens role, but more general 
phenotypes of the HiRiC MDR P. aeruginosa were the motility and biofilm assays. Unfortunately, as 
mentioned in the last section, no data were achieved in the motility assay and after four different 
attempts it was clear that the assay would be more work that the gain was worth exactly due to the 
lacking correlation to the O-antigen. It was of interest since we know that motility is changed in 
HiRiC MDR’s and maybe this could be affected by a specific O-antigen. For the biofilm assay, 
although the experiment went well and without any complications, it became clear that there was 
no specific pattern to find among strains with the same O-antigen. There weren’t any expectations 
for this from the beginning as the experiment mostly was added to test as many phenotypes as 
possible that were mentioned in HiRiC P. aeruginosa. This allowed for not only agreement on those 
that were expected to show patterns among O-antigens, but also rule out those without interest for 
further studies. The ability to produce biofilm is a complex phenotype with many factors allowing it 
to create it. This means that it could take only a single unknownmutation to make it better or worse 
in the production leaving too many variables to test, especially in the short amount of 6 months this 
project was. Interestingly, these specific HiRiCs have earlier shown significantly increased biofilm 
formation and mutant frequencies with a significantly reduced motility compared to regular P. 
aeruginosa clones further aiding them in regard to lunginfection4.  
 
For the last assay performed, the ability to survive the complement system was tested using 
human serum. Albeit there seems to be rather high standard deviations, we do see that the strains 
are affected as well as that different strains held different survival frequencies. As mentioned 
earlier, it seems that the isolates with O-antigens of interest, O4, O11 and O12, do seem to have a 
somewhat similar frequency among same strains. It would be interesting to further investigate the 
serum sensitivity with an O4 that have been turned into an O12, as have been seen. Maybe, the 
reason why a known MDR HiRiC is an O4 turned into an O12, is because O12 is more virulent, which 
==PAGE:33==
 
Page 32 of 46 
 
 
we cannot confirm in this moment, but it is more sensitive to serum and as such need some sort of 
protection. Both older and newer articles have illustrated how the length of the O-antigen affects 
the accessibility of the complement system recognition, but as the biochemical sizes of the O-
antigens in questions is still unknown, it is as such not possible to conclude whether the patterns 
observed in this assay is correlated to the specific O-antigens and their specific size. 
 
Overall it would be very interesting to be able to remove the O-antigen and see if there is a 
correlation between clinically relevant phenotypes, such as serum sensitivity and biofilm, and the 
specific O-antigen. The strains used in this study were all hospital-acquired MDR that circulate and 
evolve to better survive in clinical circumstances and should as such have been “trained” to better 
survive, create biofilm and infect. However, this is not the pattern that is shown in present results. 
There is a large diversity in the effect of the different strains, not only between strains with different 
O-antigen, but also among ones with the same O-antigen in the biofilm study. The only clear pattern 
we see is in the conjugation assay and this is not related to a clinically relevant phenotype. With the 
small amount of Pseudomonades used, and the fact that there is only MDR strains included, it is not 
possible to tell if the patterns that are seen here are a general pattern among strains with certain 
O-antigens or if it is even a pattern among MDR as opposed to non-MDR strains. As there is such a 
spread in some of the assays, it is possible to conclude that it is not a certainty that all MDR are 
eminent at all clinically relevant phenotypes. It would be preferable in future trials to include some 
sort of control group of non-MDR strains to see if the patterns observed is reserved to the MDR 
versions.  
 
For further work, what can ultimately be concluded of this study can be broken down into two 
approaches. Firstly, it is necessary to study a lot more strains to actually say something conclusive 
on the possible patterns observed. The other approach would be to take a single strain, remove its 
O-antigen gene cluster and create a mutant by inserting another one and re-testing the same assays. 
Although the best way forward would be a combination of the two which was not possible in this 
study due to the time frame. To see if not only a switch in O-antigen is necessary, but if it needs to 
be a specific switch or a specific original O-antigen, cloning of each gene cluster for the O-antigen 
will be needed. Then, these can be used in first a reference strain, such as PAO1, and, then perhaps 
==PAGE:34==
 
Page 33 of 46 
 
 
later on, in clinical samples to acquire a series of serotype switched derivatives to express different 
combinations of the O-antigens. For the clinical strains, it will be in the four known MDR strains, O4, 
O6, O11 and O12. Interestingly, further work of the O-antigen could include the switch of O-antigen 
not only in an MDR strain of the four types, but also in a non-MDR to see if it is a switch between 
already existing MDR strains or not that causes the HiRiCs. 
Secondly, work to investigate the role of the lacking CRISPR/Cas system would also need to be 
performed. Once again, this can be done in a reference strain and afterwards in one of the 
documented strains to have a lacking system to see the effect. The different serotypes created with 
the switch could afterwards be used to test the combination with the CRISPR/Cas system. 
Once multiple combinations have been made in a reference strain, clinical selected strains and 
the constructed strains, the same phenotypical tests as have been done in this study can be redone 
to require data to analyze and evaluate e.g. phylogenetically.  
 
Cross contamination between patients placed in the same room lead to the spread of these 
genotypes, possibly through hand carriage or due to contact with surfaces a patient with the HiRiC 
P. aeruginosa has touched. A hospital environment amazingly promotes a fast distribution of 
resistant strains due to these cross contaminations. An important step to reduce the increase of 
nosocomial infections, especially in immunodeficient patients, will be to increase the monitoring of 
agents of intra-hospital resistance. As there is no strong information on what makes an MDR strain, 
hospitals can only screen them after they’ve evolved in the hospital and are causing multiple cases 
of infection. By highlighting and documenting that these switches and absent CRISPR-Cas systems 
indeed holds a role in the rise of MDR P. aeruginosa HiRiCs, they can forward be used as markers 
for HiRiC P. aeruginosa emergence towards which tailored diagnostic tests can be established to 
allow for selective and, hopefully, cost-effective screening of patients.  
 
It is of importance that hospitals carry out more detailed screening of non-HiRiC infected 
patients as well as of any MDR that may occur with more focus than only their antibiotic resistance. 
With more data it will be possible to search for correlations between strains and possibly reinforce, 
or develop, the stated hypothesis in this paper. To do so, it is crucial to include a detailed 
characteristic and relationship among strains isolated at different environments and times. To do 
==PAGE:35==
 
Page 34 of 46 
 
 
so the application of more molecular methods at hospitals seems to be inevitable until any known 
markers are validated.  
 
Finally, it is essential to emphasize that the phenomenon of a species becoming a HiRiC is not 
restricted to P. aeruginosa but many others as well and this study may not only be a first step in 
discovering markers for this specific species, but to understand possible commonalities across 
different HiRiC species, which could allow in the future for a less specific and more commonly used 
screening for all HiRiCs.

5 Conclusion  
In the present study we aimed to investigate any phenotypical similarities or differences 
between well-known MDR P. aeruginosa HiRiCs sampled from hospitals. Motility, biofilm 
production, serum sensitivity, virulence and the ability to uptake foreign DNA was tested to consider 
the role of an O-antigen switch and a lacking CRISPR-Cas defense system in the emergence of 
specific MDR/XDR HiRiCs P. aeruginosa.  
Although results to some degree were lacking due to assay-sensitivity and time, it is clear that 
there are certain phenotypical differences internally between the studied serotypes. Although not 
all assays showed a pattern between strains with similar O-antigen, it was illustrated for serum 
sensitivity and conjugation. It is known that the length of the O-antigen affects both the innate 
immune response and the complement system, but as very few strains were used, as well as two 
out of five assays did not work as planned, it is at current point not possible to fully conclude if there 
are any specific markers among all MDR HiRiCs that can be used for screening. From these results, 
it seems like the common impression of MDR strains being overall better at all phenotypical abilities 
have been debunked as we saw a sizeable variation in all assays. The results acquired in this study 
emphasizes the importance of further research regarding the role of a specific O-antigen gene 
cluster in the emergence of MDR HiRiCs.  
In this study we have reported differences between six P. aeruginosa serotypes in serum 
sensitivity, biofilm creation, virulence through a modified assay by Koch G. et al. 201413, as well as 
their ability to take in foreign DNA through conjugation. The data presented highlights the need for 
more in-depth analysis of the biological mechanisms underlying the particular high-risk MDR clones 
to emerge. The emergence of new potential HiRiCs all around the world highlights the need for 
better early detection and the possibility to predict potential future HiRiCs.

Appendix B - Virulence assessment with Galleria mellonella 
Based on the article by Koch G. et al. (2014)7 and adjusted for use in this study. 
 
Materials: 
1. G. mellonella (stored at room temperature at dark until use) 
2. Insulin injection needles (29G) 
3. Long tweezers 
4. Petri dishes (1 per O-antigen, 5 larvae in each) 
5. O/N P. aeruginosa strains 
6. LB media 
7. MgSO4 (10 mM) 
 
Methods: 
1. O/N culture of P. aeruginosa: 
1. Prepare O/N culture in LB. 
2. Pipette under sterile conditions 10 ml of the media to a glass culture tube. 
3. Dilute O/N culture 1:100 into the fresh media. 
4. Incubate the strains at 37 degrees Celsius at 200 rpm until an OD600 = 0.6-0.8. 
5. Pipette 1ml culture to 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes. 
6. Spin down cultures (2700g, 5 min, 4 degrees Celsius) and wash gently with 5 ml 
MgSO4 (10 mM). 
7. Spin down again and resuspend to a final concentration of 1x105 CFU/ml (OD600 = 
0.002) using MgSO4 (10 mM). store on ice until ready for use or use immediately.

2. Infection of G. mellonella: 
1. Prepare petri dishes for the amount of strains tested 
2. Prepare a needle with 60 μl of O/N strain (5x10 μl + spillage) 
3. Grab a larva with the tweezers and let it onto the petri dish.  
4. Hold the tweezers firm, but gently, around the abdomen so it lies straight. 
5. Take a needle and inject 10 microliters at the last leg of the larva. 
6. Remove the needle and release pressure around abdomen. 
7. Repeat the procedure for all larvae.  
8. Place the petri dishes in a cardboard box with holes and let it incubate at 37 degrees 
Celsius at dark.  
 
3. Monitoring the larvae: 
1. Monitor larvae at 24 and 48 h. 
2. Evaluate the efficiency of virulence by scoring for alive and dead larvae (dead = 
black/not reacting to tapping/no movement). 
3. Create a survival curve.
