Abstract
The master thesis investigates the thermal and economical performance of a repowering application in an exist-
ing Rankine cycle power plant called Kyndbyværket, using solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC) powered by natural
gas or ammonia as the topping cycle, combined with a ﬁeld of parabolic trough solar collectors that provide
auxiliary heat to the evaporator of the heat recovery steam generator.
The thermal performance analysis is executed by modelling the repowered power plants using a software called
Dynamic Network Analysis, optimizing the initial model by doing sensitivity analysis in key input parameters
and analyzing the implementation of the solar ﬁeld by ﬁnding its allowable heat input limits and assessing
how the plants perform as the auxiliary heat is gradually introduced. Afterwards, climate data, solar collectors,
and different installed areas of solar collectors are determined, analyzed and compared to each other using
the software to gain insight on how they perform and compare thermally to each other throughout a year of
operation.
Results obtained from the thermal analysis found that the initial models using the natural gas and ammonia
SOFC without any auxiliary solar heat result in thermal efﬁciencies of 54.1% and 60.9%, fuel consumptions of
1,554 MJ/s and 1,771 MJ/s and net power productions of 841 MW and 1,078 MW respectively. By optimizing
parameters in the SOFC component, said thermal efﬁciencies are able to be increased to 55.4% and 62.0%.
When introducing solar heat, it was determined that the maximum amount of solar heat that the systems could
use while correctly operating under the set conditions is 194.3 MJ/s, and that an optimal instance of this solar
heat being successfully provided brings up the thermal efﬁciencies to 62.8% and 68.2%, and reduces both fuel
consumption to 1,150 MJ/s and 1,326 MJ/s, and net power production to 723 MW and 905 MW. It was also
determined that the climate in the site is not optimal for solar energy projects and that the energy systems would
perform better with solar energy if the plant was located in another area, which it’s climate data is introduced for
analysis and comparison purposes. Once the solar collectors were chosen for each site and different variations
of installed solar collectors area were determined, the performance across the year for 16 different power plant
variations were simulated, resulting in the best real case scenarios for the models using natural gas and ammonia
SOFC using auxiliary solar heat to have thermal efﬁciencies of 61.0% and 66.7%, fuel consumptions of 1,236
MJ/s and 1,420 MJ/s, and net power production of 824 MW and 1040MW respectively.
The economical performance analysis is carried out using a component costing tool developed by the depart-
ment of mechanical engineering at the Technical University of Denmark, the tool calculates key economical
indicators for investment in energy systems, such as costs, net cash ﬂow, net present value and pay-back time,
with the use of input parameters such as fuel and electricity prices, annual operation hours, parameters for
calculation of purchased equipment cost of components and additional cost factors.
Results obtained from the economical analysis found that under the current regulatory and economical frame-
work, all the proposed power plants are economically feasible, but that the one with a natural gas SOFC and no
solar collectors installed is the most attractive from an investment point of view, showcasing a pay-back time of
3.52 years, a yearly net cashﬂow of 682 MUSD and a net present value after 20 years of 7,066 MUSD. It was
determined that the main contributor for this result is that the ammonia prices are too high to compete with that
of natural gas and that the reduced fuel consumption using solar collectors doesn’t justify the combined losses
in proﬁts due to capital costs and a lower net power production.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Concerns of the rising CO2 emissions and depletion of the natural resources available in the world when ap-
proaching the 1990s ignited a global movement to assess and rethink the approach which humanity has taken
for industrialization and global development. Multiple scientiﬁc studies determined that the global average
temperature was rising due to human-related activities and that the consequences due to climate change could
be permanently detrimental to the environment. In the 12th of December of 2015, all the participant countries
of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change signed the Paris Agreement, while 187 of the
196 states became party to it. This agreement aims to prevent the global average temperature to rise 2 °C above
pre-industrial levels, and to pursuit efforts to keep it below 1.5 °C in order to mitigate the risk of consequences
that are predicted to happen after this threshold is surpassed. The indicator for this is green house gas emissions,
which has been determined to be the main contributor to the rising temperature. UN (2015)
A major contributor to the world’s development has been the energy sector, which is essential today to the
operation of society. Within it lie the supply, power, heat, transportation, industrial, commercial and residential
sectors, which generate and consume energy to exist, and release CO2 emissions as they operate. Electricity
generation, which happens in the power sector, is one of the main driving forces within energy, as it takes on
the task of being the main power supply to all but the transportation sector, which is powered by fuels. It is
therefore important to gain greater insight of how this section of the sector works, and how CO2 emissions can
be reduced in order to stay in line with the Paris agreement.
1.1
Large scale electricity generation
The recorded observation of electricity itself dates back to 1628 when William Gilbert published De Magnete,
where he studied static electricity produced by amber. Gilbert (1628) However, the principles for generation of
electricity were ﬁrst studied until the 1820s by Michael Faraday, when he discovered that electricity could be
generated by rotating a loop of wire between the poles of a magnet. Figure 1.1 shows the concept proposed by
Faraday.
Figure 1.1: Faraday’s disc Source: Kennedy (1909)
This principle is used to this day in power generation, and the discovery triggered a sequence of technological
advancements that eventually culminated in the second industrial revolution. When industrial electrical gener-
ators were developed, a wide variety of mechanical technologies that could be used to rotate these generators
started to bloom, some of the most important among them being the steam and gas engines, water turbines
installed under dams, as well as the more recent wind turbines.
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
Speciﬁcally, steam engines have been paramount to the upscale of electricity generation, as water is widely
available, and multiple methods of heating up said water to transform it into steam and run the engines are at
quick disposal and can be done at a large scale. The most common methods of creating steam during the 20th
century were coal and natural gas burning, while nuclear started to emerge at the end of the century. With the
prominence of coal and natural gas power plants operated under the steam Rankine cycle, the scientiﬁc and
industrial entities took an interest in increasing the efﬁciency of these power plants, as the saving in fuels could
signiﬁcantly reduce the associated operating costs and CO2 emissions. One of the most successful discovered
improvements to already installed power plants is repowering.
1.2
Repowering
Repowering of a power plant consists of replacing the original source of heat with a new one. For instance, a
coal power plant that has been operating for 25 years sees the life cycle of its boiler coming to an end. Instead
of decommissioning the entire plant and constructing a new one from scratch, said boiler can be replaced by a
number of technologies, such as a new boiler, but also with other available technologies that could provide heat
to the original steam cycle. Riekert (1993)
1.2.1
Present
Currently, the most common practice for repowering is to transform a power plant that used to operate with the
burning of coal to a natural gas power plant, this done by installing a single or multiple gas turbines and using
their exhaust heat to provide energy to the steam cycle. The exchange of heat is done with a heat recovery
steam generator (HRSG), which is a component that has different heat exchangers that transform water into
super heated vapor through different phases, steam that is then used in the steam cycle to drive the steam
turbines. Figure 1.2 shows the resulting system diagram in such cycle modiﬁcation.
Figure 1.2: Repowered steam cycle using a gas turbine Source: Rokni (2019)
Carrying out such modiﬁcations has a number of advantages: the ability to keep the original steam cycle is
a decent reuse of already installed technology. Furthermore, because now the heat generated by the burning
of the fuel is being used not only to power an original cycle, but also to further power an additional cycle, the
thermal efﬁciencies see themselves increased substantianlly. A conventional steam cycle power plant showcases
a thermal efﬁciency of around 32%, while a repowered cycle done as stated can see its efﬁciency increased to

as the added topping cycle needs to be signiﬁcantly larger than the original steam cycle to be able to operate

Multiple projects have already been undertaken using this method. In Florida, the Fort Myers and Sanford

and lowering their emissions. Briggs (2001)
1.2.2
Future
In the scientiﬁc ﬁeld, studies are being carried out assessing the thermal and economical performance of solid
oxide fuel cells (SOFC) as an alternative cycle to be used to provide heat to a bottoming steam cycle. Stud-
ies have concluded that repowering using SOFC compared to gas turbines produces more power, has a higher
thermal efﬁciency that can compete with even newly installed combined cycle power plants and the CO2 emis-
sions are signiﬁcantly lower. Rokni (2016) Figure 1.3 presents a diagram of a natural gas SOFC repowering an
existing bottoming cycle.
Figure 1.3: Repowered steam cycle using natural gas SOFC Source: Rokni (2016)
While it is not an application that has been widely adopted by industry yet, it shows a lot of potential for the
future energy system as the world moves for cleaner energy technologies. One of the focuses of this study is
assessing potential improvements to the application of SOFC when used in such an application.

1.3
Solar heat implementation in electricity generation.
Solar energy is a resource of great potential in the energy sector, as it is available anywhere in the world and has
no CO2 emissions attributed directly to its collection and transformation. Although solar photo-voltaic (PV)
is the prominent application when it comes to electricity generation from solar energy at the moment, solar
heating is still being studied as it has a number of advantages: solar heat allows for easier storage of energy
than solar PV and the current solar thermal collectors have a higher energy efﬁciency than solar PV.
One of the current applications for solar heat collectors in electricity generation include steam cycles powered
by solar heat. As solar energy is undispatchable, meaning that it is not available at all times, these types of
power plants are ﬁtted with a storage system that lets the operator generate steam during low sunlight hours by
using previously stored hot ﬂuid, therefore allowing the power plant to meet the demands of the power grid at
other times, however, down times can occur after several days of small amounts of available solar energy if the
storage is not properly sized.
A number of studies oversee applications using solar heat in electrical generation cycles in more innovative
approaches:
• Mills (2004) reviews multiple rising solar thermal technologies, such as parabolic trough, solar tower
and Stirling engine dish collectors and mentions their potential for better environmental performance and
their application in combined heat and power generation.
• With a different approach, Bienchini et al. (2013) analyze the combination of solar steam reforming of
natural gas and a steam injected gas turbine power plant to produce and utilize solar syngas.
• Kong and Holber (2010) have studied an application for simultaneous generation of electricity and water
desalination in the southwestern part of the United States, which is a desert area and has abundant solar
irradiance, but a lack of clean water supply.
Putting the previous into the context of this study, solar heat by itself would not be suitable to repower an
existing steam Rankine cycle due to these power plants normally being of great size. The operation of solar
heat is not stable enough, but also the storage would need to be of unrealistic size to be able to provide heat to
the steam cycle during days that have less solar irradiance.
However, solar heat can also be used as auxiliary heat to a process, meaning that it could be used in combination
with a dispatchable technology such as gas turbines or SOFC to repower a steam cycle. The advantage of this is
that the downside of the solar heat being undispatchable is mitigated as the dispatchable technology can always
cover the heat demand of the steam cycle if there was to be no solar heat available, which in consequence makes
the storage no longer necessary. Furthermore, the solar heat could allow for savings in fuel consumption and
lowering the CO2 emissions of the plant. This study takes on the task of analyzing how a combination of SOFC
and solar heat would perform when used together to repower an existing steam cycle.
1.4
Kyndbyværket
Kyndbyværket was chosen as the existing steam Rankine cycle power plant that is to be modelled, modiﬁed
and analyzed.
Located near Kyndby in the island of Sealand in Denmark, this plant was ﬁrst commissioned in 1940 under the
name of Isefjordværket. Two years later its name was changed to the current one. Originally, the plant was
built to cover base loads, but as time went on its role in the Sealand power grid changed to be an intermediate
and peak load generator, and eventually to a death start plant for the power grid, which is its current role today.
The plant has seen its capacity change throughout its lifetime, and as of today, the plant can produce 734
MW through a combination of steam and gas turbines, as well as a diesel engine for emergency start up. A

Methodology
This section focuses on presenting the various methods used during this study to generate results and opportu-
nity for analysis, but also the inputs and setups for said calculations. The results and discussions section of this
study is composed of two main analysis, a thermal analysis and an economical analysis. The methods that were
used to carry out said analysis are presented in the next coming pages.
2.1
Dynamic Network Analysis modelling software
The thermal analysis carried out during this study is done using the software Dynamic Network Analysis
(DNA). It is an open source software that focuses on steady state and dynamic simulations of processes and
energy systems, it was created by the Department of Mechanical Engineering at DTU. The user creates, in the
form of text, an energy system using previously deﬁned components with governing equations that interact at
speciﬁc nodes, parameters and starting values set by the user. Elmegaard (2013)
By using the component library, the user can call up a speciﬁc preset component, input the necessary parameters
and connect it to the other components. Once the system is ready, DNA uses the preset mathematical equations
as well as mass and energy conservation equations to solve the system and get to know how it performs from a
thermodynamic perspective.

2.2.1
Compressor
A compressor is a turbo machine installed with the purpose of raising the pressure of a gas and transporting it
by transferring mechanical energy of a rotating shaft to the gas. Unlike pumps, compressors reduce the volume
of their ﬂuid since it is a gas. There are many types of compressors, which are normally categorized under two
main types: positive displacement and dynamic. The compressors most commonly used in industrial applica-
tions such as the ones analyzed in this study are axial compressors, which is a type of dynamic compressor.
Bullock (2003)
Axial compressors use fans to compress the ﬂuid in stages. As the gas progresses through the compressor, the
cross sectional area of the gas cavity is reduced, raising the density and therefore the pressure of the gas. Axial
compressors are notorious for their high efﬁciencies.

2.2.2
Condenser
Condensers are a heat transfer component used to change the phase of a gas into liquid state by retrieving
energy from it through cooling. In power plants, the exhaust super heated vapor from the turbines is inserted
into the hot side of the condenser, while water is introduced through the cold side, heat is transferred from the
super heated vapor to the cooling water, extracting the latent heat needed to change the state from super heated
vapor to saturated water. Palen (2006)
Condensers in power plant are typically shell and tube heat exchangers, in which there is no physical contact
between the two streams of ﬂuids. The cooling water runs through the tubes while the vapor is ﬂoating in the
shell, once the steam condensates on the outer side of the tubes, it drips down to the shell and it’s fed back to
continue in the loop.

equilibrium.

2.3
Power plant models setup
Two different power plant models were created in order to present, analyze and discuss the parameters that
inﬂuence the performance of the plant when performing repowering and implementing solar heat. Both plants
are made up by the original steam cycle installed at Kyndbyværket, a conventional HRSG with an additional
heat exchanger that provides auxiliary solar heat to the system, a solar ﬁeld and a topping SOFC cycle. The
difference between the two models is the operating fuel for the SOFC, one with natural gas and the other one
with ammonia. Consequentially, the components used in each one of the SOFC loops are different in order to
have proper operation for each of these cycles.
Figure 2.3 showcases a general arrangement diagram of the ﬁrst model. As it can be seen, it is the original
steam cycle of Kyndbyværket topped by an SOFC operated with natural gas. Between the economizer and
the evaporator, solar heat is delivered to the HRSG system. The numbers between each component interaction
references the nodes used in DNA. Similarly to the previous, the second model presents the exact same steam
cycle and solar heat implementation, the difference being the setup of the SOFC, which is modelled to operate
with ammonia. Figure 2.4 showcases the general arrangement diagram of this plant.
Figure 2.3: General arrangement diagram with natural gas SOFC

The key interaction in both of these systems happens in the HRSG, where the SOFC provides heat from the
burned off-air and off-fuel to the steam cycle, while the solar ﬁeld provides whatever heat it has available from
the solar irradiance. The purpose of both energy systems is to be able to meet the heat demand of the steam
cycle at any amount of solar heat provided by the solar ﬁeld, meaning that in a case where no solar irradiance is
available at the site, the topping SOFC cycle shall be sized accordingly that it would meet the steam cycle heat
demand by itself. The solar heat is set up to serve as an aid to the SOFC, as in the case of where there is solar
heat available, the SOFC will be allowed to provide less heat, which is expected to reduce the fuel consumption,
improve the thermal efﬁciency and reduce emissions due to the decreased use of fuel.

2.4
Sensitivity analysis
This study uses in several occasions sensitivity analysis to create variations of data and analyze the change in
another parameter.
The sensitivity analysis method used in this study is known as "one factor at a time method", where a single
parameter is varied across a range of values that make signiﬁcant impact to other parameters. This is done to
a single input parameter while ﬁxing the rest of the parameters to their nominal value. Bevinton and Robinson
(1992)
The advantage of this method is that it is simple to conduct and identify when the variation of parameter is
having impact, and once the variation is plotted into a graph, it is easy to see the trend of the changes.
This method does has its disadvantage, as the input parameters are only being varied one at a time, this method
does not take into count dependencies of output parameters to multiple inputs. One could make the argument
that once the results of multiple variations are combined and used to compute the model at once, the dependen-
cies take place, and that is true. However, the trend of the output parameters as multiple parameters vary is not
known, but only a single point in the realm of multiple variant parameters.
Never the less, sensitivity analysis by this method is a tool that is used in this study and is considered viable, as
several relevant inter-dependencies between parameters are found and analyzed.

2.5
Solar heat implementation and analysis
This section focuses on explaining the methods used to implement and analyze the heat that is provided by the
solar collector ﬁeld.
As mentioned in Section 2.3, the key interaction in both of the initially proposed power plants, as well as all
the plants proposed throughout this study, happens in the HRSG. This is due to the fact that this is where the
heat from the topping cycle is exchanged into the bottoming cycle, therefore making the repowering possible,
but also because this is where the solar heat is being used as well, which is the state-of-the-art of this study.
To understand how the solar heat is implemented, it is important to detail how this is happening speciﬁcally in
the HRSG. Figure 2.5 presents a stand alone image of the HRSG.
Figure 2.5: Heat revocery steam generator layout
Instead of having a conventional HRSG layout, an additional heat exchanger is added between the economizer
and the evaporator, the cold side of this new heat exchanger is the steam loop, while the hot side comes from
and to the solar collector ﬁeld. That position was chosen for several reasons:
To begin with, the temperature between nodes 95 and 86 is always 303 °C. This is the case because it is the
area where there is wet steam and the phase is changing from saturated liquid to saturated vapor at a pressure
of 90 bar, meaning that the temperature is constant all throughout this section of the loop. This is advantageous
for the solar ﬁeld, as it gives a consistent temperature to be above of.
Additionally, the evaporator was found to be the section in the HRSG where the most energy was being de-
manded by the steam cycle, amounting for 386.1 MJ/s, which was a larger amount than that in the combination
of both the economizer and the super heater. This means, that setting the solar ﬁeld to aid the evaporator would
not only be the easiest to model and implement, but also potentially the most effective one, as it’s the location
where most heat is needed.
By conditioning the steam quality at node 95 to be 0 and at node 86 to be 1, this ensured that the solar ﬁeld heat
exchanger outlet would always be at 313 (conditioning a pinch temperature of 10 °C) and that the solar heat
provided would always aid the evaporator.

implemented into DNA, which are listed in Table 2.34.

solar heat would be provided, and therefore the evaporator doesn’t need to provide as much heat, which causes
a chain reaction on a number of parameters which are analyzed in the results and discussion section.
Two additional remarks are the media used, as well as the effect of the solar heat in the thermal efﬁciency. The
media used for the solar collector loop is off-gas, but this is not relevant, as any media could have been used
to simulate the heat in the solar collector loop, what matters is the heat that is being provided, which is being
measured accordingly. In regards of the effect of the solar heat in the thermal efﬁciency, this is expected to
rise, because the solar heat provided by the solar ﬁeld is not accounted in the thermal efﬁciency equation, so
providing solar heat is expected to lower the fuel consumption while only affecting the net power production
slightly, meaning that there is an expected increase in thermal efﬁciency.

irradiance, diffuse solar irradiance, wind speed and air temperature, as they are the parameters that can interact
with a solar collector.

3.3.2
Analysis of real application of solar heat
With the insight gained in the previous section on how the plants behave when there is solar heat being imple-
mented into the system, this section now takes those lessons and creates a real life application for the energy
system if there was to be an actual installation of solar collectors that take solar irradiance and provide the heat
to the systems.
There is two important considerations to make. Firstly, solar energy is undispatchable, meaning that it can’t
be turned on or off, it’s simply there or it’s not, and whatever is available can be collected. Therefore, it is
important to consider the climate parameters that are present around Kyndbyværket and how they interact with
the solar collectors. Secondly, how the solar irradiance is going to be collected, or in other terms, which solar
collector and how many solar collectors are to be used to transform the solar irradiance into usable heat for the
process.

3.3.2.1
Climate data analysis
As stated in Section 2.5, a solar collector can interact with a set of climate parameters, these being: direct solar
irradiance, diffuse solar irradiance, ambient air temperature, surrounding air speed and the total solar irradiance.
These parameters are quite speciﬁc, and are available at dedicated climate stations where there is instrumen-
tation to measure and record them for research purposes, thankfully these institutions tend to make their data
available to everyone. The DTU section of solar heating in the department of civil engineering located in
Lyngby, Denmark counts with such installations, the data was retrieved from their website. DTUByg (2019)
Although it is not data of the site itself, it is accurate and reliable data that is close enough to the site so that the
climate data will be representative of the conditions that happen at the actual site. The data retrieved is in daily
time steps and covers a full year, from 01/12/2018 to 30/11/2019.
Upon execution of preliminary tests during the project using this data, it was found that the solar irradiance
available at Sealand is not ideal for the performance of this application. This prompted the introduction of a
second data set, which would be from a location where solar energy is intense and widely available.
The chosen location is Phoenix, which is the capital of the state of Arizona in the United States of America. This
location was chosen for a number of reasons. Firstly it is in a desert environment where the climate parameters
are great for solar thermal energy projects, also, it is a popular area for solar energy where some of the biggest
solar thermal plants have already been installed. Due to these previous points, the data is expected to be readily
available.
The data retrieved for this location was acquired from the National Renewable Energy Laboratory of the USA,
and was originally in time steps of 30 minutes, which was transformed to daily time steps using averages
in order to match the data from Lyngby. This data also covers a full year, but it ranges from 01/01/2018 to
31/12/2018. NREL (2018)

3.3.2.2
Solar collector analysis
To provide the solar heat a suitable solar collector must be chosen. The conditions of the system demand
that the outlet temperature of the heat exchanger that is providing the solar heat to the HRSG (Node 45 in
Figures 2.3 and 2.4) must be 313 °C, as the steam is conditioned to exit said heat exchanger at 303 °C and
the pinch temperature in the component is conditioned to stay at 10 °C. With these considerations, the inlet
temperature from the solar heat loop was set at 400 °C, this to accommodate for proper heat exchange and meet
the previously mentioned conditions.
The type of solar collector was decided to be ﬁxed to a parabolic trough collector, as these are widely used in

3.4.1
Discussion on framework changes
While the previous part of the section gave insight for how the plants would perform economically in the
current regulatory and economical framework, it is important to discuss the changes in the setting that could
signiﬁcantly impact the results retrieved. This subsection focuses on undertaking this discussion, the parameters
that are considered to be of inﬂuence and are bound to change in the coming years are: fuel prices, CO2 emission
taxes, subsidies for clean energy source projects.
3.4.1.1
Fuel prices
Fuel prices tend to be quite ﬂuctuating, causing for the need of constant re-assessment of economical feasibility
and analysis studies. As for expectations for the future of natural gas, previous studies have concluded that the
forecasting of natural gas prices have been conservative in the past, and it has created an unfair advantage for
gas-ﬁred generation over other technologies such as renewable energy generation. Bolinger et al. (2006)
==PAGE:65==
3.4 Economical analysis
55
Speciﬁcally in this study, the price of natural gas was set at a conservative value compared to the historical data
of the last 5 years. However, prices have been much higher before, and a spike of this nature would result in
making the natural gas plants more costly to operate and therefore less attractive economically.
Ammonia price in this study, on the other hand, was set at an optimistic price, as the value stated in the study is
one of the lower prices seen for the fuel in recent years. However, as seen in multiple articles cited across this
study, there is a trend in the energy sector of academia towards hydrogen-based fuels. If in the future ammonia
demand rises and the production is able to be met, the price of ammonia could see itself signiﬁcantly reduced.
This would be outstandingly beneﬁcial to the plants in this study using this fuel, as without a doubt, the biggest
drawback for these were the high operational costs.
Lastly, it is important to mention that while the economical study resulted that installing solar collector is less
economically attractive, having a higher ratio of heat provided by a solar ﬁeld would mitigate the risks of the
variation of the fuel prices, as fuel consumption is considerably reduced when solar collectors are used.
3.4.1.2
Regulatory changes in the framework
With the current trend of aiming to achieve a more sustainable world by the younger generations, stake holding
organizations such as the UN and academia in general, regulatory instruments are being studied, assessed and
proposed to achieve the mentioned goal. One of these instruments is CO2 emission taxes for the industrial
sector. It’s considered a viable measure to signiﬁcantly see this parameter reduced. Mardones and Flores
(2018)
Another potential regulation change that would impact this study directly is changes in subsidies for project
in the energy sector, particularly those for renewable generation. If subsidies were to increase for these, the
economical framework could see itself change for this study in a way that would allow for the installation of
solar collectors to be economically competitive in this particular application.
Overall, these are parameters are to be considered as they change signiﬁcantly, and the study should be re-
assessed to understand how this application would perform in the new framework.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
This master thesis investigated the thermal and economical performance of a repowering application in an
existing Rankine cycle power plant called Kyndbyværket, using SOFC powered by natural gas or ammonia as
the topping cycle, combined with a ﬁeld of parabolic trough solar collectors that provide auxiliary heat to the
evaporator of the heat recovery steam generator. This was done with the purpose of investigating a different
approach of solar heat implementation to electricity generation in order to increase thermal efﬁciency, lower
fuel consumption and propose a power plant that is a better incentive for investment while also being better for
the environment.
The study found several conclusions. Firstly, it determined that improvements in the SOFC component param-
eters, such as utilization factor, operating temperature, number of stacks and number of cells in stack, are able
to improve the efﬁciency of the plant signiﬁcantly, increasing the thermal efﬁciency of the natural gas SOFC
repowered plant from 54.1% to and 55.4%, and the one with ammonia from 60.9% to 62.0%.
It also determined that there is a common limit in both plants in regards to the amount of auxiliary solar heat
that can be provided to the evaporator in the HRSG, as any amount above 194.3 MJ/s caused the conditions of
the models to be violated. With this in mind, the study found that as provided solar heat increased, net power
production, total power consumption and fuel consumption all decreased. This last parameter being of great
importance for this study, as it proved that signiﬁcant savings in fuel could be made, achieving lower operating
costs and CO2 emissions.
Furthermore, it determined that thermal efﬁciency was signiﬁcantly increased as solar heat was implemented,
meaning that the implementation is worthwhile from a purely thermal stance. In an optimal scenario of all the
possible solar heat being provided, the natural gas and ammonia SOFC plants saw thermal efﬁciencies of 62.8%
and 68.2%, and reduced both fuel consumption to 1,150 MJ/s and 1,326 MJ/s, and net power production to 723
MW and 905 MW respectively.
Moreover, the study concluded that the site of Kyndbyværket is not optimal for installation of solar energy
projects and that the application would be beneﬁted if it was to be carried out in a site with better climate
conditions for solar energy. When considering different solar collectors, it was found that different sites can be
more optimal than others for the solar collectors, as the Lyngby site would use the Taars solar collector the best,
showcasing an annual solar heat per square meter of 711 kWh/m2, while the conditions in Phoenix were best
for the Baidaling collector which had an annual solar heat per square meter of 2,077 kWh/m2.
When the installed areas were determined and varied, and the 16 variations of the power plants were simulated
in a real case scenario throughout a year, it was that the plants perform thermally close to their optimal scenarios
when they have a proper solar collector installation, with the numbers for the natural gas and ammonia SOFC
plants being thermal efﬁciencies of 61.0% and 66.7%, fuel consumptions of 1,236 MJ/s and 1,420 MJ/s, and
net power production of 824 MW and 1040MW respectively.
It was determined that the best performing repowering option in this study for Kyndbyværket is an ammonia
SOFC topping cycle, with the climate conditions of Phoenix and 738,255 square meters of installed area of
Baidaling solar collectors.
From the economical point of view, it was determined that every single variation of the proposed systems is
economically feasible, but that the one with a natural gas SOFC and no solar collectors installed is the most
attractive from a pure investment point of view, as it has a pay-back time of 3.52 years, a yearly net cashﬂow of
682 MUSD and a net present value after 20 years of 7,066 MUSD.
It was determined that the other technologies were held back due to the ammonia prices being too high to
compete with that of natural gas and that the reduced fuel consumption using solar collectors didn’t justify the
combined losses in proﬁts due to capital costs and a lower net power production. This, however, may be subject
==PAGE:68==

to change as the economical and regulatory framework see themselves changed, particularly through fuel prices
and further regulation around CO2 emissions and clean energy sources.
Overall, this study showed that the implementation of solar heat to the larger electricity generation processes is
thermally and economically viable, and it leaves space for further investigations to be made as the economy and
regulations change, but also as technological advances are made in the upcoming energy system components.
