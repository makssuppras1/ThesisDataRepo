Abstract
Increasing environmental awareness combined with the growth in both share and demand of greener
renewable energy sources, has created an energy transition across Europe. Europe still depends heavily on
non-renewable energy sources, with the latest 2017 Eurostat report publishing that only 17.5% of energy
currently comes from renewable sources, and that the EU is not on track to meet agreed 20-20-20 targets.
Energy needs and solutions vary within segments and societies across Europe, and the challenge is to
develop enough radical and innovative green technologies (CleanTech) to meet all these disparate needs, as
it is unlike they will be solved by one silver bullet solution. It is argued that start-up incubators, accelerators
and living labs (IALL’s) can contribute to solving this challenge, by heavily reducing the commercialisation
timeline of radical and socially transformative technologies through ’co-creation’, the integration of end-
users, academic networks, the private sector and the public sector into product development processes. To
assess how IALL’s are currently operating across Europe, a comparative analysis study of several IALL’s
from across The Netherlands and Denmark was performed. Currently, IALL’s tend to oﬀer value ’co-
creation’ services, involving selections of these aforementioned stakeholder groups within their innovation
processes and programs. Within this process however, IALL’s often focus too heavily on end-users as
sales targets and validation processes, rather than as important stakeholders; ultimately prohibiting societal
understanding, acceptance and success of these products and their underlying technologies. Additionally,
whilst there are no standardised frameworks stipulating how IALL’s should be setup and managed, each
IALL sampled contained unique components that were either beneﬁcial or detrimental to the start-ups
and they are compiled within the study below. Finally, IALL’s that exhibited greater tendencies towards
involving end-users and external collaborations within their ’co-creation’ based activities and setups were
found to create more value across fundamental user, organisation, ecosystem and societal levels, lending
them towards more successful development and steps towards helping solve Europe’s energy transition.
IALL’s face the challenge to ﬁnd the right program structure that will allow CleanTech startups to succeed
and ’take oﬀ’, ﬁlling the void left behind by the energy transition away from fossil fuel dependency, or
else the transition may occur too late.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The installation of Blue Batteries into the local Delft micro-grid recently allowed homes and oﬃces to
store ﬂuctuating energy generated by renewable solar and wind sources across the region. The installation
of this new and innovative product minimises energy losses and interruptions whilst allowing homes and
oﬃces to operate oﬀgrid as prosumers; simultaneously solving multiple problems and creating value for
multiple stakeholders. Aquabattery, the start-up who developed the system, did so from within the walls
of The Green Village (TGV), a leading Dutch think-tank and living lab dedicated to the development of
green and cleaner technologies (CleanTech). The TGV, a self described ’living lab’, joins an number
of institutions such as ’incubators’ and ’accelerators’ (IALL’s) that aim to incubate business and product
innovations; and is a fast growing community across Europe, with over 150 living labs currently registered
to the European Network of Living Labs (ENoLL). CleanTech companies are interchangeably known as
high-tech and deep-tech companies, as they are based upon signiﬁcant and substantial technological
advances and engineering innovation, however for the purposes of this paper they will be referred to as
CleanTech; and they are at the forefront trying to solve Europe’s energy transition (EET), the movement
away from fossil fuel dependency.
For most CleanTech start-ups within the TGV portfolio, their focus is research, development, implemen-
tation and commercialisation of clean energy technologies that will assist the EET. Unlike information
communications technology (ICT) or biotech innovations, developing transformational global solutions
that address water puriﬁcation, water scarcity, carbon emissions, energy storage and waste reduction usu-
ally requires input and support from a wide variety of sectors, stakeholders and organisations; "While the
ideas may be inspiring, the development and implementation of these innovations are extremely complex..
It is this unique complexity that is preventing many unique and possibly life-changing technologies from
ever reaching the market" [8].
In the context of EET, a prerequisite for large-scale societal transformation is that future products and
their embedded technologies are fully understood, designed-around, accepted and demanded by those who
will use it; “We now understand that the technology involved in building cleaner solutions is a lot more
complicated because it relies on combinations of hardware, product design and product development,” [9].
This implies that in order to develop sustainable energy transition products and technologies, knowledge-
making must go beyond scientists: “in a world put at risk by the unintended consequences of scientiﬁc
progress, participatory procedures involving scientists, stakeholders, advocates, active citizens, and users
of knowledge are critically needed” [10]. The theory and practices of co-creation, which aims to include
end-users and other stakeholders within product and technology development processes are increasingly
adopted by IALL’s, in an eﬀort to accelerate this process towards.

In the private sector, corporations are consistently challenged to manufacture their goods more eﬃciently
and end-users are deﬁned as possible co-creators who can take over speciﬁc activities ... as they contain
unique sources of product and service innovation knowledge gained from their product experiences that
can add value to the companies they collaborate with. [11] [12] [13] Further research into this interaction,
speciﬁcally within strong consumer markets, found that co-creation not only helps to build loyalty and
satisfaction amongst customers but also helps to build a competitive advantage over competitors. [14]
In the public sector however, end-users are citizens and according to the European Commission [15], ‘social
innovation mobilizes each citizen to become an active part of the innovation process’. Furthermore, if
citizen participation is considered as a necessary condition for social innovation in the public sector,
it is important that we have systematic knowledge regarding the conditions under which citizens are
prepared to embark on the ‘social innovation journey’ [16]; meaning that proper investigation must be
conducted regarding the impetus of end-users to change their behaviour, ultimately contributing to social
transformation and the shunning of energy practices that are harmful to society.
IALL’s are the latest trend of institutions oﬀering co-creation based environments, merging technologists
and engineers with end-users and other key societal stakeholders; with the single purpose of commer-
cialising and developing products and technologies faster than they would be able to on their own. The
traditional timeline for technology commercialisation is approximately 45 years, highlighting that the
products and subsequent technologies that our society is relying on to solve the EET challenge are still in
their infancy or have already been in development for decades [5]. Once these technologies have proven
their applicability and have legitimised a category of applications, the widespread adoption by incumbents
and ’fast followers’ will ultimately lead to a greener and more sustainable energy future [17].
IALL’s claim to use co-creation practices, processes and environments as the backbone of their oﬀerings,
involving many diﬀerent stakeholders and networks within their innovation processes, with the intention
of creating the most appropriate market-ready product in a heavily reduced time-frame. It is unclear
however, how much end-user driven design is actually taking place within these co-creative processes; as
"end-user validation will always occur, even if it’s not really there" [18].
This leads to the following research question:
To what extent are incubators, accelerators and living labs appropriately implementing ’co-creation’
within their innovation and technology incubation processes?
This research question can be divided into three sub-questions:
1. Do accelerators, incubators and living labs facilitate collaboration with innovative start-ups and
end-users as well as other key stakeholders through their innovation processes?
2. Is the ’input’ from accelerators and incubators value-adding to the future energy transition innovations?
3. Are innovative and radical technology start-ups centering their value propositions around end-users?

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter describes the concept of transformational and collaborative innovation and the entities who
deploy these techniques to develop products, with a speciﬁc focus on user-led innovation and technology
demand-pull. These elements combined with the value framework provide the foundation for this paper.
2.1
Deﬁning incubation and its providers
The incubation of businesses with regards to new and innovative product development is not a new concept.
The concept is very well established, however interpretations as to what constitutes business incubation
vary; with the UK Business Incubation association describing incubation as "a unique and highly ﬂexible
combination of business development processes, infrastructure and people, designed to nurture and grow
new and small businesses by supporting them through the early stages of development and change" [19].
Alternative deﬁnitions exist that describe business incubation as a collection of techniques that can be used
to "prove an idea, develop a team and de–risk ventures for later–stage investors”. [19] For the purposes
of this study, ’incubation’ will be deﬁned as per the UK Business incubation association; and the ’highly
ﬂexible combination of business development processes, infrastructure and people’ will be assessed across
three diﬀerent vehicles spanning ﬁve case studies, all who claim to oﬀer these services. [20].
2.1.1
CleanTech start-ups
Companies or ﬁrms that are young and innovative, and who are aiming to develop products that utilise
untested or unproven technologies and business models are often considered ’start-ups’. Start-ups can be
categorised into pre- start-up, start-up (validation), early and later stages, each representing a particular
level of growth, revenue and uncertainty, therefore requiring unique contributions and levels of support for
further development. [20] Clean technologies (CleanTech) are classiﬁed as any process, product, or service
that aims to reduce harmful impact on the environment whilst promoting sustainability, energy eﬃciency
and environmental protection, and can include technologies such as renewable energy, recycling, and
information technology. [21] For the purposes of this study, the term CleanTech start-up, start-up, and
CleanTech will be used interchangeably and the start-ups investigated within this study will span the four
aforementioned stages of development, each developing innovative products committed towards solving
Europe’s energy transition challenge.

2.1.2
Incubators
Innovation incubators assist technology startups with longer-term business development, providing the
necessary time and resources required to design and build successful self-sustaining business models.
Incubators often partner with start-ups at the pre-start-up or start-up stage and typically oﬀer physical
spaces, access to industry and investor networks as well as access to specialist facilities and equipment for
prototyping development. A well-deﬁned example of this is seen within biotechnology based incubators,
which oﬀer facilities such as wet-laboratories for the safe handling of and experimentation with chemical
or biological materials.
Without these oﬀered facilities, biotech start-ups would struggle to ﬁnd the
necessary infrastructure form which to continue developing from.
Incubators are often attached to universities or research science parks so they can support university IP
and independent student research projects, and do not usually take equity stakes within the start-ups they
are incubating, often charging rent instead [20] [22]. All 3 incubators investigated within this study were
attached to their immediate university and the rent charged by one of the incubators was approximately
281 Euros per square meter yearly.
Whilst incubators don’t invest as much time in establishing pilot projects or shaping the startups to receive
external investment as accelerators do, incubators provide a signiﬁcant amount of capital investment,
aimed at ensuring the technology is product/prototype ready with clear concepts that can be exploited
through partnership agreements. [23]
Figure 2.1.1: Diﬀerence Between Incubators and Accelerators
For the purposes of this study, incubators are deﬁned by the following characteristics:
1. Open-ended duration based on the particular development stage of the start-up (typically between
1-2 years)
2. Rent based
3. Prioritises physical spaces, technical facilities and oﬃces over services oﬀered
4. Rolling admissions based on an availability on an ad-hock basis
5. Co-creative services oﬀered including mentorship, entrepreneurial training, workshops
6. Selective admission with moderate acceptance rates

Virtual incubators, ones that focus entirely on delivering mentoring sessions, workshops and access to
investor and industry networks online, and who don’t need physical infrastructure or in-person face-time

2.1.3
Accelerators
"Accelerators have become an umbrella term for any program providing a service structure of mentorship,
networking opportunities and access to funding" [25], however the goal of accelerators is to help achieve a
level of business growth within these start-ups in just a few months, which outside in the regular business
environment could take upwards of 2 years. Accelerators are a more recent phenomenon than incubators,
who oﬀer their services to cohorts of start-ups for roughly six months to a year, providing the tools and
guidance on topics such as market testing, business plan development, prototyping and pitching; aiming to
rapidly scale up start-ups, strengthen their product oﬀerings and to prepare them to have the best possible
chance at securing external funding or pilot locations. Accelerators most commonly oﬀer seed funding in
return for their services however with the advent of corporate accelerators such as General Electric and
their ecomagination internal innovator network, incubative services for start-ups can also be sponsored or
subsidised.
The number of selected startups entering accelerators are a tiny fraction of startups applying, with
longstanding accelerators such as ’Y Combinator’ achieving an acceptance rate of 7 out of 1000 projects,
with even fewer start-ups reaching a commercialisation of 100 million units after 3 years [26]. Accelerators
work closely with potential corporate investors, aiming to match them with startup technologies that best
ﬁt the investor requirements. Through this method however, there is a signiﬁcant emphasis on facilitating
the bridge between partners and startups. Incubators, on the other hand, have looser aﬃliations with
corporate partners. [25]
For the purposes of this study citing prior research, accelerators will be deﬁned by the following charac-
teristics: [26]
1. Fixed-duration program usually less than a year
2. Growth-based with payment via equity
3. Provide seed funding
4. Focus on services rather than physical space or oﬃces
5. Admission in batches / cohorts
6. Co-creative services oﬀered including 1-1 mentorship, entrepreneurial training and workshops
7. Extremely selective with low entrance rates
2.1.4
Living Labs
Living Labs are user-centric communities and ecosystems who integrate research, development and
innovation processes within real life communities, situations and environments within an open innovation
setting. Living labs are based on a systematic user co-creation approach and are an increasingly popular
vehicle for research, development and the commercialisation of emerging energy transition technologies
across Europe.
Many European universities house variants of these innovation vehicles, attempting to further develop the
research, experimentation and innovation already started by their students, research groups and faculties.

Living labs are often used to ﬁeld-test products and technologies within the interactive and dynamic
localised real-life environments and physical communities of end-users, with the intention of actively
involving citizens and stakeholder groups directly within the co-creation processes [27].
Early archetypal [28] deﬁnitions describe the functions and purpose of living labs separately as either a
methodology; “a research methodology for sensing, prototyping, validating and reﬁning complex solutions
in multiple and evolving real life contexts” [29]; or as an environment that integrates end-user participation
within innovation processes “experimentation environment in which technology is given shape in real life
contexts and in which end-users are considered ‘co-producers’” [30] [31].
Modern interpretations have combined both the methodological and environmental archetypes together,
which has encouraged a broader use of term; where it is also described as part of an innovation system;
"human-technology interaction innovation entity utilizing a mix of methods, tools and principles drawn
from known disciplines (design, science, information and communications technology etc) and set in a
real environment and on a local/societal scale”. [32] [33]
For the purposes of this study, the combination of both of the aforementioned archetypes will be considered
together as a living lab [34] [28], which is deﬁned by the following characteristics: [29]
1. User centric
2. Practice-driven open innovation ecosystems in real life settings
3. Intermediaries between citizens, research organisations, companies, cities and regions
4. Focuses on joint value co-creation, rapid prototyping, or validation to scale up innovation
2.2
Transformational Innovation
Finding ’clean’ replacements for fossil fuels to solve the EET challenge will require a more complex
form of innovation, and will have to move beyond simply inventing products that sell. Transformative
innovations will have to change the behaviour of as many people as possible in order to force paradigm
shifts; as the shift will not occur if only a few change their behaviour; "if everyone does a little, we’ll
achieve only a little" [35]. Instead, product innovations will have to create multiple value propositions
for users, organisations within the ecosystem, and the broader society itself to ensure paradigm shifts
occur. [4]
Product innovations capable of addressing the EET challenge will need to create sustained and long
term value through extended use, and this is only achieved if customers are challenged and persuaded to
changed their current behaviour in place due to consistent and positive experiences with new products.
Furthermore, transformational innovations require a highly integrated network of partners and often
operate within a complex system alongside necessary and complementary related products and services.
In order to successfully operate within this complex framework, transformational innovations are often
co-created between stakeholders and actors within this complex system, ensuring mutual exchanges of
value are congruent with the objectives and relationships within the network. [4]
Transformational innovations are usually riskier than traditional incremental, disruptive or architectural
innovations because both the market and technology is new; and as a result, it is unknown whether
users will accept the transformational innovation, unknown whether a viable ecosystem can both support
and sustain this new product and unknown whether an appropriate business model can be developed to

2.2.1
Co-creation
The co-creation methodology aims to involve relevant stakeholders and end-users within innovation pro-
cesses, with the aim of ensuring that the product is creating value at multiple levels for many stakeholders;
a concept aforementioned as transformational above. There are many varying deﬁnitions and concepts of
co-creation, ranging between the entire process and the end result [37] as seen below in table 2.2.3.
Whilst many interpretations of co-creation exist, there are two major bodies of literature on the topic; the
ﬁrst identiﬁes the whole innovation process as a series of co-creation, proposing that the whole innovation
process could be categorized into 5 diﬀerent co-creation activities, co-ideation, -evaluation, -design, -test,
and -launch [38]. Meanwhile the second views co-creation as a means towards creating richer innovation
and value creation.
An essential issue in deﬁning co-creation is the level of end-user and customer
participation within the production process as in most cases, “the ﬁrm is still in charge of the overall
orchestration of the experience” [37].
For the purposes of this paper, the deﬁnition proposed by Ramaswamy & Gouillart, "Co-creation is about
putting the human experience at the center of enterprise design" [39] will be adopted as is supports the
notion of transformational innovations and the necessity for end-users to be at the core of the value creation
network. The evidence of co-creation within IALL’s will be assessed as to whether they are focusing on
the end-users within their innovation practices and whether this is creating multiple levels of value for all
of the integrated stakeholders.

2.3
The Valley of Death
Transformative innovations are deemed unsuccessful when end-users do not properly adopt or use the
aforemention new and innovative products over time, and bodies of literature often refer to the period when
this occurs as the ’Valley of Death’ (VOD). The VOD is the period during the innovation process where
CleanTech innovations fail to create ’demand pull’ from within consumer market, leading to unsustainable
sales and often commercial ’death’. This outcome is often attributed to the research and development
intensive ’pre-commercialisation’ period, with recent literature attributing this phenomenon towards the
lack of inclusion of end-users.
The commercialisation process is described as a long and very complex one [50] [5] as seen below in
ﬁgure 2.3.1, that usually occurs during the convergence of technological breakthroughs and attractive
market opportunities [51] [52]. Beyond this convergence, the commercialisation process is continued and
reﬁned through design, development, manufacturing and marketing; all with the intention to improve the
product quality and ensure it is ﬁt for market. [51] [52]
Figure 2.3.1: Empirical duration of the valley of death [5]
Studies conducted on innovation processes largely agree that successful commercialisation processes
require several key features; ﬁrstly there must be customer involvement at an early stage within the de-
velopment of these technologies; secondly there must be a strong understanding of the market and the
customer needs and lastly that products must be launched into attractive markets [51] [53]. CleanTech
start-ups however often confront challenges that signiﬁcantly impede their ability to address the afore-
mentioned key factors, such as a lack of key resources, lack of market understanding/knowledge and poor
market timing; which all coalesce creating signiﬁcant risks towards falling into the VOD. IALL’s are
viewed as a mechanism from which to bridge the VOD; as these innovation incubators oﬀer the tools,
expertise, networks and access that engineers and technologists would not otherwise have at their disposal.
Consequently, for the purposes of this paper, CleanTech case studies will be selected from varying IALL’s
across Denmark and the Netherlands.

2.3.2
An Empirical Perspective
A recent study of technology ’push’ start-ups found that technology ’creators’, companies who pioneer
new technologies are the segment most prone to pre-commercialisation company failure; with technol-
ogy ’anticipators’ and ’followers’ receiving higher survival rates. [5] The study analysed 30 innovative
industrial and energy product ’creators’ introduced to the United States between 1849-1983, and found
that 27% of product innovations failed before ﬁrm take-oﬀ, with a further 39% dropping out between ﬁrm
takeoﬀand sales takeoﬀ, and further 33% dropping out of the market even after sales had taken oﬀ. Whilst
product innovation ’creators’ suﬀered the highest dropout rates, with only 11% remaining in the market
after ’sales takeoﬀ’, companies who were designated as ’followers’ and ’anticipators’ were found to have
lower 5-year survival rates; indicating that surviving CleanTech companies who brought new technologies
to market were very market resilient to new entrants. [5]
Figure 2.3.3: Product Innovation Failure Rates [5]
Whilst CleanTech start-ups and scale-ups have been identiﬁed as the solution to Europe’s energy transition,
published success rates of CleanTech start-ups are low. A signiﬁcant number CleanTech start-ups have
died or gone unfunded, even if their technology showed promise and it is an underlying hypothesis of
this paper that IALL’s and their services, which have been identiﬁed as the drivers for developing these
EET solutions, are contributing to this high rate of failure, simply because they are not implementing
co-creation processes appropriately and are not eﬀectively involving end-users within their incubation
processes. [5]
The poor performance of CleanTech start-ups within the energy sector compared to those of traditional
software and technology startups have also been investigated, and several recurring themes were identiﬁed.
[56] Firstly, deep tech start-ups require longer commercialisation timelines and larger capital investment
requirements than are normally expected by venture capital funds, which is normally between 3-5 years
and around $100,000 USD. Secondly, deep tech start ups are more expensive to scale-up than regular
technology peers as they require large factories and laboratories before products can even be ﬁnalised
or produced. Thirdly, deep tech energy start ups are geared towards energy and commodity markets
which are highly competitive and return low margins, reducing the ability to (re)invest in research and
development. Lastly, deep tech energy start ups lag incumbent companies who dominate the industry
landscape who are able to take larger risks and often acquire start-ups popping up in their ﬁelds [56].
Whilst deep technology start ups suﬀer from more complex issues than traditional software technology
start-ups or biotechs, there are many IALL’s across Europe who are trying to to apply diﬀerent processes
at varying stages of the pre-commercialisation period in order to avoid failure and higher drop out rates,
and this study is expect to uncover some of these augmented practices.

skills to gain access and build trust, ﬁrm judgment to navigate and make sense of large quantities of data,
data analysis skills, writing skills, story telling skills, the ability to identify and incorporate convergent and
divergent evidence as well as the ability to embrace the complexities of each case. In order to ensure the
skills required are met and to avoid a poor synthesis, a small cluster of 5 case studies have been selected,
as this is manageable within the time, budget and resources available; as ﬁndings can lose reliability (via
historical, social, programmatic factors) if too much time has passed between cases because of the extent
of the research and ﬁeldwork.
3.2
Data Analysis
Interviews between IALL’s and start-ups were conducted within 1-2 weeks of each other and responses
as well as observations were compared during the conclusion of each pair of interviews. Conﬁdence and
reliability of the ﬁndings were enhanced when there was a convergence and corroboration of opinions
from the various interviewees; whilst divergent responses and conﬂicting views often led to deviation
and delays within the study. Multiple representatives from each case study pair were interviewed so as
to allow the case to be observed and viewed from diﬀerent perspectives. A multi-case method, using a
single embedded design framework was used to investigate, describe and explore the key themes within
the obtained data. The multi-case method approach [70] [62] [69] [64] was used for the purposes of this
paper as it allows for the ability to recognise and diagnose occurring phenomena beyond each IALL’s
context, helping the increase the generalizability and develop a ’common model’ behind the observations.
Each individual interview and interview responses (data) were recorded, reduced and analysed, and was
used to investigate and uncover common themes from within the data. Each individual interview analysis

4.1
Focus on co-creation
4.1.1
The challenge of incubation duration
From the aforementioned co-creation deﬁnitions, it appears that anyone could hire industry and business
consultants, deploy well documented innovation and product development processes and provide start-of-
the-art facilities; and claim that co-creation services are being oﬀered; see Kirah (2009), Vargo (2008)
and Ind & Coates (2013) in ﬁgure 2.2.3. This study however, highlights that whilst these broad deﬁnitions
of ’co-creation’ are being satisﬁed by the activities of IALL’s, they are omitting elements found within
the majority of other deﬁnitions such as ’users’, ’customers’, ’interaction’ and ’continual feedback’; that
are critical to developing truly innovative products and deploying co-creative practices. Analysis across
the IALL’s within this study found that the length of ’incubation’ time oﬀered to CleanTech start-up was
deterministic in both the way co-creation services are oﬀered but also regarding the eﬀectiveness of how
those services are adopted and practices by the start-ups.
The accelerator investigated within case study 1, section 7, oﬀered 12-16 week programs tailored towards
product development for companies working with hardware, and for the purpose of this case study, within
the energy industry. The 16 week period included 1-1 sparring sessions with dedicated product, business
and industry coaches who are meant to assist start-ups in addressing product-market ﬁt, commercialisation
and the scaling up of innovative products. This short length of exposure to co-creative professionals and
industry peers, which amounted to approximately 16-20 meetings in total, combined with the competition
based format meant that important tasks and exercises relating to [your] ’customer’, ’value proposition’,
’market’ and ’value network’, were only the focus for one week; as the next week would address other
topics.
Companies who did not ’pass’ or meet the weekly criteria for that particular session were
encouraged to revise their work in order to proceed through the competition and end-user related or
collaborative activities that would have otherwise taken weeks or months were treated as a ’homework for
the day’. It was clear that the brief length of time spent on critical issues, compounded by the ’structured’
competition format meant that fundamental aspects regarding end-users and value perspectives such as
identifying potential customers, alternative uses of technology or elaborating on user problems being
solved; remained under-developed, and this was reﬂected within the resulting value framework as seen
in ﬁgure 7.0.1. The resulting value framework clearly does not address any societal value perspectives
and only has a few value propositions targeting sociological and ecological perspectives; which are all
fundamental to transformational innovation development, especially within the energy transition.
The incubators investigated within case studies 2, 3 and 4 however, sections 7, 7 and 7 respectively,
highlighted that as the incubation period was extended from 3 months to over 1 year and without a
competition format, the ability to identify and redress areas for development as well as ﬁll knowledge gaps
was better enabled. Combined with availability of rich industry and investor networks, open-innovation
environments and prototyping facilities, greater lengths of time at incubators clearly stimulated greater
levels of collaboration amongst peers, mentors, students, researchers and the industry; who are all rich
sources of continual feedback, unique insights and whom posses large quantities of data about their
customer base. Simply by being able to spend more time deﬁning, researching and addressing each
issue, rather than within one session as per the accelerator, start-ups are better poised to take advantage
of the resources available to them and drill down into issues more heavily within the incubators. This
is best illustrated by the resulting value framework of case studies 3 and 4 as seen in ﬁgures 7.0.3 and

further aﬃrmed by responses highlighting that "over time people began to walk into our oﬃce and ask

from a theory of a new technology to a pilot location and that connection came from the longstanding

Finally, the living lab investigated within case study 5, section 7, aﬃrmed the trend that greater quantities of
time within ’incubative’ environments is conducive to greater levels of product development collaborations

4.2
Focus on technology
4.2.1
The challenge of technology that is too complex
This study has illustrated that the deployment of radical, complex and new technologies within transfor-
mative product innovations promotes both IALL’s and CleanTech start-ups to overlook and often avoid
end-user involvement and collaboration. Whilst it is important to develop technologies for product pro-
totypes and proof of concept purposes, the development of complex technologies is drawing too much
focus from start-ups, who are tending to spend the majority of their time focusing on the technology
itself, rather than focusing on the value the product is intended to create and how it will be used; thereby
ignoring end-user involvement. Whilst this was not experienced within case study 1, as the start-up had
already developed its technology, it was a signiﬁcant challenge experienced by case studies 2 and 3,
both open-innovation incubators, as described in sections 7 and 7, where end-users were simply used for
feedback purposes only. Whilst feedback is beneﬁcial if conducted properly, especially within minimum
viable product processes, feed-forward processes that integrate end-user parameters early on and directly
into the design process help to quicken product innovation timelines, create products with stronger user-
centric focus’ and deliver more value for stakeholders involved. Workshop material obtained from case
study 2 illustrated how end-users are only introduced for prototyping feedback twice during one of their
sprint programs, at stage 21 and 28 of 28 respectively; with the stage 21 text reading "Make your solution
ready for feedback... two main ways of conducting a prototype..... to create proof of feasibility and gain
real used feedback", see appendix 7. Furthermore, it should be noted that the introduction of end-user
feedback within the program material was at the last two stages of the spring (improve and inﬁltrate),
rather than within the ﬁrst three stages of introduction, inception and ideate. Whilst feedback for design
purposes is positive, case study 2 demonstrates that the development of transformational innovations is
still technologically centered rather than end-user centric; which is predictable given that the majority
of EET innovations are technology push innovations, see section 2.2.4. Case studies 4 and 5 in sections
7 and 7 however, demonstrated that the inclusion of signiﬁcant levels of end-user input, was achievable
Page 27 of 72
==PAGE:32==

August 11th, 2019
even though complex technologies were involved; with the use of direct current power transformation and
micro-grid solar electronics respectively. Analysis of the two cases attribute this towards the fact that
the respective IALL’s oﬀered regular and tailored in-depth 1-1 coaching regarding their innovations, as
opposed to the hands-oﬀ’open door’ policy experienced by case studies 2 and 3. Whilst complex tech-
nologies are often fundamental to transformative innovations, there are risks that those those technologies
often receive the largest focus, drawing focus and priority away from key user-perspectives, often to the
detriment of end-user led innovation. Furthermore, the incubation of start-ups that are utilising complex
technologies requires greater awareness from IALL’s and possibly diﬀerent practices, as it is evidently
easy to apply to a ’hands-oﬀ’ approach simply because the technology (and therefore the innovation) is
underdeveloped.
4.3
Focus on sales
4.3.1
The challenge of sales validation
Another key concept identiﬁed by the study was the contextualisation of end-users by IALL’s simply
as sales targets, who’s only function is as a customer. Whilst end-users and customers are important
and feature heavily within co-creative deﬁnitions and practices, see 2.2.1, they can oﬀer greater product
innovation insights beyond identifying whether they would simple purchase a product. As identiﬁed by
case studies 1 and 4 in sections 7 and 7 respectively, end-users were only involved for sales validation
purposes, whereby the start-ups were encouraged to "show an invoice proving that customers would
actually buy the product before it actually being developed" [72], and "[teams] pitch out to a room of
potential customers to prove customers want it" [73]. Case study 1 highlighted this theme most heavily,
with the CleanTech start-up clearly developing the product from a sales perspective, spending signiﬁcant
resources on developing lengthy case studies aimed at convincing potential customers, explaining how
much heat their technology could produce, how many dollars it would save and how much more eﬀective
it would be than existing solutions. By focusing predominantly on selling and focusing on terms such as
’savings’, ’costs’ and ’eﬃciency’, the CleanTech seemingly ignored information that is equally, if not more
important to the customer, regarding implementation, installation and integration. When probed as to why
the company, after 5 years, had only achieved one pilot location, it was clear that end-users and customers
were concerned as to the size of the land needed to created a 2MW facility, requiring over 10,000 square
metres; issues that could have been solved if it were aware of these concerns earlier. Furthermore, it was
found that the product itself was diﬃcult to integrate with existing heat generation services and would
require signiﬁcant collaboration with regional heat and energy infrastructure providers for connectivity
purposes. Similarly, by focusing so heavily on ’sales’, the company has engaged into a low-cost strategy,
"Solar generated at costs below any alternative anywhere" [72]; and does not appear to target any speciﬁc
end-user problem, except for "We aim to join the portfolio of energy solutions" [72]. The heavy focus on
selling and the subsequent omission of end-user problems is observed within the resulting value framework
as seen below in 7.0.1, which illustrates that very little value is being created for users and consequently
the broader society. Conversely, case study 5 highlighted that innovation processes within living labs
were less focused on sales and more focused on how end-user integration would lead to useful insights
and therefore better designed products resulting in widespread product adoption. Whilst case study 5
aimed to develop an entire battery and transformer unit capable of receiving and distributing renewable
DC power; as highlighted in ﬁgure 7.0.11, increased end-user input has led to a staged approach to their
product development. The start-up has scaled back and developed an interim market oﬀering, a ’hybrid
AC and DC’ system that is able to link up to both direct renewable energy sources as well as the traditional
energy grid infrastructure. This interim product was a direct result of customer concerns surrounding
power security, cost and integration, mentioning that "[now we] can reassure customers that there is no
Page 28 of 72
==PAGE:33==
MSc Thesis - Matthew John Wells - s172347
August 11th, 2019
risk of power loss or shortages in extreme market conditions ... and take advantage of cleaner and cheaper
renewable energy when it is feasible" [74]. Furthermore, by ensuring that as much end-user feedback
was captured and integrated into their oﬀerings, case study 5 has managed to successfully secure multiple
pilot-locations across the region as well as integration into the new ﬂagship co-creation facility within
the university campus; yet another example of how sales centric perspectives are not ideal and how living
labs can prove the diﬀerence. Rather than using end-users as sources of real feedback for design and
development purposes, sales focused start-ups risk developing products that do not address user value
perspectives nor encourage use amongst users.
4.3.2
The challenge of the competition format
The focus on sales was further compounded by the competition format that is often adopted by accelerators
and incubators, who oﬀer large cash prizes to winning start-ups. This competition format was adopted by
the accelerator in case study 1, 7, and this structure further promoted a strong culture and focus around
sales validation and pitches, which were rich in high level detail but lacking in speciﬁcs. Case study
1 oﬀered a major prize of $75,000 USD and as a result, CleanTech start-ups appeared only to focus
only on the ideal conditions and scenarios regarding their product, rather than the ’needs development’
and ’reality’ aspects of their product, thereby avoiding and focusing away from end-user and external
stakeholder involvement with product development. By simply treating end-users as a means for sales
and investment validation via pitching, start-ups spent a signiﬁcant amount of time creating ﬂashy and
well-rehearsed pitches in order to ’sell’ their product to customers. The resulting focus on ’sexy’ sales
pitches full of inﬂated ’ideal’ situations and addressable markets and the subsequent lack of end-user
involvement was detrimental to the start-up product development, as seen within the value framework in
ﬁgure 7.0.1; where there is a strong focus on economical value perspectives being created, but very little
with regards to ecology, sociology and psychology. The experience was totally diﬀerent with case studies
2 through 5, who did not oﬀer a competition based format. Instead, they oﬀered an ’open-innovation’
format with regular meetings, workshops and presentations with active investor circles, industry partners,
foundations and companies. In this way, sources of investment, collaboration and funding are available
via a more robust environment, full of professionals who recognise that end-user involvement is critical to
product innovation development who have deep knowledge of their customer bases and can oﬀer useful
advice or critique to the start-ups. Case study 3, in section 7, clearly highlighted the beneﬁts of having
active investor/industry involvement as opposed to a competition format, as "we were able to ﬁnd parties
who could see value in our product and got in touch with us right away.... our professor was able to
put us in touch with collaborators from within the existing local network" [75]. Another beneﬁt from
this alternative setup, is that existing industry participants are already in possession of key end-user
insights and it is more diﬃcult for CleanTech to bluﬀand present their way through decade long industry
participants.
4.4
Focus on processes and structures
4.4.1
The challenge of start-up selection and mismatch
The selection of CleanTech start-ups by IALL’s and the resulting cohort mixes is also a clear challenge that
is complicating eﬀorts to reaﬃrm the beneﬁts from incorporating end-users within product development
processes. IALL’s generally accept start-ups based on their strategic ﬁt, suitability towards the IALL,
use of deep technology and current level of development; and it was found that large divergences and
diﬀerences between the cohort start-ups, with regards to TRL level and overall ’maturity’ was often

==PAGE:34==

detrimental.
This issue was most evident within case studies 1 and 4, which were accelerators and
incubators respectively, whose respective cohorts totaling between 15-20 CleanTech start-ups spanned
across the entire TRL spectrum, as discussed in section 2.3.1. It was found that start-ups which were
more developed and placed towards the higher numerical end of the TRL spectrum, from 6 on-wards,
end-users were not the focus of their development process anymore as they had usually already secured a
pilot location and customer from which to demonstrate the technology successfully. This was explicitly
identiﬁed by case study 1, who had already set up a large lens ﬁeld for water heating within the heat
generation market within a local community, and were solely dedicated to proving the technology to only
that customer. Similarly in case study 4, the start-ups which were signiﬁcantly higher on the TRL scale
felt that they did not need to collaborate or focus on end-users feedback as they "have already secured a
pilot location and are focusing on moving and scaling up our operations" [73]. Conversely, case studies 2
and 3 which had low scoring TRL’s of 3 and 4 respectively, illustrated that CleanTech start-ups who had
not yet proven their technology within a laboratory or environmental setting, were similarly uninterested
in gathering end-user feedback or collaborating with external stakeholders. Instead, these start-ups were
more focused towards proving their technology and developing it further, "we need to be sure the theory
and the technology checks out before we can begin to look at potential customers" [75].
Alternatively however, the living lab in case study 5 highlighted the exact opposite through their demon-
strator approach, utilising project plans and operating without cohorts, see appendix 7. In this way, as long
as start-ups and their transformative product innovations had gaps in their development with regards to
society, legislation, business model and technology; they were able to be or remain being incubated. Even
though projects within the living lab were from across the entire TRL spectrum, the setup and framework
incubating them focused their attention towards showcasing their innovation, communicating it’s message
and receiving critical feedback as to it’s development; with all start-ups being incubated as if they were
all at the same TRL level. This is further conﬁrmed by the fact that start-up investigated within case study
5 has been with the living lab for 3 years and have used a signiﬁcant amount of this time demonstrating
and walking potential end-users through their physical prototype for relevant feedback and input.
As highlighted from the each of the case studies investigated, start-up cohorts can suﬀer from the disparity
of technologies being incubated all together at once, as well as from the overall level of readiness; and as
a result there is a high risk that many of the incubatees within the cohort do not need to, or are not seeking
inputs from, end-users or external stakeholders such as the government, local community or industry; as
they are more focused on technology selection, ideation, proof of concept or execution.
The challenge of peer-to-peer collaboration
A tertiary issue arising from the selection of CleanTech start-ups within cohorts, see section 4.4.1, is that
disparate TRL levels between start-ups can promote an ’us’ and ’them’ mentality during the incubation
period between the start-ups. Interviewee 2 from case study 1 described the situation aptly, "[that] there
was very little to learn from some of the other start-ups simply because they were focusing on diﬀerent
topics" [72], and there is a risk that that peer-to-peer learning is minimised. This was conﬁrmed by case
study 4 (section 7), noting that "we stopped participating in the functions, Friday afternoon drinks and
most network related events as we had other things to focus on and didn’t feel we were ’relevant’" [73]. The
sentiment of participating less within IALL events was further re-iterated by case study 1, who described
that as time progressed we stopped attending many of the events, because "maybe we were operating on
a whole other level" [72].
Both case studies highlighted that incubatees often had very little in common with each other, and as
a result lost interest in the incubator/accelerator environment and their peers.
Whilst it is useful to
develop start-ups with varying TRL’s and stages of development, there are risks that start-ups are not
being eﬀectively incubated regarding end-users, their value perspectives and user-led innovation simply

==PAGE:35==

August 11th, 2019
because others being incubated at the same time have diﬀerent priorities. The inherent risk is that whilst
start-ups are now consulted on a 1-to-1 basis rather than 1-to-many, key and fundamental knowledge areas
around end-users that are formative for all start-ups are often overlooked and not delivered to each start-up
together; simply because they are ’not ready’ for that level of workshop or session or because the start-up
would rather receive tailored and speciﬁc mentoring on topics they would prefer.
4.4.2
The challenge of tangibility
An element that was found during this study that increased the extent of end-user involvement and
external collaboration was physical presence. The study found that having physical oﬃces, research and
development facilities as well as pilot/testing locations open to the public are extremely important and
valuable when trying to engage end-users and help them to better understand transformative products
and technologies deigned for them, as was ﬁrmly identiﬁed from case study 5 in section 7. Case study
5 highlighted that many potentially socially transformative products often never leave the laboratory
before establishing pilot projects; and that many critical stakeholders perspectives regarding regulation,
societal embracement, business and systems perspectives should be addressed earlier on. The case further
highlighted that there is no better way to do this than having the technology free to touch, see, hear and
understand all in one place and that there is a signiﬁcant beneﬁt gained from having a tangible product
that end-users can come and observed in real time, "now that they can see this building is complete and
powered by renewable DC, now they are starting to listen up" [74]. Furthermore, the incubator in case
study 2 similarly supported the notion that a physical space to work from was beneﬁcial when aiming to
incorporate end-users within the development process; "as you can see we are able to build our prototype
here and quite often people will ask us what we are doing and oﬀer feedback or ideas ... and we try to
listen" [76]. Case study 2, section ?? went further to detail how having such an open innovation workspace
with industry partners constantly roaming the building, meant that there was also someone interested in
the work.
Conversely however, as seen in case studies 1 and 4, having no or insuﬃciently small work-spaces/oﬃce-
spaces that are temporary, such as the ones oﬀered by accelerators and some incubators, the inability to
showcase prototypes or relevant technology that end-users would want to see or provide feedback on is
detrimental to co-creative product innovation. As a result, critical and important perspectives regarding
the business and its proposed products are not gained because end-users simply cannot see any tangible
product and relate it to their lives. Case study 1 aptly described the situation, "We never really wanted
to spend much time here because it was so far away from our actual business facility and couldn’t really
showcase anything" [72]; which was reaﬃrmed by case study 4. Case study 4, section ??, highlighted that
because their actual business and manufacturing site was located in a separate yet nearby location, very
little except a out-reach oﬃce was kept within the incubators facilities. As a result, the start-up received
very few visitors at the incubators temporary oﬃce and spent very little time there.
Whilst the IALL’s all oﬀer physical locations of varying size and duration to CleanTech start-ups, there
exists an argument that larger and long-term on-site prototype facilities promote and receive higher/levels
of end-user and external collaborative input. If the product or product technologies are not easily accessible
by end-users or persons of interest it is then extremely diﬃcult for end-users who are expected to contribute
to, accept, understand and adopt it.

Lastly, the extensive professional and ﬁnancing networks that CleanTech start-ups are exposed to via
IALL’s is a strong promoter of end-user involvement. The ability to match-make CleanTech start-ups

Chapter 5
Discussion and Conclusion
The ﬁndings from this CCS study yielded insights surrounding whether IALL’s are providing key co-
creational services to CleanTech start-ups and the extent to which they are helping to integrate end-users,
their feedback and value perspectives within start-up development processes. It was observed that whilst
incubators, accelerators and living labs all oﬀer a suite of beneﬁts that contribute to co-creative practices
and the establishment of end-user related focuses; the extent to which they actually impact the development
of start-ups is variable. In the case of accelerators, the short and ﬁxed duration periods of acceleration
reduced the eﬀectiveness of co-creative practices and end-user centric practices did not resonate nor
permeate within the housed start-ups, who often came to accelerators to focus on one particular issue
which was usually not end-user centric. In the case of incubators, co-creative practices were more eﬀective
as start-ups were exposed to a signiﬁcantly lengthier incubation period and were able to address more
topics and leverage more of the knowledge, networks and facilities around them. It was also evident that
because incubators tend to oﬀer more oﬃce and prototyping services than accelerators, more end-user
feedback was embedded within the process as collaborators, industry persons, staﬀ, students or passers by
would often query and contribute to the process; simply by being around it. Lastly, in the case of living
labs, co-creative practices and end-user integration was most evident; largely due to the ’indeﬁnite’ length
of stay available, the project planning and grandfathering approach of regular assessments and feedback
from the dedicated co-creative team, as well as the ability to display their products and innovations
directly to end-users themselves. The key advantage of living labs is the ability to build a full scale
working prototype, often as a pilot location, that key stakeholders, the public, investors and even other
innovators can visit, observe, question, learn from and provide feedback to.
The study also helped to identify several strategic challenges that were observed within the sample of
incubators, accelerators and living lab and their respective start-ups relating to complex technologies, sales
validation and structure; that are making it diﬃcult to promote end-user centric practices and co-creation
during product innovation development. The selection of of start-ups for cohorts, the varying spread of
technology readiness levels amongst those chosen, the heavy sales validation focus, competition format and
visibility were all factors that worked counter to incubative attempts from the accelerators, incubators and
living lab. The purpose and intent of this study prohibits prescription with regards to recommendations,
however the observations made and subsequent analysis within have highlighted several key themes that
should be explored further and hopefully this investigation can provide a suitable foundation from which
to embark from, especially with regards to the establishment of living lab best practices documentation,
as the European Commission has nominated living labs to be the vehicles of innovation and development
into the future. [77] It is beyond the scope of this comparative case study to further investigate, adjust and
assess any of the aforementioned challenges, however it is am ambition of this paper that these changes

become future research directions.
Furthermore, the investigation identiﬁed the mechanisms and processes that aided co-creative and end-
user focused processes. One such mechanism for example is how on-site space and the ability to build a
physical prototype for the public to see, helped innovative and transformative products receive end-user
feedback, create awareness, gain understanding and garner interest/investment. Another such mechanism
was the formal industry, professional and investor networks available to start-ups, which proved to be
a critical means to acquiring technical, market and end-user insight. Furthermore, interviews with the
accelerator, incubator, living lab and start-up team members across the ﬁve case sample aﬃrmed the
proposition that it is beneﬁcial in the development of innovative and socially transformational products to
involve functional experts.
Whilst this comparative case study has identiﬁed a number of signiﬁcant ﬁndings regarding co-creative
practices with accelerators, incubators and living labs as well as the extent to which end-users are
integrated within their process; these ﬁndings are only assess at a high level. Further and deeper research
is needed within the identiﬁed issues and whilst the cases, IALL’s and start-ups chosen were noteworthy
for their richness of contribution and insights; there are limitations. Each of the start-ups investigated were
observed in real time as current and developing, there was no ability to gather and analyse data pertaining
to the perceptions of innovative product failure and success. It the study were to be conducted on projects
that had ’moved-on’ or had failure, or had been successful in their commercialisation agenda (to some
benchmark) then the comparative case study could instead test possible hypotheses rather than conduct an
exploratory analysis as above. Similarly, whilst each of the traditionally recognised incubation institutions
were analysed, the sample of selected start-ups was not a complete cross-section of technology readiness
levels nor business maturity and each start-up was developing a unique product using unique technologies;
and a more congruent population of start-ups could be selected to improve the analysis. Furthermore
regarding the start-up sample selection process, this process was potentially exposed to bias as the projects
proposed for investigation were volunteered by their partner IALL’s and there is high probability that
these these start-ups were selected as they were exemplary representations of the IALL’s incubative work;
whereas random sampling may have yielded a much diﬀerent sample set for analysis.
Hopefully this study will stimulate further research investigating the challenges facing incubators, accel-
erators and living labs regarding end-user collaboration the development of innovative products. The
proper setup and management of incubative environments is absolutely critical to securing the shift away
from fossil fuel dependency before it is too late.

Reﬂections
6.1
Alternative Methods
Causal Mechanisms - experimental vs exploratory
This study used causal mechanisms assumed from literature regarding end-used-led innovation and co-
creative practices and processes, whilst together they contribute to developing commercial successful
products.
The comparative case study pairs were used to highlight the diﬀerences and similarities
operating within each IALL, thereby testing the applicability and transferable of these causal mechanisms
onto other IALL’s.
Comparative case studies however, can also be used in tandem with and within other design setups. Another
methodology that could have been adopted for this paper could have included using an experimental design
setup, rather than an exploratory one, aimed at assessing the eﬀectiveness of the integration of end-users
and customers in more speciﬁc detail.
Data - qualitative vs quantitative
Regarding data gathering, this comparative study relied heavily on qualitative data gathered from semi-
structured interviews, however more quantitative data could have been incorporated within the study such
as performance measures, surveys and project documentation. A combination of both qualitative and
quantitative data sources could also have been used further justify and benchmark start-up successes and
failures.
6.2
Anonymity
Another area of reﬂection on this paper is with regards to the anonymity granted to IALL’s and CleanTech
start-up’s. When designing the CCS, an anonymous approach was adopted so as to remove any particular
bias regarding institutions, beliefs, and underlying technologies. Furthermore, the anonymity also removed
any prior knowledge of innovation networks and historical knowledge of the region with regards to IALL’s,
as well as any past failures/successes relating to both IALL’s and CleanTech’s. In order for the removal of
such bias’, signiﬁcant levels of information and details were omitted. Conversely however, a decision not

6.3
Data Gathering
Regarding the studies data gathering process, which involved over 10 two-hour face-to-face interviews
as well as four follow-up thirty-minute telephone interviews; could have been organised more eﬃciently.
Whilst a signiﬁcant amount of time was spent communicating and building rapport with IALL’s and
CleanTech start-ups to participate within an interview, several interviewees were extremely hard to catch
and lock in an interview time with; which wasted signiﬁcant amounts of time.
Many of the IALL
interviewees held high positions such as CEO, Director, Head-of-Co-creation and other titles, which also
meant their time was very limited, albeit organised. 4 out of 5 IALL interviewees were generous and
oﬀered plenty of time and follow-up interviews, however one start-up interviewee was very diﬃcult to
co-ordinate with and was interviewed much later than desired. This was compounded by the fact that
the respective IALL’s assisted in the screening, identiﬁcation and introduction of CleanTech start-ups to
investigate. This meant that delayed interviews led to delays in contacting particular start-ups. Similarly,
many of the CleanTech start-up interviewees were long-time employees or founders and were very diﬃcult
to get a hold of, and in some cases over a month of communication would elapse before arrangements
regarding interviews could be discussed. In addition to the lengthy contact periods, CleanTech start-ups
interviewees were often students who were busy juggling university studies, sport and job in addition to
working for the start-up, and this also made arranging interview times very diﬃcult. The large time frame
encompassing the interview schedule times can be see in ﬁgure 3.3.3, and as a result, the number of case
studies was revised down from 6 to 5. If this process were to be improved for future studies, a longer-time
period for the interviews would be built into the investigation time frame and a signiﬁcant amount of
preparatory introductions would be made to the IALL’s in advance to organising interviews.

The below appendix section contains ﬁve case studies, one for each CleanTech start-up and its respective

Case Study 1
Case study 1 (CS1) investigated an institution that runs cohort-based programs speciﬁcally designed
for deep-technology and CleanTech startups. Established in 2014, the institution runs four month long
programs and has assisted over 100 companies to date, with each cohort consisting of around 20 start-ups.
Accepted start-ups are oﬀered dedicated co-located working spaces, self-described as "deliberate and
on-purpose" [78], however working from the co-location space is not mandatory. Prior to 2019 it was
mandatory to work from the co-location facility however past feedback presents evidence that start-ups
are ’more prepared’ if they do so, "because without the daily interaction with their peers (competitors),
teams suﬀer and fail to take full advantage of the available services and are unable to learn" [78].
The main goal of the institution is to provide CleanTech start-ups (hardware based only) with "the full
due diligence required of a new company... to prepare them" [78], ensuring that when they leave they are
investment ready and equipped with the appropriate tools to grow. According to section 2.1, the institution
can be categorised as an ’accelerator’, who structure their program to avoid 2 common problems they
believe are impeding the success of CleanTech start-ups across Europe. Firstly, many start-ups are "not
validating properly that they are actually solving a problem" [78], and secondly that start-ups are often
"technologies looking for a problem, rather than problems looking for technologies" [78].
Entry to the accelerator is via an application process and panel interview, and upon acceptance start-ups
will receive one-on-one mentorship in varying areas of start-up development where they receive weekly
tasks and feedback over the program course. The accelerator argues that "one-to-many mentorship doesn’t
work eﬀectively, and one-to-one is really where the value is created" [78], and consequently they work
extremely close with the selected start-ups and limit the amount of start-ups they work with at a time;
however this is common among accelerators globally. Most importantly however, the accelerator oﬀers
access to an extensive alumni and investment network, as the program collaborates with and is partially
funded by many signiﬁcant industry related foundations.

==PAGE:47==

Analysis of the weekly coaching topics ranging from customer validation, business model generation,
supply chain networks, ﬁnance, marketing and management highlighted that the accelerator was value
co-creation focused, with very little consideration given to governments and regulation, society and
acceptance, the environment and end-users as citizens.
When investigating the customer validation
session in detail; it was discovered that the ideal outcome of this session was "to go out and make a sale,
get a receipt and prove that customers will buy into your solution" [72]. "Validation will always occur,
even if its not actually happening" [18], and simply by proving you can make a sale via a well rehearsed
and generous pitch, the value created is both subjective and non-collaborative.
Finally, the accelerator operates under a competition format with a ﬁrst prize of $75,000 USD. The use
of a monetary prize is unique in the way it "generates a very competitive landscape" [78] within the
accelerator; however it is evident that this can stymie the collaborative nature of the accelerator itself and
limit the transference of knowledge between the teams. The attractiveness of a prize is also unique in the
way it attracts start-ups regardless of their development progress and often results in start-ups who are
