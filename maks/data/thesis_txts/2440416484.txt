Abstract
In this Master Thesis p-multigrid method is studied. p-multigrid is an iterative strategy suited
for high order Finite Element solvers. Universal, state-of-the-art p-multigrid is developed for
1D, 2D triangular, 2D quadrilateral, 3D prismatic, 3D hexahedral mesh and its eﬀectiveness
against Poisson problem is examined.
The algorithm is then accelerated by the recent de-
velopments from the literature on this topic. Static condensation (Schur complement type-of
method) is employed to increase eﬃciency along with Weighted Additive Schwarz methods
(block smoothing) that are used to improve relaxation scheme. The method is considered as a
stand-alone solver and a preconditioner to defect correction and conjugate gradient methods.
Final version of the multigrid is established fully compatible with the solvers in the DTU-
SEM library. It’s eﬀectiveness is examined in the Fully Nonlinear Potential Flow simulation
and the results are presented. Thesis also covers some theoretical aspects of Spectral Element
framework, Multigrid methods and Prolongation/Restriction strategies.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Objectives and the scope of the Thesis
The starting point of the thesis is the work of Allan P. Engsig-Karup on Spectral Element
solver for fully nonlinear water waves ([EKEB16]). The solution developed by Engsig-Karup
will help with analysis of ocean wave propagation, wave-body interactions that involve real
geometries of oﬀshore structures and ﬂoating bodies and cost-eﬃcient engineering analysis
of structural design.
p-multigrid method is an iterative method tailored for the Galerkin-
type-of discretization strategies, therefore it enables to use full potential of SEM in practical
applications. It has a great impact on how the method handles embedded complex structures.
Exploiting high-order element’s properties allows to reduce the size of the problem like any
multigrid method, but without changing the mesh structure.
The main objective is to design an eﬃcient, scalable and robust iterative strategy that will
enable the Spectral Element solver practical calculation time. The motivation of the project
stays the same as the SE solver: to boost potential ﬂow simulations of nonlinear waves and
their interactions with arbitrarily shaped structures.
Iterative solver however, will do that
more implicitly, since the main objective is very precise and the framework is limited to the
very engine of the FNPF simulations. Aside from Introduction (ch. 1) and Conclusion (ch. 7),
the paper is divided into 5 parts:
• Chapter 2 covers theory on Spectral Element framework and multigrid (with small ad-
dition on iterative strategies). Based on the foundations of iterative methods, it also
explains in details objective of the project.

1.4
Literature and Publications
The literature that has been used for the thesis can be divided into two main groups. First
group consists of all the state-of-the-art methods and theory on SEM and MG. It contributes
to the ﬁrst part of the project until the Poisson Model problem is solved in three-dimensional
space (Section 4). The second groups is made from more novel publications, solely focused on
MG application within SE framework. Current section covers only the ﬁrst group. Review of
recent publications on SE MG takes place in the Section 5 and it is divided into several parts,
each of them examines a speciﬁc component of the MG.
Continuous Galerkin framework and Spectral Element theory is mostly based on work of Ko-
priva [Kop09] and Fisher [M.O02]. Both books put spotlight on CFD applications as well.
Multigrid theory is mostly derived from, probably the most famous book on that topic, Trot-
tenberg’s ”Multigrid” [TS01] and the publication of Brandt [Bra84]. More general theory on
iterative methods and notation inspiration is taken from [Hac94]. Those positions consist the
foundation of the ﬁrst three chapters. Besides contributing to the basics, [M.O02] references
can be found throughout the whole thesis as it is the newest publication of them all and features
several important remarks on SE, MG and Domain Decomposition (DD) methods.
Work of Rnquist, Patera, Maday and Muoz (Spectral Element Multigrid Part I [RP87] and Part
II [Mun88]) is known to be state-of-the-art for MG application within Galerkin framework. The
content of those two papers was absolutely essential for the ﬁrst MG algorithm implementation
in the Chapter 3. The second most important piece in this chapter is [DPR08], where diﬀerent
transfer strategies were studied for SE MG - a similar test was also undertaken in the Section
3.2.

2.2
Multigrid algorithm explained
In general, the main idea behind any multigrid is to accelerate other iterative strategy with
coarse solution. Most of the known iterative methods deal very well with high-frequency er-
rors, but struggle with low-frequency errors. The acceleration comes from the fact that low
frequencies on arbitrary grid can be represented by high frequencies on coarser version of the
same grid. There are several options to obtain coarse grid. The most popular is to increase
the size of the element h on the approximated mesh (or other entities, e.g. volumes in Finite
Volume Method). This method is known as a h-multigrid. In high-order methods, like SEM,
one can leave the geometry and mesh untouched and reduce only the polynomial order of the
element basis functions. That is then called p-multigrid method.
The simplest way to design a multigrid algorithm is in a recursive V-cycle, i.e. starting from
the ﬁnest level, coarsing until the coarsest grid is reached and going back to the ﬁnest level.
This is depicted in the ﬁgure 2.1, where a combination of p−and h −multigrid is presented.

2.2.1
Objectives - Multigrid scaling
The goal of every iterative method is to calculate the problem as fast as possible.
Hence,
practically speaking, one can assess particular method solely by computational time and that
is interesting from the engineering point of view, when the only thing that matters is to solve
problem as fast as possible. For elliptic PDEs, multilevel methods are the best available tool
due to linear scaling of computational eﬀort in respect to degrees of freedom (O(DOF) scaling).
Obtaining O(DOF) is also the ultimate goal of the presented project. Numerical or Computa-
tional scaling is the term that refers to the CPU/wall-clock time vs. DOF (deﬁned either by
variable K or N) dependence throughout the whole thesis from now on.
From a developer’s perspective however, it is equally important to achieve algorithmic scal-
ing, i.e. achieving textbook multigrid eﬃciency. The term used for the ﬁrst time by Brandt

This chapter review some of the novelties found in the literature in recent years. The emphasis

5.1
Static condensation
Static Condensation is a variant of the non-overlapping Schur complement ([SP05]).
The
method splits the equation 2.22 into several blocks, which then can be solved using diﬀer-
ent methods. The purpose of the static condensation for SE is to use the fact that element’s
interior nodes depends only on this element edge nodes, which signiﬁcantly decreases compu-
tational time of factorizing matrix AII (the matrix with only interior-node entries becomes
block-element diagonal matrix, where factorizing each element-block one by one equals to fac-
torizing the whole matrix). The question if static condensation method is proﬁtable depends
how fast one can factorize AII and perform matrix-matrix operations in 5.2 and 5.3.
5.1.1
Publications
Three diﬀerent publications on the technique called Static Condensation (SC) are reviewed.
The most impactful for this project is [HSN13], as it was the ﬁrst one reviewed and consists
of results for MG in combination with DD smoother, i.e. the exact same framework as in the

Coarsening strategy
The section focuses on the coarsening strategy. Of course, the goal is the same for every strategy
- restrict the scheme till the coarsest level. However, there are several ways to obtain it and
the aim of this section to ﬁnd the most eﬃcient one. Coarsening strategy can be depicted as
an array of size d x p with unique numbers that represent polynomial order on every level,
where d = 1, 2, 3 is a dimension of a given problem and p is a number of multigrid levels. Table
5.2 presents coarsening an exemplary two-dimensional case discretized by a polynomial pair
(Nx, Ny) = (5, 5) by 2 polynomial orders on every coarser grid, in each direction. Often in the
literature authors describe coarsening strategy as semi-coarsening in a-direction and standard-
coarsening. The former coarses the problem only in one spatial direction, deﬁned by a and the
latter describes an even coarsening in each direction.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
7.1
Summary of Thesis Achievements
In chapters 3 and 4 a state-of-the-art p-multigrid has been developed. A few test cases have
been solved to ensure correctness and consistency of the algorithm. In Chapter 5 several recent
developments on the p-multigrid have been gathered and implemented. Finally, in Chapter 6,
the given PDE problem has been solved.
As a result of the work, a new p-multigrid library has been developed - a library tailored to
solve any PDE using p-multigrid method, directly connected to current the DTU-SEM library.
The library consists of:
• MG V-cycle (algorithm 1) and the universal smoothing routine (algorithm 2).
• Routines for developing connectivity tables on any MG level based on inputted connec-
tivity table for the ﬁnest grid.
• Universal Prolongation operator and 3 types of Restriction operators for SEM (3.2 and
4.1).
• Static condensation routine (5.1).
• MG, MGCG and MGDC solvers. Essentially, algorithms 4, 6, 5.
• Few diﬀerent types of block smoothers described in section 5.3.
All the routines are universal for triangular and quadrilateral type of elements in 1/2/3D. It
is worth to mention that the code for SE solver for simple Poisson equation also have been
55

56
Chapter 7. Conclusion
developed in 1/2D i.e. routines for construction of reference element, mesh and the discrete
operators. Further development of these routines have been dropped when the p-multigrid
library was connected to the DTU-SEM library.
The main goal of the Thesis was to design eﬃcient iterative multigrid strategy. As the results,
p-multigrid method proves to have really good algorithmic eﬃciency for the 2D Bar Test (6.2).
In general, the algorithm results in decent numerical eﬃciency and is also quite robust, since
all solved cases converged. From all of the solver algorithms MGCG gives the best results. On
2D quadrilateral mesh star-smoother 5.6 in combination static condensation 5.1 is a method of
choice for relaxation.
Summarizing, this thesis contributes to the recent developments on the p-multigrid method and
gathers those developments not only in a single report, but also in a easy-to-use library already
connected to the existing DTU-SEM library for solving FNPF problems.
7.2
Future Work
Although, the project provided some insights on p-multigrid method, the limited time that has
been assigned to it leaves even more open questions and possible improvements to the developed
algorithm. Here are several possible approaches that can be pursued based on the ﬁndings of
the thesis.
• Possibly the most beneﬁcial acceleration strategy for p-multigrid is parallelization of the
code. SEM has a very good parallel properties since each element can be implemented
separably into global discrete operator. Additive Schwarz method can beneﬁt even more,
since the foundation of the method is the sum of factorized local matrices (5.9).
• Design of eﬃcient method on irregular 3D meshes.
• Line-smoothers did not return good results in the initial tests. Also, in the novel literature
(5.3), emphasize is put on block-smoothers, thus line type of strategies have not been
==PAGE:74==
7.2. Future Work
57
thoroughly investigated in the study. However, it is still promising strategy for high AR
problems, worth of exploring.
• Schwarz type of smoothers are quite expensive strategies. The solution to that, besides
parallel implementation, is inexact local matrix factorization proposed in [FL05].
• A deeper coarsing strategy analysis based on diﬀerent types of meshes is needed to improve
the eﬃciency of p-multigrid. It can be approached from purely theoretical perspective,
i.e. spectral radius study (examples can be found in ﬁgures in the section 5.4), which is
rather time consuming, since ρ(Miter) depends on many factors like number of elements,
approximating polynomials, AR, wave parameters, type of mesh. More practical analysis
would focus on answering
• Diﬀerent types of multigrid cycles(FMG, W-cycle). It can be very beneﬁcial and also
very interesting study, since none of the positions in Bibliography examined other types
of MG cycles in their studies.
• Stiller in [Sti17] introduces high-order weighting method for element-based Schwarz smoothers,
i.e. diﬀerent weighting matrix W in 5.10. Nodes within the elemental space Ωk have value
1, in the overlapping region ˜Ωk\Ωk each node takes an inverse on number of subdomains
it is in and they are 0 everywhere else. Stiller proposes high order polynomial weighting
from 0 to 1 in the overlapping region ˜Ωk\Ωk.
• Following section 5.3 other types of block-smoothers can be examined. Also, a deeper
study on the size of overlapping region depending on the mesh and AR would deﬁnitely
improve the eﬃciency of the p-multigrid strategy.
• More practical goals can be set based on the current work: FORTRAN/C/C++ imple-
mentation along with analysis on memory use is the natural step. Perhaps with matrix-
free implementation.
