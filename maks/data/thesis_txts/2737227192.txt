ABSTRACT 
 
The virus Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) is a zoonotic viral 
pathogen, able to be transmitted back and forth between humans and minks. This cross-species 
transmission ability is cause for concern, as it potentially enables the virus to escape antibody 
neutralisation or vaccine protection. 
Variants of SARS-CoV-2, initially found in minks from Danish mink farms, were later found 
among the human population in Denmark. These variants were claimed to have a lower susceptibility 
to antibody neutralisation, leading to the culling of over 17 million minks in Denmark.  
This thesis project aims to investigate what underlies this zoonotic and reverse zoonotic ability. 
This is done by (I) analysing SARS-CoV-2 sequences, obtained from both humans and minks, (II) 
comparing the human and mink Angiotensin Converting Enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptors, (III) performing 
a phylogenetic and transmission analysis to identify changes over time in the DNA sequences and the 
corresponding amino acid sequences of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein, (IV) modelling amino acid 
mutations into a three-dimensional structure of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein, followed by (V) 
simulations of the interaction between the spike protein and the ACE2 receptor of both hosts, and finally 
(VI) analysing the impact of these mutations by measuring aspects of the protein interaction and 
stability. This report will go over the phases of the project and the different approaches used to answer 
the aim as described above. 
Some of the work done in this project is in overlap with a paper on the emergence and spread 
of SARS-CoV-2 variants between farmed minks and humans in Denmark, currently available as 
preprint on BioRxiv (Rasmussen et al., 2024). The paper investigates the occurrence of zoonotic and 
reverse zoonotic transmission events between humans and minks. Part of the analyses and calculations 
in the paper are also used in this report. 
Several variants of SARS-CoV-2, able to transmit between humans and minks, were identified 
in this project. For each of these variants, protein properties, such as the Gibbs free energy, were 
analysed in efforts to explain the occurrence of certain amino acid changes and distinguish a pattern or 
commonality. One of the identified variants of this project is commonly known as the cluster 5 variant 
of SARS-CoV-2. This variant, carrying among others the S:D614G and S:Y453F mutations, is known 
the be able to undergo zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmission. This project identified three 
additional variants, able to undergo similar transmission. 
When ranking the signatures, signature A, consisting of the S:D614G, S:Y453F, S:delH69/V70, 
and S:N751Y mutations, seems to be most beneficial. This is according to its stability, based on the 
Gibbs free energy and steric hindrance, and according to the interaction with the human ACE2 receptor. 
Signature C, consisting of the S:D614G, S:Y453F, and S:S1147L mutations, ranks highest according to 
the interaction with the mink ACE2 receptor.

INTRODUCTION 
 
 
This chapter will go over relevant topics, concluded with a description of the aim of this project. 
 
SEVERE ACUTE RESPIRATORY SYNDROME CORONAVIRUS 2 
 
 
Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) is the virus behind the 
worldwide pandemic, starting in 2019, of coronavirus disease (COVID-19). It is an RNA virus of the 
family of coronaviruses (Shi et al., 2020). 
The virus spread globally, mainly through transmission among humans, and affects primarily, 
but not only, the human population. While resistance to SARS-CoV-2 is shown in a few species, such 
as chickens, the virus has the ability to infect several different species aside from humans, including 
minks (Cohen, 2020; Shi et al., 2020). Minks are one of the few animals from which SARS-CoV-2 is 
able to transmit to humans (Porter et al., 2023). 
As mentioned above, SARS-CoV-2 is an RNA virus. RNA viruses have mechanisms in place 
which detect erroneous mutagenic nucleotides inserted by RNA polymerase and correct for these errors. 
These mechanisms ensure that the virus maintains a high accuracy in viral replication and transcription. 
A combination of a rapid spread among different hosts, and each host-specific immune 
response, provide the virus with increased opportunities for viral mutation and natural selection for 
favourable mutations (Chen et al., 2020).  
  
 
ADAPTATION TO THE HUMAN POPULATION 
 
The ongoing emergence of new viral lineages of the SARS-CoV-2 virus indicates its ability to 
rapidly adapt to its specific host (MacLean et al., 2021; van Dorp et al., 2020). As the virus spreads 
among the human population, it needs to continuously adapt to overcome selective pressure from the 
human immune response and vaccine procedures (Kissler et al., 2020). It is therefore speculated that 
the virus, as it is circulating among the human population, will display signs of adaptive convergence 
across distantly related viral strains. The mechanisms behind this adaptive convergence can be 
discovered through the identification of patterns of nonsynonymous mutations across different viral 
strains. It has been shown that most nonsynonymous mutations, occurring across viral strains, are 
located within the receptor binding domain (RBD) of the viral spike protein. This is because the RBD 
is structurally plastic, meaning it can allow amino acid mutations without impairing the interaction with 
the ACE2 receptor (Escalera-Zamudio et al., 2023; Thomson et al., 2021). However, mutations in the 
epitope region for human antibodies have also been identified (Kemp et al., 2021).  
 
SARS-COV-2 ON DANISH MINK FARMS 
 
Until June of 2020, Denmark was responsible for 40% of the worldwide production of mink 
furs. With this significant contribution in the industry, Denmark had over a thousand mink farms, 
inhibiting a total population exceeding 17 million minks (Devaux et al., 2021; Hammer et al., 2021).  
In the summer of 2020, minks infected with SARS-CoV-2 were identified on approximately 
25% of the Danish mink farms (Larsen et al., 2021). On many of the mink farms, reverse zoonotic 
events, likely from farm workers, introduced the virus into the minks. These introductions were 
followed by rapid transmission among the minks (J. Zhou et al., 2022). Using Whole Genome 
Sequencing (WGS), novel viral variants were identified in infected minks. These variants subsequently 
spread to the human population as a result of viral zoonotic transmission (Hammer et al., 2021). The 
same spillback events had occurred in other countries, such as the Netherlands, indicating that mink 
farms represent a possible animal reservoir for SARS-CoV-2 (Hammer et al., 2021; Munnink et al., 
2021). This spread did not only affect the human population in direct contact with infected mink farms, 
but also spread further among the rest of the human population. This observed spread seems to be caused 
only by humans, as there is no evidence of viral spread induced by minks outside of Danish mink farm 
buildings (Hammer et al., 2021).

SARS-COV-2 SPIKE PROTEIN 
 
SARS-CoV-2 has a trimeric transmembrane spike glycoprotein on its envelope, it is a class I 
fusion protein which mediates both host cell binding and entry into the host cell (Yu et al., 2023). This 
spike protein is the least conservative region of the entire SARS-CoV-2 genome, meaning it is able to 
rapidly adapt to different hosts and evolve to potentially increase viral fitness or escape the host immune 
system (Chen et al., 2020; Thomson et al., 2021). 
The spike protein consists of three heterodimers, each comprised of two subunits; an N-terminal 
S1 subunit which enables receptor binding, and a membrane-proximal S2 subunit which mediates 
membrane fusion. In between these two subunits lies a cleavage site recognized by furin, which is the 
protease that can cleave the two subunits apart. This cleavage is a crucial step in the conformational 
changes required for fusion between the viral particle and the host cell, ultimately leading to host cell 
entry (Wrapp et al., 2020). The S1 subunit contains the RBD, which spans from an arginine residue (R) 
at position 319 to a phenylalanine residue (F) at position 541 (Lan et al., 2020). This region of the spike 
protein interacts with the protein Angiotensin Converting Enzyme 2 (ACE2) on the host cell surface 
through a series of molecular interactions between the amino acids (Tai et al., 2020; J. Zhou et al., 
2022). More specifically, the bond is made with the N-terminal peptidase domain of the ACE2 receptor, 
which spans from a serine (S) residue at position 19 to an aspartic acid (D) residue at position 615 (Lan 
et al., 2020).  
The interaction between the RBD and the ACE2 receptor triggers a cascade of events, resulting 
in the fusion of the membrane of the viral particle with the host cell (Lan et al., 2020). Because of its 
role in host cell entry, the RBD plays a crucial role in cross-species transmission and viral evolution 
(Letko et al., 2020; Starr et al., 2020). It has been shown that the bond, and corresponding free energy, 
between the RBD and ACE2 receptor is an indicator of viral infectivity. With a decrease in free energy 
meaning an increase in infectivity. In general, mutations in the RBD which strengthen the bond between 
the spike protein and ACE2 receptor result in a more infectious SARS-CoV-2 virus (Chen et al., 2020). 
Because there are three heterodimers, there are three RBD regions on the spike protein. From 
these, only one binds to the ACE2 receptor (Song et al., 2018). The heterodimer that interacts with the 
ACE2 receptor is in an open conformation, the other two are in a closed conformation. Figure 1 below 
shows the structure of a single spike protein heterodimer in the closed and open conformation. The 
RBD on both structures is indicated in red. From this, it can be seen how the RBD becomes more 
available for interaction when it switches from a closed to an open conformation.

AMINO ACID VARIANTS IN THE SARS-COV-2 SPIKE PROTEIN 
 
Since the start of the SARS-CoV-2 outbreak in 2019, the virus has been thoroughly sequenced 
and studied. Although the virus has genetic proofreading mechanisms, as well as a low genetic diversity, 
several changes in the spike protein sequence or the corresponding coding region of the DNA sequence 
have been identified (Fauver et al., 2020; Sevajol et al., 2014).  
 
Such mutations are partially enabled by the length of the pandemic. As antigenic drift causes 
the gradual accumulation of mutations, with time, these mutations can have fitness advantages or 
improve immunological resistance. Most mutations that increase viral fitness occur in the RBD of the 
spike protein, as this region undergoes higher purifying selection compared to other regions of the spike 
protein (Starr et al., 2020). 
 
One of the identified mutations in the spike protein is the S:D614G mutation, located in the S1 
subunit. It is located in one of the hotspot regions of the spike protein, from positions 602 to 616. These 
hotspot regions are identified regions of higher positional entropy and likelihood of mutagenesis. It is a 
mutation from an aspartic acid (D) residue to a glycine (G) residue. This mutation is caused by a 
mutation at nucleotide position 23,403, from adenine (A) to guanine (G). Additionally, it is accompanied 
by three cytosine (C) to thymine (T) mutations, at positions 241, 3,037, and 14,408 (Mullick et al., 
2021). 
 
The glycine (G) residue at position 614 has become the most prevalent in the worldwide 
COVID-19 pandemic after April of 2020. It appeared independently in human hosts on several 
geographical regions and remained prominent thereafter, suggesting that the mutation results in a fitness 
advantage. It has been shown that the S:D614G mutation results in an increase of viral load and 
infectivity, due to the promotion of the open spike protein conformation. Furthermore, it increases the 
efficiency in host cell entry (Hoffmann et al., 2021; Juraszek et al., 2021; Korber et al., 2020).  
 
The aspartic acid (D:614) residue from the S1 subunit is in contact with a threonine (T:859) 
residue from the S2 subunit. The two residues from a hydrogen bond. This bond would be eliminated 
by the S:D614G mutation, as the glycine (G) residue cannot form such a bond. This elimination might 
increase flexibility of both subunits, enabling rapid folding and maturing of spike proteins within the 
host cell (Korber et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2023). Furthermore, it is likely to support the dissociation 
between the two subunits, prompting the S2 subunit to transition from a metastable to a more stable 
state that is essential for membrane fusion (Lan et al., 2020; Song et al., 2018). The increase of 
membrane incorporation could be a means to a higher infectivity (Laha et al., 2020; L. Zhang et al., 
2020). It is interestingly shown that, although the S:D614G mutation is related to an increase of cellular 
viral load and infectivity, it is not related to an increase of disease severity (Korber et al., 2020). Lastly, 
although the mutation is found in both mink and human hosts, it has been shown that it is not required 
for zoonotic transmission (J. Zhou et al., 2022). 
 
When analysing the epistasis of the neighbouring protein structure, it is found that the S:D614G 
mutation is highly favourable. The epistasis is determined by considering the interactions between all 
pairs of amino acid residues in the neighbouring region to quantify the mutational effects (Hopf et al., 
2017; Mullick et al., 2021). Out of all possible amino acid mutations from aspartic acid (D) at position 
614, the mutation to glycine (G) is number 2 out of 19 (Mullick et al., 2021). 
 
During the time period analysed in this project, the SARS-CoV-2 G(V) clade was by far the 
most prominent in Denmark. This clade is characterised by the S:D614G mutation (Korber et al., 2020; 
Rambaut et al., 2020). It is therefore present in all sequences, with the exception of the Wuhan-Hu-1 
reference genome (GenBank accession no. NC_045512).

NOVEL VIRAL STRAIN IN MINKS 
 
The host humoral immune response increases the occurrence of viral antigenic drifts, which is 
reflected by nonsynonymous mutations in the spike protein (Kemp et al., 2021). As mentioned prior, a 
novel viral SARS-CoV-2 strain was identified in Denmark, initially specific to mink hosts. This strain 
carried the S:Y453F amino acid mutation. This mutation underlies a mutation at nucleotide position 
22,920, resulting in an amino acid mutation in the spike protein at respective position 453, namely from 
a tyrosine (Y) to a phenylalanine (F) residue. This amino acid position is not highly conserved, as it lies 
within the RBD of the spike protein, in one of the hotspot regions ranging from position 439 to 453  
(Hammer et al., 2021; Mullick et al., 2021; Thomson et al., 2021).  
The S:Y453F mutation is one of seventeen identified residues of the RBD that interact with 
twenty residues of the ACE2 receptor (Lan et al., 2020). The full overview of contacting residues 
between the SARS-CoV-2 RBD and the ACE2 receptor is listed in Table 1.1 of Appendix 1. 
For the S:Y453F mutation, its position in the RBD is in contact with the host cellular ACE2 
receptor at amino acid position 34 (Wang et al., 2020). This amino acid position differs between the 
human and mink ACE2 receptors, namely histidine (H) and tyrosine (Y) respectively (Damas et al., 
2020). The residue difference suggests that the S:Y453F mutation is a viral adaptation to the mink ACE2 
receptor, as is supported by the fact that it has arisen on several occasions independently among minks 
(J. Zhou et al., 2022). Furthermore, it has been shown that the mutation enhances binding to the mink 
ACE2 receptor, due to fact that the bond between a tyrosine (Y) and a phenylalanine (F) residue causes 
less steric hindrance and binds with more hydrophobic-driven interaction, compared to a bond with a 
histidine (H) residue (Ren et al., 2021). Lastly, the mutation increases viral fitness in mink hosts 
(Devaux et al., 2021). It has been shown that earlier strains of SARS-CoV-2 were not adapted to interact 
with the mink ACE2 receptor, and that this interaction improved in strains where the S:Y453F mutation 
occurred (J. Zhou et al., 2022). 
As mentioned previously, the bond between the spike protein and the human ACE2 receptor is 
weaker once the S:Y453Fmutation occurs. This is due to the fact that the histidine (H) residue in the 
human ACE2 receptor can form a hydrogen bond with the tyrosine (Y) residue in the RBD, but not with 
the phenylalanine (F) residue (Hayashi et al., 2023). However, the amino acid position 453 lies 
sequentially and structurally close to a leucine (L) residue at position 455, which is critical for 
interaction with the ACE2 receptor in both human and mink hosts. The S:Y453F mutation increases the 
exposure of position 455, due to the fact that a phenylalanine (F) residue is smaller than a tyrosine (Y) 
residue. This is a possible explanation for its ability to maintain in human hosts despite the decrease in 
bond strength (Hammer et al., 2021). 
In contrast, there are also studies reporting that the S:Y453F mutation has a positive effect on 
the RBD expression and affinity to the human ACE2 receptor (Starr et al., 2020). It has furthermore 
been shown that the S:Y453F mutation reduces the neutralising potential of human antibodies specific 
to SARS-CoV-2, by escaping their antiviral effect (Hayashi et al., 2023; Mullick et al., 2021; Thomson 
et al., 2021). But there are also studies reporting the opposite, claiming an easier neutralisation by the 
human humoral immune system (J. Zhou et al., 2022). In addition to this, viral strains carrying the 
S:Y453F mutation show a lower replication rate in human epithelial cells as opposed to strains without 
this mutation, as well as no significant change in viral antigenicity (J. Zhou et al., 2022).  
The human and mink ACE2 receptors share 83.73% amino acid identity overall, but only 
63.34% in the region involved in the interaction with the spike protein, as listed in Table 1.1 in Appendix  
1. It is furthermore reported that the human and mink ACE2 receptors fold differently. 
Despite these differences, the residues strictly required for interaction are conserved between 
the two. These are a lysine residue (K) at position 31, a tyrosine (Y) residue at position 41, and another 
lysine residue (K) at position 353 (Devaux et al., 2021). This can again be found in Table 1.1 in 
Appendix 1.

The studies indicating that the S:Y453F mutation is not beneficial for viral spread among 
human hosts are supported by the fact that it is not a favourable mutation based on the epistasis of the 
surrounding protein structure. I.e., the S:Y453F mutation has a destabilising effect on the surrounding 
protein structure. When all possible amino acid mutations from tyrosine (Y) at position 453 are ranked, 
based on how beneficial the mutation would be for the surrounding protein structure, the mutation to 
phenylalanine (F) is number 7 out of 19 (Hopf et al., 2017; Mullick et al., 2021). 
Lastly, the entropy of the spike protein increases when the amino acid at position 453 changes 
from a phenylalanine (F) to a tyrosine (Y), this indicates a potential decrease in protein stability due to 
the dissimilarity between the two residues (Korber et al., 2020; Y. Zhang et al., 2022). 
Despite these conflicting findings, it seems that the mutation overall attenuates viral fitness in 
human cells. This is a possible explanation for the fact that the S:Y453F mutation did not persist among 
the human population after the spread among mink hosts was eliminated by the culling of minks. 
The presence of the S:Y453F mutation among the human population in Denmark is analysed 
using GISAID data, on the public website for exploring mutations in SARS-CoV-2 (Korber et al., 2020). 
The analysed time period for this thesis project is June 1st, 2020 (week 23) until February 28th, 2021 
(week 8). June of 2020 is when SARS-CoV-2 was first detected on mink farms in Denmark (Boklund 
et al., 2021). The frequency of the S:Y453F mutation during this time period is 0.01983. Both before 
and after the defined time period of week 23 until week 8, the S:Y453F mutation is not seen. When 
considering the entire time period of available data, which is from the end of 2019 until today’s date, 
the frequency of the S:Y453F mutation is 0.00178  (Korber et al., 2020).  
It is finally worth noting that the S:Y453F mutation has also occurred in human hosts, unrelated 
to mink hosts. This is observed in humans with immunocompromising conditions. In these cases, there 
is often a second infection of SARS-CoV-2, during which the virus acquires the S:Y453F mutation. A 
possible explanation for this can be a viral immune escape effort, from antibodies formed during a prior 
infection (Choi et al., 2020).   
 
THE SARS-COV-2 CLUSTER 5 VARIANT 
 
 
After the first occurrence of the S:Y453F mutation among mink hosts, several following amino 
acid mutations were seen. A series of specific mutations is known as the cluster 5 variant. It consists of 
the amino acid mutations S:D614G, S:Y453F, S:delH69/V70, S:I692V, and S:M1229I (Hoffmann et 
al., 2021; Lassaunière et al., 2021; J. Zhou et al., 2022). This variant of SARS-CoV-2, likely originating 
from mink hosts, has been identified in both mink and human hosts.  
Within this cluster, it is only the S:Y453F mutation that is located within the RBD of the spike 
protein. It is therefore of additional interest as it is crucial in the interaction with the host ACE2 receptor. 
The combination of mutations does not seem to increase viral infectivity in human hosts and does not 
compromise the human immune response. In both human and mink hosts, it has a similar response to 
the antibody protection and an increased inhibition by the ACE2 receptor. Furthermore, it has been 
shown that the combination of mutations of cluster 5 reduce viral entry into certain human cell lines. 
This could be a possible explanation for the fact that this variant of SARS-CoV-2 did not continue to 
spread among the human population in Denmark after the close contact with minks was eliminated by 
the culling of minks (Hoffmann et al., 2021). 
 
PHYLOGENETIC ANALYSIS 
 
To understand when SARS-CoV-2 spread and adapted to the mink hosts, followed by spillover 
back into the human population, a phylogenetic analysis is performed. In such an analysis, whole 
genome sequencing (WGS) data from both hosts is analysed and visualised by inferring a phylogenetic 
tree, based on the evolutionary parameters derived from the samples (Ghafari et al., 2022).

MAXIMUM LIKELIHOOD AND BAYESIAN APPROACH 
 
There are two approaches for a phylogenetic analysis, namely a Maximum Likelihood 
Estimation (MLE) and a Bayesian approach. Both approaches estimate the phylogeny corresponding to 
the given data. In this project, both approaches are used. The tools used in each of the approaches are 
mentioned in the Materials and Methods section. 
The goal of an MLE approach is to find the optimal phylogenetic parameters, such as tree 
topology, branch length, and nucleotide frequency and mutation rates, that best describe the data. In 
other words, this method constructs a phylogeny which maximises the likelihood of the given data. 
Here, the likelihood is the probability of the observed data, given a set of parameter values (De Maio et 
al., 2023; Sullivan, 2005).  
Contrary to MLE, Bayesian estimation allows for the input of prior values. Since there is prior 
knowledge on the data used in this project, such as the infection rate of SARS-CoV-2, Bayesian 
estimation could be performed. Using a Bayesian approach allows for a sharper estimate of the 
parameters, requiring fewer measurements. 
 
PHYLOGENETIC AND TRANSMISSION TREES 
 
Although a phylogenetic analysis is informative, it does not shed any light on the specifics of 
possible transmission events between hosts. The timing of these transmission events is not indicated in 
a phylogenetic tree due to the pathogenic evolution within each host. It can therefore be understood that 
the phylogenetic tree is a representation of the sum of within-host evolution, where each node of the 
tree represents this sum (Didelot et al., 2017). 
It is furthermore important to note that the internal nodes of a phylogenetic tree do not 
necessarily correspond to transmission events. Transmission events can namely occur at any point along 
the branches of a phylogenetic tree, it is not a given that the branching times in a phylogeny coincide 
with transmission events (Didelot et al., 2017; Hall et al., 2015). 
A phylogenetic tree is constructed to understand the evolutionary relationship between species 
or samples, a transmission tree is constructed to analyse cross-species transmission events. The latter 
infers information about how a pathogen spread from one host to another. To construct a transmission 
tree, sequencing data is implemented together with temporal data, using the collection dates, and 
infectivity times (Didelot et al., 2016). 
A transmission tree can be inferred from a corresponding phylogenetic tree, but there are four 
main factors that need to be taken into consideration: transmission, mutation, case observation, and 
genetic diversity within hosts. These four factors are not well represented in a phylogenetic tree. 
Transmission is essentially who infected whom, this is not depicted in a phylogenetic tree, as mentioned 
earlier. Mutation is the fact that between two sampled sequences, the virus can undergo mutations and 
therefore be falsely labelled as more distant in the phylogeny. Case observation is the assumption that 
not all cases are observed, and so there are essentially gaps in the phylogeny. The latter, genetic diversity 
within hosts, is described below (Didelot et al., 2017; Klinkenberg et al., 2017). 
In this thesis project, a transmission tree is constructed to determine zoonotic and reverse 
zoonotic transmission pathways along a phylogenetic tree. This is done by combining information from 
a phylogeny, together with the known collection dates from the data set and parameters on the genetic 
evolution of SARS-CoV-2.

13 
 
GENETIC DIVERSITY WITHIN HOSTS 
 
Previously, it was assumed that sampled genetic sequences from a transmission pair should be 
identical, or at least near identical. However, this does not consider the principle of genetic diversity 
within a single host. This is the process of a pathogen, in this case a virus, evolving within a host. It 
results in the presence of many variants of the same virus in each individual host. This can cause three 
kinds of difficulties when constructing a phylogenetic and transmission tree: (I) When mutations in the 
sequence arise within a host, but the host is sampled after this mutation, it can falsely indicate a 
transmission event, when in reality, this mutation took place within a single host. (II) When not all 
genetic variants are sampled, transmission events may not be seen from the sampled data (Klinkenberg 
et al., 2017). (III), the sampled sequence from each host only represents a single variant, captured at the 
time the sample was taken, it does not reflect the full spectrum of viral variants. These variants are 
important to consider, since each can be separately transmitted (Didelot et al., 2012). 
The main problem arising from such diversity is that the virus isolates transmitted by a specific 
host are not necessarily genetically identical to the isolates sampled from that same host. 
 
RELEVANCE AND PROJECT AIM 
 
In context of the previously mentioned spread of SARS-CoV-2 viral mink variants among the 
human population, zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmission events between these two hosts, and 
what underlies these events, was investigated in this thesis project. Moreover, this project aims to 
investigate whether viral characteristics, related to transmission events, can be identified. 
This will be done by studying the genetic sequences of both human and mink hosts in the 
context of cross-species transmission events. Furthermore, the corresponding amino acid sequences will 
be studied to identify possible patterns which may underly cross-species transmission potential. 
Identifying such patterns in amino acid sequences, which correspond to changes in the viral protein 
functional sites, can give insight into changes in the viral biological properties. 
This project is of relevance because it is important to monitor amino acid mutations during a viral 
outbreak. If a pattern can be discovered early on in a viral outbreak, or a change that causes an increase 
in virulence, it will allow for a more thorough intervention and a more controlled environment. If a 
certain sequential pattern is identified, related to host-jump events, such events can be intervened with 
early on, or even prevented. 
It can also enable an earlier discovery of dominant strains. For example, during the SARS-CoV-1 
pandemic it only took a single amino acid mutation as a result of antigenic drifts, S:D480A/G, to 
establish dominance among all viral variants in 2003 and 2004. This mutation enabled viral escape of 
neutralising antibodies (Korber et al., 2020). If we can learn from this and monitor similar changes in 
new viral outbreaks, it can be intervened before dominance is established.

MAXIMUM LIKELIHOOD PHYLOGENETIC TREE CONSTRUCTION 
 
A Maximum Likelihood (ML) phylogeny was constructed for all 895 sequences, the full data 
set plus the Wuhan-1 reference genome (GenBank accession no. NC_045512), using both MAPLE 
version 0.3.6, and IQ-TREE version 2.0.7 (De Maio et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2015). Both approaches 
used the General Time Reversible (GTR) nucleotide mutation model. This model is the default in 
MAPLE but specified with option ‘-m GTR’ in IQ-TREE. FigTree version 1.4.4 was used to analyse 
both trees (Rambout, 2018). Several methods were applied to compare the two generated ML 
phylogenies, which are described in Appendix 4. The ML tree constructed with IQ-TREE was used for 
further analyses.

RECONSTRUCTING A PHYLOGENETIC CLOCK-BASED TREE 
 
 
Since the ML phylogeny, constructed with IQ-TREE, does not consider any information on the 
collection dates from the samples, the tree was translated into a clock-based tree. In such a tree, the 
branches are scaled so that the positions of the leaves correspond to their collection dates, and internal 
nodes are placed at the most likely time of divergence. 
 
For this, the tool TreeTime version 0.11.2 was used (Sagulenko et al., 2018). The function ‘--
time-marginal’ was set to ‘true’, to enable marginal inference of the internal node divergence times at 
every round of optimisation. The function ‘--coalescent’ was set to ‘skyline’, to enable estimation of the 
merger rate trajectory in a piece-wise linear manner. The function ‘--gtr’ was set so ‘infer’ to infer a 
model from the data. The flag ‘--stochastic-resolve’ was passed to resolve polytomies. The flag ‘--
report-ambiguous’ was passed to include transitions involving ambiguous states. Lastly, the flag ‘--
confidence’ was passed to allow for the estimation of confidence percentages. 
The resulting clock-based phylogenetic tree was used as input for ancestral sequence 
reconstruction, again using TreeTime version 0.11.2, with the subcommand ‘ancestral’. This is further 
described below. 
 
BAYESIAN PHYLOGENETIC TREE CONSTRUCTION 
 
A dated phylogenetic tree was constructed using BEAST2 (Bouckaert et al., 2019; Didelot et 
al., 2017). The GTR mutation model was used with empirical base frequencies and gamma-distributed 
mutation rates for different sites in the gene with 4 distinct categories. The strict molecular clock model 
was applied with a mean clock rate of 1.0, which is the default value. This model was calibrated by 
using the sequence collection dates, obtained from GISAID, as the tip dates of the tree. The tree prior 
was set to the Birth Death Skyline Serial (BDSS) model. This was done to allow for varying birth and 
death rates throughout the phylogeny, as well as handling data with collection dates over a period of 
time. The prior for the parameter ‘becomingUninfectiousRate’ was set to lognormal(M = 52.0, S = 1.25, 
mean in real space) per year, corresponding to a prior 95% credible interval of [1.3, 180] days for the 
duration of an infectious period. The prior for the parameter ‘clockRate’ was set to lognormal(M = 
0.001, S = 1.25, mean in real space) mutations per site per year, corresponding to a prior 95% credible 
interval of [4.0E-5, 5.3E-3] mutations per site per year. Furthermore, the number of dimensions for the 
parameter ‘reproductiveNumber’ was increased to ten. The parameter ‘samplingProportion’ was set to 
one. The model then estimates a separate effective reproduction number for each of ten equally large 
time-intervals covering the timespan from the root of the tree to the most recent tip. All other priors 
were left at their default values. 
The BEAST analysis was run in two parallel MCMC chains, both spanning 50 million 
iterations, logging every 4,000, thus accumulating a total of 12,500 parameter samples per chain. A 
burn-in of 30% was used, corresponding to 15 million generations. A maximum clade credibility (MCC) 
tree was generated using the software sumt version 3.8.1 (Pedersen, 2023c). 
The software Tracer version 1.7.2 was used the analyse the output from this analysis. The 
marginal posterior distributions from the two parallel chains indicated good convergence of the analysis 
based on their high similarity (Rambaut et al., 2018). The effective sample size (ESS) for most 
combined parameters was above the accepted threshold of 200, for example the ‘origin_BDSKY_Serial’ 
parameter was 3861. The ESS was below the threshold for all nucleotide mutation parameters.

TRANSMISSION TREE CONSTRUCTION  
 
To analyse the trajectory of SARS-CoV-2, a transmission tree was constructed using the R 
package TransPhylo version 1.4.10 (Didelot et al., 2017). This analysis was performed on the 
phylogenies constructed using both the ML and the Bayesian approach. Note that in this report, only 
the data from the Bayesian approach is shown.  
As mentioned above, the phylogeny from the ML approach was constructed with IQ-TREE 
version 2.0.7. Prior to using this distance-based phylogeny as input in TransPhylo, it was converted into 
a clock-based phylogeny using the R package BactDating version 1.1.2 (Didelot et al., 2018). 
Specifically, the function ‘bactdate’ was used to construct a tree with dated nodes from the ML tree. A 
single transmission tree analysis was then initiated for this tree. 
From the Bayesian analysis using the complete data set, 25 random trees from the 12,500 total 
generated trees were selected. This was done using the software phylotreelib version 1.27.0 (Pedersen, 
2023a). For each of these trees, a separate transmission tree analysis was initiated.  
The same was done for 100 random trees derived from the Bayesian analysis using the subset 
of around 250 sequences. Again, this analysis originally generated 12,500 trees, from which 100 trees 
were randomly selected for individual TransPhylo analyses. 
TransPhylo works by exploring the space of possible transmission trees, compatible with the 
dated phylogenetic tree that is given as input (Didelot et al., 2021). The function ‘inferTTree’ was used 
to construct multiple transmission trees. The generation time distribution was set to be a gamma 
distribution with the shape parameter ‘w.shape’ set to 60, and the scale parameter ‘w.scale’ set to 
0.0004105. 
The function ‘inferTTree’ was run for ten million iterations, by specifying the ‘mcmcIterations’ 
argument.  The argument ‘thinning’ was set to 2,000 to enable sampling every 2,000 generations. The 
argument ‘updateOff.p’ was set to ‘true’ to enable estimation of the offspring distribution. Convergence 
was checked by inspecting the generated trace plots. Initially, 5,000 samples of transmission trees were 
generated in each analysis. After applying a burnin of 50%, 2,500 post-burnin samples of transmission 
trees and other parameters were generated. 
From each of the analyses, on the complete data set and the subset, the results were collected 
into data files and analysed in R studio version 4.3.2 (RStudio Team, 2023).

ANALYSIS OF THE NUMBER OF CROSS-SPECIES TRANSMISSION EVENTS  
 
 
For each individual analysis with TransPhylo, the output consists of several thousands of 
transmission trees, as described in the previous paragraph. From these trees, the number of direct and 
indirect transmission events was counted, this was done for both zoonotic and reverse zoonotic 
transmission events. From these counts, the mean value and 90% credible interval were calculated using 
R studio version 4.3.2 (RStudio Team, 2023). 
 
These calculations were performed on the resulting 25 transmission trees from the full dataset, 
as well as on the 100 trees from the subset. For the subset, two separate calculations were performed on 
these resulting transmission trees. Namely, a calculation based on the entirety of the 100 trees, and 
separately a calculation based on the average of 100 times selecting 25 random trees from the output 
from the subset. Meaning, from the 100 trees, 25 were randomly selected. From this subgroup, the 
transmission events were counted. This was repeated 100 times. The average values over these 100 
repetitions were then used to identify whether the number of cross-species transmission events would 
alter depending on the consideration of 25 or 100 trees. 
 
Secondly, events were counted where a zoonotic transmission event was immediately followed 
by a reverse zoonotic transmission event. I.e., a mink infecting a human, which in turn infects another 
mink. Such combinations of transmission events are further referred to as chains of triplet transmissions. 
These calculations were again performed using R studio version 4.3.2 (RStudio Team, 2023). 
 
For the sampled sequences involved in the direct zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmissions, 
and those involved in triplet transmissions, an analysis of the spike protein amino acid sequences with 
tblastn was performed to identify potential changes of the spike protein related to these transmission 
events (Camacho et al., 2009). 
 
PATTERNS ON BRANCHES REPRESENTING CROSS-SPECIES TRANSMISSION EVENTS 
 
As previously described, a phylogenetic tree was constructed with TreeTime version 0.11.2, 
subcommand ‘ancestral’ (Sagulenko et al., 2018). In this tree, the leaves and internal nodes correspond 
to sequences of the spike protein coding region. From these nodes, those that had descendants of both 
human and mink hosts were further investigated. This was done to identify amino acid changes that 
occur on branches representing jumps between species.  
Internal nodes which gave rise to sequences from both host species were aligned using tblastn, 
(Camacho et al., 2009). The spike protein sequence was used as a query, and the DNA sequence of the 
node and its descendants (including other internal nodes) as the subject. As the output is an alignment 
of the spike protein sequence and the amino acid sequence corresponding to the input DNA sequence, 
several changes in the amino acid sequence could be identified.  
 
STRUCTURAL SPIKE PROTEIN ANALYSIS 
 
 
The three-dimensional structure of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein was obtained from the 
Protein Data Bank (PDB). Three different structures were used for analysis. Firstly, the structure of two 
S1 subunits, bound to two human ACE2 receptors, one to each receptor molecule respectively (PDB ID 
6VW1 version 2.1) (Shang et al., 2020). Secondly, the structure of the complete trimeric spike protein, 
unbound but in the conformation in which it can undergo interacting with an ACE2 receptor (PDB ID 
6XM3 version 1.1) (T. Zhou et al., 2020). Meaning a structure where one of the three heterodimers is 
in open conformation while the other two are in closed conformation. Lastly, the structure of two mink 
ACE2 receptors (PDB ID 7WA3 version 1.1) (Su et al., 2021). All protein structures are discussed in 
more detail in Appendix 5.  
 
The software FoldX version 5.0 was used to analyse the effect of mutations on the structural 
protein stability (Schymkowitz et al., 2005). This was done on a single chain from the second structure 
mentioned above (PDB ID 6XM3 version 1.1, chain B), as this chain is in an open conformation. This 
chain was selected from the entire structure and saved as a single pdb file after removal of hydrogens, 
using PyMol version 3.0 (Schrödinger, 2024). 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
20 
 
First, the protein structure was repaired with the function ‘RepairPDB’ from FoldX version 5.0 
(Schymkowitz et al., 2005). The ‘--ionStrength’ was left at 0.05, the ‘--vdwDesign’ at 2, and the ‘--pH’ 
at 7 as per default. The ‘--pdbHydrogens’ was set to ‘false’ as the hydrogens were removed previously 
from the structure in PyMol. This function identifies residues that have bad torsion angles or total energy 
and repairs them. For the used structure (PDB ID 6XM3 version 1.1, chain B), this repair step lowered 
the Gibbs free energy from 998.55 to 511.80, as a result of the relaxation and clash removal within the 
protein structure. In FoldX, a decrease in the value of the Gibbs free energy indicates that the structure 
releases energy, meaning the structure becomes more stable (Schymkowitz et al., 2005). The output is 
the repaired structure as a pdb file. This file was then used for further analyses. 
Hereafter, the repaired structure was used as input for the function ‘PositionScan’, again from 
FoldX version 5.0, to induce several amino acid mutations (Schymkowitz et al., 2005). First, the 
argument ‘--positions’ was set to ‘DB614G’ to induce the amino acid change on chain B from an aspartic 
acid (D) to a glycine (G) residue at position 614. Hereafter, the output file was used as further input as 
the argument ‘--positions’ was set to ‘YB453F’ to change the amino acid residue on chain B, position 
453, from a tyrosine (Y) to a phenylalanine (F) residue. This process was repeated to generate each of 
the identified signatures of amino acid mutations. The output of the ‘PositionScan’ function is, among 
others, a new PDB file with the mutated protein sequence and structure. This file was then used to 
induce further amino acid mutations in.  
After the induction of each mutation, the function ‘RepairPDB’ was ran again, using the same 
parameters as described previously (Schymkowitz et al., 2005). This was done to ensure that each new 
protein structure did not contain any bad torsion angles or residue clashes. 
After each round of the analysis, the change in Gibbs free energy after repair of the structure 
was obtained from the output files. 
Alongside this analysis, the program STRIDER was used to infer the steric hindrance related 
to these amino acid mutations (Patro & Rathinavelan, 2022). This program was used after each 
individual mutation was induced, to analyse the change in steric hindrance over time. 
 
EFFECTS OF MUTATIONS ON PROTEIN INTERACTIONS 
 
 
To analyse the effects of the found mutations on the ability of the spike protein to interact with 
the ACE2 receptor of both hosts, the program ClusPro version 2.0 was used (Desta et al., 2020). For 
this analysis, a single spike protein heterodimer (PDB ID 6XM3 version 1.1, chain B) was used to 
interact with both the mink ACE2 receptor (PDB ID 7F5R version 1.1, chain A) and the human ACE2 
receptor (PDB ID 6VW1 version 2.1, chain A).  
The simulation was performed separately for each of the two ACE2 receptor molecules. These 
single chain PDB files were again obtained by using PyMol version 3.0 to visualise the original PDB 
file, remove water molecules and select the single protein chain of interest (Schrödinger, 2024).  
 
Following this initial simulation, the analysis was repeated but instead with a spike protein 
heterodimer containing the found amino acid mutations, in order of occurrence. Each mutation was 
introduced individually, and a simulation was performed to analyse the change in protein-protein 
interaction. As described previously, the amino acid mutations were introduced using the function 
‘PositionScan’ from FoldX version 5.0 (Schymkowitz et al., 2005).  
 
For each simulation, the RBD of the spike protein was specified under the ‘Attraction and 
Repulsion’ of the advanced options. Each residue, from position 319 up to and including position 541, 
was listed in the ‘ligand attraction’ field.  
 
Each round of simulations was performed twice to ensure confidence in the result. The average 
results of each pair of two simulations are reported in the Result section of this report.

From the analysis of all nodes, several changes in the spike protein amino acid sequence were 
identified. Table 7.1 in Appendix 7 lists each of these mutations, the node or leaf it was identified on, 
the host of the descendants, and if the change in sequence was reversed or not. From this list, several 
mutations are of interest. Namely those that (I) occur on internal nodes, as opposed to those that occur 
on terminal leaves, (II) those that have descendants of both human and mink hosts, (III) those that are 
not reversed at a later date, meaning the change is maintained in all descendants, and lastly (III) those 
that occurred prior or after a cross-species transmission. In addition to this, internal nodes were only 
considered when there were at least 2 generation of nodes between the identified node and its 
descending terminal leaves. This was set to increase the confidence in the persistence of any reversion 
of the amino acid change. The chosen nodes and characteristics of interest are described below, as well 
as displayed in a phylogenetic matter in Figure 7. 
Firstly, at node 3, the mutation S:D614G first occurs. As mentioned previously, this mutation 
is known for its dominance among SARS-CoV-2 sequences collected during the time frame of the data 
in this project. It is therefore not surprising that this change occurs on a node early in the phylogeny. 
The node it first occurs at, node 3, is highly likely a mink host, namely with 99.1% confidence. Its 
parent node is node 1, which is likely a human host. This means that this mutation occurred somewhere 
during a zoonotic transmission from a human to a mink host.  
From this node, several changes are identified on descending nodes. Such as the mutation 
S:Y453F. This mutation occurs first at node 202. This node is most likely in a mink host, given the 
confidence of 99.6%. The mutation is maintained in almost all its descending sequences, as detailed in 
Appendix 3. The parent node to node 202 is node 197, which is likely a human host, with a confidence 
of 79.1%. This would indicate that the mutation first occurred during a reverse zoonotic transmission 
between a human and mink host. 
On another branch of the phylogeny, where the S:Y453F mutation is not acquired, the mutation 
S:F157L occurs. Nevertheless, the descending sequences display zoonotic and reverse zoonotic 
transmission back and forth between mink and human hosts. This mutation first occurs at node 143, 
which is a sequence of a human host with 81.5% confidence. Its parent node is node 142, which is likely 
a human host sequence, with 90.5% confidence. This indicates that this mutation was acquired during 
a transmission between human hosts. A child node of node 143 is node 144, this node is likely a mink 
host with 87.7%  confidence. This indicates that a reverse zoonotic transmission occurred after this 
mutation was acquired.  
Following one of the descending branches from node 202, where the S:Y453F mutation is 
acquired, a deletion is identified of the amino acids histidine (H) and valine (V) at positions 69 and 70, 
respectively. This deletion occurs first at node 228, which is with 99.8% confidence a mink host. The 
parent node is node 227. For this node, its host state is uncertain, namely a confidence of 53.1% and 
46.9% for human and mink, respectively. It is inconclusive if the deletion occurred during a zoonotic 
transmission event from human to mink, or during a transmission between two mink hosts. Nonetheless, 
this deletion is hereafter maintained in all descending sequences. This deletion is known to occur after 
the acquisition of the S:Y453F mutation in the RBD. Node 228 has five nodes as its direct children. 
One of these is a human host, namely node 234 with 74.7% confidence. The other four are likely mink 
hosts, namely nodes 229 with 91.3% confidence, 242 with 98.3% confidence, 297 with 99.6% 
confidence, and 439 with 99.3% confidence. This indicates that the mutation occurred prior to at least 
one zoonotic transmission from a mink to a human host.

Further down the phylogeny, it can be seen that at node 361, the mutation S:S1147L occurs and 
is again maintained in all its respective descendants. This node is likely a mink host, with 98.3% 
confidence. This host gives rise to 48 human host, and 54 mink host sequences. Its parent node is node 
322, a mink host with 99.9% confidence. This node gives rise to several human and mink host sequences 
as well, without having acquired the S:S1147L mutation. This indicates two things, (I) that the mutation 
likely arose during a transmission between two mink hosts, and (II) that the mutation is not required for 
zoonotic transmission between hosts of both species. 
At node 234, two simultaneous mutations occur, namely S:I692V and S:M1229I. This internal 
node is likely a human host, with 74.7% confidence. Its parent node is node 228, which is a mink host, 
with a confidence of 99.8%. It therefore seems that the mutations occur after a zoonotic transmission 
event from a mink to a human host. This could be an indication that after transmission from mink to 
human, these two mutations are required to increase interaction affinity with human ACE2, as opposed 
to mink ACE2.  
Lastly, at node 294, the mutation S:N751Y occurs. The node, as well as its direct parent node, 
which is node 287, are both mink hosts with a confidence of 99.8%. This indicates that the mutation 
likely occurred during a transmission between minks. Hereafter the node gave rise to six mink, and two 
human host descendants. The sister node to node 294 is node 288, which is again a mink node, with a 
confidence of 92.1%. This node has a similar number of descendants, consisting of six human hosts and 
one mink host sequence. This sister node does not acquire the S:N751Y mutation, indicating that the 
mutation is not required to maintain the zoonotic transmission ability.

EFFECTS OF AMINO ACID MUTATIONS ON PROTEIN INTERACTIONS 
 
 
As indicated above, some of the found amino acid mutations decrease the protein stability, 
although an increase in stability is found for most. 
To further investigate the consequences of these mutations on the interaction with the ACE2 
receptor, simulations were performed in ClusPro to measure these protein-protein interactions. Table 5 
below lists how the individual mutations influence the binding to both the human and mink ACE2 
receptors. Each of the amino acid mutations listed in the first column, are induced into the spike protein 
structure in an accumulative manner. I.e. the S:D614G amino acid mutation was induced in the initial 
protein structure. Then, the S:Y453F mutation was induced into this altered structure, and so on. This 
was done to mimic the occurrence of each of the signatures previously mentioned.

highly similar in conformation and binding, meaning the amino acid residues interact in a similar 
manner between the two protein structures.

where a larger cluster size means a higher certainty. For each simulation, the cluster with the largest 
cluster size was selected. From these clusters, the respective energy levels are listed in Table 5. The

cluster, i.e. the mean energy over all members of the cluster. The lowest energy is the most stable 
interaction in the cluster. The difference between the central and lowest energy indicates the level of 
agreement within the selected cluster.

CONCLUSION 
 
 
This chapter summarises the findings of this project and lists any conclusions that can be drawn 
from these findings. 
It seems that there is not one specific genetic signature, i.e. a combination of amino acid 
mutations, that underly viral transmission of SARS-CoV-2. The SARS-CoV-2 spike protein is tolerant 
to mutations, despite its crucial role in host cell interaction and entry.

effects of these mutations on either protein stability or protein-protein interaction.

in literature, such as the cluster 5 variant. However, the mutations in signatures A and C are currently 
not described as a cluster in literature.

The signatures listed in Table 7 above all have an overall stabilising effect on the spike protein 
stability despite having a single mutation which decreases the stability, as mentioned earlier in this 
report. This shows that the virus can allow unbeneficial mutations, as long as the overall result is 
beneficial for its virulence. It furthermore shows that a virus is not always improving with every step 
of its evolution, neither does it always improve after adaptation to a new host. A possible explanation 
for these unbeneficial mutations is the fact that it tries to escape the host immune system via frequent 
changes in its spike protein sequence. 
When ranking the signatures in order of spike protein stability, it seems to be A, B, and C, with 
signature A having the largest stabilising effect. When considering the stability in terms of the 
interaction with the human ACE2 receptor, the order is the same, with signature A resulting in the most 
stable interaction. But, when considering the interaction with the mink ACE2 receptor, the order 
changes to be C, B, A. It is important here to note that all signatures seem to lower the stability between 
the spike protein and the mink ACE2 receptor. But from these three signatures, the most stable 
interaction occurs with the spike protein after the mutations from signature C. 
This project shows that SARS-CoV-2 is able to undergo genetic variation, while maintaining 
functionality and virulence. This indicates the possibility of viral escape upon reinfection, from prior 
infection or vaccine-induced immunity. This project highlights the importance of sampling and 
sequencing efforts to monitor and understand variants circulating during a viral outbreak. 
Lastly, this project shows that zoonotic and reverse zoonotic jumps are not random. It is not 
coincidental that SARS-CoV-2 is able to transmit between minks and humans. This ability comes from 
the high similarity between the ACE2 receptors of both hosts.

DISCUSSION 
 
 
This chapter will discuss some aspects of the project that potentially need further improvement 
or explanation. At the end of the chapter, further research is proposed to continue this work.  
 
LIMITATIONS OF THE STUDY 
 
A limitation of this project was the need to downscale the available data. Not only from the 
initially available 46,723 sequences on GISAID to a data set of 894 sequences, but also the further 
downscaling to generate a subset of only 245 sequences to enable further analyses. The part of the data 
that was mainly downscaled were the sequences from human hosts without the S:Y453F mutation 
present, as these sequences made up 97.85% of all available sequences on GISAID, during the specified 
timeframe. Using several methods, as described previously, this part of the data set was downscaled 
from 45,719 to 188 sequences. Details can be found in Appendix 1. This downscaling of data was 
performed to enable data analysis within the 5-month timeframe of this thesis project, as well as lower 
the computational load of some of the analyses. Primarily analyses such as phylogenetic tree 
construction using a Bayesian approach, have a high computational load. The downside of such 
downscaling is loss of data and thereby potential loss of signals within the data. 
To make up for this potential loss of signal due to downscaling, an in-depth phylogeny was 
constructed of a subset, derived from the original phylogeny constructed using a Bayesian approach, 
which is shown in Appendix 6. Because this subset initially consisted of ~200 sequences, it allowed for 
sequences that were previously filtered out by CD-HIT clustering, but belonged to the clade chosen for 
the subset, to be added back into this subset. This allowed for the signal of any possible transmission 
events within this subset of the data to be fully captured. As mentioned previously, this could not be 
done for the full data set, as the size of the usable data set was limited. 
This subset contained only sequences with the S:Y453F mutation, as it was based on a clade of 
such sequences. This clade was chosen because it contains transmission events, as this is proven by the 
presence of the S:Y453F mutation in sequences derived from humans and minks. Ensuring such 
transmission events to be part of this subset, would enable a more in-depth investigation of what changes 
in a sequence prior, during, and after, a transmission event. Furthermore, the clade for the subset was 
chosen to contain the cluster 5 variant of SARS-CoV-2, as this variant has been identified in sequences 
from both humans and minks in Denmark. This enables a comparison of zoonosis of the cluster 5 variant 
compared to the other identified signatures in this project. Lastly, using data sets of different sizes for 
the counting of zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmissions, ensures that the counted values are not 
influenced by the data set size. 
Due to the relatively short timeframe of the data in this project, there is no conclusive evidence 
found for the inheritance or persistence of the S:Y453F mutation reversion. This reversion is seen in 
the data, but since it appears on a terminal branch of the phylogeny the inheritance of this reversion 
could not be studied. At the time of this project, there are no descending sequences of those where the 
reversion is found available on GISAID. Sequences of a later timeframe were analysed, but these were 
descendants from other sequences in the phylogeny and not from the part of the phylogeny were the 
S:Y453F mutation was initially present nor where its reversion occurred. 
Additionally, this mutation is present in three out of four identified signatures. From the data 
GISAID, it is known that this mutation disappeared from the human population in Denmark in week 4 
of 2021. But due to there not being any later sequences descending from this data, there is no 
information on the persistence and later disappearance of the other found mutations.  
Related to the data availability, is the limitation that not all amino acid mutations were analysed, 
due to these not being present in the available data. An example of this is the mutation S:N501T, which 
has subsequently appeared on several human SARS-CoV-2 variant lineages and has possible zoonotic 
potential. It is not analysed in this project because it was not present in the data set. Additionally, not 
all amino acid mutations, identified in the sequence data, could be studied in the three-dimensional 
structure of the spike protein, for example the S:F157L mutation. This is due to the unavailability of a 
complete, and high quality, spike protein structure, at the time of this project. Any available structures 
in the PDB that met the quality threshold had incomplete sequences. 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
35 
 
Another point for discussion is the methods used to build a phylogeny, as both an MLE and a 
Bayesian approach was used in this project. It can be difficult to determine whether one method is better 
than the other. On the one hand, it is useful that a Bayesian method allows for the incorporation of prior 
knowledge on the data in the analysis. But although useful, it can on the other hand be difficult to 
determine the correct priors. For example, the evolutionary rate prior is highly time-dependent during 
a viral outbreak and can therefore differ between timeframes of data sets. It has been shown that varying 
data set sizes and timeframes affect the accuracy of parameter estimations. Moreover, the viral mutation 
rate elevates along a phylogeny, with the rate being up to four times higher on terminal branches, 
compared to internal branches. This elevation underlies the time-dependency of the average mutation 
rate (Ghafari et al., 2022). Lastly, using an MLE approach allows in general for larger data sets to be 
used, because it is computationally less demanding. Therefore, both approaches were applied to the data 
set in this project. 
In this project, the transmission trees were constructed using the R package TransPhylo version 
1.4.10. This particular package was chosen because it is a Bayesian method, able to infer a transmission 
tree from a timed Phylogenic tree, considering that the true phylogeny is only partially sampled. This 
is the case for the data in this project, as the data does not capsulate the full COVID-19 outbreak. 
Moreover, TransPhylo handles scenarios where the sample collection date ends at a fixed time, before 
the actual end of the viral outbreak. This again is the case in this project. The chosen end date is February 
28th, 2021. This was not the end of the COVID-19 pandemic, but rather a chosen end date based on the 
presence of the S:Y453F mutation in samples from human hosts in Denmark. This is based on data from 
GISAID as described previously (Khare et al., 2021). TransPhylo lastly accounts for the possibility 
more cases may occur in the future, meaning after the most recent collection date from the analysed 
samples. This is again known to be true for the COVID-19 pandemic, when considering the set end date 
of February 28th, 2021. A downside of TransPhylo is that it does not consider differences in generation 
time between different hosts. The generation time in this case is the time between the infection of a 
primary case and one of its secondary cases. Ideally in this project, different generation times should be 
considered for human and mink hosts. 
What should be considered as well is the difference between the two hosts, with respect to 
transmission patterns. TransPhylo implements random mixing for both hosts. It is known for minks that 
they are located at a certain mink farm and can in theory only infect minks and humans at this single 
farm. This means that random mixing is not accurate for the mink population. For humans, however, it 
is not so straightforward either. On the one hand, there should be no transmission patterns considered, 
as humans can relocate freely and interact with many other humans. In that case assuming random 
mixing would be accurate. But on the other hand, studies have shown that humans do display 
transmission patterns, such as the household structure. This indicates that random mixing does not occur 
among the human population either (Didelot et al., 2016; Eldholm et al., 2016). 
Additionally, there is a loss of data when implementing phylogenetic analysis results into 
TransPhylo. This loss of data means that there is an increase in phylogenetic uncertainty. Namely, a 
single phylogenetic tree does not capture the full spectrum of results that are generated by a Bayesian 
analysis, which gives a distribution of phylogenies. To limit this phylogenetic uncertainty, multiple 
phylogenetic trees from the BEAST2 output were used as input into TransPhylo, generating several 
simultaneous analyses. 
The output from TransPhylo was additionally used to identify sequences within the data set 
corresponding to a zoonotic or reverse zoonotic transmission. These sequences were compared with the 
aim to identify patterns related to these transmissions. This data is not reported here because no pattern 
was found. However, it would be interesting to further explore this method with more data or with a 
lower threshold for the frequency. For this report, the threshold of 75% was chosen, meaning that direct 
zoonotic and reverse zoonotic events were only considered if these appeared in at least 75% of the 
output transmission trees. A further analysis with more data or a lower threshold may possibly enable 
the identification of repeating mutations corresponding to zoonotic or reverse zoonotic transmissions. 
Note here that the mutations identified in this project occurred on internal nodes, not on sampled 
sequences. For those analyses, internal nodes were used on purpose to ensure no reversion of the 
occurring mutations.  
 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
36 
 
For the protein structure analysis in this project, protein structures were used that were obtained 
with X-ray diffraction and cryo-electron microscopy. These structures, although they capture several 
states during the measurements, are available as a single structure of the spike protein. This limits how 
representative this structure is of the real protein structure which is namely more dynamic. It is 
furthermore known that during the process of cell membrane fusion between the SARS-CoV-2 viral 
particle and a host cell membrane, the spike protein undergoes drastic conformational changes (Song et 
al., 2018; Walls et al., 2020). These conformational changes make it difficult to conclude whether the 
found amino acid mutations are beneficial for virulence or not, as these findings are based on static 
protein structures. For example, it is known that the S:Y453F mutation lowers the bond between the S1 
subunit and the human ACE2 receptor, as well as the overall protein stability in this static conformation 
(Hopf et al., 2017). But if the bond is nevertheless able to form, then perhaps the lower protein stability 
is beneficial towards conformational changes, and thereby inducing rapid membrane fusion and host 
cell entry. Because protein stability, steric hindrance, and binding interactions with ACE2 were analysed 
using a static form of the spike protein, only partial insight into these processes is given. 
With respect to the interactions between these protein structures, the Gibbs free energy and 
steric hindrance were analysed in this project. These were used as measurements to determine changes 
in these interactions as a result of specific mutations. There are perhaps additional measurements that 
could be significant for these interactions that should be analysed to obtain a full representation of what 
the consequences are of certain amino acid mutations. 
Lastly, this project does not touch upon two topics, related to the virulence of SARS-CoV-2. 
Firstly, that of the host immune system, or viral neutralisation by antibodies. This is because the found 
amino acid mutations, as listed in the signatures of Table 2, do not overlap with the reported linear 
antibody epitopes (Devaux et al., 2021; L. Zhang et al., 2021). Therefore, likely do not affect the host 
immune response. Secondly, the other receptor on the host cell surface that plays a role in virulence, 
namely the neuropilin 1 (NRP1) receptor. The S2 subunit of the spike protein interacts with this receptor 
after the initial bond to the ACE2 receptor, whereafter the cleavage of the two spike protein subunits 
follows. In this project, the found mutations were only investigated with respect to their interaction with 
the ACE2 receptor, not the NRP1 receptor. This is because the NRP1 receptor is conserved among 
species, with a 96.75% identity between the human and mink NRP1 receptors. Furthermore, the residues 
of the NRP1 receptor, known to bind to the S2 subunit, are identical between the two. This suggests that 
the receptors from mink and human hosts are likely to bind to the same SARS-CoV-2 spike proteins in 
a similar manner (Devaux et al., 2021). Thus, mutations in this spike protein will cause a similar effect 
in the interaction with both NRP1 receptors. 
 
IDENTIFIED MUTATIONS 
 
 
Research has been done on predicting future amino acid mutations in the SARS-CoV-2 spike 
protein. These studies focus specifically on the RBD, and on possible mutations herein which would 
increase the infectivity. None of the mutations found in this project, as listed in Table 2, were identified 
in this research (Chen et al., 2020). This is due to two reasons. First, with the exception of the S:Y453F 
mutation, the found mutations from this project are located outside the RBD. Second, these studies do 
not take zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmission into account when evaluating infectivity, but rather 
focus solely on the interaction with the human ACE2 receptor (Chen et al., 2020).  
There are residues at certain positions of the RBD known to have a high mutation frequency, 
such as the asparagine (N) residue at position 439. A known mutation, S:N439K, was prevalent in 
Denmark at the time of which sequences were collected for this project, namely from August to 
December of 2020. At that time, around 10% of all sequences from Denmark contained this mutation. 
It did not persist, despite the fact that it enhances the binding affinity with the human ACE2 receptor 
due to the formation of a salt bridge (Thomson et al., 2021). It is however not found in the data of this 
project. Of interest is that this mutation is often followed by the delH69/V70, similar to the S:Y453F 
mutation. In both cases, the deletion is acquainted after the mutation and persist in descending sequences 
(Thomson et al., 2021). 
 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
37 
 
What is here identified as signature B, is known in literature as cluster 5. This variant of SARS-
CoV-2 is known to be a specific viral adaptation to mink hosts. It consequently spread to human hosts 
where it has a weak reaction to neutralising antibodies (Devaux et al., 2021). Additionally, the findings 
of this project suggest that this signature results in an increased stability of not only the spike protein, 
but also its interaction with the human ACE2 receptor. This could explain why this variant of SARS-
CoV-2 was able to spread from the mink to the human population. 
The amino acid mutations from signature C, as identified in this project, are S:D614G, 
S:Y453F, and S:S1147L. The amino acid residues prior to mutation, namely aspartic acid (D), tyrosine 
(Y), and serine (S), have been reported to exist together in a viral variant specific to mink hosts (Devaux 
et al., 2021). It is of interest that these residues are considered to exist and  thrive in mink hosts, as the 
mutations from signature C are also found in mink hosts in this project. That indicates that the initial 
residues at these positions are not crucial for viral infectivity of mink hosts and could potentially benefit 
from mutations. 
 
Here, some of the identified amino acid mutations from this project will be further discussed. 
Specifically, whether the findings from this project align with what is reported in literature, and if not, 
where differences occur. 
 
S:D614G 
 
 
From the conformational changes as a result of the interaction between the spike protein and 
the ACE2 receptor, as shown in Appendix 8, it can be seen that the signatures A to C have a similar 
conformation. This conformational change, as opposed to the interaction prior to any mutations, is 
therefore likely the result of two mutations. Namely S:D614G and S:Y453F, as these two mutations are 
shared between the three signatures. It is described in literature that a conformational change occurs as 
a result of solely the S:D614G mutation, as well as an increase in infectivity (Becerra-Flores & Cardozo, 
2020). However, when analysing the steric hindrance in this project, it appears that the S:D614G 
mutation does not change the steric hindrance, whereas the S:Y453F mutation increases it. This increase 
in steric hindrance indicates that it is more likely that a conformational change would occur due to the 
S:Y453F mutation. It cannot be said with certainty which of the two mutations causes the 
conformational change. 
 
S:Y453F 
 
 
The S:Y453F mutation is found in three out of four identified signatures. In literature, this 
mutation is described to be an advantageous viral adaptation, increasing the affinity towards the mink 
ACE2 receptor. It is furthermore described as a destabilising mutation in human hosts, lowering the 
bond between the spike protein and the human ACE2 receptor and thus the viral infectivity. 
Assuming that the mutation is beneficial for the viral interaction with the mink ACE2 receptor 
does not explain why the mutation is able to establish and persist in human hosts after arising in minks. 
However, the data in this project indicates that the mutation does not remain present among the human 
population for a long period of time. This is supported by literature suggesting that this variant of SARS-
CoV-2 is no longer present among the human population since September 15th, 2020 (De Fleste 
Restriktioner Lempes i Nordjylland, 2020). These findings agree with the statement that this mutation 
is not particularly beneficial in human hosts.  
Finally, there is further evidence in literature suggesting that the S:Y453F mutation is not 
specifically beneficial in human hosts. Namely, the tyrosine (Y) residue at position 453 in SARS-CoV-
2 is comparable, in both function and location, to the same residue at position 440 in SARS-CoV-1 (Lan 
et al., 2020). There has been no evidence for a mutation at this position during the SARS-CoV-1 
outbreak. If this amino acid mutation had occurred and persisted during the SARS-CoV-1 outbreak in 
human hosts, it would support the statement that a mutation at this position increases virulence or is 
otherwise beneficial. 
It is noteworthy that the S:Y453F mutation does not seem to be necessary for the virus to be 
able to undergo zoonosis and reverse zoonosis. As can be seen from the phylogeny in this project, 
transmissions across species occurred on branches with and without this mutation present. 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
38 
 
In contrast to the lowering strain and increase in stability within the spike protein structure as a 
result of this mutation, analysis of the steric hindrance revealed an increase in steric hindrance as 
previously mentioned. This increase in steric hindrance could be an incentive for the spike protein to 
undergo a more rapid conformational change after entry into the host cell. This could mean that, 
regardless of the host, this mutation increases the speed of host cell entry and the conformational 
changes necessary to induce this process. 
 
S:DELH69/V70 
 
Of importance is also the deletion of the amino acids histidine (H) and valine (V), at positions 
69 and 70 of the S1 subunit of the spike protein, respectively. This pattern is known to occur after 
mutations in the RBD, such as S:Y453F, have taken place. The deletion is reported in literature to occur 
in human hosts where it enhances viral infectivity. It is suspected that it occurs as a means to redeem 
the decrease in infectivity after the S:Y453F mutation is acquainted. However, there are also viral 
lineages reported that have acquainted this deletion, but no further accompanying mutations (Meng et 
al., 2021).  
It is therefore interesting that in this project, several clades of sequences that have the S:Y453F 
mutation did not acquire the deletion. This indicates that the deletion is not necessary for the virus to 
survive and recover its virulence in human hosts after the S:Y453F mutation is acquired. This is perhaps 
supported by the finding that the deletion does not enable an antibody escape mechanism, implying that 
the deletion is not of crucial importance for survival (Meng et al., 2021). 
Furthermore, there appears, from the phylogenetic analysis in this project, to be no difference 
in the zoonotic and reverse zoonotic events after occurrence of the S:Y453F mutation, with and without 
the deletion. 
It is of interest that in this project, the deletion first occurred in a mink host. This is contrary to 
what would be expected; if the deletion functions as a way to redeem infectivity after this is decreased 
by the S:Y453F mutation in human hosts, it is assumed that the deletion will occur in a human host. As 
mentioned in the Results section, the deletion occurred on a node of which the parent node is uncertain. 
It can therefore not be concluded if the deletion occurred as a result of a transmission between two mink 
hosts, or a zoonotic transmission between a human and mink host. 
 
S:S1147L 
 
This mutation has only been reported in analyses of sequences obtained from Denmark. It first 
occurred after the spread among minks was already established. As this spread among minks originated 
from a reverse zoonotic transmission from human to mink hosts, this could indicate that the mutation 
did not occur to enable zoonotic or reverse zoonotic transmission between these hosts. 
In this project, the mutation was first seen in a sequence belonging to a mink host. Its parent 
node belongs to a mink host as well, suggesting that the mutation occurred during a viral transmission 
between minks. As mentioned in the Results paragraph, the parent node gives rise to several mink and 
human nodes as well, this suggests that the mutation is not required for zoonotic or reverse zoonotic 
transmission. 
 
S:I692V AND S:M1229I 
 
As mentioned before, the mutations S:I692V and S:M1229I are acquired at the same time. 
These both lie outside of the RBD of the spike protein. This is an indication that these mutations do not 
play a direct role in the interaction between the spike protein S1 subunit and ACE2 receptor.  
As is mentioned earlier, the former mutation could not be included in the protein-protein 
interaction analysis due to an incomplete amino acid sequence of the S1 subunit. 
From this interaction analysis with the S:I692V mutation, it can be seen that it has no further 
stabilising or destabilising effect on the interaction with the human ACE2 receptor, compared to the 
prior occurring S:Y453F mutation. As for the interaction with the mink ACE2 receptor, the mutation is 
slightly stabilising, but the difference is negligible. 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
39 
 
When analysing the spike protein structure on its own, the Gibbs free energy becomes more 
positive, indicating a destabilisation of the spike protein.  
With high confidence in the host state of both nodes, this mutation likely occurred during a 
zoonotic transmission from a mink host to a human host. This indicates that the mutation occurred as a 
means to improve virulence or infectivity in human hosts. But, as mentioned before, there is no clear 
indication of an increase of virulence in human hosts, based on the performed analyses. 
It is also important to note that these findings are inconclusive because the second mutation, 
S:M1229I, is not modelled. Since these two mutations occur at the same time in the phylogeny, they are 
likely connected and might have an accumulative effect.  
 
FURTHER RESEARCH AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVE 
 
The amino acid mutations identified in this project are all located outside of the RBD sequence 
region of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein, with the exception of the S:Y453F mutation. This indicates 
that after this mutation occurs, a further change in the protein sequence of the RBD is not necessary to 
facilitate the binding or interaction with the human or mink ACE2 receptors.  
It is important to note that it is contrary to expectation that the identified mutations in this 
project lie outside of the RBD. It has been reported that mutations in the RBD that increase the free 
energy directly decrease viral infectivity, because of its crucial role in cross-species transmission and 
viral evolution (Letko et al., 2020; Starr et al., 2020). It should be further studied if this same effect 
occurs as a result of mutations outside of the RBD. This project shows that such mutations can also alter 
the free energy in the interaction between the spike protein and the ACE2 receptor, their effect on viral 
infectivity should be further investigated. 
Additionally, from the amino acid mutations studied in this project, none were located in any 
of the hot spots defined in literature (Mullick et al., 2021). This indicates that a more thorough 
investigation of the entire (spike protein) sequence is required, not only several hot spots which were 
identified early on in the spread of SARS-CoV-2. Furthermore, perhaps the unanalysed mutations are 
present in these hot spots. It could then be of interest to see where these differ from other mutations. 
Furthermore, it indicates that any other following changes in the sequence and structure of the 
spike protein are to improve overall protein structure, folding, or stability, as opposed to host ACE2 
receptor interaction. This perspective was analysed in this project with the use of three-dimensional 
protein structures. As indicated in a previous paragraph, it is important to keep in mind that these 
structures are obtained primarily by X-ray diffraction and depict a static formation of the protein 
structure. In reality, a protein structure is more fluid and adapts slightly different conformations over 
time. On top of this, it is known that the conformation of the spike protein drastically changes during 
its interaction with a host ACE2 receptor and cell membrane integration. Thus, the consequences of the 
found amino acid mutations should be tested further with experimentally derived protein structures to 
accurately model the consequences of these mutations in their complexity. 
It could furthermore be of interest to repeat the Bayesian phylogeny construction on a larger 
data set. When considering data from different countries as well, it would be interesting to see if the 
same signatures are found. For the timeframe of this project, the data set had to be relatively small, but 
it would be of additional value to consider more sequences when constructing a phylogeny. 
From the found amino acid mutations only a few were considered of interest for the purpose of 
this project. It could however also be interesting to evaluate the other amino acid changes as well. Some 
found amino acid mutations did not reverse, therefore technically meeting the criterion to be of interest 
for this project. However, these occurred on nodes with only one intermediate node descendant before 
reaching the terminal leaves. This means that reversion could not be studied with certainty for these 
mutations. Furthermore, several of these mutations could not be modelled into the three-dimensional 
structure of the spike protein due to an incomplete sequence. Lastly, other identified mutations seemed 
to have occurred on a transmission between two hosts of the same species and had only descendants 
from this same host species as well, thus not being relevant to study zoonotic and reverse zoonotic 
transmissions. However, studying such mutations could give insight into what potentially disables the 
virus to undergo transmissions across different host species.

It is known that SARS-CoV-2, like other viruses, exhibits an increase in evolutionary rate in 
response to transmission events between different hosts. This is a result of greater selective pressures 
upon introduction to a new host (Porter et al., 2023). This is primarily seen in exposed viral proteins 
such as the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein (Devaux et al., 2021). This suggests that the amino acid 
mutations found in this project are not enabling zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmissions but are 
rather a result. Hereafter, it is likely that mutations are conserved if these result in an increase in viral 
fitness. These conserved mutations are then transferred back to the initial host species as well via 
spillback events. This is supported by the fact that the mutations found in this project disappear from 
the human population after the mink population was culled in Denmark. As such viral evolution 
contributes to the generation of viral variants among the human population, it is important to have 
ongoing surveillance of zoonotic SARS-CoV-2 infections in the future. 
With the ability of SARS-CoV-2 to spread among several different species, transmission events 
across species will continue to be a risk for the human population. Thus, it is important to analyse the 
ACE2 receptor of potential hosts. Understanding the differences and similarities between these ACE2 
receptors will give further insight into potential zoonotic and reverse zoonotic transmissions. 
Furthermore, how the virus might evolve when introduced into a new host (Bazykin et al., 2021). 
As mentioned previously, one of the identified signatures in this project is the cluster 5 variant 
of SARS-CoV-2. This variant, according to the analyses performed here, was not the most stabilising 
from the signatures, that was namely signature A. Furthermore, the latter seemed most beneficial in the 
interaction with the human ACE2 receptor. These findings should be further confirmed by considering 
other aspects of SARS-CoV-2 virulence, such as resistance to antibody neutralisation, which is not 
studied in this project. Doing so will enable a better determination of which signature generates the 
highest viral fitness advantage.

APPENDIX 3: S:Y453F AMINO ACID MUTATION 
 
Two analyses were performed related to the investigation of the onset and reversion of the 
S:Y453F amino acid mutation. This appendix will cover both analyses. 
 
APPENDIX 3.1: PHYLOGENY-BASED ANALYSIS 
 
To visualise the relationship between all sequences, prior to clustering with CD-HIT, a 
phylogenetic tree was constructed on the entire data set of 22,734 sequences (data not shown). For this 
tree construction, the MAximum Parsimonious Likelihood Estimation (MAPLE) software, version 
0.3.6 was used (De Maio et al., 2023), with a GTR mutation model. FigTree version 1.4.4 was used for 
visualisation of this tree (Rambout, 2018). 
A clock-based phylogenetic tree was then constructed, using the MAPLE phylogeny as input, 
as well as information from the collection date. For this, the tool TreeTime version 0.11.2 was used, 
with the same functions applied as described in the Materials and Methods section (Sagulenko et al., 
2018). 
Hereafter, this clock-based phylogeny was used as input for a mugration analysis, aiming to 
identify the presence of the S:Y453F mutation in all internal nodes. Again, TreeTime version 0.11.2 
was again used, specifically the ‘mugration’ subcommand. The flag ‘--confidence’ was passed to allow 
for the estimation of confidence intervals. 
 
From this analysis, it appears that the S:Y453F mutation first appeared after a divergence at a 
node early in the phylogeny. This node was likely a sequence from a mink host, with 99.98% 
confidence. Among the descendants of this node, it appears that there was twice a reversion of the 
S:Y453F mutation, as indicated by the red coloured leaves in Figure 10 in the Results section of this 
report. The parent nodes of these two sequences are likely both human hosts, with 93.34% and 98.63% 
confidence, respectively. The first node has a total of seven descending sequences, all derived from 
human hosts. Namely 2020-06-08_H_618092, and 6 sequences with the mutation present: 2020-06-
08_H_671262_Y, 2020-06-15_H_618104_Y, 2020-06-22_H_714932_Y, 2020-06-22_H_622277_Y, 
2020-07-06_H_618190_Y, and 2020-07-20_H_618193_Y. The latter has two descending sequences 
from human hosts, 2020-11-09_H_668817_Y and 2020-11-16_H_929391.  
 
The two sequences without the S:Y453F mutation, 2020-06-08_H_618092 and 2020-11-
16_H_929391 were initially filtered out by CD-HIT clustering, as described in the Materials and 
Methods section. To preserve the signal of a possible reversion, these two sequences were added back 
into the data set. 
 
APPENDIX 3.2: ALIGNMENT-BASED ANALYSIS 
 
To ensure that the relation between the most-recent human sequences without the S:Y453F 
mutation and the earliest sequences with this mutation was preserved in the data, an alignment was 
performed using BLAST version 2.15.0 (Tao, 2010). For this alignment, all 448 human sequences with 
the S:Y453F mutation present were aligned to the 22,028 human sequences that do not contain this 
mutation, see step four of the processing steps as listed in Appendix 2. 
From this analysis, it was observed that all 448 human sequences with the S:Y453F mutation 
present, matched closely to 492 human sequences without the mutation. With a percentage identity

First, a phylogenetic tree was built using MAPLE version 0.3.6 (De Maio et al., 2023). The 
applied mutation model was GTR. Hereafter, FigTree version 1.4.4 was used to visualise the constructed 
tree (Rambout, 2018) (data not shown).

APPENDIX 4: MAXIMUM LIKELIHOOD PHYLOGENY COMPARISON 
 
This appendix describes how two constructed ML phylogenies were compared. One tree was 
constructed using MAPLE version 0.3.6, the other using IQ-TREE version 2.0.7 (De Maio et al., 2023; 
Nguyen et al., 2015). Despite both methods implementing the GTR model and being derived from the 
same data set, there were differences between the two trees. To analyse these differences in a constructed 
manner, several methods were applied, described below. 
First, the Robinson-Foulds (RF) distance was calculated using MAPLE version 0.3.6 (De Maio 
et al., 2023; Robinson & Foulds, 1981). The RF distance is a comparison of the clades of every node in 
the trees and counts how many of them are not shared by both trees (Day, 1985). The results are 
displayed in Table 4.1. The RF value is the number of branches present in one of the two trees, but not 
in the other. As this value is highly correlated to the total number of branches in a tree, the RF value is 
normalised on the number of branches in the first tree. The normalised RF thus ranges from 0 to 1, with 
0 meaning perfect similarity. The number of found branches corresponds to branches found in both 
trees. The number of missed branches is those found in the second tree, but not in the first tree. The 
number of not found branches is those found in the first tree, but not in the second tree. Lastly, the RFL 
value is the length-scaled RF value, again normalised. It is calculated from the RF; for each branch, the 
score is incremented by the absolute value of the difference in corresponding branch lengths between 
the two trees. Both the RF and RFL values have complementary qualities and are used together to 
evaluate the difference between two trees (Kuhner & Yamato, 2015; Robinson & Foulds, 1979). Table 
4.1 shows both the comparison of the MAPLE maximum likelihood tree to the IQ-TREE maximum 
likelihood tree (row A), and vice versa (row B).

APPENDIX 5: THREE-DIMENSIONAL SPIKE PROTEIN STRUCTURES 
 
In this project, three different protein structures are used in different analyses. These structures 
are obtained from the PDB in March of 2024. They are discussed in more detail below. 
First, the structure of two S1 subunits, bound to two human ACE2 receptors (PDB ID 6VW1 
version 2.1). Each S1 subunit is bound to a single receptor. An altered version of this structure is shown 
in Figure 11 in the Results section of this report. The S1 subunit of the spike protein contains the RBD. 
The RBD spans from an arginine residue (R) at amino acid position 319 to a phenylalanine residue (F) 
at position 541. The sequence of the S1 subunit that is structured here spans from residue position 19 
to 535. The structure is obtained using X-ray diffraction, it has a resolution of 2.68 Å. This value gives 
information about the quantity of data that was extracted from the crystal structure. A lower value here 
indicates a higher resolution, and thus a larger quantity of data. A good resolution should not be over 4 
Å, as this is where sidechains will no longer be modelled accurately. The structure has R-free and R-
work values of 0.229 and 0.197, respectively. The R-free is the difference between the measured data 
and the model, where the model is a reconstruction of the protein structure by assigning conformations 
to the main and sidechains, based on part of the measured data. It is important here to note that only 
part of the data is used for model construction, in order to avoid overfitting. A good R-free value should 
be less than 10% of the resolution, which is the case for this structure. Furthermore, the R-work value 
should not be more than 0.05 lower than the R-free value, which is again the case for this structure 
(Shang et al., 2020). There are a few residues missing from the PDB structure, this is likely due to a 
high B-factor, or temperature factor, in these regions. This is often the result of high flexibility in these 
regions, causing uncertainty in the model.  
Second, the structure of a complete trimeric spike protein (PDB ID 6XM3 version 1.1). In this 
structure, one of the S1 subunits is in the conformation in which it can undergo binding to the ACE2 
receptor. The structured sequence spans from residue position 14 to 1208. It is obtained using cryo-
electron microscopy and has a resolution of 2.90 Å (T. Zhou et al., 2020). Since it is not obtained using 
X-ray diffraction, there is no measure of the R-free or R-work values. The structure can be seen in figure

Figure 8.2 shows that the interaction between the ACE2 receptor and the spike protein seems 
to change in three-dimensional space in the same way for each of the signatures. This can be seen for 
both the human and mink ACE2 receptors. This indicates that this conformational change is induced by 
the shared amino acid mutations, namely S:D614G and S:Y453F. It seems that the other amino acid 
mutations induce no further change in the interaction.
