Abstract
Abstract
Injection Compression Moulding (ICM) is the leading process technology
for the mass manufacturing of high precision polymer optics. The utilisation
of ICM in Fresnel lenses production has been assessed, keeping the focus on
the technological signature of the process to the ﬁnal product. By means of
a proper design of experiments, it has been evaluated the optimal process
window, to ensure the optimal replication quality, in terms of geometrical
dimensions and surface roughness characteristics. For the case of polymer
Fresnel lenses, the biggest constraints to promote the process optimization,
are metrological related aspects. The lens proﬁle segmentation and slope,
coupled with the material transparency, require dedicated measuring equip-
ment, able to acquire accurate information in reasonable time. Remarkably,
the use of a laser scanning confocal microscope has been characterized and
evaluated to perform the measures. To do so a complete uncertainty budget
and a measurement noise estimation have speciﬁed the instrument’s physical
limit. The results have shown not only the possibility to employ the instru-
ment for the analysis of the discussed samples, but also a valuable process
optimization has been defended and proposed. At the end of this work, the
optimal process parameters will be described, weighting the importance of
both packing and compression steps.
Keywords: Injection compression moulding ICM, Laser scanning confo-
cal microscope, Technological signature, Fresnel lenses, DOE

1.1 Projects background
Fresnel lenses are particular optics with reduced dimensions and enhanced
light gathering properties. Considering a strong demand of Fresnel lenses in
mobile ﬂashlights, automotive, indoor/outdoor lighting systems and in gen-
eral in many other optical applications, the study of more precise, accurate
and repeatable moulding processes is a key step to reduce costs and improve
quality of these products. The structured shape of the Fresnel lens pattern, is
anyway a complex target to be achieved with ordinary moulding techniques.
Moreover, the lens design requires an accurate metrological eﬀort in order to
measure a transparent micro-structured component. Injection Compression
Moulding (ICM) is the leading process technology for the mass manufacturing
of high precision polymer optics [9]. The injection compression technology
can be considered a natural extension of the traditional injection moulding
process for thermoplastic materials and it is characterized by two phases.
1. Injection: during this phase the mould cavity is kept partially open
to facilitate the ﬂow of plastic inside the cavity. Mechanical design
solutions are implanted for the closure of the cavity in order to prevent
the spillage of the polymer outside the mould
2. Compression: during the injection phase or at the end of the injection
phase, the machine closing force reduces the thickness of the mould
cavity to the real thickness of the component and the polymer is driven

distribution of the pressure into the mould cavity, unlike traditional

2.1 Fresnel Lenses
Fresnel lenses are optical tools with enhanced light gathering properties com-
bined with a more compact design. Fresnel, Augustin-Jean (Broglie 1788 -
Ville d’Avray, Paris, 1827) developed such a device thanks to its genius and
intuitive understanding of ﬁnite source light diﬀraction theories. Fresnel idea
was based on a simple conceptual collapsing of a known aspherical lens. This
fact, allowed him to obtain both:
 Lighter and more compact design Figure 2.1
 Reduction of spherical aberration Figure 2.2
The possibility to decrease the thickness leads to a lighter design and a
cost reduction due to material saving. This means an extended potential
ﬁeld of applications of this device. In fact, this product has been exploited
extensively in many diﬀerent sectors despite the more complex geometrical

design and more demanding manufacturing technique with respect to con-
ventional lenses. The advantage of using a smaller device is not only eﬀective
in terms of encumbrance and space but also in terms of optical performance.
The major advantage is against spherical aberration. This phenomenon hap-
pens because incident light rays undergo a diﬀerent refraction/reﬂection path
along the lens proﬁle. Rays that strike near the edge are focused on diﬀerent
spot in comparison with those that strike nearer the centre. Hence there
is no more a well-deﬁned focal position for a point source, but an extend
region and the sharpness of an image is aﬀected [7]. In many application is
important to reduce spherical aberration without using thick aspheric proﬁle
geometries. In Figure 2.2 the advantage against spherical aberration using
a Fresnel lens is outlined. Diﬀerent studies have been published describing
how to reduce spherical aberration while designing Fresnel lenses [1,11].

2.1.1 Applications
In this subsection a short historical introduction on Fresnel lenses application
will introduce to nowadays implementations and development.
History
The very ﬁrst application of Fresnel lens was in a lighthouse. Since in the
19th century, lenses were manufactured by means of glass, mostly in semi-
spherical shapes, their capability to emit light beams at long distance was
poorly eﬀective [13]. The presence of impurities and the anisotropy of the ma-
terial, leads to uneven aberrations and absorptions of light passing through
the optical medium even for nowadays products [11]. With its invention,
Fresnel was able to create a signiﬁcantly smaller lens with lighter design,
easier to handle and at the same time with better optical transmission eﬃ-
ciency, enabling a new generation of lighthouses to be spotted at distances
never imagined before [12]. Since then, Fresnel lenses have been constantly
improved both in terms of manufacturing technologies and materials, ﬁnding
more and more industrial applications. Just to mention few of the possi-
bilities in terms of technological improvement lead by Fresnel, the Kilauea
Lighthouse2.3(a) was built in 1916 weight’s 4100 kg and holds a record focal
distance of 17 km range, a real record for a century old age.

2.1.2 Manufacturing
At the moment there are several possibilities to manufacture Fresnel lenses.
As an example, a study has been developed to produce micro-lenses with
nano-feature [21] by means of laser ablation micro-machining. Macro-scale
lenses as lighthouses are now produced by direct polymer blocks machining
and subsequent mechanical/chemical polishing.
However, moulding tech-
niques are the standard processes to produce meso- and micro-scale size

Simulation and process characterization
In this paragraph a summary of the major studies on ICM processes has been
summed up, showing the signiﬁcance factors of the process itself. This sum-
mary wants to preclude the scenario towards optimization, which is nothing
more that the scope of this thesis essay.
Optimization is a continuous need in manufacturing and also simulation
can provide a sensible help [25].
Simulations of the injection-compression moulding ICM process based
on a Leonov viscoelastic ﬂuid model was employed to study the eﬀects of
processing conditions on the birefringence development and distribution in
injection-compression moulded parts. Few of the results from this study [26]
showed that birefringence of ICM part had a low value near the gate area
and exhibits a high value at locations far away from the gate. Highlighting
the importance of a coherent mould, ﬂow design. Secondly higher packing
pressure result in lower birefringence values; however, the eﬀects are not very
signiﬁcant.
A Hele–Shaw ﬂuid-ﬂow model combined with a modiﬁed control-volume
ﬁnite-element method is implemented to predict the melt-front advancement
and the distributions of pressure, temperature, and ﬂow velocity dynamically
during the injection melt ﬁlling, compression melt ﬁlling, and post-ﬁlling
stages of the entire process. For this study [27,28] the main factor that af-
fects the warpage of the products during the injection compression process is
the melt temperature, injection speed, mould temperature, compression gap,
and compression speed. This study showed that the material ﬁrstly ﬁlled the
cavity, and then the micro structures.

3.1 Injection compression moulding machine
The experimental campaign required the utilization of an injection compres-
sion moulding machine. A dedicated tooling, allowed to ensure the desired
lens replication. A ultra high accurate insert geometry and machine construc-
tion were at the foundations of a precise process. In order to perform the
experimentation, NegriBossi ©v 70-180 ICM was exploited (see Figure 3.1).
An industrial laboratory provided this machine, which was used to test the
production of optical components. The machine is basically concept as a
conventional injection moulding, but equipped with a dedicated set-up to
enhance replication quality of micro feature for optical parts.
The injection unit
The injection unit is the rear part of the machine responsible to provide the
melt material to the mould. Inside the unit the polymer is brought to the
melt temperature at the right injection viscosity. The injection unit contains

is switched on the static pressure inside the cavity. The moment when this
transition occurs is called switch/over point, and must be accurately selected
in order to have full replication of the specimen. The switch/over point is
regulated with respect to the axial position of the reciprocating screw. After
the control is set on pressure, a post-injection step is generally performed.
This operation is called packing and serves the polymer to compensate to
shrinkage while it cools down. During this phase pressure is kept constant so
that shrinkage due to thermal relaxation is compensated in an homogeneous
way. Additional material enters the cavity in this phase. The packing pres-
sure is regulated by the operator. An hydraulic actuator presses the back of
the reciprocating screw allowing to keep the pressure constant. The packing

The confocal microscope architecture
A laser confocal microscope exploit a particular architectureFigure 3.11 that
allows a unique measuring technique. A laser source, that for the case is a
405 nm semiconductor, generates the laser beam. This one pass through a
ﬁrst movable pinhole, called illumination pinhole, setting its position allows
to select the laser intensity. After that, the laser pass through a ﬁeld lens,
a so-called tube lens, that creates a parallel beam [8]. The optical path can
be secondly deviated and pointed to the objective, the last lens before the
object. Because of that, a coaxial conﬁguration would be preferred, indeed
the chance to produce aberrations [51]. Once the beam hits the specimen,
light comes back into the objective and tube lens, up to the beam splitter.
The splitter projects the beam on the laser intensity detector. The confocal
principal is provided by the second pinhole, ﬁxed before the sensor. This
is actually a mechanical ﬁlter, that allows laser to get to the sensor only if
the optical path has a certain length. This length is designed in a way that

4.3 Measurement noise estimation
On the bases of the previous section results, a strong need to understand the
instrument physical limit arises. A further characterization of the measuring
equipment was performed. To do so, measurement noise was estimated by
means of averaging and subtraction methods [7,56] calculating Sqnoise index.
Noise is a concept that contains all the stochastic components related to not
repeatable measuring conditions. Those can be distinguished into static noise
and measurement or dynamic noise. The distinction is meant to indicate, on
the one hand side the disturbances on a static stage measure. Where static
means absence of a possible movement of the instrument stage, measuring
probe or axes. While the measurement noise contains the static noise plus
the other disturbances due to the dynamics of the measuring procedure, i.e.:
laser scanning displacement, stitching stage displacement, etc. Sq is a surface
texture parameter deﬁned in Equation 4.1 [49]:

Conclusions
The whole project has spread along an entire value chain related to a polymer
Fresnel lens production.
The injection compression moulding process has
been assessed for diﬀerent parameters conditions by means of DOE(s), while
a complete characterization of the laser scanning confocal microscope has
proofed innovative measuring strategies. The conclusion can be at last drawn
with respect to the reference topic.
On metrology:
1. Transparent polymer Fresnel lenses of two diﬀerent materials as COP
and PMMA, have been measured by means of a laser scanning confocal
microscope. A Fresnel squared pattern dimension of 40.2 mm x 40.2
mm, showing low aspect ration, with a groove pitch of 748.0 µm and
step height range from 17.3 µm to 346.3 µm have been designed. The
instrument has shown the capability to address the length dimensions.
2. To perform the roughness measures, it has been studied the most
favourable instrument set-up, tanking into account the objectives op-
tical magniﬁcation and working distance. The standard series objec-
tives with 50x optical magniﬁcation has been identiﬁed in the most
favourable roughness inspector, against long working distance series
95
==PAGE:114==
Chapter 8
and 100x optical magniﬁcation. Moreover the use of a darkened en-
vironment, freed from lighting did not mean a variation in the re-
sults mean. However a secondary noise estimation assessment has con-
strained the possibility to perform surface roughness analysis.
3. To evaluate the instrument limitations, measurement noise has been
estimated by means of averaging and subtraction method.
The use
of a calibrated optical ﬂat allowed an estimation of noise for 20x and
50x optical magniﬁcation objectives of the standard working distance
series. It has been estimated a noise of 10 nm, in accordance with the
ideal vertical resolution of the instrument.
4. An ’on-sample’ noise estimation procedure has been validated compar-
ing measurement noise on an optical ﬂat with estimated noise directly
on the Fresnel lens specimen. A direct comparison has shown the same
estimated noise of the selected objectives of 10 nm.
5. A well deﬁned acquisition and processing procedure has been selected
to perform pitch and step height measures on the Fresnel lens grooves.
A detailed uncertainty budget has deﬁned the expanded uncertainty U
related to the measuring procedures and process repeatability. For step
height measures U is equal to 1.121 µm against 1.923 µm of the pitch
measures
