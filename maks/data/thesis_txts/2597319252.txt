Abstract 
Public information and consultation for the elaboration, revision, and updating of River 
Basin Management Plans are encouraged in article 14 of the Water Framework 
Directive. In Denmark, there’s a need to engage with the public and stakeholders, and 
failure to do so might result in coordination problems between the central and local levels 
of the government. Water councils were therefore re-appointed in 2019 to improve 
stakeholder involvement. These groups are engaged, with Danish municipalities, in the 
co-creation – collaborative process to produce knowledge – of measures to restore 
streams for the 2021-2027 management cycle River Basin Management Plans. As such, 
the re-introduction of water councils consists in a step towards more co-governance, i.e., 
shared decision right between government institution and other associations, in stream 
management in Denmark. This thesis examines the framework surrounding water 
councils and their function in the decision-making process in Denmark using the following 
research hypothesis: Water councils play a key role for the Danish co-governance 
system. 
The research is based on a case study of three water councils. The collaborative process 
of assisting municipalities in developing restoration measures for the government 
conducted by water councils was assessed employing an analytical framework proposed 
in the co-production literature encompassing four principles: pluralism, goal orientation, 
contextualization, and interactivity. Data was collected via an online questionnaire and 
interviews, and resulting textual data analyzed based on a deductive coding approach 
reflective of the analytical knowledge co-production framework. 
Results indicate that researched water councils will most likely reach the objectives of 
the third consultation cycle thanks to the experience and relationship of the group 
developed throughout previous consultation cycles. However, there is room for 
improvement regarding the tools and diversity used in the process. Results also showed 
that water councils have no legal power and authority in decision-making and are 
delegated narrowed responsibility – i.e., their task in the 2019-20 consultation cycle 
focused on the physical characteristics of streams. The latter approach occults chemical 
and biological aspects or the opportunity of operating at a higher scale, both leading to 
a more holistic approach in water management, which is a hallmark of the WFD. 
Despite reaching their objective of advising municipalities in developing measures 
proposals for River Basin Management Plans, water councils play a restricted role in the 
Danish water governance system. Conclusions point out to a missing regional level in 
the Danish decision-making system, which could support an increased holistic approach 
to stream restoration and bridge the gap between central and local authorities. There is 
room and need for further investigation but enlarging water councils’ powers and 
responsibilities could be a means of addressing this gap, benefitting from existing 
arrangements and experienced groups.

1. Introduction 
The Water Framework Directive 2000/60/EC (WFD) was passed by the European 
Commission (EC) in 2000. It requires Member States to assess the qualitative and 
quantitative state of water bodies; set environmental objectives to protect, enhance, and 
restore European surface and groundwaters; and develop Programs of Measures (PoM) 
– measures to restore water bodies and reach environmental objectives. To that effect, 
River Basin Districts (RBD) had to be delimited by Member States and management 
strategies documented in River Basin Management Plans (RBMP) for each RBD. 
Environmental objectives had to be set by 2009 and achieved by 2015 (Boeuf & Fritsch, 
2016). However, the implementation of the WFD was delayed (Voulvoulis et al., 2016) 
to the following management cycles 2015-2021 and 2021-2027. In 2018, the European 
Environment Agency (EEA, 2018) reported that 40% of surface water bodies were in 
good ecological status/potential and 38% in good chemical status. Similarly, Denmark 
faced complications and delays (Jabobsen et al., 2017). Only 32% of surface water 
bodies were in good ecological status/potential, and chemical status remained largely 
under-reported in 2018 – 134 surface water bodies classified for chemical status against 
6 664 for ecological status/potential (EEA, 2018). 
Traditionally, Denmark has relied on a top-down policy strategy in its water management 
governance (Jacobsen et al., 2017) leading to a strong separation between local and 
central authorities showing limited coordination for the establishment of RBMPs during 
the first management cycle 2009-2015 (Bourblanc et al., 2013; Graversgaard et al., 
2018). This governance strategy contrasts with the approach advanced by the EC in the 
WFD. Article 14 of the WFD calls for public information and consultation in the planning, 
reviewing, and updating stages of RBPMs (EC, 2000), and there is a need for involving 
the public and direct stakeholders in Danish water planning (Graversgaard et al., 2016). 
In early results from Carnohan et al. (2021), some local communities expressed their 
feeling of exclusion from, as well as their interest in taking part in, the decision-making 
process of stream management. In other words, the centralized institutional arrangement 
of water management in Denmark does not align with the governance strategy set in the 
WFD due to the lack of opportunity for local actors and stakeholders to participate. 
Co-governance can be defined as the institutional arrangement where the decision right 
over a given matter is shared between the legally in power governmental institution and 
other organizations (Ackerman, 2004; Kooiman, 2003; Somerville & Haines, 2008). In 
environmental policymaking, co-governance or multi-level governance is a means to 
decentralize decision powers based on well informed decision-making (Nielsen et al., 
2013), improving adaptive capacity (Chaffin et al., 2014). By doing this, co-governance 
aims to a more efficient institutional arrangement than centralized governance (Nielsen 
et al., 2013). In this respect, co-governance can be seen as a critical mechanism to 
include diverse groups of stakeholders in WFD-related decision-making processes in

Denmark. In co-governance, the State does not act as the sole policy maker but also as 
the facilitator between different parties (Graversgaard et al., 2018). 
A step towards increased water co-governance in Denmark has been taken with water 
councils’ (Vandråd) introduction – in response to article 14 of the WFD (Graversgaard et 
al., 2016) – for the preparation of PoMs and RBMPs during the management cycle 2015-
2021 (Graversgaard et al., 2018). Water councils were appointed in 2014 to improve 
stakeholder engagement and provide local knowledge to water management strategies 
(Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark [MFVM], 2020a). Since 2014, the political 
system in Denmark has not changed significantly, and water councils remain the main 
institutional arrangement for stakeholder engagement as encouraged in article 14. 
Beginning in December 2019, water councils started their third consultation cycle, and 
their objective is to advise and assist municipalities in suggesting measures to improve 
streams’ physical characteristics (Danish Environmental Protection Agency [EPA], 
2019b). Municipalities have to report to the MFVM by November 2020 (MFVM, 2020d). 
This research aims to shed light on the relevance of water councils in Denmark as a 
means of bridging the gap between the targets set by the WFD and its current, limited 
implementation. It is hypothesized in this master thesis that: 
Water councils play a key role for the Danish co-governance system. 
To test this hypothesis, a case-study of three – 1.7 Aarhus Bugt, 1.13 Odense Fjord, and 
2.3 Øresund – of the 23 water councils in Denmark is conducted and their role in the 
institutional arrangement of the Danish water governance assessed. In this respect, two 
questions have to be answered in order to validate or refute the hypothesis: do water 
councils work in a sufficiently efficient manner to meet their objectives? Does the context 
and the institutional arrangement of water governance in Denmark enable water councils 
to have decision-making power? The former relates to an internal condition, while the 
later refers to an external condition. In order to structure the analysis procedure, the co-
creation process of water councils is examined based on the knowledge co-production 
framework set by Norström et al. (2020) and using two data collection methods: a 
questionnaire and interviews. More specifically, the objectives of this case study and this 
master thesis are to: 
o Investigate water councils background, including, objectives, legislative 
framework, requirements, and appointment; 
o Gather qualitative data on the knowledge co-production process conducted 
by water councils; 
o Evaluate water councils counselling process; 
o Assess the relevance of the knowledge co-production framework suggested 
by Norström et al. (2020); and 
o Discuss the role of water councils in the Danish co-governance system.

2. Background 
2.1 European Legal Framework 
In 2000, the EC adopted the WFD, which recognized water as a heritage resource rather 
than a commercial product. The directive requires Member States to set targets for water 
bodies, assess the baseline conditions, establish monitoring programs, and prepare 
management plans to achieve set targets, as detailed below. 
Article 4 of the WFD (EC, 2000) sets environmental objectives to protect, enhance, and 
restore surface water bodies in terms of ecological and chemical status, and groundwater 
bodies in terms of quantitative and chemical status. Member States had to establish river 
basin districts by 2004 (Figure 2.1). Tasks included characterizing RBDs – ecoregion 
and typology – reporting anthropogenic activities impacting the state of surface waters 
and groundwater, and conducting economic analyses of water services costs and PoMs. 
In addition, monitoring plans for surface water and groundwater bodies, as well as 
protected areas, had to be in place before 2006. Finally, PoMs had to be established by 
2009 and revised every 6 years afterwards. 
 
Figure 2.1 – Timeline of the WFD steps regarding RBMP and PoM. 
The WFD compelled Member States to establish RBMPs for RBDs by 2009 (Figure 2.1). 
RBMPs outline management strategies to guide authorities towards maintaining/ 
improving the current state so as to fulfill set environmental objectives. They summarize 
RBDs characterization, anthropogenic impacts, economic analyses, environmental 
objectives, monitoring strategies, and PoMs. In accordance with article 14 on public 
information and consultation, RBMPs have to be conceived, reviewed, and updated with 
the consideration of all “interested parties” in the decision-making process – no clear 
definition of the term is provided in the WFD. The first RBMPs were implemented 
between 2009 and 2015, and environmental objectives had to be achieved by the end of 
the management cycle. Member States could, in reference to article 4.4, ask to phase 
the execution of environmental objectives within the two following management cycles –

2.2 Danish Legal Framework 
Overall, decisions are taken at the centralized national level (Bourblanc et al., 2013), but 
some responsibilities are distributed among government levels. In the context of surface 
waters, the EPA sets minimum requirements for each main catchment – i.e., targeted 
length of remediated streams and number of removed barriers – provides the analytical 
and financial framework, is in charge of mapping and monitoring processes, and 
prepares RBMPs and PoMs (EPA, 2019b; EC, 2019c; Gravesgaard et al., 2018). 
Regional governments serve as mediators and planning coordinators, and municipalities 
are responsible for enacting local action plans (Local Government Denmark, 2007).

2.3 Framework for Water Councils and Municipalities 
Ministerial decrees, 126 of 26.01.2017 and 1060 of 22.10.2019, frame how water 
councils are appointed. Water councils are created on a voluntary basis, i.e., a water 
council is formed only if one or more organizations/associations invoke this right. The 
legislation dictates a threshold of 20 members maximum per water council in order to 
keep the process manageable. Decree 1060 requires that a fair distribution of chairs 
between organizations and associations representing different interests should be 
ensured while appointing a water council. Only organizations and associations included 
in decree 126 may have a seat in water councils. The law lists: 
• 
Relevant, nationwide professional associations; 
• 
Nationwide organizations and associations whose purpose is to protect nature or 
the environment, or which represent substantial recreational interests; and 
• 
Local organizations and associations that have interests in water protection/use. 
Decree 1060 allows the appointing of one member and one deputy, a substitute to the 
member in case of absence, per organization/ association and water council. Municipal 
officials are forbidden (EPA, 2019b). In 2014, A total of 398 members sat in water 
councils (Figure 2.4). As of February 2020, 324 members were announced over 21 water 
councils for the planning period 2019-2020. In comparison with the two previous 
consultation cycles, there were 361 and 317 members in 2014 and 2017, respectively, 
in these 21 water councils. The figures include members and exclude deputies.

2.4 Research Approach 
The work of water councils and municipalities during the 2019-2020 consultation cycle 
is located at the interface between science, nature, and society, and consists of jointly 
producing measure proposals for the government drawing upon their local knowledge. 
Such a participative, collaborative process is core to the concept of knowledge co-
production, which was highlighted by Graversgaard et al. (2018) as both a key 
opportunity and barrier to water co-governance in Denmark. The wording of knowledge 
co-production relates to other terms such as co-production and knowledge co-creation 
and are used with a certain level of interchangeability in this master thesis. 
Despite a 20-year long history, there is no clear definition of co-production as it has been 
used by academics throughout different domains (Bremer & Meisch, 2017). Two main 
perspectives draw on the concept of co-production. The more traditional and theoretical 
perspective, called descriptive, focuses on analyzing the interaction and interconnection 
between science and society (Jasanoff, 2004; Van der Hel, 2016). Bremer & Meisch 
(2017) expand the descriptive perspective definition to also include the co-production of 
certain knowledge, features, and settings of society. On the other hand, the more 
practical perspective, called normative, dictates principles to engage non-scientific 
actors in scientific knowledge production (Bremer & Meisch, 2017). The latter develops 
upon other existing research topics such as participatory research and transdisciplinarity 
(Van der Hel, 2016; Brugnach & Özerol, 2019). 
Based on the objectives of this thesis both descriptive and normative perspectives of co-
production are adopted. Water councils’ work is analyzed from the descriptive point of 
view, interpreting how they collaborate with municipalities to propose restoration 
measures. In addition, their work is also evaluated using the normative perspective – the 
goal is to assess how well the process functions. It is acknowledged that water councils 
do not produce knowledge at academic ends per say, but they engage indirectly with the 
scientific community through the tools made available by the MFVM – the MiljøGIS 
platform and the catalogue of measures both result from academic research. 
Bremer & Meisch (2017) suggest a mapping of the different lenses of co-production using 
a prism with eight facets, two descriptive lenses and six normative lenses. Their mapping 
aimed mainly at fostering reflexive thinking to help scholars employ the concept of co-
production in a transparent and explicit manner. In this regard, Bremer & Meisch (2017) 
offer a limited framework for assessing co-production processes. Despite describing the 
lenses as “more complementary than exclusory”, the authors set the descriptive and 
normative perspectives in confrontation in their mapping. Norström et al. (2020) 
theorized an analytical framework to assess knowledge co-production processes, 
adopting a broader and more comprehensive approach to the concept. The authors 
acknowledge the normative and descriptive perspectives but show that processes 
frequently employ both. In this regard, Norström et al. (2020) framework englobes the

Contextualization 
Contextualization relates to the existing setting and environment surrounding the co-
production process (Norström et al., 2020). In this regard, they state that actors must 
consider social, economic, and ecological aspects in the process in order to 
acknowledge barriers and opportunities. This includes stakeholders’ beliefs, language, 
and terminology, as well as legislative and regulatory frameworks (Norström et al., 2020). 
Finally, the task of contextualizing should follow the development of the process given 
that frameworks – legislative and economic, for instance – change constantly. 
Pluralism 
For Norström et al. (2020), pluralism refers to the co-production process diversity and 
the acknowledgement of the different ways of knowing and doing. The authors argue 
that a range of viewpoints has to be guaranteed in the co-production process by including 
not only researchers, but practitioners and the general public as well. However, pluralism 
may lead to management challenges – i.e., coordinating and integrating a very diverse 
and broad range of interests – and power imbalances challenges, undermining expertise 
legitimacy in relation to social hierarchy (Norström et al., 2020). In all, the 
multidisciplinarity of knowledge co-production requires a fine balance between inviting a 
maximum of views and opinions while keeping the process manageable. 
Interactivity 
Interactivity is vital to knowledge co-production processes and implies both quantitative 
and qualitative characteristics (Norström et al., 2020). In this respect, they advance the 
need for frequent, well-timed interactions. The authors contrast token participation – i.e., 
concerned parties are included after the process is completed – with active engagement. 
The latter refers to inviting involved parties throughout the process: setting objectives 
and boundaries, conducting the project, and communicating resulting knowledge 
(Norström et al., 2020). According to the authors, this active involvement fosters an 
evolving collective understanding. Finally, Norström et al. (2020) highlight that 
interactions of quality improve the saliency, credibility, and legitimacy of processes’ 
outcomes.

3. Materials and Methods 
3.1 Population 
In this research a case study of three water councils out of the 23 in Denmark is 
conducted, and elicited data collected by two means: a questionnaire and interviews. 
The water councils of 1.7 Aarhus Bugt, 1.13 Odense Fjord, and 2.3 Øresund were 
chosen based on existing research networks, and because they represent differing main 
catchment characteristics, e.g., demographics, compliance with the WFD, and protected 
streams extent. The cases are detailed in 4 Case Study Water Councils. In total, 40 
members are registered for the consultation cycle 2019-2020 in the three water councils. 
3.2 Data Collection – Questionnaire 
An internet-based, self-administered questionnaire was sent out to members of the three 
water councils. The data collection method was chosen as this type of questionnaire 
provides the opportunity of collecting data from a broader population than with interviews, 
saving time and resources (De Leeuw et al., 2018). 
Questionnaire development 
The questionnaire draws upon previously developed surveys that had assessed the work 
of water councils during the consultation cycles 2014 and 2017 (Graversgaard, 2015; 
Graversgaard et al., 2019) and a survey regarding public perception of water resources 
and governance in Usserød catchment, Denmark (Carnohan et al., 2021). Questions 
from these three surveys were adapted, reformulated, and organized considering the 
principles set by Norström et al. (2020). Further development of the questionnaire 
followed an iterative process, consisting of 11 revisions with the internal project team as 
well as took place over a two-month period between mid-February and end of April 2020. 
It was translated from English into Danish. Both language versions were made available 
on the online platform QuestionPro Survey Software. The final version was distributed 
on the 30th of April 2020 and can be seen in Appendix D. Questionnaire. 
Questionnaire content 
The questionnaire consists of 21 questions. In section A. General Information, the 
respondent is asked about which water council(s) (s)he is active in. In section B. General 
Opinion/Understanding, it is sought for the general opinion of the respondent regarding 
the different topics of the survey. These four open questions are designed to avoid 
framed answers. In the following sections C to F – C. Interaction, D. Goals, E. Pluralism, 
and F. Context – the different knowledge co-production principles are assessed. Each 
principle is detailed using two to five closed-ended questions. In section G. Additional 
Question, the respondent is invited to give additional opinions and information.

the question type. Closed-ended questions such as single and multiple selection and 
rating scales gave directly usable quantitative results. Open-ended questions such as 
comment boxes needed preparation. As questionnaires were completed in Danish and 
respondents answered open-ended questions in Danish, text excerpts were translated

Interviews 
Data collected with interviews had to be prepared and preprocessed before analysis. 
Preparation – namely transcription of interviews – is a crucial step to data analysis (Flick, 
2014). The goal is to achieve a trustworthy representation of an oral discourse in a written 
format. There’s no means of reaching a perfect interview transcript as it always contains 
biases and errors (ibid). These include deletion, substitutions, selections, and additions, 
among other things (Flick, 2014). Data preparation follows three steps: transcription, 
clarification, and summarization. 
Transcription aims to put in writing all elements of the interview. This entails both oral 
elements, as well as prosodic components such as pitch and loudness (Flick, 2014). It 
also includes false starts, repetitions, and fillers – uh, em, er, and um, for instance. The 
second step consists in the clarification of the text, where all unnecessary information is 
deleted. In this research, the main objective is to study the discourse of ideas and 
opinions so that all prosodic components, fillers, repetitions, and false starts were 
superfluous. First transcripts also included grammatical errors and words in Danish – 
interviewees’ mother tongue being Danish, they sometimes missed words in English and 
had to complete their ideas in Danish. Standard orthography was then employed to 
eliminate both errors and translate words in Danish. The last step, summarization, refers 
to the reduction of data extent by capturing main ideas in long sentences or groups of 
sentences and summing them up in shorter ones. In order to limit the addition of new 
biases in this step, wording employed by interviewees were used during summarization.

3.6 Textual Data Analysis 
Analysis framework 
The general methodology for data analysis follows an atomistic-logical approach. The 
approach enabled the amount of material to be reduced in a systematic and objective 
manner in a two-step process: deconstruction and reconstruction (Cropley, 2015). 
Deconstruction refers to breaking down text excerpts into content units – group of words 
or full sentences expressing a single idea – using a coding frame, whereas 
reconstruction relates to the combination of content units to give meaning. Parsimony 
and convergence are two key components of the atomistic-logical approach (ibid). These 
indicate that a minimum data extent leads to solid outcomes, and further material does 
not impact outcomes (Cropley, 2015). 
The study objectives aimed primarily to validate existing theory rather than develop new 
theory. Hence, analysis of textual data utilized a deductive coding approach as the 
research based upon the co-production framework set by Norström et al. (2020) (2.4 
Research Approach). In addition, the method qualitative content analysis was employed. 
It provides a systematic way for data extent reduction based on a coding frame (Flick, 
2014). The specific methodology for the development of the coding frame is presented 
hereafter in accordance with Flick (2014).

4.2 Stream Characteristics 
Denmark’s watercourses are small and short – hence, the term stream over river used 
in the WFD – and stretch over ca. 60 000 km (Bigi et al., 2018; Sand-Jensen, 2006). 
However, only 18 898 km are officially protected under the WFD (EC, 2019b). Streams 
are organized into three typologies set by the EPA (2019a). A stream’s typology is 
defined as a function of its drainage basin and its width (Table 4.2). Typology 1 refers to 
small shallow streams, typology 3 to larger deeper watercourses, and typology 2 to

4.3 Compliance Level of Streams 
Water quality monitoring is a competence of the MFVM as set in LBK 121 of 26.01.2017 
delegated to the EPA in order BEK nr 95 of 28.01.2019. Four indices have been 
developed based on biological and physical characteristics – Danish stream fauna index 
(DVFI), Danish fish index for streams (DFFV), Danish plant index for streams (DVPI), 
and Danish Physical Index (DFI) – and the new biological quality index, bottom living 
algae (phytobenthos), was added in the 2021-2027 RBMPs (EPA, 2019a). A stream’s 
condition is assessed using the “one out, all out” principle, i.e., the index with the lower 
quality determines the stream ecological condition (EPA, 2019a). Protected streams 
have to achieve good ecological status or potential and good chemical status. The 
chemical state of streams remains widely unknow (Bigi et al., 2018). More specifically, 
only ca. 205 km out of ca. 18 898 km of streams, in other words 1%, have been monitored 
for chemical state (ibid). Water councils’ objectives for the consultation cycle 2019-2020 
is to give recommendations regarding stream physical characteristics, the base to 
reaching good ecological status. Figure 4.3 shows compliance levels for Denmark and 
selected main catchments.

4.4 Finances and Minimum Requirements 
In both 2014 and 2019, municipalities and water councils have been given minimum 
requirements to fulfil – stream length restoration, barriers removal count, and ochre 
mitigation structures – within a financial framework for the management cycles 2015-
2021 and 2021-2027, respectively (DNA, 2014; EPA, 2019b). The framework for each 
main catchment was set in 2014 by the DNA and in 2019 by the EPA. Both agencies 
stressed that cost-effective measures would be favored (DNA, 2014; EPA, 2019b). In 
addition to the monetary resources allocated by the MFVM, municipalities may find other 
sources of financing through climate change adaption funds from wastewater 
association, for instance (DNA, 2014). 
The financial framework for the management cycle 2015-2021 equaled to 695.7 million 
DKK (EPA, 2020a). In 2019, the EPA announced a budget of 436.8 million DKK for 
measures set by municipalities and water councils with an additional 114 million DKK 
envelope for compensations for the management cycle 2021-2027, totaling 550.8 million 
DKK (EPA, 2019b). The budget for PoMs is 37% lower for the third management cycle 
than for the second. At the main catchment scale (Figure 4.4), the financial framework 
rose by 9% to 11.1 million DKK for 1.7 Aarhus Bugt and by 20% for 1.13 Odense Fjord 
to 20.6 million DKK. In 2.3 Øresund for the 2021-2027 management cycle, a budget of 
6.3 million DKK is foreseen, 19% lower than for 2015-2021.

5.2 Contextualization 
Social aspects 
Results regarding the social context surrounding the process of water councils are mainly 
derived from interviews. Two secretariats highlighted the voluntary nature of the work of 
water council members. This is of high interest knowing that these volunteers “also [have] 
a normal job and a family to take care of” and that being part of a water council is highly 
time consuming, depending on the water council and the organization as well. Such 
issues also link with resource aspects. 
Another interesting social aspect are the preconceptions of certain members and groups. 
For example, an interviewee reported that farmers felt insecure and frustrated towards 
the MFVM because the ministry could force the WFD without their consent. They couldn’t 
“understand why the government can decide what to do on their field”. As a result, farmer 
organizations and representants in water council would show the most resistance, as 
“they don't like the water framework directive”. Some members also believed that the 
secretariat was “responsible for the water framework directive” so that they would attend 
meetings to “change everything”. A second interviewee also explained that farmers found 
it difficult to participate in the discussion because they had to view streams with a “very 
different angle”, i.e., farmers are used to consider streams as irrigation opportunities and 
not as nature areas. 
In connection with these tensions in interests, it was highlighted by secretariats that water 
council members take part in the process to mainly protect their personal interests. An 
interviewee reported that water council members representing agricultural interests 
would push to discuss and prioritize actions on overloads and discharges from 
wastewater treatment plants. Furthermore, a different interviewee said, “they always 
want to discuss streams in their own backyard”. This shows the limited interests of water 
council members and that “some streams are more important than others” to them. 
Another social aspect is the understanding and knowledge of secretariats of other water 
councils. An interviewee explained in relation with the good atmosphere in the water 
council that “I know that a lot of other water councils are not functioning so good.” A 
second secretariat mentioned that “for some water councils, it's a very easy assignment 
and, for others, it's a very hard one”. Water council members also have a certain 
understanding of the broader social context of their work. Question 19 of the 
questionnaire inquired about the positivity/negativity of the process on certain

6. Discussion 
6.1 Water Councils’ Role in Danish Water Governance 
The relevance of water councils in the decision-making process of Denmark is discussed 
using two conditions – internally and externally to the process. Firstly, the work of water 
councils is analyzed within its boundaries. The objective is to examine how efficiently 
water councils interact to achieve their task. The discussion is based on the previous 
consultation cycles in 2014 and 2017. Secondly, the importance of their work is assessed 
externally regarding the system in which water councils evolve. It is aimed to analyze 
how their work has an impact on the Danish decision-making, i.e., on municipalities, the 
DNA, and the MFVM. This discussion strategy aims to highlight the elements that support 
and hinder water councils to play a key role in the Danish water co-governance system. 
Internal condition of the research hypothesis 
Water councils’ diversity is a critical element for the success of the knowledge co-
production process. It could be shown by the questionnaire’s results and backed up by 
interviewees that water council members are experienced with the process as most of 
them have taken part in the previous consultation cycles – however, some geographical 
differences were highlighted. In 2014, Graversgaard (2015) pointed out to imbalances in 
interests’ representativity. The author underlined that a greater member share endorsed 
“user” interests than “protector” interests. This has had a negative influence on the 
satisfaction of water council members with the process (ibid). Graversgaard (2015) 
explained that municipalities were challenged understanding the true interests of, and 
classifying, certain organizations. Furthermore, municipalities have reported a lack of 
volunteering from “protector” associations (ibid.). This problem has been tackled 
successfully in both 2017 and 2020. A fair distribution of interests had been achieved but 
not guaranteed in all water councils in 2017 (Graversgaard et al., 2019). This year, all 
secretariats claimed that a good mix of interests had been reached. Municipalities thus 
show a capacity to take notice of feedbacks to improve the process. 
Furthermore, Norström et al. (2020) support other dimensions of diversity such as 
gender, ethnicity, and age. Results indicate a relatively homogenous group of people 
sitting in water councils, namely, white males between 50 and 75 years old. Members 
have challenged the relevance of integrating such dimensions in the establishment of 
water councils, claiming that diversity of knowledge prevails over gender or age and that 
the voluntary nature of water council members made these dimensions insignificant. The 
relevance of these claims can be understood but doubts raised so as the true diversity 
in opinions that results from such a homogenous group. There’s little research on the 
impact of these dimensions on water governance. In a study located in eastern India, it 
was concluded that men and women hold different opinions on many aspects of water 
management (Khandker et al., 2020). Despite the widely different setting of the study in 
==PAGE:55==
Implementing Participation for Water Governance in Denmark 
47 
comparison to the present thesis, this conclusion is likely to be relevant for water council 
diversity in opinions. As for the negative impact arising from imbalances in interests’ 
distribution mentioned earlier, it is supported in this research that the inclusion of an 
increased share of women and younger members would benefit the process. The latter 
group would be of particular interest considering the use of computer technology, namely 
the MiljøGIS which is discussed further below. 
It is essential for water councils to have unambiguous guidelines in order to co-produce 
knowledge efficiently and fulfil their objectives. Historically, the goals of the previous 
consultation cycles have shown diverging clarity. After the 2014 consultation cycle, 
Graversgaard (2015) pointed to a relative unanimity on objectives’ clarity, where almost 
90% of water council members and 21 out of 23 secretariats deemed goals as clearly 
defined. For the second consultation cycle, only 50% of water council members 
considered their objectives as clear, and the figure was similar among secretariats 
(Graversgaard et al., 2019). It was shown that members representing agricultural 
interests were especially doubtful regarding the scope of their tasks (ibid). Graversgaard 
et al. (2019) could not pinpoint the exact reason for the tension regarding clarity but 
suggest that unclarity sources may stem from the guiding documents, explanation from 
secretariats to water councils, or simply from the members. In this respect, setting clear 
goals was a major challenge of this consultation cycle. Results indicate that the DNA and 
the MFVM issued a large number of guiding documents to help clarify objectives. 
However, these guidelines have had limited impact on water council members as 
interviewed secretariats spent a lot of time explaining them during meetings. In this 
regard, it can be questioned if the state fulfilled the requirement of article 14 of the WFD 
on public information. On the one hand, documentation provided by the central 
government appears to have missed its audience. On the other hand, through water 
council secretariats, the state indirectly fulfills this requirement. It is clear that there is 
room for improvement for guidelines released by the government. Altogether, it can be 
assumed that the goals of the 2019-2020 consultation cycles are relatively clear as water 
council members proved relative unanimity when answering question 13 (Figure 5.1). In 
addition, this year’s main objective – advising municipalities on restoration measures to 
improve streams’ physical characteristics – was also addressed in 2014 so that 
experienced members have already been confronted with the task. 
Another key aspect of water councils’ process is interaction. It was shown in the results 
from the questionnaire and interviews that water council members attend meetings and 
that secretariats tried, to a certain degree, to accommodate meetings’ hours to fit 
members’ calendar. In their “normal” meetings, water councils rely heavily on the 
platform MiljøGIS. Graversgaard (2015) and Graversgaard et al. (2019) highlighted in 
both 2014 and 2017 that the online platform had worked as intended for municipalities 
as a reporting tool to the MFVM. Nevertheless, they also documented the limited 
opportunity presented by the MiljøGIS to serve as a communication tool during water 
==PAGE:56==
48 
Implementing Participation for Water Governance in Denmark 
council sessions. Graversgaard et al. (2019) pointed to the lack of knowledge among 
water council members to handle the online platform. This thesis shows similar results 
during this consultation cycle because secretariats reported water council members to 
not be accustomed to working with maps and navigate computers. Even though the 
MFVM provided a 30-page long guiding document, members weren’t trained to use the 
MiljøGIS. It is evident that the objective of the online platform was not clearly defined. 
On the one hand, the MiljøGIS appears to have been designed for reporting purposes. 
On the other, water councils resort to the online platform as a key way of interacting 
during sessions. Its importance is increased by the fact that water councils engage in 
very limited ways – they have “normal” meetings during which they hold presentations, 
look at maps on the MiljøGIS, and discuss selected issues. As such, the platform 
MiljøGIS is a barrier to many water council members to inclusion and integration in the 
debate and the process. There is consequently a need to revise the platform to 
accommodate its two roles – official and unofficial – or to develop an interface able to 
support water councils in their work process. 
Besides the types of interaction, the engagement of water council members and 
secretariats is core to the process development. During previous consultation cycles, 
Gravesgaard et al. (2019) documented that approximately 60% of the water council 
members felt involved in the process both in 2014 and 2017. However, in some cases, 
the imbalance in interest representation – more representatives of “users” interests than 
“protector” interests – led to a feeling of non-involvement for certain members (ibid.). This 
year’s study demonstrates similar findings. Two secretariats described engaged water 
council members, while one secretariat shared that only 30-40% were involved in the 
process. In addition, water council members praised the involvement and engagement 
of secretariats. This could be expected as the vast majority of water council members 
were satisfied with the work of secretariats in the consultation cycles 2014 and 2017 
(Graversgaard, 2015; Graversgaard et al., 2019). It appears that the motivation of water 
council members could be improved. As the process is time consuming and results of 
their work not always rewarded, other incentives to support their engagement could be 
set in place via, e.g., remuneration or increased weight in the decision-making. 
Finally, the achievement of water councils’ objectives depends on their success, both 
tangible and intangible. The present study is timed during the consultation cycle so that 
it is too early to assess fully the success of water councils’ tangible goals – advising 
municipalities in developing measure proposals to restore streams physical 
characteristics. Results show that some water councils have taken decisions and 
reached consensus in some way or the other, while other water councils will take 
decisions in the near future. Of particular interest is the focus of secretariats on intangible 
elements as measures of success. Interviewees indicated that success relied, in their 
opinion, on the feeling of involvement, the degree of satisfaction, and the development 
of common understanding of water council members. General satisfaction with the 
==PAGE:57==
Implementing Participation for Water Governance in Denmark 
49 
process was measured in 2014 and 2017. Graversgaard (2015) relates a high degree of 
satisfaction among water council members – 72% satisfied, 20% dissatisfied, and 8% 
did not know. Three years later, the process resulted in a far lower satisfaction level: 
50% satisfied, 39% dissatisfied, and 11% didn’t know (Graversgaard et al., 2019). This 
year, the satisfaction degree of water council members was not directly measured, but 
secretariats reported a well-going process coupled with a positive atmosphere and a 
good chemistry. However, it is worth questioning why the main measure of success to 
secretariats is satisfaction. Water councils have the tangible objective of advising 
municipalities. In this respect, it is surprising that this goal was not mentioned by 
secretariats during interviews as indicator of a successful process. This might be linked 
to the conscious or unconscious acknowledgment by water council secretariats of the 
limited impact of water councils on the decision-making process discussed further below. 
External condition of the research hypothesis 
At the local level, water councils have a favorable influence on decision-making. 13 out 
of the 15 questionnaire respondents deemed their efforts as having a positive impact on 
municipalities (Figure 5.4). This aligns with results of the previous cycles’ evaluations. In 
2014, ca. 64% of water council members assessed that their work had influenced the 
measure proposals submitted by municipalities, and only 19% stated the contrary 
(Graversgaard, 2015). In 2017, despite the moderate satisfaction of water council 
members towards the process, approximately 65% of them rated that their work had 
affected measure proposals by municipalities, and 21% opposed (Graversgaard et al., 
2019). It is crucial for water council members to see that their work affect decision-
making at the municipality level. As they volunteer, the only incentive supporting their 
efforts is the opportunity to weigh in on actual measures. This results also indicate the 
strong relationship based on trust and respect developed between municipalities and 
water council members throughout the three consultation cycles.  
At the state level, the benefits of the water councils’ work have shown mixed outcomes. 
The 2014 consultation cycle ended with praise, and Graversgaard (2015) concluded that 
the work of water councils had been very satisfactory. In practice, municipalities and 
water councils’ proposals included far longer stretches of stream restoration and more 
barrier removal (Table 6.1) than what was required by the MFVM while respecting the 
set financial framework (ibid.). Almost all measures proposed by municipalities and water 
councils were included in the DNA’s draft RBMPs released to the public (ibid.). The 
results of this thesis document the deep dissatisfaction of water council members 
regarding the last consultation cycle in 2017 linked to the ingrained dividing objective. 
On the one hand, “user” interests had the incentive to remove as many streams as 
possible from the protection of the WFD or to categorize them in lower protection 
qualifications, i.e., from natural to heavily modified or artificial. On the other hand, 
“protector” interests intrinsically want the WFD to cover as many streams, and to the 
highest protection level, as possible. This inherent tension in personal objectives among 
==PAGE:58==

participants was not a favorable starting ground to achieve consensus and compromise. 
As this thesis’ results show, this tension led to a lot of frustrating debates in water 
councils in the 2019-20 consultation cycle. Even more concerning was the reaction of 
the MFVM. From the water council members’ perspective, the ministry ignored the 
outcomes of the consultation cycle and threw their work in the trash. Similarly, Wiering 
et al. (2018) assess that water councils’ impact for the second consultation cycle was 
perceived as very limited. There has not yet been an official assessment of the tangible 
outcomes of the process. However, the data from Graversgaard et al. (2019) shows a 
confused picture (4.2 Stream Characteristics), in which the MFVM has considered the 
work of water councils with respect to streams’ delimitation but mostly disregarded their

MFVM to re-classify 2 130 km of streams. This year, it is too early to draw any conclusion 
regarding the benefits of the water councils for the EPA and the MFVM. It can be 
expected that, with regard to the similarity in the assignment scope between the 2014 
and 2019-20 consultation cycles, the government will likely benefit from the work done

the social contract with water councils. Failure to do so might result in political tensions 
as in 2009 when RBMPs for the first management cycle were repealed by court ruling

are still found today as this thesis’ results show. Secretariats have stressed in interviews 
that the decision-making power lies in the hands of the municipalities and the 
DNA/MFVM and the assignment lacks flexibility and freedom to ensure a holistic 
approach to stream restoration management – one of the hallmarks of the WFD 
(Voulvoulis et al., 2016). In other word, the lack of cooperation between local and national 
governments stressed by Bourblanc et al. (2016) and corroborated in this thesis’ result 
point to the missing regional level of decision-making in Denmark’s water management 
governance. In that setting, Graversgaard et al. (2016) questioned if the Danish 
government ever had the will to change the top-down governance approach and 
concluded that the impact of water councils is not significant unless institutional 
arrangements were revised. Four years later, the situation has not changed, and water 
councils still play the very limited role of “expanded stakeholder consultation”, as 
Graversgaard et al. (2016) worded it, in Danish water management governance. With 
respect to the public consultation obligation set in the article 14 of the WFD, the central 
government of Denmark has a substantial opportunity to expand the extent of work, 
powers, and responsibilities delegated to lower levels of governance, but the question of 
their disposition to enact such changes remains. 
6.2 Reflections on the Knowledge Co-Production Framework 
The analytical framework for knowledge co-production suggested by Norström et al. 
(2020) provided the main structure for this research. The understanding of the general 
traits of the different principles was rather straightforward thanks to the wording chosen 
by the authors in defining them. However, it must be noted that the extend and the scope 
of each principle is rather superficially described in Norström et al. (2020), potentially 
leading to additional, subjective interpretations of their content for this specific study (3.6 
Textual Data Analysis). While this may limit the possibilities for comparisons between 
studies using the framework, it also gives space for reflection – possibly resulting in 
creative outcomes. In addition, certain aspects of the assessed knowledge co-production 
process can easily fall within two principles, highlighting the non-exclusive nature of the 
framework. Norström et al. (2020) principles also served as structuring elements in the 
processing and presentation of results. Of particular interest is the flexibility offered by 
the framework. Principles could be discussed and used in whichever order while still 
being coherent. This is highlighted in the diverging presentation orders used in Norström 
et al. (2020), in the questionnaire, and in the results section (Table 6.2).

One of the advantages of Norström et al. (2020) framework is its inclusion of both 
theoretical approaches of co-production, i.e., descriptive and normative. In this respect,

of the field, inviting a broader community to experiment with it. It might be argued that 
this conflicts with a previous warning made by Bremer & Meisch (2017). The authors

conducting analysis, which resulted in disorganized framing of the co-production 
concept. Norström et al. (2020) framework shows a limitation in its definition knowledge

flexible and encompasses the descriptive and normative approaches, scholars may 
freely narrow its definition by clarifying the chosen approach.

interesting feature. In their definition, the authors define knowledge co-production as “any 
process”, thus, not limiting the concept to academic research – in contrast to Van der 
Hel (2016) and, to a lower extent, Bremer & Meisch (2017). In the case of water councils, 
this expansion of the definition is particularly relevant. Members and municipalities work 
together to produce knowledge, but not at academic ends.

7. Conclusions and Future Perspectives 
Water councils were introduced to improve stakeholder involvement and local knowledge 
in the preparation of River Basin Management Plans. Through cooperation, water 
councils engage in knowledge co-production, which encompasses iterative and 
collaborative processes to produce actionable knowledge from a diverse range of 
expertise in a specific context. In this regard, this master thesis aimed at: researching 
the framework surrounding water councils’ work, investigating and assessing the 
knowledge co-production process conducted by water councils with the help of a 
questionnaire and interviews, and discussing the role of water councils for water 
governance in Denmark. The objectives were met. 
European and Danish legislations, consisting of laws and ministerial decrees over the 
last 20 years, were investigated to clarify the framework in which water councils evolve. 
This encompassed the general requirements and objectives set in the WFD as well as 
specific rules in the appointment, the conduct, and the goals of water councils. 
Furthermore, certain characteristics of the three study cases were presented, e.g., socio-
geography, economy, and ecology. 
The co-production process carried out by water councils was investigated with success 
using the analytical framework for knowledge co-production suggested by Norström et 
al. (2020). In practice, two methods were used to collect data: a questionnaire addressed 
to members of the three water councils and interviews of their secretariats. The former 
enabled a horizontal view of the process, i.e., a general overview of the co-production 
across water councils, while the latter offered a vertical approach – in other words, a 
deeper understanding of certain, specific aspects of the process. The framework gave a 
clear, flexible structure in the project methodology and proved to be appropriate for the 
research objective and hypothesis. 
It was hypothesized that water councils are crucial groups in water management co-
governance in Denmark. This echoes the thesis’ objectives of assessing water councils 
advisory process and the discussion of their role in the Danish political system. Despite 
showing directions for improvements, regarding activity and member diversity, results 
indicate that researched water councils will most likely reach the objectives of the third 
consultation cycle thanks to the experience and relationship of the group developed 
throughout previous consultation cycles. This satisfies the internal condition of the 
hypothesis. Nevertheless, there is room for further investigation regarding the impact of 
diversity, e.g., gender or age, on the outcomes of water councils and considering the 
limited set of tools employed during the co-production process.  
Selected water councils demonstrate a positive impact on municipalities’ work and 
limited influence on the higher levels of the decision-making, i.e., the Danish Nature 
Agency and the Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark. It is likely that their work 
==PAGE:63==
Implementing Participation for Water Governance in Denmark 
55 
will affect RBMPs for the third WFD management cycle because of the effort realized by 
secretariats in clarifying the objectives of the process to the group and the task similarity 
between the 2019-20 and the 2014 consultation cycles. It was highlighted that water 
councils have no legal power and authority in decision-making and are delegated 
narrowed responsibility – i.e., their task in the 2019-20 consultation cycle focused on the 
physical characteristics of streams. The latter approach occults chemical and biological 
aspects or the opportunity of operating at a higher scale, both leading to a more holistic 
approach in water management, which is a hallmark of the WFD. The political system 
does not enable water councils to play a major role in the management of streams. The 
external condition of the hypothesis is hence partially refuted. 
It is important to remember that this case study bases on only three water councils: 
Aarhus Bugt, Odense Fjord, and Øresund, out of 23 in total in Denmark. The analysis 
bases on socio-demographic, financial, and ecological the elements. It can be concluded 
that selected water catchments depict urban, populous settings contrasting with the rest 
of Denmark. However, they represent interesting cases in terms of stream compliance 
with the WFD and face similar challenges regarding minimum requirements set by the 
Danish Environmental Protection Agency. Questionnaire respondents and interviewees 
have pointed out mainly political characteristics as possible barriers or opportunities to 
their counselling and knowledge co-production process. It can thus be expected that the 
case study proves a higher representativity than the analysis suggests. 
In spite of reaching their objective of advising municipalities in developing measures 
proposals for River Basin Management Plans, water councils play a restricted role in the 
Danish water governance system. Unfortunately, the conclusion drawn by Graversgaard 
et al. (2016) that water councils serve as “expanded stakeholder consultation” rather 
than as decision-making actors still holds true – four years and two consultation cycles 
later. This points out to a missing regional level in the Danish institutional arrangement, 
which could provide an increased holistic, multi-level approach to effective stream co-
governance in Denmark. There is room and need for further investigation in finding 
innovative solutions able to bridge the gap between central and local authorities. 
Enhancing water councils’ authority and responsibilities could be a means of addressing 
this gap, benefitting from existing arrangements and experienced groups. 
The future of water councils is unclear as Denmark and other Member States of the 
European Union enter the last management cycle for water management under the 
WFD. In the current state, water councils seem to disappear from the Danish water co-
governance scene at the end of 2020. This might increase the distance between the 
MFVM and the DNA, on one end, and municipalities, on the other end, as it occurred in 
the first management cycle (Bourblanc et al., 2013; Graversgaard et al., 2018). In such 
a case, other options to consult the general public in the reviewing and updating stages 
of RBMPs before the end of the management cycle 2021-2027 could be sought for.

G.2 Stream Characteristics 
In total, 18 898 km of streams are protected under the WFD (4.2 Stream Characteristics). 
Streams extent varies significantly between regions. Approximately 3 000 km of streams 
are found in 1.2 Limfjorden while only 193 km are included in 1.14 Størebælt. (Figure 
G.2). Main catchments 1.7, 1.13, and 2.3 all encompass areas with restricted stream 
extents with an average length of 427 km. Regarding typology, most catchments show 
shares similar to the national total with typologies 1 and 2 prevailing and a fifty-fifty ratio. 
The only noticeable outsider is the main catchment 4.1 Vidå-Kruså in which only 9% of 
the total stream length is classified under typology 1. Selected main catchments hence 
represent the typology distribution of streams in Denmark but do not show the substantial 
differences in stream extent. 
All main catchments show large majorities of natural streams with shares between 78 % 
for 2.6 Østersøen and 100% for 3.1 Bornholm (Figure G.2), and the former also proves 
a significant share of heavily modified streams. In 1.13 and 2.3, streams show similar 
shares as Denmark in general. However, close to 10% of streams in 1.7 Aarhus Bugt 
were classified as artificial. Overall, the selected main catchments, especially 1.13 and 
2.3, represent the classification state throughout Denmark. It is important to remember 
that water councils worked on the re-classification of streams in the consultation cycle 
2017 (4.2 Stream Characteristics). The current status is, hence, slightly different than 
what is presented here.
