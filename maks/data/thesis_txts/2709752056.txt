Abstract
Inhomogeneous cell degradation of parallelôÙconnected battery assemblies in an electric
vehicle (EV) potentially results in less power available to drive the car. This report explores
the environmental effects of corrosion and vibration that may exist at the batteryôÙtoôÙbusbar
connections in EV battery packs. The contact resistance of resistance spot welded aluôÙ
minium (Al 1050) to nickel plated steel (Hilumin by TATA Steel) joints was characterised
via analytical, simulated and experimental means. The equipment for the experimental
investigations was first designed, constructed and calibrated. A drift in the voltmeter readôÙ
ings was found to affect 3.8% of the stated values. This was concluded to stem from the
accuracy range limits of the voltmeter. The change in resistance of the welded samples,
measured via the fourôÙpoint probe method, after the environmental tests was found to be
negligible. The simulations confirmed that the effect of a change in contact resistance
is small relative to the size of the welded samples in the model. A crossôÙsection of the
weld nugget at the interface of the Al 1050 and Hilumin sheets revealed a ãspecial brazed
jointã of molten aluminium on the face of the Hilumin sheet. Scanning electron microscopy
(SEM) images showed a thin layer of intermetallic compounds may have formed at the
interface. The type of bond found between the aluminium and Hilumin sheets was conôÙ
sistent with literature that used similar resistance spot welded metal combinations.

Introduction
1.1
Electric vehicle market and battery technology
Electric vehicle (EV) registrations saw a 41% increase, despite a worldwide decrease in
vehicle purchases made in 2020 due to pandemicôÙrelated activities which saw a 16% drop
in sales. Globally, 3 million electric vehicles were sold in 2020, adding to the 8 million that
were already on the roads which points towards an increasing rate of growth as shown
in fig. 1.1. This resilience in the EV market comes down to the supportive regulatory
frameworks from tighter CO2 emissions standards to zeroôÙemissions vehicle mandates
made to meet the carbon neutral commitments of nations worldwide. Incentives in the
form of a 2.9 billion euro fund into the research and development of battery manufacturing
by the European Commission, and extended subsidy schemes in China for example,
provide EV manufacturers with a safety net from economic downturn. The increase in
EV models and falling cost of batteries has further contributed to EV sales [40], [57].

[56].

Changing the materials used in batteries is one method of improving the efficiency in
EVs. SolidôÙstate batteries from QuantumScape and Toyota Motor for example are set
to replace the lithium ion batteries currently in use [38]. Another improvement is on the
production side of batteries. Battery assemblies connected in parallel such as the ones in
Tesla vehicles provide large amounts of power, but at a cost [21]. This cost is the uneven
current distribution to a battery cell which is influenced by a difference in connection and
internal resistance of a battery thus resulting in inhomogeneous cell degradation [17], [22].
Less energy is available to drive the car, and mechanical stresses lead to failureôÙprone
batteries. There is limited literature that covers the source of uneven current distribution in
EV batteries. Hence, this report aims to characterise which variables may affect electrical
contact resistance (ECR) at the electrode interface [17], [120].
1.2
The scope
The scope of this work is to investigate the effects of vibration and corrosion on a reôÙ
sistance spot welded aluminium to nickel plated steel joint through resistance measureôÙ
ments. A literature review on the challenges faced by batteries in electric vehicles is
carried out with a focus on resistance spot welded connections.
The experimental method carried out is outlined as follows. Firstly, the welding setup
was reôÙdesigned, manufactured and calibrated, and the resistance measurements optiôÙ
mised. Secondly, the samples of Aluminium (Al) and NickelôÙplated steel (Hilumin) were
resistanceôÙspot welded with select weld settings. Thirdly, the samples were analysed for
corrosion and vibration effects.
The contact resistance between the two metal sheets is characterised and simulated to
better understand the conditions at the weld. An analysis on crossôÙsectioned weld nuggets
is made to understand their geometry and composition at the interface of the two metals.
Finally, a comparison between analytical, simulated and experimental results is shown
and the methods are evaluated.

2.1
Battery challenges
2.1.1
Parallel connected battery assemblies
The large capacity and power requirements in electric vehicles (EVs) often require the
lithiumôÙion battery cells to be connected in parallel. As such, as the battery assemblies
become larger, the more likely parallel connections are to be present [21]. The cells are
stacked into a module which are connected in series to form a pack. For example, in a
Nissan Leaf, the battery pack contains 192 cells where two cells are connected in parallel
throughout the pack [67]. Management of said cells is done via a Battery Management
System (BMS) [74]. A typical battery electric vehicle (BEV) drive system is shown in
fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Typical battery electric vehicle (BEV) drive system and its various functions
[43].
BMS functions include estimation of battery states such as state of charge (SOC), state of
function (SOF) and state of health (SOH). SOH comprises of extent of abuse and degraôÙ
dation of performance in batteries. According to the United States Advanced Battery
Consortium (USABC), SOC is the ratio of the remaining to total charge in a battery, often
expressed as a percentage [55], [74].
SOC is calculable for a single battery cell, but for a battery module, battery uniformity is
affected by the conditions during manufacture which dictate the differences which exist
between the individual cells. This includes the individual cell voltage, capacity and its fade
rate, internal resistance and its change rate, and selfôÙdischarge rate and its change rate.

2.2
Resistance spot welding
There are several joining techniques that are possible for the joining of battery tabs and
battery tab to busbar of which the most common include ultrasonic welding, laser beam
welding, wire bonding, soldering, force fitting and resistance spot welding (RSW) [22],
[70], [141]. Each technique has its advantages and disadvantages where for RSW the
advantages include [33], [70], [129], [141]:
ãÂ High efficiency of thermal energy input due to the combination of fast heating time
and high current in which most energy is conducted to through the desired metal
ãÂ Low amounts of deformation to the work piece as thermal energy is concentrated in
the vicinity of the weld
ãÂ High rate and low cost of production as battery welding tasks are easily repeatable
allowing for mass production
ãÂ No filler material is required which lowers the energy requirement and environmental
impact
ãÂ Using the tools requires little training
ãÂ High maturity of the process
ãÂ Less prone to outside influences during the welding process
Resistance spot welding is dependent on the metal thermal conductivity and melting point
[139], [140]. Hence, the metal sheets that are to be welded should not exceed a given
conductivity and thickness as they will be difficult to spot weld. If cell currents above 20A
are required in battery assemblies then spot welding is not a viable method [22]. Other
disadvantages include possible expulsions, electrode sticking and wear, difficulty in proôÙ
ducing large joints, and a difficulty in joining more than two layers [33], [70]. As such,
RSW is used for the welding of prismatic, pouch, and cylindrical battery cell variants. For
further literature on the subject of joining processes, refer to Zwicker et al [141]. ResisôÙ
tance spot welding was the method of welding chosen in this report given its suitability in
the manufacture of batteries in the automotive industry.

2.2.2
Resistance spot welding process
The electrical resistance in Jouleãs law of heating can be broken down into steps deôÙ
scribing the individual resistances. Figure 2.4 illustrates a typical twoôÙsided resistance
spot welding arrangement with the electrical resistances at the electrodes, sheets and
interfaces. At the start of the welding process when the electrodes are forced together,
sandwiching the sheets in between known as ãsqueeze timeã, the contact resistances are
high. When a current is sent through one of the electrodes to the sheets and into the
opposing electrode, the surface layers collapse which brings more surface areas of the
two sheets into contact, creating additional current paths, and thus increasing the current
density. The contact resistances drop instantly. The temperature at the interface of the
two sheets continuously rises until the melting temperature of the metals are reached.
When the entire interface is molten, the current stops, and the electrodes cool the molten
metal, solidifying and forming a weld nugget [7], [129].

2.2.3
Electrode configurations
The most common type of welding is doubleôÙsided welding in which a current flows directly
from one electrode to another in which the electrodes are positioned precisely inôÙline with
each other. The issue with this setup is that the electrodes must be aligned accurately
down to 25 microns which is difficult to achieve with resistanceôÙspot welding due to the
accuracy required in alignment [110]. The electrodes may be misaligned such that the
electrodes are not parallel to each other, or that the tips are not coincident. Thus issues
such as expulsion, voids, insufficiently large weld nuggets, and excessive wear of elecôÙ
trodes are possible [101]. Steinmeier [110] stated that the precision of the electrode tip
should not vary by more than 10% of the electrode tip dimension. Hence, doubleôÙsided
welding requires electrode replacement systems that are able to maintain positional acôÙ
curacy and precision to ensure repeatable welds of acceptable quality [110].
To avoid the aforementioned issues encountered in doubleôÙsided welding, singleôÙsided
welding is applied and thus electrode misalignment can be circumvented. SingleôÙsided
welding consists of both electrodes touching the work piece from the same side. The
process is often used when the work piece is only accessible from one side [7]. Two setups
are possible: parallel gap welding, and step or indirect welding. The former involves two
electrodes in contact with the top sheet where the current is intended to flow from the first
electrode through the top sheet into the bottom sheet then back up through the sheets to
the second electrode, thus creating two weld nuggets [7], [22], [111], [142]. This process
is illustrated in the fig. 2.8a.

Peltier effect
The Peltier effect is a phenomenon where the direction of current affects weld quality
due to the difference in Fermi levels when dissimilar metals are to be joined. Fermi level
describes the surface of a collection of electron energy levels at a temperature of absolute
zero. When a current is passed through the contacting surface of two dissimilar metals,
the electrons will change orbit due to the difference in Fermi levels. This is accompanied
by an absorption or release of energy depending on the current direction. Hence, Peltier
heating or cooling occurs [108] .

Electrode materials
The choice of electrode material is an important factor to consider in resistance spot weldôÙ
ing applications. Electrodes must be able to withstand high temperatures and loads while
maintaining constant levels of electrical and thermal conductivity. Electrode wear may
be accelerated under circumstances of alloying reactions between the sample and the
electrode, a common occurrence in metals such as aluminium and plated steels. This
can lead to an increased electrode diameter, and thus a decrease in current density due
to the increased contact area between the electrode and sample. A smaller current denôÙ
sity decreases the weld nugget size [66]. In micro resistance welding applications, these
aforementioned effects are multiplied as the tip geometry is more sensitive to changes
than on the large scale. For example, a long electrode tip may not be able to direct as
much waste heat to the larger electrode shank in comparison to a short tip. As a reôÙ
sult, electrode tip life may decrease [110]. Hence, a balance between electrode longevity
versus accomplishment of the required function is apparent. The typical lifespan of an
electrode is in the range of 5000 to 10,000 welds although tip dressing of electrodes can
restore the shape of the electrode tip [129].

3.2.2
Welding current
Current is a large determining factor in resistance welding as heating is affected by the
power of a square [117], refer to JouleôÇs law of heating eq. (2.3). While successfully
using an Alternating Current (AC) spot welding control system is possible on the largeôÙ
scale, obtaining a high degree of welding consistency is difficult on the small scale. Mid
Frequency Direct Current (MFDC) inverters offer more precision, closed loop control over
the welding current, and higher voltage and power [110]. The sine wave of an AC cools
the weld momentarily whereas with MFDC heat can be produced continuously and in
less time [53]. A faster weld time translates to less damage of the workpiece and to the
electrodes as wear is reduced. The direct current means that the welding machine is
unaffected by an inductive voltage drop and reduces the power demand from the mains
[129].
Power was supplied by a Medium Frequency inverter DC (MFDC) Akzent TôÙ5000 welding
controller by Resistronic. A maximum current of 4800A could be delivered by the controller
[47]. The welding parameters such as heat profiles were adjusted via the control panel.

had been carried out.

Electrodes
The Resistance Welding Manufacturers Association (RWMA) has compiled a list of elecôÙ
trode materials and grouped them according their type and categorised them by class
number. Group A electrode materials are commonly used in resistance spot welding
(RSW) due to their copper rich compositions. Class number is associated with electrode
strength and electrical conductivity of the electrode. Class 1 and 2 are typically used in
resistance spot welding due to the high percentage of copper in the electrodes, and thus
high conductivity values. Therefore, copper electrodes with at least 99.5% copper were

geometry on single sided spot welded battery connections. They found that a rounded
tip provided the best weld results relative to electrode wear. Thus, the electrode tip was

4.3
Welding procedure
On the surface of the metal sheets, water vapour, oxides, dirt and possibly other contamôÙ
inants may exist. If these substances are left on the surface, they will have an effect on
the initial contact resistance which may affect the heat distribution across the interface of
the electrode to the part, and the interface between both parts [117]. Before putting the
samples on the setup, they were cleaned with acetone to remove grease and oil, then
any remaining dust was removed with compressed air.
The overlapping regions were marked with a permanent marker in order to center the
aluminium and Hilumin samples on top of each other. Given the melting temperatures
of nickel and steel [13] are higher than aluminium [12], the aluminium sheet was placed
below the Hilumin sheet. Hence, during the welding process, the Hilumin sheet was sandôÙ
wiched between the electrode and the aluminium sheet as shown in fig. 4.2. If expulsion
was to occur, the aluminium sheet would be affected before Hilumin due to its lower meltôÙ
ing temperature. Hence, this minimises the risk of electrode sticking occurring during the
welding process.
One end of the aluminium sheet was sandwiched and tightly secured between the brass
parts. The Hilumin sheet was placed at the other end of the aluminium sheet underneath
the electrode. The electrode placement was centred over the overlap region as accurately
as possible over the sample. Once the weld settings had been configured on the welding
controller and pneumatic system, and the Flexlogger parameters were chosen, the exôÙ
periments could be carried out. The foot pedal was depressed to lower the electrode onto
the workpiece, and the weld current was sent through the sample by pressing a button
linked to the activation of the welding controller.

4.4.2
Analytical method
The fourôÙpoint probe method can be applied to find the resistance due to contact between
two sheets. Brand et al. [22] proposed a method to calculate the contact resistance found
at the overlap between two metal sheets. However, their suggested method does not take
into account the geometry of the weld, and assumes that the current flowing through the
sample does so through the entire interface of the overlap region. A calculation that takes
into account the weld geometry creates more assumptions about the geometry at weld.
Therefore, the total resistance of the sample was calculated from the fourôÙpoint probe
method, and an analytical calculation was made separately. In chapter 5, a simulation
method is produced as a third form of comparison characterising the contact resistance
at the weld.
The analytical calculation represents the resistances in the sheets and at the weld. Hence,
this can be explained as three resistors connected in series as shown in fig. 4.6.
It follows then that the total resistance of the resistors is calculated as follows:
Rtotal = RAl + Rweld + RHi
(4.2)
By solving for the resistances in the aluminium RAl, at the weld Rweld, and in the Hilumin
RHi using eq. (2.4), and inputting their respective values, the total resistance Rtotal can be
found. The area of the nugget was assumed to cover a circular plane, and it was therefore
calculated from the area of a circle.

6.3.3
Discussion of corrosion and vibration results
The largest difference in resistance incurred was 3.45% for sample T25 in fig. 6.14 beôÙ
tween the corrosionôÙvibration test and 8.4Hz vibration test. This is within the 3.8% error of
the voltmeter calculated in section 6.1.2. Hence, the results show that the the resistance
of the samples did not change under any vibration and corrosion test.
One of the reasons why there was no evident change in the resistance measured may be
due to the high temperature and corrosion resistance of Al and steel joints [103] as outlined
in section 2.2.6. Fretting corrosion is reduced with nickelôÙplated materials, explained in
section 2.5, due to its high bulk hardness over softer metals such as aluminium. Hence,
fretting corrosion may be reduced at the weld.
The vibration setup used in this report represents an aggressive form of vibration in which
the interface between the two sheets was cyclically loaded. The amplitude of the vibration
was not a variable measured by Hooper and Marco [51], and nor was the direction of
vibrations incurred at the battery in the EV recorded. The 504RPM motor speed translates
to 8.4Hz of vibration frequency which is just above the urban driving response range of
7Hz explained in section 2.5. Given the run time was 1 hour, this totals 30240 revolutions
completed by the wheel. The second vibration test using the 40RPM (0.67Hz) motor was
within the urban driving response range of 0ôÙ7Hz and near the peak found of 1ôÙ2Hz (see
section 2.5). At this speed, the wheel met the sample with a smaller force compared to
when the motor was run at 504RPM. Hence, this represented a less aggressive fatigue
test over a longer period of 4 days. The number of cycles completed by the wheel was
230,400 revolutions. This represents a highôÙcycle fatigue test on the samples in which
the number of cycles exceeded 105. Aluminium will always fail when tested to a sufficient
number of cycles unlike steel which has an endurance limit where below a given stress
level failure cannot occur. However, this number of cycles is usually 108 [58]. Hence, the
0.67Hz vibration test was off by around a factor of 100 to reach the limit of failure of the
aluminium sheet. Further cycles may have produced fatigue crack growth, reducing the
contact between the sheets, and thus increasing the measured resistance.
Inside the climate chamber, the vibration test can be compared to a thermoôÙmechanical
and corrosion fatigue test. Fatigue strength generally decreases in metals for an increase
in temperature [58]. Furthermore, the anodic index of aluminium and nickel was calculated
in section 2.4, and the two metals were found to be incompatible, increasing the chances
of galvanic corrosion occurring. The metal with the high negative potential, the anode,
in this case aluminium undergoes an accelerated form of corrosion in theory. However,
this was not evident from the results as the resistance measured remained constant after
the corrosion tests. A drop in the resistance measured could be due to fractured brittle
material at the weld nugget, allowing the two sheets to contact over a larger area. Thus,
a larger amount of material is produced for the current to pass through.

6.3.4
Weld crossôÙsection analysis
The nugget diameter once peeled provides a geometrical measurement of the weld nugget
that has undergone stress and fracture during the peel procedure. However, the welded
region contains more than just the nugget itself where fusion has occurred since there is a
heat affected zone (HAZ) in which a current may still flow through. Hence, only accounting
for the fusion zone does not give an accurate representation of the possible paths which
the current can flow through.
An crossôÙsection analysis allows for the weld region in its entirety including the HAZ to be

Conclusion
The investigation on the batteryôÙtoôÙbusbar weld performance in electric vehicles has been
performed in which corrosion and vibration environmental tests on aluminium and nickelôÙ
plated steel resistance spot welded joints were carried out. The difference in resistances
of the welded samples measured via the fourôÙpoint probe method before and after the
environmental tests were found to be negligible. Reasons for the lack of change in reôÙ
sistance, such as the high temperature and corrosion resistance of aluminium and steel
joints, were discussed following the aggressive corrosion and vibration tests.
An extensive investigation and analysis was made on the Agilent 34420A voltmeter and
the current sources used to measure the resistance of samples in this report via the fourôÙ
point probe method. The findings concluded that up to a 3.8% drift in the voltage readings
was possible when making measurements due to the voltmeter used in the setup.
The weld parameters were optimised for the final welds, and the polarity of the electrodeôÙ
toôÙpart analysed according to the weld nugget diameter. The crossôÙsectioned metallurgy
analysis revealed that the weld nugget consisted of a ãspecial brazed jointã of molten aluôÙ
minium at the interface of the Hilumin sheet. Holes and gas bubbles were found in the
weld, and the effect of porosity was discussed. SEM was used to analyse the composiôÙ
tion of the weld at the interface of the two metals in which minimal amounts of fusion and
intermetallics could be found (EDS would confirm this).
The analytical, simulated, and experimental results were graphed for the resistance of
the welded sample. It was concluded that the effect of different contact resistances simuôÙ
lated at the weld was small relative to the total resistance of the sheet. The experimental
value found agreed with the simulated value within errors. Suggestions were proposed
about future work investigating the dimensions of the weld and the individual resistances
considered analytically.

Future work
8.1
Voltmeter
The Agilent 34420A voltmeter used was unable to produce accurate readings within 3.8%
of the measurements being made. This was likely due to the accuracy limits of the voltôÙ
meter. The measurements being made on the welded samples were at most around
0.2mV in size, and therefore were below the stated accuracy range of 1mV by Agilent [5].
Agilent states that larger voltage signals reduce the amount of error in the voltmeter readôÙ
ings, and hence accuracy increases [5]. The increased stability of the voltage signal found
for the long aluminium strip confirmed that a larger signal reduced the error. Therefore, a
voltmeter that can accurately measure voltages within the range of 1ôçV would improve the
accuracy of the results obtained. Furthermore, a calibrated voltmeter would also produce
more accurate results and remove inherent systematic error in the voltmeter.
8.2
Corrosion and vibration
To see a possible effect due to vibration and corrosion at the weld, further cycles in both the
climate chamber and fatigueôÙtesting environments may have facilitated this. The corrosion
test should follow the IEC60068ôÙ2ôÙ30 temperature and humidity profile more stringently.
The vibration setup itself could be improved as several issues were encountered such as
the motor overheating inside the climate chamber.
Improvements to the vibration setup include the following:
ãÂ A different material should be used to vibrate the weld, hold the sample, and support
the motor inside the cabinet. The material used to 3D print these parts was Poly
Lactic Acid (PLA) which has a transition temperature between 50 to 80ãÎC [127].
The chamber reached temperatures of 65ãÎC and hence affected and distorted the
material.
ãÂ Electric leads that are connected to an AC power source would improve the test
time, and the reliability of the power supplied to the motor.
ãÂ The setup was assembled inside the climate chamber. This proved to be tedious,
and unstable. It would be better to assemble the setup on a single bed structure
that can be inserted into the climate chamber.
ãÂ There should be better control of the parts such as the distance between the rotating
wheel and the welded sample, and the angle of contact between the sample and
the wheel. Differences in the distance between the rotating wheel and test samples
produces results that have not been obtained under fairôÙtesting conditions.
ãÂ A controlled and monitored process would allow any issues occurring with the setup
such as with the current or the rotation of the wheel, to be observed and evaluated.
ãÂ Ultimately, the vibration tests would be improved by replacing the setup with a shaker
conforming to MILôÙSTDôÙ810F and SAEJ1455.

8.4
Simulations
The analytical calculations made could be investigated to see how they match the simulaôÙ
tion. This would reveal which resistances are accounted for at the weld. For example, the
resistance due to the geometry at the weld, and the resistance at the contacting interfaces
between the materials may be factors which affect the resistance in the simulation. The
weight of each resistance may provide information on the importance of the consideration
of contact resistance at batteryôÙtoôÙbusbar connections in EVs.
The simulations would benefit from having more accurate weld geometry. The geometry
of the weld could be explored further via a 3ôÙD scan for example. This would allow for
the effect of the nugget thickness on the resistance to be characterised and for proper
modelling in the simulation. In this report, it was found that the Hilumin sheet did not
melt with the aluminium to form the weld. As such, the weld nugget should represent the
ãspecial brazed jointã on the aluminium side at the overlap between the sheets.
The simulations were limited to electric currents in this report. In the future, thermal effects
could be included at the weld joint. Hence, modelling the welding process of the weld
could explain the gas bubble formations in the nugget found in section 6.3.4.
