Abstract
Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) is a peptide therapeutic for treatment of diabetes mel-
litus. Natural GLP-1 is difficult to use as an anti-diabetic agent, since it undergoes very
rapid enzymatic degradation [1]. The enzymatic degradation leads to a short half-life
and loss of receptor activation. To overcome the short half-life, different strategies can
be used.
One important strategy is to have metabolically stable analogs. One of these analogs
is the acylated GLP-1 analogs liraglutide (Victoza®) from Novo Nordisk A/S. It is a
truncated GLP-1 with a palmitoyl group linked to an L-γ-glutamyl spacer, at Lys26.
Liraglutide has a half-life of 13 hours and due to the palmitoyl fatty acid (FA) group
form stable oligomers.
A preliminary study, conducted by Tine Frederiksen investigated a truncated peptide
library of acylated GLP-1(14-34) analogs. The analogs acylated with FA in the range
of C6 to C16 with and without L-γ-glutamyl spacer.
In the current study, the oligomerization and interaction with human serum albumin
(HSA) were studied with the phosphate buffer system at pH 10.3 containing 35 mM, 70
mM, and 140 mM NaCl. A HSA titration study was performed in 1 buffer system (pH
10.3, 140 mM NaCl) to estimate if the peptide concentration affect the extend of inter-
action. An initial study of determination of sample size, has an effect on oligomerization
size. Further peptide synthesis, purification and In vitro Biological Activity Assay of
peptides were performed.
For investigation of the oligomer size and interaction with HSA, Asymetrical flow field-
flow fractionation- Multi-angle light scattering(AF4-MALS) was used.
What was observed, were that peptides form larger oligomers with respect to the length
of FA side chains. The spacer seemed to decrease the oligomeric size. It was observed
that only liraglutide, MHR-33 (C16) and MHR-41 (C10) ineract with HSA. Liraglutide
and MHR-41 only partially interact and MHR-33 fully interact with HSA. It was ob-
served that regardless of the concentration of peptides, liraglutide and MHR-41 only
partially interact. The sample size injected into AF4-MALS system revealed no correla-
tion between injected sample size and oligomeric size. In vitro Biological Activity Assay
indicated an antagonistic behavior of all peptides. Some peptides seemed to at high
concentration have an agonistic behavior.

Dansk Resumé
Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) er et terapeutisk peptid for behandling af diabetes mel-
litus. Naturligt GLP-1 er vanskeligt at bruge som anti-diabetes medicin, da det hurtigt
undergår emzymatisk nedbrydning. Nedbrydningen betyder en kort plasmahalveringstid
i kroppen.
Forskellige strategier kan bruges for at forlænge levetiden i kroppen. En af de vigtigste
strategier er, at have en analog der er mere modstandsdygtig imod enzymatisk degrader-
ing. En GLP-1 analog er liraglutid (Victoza®) fra Novo Nordisk A/S. Det er en forkortet
GLP-1 med en palmitoyl (fedtsyre) gruppe linket til Lys26 med en L-γ-glutamyl spacer.
Liraglutid har en plasmahalveringstid på 13-14 timer, pga. fedtsyren danner oligomerer.
Et forløbig studie udført af Tine Frederiksen undersøgte flere forkortede acylerede GLP-
1(14-34) analoger.
Analogerne var acyleret med fedtsyrer (C6-C16) med og uden spacer.
I dette studie er, oligomeriserinen og interaktionen med human serum albumin (HSA)
undersøgt med et fosfat buffer system, pH 10.3 indeholdende enten 35 mM, 70 mM,
eller 140 mM NaCl. Et HSA titrerings studie blev udført, for at estimere om peptid
koncentrationen har en betydning for interaktionen med HSA. Yderligere blev der ud-
ført et indledende studie udført, som skulle bestemme om den injektieret prøve volumen
har en betydning for oligomeriseringen. Endvidere blev peptid syntese, oprensning og
In vitro biologisk aktivitets studie udført. Til at undersøge oligomer størrelsen på pep-
tider og inderaction med HSA Asymetrical flow field-flow fractionation- Multi-angle light
scattering(AF4-MALS) blev brugt.
Der er blev observeret, at peptiderne danner større oligomere ved længere fedt-
syre kæder. Studiet vidste også, at når en spacer er indsat, formindskes størrelsen på
oligomerene. Det blev observeret at kun liraglutide, MHR-33 (C16) and MHR-41 (C10)
interagerer med HSA. Hvor liraglutid og MHR-41 intergerer kun delvis og MHR-33
intergerer fuldt ud. Det er også observeret at dette gør sig gældende uanset koncentra-
tionen af peptid. Desuden viste det sig at volumen injektieret in i AF4-MALS systemet
ikke ænderede størrelsen af oligomerene. In vitro biologisk aktivitets studie indikerer en
antagonistisk opførelse af alle peptider. Endvidere viste det sig, at nogle af peptiderne
har en agonostik aktivering af receptoren ved høje koncentrationer.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In modern society, obesity becomes an increasing problem. Obesity is a consequence of
an increasing variety of cheap and high-calorie food. Obesity is associated with different
diseases resulting in a higher mortality rate.
The diseases which are associated with obesity are diabetes mellitus, cardiovascular
disease, and various cancer. [2, 3] As a consequence of diseases associated with obesity,
many pharmaceutical companies are investing a considerable amount of time and money
in research and development of new and better pharmaceuticals.
Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) is a peptide therapeutic for treatment of diabetes
mellitus. However GLP-1 undergoes very rapid enzymatic degradation [1] leading to a
very short half-life. Different approaches to overcome the short half-life has been studied.
One approach is to have an acylated GLP-1, which is marketed as Victoza®from Novo
Nordisk A/S.
The current project is based on a preliminary study that investigated GLP-1 analogs.
This project aim to elucidate if these peptides form oligomer and bind to human albumin
serum.

This chapter focuses on the theory of GLP-1 and its analogs.
2.1
Glucagon-like Peptide-1
Glucagon-like Peptide-1 (GLP-1) is an incretin hormone, produced in the intestinal
L-cell upon ingestion of nutrients. It is produced as the precursor proglucagon, by a
posttranslational process.
Proglucagon is cleaved to GLP-1 (1-37) and further trun-
cated to GLP-1(7−36)-NH2 (hereafter referred to GLP-1) and GLP-2(7−37).
Both GLP-1 and GLP-2 are biologic active, however, GLP-1 is the main form found
in the human body. [4, 5] The amino acid sequence is displayed in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: The native human GLP-1(7−36)-NH2 (1) adapted from [4]
2.1.1
Physiological Actions
GLP-1 affect different physiologic targets, such as the endocrine pancreas, gastrointesti-
nal tract (GI) and central nervous system (CNS). The most important target in terms
of treating diabetes is the pancreatic. Since GLP-1 has the ability to stimulate meal-
induced insulin production in the β-cells of the pancreatic islets. GLP-1 possesses a huge
amount of properties, of which it can become an ideal antidiabetic agent.
==PAGE:18==
4

• GLP-1 has an insulinotropic effect, due to the fact that it is self-regulating. Normal
treatment of diabetes by other drugs can lead to hypoglycemia. This risk is reduced
significantly by using GLP-1. [4, 5, 1]
• GLP-1 enhances all steps in the biosynthesis of insulin, and it has an effect on β-cell
proliferation and differentiation. This leads to an increased β-cell mass. Besides
that, GLP-1 can inhibit glucagon secretion. [5, 1]
• GLP-1 acts as an inhibitor on the frequency of gastric emptying, and decrease the
movement of the small intestine. This leads to reduced absorption of nutrients
from GI. [5]
• GLP-1 acts upon CNS, in the manner of effecting the hypothalamus. Hypothala-
mus contain GLP-1 receptors, which leads to a decrease in the sensation of hunger
and an increase of satiety resulting in a decrease in food intake. [4, 5]
2.1.2
Mode of Action
GLP-1 binds to GLP-1R receptor, mainly upon the pancreatic β-cells. The receptor
belongs to G-protein-coupled receptors, class B, and consists of a seven transmembrane
domain (TMD), and an N-terminal extracellula domain (ECD). [6, 7]
When GLP-1 binds in the receptor, GLP-1 triggers a change of the conformation in
the receptor. As a consequence of conformational change of GLP-1R, a transduction
cascade is initiated. The Gs protein is activated, and leads to activation of adenylyl
cyclase. This causes an increase in the secondary messenger Cyclic adenosine monophos-
phate (cAMP) and Ca2+.
The secondary messenger cAMP mediates the next steps in the signal transduction pro-
cess, which are the excretion of insulin and activation of PKA and EPAC2. PKA and
EPAC2 activate insulin secretion. The cascade is shown in figure 2.2 [6, 7, 8, 9]

2.1.3
Metabolism and Excretion
Despite the fact, that GLP-1 has a promising antidiabetic agent, it has some disadvan-
tages as well. When GLP-1 is intravenous or subcutaneous injected, it will within a few
minutes undergo metabolic degradation.
The metabolic degradation is a consequence of two proteases dipeptidyl peptidase IV
(DPP-IV) and neutral endopeptidase 24.11 (NEP 24.11) [4, 10]. Where the most pre-
dominant degradation is due to DPP-IV.
DPP-IV cleaves the peptide bond between Ala8-Glu9, this cleavage results in the metabo-
lite GLP-1(9−36)-NH2, which has negligible activity. The binding affinity towards the
GLP-1R receptor is 100 fold lower compared to the GLP-1 (7-36)-NH2. DPP-IV is found
both in the bloodstream and as a membrane-associated molecule on the surface of T-cells.
GLP-1 is further cleaved by NEP 24.11 in between bond Asp15-Val16, Ser18-Tyr19,Tyr19-
Leu20, Glu27-Phe28, Phe28-Ile29, and Trp31-Leu32. The metabolites undergo rapid renal
clearance, due to the limited size. Cleavage sites are shown in figure 2.4. [1, 4]

2.2
Glucagon-like Peptide-1 Analogs on the
Marked
One strategy to overcome the short half-life is to inhibit DPP-IV. However this stategy
has adverse effects, leading to an immune response, and inhibiting DPP-IV’s cleavage
of other endogenous substrates. Another strategy was to find analogs of GLP-1 which
not undergo DPP-IV metabolic degradation.
[4, 1] Three important GLP-1 analogs
are described below. Two of them are described briefly, and the third, Liraglutide is
described in more depth, since it is of great importance for the current study.
2.2.1
Exenatide
The first analog which was approved in 2005 is Exenatide (AstraZeneca). It is an agonist
comprised of 39 amino acids.
The sequence is H-GEGTFTSDLSKQMEEEAVRLFIEWLKDGGPSSGAPPPS-NH2.
It is a synthetic version of the hormone Exendin-4, which is found in the saliva of
the lizard species, Gila monster. The half-life of Exenatide is 2.4 hours, and therefore, it
is suitable for twice-daily administration. One of the major advantages is that instead of
Ala8, it contains a Gly in position 8. This means that Exenatide has an exceptional resis-
tance towards DPP-IV cleavage. Furthermore, it has a similar binding affinity towards
the GLP-1R receptor as GLP-1. [4]
2.2.2
Semaglutide
The second analog is semaglutide (Ozempic, Novo Nordisk A/S). In this peptide Ala8
is substituted with Aib8 and Lys34 is substituted by Arginine. It contains a fatty acid
that is bound to Lys 26 via a miniPEG spacer. The sequence can be seen in figure 2.5
Figure 2.5: Semaglutide peptide sequence [4]

Semaglutide has a half-life of 165 hours, which is a result of Semaglutide binding to
human serum albumin. As a consequence of the long half-life, Semaglutide is suitable
for once-weekly administration.
2.2.3
Liraglutide
An important analog on the marked is liraglutide (Victoza®, Novo Nordisk A/S). Li-
raglutide has a palmitoyl group linked to an L-γ-glutamyl spacer attached to Lys26.
In position 34, Lysine is substituted with an Arginine. This substitution is used to

The FA chain at Lys26 increases the stability towards DPP-IV, due to sterical hin-

and to bind to human serum albumin (HSA). As a result, the half-life is significantly
prolonged and has been determined to be approximate 13-14 hours.[6, 11]

2.3
In vitro Biological Activity Assay
For testing the activity of the GLP-1 analogs, a Hithunter cAMP assay kit is used. This
is a cell-based functional assay. The assay exploits the fact, that activation of GPCR
leads to cAMP production. The cell line used in the assay overexpresses GPCR, and is
suitable for both detection of agonistic and antagonistic behavior of ligands.
The assay is based upon production of a luminescence signal. The signal is derived
from the fact, that an antibody (anti-cAMP Ab) and a β-galactosidase enzyme are
present. The β-galactosidase enzyme will form two parts, an enzyme donor (ED-cAMP)
and an enzyme acceptor (EA).
ED and EA by itself, have no luminescence effect. Cellular cAMP and ED-cAMP will
compete for binding to anti-cAMP antibody (Ab). When Ab is occupied with mostly
cellular cAMP, unbound ED-cAMP will interact with EA to form an active Enzyme
Fragment Complementation (EFC). The active EFC enzyme has luminescent signal,
which is proportional to the amount of cAMP produced by the cells [12] An overview is
provided in figure 2.7. [12]
Figure 2.7: A overview of the process causing a luminescence effect [12]

CHAPTER 3
Oligomerization
This chapter focuses on the oligomerization of GLP-1 analogs. The aim and framework
for the thesis, includes a discussion of preliminary studies, which is the foundation for
the current study.
3.1
Principle
Oligomerization is the chemical process where an ensamble of monomer form larger
species by interacting forces.
GLP-1 itself does not contain fatty acid (FA), and thereby GLP-1 is not prone to
oligomerize. To do so, it must contain FA side chains. Such is present in liraglutide
and other acylated analogs.
The driving force to oligomerize is to maximize the hydrophobic interactions. The hy-
drophobic side chains aggregate together at the interior of the larger species, away from
water, and van der Waals forces stabilize the oligomer.
When adding salt, the size of the oligomer increases. The reason for this effect could be
the increasing salt concentration leads to an increase in protein charge. The increase of
protein charge is due to the chloride ions bind to surface.

Importance and Function
The most predominant blood plasma protein is human serum albumin (HSA).[15] It
accounts for 60% of total blood plasma proteins, and have a normal concentration of
approximately of 42g/L.[16, 17]
The main function of HSA in the blood plasma is to regulate colloidal osmotic pres-

through the vascular system.
HSA has a remarkable capability of ligand binding.[16] This capability makes HSA very
important in terms of the pharmacokinetics of drugs. HSA affects the behavior of drugs
in such way, that it affects the efficacy and rate of delivery. [16, 18]
4.2

HSA is a polypeptide with a molecular weight of 66.5 kDa, and consisting of 585 amino
acids.[16, 17, 18] HSA is synthesized and excreted from the liver as a single polypeptide
chain, folded into an α helical structure. HSA has three homologous domains (I, II,
III) and two subunits A and B. The 3-D structure of HSA is stabilized by 17 disulphide
bridges. HSA is one of the few plasma proteins that are unglycosylated. [16, 18]

Fatty Acid Binding Sites
The binding affinity towards the seven binding binding sites is different. Binding sites
1-5 have a high binding affinity by polar/electrostatic interactions, whereas 6-7 are low
affinity binding sites. [18]
Binding site 1 is located in subdomain IB (4.2). This is a D-shaped cavity in the
center of the four-helix bundle. This FA binding site is in com comparison to the other
six binding sites more accessible for solvents. [19, 20]
Binding site 2 and 2’ are between subdomain IA and IIA, these binding sites are
the most enclosed in HSA, the FA is shielded the most from the solvent. When FA binds
to this site, this site is dislocated into two half-sites, leading to a conformational change
of HSA.
The conformational charge is due to rotation of domain I relative to domain II. It seems
when smaller FA binds to this site, FA can bind at both (2 and 2’ ) antiparallel, whereas
bigger FAs have such long tail, that they consume the space in such way that another
FAs can not fit. [19] For all FA length the carboxylate head-group of FA is located in
subdomain IIA. [19, 20]
Binding site 3 and 4 are in subdomain IIIA. The pocket is a T shaped cavity. [20]
The FAs are bound at right-angles each other with the methylene tails in contact. In
site 3 as the tails of the FAs increases the cavity forces the tail to bend in a U shape.
The bending of the tail, limit the lenght of the FA which can bind (up to 14). [19] Site
4 is longer and more narrow then site 3. [19, 20]
Binding site 5 is in subdomain IIIB, and is a hydrophobic channel in the full length
of the subdomain. The methylene tail expands into the tunnel. [19, 20]
Binding site 6 is located at the surface of the protein. It is located in the interface
between subdomain IIA and IIB. The methylene tail seems to attach due to salt-bridges.
[19, 20]
Binding site 7 is in subdomain IIA. This is a hydrophobic cavity, and analog to IIIA
but it is smaller. The FAs binds in a curved configuration.[19, 20]

6.1
Asymmetrical Flow Field-Flow
Fractionation
Asymetrical flow field-flow fractionation (AF4) is a fractionation and sepration method.
The preliminary field-flow fractionation (FFF) was invented in 1966 by Calvin J. Gid-
dings. [25] In 1987 FFF, was altered to AF4 [26]. The main difference between FFF
and AF4 is the channel geometry and the amount of permeable walls. [26]
The AF4 is today one of the golden methods, and is a commonly used method for
separation of biomolecules.
It resembles liquid chromatography, but it distinguishes
itself in the absence of a stationary phase. The separation is achieved by flow, where
a cross-flow perpendicular to the carrier liquid is applied. It is a gentle method, that
eliminates possible nonspecific interactions and shear due to the lack of stationary phase.
6.1.1
The Channel
AF4 has a simple design, where a liquid carrier containing the sample, is pumped into
a narrow, ribbon-like channel with a trapezoidal geometry.
==PAGE:42==
28
6 Instrumental Techniques
Figure 6.1: The trapezoidal geometry and layer of the channel [26]
The channel which is displayed in figure 6.1 consists of three layers.
• The top layer is a acrylic nonpermeable window.
• The second layer is a spacer, consisting of a thin Teflon spacer and is usually 350
µm thick. In this study, a 490 µm spacer is used to have a larger seperation volume.
• The third layer is a permeable plate which is covered by a membrane. The mem-
brane can be made of different materials. In this study a polyethersulfone (PES)
is used with a 1 kDa molecular weight cutoff.
Then a liquid cross-flow is applied perpendicular to the detector flow direction and the
membrane accumulates the biomolecule while the cross-flow passes through the mem-
brane. The flow profile within channel is parabolic. [27]
Due to the cross-flow, the biomolecules are forced towards the membrane. Fick’s law
will apply, creating a counteracting force (diffusion) until steady-state distribution is ob-
tained. [26] Smaller biomolecules have a higher diffusion coefficient and will be localized
toward the middle of the channel where the larger biomolecules have a smaller diffusion
coefficient and will be placed at the membrane surface. Due to the parabolic shape of
flow, the smaller biomolecules will experience a higher velocity of flow and therefore be
carried faster through the channel.

common arangement is based on 18 angles. The principle is that light consists of an

figure 6.5

to be displaced from the original shape by the external field. This phenomenon is called
polarizability, and is proportional to the refractive index increment dn/dc. [27, 31]

To make sure the correct peptide was obtained, UPLC-MS was used, followed by prepar-

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and Ultra-performance liquid chro-
matography (UPLC) are analytic methods to separate and identify different compounds.
[32, 33]
Both HPLC and UPLC are an adsorption chromatography, which means that the com-
pounds adsorb or adheres to the surface of porous silica in the stationary phase.
Both HPLC and UPLC are reverse phases. [32] The stationary phase is a nonpolar
silica media which is a porous material. The most popular column is an octadecyl car-
bon chain (C18)-bonded silica, where the compounds interact. Based on the difference
in polarity, the compounds are separated. This means that the nonpolar compounds
are retained longer in the column then the polar compounds which consequently eluate
first.
The separation can be performed under either isocratic or gradient conditions. The
isocratic is where the solvent composition is fixed at the beginning and throughout
==PAGE:50==
36
6 Instrumental Techniques
the run. When separation is run under gradient conditions, the solvent composition
is continuously changed throughout the run, which leads to increase in mobile phase
strength.
Gradient run is often used, since it ensures a better separation, and it shorten the run
time, which lower the cost. [32]
6.3.1
MS Detector
A mass spectrometer (MS) is a detector which ionizes the compound, and sorts the ion
based on their mass-to-charge ratio. The MS is located at the column outlet, and it is
a sensitive detector, which can be run as either a selected ion monitoring or total ion
chromatogram. [32]
When the analyte leaves the column, it has to be ionized before entering the MS. It
can be done in multiple ways, but most common is the electrospray ionization (ESI)
which is classified as a soft ionization technique.
The ESI can handle size from 20-
50,000 Da, and it can detect both the positive and negative ions. When the analyte
leaves the column as a solution, the solvent will evaporate and becomes gas phase, and
the ions are sent into the MS.
The mass spectra will result in an ESI−and an ESI+ spectrum. In the ESI+, a
positively charged species is added. This is often H+, Na+, K+, or NH4+. In the ESI−,
a loss of positively charged species is observed, which is often H+. [32]
6.3.2
ELS Detector
Evaporative light scattering detector (ELSD) is located at the column outlet. The eluent
from the HPLC is forced through a nebulizer, where it is transformed into an aerosole
droplets.
The mobile phase is then evaporated off inside a heated drift tube, and within min-
utes the analyte is dried as particles (solid). The particles are mixed with a carrier gas
and let into the detection region. From here a beam of light crosses the analyte and the
scattering of light is measured. The detector response relates the absolute quantity if
analyte present, independent of the solvent. [34, 35]

General Remarks
General remarks about analysis of data in Astra:
• Since the molar mass is based on light scattering (LS) signal, the retention time is
not a parameter which is important in this setup.
• The molar mass is very sensitive towards how the baseline is set, and very often
have to be adjusted during analysis. If the baseline is set to low the molar mass
becomes too high. If the baseline is set to high, the molar mass becomes too low.
• If some LS signals from different angles have a large standard deviation/uncertain-
ties (from here referred to SD) those can be disabled. This is often the case with
the first two or three LS angles and the last two angles.
• If the SD is above 100 % then Astra often state n/a in the molar mass.
• Mostly Zimm model was used to calculate the molar mass. However, other models
can be applied if Zimm has a high SD. The molar mass should have an SD below
10%, if not other models can be applied in order to have lower SD. However,
sometimes it can be necessary to have an SD up to 25%. Zimm, Debye and Berry
models can be adjusted in the degree of fit from 0-9 degree. It has been observed,
that a higher degree of fit, gives a higher SD.

Results and Discussion
Peptide oligomerization was only studied at pH 10.3 at 3 different NaCl concentrations
(140 mM, 70 mM and 35 mM). These conditions were chosen because of limited time
and limited amount of peptides. The preliminary study by Tine Frederiksen indicated
that the pH was not a signifant parameter for oligomerization of peptides.
The same conditions were chosen to study of binding of peptides with human serum
albumin (HSA). An HSA titration study was performed at pH 10.3 and 140 mM NaCl.
Furthermore the injected sample size of peptides in AF4-MALS was tested at pH 10.3
and 140 mM NaCl.

The mass recovery % from the different peptides were often very low. In the prelimi-

It was observed in the preliminary study that liraglutide had a high mass recovery (88%),
which was not the case here. It appears that the mass recovery varies notable.
Plausible explanations for the low mass recovery could be the membrane. Since the
membrane was chemically made, there was a probability that the pore size was not
evenly distributed. When the peptides were focused on the membrane, some might have
been flushed out with the channel outlet flow. However, the variance of the mass recov-
ery could not be due to the membrane, since the same membrane was used.
For another explanations for the varying mass recovery could be the time span from
dissolving the peptides and peptide/HSA to run of experiments. The peptides and pep-
tide/HSA were dissolved in February and run in June. However, in the HSA titration
study, the peptides/HSA samples were dissolved just before use. It was observed with
liraglutide/HSA that the mass recovery was 95% with the fresh dissolved liraglutide
while for the stored liraglutide/HSA the recovery was only 1 %. This indicates that
time from dissolving and to run the experiment could be a very important parameter.

the membrane pores leading to a decrease in pressure, and if the pressure would become
lower than 1 bar the detector and cross-flow would not retained.

Future Work
For future work different alteration could be made. For other studies some suggestions
are listed below:
• It has been observed the spacer decrease the size of oligomer, therefore another
spacer could be investigated. Semaglutide has a miniPEG spacer, this could be an
option on the peptides to see if it could increase the oligomer size.
• It has been observed the time from dissolving the peptide to analysis on AF4-
MALS has a significant effect on the mass recovery. This could lead to a study of
the time frame from dissolving the peptide to analysis.
The study of time frame could give a a perspective on the shelf life for the acylated
peptides.
• The temperatures effect on the stability could be investigated further.
• Another perspective could be the buffer the peptides were dissolved in. To in-
vestigate if the buffer system the peptides are dissolved in have an effect on the
oligomer size and stability.
• Optimizing the Af4-MALS procedure could be parameter to adjust. Combined
with better estimation for the UV extinction coefficient for peptide/HSA species.
• Further investigation in In vitro biological activity assay would be of interest. Since
a few of the peptides reveal a weak agonistic behavior at high concentration, they
could be tested at even higher concentration. If the truncated GLP-1 analogs used
could be altered, maybe with a His at 14 position or another spacer, this could
maybe give a agonistic behavior.

lamp did not work properly and at the end stopped working at all. To solve the

The second problem: When the peptides were measured, a UV spectrum did not
appear. There was only noise within the collected data. (see figure 12.2) On the

meant that it was not the UV lamp itself that did not work.
Figure 12.2: UV signal noise within collected data. blue line is RI spectrum and light

To resolve the problem, the UV lamp should be switch off in the software and
then the power should be switched off upon the instrument. For turning on the
UV lamp, the power is switch on at the instrument before the UV lamp is started
in the software. This resolved in normalization and proper spectrum recordings.
Since the problem appeared at every time it was turned on, the software was
updated. This update was enough for resolving the problem.
• Stabilization and Particles:
The AF4-MALS is very slow regarding stabilization before any measurement can
be made. This is also the case whenever a change of buffer system is made. It
can take everything from 30 minutes to 2 hours before the system is stable enough
for any measurement. The system is furthermore very sensitive towards particles
within the buffer system and in the samples. The solution was to filter the buffer
system through a 0.1µm filter and the samples through a 0.22µm filter.
• Clotting:
Some proteins have a tendency to aggregate, and form clots within the probe. This
was a problem, not due to the peptides within this current study, but due to other
experiments.
As a consequence of that, it was not possible to do experiments
before the probe, system and other parts were flushed to dissolve the clots.

• Aborting Sequence:
The total pressure of the system is often with 1 kDa membrane at 38-41 bar. The
preset of total pressure limit is 40 bar. This means when the total pressure reaches
40 bar, the Eclipse abort sequence to protect the system. To avoid this, the total
pressure limit should be set to 120 bar, which is the upper pressure limit for the
detectors.
• Leakage:
Since the membrane has a low cutoff of 1 kDa, leakage appears much easier. Leak-
age will effect the pressure of the channel. To resolve this the tubings must be
tightened more.
==PAGE:98==

• Pressure:
With a low cutoff membrane, pressure is more difficult to retain. The pressure
loss when cross-flow crosses the membrane is significant. The cross-flow pressure
should be kept over 1 bar to ensure proper cross flow and detector flow. [29, 37, 30]
This pressure is not the most important parameter, but if the pressure is 0-0.1 bar
the flows are not retained correctly. If detector and cross-flow are not correspond
to the set flow, the separation and results are not reliable. To ensure the correct
pressure and flows a more narrow tube can be installed to ensure a higher pressure
before entering the first detector. The position of the extra tube is displayed in
figure 12.3
