Summary
Encoding invariance into deep learning models are important for the ongoing progress
within the ﬁeld. In this thesis we present an extension of the current state-of-the-
art layer for encoding invariances, the spatial transformer layer. We extend the
layer from aﬃne transformations to diﬀeomorphic transformations, where the dif-
feomorphic transformations are based on recent work on integration of continues
piecewise-aﬃne velocity ﬁelds. We evaluate our implementation by training a num-
ber of deep learning models on two diﬀerent datasets. Our results shows that the
developed diﬀeomorphic transformer-layer outperforms the aﬃne transformer-layer
in most cases, however the implementation is currently not as fast as expected,
mainly due to the implemented gradient. We also investigate what the diﬀerent
learned transformations look like for the diﬀerent transformations-layers, and we
can conclude that the main purpose of the transformer layer is to enlarge features
in the images, regardless of transformation type. Surprisingly we found that by
combining the two layers i.e. one layer that allows for both aﬃne and diﬀeomorphic
transformations, outperformed all other models. Thus for future study we propose,
a further investigation of this combined layer. Additionally we also tried using the
developed diﬀeomorphic layer in a unsupervised setting, by learning data augmen-
tation, and our results showed that the diﬀeomorphic transformations ourperformed
conventional data augmentations schemes.
Contributions
• Extension of the spatial transformer layer from aﬃne transformations to dif-
feomorphic transformations.
• Insight into learned transformations of the spatial transformer layer for both
the aﬃne and diﬀeomorphic transformations.
• Learned data augmentation scheme based on the same diﬀeomorphic transfor-
mations.

1.
Introduction and motivation
When designing a mathematical model of a natural phenomenon, it is important to
think about invariances. A general deﬁnition of invariance is, that if an object A
is invariant to operation B, then A remains constant under the B operation [26].
An example within classical mathematics of invariance, is that the eigenvalues of a
square matrix are invariant to an orthogonal change of basis.
Within the ﬁeld of visual recognition like object detection, image classiﬁcation etc.
one often want to design models that are invariant to some variations in the visual
space. Variations in the visual space arrives from the fact that objects can be ro-
tated, translated, photographed using lenses with diﬀerent angles of view and have
diﬀerent illumination or backgrounds [22]. This is illustrated in Figure 1.1, where
the same cow is present 8 times in the image, but looks diﬀerent each time. All these
variations may contribute to the cow looking completely diﬀerent in two images, but
it still remains a cow. Mathematically this means that the image label is invariant
to certain transformations of the image. The goal is then to design a model that
is invariant to all these variations but still is able to detect the variations between
diﬀerent objects.
Figure 1.1: Illustration on how visual variations can make the same object (here a
cow) look diﬀerent, even though the ground true identity of the object is the same.

Because most models within visual recognition today is based on deep learning, it
is important to think about invariances within this domain. Without invariances
build into the deep learning models, the progress within this ﬁeld is limited by the
growth of computational power and data. Even though both of these are increasing
rapidly [20], it seems worthwhile to investigate what kind of transformations that
deep learning architectures need to be invariant towards, to still have an ongoing
progress within the ﬁeld.
So the question is then how to encode invariances into the deep learning framework.
In the following we will describe two diﬀerent approaches, and how this thesis aims
to investigate them both using the same general class of transformations.
Approach 1: Changing the architecture
The classical way to deal with invariances are through a Bayesian approach, where
priors are included into the model. Priors are what the model knows before it has
seen any data. In this approach one engineers most parts of the model, and then
leave enough ﬂexibility to capture the parameters not included in the prior. Even
though deep learning models is not Baysian models, they follow the same strategy
for encoding invariances, which is expressed as the invention of diﬀerent layer.
The architecture of the simplest artiﬁcial neural networks, the feed forward network,
do not include any form of transformation invariance. However by the introduction
of convolutional neural networks (CNN) [30] and pooling layers (in particular the
max-pooling layer) neural networks became somewhat invariant to translation. The
reason for this is that by pooling over a region it does not matter where exactly
within the pooling region the high activations are because the max pooling will
ignore everything else. This property has made CNN the standard model within
the ﬁeld of large-scale image recognition (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton [27],
Sermanet and LeCun [43]) in the resent years.
However, the research world in deep learning have converged into typically very
small pooling regions (2×2 or 3×3) [46]. Therefore the input data is only invariant
to transformations which shift the input a few pixels. The generated feature maps
by the network are therefore not invariant to large transformations of the input data.
Thus CNN is limited by its pooling mechanism to deal with variations in the spatial
arrangement of data. This is illustrated in the second row of Figure 1.2, where the
original images are perturbed by a few pixels, to show how little variation in the
input max-pooling is invariant towards.
To make deep learning invariant towards large transformations, Jaderberg et al. [23]
introduced a spatial transformer layer (ST-layer) which explicit allows for spatial
manipulation of data within the network. Through a regression network the ST-
layer learns an input dependent aﬃne transformation to apply to the input, so
that the remaining network preform as well as possible. Since the introduction of
the ST-layer, it has been implemented in many major deep learning frameworks

However, the full potential of the spatial transformer is not being utilized. The
framework allows for arbitrarily diﬀerentiable transformations Tθ, however only
aﬃne transformations seems to be used at this point, illustrated in the third row
of Figure 1.2. Therefore it seems worthwhile to investigate more ﬂexible transfor-
mations. This thesis therefore aims to investigate and develop a layer that allows
for much more complex transformations, illustrated in the last row of Figure 1.2.
The transformations we will investigate are based on the work of Freifeld et al. [15]
and Hauberg et al. [18] on diﬀeomorphic transformations called continues-piecewise
aﬃne based (CPAB).
Approach 2: Encoding data
In the second approach for encoding invariances into the deep learning framework,
the invariances are not accounted for within the network, but instead data is ex-
tended such that the network learns these invariances in a generic way. The ﬁrst
way to do this is preprocessing the data, such that each data observation have a
feature representation that is transformation invariant. Within vision recognition
the SIFT feature [33] is often used because it is scale and rotation invariant.
Another way to extend ones data is to use data augmentation (DA). DA is the
process of synthesizing new observations by applying pre-speciﬁed transformation
to the original dataset. DA is a rather old idea [44], but is now considered a key
element to training high-dimensional models. By augmenting data such that key
transformations are captured, one can both make the network invariant to these
transformations and train much deeper networks because of the extra data. DA was
a critical component in the famous deep-learning paper by Krizhevsky, Sutskever,
and Hinton [27]: "Without this scheme [DA], our network suﬀers from substantial
overﬁtting, which would have forced us to use much smaller networks."
However current DA schemes are based on manual speciﬁcation of the applied trans-
formations, and the method therefore relies on a best practise approach [45]. The
current most elaborate augmentation strategy is by Loosli, Canu, and Bottou [32]
where they considered horizontal and vertical translation, rotations, horizontal and
vertical scaling, hyperbolic transformations, and random Gaussian perturbations.
Together with the idea of Tangent Prop [44] they created an augmented version of the
MNIST [29], called InﬁMNIST, consisting of 8 million observations. They showed
a signiﬁcant diﬀerent in classiﬁcation performance between a classiﬁer trained on
the InﬁMNIST dataset than on the MNIST dataset (0.89% error rate compared to
1.42%).
To overcome the problem with manual speciﬁcation of the transformations, there has
within recent years been research into learning data augmentation i.e. such that the
implicit feature engineering with current data augmentation schemes are avoided.
For example, in Fawzi et al. [14] the authors optimize a class of transformations Tθ
such that the augmented samples yields maximal classiﬁcation loss. However, they
author restrain Tθ to a small group of aﬃne transformations .

As with the ﬁrst approach presented here, the full potential of learning data aug-
mentation, does not seem to be utilized because the transformations are often re-
strained to aﬃne transformations. This thesis therefore also aims to use the CPAB
transformations as augmenters in a learned data augmentation scheme, similar to
augmentation strategy by Hauberg et al. [18].
1.1
Project objectives
To summarize the goals of this thesis: the main goal is to develop a diﬀeomorphic
deep learning as a extension of the ST-layer [23], but where the aﬃne transforma-
tions are replaced by CPAB transformations [15]. The layer will then be tested
in two settings: the ﬁrst being a pure supervised setting, where the layer will be
inserted into a deep learning architecture, and then by training the network we will
see if the architecture gains anything from the large expressiveness the CPAB trans-
formations oﬀer. Secondly, the layer will be tested in a unsupervised-supervised
setting, where we will ﬁrst try to explore which CPAB transformations are present
in a dataset, and then based on this knowledge use the CPAB-layer to create a
augmented version of the dataset we can train a network on. Again this is done, to
test if the expressiveness of the CPAB transformations can outperform conventional
ways to do data augmentation.

Structure of the thesis

for making deep learning models invariant to spatial transformation of the input,

presented.
Chapter 5 presents the results obtained in this project and a discussion follows here-
after.
Finally in Chapter 6 we conclude on the results obtain.

Supervised deep learning
Deep learning models are a sub-ﬁeld of machine learning, that tries to learn a hier-
archical representation of data in order to model complex relationships in data [9].
This is achieved by using an architecture consisting of multiple nonlinear-processing
units. The ﬁeld is highly inspired by neuroscience, since each nonlinear-processing
unit can be seen as a analogous to the large network of interconnected neurons in
the brain [17].
In general the deep learning framework can be divided into 3 categories, dependent
on the task that is being modelled [9]:
1. In supervised deep learning the models try to learn a posterior distribution
p(y|x) by learning a mapping from input data {xi}N
i=1 to the target labels
{yi}N
i=1.
2. In unsupervised deep learning the models tries to ﬁt probability densities
p(x) over the input data x. These type of models try discover underlying
structure in the data.
3. Lastly semi-supervised deep learning are models that inherit the discrim-
inative models from point 1, which is supported by unsupervised learning
methods from point 2. Most notably within this category is reinforcement
learning. .
In this thesis we consider the models described in point 1. and will give a brief
description of the background for these type of models.

just one of several updating rules that can be used to iteratively update the weights
of the network. Other commonly used updating schemes are momentum, Adagrad
[13] and Adam [25]. Common for all these methods, is that they requires that we
can compute the gradient of the loss with respect to the weights of the network. For
very deep networks this is very cumbersome, even though it is just simple algebra.
Additionally any small change to the network, would require the gradient to be de-
duced ones more.

of errors or backpropagation [42]. The concept of backpropagation is best explained

task becomes easier.

2.3.1
Localization network
The ﬁrst part of the ST-layer is the localization network. The localization network
takes as input a feature map U ∈RH×W×C (3D tensor) with width W, height H and
C channels. In the simple case this can be a RGB image, where H × W would be
the dimensions of the image, and C would be 3. However, the layer can be inserted
arbitrarily into the network, and U is thus a general feature map. The localization
network then apply a regression network floc to U to produce a parametrization
θ = floc(U), for a given family of transformations Tθ. The size of θ is dependent on
the transformation family that is parametrized e.g. for an aﬃne transformation θ
would be a 6 dimensional vector.
floc can be any type of regression network, including both convolutional layers and
fully connected layers, but should end with a fully connected layer with d neurons,
where d is the dimensionality of θ.

3.
CPAB transformations
As stated in section 2.3, the full potential of the ST-layer is not being explored due
to Tθ only being modelled as aﬃne transformation. This may be due to a good
trade-oﬀin the aﬃne case between an expressive transformation that is diﬀeren-
tiable and has low computation time. Thus to extend the ST-layer we should ﬁnd
transformations that are more expressive than the aﬃne transformation, but not
much more computationally expensive.
By intuition, we want to design a transformation that is both diﬀerentiable and
non-destructive. The diﬀerentiability is required for the loss to be backpropagated
and the non-destructive property is wanted, else information is loss when we begin
to transform samples1. These two properties are fulﬁlled for diﬀeomorphisms. The
space of diﬀeomorphic transformation, oﬀer a space of highly expressive transfor-
mations that are also diﬀerentiable. By deﬁnition a transformation T : M →N is
a diﬀeomorphism that maps from one diﬀerentiable manifold to another such that
both the transformation and its inverse T −1 : N →M are smooth. The smoothness
will prevent "kinks" in the deformations while the invertibility prevents "folds". How-
ever, current diﬀeormorphic representations have been overly complicated, thereby
having a very long computation time, making them unfeasable to combine with any
deep learning model. Diﬀerent approaches have been made to overcome this prob-
lem [4, 51], but we will here present the recent work on continues piecewise aﬃne
based (CPAB) transformations by Freifeld et al. [15].
The foundation of the of the CPAB transformation is based on velocity ﬁelds, as
shown in Figure 3.1 (a).

4.3
Data augmentation setup
As stated in the introduction and project objectives, another goal for this thesis
was to use the CPAB transformations to do data augmentation similar to Hauberg
et al. [18]. The author has previously been working with this [47], as a initial study
leading to this master thesis, based on the original CPAB implementation [39]. The
code for this part can be found at the authors github [37]. In the following, a brief
overview of this setup is given.
In this setting we want to use the CPAB transformations for learning data augmen-
tation. The learning is done by investigation which transformations naturally occur
in the dataset and then trying to build a statistical model based on this knowledge.
We are in this setting only working on the LFW dataset. Pseudo-code can be seen
in Algorithm 3, but in short the process involves 3 steps:
1. Generating dataset of parametrizations: In the ﬁrst step we consider
all possible positive (same identity) image-pairs in LFW. For each pair, we
estimate a parametrization θ that describes the transformation between them.
This give us a large collection of parametrizations θ, that lies in a d-dimensional
space.
2. Building a statistical model: With the collection of parametrization, called
Θ, we can estimate a statistical model p(θ), which we can sample from. In this
we estimate p(θ) as a Zero-Mean Variational Mixture of Gaussian [5], which
automatically estimate the number of cluster and the cluster covariances. The
reason we use a zero-mean model, is due to the fact that we choose to include
both θ and −θ in Θ.

3. Sampling phase: With the statistical model, we can for each observation in
the training set, sample a number of transformations to augment the obser-
vations with. This is done by ﬁrst sampling a cluster index and them sample
a parametrization from the given cluster. We have thereby created an CPAB
augmented version of the dataset.

It is clear from the table, that the performance of the diﬀerent models does not diﬀer
a lot, which indicates that the MNIST dataset may be to simple to see the eﬀect of
the transformer layers. That said, we observe when we have very few observations
the networks with transformer layers have diﬃculty estimating a reasonable trans-
formation of the samples to apply, thus the performance is lower than the model
without transformer layer. Increasing the number of training samples to 1000, we
observe that the model with the ST-layer has the best performance. This can be
explained by dim(θ) for the diﬀerent transformations. For the ST-layer, dim(θ) is
38 of 62
==PAGE:42==
equal to 6, meaning that we converge fast onto a reasonable transformations. For the
CPAB-layer, dim(θ) is equal to 20 for P2-tessellation and 58 for P4-tessellation, and
convergence onto a reasonable transformation is therefore not guaranteed within
the 50 epochs when using only 1000 training samples. Increasing the number of
training samples beyond 1000, we now see that the P4-CPAB-layer has the best
performance1. However, as it can be see from the timings it takes more time to
train networks with a CPAB-layer than networks with the ST-layer. It is clear the
CPAB-layer should take longer time to compute due to the added complexity and
expressiveness of the layer, however from these set of simple experiments, we cannot
conclude that the extra expressiveness that the CPAB-layer oﬀers outweighs the
increase in training time.
The high training time of the of the CPAB-layer can mainly be explained due to
the gradients of the layer being computed by ﬁnite-diﬀerence. Without a working
analytic gradient, ﬁnite-diﬀerence oﬀers a simple solution to getting the gradient
working, however it scales with O(d) where d = dim(θ), making the computations
infeasible for tessellations ﬁner than P4. Secondly it should be noted, that the code
could probably be optimized in several ways be make the transformation computa-
tions faster. It is therefore clear that if the CPAB-layer is to be practically applicable
in the future several optimizations of the implementation need to be made.

5.1.2
Distorted MNIST
In the second set of experiments, we have created two distorted versions of MNIST,
called rts and cpab (ref. section 4.2.2). The ﬁrst dataset is engineered to match the
ST-layer and second is engineered to match the CPAB-layer. Table 5.2 shows the
classiﬁcation accuracy and training time for the diﬀerent models (see Table 4.2). It
is important to state that in these experiments the total number of trainable pa-
rameters are approximately the same for all the models (285K), thus models with
transformer layers have fewer parameters in their classiﬁcation network. The com-
parison between the diﬀerent models should therefore be fair.
From the table we observe that the transformer enabled networks outperform net-
works without transformer layers. This is not surprisingly since the datasets was
created with the focus that the transformers should have a active role. However it
still indicates that it is worth distributing some of the parameters to the localization
network and thereby getting optimized transformed images to classify, at the cost
of a smaller classiﬁcation network. Secondly we observe that the ST-layer outper-
forms the CPAB-layer on the rts MNIST dataset, and the CPAB-layer outperforms
the ST-layer on the cpab MNIST dataset. This is nor surprisingly since we have
engineered each dataset to ﬁt one of the two layers. The best performing model in
both cases are the model which have both a ST-layer and a CPAB-layer. It is a
normal procedure in many models to ﬁrst use a linear model (here the ST-layer) to
do a rough estimation before one apply a non-linear model (here the CPAB-layer).

This is also the case here. The high training time of the ST-CPAB-CNN network
is due to the CPAB-layer, and it therefore seems that if one choose to insert a
CPAB-layer into a neural network, one can as well insert a ST-layer in front since
this only increase performance without increasing the training time signiﬁcantly. At
last we can observe from the table that there still is a diﬀerence between using a
FCN or a CNN network, with or without transformer layers. It seems that the spa-
tial invariance that CNN oﬀers, is added to the spatial invariance that the diﬀerent
transformer layers provide. The convolutional layers are also better at modelling
local structure than fully connected layers, meaning that we extract better features
in our localization network that in the end will give us better transformations.

LFW
The third set of experiments is performed on the more challenging LFW set. The
task was to predict if two images are the same person or not. Table 5.3 show the

layer, that can justify the increased training time. The combined ST-CPAB-layer
again achieves the best performance, which indicates that it is beneﬁcial for the
network to be able to deform the images both aﬃne and diﬃomorphic.

It should be noted that both the ST-layer and CPAB-layer sometimes suﬀers from
local minimum. If the learning rate is not set probably in the beginning and adjusted
while training, both transformer layers can move the faces outside the image, mak-
ing all training examples looking completely black before the classiﬁcation network.
At this point, the network cannot learn to move the faces back inside the image,
and therefore unable to learn anything. The ST-layer seems to be suﬀering most
from this problem, since the translation is determined by 2 parameters, compared
to 58 for the CPAB-layer (non-linear combination of all parameters). It therefore is
harder choose a "bad" setting for the parameters in the CPAB-layer.

end supervised task, while using the CPAB transformations as data augmentation
is a unsupervised-supervised task. Using the CPAB-layer we got 0.8781 ± 0.0113
in accuracy on this task, while we got 0.8342 ± 0.03640 using the 10x CPAB aug-
mentation scheme. Unsurprisingly the performance is highest when the network
gets to learn the transformations itself, instead of getting fed transformed images
that we hope encode the diﬀerent invariances we want the network to be invariant
towards. The "bottleneck" in the unsupervised-supervised setting is therefore the
unsupervised part i.e. the statistical model we are sampling form. An even more
advanced sampling scheme could yield higher performance.

6.
Conclusion
In this thesis we have investigated how diﬀeomorphisms can be incorporated into
the deep learning framework, such that neural networks could become invariant to
general transformations. If invariances are not build into the deep learning models,
it would require large amount of data feed into the models, before they learns the
invariances in a generic way.
The current state-of-the-art framework for modelling invariance in deep learning,
spatial transformer layers, only accounts for aﬃne transformations of the input.
The goal of the project has been to extend this framework by exchanging the aﬃne
transformations with the much more complex and expressive diﬃomorphic transfor-
mation called continues-piecewise aﬃne based (CPAB).
The developed layer was ﬁrst tested on the MNIST dataset, both in its original
form and in a cluttered version.
The CPAB-layer outperformed the ST-layer in
most cases, however the computation time is substantially longer than the ST-layer,
and for these set of experiments we cannot conclude that the performance gain jus-
tify the longer training time. For the CPAB layer to practical applicable in the
future several optimization need to made to the implementation to make it faster,
especially to the implemented gradient.
Hereafter, we tested the CPAB-layer on a more challenging dataset, namely the
labelled faces in the wild (LFW) dataset. Again the CPAB-layer outperformed the
ST-layer. By investigation of the learned transformations, it is clear that both the
ST-layer and the CPAB-layer learns to enhance facial features, either by zooming
in on the face or by expanding the face.
For the two sets of experiments we also tried to combine the two layers into a ST-
CPAB-layer that allow for both aﬃne and diﬃomorphic deformation of the input.
This layer is outperforming the individual transformation layer in all cases. It there-
fore seems worthwhile to investigate this layer further in the future.
At last the CPAB-layer was tested in the setting of data augmentation, where we
unsupervised learned which transformations was present in the LFW dataset and
then augmented the LFW dataset with them. The results clearly showed that this
form of learned data augmentation scheme outperforms more conventional data
augmentation schemes. However, it is still more beneﬁcial to put the CPAB-layer
inside the network, such that the network can learn the transformations on its own.

A.
Learning goals
Following has been the learning goals for this project:
1. Study the structure of the spatial transformer [23] and the diﬀeomorpic trans-
formation in [15] called continues piecewise aﬃne based transformations (CPAB).
2. Combine the theory of these two topics into a CPAB transformation layer.
3. Get familiar with the Tensorﬂow [1] deep learning framework.
4. Implement the CPAB transformation layer in Tensorﬂow.
5. Test the layer against reference implementation [39]. Additional test the speed
of the layer, and if necessary improve the implementation until satisfying speed
is achieved.
6. Use the layer in a supervised learning setting i.e. supervised deep learning.
Compare the developed model, to a model without transformer layer and with
aﬃne spatial transformer layer.
7. Use the layer for in a unsupervised learning setting i.e data augmentation.
Speciﬁc use the layer for learning data augmentation similar to [18]. Compare
the developed model, to conventional data augmentation schemes.
8. Investigate what the learned transformations look like and see if there seems
to be general pattern in the learned transformations.

B.1
Further implementation details
As stated in section 4.1, we have made 3 diﬀerent implementations of the CPAB-
transformer in the project, each being a faster version of the former.
Pure TF: The ﬁrst implementation was based on pure Tensorﬂow. By this we mean
that the implementation only consist of basic Tensorﬂow functions. The advantage
of doing this is that each basic function in Tensorﬂow already has a gradient equipped
to it, and when the computational graph is build it is ready to use for training neural
networks. This is how the original spatial transformer layer was implemented [40].
However as it is shown in Table B.1 the resulting transformation takes very long
time to compute and scales badly with tessellation size. This is mainly due to the
fact that Tensorﬂow handles loops and conditional statements (if, else) badly, which
is part of Algorithm 2. The reason is by intuition that the computational graph,
as in Figure 4.1, becomes very complex when loops and conditional statements are
included, and Tensorﬂow is therefore poorly optimized in this case.
TF-CUDA: A second implementation was therefore made that moves most of the
Algorithm 2 to c++, where we could take advantage of the CUDA GPU program-
ming platform. CUDA is the common language for doing GPU programming, and
Tensorﬂow oﬀers a great interface for doing this. The disadvantage of doing this, is
that the layer did not automatically had a gradient equipped to it. To equip an user
deﬁned gradient (either an analytic or ﬁnite-diﬀerence) to an user deﬁned function,
took longer time than anticipated because this functionality is very experimental
and was not fully developed in the beginning of the project. After solving diﬀerent
bugs, in the end we succeeded. As it can be seen in Table B.1, the computation
time was reduced, close to the reference implementation. The reason it still takes
a little longer is due to a overhead by the Tensorﬂow framework. For the function
to work within the deep learning framework, it need to accept a batch of images
and parametrizations (ref. section 2.1.2). To do this parallelization, the build in
mapping function tf.map_fn(·) was used, which oﬀers a quick way to parallelize
functions.
TF-CUDA-Batch: By investigating the GPU usage of the previous implementa-
tion, it showed that the GPU was poorly utilized. Therefore instead of using the
tf.map_fn(·) to parallelize the function, the parallelized computations was moved

to CUDA. This better utilized the GPU and also reduced some of the overhead from
Tensorﬂow. Looking again at Table B.1 we see that we get timing that a similar to
those of the reference implementation on average.
