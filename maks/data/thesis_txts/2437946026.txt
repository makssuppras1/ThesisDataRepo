Abstract
Model predictive control (MPC) is a class of control algorithms that predict future outputs
using a model of the system and compute the optimal control intervention according to
a cost function. This thesis investigates the possibility to apply MPC to engine speed
control during shifting phases to improve fuel economy.
First, a control-oriented model of the engine speed and torque dynamics is developed.
Non-linearities in the engine behaviour are addressed by adopting a grey-box linear
parameter-varying (LPV) model, obtained through Jacobian linearization, from which a set
of linear time invariant (LTI) state space models is derived. The parameters estimation is
performed upon real measurements collected during experiments in a test rig.
A ﬁrst MPC formulation is proposed, referred to as non-integral MPC. The cost
function and the constraints are deﬁned and the optimization problem is expressed as a
Quadratic Programming (QP) problem. This controller performs well in ideal conditions
but is sensitive to model inaccuracies and is affected by output offsets. An integral MPC
is therefore developed, based on a velocity form of the model, that guarantees offset-free
reference tracking in the presence of constant modelling errors. Both MPC schemes are
based on the LPV model. Depending on the operating conditions, the appropriate LTI
model is selected to generate the predictions. For this reason, the controllers are referred
to as Scheduled MPC.
The performance of the controllers is evaluated under ideal conditions to assess the
output speed and control effort properties. Experimental data is then used to simu-
late different downshift scenarios and fuel consumption is estimated through an engine
map. According to simulations, the proposed MPC leads to a reduction of 2-5% in fuel
consumption, compared to the current controller.

In the recent years, an increasing awareness regarding pollution and sustainability together
with the introduction of new regulations have pushed cars producers towards a new type
of research, more oriented to the development of vehicles with a reduced impact on
the environment. Along with these new regulations, the processing power of computers
available inside vehicles has increased, making it possible to use more sophisticated and
complex control algorithms. Control strategies play an important role in enhancing an
engine performance, both in terms of power and torque delivered but also in terms of
efficiency and fuel consumption. For this reason, one branch of this research is dedicated
to the study of new and different control algorithms.
Model Predictive Control (MPC) has become increasingly popular in the automotive
ﬁeld (examples are found in [11], [15] and [7]). MPC uses a model of the system to predict
outputs over a future horizon. At each step, the next control action is computed based
on the minimization of a performance index or cost function over the future horizon.
MPC mainly differs from ordinary controllers, like Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID)
controllers, thanks to its predictive nature, which allows the controller to react faster, to
reduce the control effort and to adjust in advance to future changes in the reference
trajectory. The control objective can be intuitively formulated as a cost function, a more
direct approach compared to PID. Furthermore, MPC allows to include constraints in the
calculation of the control sequence, to better model limitations of the plant and achieve
a physically feasible solution [6].
1.2
Aim
The aim of this thesis work is to develop an MPC solution for engine speed control
during shift phases, with the ﬁnal purpose of reducing fuel consumption in comparison
to the current controller, which will be referred to as baseline controller. The project can
be divided into three milestones:

1.3
Limitations
The model parameters are estimated upon measurements collected during tests carried
out on an S60 model, in a dynamometer test bench. The tests are performed on the
entire vehicle, which means that the recorded signals are subjected to multiple effects
and many properties of the engine subsystems are not accessible. In particular regarding
the ignition efficiency, the available data can provide just a raw estimation. The engine
is tested in a speed range of about 2000 rpm and the gearshifts are performed at low
torque. As it will be shown, the estimated parameters and the model provide a good
ﬁtting of the measurements, however the model could be improved by carrying out more
extensive and precise tests. The controller is simulated with a simple model of the engine,
therefore the results that are obtained are just indicative. A ﬁnal assessment of the fuel
consumption can be obtained only by testing the MPC on the real engine.
1.4
Specification of issue under investigation
This thesis focuses on engine speed control during downshifting phases. In the consid-
ered vehicle, the engine is connected to the wheels through an automatic transmission. In
normal conditions, a clutch couples the engine output shaft with the transmission input
shaft and torque is transmitted from the engine to the wheels and vice versa. During a
shift, the clutch is open and the torque applied by the transmission to the engine shaft is
reduced. The clutch is released because the engine speed has to change. More precisely,
a downshift requires the speed to increase while an upshift causes the speed to decrease.
A controller is needed to handle this transition. Speed is controlled by acting on the
torque produced by the engine, according to the current load. Depending on how the
torque is used, the speed trajectory changes and so does the achieved fuel consumption.

engine modeling, MPC and previous works in the ﬁeld. The second part deals with engine
modeling. A preliminary model of the engine is formulated, highlighting which elements
have to be included and which shall be disregarded. The model allows to deﬁne the

2.1
Introduction
An engine is a machine that generates mechanical power from the combustion of a
certain type of fuel.
When the fuel is burned, the chemical energy stored within is
released as heat, which is used to actuate the motion of the mechanical components.
The Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) receives its name from the fact that the com-
bustion takes place inside the engine itself. A mixture of air and fuel is injected into
speciﬁc chambers, called cylinders, where it gets compressed and ignited. The air acts as
an oxidizing agent and allows the combustion to take place.
Modern ICEs are the result of over a century of research and development and their
complexity and performance have dramatically increased since the early prototypes. One
milestone in ICEs history, is the introduction of the atmospheric engine by N. A. Otto and
E. Langen, in 1867. Later in 1876, Otto proposed a ﬁrst prototype of the four-stroke engine,
whose working principle is still in use today. ICEs are the most popular and widespread
type of engine used in nowadays vehicles and, despite the recent development of new
technologies such as electric engines, they are going to play an important role in the ﬁeld
of transportation for many years to come.
Depending on the type of combustion, ICEs can be divided into two categories:
Spark-Ignited (SI) and Compression-Ignited (CI) engines. In SI engines, the air and fuel
are mixed and compressed inside the cylinders, where the combustion is initiated by an
electric spark. In CI engines, instead, the air is compressed and the fuel is injected into
the cylinders directly. The ignition takes place because of the heat of the compressed air.
Furthermore, SI engines use petrol or gas as fuel whereas CI engines run on diesel. CI
engines outperform SI engines in terms of efficiency, but at the same time produce more
harmful chemicals. This thesis was based on an SI petrol engine by Volvo, therefore only
SI engines will be considered from now on.

Work and power
If a movement is produced as a consequence of the applied force, than it is said that the

potential rotation of the transmission shaft and wheels that the force exerted by the

The core of the engine, where the power is produced, is the cylinders. A cylinder
is a metal chamber where air and fuel are compressed and combustion takes place.
Inside, another component called piston moves up and down along the cylinder, in a
reciprocating motion, and it transfers the force from expanding gas to the crankshaft
through a connecting rod. The piston is said to be at Top Dead Center (TDC) when it is
farthest from the crankshaft, corresponding to the crankshaft angular position θ = 0◦.
In this case, the available volume in the cylinder is at its minimum value Vc, called
clearance volume. The piston is at Bottom Dead Center (BDC) when is closest to the
crankshaft, corresponding to θ = 180◦. In this position the volume inside the cylinder is
maximum and called displaced volume Vd. The ratio between maximum and minimum
volume is called compression ratio rc.
The crankshaft is driven by the movement of the cylinders and converts the recip-
rocating motion into rotational motion, which is then transmitted to the wheels through
the transmission driveline.
After the combustion, the mixture exits through the exhaust valve into the exhaust
manifold, responsible for collecting gasses from all the cylinders. The exhaust mixture
is sent either to a catalytic converter directly and is then emitted, or is used to drive a
turbine in turbocharged engines, as in the considered case.
2.4
The ideal four-stroke cycle
The working process of the SI engine is the four-stroke cycle, illustrated in ﬁgure 2.3. As
it moves back and forth, each piston completes a cycle composed of four phases:
==PAGE:27==
2.4 The ideal four-stroke cycle
9
1. Intake: During this phase, the intake valve is open and the piston moves down
from TDC to BDC, letting fresh air ﬂow into the cylinder. At the end of the intake
stroke, the available volume is Vd and is ﬁlled with a mixture of air and fuel.
2. Compression: Once the piston has reached BDC, the intake valve and fuel injector
are closed and the compression stroke begins. The piston moves back to TDC and,
as the volume changes from Vd to Vc, the gas inside the cylinder is compressed.
As a consequence, pressure and temperature rise. At the end of this second phase,
an electric spark ignites the combustion. The exact spark timing depends on many
factors and on the current operating conditions of the engine, but usually occurs
about 30◦before TDC. In this phase, mechanical work has to be produced by the
rotating crankshaft on the piston in order to push it up to TDC.
3. Expansion: The combustion continues until about 40◦after TDC. The combustion
causes the pressure and heat to increase rapidly and the piston is pushed down
by expanding gasses. Therefore, in this phase, work is extracted from the energy
stored inside the fuel and power is transferred to the crankshaft to keep it rotating.
In the end of the expansion stroke, the exhaust valve is open and the mixture
starts ﬂowing out because of difference in pressure between the cylinder and the
exhaust manifold.
4. Exhaust: During the last phase, the exhaust mixture exits through the valve,
pressure decreases and the piston moves back to TDC.

of the cycle, at a certain instant, so that the crankshaft does not slow down.

2.6
Control of SI engines
2.6.1
The control structure
An engine is a complex and heterogeneous system, in which all elements have to work
in a precisely coordinated manner. A control structure is therefore needed to make sure
that each component works nominally and that the overall system is able to deliver the
torque required to actuate the vehicle motion.
The control structure is composed of many layers. At low level, local controllers
regulate, for example, the position of the throttle, the pressure in the intake manifold,
the injection timing, the valves opening, the cylinders temperature and so on. Local
controllers just make sure that each sub-system state follows a certain reference, with a
speciﬁc type of response, but do not determine what the value of that reference is. This
is done by higher level controllers, instead, according to the current operating conditions
of the engine and the torque demands of the vehicle and the driver.
The control structure should guarantee, at the same time, the best efficiency, good
driveability and should ensure safety for the driver and passengers. As these three objec-
tives are often in contrast with each other, the control action is chosen as a compromise.
2.6.2
Torque-based control structure
From the driver’s prospective, the engine is nothing more than a very complicated actuator
used to make the wheels spin. When driving, a person does not need to know what is

2.6.3
Torque demand conversion and realization
The last block in the torque-based control structure is the engine itself. The internal
control system has to act on the engine hardware in order to make sure that the requested
torque is produced. This means that the torque request has ﬁrst to be translated into a
suitable set of references for the engine internal controllers.
Eq. (2.13) shows that the average produced torque depends on many variables and
parameters, such as air mass, efficiency, intake and exhaust manifolds pressure, type of
fuel and others. However, from a control prospective, the torque is mainly controlled by
acting on:
• The air-ﬂow into the intake system and the cylinders
• The combustion ignition timing
• The air-to-fuel ratio.
As a result, although the control architecture is quite complex, three main loops can be
usually identiﬁed, dedicated to the control of the air-system, the ignition and the fuel
injection.

Feedback control is instead the main structure used to regulate the air-system, the
ignition and the injection. Feedback control architecture is based on the principle that
the information about the current condition of the system, if accessible, can be used
to compute the appropriate control action. The states of a system are a set of physical
quantities that describe the current conditions of the plant well enough for the control
purpose. The outputs of the system, instead, are physical quantities that can be measured
through sensors and expressed as a function of the states. The reference signals indicate
the value one or more of the states and outputs have to be regulated at. Generally, the
control action is governed by the difference between the reference and the corresponding
state or output multiplied by the appropriate gain. Thus, the controller and the system
form a closed-loop. This type of architecture usually ensures a better performance in
terms of stability and lowers the controller sensitivity to modelling errors.
The real control structure inside the engine, in practice, is not limited to simple
feedback loops, but implements multiple and sometimes nested feedback loops, together
with feed-forward control and look-up tables. However, describing the engine internal
control architecture in detail lies outside the scope of this thesis.
As mentioned, sensors make it possible to measure just a limited set of states di-
rectly. For example, torque and speed are both important states of the engine system,
however only the speed sensors are installed in the vehicle, as measuring the torque is
complicated and expensive. The missing states, including the torque, have then to be
estimated. Moreover, the measurements are often affected by noise and therefore are not
totally reliable. For these reasons, observers are implemented to work in parallel to the
controllers. An observer uses the available information, including a model structure of
the system and the sensors measurements, to produce a more reliable estimation of the
states and outputs needed by the controllers.
The three loops will now be described shortly, focusing in particular on how the
air-ﬂow, the ignition timing and the fuel injection inﬂuence the produced torque.
Fuel injection control
The injection control regulates the amount of fuel and how it enters into the cylinders.
The main reference is λref, that allows to deﬁne the proportion between air and fuel. The
value of λ has an impact on the emissions, fuel economy and efficiency in general. In
nowadays engines it is usually regulated to the optimal value, also called stoichiometric
value, that offers the best overall performance.
In practice, given the amount of air
ﬂowing into the cylinders, the injection controller makes sure that a fuel mass is injected
in the cylinders to keep the normalized λ close to 1 as much as possible. For this reason,
λ is from now on not considered as a control input but just as an engine parameter.

Even though θi,opt offers the best efficiency, there are many reasons why the actual
timing is often different from the optimal value. For example, if the ignition timing is
not optimal, it means that the engine is running with a certain output torque but has
the potential to produce more torque, if θi is moved closer to θi,opt. In this case, it is
said that the engine has a torque reserve, that can be used against disturbances. The
ignition timing is also used to protect the engine against knock. Engine knock is a serious
problem that occurs when there is an abnormal combustion inside the cylinders and has
to be avoided as it can damage the engine structure. Another reason why ignition timing
is changed is that the air-system is not very fast to adapt to rapidly changing torque
demands, because of the intake manifold ﬁlling dynamics, while the ignition can be
adapted almost instantaneously. Therefore, ∆θi is used to cut the produced torque when,
for example, there is a sudden drop in the torque request.
Although spark retard and spark advance can be very useful and sometimes are
necessary, there is also a major drawback.
If the torque is lowered by reducing the
air-ﬂow, then the fuel injected in the cylinders is reduced accordingly. If the torque is
reduced by changing the ignition timing, instead, more fuel is injected in the cylinders
than in the previous case to produce the same torque. Hence, a deviation of the ignition
timing from the optimal value leads to a higher fuel consumption. This will be considered
when designing the cost function for the MPC.
2.6.4
Torque base, instantaneous torque and torque reserve
Equation (2.13) is useful to highlight torque dependencies, however is not really practical
from a control perspective.
One reason for this is that air and ignition have quite
different dynamics and quite different effects on the torque. The air is characterized by
slow transients and thus has an impact on the long-term, while the ignition is used for
short-term intervention. Therefore a distinction can be made between long-term torque,
controlled by the air, and short-term torque, depending on the ignition timing.
Another point of view can be adopted, but the result is unchanged.
It was also
mentioned earlier that the maximum torque, that the engine can generate, is limited by
the air-ﬂow. The ignition timing has instead a modulating effect. As a consequence, it
makes sense to distinguish between potential torque, depending on the air-ﬂow only, and
the actual instantaneous torque, that can be equal to or lower than the potential torque
depending on the ignition timing.
It comes natural then, to deﬁne the following concepts:
• Torque base: is the maximum or potential torque that the engine can generate.
It is controlled by controlling the air-ﬂow, but depends also on engine speed,
temperature and other parameters. It will be indicated as Tq,base.
• Instantaneous torque: is the actual torque in a certain instant, without including
losses. It cannot exceed the base torque and is modulated by the ignition efficiency.
It will be indicated with Tq,inst and is described by the following equation:
Tq,inst = Tq,base ηi(∆θi)
(2.14)

• Torque reserve: is the difference between the torque base and the instantaneous
torque. It represents the margin between the realization of the torque and the

Tq,rsv = Tq,base −Tq,inst = (1 −ηi(∆θi)) Tq,base

This representation of torque is frequently adopted and is in use at VCC. Referring to
the torque-based control structure, the torque request that the engine receives is actually
separated into its two components: a torque base request, which will be indicated with
Tq,base,req and a requested torque reserve, indicated with Tq,rsv,req.

3.1
Overview
The powertrain is deﬁned as the group of components that generate power and deliver
it to the road [12]. It includes the engine, the transmission, drive shafts and the wheels.
The term driveline indicates the elements of the powertrain excluding the engine.
In this context, the transmission refers to the components responsible for the conver-
sion and transmission of the engine output torque to the drive shafts, namely the torque
converter, the gearbox and the differential. The torque converter is used to couple and
decouple the engine and the transmission. The gearbox contains a gearset that is used to
change the transmission ratio between the engine and the drive wheels. The differential
is a constant ratio gearbox that transfers torque from the transmission to the drive shafts
and allows the wheels to rotate at different speed, when the vehicle is on a turn. At the
end of the chain, the drive shafts transmit the torque from the differential to the wheels
[9].
This thesis is based on an S60 Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle (PHEV), represented
in ﬁgure 3.1. This model is characterized by all-wheel drive powertrain, combining an
SI turbocharged petrol engine, in the front, with an Electric rear axle drive (ERAD). The
ERAD is used to drive the rear wheels, to recharge the battery and to supply power boost.
The PHEV powertrain can be powered by the SI engine or by the electric motor only or
by both. In other conﬁgurations, the battery can be recharged by the running engine or
by exploiting brake energy [14].
The vehicle uses an AW TG-81SC automatic gearbox. The engine is connected to the
transmission through a torque converter. An Integrated Starter Generator (ISG) is mounted
at the engine crankshaft. The ISG works as a starter but is also used as a generator to
recharge the battery and, under certain conditions, provides power boost [14]. The torque
converter is a device used in powertrains with an automatic gearbox. It is the mechanical
interface between the engine crankshaft and the transmission shaft, connected to the
gearbox. Inside the torque converter, the crankshaft is rigidly connected to a component
called impeller and the transmission shaft is connected to the turbine. The rotational
motion is transferred from the impeller to the turbine through hydraulic coupling [11].

Modeling
4
4.1
Introduction
An MPC relies on a dynamical model of the system to compute the optimal control
intervention. The model should be developed considering two conﬂicting principles:
1. The model should be complete enough to describe the system dynamics.
2. The model should be kept as simple as possible.
If the ﬁrst principle is violated, then the MPC tries to control the system with missing
information. Model inaccuracies and errors affect the controller directly and can degrade
its performance, leading to worse transients than expected from simulations. In the worst
case, the system can become unstable. At the same time, a complex model is difficult to
handle and, especially in the case of MPC, it causes the computational effort to increase
dramatically. In real-time applications this becomes a serious issue and it may be not
possible for the controller to meet the time constraints. Therefore, the complexity of the
model is limited by the characteristics of the computing platform. It has to be underlined,
also, that a very complicated and accurate model is not necessarily a better model. The
quality of the model is determined in relation with the control objective. A controller
may, for example, not need information about high frequency dynamics, or about some
disturbance effects, and this allows to simplify the problem.
As it will be shown in this chapter, it is possible to describe the engine behaviour with
a set of differential equations. It is then decided to use a State Space (SS) model structure,
because it allows to describe the inner dynamics of the systems and is a convenient way
to represent Multi-Input-Multi-Output (MIMO) systems. Moreover, MPC design based on
SS models is a consolidated practice and is easy to implement Matlab.
A single SS model is not sufficient because the engine has a non linear behaviour
and the model is applicable only near a speciﬁc operating point.
It will be shown
that, by using Jacobian linearization on the non-linear functions describing the engine,
it is possible to derive a Linear Parameter-Varying (LPV) model based on the state space
structure. For practical implementation, the LPV model is then approximated by a ﬁnite
set of SS models.

4.3
Model formulation
4.3.1
The objective
When it comes to modelling, one has to ﬁrst to set the control objective. This allows
to determine all the variables that have to be taken into consideration and to disregard
useless information. In this thesis, a controller has to be designed for the engine speed.
Thus, the main objective of the model is to describe the speed dynamics.
The speed depends on the output torque Tq,e and on the load torque Tq,load. The
controller acts on the speed by requesting a speciﬁc value of torque to the engine, which
can then be considered as a torque actuator. This is possible thanks to the torque-based
control structure described in 2.6.2. It is clear that the controller operates at very high
level and thus does not need any knowledge of the engine internal processes. What is
important to capture is the closed-loop response to a torque request.

supposed that the torque requested during a shift is low so that the effects of the turbo

From eq. (4.16) it is seen that the parameter α determines how fast the output settles

−β/α is the system steady gain, indicating the ratio between the output and the input

When an increased torque base request reaches the engine, the pressure in the intake
manifold has to increase and more air comes into the cylinders. If the crankshaft is
rotating at high speed, the cylinders move rapidly allowing a quick pressure change.
Conversely, if the engine speed is low, the pressure change is also slower. This has a
direct impact on the torque base dynamics: the torque changes faster at high speed than
at low speed. It is clear, then, that the two parameters α and β are speed-dependent and
their absolute value is expected to increase with the engine speed. This already shows
that a single LTI model is not sufficient to properly model the engine behaviour in the
entire operating range that is considered.
In 5.1, it will be shown that, according to measurements performed on the car, the
ratio −β/α has a strange behaviour when a step function is applied to Tq,base,req. Before
the step, the ratio is almost unitary. After the step, when a new stationary condition
is reached, the ratio is slightly lower than 1. This behaviour is observed in any step
response, independently of the initial value of the torque. If this mismatch in the ratio
is not somehow corrected, then α and β become also torque-dependent and the torque
should be used as a scheduling parameter for the LPV model as well. Ideally, the torque
should follow the request with a unitary gain, so it makes sense to impose β to β = −α.
This provides a considerable reduction in the LPV model complexity.

5.1
Introduction
In chapter 4, a candidate state space model was derived.
The next step is to verify
if the proposed model structure is adequate to the control problem and determine the
values of the parameters. If the model results to be too simple and unable to ﬁt the real
measurements, a more detailed model has to be used.
Usually, when no knowledge about the plant is available or if the process is described
by very complex equations, a black box model is adopted. This type of model attempts
to describe just the input-output relationship by means of mathematical functions (often
polynomials), whose parameters do not have any physical meaning but are calculated to
provide the best ﬁt to measurements. An iterative procedure is applied, that starts from
estimating a very simple candidate model, characterized by a low number of parameters.
At each iteration, the complexity of the model is increased and the output prediction
is compared with the measurements.
The procedure continues until adding further
parameters does not provide a signiﬁcant improvement in the measurements ﬁtting. This
type of procedure is commonly referred to as system identiﬁcation.
In the considered case, it was possible to develop a model based on physical rela-
tions. This type of model, characterized by a deﬁned structure and a set of unknown
parameters, is called a grey box model. The unknown parameters can be classiﬁed into
three categories:
• Parameters describing the torque base generation process, in eq. (4.15)
• Parameters describing the speed dynamics, in eq (4.21)
• Parameters describing the ignition efficiency, in eq. (4.19).
The estimation is based on real measurements performed on the vehicle. In this
chapter, the type of tests that were carried out are described and the measurements are
brieﬂy analyzed. This provides an insight about how the gear shifts are performed by the
current controller.

5.2
Tests and measurements analysis
A large amount of data is required for parameters estimation. It was not possible to
test the engine or other singular components directly. Tests were performed on a S60
mounted on a rig at Chalmers University of Technology. The ISG and the ERAD were
disconnected.
The rig allows to simulate driving conditions very closely. The advantage of this type
of assessment is that the engine is tested under realistic conditions, including the effects
of the road and all the other components in the vehicle. At the same time, though, the
engine is less accessible and controllable by the test operator. It is also more difficult to
relate each effect to the corresponding cause.
The principle of the test was to send a varying torque request to the engine and
measure the response. As the test was to be performed on the complete vehicle, it was
not possible to send a speciﬁc request to the engine directly. The only way to produce a
change in the torque request, was to act either on the accelerator pedal or on the load
applied to the wheels. The test rig can be conﬁgured to control the vehicle speed on
the ground to a constant reference. As a consequence, according to eq. (3.12), the engine
speed is kept constant. By setting vveh and changing the gear ratio, it is then possible to
test the engine at different speed levels.
Figure 5.1 reports a scheme of the setup adopted during the test. The inputs that can
be manually controlled are the accelerator pedal position, the desired gear and the desired
speed on ground. The rig implements a feedback loop that keeps the engine at constant
speed by adjusting the torque applied to the wheels. The torque is computed according
to the speed error, the engine torque, the current wheel torque and the gear ratio. The
control system of the car receives the pedal position and the gear ratio and produces a
torque request, as already explained in 2.6.2. If a step is applied to the pedal position,
a step is also produced in the torque request. Hence, this setup enables to perform step
responses in the engine torque at different levels of engine speed. Step responses are
commonly used because are usually very simple and their transient characteristics can
be directly linked to the model parameters.
If the engine speed is effectively kept constant during the steps, its effects appear only
in the estimated values of the parameters α(Ne) and β(Ne), which would be constant
during the step. Performing step responses at different speed levels is not only useful for
estimating the values of α and β but also for attempting to formulate their dependency
on the speed as a mathematical function. Conversely, if the rig is too slow in adjusting
the wheel torque and the speed changes, then α(Ne) and β(Ne) cannot be considered
as constants anymore and it is much harder to capture the effects of the speed. This
criterion will be used to assess which measurements are more reliable.

speed, the instantaneous torque has to be rapidly reduced. As always, Tq,inst is controlled
by both acting on the torque base request and on the ignition timing. The torque base
request drops, however Tq,base dynamics are too slow to adapt in such a short time
interval. Spark retard is used to reduce the ignition efficiency such that Tq,inst matches
the requested instantaneous torque, while Tq,base gradually decreases. This is clearly seen

At the end of the transition phase, the engine has reached the synchronization speed

The MPC has to operate during the transition phase to speed the engine up from
Ne,b to Ne,sync. This analysis conﬁrms that the speed is to be controlled by acting on the

It is also seen that there is indeed margin for improving fuel consumption. The fuel ﬂow
is proportional to Tq,base, which means that it is more desirable to reduce Tq,base instead
of using spark-retard. However, the baseline controller uses only past information and
is forced to use spark retard when the load decreases. The MPC, on the other hand, is
based on future predictions and should be able to reduce the torque request in advance,
avoiding the need to increase ∆θi.
Data collected during shifts are useful to re-create simulation conditions as close as
possible to reality and will be used to test the MPC behavior.
Upshifting phases
Figure 5.6 shows a typical upshift recorded during tests.
An upshift is characterized by a decrease in engine speed. The transmission ratio is
reduced from it,b to it,a. As eq. (5.1) still stands, then it means that Ne,a < Ne,b.
The way an upshift is performed is radically different from a downshift. In principle,
the engine could slow down by simply cutting the produced torque and exploiting the
load torque. However, it is also known that the requested torque is higher after the upshift.
According to eq. (3.10), the load torque is inversely proportional to the transmission ratio.
Therefore, assuming that the load on the wheels does not change during the shift, the
load torque at the engine results to be higher after the upshift has taken place.
From ﬁg. 5.6-Torques, it is seen that Tq,base is not reduced at all but on the contrary
is slowly raised up to match the future torque request. Then, spark retard is used to cut
the instantaneous torque, which would act against the speed reduction.
At the beginning of the transition, ∆θi is set very high and Tq,inst drops to 0,
causing the engine to slow down fast. Once the speed is approaching Ne,a, the ignition
timing gradually goes back to the optimal value so that the instantaneous torque grows.
Therefore, the engine deceleration decreases.
In this case, fuel economy could be improved by reducing Tq,base during the transition
phase, instead of using spark retard. However, considering that the engine behavior is
non-linear and that the torque demand is higher after the upshift, it may be unfeasible
to reduce the torque base. Another strategy could be to cut the fuel injection when the
instantaneous torque has to drop to 0 without acting on the torque base. The controller,
in this case, could be quite simple and probably there is no need for implementing an
MPC.

6.1
Introduction
In this chapter, the engine speed controller design is presented. First, a general overview
of model predictive control is provided. Then, the adopted cost function and the system
constraints are illustrated. Two approaches are considered for the controller design. In
section 6.5, a simple MPC is developed, showing how the optimization problem can be
expressed as a standard quadratic programming (QP) problem. This controller requires the
load to be estimated and does not naturally compensate for inaccuracies in the estimation,
which in turn cause an offset error in the output engine speed. The performance of this
controller therefore depends on the type of disturbance estimation that is performed. In
section 6.6, the problem of steady state offsets is addressed by designing an MPC based
on a velocity form model. To distinguish this controller from the previous one it will be
referred to as integral MPC. This approach is inspired by [13].
6.2
Theory background
6.2.1
Definition
Model Predictive Control is a general name used to indicate a class of control algorithms
that explicitly use a mathematical model of the system to compute the optimal control
action according to a cost function. These algorithms generally differ only in the model
and in the cost function adopted [6].
6.2.2
The cost function
The choice of the cost function and its parameters is a critical phase of the controller
design. The cost function is used to represent the control objective by modeling the cost
associated with a system behaviour that is not as desired. The key of MPC is that this
cost is not calculated upon past information about the system but, instead, according

Constraints
In reality, the system is always subjected to constraints.
The operational range and

Safety issues often require some

from requirements about quality or economy [6]. The optimal control action has to be
computed taking these conditions into account, otherwise the controller performance
results to be badly degraded and safety can be compromised.

limitations. The constraints can be of many types but, like the cost function, have to be

6.2.7
Advantages and disadvantages
Model predictive control offers many advantages and has become increasingly popular in
recent years. It is a very versatile control strategy that can be applied to many different
processes in a fairly intuitive way.
When the future reference is known in advance
and/or it is possible to predict future disturbances, an MPC is able to react faster and
with reduced cost compared to a PID, thanks to its predictive nature. Another great
advantage with respect to traditional controllers, even to optimal controllers such as
Linear Quadratic Regulators (LQR), is the possibility to handle constraints. Moreover, the
cost function is an intuitive and ﬂexible way to specify the control objective [6].
A major drawback of MPC is the computational power that is often required to solve
the optimization problem. The algorithm time complexity grows with the prediction and
the control horizons and, in the case of state space MPC, with the number of states.
MPC is not applicable when the sampling interval is very short because the next control
action may not be computed in time. For this reason, MPC was so far applied only
to slow processes [6]. Nowadays, computers have become more powerful, extending the
range of applications of this type of control strategy. The timing issue has also been
partially solved by using explicit MPC [2]. Explicit MPC is an equivalent formulation of
a traditional MPC, in which the variables space is partitioned into polyhedral regions
and for every region an explicit gain is computed. This procedure can be performed
offline and, when running, the MPC essentially behaves as a look-up table. In this way,
the computational cost can be dramatically reduced. It has to be taken into account,
however, that the number of regions increases rapidly with the number of states and with
the chosen horizons so this technique is not always applicable.
6.3

In this section, the cost function adopted for the engine speed controller is described.

1. The engine speed has to be controlled and has to reach the new reference value

6.7
Parameters scheduled MPC
The two MPC schemes described earlier are supposed to work on an LTI system. In
practice, the values of Ne and ∆θi change during the same shift, which means that
the model used by the MPC should change accordingly. For this reason, a parameters
scheduled MPC is proposed, based on the model deﬁned in 4.4.1. The only difference
from the previous controllers is that the LTI model used for predictions is updated on
the basis of the scheduling parameters. It is assumed that the conditions do no change
over the prediction horizon, which of course is a simpliﬁcation, so that the model is ﬁxed
over the entire horizon.
The ﬁnal MPC algorithm is then the following:
1. At instant k, measure ys and build the state vector ξk.
2. Choose the appropriate LTI model according to:

3. Express the optimization problem as a QP problem using eq. (6.30) and (6.35) and

4. Find zk by solving the QP problem
5. Apply uk to the plant
6. Update the sampling instant a start again from 1.

6.9
Reachability
Reachability is a property of the system that describes the possibility of a controlled input
to drive the system states as desired. The discrete-time linear system:
xk+1 = Fk xk + Gk uk
(6.39)
is said to be reachable in the ﬁnite time interval [k0, kf] if there exists an input sequence
uk, uk+1 . . . that drives the system from the origin to any ﬁnal state xkf [8]. Several
criteria are available to prove reachability of linear systems. In the case of LTI systems,
the following theorem can be demonstrated:
Theorem : The LTI system model:
xk+1 = F xk + G uk
(6.40)
is reachable if and only if the reachability matrix Mr has full rank. The reachability
matrix is deﬁned as:
Mr =
[
G
F G
. . .
Fn−1 G
]
(6.41)
where n is the size of the state vector [8].
Unfortunately, this theorem cannot be applied to the engine model but only to the
LTI models individually. Global reachability for LPV systems is in general difficult to
prove and is still an open problem. For this reason, a weak deﬁnition of reachability is
considered here.
The proposed LPV model is a set of LTI models in the form (6.40). The matrices
of the LTI models have the same structure and the elements change in a ﬁnite set of
non-zero values. The reachability matrix of the LTI models is calculated in Matlab and
it is veriﬁed that it has has rank equal to the size of the state vector. Therefore each
LTI model is reachable. This is not sufficient to prove that the LPV model is globally
reachable, however it can be concluded that reachability is satisﬁed locally for every point
in the scheduling grid.

7.1
Objective
The controller performance is determined by the accuracy of the internal model, by the
type of cost function and by the values assigned to its parameters, namely the prediction
and control horizons and the cost function weights.
In principle, a long prediction horizon is preferable to ensure closed-loop stability
and improve the reaction to predicted future changes. With a long control horizon, the
controller has more freedom in the choice of zk, which can be better adjusted. A direct
consequence, though, is that the optimization problem becomes more complex and the
execution time increases.
In general, it is very intuitive to translate the control objective into a cost function,
whereas choosing the appropriate weights can be very challenging and time consuming.
The number of degrees of freedom is extremely large and the relation between the chosen
values and the controller performance is not always evident. For these reasons, tuning is
the most critical phase in the MPC design.
To simplify the problem, it is assumed that the weights are constant over the predic-
tion horizon. Hence, the following values have to be found:

7.2
Tuning algorithm
The proposed tuning method is inspired by genetic algorithms. Genetic algorithms (GAs)
are a heuristic search and optimization technique inspired by natural evolution [5]. The
GA simulates an abstract process of evolution on a population of individuals.
Each
individual represents a solution to the considered problem and, as it happens in nature,
is fully characterized by a set of genes. The genes encode a description of the solution.
Each individual is associated with a ﬁtness score that represents the quality of the
solution. The algorithm is an iterative process that starts from an initial population of
individuals, whose performance is tested and evaluated using the ﬁtness score. Among all
the individuals, only those characterized by the highest score are selected and are used to
generate children that will form a new population, imitating the way organisms reproduce
in nature. Children’s genes can be obtained by the recombination of the parent’s genes
and can be subjected to mutation [5]. The new population is tested and the procedure
is repeated. As only the best individuals are selected, iteration after iteration the overall
score of the population is improved until a satisfying solution is found.
The idea is to apply the same mechanism to the search of the cost function weights. It
is fairly easy to represent this optimization problem into a genetic form. The individual’s
genes are simply represented by a vector containing the possible weights.
The proposed algorithm has the following structure:
Initialization: A population of np individuals is created:
Φ = {ι1, ι2, . . . ι3}
where the individuals have the form:
ιi = {wo, wMV , w∆MV }
The MPC weights are chosen randomly from a predeﬁned set: {0, 50, 100, . . . 1000}.
A second population, that will contain the nb < np best individuals, is also created:
==PAGE:97==
7.3 Results
79
Φbest. At the beginning, all the individuals in Φ are assumed to guarantee the same
performance so Φbest is by default initialized to null individuals whose scores are
equal to inﬁnity.
Iteration: This is the iterative part of the algorithm and consists of the following
steps:
Evaluation: The performance of each individual in Φ is evaluated. In order to
do so, the MPC is simulated using the set of gains deﬁned in the individual’s
genes and a performance score is calculated from the achieved reference tracking
and fuel consumption. A score is assigned to each individual.
Selection: The best individuals according to the score in both Φ and Φbest are
found and Φbest is updated.
Evolution: The current population Φ becomes old and is discarded.
A new
population Φ is generated based on Φbest. For each individual in Φbest, four
children are generated by increasing and decreasing the parent’s MPC weights.
The remaining individuals in the new population are generated by mixing the
characteristics of the individuals in Φbest. At this point, a new evaluation phase
begins.
Termination: The algorithm terminates after a ﬁxed number of iterations that, after
some try-outs, was set to 4.

A drawback of using this algorithm is that the output really depends on how the
ﬁtness function is deﬁned. For example, one could decide to penalize the fuel consump-
tion more than the output tracking or the opposite, which means that weights have to
be set in the ﬁtness function. Furthermore, the problem of ﬁnding the optimal set of
gains is not well deﬁned. First of all, it is necessary to decide what “optimal” means,
which is done by designing the ﬁtness function. Due to the large number of degrees of
freedom, the problem is affected by local minima. This means that a set of gains is found
that ensures a good performance of the controller, but this does not guarantee that the
found solution provides actually the absolute minimum of the ﬁtness function. However,
it is also true that different sets of gains provide very similar results and, as long as the
controller behaves as desired, the local minima problem should not be an issue.

8.4
Downshift simulations
Downshift simulations are performed in the same way as the ideal simulations, but using
real signals. Instead of assuming that the engine has a constant load, the real Tq,load is
used as input to the plant model. Again, a step and a ramp references are tested. The
initial and the ﬁnal values of the reference are set equal to the initial and ﬁnal values
of the recorded engine speed.
The states are initialized with the initial value of the
corresponding signals.
VCC has provided a fuel consumption map that allows to estimate the fuel rate
depending on the engine speed and torque The MPC and the baseline controllers are
compared based on the total fuel consumption at the end of the shift. The reference for
the MPC should be adjusted so that the two controllers produce a very similar output,
especially in terms of speed rate. The reason is that a fast reference would lead to a fast
response, which obviously requires more fuel than a slow response.
Figures 8.5 and 8.6 show the results achieved by simulating the ﬁrst downshift
recorded during Test 1 using the step and the ramp references, respectively. It is seen
that using the step reference, as in the ideal simulations, concentrates all the torque
request in a short time interval. Right before the step, the engine speed is raised by the
fact that the load is decreasing. The MPC does not oppose to the acceleration because
it can predict the future p changes in the reference. As displayed in engine speed plot,
the speed increases slowly at the beginning. Then, the step makes the engine accelerate
fast and the speed reaches the target value even before the baseline speed. The plot
Fuel consumption in a sampling interval shows the fuel injected in the cylinders during a
sampling interval. If this signal is integrated, one gets the total fuel consumption, shown
in the plot Total fuel consumption. It is seen that, despite the MPC produces a faster
response, the fuel consumption is lower compared to the baseline. If a ramp reference is
used, the torque demand is lower and distributed, as expected. In this case the output is
much more similar to the baseline output. Also in this case, the MPC appears to reduce
the fuel consumption.
In order to understand why MPC is able to save fuel, one can look at the Ignition
timing plot. MPC is able to foresee that the torque has to be decreased and, since the
spark timing is penalized more than the torque, it reacts by reducing the torque request
in advance, instead of reducing the ignition efficiency by changing the spark timing.
By looking at the cost function weights used with the step reference and the ramp
reference, one can notice that in the former case the control action is much more heavily
penalized. A reason for this is that the step tends to produce a spike in the torque
demand, which can be reduced only by penalizing it in the cost function. Furthermore,
if the output tracking was penalized more, than the engine speed would grow too fast,
leading to an unnecessary high fuel consumption. The drawback of this choice of gains
is that the controller does not react very well to fast changes in the torque because the
ignition timing is almost always left unchanged. The ramp reference, instead, is much
easier to follow and thus the weight on the output can be set higher. In this case, the
controller is also able to use spark retard when necessary.

Conclusions
9
Regarding the three milestones outlined in chapter 1, the following conclusions are taken:
1. Modeling: Using a set of LTI models seems to be lightweight and easy way to deal
with the engine non-linearities. The model is able to describe the engine behaviour
in different conditions. At the same time, the design of the MPC is fairly simple and
can be based on standard and consolidated procedures adopted for linear systems.
Recommendations and future work: Further tests should be performed to extend
the model to other operative regions. The ignition efficiency estimation should be
improved by performing dedicated tests. The scheduling points were chosen in an
empirical way.
A more structured approach should be used.
The effects of the
driveline on the engine should be better investigated. Furthermore, shifts performed
at different gears should be characterized, especially in terms of duration and load
torque, so that a reference trajectory can be designed properly.
2. MPC: Two types of MPC were successfully developed and implemented in Matlab.
It was seen that by simply reformulating the model into a velocity form it was possible
to eliminate offsets from the output. As the implementation of the two controllers
is equivalent in terms of cost and complexity, only the integral-MPC should be
considered as a valuable solution.
Recommendations and future work: The MPC has to be translated into a practical
implementation. One could consider implementing an explicit version, in case the
time constraints cannot be met by the implicit controller. The control horizon was
kept short also for this purpose.
3. Evaluation: Simulations proved that the proposed MPC schemes are able to con-
trol the engine speed and that the speed trajectory is comparable with the one
obtained with the baseline controller. Furthermore, simulations performed in down-
shifting scenarios show that the fuel consumption, when MPC is used, is reduced by
2-5% in comparison with the baseline controller. The main objective of this thesis,
which was to reduce the fuel consumption, is therefore met. The ramp reference
seems to be a better solution than the step reference.

Conclusions
Recommendations and future work: The evaluation of the MPC does not consider the
computational power required and if the algorithm is feasible from a timing point of
view. The results are obtained in simulation and therefore have limited validity. These
results are useful to show that MPC is a promising technique but a ﬁnal assessment
can be made only by testing the controller on the engine.
It is concluded that MPC is a valid strategy for engine speed control. At the same
time, though, one should also keep into account that MPC is more difficult to implement
and requires a computational effort that may not be justiﬁed by just a small improvement
in fuel economy. A ﬁnal decision can be taken only when experimental data are available.
Nevertheless, this thesis proposed a methodology that can be applied also to other control
problems. The MPC formulation and design was performed in a very general way and
may still be used if the model will be extended or changed after other tests, with the
only conditions that a state space structure is adopted.
