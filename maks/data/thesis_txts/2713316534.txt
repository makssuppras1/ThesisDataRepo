Wind Farm optimization to maximize AEP and Energy Density under load constraints
Abstract
Since the beginning of this decade, there has been an exponential growth in the number
of wind energy projects globally; by the year 2050, it is expected that wind energy will
produce 25 to 30 percent of the world’s electricity. Wind farms are expected to expand in
size and quantity under these circumstances. Energy density optimizations may be required
as wind farm sizes grow in the existing wind society, where most wind farm optimizations
are geared toward either optimizing Energy production or Economic considerations. This
thesis aims to ﬁnd techniques to increase both the energy production and energy density of
wind farms. Two examples will be analyzed for this project, one with a tiny hypothetical
wind farm architecture and the other with an actual wind farm subject to fatigue load
restrictions. All layout optimizations are carried out with the help of the TOPFARM and
pywake packages from DTU. The optimizations’ results showed that wind farm layout with
high energy densities and equally good energy productions are possible.

Introduction
1.1
Premise
Since its inception, wind energy has been fastest growing renewable technologies with total
worldwide installed capacity been around 837GW. In 2021, the global wind industry installed
around 93.6 GW of new capacity[1]. Wind power is a cornerstone of achieving net-zero and
powering a green recovery as a cost-competitive, resilient power source with the most decar-
bonization potential per MW.[2] In addition, wind energy is the most eﬃcient technology to
produce energy in a safe and environmentally sustainable manner.
In a report to analysis and forecast of renewable energies by International Energy
Agency(IEA), 2021 shows a steep increase of onshore and oﬀshore wind energy share in
the global renewable market.49 This increase has been achieved from an exponential growth
in wind energy projects globally over the past few years. It is projected that electrical energy
generated from wind will reach a world share of 25-30% by 2050. The ﬁgure to the right 49
shows a forecast of annual energy addition of 175GW to achieve net-zero by 2050. To ensure
further growth, wind farms are expected to increase both in number and spatial scale.
Figure 1: Wind Energy analytic over the years (to the left), Wind energy forecast(to the
right)
Due to the evolution of wind farms to more extensive size and advanced capabilities, the
development of wind farms becomes complex with multiple objectives under diﬀerent con-
straints. With Wind farm Layout optimization(WFLO) garnering attention from in-
dustries and researchers to improve the overall eﬃciency of a wind farm. WFLO refers to
position of turbines/ optimal placement of turbines to maximize or minimize an objective
function while satisfying various constraints. One of the critical challenges confronting the
wind energy industry is enhancing the performance of large-scale wind farms. Many vari-
ables may be used to analyze the performance of the wind farm from various angles, such
as Annual Energy Production (AEP), Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE), Net Present Value
(NPV), etc. Energy density is one of them that is used to describe the performance of wind
farms. Energy density for a wind farm is determined by dividing the annual energy output by
the entire area of the wind farm, which shows how much energy is accessible at the location
for conversion by wind turbines.

Wind Farm optimization to maximize AEP and Energy Density under load constraints
Maximizing the energy density of wind farm is done through various methods choosing an
advanced control strategy, designing a wind farm with multiple hub heights, using load
constraints in wind farm optimization, etc. This thesis investigates methods to beneﬁt the
annual energy production and energy density of a wind farm when turbines are under load
constraints and is achieved through a sequential approach.
High-density wind farms are characterized by their proximity to one another, making turbine
spacing a key factor in energy density optimizations. Due to the wake eﬀect, turbine spacing,
a metric dependent on the direction of the prevailing wind, is crucial and is one of the design
variables in this project. In general, wind farm turbines in the free ﬂow condition do not
obstruct each other’s wakes. However, partial and full wake turbines encounter slower wind
and more intense turbulence, which signiﬁcantly reduces energy output and increases fatigue
loads[3]. So there is a necessity to constrain these loads to ensure lifetime of wind turbine.
1.2
Research questions
The following research questions are addressed in this master’s thesis: When a wind farm
subjected to load constraints and optimized to maximize energy density? What percent of
AEP is sacriﬁced in order to boost energy density? Does the quantity of loss help to attain
the desired energy density?
• How do the positions of wind turbines change with and without load constraints?
• How are the optimization results aﬀected by diﬀerent turbulence intensity values?
A hypothetical wind farm with 16 turbines produced one of the two case studies. Another
real-world example of the Princesses Amalia wind farm is optimized to maximize AEP,
decrease Area, and maximize AEP and Energy density under load restrictions. It is ac-
complished by using a sequential optimizations strategy to narrow the design space in the
initial optimizations. The best design parameter results are then used for the next round of
optimizations.

2
Methodology
A wind energy system includes the plant’s energy output, the turbine, the cost of the
ancillary system, the plant’s eﬀect on the electric grid, the environmental factors, and several
components from various disciplines. The eﬀectiveness of such intricate systems must be
increased. The analysis and investigation of the ideal design of wind farms and hybrid energy
systems are facilitated by system engineering approaches. The area of systems engineering
focuses on creating, integrating, and managing complex systems, and it is interdisciplinary
and belongs to engineering management[4]. Wind farm layout optimization in this project
involves multiple objectives and complex constraints to be met. This section briefs on the
evolution of optimization techniques in system engineering and how they are applied to the
current model.
2.1
Literature review
Many mathematical approaches exist to solve the WFLO problem. Amongst them is a
genetic algorithm from mosetti’s, Greedy and GAO’s - multi-population Genetic Algorithm
(GA) to improve the diversity of solutions and a hill-climbing approach to reduce computa-
tion costs. These implementations of Genetic Algorithm(GA) have shown reasonable success
in dealing with the WFLO problem. However, Genetic Algorithm(GA’s) have some limi-
tations regarding computational cost, mainly because of their slow convergence behavior,
which typically requires a large number of function evaluations [5].
Local search approaches have also received attention from the WFLO community. For
instance, Wagner developed a randomized problem-speciﬁc local search method to speed
up the farm layoutâs evaluation and deal with large farms eﬃciently. Another approach
proposed by Feng and Shen, who introduced a random search with a wake inﬂuence matrix
for layout evaluation.
The ﬁnite-diﬀerence approximation was used to approximate the

Constraints
In an optimization problem, constraints are limitations placed on the design variables by

• Lower and upper bounds of the design variables for all the optimizations.

5
Conclusion
Energy density optimization was found to increase energy density while also improving
energy production, according to the carried out optimizations. AEP lost from optimizations
with and without load constraints for multiple objectives in both case studies is statistically
insigniﬁcant. If there aren’t many locations for future wind farms, creating wind farms with
higher energy densities may be advantageous as wind farm sizes expand globally.
According to the sensitivity analysis of fatigue loads, tower base and ﬂap-wise loads
increase as turbulence intensity and wind speed do, and vice versa, as the distance between
turbines decreases. When there is greater turbulence intensity surrounding the turbines, a
highly dense wind farm may not be a practical layout. This demonstrates how crucial it
is to take fatigue loads into account when optimizing layouts with energy density as the
goal. Additionally, if the turbines are already closer to one another in the initial condition of
the optimization, they do not change much from original positions and remain in the wake
region.
To sum up, layout optimizations for the test case wind farm and Princess Amalia wind
farm have helped in enhancing densities compared to the original layouts.
6
Future Work
• The variation in wind speed at higher altitudes is not taken into account in any of the
optimizations that have been done. In order to validate AEP produced and comprehend
load behavior at various shear exponent values, a sensitivity study on shear exponent
may be helpful.
• To more accurately account for fatigue loads, additional turbulence models that can
describe partial wakes might be taken into consideration.
• The optimized layout’s viability under the turbine’s loads might be veriﬁed by taking
into account economic constraints like the costs of fatigue damage.
• Similar study with economic parameters like Net present Value(NPV), LCOE would
be helpful to understand economic limitations for the quantity of energy lost.
