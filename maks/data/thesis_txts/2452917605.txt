Abstract 
Food waste have since 2013 been one of the main topics on the political agenda and several 
studies have been conducted on solid food waste generation. In Denmark however, there is little 
to no knowledge on liquid food waste generation and composition as most focus has been on solid 
food waste. Liquid food waste is defined as food and drinks waste disposed via the kitchen sink or 
other liquids disposal areas in the household. 
This paper developed a method to quantify liquid food waste in Denmark and investigate its 
potential impact on the urban sewerage systems. Two methodologies were used in the study, 
which included survey and diary method and several laboratory analyses. In the survey and diary 
method, participating households registered category, type, amount and reason for disposal for 
the food and drinks waste. For the laboratory, samples were collected in households directly from 
underneath the kitchen sink. BOD, COD and the nutrients nitrogen and phosphorus were 
measured for chemical characterization and TSS and VSS for physical characterization. 
The results of the survey showed an annual liquid food waste generation of 11 kg per household 
and 30,693 Mg in total for Denmark, where 56% was considered as avoidable food waste. The 
most significant food category were drinks (43%) and other (35%), while the most significant food 
and drinks types were coffee (17%), milk (13%) and brine from cans and cooking (10-13%). The 
most registered reasons for disposal were during cleaning of dishes (48%) and leftover (30%).  
The results of the laboratory analyses showed higher values than typical values for inlet to 
WWTPs, which were expected as the liquid food waste is not mixed with other sources of 
wastewater e.g. rainwater and showers. Studies also suggested, that an increased generation of 
liquid food waste will not negatively impact the urban sewerage system and potentially increase 
the biogas production from sludge. 
Given the uncertainties encountered during analyses and data treatment, the methodologies 
provided the necessary results with reliability. Though larger sample sizes and data collection 
might be needed to ensure reliability and to provide sufficient data for future use. Moreover, a 
future study should also be repeated to cover seasonal and geographical variation.

Sammenfatning 
Madaffald har siden 2013 været en af de større emner på den politiske dagsorden og der er også 
udarbejdet flere studier på produktion af solidt madaffald. I Danmark er der derimod lidt til ingen 
viden omkring produktion og sammensætning af flydende madaffald, da det meste fokus har 
været på solidt madaffald. Flydende madaffald er defineret som mad- og drikkeaffald, der bliver 
disponeret gennem køkkenvasken eller andre områder, hvor væsker disponeres i husholdningen. 
Dette studie udviklede en metode til at kvantificere flydende madaffald i Danmark og undersøge 
dets potentielle effekt på det urbane kloaksystem og rensningsanlægget. To metoder blev brugt i 
studiet, som inkluderede en survey- og dagbogsmetode og flere forskellige laboratorieanalyser. I 
survey- og dagbogsmetoden deltog husholdninger og registrerede deres flydende madaffald ift. 
kategori, specifik type, mængde og årsag til disponering. For laboratorieanalyserne blev fem 
prøver indsamlet i husholdninger direkte fra køkkenvasken. BOD, COD, og næringsstofferne 
nitrogen og fosfor blev målt for kemisk karakterisering og TSS og VSS blev målt for fysisk 
karakterisering. 
Resultaterne af survey’en viste en årlig produktion af 11 kg flydende madaffald for en husholdning 
og 30,693 Mg totalt for Danmark, hvor 56% var undgåeligt madaffald. De mest betydelige 
kategorier var drikkevarer (43%) og andet (30%), mens de mest betydelige typer af mad- og 
drikkeaffald var kaffe (17%), mælk (13%) og lage fra dåser og madlavning (10-13%). De mest 
betydelige årsager for disponering var under opvask (48%) og rester (35%). 
Resultater af laboratorieanalyserne viste højere værdier end typiske værdier for rensningsanlæg, 
hvilket var forventet, da det flydende madaffald ikke er blandet med andre kilder af spildevand 
såsom regnvand og bade. Andre studier foreslog også, at en øget produktion af flydende 
madaffald ikke vil påvirke det urbane kloaksystem eller rensningsanlægget negativt og potentielt 
øge produktionen af biogas fra slam. 
Ud fra usikkerhederne under analyserne og databehandlingen gav metoderne de nødvendige og 
pålidelige resultater. Selvom større skala for prøver og dataindsamling er muligvis nødvendig for at 
sikre pålidelighed og give tilstrækkelige data for fremtidig brug. Derudover skulle et fremtidigt 
studie også gentages for at dække variationer i sæson og geografi.

Introduction 
Food waste is a globally growing problem, where it has become one of the main topics on the 
political agenda. In the last six years, both domestic and international guidelines, policies and goals 
have been created for the purpose of decreasing the consumption of and increasing the recycling 
of resources. In Denmark, the goal is to recycle 50% of all organic waste by 2022 (Miljøstyrelsen, 
2013). In the European Union (EU), the 7th Environment Action Programme (EAP) were presented 
in 2013 with the objective “Doing more with less” and focus on reductions in environmental 
impacts on consumption, where cutting food waste and utilizing biomass are mentioned as work 
areas (European Commision, 2016). Lastly in 2015, the United Nations (UN) presented its 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), where SDG 12 focuses on responsible consumption and 
production (United Nations, 2019). 
In 2015, Denmark generated approx. 183 kg of solid food waste per household, where 103 kg was 
avoidable. The biggest contributors to avoidable food waste were fresh vegetables and salads and 
bakery with respectively 14% and 13% of total food waste (Edjabou, et al., 2016). In Denmark, 
there is limited knowledge in the composition and generation of liquid food waste and its 
potential impact on the urban sewerage system. Liquid food waste is defined as food and drinks, 
that is disposed through the kitchen sink, toilets or other alternatives to enter the sewerage 
system. Internationally, several studies have been carried out, where especially countries with use 
of food waste grinders (FWD) are more aware on potential impacts on the wastewater system. An 
FWD is a grinder placed in the kitchen drain and are commonly found in USA, the UK and Canada 
(Køkkenkværnen, 2019). The purpose is to grind the solid food waste to small pieces for it to be 
carried away with the wastewater and handled at the wastewater treatment plant (WWTP). 
In the UK in 2008, the Waste and Resources Action Programme (WRAP) conducted a study on 
quantification and composition of food and drinks waste disposed through the kitchen sink in 
households. The study was based on a diary method, where participants noted their estimates on 
food and drinks waste generation and composition for seven days. The study showed, that an 
excessive amount of 1.8 mil. tons were disposed through the sink annually and approx. 83% was 
avoidable and due to overpreparation and -cooking (WRAP, 2009a). 
The international knowledge on liquid food waste is not potentially representative for Danish 
research as both integrated solid waste management (ISWM) and WWTP might differ in methods 
and technologies. Therefore, a detailed and comprehensive study is necessary in Denmark to 
understand the issues with liquid food waste and provide potentially new initiatives for changes in 
behavior in households in Denmark. 
Objective of study 
The main objective is to quantify and investigate the liquid food waste generated in Denmark and 
its potential impact on the urban wastewater system by developing, testing and validating a 
characterization method for the generation and composition of liquid food waste. For the 
investigation of the potential impact on WWTP, known tests and measurements will be carried out 
for greywater samples collected before mixing with brown water.

Theoretical background 
In this section, the background for the study will be described in detail for both food waste and 
wastewater treatment and potential impacts on this.  
Food waste 
Food waste is defined as any food substance, raw or cooked, which is discarded. Food waste can 
be described overall in two categories; 1) edible or not and 2) origin (meat or vegetable). Avoided 
food waste is often food waste, which have been discarded due to overcooking, leftover or has 
expired before use or otherwise gone bad, where unavoided food waste could be inedible food 
such as bones, peels and coffee grounds. Under avoidability, food waste can also be described as 
processed or un-processed. Therefore, it is possible to group food waste in six categories (Edjabou, 
et al., 2016): 
• Avoidable processed meat (APAFW) 
• Avoidable unprocessed meat 
(AUAFW) 
• Unavoidable meat (UAFW) 
• Avoidable processed vegetables 
(APVFW) 
• Avoidable unprocessed vegetables 
(AUVFW) 
• Unavoidable vegetables (UVFW) 
To investigate food even further, a classification of food and drinks have been conducted, where 
11 groups are considered. These groups are as following: bakery; canned food; condiments, 
sauces, herbs and spices; dairy and eggs; drinks, confectionary and desserts; fresh fruit; fresh 
vegetables and salads; meat and fish; other food; pet food; stable food. In Appendix 1, examples 
of what it could include are shown (Edjabou, et al., 2016). 
Analysis of avoidable food waste often includes reason of its disposal. Most common reasons are 
food not being used in time or there have been cooked, prepared or served too much. These 
reasons can happen in any food state, whether the food is in raw, pre-prepared or -cooked or 
home-cooked. In the study, most commonly reason will be cooked, prepared or served too much, 
but in overall there is little knowledge on liquid food waste (WRAP, 2009a). 
In Denmark, there are several studies on food waste disposed of in waste bins both as source 
segregated or as residual waste. Below in Table 1, source segregated organic waste from a study in 
2017 for different municipalities and household types are shown. Source segregated organic waste 
was analyzed for composition and compared with the composition of food waste. The study shows 
the potential in organic waste and especially food waste and the public’s efficiency as of 2017. This 
efficiency might lead to a small amount disposed through the kitchen sink (Miljøstyrelsen, 2018b).

In 2015, an investigation of food waste generation and composition in Denmark was conducted for 
nine household areas. In total, 12.2 Mg wet weight residual waste in 1,474 households was sorted 
and analysed. The study showed, that of the residual waste, 8.5±0.3% was animal derived and 
33.4±0.7% was vegetable food waste. The avoidable food waste was 56.2±1.8% of the total food 
waste, where the majority were unprocessed food. The composition of the food waste in the most 
significant categorized fractions were found to be 30% fresh vegetables and salads, 17% fresh 
fruit, 13% bakery and 13% drink, confectionery and desserts (Edjabou, et al., 2016). 
Liquid food waste 
Liquid food waste is a term used for food waste, that is disposed through the sewerage system 
with the wastewater. In USA and some other countries, this type of waste is often associated with 
kitchen grinders and flushed down during cleaning processes. Liquid food waste can consist of 
both solid and liquid food, where solids often are small parts, which are either grinded or fits 
through the drain in the sink. The challenge of quantifying liquid food waste is, that there is little 
to no knowledge on quantities generated from households. In the literature review for this study, 
the most useful study was WRAP (2009a). Sala et al. (2019) highlights, that there is a challenge in 
having a robust knowledge in liquid food waste due to a lack of reports and data. However, it is 
also stated, that opportunities and further advancements in the field should focus on reliability of 
existing reports and guidelines, while increasing the availability of data and consider differences 
between different waste management plans and nations’ approach (Sala, et al., 2019). 
In 2009, the organization WRAP conducted a study in the UK on food and drinks waste disposed 
through the sewerage system as part of a national food waste investigation. In the study, 319 
households participated, where the collected data was used to estimate the total disposal in the 
UK and for different food groups. The study showed, that the UK disposed in total 1.8 mil. Mg of 
food and drinks waste through the sewerage system, when not including water used in cooking, 
cleaning or flushing. With addition of water, the amount rose to 3.5 mil. Mg, where also the drinks 
waste went from a fraction of 44% to 66%, which is probably caused by the addition of water in 
coffee or tea. When analyzed avoidability, the study showed, that 78% of food waste and 92% of 
drinks waste were avoidable. Finally, the study showed, that nearly 80% of the waste was disposed 
due to excessively cooking, preparing or serving and most of the remaining were due to food and 
drinks not being used in time (WRAP, 2009a). For solid food waste generation, a total of 5.8 mil. 
tons were disposed annually and collected by local authorities, which were only 3 times higher 
than liquid food waste. Therefore, the investigation of liquid food waste was necessary in the 
quantification of total food waste generation (WRAP, 2009b).  
Disposal and utilization of food waste 
Food waste can be disposed and utilized in different ways both in households or at publicly owned 
facilities, where in Denmark, incineration or composting is most common. In Denmark, most of the 
food waste is incinerated, but after the new resource strategy from Danish EPA, the municipalities 
have started source segregation and collection of biowaste, where the goal were to sort 25,000 
Mg of biowaste by 2018 to use in biogas production (City of Copenhagen, 2014). In Denmark, 51% 
of total household waste collected at residences, central cubes and recycling stations was 
incinerated and 48% recycled or utilized otherwise in 2016 (Miljøstyrelsen, 2018a).

Food and other organic waste collected at households and brought to the incineration plant 
represented 45% of the total waste entering the incineration plant. For food waste, the moisture 
content is often high at 66% and solids content is low at 34%, which yields to a lower heating value 
(LHV) at 1,912 kJ per kg ww. LHV is the energy content subtracted with the energy used for the 
evaporation process of moisture (Hulgaard & Vehlow, 2011).  
Anaerobic digestion is mostly used for manure and sewage sludge, but in the more recent years, 
organic household has been considered a suitable feedstock as of City of Copenhagen (2014). In a 
typical design of a reactor for municipal solid waste (MSW), the dry matter is usually 35% with a 
volatile solids (VS) content of 80%. Depending on the process used, the retention time differs 
between 20 days at mesophilic conditions (~35°C) and 10 days at thermophilic (~55°C). In a 
German digestion plant in Münster, Westphalia, only source segregated household is used. The 
capacity was 10-15,000 Mg per year with a biogas production of 90 Nm3 per Mg wet waste with a 
methane content of 45-65% (Jansen, 2011). In Denmark, biogas production is expanding as the 
government have a goal to increase energy from renewable energy resources. 
In Denmark, both central and home composting is used for organic household waste (OHW), 
where 5% of total OHW is sent to central composting and an estimated 2% is home composted. 
Usually, composting is a low-energy process with limited environmental impacts, but the output is 
mostly used for nutrient recycling and increase of the natural soil quality. Composting of food 
waste occurs less and less due to the lack of energy recovery unlike anaerobic digestion. A typical 
production of compost in Denmark is 0.4 Mg per Mg input material, but in Denmark, composting is 
mostly used for garden and park waste and sludges. As example, a Danish facility in Klintholm 
receives 11,000 Mg of garden waste per year compared to 3,300 Mg of kitchen waste (Scheutz, 
2018).  
Wastewater treatment 
Wastewater treatment consists usually of three overall processes – mechanical, biological and 
chemical treatment. The most typical mechanical processes are filtration via filters and screens, 
sedimentation via grit chambers and clarifiers and flotation for removal of oils and fats, which is 
combined with grit chambers as well. Smaller solids as food residues and feces are removed in a 
primary sedimentation tank, where the solids sink to the bottom and water overflows further into 
the secondary treatment. Typically, primary clarification is combined with chemical treatment for 
enhanced solid removal via coagulation-flocculation. In the biological process, activated sludge 
reactors are commonly used for combined carbon and nutrient removal (Nazaroff & Alvarez-
Cohen, 2001).  
After the biological reactor, a secondary settler is typically used for recovery and thickening of 
bacterial biomass. Alternatively, biofilm systems such as trickling filters or moving bed biofilm 
reactors can be used without secondary clarifiers. Finally, chemical processes may be used for 
enhanced phosphorus removal. Disinfection and de-chlorination may occur if requested by 
legislation, before the water is discharged (Nazaroff & Alvarez-Cohen, 2001).

When treating wastewater, sludge is generated leading to a generation of organic waste consisting 
of feces, bacterial biomass and other bio-wastes (e.g., food waste). Wasted sludge is treated with 
mesophilic anaerobic digestion. The treated sludge also has different disposal methods, which 
utilizes the energy and nutrient content of the waste. In 2016, 132,000 Mg of sludge from WWTP 
were generated in Denmark and 64% were used as fertilizer on agriculture, 9% were composted or 
utilized otherwise and 26% were incinerated leading to material substitution of mineral fertilizers 
and energy recovery (Miljøstyrelsen, 2018a).

Sustainability in WWTP 
Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) are often used to determine environmental impact of products or 
systems and can also be used for WWTPs for current and new technologies. In a study from 2016 
by Fang et al. (2016), a recovery system was analyzed using a modelling program called EASETECH. 
For the modelling, measurements of recovered nutrients and water were used as parameters to 
simulate a full-scale system and calculate environmental impacts. For the estimation of potential 
impacts of liquid food waste, measurements are conducted and can potentially be used to 
simulate environmental impacts from the WWTP and sewerage system in modellings software 
such as EASETECH. This study can therefore be the basis for a future LCA of liquid food waste 
impact in WWTPs, which is also the reason for measurement of BOD, COD and nutrients.

Methodology 
In this section, the tools and methods are explained in detail. In this study, a survey, on-site 
sampling and measurements were used to collect data. 
Quantification of liquid food waste with survey 
For the survey, three parts were conducted to receive useful and reliable data. The survey was 
inspired by the methodology from WRAP (2009a) and was created in Google Forms. First, a pre-
questionnaire was created as a registration of participants and to gather information about the 
specific household, their mindset on environmental awareness and an estimation of the food 
waste generation (See Appendix 2A). The pre-questionnaire was also used as part of the discussion 
of the results. After this step, an application was created using Google Forms and an add-on 
program called “AppSheet”, which converted the form to an application to use on smartphones, 
tablets and web browsers. In the application, the participant could register and quantify the food 
or drinks waste, that was disposed through the sink and the reason for disposal. In Appendix 2B, 
the form is shown. Reason for disposal were included to evaluate on whether or not the food 
waste could be avoidable. The survey lasted for seven days with start Wednesday the 5th of June 
and end Tuesday 11th of June. This was done to ensure the weekend was included before 
participants potentially got tired or otherwise dropped the registration, which could lead to a 
source of error in the data collection. 
Finally, the survey contained a post-questionnaire. This form functioned as an evaluation of the 
survey week and how the household lived the specific week compared to a typical week. Also, the 
questionnaire functions as an evaluation of the methodology. The questionnaire is shown in 
Appendix 2C. 
Data treatment of quantification of liquid food waste 
Data collected for the survey were treated through Excel in Appendix 4. The data will be used in 
both results section and as basis for discussion topics.  As registrations come in volumes (L), the 
conversion of the collected data to mass (kg) was done with a conversion list from WRAP (2009a) 
and two websites, Click-Chef (2015) and Carter (1968), when WRAP (2009a) was insufficient. For 
estimations of food and drinks categories and specific food types, various count- and sum-
functions were used in excel along with interpretation of registrations and combining these to a 
uniform food type. For the pre- and post-questionniare, primarly count-functions were used to 
present the results. For the data treatment of survey results, registrations were interpreted and 
converted into mass (kg) to quantify liquid food waste generation per person, household and total 
generation in Denmark. For quantification, Danmarks Statistik (2019a) was used for household and 
total generation. Other aspects were also treated such as registrations per day and time slots, 
distribution for reason of disposal, avoidability and housing distribution, where again count- and 
sum-functions were used along with numbers from Danmarks Statistik (2019b).

Total suspended solids (TSS) and volatile suspended solids (VSS) is a quantification based on 
gravimetric analysis, where filters are weighed before and after filtration of known volumes of 
wastewater and afterwards heated to 105°C and 550°C. TSS is done to evaporate residual water 
and VSS is done to burn organic matter and find volatile solids. For filtration, glass fiber filters are 
used with a pore size of 0.7 µm produced by GE Healthcare. Using equations 1 and 2, TSS and VSS 
can be calculated given V is volume of sample filtered, A is initial weight, B is weight after 105°C

BOD procedure 
BOD measurement is obtained over time, where data are collected through an automatic system 
called OxiTop® Control from the company WTW. The system consists of a series of measuring 
heads called OxiTop®-C and a controller called OxiTop®-OC100, which register oxygen depletion 
from the samples, from which an oxygen degradation curve can be generated. A pre-test was 
conducted to get a sense of the BOD value for later designing the experiment. For the pre-test, the 
experiment ran at 20°C for 13 days, where all five ranges from the protocol were controlled. The 
pre-test was performed before filtration, where for the samples, it was done both with and 
without filtration. For the measurements of the samples, the experiment ran for 10 days, which 
were chosen as the test measurements showed a flattening at this time slot. For calculation of 
values for BOD and BCOD values, Maple was used to calculate values a and b (See Appendix 5.4). 
COD and nutrient analysis procedure 
COD analysis is based on the oxidation of organic matter, where the test kit LCI 400 from Hach 
Lange GMBH in Germany was used for measurement of total COD. Nutrient analysis in this study 
consist of measurement of total nitrogen (TN) and total phosphorus (TP), where the use of 
chemical reagents in test kits and spectrophotometry measures the quantification of the nutrients. 
For TN, test kit LCK 338 was used and for TP, test kit LCK 350 was used. These test kits are 
produced by Hach Lange GMBH in Germany. For the COD and nutrient analyses, the TSS, VSS and 
BOD measurements were first conducted to estimate measurements and from these conduct a 
suitable dilution.

nitrogen and phosphorus can be calculated, where there are several relations between the 
chemical parameters. Especially for COD, several fractions can be calculated from the BOD values 
and from COD, fractions of total nitrogen (TN) and total phosphorus (TP) can be calculated.

Biological oxygen demand (BOD) 
For the BOD measurement, a test run was done to estimate the BOD in order to use an 
appropriate dilution to ensure the measured range were correct. The sample were collected from 
a kitchen sink at the institute, where approx. 15 people used the sink in time slot of 1.5 hour. The 
BOD was measured to 1,245 mg/L with a volumetric flask of 97 mL and diluted with 2 mL active 
bacteria mass and 8 mL tap water. Thus, it was necessary to use a measurement range of 0-800 
mg/L with a slightly higher dilution. The new dilution ratio was then chosen to be 2/3 sample and 
1/3 biomass and tap water, where 10 mL of active biomass and 22 mL of tap water was added and 
finally 65 mL of sample. For the blank with and without soap, 10 mL of biomass was added, and 1 
mL of dish soap was added for the soap blank.  
From the BOD measurements, the total BOD and the BCOD values were calculated (See Table 8). It 
is clear to see a significant error in sample 3A, while sample 5A and 5B are also remarkably low 
compared to the others. For sample 3A, the filtration could have been too efficient as it then has 
removed all organic content from the sample. Another error could be, that the measuring head 
were not tightened enough. For sample 5, this could be caused by a high water content in the 
collected sample compared to organic content. When interpreting the results, there is a clear 
trend, that both total BOD and BCOD are higher for the unfiltered samples, which is expected as 
there are more organic content to contribute to the oxygen demand. Sample 2 has the highest 
values, which also were expected as sample 2 were collected at the largest family. The visual 
impression of the sample was also, that there was a significant content of solids from coffee 
grounds and herbs, which is supported by TSS and VSS values. All values are again significantly 
higher than the typical values described in both Nazaroff & Alverez-Cohen (2011) and Valverde-
Pérez (2019b) except for sample 5.

Discussion 
In this section, results are discussed and evaluated along with potential uncertainties and subjects 
for validation of the objective. 
Liquid food waste generation in Denmark 
The liquid food waste generation in Denmark were calculated to around 30,000 Mg per year with 
an average household generation of 11 kg per year. For the investigation in 2015 of non-source 
segregated solid food waste in residual waste, the total waste generation were estimated to 183 
kg per household, which is 16 times larger than the generation of liquid food waste (Edjabou, et 
al., 2016). Therefore, when comparing the two disposal methods, the liquid food waste is of 
insignificant amounts compared to the potential in solid food waste, which is disposed incorrectly 
and sent to incineration, where there still is some energy recovery. 
The solids, fats and other sediments in liquid food waste is more likely to be collected with the 
sludge and used for biogas production or as fertilizers, which is a more circular method to use the 
organic waste. Thus, both waste generations were 56% avoidable food waste, the liquid food 
waste is more likely to be utilized efficiently, where solid food waste often is wet and can lead to a 
drop in the LHV in the incineration plant and therefore a lower energy recovery. With the 
insignificant amount disposed through the kitchen sink annually, a critical need for further focus 
on downsizing this generation would not be necessary as there is a larger potential lost in solid 
food waste and source segregation of this.  
When comparing this study to WRAP (2009a) in liquid food waste generation and composition, 
there is some similarities and some differences, which is not unexpected. However, the UK 
generated 1.8 mil. Mg liquid food waste in 2009 and with a population of 62 mil. people, the waste 
generation per person is 29 kg per year (EUROSTAT, 2019). In this study, it was estimated, that 
Denmark generates 5.3 kg per person per year, and the significant difference in waste generation 
may be caused by the use of FWDs in the UK (Køkkenkværnen, 2019).

For the calculations of the total generation of liquid food waste per year, the total registered 
weight was considered as the total amount, the 202 participants could generate. This assumption 
lead to a total of 30,693 Mg per year in Denmark. Another direction could be to consider one 
registration as one meal. In Appendix 4.4, different calculations have been done to investigate this 
assumption. The total generation rose drastically to 164,756 Mg per year if we assumed one meal 
a day and 494,269 Mg per year in case of 3 meals a day. This was done in order to show the range 
as most people do not eat lunch at home during workdays, but students close to home are more 
likely.  
In Denmark, households consist of 44% single-family and 56% multi-family such as apartments, 
terraced houses and student dorms (Danmarks Statistik, 2019b). For this study, the sample 
consisted of 37% single-family and 63% multifamily. Of the 30,693 Mg calculated as the total liquid 
food waste generation in Denmark, 19,366 Mg are from multifamily and just above 11,327 Mg are 
from single-family. When using Danmarks Statistik (2019b), the numbers are quite alike, but there 
is a small increase in single-family, as there is an increase in the number of houses. Here, single-
family houses generate 13,539 Mg of liquid food waste and multifamily generates 17,154 Mg. This 
shows a good correlation between the survey’s housing distribution and the national distribution.  
Uncertainties in quantification of liquid food waste 
When the registered liquid food waste was grouped and identified to convert volume into mass, 
some assumptions were made based on the conversion table in WRAP (2009a). These assumptions 
might lead to differences in results of method, as the author’s chosen conversion might not be the 
same as others. This is also the case with the grouping in both avoidability and origin of the food 
waste (food or drink), where a table from Edjabou et al. (2016) was used. However, as the 
objective for this study is also to validate a method for quantification of liquid food waste, these 
uncertainties might contribute significantly to the results. In WRAP (2009a), it was stated, that 
internet sources were used for conversion factors and, where the source seemed unreliable, a 
testing was performed to determine the density of the food. This statement might help support 
the methodology and reduce this uncertainty 
During the survey, there was a tendency in fewer registrations along the period and few 
registrations in total. In the post-questionnaire, participants had the chance to evaluate and 
comment on both their own registrations and the methodology. Though only 17 answered, there 
was a tendency in answers, that people were gone for the national holiday and therefore in doubt 
if potential food waste should have been registered (See Appendix 4.5).

Estimation of potential impact of liquid food waste on WWTP

household based on the average TSS and liquid food waste generation. By multiplying the average 
concentrations of BOD, COD, TN and TP with the water consumption, the total load per year can 
be calculated (See Table 12). According to Valverde-Pérez & Smets (2019), the stoichiometry of 
nitrification in N removal, 4.57 mg O2 is needed per mg N, which gives a total O2 consumption of 
11.1 kg per year for 2.4 kg TN per year. Another approach for connection between the two 
methodologies could be to use the average household water consumption for food and drinks and 
dishwashing in Denmark, the liquid food waste generation and the typical ratios between 
measurements in municipal WWTPs found in Valverde-Pérez (2019b). On average, a Danish 
household consumes 81 m3 of water each year, where 7% are from drinks and cooking and 10% 
are from cleaning and dishwashing, which are shown as the two bottom rows in Table 12 (DANVA, 
2018) (DANVA, 2019).

Uncertainties in laboratory measurements and calculations 
The most significant uncertainty in the sampling process would be the homogenization and 
extraction of samples from the large 10 L containers in which the samples were initially collected.  
This uncertainty is shown in TSS and VSS calculations, where some duplicates vary between 1 and 
3 g/L. These uncertainties might have had an effect on the BOD measurements, where the filtered 
samples were used. However, it could be avoided by testing different mixing methods and 
extraction methods, where the composition of the sample is preserved.  
The method in this study were to shake the container and pour it into beakers and from there mix 
it with a pipette and extract the needed volume. For the COD and nutrients analyses, a grinder 
was used after extraction from large containers, which could grind the solids to a certain point, but 
it was more likely solids were not grinded, as the blades were too rough. Other solutions could be 
to use mixers, pumps or tubes to mix the sample in the large containers. 
Another significant uncertainty found during the measurements was during the BOD 
measurements, where especially sample 3A had a large uncertainty when calibrating and fitting 
the BOD values to a graph. However, this sample also missed a duplicate as the data was lost 
during the process due to an error on the Oxitop Controller. This error though should not affect 
the results as they are average of two duplicates, but if one duplicate is significantly off from the 
actual result, this will lead to an incorrect measurement. The potential error in sample 3A lead to a 
continuous uncertainty throughout the measurements and calculations in fractions of COD, TN 
and TP. This uncertainty also shows the sensitivity of the methodologies and the importance of 
correct handling and procedure in the laboratory.

Food waste grinders and impacts on sewerage system 
In Denmark, the use of FWDs is not well used as some municipalities have banned them or the 
Danish EPA advice against FWDs as the sewerage system might be damaged by the increased load 
of food waste (City of Gladsaxe, 2019) (Miljøstyrelsen, 2019). 
Several studies have been conducted worldwide to estimate both the impacts on the WWTP but 
also the environmental impacts. In a study from Wisconsin, USA by Diggelman & Ham (2003), 
different scenarios were compared through an LCA for 100 kg of food waste in Wisconsin, USA 
(See Table 16). In the studies, use of land, materials and energy were the background for 
calculation of environmental impact. As it is shown, emissions to the environment decrease when 
using FWDs, but as expected BOD and COD increase. However, the LCA might not be complete as 
food waste mixed with wastewater, some potential in resource recovery in the food waste might 
be lost as it is mixed with sludge. The sludge then might contain antibiotics and pharmaceutical 
substances, heavy metals, which are difficult to remove from the sludge. Therefore there might be 
a higher interest in source segregating the food waste in the household and use as a pure input in 
biogas facilities. 
In another study by Güven & Ozturk (2018), the water consumption and electricity consumption of 
FWDs were 12.4 L/kg waste and 0.02 kWh/kg waste and the BOD, COD and TS load increased 
respectively 25%, 15.8% and 22.2% for using FWDs compared to not using.

As shown, there are decreases in environmental emissions, but also increases in the impact on 
infrastructure and sewerage system, when using food waste grinders. In Denmark, DANVA (2011)

not to be affected by the extra load except, if the WWTP have a digestion tank on site.

Conclusion 
The objective of this study was to investigate the generation and composition of liquid food waste 
by developing, testing and validation a characterization method while estimating the potential 
impacts on the WWTP. 
The study has shown, that Denmark annually generates 5 kg of liquid food waste per person, 11 kg 
per household with an average size of 2.2 persons and a total generation of 30,000 Mg with a 56% 
being avoidable liquid food waste. Some of the most significant food and drinks types entering the 
sewerage system is coffee (17%), milk (13%) and brine from cans and cooking (10%-13%). The 
typical reason for disposal was during cleaning of dishes (48%) in the sink or in the dishwasher, 
where leftover (30%) also was quite significant. When compared to solid food waste generation in 
Denmark, the amount is significantly low as Danish households produces 183 kg of solid food 
waste per year. 
The characterization of liquid food waste reveals, that the values for the chemical characterization 
are significantly higher in most cases compared to typical values for inlets to WWTPs or studies on 
greywater from kitchen sinks. Thus, it is mainly caused by a missing dilution from other sources of 
wastewater e.g. rainwater and showers. On average, the parameters were as following: 2.1±0.5 
g/L for TSS and VSS, 803±347 mg/L for BODtotal, 1,020±430 mg/L for BCOD, 663±420 mg/L for 
BCODs, 3,601±1,961 mg/L for COD, 44±27 mg/L for TN and 10±5 mg/L for TP. 
In studies based on liquid food waste generation for households with FWDs, environmental 
impacts of greenhouse gases and acid gases showed a lower impact for treatment with FWD and 
WWTP compared to incineration or composting. Though there is a loss of potential resource and 
energy recovery with the mixing of food waste and wastewater. 
Though both methodologies had uncertainties and assumptions for data collection and treatment 
and sample preparation, they provided sufficient results to quantify liquid food waste and its 
potential impact on WWTP. Therefore, the objective of developing and testing methodologies for 
quantification and estimation of impact are seen as fulfilled, though more work should be done 
with the topic in order to provide sufficient data and results for comparison and use in other 
studies.

Further work 
In order to work further with this topic, some possible uncertainties and assumptions have to be 
evaluated and worked with. In the quantification methodology, more surveys might be needed to 
estimate a more reliable liquid food waste generation and composition and small changes in the 
set-up and information have to be done to ensure participants registering food and drinks waste 
during the period. Different aspects of this have been discussed and evaluated and the 
implementation of these changes could lead to new findings and results. This could include 
participant registration with username, addition of zero food or drinks waste in registration, 
examples list for each overall food category and giving participants more information on the 
process, so many not only respond on the pre-questionnaire, but also the survey and the post-
questionnaire.  
For the estimation of potential impacts, there were some uncertainties, which were mostly caused 
by insufficient sample preparation and handling in the laboratory. These uncertainties are most 
likely dealt with by performing the procedures several times to ensure uniform handling and 
preparation for the final measurements. For a possible connection between the two 
methodologies, laboratory measurements should be done several times with a significant variety 
of samples to calculate average conversion factors to estimate the load on the WWTP per kg liquid 
food waste. Overall, the further work has to work with removal of uncertainties and creating 
sufficient data and results to estimate reliable data for future handling of liquid food waste. Also, 
this study focused on households without FWDs, so a potential investigation of households with 
FWDs could be done in other to compare with and contribute to future solid waste management.
