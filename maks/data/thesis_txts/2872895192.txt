Abstract 
 
This master’s thesis investigates the hydraulic behaviour of water in an unplanted, lab-scale hor-
izontal subsurface flow treatment wetland. The study builds upon a previous study, in which a 
sensor network and software to enable real-time local monitoring and visualisation of conductivity 
within the pilot treatment wetland. To characterise the system’s internal hydrodynamics, a series 
of tracer tests were carried out using sodium chloride (NaCl) as the tracer. Both pulse and step 
injection methods were employed to visualise the flow path and to identify hydraulic inefficiencies. 
Experiments were conducted using two different substrate materials: lightweight expanded clay 
aggregate with 48% porosity and a grain size of 10–20 mm, and medium gravel with 40% porosity. 
Gravel is a conventional substrate used due to their affordability and high hydraulic conductivity, 
expanded clay offers advantages such as phosphorus sorption and biofilm support. Residence 
time distribution analysis served as the primary tool for evaluating system performance, given that 
pollutant removal in treatment wetlands is closely tied to contact time between water and func-
tional components. Most tracer tests showed recovery rates above 80%, validating the method. 
Hydraulic performance was generally poor. All tests showed low volumetric efficiency (𝑒𝑣<1), in-
dicating significant dead zones and non-uniform flow distribution. Hydraulic efficiency was also 
low (λ ≤ 0.5) in most cases, except for one test using gravel (λ = 0.53), considered satisfactory. 
Additionally, mean detention times (τ) were significantly lower (by 70% to 90%) than the nominal 
residence times (τₙ), highlighting severe short-circuiting, especially in experiments with expanded 
clay. While tracer tests with both materials suggested horizontal bottom flow, step results with 
gravel displayed moderate dispersion, suggesting improved flow behaviour in comparison to ex-
tended clay. In conclusion, tracer test results, particularly with expanded clay, strongly indicate 
non-ideal flow paths and bottom short-circuiting. These issues may be mitigated by increasing 
the aspect ratio (length-to-width) and redesigning the inlet/outlet configurations to promote more 
uniform flow and reduce dead volume, ultimately enhancing hydraulic performance. 
 
Keywords: Constructed Wetland, Tracer Tests, Preferential Flow, Expanded Clay

1. Introduction 
Natural wetlands are complex ecosystems defined by a range of parameters. According to Fonder & 
Headley (2013a) these ecosystems are typically transitional zones between terrestrial and aquatic en-
vironments. Three key characteristics help the characterization: 
 
1) the presence of water, 
 
2) hydric (water-saturated) soils, and 
 
3) vegetation adapted to saturated soil. 
 
For centuries, natural wetlands were used as disposal sites for domestic and industrial waste, often 
without understanding their ecological limits. In many developed countries, particularly in the 1950s and 
1960s, awareness of wetland degradation began to rise. However, many wetland sites remain under 
threat due to ongoing pressures such as population growth, urbanisation, peat extraction, deforestation, 
and land conversion for agriculture or construction. These activities often push wetlands beyond their 
assimilative capacity, leading to damage that is irreversible (Addo-Bankas et al., 2024; Asselen et al., 
2013; Vymazal & Kröpfelová, 2008). 
 
Traditional water management systems tend to follow a linear model: water is extracted from a source 
(e.g., river or aquifer), used, and then treated just enough to be discharged back into the environment. 
This approach operates under the assumption that water is an unlimited resource. This encourages 
water loss and misuse, contributing to inefficient and unsustainable resource use (Langergraber et al., 
2020). This is further exacerbated by altered precipitation patterns due to climate change, responsible 
for prolonged droughts in some regions and intense rainfall in others. While droughts limit water avail-
ability, heavy rainfall events often lead to flooding and water contamination, rendering the excess water 
improper for use. 
 
Constructed wetlands (CW), also referred to in the literature as treatment wetlands (TW), offer a sus-
tainable and nature-based solution to these challenges(Kadlec & Wallace, 2009; Langergraber et al., 
2020). With more than 70 years of development, CWs are well-established as an effective wastewater 
treatment technology (Vymazal & Kröpfelová, 2008). They indirectly contribute to the preservation of 
natural wetlands and additionally serve as a tool for ecological restoration. 
Treatment wetlands are engineered systems designed to replicate the purification processes of natural 
wetlands, within a controlled environment (Dotro et al., 2017; Kadlec & Wallace, 2009; Langergraber et 
al., 2020). These systems rely on the interaction between three functional components: vegetation, 
porous substrate media, and microorganisms. 
 
In the context of climate change and urban resilience, constructed wetlands are increasingly being in-
tegrated into urban infrastructures (Addo-Bankas et al., 2024). Their ability to retain water, reduce flood 
risk, and trap nutrients and emerging pollutants makes them valuable tools for sustainable water man-
agement. When properly designed, they cause minimal disruption to their surroundings, are cost-effec-
tive to scale up, and require relatively simple maintenance (Addo-Bankas et al., 2024; Stefanakis et al., 
2014). Importantly, they can be tailored to meet specific treatment objectives, supporting a more effi-
cient and sustainable use of water resources (Langergraber et al., 2020). 
In water purification through subsurface flow wetlands, several parameters influence treatment effec-
tiveness, including wetland dimensions, substrate features and they type of biomass.

Pollutant removal in treatment wetlands occurs through a combination of physical, chemical, and bio-
logical processes, such as sedimentation, adsorption, plant uptake, and microbial degradation (Lang-
ergraber et al., 2020). The residence time distribution helps quantify the impact of design choices on 
these processes by evaluating how uniformly or not water flows through the wetland system. This is 
key to understanding hydraulic efficiency, which reflects the system’s capacity to distribute flow evenly 
and maximize contact time between the functioning elements, which is critical for optimizing pollutant 
removal. 
 
Flow irregularities such as short-circuiting (preferential flow) or dead zones (regions with little to no flow) 
reduce the efficiency of treatment wetlands (Fogler, 1999; Kadlec & Wallace, 2009; Levenspiel, 1999). 
These flow patterns can be studied using tracer tests, which involve injecting a chemically inert sub-
stance that mimics the behaviour of water. By tracking its movement through the wetland and analysing 
the resulting breakthrough curve, it is possible to infer important hydraulic characteristics of the system. 
 
This thesis explores these aspects through experimental investigation in a lab-scale horizontal subsur-
face flow treatment wetland, and is guided by the following research questions: 
• 
Can spatial conductivity monitoring confirm a predominantly horizontal flow path through the 
wetland? 
• 
What is the efficiency of the lab-scale HSSF treatment wetland under study, filled with ex-
panded clay and gravel will be used for comparison?  
• 
Are there any operational inefficiencies, and what possible design changes could be imple-
mented to improve its performance?

1.1 Objectives 
 
This master’s thesis is an experimental project that aims to assess the hydraulic behaviour of water in 
an unplanted, lab-scale horizontal subsurface flow treatment wetland (HSSFTW) with lightweight ex-
panded clay aggregate as a substrate. It builds upon the work of (Overlund, 2024), which involved 
creating a sensor network to enable real-time local monitoring, along with the development of a software 
for data acquisition and storage of conductivity readings from within the pilot treatment wetland. This 
groundwork was essential for conducting this experimental work focused on characterizing the flow 
behaviour in the pilot treatment wetland. 
 
To achieve the main objective, the following specific objectives were defined: 
• 
Gain a deeper understanding of existing research on constructed wetlands, particularly hori-
zontal subsurface flow systems. 
 
• 
Investigate the flow direction to identify preferential patterns or zones of reduced or non-existent 
flow through tracer test experiments. 
 
• 
Explore the most common tracer test implementation methods: pulse and step injection. 
 
• 
Compare tracer test results from lightweight expanded clay and those of gravel, a conventional 
substrate, in terms of hydrodynamic parameters, actual hydraulic detention time, volumetric 
efficiency, and related metrics. 
 
Overall, the wish for this work is for it to contribute positively to the knowledge related to the design of 
efficient HSSF treatment wetlands.

2. Literature review 
There is not an universal definition for wetlands however the one proposed by the Ramsar Convention 
on Wetlands, established in 1971, provides a widely accepted international definition, describing wet-
lands as: “areas of marsh, fen, peatland or water, whether natural or artificial, permanent or temporary, 
with water that is static or flowing, fresh, brackish or salt, including areas of marine water the depth of 
which at low tide does not exceed six meters” (An Introduction to the Ramsar Convention on Wetlands, 
2016). 
 
 
2.1 Natural wetlands  
 
Natural wetlands are naturally occurring transitional ecosystems between terrestrial and aquatic envi-
ronments, presenting characteristics of both. The abundance of water, combined with the high rates of 
primary productivity favour the production of organisms at the base of the food chain, making wetlands 
biologically rich and attractive to wildlife (U.S. EPA, n.d.).  
 
Natural wetlands use natural energy, such as sunlight, to transform pollutants found in typical 
wastewater into simpler by-products, making this an affordable system for wastewater treatment 
(Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). 
In addition to water supply and purification services, natural wetlands provide food and shelter for wild-
life. For coastal nations, wetlands play a key role in coastal and flood protection (An Introduction to the 
Ramsar Convention on Wetlands, 2016; U.S. EPA, n.d.).  
 
Wetlands are being subjected to increasing pressures from urbanisation and industrialisation, driven by 
population and economic growth (Asselen et al., 2013). These pressures have led to the drainage and 
conversion of wetlands for agriculture, peat extraction, and other anthropogenic activities. An inevitable 
consequence of this phenomenon is the increase in pollutant loads, causing various natural wetlands 
to reach or even exceed their assimilative capacity. 
 
 
2.2 Constructed wetlands  
 
In response to the degradation of natural wetlands, constructed wetlands (CWs) have emerged as a 
sustainable alternative for wastewater treatment and ecological restoration.  
The overarching goal of constructed wetlands is to protect, mitigate and rehabilitate natural wetlands 
that are increasingly under threat. This can be achieved directly, through the restoration of degraded 
ecosystems, or indirectly, by functioning as a treatment system designed solely to improve water quality, 
in that way reducing pressure on existing natural ecosystems (Fonder & Headley, 2013). 
 
Treatment wetlands, a type of constructed wetlands, are engineered systems designed to replicate the 
natural processes occurring in natural wetlands, but within a controlled environment (Dotro et al., 2017; 
Langergraber et al., 2020; Stefanakis et al., 2014). They can be conceptualized as bioreactors filled 
with porous media that supports the growth of microorganisms and vegetation, with the main purpose 
of water purification.

TW are financially attractive alternative, particularly in areas where land availability is not a limiting

factor. The lower capital and operational costs of TW are due to minimal energy demand, relying on 
solar energy to support vegetation growth and less need for skilled labour.  
These infrastructures are simpler to build and can have a long operational lifespan of over 30 years.  
Furthermore, their design makes them suitable for decentralized treatment applications in small to me-
dium communities in rural and remote areas where centralized sewage infrastructure is often lacking 
(Brown et al., 2000). 
However, constructed wetlands require relatively large land areas (3-10 m2/p.e) which limits their fea-
sibility in densely populated urban settlements where space is limited and expensive. Moreover, treat-
ment wetland’s performance is climate dependent, with efficiency decreasing in winter months. Finally, 
the 1 or 2 years needed for macrophytes to be fully mature poses a constraint in performance (Stefana-
kis et al., 2014). 
 
 
2.2.1 Types of treatment wetlands 
 
Although the terms “constructed wetland” and “treatment wetland” are used interchangeably in the lit-
erature in the present work the term “treatment wetland” will be employed. 
The spectrum of treatment wetland (TW) designs is ample, depending mainly on the specific objectives 
of pollutant removal. Fonder & Headley (2013) proposed a classification system that primarily focuses 
on the physical and operational characteristics of treatment wetlands, in this system they organize them 
based on two criteria: hydrology and predominant vegetation. This vegetation consists of macrophytes 
(aquatic plants growing in or near water), which can be classified as emergent, submerged or floating 
based on their position in relation to the water's surface. 
 
The hydrological classification considers several attributes, including the position of water relative to 
the substrate (above or below), the direction of flow (horizontal or vertical), the level of substrate satu-
ration, and the mode of influent loading. In parallel, classification by vegetation is guided by how plants 
interact with the water level, specifically, the position of their roots and their growth pattern relative to 
the water surface. Among these criteria, the flow pattern is the most used in the literature to distinguish 
treatment wetland types (Addo-Bankas et al. 2024). Based on flow characteristics, TWs are generally 
categorized into two main types: surface flow (SF) and subsurface flow (SSF) wetlands. The key dis-
tinction is that in SF wetlands, water flows visibly above the substrate, whereas in SSF wetlands, flow 
occurs below the surface, within the porous media. 
 
The appearance of surface flow wetlands, also referred as free water surface wetlands, resemble that 
of natural wetlands (Fonder & Headley, 2013; Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). They are typically used for 
tertiary treatment, however as calm open water surfaces attract mosquitos, they can expose humans 
to pathogens (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). 
In contrast, subsurface flow wetlands are engineered so that water moves through the substrate, mak-
ing the flow invisible. It is the configuration of the inlet and outlet that determines the flow direction in 
the wetland. When arranged horizontally, the system is referred to as a horizontal subsurface flow 
(HSSF) wetland. Whenever the configuration is arranged in a vertical position, it is designated a vertical 
flow (VF) wetland. A specific variation of VF wetland is the French VF system, which accepts raw sew-
age directly. The most implemented subsurface flow designs are HSSF and the vertical down-flow wet-
lands. 
Finally, hybrid wetlands combine two or more of the previously mentioned systems in series to maximize 
treatment performance. These systems are designed to exploit the strengths of one configuration while 
compensating for the limitations of another (Fonder & Headley, 2013). The Table 2-1 provides a concise 
overview of the most common treatment wetland types.

2.3 Horizontal subsurface constructed wetland 
 
The focus now shifts specifically to HSSF systems, as the experimental work presented in this thesis is 
within this category. 
The general constituents of a treatment wetlands include: an inlet for the inflow of contaminated water 
and an outlet through which the treated water exits the system; a pipe system for water to travel through; 
a liner, which serves as an impermeable barrier at the bottom of the wetland (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009); 
and a pump to regulate flow. Additionally, water level and other monitoring devices are often installed 
to ensure operational control and performance evaluation (Brown et al., 2000; Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). 
 
The functional components of horizontal subsurface flow systems, common to other treatment wetlands, 
are macrophytes, microorganisms, and the substrate bed. These components function synergistically: 
the plant root systems stabilize the substrate and enhance oxygen transfer; microorganisms in the rhi-
zosphere (the soil zone surrounding the roots) utilize this oxygen for their metabolic processes, facili-
tating the degradation of organic matter and nutrients. The substrate provides structural support for root 
development and the formation of microbial communities. 
 
The interaction between the plant, the substrate and the microorganism enables key removal mecha-
nisms for pollutants such as nitrates, phosphates, and suspended solids (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). For 
optimal performance, it is essential that wastewater remains within the system long enough to allow 
these removal processes to occur effectively. 
Conceptually, an HSSF wetland have a rectangular shape, sealed at the bottom with a liner, filled with 
a substrate that remains permanently saturated, as shown in Figure 2.1 (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009; 
Vymazal & Kröpfelová, 2008). Wastewater is introduced either at or just below the surface, usually 
through a perforated pipe that covers the width of the system (Akratos & Tsihrintzis, 2007).  
These systems are commonly planted with emergent macrophytes, whose roots increase bed porosity 
and form voids that facilitate water movement. The water level is maintained 5 –15 cm below the surface 
of the porous medium, which typically has a depth of 30 to 80 cm (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). 
 
 
Figure 2.1 Elements of HSSF treatment wetland (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009) 
 
The combination of substrate and plant roots supports biofilm development, which contributes to the 
removal of organic matter and suspended solids (with efficiency typically above 80%) (Dotro et al.,

2017). However, nutrient removal, particularly of ammonium nitrogen, is less efficient due to the sys-
tem’s limited oxygen transfer capacity (Dotro et al., 2017), an inherent constraint of the saturated con-
ditions. 
And as the system matures, it may become susceptible to clogging, the most common operational issue 
in HSSF treatment wetlands. Clogging is either caused by the accumulation of organic and inorganic 
matter or by a reduction in permeability of the chosen substrate (Dotro et al., 2021; Kadlec & Wallace, 
2009; Vymazal & Kröpfelová, 2008) . Over time, this can reduce both the lifespan and efficiency of the 
system by limiting the contact area and interaction time between wastewater and functional elements 
of the system responsible for pollutant degradation or removal. In severe cases, clogging can force the 
water to bypass treatment entirely by circulating over the surface of the bed.  
 
 
2.3.1 Substrate selection 
 
The appropriate selection of the substrate material is a crucial step when designing a HSSF treatment 
wetland to prevent or reduce the likelihood of clogging and ensure hydraulic performance. For instance, 
the substrate particle size influences the system’s permeability, and as a result, determines the maxi-
mum hydraulic loading rate that can be applies without causing the system to overflow Dotro et al. 
(2017). 
To mitigate the risk of clogging, substrates should be washed, have a round shape, structurally stable 
and inert, so they don’t degrade or leach pollutants when exposed to wastewater (Dotro et al., 2017). 
  
In treatment wetlands the substrates used can be broadly categorized based on their origin (natural 
materials, industry by-products or man-made materials) and by their functional role in pollutant removal. 
The later classification includes ion-exchange, phosphorus sorbing, electron donor and all those sub-
strates that do not fall into these categories (Zhao et al., 2025). 
 
Ion-exchange substrates have the capacity to retain and exchange cations from wastewater onto their 
surface. Examples include vermiculite and biochar (Zhao et al., 2025). 
 
Phosphorus sorbing or P-sorption substrates remove phosphorus through sorption mechanisms.  The 
removal capacity is influenced by their levels of calcium, magnesium, aluminium or iron oxides (GARCÍA 
et al., 2010).  Vohla et al. (2011) showed that substrate materials with high levels of calcium are more 
efficient at removing phosphorus. An example of a P-sorption substrate is expanded clay, light weight 
expanded clay. 
 
Electron donor substrates support microbial activity by supplying organic or inorganic carbon sources 
necessary for denitrification, a nitrogen removal process. In many HSSF systems, denitrification is lim-
ited by the lack of electron donors, which can be provided in the form of organic carbon or reduced iron 
or sulphur compounds (Dotro et al., 2017; Ilyas & Masih, 2017; Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). 
 
Conventionally, HSSF treatment wetlands have relied on substrates such as gravel and coarse sand, 
as they were abundant, affordable and presented high hydraulic conductivity. While gravel is effective 
for supporting flow and biofilm attachment, it offers limited capacity for phosphorus removal, an element 
of major concern in wastewater treatment (Vymazal & Kröpfelová, 2008). To address this limitation, the 
use of alternative substrates with P-sorption capacity has been favoured as a targeted solution.

2.4 Hydraulics Characterization 
 
The interaction between the plant, the substrate and the microorganism promotes the pollutant removal 
processes, which are contact time-dependent biochemical reactions. To obtain optimal performance, it 
is essential that the wastewater remains in the system for long enough to allow the reactions to occur 
effectively. Operational inefficiencies in the system, such as preferential flow, reduce the duration that 
the wastewater spends in the wetland, defined as the hydraulic retention time. This reduces the treat-
ment efficiency, influenced by hydraulic characteristics (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009; Levenspiel, 1999).

2.4.1 Flow Distribution Inefficiencies 
 
Levenspiel (1999) explains that the flow pattern in reactors deviates from the ideal and is dependent on 
a combination of factors: (i) the level of aggregation of fluid molecules; (ii) the residence time distribution 
of the fluid flowing in the reactor, and in the presence of two fluids (iii) the timing of the fluid mixing 
process, whether it occurs at an early or late stage in the system's flow pathway. 
 
In the context of treatment wetlands, the RTD is the most relevant factor affecting flow pattern, as it 
provides information on the operational inefficiencies that cause the real flow to diverge from ideal. The 
residence time distribution curve, E(t), reflect the flow behaviour in the system and it is obtained by 
conducting a stimulus-response experiment, such as tracer tests which will be introduced in the follow-
ing section. 
 
An example of a reactor that does not present ideal flow, as described by Fogler (1999), is a packed 
bed reactor. This is a reactor filled with catalyst particles where flow is not uniform. In such systems, 
certain zones offer more resistance to flow, while others provide easier pathways, allowing the fluid to 
bypass parts of the medium. As consequence some molecules exit the system quickly while others 
remain longer, creating a range of residence times. 
 
In the context of treatment wetlands, flow through less resistant paths, reduces the contact time be-
tween pollutants in the wastewater and the reactive zones (with biofilms, plant roots, and the substrate) 
which is crucial for effective treatment. 
 
The RTD curve analysis enables identification of hydraulic inefficiencies, or what Levenspiel (1999) 
called “diagnosing reactor ills”. These inefficiencies include dead volume regions, short-circuiting, inter-
nal recirculation, and dispersion, all of which diminishes treatment performance. 
 
For example, when the mean residence time (τ) is greater than the nominal residence time (τₙ), the 
resulting E(t) curve is delayed, suggesting that the tracer was retained in the system longer than antic-
ipated. This could indicate areas of stagnant or slow-moving flow, or it might suggest that the tracer is 
not completely inert. 
In contrast, when τ is less than τₙ, two distinct patterns may emerge. One possibility is an E(t) curve 
with a long tail, which points to the existence of hydraulically inactive zones and potential short-circuit-
ing, as shown in Figure 2.2. Another scenario involves an E(t) curve with two distinct peaks: the first 
peak represents fluid elements that exit the system rapidly, while the second peak corresponds to fluid 
that remains within the system for a longer period. 
Moreover, if the E(t) curve displays multiple decaying peaks, it may signify internal recirculation. In such 
cases, fluid elements re-enter upstream sections of the system, leading to delayed exit times and a 
breakdown of uniform flow dynamics.

When a portion of the flow follows a preferential path, it reduces the average residence time and limits 
interaction with the system’s removal agents, leading to the hydraulic isolation of large areas within the 
treatment wetland. Although some mixing may occur in these dead volume zones through diffusion and 
dispersion, these processes are significantly slow, resulting in variations in water quality across the 
system. 
 
 
2.4.2 Tracer tests 
 
Tracer tests are utilised in this field of research because they enable a correlation to be made between 
the behaviour of a soluble inert tracer and the movement of water. This assumption is based on the 
premise that both have similar flow patterns. Hence, tracer tests provide insights about the internal 
hydrodynamic characteristics of water within a wetland, which in turn aids in the identification of devia-
tions from the ideal flow (Kadlec & Wallace, 2009). 
 
A tracer test is defined as a procedure that involves the injection (the stimulus) of an inert soluble tracer 
at the inlet. Subsequently, the tracer concentration is measured over time at the outlet (the response). 
 
There are a few different approaches to tracer input, and the simplest methods in terms of data inter-
pretation are the pulse and step input (Levenspiel, 1999).  
 
The pulse method involves the administration of a concentrated tracer solution at once at the start of 
the test, over a short period of time, with the objective of inducing a substantial increase in concentration

2.4.3 Models for Hydraulics 
 
Since the flow in constructed wetlands is not ideal due to operational efficiencies, it is important to define 
what is considered the ideal flow. 
 
Knowledge from internal hydraulics evolved out of the field of chemical reaction engineering (Fogler, 
1999; Levenspiel, 1999; Rawlings & Ekerdt, 2002). This link to the subject in discussion because mod-
els taken from that field can also be used to parametrize RTD and other useful informative parameters. 
 
The following two ideal steady state flow reactors that were adapted to constructed wetlands and that 
are used to analyse pulse tracer experiments (Headley & Kadlec, 2007). Representing extremes in 
terms of mixing: 
• 
plug flow reactor (PFR), 
• 
continuous flow steady-state reactor (CFSTR). 
 
The PFR presupposes that water flows through the reactor without axial mixing or diffusion in the flow 
direction, and that all water fractions have the same velocity. This pattern of flow piston-like, it does not 
change over time; the reactor has a single residence (Headley & Kadlec, 2007; Levenspiel, 1999). 
 
The CFSTR reactor considers well stirred and uniform mixing of the water throughout the reactor, with 
any inflow water being instantaneously blended with the existing water and the water exiting the reactor 
possessing a composition equivalent to that within it. The type of flow pattern is mixed and under steady-
flow operating conditions, this reactor exhibits multiple residence times (Headley & Kadlec, 2007). 
 
The concept of ideal reactors represents the theoretical designs that maximise the efficiency of reactant 
contact (Levenspiel, 1999). These models offer a simplified representation of the intricacies involved in 
real reactors, thereby aiding in the comprehension of their operational dynamics. 
 
Early literature on constructed wetland design assumed ideal plug-flow behaviour as seen in publica-
tions by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (1988) and the Water Pollution Control 
Federation (1990) as cited by Headley & Kadlec (2007). However, more recent literature acknowledges 
that flow within treatment wetlands is not ideal due to dispersion, short-circuits and other factors (Kadlec 
& Wallace, 2009; Metcalf & Eddy Inc., 2003; Persson et al., 1999; Thackston et al., 1987). 
Now that we have an idea of what ideal reactor and consequent flow mean and based on previous 
studies, we can say that in systems like HSFCWs, the flow through a CW is not ideal. There is some 
degree of mixing, diffusion in all sorts of directions and in they are between the plug flow and the com-
plete mixed flow (Headley & Kadlec, 2007 as cited in Bowmer 1987; Bavor et al. 1988; Kadlec 1994; 
Kadlec 2000; Werner, Kadlec 2000). 
 
More realistic models that consider non-ideal flow behaviour include the Dispersion and the Tanks-in-
Series model. Which are approximately equivalent, because they are both use one parameter to de-
scribe or quantify deviation from ideal flow. And this parameter is assessed by analysing the RTD ob-
tained from a tracer test (Fogler, 1999; Levenspiel, 1999).

dispersion on plug flow, it is expressed by the following convective diffusion equation (Fogler, 1992):

The methodology of this study can be divided in five states, as shown in Figure 3.1. As the objective of

Next, an execution plan was developed to serve as a protocol during the experiments. Using the hy-

3.2 Granular material characterization 
 
The materials previously described were initially subjected to a grain size analysis using dry sieving, a 
method widely accepted across scientific disciplines as a standard procedure for evaluating grain size 
distribution (López, 2016). Sieving is particularly relevant for the classification of unconsolidated mate-
rials and for grains larger than 0.06 mm (Ovesen et al., 2007). It is conducted using a sieve, a cylindrical 
metal frame with a woven wire screen base and square apertures. 
 
As Wills & Finch (2016) explain, most natural and manufactured particles vary in shape and size distri-
bution, therefore characterization based on a single dimension, as it is done for spheres using a diam-
eter, is difficult. To overcome this limitation, an equivalent diameter is used to represent the particle 
size. This refers to the diameter of a supposed sphere that would behave in the same manner as the 
particle under the same conditions. The sieve aperture diameter is an example of an equivalent diam-
eter (Endecotts Ltd., 2023; Wills & Finch, 2016). As such, sieve analyses offer an effective way to 
assess the grain size distribution of particles, even when they are irregular, therefore it is a suitable 
approach for this study. Furthermore, dry sieving was selected for its cost-effectiveness and ease of

execution. In the context of this study, determining the particle size distribution of expanded clay and 
gravel is relevant, as this information is critical for evaluating permeability, a property known to influence 
water flow within a wetland. 
 
The experimental setup included a WS-150 weighing scale (maximum capacity of 150 kg and 10g res-
olution), a set of sieves with apertures of 31.5, 16, 12.5, 9.5, 4.75 and 2.36 mm, and a box that func-
tioned as a sieve pan (EUROBOX NEXTGEN ECONOMY, 400x300x120 mm, with open grips). 
 
The procedure followed a protocol, outlined in the Test Sieving — Part 1: Methods Using Test Sieves 
of Woven Wire Cloth and Perforated Metal Plate (1988). However, given that the analysis was not 
intended for geotechnical purposes, where engineering specification standards need to be meet, not all 
steps or recommendations were strictly held to. As the goal with the sieve analyses was purely to char-
acterize the grain size distribution of the granular materials. 
 
The empty six sieves and pan were weighed. Then, 500 g of dry samples of expanded clay and gravel 
were measured using the available scale (precision ±2%, above the recommended ±1%). According to 
Endecotts Ltd. (2023), 500 g is the appropriate sample size for particles larger than 2 mm, which is the 
case for the materials selected in this study by visual observation. 
 
The sieves were arranged in descending order of aperture size. The sample was introduced to the sieve 
with the coarsest openings and subjected to manual dry sieving, by holding the sieve with both hands, 
moving it in a circular motion, and tapping repeatedly. 
Sieving continued until no noticeable amount of material passed through within one minute. The mate-
rial that passed through was collected in the sieve pan and weighed. This process was repeated for 
each sieve in decreasing aperture size. 
Both substrate materials were tested in this manner, and duplicate tests were conducted to increase 
the reliability of the results.  
After sieving, the mass of each sieve (empty and full) was used to determine the mass of particles 
retained. Finally, the data was used to plot the particle size distribution curve, a plot of cumulative per-
centage passing against sieve aperture size.

approach.  
 
Before these tasks could be addressed, the inflow pumping rate was established using the areal hy-
draulic loading rate, which corresponds to the amount of wastewater applied per unit area of the treat-
ment wetland, expressed in m³/m².day (or mm/day). 
 
To define a realistic HLR, various references were reviewed. Kadlec & Wallace, 2009 citing Cooper et 
al. (1996) and Griffin et al. (2008), report an HLR of 0.20 m³/m²·day used in a HSSF treatment wetland 
with a grave bed (10–12 mm particle size) for tertiary treatment in the UK, with surface inflow distribu-
tion. The study by Wang et al. (2023) evaluated high HLRs ranging from 1.0 to 1.3 m³/m²·day in full-
scale subsurface constructed wetlands. If applied to the research experimental setup, these rates would 
correspond to flow rates between 41.67 and 54.17 L/h, which were deemed excessive given the lab-
scale dimensions of the object of study. 
 
Furthermore, it is documented that treatment wetlands generally operate under low HLR, typically less 
than 0.5 m³/m²·day, as recommended by the U.S. EPA (2000). Therefore, for the present study a more 
conservative range of HLR values suggested by Sezerino & von Sperling (2018) were adopted, between 
0.04 to 0.12 m³/m²·day for secondary treatment in horizontal subsurface flow wetlands. The Table 3-1 
was used to make the selection of the most suitable flow rate for experiments according to the dedicated 
8 weeks for experiments.

Sodium chloride (NaCl) was selected as the tracer due to its affordability, high solubility, chemical in-
ertness, and its presence at low background concentration in water. Its conductive nature enables de-
tection with conductivity probes, such as those installed along the wetland (Headley & Kadlec (2007) 
as cited in Taylor et al. 1990; Whitmer et al. 2000).  
 
However, a known limitation of using NaCl as a tracer is the need for high concentrations to induce a 
detectable conductivity signal above baseline values. This may lead to density driven stratification, 
where the water containing the dense tracer sinks to the bottom of the wetland, potentially distorting the 
measured flow patterns Headley & Kadlec (2007). Evidence of this issue is observed when outlet sen-
sors record a conductivity peak prior to detection by internal sensors. 
 
To address this, preliminary laboratory tests were conducted to identify a suitable tracer concentration 
that would minimize stratification effects. Following the practical guide provided by Headley & Kadlec 
(2007), the tracer solution was prepared to remain within 1% of the ambient water density in the wetland. 
This constraint translated to a target concentration of 1 g/L for the NaCl tracer used in the initial exper-
iments. 
 
With the tracer solution chosen, the experiments began using the pulse injection method, a common 
approach in treatment wetland tracer studies. It is important to state that earliest tests are not included 
as they were deemed unusable, because tracer recovery in those tests was very low.  
 
During the preliminary testing phase, the injection duration was gradually increased. These results in-
dicated that a step injection method would better suit the study's objective of observing the water flow 
trajectory along the wetland. To achieve that conductivity was maintained bellow the maximum meas-
urable range of the sensors (2.5 mS/cm) to ensure detention by the most sensors.

The short duration of the preliminary tests negatively affected sensor detection, limiting the ability to

Inefficiencies in flow were evidenced by key indicators. First, the mean tracer detention time was as 
expected for non-ideal flow reactor lower than the nominal residence time. 
Although Kadlec (1994) observed that nominal detention in free surface flow treatment wetlands was 
generally 50% greater than the mean tracer detention time.  
This observation can be extended to horizontal subsurface flow wetlands because in these systems 
water also faces resistance to flow due to the substrates and plant root system which promotes disper-
sion and back-mixing (von Sperling et al., 2023).  
In the preliminary experiments conducted for this study, the mean residence time was found to be 78%, 
92%, and 76% lower than the nominal detention time for Experiments I, II, and III, respectively. Such 
discrepancies are indicators of poor hydraulic performance, likely due to the occurrence of short-circuit-
ing.  
 
The findings are in line with the U.S. EPA (2000), which reported that real retention times in many HSSF 
systems can be 40 to 80% lower than nominal values due to the presence of dead zones, loss of pore 
volume, or preferential flow paths. However, Bandeiras (2009) reported that 𝜏> 𝜏𝑛 potentially due to 
the existence of large zones where flow was minimal. 
 
Secondly, the hydraulic efficiency (λ) values obtained were all below 0.5, which, according to the clas-
sification proposed by Persson et al. (1999), corresponds to low efficiency flow. Similarly, volumetric 
efficiency (𝑒𝑣) values were consistently below 1, indicating non-uniform flow distribution. This suggests 
that a significant portion of the wetland’s available volume was not effectively utilised, highlighting the 
presence of considerable dead zones as observed from Figure 4.5 to Figure 4.7. 
 
The short-circuit index (SCI) close to zero for Experiments I and II, suggests that the influent water 
exited the system faster than expected. According to Metcalf & Eddy Inc. (2003), this is indicative of 
severe short-circuiting. In the case of Experiment III, it was not possible to calculate the SCI due to 
elevated background concentrations resulting from insufficient flushing between experiments. Although 
background levels were recorded, the monitoring period may have been too brief to distinguish between 
residual tracer and new detection signals. 
 
For experiments I and II, the apparent number of tanks in series was less than 1. It is expected for N to 
be a positive integer, values below 1 suggest extremely high levels of mixing, where the variance (σ²) 
is much greater than the square of the mean residence time (τ²). This indicates hydraulic behaviour 
worse than that of a completely mixed reactor. In contrast, Experiment III the number of tanks in series 
obtained was 3, which may be misleading as the longer injection period allowed tracer to distribute 
more likely masking short-circuit and reducing variance and potentially lead to an overestimation of 
hydraulic efficiency. 
 
The dispersion number obtained for Experiments I and II was below 0.05, indicating a low degree of 
dispersion. However, this contradicts the high variance observed in the results. This inconsistency could 
be attributed to noise in the measurements and/or the presence of short-circuiting, as suggested by 
other parameters, which may have influenced the variance and consequently affected the estimated 
number of tanks in series.

4.4 Suggested Improvements  
 
Based on multiple tracer tests, it was possible to observe that the horizontal subsurface flow treatment 
wetland under study consistently exhibited horizontal bottom flow, as similarly observed by García et al. 
(2004). The system reveals poor hydraulic performance, primarily due to strong short-circuiting. Improv-
ing these results does not solely depend on replacing the filter material. Although both lightweight ex-
panded clay and gravel were tested (even if duplicate tests with gravel would have strengthened con-
clusions), the differences in hydraulic performance were not substantial. Moreover, Alcocer et al. (2012) 
noted that porous media size had the least influence on the hydraulic behaviour of constructed wet-
lands. 
 
As highlighted by Thackston et al. (1987), modifications in wetland geometry, particularly by increasing 
the aspect ratio (length-to-width ratio) have the greatest impact on improving hydraulic efficiency. It was 
observed that an increase towards 3:1 brings the mean residence time of water closer to the theoretical 
nominal detention time. This adjustment promotes more uniform flow, reduces dead zones, and in-
creases contact between the wastewater and functional components of the system, thereby enhancing 
pollutant degradation. According to the U.S. EPA (2000), dispersion also decreases as the aspect ratio 
increases. García et al. (2004) also reported that increasing the aspect ratio by one unit and using finer 
substrate material can improve hydraulic behaviour by reducing the dispersion number to approximately 
0.3. Increasing the aspect ratio of the lab-scale wetland under study from 2:0.5 to 3:1 could be sugges-
tion for future experiments. Furthermore, in this context it could be also interesting for future experi-
ments to test a higher HLR to allow for the usage of a distributed inflow across the wetland’s width, 
which wasn’t useful at the time of this work given the flow rate chosen for the tests. As this would avoid 
the current single point inlet configuration, which tends to induce channelling and short-circuiting.  
 
According to Persson et al. (1999) systems with a point source inflow or similar configuration are unlikely 
to achieve good hydraulic efficiency unless efforts are made to distribute inflow evenly across the de-
tention area. Suggested modifications include installing baffles, elongating the wetland, or using internal 
islands to redirect flow, designs that have been associated with improved hydraulic performance in the 
literature. A simpler, short-term solution to be tested could involve placing large stones at the inlet and 
outlet to help diffuse and redirect flow, as done in (Chazarenc et al., 2003). 
 
Although Thackston et al. (1987) focused on free surface flow wetlands, their recommendations remain 
relevant for improving hydraulics in subsurface flow wetland systems. To improve hydraulic efficiency, 
they emphasize the need to extend detention time through design alterations such as the ones pro-
posed previously by to Persson et al. (1999). And alert against aspect ratios exceeding 10, which can 
lead to diminishing returns.

5. Conclusions 
The present research aimed to evaluation the hydraulic performance of a lab-scale treatment wetland. 
The principal research questions que that motivated this investigation were the following: 
 
1.  Can spatial conductivity monitoring confirm a predominantly horizontal flow path through the 
wetland? 
2. What is the efficiency of the lab-scale HSSF treatment wetland under study, and do outcomes 
differ when different substrates are use?  
3. Are there any inefficiencies in the flow, and what possible design changes could be imple-
mented to improve its performance? 
 
Overlund (2024) developed a TDS sensor network capable of real-time, spatially distributed monitoring 
within the lab-scale treatment wetland. This included a custom software for data acquisition and storage. 
In the current research this software was adapted to meet the specific requirements, including custom-
ized sampling rates and visualisation tools suited for tracer test analysis. 
To investigate flow direction and identify preferential pathways or stagnant zones, tracer experiments 
were carried out, first as pulse and then as step injection. Step injection ensured that the tracer signal 
would be detected by the sensors, thus enabling conductivity readings to serve as proxies for water 
movement. This data was then visualised through heatmaps of the longitudinal sections of the wetland. 
Results from the tracer experiments and corresponding heatmaps revealed that, in the section where 
the point inlet is located, flow initially moves downward to the base of the unit before shifting horizontally 
along the bottom. This bottom flow pathway is characteristic of severe short-circuiting, as evidenced by 
mean detention times that were 70% to 90% lower than the nominal residence time. The tracer effec-
tively bypassed much of the wetland and its sensor array, reappearing only at the perforated outlet at 
the opposite end in most cases or sometimes as a stratification layer at the bottom of the unit. 
 
Tracer test results, specifically those with light weight expanded clay, strongly suggest the presence of 
short-circuiting along the bottom of the wetland. To verify this hypothesis, future modifications to the 
pilot unit could include the addition or relocation of sensors to be specifically positioned along the wet-
land base. It would also be beneficial to install sensors at different depths near the cross-sectional area 
where sensors 1, 2, and 3 are currently located, particularly beneath them, close to the bottom, to better 
capture vertical and horizontal flow dynamics. 
 
Further analysis of tracer-based hydraulic parameters indicated that both hydraulic and volumetric effi-
ciencies were low across most experiments. An exception was observed in the step injection test using 
gravel, which demonstrated satisfactory hydraulic efficiency. However, additional testing would be re-
quired to determine whether gravel or lightweight expanded clay offers superior hydraulic performance 
in the system. 
While dispersion was small, a more critical issue was the presence of extensive dead zones regions of 
the wetland that do not effectively contribute to treatment. These inefficiencies could potentially be mit-
igated through design improvements, particularly by increasing the aspect ratio (length-to-width) and 
modifying inlet and outlet configurations. Features such as flow-distributing islands, baffle systems, or 
perforated inlet pipes could be implemented to promote more uniform flow distribution and reduce dead 
volume.

# --- Step 5: Interpolation grid --- 
grid_x, grid_y = np.mgrid[0:200:200j, 0:60:100j] 
# --- Step 6: Process frames for all sections --- 
section_frames = {} 
vmin, vmax = np.inf, -np.inf 
for section, sensors in section_sensors.items(): 
   positions = pd.DataFrame(sensors, columns=["Sensor"]) 
   positions["X_cm"], positions["Z_cm"] = zip(*xz_coords) 
   pos_array = positions[["X_cm", "Z_cm"]].values 
   frames = [] 
   for _, row in df.iterrows(): 
       values = np.array([row[sensor] for sensor in sensors]) 
       # 1. Interpolate with linear method (less prone to edge artifacts) 
       grid_linear = griddata(pos_array, values, (grid_x, grid_y), 
method='linear', fill_value=np.nan) 
       # 2. Fill remaining NaNs with nearest-neighbor 
       grid_nearest = griddata(pos_array, values, (grid_x, grid_y), 
method='nearest', fill_value=np.nan) 
 
        # 3. Combine: linear where possible, nearest only where linear fails 
        grid_combined = np.where(np.isnan(grid_linear), grid_nearest, 
grid_linear) 
 
        # 4. (Optional) Mask grid points outside the convex hull of the sensor 
locations 
        # This avoids interpolating in regions with no nearby sensors 
        points_flat = np.c_[grid_x.ravel(), grid_y.ravel()] 
        hull = Delaunay(pos_array).find_simplex(points_flat) >= 0 
        mask = hull.reshape(grid_x.shape) 
        grid_combined[~mask] = np.nan  # Set values outside hull to NaN 
 
        # 5. Smooth final result (optional but improves appearance) 
        smoothed = gaussian_filter(np.nan_to_num(grid_combined, 
nan=np.nanmin(values)), sigma=2) 
        frames.append(smoothed) 
        vmin = min(vmin, np.nanmin(smoothed)) 
        vmax = max(vmax, np.nanmax(smoothed)) 
 
    section_frames[section] = { 
        "positions": pos_array, 
        "frames": frames 
    }
