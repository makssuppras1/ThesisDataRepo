Abstract 
 
Fish meal is the preferred protein source when building a fish feed due to 
its amino acid profile and protein, lipid and mineral content. Nevertheless, it is 
an expensive ingredient with little to no sustainability, driving nutritionists to 
look for alternative replacements. Plant-based protein sources are the most 
commonly used when supplementing or replacing fishmeal, yet their amino acid 
profile is not as promising as fish meal’s is. Another alternative is to used waste 
by-products originating from the fisheries sector and processing facilities. 
Shrimp waste meal is an ingredient that shows potential as an alternative protein 
source, but its chitin content worries many producers. In this thesis, the 
evaluation of the performance of shrimp waste meal was made, when replacing 
plant based-protein sources. A control feed was designed (R1) and three other 
experimental feeds were built where SWM replaced soybean meal and pea meal 
25% (R2), 50% (R3) and 100% (R4). These feeds were administered to adult Nile 
Tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) and the feed’s performance was measured by 
analysing growth parameters, digestibility and chitinase activity. Overall, no 
statistical differences were found in growth between the fish fed the different 
feeds, allowing the complete replacement of plant-based protein sources to be 
made by SWM, without any changes in feed performance. The digestibility trial 
achieved the same result, portraying no difference in digestibility of dry matter, 
protein, lipid, nitrogen-free extract and ash. β-(1,4) N-acetylglucosaminidase 
activity also showed no difference between the intestines of the fish that were fed 
the different experimental feeds. 
 
 
Keywords: Plant-based protein sources, substitution, shrimp waste meal, 
Oreochromis niloticus, growth, digestibly and chitinase activity.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Aquaculture 
Aquaculture is an ever-growing industry that tries to accommodate to the 
high demand for feed and the need of different protein sources worldwide. In 
2018, aquaculture production was at an all-time high reaching 114,5 million 
tonnes in live weight, where 82,1 million tonnes corresponded to aquatic animals 
and 51,3 million tonnes came from inland aquaculture, accounting for 62,5% of 
the world’s farmed food fish production (FAO, 2020). According to the Food and 
Agriculture Organization (FAO), aquaculture has been growing steadily at an 
average annual rate of 6–7% and now contributes to over half of the fish 
produced for human consumption (Bush and Oosterveer, 2019). As expected, an 
increase in aquaculture practices would directly correspond to a higher demand 
for fish feed. 
In response to said demand, nations worldwide have been striving for better-
developed and highly efficient feeds, as well as its manufacturing procedures and 
technological methods (FAO, 2012).  
 
1.2. Fish Feed 
Good nutrition is the key to achieving an economical production of a healthy, 
high-quality animal. In aquaculture, the development of species-specific diet 
formulations promote affordability, security and high-quality fish and seafood 
products. Although, it is empirical that the diets possess the essential nutritional 
requirements necessary for each fish growth, meaning they must include the 
necessary contents of protein, amino acids, lipids, carbohydrates, vitamins, 
minerals and energy. 
The most important and expensive component in fish feed is protein (Toshio 
et al., 1997; Craig and Helfrich, 2017). Requirements in protein depend on the 
fish species and its life stage. The more advanced the fish is in its life stage and 
the bigger it gets, the less it requires protein (Mjoun et al., 2010). It is important 
to not go over the fish’s necessities to reduce costs. In aquaculture, protein levels 
in feed tend to vary from 18 to 50% with most produced species such as shrimp 
requiring 30 to 35%, catfish about 28 to 32%, tilapia about 25 to 40% and trout 
and other marine fish requiring 40 to 45% (Craig and Helfrich, 2017). Overall, 
herbivorous and omnivorous fish tend to require less protein than carnivorous 
fish. 
==PAGE:15==
2 
 
Proteins are composed of amino acids, which are released for absorption into 
the blood following protein digestion (Miles and Chapman, 2006). It is specific 
amino acids that are required by the animal, which will build proteins from 
combinations of about 22 amino acids, even though it cannot make all of them 
in its body (Miles and Chapman, 2006). The only way of getting amino acids that 
are not synthesized by the animal is through the feed, making them "essential". 
Ten essential amino acids (EAA) that must be found in fish diets are arginine, 
histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, 
tryptophan and valine (Webster and Lim, 2002). Of these lysine and methionine 
cannot be heavily supplied by incomplete proteins (plant source), making them 
limiting amino acids (Gorissen et al., 2018). 
Lipids are the best source of energy, when comparing to the energy density in 
proteins and carbohydrates (Webster and Lim, 2002). In fish feeds, lipids tend 
to range from 7 to 15%, supplying essential fatty acids, and serving as 
transporters for fat-soluble vitamins (Craig and Helfrich, 2017). Fatty acids are 
the key component to all lipids, which out of all of them, the omega-3 and -6 (n-
3 and n-6) families are those that fish require the most. All fatty acids can be 
categorized by their number of double bonds, such as saturated (no double 
bonds), polyunsaturated (>2 double bonds), or highly unsaturated (>4 double 
bonds) (Webster and Lim, 2002; Craig and Helfrich, 2017). Linolenic acid is an 
18-carbon n-3 essential fatty acid for freshwater fish, meaning it cannot be 
produced by freshwater fish, making it necessary to be included in their diet in 
quantities of 0,5 to 2%. With the help of enzyme systems, freshwater fish can 
elongate and desaturate linolenic acid resulting in longer-chain omega-3 fatty 
acids EPA and DHA. These are required for many metabolic functions in addition 
to being cellular membrane components. On the other hand, marine fish do not 
possess said elongation and desaturation enzyme systems and require long-
chain omega-3 fatty acids in their diets in quantities ranging from 0,5 to 2%. 
Therefore, marine fish require different essential fatty acids from freshwater fish, 
these being eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA: 20:5n-3) and docosahexaenoic acid 
(DHA: 22:6n-3) (Craig and Helfrich, 2017). 
For most land animals, carbohydrates are a major source of energy, although 
the same cannot be said for fish. Even between fish the utilization of 
carbohydrates varies, with warmwater fish being able to utilize much higher 
levels of dietary carbohydrate than coldwater and marine fish (Wilson, 1994). 
Carbohydrates (starches and sugars) are divided into three main categories: 
monosaccharides 
(simple 
sugars), 
oligosaccharides 
(disaccharides, 
trisaccharides and tetrasaccharides) and polysaccharides (large sugar complexes 
containing repeating chains of monosaccharides) (Webster and Lim, 2002).  The 
==PAGE:16==
3 
 
latter is even subdivided into pentosans, hexosans and mixed polysaccharides 
(ex.: chitin). These sugars and starches are stored as glycogen that can be 
mobilized to satisfy energy demands, containing 17,2kJ of gross energy per gram 
and are the least expensive dietary source, being extremely relevant for 
herbivorous and omnivorous fish (Webster and Lim, 2002).  In addition, the 
inclusion of carbohydrates in aquaculture feeds reduces feed costs and 
contributes to the binding activity occurring during feed manufacturing. 
Generally, carbohydrate inclusion falls between 15 to 25% of most aquaculture 
feeds for salmonids and marine fish, whereas carbohydrates inclusion in feeds 
for herbivorous and omnivorous fish can go up to 50% (NRC, 2011). 
Other compounds necessary for fish are vitamins (organic) and minerals 
(inorganic).  Vitamins help on fish growth and fish health and since they are 
often not synthesized by fish their inclusion in diets is crucial. Minerals are 
divided into macrominerals and microminerals, relative to dietary requirements. 
Macrominerals contribute to the regulation of osmotic balance and aid in bone 
formation and integrity, whereas microminerals are used as components in 
enzyme and hormone systems (Craig and Helfrich, 2017). Both vitamins and 
minerals can be found in trace amounts in fish feed.  
 
1.3. Fish meal 
Fish meal is ubiquitous in feed manufacturing. In 2019, it was reported that 
in almost every year, one-fifth of the world’s wild-caught fish is transformed into 
fishmeal and fish oils, which are implemented into feeds for many different 
species of fish and crustaceans (FAO, 2018). Furthermore, in aquaculture the 
production of fishmeal is about 5 million tonnes each year, deriving mainly from 
fish by-products that contribute to up to 35% of the total volume of fishmeal and 
fish oil (FAO, 2020). 
This excessive focus in fishmeal production is due to its nutritional 
composition. Fishmeal has a very high level of protein, between 60% and 72% 
crude protein by weight (Miles and Chapman, 2006), as well as energy and 
minerals such as calcium and phosphorus. It is also a natural source of vitamins 
such as choline, biotin and vitamin A, B12, D and E (Cho and Kim, 2010). The 
main component that distinguishes fishmeal from all the other protein sources 
is its content of EAA. 
The lack of sustainability is what undermines this ingredient. It has been 
predicted that with the increase in demand for aquaculture, availability will have 
a noticeable decrease, consecutively increasing the price on an already pricy 
==PAGE:17==
4 
 
material (Tacon and Metian, 2008). It is facts like this that are driving 
researchers and nutritionists to look for alternative protein sources. 
  
1.4. Plant-based protein sources 
When looking for an alternative protein source it is important to consider the 
ingredient’s protein content alongside its EAAs profile. Plant-based protein 
sources are some of the more reoccurring solutions when trying to lessen the 
use of fishmeal in a diet, due to being cost-effective and nutritionally valuable 
protein sources in shrimp and fish feeds (Guo et al., 2018). The most commonly 
used amongst them is soybean products (Hasan and Tan, 2020). Soybean-based 
products by themselves or in combination with other plant-based ingredients, 
such as vegetable blends, seem to show positive results, when trying to reduce 
fishmeal inclusion in diets (Catacutan, 2017). A noticeable disadvantage to using 
these products is the market instability. Alongside many other plant-based 
protein sources, they have many other industrial uses that can increase demand 
and consequently, increase their price. In addition, soybean cultivation demands 
large quantities of water to promote optimal growth, diminishing and degrading 
local water quality in the process. The use of agrochemicals such as pesticides 
and fertilisers result in the indirect contamination of surrounding water sources 
such as rivers and estuaries (FAO, 2020), posing a big threat to the environment 
and biodiversity, as well as health risks to local populations that habit said 
bodies of water. 
The biggest disadvantage to using protein sources that originate from cereal 
grains and other plant concentrates is the lack of a complete amino acid profile, 
where usually lysine and methionine, two of the essential amino acids cannot 
fulfil the necessary requirements for fish (Gorissen et al., 2018). Soybean meal 
together with other legume meals are good sources of the amino acids lysine and 
tryptophan (Cho and Kim, 2010). Although they have limited sulphur-containing 
amino acids such as methionine (EAA) and cystine, which have a huge role in 
the animals’ growth development and resistance to numerous diseases (Gaines 
et al., 2005). On the other hand, fish meal contains a high level of sulphur-
containing essential amino acids, such as methionine and lysine, making the 
replacement by plant-based protein sources inefficient (Cho and Kim, 2010).  
Furthermore, plant-based ingredients tend to have several limitations and 
challenges when included into aquaculture diets, such as the presence of anti-
nutritional factors (ANFs), fatty acid and mineral composition (when compared 
to fishmeal, fish oils and other animal-based products), palatability and 
==PAGE:18==
5 
 
mycotoxin contents (Hasan and Tan, 2020). Their inclusion may lead to slower 
growth rate, a negative effect in the animal’s health status and, in extreme cases, 
possibly increasing mortality rate.  
The most damaging mycotoxins that affect fish growth are produced by 
Aspergillus, Penicillium and Fusarium species, with Fusarium mycotoxins being 
the most common in plant-based ingredients followed by aflatoxins (Gonçalves 
et al., 2017). Two commonly used ingredients in aquaculture feeds that possess 
a high level of contaminants are corn gluten meal and corn distiller's dried grains 
with solubles (DDGS). Said contaminants are Deoxynivalenol (DON) and 
Fumonisins (FUM) (Gonçalves et al., 2017). Both mycotoxins show adverse 
effects in aquaculture, with rainbow trout being one of the most sensitive species. 
FUM induce changes in the sphingolipid metabolism even when contamination 
levels are lower than 100 μg/kg (Meredith et al., 1998), inducing cancer in one-
month old trout (Riley et al., 2001). 
 
1.5. Shrimp Waste meal 
Another approach could be to use animal waste products that seem to have 
little to no economic value or use in any industry, ending up discarded most 
often. Shrimp waste meal is one of those ingredients that was formulated with 
that exact idea in mind.  Being a by-product of the shrimp industry, shrimp 
waste meal can consist of whole marine or freshwater shrimps and/or shrimp 
parts such as the head, viscera, and exoskeleton (Kandra et al., 2011). 
Shrimp waste availability is almost endless, since crustacean aquaculture is 
the largest saltwater seafood production sector around the globe (FAO, 2020). 
With shrimp and shrimp products being considered as a delicacy all over the 
world, their demand increases yearly. In 2020, global shrimp production reached 
5,03 million tons and it is expected to reach 7,28 million tons by 2025 with 
compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 6,1% from 2020 to 2025 (IMARC, 2020). 
To bolster the previous claim, the shrimp market turnover value is assumed to 
reach 67,6 billion US dollars by the end of 2025 (Marketstudyreport, 2019, p. 
102), proving that shrimp aquaculture production has no signs of slowing down. 
When marketing shrimp, they are normally stored and exported frozen with 
or without shell, depending on the market demand. In shrimp processing, 
approximately 50–60% of solid waste is generated as by-products (Senphan and 
Benjakul, 2012). Furthermore, shrimp and prawn catches recorded new highs 
in 2017 and 2018 at over 336 000 tonnes (FAO, 2020). In fisheries alone, around 
70% of total shrimp landings become waste (Göhl, 1982), adding to the already 
==PAGE:19==
6 
 
astonishing total generated from other industries. Its disposal signifies a great 
loss of valuable bioactive components, some of which are very important for its 
inclusion in aquaculture feed. 
Studies confirm that shrimp by-products can reach 50,72% of protein in case 
of head silage and 46,2% in case of shrimp shell meal. It is its amino acid 
composition that makes shrimp waste meal a protein source with such high 
potential for fish growth and development. Shrimp by-products contain a big 
presence of amino acids some of which are essential amino acids such as 
methionine with 4,35% and 0,65%, lysine with 6,98% and 6,19%, arginine with 
19,22% and 5,89%, phenylalanine with 6,71% and 2,83%, valine with 4,22% 
and 3,10%, isoleucine with 1,21% and 3,37%, tryptophan with 8,19% and 5,77% 
and histidine with 6,50% and 4,4% in head and shell shrimp, respectively 
(Belandria and Morillo, 2013). 
In addition to its protein contents, shrimp head silage may also reach 12,03% 
of lipids with shrimp shell meal only reaching 1,13%. Other valuable bioactive 
compounds found in shrimp waste meal include chitin/chitosan (Paul et al., 
2015), minerals (Gómez-Estaca et al., 2019), vitamins (Nair et al., 2017), 
enzymes (Senphan et al., 2014) and pigments (Sila et al., 2015) such as the 
carotenoid astaxanthin with 38,59 (shell) and 55,71μg/g (head), which promotes 
health benefits. The incongruous factor that prevents SWM from being 
implemented in every aquaculture feed is its high level of chitin, which is not 
readily digestible (Göhl, 1982).  
Chitin is a mucopolysaccharide that consists of N-acetyl-β-D-glucosamine 
(GlcNAc) linked by β-1,4-glycosidic bonds (Lindsay et al., 1984).

It is the second most abundant carbohydrate in nature, present in the 
exoskeleton of crustaceans, insects, fungi, yeast, and algae (Bhattacharya et al., 
2008). In aquatic environments alone, 10 billion tons of this compound are 
==PAGE:20==

produced annually (Keyhani and Roseman, 1999). Despite its tremendous 
production, chitin is rarely utilized in animal feeds due to its complex cross-
linked molecular structure. Chitin has a caloric value of about 17,1 kJ/g and 
the chitinous exoskeleton of some arthropods can contain over 20% of the 
available energy (Karasov, 1989; Gutowska et al., 2004). Thus, when utilized, it 
is likely that chitin might have a significant contribution towards energy intake 
and as a nitrogen source (Danulat, 1987; Krogdahl et al., 2005).  
The most frequent method of adding chitin to fish diets is by doing 
degradation 
with 
the 
help 
of 
chemicals, 
transforming 
it 
into 
Chitooligosaccharides (COS), N-Acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc), or chitosan (Lin et 
al., 2012a, b; Shiau and Yu, 1999). However, the use of said chemical methods 
come with a high cost, as well as low yield, residual acidity, and serious 
environmental pollution (Prashanth and Tharanathan, 2007). Thus, in order for 
chitin to be administered to fish in their feed, they would need to possess the 
enzyme system necessary to be able to perform its digestion.  
Chitinases or chitinolytic enzymes are enzymes which catalyse the random 
hydrolysis of β-1,4-glycoside bonds between GlcNac-units of chitin and 
chitooligomers (Muzzarelli, 1993; Stoykov et al., 2015). These are divided into 
two major categories based on their functional characteristics, endochitinases 
and exo-chitinases. Endochitinases (E.C.3.2.1.14) cleave chitin at random 
internal β-1,4-glycoside bonds, generating the dimer diacetylchitobiose and low 
molecular mass multimers such as chitotriose and chitotetraose in the process 
(Dahiya et al., 2006; Hamid et al., 2013). In the case of exo-chitinases, these are 
further subdivided into chitobiosidase and β-(1,4) N-acetyl glucosaminidases 
(Dahiya et al., 2006; Hamid et al., 2013). Chitobiosidases (EC 3.2.1.30) catalyse 
the progressive release of diacetylchitobiose starting at the non-reducing end of 
chitin (Cohen-Kupiec and Chet, 1998; Hamid et al., 2013), whereas the β-(1,4) 
N-acetyl glucosaminidases (NAGase) (E.C. 3.2.1.29) cleave the end-products of 
endochitinases and chitobiosidases, resulting in monomers of GlcNac (Cohen-
Kupiec and Chet, 1998; Dahiya et al., 2006). This enzymatic hydrolysis of chitin 
to N-acetylglucosamine units is often referred to as the chitinolytic system 
(Jeuniaux, 1993). 
According to the Enzyme Nomenclature Committee of the International Union 
of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology, 
chitobiosidases and N-acetyl 
glucosaminidases, together with other enzymes capable of hydrolysing N-
acetylglucosides and N-acetylgalactosides from the non-reducing end, are 
included into a group named the β-N-acetylhexosaminidases (E.C. 3.2.1.52) 
(Patil et al., 2000). 
==PAGE:21==
8 
 
Even though the role of chitinases is not well determined in digestion, two 
major roles are often portrayed in literature, a disruptive role or a nutritive role, 
varying from species to species. In the chemical disruption role, chitinases 
facilitate the disruption of prey and mechanical breakdown in the stomach prior 
to the passage into the intestine. In the nutritive role of the chitinases, they 
facilitate the enzymatic degradation of chitin into its monomer GlcNac, which is 
subsequently absorbed and serves as an energy source (Lundblad et al., 1979; 
Jeuniaux, 1993; Karasov and Hume, 2011). In order to do so, the fish must 
possess a complete chitinolytic system, this being the presence of endochitinase 
and NAGase in sufficient quantities in its digestive tract (stomach, midgut and 
hindgut) in order to achieve a total break-down of chitin (Jeuniaux, 1993; 
Karasov and Hume, 2011). Most chitinases in fish can be found in the stomach, 
where they degrade chitin into chito-oligomers, although NAGases are mainly 
found further down the digestive tract, in the midgut and/or hindgut (Fänge and 
Grove, 1979; Clark et al., 1988; Jeuniaux, 1993; Gutowska et al., 2004; Abro et 
al., 2014). 
A more predominant utilization of shrimp waste in aquaculture feeds would 
promote not just economic benefits, but ecological benefits as well. Shrimp waste 
dumping is one of the illegal practices made to dispose of this by-product, not 
realizing how big of a potential shrimp waste has for other industries. In the 
main countries where this practice is predominant, the climate is tropical which 
expedites the decaying to start within an hour after processing, leading to the 
production of biogenic amines, which have a very offensive and potent smell 
(Kandra et al., 2011). If shrimp waste dumping continues to happen and its 
practices cannot be stopped, this biomaterial will turn into a real threat due to 
its high protein content, originating very negative side effects to the environment 
and a financial burden if not discarded accordingly.

Some studies have been made where shrimp waste meal was present in the 
feed composition. Nandini et al. (2014) conducted a study to determine the 
effects of total replacement of fishmeal with shrimp waste meal in the diet of koi 
carp. In it, they concluded that shrimp waste meal could be used to replace 
fishmeal up to a maximum of 50% in the diet of koi carp, achieving better results 
in growth rate and pigmentation. In 2020, Fall et al. did a study on trying to 
determine the effect of crustacean meal (Callianassa-a genus of mud shrimp) as 
a replacement for fish meal in the diet of mixed-sex Nile tilapia (Oreochromis 
niloticus) fry. It revealed that a 50% replacement with crustacean meal provided 
better feed conversion ratio (FCR) and that a 25% replacement of fishmeal in diet 
had the same effect as 100% fishmeal for Nile tilapia. Another study was made 
for carp by Al-Jader and Al-Khshali in 2021, where they studied the effect of 
using different levels of shrimp meals as a partial substitute for the protein 
concentrate in the diets of common carp (Cyprinus carpio) on growth 
performance. They observed that shrimp meal at 32% in the diet of common carp 
provided superior overall results, improving the growth parameters of common 
carp. 
The aim of this project was to test the possibility of using shrimp waste meal 
as a protein source in practical diets for Nile tilapia at 4 different inclusion levels. 
It was tested as a replacement for the plant-based protein sources in order to 
evaluate the fish´s optimal growth. In addition to the feeding trial, a digestibility 
trial was made in order to evaluate the performance of the absorption of 
nutrients. Chitinase activity was also measured to try to note a difference in 
enzymatic activity.

2.2. Producing the feed 
2.2.1. 
Ingredients 
Both fishmeal (FF CLASSIC) and fish oil (FF GOLDEN OIL) were obtained from 
FF Skagen Denmark, where two 5kg bags of fishmeal were ordered, as well as a 
5L tub of fish oil. These were stored in a fridge a 4 ºC until they were used in the 
making of the feed. Other protein sources (plant-based) were ordered from 
Aminola, a company located in the Netherlands that specifies in making high-
grade vegetable ingredients. For soybean meal, one 10kg bag was sent of the SOY 
PROTEIN CONCENTRATE 60 and for the pea meal, five 2kg bags were sent of 
the PEA PROTEIN CONCENTRATE 55. Each ingredient formula was chosen 
according to its nutritional components. 
Ingredients such as wheat bran, wheat flour and corn starch were picked up 
at the local supermarket.  The wheat bran came in two 500g bags of the 
Valsemøllen brand, for the wheat flour two 2kg bags were bought from the AMO 
brand and eleven 500g boxes were gotten from the local supermarket brand 
(REMA). The vitamins and Minerals Premix was provided by Biomar. The 
powdered cellulose was purchased from Sigma Aldrich. 
All the ingredients came already in powder form apart from the shrimp waste 
meal. The shrimp waste (Pandalus borealis) was provided by a local processing 
facility (Launis A/S), dried in an oven at 60ºC and stored in a freezer at -20ºC, 
until the start of the feed production. It was micronized in a lab, using Ultra 
Centrifugal Mill ZM 200 (Retsch). This high-speed rotor mill converted all the 
shrimp waste into 0,12mm particles or smaller. Once it was all converted into a 
fine powder, it would be stored back in the freezer at -20ºC.  
 
2.2.2. 
Feed Production 
Each ingredient was weighed with a digital scale with a degree of precision of 
0,01g. The corn starch and the wheat flour were weighed in a separate container 
from the rest of the ingredients. The contents on each container were mixed 
either by hand or with an electric mixer. In a pot, 2L of water (1L water/ 400g of 
==PAGE:26==
13 
 
flour and starch) were added, since 2kg of each feed was made in every batch. 
Wheat flour and corn starch were added next to the pot and the pot was heated 
in order to start the gelatinization process. 
Gelatinization is the process of breaking down the inter-molecular bond 
present in starch by applying heat and water (Fu et al., 2013). Starch is a 
semicrystalline material (Waigh et al., 1997) and its granules are insoluble in 
cold water which, when heated, they absorb water and swell (Donovan, 1979). 
Continuous heating melts the crystallites and allows granules to swell to a 
greater extent, resulting in increased molecular motion that eventually leads to 
complete separation of amylose and amylopectin (Donald, 2001). The separation 
of the amylose molecules gives enzymes access to glycosidic linkage and 
consequently increase digestion (Svihus et al., 2005). 
With better digestibility, protein and energy retention would also be 
significantly improved since gelatinized starch helps reduce the amount of 
digestible protein and digestible energy needed for growth (Peres and Olivia-
Teles, 2002). This process also facilitates the binding between the ingredients in 
the feed making, improving the quality of the feed (Adhikari and Adhikari, 2015). 
The pot was constantly stirred and heated up until the mixture was 
homogenous and gelatinized. Constant stirring was done to also prevent the 
starch from burning at the bottom of the pot. The best consistency was achieved 
at a temperature of 85ᵒC. Once said temperature was reached, the gelatinized 
starch would cool down at room temperature for about 20 minutes, or until it 
reached 50ᵒC. At that point the rest of the ingredients would be added and mixed 
with the help of an electric mixer. The resulting paste was passed through a meat 
grinder (without inserting the rotating knife) and the spaghetti like filaments of 
feed were spread and placed in different trays. These trays were placed in an 
oven at 40ᵒC for 72 hours in order to dry the mixture. Drying was done for 72h 
instead of 48h since in those last 24h about 3 grams of water weight would be 
lost, corresponding to about 1% of the initial weight (figure 3). Every 24h the 
strands were broken apart and flipped to facilitate the drying process.

numbers of five and placed into one of 12 cylindro-conical tanks (modified 
Guelph type system) with a volume of 135L each and continuous aeration. The 
fish were equally distributed with no significant difference between the total 
biomass of the twelve tanks (figure 4).

The fish were maintained at 26°C on a 12 h:12 h light cycle where both the

Tank cleaning was done daily using a brush and a sponge attached to a 
magnet for cleaning the inside walls of the tanks. Ammonia and Nitrite levels 
were also monitored and tested daily with the use of test kits. 
An appropriate estimation of how much the fish would need to be fed was 
made, corresponding to 3% of their total initial biomass (per tank) (Skov et al. 
2017). This amount was kept unchanged, and it was fed daily throughout the 
experiment (Table VI – Appendix). 
The fish were fed twice a day, once in the morning (between 9AM and 11AM) 
and once in the afternoon (between 3PM and 5PM), for 36 days. Since the pellets 
sank, the pellets that were not eaten right away were collected and fed to the fish 
until all of them had been ingested. 
  Weighings were planned for the end of the first, third and last week but 
handling had to be reduced to a minimum to cause less stress to the fish and 
prevent death in the middle of the trial. 
 
2.4. Faeces collection 
The faeces collection was done in the last 10 days of the feeding trial. A 
settling column was attached to the bottom of each tank and under it, was a 
Styrofoam box filled with ice and cold water that was renovated once per day. 
This was done in order to prevent degradation. Once disattached, the media was 
poured into a bottle associated to the respective tank. This collection was done 
once every day, from 9 to 10 AM, before feeding.  
After the media collection, the bottles would be carried into a freezer where 
the temperature was -20ᵒC. The bottles would only leave said freezer on the next 
day for the next collection. Two periods were performed, with each bottle 
containing media from 5 days of trial (Dalsgaard and Pedersen, 2011). However, 
only the first period (first five days) would end up being analysed. 
==PAGE:30==

Once the collection was finalized, the faecal samples were thawed, and 
gross composition analysed (protein, lipid, ash and dry matter). The same 
protocol was followed as the one done for the feed nutritional analysis.  
 
2.5. Fish Dissection 
The dissection of the fish was done on the day after the trial had finished. The 
fish were fed on the same morning according to their appetite, around 6 AM. The 
dissecting of fish started between 3 to 4 hours later. Six fish were taken from 
each feed to be analysed, corresponding to two fish being taken from some tanks 
and 3 from other tanks to compensate for the disregarded tanks. 
To help with the fish catching and reduce stress in fish, half of the water from 
the tanks was emptied out. The fish were collected individually and randomly, 
with a net and placed in a bucket with Benzocaine, the anaesthetic solution. 
Each tank was done separately, only filling up the bucket with two or three fish 
each time. Contrary to the weighing, the concentration of the anaesthetic was 
4ml to 10L of water, in order to humanely kill the fish. Once the fish had 
responded accordingly to the effect of the solution, they were placed in a bucket 
with ice and cold water and taken into the dissection station counter. 
Here, the fish were collected from the ice bucket and its intestines would be 
isolated, cleaned and placed into Eppendorf tubes. These Eppendorf tubes were 
named according to the fish and its fish tank and later stored in a Styrofoam box 
filled with dry ice. Each fish was dissected individually, and fish would only be 
captured after each tank was finished.

intestinal samples were homogenised using a tissue tearor and diluted 1:8. In 
order to retrieve the supernatant from the samples, they were centrifuged at 
4248g for 15 minutes at 4ᵒC. Once centrifuged, 2ml of the supernatant were 
collected and placed in Eppendorf tubes. The supernatant was collected twice 
more from the Eppendorf tubes, first 1ml and after 0,5ml. Before each collection 
both the 2ml and 1ml samples were centrifuged at 13000g for 10 minutes at 4ᵒC. 
During the analysis, samples were diluted once more where 10µl of the 
sample would be diluted and homogenised in 490µl distilled water. A fluorogenic 
substrate was prepared by diluting 4-methylumbelliferyl N-acetyl-β-D-
glucosaminide (M2133, Sigma-Aldrich) in phosphate-citrate buffer (pH=5,2) to a 
final concentration of 0,2 mg/ml. In a black flat-bottom 96-well plate, 5µl of the 
diluted sample was added followed by the addition of 95µl diluted fluorogenic 
substrate. The plate was incubated at 37ºC for 15 min followed by the addition 
of 200µl glycine-NaOH buffer (pH=10,6), and fluorescence was measured on a 
plate reader (CLARIOstar, BMG Labtech, Germany) applying an excitation 
wavelength of 360nm and an emission wavelength of 450nm.  
Enzyme activity was calculated using a standard curve treated similarly as 
the diluted crude enzyme extracts with 4-methylumbelliferone (M1381, Sigma-
Aldrich) dissolved in glycine-NaOH buffer (pH=10,6) at different concentrations, 
and replacing the diluted fluorogenic substrate with phosphate-citrate buffer 
(pH=5,2). One unit of enzyme activity (U) was defined as the amount of enzyme 
needed to release 1µmole of 4-methylumbelliferone per minute, expressed per 
gram of wet tissue.

4. Discussion 
When analysing the weight gained after the 36-day feeding trial there were no 
statistical differences between the data. Meaning the replacement of plant-based 
protein sources by shrimp waste meal showed no differences in weight gain up 
to 100%. However, there is a tendency on fish to gain more weight when using 
5% shrimp waste meal with 15% plant-based protein sources and a tendency on 
fish to gain less weight when replacing the plant-based ingredients by shrimp 
waste meal completely. 
Regarding the SGR, similar to the weight gain, no statistically significant 
differences were found between the feeds. This result is similar to the study made 
by Fall et al. (2012) where the effects of replacing soybean meal by shrimp shell 
meal (SSM) on the growth of hybrid tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus x Oreochromis 
aureus) were tested. In it they built feeds where 90% of the protein content (30% 
overall feed) came from soybean meal and would be replaced by SSM. Up to 67% 
replacement of the total soybean meal showed no statistically significant 
differences between the SGR of the other contents measured (0, 33 and 50%), 
with only the 100% replacement having statistically significant differences. In 
the present study 100% replacement of plant-based protein sources by SWM had 
no difference in performance when compared to the Fall et al. (2012) study 
perhaps due to the difference in fish meal content found in the feeds.  
When testing the inclusion on SWM on tilapia fry feed, Diop et al. (2013) was 
able to find significance in SGR in the fish fed the different feeds. The highest 
SGR achieved was 3,79%/day from tilapia fry fed with a diet containing 45% 
SWM. Leading to the conclusion that the replacement of protein sources by 
shrimp by-products varies from ingredient to ingredient. Shrimp waste meal is a 
better substitute for ingredients that share both the same or lower protein 
content and amino acid composition. 
For Nile Tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus), reported FCR values vary widely 
among studies, ranging from 1,5 to 2,5 in pond environments and from 1,0 to 
1,71 in cage environments (Rana and Hassan, 2013). Thoa et al. (2016) reported 
FCR values of 1,08 and 1,89 in freshwater and saline water pond environments, 
respectively. Overall, an FCR below 2,0 is considered acceptable (Craig and 
Helfrich, 2017) making feeds R2 and R3 potential commercial feeds (depending 
on the price). The same cannot be said for the control feed built and R4 which 
both had an FCR above 2,0. Either way, in terms of feed conversion ratio, all 
feeds performed the same, cementing the hypothesis of replacing plant-based 
protein sources up to 100%. Fall et al. (2020) was able to find that a 50% 
==PAGE:38==
25 
 
replacement of fish meal with crustacean meal provided better feed conversion 
ratio (FCR) with 25% replacement having no statistical differences from the 
control diet (no crustacean meal). 
According to Mengistu et al. (2019), the parameter that affects the FCR the 
most is temperature, with the best FCR being achieved between 27 and 32ᵒC for 
Nile Tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus). In this experiment, water temperature was 
maintained constant at 26ᵒC, although fluctuations occurred periodically since 
intake water was at 5ᵒC. To improve the FCR from all feeds, temperatures should 
have been raised and with less fluctuations. 
The data retrieved from the feeding trial indicated no significant differences 
in the protein efficiency ratios of the feeds. Nevertheless, the results were slightly 
lower to those published by Plascencia-Jatomea et al. (2002). In trying to replace 
fishmeal for shrimp head silage protein hydrolysate in Nile tilapia feed, 
Plascencia-Jatomea et al. (2002) also found no significance in the PER of 
fishmeal replacement up to 30% by shrimp silage protein hydrolysate. A 
relatively low PER was expected since protein efficiency ratio tends to decrease 
with the increase of fish weight (Hafedh, 2001). 
According to Skov et al. (2017) the ideal feed amount that should be 
administered daily to achieve an optimum growth rate and digestibility is 3 and 
4% of the total biomass of the tank. Since the amount of administered feed stayed 
the same throughout the experiment, by the end of it the 3% of the initial 
biomass corresponded to about 1,5% of the final biomass (varying on tank). With 
the decrease in feed amounts being fed to tilapia, energy derived from non-
protein sources such as fat and carbohydrates was no longer meeting tilapias’ 
daily requirement, forcing fish to rely in part on amino acid oxidation for fuel. 
The apparent digestibility coefficients calculated for all the nutrients were 
very similar to those achieved by ideal feeds. Köprücü and Özdemir (2005) 
studied the apparent digestibility of selected feed ingredients for Nile Tilapia. In 
order to do so they built an ideal feed according to the nutritional requirements 
established by Lovell (1989) and built other test feeds which included 70% of the 
reference feed plus 30% of the test ingredient. The ADCs in their ideal reference 
feed where 89% for dry matter, 87,1% for protein, 90,6% for lipids, 88,7% for 
nitrogen-free extracts and 63,5% for ash. All of the apparent digestibility 
coefficients calculated in this study are similar to those established by Köprücü 
and Özdemir (2005). Overall, no significant differences were found between the 
feeds, concluding that the chitin levels did not affect digestibility. 
==PAGE:39==
26 
 
Since there was no statistical significance between the data, it seems that the 
inclusion of shrimp waste meal as an ingredient did not alter the enzymatic 
activity of β-(1,4) N-acetyl glucosaminidases in the entire intestine. 
Endochitinase and chitobiosidase activity was also intended to be measured 
although, the substrates to perform such were not available. These results can 
also be justified by chitin contents being too low (max 2,69%) to note a difference 
between fish. In addition, the analysed fish differentiated a lot in size with the 
smallest fish ranging from 212g to the biggest 340g, since this analysis was done 
after the feeding trial and the fish were chosen randomly in each tank. 
Chitinase activity may also vary throughout the digestive tract. The arctic 
char (Salvelinus alpinus) is one on the fish where different chitinase activity has 
been reported along the gastrointestinal tract. Abro (2014) noted that the highest 
exo-chitinase activity was observed in the distal intestinal tissue of fish fed a 
chitin-rich diet and the lowest activity was found in the stomach tissues of fish 
fed a fish meal-based diet. In the case of endo-chitinase, the highest activity was 
reported to be in the stomach and the lowest in proximal intestine. Endo-
chitinase activity was also observed to me more active in the stomach tissue and 
in the distal intestine than the exo-chitinase activity in stomach tissue. 
Disturbed intestinal integrity and increased uptake rate of the amino acid lysine 
was also observed in the distal intestine of fish fed the chitin-rich feed. However, 
the same could not be stated for the proximal intestine of the arctic char. 
For future analysis, it would be interesting to divide the samples from 
proximal and distal intestine in order to try and observe if there is noticeable 
difference in chitinase activity between the different intestinal sections of Nile 
tilapia. The stomach is another organ that would be of interest when measuring 
chitinase activity, in addition to the intestine. A chemical analysis of the tilapias’ 
carcass after the feeding trial would have cemented even more the statements 
made about the performance of the experimental feeds, related to the fish 
composition.

5. Conclusion 
In this study, the use of shrimp waste meal as a potential replacement for 
plant-based protein sources proved to not differ statistically in terms of growth 
performance and digestibility. When complementing fishmeal, plant-based 
protein sources can be replaced up to 100% by SWM without observing a change 
in the development and survival of adult Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus). 
Chitin also proved to not have an impactful role in digestion and nutrient 
absorption when it represents up to 2,69% of the total feed. 
Further research is required to evaluate the influence of higher shrimp waste 
meal contents in feed for Nile tilapia, as well as other aquaculture species of 
interest.
