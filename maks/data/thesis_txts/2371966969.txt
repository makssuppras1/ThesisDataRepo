Abstract 
According to the hue-heat hypothesis, colours affect human perception of temperature, so 
the same environment may be perceived warmer for warm hues and colder for cold hues. If 
the hypothesis can be documented, the range of temperatures that building occupants find 
comfortable potentially can be expanded and lead to energy savings. The aim of this study 
was to investigate this potential association between the correlated colour temperature (CCT) 
of LED lighting and perceived temperature.  
The hypothesis was tested through experiments conducted at DTU where 44 human subjects 
were exposed to different operative temperatures and CCTs. The operative temperature was 
kept constant at either 19 °C, 22 °C or 27 °C during an entire experimental session. The 
illuminance level was kept at 1000 lux on the working plane, leaving the CCT as the only 
varying parameter during one experimental session changing from 2700 to 6200 to 2700 K 
or in a reverse order from 6200 to 2700 to 6200 K. The comfort level was evaluated through 
subjective questionnaires consisting of 19 questions where the subjects had to indicate their 
perception of the thermal, atmospheric, visual and acoustic environment. In addition, the 
subjects’ performance level was evaluated by the use of d2-tests.  
The results were analysed through summary statistics and more advanced statistic models in 
the software JMP. Contrary to anticipated, the association between the CCT and operative 
temperatures at 19 °C and 27 °C were not significant, but for 22 °C a significant difference 
of 0.42 on the nine-point scale was found when comparing the results for the extreme CCTs. 
Depending on the clothing- and activity level of the occupants, this corresponds to an 
approximate operative temperature difference of 2 °C confirming the hue-heat hypothesis. 
Furthermore, the air quality was perceived as being better and the lighting was perceived as 
being brighter when the CCT increased. No association between the CCT and the 
performance level was detected.  
It can be concluded that there was a significant association between the CCT of LED lighting 
and the thermal perception at operative temperatures in the middle of the thermal comfort 
temperature range.

It is a conventional idea that colours in the red end of the visual spectrum are defined as 
warm while colours in the blue end are defined as cold. According to the hue-heat 
hypothesis, this association even affects how people perceive their thermal environment. The 
hypothesis is supported by the fact that Nordic countries tend to use light sources with warm 
hues while countries closer to equator often use light sources with cold hues. If colour 
temperatures of lighting actually affect human perception of indoor temperatures, lighting 
might be a variable that expands the range of temperatures that building occupants find 
comfortable. Thus, a potential of energy savings exists if the hypothesis is confirmed.  
The aim of this project was to test the hypothesis that the CCT of LED lighting affects the 
perceived temperature. This was done through controlled human experiments carried out in 
a test room at the Technical University of Denmark.  
1.1 Context 
Every year, ELFORSK awards a total amount of 25 million Danish kroner (DKK) to support 
research and development that can lead to energy savings. This master thesis is a part of an 
ELFORSK supported project under the title “Warm or Cool? Lights influence on thermal 
comfort”. The project is a collaboration between DTU Civil Engineering, DTU Photonics 
Engineering and Aalborg University Copenhagen, the Danish Building Research Institute 
(AAU/SBi). It was initiated in March 2016 and will end in December 2018 and thus spans 2

1.2 Objective and delimitation 
The objective of the study was to quantify the association between the CCT of LED lighting 
and human thermal comfort and -performance. In terms of thermal comfort, only the 
perceptual response was taken into account, neglecting any physiological response. Since 
the field tests will be carried out in an office environment, the experiments were designed to 
represent typical office work and thus design decisions and evaluation methods are based on 
standards for offices. To minimise the number of variables, only artificial lighting was 
included in the experiments and thus the effect of daylight was eliminated.

2 Theory 
 
 
 
2 | Theory 
==PAGE:21==
2| Theory

The thermal, atmospheric, visual and acoustic conditions in an indoor environment affect 
the occupant comfort level and thus the indoor environmental quality (IEQ), see Figure 2-1.  
The thermal indoor environment depends on the air temperature, the mean radiant 
temperature, the air velocity and the air humidity, which all affect the temperature registered 
by the human thermal receptors. The atmospheric indoor environment depends on the air 
quality and thus the indoor concentration of pollutants. The visual indoor environment 
depends on the amount, distribution and properties of the daylight and the artificial lighting. 
Finally, the acoustic indoor environment depends on the levels and properties of sounds 
and/or noise emitted by indoor sources or transmitted to the room through the construction, 
the ventilation system, etc.  
 
 
 
Figure 2-1: The four environmental parameters (The Noun Project 2017).  
Since people on average spend more than 90 % of their time indoors, the IEQ has an impact 
on the occupants’ well-being but also on their productivity, performance and even their 
health (Geng et al. 2017; Sundell 2001). Thus, it can be expensive for the individuals, the 
building- and company owners and the society not to invest in good indoor environments. 
Luckily, new technologies to improve the indoor environment have been developed 
throughout the last decades giving rise to higher IEQs and higher occupant expectations. 
Unfortunately, a higher IEQ naturally has been equal to a higher energy consumption, which 
makes it crucial to optimise and rethink these technologies to make them more energy 
efficient and sustainable (Ansaldi et al. 2011).  
Light-emitting diodes (LED) is one example of a new technology since it consumes less 
energy, has a longer life span and a faster reaction time compared to traditional incandescent 
light bulbs. Among other qualities the LED technology also makes it possible to control the 
CCT of the lighting (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004).

In order to understand a potential association between the thermal and visual environment, 
the two subtopics are presented individually. Since daylight is neglected in the experiments, 
the visual environment is only explained in terms of artificial lighting.  
2.1 Thermal comfort 
According to ISO 7730 (2005) thermal comfort is defined as “that condition of mind which 
expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment”. This is however a very subjective 
definition that makes it difficult to quantify and thus difficult to predict and apply to indoor 
environments. Since people have different experiences of the thermal environment due to 
physiological and psychological variances it is impossible to satisfy everyone 
simultaneously and this might be the reason for the vague definition. When designing 
thermal environments and indoor environments in general, the aim must be to satisfy the 
highest possible percentage of the occupants. Thermal neutrality is strongly related to 
thermal comfort and is defined as the condition in which a person prefers neither warmer 
nor cooler surroundings. All people do, however, not feel most comfortable when 
experiencing thermal neutrality meaning that some people prefer feeling slightly cooler or 
warmer than neutral. This is another example on the complexity of thermal comfort that 
makes it impossible to satisfy everyone at the same time (Fanger 1970). 
2.1.1 Neural pathways 
The warm- and cold thermal receptors are located right under the skin of the human body 
and responds when the skin temperature increases or decreases (Parsons 2013). The thermal 
receptors send signals to the hypothalamus, which is the part of the brain that controls the 
body temperature. If the body temperature increases, the body’s heat loss is increased by 
increasing the blood flow to the skin and/or by sweating. If the body temperature decreases, 
the heat loss is reduced by reducing the blood flow from the body core to the skin and/or the 
heat production is increased by shivering. Thus, the hypothalamus keeps the core 
temperature constant at approximately 37 °C by balancing the heat production and the heat 
loss. The heat balance is given as:

2.1.2 Quantifying thermal comfort 
Keeping the human heat balance in equilibrium and eliminating local thermal discomfort are 
criteria for thermal comfort. When quantifying thermal comfort, six parameters are taken 
into account: Two personal parameters including clothing and activity level and four

10 
environmental parameters including the mean radiant temperature, the air temperature, the 
air velocity and the relative humidity. Thermal comfort can be achieved with many different 
combinations of these six parameters that all affect the human heat balance (Fanger 1970). 
Based on the six parameters, it is possible to predict the thermal sensation of the body as a 
whole. The most commonly used method is the predicted mean vote (PMV) index which 
expresses the mean assessment of people’s thermal sensation in a given environment. It was 
developed by Ole Fanger and is based on observations of a large number of human subjects 
in a climate chamber (Fanger 1970). The PMV-index indicates on a seven-point scale going 
from -3 to +3 where -3 is equal to cold, 0 is equal to neutral and 3 is equal to hot. From the 
PMV-index the predicted percentage of dissatisfied (PPD) can be obtained. Where the PMV-
index predicts the thermal sensation, the PPD-index predicts the percentage of people that 
will be dissatisfied with the given environment (-3, -2, +2 and +3 on the seven-point scale). 
According to the PPD-index, it is impossible to satisfy more than 95 % of the occupants due 
to individual preferences (ISO 7730 - 2005). The PMV-scale and the correlation between 
PMV and PPD are illustrated in Figure 2-2.  
 
 
Figure 2-2: The PMV-index (left) and the correlation between PMV and PPD (right) (Fanger 1970).

draught, vertical air temperature differences, warm/cold floors or radiant asymmetry.  
Draught is the most common reason for local discomfort and is defined as an undesired 
cooling of a body part due to air movement. The draught rate depends on the ambient air 
temperature, the local mean air velocity and the local turbulence intensity and at the neck it

2.2 Lighting 
Light is electromagnetic radiation while visible light is the range of the electromagnetic 
spectrum that can be detected by the human eye. This covers the wavelengths from 380 nm 
to 780 nm going from violet to red light (see Figure 2-3).  
 
Figure 2-3: The electromagnetic spectrum (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004). 
==PAGE:28==
Colour Temperature of Lighting and Thermal Comfort

Within physics, light both behaves like particles and waves. The emission and absorption of 
light are explained by its particle behaviour while the propagation of light is explained by its 
wave behaviour (Young & Freedman 2014).  
2.2.1 Neural pathways 
 
Figure 2-4: The human eye (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004). 
The human eye detects the light and converts it to electrical impulses transmitted to the brain. 
The light hits the cornea, which is the thin, transparent, outer layer that protects the eye. The 
cornea refracts the light, which then enters the eye through the pupil and gets refracted 
further by the lens. These refractions produce an inverted image onto the retina. The retina 
is the inner layer of the eye which consists of a large number of human photoreceptor cells 
called rods and cones. The rods are responsible for the human night vision as they function 
under low light intensities. The cones include the red, blue and green cones which are 
responsible for the colour vision at normal light intensities. When the light hits these 
photoreceptors, electrical impulses are transferred through the optic nerve to the brain and 
thus converted to visual information (see Figure 2-4) (Boyce 2014).  
2.2.2 Lighting quantity 
Radiometry is the science of measuring electromagnetic radiation in the full electromagnetic 
spectrum while photometry is the science of measuring visible light. Photometry uses 
weighting to account for the sensitivity of the eye which is most sensitive to yellow and 
green light. Luminous intensity, luminous flux, illuminance and luminance are the most 
important photometric terms when quantifying the visible light (see Table 2-4).

The luminous intensity (I) defines how much luminous power a source is emitting in a

given in lux (lx) which is derived by dividing lumen with the area.

When a light beam hits a surface, the light is either absorbed by, reflected by and/or 
transmitted through the surface depending on the material properties of the surface. The light 
reflected by a surface is what is registered by the human eye and thus luminance is the most 
relevant parameter in terms of communicating the human perception of light quantities. It is 
however easier and cheaper to measure the illuminance and therefore this is the most 
commonly used measure when evaluating lighting quantities (Tsangrassoulis et al. 2004).  
2.2.3 Lighting quality 
Ensuring enough light in terms of photometric quantities is not enough to ensure a good 
quality of the light. Amongst others, lighting quality depends on the uniformity of light, 
control of glare, the colour rendering and the correlated colour temperature. 
The uniformity of lighting is often defined as the ratio between the minimal- and the average 
illuminance level and thus describes the spatial differences in illuminance. If the uniformity 
==PAGE:31==
2| Theory 
17 
in a room is too high the room will appear uninteresting because of the lack of contrasts, but 
if the uniformity is too low it will cause distraction.  
Glare is caused by excessive luminance differences or excessive brightness in the visual 
field causing discomfort or even temporary disabilities to see.  
The colour rendering index (CRI) indicates how well a lighting design reproduces the actual 
colour of an object.  
The correlated colour temperature (CCT) indicates the colour appearance of the light 
emitted by a lighting source and is given in Kelvin. A CCT of 2700 K corresponds to a warm, 
reddish light, 4000 K corresponds to a neutral, white light and 5000 K and above corresponds 
to a cool, blueish light. By dividing 1,000,000 with the CCT, the reciprocal colour 
temperature (RCT) is achieved. CCT is more commonly used, but RCT is in better 
correspondence with the human eye’s perception of colour differences (Boyce 2014).

3 Previous studies 
 
 
 
3 | Previous studies 
==PAGE:35==

The division into warm and cold colours was defined in the 19th century, but Mogensen & 
English (1926) were the first to investigate if colour actually had an impact on thermal 
perception. They covered some slide rheostats in six different colours of paper including 
yellow, orange, red, green, blue and purple. 25 subjects were then presented with two colours 
at a time and after touching them briefly had to judge which of the colours were warmest, 
even though they in fact had a constant temperature of 42 °C. Green was judged as the 
warmest and purple as the coldest, and thus no relation between thermal perception and 
cold/warm colours were found. This might have been influenced by individual colour 
preferences being more dominant than colour temperatures.  
In the period from 1940 to 1980 a number of other studies testing the hue-heat hypothesis 
were carried out using different methods. Houghten et al. (1940) projected blue, red and 
white colours on a screen, Berry (1961) used five different colours of lighting, Bennett & 
Rey (1972) made subjects wear red, blue and clear plastic goggles and Greene & Bell (1980) 
painted the walls in either red, blue or white. Despite of different methods in terms of 
application of colour, none of the studies showed any acceptance of the hue-heat hypothesis. 
Only one study carried out by Fanger et al. (1977) seemed to confirm the hypothesis. In this 
study 16 subjects were exposed to two types of coloured light and two noise levels in the 
four possible combinations. The subjects were placed in a climate chamber for 2.5 hours per 
combination and each subject was to adjust the ambient temperature every 10 minutes to 
maintain comfortable. The noise levels of 40 and 85 dB(A) did not affect the thermal 
preferences but a 0.4 °C lower ambient temperature was preferred for the extreme red light 
compared to the extreme blue light. Thus, the hue-heat hypothesis was confirmed but not to 
an extent that would cause significant energy savings since the difference was so small and 
had been proven only for extreme colour conditions.  
After disappointing results, the hue-heat hypothesis was not investigated further for a long 
period, but within the past few years the hypothesis has regained popularity. In general, the 
new studies make use of the methods developed by Fanger et al. (1977) by applying different 
colours of light. Most of them continue to use strong colour differences (Winzen et al. 2014; 
Ho et al. 2014; Vitali et al. 2014; Albers et al. 2015), but some of the most recent studies 
instead use different CCTs (Huebner et al. 2016; Baniya et al. 2016). Finally, two studies 
apply the colours to objects similar to Mogensen & English (1926) but where one study 
==PAGE:36==

applies the blue and red colours to first an object and then the subjects’ hand (Ho et al. 2014), 
the other study makes use of new technology by using virtual reality to apply the colour to a 
cup (Ziat et al. 2016).  
Four out of seven recent studies seem to confirm the hue-heat hypothesis, whereas the last 
three do not. Ho et al. (2014) even account for the opposite effect, since a blue object was 
judged to be warmer than a red object at similar temperatures throughout the study. Ho et al. 
(2014) assume this to be caused by expectations: When seeing a red object it is expected to 
be warm because of its warm colour temperature, whereas a blue object is expected to be 
cold.

Based on experiences from the previous studies the following should be considered when 
designing similar studies: 
 Number of variables  
 Extreme- or realistic setup 
 Interval of ambient- and colour temperatures 
 Evaluation method 
 Subjects 
Number of variables: The aim of carrying out experiments in a climate chamber compared 
to field experiments is to eliminate variables so associations are easy to detect. In previous 
==PAGE:37==
3| Previous studies 
23 
studies, the luminance/illuminance varies with the different colours (e.g. Berry 1961; Fanger 
et al. 1977; Albers et al. 2015) and other parameters known to influence the thermal comfort 
level were not controlled (Bennett & Rey 1972; Greene & Bell 1980). These parameters 
should be measured continuously throughout the whole experiment and local differences and 
differences between sessions need to be accounted for as well (Bennett & Rey 1972; Huebner 
et al. 2016).  
Extreme- or realistic setup: When using extreme colour differences (e.g. red and blue) it 
is hard to apply the findings, since it would not be acceptable in an office environment 
(Fanger et al. 1977). On the other hand, other sources of light that exist in an office like 
natural daylight, task lighting and light from computer screens need to be eliminated as it 
would increase the number of variables. A computer screen takes up a quite large part of a 
user’s field of vision and thus testing with computers might be an investigation of the colour 
temperature of the computer screen rather than the general lighting (Baniya et al. 2016).  
Interval of ambient- and colour temperatures: In previous studies, the ambient 
temperatures used throughout the studies have been too close to the ideal temperature 
(Houghten et al. 1940; Huebner et al. 2016). In order to register any changes, it is of great 
importance to test the upper and lower limit of the comfort zone. It is however good also to 
test neutral values for comparisons both in terms of ambient- and colour temperatures 
(Winzen et al. 2014; Ziat et al. 2016).  
Evaluation method: Evaluation methods need to be evaluated critically to ensure that the 
experiment will present the desired outcome. Since small response differences can be 
expected, the scales should be sensitive to fine changes (Bennett & Rey 1972; Greene & Bell 
1980). The subjects should not be doing tasks that are so demanding that it will move their 
focus from the actual experiment (Berry 1961).  
Subjects: The subjects should be tested for colour blindness before the experiments (e.g. 
Berry 1961; Fanger et al. 1977; Winzen et al. 2014) and the number of subjects should be 
large enough to ensure representable results (e.g. Houghten et al. 1940; Vitali et al. 2014). 
Huebner et al. (2016) suggests using a wide age range to test if the preferences vary with 
age, but otherwise no recommendations are given regarding age and gender combinations.

The objective of the study was to quantify the effect of the CCT of lighting on thermal 
comfort and performance of humans. The experiments were carried out with human subjects 
in a controlled environment chamber, where the lighting and the thermal environment were 
accurately controlled. During one experimental session, the operative temperature and the 
lighting intensity were constant and the CCT the only varying parameter. During the 
exposures, subjects assessed their perception of the thermal environment by the use of a 
questionnaire and their performance was evaluated by the use of standard performance tests.  
4.1 Conditions 
The experiment was carried out with three different operative temperatures; 19 °C, 22 °C 
and 27 °C. According to ISO-7730, 19 °C represents the lower limit for category C during 
the heating season, 22 °C represents the ideal temperature during the heating season, while 
27 °C represents the upper limit for category C during the cooling season. Thus, 19 °C and 
27 °C are on the boundary of the comfort range while 22 °C is an ideal operative temperature 
for reference. The operative temperature was kept as constant as possible during one 
experimental session but changed from one session to another. The CCT increased and 
decreased systematically according to its corresponding reciprocal colour temperature 
(RCT) throughout one session. Either it went from low-to-high-to-low (LHL) going from 
2700 K to 6200 K and back to 2700 K or in reverse from high-to-low-to-high (HLH) (6200 
– 2700 – 6200 K). This was done to eliminate bias caused by the order of exposure. In 
addition, it should minimise the risk of the subjects realising that the light was changing 
throughout the experiment by letting them into and out of the room at the same lighting 
conditions. Based on the pilot study carried out by Christensen & Støttrup (2017), the 
luminous flux was controlled to ensure a constant illuminance of approximately 1000 lux on 
the tables. 
Due to the hypothesis that subjects would experience an effect on the boundary of the 
comfort zone, all subjects were exposed to 19 °C and 27 °C sessions while only 50 % of the 
subjects were exposed to 22 °C. The CCT exposures were balanced between subjects so 50 
% of the subjects experienced a LHL CCT loop and the other 50 % of the subjects 
experienced a HLH CCT loop (see Table 4-1).

3. The individual RCT-differences were divided with the sum of all RCT-differences

4. The weights were multiplied with the total time allocated for CCT variations to 
determine the corresponding time step. 
Calculations for all time-steps are presented in Appendix F.

4.5.2 Performance test 
During each experimental session, the subjects were asked to do three performance tests as 
a measure of their concentration ability. The d2-tests originally developed by Brickenkamp 
in 1962 were used for this purpose. It is a paper-and-pencil test that consists of the characters 
d and p spread out on 14 rows with 47 characters in each. The test is carefully timed and the 
subjects had 20 seconds per row during which they were to cross out as many target 
characters as possible. The target character is the d2’s meaning the letter d with two dashes; 
either two above the d, two below the d or one above and below. An example of a row of 
characters is given in Figure 4-16 and a full d2-test is presented in Appendix H.

4.5.3 Tablet

number of:

o Wrongly marked distractor characters (false-positives) 
From these, the error percentage (E%), the concentration output (CO) and the concentration 
performance (CP) were calculated. E% is calculated as the percentage of errors out of the 
total score, CO is the total score minus the number of errors and CP is the number of correctly

concentration level as they gather speed and accuracy (Brickenkamp 2002).

5.1.4 Noise 
Based on the measurements carried out by Christensen & Støttrup (2017), the sound pressure 
level in the room was 48 dB(A). According to DS/EN 15251 (2007), the default design 
values are 35 dB(A) for small offices and conference rooms and 40 dB(A) for landscaped 
offices and office cubicles. Thus, the sound pressure level in the room exceeds the design 
values and the noise may have caused annoyance to the subjects.

6.2 Lighting 
For several questions, the perception of the lighting was significantly associated with the 
CCT. In general, the lighting was perceived as being brighter and less acceptable with 
increasing CCTs. In principle, it suggests that the lighting intensity can be reduced if the 
CCT is increased and hence the energy consumption for the lighting can be reduced. It is 
however not possible to convert the perceived difference into a specific difference in 
illuminance. 
==PAGE:85==
6| Evaluation 
71 
The association between the spectral properties of lighting and the perception of brightness 
is called spatial brightness. A large number of studies have investigated the concept, and 
some of them confirm the same association between CCT and brightness as found in this 
study. They argue for the need of a new metric that corresponds better to the colour 
sensitivity of the human eye than illuminance does. A large number of studies however deny 
the association and thus spatial brightness is a much debated concept open to further 
investigations (Fotios et al. 2013). 
6.3 Air quality 
The perception of the air quality was significantly associated with the CCT for an operative 
temperature of 22 °C. 40 % of the subjects perceived the air as stuffy or slightly stuffy at 
2746 K against 20 % at 6231 K. Thus, the air quality was perceived as being better with 
increasing CCTs. In principle, it suggests that the air change rate can be reduced if the CCT 
is increased and hence the energy consumption of the ventilation system can be reduced. 
Due to the scale used in the questionnaire, it is difficult to translate the difference in the mean 
vote to a specific reduction. If the acceptability scale illustrated in Figure 6-2 was used 
instead it would be possible to convert the results into a specific percentage of dissatisfied 
and a corresponding ventilation rate using the method described by Wargocki (2004).

7.1 Outcome  
The aim of the study was to quantify the association between the CCT of LED lighting and 
human thermal comfort and -performance.  
In terms of comfort, question 1 assessing the subjects’ thermal perception verified a 
significant association between the CCT and the thermal perception at an operative 
temperature of 22 °C. A difference of 0.42 on the nine-point scale was found when 
comparing the extreme CCTs corresponding to an operative temperature difference of 2 °C. 
Thus, a CCT of 2746 K at 21 °C should cause the same thermal sensation as a CCT of 6231 
K at 23 °C. This association is in accordance with the results found by Fanger et al. (1977) 
and Huebner et al. (2016). The thermal perception at 19 and 27 °C and the outcome of the 
other questions assessing the thermal environment were not significantly affected by the 
CCT. Instead, significant associations between the perception of the lighting intensity and 
the air quality were found. The lighting was perceived as being brighter and the air quality 
was perceived as being better with increasing CCTs. The latter is in accordance with 
associations found by Winzen et al (2014). 
In terms of performance, no associations between the CCT and the performance indicators 
of the d2-test were detected.  
7.2 Lab experiments 
The design of the lab experiments was based on experience from earlier studies and 
attempted to account for their sources of error. Many elements in the design are however 
still open for discussion and further investigation.  
7.2.1 Experiment conditions 
Houghten et al. (1940) and Huebner et al. (2016) evaluated that the temperatures used for 
their experiment were too close to the ideal temperature to cause noticeable variation in the 
perception of the thermal environment. Thus, it was expected that the CCT would have the 
largest impact for 19 and 27 °C, but in the experiments the only clear association between 
the perception of the thermal environment and the CCT was found for 22 °C. Since this set 
point was included only as a reference, fewer subjects were exposed to this operative 
temperature. Instead, 19 and 27 °C should have been used as reference conditions since the 
subjects felt cold at 19 °C and warm at 27 °C, no matter what the CCT was. More subjects 
==PAGE:88==
Colour Temperature of Lighting and Thermal Comfort 
 
74 
should thus have been exposed to 22 °C and the ideal operative temperature for summer 
conditions (= 24.5 °C) should have been included as well.   
In terms of the lighting exposure, Christensen and Støttrup (2017) decided on a lighting 
intensity of 1000 lux on the working plane. According to their argumentation, an extreme 
intensity would cause the CCT to have a larger impact than a moderate intensity. Since the 
experiments were designed to represent typical office work it would make more sense to aim 
for an illuminance level of 500 lux on the working plane. This is supported by the fact that 
the lighting was evaluated as bright for all exposures of CCTs and operative temperatures.  
7.2.2 Subjects 
Due to limited time available for recruitment of subjects, the gender distribution for the 
experiments was 61 % men and 39 % women and more women were exposed to the HLH 
than the LHL lighting sequence. It would have been ideal to have 50 % men and 50 % on 
the six different types of exposures to ensure that the variation caused by gender was 
balanced. 
7.2.3 Questionnaire  
During the introduction meetings, the subjects were encouraged to ask if they were unsure 
about the sense of the words in the questionnaires. Since the questionnaire was written in 
English and 89 % of the subjects had Danish as their first language, it is possible that 
language barriers still existed. Possible solutions would have been to have a Danish and an 
English version of the questionnaire or to make the questionnaire in Danish and only recruit 
Danish subjects. 
Before each experimental session, it was emphasised that the subjects had to take some time 
to evaluate how they perceived the indoor environment at the specific moment and not how 
they in general felt throughout the whole session. The more questionnaires they filled out, 
the faster they filled them out. This may indicate that they became more conversant with the 
questionnaire but it may also indicate that they were less focused when evaluating and 
answering the questions. If the latter was the case, it is possible that the number of 
questionnaires should be reduced in the future. 
Continuous scales were used for some of the most essential questions to ensure that the scales 
were sensitive enough to detect small variations. For most of these scales, indicative points 
==PAGE:89==
7| Discussion 
75 
between the extremes were added. A considerable number of the subjects tended to mark 
their answers on these predefined points even though it was emphasised that they were 
allowed to use the full scale. It is possible that this reduced the resolution of the responses.  
As described in Section 6.3, the scale used to evaluate the IAQ should be replaced with 
another to enable evaluation of the CCTs effect on the percentage of dissatisfied and a 
corresponding ventilation rate.  
7.2.4 Performance test 
As expected, the subjects’ learning curves for the d2-tests were quite steep. Therefore, 
corrections were made by weighting the performance indicators with the outcome of the first 
d2-test round, but the issue could be solved by other approaches. Firstly, the subjects could 
have more experience with the test in advance of the experiments. This would however mean 
that more time and more money should be put aside for preparations of the experiments. 
Secondly, the tests could be based on elements that the subjects already know like spelling 
or calculation tasks. Thirdly, the tests could be planned so they were fully balanced. From 
the beginning the aim was to make the experiments fully balanced in terms of gender, 
lighting animations, operative temperatures, etc. but this was not fully succeeded. Half of 
the subjects skipping an operative temperature of 22 °C, the first performance test during 
each experimental session always being completed at a CCT of 3560 K etc. are examples on 
elements that disturbed the distribution of the performance tests.  
As emphasised by earlier studies and the results from the questionnaires, small variations 
were expected and the d2-test may have been to insensitive to detect these. For example, the 
subjects often finished the entire line before the 20 seconds expired. For future experiments, 
the 3 min. Baddeley's Grammatical Reasoning Test may be more suitable as it should be 
very sensitive (Baddeley 1968) 
7.2.5 Experimental procedure 
Since three experimental sessions were conducted per day the breaks between one group 
leaving the room and the next group entering were relatively short. Therefore, the subjects 
acclimatised in another room than the test room to ensure that the test room had time to 
stabilise at the reference conditions in terms of temperature and air quality. This other room 
did not have the same thermal environment as the test room and thus the subjects spent a 
significant part of the experiments adjusting to the new environment. It would have been 
==PAGE:90==
Colour Temperature of Lighting and Thermal Comfort 
 
76 
better if the subjects acclimatised in the test room or an adjacent room with the same thermal 
environment.  
90 minutes seemed to be a fine duration of the experiments, but it is still open for discussion. 
It seemed to be enough time to ensure that the subject did not perceive the change in CCT, 
but the subjects also expressed that they got bored quite quickly and thus longer experiments 
do not seem to be a good idea. The subjects getting bored may have caused them to get 
unfocused.  
7.2.6 Data processing 
First step of the data processing was to convert the answers from the questionnaires and 
performance tests to data by correcting and typing them in and it is possible that few mistakes 
have been made in this manual process.  
The analyses of the results were based on summary statistics and statistical analyses 
completed in the software JMP. These initial analyses were made on a very simple level due 
to a lack of previous experience with statistical modelling. A foundation for much more 
advanced statistical analyses however exist due to the large extent of data.  
7.3 Field experiments 
The field experiments should be planned so they are comparable with the lab experiments 
but more elements need to be considered.  
The CCT of the ceiling lamps probably will be the only independent variable controlled by 
the project group, but it is important to register all other relevant parameters including 
daylight, lighting from table lamps and computer monitors, thermal parameters, air quality 
and a number of person specific parameters like clothing level and metabolic rate. The range 
of the operative temperatures is expected to be narrow compared to the temperatures 
investigated in the lab tests, but other parameters affecting the thermal environment are 
expected to vary more. Similar, larger variations in metabolic rates and clothing levels are 
expected.  
Since the subjects will be at work, the number and duration of interruptions must be reduced 
to a minimum. Thus, the questions in- and the timing of the questionnaires need to be planned 
carefully. If the questionnaires are too demanding the subjects might stop answering them.

8 Conclusion 
 
 
 
8 | Conclusion 
==PAGE:93==
8| Conclusion 
79 
The aim of this study was to test the hue-heat hypothesis by examining whether the 
correlated colour temperature of LED lighting affects the perceived temperature or not.  
The study was based on experiments where 44 human subjects were exposed to different 
operative temperatures and correlated colour temperatures (CCTs). The potential association 
was evaluated through subjective questionnaires and performance tests.  
When comparing the extreme CCTs, a difference of 0.42 on the nine-point scale was found 
for an operative temperature of 22 °C corresponding to an operative temperature difference 
of 2 °C. Thus, a CCT of 2746 K at 21 °C should cause the same thermal sensation as a CCT 
of 6231 K at 23 °C. No association between the CCT and operative temperatures of 19 °C 
and 27 °C were found but significant associations between the CCT and the perception of 
the air quality and the lighting intensity were found. The air quality was perceived as being 
better and the lighting was perceived at being brighter with increasing CCTs. No association 
between the CCT and the subjects’ performance was detected.  
It can be concluded that an association between the CCT of LED lighting and the perceived 
thermal sensation exist for operative temperatures in the middle of the comfort range. Thus, 
the hue-heat hypothesis was confirmed demonstrating that lighting can expand the range of 
temperatures that building occupants find neither too warm or too cold. To quantify potential 
energy savings, more experiments should be conducted with an illuminance level of 500 lux 
on the working plane and operative temperatures of 22 and 24.5 °C corresponding to the 
optimal comfort temperatures during the non-heating and heating seasons, respectively. The 
magnitude of the effect on thermal sensation of the CCT is expected to be reduced when the 
association is tested in buildings in practice where daylight and computer monitors also may 
affect occupant perceptions of indoor environment.
