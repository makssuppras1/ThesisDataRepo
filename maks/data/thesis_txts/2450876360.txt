Abstract
This study presents a machine learning (ML) approach to detecting and classifying
the heliospheric impact of Interplanetary Coronal Mass Ejection (ICME) and Stream
Interaction Regions (SIR) in the solar wind as early as possible. This is accomplished
using solar wind data from satellites in the Lagrangian L1 point.
Four diﬀerent ML algorithms are compared and analysed. They are trained and
validated on 22 years (1995-2016) of solar wind data and checked against Venner-
strøm’s SIR catalogue and a combination of Richardson & Cane’s ICME catalogue
and AWARE’s ICME classiﬁcations. Finally, one model is tested on 2 years (2017-
2018) of data, not seen by the model, to evaluate it’s performance. An implemen-
tation using data windows are utilised to select solar wind event starts. The Sparse
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) achieves 82.2% and 82.4% accuracy on the
validation and test data set, respectively. This is done using only the ﬁrst 2 hours
of solar wind data for each event. An analysis showed that SIRs and No Events are
classiﬁed correctly the most, with 83% and 85.5% correct respectively. The ICMEs
are classiﬁed correctly 60.5% of the time.
The incorrect classiﬁcations are analysed to reveal causes for the errors. These are
found to be a combination of overlap in event catalogues, and diﬃculty in predicting
weak events.
An analysis of the geomagnetic activity index, Kp, showed that events triggering
geomagnetic storms (Kp>5) were predicted as ICMEs or SIRs in almost all cases.
The model’s performance is also analysed on near real-time data from DSCOVR.
Most ICMEs and SIRs are detected by the model, however with some false alarms
presents.
There is a clear potential for implementing this algorithms as a warning system for
solar wind events. Events are detected with 80% accuracy seeing merely 60 minutes
of data according to catalogues. This is unprecedented. Applying the algorithm on
e.g. DSCOVR will in many cases allow a warning to be sent, even before the solar
wind reaches Earth. In all cases, warnings can be sent before a geomagnetic storm
has build up.

Introduction
The key to life on Earth is held by the Sun. This complex entity is aﬀecting Earth in
both positive and negative ways. It is emitting a constant stream of charged particles,
plasma, that is aﬀecting the magnetic ﬁeld around Earth. Earth’s ﬁeld protects us
and our surroundings from the damaging eﬀects of these charged particles. However,
variability in solar activity highly aﬀects our surroundings, and changes in space
weather can have devastating consequences to satellite electronics and humans in
space.
1.1
The Solar Wind and Space Weather
This constant stream of charged particles is called the solar wind. It emanates from
the Sun and is a product of the Sun’s outer atmosphere being so hot it expands fast,
with the gravitational pull not being able to contain the plasma. This outﬂow of
plasma carries the solar magnetic ﬁeld with it. The resulting ﬁeld is often referred
to as the Interplanetary Magnetic Field (IMF) (Baumjohann and Treumann 2012).
Due to the nature of this system, a change in the surface of the Sun will also be
visible/recordable in the solar wind. One of the more prominent variations is that
of the solar cycle. This is an 11-year cycle the Sun undergoes aﬀecting the solar
activity. A clear correlation has been found in the number of sunspots and solar
activity - more sunspots cause more solar activity. Especially in regards to solar
events aﬀecting the heliosphere. This will be explained in further detail in the next
chapter.
Besides the varying ambient solar wind, violent solar events can occur on the solar
surface, increasing the stream of particles outwards. Coronal holes can result in a
High Speed Stream (HSS) and a Stream Interaction Region (SIR). Furthermore,
solar eruptions can cause a Coronal Mass Ejection (CME). If the latter reaches
the interplanetary medium, it is called an Interplanetary Coronal Mass Ejection
(ICME). Events like this will increase the amount of charged particles in the solar
wind, resulting in a more violent solar wind blowing towards Earth (Schwenn 2006).

1.2
Interaction with our magnetic ﬁeld
The solar wind will travel through the interplanetary medium to reach Earth. When
it reaches Earth, it will interact with Earth’s magnetosphere. Figure 1.2.1 shows
a simpliﬁed version of this interaction.
The main part of the solar wind will be
deﬂected by the magnetosphere. However, depending on the strength and direction
of the magnetic ﬁeld in the solar wind, our magnetic ﬁeld can be pushed inwards,
leaving distant satellites prone to damage from the solar wind. Furthermore, events
like an ICME can increase the rate of magnetic reconnection, further increasing the
damaging potential of both satellites and Earth-situated electronics.

1.3
Detecting the solar wind events
Being able to detect these solar wind events as early as possible, can ensure vital
time to turn oﬀsatellite electronics or even Earth-located power stations, in case
of geomagnetic disturbances. Using imaging satellites of the solar corona, one can
typically detect CMEs. Using triangulation, one can attempt to ﬁnd the direction
of propagation of the ICME that enters interstellar medium. However, one needs at
2
==PAGE:9==

least two images to perform triangulation to a viable degree. These images are not
always reliable, and even when they are, the triangulation is not necessarily perfect.
Finally, not all events can be detected this way (Bronarska et al. 2017).
Using solar wind data from the L1 Lagrangian point, one can detect the heliospheric
impact of the events. Especially ICMEs and SIRs possess typical properties, that
can be recalled in magnetic and plasma data of the solar wind. Due to the location
of the L1 point, it is almost certain, that particles hitting satellites situated here,
will reach Earth shortly after.
Hence, if one can detect events using the satellite data, one can detect events that
are certain to hit Earth. Though, the amount of available time to create a prediction
ranges from minutes to a few hours.
1.4
Machine Learning
This time-limit calls for a tool that can look at data and create predictions as quickly
as possible. Machine Learning (ML) will be that tool. In its basic form, ML takes
data and trains an algorithm to solve some sort of problem, using that data (Fagella
2019). Using the L1 solar wind data, a ML algorithm can be taught to detect the
heliospheric impact of ICMEs and SIR in the solar wind. If successful, prediction
and classiﬁcation of these events can be carried out before they hit Earth. From here
on, the heliospheric impact of the two event types will be described as "solar wind
events".
1.5
Previous work
Several researchers have published papers regarding these solar wind events, all with
the common goal of gaining a better understanding of the driving forces behind them,
to ﬁnally be able to predict them.
L. Jian et al. 2006, and I. G. Richardson and H. V. Cane 1995 present manually
created catalogues of the solar wind events, SIRs and ICMEs respectively. Using
several physical parameters, that will be utilised in this study as well, event cata-
logues are created.
Wang, Jingjing et al. 2018 presents an operational system that translates solar ob-
servations of the photosphere and coronograph into solar wind event arrival times
at Earth. Their system is a combination of automatic and manual systems. They
achieve 60% accuracy while having 30% false alarms. They also present earlier at-
tempts at similar processes, none which perform better.

The inclusion of ML in space weather has also been done for diﬀerent tasks. Inceoglu
et al. 2018 used ML to predict if solar ﬂares will be associated with CMEs and Solar
Energetic Particles (SEP)s. This was done using magnetograms of the solar surface
and ﬂare data from the DONKI webpage.
Xu and Borovsky 2015, and Camporeale et al. 2017 presents a way to classify the
solar wind plasma origin into four diﬀerent classes, two of which are directly related
to solar wind events: Ejecta and Coronal Hole. The second paper presents a ML
implementation of this classiﬁcation. Some inspiration from this classiﬁcation will
be used in this study.
Camporeale 2019 presents an extensive summary of all attempts to use ML on solar
wind data. Several diﬀerent problems and implementations are discussed. However,
during my research, no published papers attempt to classify SIRs and ICMEs using
solar wind data. This study presents exactly this.
1.6
The outline of the Thesis
The aim of this study is the detection and classiﬁcation of these solar wind events
using solar wind data. The detection will be done using ML algorithms. Being able
to both detect and classify ICMEs and SIRs with high accuracy, and within a few
hours, will be an impressive feat and can result save crucial time to save electronics
in space.
Chapter 2 and 3 of this study will present the theoretical background concerning
space weather. The physical backgrounds for solar events will be presented, leading to
how these events can be detected in the solar wind data. Lastly, ML algorithms will
be presented and introduced, and their capabilities in solving this task are presented.
Chapter 4 will present the sources of data used for this study, including catalogues
for solar wind events.
Chapter 5 will present the methods used to prepare the necessary satellite data.
Furthermore, it will show the implementation of the presented ML algorithms, and
how these are used to solve the problem.
In Chapter 6, the results of the ML classiﬁcation and prediction will be shown.
Furthermore, comparisons between several diﬀerent approaches will be presented.
The chapter will show the performance on data, not seen by the ﬁnal algorithm in
order to simulate real-life performance.
Chapter 7 contains a discussion about time dependence on solar wind features and
about the usage of ML algorithms as a warning measure, including the importance
of detecting geomagnetic storms. Finally, chapter 8 is the conclusion.

The Sun and Solar Wind
2.1
The solar cycle
The sun has an activity cycle of 11 years. When the Sun is in a high activity stage,
the amount of high energy radiation (X-ray or ultraviolet) is increased, leading to
eﬀects in Earth’s upper atmosphere. Furthermore, higher solar activity leads to an
increase in solar ﬂares and CMEs. An increase in these events also increases the risk
of very violent events, that can be potentially damaging for satellite instruments and
even astronauts.
To measure the activity of the Sun, one measures the number of sunspots on the
surface of the Sun. This has been measured for a few hundred years, and have been
shown to correlate very well with solar activity. These sunspots are dark patches
on the surface of the Sun, where the magnetic ﬁeld loops up through the surface.
They appear darker, due to them being colder as a result of the strong magnetic
ﬁeld damping convection to the surface.
The sunspot number is however not an easy thing to measure. First of all, it is
subjective: you need a person/machine to actually count the number of sunspots.
You can then produce averages over several values, but it still introduces a bias.
Furthermore, counting sunspot numbers becomes near impossible with the opacity
of clouds or the day/night rotation of Earth.
Luckily, the 10.7 cm Solar Flux was discovered. Measuring radio wavelength emission
at that certain wavelength was proven to correlate extremely well with the sunspot
number, and the general activity cycle of the Sun of 11 years. This measure can be
made in all weather conditions and is completely objective. Hence, this measure will
be used when classifying the solar activity cycle in the ML program in this study
(D. H. Hathaway 2015).

2.1.2
The solar wind
The solar wind ﬁlls the area between the Sun and Earth and continues further into
our solar system. It emanates from the expansion of the solar corona. This pushes
solar plasma into space. The magnetic ﬁeld follows the plasma since it is frozen in
the plasma. This creates the solar wind. This wind blows towards Earth and will
interact with our magnetosphere (Baumjohann and Treumann 2012).
The solar wind is also highly variable depending on the solar cycle. This change
in solar wind intensity comes from coronal holes and helmet streamers, and their
diﬀerent locations during the solar cycle (Schwenn 2006).
A coronal hole is a region of the solar surface where the corona is dark and the corona
is less dense and colder. These are known to be caused by open magnetic ﬁeld-lines
extending into interplanetary space. The open ﬁeld-lines ensure that solar wind ﬂows
6

faster from coronal holes. Hence, if a coronal hole points towards Earth, there is an
increased risk of a HSS hitting Earth (D. Hathaway 2014; Wikipedia 2019[a]).
Helmet streamers are located over closed magnetic loops over the Sun. These are
seen as bright loop-like structures over active regions on the surface. The slow solar
wind emerges from these streamers, as the magnetic loops are "connecting". The
magnetic ﬁeld is not strong enough to contain the plasma, resulting in the solar wind
shaping them into looking like helmets (D. Hathaway 2014; Wikipedia 2019[c]).
The location of open and closed magnetic ﬁeld lines vary during the solar cycle.
During the declining phase, the coronal holes are typically extending from the polar
areas down towards lower latitude. In solar maximum, the coronal holes are conﬁned
to the polar regions. The helmet streamers move from the equatorial region in solar
minimum, to a complex situation in solar maximum. This results in a faster ambient
wind in solar maximum. The Ulysses satellite has captured this diﬀerence in the
coronal structure during both solar maximum and minimum, see ﬁgure 2.1.2.
Figure 2.1.2: Solar wind speed as a function of solar latitude as measured by Ulysses.
The two orbits show a time of low and high solar activity; see the bottom row of sunspot
numbers (McComas et al. 2003).
The ﬁgure clearly shows that the average solar wind speed around the equatorial
region is higher in solar maximum than in solar minimum. The typical velocity of
the solar wind in a distance of 1 AU is around 300 km s−1 in solar minimum and

2.1.3
Measuring the solar wind
A direct consequence of the solar wind ﬂowing into space is that it is measurable
by satellite. Several satellites exist that measure the solar wind. STEREO A and
B orbit the Sun in an orbit similar to Earth’s. Simultaneously, in the Lagrangian
L1 point, several satellites measure the solar wind.
Especially ACE, WIND and
DSCOVR are important, all measuring solar wind magnetic ﬁeld and plasma. The
Lagrangian L1 is a gravitationally stable point between Earth and Sun. This is an
optimal spot to measure solar wind for two main reasons: ﬁrst of all, full visibility of
the Sun at all times. Secondly, due to the location, solar wind that passes through
the satellites in the L1 point will also reach Earth. This ensures a great opportunity
to measure the interaction between the solar wind and Earth’s magnetosphere.
2.2
Interplanetary Coronal Mass Ejection
An ICME is the interplanetary component of a CME. A CME is the ejection of
a large-scale structure from the corona. Visually, this will present itself as a bright
white light feature in the coronal ﬁeld of view, that happens over a period time of
several hours. Usually, it will present itself as a "light-bulb" shape, which is also
the reason for the shape of an ICME, when it travels into the solar system. The
CME typically occurs together with a solar ﬂare; both manifestations of a solar
eruption. A driving force of these is magnetic reconnection, see ﬁgure 2.2.1. This
occurs when anti-parallel ﬁeld lines reconnect and change the magnetic topology.
(Shea 2018). This reconnection will release large amounts of energy. It converts the
stored magnetic energy into kinetic energy, propelling everything above this recon-
nection outwards; the CME. Furthermore, the reconnection releases electromagnetic
energy, which can be measured as violent ﬂares in X-ray; solar ﬂares. The ﬂares are
measured at Earth shortly after the event. If the reconnection is strong enough, the
ejecta is sent beyond the reach of Sun’s gravity where it continues through the inter-
planetary medium, and an ICME is created (Schwenn 2006; Masson et al. 2013).
Travelling into interplanetary medium, the ICME will attain a typical shape in 3
dimensions, as seen on ﬁgure 2.2.3a. This shape has important characteristics, that
can be recalled when looking at in-situ solar wind data, resulting in the identiﬁcation

of an ICME, solely on solar wind data. The arrival time of ICMEs depends on the
velocity of the ICME. Typically, it ranges anywhere between 20 hours up to 4-5
days.
An in-situ example from Stereo A is seen in ﬁgure 2.2.2. Going through the steps
of the event, the ﬁrst thing that will reach the satellites is the shock. This is gen-
erated by the fast forward movement of the ejecta, that is compressing the ambient
solar wind ahead of it. The magnetic ﬁeld in the ejecta and that of the solar wind
follows, to an extent, the frozen ﬂux approximation. The two magnetic ﬁelds, there-
fore, can’t merge, compressing the solar wind ahead of the ejecta. This creates a
shock eﬀect. This is a very typical indicator of an ICME arrival and is present in
many ICMEs. However, due to the 3-dimensional structure of this ejecta, a shock
can happen without any ejecta following it, seen from the observer since the shock
happens all around the front of the ejecta. Furthermore, if the ICME is slower than
the ambient solar wind, no shock will be generated. These cases are problematic if
one wants to identify ICMEs in satellite data. However, the ICMEs that pose a
risk for a geomagnetic storm are often those with the most energy. Especially, high
velocity is important here, meaning that those ICMEs typically begin with a shock.
This is, therefore, an important indicator of these events (H. Cane and I. Richardson
2003).
If the shock is present, it is indicated by a sharp increase in magnetic ﬁeld and plasma
velocity, density and temperature. All caused directly from the compression front

behaviour. These typical solar-wind features for a sheath must be looked for, since
they pose a vital indicator for an ICME, especially in the ﬁrst few hours.
The last part of the ICME is the ejecta. This comes in several diﬀerent shapes
and sizes. A typical case is the magnetic cloud. In this case, a ﬂux rope exists in
the ejecta, causing smooth ﬁeld rotations, while having an enhanced magnetic ﬁeld.
Many ICMEs contain regions of looped magnetic ﬁeld, closing at the Sun. This is
often seen by bidirectional particle ﬂows in the ejecta (H. Cane and I. Richardson
2003). The smooth rotation of the ﬁeld in magnetic clouds causes a low magnetic
ﬁeld variance. This is seen if the observer passes through the entire cloud, seeing
the shift in magnetic ﬁeld direction through the magnetic rope. The ﬁeld itself will
be higher than average, however, declining smoothly over time as the ejecta passes.
The velocity of the ICME also declines in the ejecta. Both of these eﬀects are due
to the expansion of the ICME when it travels. The ﬂux rope itself is meant to be
created during the magnetic reconnection, as visualised in ﬁgure 2.2.1.

relation. These temperatures are meant to be due to expanding, magnetically iso-

Stream Interaction Region
A product of the HSSs can be a Stream Interaction Region (SIR). This region forms
when a slow solar wind is caught up by a fast wind. The frozen-in ﬁeld from the
diﬀerent origins on the Sun ensures that the fast and slow stream cannot merge.
The fast stream will therefore push the slow wind ahead of it while outrunning the
ambient solar wind behind it. This creates a compression ridge in the front of the
high-speed stream and rarefaction of "missing" solar wind behind the fast stream,
see ﬁgure 2.2.3b. These interaction regions are called SIRs. A special case of the
SIR is the Co-Rotating Interaction Region (CIR). Due to the Sun’s rotation, Earth
will see the same side of the Sun each ∼27 days. If the solar wind and HSS is
time-stationary, this creates an interaction region that follows the rotation of the
Sun, and return 27 days later; hence it is co-rotating. However, for this study, this
special case will still be considered a SIR, since their heliospheric eﬀect on the solar
wind data is similar.
As with the ICMEs, a SIR also has a typical signature in the solar wind in-situ
data. However, where the ICME typically can be seen by an early shock, the SIR
has a build-up period, as seen in ﬁgure 2.3.1. This is comparable to a wave in water,
and appears due to the driving of the HSS.
The SIR in the ﬁgure is marked with dotted lines, beginning at point a and ending
at point c. From the start, an increase is seen in magnetic ﬁeld and the plasma
parameters: velocity, density and temperature - this is the acceleration phase (from
a to b).
In the wave analogue, this corresponds to seeing a gradual increase in
water height, as the wavetop comes closer. At b is the stream interaction. Here a
sharp drop in density is seen, as well as an increase in temperature. L. Jian et al.
2006 mentions that the cause of this drop is that the two streams originate from
diﬀerent regions of the solar atmosphere. This causes a plasma separation, with
lower density plasma originating from a coronal hole. Thereafter all values gradually
decline, except for the velocity that increases all the way to point c. This last part is
the fast stream, and the phase is called decelerating (since the interaction between
the streams is decreasing). One parameter that typically shows the entire SIR is the

Geomagnetic storms
The main purpose for an early classiﬁcation and detection of solar wind events is the
risk they pose of a geomagnetic storm. A geomagnetic storm is a global magnetic
disturbance in the near-Earth environment. The disturbances can last several days
and are caused by space weather events and the IMF’s interaction with our magne-
tosphere. Two main factors determine the size of a storm. The velocity of the event
and the magnetic ﬁeld. Speciﬁcally, large negative southward ﬁeld (BZ) have a large
impact on the magnetic reconnection occurring.
Earth experiences storms in two categories, recurrent and transient. The recurrent
storms are periodic events created by a CIR, as described earlier. These, therefore,
follow the rotation of the Sun and will return roughly every 27 days. These typically
occur during the declining or minimum phases of the solar activity.
The transient storms, however, are mainly due to ICMEs. They occur most fre-
quently during solar maximum, as was shown in ﬁgure 2.1.1. This category also
holds the SIRs that are not co-rotating.
The geomagnetic storms start with the interaction between Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld,
more speciﬁcally our magnetosphere, and the IMF. A pressure balance between the
solar wind dynamic pressure, Pdyn and the outwards magnetic pressure, Pmag exerted
by our magnetosphere is determining the location of the magnetopause, see the blue
dotted line in ﬁgure 2.4.1. Clearly, a larger solar wind dynamic pressure will push
this magnetopause inwards. However, the closer to Earth this limit is pushed, the
larger a magnetic pressure pushes outwards, as it originates in the core of Earth. The
distance to the magnetopause is typically around 10RE, RE being Earth radii. Solar
wind conditions, however, aﬀect this distance heavily. In very active conditions, the
magnetopause can be pushed below 6.6RE, the distance of geosynchronous orbit,
causing satellites to be directly hit by the solar wind (Tátrallyay et al. 2012).
As mentioned, especially the southward magnetic component of the IMF, the Bz
component is important. If this component is strongly negative, magnetic recon-
nection occurs at increased rates at the magnetopause. This can further decrease
the distance to the magnetopause. Furthermore, this will cause a release of large
amounts of energy, propelling particles towards Earth. As reconnection happens on
the day-side (the side facing Sun), the magnetopause is pushed inwards. At the same
time, magnetic ﬂux is transported to the magnetic tail, moving from the dayside to
the nightside of Earth, see ﬁgure 2.4.1. This changes the shape and increases the
pressure in the magnetotail, also increasing reconnection rates here. This builds up
magnetic energy, that will release at a certain point, causing a polar substorm: a few

hours of drastically increased magnetic ﬁeld in the polar areas. This usually leads to
Aurora, but can also be damaging for electronics in the polar cap. This can happen

by the Field-Aligned Currents (FAC). The ring current will be ampliﬁed, increasing
magnetic disturbances on the ground. This is measured by the Disturbance Storm

and geomagnetic storms. The main planetary geomagnetic index, Kp is also com-

of magnetic disturbances are converted to a quasi-logarithmic scale, ranging from 0
to 9. Everything above Kp 5 constitutes a geomagnetic storm (Menvielle and Mar-

Machine Learning
This chapter will concern the Machine Learning (ML) theory needed, to understand
and implement algorithms, to predict and classify solar wind events.
3.1
Supervised Learning
Two major categories exists in ML: Supervised or Unsupervised ML. The main dif-
ference is the predicting target is supplied to the algorithm or not. In supervised ML,
one must supply both data and target, where the target is left out in unsupervised
ML. A typical unsupervised algorithm could cluster your data into groups of similar
features.
The problem presented in this study is clearly a supervised learning problem. The
goal is to use solar wind data and solar event catalogues (targets) to predict and
classify solar events. Hence, the problem at hand is simplest described as:
y = f(X)
(3.1.1)
Where the data, X, must be mapped by some algorithm, f, to match a target, y.
This mapping function is determined by the algorithm. Typically, an error estimate
is also present in the mapping (Brownlee 2016).
Several algorithms will be used and compared in this study. One major diﬀerence
between the algorithms is if they can be categorised as "black-box" or "white-box"
algorithms. A white-box algorithm allows you to investigate the road from data to
a ﬁnal result. In a black-box algorithm, this information is not accessible.
3.2

this is a linear classiﬁcation algorithm. The model aims to compare and produce

Neural Network
The two former algorithms are "simple" in the sense that they can be understood
intuitively, and the road from data to a result can be mapped and showed. However,
if one presented a very complex process, such as language or image classiﬁcation,
these algorithms will probably not suﬃce, due to the high complexity of the data.
This has lead to a new branch of machine learning that is inspired by the human
23

Section 3.4
brain: artiﬁcial neural networks (NN). These are powerful and versatile and especially
ideal to solve highly complex systems.
The idea of neural networks for computing dates back to 1943. However, only in the
last tens of years, the amount of data and computing power have reached a point,
where these networks can shine (Géron 2017)
As the brain consists of millions of small neurons, a neural network also consists
of several units, typically called nodes or neurons (after the brain). A simple node
can be seen in ﬁgure 3.4.1. A single node consists of a certain amount of inputs.
Figure 3.4.1: Basic Neural Network node/neuron converting inputs to output through
weights and an input function (Skymind 2019)
These are multiplied with weights, that will amplify or dampen the signal.
The
weights come from training, to match the ML problem at hand. These products are
combined by the input function and passed on to the activation function, that will
determine if this node is be activated or not (similar to a neuron "ﬁring" in a brain).
In the simplest sense, the output can be a 1 or a 0, for activated or not. Typically,
however, the output is continuous, giving a probability for a node to be activated
based on the data. This activation function is any type of step-function, that returns
a certain probability, based on the input. The choice of activation function depends
on the location in the network. Typically, the ReLU function: f(x) = max(0, x) is
used. It only activates a node, if the input function returns a positive value. This
activation is quick to calculate and promotes model sparsity, reducing overﬁtting,
(Liu 2017). At the end of a network, the activation is typically chosen to match your
problem. So for a classiﬁcation task, the softmax function mentioned earlier is ideal
(Skymind 2019).

A neural network is a combination of several of these nodes, typically in layers. These
layers work collectively to classify the data. A simple example of a layered structure
is the feed-forward network, see ﬁgure 3.4.2. Each circle is a node, and the arrows
serve as the connections between them. These connections are also weighted, as in

function. Adding a second layer, convex lines can be drawn instead, increasing the
complexity of the decision boundaries as several layers are added (Brownlee 2018).
Already at this point, there are several options to tune the network to your speciﬁc
task. Using layers, each new layer will work on the result of the previous. Eﬀectively,
this means that each new layer will see more complex features, thereby learning
deeper features in the data (Skymind 2019).

This is where Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) comes in handy. These net-
works are again inspired by a biological process: this time the human eye, and the
ability to perceive objects from images. The human eye is excellent at detecting
edges and patterns, and the CNN setup is designed to do this exactly. Figure 3.4.3a
shows a simpliﬁed example of the process. Compared to the aforementioned fully
connected NN, CNN has a limited amount of connections between each layer. This
allows for a network that only passes on important features of e.g. an image. The
connections are created by letting a window of a certain size move across the image.
A function summarises each window to a single value. For images, this would capture
simple features like edges or colour gradients. The more layers in the network, the
higher order features are seen. The connections are created according to the task at
hand.
A CNN is often the go-to neural network when one wishes to classify images. With
fully connected layers, it can be vital to preprocess your data set in regards to noise
and measurement errors. But in a CNN, the nature of the algorithm will attempt to
only pick important features, thereby being less prone to noise and errors.
Furthermore, they are highly customisable in regards to the size of the convolutions
and how many features should be passed on. Typically, one wishes to reduce the
number of features, only keeping important ones, through several layers.
This corresponds to converting an image of 28 × 28 pixels to e.g. 4 × 4 pixels, now
only containing the important features. Figure 3.4.3b shows a very simple example
of this process. A 4×4 image is reduced to 2×2 pixels. To create ﬁnal predictions, a
fully connected layer is applied to this much smaller image and classiﬁcations can be
produced. The fully connected layer now only sees the high-level features, important
for solving the problem at hand (Géron 2017).
In this study, these CNNs will be a vital part of the solution. The algorithm will
see 1-Dimensional images in the form of time-series with solar wind features corre-
sponding to RGB channels in an image. This means, that for each observation, the
algorithm will "layer" the solar wind features, and ﬁnd crucial features correspond-
ing to the classiﬁcation task. This will happen over the entire data-set, hopefully
identifying the most crucial features needed to predict CMEs and SIRs (Géron 2017).

Performance measures
To be able to compare diﬀerent machine learning algorithms, performance measures
must be established. Four main parameters are used to deﬁne the diﬀerent measures:
• TP: True positives: Positive prediction; Actually positive.
• TN: True negatives: Negative prediction; Actually negative
• FP: False positive: Predicted positive; Actually negative.
• FN: False negative: Predicted negative; Actually positive.
From these, the most typical and intuitive measure is Accuracy:
Accuracy =
TP + TN
TP + FN + TN + FP

This is the percentage of correct predictions in the entire data set. However, this is
heavily aﬀected by the class distribution of the data. In a data set with events 10%
of the time and no-events 90% of the time, a classiﬁer that classiﬁes everything as
no-event is 90% accurate, which sounds like a great classiﬁer. Hence, more speciﬁc
measures are needed.
Two of these are the Precision measure and the Recall
measure. Precision is how many of the predicted positives that are truly positive;
how many selected items that are relevant. The Recall, however, is the number of
positive predictions out of the total amount of the positive class; hence how many

subset. The algorithm becomes too familiar with the training set, achieving much
higher performance compared to what should be expected. To calculate a better

idea is to split the entire dataset into a training set and a test set. The test set is
supposed to be hidden away until a ﬁnal model has been created. This can produce

To train a model, one performs another cross-validation on the training subset. This
entails picking e.g. 80% of the training subset and using that for training a model.

model. This produces an error estimate on data that is not seen by the algorithm to
avoid unintentionally good performance from overﬁtting. A new 80% / 20% split is
generated from the training subset, giving a new error estimate. This is done K times
and the mean of these error estimates is a good indicator of the performance one
can expect of the algorithm. The best of these models are ﬁnally used for analysis
on the hidden data set. Figure 3.6.1 shows this idea.
The choice of cross-validation algorithm also has an impact on the performance of
the algorithm, especially in the case of classiﬁcation tasks with unbalanced classes.
In this case, it is important to stratify the cross-validation subsets in such a way,
that the actual class distribution is represented in all subsets. This way, one can
achieve the fairest error estimate.

Data
This chapter will describe the relevant sources of data used for the study.
Two
separate things are going to be important in creating a ML algorithm that can

4.1
OMNI data
The most comprehensive collection of data for solar weather phenomena is the OMNI
dataset located at https://omniweb.gsfc.nasa.gov/. This web page contains a
collection of data from multiple satellites, focusing on observing space weather near
Earth. It also contains collections of Earth-related phenomena that can be a conse-
quence of certain space weather events.
The OMNI dataset is created from several solar wind satellites, ensuring that the
data is of the highest quality. Furthermore, the data is shifted to the bow-shock of
the magnetosphere. This ensures the best possible conditions to study solar wind
- magnetosphere couplings. However, this shift also introduces a slight risk of mis-
alignment between features. This is not thought to be a source of error, due to the
widespread use of the OMNI set. The typical shift is anywhere between 15 minutes
and two hours, typically around 30-60 minutes (King and Papitashvili 2019).
In the search for potentially geoeﬀective solar events, especially magnetic and plasma
data are important, as presented in chapter 2. To have the most potential for train-
ing a ML algorithm that can predict solar wind events as early as possible, data at
1-minute resolution is used in this study.

4.2
ICME catalogues
The ﬁrst important catalogue is the Richardson & Cane (RC) ICME Catalogue.
This catalogue aims to summarise ICMEs, that have aﬀected Earth or the near-
Earth surroundings. The catalogue is created from a thorough analysis of solar wind
parameters, many of which are also presented in this study. This includes, but is not
limited to: the expected temperature, Tex, the magnetic ﬁeld, it’s vector components
and it’s ﬂuctuations and plasma data, mainly to search for shocks. The solar wind
parameters they analyse are from satellites that are included in the OMNI dataset,

primarily ACE. This means, that the ICME indicators used by the two authors
should be present in the data in this study. The trained machine learning algorithm
will, therefore, be able to identify these indicators and learn from them.
The RC catalogue provides an estimate of the disturbance start time of ICMEs
- that is when the shock reaches the observer. Furthermore, an estimate of when
the coronal plasma reaches the viewer, and when the disturbance has ended is also
supplied (H. Cane and I. Richardson 2003). In this study, especially the disturbance
start time is interesting since this is key to identifying ICMEs as early as possible.
The catalogue contains a total of 457 events from 1996 to 2018. Each event lasts an
average of 38 hours.
4.3
SIR catalogues
For the SIR catalogues, two catalogues are presented. Both of these rely heavily on
the evolution of the transverse pressure, Pt as mentioned in chapter 2. L. Jian et al.
2006 has created a catalogue of SIR’s from 1995-2009. This catalogue is created
by manually looking through the data, similar to what RC did with ICMEs. This
has its advantages and disadvantages, mainly regarding how easy a mathematical
algorithm could detect the same events. The catalogue has a total of 575 events.
The events have an average time of 40 hours. Susanne Vennerstrøm has made a
catalogue over SIR events utilising an automatic approach. The criteria are similar
to that of Jians. This catalogue has also been used in this study. This catalogue
consists of 1023 events during 1995 - 2017. Each event averages to 30 hours. (Krauss
et al. 2018)
4.4

hour timestep solar wind data and provides a classiﬁcation of events if any. This
is done by a decision-tree-like implementation, where several solar wind parameters
are analysed for patterns. These patterns can be matched with event types.
A total of 907 SIRs with an average of 18.8 hours and 457 ICMEs with an average

Scaling the data
The diﬀerent features of the solar wind data lie on very diﬀerent numerical scales.

To be able to eﬀectively make a ML algorithm understand the correlations in the
data, one must normalise with respect to the standard deviation and the mean (Géron
2017). This gives the new scaled attribute x′:

Furthermore, having the features on equal scales allows one to compare weights and
coeﬃcients obtained from several algorithms, which is a vital part later in this study.

5.2.1
Division of training and test sets
After the data is selected, a division of training and test data must be created. As
presented in the theory section, this division is vital to produce a generalised error
estimate of the algorithms tested. Following the idea shown in ﬁgure 3.6.1 and 3.6.2,
a test set is created and never used for any algorithm training. This test set consists
of all data from 2017 and forward, hence the last two years of data. This test subset
will be used for all ﬁnal testing of algorithms.
Furthermore, during training, the training subset will be split using cross-validation
implemented in scikit-learn’s StratiﬁedKFold. This is done to produce the fairest
classiﬁcation estimate (ScikitLearn 2019[b]).
The training subset will be split 4
times, giving a 75% / 25% division of training and validation data. In general, this
gives around 750 events for training and 250 events for validation. Typically, this is
combined with an equal amount of No Events, which are selected at random from
data, where the chosen catalogues do not indicate any event. The split themselves
are randomly selected, again to ensure a generalised error estimate, while keeping

5.2.2
Machine Learning algorithm setup
Finally, before results can be presented, the setup of the diﬀerent ML algorithms
must be reviewed. An important mention here is that none of the models is perfectly
tuned. For all models, a select few parameters have been tested. To be able to make
a complete overlook over the best models and best parameters, several weeks of study
is needed for every single algorithm. The focus was instead chosen to be an analysis
of ML’s ability to, in general, predict solar wind events. Alongside this, a thorough
analysis of the correct and incorrect classiﬁcation was thought more important than
ﬁnetuning algorithms. This is presented in the coming chapters.
Logistic regression
The logistic regression is implemented through scikit-learns interface (Scikit-Learn
2019). For most purposes, the algorithm will be trained with L1 penalty, as presented
in the Theory chapter. To do this, the SAGA solver is used. This is a fast gradient
method that supports L1 penalty. A further description is not presented in this
study, due to it not being an important part of the goal, (Defazio et al. 2014).
Lastly, the number of iterations must be set. The optimal amount was not found
here. However, for most cases, 150 iterations are used. This is equal to the number of
iterations/epochs used in the neural networks; an attempt to ensure a fair comparison
standpoint. The concept of epochs is described in a moment.
Random Forest
The random forest model has a vast amount of parameters to tune regarding how
the individual trees are created, and how they supply to the ﬁnal decision making.
The implementation is again done using scikit-learn, (ScikitLearn 2019[a]). The in-
dividual trees have not been tuned, and the standard parameters are used. However,
the amount of trees used is tunable and has a high impact on both performance
and training time. Typically, 100 trees were found to do very well. The information
criteria is the gini criteria, as presented earlier.
Convolutional Neural Network
Lastly, the neural networks also need to be set up. The combinations for a model
here are endless. Where scikit-learn supports several pre-made algorithms with easy-
to-understand parameters, the neural networks are built up in layers. Each of these
45
==PAGE:52==
Section 5.2
layers consists of its own set of unique options. Depending on the layers used and
how they are combined, a completely new model is created.
As presented, the NN for this study is the Convolutional Neural Networks. These
consist of several layers of convolutions and pooling to reduce the number of features
to certain important ones, that will classify your problem.
Two major options to consider is the number of epochs and the batch-size for each
epoch. An epoch has passed when the algorithm has completed one full view of the
data at hand. The batch size is how many observations are seen at a time. The batch
size poses a trade-oﬀbetween computational time and convergence of the model. A
very small batch size takes a long time to calculate since this increases the total
amount of batches needed to go through all the data. At the same time, the small
batches converge faster, simply because they contain fewer data. For each epoch, the
weights of the algorithm are trained to solve the problem. However, due to the nature
of gradient descent, which is the general backbone of weight optimisation, several
epochs are needed to update the weights to achieve a good model. Repeatedly going
through all data allows the algorithm to update the weights to ﬁnd the best possible
model. However, too many epochs result in overﬁtting, similarly to applying too
many trees in Random Forest (Sharma 2017).
The number of epochs is set to 150, with a batch size of 60. This was found to
equal a great tradeoﬀbetween training time, accuracy and reduction of overﬁtting.
Furthermore, the model improvements were non-existent past this point.

and poolings. The convolutions search for the relevant features, while the poolings
reduce the total amount.

The table shows how the shape changes throughout the algorithm, as well as the

Basis for Machine Learning
Before showing the results of the ML algorithms, one question needs to be answered.
"Is the problem possible to solve?".
A short analysis is carried out in order to
show diﬀerences in the solar wind data split into the 3 classes available: ICME,
SIR and No Event. Figure 6.1.1 shows the distribution of solar wind data in the
3 classes. Shown is the ﬁrst 2 hours of the event types, as given by the catalogues
described previously. The two event types are compared with the No Event class in
order to detect diﬀerences in all distributions. The individual event histograms are
normalised for comparative reasons. Fortunately, there are several clear distinctions.
In all parameters, ICMEs contain the highest values. This is most likely due to the
shocks leading many ICMEs. Furthermore, the ﬁrst hours of ICMEs include a sheath,
that also typically contains high values of most magnetic and plasma parameters;
remember ﬁgure 2.2.2. The SIRs also show clear distinctions. They contain higher
magnetic ﬁelds, number densities and pressures than the No Event class, but lower
than the ICME class. This matches well with the ﬁrst hours of SIRs being a gradual
increase in magnetic and plasma parameters, due to the compression in front of the
stream interaction.
One can conclude, that these clear distinctions in distributions should be something
a ML algorithm can detect and use for classiﬁcation!

Performance of Machine Learning - a general
result
Four models have been trained and tested throughout this study. Three have al-
ready been introduced: The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), the Random
Forest (RF) and the Multinomial Logistic Regression (MLR). The ﬁnal model is a
sparse version of the CNN model, using only certain important features. The choice
of these features will be described in detail in a later section - however, the result
from the model is still included for comparison.
The four tested models have been trained using similar parameters, to ensure a fair
comparison. This includes training the models for equal amounts of iterations. The
presented performance measure are all based on cross-validation averages on the val-
idation data set. The settings used for creating the data windows correspond to the
base values in table 5.3.1.
Table 6.2.1 and ﬁgure 6.2.1 shows the performance measures and confusion matrices
for all 4 models. Note that the performance measures have been created by using
Stratiﬁed Cross-validation with 75% training data and 25% validation data. The
measures are an average of 20 random picks of validation and training data, along-
side their 95% conﬁdence interval. Note, however, that the individual splits are still
correlated since all events are used for every split, just in a diﬀerent combination
52
==PAGE:59==

of training and test. Several papers and books regarding the bias in K-fold cross-
validation agree on one thing: it is very diﬃcult, if not impossible to generate an
unbiased estimator of performance on K-fold cross-validation, (Bengio and Grand-
valet 2004; Bengio and Grandvalet 2005). Hence, for the purpose of showing general
performance, mean and 95% conﬁdence intervals are shown in both tables and con-
fusion matrices. This is considered a decent comparison standpoint.
Looking at the tables 6.2.1, the positive/negative tag refers to if the measure is taken
on the positive or negative of the corresponding class. Hence a negative ICME F1
score describes how well the algorithm predicts times of no ICMEs only.
Highlighted in the table is the highest of certain scores in all 4 models. Of impor-
tance here is especially the accuracy and F1 scores. The Sparse CNN clearly has
the highest accuracy, followed by the non-sparse CNN. Both RF and MLR scores
below 80% accuracy. Furthermore, the Sparse CNN seems to outperform the rest at
positive events, scoring slightly higher in F1 (positive) than the other models. Note,
however, that many models will still coincide within the 95% conﬁdence interval.
Looking at the confusion matrices, one can attempt to highlight the performance of
the model in terms of the predictions vs true classes, remember the theory from 3.5.
Looking only at true positives, Random Forest has the highest amount of correct
ICME predictions. The Sparse CNN predicts SIRs best and the MLR model pre-
dicts No Events best. However, it is vital to also look at the false positive and false
negatives. Both CNNs have a very low false positive rate of ICMEs. However, they
both have a relatively high false negative rate as well. This means that the amount
of false alarms is very low, however, the algorithm also misses some events. The RF
model has a much higher false positive rate, which in turn also increases it’s correct
ICME predictions - it is simply more inclined to predict events.
The reason for the higher accuracy on the Sparse CNN is due to the amount of
correctly predicted SIRs and No Events. These classes are larger, posing a larger
inﬂuence on accuracy. In Appendix, ﬁgure A1 similar ﬁgures are shown for the same
predictions, however with counts rather than percentages.
These results will be used in conjunction with the coming, in order to choose one
ﬁnal model. An analysis of the incorrect predictions will also be shown.

Performance when changing major parameters
for selecting data
This section will cover how the performance of ML algorithms changes while varying
certain key parameters. The chosen parameters all have a large inﬂuence on the data
set and are vital to deﬁning the problem the algorithm needs to solve. Therefore it
is also vital to understanding how well the algorithms could do if implemented in
a real-life scenario. All parameters are tested independently, only varying one at a
time. The remaining are ﬁxed according to table 5.3.1.
6.3.1
Interpolation limit
A major parameter presented from the Method chapter is the interpolation limit. It
was shown that this heavily inﬂuenced the data seen by the algorithms, as well as
how much data was available for training. Therefore the performance while changing
this parameter has been investigated. The result is seen in ﬁgure 6.3.1. The ﬁgure is
split into three subﬁgures. The top ﬁgure shows the average accuracy of the tested
models. The colour deﬁnes the tested model and the shaded areas the 95% conﬁdence
interval. The two bottom ﬁgures show scores for the individual classes: ICME, SIR
and No Events split into the AUC score and the F1 score. The marker types depict
the three classes. In all scores, higher is better.
At ﬁrst glance, it seems like the interpolation has no clear eﬀect on the performance.
However, using less than 10 minutes, it is clear that the uncertainty of the accuracy
is larger. This matches well with the increase in the amount of data allowed, as was
shown in table 5.1.1. Simply, more data makes a more certain performance measure.
This, therefore, stands to show, that too little data have a risk of impacting the
performance negatively. However, the ﬁgure also shows that there is no performance-
based reasoning to set a very large interpolation limit. Increasing the limit above
e.g. 12 minutes will slowly increase the amount of data. This "releases" the last 20%
of ICMEs and SIRs, while not providing any noticeable diﬀerence in performance.
Therefore one can choose the physically feasible solution, as mentioned earlier since
it has no impact on performance! A 12 minute interpolation is chosen.
This could introduce a new question though: "Why does a large interpolation not
worsen the performance?". It was clear, that a large interpolation limit could erase all
the information about event starts. Therefore it would be intuitive, that these events
would cause errors in classiﬁcations. However, since these only constitute the last
20% of the dataset it is not enough to drive the performance down! The algorithm

6.3.2
Predicting events before they reach Earth
Another major point of interest is determining if events can be predicted before they
reach Earth. Remembering the selection method in ﬁgure 5.1.4, one can customise
the problem at will. Keeping the event answer ﬁxed 120 minutes into an event, the
data window can be moved backwards in time. The start of this window aligns with
the listed start time from the chosen catalogues. Moving it, however, changes the
problem the algorithm must solve. Moving it e.g. 10 minutes backwards means that
the ﬁrst 10 minutes of data it sees is before an event, with the remaining 110 minutes
being the event. This timing is tested, increasing this shift by more and more. At a
certain point, one would see nothing of the event and still be trying to predict it.
This test can reveal if ML can detect any form of precursors of events. Furthermore,
it can show how many minutes of an event is needed to create a sensible prediction.
The earlier a prediction is created, the earlier a warning can be sent out if imple-
mented as an alarm system.
The result of this analysis is seen in ﬁgure 6.3.2. Similar to before, the ﬁgure is split
into three subﬁgures.
First, it is clear that there is no distinguishable diﬀerence in accuracy when going
from 0 minutes ahead and to around 40-50 minutes ahead. This result in itself is very
interesting. This indicates, that somewhere in the ﬁrst 70-80 minutes of an event, a
deﬁning structure appears, that the algorithms can use for classiﬁcation. Note that
the input windows are 120 minutes in this case.
Trying to move the window further backwards in time, all prediction performances
are dropping. They keep dropping until they reach a certain threshold. Investigat-
ing the individual scores, it is clear that the ICME prediction drops furthest, unto
a point where no positive ICME predictions exist. This is seen as a 0 F1 score and
an AUC score close to 0.5. This can be explained by the shocks of the ICMEs, not
being part of the data selection anymore.
At this point, the accuracy has dropped to levels around 50-60%, which is very close
to the baseline accuracy of ∼58%, seen as the black dotted line. This baseline is
merely the performance if all events are predicted as No Events. All predictions are
therefore expected to be No Event now. However, the SIR class achieves an F1 score
of 0.4 even when predicting several hours into the future. This is not sensible! The
reason is probably that SIRs are typically categorised by their increase, peak and
decrease. The peak usually happens several hours into the event. Seeing only the
ﬁrst two hours, there is a chance, no pattern is showing. The same (non-existent)
pattern is present several hours before the SIR. That’s why the No Event and SIR

prediction coincide. This also means, that No Events and SIRs seems to have a
tendency to be falsely classiﬁed as each other; at least in the case of looking hours
the future.
Besides showing the performance of predicting ahead of time, this also stands to
show an important point about trivial predictions. A trivial prediction is easiest
understood in terms of regression. If one, for example, tried to predict the tem-
perature of tomorrow using the last 10 days of data, a trivial prediction would just
predict tomorrow’s temperature exactly as today’s. The algorithm is not using any
information about the preceding days.
A trivial prediction in the case of this scenario could be, that the algorithm learned
from the f10.7 index. This is directly correlated with the sunspot number, and there-
fore also with the solar activity. It would be reasonable to assume that events are
more likely to occur at high solar activity. Hence, if the algorithm always predicted
an event at a high sunspot number, it would be a trivial prediction. If this was the
case, the performance would NOT change, when predicting further into the future.
This ﬁgure shows, that the algorithms do learn crucial aspects of the data. When
the events are not a part of the selected data window, these aspects are gone, and
the performance clearly drops.

6.3.4
Summarising remarks
Three key parameters, vital to deﬁning the problem a ML tries to solve, have been
tested. They are tested as independent parameters. These results supply the basic
parameters for future model runs: An interpolation limit of 12 minutes and a window
size of 120 minutes. As a general rule, all event windows start according to the
catalogues chosen. This maximises the learning potential for classifying event starts.
However, it is clear that the performance on these parameters cannot be seen as
independent. The future prediction was clearly correlated with the window length
of 120 minutes. To ﬁnd the best parameters, one would have to create an exhaustive
search of all parameter combination; something that is not possible within the time
frame of this study.
Besides that, this section stands to show, that there are an unending amount of
possibilities, merely for selecting the data. The choice of parameters shows that ML
is perfectly able to learn from solar wind data and classify solar wind events with
> 80% accuracy.

One thing is showing that ML can be used to successfully classify solar wind data
as SIRs and ICMEs. However, trying to unveil the algorithm’s road from data to
classiﬁcation could be able to provide new information, both statistical and physical,
for future research in this area. Being able to see which features the algorithms prefer
and which they don’t can help create a more sparse, focused model. Furthermore,
it might also reveal physical properties important for event detection, not currently
known.
However, as some of the tested algorithms are "black-box" algorithms, it can be dif-
ﬁcult to directly assess which features it prefers. It is therefore not possible to gain
any reasonable feature importance from the CNNs - they are simply too complex.
Though, seeing as the MLR models tend to perform close to the neural networks,
this model will be investigated. As introduced in the theory section, the logistic
regression models create weights, that can be used for classiﬁcation by simple linear
algebra, remember equation 3.2.5. This means, that these weights are directly trans-
latable to feature importance. Since the data is normalised, all features have the
same mean and standard deviation, allowing for direct comparison of coeﬃcients. A
high weight on a feature will produce a high probability for an event!
Furthermore, implementing the logistic regression using the L1 regularisation term,
it is possible to investigate in which order these features become important to the
algorithm. Having a highly regularised model, all coeﬃcients will be zero and all
predictions No Event. Reducing the regularisation in steps slowly introduces more
and more features by allowing more information into the model. This is used to
investigate, which features are important for a sparse model - hence also, which
features contain the most information in creating a good performing model, while
limited to a certain amount of information.
120 weights exist for each feature for each regularisation value, one for each of the
120 minutes of input data. These are summed and normalised to be able to create a
fair comparison between the 3 classes. However, note that this means that for each
regularisation limit, the sum of the coeﬃcients is 1. If only one active feature exists,
it’s coeﬃcient will be 1.
There are two important options to be taken into consideration when considering
the feature importance. First of all, the choice of classifying in a one-vs-rest scheme
or in a multinomial scheme. The one-vs-rest scheme will take a class, e.g. ICME
and compare to all Non-ICMEs. That is both SIRs and No Events. This can reveal
how to distinguish ICMEs from anything else. On the other hand, the multinomial
65
==PAGE:72==
Section 6.4
scheme will attempt to weight all 3 classes against each other, forcing it to learn in-
dependent feature combinations for each class. Depending on this choice, the feature
importance outcome might be diﬀerent.
The other important option is how much weight each class attains in the model. The
base setting sets equal weight to all classes, so 1 ICME is weighted equally to 1 SIR.
Naturally, this means that the algorithms will focus on the large classes since the loss
function will be reduced most by correctly classifying these. The other possibility is
setting a balanced weight that reﬂects the relative size of the classes. In this case,
1 ICME might be weighted equal to 4 SIRs, since there are 4 times the amount of
SIRs as ICMEs. This will naturally change the order features are important in.
Figure 6.4.1 and 6.4.2 shows the multinomial logistic regression case, with equal
weights and with balanced weights respectively. The vertical axis shows the inverse
of the regularisation coeﬃcient: smaller number is larger regularisation. Or set in
another way, a smaller number allows less information in the model. The rows split
up the feature importance for each type of event: ICME, SIR and No Event. The
columns are the positive and negative coeﬃcients split. This is done to emphasise
what is increasing and decreasing the probability of the respective class. As a general
rule, a positive coeﬃcient will increase the probability of that class, for that feature,
whereas a negative coeﬃcient will decrease that probability.
From the top of the y-axis, the least information is allowed. Reaching the magenta
dotted line indicates the regularisation that corresponds to models with more than
75% accuracy. Hence, all models above the magenta line will have worse than 75%
accuracy, due to higher regularisation / less information.
Starting in ﬁgure 6.4.1, it is very clear that the variance of velocity and magnetic
ﬁeld is most important for a highly regularised model, predicting ICMEs. Reaching
the magenta line, Pt also becomes important. Allowing more information into the
model, the feature importance is split more equally to several features, with no single
deﬁning features, hence why the individual sums get close to 0. This behaviour is
seen in all cases. It is also clear, that Borovskys solar-wind classes 3 and 4 decreases
the probability of ICMEs.
For SIRs the most important feature is singularly the proton density, Np. The vari-
ance of velocity is reducing the probability of SIRs.
Lastly, for the No Event category, The Borovsky classes, excluding class 1, seem to
increase the probability of No Events. At the same time, high values of the mag-
netic ﬁeld, proton density, dynamic pressure and transverse pressure all reduce the
probability of a No Event scenario.

Look into the actual predictions
It is time to switch focus from performance measures to visualisation on satellite
data. To do this, a ﬁnal model must be chosen. During this study, several have been
shown and tested. However, through testing several tuning parameters, as presented,
the sparse CNN model performed best. It slightly outperformed the other models
in general performance with 82.2% accuracy. Furthermore, it showed very few false
positives, which is vital in critical alarm systems. It showed some false negatives,
but these are investigated here. It also showed the best performance in all cases
while changing important parameters, as shown in section 6.3. This model is used
to present the results for this section. The parameters used for the sparse model
is |B|, Np, Dynamic pressure, magnetic pressure, transverse pressure, variance in
velocity and magnetic ﬁeld and Borovskys solar-wind classes. These are the main
features found in the feature importance analysis. This reduces the total amount of
features from 25 to 8. The chosen features all showed high feature importance on a
regularised model.

The potential of ML has been showcased in several events in the previous section.
Most classiﬁcations are correct. However, some errors also exist, partly due to cata-

This was from the validation data set.
So far the "hidden" data set have not been seen or analysed. This is the solar wind
data between 2017 and 2018. No algorithms have been trained on this part of the
data. This introduces the possibility of gaining a generalised error on "new" data.
To check the performance, the sparse CNN is again used. At the beginning of this
chapter, 20 sparse CNN models were trained using K-fold cross-validation. The best
of these models (based on the performance on the validation set) is picked and used
to check the performance. The data from these last two years is selected in the usual
way to compare the performance with the validation dataset. This summary will
establish if the algorithm actually learned from the solar wind data, and is able to
use that on an unseen dataset.
Table 6.6.1 and ﬁgure 6.6.1 shows the performance tables and confusion matrices for
the test dataset. In both cases, the performance on the best validation split mea-

6.7
Real-time data test
A last and very important aspect to consider is how well the algorithm can per-
form in a near real-time setting. The OMNI dataset that has been used so far is
a processed dataset, where major errors have been removed and noisy bits cleaned.
However, if a model is to be implemented as a warning measure for solar wind events,
it must work on data directly from the satellite. This data is typically noisier and
can contain more errors. The algorithm must be able to handle that, otherwise, it
will send out false alarms.
Data from the DSCOVR satellite is available and has been supplied by Susanne
Vennerstrøm for this study. 2 months of data from the 18th of March to the 19th
of May, 2019 is used. The data available is the vector components of the magnetic
ﬁeld, as well as the temperature, proton density and ﬂow speed. Using these, all the
features of the sparse CNN model can be calculated and used.
To be able to verify if the predictions created are correct or incorrect, some form of
catalogue is also needed. Fortunately, DONKI exists. From this collection of solar
wind data interpretations, a list of interplanetary shocks in connection with ICMEs
can be found. Furthermore, a list of HSSs is available, which is directly linked to
SIR events. These will be used as conﬁrmation of the classiﬁcation on the data set.

DONKI with blue being interplanetary shocks and orange being HSS. Similarly, the
probabilities in the bottom subﬁgure show the probability of each class in the re-
spective colour; green being No Event.
Taking a quick look into the predictions, it seems clear, that when a dotted line is
present, the probability of No Events (the green line) is deﬁnitely close to 0. This is
a good tendency! However further investigation of the individual events is needed.

6.8
Summary
A short summary is needed to highlight the important points of the results of this
study. First of all, it is vital to note, that the number of options to create the dataset
and to construct the ML models are unending. Several choices were analysed, and
both the interpolation limit, the window size and the ability to predict into the future
were investigated. The ﬁnal model choices have a basis in both ML performance and
physical meaning.
Four models were tested, all performing close to 80% accuracy with minor diﬀer-
ences. Analysis showed a clear division in solar wind data for correct and incorrect
classiﬁcations, especially for ICMEs. This also means, that there are improvements
to be made, mainly regarding the weaker solar wind events.
The Sparse Convolutional Neural Network was found to have the highest accuracy
in most cases and was used to analyse both the ﬁnal test-set and a dataset from
DSCOVR. The model achieved 82.2% accuracy with very few false alarms on the av-
erage validation set and 82.4% accuracy on the test set, mainly due to SIRs and No
Events. The correct and incorrect classiﬁcations were analysed, showing two similar
conclusions: First, it was clear, that the algorithm has a diﬃcult time predicting
weak events. Furthermore, clear contradictions in catalogues were found, which was
also visible in the predictions of the algorithm. Especially ICMEs that do not follow
the typical shock/sheath structure early was diﬃcult to classify.
With a general accuracy above 80%, there is a clear incentive to continue developing
algorithms like this! However, more training is needed to reduce the false negatives
encountered.

Discussion
7.1
Using data windows
The choice of utilising data windows to present solar wind events has the advantages
of being very versatile, resulting in easy-to-interpret results. However, due to the
limitations posed by my interpolation limit, as well as the limit to the amount of No
Events selected, some solar wind data is never seen by the algorithm. It highlights
the speciﬁc problem of classifying event starts but disregards some of the general
solar wind structure. One could have used much larger time-series of several days,
months or even years, training an algorithm that sees larger temporal structures in
the solar wind. This could have been achieved with a Long Short-Term Memory
Neural Network which is specialised in understanding temporal aspects of long time
series (Géron 2017). This would, however, focus on complete events rather than
event starts. Furthermore, the possibility to produce feature importance, represent-
ing physical parameters linked to event starts, would not be possible.
Remembering table 5.3.1, only certain parameters vital to the data set were inves-
tigated. Diﬀerent setups of catalogues could have improved the algorithms under-
standing of the classes. The ratio of Event to No Events was also kept throughout
the study. Selecting more No Events could have reduced some of the false alarms
presented in the previous section. Simultaneously however this could increase the
number of false negatives, by putting more weight on No Events.
The ﬁnal reasoning for the choices is the focus on detecting solar wind event as early
as possible while being able to clearly interpret the results.

the physical properties of the events visible in the solar wind data.
7.3
Potential for learning whole events
Having shown the potential for predicting event starts, it is natural to think about
ongoing events.
As was seen in ﬁgure 6.5.2a, a SIR start is correctly predicted.
However, a few hours into the event, the algorithm believes it sees an ICME. This
behaviour could be improved by training a new algorithm to learn ongoing events.
This would entail learning how the ejecta of ICMEs presents itself in the solar wind,
as well as learning the evolution of SIRs when it reaches the compression ridge. A
setup could be created of one algorithm predicting event starts and another to predict
if the event is still going. This can help to increase our understanding of these events,
and help expand the catalogues of ICMEs and SIRs, without the need for human
interaction! A successful implementation would provide new possibilities in using
87
==PAGE:94==
Section 7.4
near real-time data to provide alarms, while also updating these catalogues. This
can be used in any aspect of science that is aﬀected by space weather disturbances.
Furthermore, this allows for constant training of ML on new data, further increasing
its predictive capabilities.
7.4
Alarm system and model improvements
Finally, some thoughts about the potential of implementing a ML algorithm as an
alarm system for space weather. The algorithm is able to create predictions on satel-
lite data, even with data gaps. It outputs probabilities which can be used as a part
of an alarm system. And in many cases, it classiﬁes events correctly. There is a clear
potential, which has been highlighted in this study.
It does, however, require further improvement to make it even more certain. The
amount of false alarms is fortunately low. The amount of false negatives, however,
is still relatively high. Figure 6.2.1 showed 13% true ICMEs and 15% true SIRs
predicted as No Events. In total, a quarter of the events are events that are not
detected! This is too many if the algorithm were to be used as a stand-alone alarm
system. Analysis did show that a part of the missed events was due to them being
weak. The importance of correctly predicting these are naturally less, compared to
the strong events. The histogram in ﬁgure 6.2.2 showed that correct predictions were
related to larger solar wind parameters.
A possible solution is to set a lower limit to the probability before an alarm is sent.
Requiring e.g. at least 30 minutes or more of one class classiﬁcation could help re-
duce classiﬁcations and false alarms. Figure 6.5.2a and 6.7.2b both showed cases of
quickly varying predictions, which could have been avoided by a temporal limit to
predictions.
Another possibility could be to combine the current model with a model trained to
only detect Events vs No Events. This could reduce the number of false alarms since
the new model is specialised in ﬁnding both ICMEs and SIRs. This combination
could improve the predictive capabilities of the model.
7.5
Geomagnetic activity
It is also important to clarify the impact of incorrect classiﬁcations in terms of
geomagnetic activity. There is a clear diﬀerence in an ICME being classiﬁed as a
SIR or a No Event. Both are incorrect, but the SIR prediction can still trigger alarms.
88
==PAGE:95==
Chapter 7 - Discussion
Figure 7.5.1 shows histograms of the geomagnetic activity index, Kp, as a function
of classiﬁcations. This is the maximum Kp value achieved during the event. Note
that predictions are still only made on the ﬁrst 2 hours! The left and right histogram
correspond to a true ICME or SIR, respectively, according to the catalogues. For
each class, the correct and incorrect distributions of Kp is shown. Typically, Kp
values above 5 indicate a geomagnetic storm. The ﬁgure clearly shows that events
that ended up generating a large Kp value are almost always correctly predicted!
Furthermore, in the case of incorrect predictions, events are often predicted as the
other class (hence, a true ICME is predicted a SIR). These would therefore still be
able to produce warnings! This also shows that the relatively large fraction of false
negatives shown in ﬁgure 6.2.1 is mainly due to events that would not trigger a global
geomagnetic storm. Unfortunately, some No Event predictions exist, that resulted
in a very high Kp value, especially for the true ICMEs. These would have to be the
focus for further improvement of the algorithm.
Instead of attempting to classify SIRs and ICMEs, one could simply have tried to
predict the Kp value, based on the solar wind data. It is simple to argue, that our
main care is if a global geomagnetic storm is about to happen. However, being able to
classify the event type, provides crucial information. Chen et al. 2014 showed that
ICME storms typically are more powerful than SIR storms. This could probably
be shown by predicting Kp directly. However, they also showed that SIR storms
last almost twice as long, compared to ICME driven storms. This information is
crucial in understanding the impacts the entire storm can have, rather than only
understanding the maximum geomagnetic storm potential. This is especially crucial
in understanding satellite orbit decay due to ionospheric heating.
A last and important note about the potential of an alarm system using the presented
model is its ability to forecast events. Following the analysis of the predictions ahead
in time, ﬁgure 6.3.2, it was possible to predict events with > 80% accuracy with as
little as 60 minutes of data. This is already impressive compared to current solutions.
Solutions like AWARE requires several hours of event data to produce a classiﬁcation,
especially since it functions on hourly data. Wang, Jingjing et al. 2018 presented a
semi-automated system predicting ICMEs with 60% accuracy and 30% false alarms,
generated from solar images and solar wind data. The results presented here are
fully automatic, with larger accuracy and lower false alarm rate.
Furthermore, the data used in this study is from the OMNI database, shifted to the
Earth magnetopause. This means, that working directly on the satellite data, as was
showed attempted on DSCOVR, ﬁgure 6.7.1, allows for 15-60 minutes of extra time,
before the event actually reaches the magnetopause. Global magnetic disturbances

Chapter 8 - Conclusion

Conclusion
This study presents a machine learning approach to detecting the heliospheric im-
pact of Interplanetary Coronal Mass Ejections and Stream Interaction Regions from
solar wind data. Several algorithms are presented and analysed, in an attempt to
highlight the best performing model.
A total of 25 features have been used to identify solar wind events. This includes
magnetic ﬁeld measurements, plasma data and derived parameters of these. The
data is supplied by the OMNI dataset.
Data windows are created in order to select solar wind data needed to train ML
algorithms. These are combined with Vennerstrøm’s SIR index and a combination
of Richardson & Cane’s ICME index and AWARE’s ICME classiﬁcations. This com-
bination allows for a supervised ML problem to be solved.
The selection of data windows has a clear impact on the performance of ML. A bigger
window was found to increase the ML performance, but also decreased the number
of available events. It also showed that windows as small as 60 minutes achieved
performances above 80% accuracy. This was further substantiated by investigating
the ability to predict events as early as possible. Using 2-hour windows, only the
ﬁrst 40 minutes of an event was needed to achieve > 80% accuracy. Both of these
results indicate a potential for predicting solar wind events before they reach Earth.
The best performing model is a Convolutional Neural Network trained on a sparse
data-set consisting of only 8 features. These features are found by analysing the
multinomial logistic regression weights in L1 regularised models. Eﬀectively, this
presents the few features that contain most information to predict solar wind events.
The parameters used for the sparse model is |B|, Np, Pdyn, Pmag, Pt, variance in
velocity and magnetic ﬁeld and Borovsky’s solar-wind classes. Following the analysis
in ﬁgure 7.2.1, the temporal aspect of the features were investigated. Variances in
V and B increase probabilities for ICMEs, matching the shock and sheath of said
event. The Pt is also found to indicate ICMEs, especially late in a data window.
This is again linked to sheaths, typically containing high transverse pressure due to
its chaotic state.
91
==PAGE:98==
The number density, Np, is found to indicate SIRs, following the gradual increase
typically seen as the fast solar wind stream is compressing the slow ambient wind
ahead of it. Lastly, No Events are shown to have a reduced probability in cases of
high magnetic ﬁeld, number densities and pressures.
The algorithm reaches 82.2% accuracy using cross-validation on the validation data
set. It reaches 82.4% accuracy on the test dataset, which it was not trained on. In
both cases, the number of false alarms are very low. The amount of false negatives is
around ∼15% for both cases. Especially SIR and No Events are predicted correctly,
with some ICMEs being predicted as SIRs.
The algorithm prediction was presented on solar wind data. Analysis showed, that
most incorrect predictions occurred with weak events, in regards to magnetic and
plasma data. Furthermore, it was shown that some errors were due to inconsistencies
in, or missing events in the chosen catalogues. However, an analysis of the geomag-
netic Kp index revealed, that the main occurrence of incorrect predictions resulted
in events with low Kp. Almost all events causing global storms were predicted as
either an ICME or a SIR.
The performance of the algorithm was analysed on near real-time data from DSCOVR.
This revealed a general well-performing model, however with a tendency to cause false
alarms.
The resulting model predicts accurately more than > 80% of the time, enabling the
classiﬁcation of geoeﬀective solar wind events, with as little as 2 hours of data from
satellites located in L1. In many cases, predictions can occur even before the event
reaches the magnetopause.
Following the analysis of DSCOVR data, the algorithm is believed to lack some
improvements in order to function as a stand-alone alarm system. The general pre-
diction certainty must be improved. Further training, model tuning or combination
of models can achieve this. In the current state, it can alert satellite operators to
make a manual investigation of solar wind data. It can further improve algorithms
like AWARE, increasing its ability to predict event starts and help classify events
early on.
In an improved state, there is no limit to the possibilities. The future of detecting
solar wind events using machine learning is very promising. It is deﬁnitely possible
to detect ICMEs and SIRs in solar wind data using machine learning algorithms.
