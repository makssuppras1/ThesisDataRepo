Abstract
This study assesses the environmental impact of building materials in the Greenlandic context through
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and explores the challenges and opportunities of implementing more sus-
tainable construction practices in Greenland. The research integrates both quantitative and qualitative
approaches, combining LCA of three case buildings with stakeholder interviews to gain insights into in-
dustry perspectives. Furthermore, the challenges and possibilities of a Greenlandic DGNB certification
and a related CO2 boundary are investigated.
The LCA study compares buildings built with Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT), concrete and half-
timbered concrete. The results indicate that the CLT building exhibits the lowest environmental impact
among the three building types. However, the feasibility of widespread CLT construction in Greenland
remains uncertain due to limited experience in Greenlandic context.
In addition to the LCA study, interviews with key stakeholders, including architects, clients, contractors,
and consultants reveal significant barriers to adopting sustainable construction methods. High costs,
limited availability of materials and expertise, and a lack of reliable data on sustainable construction
in Greenlandic context pose major challenges. Despite growing interest in sustainability, stakeholders
express skepticism toward implementing the DGNB certification system, primarily due to concerns about
increased construction costs, additional administrative burdens, and its relevance to Greenland’s unique
conditions.
Nonetheless, the study highlights potential benefits of adapting DGNB to Greenlandic needs, such as en-
couraging LCA data collection, better project planning aligned with seasonal constraints, reduced energy
consumption, life cycle cost, and increased use of renewable energy. Additionally, the research suggests
that establishing a CO2 boundary is currently challenging, due to large regional differences and data
limitations.
The findings emphasize the necessity of continued data collection, policy adjustments, and industry
collaboration to facilitate a transition toward more sustainable construction practices. By addressing
logistical, regulatory, and knowledge-based challenges, Greenland can develop a tailored approach to
sustainability that aligns with its environmental and economic realities.

Introduction
1
Introduction
Climate change is a global challenge also present in Greenland. The Arctic region is particularly sen-
sitive to temperature increases, with data showing that temperatures in the Arctic have risen at least
twice as fast as global averages since 2000 [1].
The building industry is the largest emitter globally
and is responsible for 37% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions [2]. This, along with urbanization
and migration, particularly to Nuuk, creates a growing demand for sustainable housing in Greenland’s
larger cities. Nuuk now houses over 30% of Greenland’s population, compared to around 20% in 1990
[3]. Moreover, international initiatives such as the EU’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive
(CSRD) set new requirements for companies to report on sustainability. These regulations highlight the
need for the Greenlandic building industry to align with global sustainability practices in order to secure
funding for future building projects [4]. Given these challenges, there is an urgent need for knowledge
of sustainable building practices in Greenland. In response, efforts are underway to adapt the DGNB
certification system to the unique context of Greenland [5].
This thesis aims to evaluate the environmental impacts of buildings with various materials in Greenland
and to explore the perspectives of stakeholders in the Greenlandic building industry on sustainable build-
ing practices. The research was conducted during a three-month stay in Sisimiut, allowing for direct
engagement with Greenland’s local building industry and cultural context. Additionally, trips to Ilulissat
and Nuuk provided further insight into the building practices across the country.
The thesis focuses on three case studies: a Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT) building in Nuuk, a concrete
building, and a half-timbered concrete building, both of which are located in Sisimiut. By conducting Life
Cycle Assessments (LCA) on these buildings, this thesis will assess the environmental impacts of different
building materials in Greenland and contribute to the discussion on establishing a CO2 boundary for the
Greenlandic DGNB framework. To further enrich our understanding of building sustainably in Greenland,
we interviewed key stakeholders in the building industry, including clients, architects, contractors, and
consultants from Ilulissat, Sisimiut, and Nuuk.

Problem Statement
2
Problem Statement
This master thesis aims to explore sustainable building practices in Greenland by investigating the envi-
ronmental impacts of different building materials and construction methods. The study will analyze the
feasibility and sustainability of these materials across different Greenlandic locations, taking into account
the unique climatic and environmental challenges of the region. A key aspect of this research will be
the evaluation of the environmental impact of a case building, which could be utilized to discuss the
establishment of a CO2 boundary within the framework of the DGNB certification system. Furthermore,
the thesis will investigate how the building industry in Greenland perceives the challenge of building
sustainably.
2.1
Learning Objectives
• Identify the challenges of building both traditional and sustainable buildings in Greenland.
• Use LCA to assess the environmental impact of different case buildings exploring the difference
between building materials.
• Analyze the current view on building sustainably in the local building industry.
• Suggest recommendations for building more sustainable buildings in Greenland.
• Discuss the establishment of a CO2 boundary within the framework of the DGNB certification
system based on case studies.
• Discuss the social and economic challenges of the Greenlandic building industry.

3
Background
This section provides the necessary background to understand the historical and current building situation
in Greenland. It also provides an overview of the energy supply in Greenland and the basics of LCA.
3.1
Introduction to Greenland
Greenland is the world’s largest island, with about 81% of the land area covered by continental glaciers.
Despite the size of the island, Greenland has a relatively small population of 56,699 inhabitants, primarily
living in coastal regions. Most of the population lives on the southwest coast, where the five largest cities
are located [6]. The capital, Nuuk, is the largest city with 19,872 inhabitants, followed by Sisimiut further
north with 5,412 residents [7]. A map of Greenland is shown in Figure 1. The 10 cities marked on the
map, along with their respective populations, are the ones analyzed later in this thesis. Additionally, the
Arctic Circle is represented by a dotted line, dividing Greenland into Zones 1 and 2. The influence of
these zones will be described later.
Figure 1: Illustration of Greenland with the 10 cities and their number of inhabitants as well as the Arctic Circle
dividing Greenland in Zones 1 and 2.

==PAGE:26==
Background
The climate in Greenland is Arctic, ranging from sub-Arctic in the far south with mean temperatures
in July of more than 10 ℃to high-Arctic in the northern and northeastern regions.
The southwest
part of Greenland, where most of the population lives, experiences relatively mild winters, strong winds,
heavy snowfall, and occasional melting periods.
In contrast, northern areas have very cold winters
with temperatures down to minus 50 ℃and summer temperatures rarely above 0 ℃. Some parts of
north Greenland have desert-like conditions, receiving only about 1% of the precipitation found in the
south. These extreme and varied weather conditions create a challenging building environment, with
significant differences across Greenlandic locations. Climate change further intensifies these challenges,
as the weather becomes increasingly unpredictable [8].
3.2
Historical Building Situation
Greenland became a part of Denmark after the Middle Ages, in 986, when Norway ceded it to Denmark.
European trading and whaling activities were emerging, and Denmark established trading centers in key
locations to utilize Greenland’s resources [9]. In 1721, Hans Egede arrived in Nuuk to begin his missionary
work. Expecting to find the Norse people, whom he presumed to be Catholics, Egede instead encountered
the Inuit. In the following years, Greenland was colonized. At the time, the Inuit lived across Greenland
in peat houses constructed from peat, rocks, and driftwood. A reconstruction of a peat house is shown in
Figure 2. These homes were typically built on slopes with roofs made from driftwood or whale bones, old
umiaq skin, and peat for insulation. Following Hans Egede’s arrival, traditional construction methods
began to change. Glass windows replaced the Inuit’s original windows made of intestines, improving light
but also causing condensation and ice to form inside. Additionally, permanent roofs with a low slope
supported by columns and internal wood cladding were introduced, furthering the Danish colonial agenda
of encouraging the Inuit to adopt a more settled lifestyle. However, these changes brought challenges as
sinking of the peat and rock walls over time created gaps that allowed snow and rain to infiltrate the
structures [10].
Figure 2: Reconstruction of a peat house in Sisimiut [11].

==PAGE:27==
Background
The initial contact with Europeans brought infectious diseases to Greenland, resulting in significant pop-
ulation losses in some Inuit communities. As the Inuit transitioned to a more settled lifestyle, they also
became more vulnerable to the spread of diseases such as tuberculosis, which increased in densely popu-
lated areas [12].
The Greenlandic self-rule began in 1856, granting the Inuits political influence. During World War II in
1942, Greenland was administered independently of Denmark. During this period, the establishment of
American airbases in Greenland introduced new building materials such as concrete, plywood, corrugated
iron sheets, insulation, and paint. These materials gradually replaced traditional peat houses with more
modern wooden structures. This shift also led to the rise of self-building as a popular construction method
[13]. In 1950, Denmark increased its investments in Greenland’s infrastructure. The establishment of
the Technical Organization of Greenland led to a surge in construction activity, including multi-story
buildings equipped with heating and running water. However, maintaining living conditions in smaller
settlements remained a challenge. By the 1970s, a plug-and-play self-build house design gained popularity,
offering a practical solution for housing in rural areas [14]. A sketch of such a plug-and-play self-build
house is shown i Figure 3.
Figure 3: Sketch of a plug-and-play self-build house [15].
Greenlandic building traditions, shaped during the colonial period, remain highly influenced by Danish
construction practices [9].
3.3
Greenlandic Ownership and Housing Policies
Greenland is divided into five municipalities, with the five largest cities, Nuuk, Sisimiut, Ilulissat, Qaqor-
toq, and Aasiaat, all located in a municipality each [6]. Approximately 60% of Greenland’s housing is
publicly owned by the Greenlandic government, further referred to as Naalakkersuisut. Despite this high
percentage, efforts have been made to encourage homeownership. In 2002, the authorities introduced
the ”From Tenant to Owner” program, designed to enable renters to purchase their homes at market
prices with favorable loan opportunities [16]. However, the program has seen limited success. In its first
five years, only 123 homes transitioned from public to private ownership, representing just 1.5% of pub-
licly owned housing [17]. Greenland’s geographic and societal conditions significantly influence housing
trends. Frequent relocations for better work and educational opportunities contribute to a hesitancy
toward homeownership. Additionally, individuals, cooperatives, and businesses in Greenland do not own

the land on which their buildings are constructed. Instead, they must apply for land allocation from the

3.3.1
Housing Shortages
Like many other countries in the Western world, Greenland is experiencing urbanization. People are
moving to larger cities such as Nuuk, Sisimiut, and Qaqortoq, leading to housing shortages in these
areas. Meanwhile, smaller cities and settlements are left with increasing numbers of vacant houses, with
some settlements left entirely vacant. One such example is the abandoned settlement Assaqutaq, located
south of Sisimiut, as shown in Figure 4.
Figure 4: The abandoned settlement Assaqutaq south from Sisimiut [19].
Larger cities operate with extensive waiting lists for publicly owned housing, disproportionately affecting
citizens with limited options in the private market.
In addition, some public housing units remain
unoccupied due to poor conditions, such as wear and mold. The housing shortage forces many to live in
buildings that do not meet modern living standards. However, these buildings cannot be renovated or
demolished because of the lack of replacement housing. As a result, the average waiting time for a rental
property in Nuuk is approximately 7.5 years [20]. Since Naalakkersuisut and the municipalities fund most
of Greenland’s new buildings, the pace of building activity is constrained by budgetary limitations, as
resources must also be allocated to other public services. In recent years, a new trend has emerged to
address these challenges. Clients have begun constructing homes for long-term rental by Naalakkersuisut
and the municipalities. This approach increases the housing supply without requiring Naalakkersuisut or
the municipalities to pay the full cost of new construction upfront [21].

3.4.1
Current Building Strategies
Most buildings in Greenland have been constructed within the past 70 years.
Multi-story buildings
have primarily been constructed using the most common construction method with facades combining
in-situ cast concrete walls and half-timber. This approach is widely adopted due to its familiarity among
workers, reducing the likelihood of construction errors, which are often costly, both economically and
environmentally. However, the technique requires a long drying period to eliminate moisture from the
in-situ concrete, which can promote mold growth [21]. During the winter months, it is important to keep
the concrete warm, as temperatures below 4 ℃significantly slow down the hydration, which can lead to
reduced strength, cracks, or other surface defects [24]. Maintaining the concrete at a specific temperature
to prevent both dehydration and moisture accumulation can make the process energy-intensive and
time-consuming. For many years, Greenland has faced mold issues, leading Naalakkersuisut to avoid
organic materials in buildings. The goal is to prevent mold growth and ensure longer-lasting, healthier
indoor environments. As most buildings in Greenland are constructed by Naalakkersuisut, this method is
commonly used [25]. However, mold can thrive with minimal organic material, meaning moisture control
is critical in preventing mold [26]. Consequently, focusing solely on avoiding organic materials may not
be the most effective strategy. In addition to traditional methods, alternative building techniques are
emerging in Greenland. Buildings constructed with CLT have been introduced in some cities, potentially
creating healthy indoor environments if the wood is treated correctly [27]. CLT offers several advantages,
including prefabricated elements shipped from Europe in the correct dimensions.
Furthermore, CLT
buildings can be assembled much more quickly than traditional construction methods, making them

All buildings in Greenland must comply with the Greenlandic Building Regulations (BR). The current

3.4.2
Energy
Greenland’s cold and harsh climate leads to large temperature differences between outside and inside.
The building envelopes are typically not insulated to account for the cold outdoor temperature, which
results in a high energy demand.
The energy sources available in a city depend on its location and
size. Since 1990, five hydropower plants have been constructed to provide renewable energy to Nuuk,
Qaqortoq/Narsaq, Sisimiut, Ilulissat, and Tasiilaq, even though the residential buildings at these locations
are still being heated by oil, except from in Nuuk, where a part of them are connected to the district
heating grid. In the smaller cities and settlements, fossil fuels remain the primary energy source [31].
Nukissiorfiit, Greenland’s largest utility company, produces and supplies electricity, water, and heat to
most consumers across 17 cities and 53 settlements. While 70% of the energy produced by Nukissiorfiit
is renewable [32], only 17% of the total energy demand in Greenland is met by renewable sources [31].
This percentage is notably lower than in Denmark where 43% of the total energy demand was covered by
renewables in 2022 [33]. It is also lower than in the EU, where 24.5% was covered by renewable energy in
2023 [34], but higher than in the US, where only 9% of energy consumption came from renewable sources
in 2023 [35].
3.5
Life Cycle Assessment
LCA is a tool used to evaluate the environmental impact of a product or service throughout its entire life
cycle. The primary reason for considering the complete life cycle is to prevent burden shifting in decision
making. Burden shifting occurs when an environmental impact is transferred from one life cycle stage to
another or from one environmental impact category to another [36].
LCA is commonly used for comparative assessments, where the environmental sustainability of one prod-
uct system is evaluated relative to another. Additionally, LCA can identify life cycle stages with the
highest environmental impacts, referred to as ”hotspots”. A hotspot LCA can guide decision-making by
indicating where efforts should be focused to reduce the environmental impact of a product or service [36].
The assessment is based on a Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) and a defined Functional Unit (FU). The LCI
is a detailed list of quantified physical flows, which includes the input of resources and materials required
to achieve the defined function, as well as the output of emissions and waste to the environment. These

and flows, but a limitation is that data constraints often require assumptions and reliance on generic
data. The quality of an LCA depends heavily on the quality of the data used and the assumptions made,
making LCI analysis a crucial part of the LCA process [36]. The life cycle stages considered in an LCA
depend on the product system but typically include raw material extraction, manufacturing/processing,
transportation, use, and disposal. The life cycle stages for buildings include product stage, construction

3.5.1
EPD
A building LCA is typically based on data from Environmental Product Declarations (EPD) to the ex-
tent possible. An EPD documents the environmental performance of a building material throughout its
life cycle. It follows internationally recognized standards, such as ISO 14025 and EN 15804, ensuring
consistency and reliability across EPDs. The foundation of an EPD is an LCA, which quantifies the envi-
ronmental performance of the building component over its life cycle and presents the results in key impact
17 of 130
==PAGE:34==
Background
categories. The primary purpose of an EPD is to assess and compare the environmental performance of
building materials, thereby supporting stakeholders in sustainability-driven decision-making. The EPDs
used in this thesis include two types of EPDs: sector EPDs and product-specific EPDs. A sector EPD
represents an average for a specific industry or product type, such as concrete elements or construction
timber. Product-specific EPDs are developed for a specific product from a specific manufacturer, such as
insulation material of a specific type from a specific manufacturer [38].
3.6
DGNB
The DGNB framework is a voluntary system designed to measure and promote collaboration on sus-
tainable building projects. It adopts a holistic approach, including social, environmental, and economic
dimensions of sustainability. The certification serves as a shared tool for clients, contractors, consultants,
and architects to collectively work towards more sustainable construction practices. The framework is
structured around measurable parameters for social, environmental, and economic performance.
De-
pending on its performance, a building can achieve a DGNB certification at one of three levels: silver,
gold, or platinum [39]. The DGNB criteria ”ENV1.1 – Life Cycle Assessment” focuses on environmental
sustainability, utilizing LCA to assess the building’s environmental impact, including Global Warming
Potential (GWP). As a minimum requirement in the Danish DGNB, this criteria must comply with the
Danish BR, which, as of January 2023, introduced a CO2 boundary of 12 kg CO2-eq per m2 per year for
the entire building life cycle [40].
The Bank of Greenland took the initiative to assemble a team to develop a Greenlandic version of the
DGNB certification system. The team now consists of representatives from themselves, DTU, Nukissior-
fiit, Rambøll Greenland, EMJ Atcon, Inuplan, and TNT Nuuk. This team held a conference in Nuuk in
February 2023, inviting all stakeholders in the industry to debate the future buildings in Greenland and
the establishment of the Greenlandic DGNB certification. At this conference, The Bank of Greenland
made it clear that the main incentive of a Greenlandic DGNB certification is the increased opportunity
to secure external financing for projects, as investors must report on the number of sustainable projects
in their portfolio [41].

Qualitative Methods

5.1.1
Qualitative Method - Interview
To gain insight into the challenges in the Greenlandic building industry and achieve knowledge about the
barriers to building sustainably as well as how these can be overcome, a qualitative research method is
used as a semi-structured interview. A semi-structured interview is a method to collect qualitative data
characterized by a flexible framework. It contains predetermined questions from which the interviewer
can deviate, if relevant, to explore the given response more deeply. This approach makes structure and
spontaneity possible and provides a deeper understanding of the respondent’s opinions and experiences
[42]. As a part of the semi-structured interview, the respondents will be asked to answer linear numeric
scale questions. These are included to collect quantitative data from the respondents, which is easily com-
parable across interviews. For the interview preparation, open-ended questions were prepared, focusing
on key topics related to the purpose of the interview. The interview questions can be seen in Appendix A.
Eleven stakeholders with different roles in the Greenlandic building industry were interviewed.
The
interviews lasted approximately half an hour, and when possible, were conducted in person in Sisimiut,
Nuuk, and Copenhagen. The remaining interviews were conducted online. Table 2 lists an overview of

5.2
Quantitative Method - Life Cycle Assessment
This thesis includes three LCAs conducted on case buildings with different building materials. The cases
are presented and described in detail in Section 4. In this section, the quantitative methodology of the
LCAs are described. For reporting the LCA study, guidelines from chapter 38 in the LCA Handbook [36]
are utilized to create a clear understanding of the study and ensure transparency.
5.2.1
Goal Definition
In this section, the goal of the LCA study will be defined to specify its intended application, including
limitations, reasons for conducting the study, and target audience.
5.2.1.1
Intended Application
The intended application of the LCAs included in this study is to assess the difference in the environmen-
tal impact between three Greenlandic apartment buildings with different construction methods, described
in Section 4. Furthermore, the LCAs aim to assess the environmental impact of a Greenlandic dwelling
comparable to the Danish BR. This is done to help guide a decision to quantify a CO2 boundary in the
context of a DGNB certification system being developed in the Greenlandic context. Therefore, the LCA
is conducted in accordance with the Danish building code BR18, EN15978, and as recommended in the
LCA Handbook [36].
5.2.1.2
Method Assumptions and Impact Limitations
As this study is only based on three case studies, its findings cannot be used to conclude the environmen-
tal impact of buildings in Greenland in general, as this would require more case studies. Furthermore,
the LCAs are based on drawing material reflecting the ideal construction scenario without potential
construction errors, additional replacements, or waste. In the current version of the Greenlandic BR,
there is no requirement to create an energy frame, which is why it is not commonly practiced [44]. To
enable a meaningful comparison between the Greenlandic LCAs and Danish LCAs, it would have been
ideal to use energy frames for operational energy. However, since energy frames were unavailable for
the cases analyzed, we performed a heat loss calculation for each case, as detailed in Section 5.2.4. It is
worth noting that the upcoming version of the Greenlandic BR is expected to include a requirement for
energy frames. Furthermore, previous studies have shown that transportation is not the most significant
contributor to environmental impacts of a Greenlandic dwelling. Instead the main contributing life cycle
stages are shown to be the production stage and use stage [45]. Thereby transport from production to
the construction site is not included, meaning the impact of this additional transportation from Denmark
to Greenland is not accounted for.
34 of 130
==PAGE:51==
Methodology
Treatment of waste in Greenland varies significantly from waste treatment in Europe, and there is little
data available for Greenlandic waste treatment. This introduces uncertainty regarding End-of-Life (EoL)
scenarios, as the best estimate and generic processes for the Rest of the World (RoW) may only partially
reflect waste handling in Greenland, which might change significantly during the building’s lifespan. Ad-
ditionally, only five impact categories are considered, based on our choice to use EPDs made according
to the EN15804+A1 standard described in Section 5.2.2.6, and thus, no conclusions can be drawn about
other environmental impact categories.
5.2.1.3
Reasons for Carrying Out the Study and Decision Context
The study investigates the possibilities of making the Greenlandic building industry more sustainable
by examining the choice of materials. Furthermore, it can contribute to the decision to quantify a CO2
boundary in the context of DGNB Greenland, which is currently being developed. The decision context
is situation C1, as the LCA study documents the environmental impacts of already-built buildings.
Therefore, the study will not lead to any direct changes.
5.2.2
Scope Definition
This section will describe the scope of the LCA study, including system boundaries and representativeness
of LCI data.
5.2.2.1
Deliverables
The deliverables for this study include a detailed LCI for each of the three building cases and the char-
acterized results of the Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA). The results from the LCAs will be used
to identify hotspots of building parts and building materials for each case and compare the three cases.

5.2.2.2
Function, Functional Unit, and Reference Flows
The system is determined to be a Greenlandic multi-story apartment building that is compliant with
Greenlandic BR and relevant across different Greenlandic locations. Based on these properties and an
initial investigation of the building stock in Greenland, the following FU is defined:
”Construction, use, and disposal for one year of 1 m2 of a low-rise Greenlandic apartment building with
a service life of 50 years.”
The estimated service life (ESL) of 50 years is chosen to make a comparison to Danish LCAs possible
in the context of both the Danish BR and DGNB. However, it is acknowledged that this introduces
uncertainty as the harsh climate and potential building errors might shorten the service life of the build-

be explained later. Thus, the reference flows include all building materials used to build a Greenlandic
dwelling, as well as energy and materials used to operate and maintain the building during its lifespan.

An attributional LCA is used for this study, as it aims to represent the system independently without
considering the remaining technosphere or economy. This approach is chosen because the results for each
case need to be used individually and in comparison.

5.2.2.5
Representativeness of LCI Data
Technological Representativeness.
The technology used for manufacturing the building materials should ideally correspond to the specific
manufacturing methods employed by each material manufacturer. Similarly, the data for heating the
building should represent the actual heating system in use, such as local district heating or central oil
heating. For waste treatment, the data should ideally reflect the technology expected to be used in Green-
land 50 years from now. However, predicting the future development of Greenland’s waste infrastructure
over such a long timespan is challenging, which may compromise the technological representativeness.
Geographical Representativeness.
All building materials for the three cases are imported from Denmark. Consequently, the data should
ideally represent building materials available on the Danish market and manufactured within Europe.
For energy use during the operation phase and waste treatment at the End-of-Life (EoL) stage, the data
should reflect the geographical conditions of Greenland, where the buildings are located.
Temporal Representativeness.
The three building cases were constructed between 2020 and 2024. Therefore, the data for material man-
ufacturing should ideally correspond to manufacturing practices within five years of this period, aligning
with the typical temporal validity of an EPD. Since the service life of the buildings is assumed to be 50
years, the data for waste treatment should ideally reflect Greenland’s waste infrastructure as anticipated
between 2070 and 2075. Ideal data were not available for all aspects of the LCA. Consequently, data
choices and assumptions are thoroughly detailed in Section 5.2.3.3.
5.2.2.6
Basis for Impact Assessment
Based on data availability, the LCIA method was chosen in accordance with the EPD EN15804+A1
standard so that product-specific EPDs in this standard could be used for the assessment. This choice
was made based on the expectation that better representativeness is achieved using EPDs than generic
datasets for all materials.
Furthermore, product-specific EPDs make the LCAs more comparable to
Danish building LCAs, as EPDs are used for LCAs in the Danish building industry to the extent possible.
The choice to use the EPD EN15804+A1 standard instead of the EPD EN15804+A2 standard was based
on an investigation of the available EPDs for the half-timber case building. This limited the study to
five impact categories. These include GWP, Acidification Potential (AP), Eutrophication Potential (EP),
Ozone Depletion Potential (ODP), and Photochemical Oxidation Potential (POCP).

5.2.3.1
Software tool
A key aspect of the LCI modeling was the selection of software for conducting the LCA. The software
options considered were LCAbyg [46], OpenLCA [47], and SimaPro [48].
LCAbyg is the most com-
monly used software in the Danish building industry, tailored specifically to the Danish context and the
Danish DGNB certification system. Designed exclusively for building LCAs, it features a user-friendly
interface. However, its specialized focus limits flexibility in system modeling, and its default database
is the German building database, ¨Okobau [49], limiting geographical representativeness for our context.
In contrast, OpenLCA and SimaPro are general-purpose LCA tools that support various applications.
Both offer extensive flexibility for LCI modeling, including integrating various databases and creating
custom processes. Ultimately, SimaPro was chosen due to prior experience with the software from pre-
vious projects. The choice of using SimaPro was made to maintain modeling flexibility and recognize
that some LCA processes might differ significantly from the Danish context, particularly in areas such
as energy mix composition, concrete production, and waste infrastructure. Additionally, the Ecoinvent
database accessible in SimaPro provides global datasets, unlike the ¨Okobau database. To enhance data
processing flexibility, we developed a dynamic Excel sheet that includes results for one unit of each con-
struction element, covering both A1-A3 and C3-C4 stages. We then consolidated all data into a master
sheet, allowing us to efficiently manage the quantity of each construction type and the ESL of each ma-
terial. This setup enabled precise control of how many times the production stage (A1-A3) and the EoL
stage (C3-C4) were accounted for in the final results based on the number of replacements. Furthermore,
this approach facilitated easy parameter adjustments for the sensitivity analysis, as described in Section
5.2.3.5. The result sheets for each case building can be accessed in the supplementary material seen in
Appendix J.5.

5.2.3.3
System Modelling Per Life Cycle Stage
In the following section, the life cycle stages, data choices, and assumptions of this study will be explained.
A detailed LCI including all assumptions for all three case studies can be found in Appendix J.3.
Product stage (A1-A3)
The product stage includes the extraction of raw materials, transportation to the manufacturing facility,
and the production of building materials. For this LCA study, primary data on the quantities of building
materials were obtained directly from the contractor and/or architect involved in the building project.
The drawings received of the buildings can be seen in Appendix J.2. These reference flows are assumed to
represent the building materials used in constructing a Greenlandic dwelling. However, these quantities
do not account for potential material losses during transportation, storage, or construction. The primary
data have been supplemented with assumptions made in collaboration with the contractor and architect,
as well as EPDs and generic data from the Ecoinvent database. For the production stages (A1–A3),
product-specific EPDs were used when available, as these provide the most accurate representation of
the production processes for the specific building materials used. This approach aligns with the DGNB
framework [40].
Generic datasets were used for 10%–13% of the total mass of building materials used across the three case
studies (see Section 5.2.3.2 for mass fractions). In cases where product-specific EPDs were unavailable,
sector-specific EPDs from Denmark were applied. Since all building materials, except sand, gravel, and
water for concrete, are shipped from Denmark, sector-specific EPDs are considered more contextually
40 of 130
==PAGE:57==
Methodology
accurate than generic data from the Ecoinvent database. For materials without product-specific or sector-
specific EPDs, generic data from Ecoinvent were used based on the following priority: ”Europe without
Switzerland,” ”RER” (Europe), and ”GLO” (Global). The absence of product-specific data for certain
materials reduces representativeness, as it is unclear whether the datasets reflect the same manufacturing
methods or energy mix used in production. To ensure consistency across the case studies and address
the lack of specific material data in the concrete and CLT cases, identical EPDs were applied for the
same materials when product-specific information was unavailable. This approach was taken based on
our study comparing EPDs and generic datasets, which showed significant variability between datasets
for the same material. The results of this study are described in Section 5.2.3.4.
Construction process stage (A4-A5)
The construction process includes transportation of the materials to the construction site, waste of ma-
terials during construction, and energy used for the construction process. These stages are not included
in the scope of LCA in the context of Danish BR [37] and, therefore, not included in this study.
Use stage (B1-B7)
The use stage of a building encompasses maintenance, operational energy and water consumption, and the
replacement of worn-out building materials. For this study, the use stage includes material replacement
(B4) and heating for building operation (B6) illustrated in Figure 27. Materials requiring replacement
were identified based on their ESL, following the guidelines established by BUILD [52]. This approach
ensures comparability with practices in the Danish building industry and was chosen due to the absence
of more context-specific data. However, it is important to note that the climatic differences between
Greenland and Denmark may affect the lifespan of buildings and their materials. For the operational
energy use, two scenarios have been developed and are detailed in Section 5.2.4.
End-of-Life stage (C1-C4)
The EoL stage includes demolition, transportation to waste treatment facilities, waste processing, and
final disposal. In accordance with Danish BR, only waste processing (C3) and disposal (C4) are manda-
tory. Consequently, this study also limits its scope to these two stages. Waste treatment practices in
Greenland differ significantly from those in Denmark and the rest of Europe. As a result, EPDs are
generally not used for the EoL stages in this study. In Greenland, most waste is either landfilled or incin-
erated. Based on interviews conducted with the local incineration plant in Sisimiut and representatives
from the Municipality in Sisimiut, it is assumed that all combustible waste is incinerated. In contrast,
all metal waste is sorted and shipped to Denmark for recycling. Non-combustible waste is disposed of in
landfills. C3 and C4 data from EPDs are used for combustible materials and metals, as these align with
Danish waste management practices. For metals, transportation to Denmark by container ship has been
included. The sailing distance is based on info from Royal Arctic Line and the sailing distance [53][54].

For non-combustible materials and materials without EPDs, generic processes from the Ecoinvent database

General assumptions
The foundation height varies significantly across the building cases due to differences in the terrain. To
ensure comparability and eliminate the impact of terrain-related height variations on the results, a uni-
form foundation height was applied to all cases. This height, set at 1.8 m, was chosen based on the
approximate average provided by the contractor for the half-timber case.
Due to limited data on technical installations in our case buildings, we have chosen to use standard
environmental impact values for installations for sewage, water, heating, and ventilation from the Danish
BR in our calculations [56]. The values are based on case buildings from Denmark and are calculated
conservatively by taking twice the average for sewage and water and 1.25 times the average for heating
and ventilation [57]. These values are only available for GWP, which would limit our analysis to this
impact category. We have used a standard multi-story apartment building case from LCAbyg to derive
the numbers for the other considered impact categories. Based on the results from the case building, we
have obtained multiplication factors to reach the standard values for GWP. These factors are used to
find the right quantities of materials in LCAbyg to reach the standard values. Then, the results from
the standard case building with the adjusted amounts for technical installations are used as standard
values for all impact categories. The standard values are given in m2/year and, therefore, multiplied
with the heated floor area, including the potentially heated basement, according to the approach in the
Danish BR [56]. This approach introduces some uncertainties and provides a conservative estimate of
the environmental impact, as the standard values are intended to encourage the use of actual quantities
for technical installations. However, since the same methodology is applied consistently across all cases,
it does not affect their comparability. The calculation can be seen in Appendix C.

To make informed data choices, we conducted a study to compare differences between EPDs and generic
datasets. This study examined four constructions: a load-bearing facade, an inner wall, a strip foun-
dation, and a roof. Generic datasets were compared with EPDs at both the material and construction
levels. The results showed that the product-specific EPDs had lower environmental impacts across all
considered impact categories in four out of six materials. Using the generic datasets as a baseline (100%),
the impacts of the product-specific EPDs ranged from 0.1% to 82%. However, for one material, the EPD
impact was higher, with the generic data representing between 13% and 73% of the EPD impact. For

The stakeholders were asked to identify the biggest challenges associated with building in Greenland.
Commonly cited issues included economic constraints, the harsh climate, long delivery times, and a
shortage of qualified labor. As in many other countries and industries, economic factors play a significant

sometimes be higher than in other European countries. Consultant 2 highlights several of these economic
challenges in Quote 1.
”I believe one of the biggest challenges is that construction is expensive. We are far away
from everything, and preparing land for construction on hard bedrock is costly, whether it

skills needed for construction.”

6.1.2
Challenges of Building Sustainably in Greenland
In addition to sharing their views on the biggest challenges of building in Greenland, the stakeholders
were also asked about the specific challenges of building sustainably. All consultants and clients identified
the lack of knowledge across the industry as the primary obstacle. They emphasized the importance of
establishing a common language for sustainability and a unified vision within the industry.
”Knowledge. [...] I think people build the way they’re used to, using the solutions they’re
accustomed to, regardless of whether they’re good or bad. So if we’re going to build more sus-
tainably, people need to have its value explained to them, and there need to be given solutions.
[...] But the building industry is generally conservative, and even more so up here. So I think
it’s about knowledge and easy solutions.”
— Consultant 1 (Quote 4)
As mentioned in Quote 4, the lack of knowledge is a significant challenge, in addition to the industry’s
conservative nature. While various new building techniques and alternative materials have been tested
across the industry in Greenland, many of these attempts result in poorly constructed buildings that
struggle to withstand the harsh climate. This issue is compounded by a general lack of experience in
the field. As a potential solution, the Housing Company suggests developing building-ERFA guidelines
similar to those in Denmark. This could be beneficial, as much valuable experience already exists within
the Greenlandic building industry, but it is not widely shared among all stakeholders. Additionally, the
potential for reusing existing concrete structures instead of demolishing them to build more sustainably
is highlighted. Consultant 2 further points out that the most effective and robust construction method is
the traditional half-timber building, which should continue to be used until a more sustainable alternative
is proven equally effective. The importance of location-specific solutions is also emphasized, with stake-
holders noting that a one-size-fits-all approach across the country is impractical. Consultant 3 explains
that different weather conditions and energy mixes across the country necessitate tailored solutions. For
example, the energy mix can significantly impact CO2 emissions, depending on whether hydropower or
oil is used. Both contractors highlight the challenges from a material perspective. They agree that the
selection of materials tested to withstand Greenland’s climate is limited, yet many materials used in
54 of 130
==PAGE:71==
Results
Europe are being applied in Greenland. This practice results in a reduced lifespan and an increased
need for maintenance. In light of this, both contractors believe that the range of available materials in
Greenland should be restricted to those specifically tested and developed for the Arctic environment.
”[...] the full understanding and acceptance that Greenland, yes, I get that it’s the world’s
largest island but has a very small population. [...] We can’t do the same as the rest of the
world, and we shouldn’t have to either. [...] We don’t need to go out and invent a whole lot
because it doesn’t make sense to us. Greenland is simply too small for that, and there are too
few building projects.”
— Contractor 2 (Quote 5)
Quote 5 from Contractor 2 supports the shared opinion of both contractors on the importance of utilizing
existing knowledge and not assuming that materials suited for southern Europe are equally suitable for
Greenland. They also mention the issue of material waste, as they often order more than needed to avoid
delays from waiting for specific materials. One contractor suggests that involving the client, consultant,
and contractor early in the process would allow them to share their respective expertise and experience
with building in Greenland. Another proposed solution is the creation of a common heated warehouse
to store surplus materials. The contractors disagree on this idea: one sees it as a good way to reduce
waste, while the other believes that limiting the selection of materials would make it easier for contrac-
tors to exchange resources, thus eliminating the need for a warehouse. However, both agree that greater
alignment in material choices could help steer the Greenlandic building industry toward more sustainable
practices. Similar to the Housing Company, the contractors also highlight the economic aspect, noting
that sustainable solutions are often seen as more expensive from their perspective. They believe that
for change to occur across the industry, the more sustainable option must also be the more cost-effective
choice. Architect 1 highlights the conservative mindset of consultants and contractors and their lack of
long-term thinking as the main obstacle to sustainable buildings in Greenland. All stakeholders share
this view of the building industry as conservative. It could be argued that this resistance to change stems
from tradition, limited local experience, and challenging environmental conditions, all of which require
testing new approaches. The same architect stresses the need for a shift in mentality toward a more
holistic approach to building.

The Housing Company proposes a fundamental change to the Greenlandic tax system as a solution.

has remained unchanged while labor has become more expensive. In the building industry, one of the
things required to work sustainably is, e.g., selective demolition. However, this process demands many
labor hours, which is the most expensive resource currently available, making it financially unfeasible. As
a result, the industry frequently evaluates whether purchasing new materials is more cost-effective than
using labor hours e.g. reusing existing ones. The Housing Company, therefore, believes that it should be
investigated how taxes could be shifted from income to resources.
6.1.3
DGNB Certification in a Greenlandic Context
As mentioned in Section 3.6, a DGNB framework is currently being developed for use in a Greenlandic
context. Among the stakeholders, there is a shared skepticism about whether the DGNB framework is
an appropriate solution for Greenland’s context. Many stakeholders mention the economic implications.
Despite the potential financial advantages of financing from foundations, this does not entirely convince
them of its feasibility. One concern is that it will require additional labor, which is already in short supply
in Greenland. This viewpoint, among others, is expressed by Contractor 1, as illustrated in Quote 7:
”It will likely provide some employment, but that’s not really what’s lacking in Greenland
[...] I mean, there’s a labor shortage in Greenland, so it will just be an additional expense.
You have to go and find resources that aren’t really available [...], who have to do these things,
and in the end, I don’t think it will reach the point where it has any effect.”
— Contractor 1 (Quote 7)
Furthermore, Contractor 1 does not believe the DGNB framework will ultimately lead to meaningful
results. He is also skeptical about the certification, arguing that the building volume in Greenland is too
small to justify the additional cost and work hours of a certification. However, he still ranks it relatively

Many stakeholders, including Consultant 2, believe that the DGNB framework is better suited for the

Discussion
In the following section, the results of this thesis will be discussed in relation to the applied methods and
main findings. It examines the methods used to explore sustainable building practices in Greenland and
investigate the environmental impacts of different building materials. Furthermore, it explores how an
establishment of a DGNB certification could address industry challenges as well as the establishment of
a CO2 boundary.
7.1
Interview Method
The use of semi-structured interviews has allowed for in-depth insights into the challenges faced by the
Greenlandic building industry and the barriers to sustainable building practices. A significant strength of
this approach is the combination of qualitative open-ended questions with quantitative linear scale ques-
tions. The qualitative data provides a deep understanding of the personal experiences and contextual
factors influencing sustainability in construction, while the quantitative data allows for numeric compar-
ison across interviews. The sample size of 11 interviewed stakeholders can be debated, as it may not
be sufficient to draw broader, generalizable conclusions about the entire building industry in Greenland.
Nevertheless, this sample size is appropriate for the analysis’s exploratory nature, which aims to identify
the main issues rather than perform a statistical analysis. A larger sample size could have provided more
comprehensive data, particularly with a wider geographical spread of respondents’ workplaces, poten-
tially revealing regional differences in building practices and views on sustainability. Even though the
goal is to represent the respondents’ opinions accurately, there is a potential for bias in data processing
as we have analyzed the interviews ourselves. We have selected relevant quotes and summarized the
respondents’ views. Therefore, while the analysis aims to provide a broad and objective understanding
of the challenges in the Greenlandic building industry, the risk of bias in the selection and interpretation
of data should be acknowledged. To mitigate this bias, the transcribed interviews were processed using
the qualitative data analysis software QDA Miner as described in Section 5.1.1.
7.2
DGNB in a Greenlandic context
The interviews with the 11 stakeholders reveal a shared skepticism regarding the implementation of a
Greenlandic DGNB certification.
While the industry faces numerous challenges, stakeholders do not
necessarily view DGNB as a viable solution to address these issues.
However, a DGNB certification
could help mitigate many of these challenges if tailored appropriately. Some stakeholders highlighted
improved building project planning as a key solution, emphasizing that effective planning, accounting for
time and economic factors, is critical for reducing errors and minimizing unnecessary resource use during
construction. Dividing building projects into phases based on optimal climatic conditions can further
enhance efficiency. Indoor work and prefabrication processes can be intensified during the winter, while
77 of 130
==PAGE:94==
Discussion
outdoor construction activities are concentrated in warmer months. However, achieving this requires a
high level of coordination among various stakeholders throughout the construction process, as well as the
acceptance of potential delays due to the project’s overall time frame being exceeded. Such a challenge,
which affects most building projects, could be addressed through a DGNB certification, which could
enhance seasonal planning and help optimize the construction process.
As mentioned in Section 3.6, the main incentive of a Greenlandic DGNB certification is the increased
opportunity to secure external financing for projects. However, based on the interviews, the industry
does not yet trust this statement. The economic aspect is a significant concern for many stakeholders
as construction costs continue to rise. Consequently, stakeholders often prioritize initial costs over en-
vironmental performance, which can impede the adoption of sustainable practices. To overcome this, a
shift toward considering long-term economic and environmental benefits is necessary. Prioritizing total
cost, including maintenance and life cycle considerations, can ensure better maintained buildings with
extended lifespans. This approach is especially critical for publicly owned buildings, where limited bud-
gets often delay essential renovations. Long-term planning can be encouraged by incorporating total cost
requirements into public tenders. Such strategies reduce expenses over time and significantly decrease
the environmental footprint of building activities in Greenland. A total cost calculation is also a part of
the Danish DGNB, and a DGNB Greenland might also encourage a life cycle approach to the economy
of a building.
As highlighted in Section 6.1.2, the current tax system imposes high labor costs relative to material costs.
Restructuring the economic framework, where materials are taxed more heavily than labor, could incen-
tivize more resource-efficient practices. Although such a change may seem ambitious at present, Green-
land’s small population and community-driven mindset provide an opportunity for rapid implementation
with public support. Economic incentives and subsidies could also play a vital role in supporting this
transition. Additional measures, such as tax breaks for sustainable materials, subsidies for renewable
energy installations, and grants for retrofitting existing buildings, could further incentivize the transition
to sustainable building practices. This shift would make material reuse and circular construction methods
more financially attractive. Together, these strategies could help create a more conducive environment
for sustainable building practices in Greenland, fostering both economic and environmental progress. A
well-implemented DGNB certification could play an important role in guiding this transition.
7.3
The Greenlandic Housing Market
The Greenlandic housing market must balance public and private interests to effectively address its unique
challenges. As mentioned in Section 3.3, a significant characteristic of the Greenlandic housing market
is the predominance of publicly owned properties. Public ownership provides essential housing security,
78 of 130
==PAGE:95==
Discussion
particularly in a sparsely populated and geographically remote region. However, living in rental housing
managed by public institutions can diminish individual incentives to maintain buildings adequately.
When tenants do not have a direct stake in the property, maintenance is often de-prioritized, leading
to a decrease in the service life of the building. Different approaches could initiate and encourage more
proactive maintenance from the tenants. For instance, tenants who actively contribute to the care of their
homes might benefit from lower rents. Similarly, a stronger emphasis on long-term investment in public
budgets for housing could ensure consistent upkeep and prevent the costly deterioration of properties over
time. Integrating maintenance and life cycle costs into budgetary planning makes it possible to reduce
the need for new buildings while extending the lifespan of existing buildings. Furthermore, a solution
would be to shift the mentality, as many locals currently find owning a house intimidating due to the
potential for unforeseen expenses and the less flexible lifestyle it entails. More cooperative housing is being
built, which is an excellent step on the way and may influence people to buy instead of rent. Involving
community members in the planning and decision-making processes ensures that new buildings align with
local needs and preferences, which can foster a sense of ownership and encourage proper maintenance of
the buildings.
7.4
LCA Method
As described in Section 5.2.3.1, we chose to conduct the LCAs in SimaPro to achieve greater flexibility
in modeling. Our initial goal was to model the Greenlandic concrete production and energy mix, as we
expected these factors to significantly impact the results. However, due to data limitations, this was not
feasible. In hindsight, conducting the LCAs in LCAbyg might have been a more practical choice, as it
would have allowed us to use generic datasets from ¨Okobau instead of Ecoinvent, potentially making the
results more comparable to Danish LCAs. However, this might also compromise geographic representa-
tiveness.
Additionally, our analysis of different data sources has highlighted significant variations between datasets
for the same type of material, indicating that the results could have differed had we used another database.
Nevertheless, our decision to use SimaPro, followed by processing the data in Excel, provided the flexibility
to easily adjust the ESL and material quantities, allowing us to assess the impact of different choices on
the final results.
7.5
LCA results
The LCA results indicate that the CLT building in Nuuk has the lowest environmental impact from
materials across all investigated impact categories except for POCP. Furthermore, the CLT building ex-
hibits the lowest heating demand. Based on this theoretical study, CLT would be the most sustainable
construction method among the three investigated. Additionally, it can be argued that CLT buildings
79 of 130
==PAGE:96==
Discussion
are likely to generate less material waste compared to the other two construction methods, as the pre-
fabricated CLT elements can be delivered in precise measurements and ready for assembly. Furthermore,
CLT is less dependent on weather conditions during assembly than concrete, making it a potentially more
efficient construction process in Greenland’s climate. However, it is essential to acknowledge that CLT
remains a relatively new construction method in Greenland. Consequently, its long-term durability under
the Greenlandic climatic conditions must be evaluated over several years before drawing definitive con-
clusions about its resilience. As no extensive long-term testing has been conducted, relying primarily on
CLT for future building projects would be a considerable uncertainty. This uncertainty is also reflected in
the fact that the CLT building in this study was ultimately constructed in half-timbered concrete rather
than CLT, as no contractors chose to bid on the building built in CLT. This suggests that the indus-
try may still perceive CLT as a somewhat uncertain choice due to limited local experience and knowledge.
With this in mind, it is worth questioning whether CLT is truly the most sustainable construction method
in practice at the moment despite its lower theoretical environmental impact. Instead, the half-timber
building, which demonstrated the second-lowest environmental impact in the study and is a well-known
construction method used in the harsh climate, may currently represent the most sustainable and viable
solution.
There is limited global experience with CLT buildings in Arctic environments. However, existing research
indicates that the high initial moisture content in CLT elements significantly increases the risk of mold
growth, emphasizing the need for careful planning to minimize construction time and exposure to snow
and rain during assembly [72]. Nonetheless, one study concludes that CLT construction can be success-
fully implemented without a significant increase in mold risk, provided that the necessary precautions
are taken [73]. Despite these findings, researchers agree that further studies are needed to assess the
long-term durability of CLT buildings in cold climates.
As mentioned in Section 5.2.1.2, the LCA results primarily reflect theoretical environmental impacts and
do not account for material losses, potential mistakes, or the environmental burden of correcting those
mistakes, which is important to note when determining whether one construction method is more sus-
tainable than another. For example, a study examining the environmental consequences of a construction
error in a facade system [74] found that such errors could increase the environmental impact by 11% to
79% per m2 of facade across various impact categories. While this study focuses on a single type of
error, it highlights the significance of the potential environmental cost due to construction errors. The
conducted sensitivity analysis also revealed a high sensitivity to the service life of the building. These
findings suggest that minimizing errors during construction and maintaining the building to prolong its
life span could have a greater environmental benefit than the choice of building material. Furthermore,
as mentioned in Section 5.2.1.2, the LCA results do not account for the impact of material transporta-

==PAGE:97==

tion, as a previous study has found it not to be a significant contributor to the environmental impact.
However, as mentioned in Section 6.1.1, Contractor 1 explains that certain materials occasionally need
to be flown in when not available in the city. This factor was not considered in the referenced study
and would likely increase the overall transportation impact, as air transport has a significantly higher
environmental footprint than shipping. This emphasizes the importance of efficient project planning to
minimize the need for air transportation.
The GWP results indicate that materials assumed to be landfilled have lower GWP in the EoL stage than
those incinerated. This is evident when comparing the EoL results for GWP across the case buildings.
The concrete building, where almost 95% of the materials are landfilled, exhibits the lowest GWP in the
EoL stage. In contrast, the GWP increases in the EoL stage for the two other buildings, which contain
wood incinerated at the EoL stage. As Greenland’s waste infrastructure evolves and reliance on landfill-
ing decreases, the GWP associated with the EoL stage will likely increase due to a shift toward other
waste management practices, such as incineration. Consequently, the current results may underestimate
the future environmental impact during the EoL stage. However, the waste infrastructure for wood is
also expected to evolve. The architect designing the CLT building also mentioned that the intention was
for the CLT elements to be disassembled and reused. As shown in Figure 36 in Section 6.2.3, the EoL
stage of the CLT building accounts for 77% of the GWP impact due to the incineration of wood. By
increasingly reusing or recycling wood, the environmental impact of wooden buildings at the EoL stage
would be significantly reduced. Although the intention of the architect is positive, it remains uncertain
whether the CLT element will actually be disassembled and reused in practice, as no CLT building in
Greenland has yet reached the end of its lifespan.
Another life cycle stage worth further investigation in the Greenlandic context is the construction process
(A4-A5). During winter, significant amounts of energy are consumed to heat construction sites to enable
concrete to harden. This energy typically comes from oil, which has a very high GWP. This seasonal
heating is likely to substantially increase the environmental impact of the construction process in Green-
land, making it an important area for further analysis and potential optimization. This issue could also
potentially be a part of a Greenlandic DGNB.
7.6
Energy
As mentioned in Section 5.2.1.2, a heat loss calculation has been performed for each case, as no energy
frames were available. This introduces a degree of uncertainty regarding the operational energy use, as
the calculations conducted are simplified compared to those performed using the energy frame method
in the BE18 software [75]. It is important to note that although the theoretical energy use has been
calculated for each case, these results do not reflect the actual energy consumption of the buildings. The
81 of 130
==PAGE:98==
Discussion
precise energy use is difficult to predict, as it depends on various factors such as occupancy patterns,
user behavior, and the performance of building systems over time. These factors are often challenging
to incorporate fully into theoretical models, meaning that actual energy consumption may vary signifi-
cantly from the calculated values. For the LCA study, the theoretical energy use was assumed to be the
most representative, as it is the one used for LCA in accordance with the Danish BR. Furthermore, the
simplified calculations are considered sufficient to determine the theoretical energy use for the purpose of
this study since the focus was primarily on the materials.
In the calculation of operational energy for the three buildings, a key assumption was made to exclude
solar gains. This assumption significantly impacts the results, as neglecting solar gains likely leads to
overestimating heating energy demand. This effect is particularly pronounced during periods of high
solar exposure, such as the Greenlandic summer months, when the sun remains above the horizon for 24
hours. If solar gains had been included, the energy calculations would likely have shown a reduced heating
load and, consequently, a lower CO2 impact during the operational phase. Additionally, the potential for
overheating must be considered. Newer, well-insulated, and airtight buildings with large window areas
are particularly susceptible to overheating during summer. However, due to Greenland’s cold and windy
climate, conventional strategies for preventing overheating, such as opening windows or using external
shading, are often impractical. More suitable solutions could include interior shading, windows with
reduced size or, reduced solar heat gain coefficient, or increased air exchange rates. It is important to
note, however, that in Greenland, there is a tendency for occupants to seal natural ventilation intakes in
buildings to reduce cold drafts and improve thermal comfort. This practice often results in ventilation
rates below optimal levels, further complicating efforts to balance energy efficiency, mold prevention,
comfort, and indoor air quality.
The results of the CO2 impact assessment over the building’s lifetime clearly demonstrate that energy
consumption has a significantly greater impact than the materials used for most locations. Consequently,
prioritizing energy-efficient solutions and renewable energy sources is more critical than optimizing ma-
terial choices. As illustrated in Figure 41, the CO2 impact during the operational phase is substantially
lower when heating with district heating compared to oil boilers. This reduction is primarily due to the
incorporation of renewable energy in district heating systems. However, as discussed in Section 3.4.2,
only Nuuk has residential buildings connected to the district heating grid, while all other locations rely on
oil-based heating. Notably, more than one-third of Greenland’s population resides in Nuuk, representing
a significant portion of the national building stock. Therefore, considering building materials might make
a significant difference in Nuuk, while focusing on energy use to decrease environmental impact will be
more efficient for other locations. This emphasizes the importance of considering location-specific envi-
ronmental factors in construction planning. It is also important to note that environmental impact data
for Nukissiorfiit’s district heating system are only available for the GWP impact category. As a result, the
82 of 130
==PAGE:99==
Discussion
LCA of operational energy is limited to this category. However, given that the primary objective of includ-
ing the operational stage is to establish a CO2 boundary, the available data are sufficient for this purpose.
The insulation levels of buildings and their relationship to overall energy demand are other critical factors
to consider. High insulation levels can significantly reduce heating requirements, but this often comes
at the expense of increased material usage. In a city where energy is primarily sourced from renewable
hydropower, a lower level of insulation, leading to a higher heat demand, could potentially be more envi-
ronmentally favorable. This emphasizes the importance of tailoring insulation levels to specific locations,
considering the availability and type of energy sources to minimize the overall environmental impact.
However, given the limited capacity of the existing hydropower plants, reducing building energy demand
remains an important consideration to prevent increased reliance on fossil fuels.
As discussed in Section 3.4.2, hydropower is currently the most widely used renewable energy source
in Greenland, but other renewables are expected to play an increasingly bigger role. Solar panels are
particularly promising in settlements where hydropower is not a feasible option, and wind energy is being
tested in Sisimiut to assess its reliability as a renewable energy source.
Integrating these renewable
energy systems into building designs can significantly reduce the GWP of buildings. Considering this,
along with the ESL of the buildings of 50 years, the assumption of 100% heating by oil may be somewhat
conservative.
7.7
CO2 boundary
Based on the LCA and energy calculations of the three case studies, the establishment of a CO2 boundary
within the framework of the DGNB certification is discussed.
Integrating a CO2 boundary within the framework of a Greenlandic DGNB certification system presents
both opportunities and challenges. These considerations must balance the unique environmental context
of Greenland, the availability of reliable data, and the goal of promoting sustainability within the building
industry. As indicated by the results, the GWP is dominated by operational energy use in most locations,
highlighting that the greatest improvement potential currently lies in the use stage of buildings. However,
the energy scenarios reveal significant variations depending on location and heating methods, making it
challenging to define a standardized CO2 boundary.
One potential approach is to exclude energy consumption from the CO2 boundary and instead address
the sustainability of energy sources under other DGNB indicators. Greenland’s energy infrastructure is
already transitioning toward renewable energy, with 70% of the energy delivered by Nukissiorfiit cur-
rently sourced from renewable sources. Emphasizing the use of renewable energy sources in other DGNB
83 of 130
==PAGE:100==
Discussion
indicators could encourage the development and adoption of sustainable energy systems without the need
for detailed operational energy calculations, which may be hindered by data limitations.
Another significant obstacle to establishing a CO2 boundary is the lack of comprehensive data for LCAs
in a Greenlandic context. The limited availability of reliable environmental impact data for materials,
energy consumption, and waste scenarios complicates the calculation of accurate LCA results. Under
these circumstances, including LCA calculations in the certification process without imposing a strict
CO2 boundary may be a more practical approach. Incorporating LCA requirements into the DGNB
certification, even without a fixed CO2 boundary, could serve as a catalyst for building a stronger data
foundation.
Requiring project teams to perform LCAs and document assumptions would encourage
stakeholders to collect and share data, gradually enhancing the robustness of Greenland-specific LCA
methodologies. This approach aligns with the broader goal of promoting sustainability while addressing
current data limitations.
Given the uncertainties surrounding waste scenarios, another approach would be to limit the LCA re-
quirement to the A1-A3 and B4 stages. This would simplify calculations and focus on the environmental
impacts most directly linked to material choices, where data is more accessible. Including standardized
values for waste scenarios by material group could provide consistency while addressing data gaps in EoL
stages. Such an approach would give an incentive to reduce embodied carbon while limiting additional
costs and data uncertainties.

Conclusion
8
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to explore sustainable building practices in Greenland, focusing on the environmental
impacts of three different construction methods: half-timber, CLT, and concrete. A key element of this
research was to evaluate the environmental impact of three case buildings over 50 years. Furthermore, it
investigated how the Greenlandic building industry perceives the challenge of sustainable building prac-
tices through interviews with local stakeholders. Additionally, the thesis discusses the establishment of a
CO2 boundary within the DGNB certification framework in a Greenlandic context.
The findings of the interviews show that while the building industry in Greenland faces significant chal-
lenges in transitioning toward more sustainable practices, there is also a growing recognition of their
importance. Based on the interviewed stakeholders, the challenges of building in Greenland are related
to the lack of skilled labor, the need to build with the seasons, and limited data and knowledge about
building sustainably in Greenland’s extreme climate. However, the most recurring concern is from an eco-
nomic perspective, as building activities are getting more expensive. Incorporating certification systems
like DGNB is a way of achieving external financing, but the costs of meeting DGNB standards as well as
hiring the needed labor to perform the certification are still being considered a concern by the stakeholders.
The LCAs on the three case buildings, focusing on life cycle stages A1-A3, B4, and C3-C4, indicate that
CLT building is the most sustainable building method, exhibiting the lowest environmental impact in
four of the five assessed impact categories. However, concerns remain regarding its long-term resilience
in Greenland’s harsh climate, as CLT is still a relatively new construction method in this context. As a
result, successful implementation may require additional experience and education before it can become
a widely adopted building technique in Greenland. Given that construction errors can have a greater
environmental impact than material choice alone, it is essential to thoroughly assess the feasibility of
CLT building. Additionally, the analysis shows that the concrete building has the highest environmental
impact in four out of five impact categories, making it the least sustainable option among those assessed.
Across all three buildings, concrete consistently contributes the highest GWP. Minimizing concrete use
in buildings and reserving it only for essential applications would significantly reduce the overall environ-
mental footprint across various building methods. Furthermore, concrete requires specific temperature
conditions for curing, increasing energy demand during construction. Addressing this issue by optimizing
material use and building practices could further enhance sustainability in building projects.
The environmental impact from the operational stage B6 was determined based on heat loss calculations.
These results demonstrated that energy consumption, rather than material choice, has the greatest sig-
nificance on a building’s CO2 impact throughout its lifespan.
Therefore, prioritizing energy-efficient
solutions and renewable energy sources, such as hydropower, wind, and solar energy, is critical to mini-

Conclusion
mizing a building’s environmental impact. However, the GWP of building operation varies significantly
across locations, requiring location-specific strategies to effectively minimize a building’s GWP.
Based on the LCA results, a suitable CO2 boundary for the DGNB certification system in Greenland is
discussed. However, the lack of specific data for LCAs in Greenland presents a significant challenge to
establishing a CO2 boundary. To address this, incorporating LCA requirements without a strict CO2
limit could encourage data collection and improve the data foundation. Additionally, given the uncer-
tainties surrounding energy calculations, another solution could be to exclude energy consumption from
the CO2 boundary and address it through other DGNB indicators, focusing on renewable energy sources.
Furthermore, the Greenlandic building industry must embrace resource recycling and waste management
strategies to reduce the environmental impact of buildings.
Sustainable building in Greenland is necessary and achievable, but it requires a multifaceted approach
involving governmental policy changes and shifts within the building industry. Further research is needed
to refine data on Greenland’s specific conditions and further explore the feasibility of establishing a CO2
boundary within the DGNB framework. The future of sustainable building practices in Greenland de-
pends on continuous research, data collection, and involving the entire industry in the transition.
This thesis marks a step toward guiding the Greenlandic building industry in a more sustainable direction.
It identifies the main challenges and key opportunities to effectively reduce environmental impact and
highlights critical data gaps that require further investigation.

9 Further Work
==PAGE:106==
==PAGE:107==
Further Work
9
Further Work
This study assessed the environmental impact of three different building methods, with the results con-
sistently showing that concrete has the highest environmental impact across all three case buildings. In
this analysis, concrete production was assumed to occur under Danish conditions, and a Danish sector-
EPD was used for the production stage of the LCAs. For more accurate results, future research should
investigate the environmental impact of concrete production in Greenland, as it may differ from Danish
production due to variations in energy sources, transportation, and material extraction. Additionally,
insights from industry interviews and a site visit to the concrete factory in Nuuk highlighted the high
energy consumption required during the construction phase, primarily due to the need to maintain opti-
mal drying conditions. A more detailed analysis of the construction process (A4-A5) is likely to reveal
an even higher environmental impact from concrete use, reinforcing the need to minimize its application
to reduce the environmental impact of Greenland’s building industry. A study on this topic could also
explore the potential benefits of seasonal construction and opportunities for optimizing energy use during
the building process.
The LCA results further demonstrated that the environmental impact of operational heating is sub-
stantial, particularly when using oil-based heating instead of district heating, as this partially relies on
renewable energy sources. However, the energy demand in this study was estimated using simplified en-
ergy calculations. A more comprehensive energy study would be beneficial, incorporating a more detailed
energy frame calculation as well as an accurate assessment of Greenland’s current energy mix across dif-
ferent regions. Furthermore, developing a long-term energy projection for the next 50 years would be
essential given the country’s ongoing energy transition.
Another critical area for future research is Greenland’s waste management system and the feasibility of
reusing building materials. This would require a detailed examination of material recovery processes,
logistical challenges, and potential policy measures to enhance circular building practices. Furthermore,
a deeper investigation of the ESL of building materials under Greenlandic conditions is necessary. In this
study, the ESLs of materials were based on Danish standards, which may not accurately reflect the ESL
of materials in Greenland’s harsher climate. Understanding these factors would provide essential insights
for improving material sustainability and durability in Greenlandic building practices.
