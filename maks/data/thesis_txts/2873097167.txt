Abstract
In recent years, the increasing penetration of renewable energy sources (RES) such as
wind and solar has introduced new challenges for maintaining stability and ensuring reli-
able control performance in modern power systems. A growing solution to these uncer-
tainties and challenges is the deployment of Hybrid Power Plants (HPPs). These systems
combine wind power plants, solar power plants and battery energy storage systems to
exploit the complementary characteristics of diverse energy assets by sharing the same
power system infrastructure at a single point of connection (PoC). This thesis presents the
modeling and simulation of a utility-scale HPP, with a particular focus on the active power
control strategy and the impact of communication delays within the control architecture.
A detailed modeling framework is developed to simulate the active power control loop un-
der realistic operating conditions. Two delay modeling approaches, discrete-time delay
and second-order delay are implemented at the sub-plant’s PoC to reflect latencies origi-
nate from hierarchical control and communication infrastructure. The study analyzes how
these delays influence system stability, dynamic performance, and control coordination,
evaluated through metrics including response time, settling time, and damping behavior.
Beyond delay analysis, the work explores the internal dynamics of the HPP controller
and critically assesses the role of the Hybrid Power Plant Controller (HPPC) in managing
sub-plants system coordination. Additionally, to investigate the influence of HPPC and
analyzing the different control flows, a new control architecture without HPPC is briefly
discussed.
Overall, the findings contribute to a deeper understanding of HPP system dynamics and
active power control under delay conditions, provide insights for designing robust, adap-
tive control architectures suitable for scalable renewable integration.

Introduction
This chapter establishes the foundational context and rationale for the research by dis-
cussing the challenges and opportunities of integrating hybrid renewable energy resources.
It outlines the key objectives, research questions, assumptions, and contributions guiding
the study. Together, these elements frame the scope and direction of the thesis.
1.1
Motivation
Renewable energy sources (RES) are increasingly being integrated into power systems
worldwide, transforming the energy landscape toward sustainability and reduced environ-
mental impact [1]. Wind energy, which has been a prominent renewable source, is inher-
ently variable and subject to performance uncertainties raising critical questions about its
controllability, predictability, and dispatchability. Moreover, the increasing penetration of
renewables is gradually transforming the power system into a converter dominated net-
work, as converter interfaced generation replaces conventional power plants [2]. These
evolving requirements highlight the necessity of hybrid power plants (HPPs) as in figure
1.1, which combine to utilize the power system infrastructure with prominent active power
generation and wider range of reactive power capabilities.
By combining the renewables assets like wind and solar together with storage provides
multiple benefits, enhancing power reliability, and with integrated design to optimize the
Annual Energy Production (AEP) [3], more details in 2.2. Additionally, the integration of
utility-scale RES using power electronic interfaces enables controlled current injection
and inherent power smoothing capabilities [4]. This is often complemented by small-
scale energy storage systems, which play a crucial role in managing transient events and
delivering essential grid services such as frequency regulation [5, 6].
Figure 1.1: Generic HPP - inspired from [3]
1
==PAGE:10==
Although integrating multiple RES in HPP offers substantial benefits, it also brings sig-
nificant challenges [4]. One of the primary challenges is the coordination between dif-
ferent technologies within the hybrid system to ensure stable and efficient operation [2].
This coordination demands robust control strategies coupled with optimization methods to
maximize plant utilization while enabling participation in various grid services and energy
markets [7]. One of the critical challenge in hybrid power plant control (HPPC) lies in man-
aging communication delays and conflicts among controllers of multiple sub-plants, which
can significantly impact system dynamics and stability. Recent research has focused on
delay sensitive predictive control methods and centralized optimization strategies to ad-
dress these challenges and improve overall robustness [8].
This project focuses on advances in the control architecture of HPPs, with a particular
emphasis on active power control. It offers a detailed analysis of control flow mechanisms
in hierarchical framework and examines the impact of disturbances, including time-delays.
The impact of these delays originating from various sub-plants is evaluated in terms of
their influence on the internal dynamics of the HPP, the behavior of the HPP controller,
and the overall coordination performance. Moreover, there has been a significant gap
between theoretical research based on mathematical modeling and its validation through
practical simulation in HPP. This project also addresses that gap by implementing and
testing control concepts through detailed modeling and simulation.
1.2
Objectives
The objective is to develop and implement a utility-scale HPP model in DIgSILENT Pow-
erFactory software, combining the multiple converter-based systems, like offshore wind
power plants (OWPPs) and solar power plants (SPP) together with battery energy stor-
age systems (BESS). The main focus involves developing active power control strategy
in hierarchical control architecture, while evaluating using a dynamic model developed.
It involves creating three level control layers from asset to plant to HPP level connecting
with each voltage level. As the study focuses on investigating the active power control
flows and impact of communication delays, the key objectives of this study are organized
into the following tasks:
1. Modeling utility scale Co-located converter-based RES like wind and solar with con-
necting to a Point of Common Coupling (PCC).
2. Modeling a control architecture from asset level, sub-plant level and HPP level con-
troller
3. Modeling an active power dispatch strategy at HPP level.
4. Investigation and analysis of the HPP controller dynamics and boundary conditions.
5. Modeling and Investigating the impact of communication delays in sub-plant Point
of Connection (PoC) with comparison.
1.3
Research Question
The project objective is to model and test an advanced control strategy and framework
for active power regulation in HPPs, where effective coordination between sub-plants is
essential. This demands a robust control architecture and a thorough investigation of both
system and controller dynamics. Accordingly, this research aims to address the following
key questions:
1. How effective is the HPP and its control architecture in regulating active power
across different operating conditions?
==PAGE:11==
2. How can active power control in HPPs be optimized to maintain stable and efficient
power injection despite disturbances such as communication delays?
3. What are the dynamic capabilities and boundary limits of the system and controllers
when operating with and without latencies, while fulfilling active power demands?
4. What role does a supervisory controller play in HPPs, and what challenges arise in
its absence?
1.4
Assumptions and Limitations
The following assumptions were made during the modeling and investigation phases of
this research:
1. The available power is assumed to be constant for all the assets, also no aerody-
namic effects for wind or no shadow effect in solar is considered. Since our goal
is to analyze the system stability and control conditions, all the assets are modeled
from the DC link of the back-to-back converter (a constant DC source connected to
grid-side converter (GSC)). Also the available power from the renewable assets are
considered to be constant.
2. To reduce the computational resources, all assets are categorized to aggregated
version of each technologies.
3. Since the research work objectifies to active power control and system control ca-
pability, the reactive power capability or compensation is to be met at PoC is not
considered (mainly for offshore HVAC transmission).
4. Even though BESS connecting with HPPC with SOC, to analyze the dynamic reac-
tions, no degradation of the battery is calculated.
5. No economic or market conditions are taken in consideration.
1.5
Project contribution
This research contributes to advancing the internal coordination of HPPs by developing
a comprehensive methodology for modeling and analyzing their active power control ar-
chitecture under both normal and disturbed conditions. Key aspects of the methodology
include:
• Developing a detailed model of HPP active power control architecture, emphasizing
system stability and performance under extreme operating conditions.
• Investigating the effects of communication delays, modeled as discrete and second-
order delays originating from the sub-plant PoC on system dynamics and controller
behavior.
• Assessing the role and impact of the supervisory controller (HPPC) within the overall
control framework.
The proposed methodology is validated through extensive simulations implemented in
DigSilent PowerFactory. Simulation scenarios include communication delay variations in
different dispatch conditions, controller boundary limitations via active power response
duration with and without disturbances (delays), and supervisory control effects. The re-
sults demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the control strategy in maintaining
power quality, enhancing system stability, and optimizing resource utilization within HPPs.

2
Literature Review
This chapter provides an overview of the literature related to renewable energy technolo-
gies, including their implementation, control strategies, active power control, and the com-
munication delays.
2.1
Renewable Energy Technologies
Over the past decade, the demand for deployment of RES at utility scale, particularly those
connected to transmission networks have increased significantly [9]. This growth has
been largely driven by technological advancements, especially in wind energy, enabling
more efficient, larger, and grid-compliant systems.
2.1.1
Wind Turbines
Traditionally, wind turbine generators (WTGs) were directly connected to the distribution
grid, offering limited control capabilities and primarily operating at maximum power under
normal conditions. They would disconnect in the event of grid faults, providing minimal
grid support [10]. However, in recent decades, advancements in wind turbine technology
have introduced variable-speed operation and enhanced control through power electronic
interfaces. For this study, we focus exclusively on Permanent Magnet Synchronous Gen-
erator (PMSG), supported by a comparative analysis with Doubly-Fed Induction Genera-
tor (DFIG) WTG types.
Doubly-fed Induction Generator (DFIG)
Figure 2.1: DFIG configuration — inspired from [10, 11]
The DFIG is a widely used wind turbine technology that features a wound rotor induction
generator with the stator directly connected to the grid and the rotor connected through
back-to-back voltage source converters. These converters, typically rated at about 30%
of the turbine capacity, include a Rotor Side Converter (RSC) for controlling active and
reactive power and a GSC for maintaining a constant DC link voltage and supporting grid
voltage stability [10]. DFIG systems enable variable-speed operation and efficient Maxi-
mum Power Point Tracking (MPPT), offering a cost-effective solution due to their partial
converter rating [12]. However, since the stator is directly grid-connected, DFIG turbines
are sensitive to grid disturbances, which require protective devices (i.e., crowbar) and
advanced control strategies to meet fault ride-through (FRT) requirements, making them
less ideal for weak or highly unstable grids compared to full-converter systems like PMSG.
5
==PAGE:14==
Permanent Magnet Synchronous Generator (PMSG)
Figure 2.2: PMSG configuration — inspired from [11]
The PMSG can be either synchronous or asynchronous generator is fully de-coupled
from the grid, by the means electronic converter. So, the converters are rated with 100%
capacity of the WTGs. Similar to DFIG, PMSG also has back-to-back voltage source
converters (named as Machine side converter - MSC and Grid Side Converter - GSC)
that are directly connected to the stator of the WTGs. Since this type of the generator is
completely decoupled from the grid frequency, the reactive power transmission from the
machine is not possible. Usually, the reactive power compensation from this WTG’s is
depending upon the rating of the GSC only. During FRT events, PMSG offers enhanced
capabilities compared to other generator types. The presence of a chopper connected
to the DC link allows excess power to be dissipated as heat through a braking resistor,
thereby maintaining DC-link voltage stability. Additionally, the GSC can operate as a
STATCOM to supply reactive power as required by grid codes. The electrical decoupling
from the grid further enhances fault performance, making PMSG advantageous during
short-circuit and low-voltage events.
2.1.2
Solar PV
Solar PV technology primarily relies on the type of silicon cells used, most commonly
monocrystalline and polycrystalline. These two are often compared based on their energy
capture potential and efficiency. However, such in-depth material level comparisons are
beyond the scope of this project.
Figure 2.3: Solar PV configuration — inspired from [11]
Solar panels generate DC power and, by themselves, lack inertial response capability.
This limits their standalone ability to support grid stability services. However, when cou-
pled with energy storage systems, PV installations can contribute to various grid services,
including frequency support and fast power injections.
Control strategies such as MPPT and curtailment are employed in solar systems to man-
age power generation in response to grid requirements. In cases where negative or pos-
itive active power is needed rapidly, support from a BESS becomes essential. Moreover
each PV with storage has its own advantages and disadvantages [13, 14, 15, 16, 17].
Typically, excess curtailed PV power can be stored in the BESS, while power deficits can
be compensated by discharging the storage system.
==PAGE:15==
2.1.3
Battery Energy Storage Systems
Energy storage technologies are diverse, with varying characteristics and performance
metrics. Although a detailed technical comparison is outside the scope of this project, a
brief overview is helpful.
Figure 2.4: Battery configuration — inspired from [11]
BESS technologies can broadly be categorized into following categories [18] and [19]:
• High-energy density systems: These are suitable for long-duration storage and
include lithium-ion batteries, vanadium redox flow batteries (VRB), and fuel cells.
• High-power density systems: These are designed for fast response and short-
duration applications and include flywheels, supercapacitors, and superconducting
magnetic energy storage (SMES).
Figure 2.5: Battery trends — inspired from [18, 19]
In recent years, lithium-ion batteries have emerged as the most commonly used and eco-
nomically viable technology for BESS, owing to their high efficiency, scalability, and cost
reductions due to mass production and technological advancements.
2.2
Hybrid Power Plant
A HPP integrates multiple power generation sources such as wind and solar with energy
storage systems to enhance overall performance and grid compatibility. This combination
allows renewable based facilities to deliver power support similar to conventional genera-
tion systems. One of the key challenges of RES lies in their intermittent and unpredictable
==PAGE:16==
nature, which can compromise reliability and power quality. However, technological ad-
vancements and improved control strategies have made it increasingly possible to miti-
gate these limitations while driving down the levelised cost of energy (LCOE). The figure
2.6 refers the IEA task 50 to create a framework by combining the research and industrial
finding for HPP design and control [20].
Figure 2.6: HPP diagram - from [20]
One of the central motivations behind the development of HPPs lies in their ability to over-
come the technical and economic limitations of standalone renewable sources. When
wind and solar generation are co-optimized and supported by storage, the system bene-
fits from interdependent generation profiles connected at single PoC, which allows short-
term fluctuations in one source to be offset by the other. Additionally, advancements in
control strategies have made it possible to manage these resources collectively, enabling
coordinated power delivery, grid support, and compliance with regulatory requirements.
The operational and economic benefits of HPPs are well recognized [21] and [22]:
• Enhanced power reliability and stability by leveraging the coherent nature of different
renewable sources.
• Shared use of infrastructure, such as substations, transmission lines, and control
systems reduces both capital and operational expenditures.
• Fluctuations in output from one source can be balanced by another, resulting in more
consistent power delivery at the PoC.
• Integrated plant design reduces the need for additional infrastructure expansion.
• Improved system utilization through higher AEP and better capacity factors.
• Increased operational efficiency supports stronger revenue potential.
• Flexibility to tailor the system to specific site conditions and owner-defined energy
requirements.
==PAGE:17==
Das et al. [23] emphasize that combining wind, solar, and storage technologies not only
boosts energy generation but also strengthens participation in spot markets and ancillary
services. For power developers, HPPs unlock broader market access, particularly in of-
fering grid services that go beyond conventional energy delivery. Moreover, this hybrid
configuration can be adapted to existing installations, improving grid efficiency, reducing
transmission congestion, and supporting system wide stability and supply security.
Martinez-Rico et al. [24] provide an in-depth evaluation of HPPs, addressing aspects
such as energy curtailment, variability, storage value, and capacity factor improvements.
Their study presents both technical and economic analyses, highlighting the integration
challenges and benefits, including reduced environmental impact and improved cost-
effectiveness for future grid planning.
2.3
Active Power Regulation
Before analyzing system and controller dynamics, it is important to present how the con-
troller has been tuned under the base case conditions. The tuning process is centered
around achieving a timely response of the system’s active power to its reference, in align-
ment with grid code requirements. According to section 15.2.a in [25] (Danish grid code
requirements), the active power setpoint change can be upto 20% of Pn/min for the Power
Park Modules (PPM). Also, the guidelines from [26] are referred to, specifically those set
by the Transmission System Operator (TSO) regarding response duration for frequency
services. To highlight,
”For power-generating modules with inertia, the maximum admissible initial
delay t1, unless justified otherwise in line with Article 15(2)(d)(iv), is 2 sec-
onds.”
Figure 2.7: Active Power Frequency Response as defined in [26]
Figure 2.7 illustrates the general structure of the active power frequency response curve:
- t1 denotes the initial delay before the active power begins to respond,
- t2 is the time required for full activation of the power change,
- ∆P represents the change in active power from the generating unit,
- Pmax refers to the maximum output power used as the reference for ∆P

Although the testing only covers active power setpoint changes, a maximum response
duration of 2 seconds to reach 95% to 98% of the active power setpoint is assumed
throughout the analysis to ensure completeness and robust evaluation.
2.4
Converter - Asset Control
2.4.1
Grid-Following Inverter
The GFLIs plays a major role in grid stability in the RES integration. GFLIs is a control
mode for GSC, a power electronic device that is connected to the DC link of RES, converts
DC to AC dynamics compatible with the electrical grid. They operate by following the
grid’s voltage and frequency, adjusting their output to match the grid’s parameters. This
synchronization is achieved through control mechanisms that monitor and respond to grid
conditions which involves the following process with the respective components.
1. The control process starts with the Phase Lock Loop (PLL), which takes the grid
voltage (Vabc), converts it into the dq reference frame, and extracts the phase angle
and angular frequency by aligning with the q-axis component of the voltage.
2. This phase information from the PLL is then used to generate the active (P) and
reactive (Q) power references, which define the desired power exchange between
the inverter and the grid.
3. These P and Q references are compared with the actual power values measured
from the inverter output, and the resulting error is processed by the power controller
to compute the corresponding current references in the dq frame.
4. The current controller receives these current references and compares them with
the actual inverter current, generating a complex voltage signal in dq coordinates to
correct the error.
5. This voltage signal is sent to the PWM generator, which produces switching sig-
nals for the inverter, enabling it to perform DC-AC conversion and inject the correct
amount of power into the grid in a synchronized manner.

2.4.2
Grid-side Converter - Control strategies
While the control strategies of converters may differ depending on the underlying tech-
nology, their fundamental objective remains the same; to regulate grid voltage and fre-
quency through the control of active and reactive power, with the DC-link voltage (Vdc).
The previous section discussed the GFLI operating in PQ control mode, whereas the fol-
lowing sections introduce alternative control modes, such as Vdc-Q mode. Converters, as
real-time power electronic devices, are responsible for manipulating power flow by con-
trolling voltage and current at high precision. In this project, the focus is on the GSCs of
each technology, where a cascaded PI control structure is employed. The outer (slower)
control loop manages active and reactive power, while the inner (faster) loop regulates
converter current in the dq reference frame. The previous section on GFLI is explained
with PQ control mode, while in the following content other control modes like Vdc-Q mode
is introduced.
This cascade control design ensures both dynamic stability and accurate tracking of power
references in figure A.1. All converters in the model operate in GFL mode, meaning they
synchronize with and respond to the grid’s voltage and frequency rather than setting them.
In the implemented simulation model A.2, a DigSilent Power Factory based representa-
tion of a GFLI is used for Grid-side WTG controller. This model includes a built-in current
controller, while the power controller is developed separately. The power controller gen-
erates current references in the dq frame, which are sent as inputs to the converter model
for real-time control.
Well, the solar and battery are imposed with similar modeling strategy with different control
modes and architecture. The Solar inverter is responding in Vdc-Q control mode A.3,
where the active power reference is directly led to the DC current source from the plant-
level controller. The asset PI controller takes the reactive power reference from the plant-
level controller and DC Voltage reference as a constant value. Later, the asset power
controller provides the dq current references in figure A.3.
Similar to the wind plant, the battery system also operates in PQ control mode A.4, where
the active and reactive power references are generated at the sub-plant level and passed
to the asset-level PI controllers. These PI controllers process the error between refer-
ence and measured values and generate the required rotor current components in the dq
reference frame.
However, unlike other converter controllers, the BESS includes a dedicated charge control
block. This block plays a crucial role by monitoring the battery’s internal state, specifically,
it receives signals such as cell voltage (Ucell), cell current (Icell), and state of charge (SOC)
from the battery management system. Based on these inputs and the incoming power
reference, the charge control block dynamically adjusts the current commands to ensure
safe and efficient operation.
The charge control output includes the updated rotor current references (idref and iqref),
which are then sent to the converter. Additionally, it generates a delta current signal, which
is looped back into the PI control structure to fine tune the system’s response.
A current limiter block is also integrated to ensure that the calculated rotor current val-
ues remain within permissible limits, verified against real-time AC voltage measurements.
This ensures both system protection and stable dynamic performance under varying op-
erating conditions.

2.5
Communication and Sensor technologies
The integration of communication networks and sensor technologies plays a critical role
in the operation of modern smart grids. As more information technology (IT) systems
are incorporated into the electrical grid, concerns are growing about increased vulnera-
bility, particularly due to the complex interactions between the cyber (digital) and physical
components of the system, which may lead to unforeseen behaviors or risks [28]. One
of the key challenges that emerges from this interaction is communication delay, which
can affect the real-time performance and reliability of the grid. Before delving into the de-
lays, it is important to first establish a foundational understanding of the communication
technologies that underpin these smart grid systems.
2.5.1
Communication Technologies
1. Power Line Communication (PLC) is known for its reliability; however, it suffers
from relatively high delays and packet loss, which can affect real-time control.
2. 2G/3G and other GPRS technologies offer effective communication for small-scale
wind farms with moderate delays and lower packet loss, making them suitable for
distributed generation systems.
3. CANBUS is reliable primarily for one-way communication and requires message
prioritization to ensure critical data transmission.
4. Fiber Optic Cables provide high Quality of Service (QoS) and are ideal for newly
developed wind farms. For existing farms, fiber optics can be integrated with CAN-
BUS or Ethernet Passive Optical Networks (EPON) using copper wire lines.
2.5.2
Real-time Impact of delays
Delays in communication systems are significant concerns, especially in scenarios in-
volving long cable connections in offshore wind farms [29], multiple-vendor equipment
integration, sensor noise, control signal flows, and slow switching or PWM delays. These
factors contribute to different types of delays, which can be categorized as follows:
• Measurement Delay: The time taken for sensors to capture and process data be-
fore it is transmitted.
• Communication Delay: The latency introduced during data transmission through
communication networks.
• Digital Time Delay: Delays caused by processing time in digital controllers and
converters.
Since modeling is crucial for advancements in modern power systems, many studies have
focused on delays and their impact on system behavior, both through simulation modeling
and hardware-in-the-loop testing. However, in practice, delays are neither constant nor
predictable. Therefore, it is important to analyze system stability considering communi-
cation delays that vary randomly, along with random disturbances that have a non-zero
average value [28]. To provide a general overview of communication delays associated
with generation technologies, the following literature has been reviewed, to attest the pos-
sible delays related to generation technologies, although some network protocols ensures
fixed communication delay, a study on investigating a real-time control system with ran-
dom delays [30], also [31] delivers the experimental measured delay errors applied in
Ethernet-based communication network used for power systems. Further, the [32] shows
how delays influence the operation of electricity markets.

2.5.3
Discrete Time delays
The discrete-time delays refer to the intentional or inherent shifting of digital signals by
a fixed or variable number of sampling intervals. These delays often arise due to limita-
tions in communication channels, such as packet transmission rates, sampling periods,
and network-induced delays, particularly in systems where sensors and actuators inter-
face over a Control Area Network (CAN) [30]. As mentioned in previous sub-section, the
communication delays are not imposed as a constant value, the values depend on the
different cases with variables [28]. Also, constant delays might occur due to multi-vendor
system installation in co-located HPPs or at decentralized HPPs and long transmission
lines within the power plants (i.e., offshore wind farms). Mathematically, this can be ex-
pressed as Equation (2.1):
y[n] = x[n −D],
(2.1)
where D is the delay in discrete time steps, x[n] represents the original input signal at
discrete time n, and y[n] is the delayed output signal.
Modeling these delays in discrete time is essential, as most communication systems op-
erate on sampled signals and digital protocols. Discrete-time delay models enable the
simulation of transmission lags and facilitate the analysis of their impact on control loops
in networked systems, particularly in smart grids, where precise timing is critical for main-
taining stability, performance quality, and system responsiveness. According to [33], the
testing of active and reactive power was carried out using a HIL setup, during which a de-
lay of approximately 300–400 ms was observed in the solar PV system, while the BESS
exhibited a delay of around 100 ms. These delay timestamps, as reported in the study,
were adopted and implemented in our HPP model for realistic performance evaluation.

3.2
Control Architecture
The adoption of RES such as wind and solar in modern power systems is driven by techno-
logical advancements, economic viability, and strong policy support. However, the inher-
ent weather dependence of RES introduces dynamic uncertainties and potential stability
issues. To mitigate these challenges, energy storage systems (ESS) are increasingly co-
located with renewable sources, forming HPPs. These utility-scale HPPs offer notable
advantages, including enhanced controllability, improved dispatchability, and reduced in-
frastructure costs [9].
When these technologies are integrated into a single power-generating facility with a
grid connection point either non-synchronously connected to the network or connected
through power electronics, they are classified as PPM [26]. Despite their benefits, such
integrated infrastructures require a robust energy management system (EMS) and a co-
ordinated control architecture. This study highlights the importance of supervisory control
and focuses on the development and implementation of a centralized HPPC for effective
operation and grid compliance, which leaves the HPP EMS out of the scope.

3.2.1

The HPP EMS level focuses on maximizing HPP revenue with utility regulations and phys-

ergy and balancing markets and the reserve market (ancillary services) while considering
market forecast and renewable forecast [9]. Aforementioned, this study focuses on the
control architecture, the system is modeled with constant active power reference which is
in real-life is delivered from the E2P block from the HPP EMS level.
The HPP control level functions as a centralized controller that coordinates the operation
of all sub-plants and optimizes energy production according to grid code requirements and
energy market demands. Its core tasks include ensuring that the committed power reserve
is always available and that energy delivery meets specified timing constraints, including
ramp rate limitations. It also enforces an upper power limit on active power injection to
comply with grid constraints while maintaining reactive power output as specified by the
system operator (SO) to support voltage stability, in coordination with other reactive power
compensation equipments [9].

3.3
Dispatch Function - Active Power Control
In power system operations, the dispatch function is typically responsible for coordinating
all key control tasks, including active power control, frequency regulation, reactive power
control, and voltage regulation. However, the scope of this project is specifically limited to
active power control, and thus, the dispatcher is designed to focus solely on active power
dispatch.
Figure 3.8 presents a general overview of how active power references are distributed
across different assets within the HPP, as managed by the EMS. The core responsibility
of the HPP Dispatcher is to evaluate the dynamic capabilities of each sub-plant, primarily
their available generation capacity with response behavior and then allocate active power
references accordingly. This allocation ensures that the total power delivered by the HPP
at the PoC meets the contractual active power commitments made by the EMS.
While energy is traded in terms of total energy content, the EMS generates energy refer-
ences, which are then converted into active power references via E2P block. These active
power references serve as inputs to the HPP controller, enabling real-time regulation of
plant output.
==PAGE:32==
Figure 3.10: SOC- Dispatch - Inspired from [9]
To simplify the dispatch logic, the control strategy is limited to two operational modes:
MPPT and Optional Curtailment - BESS charging. The mode selection is governed by the
difference between the total active power reference and the measured available power.
Before determining the appropriate control mode, the system also considers the current
SOC of the BESS, as shown in Figure 3.10. This assessment provides a real-time under-
standing of the flexibility and availability of the BESS for absorbing or delivering power.
Once the SOC and availability data are gathered, the dispatcher collects the real-time
available power from each sub-plant, along with the active power reference from the EMS.
The available power from all sources typically wind and solar is aggregated to determine
the total available power from the HPP. The dispatcher then evaluates the control logic:
• If the active power reference from the EMS exceeds the total available power, the
system enters MPPT mode (red-dotted lines at 3.11), allowing each sub-plant to
operate at its maximum capability.
• Conversely, if the active power reference is less than the available power, the system
transitions to Optional Curtailment - BESS charging (green-dotted lines at 3.11).
- In Optional Curtailment - BESS charging mode, the dispatcher first calculates the excess
power, defined as the difference between the available power and the reference power.
The system then determines whether this excess can be entirely stored in the BESS.
If the excess power is within the storage capacity limit and SOC conditions permit the
dispatcher sends a command to store the entire excess in the battery (yellow-dotted lines
at 3.11).
- However, if the excess power exceeds the storage capability, only capable limit is stored
in the BESS, and the remaining power must be curtailed. The dispatcher then computes
the required curtailment by subtracting the storable energy from the total excess. This
curtailment requirement is distributed among the assets based on their respective contri-
bution to the HPP (blue-dotted lines at 3.11).
==PAGE:33==
Figure 3.11: Active power Dispatch function
According to selected HPP sizing from HyDesign, as mentioned in 3.1, wind and solar
contribute 81% and 19% of the total available power, respectively. Therefore, the curtail-
ment effort is split in the same proportion across these two sources. This ensures that the
overall reduction in output is consistent with each RES generation capacity while main-
taining HPP system balance in accordance with the control strategy. This proportional
approach offers several key benefits:
• This method ensures that curtailment effort is shared fairly among all assets, aligning
the control logic with the design capabilities of each asset and maintaining system
balance.
• The proportional approach is simple and requires minimal data inputs, making it
highly suitable for fast real-time decision-making and integration into existing EMS
or SCADA frameworks.
• By distributing curtailment evenly across available assets, the strategy avoids stress-
ing a one asset, thus improving operational balance and supporting long-term reli-
ability of equipment.
On the other hand, curtailing from only one asset, such as wind or solar alone, can create
inefficiencies. Relying on one asset may create risk factors, may lead to suboptimal en-
ergy dispatch, and hard to adjust to changes in the grid fluctuations or market conditions.
Therefore, the proportional curtailment strategy not only enhances technical performance
but also contributes to the economic and reliability of the HPPs.

Impact of Second-order Communication Delay in HPP
To evaluate the effect of second-order delay in active power control of HPPs, the pade
approximation was introduced in the communication signal flow at plant-level PoC. To
have a better understanding of this block, the variables are explained as follows. The
input signal, denoted as yi, represents the active power signal, while the output yo is the
delayed version used by the control system. Internally, the model defines two dynamic
states: x1, which tracks the smoothed value of the input, and x2, which represents the
rate of change of x1. The output yo is formed as a weighted combination of x1 and x2,
which is defined as follows:

Where,
• x1: main smoothed state approximating delayed yi
• x2: derivative or rate of change of x1
• yo: the delayed or filtered output
• offset = yi: tracks initial input to maintain continuity
4.2.1

As we know, discrete delay is a type of delay where the output is held for a fixed duration
before responding, whereas second-order delay introduces a gradual response over time.
Renewable sources like wind (especially under offshore conditions) and solar often expe-
rience critical conditions that result in delayed communication signals, which degrade the
controller ability to respond effectively. Although second-order delays are typically used
to model sensor related delays, in this study, the delay block is also placed at the plant
PoC or common PCC, similar to discrete delay placement, to allow a fair comparison of
system dynamics. Given that wind energy dominates the system, the investigation starts
with delays of 200ms, 400ms, and 600ms. These tests revealed a sluggish dynamic re-
sponse characterized by under-damped behavior. So, testing is carried out with a step
change of 100MW increased from 350 MW and vice-versa.

BESS - supervisory control - Controller dynamics
To have a better understanding of the boundary conditions and failure limits, the BESS
supervisory control architecture is testing with capable ramping limits. First 5 seconds has
neglected due to initial conditions, the step-change in power was started from 0.5 p.u to 1
p.u. at 10th second, later the steps are reduced to 0.15 p.u. for every 5 second till 0.25 p.u,
in figure 4.25. As noticed in edge cases, the BESS-supervisory control is very sensitive
with limited ramping capability. Since the BESS acts as superior controller, which gives
the faster active power response also comes with disadvantages like transient effect for
every step in power transfer, which will often lead to the battery degradation in real-life.
To better understand the boundary conditions and failure limits, the BESS supervisory
control architecture is tested under scenarios with defined ramping capabilities. The first
5 seconds are excluded to account for initial conditions. A step change in power is in-
troduced at the 10-second mark, increasing from 0.5 p.u. to 1 p.u. Subsequently, the
step size is reduced to 0.15 p.u. every 5 seconds until reaching 0.25 p.u., as shown in
Figure 4.25.
==PAGE:59==
Figure 4.25: Controller limits - BESS - Supervisory control
In this edge-case scenario, the BESS supervisory control strategy demonstrates high
sensitivity when operating near its ramping limits. While this approach offers certain ad-
vantages, it also presents critical trade-offs. One key benefit is that reducing one of the
PI controllers can result in a faster response compared to a traditional hierarchical control
framework. However, a major drawback is the system’s reliance on a single sub-plant.
If the supervisory sub-plant were to shut down, it could lead to the disconnection of the
entire HPP. Although the BESS acts as a fast-responding upper-layer controller for ac-
tive power, it introduces challenges such as pronounced transients during power steps.
In practical applications, these repeated transients may accelerate battery degradation,
raising concerns about long-term reliability. The level of transients is also caused by
the BESS controller indirectly replicating the error at the HPP level, while simultaneously
summing the entire HPP error, which already includes the actual BESS error signal from
the PCC. This overlapping error contribution leads to redundant corrective action and
increased transient response.

5
Conclusion and Future work
This chapter concludes the current research work and highlights key directions for ex-
tending the study on HPPs. It emphasizes potential improvements in control strategies,
system modeling, and experimental validation. The focus is on advancing system robust-
ness, grid support functionalities, and real-time applicability to support future renewable
energy integration.
5.1
Conclusion
This thesis presents a comprehensive investigation into the modeling and analysis of ac-
tive power control in HPPs, with a particular emphasis on coordination challenges arising
from communication delays and sub-plant integration. A utility-scale HPP model, com-
prising converter-based renewable energy systems such as offshore wind and solar PV,
along with BESS was developed in DIgSILENT PowerFactory, to evaluate system and
controller dynamics under various operating conditions.
The introduction chapter provides the foundational motivation for integrating RES as HPPs
within modern power systems and discusses the associated challenges in converter-
dominated networks. It highlights the necessity of coordinated control strategies in HPPs
that combine wind, solar, and storage resources. Furthermore, it outlines the main re-
search objectives, including the development of a multi-level control architecture, the im-
pact assessment of communication delays, and the analysis of a supervisory controller’s
role in ensuring stable and efficient active power regulation.
Further, the work reviews key renewable energy technologies, including wind turbines
(PMSG and DFIG), solar PV, and BESS, along with their simplified modeling approaches
for grid integration. It details how these assets are represented at the DC-link stage and
managed through GSC employing cascaded PI controllers. The concept of HPP is thor-
oughly explained, emphasizing the integration of multiple renewable sources and storage
to improve grid reliability, deliver ancillary services, and reduce overall energy costs. Ac-
tive power regulation techniques and relevant grid code requirements for frequency re-
sponse are discussed. Converter control strategies are explored, particularly focusing on
GFLIs and their role in synchronization and power injection. Lastly, the chapter analyzes
communication technologies and the effects of delays modeled using discrete-time and
second-order Padé approximations on real-time control performance and system stability
in HPPs.
In chapter 3, the design and modeling of HPP to support utility-scale renewable inte-
gration. It details the configuration and control architecture of the HPP, including the
hierarchical coordination from energy management - HPPC - sub-plant level - asset-level
controllers. The dispatch strategy for active power balancing, including MPPT and curtail-
ment with battery charging, is explained with a focus on extreme SOC conditions. Various
operational scenarios, including edge cases with different battery SOC levels, are ana-
lyzed to evaluate system stability and dynamic response. Overall, it lays the foundation
for understanding the coordinated control and operational behavior of HPPs.
Finally, this chapter presents a detailed sensitivity analysis of active power response in
HPP under various communication delay scenarios, including discrete and second-order
delays. It evaluates how delays in wind, solar, and battery controls impact system stability
53
==PAGE:62==
and power delivery at the PoC. The discrete delays highlight the limitations in BESS sup-
port and oscillations caused by delayed signals, especially in wind-dominant scenarios.
Second-order delays introduce smoother but under-damped responses, affecting curtail-
ment strategies and controller dynamics differently. Additionally, the chapter investigates
the the controller dynamics under step changes and communication delays, assessing
the limits and performance of the supervisory control system. Further, the supervisory
control of BESS without the HPPC, demonstrating faster but more sensitive responses
that risk transient effects is discussed.
As concluding the research work with following statements, as follows,
• The designed control architecture was effective in regulating active power dispatch,
maintaining system stability, and managing boundary conditions under typical and
stressed scenarios.
• Communication delays significantly influenced controller performance, introducing
oscillations and transient deviations, particularly when delays exceeded certain thresh-
olds.
• The presence of a supervisory controller (HPPC) played a critical role in harmoniz-
ing sub-plant responses, reducing control conflicts, and improving overall system
stability.
These findings validate the robustness and relevance of the proposed control methodol-
ogy, demonstrating its ability to enhance the performance of HPPs in the evolving converter-
dominated power system landscape.
5.2
Future Work
While this study focused on active power control and system dynamics, several areas
remain open for future exploration:
• Model a suitable BESS configuration: In this thesis, some limitations were ob-
served in the BESS model’s response to dispatch functions, particularly under ex-
treme SOC conditions. This is mainly due to using the default BESS model in DIgSI-
LENT PowerFactory, which includes internal constraints that do not fully align with
externally defined dispatch strategy (mainly in power curtailment). Due to time con-
straints and the scope of the project, these discrepancies were not addressed in
detail.
• Evaluate the BESS supervisory control: The final section of this thesis highlights
the limitations of the BESS supervisory control hierarchy, particularly regarding tran-
sient behavior. This architecture can be further improved by modifying the dispatch
function to minimize redundancy and reduce transients. More efficient control strat-
egy could involve bypassing the dispatch function for certain assets, allowing the
error at grid PoC to be distributed directly to the storage system. This would sim-
plify coordination and enhance response effectiveness under dynamic conditions.
• Reactive Power and Voltage Control: The current work did not include reactive
power management, which is vital for voltage support, particularly in offshore HVAC
transmission. Future work could extend the model to incorporate dynamic reac-
tive power sharing and voltage regulation strategies to improve voltage stability and
power quality.
• Forecasting and Power Availability Models: A constant power availability as-
sumption was used for wind and solar assets. Future studies could integrate more
==PAGE:63==
realistic forecasting models and aerodynamic or photovoltaic panel behavior to bet-
ter capture variability and intermittency, enhancing system accuracy and reliability.
• Frequency Services Modeling: The study primarily addressed primary active power
control, but the integration of frequency regulation services such as inertia emula-
tion, fast frequency response, and secondary frequency control could be explored
to support grid stability.
• Grid-Forming Capabilities: Investigating grid-forming inverter control strategies
for hybrid renewable systems would enable enhanced black-start capability and sys-
tem resilience. Future work could focus on developing and validating grid-forming
controls compatible with multiple distributed energy resources.
• HiL Testing: To bridge the gap between theoretical modeling and real-time de-
ployment, future work could explore HiL validation and integration with digital twin
frameworks. This approach would facilitate robust testing of control algorithms un-
der realistic and dynamic conditions.
• Development of EMS: Building upon the proposed control framework, future re-
search could focus on designing advanced EMS that coordinate energy dispatch,
storage, and demand response to optimize system performance and economic op-
eration under varying market and grid conditions.
This thesis provides a solid foundation for further research in robust and scalable control
architectures for hybrid renewable energy systems, particularly in active power controlla-
bility. The proposed framework can serve as a baseline for extending real-world applica-
tions in next generation grid interactive power plants.
