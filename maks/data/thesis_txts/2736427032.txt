Abstract
Being utility­scale Hybrid Power Plants a recent concept and area of research, there is still much
that can be investigated. This thesis presents a methodology for assessing the profitability of
hybridizing existing VRES generation with additional renewable technology and batteries, by
comparing the Net Present Value (NPV) over the initial investment (CAPEX) of the stand­alone
existing technology and of the new hybrid. The methodology is built upon an existing open­
source tool capable of optimizing hybrid power plants, by implementing new components to it.
Two case studies are conducted, one for existing wind and another for existing Photovoltaic (PV)
systems. Sensitivity analyses are then performed, to evaluate the influence of key parameters.
The findings reveal that the timing of hybridization plays a role in decision­making, with optimal
hybridization occurring after 12 years in the studied case of existing wind and in year zero in
the existing PV case. Inflation rates, discount rates, and investment costs significantly impact
the results. Furthermore, future scenarios are investigated to estimate the potential influence
of electricity price variations, due to the renewable cannibalisation effect.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The transition to a low­carbon society is a hard challenge, but at the same time it can represent
an opportunity to develop a better future for the world. A climate­neutral society aims to achieve
a balance between greenhouse gas emissions that are released with those that can be absorbed
or offset. According to this vision it is urgent to transition away from fossil fuels, towards cleaner
and renewable energy sources. The European Union aims to achieve climate neutrality by
2050, by reducing the greenhouse emissions to zero. This goal is the main character of the
European Green Deal, according to the commitment of Europe under the Paris Agreement. [1]
The achievement of this target has been pursued in the last years, increasing projects and re­
search around renewable energies and improving the regulations that support them. PV solar,
in particular, has experienced exponential expansion, with solar panels becoming increasingly
affordable and efficient. Similarly, advancements in wind turbine technology have made wind
power an increasingly viable and competitive option for electricity generation. As a result, re­
newable energy capacity has surged globally, surpassing previous projections.
Renewable energy technologies, like PV solar and wind plant, have indeed achieved notable
cost reductions during the last 20 years, with average learning rates of 24% & 15% [2]. Looking
ahead, projections indicate a continued decrease in the cost of renewables, driven by techno­
logical advancements and increased investment in clean energy infrastructure.
Figure 1.1: Historical trends of LCOE for utility­scale wind (left) and PV (right) in the United
States, from [2]
Nowadays, approximately 12% of electricity is generated from renewable energy sources [3].
However, this increased reliance on RES has introduced a phenomenon known as the ’can­
nibalization effect’ [4]. This effect comes from the intrinsic intermittency of renewable energy,
such as solar and wind power, which often produce electricity at the same times of the day, lead­
ing to periods of oversupply and subsequent price suppression in energy markets. As a result,
renewable energy technologies may experience lower market values than the average market
price, resulting in an economic challenge for renewables developers and investors. Moreover,
the cannibalization effect highlights the importance of implementing effective grid management
strategies and energy storage solutions to mitigate the impacts of intermittency and ensure the

stability and reliability of renewable energy integration into the electricity grid. Another conse­
quence of the increased share of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) in the power system is that,
in many countries, it has become difficult to obtain connection right to transmission and distri­
bution networks, for renewable energy investors. In Spain, for example, the technical report [5]
shows that the delay in the process to get grid permits represents a high risk to achieving the
emission reduction targets.
These multiple reasons are the main motivation when it comes to explain why hybrid power
plants (HPP) of renewable sources are becoming fundamental and have gained an important
role in the energy sector. By definition, hybrid power plants (HPP) of renewables are plants that
integrate more then one technology, and they may include: wind turbines, solar Photovoltaic
(PV), solar Concentrated Solar Power (CSP), storage, geothermal power, hydro power and
biomass [6]. Nowadays, one of the main focus regards the combination of solar PV, wind and
Lithium Ion batteries, even if the research is moving in the direction of integrating Hydrogen
and Ammonia production as well. HPP plants are meant to reduce the costs, to have higher
efficiencies and provide more stability to the sytsem, since they can provide more reliability.
Even newer is the concept of ’hybridization’. As commented by EDPR, in [7], ”The hybridization
of pre­existing assets via the adaptation of wind and solar technologies is one of the main
pathways for growth within the renewable energy sector”. Hybridization can offer a number of
benefits, like for example higher efficiencies projects, the use of existing electrical infrastructure,
higher cost stability and a lower environmental impact.
1.2
Research objective
Individual technology plants are gaining significance as subjects of study due to their poten­
tial suitability for hybridization. This thesis aims to comprehensively analyze the concept of
hybridization, with the primary goal of identifying the key factors that significantly influence an
owner’s decision­making process. The focus is on understanding whether it is economically
viable to invest in hybridization, or to keep getting revenue only from the existing one. As a
consequence, an additional objective of this thesis is to develop a methodology that enables to
assess the hybridization of existing RES plants, by solving an optimization problem.
1.3
Research questions
In order to enable the hybridization of existing wind or solar power plants, a number of open
issues need to be addressed. The main one is formulated below and it is meant to serve as a
guide for the literature review and for the development of this project:
• In which conditions hybridization makes sense for the owner of an existing plant?
Some subquestions are also formulated, to address more specific problems:
• What is the optimal capacity of new generation for hybridizing an existing plant?
• What are the investment costs for the hybridizing technology that make sense for prof­
itability?
• What is the impact of the future energy price reduction, due to renewables cannibalisation
effect?
• How do hybridization impact the revenue stability?

Structure of report

explained in Section 3 and, Section 4 describes how the method is implemented and validated.

2
Literature Review
2.1
State of the art design of hybrid power plants
Renewable hybrid power plants integrate different renewable energy technologies, that may in­
clude energy storage as well. The contributions of each energy source are joint and coordinated
to obtain a more affordable, sustainable and reliable energy system for the supply.
2.1.1
Taxonomy
According to [8] hybrid systems can be divided in three categories, depending on the locational
and operational linkages between the different technologies.
• Co­located resources, they include technologies which are geographically linked, but
whose control operations are entirely independent of one another, indeed, they have dif­
ferent points of connection, but same substation. Their primary benefit is the cost saving
coming from the Balance of System (BOS) and the transmission interconnection costs,
which is possible because co­located plants usually belong to the same owner. More­
over, co­located plants may follow the permitting procedures for independent systems,
which could result being advantageous from a financial risk point of view.
• Virtual power plants, they include technologies that are located in different places, but
that share the same control operations, meaning that they contribute to the grid together.
Their main benefit is that each constituent technology can be sited in its optimal location,
taking advantage from the resource complementarity. Moreover, they can contribute to
the energy markets as an aggregate, providing higher­value grid services, even if this can
depends a lot on the specific case conditions.
• Full hybrids, they share both the same location and the same operations, so both the
same substation and the same point of connection to the grid.
Figure 2.1: Co­located (left), Virtual Power Plants (middle), Full Hybrid (right) [8]
Full hybrid power plants
Full hybrid power plants are described more in detail, because they constitute the main subject
of the thesis. They refer to a facility that integrates various energy production systems and
they can include or not energy storage. One of the main aspects of the full hybrid plants is
that they represent a joint system, which shares components and strategies of control. This
aspect allows to mitigate the the variability of single RES sources and maximize the utilization

==PAGE:12==
of available resources, while reducing energy losses. As a consequence, the efficiency of the
whole energy system is expected to increase, compared to a single technology one.
From an administrative and organizational point of view, it is crucial that a single company over­
sees the entire generation process, including control strategies and operations. It is therefore
fundamental the involvement of a unified connection point and the implementation of a consis­
tent control and monitoring system.
Methods for evaluation of full hybrid plants require an optimised design of the joint systems,
introducing modelling complexities. The latter are given mainly by the increasing number of
decision variables, which are in charge for the component sizing and for the strategies of coor­
dination between the technologies.
In the particular case of full hybrids that combine solar and wind resources, the literature agree
that one of the main motivations for joining these two technologies is the reduction in the shared
BOS costs, like management, substations, and grid connection costs, and the transmission
interconnection costs. This option can be especially interesting for existing wind farms, by
installing PV modules close to the wind turbines. However, a beneficial output would depend
on several factors, like the possibility of using the land, due to its characteristics, the quality of
both wind and solar resource and the local policies and market.
It is evident that a hybrid plant does not maximize the energy output of individual generation
technologies, but the generation and operation of the hybrid power plant as a whole. The de­
sign of the different energy technologies should, indeed, allow them to complement each other,
through their technology configurations. This configuration doesn’t need to be the optimal as
if the plants were stand­alone, but it must be the optimal for the whole system. For example,
different hub heights can be examined to select which is the wind resource that can complement
the generation of the PV array configuration, leading to the maximum production.

Examples of existing hybrid plants
Hybrid power plants have been developing with an increasing rate in the past decade, all around
the world. The following paragraph provides an overview of notable hybrid power plants that
combine wind, PV and batteries or only wind and PV, since they are the main focus of this work.
These plants come from various regions, with different weather resources and each of them
show diverse configurations and contributions to the green transition.

Haringvliet project, Netherlands
The Haringvliet plant was commissioned in 2022, in the Netherlands. It includes three different
technologies: 21 MW of wind, 41 MWp of solar PV and 12 MWh/12 MW of battery storage. The
plant falls in the category of ’co­located’ type and it has a grid contracted capacity of 50 MW.
The plant will have a production of approximately 140 GWh of electricity per year, enough to
give power to around 40000 households. [10]
Minnesota Community Site, United States of America
Trondhjem Township, in Minnesota [11], hosts since December 2018, a hybrid power plant that
includes wind and solar PV and is meant for commercial use. The plant includes a single 2.3
MW wind turbine and a 500 kWp solar array and it provides clean and renewable energy to
the local community of Lake region. Despite all the advantages, the project had to face some
challenges, mainly related to grid connection requirements, due to the innovative nature of the
plant. It was finally agreed that the total output power of the joint system wind and solar would
be limited to the nameplate capacity of the wind turbines.
Graciosa project in Azores, Portugal
By supplying the local grid with 4.5 MW of wind, 1 MW of solar and a energy storage of 6
MW / 3.2 MWh, from 2018, the Graciosa Hybrid Renewable Power Plant [12] contributes to the
island independence from petroleum imports, lowering its greenhouse gas emissions. The plant
control is managed by an energy management software system, based on artificial intelligence,
which can balance the different energy sources. This system considers weather forecasts and
load patterns to optimise the energy generation. As a result, the utilization of renewable energy
rises, leading to a lower cost of diesel for power generation and to an increase of reliability of
the island’s grid.
Kennedy Energy Park, Australia
The region of central north Queensland, in Australia, is home to both wind and solar resources,
which have high complementarity between them. Therefore this location hosts one of the first
and biggest hybrid power plants of the country: the Kennedy Energy Park [13]. It is a hybrid

Hybridization
Currently, the word ’hybridization’ has acquired a new and important meaning, since research
is increasing a lot around the possibility of hybridizing existing RES power plants with new
generation sources.
Hybridization offers numerous benefits for production companies, institutions, and for the soci­
ety [15].

• The environmental and landscape impact is greatly minimized, compared to building a
new plant, by sharing infrastructure, such as power lines and substations,

Regulations
Since ’hybrid power plants’ and ’hybridization’ are relatively new concepts, the regulations on
the topic are still very limited and not precise, almost anywhere in the world[16]. India is among
the leading nations in this area. It has a special policy active since 2017, with the objective
of offering a framework for the development of utility­scale wind­photovoltaic hybrid systems.
Australia is also seeing an increase in hybrid power plants, as a result of stricter grid regulations.
Regarding the European continent, countries such as Spain, Portugal and Ireland have already
taken measures to include the concept of hybridization in their regulations.
Spain
In Spain, the latest regulation, the ’Royal Decree 1183/2020’ [17] states that it is possible to
hybridise an existing generation by integrating a new technology or storage, without requiring
a new permit to access the grid connection. It is also possible to use the already contracted
capacity to discharge energy.
There are some requirements that must be taken in consideration by promoters that are planning
to hybridize an existing generation, by using the same contracted grid capacity. Some of these
requirements are listed below:
• The hybridization should not involve an increase of the original granted access to the grid
higher then 5%. 5% is, indeed, the limit in order to consider the new hybrid as the same
installation as before.
• The owner of the existing technology must already have a connection permit.
• The existing technology that is going to be hybridized must never represent less then the
40% of all the already contracted grid capacity. For instance, if a photovoltaic plant has
a granted grid connection of 20 MW, whenever it is hybridized, its capacity may in no
case be lower then 8 MW. This regulation is created in order to prevent hybridization from
becoming a way to substitute an old technology with a new one.
Portugal
Portugal as well, has recently adapted its legislation, making administration processes easier.
With the publication of ’Decree Law 15/2022’ [18], hybridization becomes exempt from TRC
(Grid Capacity Reserve Title), since it doesn’t increase the contracted capacity. Moreover, hy­
bridization may be granted to a different owner of the generating plant to be hybridized without
the need for a dominance relationship.
These regulations contribute to facing the problem that nowadays, obtaining a connection right
for building a new renewable plant can take several years, due to the administration saturation,
caused by the increase of renewable projects of the last years.

2.2
Hybridization methodologies
This section is going to examine different methodologies described in the literature, to asses
the hybridization of existing renewable plants.
The authors of [19] have as objective to develop a decision­making tool that can optimally de­
termine and size candidate generation and storage technologies that could be used to hybridize
an existing solar PV farm. They formulate a two­stage stochastic programming problem to de­
termine the installed capacity for each candidate technology. Moreover, particular attention is
taken by the authors for the pool prices, which are characterized as a stochastic process repre­
sented using a set of pool price scenarios, in order to account for their uncertainty nature from
the point of view of the long­term investment problem. The optimization objective function con­
sists of a maximization of the profit, which is calculated as the difference between the revenue
and the costs, that include the cost of buying electricity from the grid, with hourly values over
the lifetime and the annualized capital investment. The optimization assumes specific decision
variables, for solving the sizing problem: they are all the installed capacity values, excluding, of
course, the PV capacity. The specific RES sources are not modelled with a very high degree
of design, like number of wind turbines or hub height and degradation models are not imple­
mented, neither for wind, nor for batteries. Results show that the hybridization of existing solar
PV units allows better use of already contracted grid connection rights and a significant reduc­
tion in the time spent on the bureaucracy involved in installing new equipment. In particular, for
the analyzed Spanish location, Puerto Real, wind power is found to be the best of the options
to hybridize the existing PV generation.
The author of [20] assess the feasibility of a wind­PV HPP, within the Brazilian market. Several
scenarios are evaluated, in order to analyze the impact of adding new PV capacity into an ex­
isting wind plant, trying different shares of wind and PV capacities. The methodology employed
for the economic assessment includes using NPV for financial evaluation and the Monte Carlo
Simulation, to asses the probabilistic nature of the considered variables, through a stochastic
approach. The results indicate that the pure wind scenario is the ideal case, while a constant
increase of PV power results in a decrease of the likelihood of getting a positive NPV. This is
probably due to the highest capital investment of PV, compared to the investment for wind and
to a lower capacity factor for the solar resource. However, some of the scenarios maintain a
high profitability, meaning a high NPV, even if not the optimal one.
In [21], the authors attempt to determine the optimal size of a photovoltaic facility which will
be installed within the existing wind power plant, to face the problem of the hours when the
stand­alone wind can’t cover the load itself. The optimal size of the PV plant is determined
maximising the NPV of the PV investment, assuming that the grid connection is not increased
and the investor doesn’t have to pay for it. The methodology used in this paper faces a real­
life problem and therefore it considers 62 wind plants around Spain and couple them with PV
productions generated with PVGIS in the same geographical areas. In order to get the new
revenue, the new energy production is multiplied by the energy price. If the PV production is
higher than the difference between the grid capacity and the wind production, the revenue will
include only the amount PV production that fills the grid­wind gap. The results show a high level
of diversity, given by the different geographical locations analysed, which play a fundamental
role for the choice of hybridization. Moreover they also show that the optimal capacity of the PV
plant is around the 8% of the existing wind capacity, if the optimization is realised maximising
the NPV over the PV CAPEX.
Also the authors of [22] investigate the hybridization of existing wind farms, adding different
shares of solar PV, but they focus more on how these power plants can become more prof­
itable within the energy markets and how their joint forecast errors can be reduced. They apply
Hybridization of single technology plants to hybrid power plants
10
==PAGE:17==
their methodology to three locations in Portugal, to understand the effect of varying levels of
complementarity, between wind and solar generation. The results from the new hybridized
plant show that the total remuneration can increase by up to 30% and market value by up to
5%, if it is compared to the existing wind power plant.
2.3
Assessment of existing tools
Different online tools are available for simulating the performance of single plants or hybrid
plants, encompassing sizing, design, and economic analysis. At the moment, the majority of
the available tools allows the modelling of single technology plants and, more rarely, of co­
located plants, like in the case of HOPP. Moreover, these tools are often not open­source and
can’t be used for free, like for instance HOMER. This paragraph will give an overview of some
of the existing tools that could be helpful to design hybrid power plants.
2.3.1
SAM
When it comes to single technology plants, SAM, System Advisor Model [23] is a free software
that support people in the renewable energy sector to face decision making problems within the
techno­economic analysis of energy plants. It can solve models of different types of renewable
energy systems: photovoltaic systems with or without battery storage, industrial process heat,
concentrating solar power, solar thermal, wind, biomass, geothermal, and conventional power
systems. These systems can either deliver electricity to buildings or facilities, or directly to the
grid. SAM is able to provide detailed financial modelling within different market sectors, includ­
ing residential and commercial, PPA projects and third parts projects, combined with detailed
performance modelling.
2.3.2
HOPP
The HOPP model, ’Hybrid Optimization and Performance Platform’ [24], is an open­source mod­
elling tool, Python based, developed by NREL. It is cable of detailed analysis and optimization
of hybrid power plants, down to the level of the components. It relies on other NREL softwares,
such as SAM or ReOpt, to size and analyse, managing to obtain a detailed output. The tech­
nologies considered in HOPP include: solar PV, wind power plants, CSP (Molten Salt Power
Tower Plant and Parabolic Trough plants, battery storage. The goal of HOPP is to simulate and
optimize a range of hybrid energy systems, including techno­economic analysis and system
design and control.
2.3.3
HOMER
Moreover, Homer model, ’Hybrid Optimization Model for Electric Renewables’[25] is another
tool developed originally by NREL, but now it is on a commercial level and it is not open­source
anymore. Its main scope is to optimize microgrids and distributed energy resources, and it has
many functionalities, such as conducting pre­feasibility assessments, optimizing system con­
figurations, and performing sensitivity analyses. Moreover, the model takes into account inputs
related to weather resources, power load and control parameters. As a result, it provides as
output such as the optimal system design and costs. Currently it consists in different softwares,
including HOMER Pro, which explores solutions for remote power systems, microgrids and is­
land utilities and HOMER Front, which optimize the return on utility­scale systems with storage,
that can either be with or without solar and wind.

Methodology
3.0.1
Software
The methodology is developed using HyDesign. HyDesign [26] is an open­source tool, Python
based and developed by DTU. The tool allows to get the optimal sizing of a grid­connected
hybrid power plant including wind, solar photovoltaic, Li­ion batteries and electrolyzers. The
methodology for the sizing of the HPP plant is an optimization problem that either maximizes
the net present value (NPV) over the total investment (CAPEX), or minimize the Levelized Cost
of Energy (LCOE), depending on the user choice. The sizing problem is implemented in Open­
MDAO [27], which is free­access optimization framework for efficient multidisciplinary optimiza­
tion. It is ideal for a sizing optimization due to its modular structure, which allows to model
independently different components and to integrate them to the entire system.
3.0.2
Contribution
Two models are going to be implemented, to answer the research questions of two different
scenarios:
• Hybridization of an existing wind farm with new solar PV and new lithium batteries.
• Hybridization of an existing PV plant with new wind turbines and new lithium batteries.
In order to do so, the base HyDesign has some limitations when it comes to assess the po­
tential ’hybridization’ of existing technologies, for both the ‘existing wind case’ and the ‘existing
PV case’. In response to this gap, this study undertakes the task of enhancing the existing
model by introducing new components and functionalities to the base HyDesign. A list of all the
modifications and new components added to the model is given below.
• Implementation of the existing wind generation model, that will be called ’mid­fidelity
wind model’, for the ’existing wind case’. The wind model in HyDesign will not be changed,
but it will take as input new more precise data regarding the specific wind farm layout and
wake losses, realised through a pre­processed model.
• Implementation of the existing PV generation model, that will be called ’mid­fidelity PV
model’, for the ’sxisting PV case’.
• Implementation of the decommissioning cost model
• Update of the cost model
• Update of the finance model,
• Addition of a new design variable, δlife, that allows to include the time in the optimization
problem.
• The structure of HyDesign is changed in order to become more flexible and time­dependent.

3.1
Assumptions
Some assumptions are made.
• The grid capacity, GMW, is considered fixed, which means that the capacity already con­
tracted by the existing plant is not increased when the power plant is hybridized with
new generation. As stated in the literature review, one of the advantages coming from
hybridization is that it can speed up the administrative process of adding a new RES gen­
eration, but this works only if the already existing grid capacity is used.
• The batteries are always considered a new technology. This choice is made in order to
simplify the problem. If they were part of the existing generation, the battery degradation
model would require some additional implementations.
• The lifetime of each plant is assumed to be 25 years.
• The hybrid business case assumes as ’year zero’ the first year of operation of the existing
generation and as ’end year’ the last year of operation of the new added generation.
Therefore, the existing generation is dismissed at the end of its lifetime.

Cell temperature
The cell temperature time­series are obtained with the Faiman Temperature Model [44], be­
cause they are required as input for the DC power calculation.
DC Power
The MPP is initially computed for one module, using the CEC parameters obtained with the
Single Diode Model Equation.
The Single Diode Model requires as input a set of coefficients that describe the shape of the
module’s I–V curve at standard conditions. These coefficients are derived from the parameters
found on the module datasheet, using a specific calculator in pvlib [45].
Subsequently, the MPP is scaled, given the number of modules and strings of it, in order to get
the inverter DC power. The total system losses are calculated with the NREL’s PVWatts system
loss model [46] and they are deducted from the DC power.
AC Power
Finally, the efficiency curve of the inverter is interpolated to get the efficiency time­series, which
are needed to calculate the AC power time­series, through the PVWatts inverter function of pvlib
[47]. Lastly, the inverter AC power is scaled to the system power, multiplying by the number of
inverters.

Tests and validation
HyDesign is the main tool employed to apply the methodology previously described. However,
the tool is not initially capable of solving an ’hybridization problem’, because it can only evaluate
and optimize hybrid power plants that are built all in once. Therefore the new model needs to be
tested and validated, in order to prove that the new updates and the new added components are
working properly. All the tests are executed via evaluation, which means, fixing the values of the
design variables, without running the optimization. The tests are realized through a comparison
with the ’base HyDesign’ or via validation with real data. From now on, ’base HyDesign’ will
refere to the initial HyDesign model, before adding the new implementations.

Sensitivity analysis
Some sensitivity analysis are carried out, in order to check the influence of the most important
parameters. To conduct these analysis, one specific design needs to be fixed. In particular, the
Hybridization of single technology plants to hybrid power plants

==PAGE:47==
design with δlife=8 is chosen from Table 6.2, because 8 represents an average value within the
set of potential δlife values, avoiding the edges. Figure 6.2a shows, as already found, that the
NPV/CAPEX reaches its maximum after 12 years. However, after 9 years the values show a
very low difference between one another, and always very much higher then the ’stand­alone
wind’ case. This means that, towards the 13th and 14th years, the influence of δlife doesn’t
have a crucial impact on the business case anymore.
Moreover, a sensitivity analysis is performed about the PV and batteries investments, which
are tested in the range between ­/+ 40% of the original costs, in order to find out which is the
CAPEX that makes hybridization possible. The graph in Figure 6.2b shows that the hybridization
remains profitable, compared the the ’stand­alone wind’ case, only if the PV and batteries initial
investment is increased by a 15% maximum. In addition, even if the investment was 40% higher,
the NPV would still be above zero.
The discount rate and inflation impacts are evaluated as well, through the sensitivity analysis
shown in Figures 6.2c and 6.2d. It is clear that they have an important impact, proving that they
both are strong mechanisms that govern the business case. For a discount rate higher 7.2%
the NPV reaches zero. It can als o be noticed that, for higher discount rates, the NPV/CAPEX
has a higher sensitivity to the discount rate, with a higher relative increase with respect to the
’stand­alone wind case’. Regarding the inflation rate, it makes the business case negative for
values below 0.8%, and it has the same behaviour as the discount rate, for high inflation rates.

6.1.4
Revenue stability
Another important aspects of hybridization is that it is supposed to make the power to the grid
more stable, taking advantage of the complementarity of the wind and solar resources. To quan­
tify the impact of the hybridization, indeed, the standard deviation of the revenue is calculated
and then divided by its mean value, to get the Coefficient of Variance. Figure 6.6 shows the
first two weeks of June of the 12th year of operation of the plant, for the two cases: the wind
stand­alone case and the hybridized wind case. The Coefficient of variance is then calculated
over the whole year 2012 for both cases and the results are 89% for the first case and 65% for
the second one. This shows that, by hybridizing the existing wind generation, the revenues get
more stable. The low revenues of the wind stand­alone stretch significantly the standard devi­
ation, while the hybrid has more compact revenues. In addition to being more stable, revenues
also become higher, because they are shifted above, by the supplement of PV and batteries.

As for the ’hybridization of wind’ case, several optimizations are run, fixing δlife to different values,
0, 5, 8, 12 and 15 years. The trend shown by the results, in Table 6.5, is that the NPV/CAPEX
decreases with increasing δlife. This behave is opposite, with respect to the ’hybridization of
wind’ case, whose NPV/CAPEX increases with increasing δlife. It can be explained by the fact
that the wind resource, in both the Danish locations, is stronger with respect to the solar re­
source. As a consequence, adding wind generation to a PV technology will result much more
profitable and, the sooner it will be add, the higher the profit will be. In the ’hybridization of
existing wind’ the NPV/CAPEX was increasing with time, pushed by the mechanism of inflation,
which, in this case is not stronger enough, compared to the effect of new wind generation. On
the other hand, the trend of LCOE is the same for both cases, it always increase with increas­
ing δlife, due to higher CAPEX and OPEX and to the discount rate. This means that for the
case analysed in this section, both the NPV/CAPEX and the LCOE metrics align with the opti­
mal timing for hybridization, while for the ’hybridization of existing wind’ they exhibit conflicting
outcomes regarding the ideal timing.
The optimization suggests a wind generation of 3 MW, if the plant is considered hybrid since year
zero or after 5 years, then it increases to 6 MW, if the hybridization is realized after 8, 12 or 15
years. The batteries storage capacity doesn’t follow a specific trend. If we consider the capacity
in terms of power, it represents a high percentage of the hybrid total installed capacity, also, if
compared to the ’hybridization of existing wind’ case (see Figure 6.1b). As a consequence,
the total curtailment in this case doesn’t exceed 0.05% of the total production, whereas for the
’hybridization of existing wind’ case it reaches the 0.9%.

Conclusion
• An methodology was elaborated, based on HyDesign tool to assess the profitability of
hybridizing existing renewable power plants like PV and wind plants, with storage and,
respectively, wind and PV new generations.
• The model developed was put into practice through two cases of study. For the 72 MW
wind farm, the optimal capacities of PV and batteries to hybridize were found to be 91 MW
and 120 MWh, with an NPV of 13.4 MEuro, while, for the existing 7.48 MW PV plant the
optimal capacities of wind and batteries to hybridize were found to be 3 MW and 60 MWh,
with an NPV of 2.81 MEuro.
• For the ’existing wind case’ It was found that hybridization remains profitable only if the
investment costs of new batteries and PV do not exceed a 15% increase. Conversely, for
the ’existing PV case’, the costs don’t show a great impact on the business case.
• It was found that it makes sense to hybridize an existing generation of wind with PV and
batteries, but it can make even more sense to do that in the future, when the electricity
prices will be lower. Moreover, it was also found that hybridizing the wind is not as much
affected as hybridizing the PV, by electricity prices.
• Hybrid power plants have a different sensitivity to discount rate and inflation, compared to
single technology plants.
• Moreover, the impact of hybridization was assessed through an analysis of the revenues.
It was found that, in both cases, hybridization leads to get higher and more stable rev­
enues, reducing the amount of ’zero’ revenues and pushing these values up.

Future work
This research could be improved by considering the following suggestions for future work:
• A reassessment of the design variables of the PV model. It could be useful to consider
reorganising them and adding new ones, in order to include more parameters in the model,
such as the number of inverters, strings and modules.
• In the pre­processing of the ’mid­fidelity wind model’, considering the use of a more spe­
cific wake model, instead of the NOJ one, which is more general.
• Model an additional scenario where the batteries are an existing technology, instead of a
new added one.
• Evaluate the impact that the introduction of subsidies, to address the drop in electricity
prices, could have in the future.
