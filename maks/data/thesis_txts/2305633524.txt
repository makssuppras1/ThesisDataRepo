ABSTRACT 
Looking back in history the biogas production was started as an alternative method to the post sanitation and 
stabilization of agricultural wastes. After the price soar, there was a need for implementation of alternatives in 
energy production. This led to the successful utilization of organic wastes (and not only wastes) in order to produce 
beneficial “green energy” in the form of biogas etc. Considering the benefits that organic waste degradation 
provide, such as Greenhouse gas emissions reduction and sanitation or fertilizing or storage, biofuels will never 
be replaced from alternatives such as solar or wind energy. 
In order to ease the degradation of lignocellulosic material, a variation of pre-treatment and treatment methods 
have been investigated over the last years. Beyond the classic applications of physicochemical (such as steam 
explosion), or chemical (acid or alkali) pre-treatments, two promising methods are rising recently. The first one is 
microaeration which has resulted contradictory results, but is a very interesting and applicable method. 
Microaeration is the injection of trace amounts of oxygen which could boost hydrolysis in an AD system, always 
depending from the substrate. The target-microbes are the facultative anaerobic hydrolytic microbes which could 
be boosted to hydrolyze the recalcitrant lignocellulosic matter. In that way anaerobic process will not exceed the 
“anaerobic threshold” by just consuming rapidly the minor amounts of oxygen. In the present work this facultative 
consortia of microbes was enriched by the addition of “methano-compost” as Scherer & Neumann (2013) 
characterized it. Many other researchers had the opportunity over the last years to enhance the anaerobic 
procedure of degrading lignocellulosic biomass such us  Fu et al. (2015)who gave the definition of "TMP" 
(thermophilic microaerobic pre-treatment) for the series of experiments of microaeration of corn straw under 
thermal environment, or Botheju et al. (2010) by providing a new model called “ADM-1 Ox”. 
Moreover, an uprising treating method is the biological treatment such as bioaugmentation. By definition, 
bioaugmentation represents the injection of pure culture of microorganisms in a biochemical system in order to 
boost a specific process without inhibiting or disturbing others. In the following work the process-target was 
hydrolysis, and more precisely the demand from the injected microbes to adapt and excrete hydrolysing enzymes 
in order to enhance the overall biogas production by boosting hydrolysis.

CHAPTER 1: PREFACE 
 
1.1 Purpose of this work-Framework 
 
 
The main purpose of this work was to examine the treatment of lignocellulosic substrate in order to 
enhance the hydrolysis step in anaerobic digestion (AD) process and subsequently increase the methane yield. In 
order to enhance hydrolytic step and biodegradability, two different methods were investigated under 
thermophilic anaerobic conditions, namely Microaeration and Bioaugmentation. Thus, this thesis is divided in two 
parts. The first part is focused in the microaeration of AD inoculum, compost inoculum and wheat straw through 
BMP assays. Whereas for the second part the effect of routine bioaugmentation to cattle manure and wheat straw 
is investigated through continuous reactors (CSTR) with two different strains of microbes, Clostridium 
thermocellum (DSMZ 1237) and Melioribacter roseus (DSMZ 23840). Additionally, in order to define the exact 
methane potential, series of BMP assessments were conducted. 
In the first experiment 182 batches were prepared in order to probe the competence of facultative anaerobic fungi 
to hydrolyse wheat straw respiring small amounts of oxygen. The extended assessment of the aforementioned 
methods is following with main objective to indicate efficient alternative treatment processes for co-digestion of 
cattle manure and lignocellulosic energy crops such as wheat straw. Compost was used in terms of biological 
treatment in BMP assays, by means of “methano-compost’’ microflora.  
Routine bioaugmentation has been reported by many authors as a promising method to interfere in a complex 
biological system. In the present thesis highly efficient hydrolytic bacteria were injected in order to excrete 
degrading enzymes and disrupt the recalcitrant part of wheat straw, lignin. More specifically, these strains were 
Clostridium thermocellum (DSMZ 1237) and Melioribacter roseus (DSMZ 23840). Both strains require for their 
growth anaerobic thermophilic conditions, except from M. roseus which is facultative anaerobic. The 
implementation and main objective of bioaugmentation was the long term infection and colonization in the new 
environment, avoiding the wash out of injected microorganisms. Nevertheless, the main aim was to obtain a new 
eco-system slightly adjusted by the new hydrolytic strains in aid of us.

1.2 Sustainability 
 
From 1950, modern technologies and massive assessments were conducted for anaerobic digestion of organic 
wastes, which is until nowadays an emerging technology. Biogas production under anaerobic conditions figures 
many sustainable advantages which make it as one of the best alternative processes for green energy production. 
First exemplary merit of AD is the diminished odorous effluent due to the fact that the 99% of volatile odorous 
substances are decomposed (Smet et al., 1999). Additionally, the slurry effluent maintains agricultural nutrients, 
namely ammonia or other phosphoric or nitrate molecules, therefore, is a favourable alternative as a fertilizer. 
The most beneficial merit to the process is the degradation of organic matter to methane and carbon dioxide. 
Carbon dioxide and methane are a part of greenhouse gasses and contribute in the global warming and the overall 
greenhouse effect (Lansche, 2012). Moreover, carbon dioxide is a mineralization natural product, and normally it 
is recirculating in the carbon cycle and CH4 is led to ignition for energy production. As far as it concerns, AD is 
preferred instead of combustion or other alternatives because of the lower energy demand and the production of 
bio-fuel, methane, instead of 100% CO2 emissions. It must be noted that methane impacts stronger than CO2 to 
the greenhouse effect if it is released in atmosphere before its ignition. Therefore, the slurry digested effluent 
should be strictly controlled. Simultaneously the bio-energy that is gained from biogas utilisation replaces natural 
gas which is a fossil fuel (Ward et al.,  2008). Further on, AD is an important alternative to aerobic or half anaerobic 
processes such as composting. To prolong, the composting of organic wastes such as municipal or manure in 
extended landfills leads to the methane production in very slow rates and its release to atmosphere, contributing 
to the greenhouse effect (Bergland et al., 2014). However, the receiving effluent from AD process is not 
appropriate for direct exploitation, because of its humidity and the high volatile acids concentration. Therefore, it 
must be utilised with post treatments such as chemical additives or aerobic composting. Compared to fully aerobic 
composting, the overall effect of the combination AD-composting leads to the reduction of CO2 emissions (i.e. 25-
67%) and to the excess of electrical energy. 
Over the years manure is mostly applied on the anaerobic digestion process. Rumen does not fully digest the cows’ 
regime. Hereupon, the exploitation of surplus consisted by biofibers and other organic matter that manure 
contains is conducted. Nevertheless, in order to raise the methane potential of manure, the supplementary use 
(co-digestion) of rich-cellulose energy crops is turning as a promising method (Angelidaki & Ellegaard, 2003). 
Nowadays, there is an abundant amount of agricultural lignocellulosic wastes which are offered for utilization in 
order to produce biofuel. Moreover, cattle manure which is already exploited in order to produce biogas contains 
undigested fibbers which are constructed mostly by lignin. In a research by Eurostat, (2012), it was showed that 
more than 60% of biomass is appropriate for biofuel production in the European region. Although it is claimed 
that biomass is figured as only 10% of the source for bio-energy (Mata-álvarez, 2015). Of course this could be 
explained by the fact that lignin is not degraded leading to the rigid, recalcitrant nature of lignocellulosic biomass, 
makes it an avoided substrate for utilization. However, throughout the years after the exploitation of easily 
degradable biomass such as lipids, proteins and easily degradable carbohydrates, nowadays research community 
faces the challenge of recalcitrant matter utilization.  
The second most abundant polymer in nature, after cellulose, is lignin which does not belong in the group of 
sugars, and it is considered to be non-degradable. Hence, lignocellulosic substrates are, recalcitrant to degrade. 
Lignin is an aromatic polymer which protects cellulose, and gives the characteristic rigid structure of lignin rich

CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE 
 
2.1 AD systems 
 
Amongst the AD products, the most valuable is the gas fraction, which is consisted mainly by methane (50-60%) 
and carbon dioxide (35-45%). Furthermore, inoculum which is the digested substrate from the effluent of a 
reactor, consists the liquid fraction and it can be used as a fertiliser because it maintains the N, P and nutrients 
after the digestion. 
Biogas is the gas that is produced from anaerobic digestion through a very complex biochemical procedure. The 
digestion of high molecular organic wastes to, methane and carbon dioxide is a result of a simultaneous process 
which will be discussed in the present chapter. AD takes place normally in nature where a huge consortia of 
microorganisms under oxygen free conditions and a nutrient rich substrate produce biogas. Subsequently, the 
sector of science which deals with the utilisation of organic wastes for biogas production it belongs to 
environmental and chemical science, it uses biotechnology to produce bioenergy. 
Kinetics in each step of this multistep process determine the overall time that is needed for the biogas production. 
Accordingly to the biomass that is used as substrate, there is effect on each step. Thus, if the substrate is difficult 
to degrade by hydrolytic microbes, then the process "stacks" in the first step. But if the implemented biomass is 
easily degradable then the last step of methanogenesis is supposed to be the rate limiting. In any case in the first 
situation it is rational the process to take more time, if both are operating in the same conditions. Therefore, as 
far as it concerns if the first AD step is accelerated then the overall biogas generation would be enhanced. In nature 
we can find hydrolytic bacteria in rumen (anaerobic conditions), hot springs, municipal wastes, sewage wastes, 
tree branches, piles, and in soil generally.  
 
2.1.1 Steps of AD 
 
The process of AD follows 4 concurrent steps 
1st step: Hydrolysis  
Hydrolysis is called the procedure in which hydrolytic extracellular enzymes are responsible for the degradation 
of high molecular polymeric compounds that consist the organic biomass. These polymeric compounds vary 
accordingly with the kind of waste. Consequently, food industry wastes could be rich in lipids (triglycerides fat or 
oil-C57H104O6-), proteins (C5H7NO2)n and complex carbohydrates like polysaccharides (C6H10O5). Macromolecules 
that consist the biomass are even bigger in size than microbes in theirself. Thus, due to the adequate biomass 
utilization, hydrolytic enzymes are produced by the relative fermentative bacteria. For instance, proteases and 
lipases disrupt proteinic and lipid polymers to glucose and Long chain fatty acids (LCFA) respectively. In the 
following work the most dominant and favoured enzymes are cellulases and xylanases which are secreted by 
cellulolytic and xylanolytic bacteria in order to disrupt recalcitrant biomass. Furthermore, polysaccharides such as

xylose (pentoses-(C5H10O5). Additionally, xylose is formed as a result of xylan disruption and is a very abundant 
hemicellulotic compound in straw which was confirmed also in this work. 
In the agricultural sector which is the most common for biogas production, carbohydrates are the most common 
substrate.  In the present work the selected biomass is lignocellulosic crop, hence hydrolysis is the most 
considerable and rate-limiting step. It is commonly known that lignin is a non-degradable aromatic, branched 
polymer. Whenever a recalcitrant biomass is used in an AD procedure, hydrolysis is the rate limiting step ( Eastman 
& Ferguson, 1981). Nevertheless, all plants in nature are lignocellulosic and consisted by cellulose, hemi-cellulose 
and lignin in different compositions. For lignocellulosic substrates the main intermediates during the degradation 
are sugars. Thus, further degradation of cellulose and starch, leads to the formation of sugars like glucose. The 
enzymes that contribute to this formation are called cellulaces which absorb on cellulose layers and destroy their 
crystal structure. Furthermore, acids which are simultaneously formed in this step decrystallize cellulose and 
enhance with their way the hydrolytic step. The following chapter describes the lignin structure more 
comprehensively. 
2nd step: Acidogenesis 
Generally, the best pathway to methane production is the direct utilization of hydrolysed products to acetate, CO2 
and H2. Nevertheless, only the 70% of the degraded polymers consist the aforementioned methanogenic nutrients 
and the rest 30% form intermediates such as volatile fatty acids (VFA),lactate and alcohols (Angelidaki & Ellegaard, 
2002).  Acidogenesis gains the advantage of a side-branched metabolism whereas can overtake the inhibitory H2 
accumulation which can occur due to the intense substrate oxidation.   
Acidogens are responsible for the transformation of long chain fatty acids or sugars to short chain fatty acids and 
other intermediates. In particular, monosaccharides such as hexoses (glucose) and pentoses are further degraded 
to reduced electron carriers (NADH) which will significantly contribute in anaerobic fermentation for the 
production of fatty acids and alcohols, such as lactate, propionate, propionate, butyrate acetate and ethanol. From 
the aforementioned the most favourable is acetate as it consists the first order nutrient for aceticlastic 
methanogens.  
3nd step: Acetogenesis   
This is the step in AD process where fermented intermediary products are further degraded by acetogenic bacteria 
to acetate and H2, CO2 (Mccarty, 1964). Hydrogen producing bacteria use linear metabolism, and thus they are 
coerced to degrade VFA to acetate and H2. The measured and most abundant volatile fatty acids are acetate, 
propionate, butyrate, iso-butyrate valerate, iso-valerate. Whereas, due to the high negative free energy of acetate 
formation by glucose fermentation; the acetogenesis is more facilitated than propionate or butyrate formation. 
4rd step: Methanogenesis 
In this last step, obligate anaerobic methanogens use acetate, H2 and CO2 as substrate to grow and through their 
metabolic reaction they relieve methane and CO2 as products.

4 stages of methane generation process in time 
Bacterial/microbial life is divided in four steps. In the first step, the lag phase, cell consumes substrate in order to 
survive and not to divide, thus the kinetics follow the zeroth constant rate. When microbes are robust and the 
nutrient substrate is abundant they follow a linear growth rate until the reduction of substrate whereafter the 
stationary phase follows. At the stationary phase, microbial rise is equal to microbial death. Hence, microbes 
preserve themselves until their reduction because of starvation or adequate life growth.  
Methane is produced from the last step of anaerobic digestion. Methanogens prioritize methanogenesis instead 
of doubling their mass, by means of carbon is led to methane production and not biomass generation and thus 
the archaea growth is slow. The carbon that is utilized to biomass is more or less 5%. 
1st step. “Lag phase”. In this step hydrolytic bacteria are “waken up” and excrete enzymes due to the injection of 
oxygen and their first interaction with substrate. If hydrolysis is fast and adequate, nutrients are produced, and 
then methanogens start the consumption faster, thus lag phase is short.  
2nd step. During the second step methane yield rises rapidly. This indicates an accelerating hydrolytic and 
methanogenic activity which follows a linear increase as long as VFAs and more specifically acetate are abundant. 
3rd step. It is considered that after a pinpoint methanogens consumes the overall amount of acetate, and the most 
degradable part of substrate. Thereafter, methanogenic activity slows gradually until the last week.

2.1.2 Microbial community 
 
In general the AD system is consisted by strictly anaerobic microbes such as clostridia , methanogenic archaea , 
and sulphuric consumers (Stamatelatou et al., 2011) . 
Anaerobic hydrolytic bacteria could be divided in four phylum categories, Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Fibrobacter, 
Thermotogae and the minor subcategory of Chlorobi. The most abundant phylum in biogas plants are the first two 
categories (Campanaro et al., 2016). In the present work the implementation of Clostridium thermocellum and 
Melioribacter roseus turns our interest in phylum Firmicutes and Chlorobi, respectively (Sun et al., 2013a). 
Nevertheless, in previous cattle manure experiments, ubiquitous influence detected by genera Clostridium (Sun 
et al.,  2013). However, slightly lower bacterial species richness was observed in digesters that obtained co-
digestion with wheat straw and manure. In contrast, for archaea, no clear trend in species richness was observed 
when comparing the manure itself and the mesophilic digesters operating with manure, alone or combined with 
straw (Sun et al., 2013b). Although, in digesters which contained cattle manure and wheat straw Bacteroidetes 
were more abundant than in cattle manure digesters. Apparently, after the addition of wheat straw in the influent 
there was a rise of Bacteroidetes.  
Karakashev et al. (2005) tried to clarify the different methanogenic communities in 7 full scale Biogas plant 
reactors which were fed with manure and various supplementary substrates. The most dominant methanogens 
were species similar to Methanosarcinaceae in rich ammonia, VFA and organic acid environment. However 
Methanosarcinaceae is an aceticlastic community which unlikely does not belong to bacterial gene, but in Archaea 
domain. Nevertheless, hydrogen-consuming bacteria or hydrogenotrophic, Methanobacteriales correspond 
favourably in manure digesters. On the other hand, sludge digesters contain different methanogenic species such 
as  Methanosaetaceae and thereby contain lower VFA and organic acid concentrations than manure digesters. 
However it is known that in thermophilic anaerobic conditions, the hydrogenotrophic methanogens are dominant 
in rich ammonia environment (Sun et al., 2013b).

In broad terms, hydraulic retention time is defined as the average time that takes the reactor to change the 
working volume. In a biotechnological reactor the threshold of HRT is at least double the doubling time of microbes 
in order to avoid wash out (Yadvika et al., 2006).

OLR 
It is measured in terms of gVS/L/day. It is reported that in co-digestion systems of recalcitrant substrates such as 
lignocellulosic, OLR reaches the amount of 1.5-3.5 gVS/L/day, which is low in comparison with the main co-

is alleviated. By means of this, when propionate accumulates, it is not consumed adequately  until the end of the 
experiment, even when conditions has turned optimum again (Boe, n.d.).

Thermophilic conditions are ranging at 45-55 oC and mesophilic at 30-35 oC. In general thermophilic conditions are

Single stage CSTR reactors at thermophilic conditions perform higher acetate production than at mesophilic.

washing out microbes. Additionally, in high temperatures there are limitations in the system such as low process

eliminating AD factors due to the positive ΔG. 
Two stage reactors are beneficial when substrate is easily hydrolysed, thereby, acids accumulate and pH drops to 
approximately 5.5 developing an acidic environment in which methanogens are not able to grow up. In that case 
hydrolysis takes place in a separate reactor and the effluent is treated to the second reactor where 
methanogenesis is conducted.

Excess of volatile fatty acids could cause inhibition of methane production (Nielsen et al., 2007). Thus, the specific

devastating for the process. To this effect, researchers should rely firstly on buffering capacity and determine it

volume, or more drastically by the addition of bases or carbonate salts in order to increase pH and remove the

pH 
Methanogens grow optimally in neutral pH (Chandra et al., 2012). Furthermore, alkalinity can be defined with pH 
measurement. At pH values less than 5.5, the process is severely damaged pinpointing the acidic accumulation.

(Karakashev et al., 2005).

co-digestion with high organic energy crops. 
However, regarding to the feedstock, if ammonia is greater than adequate proportion for microbial growth it can 
cause inhibition to AD. High concentrations of ammonia can be counteracted when used with other feedstocks 
which have low nitrogen concentrations such as wheat straw (Hansen et al.,  1998; Sung & Liu, 2003). It is known 
that, at high pH, dissolved ammonia is more dominant and inhibitory than the ionised ammonium (NH4+), however, 
free ammonia should be calculated for investigation of inhibition (Fotidis et al.,  2013).

2.2 Lignocellulosic structure 
 
Lignocellulose structure is mainly consisted by cellulose repeating monomers (C6H10O5)n, hemicellulose (C5H8O4)m, 
lignin C9H10O3(OCH3), pectins, extractives, and proteins (Sjostrom, 1993). Lignocellulosic biomass could be found 
in nature abundantly. Hence, it could be found in forest residues such as wood, or in agricultural sector's left overs 
such as sugarcane bagasse, silages (e.g. grass), and a big variety of straws (e.g. corn, wheat, rice), in general energy 
crops. 
In order to approach the rigid lignocellulosic nature it must be clear that cellulosic and hemicellulosic component 
of the biomasses are tightly linked to the lignin. As a result the robust body is constructed by hydrogenic bonds. 
Hence for its better utilisation, under a treating method biomass could give a valuable result. As it seems, the 
interaction between lignin and holocellulose is the limiting factor for fiber degradation. The main parts of wheat 
straw are lignin, hemicellulose and cellulose in the approximate proportion of 15%-25%, 26%-31% and 33%-38% 
respectively (McKendry, 2002). 
Cellulases are enzymes secreted by fermentative bacteria which access to amorphous cellulose more easily than 
in crystal cellulose and lead to its degradation. Thus, one parameter for the yield improvement is to implement a 
treating method which would degrade the crystal part of cellulose. Due to this fact the usage of hydrolytic bacteria 
for bioaugmentation would be compelling, as they secrete hydrolytic enzymes (cellulases) which desire crystal 
cellulose. 
Lignin firstly introduced by Anselme Payen in 1838 describing the rigid structure of lignocellulosic plants (Wisniak, 
2005). Later in 1957 Adler introduces the first model of lignin in order to show that is a 16-phenylpropane 
structured polymer connected with C-O-C and C-C bonds. After many investigations on lignin, finally Sarikaya in 
1983 proposed a figure for softwood lignin in 2-D depiction, as it follows. Sugars (xylose, glucose and arabinose), 
degradation products (furfural, hydroxymethylfurfural and soluble lignin) and acetic acid concentrations were 
determined for each set of hydrolysis conditions (Mussatto et al., 2008).

 Co-digestion 
Wheat straw is a lignocellulosic fibrous biomass, which is difficult to be degraded as it was aforementioned. 
Livestock manure also contains biofibers because ruminant animal regime contains fibrous grasses and wheat. 
Unfortunately these lignocellulosic fibers are not digested totally in the rumen. Moreover, it was found that when

of the complex lignin matrix. Furthermore, a noteworthy application of wheat straw in anaerobic digestion is as 
biofilm carrier in packed bed reactors (Anderson et al., 2003). 
                          
2.3 Pre-treatment methods 
 
Effective pre-treatment depends on many parameters. Firstly, the chemical use or the addition of nutrients makes 
the procedure more efficient but in overall, they are more costly and intricate since in the end of the procedure 
the surplus should be detracted. Furthermore, attention should be paid in the danger of inhibitory by-products. 
Pore size is important wherein enzyme cannot reach cellulose or enzymes get trapped in pores. Moreover as 
greater as crystallinity and cellulose chain length are, degradation is much more difficult. Therefore one of the 
main purposes in pre-treatment methods is to decrease the crystallized cellulose in order to degrade a bigger 
portion of it, by means of degrading crystalline cellulose and amorphous cellulose.  
On the other hand, Veeken et al. (2000) proposed that hydrolysis rate is totally related to the absorption of 
hydrolytic enzymes on the fiber waste. This is in line with Sanders et al. (2002) which were led to the same 
conclusions. Mata-Alvarez et al. (2000) tried to further enlighten the contribution of surface to the hydrolysis with 
the implementation of a surface based kinetic mathematic model. However, the total lignin removal or the 
quantity improvement of sugars is possible by maximising the cost. It is stated that the removal of lignin leads to 
desirable contribution for the further digestion of cellulose of lignocellulosic materials. Nevertheless, researchers 
have expressed the correlation of hydrolysis with the surface (Sanders et al., 2002). Additionally, the first order 
hydrolysis is expressed with the formula of dXdegr/dt =-kh*Xdegr (Eastman & Ferguson, 1981).

2.3.1Microaeration 
 
Botheju (2010) first defined the injection of small amount of oxygen in anaerobic environment as "microaeration". 
However, another author defined the treating method as “TMP" (thermophilic microaerobic pre-treatment) 
presenting both thermal and microaerated conditions (Fu et al., 2015). For Wei & Wang (2013) microaeration is 
the controlled introduction of small amounts of oxygen into an anaerobic biochemical process to enable both 
anaerobic and aerobic biological activities to occur within a single bioreactor. 
Regarding to the oxygen tolerance, there are four types of microbes. Firstly, the obligate anaerobes for which 
oxygen is suffocating, secondly facultative anaerobes who can tolerate and utilize small amounts of oxygen, thirdly  
the microaerophilic  which require small amounts of O2 and lastly fully aerobes, which can respire normally in fully 
aerobic conditions. Regarding the archaeal population, in the literature it is stated that Methanosarcinaceae, the 
hydrogenotrophic and carbon dioxide consuming methanogen is more tolerant in micro-oxygenic atmosphere

2.3.3 Bioaugmentation 
 
Bioaugmentation could be defined as the injection of pure culture of microorganisms in a biochemical system in 
order to enhance a number of processes without inhibiting others. In the biggest part of the literature 
bioaugmentation has been implemented on wastewaters treatment in order to alleviate the contaminant 
environment and to provide an efficient removal (Herrero et al., 2015). However, the selection and critical 
parameters researchers have applied in aerobic wastewater treatment are also applied for the anaerobic process. 
For instance the selection criteria are still the same, such as the injected culture to be competitive and 
subsequently compatible with the new environment (Martin-Ryals et al., 2015). Thus, in combination with the 
appropriate substrate it should colonize together with the integral microbial community, without exceeding or 
change the community due to inhibitions or competition by producing toxic by-products (Van Veen et al.,  1997). 
Moreover a critical parameter for the selection of a microbe is to stay in the new system, enhancing it, with slow 
and steady degradation. 
Many investigations have been conducted in recent years on bioaugmentation. For instance, Li et al. (2015) 
injected cultivations of Clostridium cellulolyticum in BMP batches with wheat straw AD inoculum and resulted 
higher methane potential. Additionally, Martin-Ryals et al. (2015) bioaugmentated the first digester in a two-phase 
CSTR system with Clostridium sp. gene. The substrates were consisted by manure and maize. Furthermore, (Zhang 
et al., 2015) implemented fermentative bacteria instead of hydrolytic microbes. In particular Acetobacteroides 
hydrogenigenes in maize straw, resulted an increase of biogas potential up to 23%. Caldicellusiruptor lactoaceticus 
was injected in the first stage of two-stage reactors at 68oC, resulting in approximately 93 % in methane yield. 
However, the bioaugmentation did not achieve significant difference in the total methane yield at the end of one 
HRT (Nielsen et al., 2007). In two stage CSTR experiment, routine bioaugmentation achieved significantly greater 
soluble chemical oxygen demand (sCOD) generation (+25%) and methane production (+15%) compared to one-
time bioaugmentation. In a continuous bench-scale system, routine bioaugmentation increased acid-phase sCOD 
by the range of 29–68% and acetic acid concentrations by 31–34%. Boosted hydrolysis and acetogenesis 
subsequently led to sustained increase in methane production (+56%) compared to non-bioaugmentated habitat 
(Martin-Ryals et al., 2015). Moreover, the previews years wide investigations has been obtained on the external 
effect of hydrolytic microbes on the applied anaerobic habitats. Eventually, when a biological treatment is 
implemented in an AD system it is very important to gain the knowledge of which microorganisms could favour or 
survive in the selected environmental conditions. In broad terms, under intense conditions such as high pressure 
or high temperatures, AD habitat produce higher amount of inhibitors (Hansen et al., 1998).

Practically the growth rate of cultivation could be observed with optical density (OD) measurement with a

It is very important to determine the characteristics of the inoculum and substrate before starting an anaerobic

AD Inoculum 
The initial AD inoculum with which both experiments were started was supplied from Snertinge, Denmark. When 
inoculum is very fresh, it is rich in undigested nutrients and has high organic load. Because of that, it was flushed 
and placed in incubator at 55 oC to digest the surplus of organic matter and degassed for 10 days. After its 
incubation, inoculum was completely homogeneous, the undigested leftovers (biofibers) were discarded and had 
the appropriate productivity.  
Compost inoculum 
It was provided by a compost plant from Zealand (Sjaelland), Denmark. It was consisted by field organic waste 
such as grass, fallen leafs, flowers, branches, and its temperature ranged between 50-70oC. A succinct experiment 
was conducted in order to choose the most appropriate step from composting. Compost was collected from 3 
steps, 1 week, 5-6 weeks and the last step. For the brief determination, 3 duplicates where prepared containing 
0.8 ml acetate, 20 ml BA medium and 1 g compost. The best potential resulted by the second step from which the 
methano-compost was extracted. 
 
Manure 
The raw manure that was used in experiments was provided by a biogas farm plant in Snertinge, Denmark. In 
order to maintain cattle manure undigested until the feed in the reactor, it was stored in 5L bottles at -20 oC and 
when it was about to be used it was held in 4 oC for maximum one week.  
Cattle manure, is common to be sieved in order to remove the remnant undigested biofibers in lab-scale 
experiments. After the sieving the TS, VS and subsequently, the OLR were reduced. Hereafter the lower TS, VS, 
OLR gave better estimation for the contribution of wheat straw in the final methane generation and a clearer 
picture of its biodegradability.

Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) 
COD is determined in g O2/litter and it describes the quantity of O2 that is needed in order to oxidize both inorganic 
and organic fractions of an organic waste. This is supposed to be fulfilled with the addition of Cr2O7(2-) and a 
catalyst (silver sulphate) under heating conditions (148 oC, 110 minutes). The main purpose is the reduction of 
Cr(6+) to Cr(3+) and determination of Cr(6) surplus with FAS titration. For the titration we used a 25ml Brand 
digital II titration column for measurement accuracy. It is noteworthy that, intense dilution was firstly followed in 
order to approach the appropriate COD range. Afterwards the samples were removed from the heating machine. 
After the heating, their colour was switched to blueish green from light orange colour, and after the FAS titration 
they got red, due to the addition of indicator. 
COD protocol : successive steps 
i. 
Centrifuge 
ii. 
Dilution 95% (1 ml diluted in 19 ml distilled water) 
iii. 
Collect 3.5 ml in 50 ml tubes. 
iv. 
Add 2 ml potassium dichromate , and 4.5 ml of dense sulphuric acid. 
v. 
Put the samples in special heating system to digest at 148 oC for 110 min. 
vi.

3.3 BMP 
 
BMP theory and protocol-set up 
BMP experiments take place in Batch reactors, which are sealed hermetically with rubber stoppers, thus the whole 
system is closed until the end of the experiment, when all the substrate is been utilized. Although, if there is need 
of either injecting or pulling liquid or gas, a 10 ml or 1 ml-syringe is used respectively. Furthermore, it is very 
convenient to control the internal pressure with degassing the produced biogas until it is alleviated to ambient 
pressure. In this way it is ensured that no explosions will take place during the experiment because of over-
pressured conditions, and neither toxic compound would be produced. In order to avoid the often degassing great 
headspace volume has to be considered before the assembly of experiment.  
Methane Potential calculations 
The methane potential is usually measured in terms of methane per substrate such as ml CH4/gVSsubstrate or ml 
CH4/gCODsubstrate. Although for the examined substrates it is preferred to measure methane potential in L 
CH4/gVSsubstrate, which gives a better estimation for lignocellulosic fibers. On the other hand litter methane per g 
COD is more appropriate for waste waters than in slurry estimation. The determination of the methane yield could 
be conducted regarding the pressure in the bottle, or more accurately with Gas chromatography Chromatograph 
analysis with frame ionization. Considering that, the most appropriate way for methane determination is pulling 
a specific quantity of biogas from the headspace volume and injects it in Gas chromatograph. 
With a thin needle adapted on 1 ml-syringe, a small quantity of biogas is collected from the headspace. The 
amount of biogas is in the ranges of 0.1 ml - 1 ml, depending on the Gas chromatograph and its calibration. 
Calibration of the chromatography is obtained with the injection of known composition (standard) gases 
containing methane in a specific concentration. Each sample peak from the batches is compared with the range 
of standard peaks, thus the precise methane concentration is determined.  
In the present work BMP procedure was prepared according to the proposed protocol for solid organic wastes 
and crops (Angelidaki et al., 2009). As was detailed above, because of the diversity of feedstocks and the variety 
of experimental methods the methane productivity of a substrate should be measured in common metric units. 
Firstly, Owen et al. (1979) proposed the BMP assay, which indicates the biochemical methane potential,  which is 
equal to the maximum of methane yield that a feedstock could reach under strict anaerobic conditions. Methane 
potential is measured in Litres and expressed with STP metric system in L/gVS.

4.1Microaeration 
 
In the present work the amount of injected oxygen was named as Oxygen Load (ml O2/gVS per day). From 
hereafter, OxL or ml O2/gVS per day in terms of Oxygen Load is issued. Measurements of pH, VFAs and sCOD were 
conducted before microaeration, after microaeration (the last oxygen injection) and at the end of the experiment 
in order to evaluate the effect of oxygen on specific quantity of wheat straw. 
Hydrogen 
The results indicated low propionate; therefore, H2 pressure was low. In order to testify this, hydrogen 
concentration was measured for the most and for the least promising BMP treatment. Finally the results did not 
show any steep increase of H2. Otherwise, hydrogen is not an indicating parameter for BMP assays due to the 
stable OL, in comparison  with continuous feeding where due to the increasing of OLR there is a reflected 
disturbance in H2 (Boe et al., 2010). 
Sulphate 
In literature it is stated that microaeration could boost the activity of sulphide reducing bacteria. The 
aforementioned bacteria pre-exist in the anaerobic digester in low concentrations and are responsible for the 
depletion of sulphide by oxidizing it to elemental sulphur. However the increase of elemental sulphur in a digester 
could cause clogging in the pipes (Krayzelova et al., 2015). 
Ethanol 
Ethanol measurements were not essential because ethanol inhibits the utilization of propionate to acetate and as 
it was shown propionate maintained in very low amounts (Lyberatos et al., 1997). However ethanol concentration 
was measured by GC, resulting negligible amounts which were not represented in this chapter. 
Temperature 
Regarding to the self-heating properties that have been observed in previous studies with implementation of 
oxygen for delignification, in this case due to the low oxygen load was not expected an adequate self-heating and 
therefore, each sample was incubated properly at 55 oC. Of great importance was the low amount of propionate 
which was lower than acetate as shown in figures in the following chapter. This indicates that there was not 
inhibition due to the temperature or other factors. As it was expected, microaeration of the mixed aerobic and 
anaerobic culture does not unfold any self-heating ability.

ORP (oxidation-reduction potential) 
In the present work ORP was measured during the microaeration in order to ensure that conditions did not switch 
from anaerobic to aerobic due to the intensity of respiration. In strict anaerobic conditions ORP fluctuates from -
300 to -200mV , in opposition to fully aerobic conditions which indicate +50 mV (Jenicek,  2008).  Although it was 
stated that the trace oxygen that already exist in the system from the start up, leads the system to ORP equal to -
400mV (Stamatelatou et al., 2011). 
In a recent research, Wei et al. (2013) obtained -278 mV to -220 mV under microaeration treatment. However in 
the present work during the highest 3-day microaeration of 15 ml O2/gVS per day the ORP fluctuated between -
65 and -72 mV from which is shown that anaerobic archaea were not severely inhibited by the presence of oxygen. 
As it was aforementioned the facultative wood rotting fungi that thrive on wood surface, excrete versatile 
enzymes which disrupt lignin. This enzymatic system, namely “feed-back”, is responsible for both disruption of 
lignin and cellulose (Eriksson et al., 1974). In this way, it could be assumed that under microaeration the boosting 
activity of these bacteria could lead to the lignocellulosic degradation. Eriksson & Pettersson (1974), also explained 
that during the fungal degradation of cellulose, beyond the effect of hydrolytic enzymes, additionally the produced 
oxidizing enzymes are contributing in the hydrolytic process.

4.1.2 Methane Production Results 
 
Control 
As it was mentioned above in this work the standard/control substrate was microcrystalline cellulose (Avicel). 
Methane is produced from the last step of anaerobic digestion. Methanogens prioritize methanogenesis instead 
of doubling their mass, by means of carbon is led to methane production and not biomass generation and thus 
the archaea growth is slow. The carbon that is utilized to biomass is more or less 5%. 
1st step. In this step hydrolytic compost and AD inoculum bacteria were “waken up” and excreted enzymes due to 
the injection of oxygen and their first interaction with substrate. If hydrolysis is fast and adequate, nutrients are 
produced, and then methanogens start the consumption faster, thus lag phase is short. In the present results it 
could be assumed that is a result from the slight inhibition of methanogens by trace amounts of oxygen (Li et al., 
2013). 
2nd step. The methane yield rose rapidly in the first 14 days. This indicates an accelerating hydrolytic and 
methanogenic activity which follows a linear increase as long as VFAs and more specifically acetate are abundant. 
3rd step. Altogether, the charts indicated a successive drop of slop after the 14th day. Therefore, it is considered 
that between 10th and 14th day methanogens consumed the overall amount of acetate, and the most degradable 
part of substrate. Thereafter, methanogenic activity slows gradually until the last week. As it is depicted in the 
next bar figures, in the end acetate leftovers were minor.  
4th step. Afterward, with the acetate consumption it followed the last step, where methane yield stabilized until 
it dropped. It is obvious that the experiment continues until the stabilization of production instead of the drop of 
it. Of course, due to the limited amount of nutrients it is rational that the drop of methane production will follow 
next. 
The figure 4.3 below depicts the increasing methane yield in the first two weeks and the sharp stabilization after 
14th day. Subsequently, there is a slower rise of the yield until the trend reaches the maximum potential of 373.82 
ml CH4/gVS on the last week of experiment (35th day). Thus methane potential resulted the 84.5% of theoretical 
methane potential of wheat straw. Apparently, the overall progress of experiment was generated properly for 
specific microbial system under the thermal environment.

Statistical analysis   
Each concentration was prepared in groups of triplicates for the purpose of ensuring the reproducibility of the 
statistical analysis (Angelidaki et al., 2009). After the experimental part, for the extraction of valuable results, 
Anova statistical analysis was conducted. More precisely Anova Analysis Tukey post hoc test: p<0.05, 
occurred in order to render the different and significant variations among the treated samples. The results 
showed that only one sample (15-1-3) was significantly different illustrating p<0.05, which consequently was 
the less desirable as it indicated the worst effect in the system. Methane potential was -12.7% lower than 
untreated for this individual sample. The results for the rest samples were not significant different (p>0.05). 
The software that was used to perform the analysis was the Figurepad Prism 5 software package (Figurepad 
Software, Inc., San Diego, CA). 
 
 
Complementary discussion on microaeration results in comparison with 
literature  
 
Regarding the increase of VFA and solubilisation after microaeration in anaerobic system there are some authors 
who have ended up to similar conclusions with this work.  
In combination with the inhibition of methanogens due to the presence of oxygen, methanogenic step was vitiated 
despite the soared hydrolysis. 
In a recent study, (Fu et al., 2015) conducted lower lag phase after the injection of oxygen. Regarding to this work, 
microaeration could not occur depletion on lag phase, in opposition to Fu, there was a longer lag phase in the 
most samples. Although it must be noted that Fu et al. (2015) implemented microaeration on a completely 
different substrate. Nevertheless, the highest methane yield occurred when the oxygen load was 5 ml O2/gVS, 
16.24% higher than the untreated group. Towards that, in 5 ml and 10ml oxygen loads the higher methane yield 
was observed. In the present work, although the similarity of conclusions in the 5 ml O2/gVS and 10 ml O2/gVS 
production, the lag phase was not shorter. Additionally, in the aforementioned study the VFAs profile were stable 
for each load, by contrast, in the current work after microaeration the amount of VFAs was increased.  
Moreover, Wei & Wang (2013) proposed that microaeration prevents the accumulation of toxic products such as 
ethanol and lactic acid. In line with the aforementioned result, the reduction of propionate was stated in the 
present work. Additionally there was not any accumulation for the measured lactic acid or ethanol. Definitely Lim 
defined comprehensively through their work the same effects of microaeration when food waste was used as a 
substrate with brown water. In the same line with literature, in the present work the results after microaeration 
indicated higher COD solubilisation, greater VFA generation and conversion of VFA to acetate. Nevertheless on 
the grounds that acetate is the precursor for biogas generation, the increased production of acetate was adequate 
to make us assume that through this way the methane production was increased (Xu et al., 2014). Regarding to 
Miller & Trfisch (1986), 22 basidiomycetes  degrade wheat straw with most favoured the Wight rot fungi. They 
used wheat straw which was consisted of 28.0%, 42.9%, 16.0% and 0.87% of hemi-celluose, cellulose, lignin and

Complementary discussion on bioaugmentation results in comparison with

Nevertheless, it would be noteworthy to correlate the present results with the results that other researchers have 
obtained in order to interpret further the results. 
Firstly, a critical parameter that characterize the bioaugmented system is the critical amount (‘’critical biomass’’) 
of injected microbes in order to avoid the wash out from the digester (Fotidis et al., 2013). This parameter in 
combination with the optimum environmental conditions for the injected microbe would set the real success of 
treatment in time. In the present work, in order to prevent the hasty wash out, stirring was stopped for one hour 
before and after the injection, and additionally 100 ml were collected from the working volume of reactor, just 
the same amount of injected pure culture (100 ml). Fotidis et al. (2013) bioaugmentated routinely for 6 days 
hydrogenotrophic methanogens in a lab scale CSTR system in order to observe the alleviation of ammonia 
inhibition. In the same line with the present work the methane yield decreased in the control reactor for the first 
days due to the abiotic injection. 
Furthermore, the rapid production of C. thermocellum could be explained due to the synergies with the 
ambient consortia of microbes in the digester. More precisely, C. thermocellum, as it was aforementioned in 
the first chapter, favours hexose sugars, nevertheless, when it is in the same environment with another 
thermophilic anaerobic hydrolytic bacterium such as Thermoanaerobacterium saccharolyticum, it develops 
synergistic and syntrophic action (Wiegel, 1980). Hence, lignocellulosic matter was degraded by cellulosomes 
into 5-carbon and 6-carbon sugars, which were consequently further degraded with high efficiency to 
fermentation products such as acetate and lactate. Indeed, this synergy which was investigated by Herrero 
et al. (1985) and it was unflavoured for their results, because it covers the ethanol production due to the

CHAPTER 5: CONCLUSIONS –Interpretation 
 
The main purpose in the present work was to obtain the adequate exploitation of a lignocellulosic matter such as 
wheat straw. The process that was used for the treatment and utilization of wheat straw was AD. It is well known 
that hydrolysis is the rate limiting step during the intricate procedure of AD when recalcitrant biomass is 
implemented. Therefore, the system was adjusted in order to accelerate hydrolysis and enhance the methane 
production. After the implementation of two methods for first time in the specific system, promising results were 
escalated.  
For the microaeration method, series of batch experiments were performed in order to observe the methane 
potential of wheat straw combined with compost in an AD system. After microaeration (1,2 or 3 days), VFA and 
solubility increased, in contrary with pH which was decreased. Furthermore, all methane yield charts illustrated 
longer lag phase for the first week except for the 10 ml O2/gVS samples which converged with untreated wheat 
straw. 
At the end of experiment, the injection of trace amount of oxygen showed an increase of methane potential up 
to 7.6%, reflecting 62.3% biodegradability. Both 5 ml O2/gVS per day and 10 ml O2/gVS per day indicated a similar 
tendency to enhance slightly the methane potential in closed batch assays, by contrast with the 15 ml O2/gVS per 
day which obtained a lower methane yield during the degradation of wheat straw, indicating the marginally 
inhibition by oxygen. Additionally, the adequate decrease of COD and VFA confirmed that microaeration could 
lead to disruption of lignocellulose matrix without severely inhibiting methanogens. Overall, microaeration could 
be considered as a reliable treating method for low concentrations of oxygen such as 5, 10 ml O2/gVS per day. 
For the BMP experiment on bioaugmentation, the remarkable increased methane potential was clear for the C. 
thermocellum resulting +85% of methane yield for lignocellulosic environment (mono-digestion). Conversely, M. 
roseus marginally acclimatized in the system indicating similar tendency for both mono digesting and co-digesting 
environments, by result the maximum methane yield was +17%. The aforementioned experiment was conducted 
as a premise work, giving promising results for further application in a continuous lab-scale CSTR system. 
Thereafter, for the further investigation of tendency and massive adaptation in a continuous system with co – 
digestion of wheat straw and cattle manure, bioaugmentation was implemented in 3 CSTR digesters. 
Bioaugmentation resulted significant increase in biogas production for injected C. thermocellum under strict 
anaerobic conditions. Instantly few hours after the injection, the biogas production was soared from 1440 ml 
biogas/ day to approximately 2500 ml biogas/day, while methane concentration was fallen in 68% from 72%. This 
led to the maximum enhancement of methane yield about to 27.3% in the second day after bioaugmentation. 
Further on after 1 HRT, during steady state conditions methane enhancement was maintained at 8.4%.  On the 
other hand M. roseus did not obtain with such good results as C. thermocellum, but instead is led to the minor 
steady enhancement of 1.2% after 1 HRT. Finally, it could be said that thermocellum is an appropriate hydrolytic 
bacterium to select in order to make biogas production rapid and seems a better alternative to boost the digestion 
of lignocellulosic substrate even in real biogas plant digester.

According to literature M.roseus is a facultative microbe and it favours aerobic environment despite the fact that 
it thrives in anaerobic sediments in nature (Podosokorskaya et al., 2013). The significant advantage is that it grows 
under both aerobic and anaerobic environment, which makes it very appropriate microorganism for 
microaeration. Hence, in order to exploit the aerobic abilities of M. roseus, the system could be boosted by the 
injection of minor amount of oxygen (i.e. microaeration).  
Therefore, on the next, the combined bioaugmentation and microaeration is being tested. After the 
implementation of routine bioaugmentation and microaeration, the preliminary results were promising. More 
precisely, after the expected 1-week lag phase, which M. roseus repeated, biogas yield is rising gradually. However, 
the most promising result until now is that, as it seems, M. roseus could improve the AD process leading to biogas 
production with higher methane percentage. Although, the results and conclusions could not be extracted and 
interpreted yet, due to the fact that this experiment is still in progress.  
Nevertheless, if the combined treatment in the 1-phase system fails then it could be implemented in a two-phase 
reactor system. More precisely microaeration together with the bioaugmentation could be implemented in the 
first, acidic reactor in order to prevent the inhibition of methanogens by oxygen or even to the possible 
acidification. Thus, the first acidic reactor would enhance hydrolysis and the second would utilize the produced 
nutrients. The feedstock would be the same with the CSTR system, cattle manure and wheat straw. Additionally, 
in order to let facultative microbes utilise all the amount of injected O2 in the continuous system, a small 
adjustment would be implemented, adding gas phase recirculation. 
Moreover, the remarkable ability of C. thermocellum disrupting adequately wheat straw, gave the evidence that 
even under higher OLR the same amount of injected pure culture would maintain the great utilisation of wheat 
straw. Therefore, the next step, a higher OLR would be examined by increasing the amount of injected wheat 
straw in the system. However, it should be more proper to use a two-stage CSTR system in order to separate the 
acidic phase, in order to achieve further enhancement.
