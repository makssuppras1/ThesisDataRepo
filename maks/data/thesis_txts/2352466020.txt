Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to apply density based topology optimization to a spe-
ciï¬پc branch of multiphysics, concerning Electro-Mechanical-Acoustic Interaction
(EMAI). The topology optimization framework is setup by deï¬پning a set of mono-
lithic ï¬پnite element formulations which are solved on a uniï¬پed domain.
The elasticity equations are nonlinearly coupled to the electric ï¬پeld trough
Maxwellâ€™s stress tensor, and geometrical changes in the dielectric domain are ac-
counted for by applying a transformation of the diï¬€erential operators. The acoustic-
structure coupling is assumed linear and modeled using a stable mixed u/p for-
mulation. A two-step solution strategy is chosen in order to solve the monolithic
equations in the frequency domain. The electromechanical problem is solved to ï¬پnd
a static equilibrium, about which the dynamic system is linearized. The system is
perturbed by a time-harmonic acoustic or mechanical excitation and the optimiza-
tion is based on the structural or acoustic response. The sensitivities are computed
using the adjoint method and the optimization problem solved using the Method
of Moving Asymptotes (MMA). A sensitivity ï¬پltering scheme is employed to avoid
mesh dependency and in order to regularize the problem.
The ï¬پnite element implementation is carried out in Matlab and validated by
analytical benchmark problems and solutions from commercial available ï¬پnite ele-
ment software. Discrepancies is observed for the u/p formulation due to a spurious
behavior possibly induced by the strong electromechanical coupling.
The developed framework is used for optimization of the material layout in a
ï¬پxed electrode. It is demonstrated that the optimized electrodes can be used as a
catalyst to provoke acoustic and mechanical mode shape transitions when subjected
to a speciï¬پc potential. The performance of the optimized topologies are shown to be
superior to naive intuitive design guesses. The numerical examples presented con-
ï¬پnes them self to two-dimensional problems optimized for displacement and acoustic
pressure.

1.1
Motivation
The general trend towards smaller electronic circuits and the demands for sensing
devices that interact with the physical world, impose a demand for high perfor-
mance, cheap and reliable micro-electro-mechanical systems (MEMS).
MEMS are deï¬پned by the interaction of electrical circuits and mechanical moving
parts, often on the scale of 1 to 100 micrometers. Due to the small size, the forces
dominating the micro-systems are not equivalent to the forces normally considered
in the macro world. When considering elasticity on the microscopic scale one must
contemplate e.g. adhesive/cohesive forces, temperature induced strains or electro-
static forces.
One of the ï¬پrst MEMS devices can be tracked back to the pioneering work con-
ducted by Harvey C. Nathanson et al. with the seminal paper in 1967 [18], describing
a micro-mechanical resonator applicable as a radio tuner and a method for batch
fabrication. Recent days breakthroughs in lithography have drastically decreased
the size and fabrication costs of MEMS, which entails that they are now found in
most everyday electronic items, including smartphones, watches, hearing aids etc.
A typical example of a linear MEMS actuator is the comb-drive, shown in ï¬پgure
1.1. When a voltage is applied between the electrodes, electrostatic forces will drive
the combs together. The forces developed by the actuator is proportional to the
voltage and surface area, and inverse proportional to the square of the gap between
the teeth. A constant voltage will alter the stiï¬€ness of the system, making it possible
to tune the natural frequency. Similarly a resonating motion can be achieved by
harmonically varying the voltage [18, 22].
The term transducer is used for a device that converts energy from one form to
another. The coupling between the motion of the mechanical system and the elec-
trical input/output makes construction of various MEMS transducers applicable,
namely proximity sensors, accelerometers, gyroscopes etc. MEMS that interact with
the surrounding environment, opens up for numerous other applications. This the-
sis considers transducers that indirectly converts acoustic energy to electric energy
or vice versa. This constitutes applications such as; pressure sensors, electrostatic
loudspeakers and condenser microphones. Figure 1.2 shows a schematic illustration

The aim of this thesis is to apply topology optimization to the speciï¬پc branch
of multiphysics, concerning electro-mechanical-acoustic interaction (EMAI). This
statement is split up into two main objectives.
1. Develop a methodology for modeling the EMAI coupling which is suitable for

2. Use this framework to optimize simple problems and show the importance of

The electromechanical coupling appears when a potential is applied to a capac-
itative system containing a deformable electrode. A strong coupling is considered,
where deformation of the electrode will entail a change in both the electric ï¬پeld and
the Coulomb forces, giving rise to a highly non-linear dependency [5, 18, 22].
The mechanical-acoustic interaction deals with the interaction of an elastic body
and an acoustic medium. The ï¬‚uid is considered inviscid and hydrostatic. The only
coupling considered is the pressure of the ï¬‚uid working on the structural boundaries
and the exchange of acceleration between the structural domain and the ï¬‚uid [36].
Discrete modeling of the coupled system by monolithic ï¬پnite element formula-
tions, makes topology optimization applicable. The numerical examples presented
conï¬پnes them self to two-dimensional problems optimized for displacement and
acoustic pressure.
1.2
Introductory Topology Optimization
The engineering objective of general structural optimization is to methodically im-
prove the material layout within a given design space for a given set of loads and
boundary conditions. The optimization is carried out with respect to one or more
performance measures, often called objective functions, while being subjected to
speciï¬پc design constraints [3]. Structural optimization can be divided into three
diï¬€erent methods; size, shape and topology optimization. Conceptually sketched in

When optimizing for size only, one or more structural dimensions compose the
design variable. The design is hereby limited to the initial design, since the ap-
proach does not allow for changes in geometry. For shape optimization, the strategy

advantage of topology optimization is that boundaries and holes are allowed to form

1.3
Solution Strategies
The structural stiï¬€ness and the structural-acoustic coupling is assumed geometri-
cally linear.
This means that only a weak coupling is considered and the state
equations are solved on the initial reference geometry. The electric ï¬پeld however,
holds a nonlinear dependency of the distance between electrodes. Consequently the
change in domain size cannot be neglected. Here a strong coupling is considered
between the elasticity equations and the electrostatic equations.
The physics are governed by four diï¬€erent partial diï¬€erential equations. For
proper implementation into a density based topology optimization framework, the
state equations can not be solved using a standard boundary coupling method. Most
commercial available ï¬پnite element codes choose a segregated/staggered approach
towards multiphysics problems. This means that the overall domain is divided into
segregated sub-domains, where the speciï¬پc physics are deï¬پned. The sub-domains
are coupled by their interface boundaries and solved with distinct solvers.
The
state variables are then interchanged between solvers until convergence is achieved.

This approach demands that boundaries are explicitly deï¬پned.
It is possible to
approximate the adjacent boundaries by parametric curves, which can be controlled
by design variables, and used as input for a shape optimization algorithm [36].
In topology optimization however, changing element densities entails that new
boundaries will appear spontaneously during the optimization process. It is chosen
to apply a set of monolithic equilibrium equations described in [38, 36, 24] for the
acoustic-structure coupling and for the electromechanical coupling in [23, 35, 19].
This choice of equations makes it possible to model all physics on a uniï¬پed do-
main and solve the state equations simultaneous as a single PDE system. Instead
of changing the governing equations, the diï¬€erent physics can, by the monolithic
approach, be modeled by interpolating several material properties. Here خ³e = 1 will
represent a solid conducting material, while خ³e = 0 represents an insulating acoustic
medium. Consequently a solid or acoustic phase will also necessarily entail speciï¬پc
electric material properties. This methodology is schematically illustrated in ï¬پgure

3.1
The Galerkin Methodology
The Galerkin method refers to a class of methods for converting continuous partial
diï¬€erential equations to discrete ones. The method is credited to the Russian math-
ematician Boris Galerkin and has been widely used within the ï¬پeld of FEA since its
introduction, due to its robust and systematic nature. A fundamental overview of
the method can be found in [21, 37].
The Galerkin method of weighted residuals is a common method for obtaining
the stiï¬€ness matrices for ï¬پnite element formulations, and is the method used in this
chapter. The method can shortly be summarized in the following points:
1. Obtain the Galerkin weak form of the governing equations
(a) Multiply the equations by a test function.
(b) Integrate over the domain.
(c) Employ integration by parts to reduce the diï¬€erential order of terms
where deemed appropriate.
2. Discretize the domain into a number of ï¬پnite elements.
(a) Approximation of the ï¬پeld variable by basis functions.
(b) State the analytical element integrals and derive the system matrix.

Derivatives
A closed form matrix expression for the three derivatives in (3.38) has proven diï¬ƒcult
to derive due to their dependency on F. The derivatives contained in the tangent
stiï¬€ness matrix are therefore currently computed analytically using Maple. The
integrands are converted to Matlab input by use of the CodeGeneration package.
The output is optimized, but still results in close to two-thousand lines of Matlab
code, that has to be evaluated in each Gauss point for the numerical quadrature.
3.5.2
Integration of element matrices
Analytical integration of the element matrices are not possible due to the defor-
mation gradient.
Thus numerical integration by Gauss quadrature is performed
[21, 37]. When using equal sized elements, integration only has to be performed for
a single element when considering the linear part of the system. For all matrices and
vectors related to the electrostatic analysis, their dependency on the state variables
demands that integration is performed for each element, and for each update of the
state variable. The numerous lines needed to deï¬پne the integrands of the tangent
stiï¬€ness matrix, evaluated in each Gauss point, makes the numerical integration by
far the most costly operation. Appendix A.4 contains a time proï¬پle from a common
Matlab run.
3.5.3
Reduced Integration
Numerical integration by 2أ—2 Gauss points has been used for the electrostatic stiï¬€-
ness matrix and the electrostatic forces. This order of integration may be considered
reduced due to the presence of the deformation gradient. This scheme has proven to
produce results equivalent to higher order integration for all the examined problems.
Thus the reduced integration order has been used for the examples presented in this
thesis, as it severely reduces the computational cost.
3.5.4

The mixed displacement-pressure formulation is known to produce spurious non-
zero modes when applied in linear frequency analysis.

of mixed ï¬پnite element formulations have been conducted. In this thesis elements
with quadratic interpolation of displacement ï¬پeld and linear interpolation of the
pressure are used.
In some literature this combination is referred to as Q9/C4,
where C refers to the continuous approximation of the pressure.
The choice of

exhibits best performance out of the quadrilateral element combinations examined.

5.1.3
Summary and Discussion
The two benchmark examples show that the monolithic u/p formulation is not able
to correctly represent the electromechanical coupling with an error margin of up to
8% for high voltages and down to 1% for low voltages. It also evident that the size
of the error depends on the problem design. This is presumably related to the load
case and its resemblance to a point load. These discrepancies will be present in all of
the topology optimization examples. The premature convergence issue signiï¬پcantly
inï¬‚uences how much the natural frequency of the system can be altered, by the
electrostatic forces. The upcoming examples will show that this eï¬€ect is further
reduced by the introduction of acoustics. The voltage where the system ceases to
converge will, for the u/p formulation, be far from the true pull-in voltage. From now
on this voltage will instead be referred to, as the critical voltage. For the cantilever
beam example the concerned material properties resulted in a poorly scaled system
matrix. If no reference solution exists, the structural and acoustic properties of the
upcoming examples will be scaled to avoid potential numerical complications.

deï¬پnes a relatively low feasible resolution of the FE problems used for the linearized

6
Discussion
This chapter contains a discourse of the capabilities and limitations of the developed
EMAI topology optimization framework. The complications encountered during the
project will be discussed and possible directions for future research on the subject
is suggested.
6.1
Comments on the Modeling
6.1.1
The u/p Formulation
The monolithic u/p formulation was used to model the linear elasticity and acous-
tics. The formulation demands higher order interpolation of the displacement ï¬پeld
to secure stability. This led to complications with the deformation gradient tensor
which were overcome by using a lower order mapping and keeping the interpolation
of the potential ï¬پeld linear. The author believes that the this lower order mapping
is a part of the explanation to why the u/p is too soft compared to the standard
displacement formulation and analytical solutions. Appendix A.3 shows that some
spurious behavior of the mixed formulation is induced by point loads.
It seems
that the lower order mapping induces this spurious mode. The problem might be
helped by quadratic interpolation of the potential ï¬پeld, making the higher order
mapping feasible. This hypothesis has however not been tested. The second part
of the explanation is that the electrostatic forces will resemble point loads for large
displacements as the charges will gather where the gap size is smallest. This was
shown for the cantilever beam example. This problem is not overcome by raising
order of interpolation.
The majority of the complications encountered in this project have indirectly been
related to the u/p formulation. The formulation is incapable of producing satisfac-
tory results as the author deems the discrepancies too large to bee ignored. Moreover
the formulation is tedious to work with due to slow convergence, i.e. a very ï¬پne dis-
cretization is needed before the ï¬پeld quantities converges. As a sum of the previous
point, the authorâ€™s assessment is that the u/p formulation is not an ideal choice for
the purpose of modeling the electromechanical coupling. To the authorâ€™s knowledge
it is nevertheless the only well documented monolithic formulation used extensively
for topology optimization of acoustic-structure interaction problems.

6.1. Comments on the Modeling
6.1.2
Assumption of Geometric Linearity
The structural stiï¬€ness and the acoustic coupling is assumed geometrically linear.
This entails that element distortions due to displacements are neglected and the
change of size of the acoustic domain is neglected. When considering the eï¬€ort re-
quired to derive and implement the non-linear framework and the strong electrome-
chanical coupling, a non-linear implementation of the acoustic-structure interaction
would be an obvious next step. A strong acoustic-structural coupling would be of
great interest for the chosen optimization approach, since the electrostatic forces
would not only alter the stiï¬€ness of the mechanical structure, but also the geometry
of the acoustic domain. This additional coupling would open up for at more com-
plex application of the electrostatic forces, and presumably produce more complex
topologies.
6.1.3
Linearized Vibration Analysis
The linearization approach was chosen in order to solve the monolithic equations
in the frequency domain. The electrostatic eï¬€ects were therefore limited to altering
the stiï¬€ness of the linear mechanical system. A more interesting approach would
be to solve the transient equations. This would allow the use of electrostatic forces
to excite the system, hereby opening up for possible modeling of speakers and mi-
crophones. The electrostatic assumption should here be reconsidered, as the time
derivative in Ampereâ€™s law would presumably come into play. See section 2.3.4. A
transient application would demand a more eï¬ƒcient implementation, and a platform
other than Matlab with parallel capabilities.
6.1.4
Matlab Implementation
All FE matrices were derived in the framework of Maple and exported to Matlab
using the code generation package. The inverse of the deformation gradient tensor
drastically increases the number of terms in the Jacobian matrix, resulting in close
to 2000 lines of code to be evaluated in Matlab for each Gauss point evaluation.
This is time consuming to evaluate and the numerical integration has proven to be
the most time consuming part of the code. Despite attempts of optimizing the code
generator output, no good solution were found. An obvious solution is to derive
analytical matrix expressions for the Jacobian matrix components, as done in a lot
of FEM related literature (see e.g. [23, 21, 15]). These derivations would be time
consuming and have for this reason not been assessed a priority in this project. The
payoï¬€would however be rewarding, as a more eï¬ƒcient implementation would allow
ï¬پner discretization of the problems. The presented examples have been optimized
using a fairly coarse discretization due to the computational expense of the current
implementation. The key focus for future iterations of the code should entail the
derivation of these analytical expressions.

6.2
Comments on the Results
6.2.1
Decoupling of the Design Domain
A topology optimization approach were chosen where the design domain was as-
sumed non-deformable and decoupled from the acoustic domain. The coupling be-
tween the design domain and the acoustics was therefore only indirectly considered
trough the electromechanical coupling. That is, the topology can only aï¬€ect the
acoustic behavior by altering the stiï¬€ness of the deformable electrode. This ap-
proach were deemed a necessity as the eï¬€ect of the electromechanical tuning would
otherwise be negligible compared to mechanical and acoustic eï¬€ects directly related
to the material layout. The eï¬€ect of the electromechanical tuning were severely
restricted by the mixed formulationâ€™s limited ability to converge, which was further
limited for ï¬پnite values of the bulk modulus. This limitation is presumably one
of the main reasons that the electrostatic forces did not have a noticeable eï¬€ect
when optimizing a directly coupled design domain (please confer section 5.3.1). The
author believes that optimization close to the true pull-in voltage would create an
eï¬€ect comparable to mechanical and acoustic eï¬€ects. By clever problem design it
can not be ruled out that the electrostatic eï¬€ects would noticeably aï¬€ect the results,
even for optimization far from the pull-in voltage.
6.2.2
Physical Relevance and Triviality
The physical relevance of the presented results are questionable due to the coarse
discretization and the choice of material properties. The results are therefore mainly
of academic interest, and should be regarded as a proof of concept.
The majority of the results are intuitive and do not provide designs that could not
have been thought up by using simple logical reasoning and engineering intuition.
This is partly the result of the simple (but necessary) approach on optimizing a
static electrode that were not directly coupled to the acoustic domain. More complex
designs would presumably have been seen if the optimization could have been carried
out closer to the pull-in voltage, where the strong coupling is more prominent. More
complex problems and/or design domain(s) would also increase the complexity of
the results.
Despite the triviality, the presented results still provides important insight and
useful observations important for further development of the methodology.
6.2.3
Non-Convexity and Intermediate Design Values
The optimization problem is non-convex, which means that the solutions are highly
dependent on initial conditions, choice of interpolation scheme, penalization param-
eter and ï¬پlter. This was emphasized by the static optimization results presented in
section 5.2. When considering the chosen design approach for the EMAI problems
the design domain was decoupled. The optimization problem therefore comes down
to reducing the gap distance between the two electrodes at the right places. This can
be done in a waste amount of ways for a given problem, hence the chosen approach
is highly non-convex. The potential ï¬پeld converges rapidly for design values larger
than zero, this entails that a discrete design is often not advantageous with the

chosen optimization approach. The presented results are therefore the culmination
of extensive parameter studies for each of the presented problems.
A continua-
tion approach was generally observed to help the convergence towards black and
white designs. For future applications the author suggests to implement additional
penalization of intermediate design values into the monolithic electrostatic formu-
lation. Alternatively projection ï¬پlters or robust methods could be applied to yield
completely binary designs and help manufacturability [33, 27]. Robust projection
methods have with success been applied to acoustic-structure interaction problems
and electromechanical problems in references [19, 17] and is therefore an obvious
suggestion for future research.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis the ï¬پeld of topology optimization has been extended and applied to
electro-mechanical-acoustic interaction problems.
This branch of multiphysics is not very well documented in the literature and,
to the authors best knowledge, not at all when topology optimization is considered.
This thesis contributes with a detailed description of the methodology, derivation
and implementation. Numerous complications was encountered related to the im-
plementation. Most diï¬ƒculties were resolved and the process is well documented in
this work. As an application example, the framework was used for optimizing the
material layout of a ï¬پxed electrodes and it is shown how the optimized topologies
can be used as a catalyst to provoke acoustic and mechanical modeshape transitions.
The presented results are trivial in nature and remains of purely academic inter-
est. It is assessed that further development of the methodology is needed before it
can be applied to realistic industrial problems, such as designs of condenser micro-
phones or electrostatic loudspeakers.

prioritized. Areas that, in the authorâ€™s opinion, are the most critical for establishing
and maturing the methodology towards a future goal of analyzing real engineering
