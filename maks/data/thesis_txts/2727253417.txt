Abstract
Metal halide perovskite-based photovoltaics offer high efficiencies compared to conventional solar
cells, but degradation processes are known to impact their lifespan. Self-healing of these structures
has been experimentally observed, but the mechanism is not clear yet. In this project, Density
Functional Theory (DFT) is employed to identify computational self-healing descriptors, aiming
to accelerate the discovery of novel materials for long-lasting, efficient solar cells.
Point defect formation and ionic migration calculations have been performed, comparing struc-
tures with (Cs2AgInCl6 and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6) and without (CsPbBr3) self-healing properties. Us-
ing a calculation workflow, the structure analysis is performed and material stability windows,
formation energy of defects, light-electricity conversion efficiency and kinetic properties (using
CINEB) are obtained.
The results indicate that for all the investigated structures, the formation energy of vacancy
point defects are found to be the overall lowest when compared to other kinds of possible point
defects, showing that they are likely involved in facilitating a pathway for multiple species migra-
tion. In the non self-healing structure CsPbBr3, interstitials have a low formation energy and are
thought to be present in the structure in rest. Upon illumination, these defects can, however, also
be induced in the self-healing structures. Moreover, some antisite point defects show low formation
energies, indicating that a migration pathway through different sites is probable. Simultaneously,
vacancy-based ion hopping mechanisms are found, where the halide ions show the lowest activation
barrier and a clear difference in preferred path can be observed between self-healing and non self-
healing structures. This can be attributed to the different local environments that are created in
double perovskites. Resolvement of halide vacancies likely does not form the time-determining step
of the self-healing process, but the healing of A or B-ions could be time-determining. Additionally,
within the investigated GGA-level, Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 shows deep states caused by low energy defects
which can limit the photoelectronic efficiency. CsPbBr3 and Cs2AgInCl6 are thought to maintain
their electronic quality, despite intrinsic defects being formed.
Although more investigation in point defects and ion hopping pathways is needed to confirm
these findings across a broader set of materials, for example through using hybrid functionals, this
study serves as a valuable starting point for the continued search for computational self-healing
descriptors.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Road to Renewables
As many parts of the world are undergoing development and experiencing a rapid increase in
economic growth, the need for energy is expected to rise even further in the years to come. The
current outlook by EIA shows that throughout 2030, the world energy demand will keep increasing
each year with 1.4% on average according to the stated policies scenario. [1]
In addition to the increase in demand, there is another incentive to reconsider energy sources
and the way of harvesting this energy. In the past centuries, fossil fuels have been the main source of
energy to accommodate the energy demands for the development of the western world that initiated
during the industrialisation period. However, these fossil fuels are known for their contribution
to the green house effect, polluting effects to nature and their non-renewable characteristics. In
order to control and decrease the world’s greenhouse gas emissions, the Paris climate agreement
of 2015 has been signed by 194 states and the EU who together are responsible for over 98% of
the CO2 emissions. [2] The Paris climate agreement aims to limit the increase in global average
temperature to below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, and by itself provides a great incentive to
shift from non-renewable to renewable energy sources.
To achieve the net zero emissions by 2050 scenario, a significant decrease in fossil fuel demands
and a rise in renewable energy sources are essential. It is therefore not only desirable, but incredibly
vital to the worlds ongoing development to explore the possibilities of renewable energies in an
effort to increase the efficiency of already existing technologies and discover new renewable energy
sources. Wind and hydro energy currently provide the largest share of renewable energy production
in Europe, but solar energy is the fastest growing source, with its share in renewable energy rising
from 1% in 2008 to 15.1% in 2021. [3]
In a photovoltaic (PV) cell, solar energy is captured and converted into electricity through the
photoexcitation of electrons in a solar-sensitive material. Although the concept of the solar cell
has been well studied since the previous century, challenges still remain to be overcome when using
this energy harvesting method for large scale or personal applications (e.g. costs, efficiency and
durability). A vast variety of solar cell materials exist, and many more materials are discovered
and remain to be added to this list. The production of solar cells is often mainly limited by their
costs, efficiency and/or long-term stability. A promising, rapidly developing material class of solar
cells are the perovskite-type solar cells, which are especially interesting due to their simple and
low-cost synthesising process and their relatively high photoconversion efficiencies, despite being a
novel emerging technology.

1.2. Overcoming Bottlenecks
1.2
Overcoming Bottlenecks
Although perovskite-type solar cells are a very promising material to aid in the energy conversion
from fossil fuels to renewables, their development is hindered by a significant challenge: degrada-
tion. The degradation of halide perovskites has been studied for many years, as it directly impacts
the extent of their potential applications.
Multiple pathways contribute to the degradation of
these crystal structures, with the most common culprits being air and moisture, photodecompo-
sition, mechanical decomposition, and temperature-induced decomposition. Although addressing
all these stability factors is crucial for creating efficient energy harvesting materials, some mech-
anisms are influenced by external factors rather than inherent material properties, making them
more manageable through controlling operating conditions. For instance, the ‘capping’ technique
(where the perovskite is covered by a protective nanolayer that prevents outgassing) has already
proven successful in preventing degradation caused by air and moisture. Similarly, temperature
and mechanical forces are external factors that can be adjusted accordingly to oppose degradation
as much as possible. On the other hand, inherent properties of the crystal structure cannot be
altered externally and play an essential role in determining whether a structure is a promising can-
didate for solar cell applications. It is therefore desirable to find the key descriptor for favorable
intrinsic properties that prevent, oppose or reverse degradation. Once favourable intrinsic material
properties are found that are linked to self-healing properties, effective material discovery in this
field can be enabled.
Advancements in computational power have made computer simulations increasingly appeal-
ing for exploring material characteristics, as they offer a faster and more cost-effective alternative
to ‘traditional’ experiments. In the past, material discovery relied heavily on iterative experimen-
tal processes that consumed a significant amount of both resources and time. However, with the
emergence of computer simulations, the number of potential candidates for specific applications can
now be quickly narrowed down based on desired design criteria. The aim is to find computational
descriptors that are able to connect material properties on the microscopic scale to macroscopic
materials properties. These simulations can then suggest materials for further experimental inves-
tigation. This screening process provides a selection of promising materials that can be further
explored experimentally.
1.3
Research Goal
This project aims to uncover computational-level self-healing descriptors that can aid in material
engineering and high-throughput discovery of self-healing materials contributing to long-lifetime,
high efficiency solar cells.
The objective is to conduct literature research to identify a viable
photodegradation mechanism in halide perovskite structures. Subsequently, descriptors related to
photodegradation need to be identified. It is assumed that the same descriptors that signify a
degradation are involved in the self-healing mechanism, as this process is the direct reversion of
photodegradation. These descriptors can therefore be reverse-engineered to investigate the self-
healing properties.
In this project, the inherent defect formation energies and ionic migration pathways are compu-
tationally investigated for one structure that does not possess self-healing properties (CsPbBr3),
and two that do (Cs2AgInCl6 and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6).
A calculation workflow based on density
functional theory (DFT) is set up and used to calculate compound stability windows to retrieve
chemical potentials at which the target compound can be formed. Structure relaxations using spin-
orbit coupling corrections are done to obtain the energetics of a range of charged and uncharged
defect states. The band-structure and density of states of the compounds are determined to locate

The Solar Cell
In this chapter, the reader will get familiar with the concepts that are used to build a functioning
solar cell and which parameters are important when assessing the potential of a certain material
to be used for photovoltaic applications. Furthermore, the classical limitations of solar efficiency
are reviewed. Finally, it is discussed what perovskite materials are, how they can be used for

2.1.1
Semiconductors and Their Properties
Electronic conduction through photoexcitation is an important property for solar cell materials.
To understand which type of materials are both capable of absorbing the photonenergy harvested
from light, as well as turning this energy into an electronic current, the electronic band structure
of a material should be investigated. While energy levels of molecules exist in distinct (sometimes
degenerate) energy levels, the energy levels of electrons in solids rather exist in a ‘continuous’ band,
that originates from introducing a larger number of atoms (and with that, increasing the number
of energy states) to the system. [4]

2.1. The Solar Cell
The focus will be put on the first excited non-filled band (Conduction Band (CB)) and the highest
occupied energy band (Valence Band (VB)). Between these distinct energy bands, an energy gap
is present that is called the ‘band gap’ and denoted by Eg. The size of the bandgap between
the VB and CB determines whether a material can be classified as a conductor, an insulator or a
semiconductor. The difference in bandstructure between these three material categories are shown
in Figure 2.1.
In conductors, the valance band and the conduction band overlap, so that electrons are directly
excited to the conduction band. In insulators, the band gap energy is so large, that hardly any
electrons are excited to the conduction band. In semiconductors, electrons can be excited across
the band gap by external stimulus such as light, when photons have a greater energy than the

2.1.2
Principle of The Solar Cell
A photovoltaic (PV) cell works through the capture of solar energy and its subsequent conversion
into electricity. This process happens, as mentioned previously, through the photo-excitation of
electrons. In the conventional c-Si solar cell, both a p-type and an n-type semiconductor are used,
and placed next to each other. Because of the excess of electrons in the n-layer and the excess of
holes in the p-layer, a depletion zone is created. In this zone, the electrons from the higher energy
CB on the n-type semiconductor side will move into the holes in the lower energy CB on the
p-type semiconductor side. However, as the diffusion continues, the energy level on the n-region
side within the depletion zone begins to decrease, while the energy level of the p-region increases.
When equilibrium is reached, no electrons are left that have enough energy to cross the junction.

A few parameters are important to keep in mind when designing a solar cell.
The dielectric
constant (ϵr) is a measure for the ability of a material to store electrical energy in an electric field.
The higher the value of ϵr, the lower the dissociation energy for excitons. The effective mass of
electrons and holes can also be an indicator for PV efficiency. In general, solar cells with lower
effective masses for both electrons and holes tend to exhibit better performance because charge
carriers can move more easily through the material. [5] Carrier mobility and electron-hole diffusion
length should both be maximised. Finally, the band gap size is one of the most crucial properties
of a photovoltaic material, since this value determines which part of the solar spectrum can be
absorbed by the semiconductor. If the photon energy Ef is smaller than Eg, the energy can not
be absorbed. When Ef is bigger than Eg, the photon will be absorbed, but the excess in energy is
used to excite the electron somewhere in the valence band. When the electron relaxes back down
to the conduction band minimum (CBM), the excess energy is lost. The bandgap should therefore
be within the ideal range, where enough photons are absorbed, but not too much energy is lost
during relaxation to the CBM. This trade-off implies that a theoretical maximum efficiency for a
PV device exist, which is called the Shockley-Queisser limit.
2.1.3
Shockley-Queisser Limit
The maximum theoretical efficiency of a solar cell that utilizes a single p-n junction to collect
power from the cell, with radiative recombination being the sole loss mechanism, is known as the
Shockley-Queisser (SQ) limit. Originally derived to be around 30% for a bandgap of 1.1 eV in the

In halide perovskites, the X-anion is a halide, meaning that it has a monovalent charge.

Tuneable Optical Properties
Halide perovskites show great potential for PV applications because of their appropriate (and
tunable) bandgap, high absorption coefficient, long and balanced electron-hole diffusion lengths,
and large dielectric constant. [15] The s-state of the 6s2 cations decreases in energy relative to the
p-state once built into a halide perovskite structure, and hybridization occurs between the B-cation
s-states and the p-states of the X-anion. [15] The bandgap of the material is determined by the
previously mentioned orbitals. [17] By changing certain parameters in the composition of the halide
perovskite, the band-gap can be tuned to achieve optimal values. The band gaps are proven to be
dependent on the following parameters:
• Symmetries: lower symmetries show larger gaps. For example, the orthorhombic phase has
a larger band gap than the tetragonal phase, which in its turn has a larger bandgap than the
cubic phase. [17]
• Volume/lattice parameter: the bandgap increases with increasing lattice parameter.
• Octahedral distortion: it is computationally shown that the gap is reduced linearly with
the tilting angle as ≈0.02 eV/°. [17] In the real systems however, the rotation of the octahe-
dron is followed by changes in the lattice constants with the effect of increasing the bandgap
as mentioned in the previous point.
• X-anion: The bandgap increases with an increase in the electronegativity of the anion. [17]
• A-cation: The substitution of the A-ion does not directly alter the band gap, but it changes
the B-X bond distance which in its turn has an impact on the bandgap.
The bandgap
increases with a larger A-ion. [18]
• B-cation: The bandgaps is influenced by the electronegativity of the cation. For example,
the Sn systems have a smaller bandgap compared with the Pb perovskites. [17]
The possibility of combining different cations and anions in the structure leads to even more
interesting structures with other properties. In addition to bandgap tunability, the plethora of
different combinations of cations can also increases the stability of halide perovskites. [19]

Flaws in Halide Perovskites
Although halide perovskites have many advantages when it comes to photovoltaic applications,
there are also points of weakness that present themselves through easy degradation, a decreased
stability of the structure and the presence of toxic compounds. Halide perovskites are known to
degrade easily through various processes like light, temperature mechanical stress or the combina-
tion of air and moisture. [23] As can be concluded in the previous subsection about stability, the
stability of the perovskite lattice shows great dependence on the present ions and not every combi-
nation of ions will result in a structure that is capable of retaining its stable original composition.
Finally, the majority of documented well-performing halide perovskites contain lead, which is a
toxic element and should ideally be avoided.
Although degradation is a typical occurrence in halide perovskites, they are also known to exert
self-healing properties where the crystal lattice can return to its original state. Despite extensive
research on self-healing in halide perovskites, the underlying cause of this phenomenon remains
unclear. If this can be identified, it would be feasible to adjust the properties of halide perovskites
to allow for self-healing after degradation, thus enhancing their attractiveness and sustainability
in solar cell applications.

Density Functional Theory
Density Functional Theory (DFT) aims to solve an interacting system by mapping it to a non-

the modern DFT methods that are used today.

Kohn-Sham Equations
The Kohn-Sham (KS) system is defined as a collection of non-interacting electrons that have the

2.2.7
Limitations of DFT
As made apparent in this chapter, DFT is a powerful method to computationally probe electronic
structure properties of materials. However, despite its widespread use, there are some limitations
to DFT that should be taken into account when interpreting its results. Below, the limitations of
DFT that are particularly relevant to interpreting the results of this thesis are lined out.
The Bandgap Problem
The predicted band gap energies using DFT methods are generally an underestimate of the true
band gap.
The fundamental gap (or quasiparticle gap) of an electronic system containing N
electrons is defined as the difference between the ionization energy I (the energy needed to remove
one electron from an isolated system) and the electron affinity A (the change in energy for a system
that receives an electron), according to Equation 2.19. [34]
Efun
gap = I(N) −A(N) = (EN+1 −EN) −(EN −EN−1)
(2.19)
However, the fundamental gap in the Kohn-Sham bandstructure that is calculated through DFT
is not equal to the true fundamental gap of I(N) −A(N). This is a direct consequence of how
the bandgap is calculated in DFT methods, being the difference in energy between the lowest
unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) and the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO). [35]
The energy of the HOMO is exact, because it is described by the exact ground state density,
and equal to the negative of the ionization energy according to Janak’s theorem [36] (the DFT
counterpart of Koopmans’ theorem [37]). Determining the energy of the LUMO is more troublesome,
since DFT is not able to account for changes in the electron density when adding an electron
to the system. [26]

Self-Interaction Error
The self-interaction error becomes apparent from Equation 2.14, as the energy is a functional of the
single-particle density and simultaneously contains a Coulomb interaction term between an electron
and the entirety of electron density. Since each electron contributes to the total electron density of
the system, coulomb interaction contains an interaction term of the electron with itself. [38] Usually,
the XC-functional introduces a correction term to counteract this error.
Derivative Discontinuity
When systems with a non-integer number of electrons are considered, the density of the state ρN+ϵ

Spin-Orbit Coupling
Spin-orbit coupling is a phenomenon that is known to influence the bandgap as well. Spin-orbit
coupling (SOC) is defined as the interaction between the spin of the electron (⃗S) with its orbital
angular momentum (⃗L).
This so-called ‘L-S coupling’ is expressed through the total angular
momentum quantum number ⃗J = ⃗S + ⃗L. For heavier elements, coupling occurs through the ‘j-j’
scheme, where⃗j of each individual electron in the system couples. [40] The existence of multiple total
angular momentum quantum numbers causes the energy levels to split to multiple non-degenerative
states, which impacts the energy of the system with an additional term that scales exponentially
with the nuclear charge (Z4) of a system. [41]
Hence, heavier elements have a more noticable
dependence of the bandgap on SOC. The VBM is typically raised, while the CBM is lowered,
causing the bandgap to narrow. [42]
When spin orbit coupling is not considered, an acceptable

does not approximate the true band edge positions indefinitely. This artifact of spin-orbit coupling
poses serious implications for calculations where the location of band edges play a role, like charge

Lattice and Electronic Defects
Many solids are subjected to crystallographic defects: points in the lattice where the ordering is
disturbed. In this section, the concept of point defects is introduced and the method of determining
the formation energy of point defects and the consequences of their formation in the lattice for
transport properties are discussed. In addition to lattice defects, electronic defects are also present
in crystalline solids and present themselves as mobile electrons and holes. The defects can be
formed through excitation, but also through the introduction of point defects. In this section,
different aspects of electronic defects are discussed and their relation to point defects that are
relevant to the functioning of semiconductors.
2.3.1
Types of Point Defects
Point defects are imperfections in the lattice that are limited to one site or its immediate vicinity, in
contrast to line or plane defects, which are characterised by directional displacement that extends
over a larger area. [44] A separation can be made between doping defects and intrinsic defects, where
the previous are defects formed by introducing a foreign ion or atom in the crystal lattice, whereas
the latter defects are formed through ‘rearrangements’ of atoms or ions that are naturally present
in the structure. Since the aim of this thesis is to investigate self-healing properties of materials,
which is likely governed through intrinsic properties, only intrinsic defects are of interest here. The
intrinsic defects that are further analysed in this thesis are vacancies, antisites and interstitials,

Chemical Potential
When aiming to calculate the formation energy of point defects, the Gibbs free energy is an
important parameter that serves as a criterion for the spontaneity of a process under constant
temperature and pressure. The Gibbs free energy of a system can be given in terms of chemical
potentials of species, which is defined as the amount of energy that is released or absorbed when
the number of a particular present species changes (which is the case in a chemical reaction or
phase transition), while remaining a constant number of other constituents. The Gibbs energy
allows for the introduction of partial molar thermodynamic quantities. The Gibbs free energy is
defined as the sum of partial molar Gibbs energies, which is called the ‘chemical potential’. When
an equilibrium is reached under these circumstances, the Gibbs energy is the minimized chemical
potential as given in Equation 2.21. [44]

The chemical potential is important when calculating the formation enthalpy of point defects

or ions from a material. Since the formation of a point defect involves the transfer of atoms or
ions between different sites in the crystal lattice, the formation enthalpy can be expressed as the
difference between the energy of the defective crystal and the energy of the perfect crystal corrected
for the change in chemical potential associated with the formation of the defect. Another way to
describe the physical meaning of the chemical potential in a more instinctive way is to regard it as
the reflection of the energy of the reservoirs from the atoms that are involved in creating the point
defect. [46]
However, it is not always possible to assign a true chemical potential to one specific element
when looking at a complex system containing many different elements and different arrangements
of them within the crystal lattice. The reason for this is that the addition of an ion or atom may
require a change in the number of complementary structure elements, and this change may in its
turn violate the requirement of a definite site ratio. [44] Simply put, when a structure element is
added to or removed from a crystal lattice (which is the case in a point defect), it may require
the removal or addition of other structure elements to maintain the proper balance of structure
elements at each site in the crystal lattice. Because of the interplay of different structural elements
and their arrangement within the lattice, the overall chemical potential of the system can be
affected and a true chemical potential to an individual structure element is quite hard to assign.

2.3.5
Considerations and Constraints in Executing Point Defect Calcu-
lations
Accurate point defect calculations necessitate careful parameter selection and awareness of poten-
tial pitfalls that can introduce inaccuracies. This subsection serves as an introduction in which
important considerations and their implications for the calculation are introduced.
In DFT, calculations are performed on a finite-sized unit cell, which represents a small portion
of the entire crystal. To mimic the behavior and electronic properties of the bulk material, periodic
boundary conditions are applied that imply periodic replication of the unit cell in space.
By
including periodic images, DFT calculations capture the long-range interactions in the material.
When introducing defects, long-range interactions between charged defects can be introduced. [52]
For this reason, the size of the supercell used to model the material and the dispersion of defects
contained within should be large enough to ensure that the defect does not interact with itself
through periodic boundary conditions.
For semiconductors, the most prominent of the root for inaccuracies in point defect formation
and charge transition level determination is the previously discussed bandgap problem in DFT. It
becomes apparent from Equation 2.25 that the bandgap problem poses difficulties for the accurate
determination of the point defect formation energy, as it is a function of the fermi-level (also re-
ferred to as the electronic chemical potential), the VBM of the perfect structure, and the VBM of
the defected structure. It is known that the defects localized on an atomic scale are already well
described by GGA when referenced to the so-called average electrostatic potential ϕ. [52] However,
delocalised states such as the VBM and CBM are not well-described on GGA level and suffer from
the bandgap problem. Since the electronic defects in semiconductors that present themselves near
the band edges or within the bandgap itself are the most detrimental for the semiconductor’s elec-
tronic properties, the bandgap edges require very accurate determination. To determine the band
edge positions more accurately, GGA + GW or HSE functionals are often employed. [48,51,53–56]
However, being able to reproduce a theoretical bandgap that is in correspondence with the ex-
perimentally determined one, does not guarantee that the description of defect levels is correct.

2.3.6
Types of Electronic Defects and Carrier Recombination
The mobility and lifetime of charge carriers is an important property of solar sensitive materials,
as maximum efficiency is reached when the charge carriers are collected at the electrodes. Recom-
bination of holes and electrons can occur through radiative processes, where an electron combines
with a hole and releases a photon, or non-radiative processes, where recombination leads to phonon
release instead. Because of the presence of a bandgap in semiconductors, radiative processes are
less common (due to direct separation of charges) and not problematic to the device efficiency,

2.3. Lattice and Electronic Defects
unlike non-radiative recombination, that often occurs in materials with point defects that form
‘trap’ charge transition states.
The types of trap states are generally divided in ‘deep’ and ‘shallow’ levels. The position of
deep levels are energetically deeply placed in the band gap and away from the band edges, so that
the energy to remove or add an electron to the VB or CB respectively is high in comparison to the
thermal energy. The generation of deep traps is known to decrease the lifetime of photo-generated
carriers, since they provide uncontrolled nonradiative recombination channels. [35] On the other
hand, shallow level defects present themselves close enough to the band edges to be easily ionised.
A Coulomb attraction arises between the electron and the hole in the Cb or VB. This attraction
leads to the formation of a hydrogen-like defect state (the electron moves in the vicinity of a positive
charge, as in a hydrogen atom), where electrons are merely bound in an effective mass-state and
not in a hard-ionisation state like the deep traps. [35] Shallow defects are therefore less detrimental
to the photoelectronic efficiency of a solar cell.
2.3.7
Ionic Migration
The formation of point defects in a structure is often accommodated by diffusion of ions. A vacancy
mediated hopping mechanism is quite commonly found to be the root of ionic diffusion, as well
as the interstitial-mediated mechanism. [35]
In the first case, it is the vacancy defect that can
move through the lattice as the vacancy gets consistently filled up by neighbouring species. In the
case of an interstitial-mediated mechanism, the interstitial defect can ‘jump’ to other interstitial
sites.
First-principle calculations using the nudged elastic band (NEB) method are capable of
providing the energetic activation energy of a diffusion mechanism. The NEB method is based
on the principles of transition state theory, [57] where an initial and final state of a system are
specified and connected through ‘elastic bands’ with intermediate images, that are projected on
the pathway between them. The NEB algorithm works by iteratively adjusting the positions of the
intermediate states based on forces and energies calculated at each relaxation step. These forces are
then projected onto the tangent space of the band to determine the direction in which the replicas
should move. [58] The replicas are nudged along the band in a way that minimizes the energy. This
process continues until the forces and stress tensor components on all replicas converge, indicating
that the system has found a minimum energy pathway (MEP). The final result is a set of optimized
replica positions that represent the most likely pathway for ionic migration. [58] Climbing image
nudged elastic band (CINEB) is an extension to NEB, that is capable of finding the transition
state more accurately, by allowing the intermediate image to ‘climb’ up the energy pathway. Its
position is adjusted in a way that it moves closer to the true transition state by searching for the
maximum force acting on the atoms along the pathway. [59]
Migration Rate
The migration rate can be calculated using the Arrhenius equation, which gives an indication for the
timescale at which hopping events take place. Although the expression of the pre-exponential factor
contains a plethora of microscopic interpretations, we are only interested in estimating the order
of magnitude and therefore don’t consider the contribution of translational, rotational, vibrational
and electronic energy states [60] and assume that they are exactly equal at the starting point, the
transition state and the final point. By assuming this, the migration rate can be expressed as a
simplification the reaction rate for a unimolecular reaction, as given in Equation 2.28.

Chapter 3
Problem Review
In this chapter, extensive literature research is done to understand the degradation mechanism of
halide perovskites. If it is well understood which factors determine the extent of degradation, a
reversing mechanism can be suggested for self-healing properties. In addition to studying the degra-
dation, literature that describes self-healing properties in structures is assessed and a mechanism
is proposed. Based on both mechanisms, distinct self-healing properties are further investigated
and a hypothesis for computational descriptors is formed.
3.1
Degradation
The degradation and self-healing of perovskite structures can originate from various pathways.
Air and moisture, mechanical and thermal degradation have all been reported in HP structures.
As mentioned in section 1.2, these types of degradation are not inherent properties of the crystal
structure and can therefore be altered externally. Photodegradation is inherent to the structure
and is therefore a crucial indicator for inherent self-healing. For this reason, photodegradation
forms the focus in this thesis as the primary degradation mechanism.
Photodegradation is the process of degradation of materials due to irradiation of light. This
type of degradation is known to occur in many classes of materials, including perovskite struc-
tures. Especially in the case of light-harvesting structures, this type of degradation imposes severe
implications for the application and efficiency as functioning materials in solar cells. Both the pho-
todegradation in organic-inorganic halide perovskites (OIHPs) and all-inorganic halide perovskites
(AIHPs) are discussed in the following subsections.
3.1.1
Photodegradation in OIHPs
Research related to degradation mechanisms in perovskites have mainly been focused on MAPbI3,
an organic-inorganic halide perovskite. The study of this system is of great relevance to relate obser-
vations seen in organic-inorganic halide perovskites to those in all-inorganic perovskites. Although
in MAPbI3 degradation products and intermediate steps have been concluded, the photodegra-
dation mechanism remains to be fully uncovered. It is well understood that the 3D structure of
MAPbI3 can degrade to the 2D PbI2 structure, but no firm conclusion is reached yet on how
exposure to light aids the formation of PbI2. Several plausible mechanisms have been suggested
over the past years. Some possible mechanisms are discussed below.
It is thought that the degradation can be initiated at any position in the crystal lattice, and
that it gradually spreads from the initiation point. [61]
This starting point can very well be a

Photodegradation in AIHPs
After having studied MA+ and FA+ containing perovskites, some further delving into all-inorganic

documented than the organic counterparts.
Only cesium lead double halide perovskite has a

(3.3)

The formation of lead(hyro)oxide or carbonate on the surface of the AIHP CsPbBr3 has been

3.1.3
Conclusion on Photodegradation
When comparing the proposed mechanism for the two classes of perovskites, it becomes apparent
that similar degradation products are formed. For example, the formation of BX2 forms a probable
degradation product in both classes of perovskites. However, while for OIHPs the outgassing of
A-ions poses a serious risk for irreversible degradation, the studied AIHP suggests the out-diffusion
of Pb2+, while Cs+ stays stable in the structure. In both cases however, it becomes apparent that
ionic diffusion and the formation of trap states play a role in fast photodegradation. The latter
is induced by the formation of point defects, which is the result of ionic diffusion.
Naturally,
investigating point defects, ionic diffusion and the role of the A-ion this would form an initial
starting point for the exploration of self-healing processes.
3.2
Self-Healing
If many degradation pathways exist for HPs, the materials would not be suitable for PV appli-
cations. However, some HPs show self-healing properties, which allow them to bypass this initial
problem and prove suitable candidates for long-term operation. Self-healing is defined as the pro-
cess of internal repair of a material without any external intervention except for environmental
conditions. Just like the photodegradation mechanisms show some differences between OIHPs and
AIHPs, their documented self-healing properties and mechanisms also vary to some extent. In
the following sections, the documented self-healing in OIHPs and AIHP is discussed to gain an
understanding of self-healing and identify descriptors for the process.
3.2.1
Self-Healing in OIHPs
Experimental research has shown that the photodegraded MAPbI3 shows self-healing properties
through a darkness treatment, but also through intense laser irradiation. Under prolonged laser
treatment, however, the structure irreversible degrades again. [63]
This self-healing in organic-
inorganic hybrid perovskites can according to Lu et al. (2021) be ascribed to ionic transportation
of halide and monovalent cations after being exposed to light. [68] The ionic conductivity in MAPbI3
does not only happen under light irradiation, but also in dark conditions. [72] It is shown that light
irradiation promotes ion-diffusion and generates meta-stable perovskite configurations, while these
processes can be reversed under dark conditions. [73] It was found that certain defects in HPs show

two configurations: either shallow traps with long carrier lifetime, or deep traps with short carrier
lifetime. If the kinetic barrier associated with changing between these configurations is low enough,
self-healing can occur. [73]
Previous research on self-healing on a 2D Ruddelsden Popper (RP) structure (BA2PbI4), a
quasi-2D structure (BA2MA2Pb3I10) and a 3D structure (MAPbI3) show that a stronger 2D-

3D perovskite, remains trapped in the inorganic layer so that the reverse reaction of degradation
can occur. For B = Sn structures, additional stability in 2D perovskites is obtained through sup-
pressing the oxidation of Sn2+ into Sn4+, and their ionic out-diffusion. [74,75] In a 1D perovskite
formed upon the insertion of 2-(1H-pyrazol-1-yl)pyridine into a 3D lead-based perovskite, the ion-
migration channels of A-site ions are blocked, which inhibits the irreversible decomposition of
the structure and self-healing can be ensured. [76] Upon introduction of cetyltrimethylammonium
(CTA) to the 3D MAPbI3 perovskite, a 2D perovskite is formed that shows self-healing prop-
erties. [77] The self-healing ability of structures increases when larger volume organic A-ions are
introduced. Unsurprisingly, DFT calculations of the formation energy of these structures shows
that the structures with a lower formation energy also show the highest degree of self-healing. [75]
If reaction products remain contained over time in the structure, a recovery reaction can
occur. In this proposed self-healing mechanism for (BA)2PbI4, oxidation of Pb0 to Pb2+ occurs as
illustrated in Equation 3.4: [64]

ber of defects in the structure. The self-healing can then be attributed to the ion and/or atom
displacement. [78] Another report claims a relation between the self-healing of bulk in perovskites
and the localisation and deformation formed through the formation of an X-interstitial. [66] The
stability of the point defect in OIHPs FAPbBr3 and MAPbBr3 have been found to be low, whereas

3.2.3
Conclusion on Self-Healing
From comparing self-healing observations in OIHPs and AIHPs, it can be concluded that pho-
toirradiation always promotes the formation of defects, that get resolved through ionic migration.
It is also found that the A-cation influences the self-healing in HPs, and that the prevention of
out-diffusion forms a prerequisite for self-healing phenomena. The most natural step to proceed is
to look at intrinsic defect formation energies and ionic mobilities, since these are the most likely
structural properties to be involved in self-healing.
3.3
Ionic Mobility
For many years, ionic mobility in HPs has been studied, since it is believed to be a root cause
for many inherent halide perovskite phenomena such as ferroelectric properties, thermoelectricity,
structural changes and instability. [84] These characteristics are thought to originate from the soft
crystal structure that is inherent to halide perovskites, since the ionic bond between the monovalent
halide anion and the divalent cation is not so strong. Halide ions can therefore migrate quite easily
through the crystal lattice.
These X-ions can interact with the electric field during solar cell
operation, which can inhibit migration of ions or the introduction of point defects. [23] The reason
why unveiling the ionic transport in these structures remains a challenge, is the plethora of possible
mobile ions, the possibility of ionic transport being coupled to electronic transport, and the high
degree of defects that are naturally present. [84] Ionic migration offers a plausible explanation for
both photodegradation and self-healing, since the expected timescale of this process is around the
same order as the time taken to degrade or to recover.

3.4. Formation of Point Defects
3.3.1
Ionic mobility in OIHPs
One of the most studied halide perovskite classes for ionic movement in the class of OIHPs is
MAPbI3. Several migration pathways in (tetragonal) MAPbI3 have been researched. It has been
found that the iodine vacancy migration energy is isoenergetic between an axial-equatorial and
an equatorial-equatorial exchange. [62] For trigonal FAPbI3 however, axial-equatorial migration is
preferred. [85] This migration path is likely preferred because of the weak bonding character of
the Pb-I bonds which have a strong Pb-s and I-p anti-bonding character. [21] The MA+ vacancy
migrates to its nearest neighbour MA+ in either the ab or the bc plane. Although in some cases
found to be isoenergetic for MA+ [62] and FA+, [85] migration in the ab plane for MA+ shows a
lower activation energy than migration in the bc plane. [85] The Pb2+ vacancies prefer to move to
the nearest Pb2+ neighbour compared to their diagonal one, [62] although diagonal movement has
been investigated. [86] Interstitial migration has not been observed in OIHPs because of the lack
of interstitial space in the structures. [86]
3.3.2
Ionic mobility in AIHPs
Migration of defects (or ionic movement) in AIHPs are less investigated than in OIHPs. It has been
found that under dark conditions, the activation energy for total ionic movement in MAPbI3 is 0.62
eV, while for CsPbI2Br the activation energy is 0.45 eV. [87] If we can say that ionic migration indeed
is a measure for self-healing capacities, it would mean that self-healing through ionic movement
in dark conditions is easier in AIHPs than in OIHPs. Simultaneously, MAPbI3 has an activation
energy for ionic movement of 0.06 eV under illumination, in contrast to the 0.43 eV of CsPbI2Br, [87]
suggesting that OIHPs can also be more prone to photodegradation than AIHPs.
The vacancy-mediated hopping mechanism of hailde ions in perovskties are well defined
through the movement of ions along the octahedral edges. The halide ion migration (or halide
vacancy migration) in Cs2SnX6, where X=(I, Br, Cl) shows the lowest activation energy when
hopping occurs from equatorial site to equatorial side within the same octahedral. [88] In CsPbBr3,
the lowest ion migration activation energy of Br– is the hopping from equatorial to equatorial
spots, and not from axial to equatorial positions. For double perovskites, that consist two types
of B-ions as a solution to replace the toxic Pb2+, the hopping mechanism of the X-ion can occur
through two pathways: one pathway around B1 and one around B2. [89] The reason for differences
in ion migration energy can be due to the anisotropic character of ion migration through different
migration pathways. [90] For the A- and B-ion hopping mechanisms, it has been found that this
occurs through a nearest neighbour exchange. While the migration of A+ occurs to neighbours
in the ab plane and B2+ moves diagonally. [91] Although differences are present, all calculations
indicate that the movement of the halide is more likely than that of the A- or B-site cations.

It can be seen that in all cases, the formation energy of the halide vacancy is low at a Fermi level of 0
referenced to the VBM compared to the formation energy of other point defects. When comparing
the formation energy of the B-ion vacancy, it can be observed that this value is on the higher side
compared to other present defects in the structures for both the SH structure Cs2AgInCl6 and the
NSH structures CsPbI3 and CsSnI3. Only the NSH structure CsSnBr3 forms an exception to the
trend that is seen in the other structures, where the vacancy formation energy of Sn is comparable
to that of the halide vacancy. Since the vacancy formation energy for the B-ion does not show
remarkable differences between SH and NSH structures, this defect is therefore not immediately
considered a direct descriptor for SH properties.
A distinct difference between the SH structure and the NSH structures is the position of the
formation energy of the A-ion vacancy at a Fermi level equal to the VB maximum. For the SH
structure, the VCs defect is higher than both the B-ion and X-ion vacancy defect, while for the
NSH structures the B-ion vacancy always has the highest formation energy among the vacancy-
type defects. The fact that the formation energy of B-ion defects are lower in the self-healing
structure means that the chance of encountering these type of defects is also higher. In a structure
that is trying to heal, an ion hopping mechanism through vacant B-ion sites now seems possible.
Halide ion hopping through B-ion sites might prove to be an indicator for SH properties and has
not been investigated before, only a hopping mechanism from equatorial X-X site. [89] Therefore,
expanding the knowledge on halide movement through other possible pathways and comparing
their activation energy between SH and NSH structures could provide an indicator for self-healing
properties.
Another defect with a remarkably low formation energy is the the formation of a AX substitu-
tion defect. When comparing the formation energy among the four different structures, it becomes
apparent that both for SH and NSH structures this characteristic is observed, with exception for
the NSH structure CsSnBr3 where the defect shows a higher defect formation energy. As was
the case for the vacancy formation energy for the B-ion, the AX defect does not show prominent
distinctions between SH and NSH structures.
An interesting finding is the low activation energy of the InAg substitution defect that is
inherent to the SH Cs2AgInCl6 structure. Since this structure is the only structure compared
to the other structures in Figure 3.2 that is both self-healing and consisting of two B-type ions,
it is unclear whether this point defect could insinuate a migration pathway of B-ions during the
self-healing process, or if it is just a result of the two present B-ions in the structure that can easily
substitute. Simultaneously, the formation energy of the reversed defect (AgIn) is much higher. This
could be an effect of the atomic radius of Ag2+ (0.94 Å) being larger than the ionic radius of In3+
(0.8 Å). [96] It therefore becomes increasingly hard to substitute a larger ion in a place reserved
for a smaller one. Based on this, one can assume that the low formation energy of InAg is likely
a result of the structure containing multiple B-ions with different characteristics than it being an
indicator for self-healing. On the other hand, since self-healing properties have been observed in
Cs3Bi2Br3I6 [80] and CsPbI1.2Br1.8 [81] (both structures containing multiple species of X-ions), it
might be the case that structures that contain multiple A-, B- or X-ions have more possibilities
for the formation of point defects that can induce different vacancy hopping mechanisms. This
hypothesis should be tested by calculating the ion migration activation energy barriers for multiple
plausible pathways for a series of structures that are known to self-heal.
A hypothesis is that the formation energy of other vacancy defects than the halide one are
too large to occur in self-healing structures, whereas a new halide ion migration pathway can
be ‘unlocked’ that is mitigated through the A-site ions. This mechanism is deemed unlikely in
non-self-healing structures.

3.5. Conclusion
3.5
Conclusion
It is now necessary to put the puzzle-pieces together formed by the conclusions of previous literature
and determine how one can further build on the knowledge to come up with a suitable descriptor
for self-healing in structures, which is the main objective in this project.
The ionic migration has been investigated in both OIHPs and AIHPs. Within these two types
of structures, the same migration mechanisms are mentioned for A, B and X-ions. It becomes clear
that it is mostly the X-ions that have the lowest migration energy and are therefore the most likely
to be the most prominent cause of ion-migration induced material degradation. It seems likely
that for self-healing, a high chance of ionic migration (i.e. a low migration energy) seems to be a
determining factor, as well as how easily certain defect types are present. These are two inherent
material properties that can be studied computationally, and will form the further focus of this
thesis.

Chapter 4
Methods
Electronic structure calculations are performed using the software packages presented in this chap-
ter. A workflow has been designed to ensure an efficient streamline of calculations. The different
steps the workflow consists of, as well as the used calculation parameters in each step, are presented
in this chapter as well.
4.1
Software Packages
4.1.1
Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package
The Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP) [97] is a computer program used for performing
ab initio quantum mechanical calculations of materials, particularly for the study of electronic
structures and related properties through DFT. VASP utilizes a plane-wave basis set and the
projector-augmented wave method (PAW) for solving the electronic structure problem, making it
capable of handling large and complex systems.
4.1.2
Atomic Simulation Environment
Atomic Simulation Environment (ASE) is a software package and Python library designed to
facilitate the setup, execution, and analysis of atomic-scale simulations. [98] The main purpose of
ASE is to simplify the process of setting up and running atomic-scale simulations by providing
a consistent interface for analyzing simulation results and the creation of streamlined workflows.
ASE supports multiple simulation codes, including VASP. In this project, the ASE package is used
to calculate the bandgap, bandstructure and density of states (DOS) of the structures.
4.1.3
Pydefects
Pydefects is a Python package designed for the analysis and characterization of point defects in
crystalline materials. [99] Drawing from functionalities in Pymatgen [100] (another powerful python
library for material analysis), it provides a set of tools and functionalities to study defects in
materials and thereby forms a useful assistance for researches to manage these types of calculations
(that are often time demanding, multifaceted and prone to human error) in an orderly manner.
Pydefects is fully compatible with VASP and is used in this project to verify the calculations done
‘by hand’ for Cs2AuIAuIIICl6.

4.2. Workflows
4.2
Workflows
4.2.1
SOC Relaxation
All structures are relaxed with spin-orbit coupling employed. The structure is first relaxed in a
collinear step. During this relaxation step, the magnetic moments are not considered yet and the
main objective of this calculation is to provide a well-approximated starting point for the magnetic
calculation.
In the collinear step, ionic relaxation using the PBEsol functional is employed. The search
directions for the (local) minima are determined by the forces and the stress tensors using the
RM-DIIS algorithm. The number of maximum ionic steps is set to 500 and the maximum number
of electronic steps is set to 128 (the minimum number of electronic self-consistent steps is set to 4).
The plane wave cutoff energy is set to 520 eV using Guassian smearing (σ=0.05) and a Γ-centered
k-mesh (the number of k-points is determined automatically). The unit cell is allowed to relax
using 3 DOF. The system is considered converged if the calculated energy difference between two
iterative electronic relaxation steps is smaller than 10−6 eV or smaller than 5 · 10−3 eV between
ionic relaxation steps.
The magnetic calculation, otherwise called the non-collinear step, is initiated after the collinear
relaxation step has converged. Because spin is considered in this calculation, specific parameters
have to be set. First of all, the number of bands is retrieved from the collinear relaxation and set to
twice this amount, since the Pauli exclusion principle states that no equal spins can occupy the same
band. Secondly, the magnetic moments are considered by setting the VASP setting to NCL=TRUE
and spin-orbit coupling is taken into account by setting LSORBIT=TRUE. Consequently, the
symmetry of the system is turned off. Setting ISPIN=2 allows spin-polarized calculations. For this
relaxation, the PBEsol functional is used and the minimisation algorithm is set to the conjugate
gradient algorithm. The number of maximum ionic steps is set to 240 and the maximum number
of electronic steps is set to 128 (the minimum number of electronic self-consistent steps is set to 4).
Just as in the collinear step, the plane wave cutoff energy is set to 520 eV using Gaussian smearing
(σ=0.05) and a Γ-centered k-mesh (the number of k-points is determined automatically). The unit
cell is allowed to relax using 3 DOF. The system is considered converged if the calculated energy
difference between two iterative electronic relaxation steps is smaller than 10−6 eV or smaller than
5 · 10−3 eV between ionic relaxation steps.
4.2.2
Bandstructure
To calculate the bandgap alone, using the ASE bandgap function usually suffices. The bandstruc-
ture calculation offers more information on the type of bandgap (i.e. direct or indirect), or can be
used to obtain more accurate bandgap values under higher k-point sampling.
GGA Bandstructure
The bandstructure calculation takes spin-orbit coupling into consideration and can be divided
into two calculations.
First, a self-consistent calculation is done, where the charge-density of
the material is calculated using the electronic charge density written in the CHGCAR file of the
previous GGA relaxation. This calculation results into a relaxed charge-density as well as the
number of bands that are employed in this calculation.
After this first self-consistent step, the charge-density and the number of bands for the non-
self-consistent band structure calculation, where the charge density is not altered anymore (i.e.
ICHARG=11). To increase the amount of sampling points at which the energy of each band is

HSE Bandstructure
The determination of a bandstructure employing a hybrid functional works differently from a
bandstructure using GGA as the hamiltonian can no longer be expressed in terms of the electronic
density alone (Equation 2.18). To restart a bandstructure calculation using a hybrid functional,
the WAVECAR file from a previously converged GGA relaxation is used instead (after a single
point calculation). Then the high-symmetry path is determined along which the band structure is
calculated, but this is done differently for hybrids than for GGA: an additional KPOINTS_OPT
file is created that generates zero-weight k-points at the high-symmetry path between the specified
point in the KPOINT file. In this way, a regular k-mesh and k-points are supplied to restart
the hybrid bandstructure calculation. The density of k points is set to 12 to ensure convergence
in the given amount of time. The charge density is not kept constant during the band structure
calculation, but instead ISTART is set to 1, so that the HSE band structure calculation is restarted
with constant energy cut-off, and orbitals are read from the previous WAVECAR file.

4.2. Workflows
4.2.4
Point Defect Formation Energy
The following subsection shows the workflow for point defect calculations. The following steps are
performed by hand in this thesis, but the Pydefects code is based on a similar automated workflow.
Supercell Creation
As described in subsection 2.3.5, a supercell approach must be used when executing point defect
calculations. For the Cs2AgInCl6 and CsPbBr3 supercells, 80 atoms are used in a 14.7×14.7×10.4
Å cell. For the Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 and CsPbBr3 supercells, 40 atoms are used in a 7.5×14.9×11.0 Å
and 11.7×11.7×11.7 Å cell respectively. All supercells are relaxed as described in subsection 4.2.1.
A supercell of 40 atoms is enough to perform reliable defect calculations and is often employed in
literature. [49,95]
Defect Creation
Defects are created in the supercell structure through removing (vacancies), replacing (antisites)
or adding atoms (interstitials) with ASE. For the interstitial defects, all the interstitials are placed
manually in the tetrahedral interstitial site. These structures are relaxed as described in subsec-
tion 4.2.1.
Charged Defects
Charged defects are created from a relaxed neutral defect structure by altering the NELECT
(number of electrons) parameter. In the case of a positively charged structure, the uncharged
NELECT parameter is retrieved and the new one will be a decreased NELECT value reflecting the
charge. For a negatively charged structure, the NELECT parameter is increased. The structure is
then relaxed as described in subsection 4.2.1.
Formation Energy Plot
Using chemical potentials sampled at specific points in the stability window of the target material,
the formation energy of each point defect is calculated using Equation 2.25 as a function of the fermi
level referenced against the VBM. To account for a shift in VBM for (charged) defect structures
compared to the perfect supercell (∆V in Equation 2.25), a total density of states calculation is
done for those charged defects that show a bandgap. Both the bandgap and DOS can be simply
calculated from the output of the relaxed structure through functions from the ASE package.
Referencing of the VBM occurs through the energetic position of the first core state, which is
assumed to be stable for perfect and defected structures.
Charge Transition Energy Plot
Where the formation energy of two differently charged states is equal, a charge transition level
(independent of µ) is found and plotted in the charge transition energy plot.

complete flowchart of the described calculation strategy is included in Figure 4.1.

4.2.5
Nudged Elastic Band
In CINEB calculations, a supercell of the same size as described in subsubsection 4.2.4 is used, since
an ionic hopping movement is simulated in DFT through the movement of a vacancy point defect.
In the CINEB calculation, intermediate structures are generated along the path. For well-defined
paths (straight movements in space) and/or halide ions, the number of intermediate steps is set to
1, while for other paths and/or ions, the number of steps is set to 3. For especially ill-defined paths,
where an ion moves around another ion, an intermediate state is forced on the system. An initial
and final structure are generated by removing ions at the places the ion is moving to from a fully
relaxed (subsection 4.2.1) perfect structure. The initial and final image are relaxed independently,
but this time allowing the structure to only relax the position of the atoms, but not the size of the
unit cell (ISIF=2).
It is important that the list number of other ions in the list remains constant in both structures,
as otherwise all ions in the structure would start moving and not just the hopping ion of interest.
To do so, the index of the moving ion is always placed last in the list, ensuring that the rest of the
list remains unaltered.
For each intermediate image, a calculator is created that relaxes the images using PBEsol
including SOC, using the same convergence criteria described in subsection 4.2.1. The activation
barrier is found through the FIRE local optimizer code using a convergence criterion of a maximum
force of 0.05 eV Å−1.

Chapter 5
Results and Discussion
Three material types have been computationally investigated on lattice characteristics, bandstruc-
ture, intrinsic point defect formation, ionic migration pathways and charge transition levels. One
of the studied structures, CsPbCl3, is known to be non self-healing, while the other two structures,
Cs2AgInCl6 and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6, are self-healing.
Although intrinsic point defect calculations
have been previously investigated for CsPbCl3 and Cs2AgInCl6, [48,51] the possible relation to
self-healing or non-self-healing properties have not been explored within the framework of DFT.
A defect studies on Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 has not been performed to date. Therefore, this set of cal-
culations forms in itself a valuable contribution to the knowledge of intrinsic properties of this
structure that could help experimentalists to reduce the formation of defects and provide insight
for the photoelectronic efficiency of the material. The aim is to combine the results for SH and
NSH structures and find fundamental differences in the creation and migration of defects to gain
an understanding of the possible mechanism of self-healing based on computational descriptors.
5.1
Crystal Structure
The structures of CsPbBr3, Cs2AgInCl6, and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 have been relaxed according to the
method described in subsection 4.2.1.
The crystal structures of the investigated materials are
presented in Figure 5.1.
The charge allocation on ions in CsPbBr3 and Cs2AgInCl6 will not pose any problems being
described by DFT, as the ions are only present in one oxidation state. However, this is not the
case for Cs2AuIAuIIICl6, where the present Au ions can either take on charge +1 or +3. The
question arises whether DFT is able to describe this charge difference and the impact on the direct
surrounding between the two differently charged Au ions. As described by Dalpian et al. (2018),
the different local environment in Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 (i.e. the periodic presence of Au+ and Au3+ ions
that adhere to a different oxidation state) can be well described by bond geometries alone, since
the physical charge density is unchanged around the B-site ion. [102] This phenomenon is called the
‘charge self-regulating response’ and it originates from the rehybridization of B-ions and X-ions in
order to prevent the formation of highly charged cations through the X-to-B charge transfer. [103]
In other words: the structure is described as ‘bond disproportionated’ instead of ‘charge ordered’.
The fact that different polyhedral geometries can be identified after relaxation, indicates that
these sites are occupied by Au+ and Au3+ and not just Au2+. Since Au+ is the largest ion of
the two, this ion will take the site with the largest polyhedral volume. Yellow polyhedrons have a
volume of 23.2 Å3, while the white polyhedrons have a volume of 26.6 Å3. Therefore, it is assumed
that Au+ occupies the white octahedrals, while Au3+ resides in the yellow octahedrals.

5.4. Point Defect Formation Energy
5.4
Point Defect Formation Energy
After the stability windows have been analysed for the three structures, the formation energy of
several point defects has been calculated as described in subsection 4.2.4. This formation energy
offers a better understanding of the extent of photodegradation in the structures and serves as a
starting point for the search of a plausible self-healing mechanism. When point defects are formed
very easily (i.e. have a very low or even negative formation enthalpy), we assume them to be
present in the system irrespective of whether the structure has been irradiated or not. The point
defects that could be induced by irradiation, however, are the point defects that have a formation
energy of a couple of eV. This is within the order of magnitude of the photon energy originating
emitted by the sun. For understanding self-healing properties, it is therefore not only important
to consider the readily available point defect that have the lowest formation energy, but also those
point defects that could contribute significantly to the defect concentration once the structure has
been irradiated and photodegradation has occurred.

The formation energy of defects in Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 can again be used to deduce possible pho-
todegradation paths. Within the formation energy region that could be achieved through solar
irradiation (1-3 eV), not only Au3+ seems to be an attractive antisite spot, but Au+ as well. Inter-
estingly, it becomes apparent that the formation of interstitial sites is more likely after irradiation of
the structure. The same is the case for the self-healing structure Cs2AgInCl6. The behaviour of the
VCl defect also shows similarities between the two structures, in the sense that the generation of the
defect is more plausibly evoked by irradiation, than being inherently present in the structure. The
role of the halide vacancy should be further investigated through ionic migration. Like Cs2AgInCl6,
Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 is expected to contain a high concentration of vacancy point defects and antisite
defects (involving species taking the place of Au3+). Photo-degradation could increase the number
of point defects by introducing interstitials and the halide vacancy. This defected structure would
then form the starting point of a self-healing process. Since Cs2AuIAuIIICl6 shows self-healing
properties as well, it is expected that antisites and vacancies could either be resolved easily to
regenerate the pristine structure, or offer a pathway for other migrating species.

5.4. Point Defect Formation Energy
5.4.4
Differences in Point Defect Formation Energy between Self-Healing
and Non Self-Healing Structures
From comparing the activation energy of ionic migration in non-self-healing structures (CsPbBr3)
and self-healing structures (Cs2AgIncl6 and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6) multiple observations can be made:
(1) In the non-self-healing structure, the interstitial point defects have a lower formation
energy than the self-healing structures and are likely present regardless of the structure being
exposed to (sun)light. Instead, the formation of interstitial defects can be provoked by irradiation
in the self-healing structures, since the formation energy of these defects falls within the photon
energy emitted from the sun.
If this process occurs, and can be easily resolved through ionic
migration (while non self-healing structures always inherently contain interstitial defects due to
the low formation energy) a possible explanation to self-healing mechanics can be proposed. The
involvement of the halide interstitials for self-healing mechanisms has been previously studied by
Ceratti et al.
(2018), where self-healing properties were linked directly to the stability of the
distortion of the lattice upon the introduction of Bri . [66] From the defect studies performed in
this thesis, it is likely that self-healing properties can not only be attributed to one specific defect
and its stability in the lattice under given circumstances (like tested by Ceratti et al. (2018)), but
most probably also involve the migration of ions to reverse the generation of defects indefinitely.
(2) Not only is there a difference between the inherent abundance of interstitial defects, a
contrast with the halide vacancy point defect formation energy between SH and NSH structures is
observed. In the non self-healing structure CsPbBr3, the formation enthalpy of the halide vacancy
is higher than in the self-healing structures Cs2AgInCl6 and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6. If the ionic migration
activation barrier for halide vacancy migration is not low enough to resolve the defects introduced
by irradation in CsPbBr3, the structure is irreversibly destructed. On the other hand, if instead
the halide vacancy point defect is resolved easily in both structures, this point defect might in itself
play no active role in the self-healing properties of AIHPs, but rather a passive role, where the
vacancies in the self-healing structures are mainly used as an open pathway for other migrating
species in the structure.
(3) Although mainly halides are considered in a plethora of articles concerning defects and
migration in HPs, our point defect formation energy plot shows that is probable in all structures
that the A-ion vacancy Cs+ are more abundant than halide vacancies. This could indicate that in
addition to halide-assisted self-healing mechanisms, Cs+ migration could play a role in vacancy site
hopping and (as discussed in point 2) be one of the predominant indicators of self-healing processes.
Although the point defect formation energy calculations show interesting results and suggest start-
ing points for the search for an ionic migration pathway, now is a good point to raise a concern for
the validity of the found formation energy of defects. As voiced in subsection 2.2.7, the bandgap
problem poses limitations to the accuracy of DFT-obtained results. As shown in Equation 2.25,
the point defect formation energy is calculated through data that is directly derived from the total
energy of a system after relaxation, and therefore does not require information on the band gap.
The problem arises in the last ‘∆V ’ term, where the position of the VBM in the defected structure
should be retrieved. Of course, when the bandgap closes completely through the introduction of
a point defect, no VBM can be identified and the position of charge transition states can not be
determined accurately, since no ∆V term can be computed. When this is the case, the formation
energy of the charged state is not plotted, which means that within the bandgap energy range, the
calculated formation energy of defects is not correctly estimated. HSE bandstructure calculations
are required to open up the bandgap and extract the VBM. The DOS for the charged states that
show a bandgap are included in the Appendix.

5.5. Ion Migration Pathway
5.5
Ion Migration Pathway
After having investigated point defect formation energies, the ion migration pathway is further ex-
plored. Although interstitial-site hopping could offer an interesting insight in migration properties
(as seen in section 5.4). We decide to focus on the vacancy medaited pathway, taking the work of
Khalfin et al. (2022) [79] as inspiration in our approach where a self-healing mechanism in AIHP
structures could be observed through the out-diffusion of voids. The diffusion of A and X-site
ions in the three AIHP structures through the vacancy mediated mechanism is investigated. Not
only will this approach give insight in the migration of positively charged species (which, to the
best of our knowledge, has not been investigated for all of these structures before), but it will also
supply knowledge on a wider scope of possible paths. Through the assumption that these diffusion
mechanisms can take place in a repeated manner, the long-range diffusion pathways that lead to
long-distance self-healing will be better understood.
5.5.1
Ionic Migration in CsPbBr3
For CsPbBr3, 3 possible ion migration paths are identified. Since the lattice is symmetrical in
3 dimensions, the movement of these paths can be projected on all possible axes. Path 1 is the
movement of the Cs+ ion, where a Cs+ ion moves to the nearest neighbour Cs+-vacancy. Path
2 and 3 both describe the diffusion of a Br– ion. In pathway 2, the Br– ion moves from one
perovskite octahedral to the next, while on path 3 the Br– ion moves across the octahedral edges.
Although pathways for the diffusion of Pb2+ through the vacancy mediated hopping mechanism
exist, the calculation of the activation energy did not converge in time. The pathways for Cs+ and
Br– are visualised in Figure 5.16c, d and e. Figure 5.16a and b show the activation energies of the
vacancy hopping through the respective pathways, calculated through CINEB.
From Figure 5.16, it becomes apparent that the activation energy of the Br– ion migra-
tion (0.353 eV for path 2 and 0.145 eV for path 3) is lower than the migration energy of Cs+
0.448 eV). This result is in correspondence with literature on halide perovskites, where the halide
ion is the most loosely bound in the lattice and shows the lowest migration activation energy
amongst other vacancy mediated migration mechanisms. [62,86,89,113] Existing literature mentions
a vacancy-mediated hopping mechanism in CsPbBr3 with an activation energy of 0.692 or 0.80
eV for Cs+ and 0.286 or 0.51 eV for Br–. [91,114] In these particular articles, only the equivalent
of path 3 from this thesis are considered for halide movement and the crystal structure contains
tilted octahedrals (i.e. it is not a symmetrical cubic system, but orthorhombic), which could be
the root for slight differences between the calculated formation energies.
The trend shown in literature that Br– migration has a lower activation energy than Cs+ mi-
gration, is consistent. The difference in energy between path 2 and 3 in itself also shows interesting
characterics of ionic migration in bulk. The distance equivalent of Br– moving according to path
2, can be described as a double-hop of path 3. Even after taking into consideration that a double
activation energy is required for this step, the activation energy of this double step hopping (0.290
eV) is lower than a direct hopping through the interstitial space (0.353 eV), rather than along
octahedral edges. This indicates that the preferred mechanism occurs through site-to-site hopping
along the octahedral edges, where the Br– ions never lose interaction with the Pb2+ ions in the
center of the octahedral.
Br– migration having a lower activation energy than Cs+ migration, can have implications
for the photo degradation mechanism and self-healing properties. From a photodegradation point
of view, it could mean that once initial point defects are created, the Br– ion moves around the
lattice more easily than Cs+ and has a higher chance of diffusion far from the site where the point
defect was initially created. At the same time, from a self-healing point of view, it could at the

octahedral (path 4).

the initial and final

moves from an axial position on the top of the Au1+ octahedral to an axial position on the bottom
of the Au3+ octahedral, the energy pathway is not symmetrical.
Because this structure has not been studied in literature before, either experimentally or com-
putationally, the activation energy of hopping ions can not be compared to previous data. The
different activation energies can however be compared amongst each other. It can be seen that
the asymmetry of the system causes the two distinct pathways to differ in activation energy. The
movement of Cs+ ions in the ab-plane (0.505 eV) is much preferred over the movement of Cs+ in
the c-direction (1.621 eV). Therefore, Cs+ diffusion through the vacancy-mediated mechanism is
thought to occur in only one direction. This could mean that, just as was the case in Cs2AgInCl6,
that photodegradation will occur in a more orderly manner, which eases the self-healing process.

effective long-range migration of ions can only occur through a linking network of the investigated
Cl– pathway. This results into a seemingly unlikely energetic pathway for Cl– compared to Cs+.

5.5.4
Differences in Ionic Migration between Self-Healing and Non Self-
Healing Structures
From comparing the activation energy of ionic migration in non-self-healing structures (CsPbBr3)
and self-healing structures (Cs2AgIncl6 and Cs2AuIAuIIICl6) multiple observations can be made:
(1) Once a second distinct B-ion gets introduced, the symmetry of the system is broken and
halide ions start having clearly preferred pathways around the distinct B-ions, (2) the pathways
for Cl– are generally higher in energy than the pathways for Br– (3) The B-ions are generally
well-contained and stable in the octahedrals, making it difficult in all structures to move the B-ion
around. Based on the absence of results due to time consuming and computationally expensive
CINEB calculation, no justifiable conclusions can be made on the involvement of B-ions in a
possible self-healing mechanism.
Like mentioned before, the creation of distinct electronic environments that could reversibly
‘trap’ diffusing species in the bulk lattice rather than collecting them at grain boundary interfaces,
could offer an explanation for self-healing properties found in a selection of double perovskites.
At this point though, the formation energy of point defects has not been considered in the ionic
migration analysis. In self-healing processes, both phenomena are of importance, as in the first
place inevitable photodegradation will take place by the formation of point defects, which is followed
by self-healing, where these defects find their original place in the lattice through ionic migration.

From Figure 5.11-Figure 5.14, the point defects that show the lowest formation energy over the

Research into trap states in Cs2AgInCl6 conducted by Li et al. (2018) shows that VIn, CsIn, InAg
and AgIn are the most prominent deep state creators. The charge transition levels in AgCl has not
been investigated. The results vary significantly from the results obtained in this thesis as the other
mentioned deep states show charge transition levels well within the CB in Figure 5.20b. Kang et
al. (2018) mentions the InAg defect to be of most significance in In-poor, Cl-rich conditions, since
the formation energy of this defect is then sufficiently low to increase the concentration and the
number of trap states. [48] However, this is the only defect with low enough energy to do that, and
it is therefore expected that the solar harvesting efficiency of Cs2AgInCl6 is not inherently and by
default destined to generate low efficiencies.
Just as what was the case for CsPbBr3, the results shown in Figure 5.20 provide limited
information due to inaccuracies originating from the band gap problem. This affects both the
position of the charge transition level in relation to the band edges, as well as to what extent the
VBM in the charged defected structure can be probed. As mentioned before, the experimental
bandgap of Cs2AgInCl6 is 3.23 eV. [105] We can not predict how the charge transition levels will
change when the bandgap is opened up correctly.
In addition to those faults, some charge levels that get measured, might not be real transition
level that we can expect to exist in the material. Defects like VIn, AgIn and ClAg show a charge
transfer level from 0 to directly either -3 or -4. However, it us more likely that the charge changes
from 0 charge to -1, -2, -3 and -4 in a stepwise motion, rather than an immediate jump. This
means that the charge transition level that is shown in the Figure forms at a formation energy that
is higher than the true formation energy (as an example, the transition point (-1,1) in Figure 2.9
shows such a situation). Therefore, the results of this charge transition state analysis need further
refinement and confirmation using HSE+SOC.

Conclusions
In this thesis, computational-level self-healing descriptors for halide perovskite structures are in-
vestigated in their ability to describe and understand self-healing characteristics. Self-healing (SH)
and non self-healing (NSH) structures, were investigated on their intrinsic defect formation energy
and ionic migration activation energy in a vacancy-mediated pathway. In addition, the photoelec-
tronic efficiency of the explored structures is examined. The four research questions posed in the
introduction have been investigated and can be answered.
First of all, it has been found that the Goldschmidt tolerance factor can serve as an indication
of the stability of a structure, and therefore predict the ability of a structure to self-heal. The
investigated SH structures show higher Goldschmidt tolerance factors than the NSH structure,
that in return is likely to exhibit difficulty in returning from a defected state to the pristine state.
In addition, defect formation studies have proven several differences between SH and NSH
structures. First of all, photodegradation is likely to induce interstitial defects and halide vacancies
in SH structures, while these types of defects already inherently exist ( and therefore likely harder
to resolve) in NSH structures. Additionally, the low formation energy of vacancies indicate that
they are likely involved in ionic movement by facilitating a pathway for different species.
Ionic migration calculations using CINEB show that SH structures have preferred pathways
for ionic species, in contrast to the investigated symmetrical NSH structure, which is due to the
presence of two distinct B-ions that create a different local environment. It is hypothesised that
the double halide character plays a role in the lattice stability by trapping migrating species and
preventing them from drifting to grain boundaries. It is also found that the migration energy for
Cl is generally higher than the migration energy of Br, despite smaller in size, which is thought to
also be a consequence of the highly asymmetrical structure of the investigated SH structures.
Upon combining the defect formation energy and ionic migration pathways, it can be seen
that no distinct difference between healing time can be found in the investigated vacancy hopping
mechanism. This leads us to believe that other hopping mechanisms, that allow the migration of
species through other sites to play a role in the self-healing and out-diffusion of voids. Although it
can be concluded that the migration and healing of vacancies due to halide species does not form a
time-determining step, no conclusion can be made on the involvement of the A (although present
data suggest this) or B ion in determining the SH rate, due to incomplete convergence.
Finally, it has been shown that the limitation on bandgap determination using DFT poses
complications in the determination of charge transition states.
Preliminary results show that
Cs2AuIAuIII could suffer from low photoelectronic efficiency due to the presence of deep trap states
caused by easily induced intrinsic defects, while CsPbBr3 and Cs2AgInCl6 show more promising
results. To obtain accurate band gap edges, HSE calculations should be performed.
The results of this thesis form a starting point for the continued search for computational self-
healing descriptors. Different strategies are discussed in the outlook.

Outlook
Suggestions of possible future developments of this work are either based on (1) overcoming chal-
lenges and using more accurate methods to obtain more descriptive results (i.e. refining the work)
or (2) expanding the current research to find common self-healing characteristics in a larger control
group, searching for more material characteristics.
The bandgap problem in DFT has shown complications throughout this project in the determi-
nation of bandgap edges, which is crucial in both point defect calculations, as well as for the
determination of charge transition levels. Performing a hybrid functional calculation using a pre-
converged GGA calculation as a starting point, could open up the bandgap enough to investigate
the position of the valence band maximum. Additionally, a more streamlined workflow should
be implemented in the creation of a supercell and defected structures for defect calculations. It
is preferred that the supercell has a cubic structure to ensure minimised periodic interaction be-
tween induced defects. Additionally, as we have found that insterstitial defects could play a role in
the healing mechanism in SH structures, the placement of interstitials in the lattice should occur
through a more refined method such as voronoi analysis, rather than placement by hand. Further-
more, it should be investigated how capable DFT is of supplying accurate results for systems such
as Cs2AuIAuIIICl6, where the physical charges on the B-site ions mainly remain unchanged due
to the charge self-regulating response, in doing point defect calculations.
Possible future developments of the present work include the extension to an automated work-
flow such as Pydefects, for which the foundation is laid in this thesis. An automated workflow
allows for a fast search through a wider range of structures and the investigation on the presence of
a general trend within self-healing structures. This wider range of structures would ideally consist
of OIHP alongside AIHP, since these are known to self-heal as well. Furthermore, different ionic
migration pathways should be investigated such as interstitial hopping, but also the migration of
species to an antisite position. In this work, only the vacancy-hopping mechanism is discussed, but
the low formation energy of antisite defects suggest that antisite hopping is a plausible mechanism
as well. One of the bottlenecks in this thesis is the long computational time a CINEB calculation
requires. To enable high throughput material discovery, the time consuming CINEB calculations
could be replaced with a surrogate model that is capable of finding the transition state, greatly
reducing the number of DFT calculations needed. This method has been proven successful for
antiperovskite structures, where Li+, Na+ and Mg2+ are the mobile species. [116] Machine learning
could also aid in predicting a transition state, enabling faster material sampling. [117]
