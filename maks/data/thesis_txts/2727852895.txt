Abstract
Monitoring the condition of the PV modules, and detecting failures on time, are one of
the main tasks in the operation and maintenance of a utility­scale PV park. Traditional
methods rely mainly on visual inspections and the monitoring of the maximum power point
of the array, whereas the full current­voltage (I­V) characteristic of the array can provide
much more diagnostic information. The potential of using inverter­measured I­V curves
to assess the condition of the PV arrays is investigated in this thesis.
The impact of different affections (shading, PID, cracked cells, and high series resistance)
on the I­V curves was researched, and the results were used to identify these affections on
utility­scale PV parks. The advantage of this method is the possibility to discern, out of a
power loss, what faults might be affecting the array, compared to standard MPP tracking.
Also, the possibility to make remote measurements, covering a whole PV park in minutes,
defines a huge advantage compared to visual inspection and imaging methods.
The weaknesses that this method presents are the need for several measurements until
a string of PV modules can be categorized as faulty, and the uncertainty of the meteo­
rological conditions that each module is undergoing at the time of the measurement. It
is also not possible with the use of this method, to address the specific fault, unless an
on­site inspection of the affected array is performed.
Overall, the results suggest that using inverter­measured I­V curves as a condition mon­
itoring method is advantageous, performing well on fault detection, since it was able to
identify a few faulty PV strings out of three commercial utility­scale PV parks from Euro­
pean Energy.

1
Introduction
According to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), the world has
commissioned a total capacity of 257 GW of renewable energy in 2021, out of which 133
GW was solar energy. Photovoltaic (PV) systems currently make up a total capacity of
843 GW, being the 2nd biggest renewable energy source by capacity, 19 GW more than
wind energy, and only exceeded by hydropower in capacity. Of those 843 GW installed
PV, 65% of it was deployed within the last 5 years [5]. With this many PV parks worldwide,
there are many different operational and maintenance procedures depending on location,
size, resources, and technology available [6].
With most of the PV installed in large utility­scale parks, it is necessary to have an
overview of the park’s production. For example, Bhadla Solar Park in India is the largest
park taking up around 57 km2 containing over 10 million panels, and with a capacity of
2.3 GW. This means that checking the status of each module adds up to be a huge task,
and if faults are left unattended, they can lead to a significant power loss and a revenue
loss for the park owner [7].
Utility­scale PV parks are constantly being affected by several faults and failures.
Although most of the lost power usually comes from failures in the alternating current (AC)
part of the plant, there is still a considerable portion of failures and degradation processes
­ around 25% ­ present on the direct current (DC) part [8].
Current failure prevention and detection methods rely heavily on human resources
and visual inspection of the modules [6]. These methods have a huge impact on the cost
of operating the PV plants and, therefore, on the final levelized cost of energy (LCOE). In
modern PV plants, where some reaching over one million modules, operation and main­
tenance tasks become an activity that demands an increasing amount of time and re­
sources.
Consequently, there is a need for automatic failure detection methods to cover the
DC section of the PV parks. Modern inverters can be used for performance analyses
of the strings connected. This is done by applying different voltages to the strings and
measure the generated current, creating a current­voltage (I­V) curve for each string.
These inverter­measured I­V curves of the PV arrays could be used to generate a failure
detection method, that can identify some of the possible failures affecting a PV park, and
by doing so, avoiding the park operator from performing costly activities.
1.1
Project Objectives
The main objective of this project is to investigate the capabilities, advantages, and
disadvantages of inverter­based I­V monitoring for operating and maintaining a utility­
scale PV park. Furthermore, it is to understand the different failure mechanisms and
existing methods which are crucial to properly analyze the opportunities given by this
method.
As the end result of this project, it is intended to develop and implement a validated
method using inverter­based I­V measurements to detect faults on the PV arrays in DTU’s
PV park.
The objectives of this thesis are summarized in the following list:

1.2
Project limitations
During the realization of this thesis, several limitations were faced that shaped the
end result being presented. Multiple activities were planned (and some of them even
performed) that, in the end, had no deterministic result.
One of the main activities was the deployment of the indoor­tested modules at DTU PV
park. Due to delays in the installation, the new modules were never deployed outdoors.
With those modules being installed, it would have been possible to recreate string level
measurements in a controlled environment, on the same modules that have been tested
on the module level. This would have meant that the impact of the different failures could
be analyzed and better understood on the string level, strengthening the results of our
condition monitoring method.
Another limitation encountered was during a set of experiments performed with the
I­V tracer. Since the error of the instrument being used is 2% of the power output, it
was not possible to determine a power loss of 3% like the one presented on the PID­
affected modules. The error from the instrument, combined with the uncertainty given
by the correction methods (when trying to correct for irradiance and temperature), made
indistinguishable the difference between the affected and the non­affected modules. The
obtained results of the measurements performed on the cracked modules were also not
consistent nor interesting to further investigate.
Along with these challenges, there was no available resistor that would be safe to
connect on a string level at DTU. Having this equipment would have allowed us to make
string­level measurements on added resistance scenarios, instead of simulating them. A
similar situation was faced with the cracked cell modules in string­level measurements. By
the time we were performing string­level measurements on cracked modules, the power
loss of the available cracked modules was not big enough to be distinguishable. It was
not until later that a module for further cracking was available and interesting results could
have been obtained.
Finally, some limitations were faced when analyzing the data from European Energy’s
PV parks. It was intended to further analyze the strings that showed signs of underper­
forming on the first round of measurements. The planned measurements for that analysis
were planned, but the needed inverters were offline, and therefore measurements could
not be triggered. Also, it was intended at first to be able to physically attend the PV parks
to address the underperforming strings, but time constraints made it not possible.

2
State of the art of PV failures and
condition monitoring
This chapter will give an overview of the state of the art of PV module failures, op­
eration and maintenance procedures, condition monitoring, and fault detection methods
used both in the field and in indoor laboratory facilities. The focus will be on monofacial
and bifacial mono­/multi­crystalline silicon modules.
A PV module failure occurs in the system on the module/string level on the DC side. To
distinguish between if it is a failure or not, there will be used the definition of a PV module
failure presented in report [9] from 2014 by the International Energy Agency (IEA): A PV
module failure is either an effect that:
• (1) degrades the module power, which is not reversed by the normal operation
• (2) creates a safety issue
Failures can occur under the normal operating conditions the module experience.
The local environment or a mishandling causing a problem would not be considered a
PV module failure by the definition stated above. This means that a lighting strike, soiling
and shading is not a failure. However, failures caused by heavy snow load will be seen as
a failure because the PV modules are designed to withstand that to a certain degree[9].
A fault is an error within the PV system including electrical circuits, inverters, and
transformer stations. Animal bites are not a PV fault, but can cause a fault in the circuit.
The faults are important to discover to prevent safety issues and power loss. Faults and
failures on the AC side will not be described in this thesis [9].
Failures in a PV module can be divided into two subcategories: degradation and
sudden occurring failures/infant failures. The degradation of the PV modules are losses
due to failures in the materials occurring throughout the lifetime. This degradation can be
either mechanical or electrical.
PV module manufacturers usually state a degradation rate in the respective datasheet
as around 0.45­0.5 % annual linear power attenuation from the stated power output for the
module under standard test conditions (STC irradiance 1000W/m2 and a cell temperature
of 25◦C) [Appendix C]. There is an exemption on typical 2% in the first years of operating
due to Light­induced degradation (LID) [2]. LID is a known degradation failure occuring
in crystalline silicon modules, which the manufacturer has to take into account as it is
defined in standard EN 50380 or stated in the datasheet [9]. LID is therefore not covered
in this thesis.
The warranty from the manufacturers is depending on the stated degradation rate,
which is a prediction. PV modules live up to the accelerated stress test IEC 61215 stan­
dard qualification tests. The actual degradation rate will depend on the climate and in­
stallation of the module and be different from a linear degradation rate as stated in the
datasheet [10].
A thorough analytical review done by D.C. Jordan et al. [2] state that the majority of
the studied mono facial PV had a degradation rate under 1 % / year and mono­crystalline
silicon and multi­crystalline silicon had an average of 0.36%/year and 0.65%/year respec­
tively. For bifacial C­si PV, the degradation rate is typically under 1%/year [11].

2.1.2
Electrical failures
Burn marks/hot spot
Burn marks are a failure related to the presence of a hot spot. The burn marks can be
both on the front cells or the back sheet/rear cells. The hot spots occur because one solar
cell produces less current than others in the string. This could be because other failure
types have materialized as broken interconnect ribbons or other metallic interconnections.
A hot spot can also be caused by a cell or a part of a cell being forced into reversed
bias. This could be from partial shading because of bird drops, vegetation, non­uniform
soiling, or cracked cell. This could lead to a burn mark or discoloration.
Under certain circumstances, they can create an arc, which can lead to a fire. This
could, for example, happen if both a bypass failure and the interconnect ribbons failed.
That is why this failure is placed in the safety category [B(f,e,m)]. For a power loss due to
burn marks other failures need to be present as well. In some cases, the power loss will
be neglectable, and therefore the power loss category [D][E] [9].
PV Module Connectors failure
Quick connectors are used to electrically connect PV modules to a PV array. These
connectors can fail due to corrosion in the connector, or it can occur as infant failures

while stored in the field before being applied or degrade over time. This can create arcing
and cause a total power loss in the string if the connectors are not connected completely.
The connectors can be exposed to a sudden occurring fault. The insulation can degrade
which can create faults in the string. Safety category [B(f,e,m] and power category [G]
[9][12].
Bypass diode failure
Bypass diodes reduce the power loss during partial shading by bypassing the current
from that part of the circuit. They are also used to avoid reverse biasing of a sub­string
with negative voltage for protecting the individual cells. The bypass diodes fail in most
cases by energy over stress or in minor cases by cables without clamping. The standard
is three bypass diodes per module. If the bypass diodes fail, it can cause hot spots and
therefore browning, burn marks, or fire. Defect bypass diodes are difficult to detect with
a visual inspection. Safety category [B] and Power category [A][E][9][13].
Busbars, cell­ and string interconnect ribbons, and metallic corrosion
A PV module consists of multiple solar cells connected together. The solar cells con­
tain several fingers connected to busbars which are connected to the next solar cell with
a ribbon from each busbar. The purpose of these four elements of the PV module is to
collect the generated current and move it around through the system, and consists of
conductor materials.
The disconnection of the cells and string interconnect ribbons can be caused by sev­
eral factors. The most common is due to poor soldering during the production process. It
can also be caused by physical stress during transport or deformation during production.
During long­term operations of the PV system, the soldered joint can slowly degrade due
to natural thermal cycles and hot spots, or combined failures as delamination.
If this failure occurs, high power losses can be experienced, up to almost half the nor­
mal output (depending on how many ribbons are disconnected from the cells). It can also
lead to other failures as burning marks through hot spots and overstress of the bypass
diode which can fail. The standard detection methods are electroluminescence imaging
(EL imaging) and Infrared/Thermal imaging (IR imaging). EL imaging utilizes the light
emitted by a solar cell when injected with an electrical current to detect non­uniform cur­
rent. IR imaging can be used to distinguish localized heat and local bright spots in the
PV module, meaning that a failure is occurring. EL and IR imaging are described more
thoroughly in section 2.4.1. The safety category is B(f,g,m), and power category [E] [9].
These metallic electrical components can corrode over time being exposed to the
environment. The corrosion can be accelerated, especially when water is present. This
would lead to a power loss because of an increase in the series resistance [1].
Potential­Induced Degradation (PID)
Over the last decade, there has been a rising interest in PID failure in PV modules
because of the implementation of strings with higher voltage and inverters capable of han­
dling this. PID occurs more often in systems with high voltage. Modern string layout reach
up to 1500 V. There are different causes of PID for different types of module technologies.
Overall it is a degradation that presents in the panels there are exposed to high voltage
and the module frame is grounded. This potential difference causes leakage currents to
travel between the frame and the solar cells. The cells closer to the negative pole are
more exposed to PID. This failure is not visual, but can be detected through IR imaging,
EL imaging, or module I­V curve because of a decrease in the cell shunt resistance. It
does not have any influence on safety, therefore, it is considered a category A. The power
loss can be difficult to detect especially at high irradiation and on utility­scale parks. The

2.1.3
Mechanical failures
Cell cracks and snail trails
C­Si wafers are very thin and fragile. That means that under production and mounting
the panels should be handled with caution, or else the cells can crack even underneath
the glass. It can also occur during harsh weather like moisture and freezing temperatures.
Cell cracks can be very small or on the whole cell level. The power loss from the cells
can therefore be very difficult to estimate since it depends on the orientations, distribution,
dimensions, and number of cracks. Usually, power loss is estimated from the size of the
non­active area of the cell, and can have a module impact of up to 22% [16]. In most
cases, the cracks are not visual to the eye. Therefore EL images of the cell are needed.
Different types of cell cracks can be sub­categorized. However, this will not be described
in this report. It does not have any impact on safety therefore category A and power will
degrade in steps over time, hence the cracks can evolve during thermal stress throughout
their lifetime and therefore degrade more, which is category E [9].
Snail tracks are a grey/black discoloration of the silver paste of the front metalliza­
tion of the screen­printed solar cells. They occur usually where there are invisible cell
cracks, and the discoloration is visible. Depending on seasons and environment it occurs
3 months to 1 year after installation. It is the cracks themselves that lead to the power
loss [9].
Junction box failure
The junction box is attached to the backside of each module, where the connection of
the string is connected to the cables, and the bypass diodes are placed. The junction box
protects the above­mentioned from the environment. The junction box can fail in different
ways, and one of them is infant failure because of a poor manufacturing process, such
as a bad fixing of the adhesive to the backside. If moisture enters the box it can cause
corrosion of the connections and string connections. Bad wiring can cause arcing and,
as a result, lead to a fire [9]. The power loss of these different failures is difficult to state
because it can cause other failures and cause complete power loss in the module. The
Safety category [B(f)] and power category [F][G].
Delamination
The adhesion between the glass, encapsulant, solar cells, and back layers can fail.
This will, in many cases, end with delamination, which will be followed by moisture and
corrosion in the active layer. This is caused by environmental factors or glass contam­
ination before being sampled. This failure can be visual both on the front side and the
backside. It can be purely aesthetic, or if it is in front of the optic layers and the cell, it
can cause up to 4% power loss and be the root cause for other failures, as already men­
tioned. This failure is in power category D and safety category A [9]. This failure type is

Encapsulant Failure
The encapsulant, which is placed in front or behind solar cells, can discolor. Some
encapsulant materials will be more prone to this than others. The most used for C­si
wafers is ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) which is prone to discoloration. This failure is also
called the browning of EVA. In 2014 EVA discoloration could be expected to contribute to
an annual degradation of 0.5%. This failure is in power category [E] and safety category
[A] [9].
Frame/Module breakage
The frame or the whole module can break as an infant failure (e.g. a breakage that
occurs under transport) when the module has been exposed to too much stress, tension,
pressure, or just because of bad handling during mounting. It can also present during
harsh weather, strong storms where the module is torn completely off and results in a
complete power loss of that string, or it could be a heavy snowfall there under melting can
make a torque effect on the frame, which can lead to other failures. This failure doesn’t
go under the definition stated at the beginning of section 2, but a detection failure method
should detect if a module is completely turned off and there is no power in the string [9].
Glass Breakage and Glass Corrosion
During extreme weather, like hail storms, for example, there is a chance that the
glass of the module break. This would worsen the optical properties and, therefore, less
irradiation penetrating the solar cells. The less irradiation leads, in the end, to a power
loss. If a module glass is broken before mounting, it is expected to be replaced before
the commissioning date. The anti­reflective coating and the transparency or structure of
the glass can degrade or corrode. This is not a safety issue but can create a power loss
in category [E] [9].
2.1.4
PV Array faults
When several modules are connected together, forming a PV array, electrical faults
can occur on the circuit within the array. Although the previously mentioned faults were
mainly faults on the PV modules, they can cause a mismatch in the string and affect the
output. Array faults are related to the connections within the array, and they can be open
circuits, line­to­line, line­to­ground, or arc faults.
Open circuit faults take place when there is a bad connection through the electrical
circuit, which can be some of the failures described as corroded connectors, junction box
failures, or broke cells. They have a low impact and are rare [12].
Line­to­ground faults occur when a current­carrying conductor drops to the ground
or comes in contact with a noncurrent conductor, for example, the PV module frame or
mounting racks [12].
Line­to­line faults occur when there is an accidental short circuit between two points
in the PV array at a different potential. This fault is uncommon but has a high impact and
is hard to detect [18].
Arc faults can materialize with insulation breakdown (parallel arc fault) or when there
is a discontinuity in the current­carrying conductors (CCC) (serial arc fault). As described
earlier, the serial arc fault can occur due to other failures among corroded conductors and
ribbon joints. This fault is rare but has a high fire risk and is difficult to detect [18].

2.1.5
Soiling and Shading losses
Soiling and shading are not a failure from the definition in 2. However, there is a
power loss that it is worth to be able to detect. Soiling loss can occur and vary during
the year, especially during dry seasons. This is only temporary since the panels can be
cleaned. Shading, on the other hand, can be more constant and mainly depends on the
sun’s angle and azimuth. There are some shading problems that are temporary as well,
here is to mention partial shading from vegetation, both far or near shading factors, where
they would vary through the year and can be reduced with maintenance. These two loss
types have to be mentioned as well and contribute to a power loss that can be avoided.
From the thesis vision, it is also essential to distinguish between shading, soiling, and
other fault types. Some areas with low precipitation and dust can experience high annual
soiling losses of up to 5% to 20%. Biological soiling as bird drops, leaves, and algae,
can have a partial shading effect on the module, contributing to the chance of burn marks
caused by hot spots [1]. This is why it appears as a sub­tree in the fault tree Figure 2.1.
2.1.6
Future tendencies of PV failures
Since the first silicon PV module was developed and commercialized to where it is
today, some failures and problems have been solved, and others have decreased sig­
nificantly. Therefore, some of the current failures are expected to occur less often in the
future. However, some failures are likely to occur more often than others, and with new
module types and technologies, new failures and the frequency of certain failures should
be expected [17].
The reason for failures to decrease in frequency or be solved completely resulted
from standard tests for commercial PV modules. This is because when there are tests to
pass, it enhances the technology to evolve. By addressing the failures, some might be
prevented earlier. Below, a list of failures likely to occur less in the future is shown [17].
• I. Corrosion of cells, metal, and connectors is likely to occur less frequently be­
cause of the 1000 hours of damp and heat exposure tests of modules. The test was,
at the invention time in 1994, difficult to pass. That resulted in the development of
the EVA and improved front metal pastes. This led to almost eliminating the failure
[17].
• II. Encapsulant failure, because of non­browning EVA becoming widely available
along with a UV test to assess if new material suffers from discoloration.
• III. Broken interconnectors, due to the accelerated stress test of thermal cycling
and cyclic dynamic loading. The manufacturers are expected to improve the design
and materials for interconnect ribbons.
• IV. Delamination, because with the improvement in adhesion and the understand­
ing of the thermal requirements for the primer, the delamination failure has been
already minimized.
• V. Solder bond failures, because of the use of automated processing equipment,
an increase in bus bars and solder bonds per cell, and improved testing, the solder
bond failures should be reduced in the field [17].
The failures likely to take place more frequently in the future are stated below.
• a. Broken cells will likely arise more often because there have been developed
larger and thinner solar cells [16].

Bifacial PV related failures
Generally, the same failures on C­Si mono facial PV modules will occur on bifacial PV
modules. However, some failures will have a difference in how several or how often they
occur[19]. Among them, one failure is PID. Jorne Carolus et Al. [20] conclude in 2019

Operation and Maintenance procedures
The installation of PV systems worldwide creates the need to tailor the operation
and maintenance procedures to consider the variances in legislation, climates, and other
stress factors such as temperature, humidity, UV­light exposure, rain, and wind [6].
The objective of the present section is to make an overview of the usual services
being offered by operators and the performance measurements being done, considering
all these variances.
2.2.1
Contractual Framework for operators
One of the principal definers of the maintenance activities to be performed in a PV
park is the agreement between the owner of the park and the operator. Such agreement
is defined in a contract framing the activities included in the service, the frequency to
perform them, and the cost of the service, among various other clauses. These contracts
usually have a duration of 10 to 15 years, so they should be reviewed two or three times
in the lifetime of the PV park (usually between 25 and 30 years) [6]. The periodic review
gives the opportunity to include in future versions the actualization of new procedures not
yet defined when previous contracts were drafted.
The scope of the works usually included in the operating agreement is frequently
divided into two big groups: preventive maintenance and corrective maintenance. A third
group could also be included to accommodate tasks related to predictive maintenance,
mainly pushed by the advance in the data analysis and machine learning field of studies
[6], but it is not a common procedure. The activities to be executed on each of these
groups will mainly depend on the owner’s budget for operation and maintenance, the
capabilities of the selected operator, and the necessary activities for the specific PV park.
Preventive maintenance
Preventive maintenance represents all the activities and services regularly scheduled
to maximize the long­term energy production and the lifetime of a PV park. The goal of
such activities is to prevent the occurrence of failures, and in the case of an existing failure,
to reduce the time until the detection and prevent a more complex problem [6].
It is under this type of service that not only the site maintenance activities are per­
formed (such as module cleaning, vegetation management, removal of loose objects,
water drainage, etc.) but also the inspections and fault detection methods are done [6].
The most common preventive maintenance activities are listed below and will be further
decribed in Section 2.4:
• I­V measurements at the module level
• Thermography and/or Electro­Luminescent imaging of the PV modules or strings
• Inspection, servicing, and maintenance of the balance of plant components
• Inspection, servicing, and maintenance of the data­acquisition systems
Finally, a major part of preventive maintenance is represented by the tasks done
to monitor the performance of the power plant. These actions are usually grouped as
”Condition Monitoring” and are performed to obtain operational excellence [6]. It is under

Corrective maintenance
Corrective maintenance, on the other hand, represents all the activities and services

of the PV park to its proper state, in the least amount of time possible. The actions per­

get replacement parts and/or its budget [6].
The present study focuses on condition monitoring and fault detection, so the actions

Key Performance Indicators
The end goal of an operator working on a PV park is to ensure that the actual produc­
tion level meets the expectations for that specific park. In order to measure this, several
indicators have been developed and they are commonly used in the operating agreement
between the owner of the park and the operator of it to evaluate its performance [21].
When interviewing European Energy’s Operations and Maintenance team, it was
mentioned that the two more widely used indicators are the Performance Ratio (PR) and
the Availability (also called Uptime). Subject experts assured that this is a common proce­
dure in the sector. In essence, the PR is a measure of how good the park is at converting
solar irradiation to energy [6].
To be able to use the same parameters for different parks, ratios considering the park
size and location are used. To address the park size, the plant yield (Yf) is used. The
plant yield is obtained by dividing the AC energy output of the plant (Eout) by the plant
peak DC power (P0) [6].
Yf = Eout/P0
(2.1)
The reference yield (Yr), is used to correct for the available solar resource. It is the ra­
tio between the horizontal plane of array insolation (HPOA), and the irradiance of standard
testing conditions (GSTC) [6].
Yr = HPOA/GSTC
(2.2)
The PR is then expressed as the ratio between the plant yield (Yf) and the reference
yield (Yr) [6]:
PR = Yf/Yr
(2.3)
Several corrections can be made to the PR in order to account for issues that the
operator has no control over, such as module temperature, curtailment, and sometimes
even soiling. These corrections will vary for each plant and operator agreement, and
usually, operators are required to guarantee a minimum PR value as part of their contract.
Availability, on the other hand, refers to the time in which the plant was generating
energy during a whole period of examination. Unlike the PR value, the availability does
not measure how much energy the plant is producing, only the time that it is producing
energy [6]. A common way of calculating the availability of a plant (or Uptime) could be

==PAGE:26==
by taking the time that the plant could be generating power (Tuseful) and subtracting the
portion of time out of it, that there was no power generation (Tdown), forming an equation
like this:
Uptime = Tuseful −Tdown
(2.4)
It is important to consider the same period of examination when calculating each of
these factors so that the Uptime really measures the portion of time that the plant was
successfully operating.
Corrections can also be made to account for unavailability periods that exceed the
operator’s control, such as network downtime or plant owner’s mandated actions. A min­
imum Availability value is also usually required by the owner as a part of the contract,
along with a maximum response time (the time spent from the occurrence of an incident

2.3
Condition monitoring procedures
Part of the operation of a PV plant is the regular monitoring of the condition of the sys­
tem. Various climatic and environmental conditions affect the PV systems and downgrade
their performance. The activities performed to monitor, analyze and report the status of
the system, and thus obtain operational excellence, are grouped together in condition
monitoring [23].
The monitoring can be done for both the AC side of the system and the DC side of
the system. Monitoring the AC side can help the utility company to maintain and improve
power quality and prevent system outages, but cannot be used to determine the fault and
its location within a PV plant [23].
Contrary to that, DC side monitoring can help to identify faulty modules, calculate
the efficiency of operation and determine short and long­term trends of the system. It is
understandable, then, the need for DC monitoring in order to maintain a good performance
of a PV plant over the years [21].

2.3.2
Different condition monitoring techniques
Depending on the purpose behind the monitoring, different variables would be neces­
sary to measure, and the frequency of those measurements can be variable as well. As
a main distinction, these systems can be divided between just a performance verification
and a system evaluation [21].
Since a PV system is capable of creating revenue for the owner, a performance ver­
ification would be a system where the user compares the claims of the PV array with its
output. The simplest of these systems would be a ”smart” meter, where the power output
of the PV array is measured and evaluated over time [23].
On the other hand, a system evaluation consists of further analysis of the status of
the PV array and gathers not only the power output measurements but other parameters
such as solar radiation, DC current and voltage, and meteorological variables. All these
measurements can be then combined with fault detection methods to create a full picture
of the condition of the PV array [12].

This method consists of applying ultrasonic vibrations to the modules and analyzing

2.4.3
Fault detection methods based on electrical characterization
One main branch of fault detection on the DC side of the system consists of analyzing
the electrical characteristics of the system. These values can be analyzed with several
methods, including I­V measurements, which is the main method used in this thesis.
Power Loss Analysis (PLA)
This method is based on the comparison between the monitored value of the power
production for a specific PV system to the simulated results expected for that system.
The idea of this method is to raise an alarm if the difference between the measured and
computed powers reaches a threshold. Although it is a good technique for detecting
under­performing systems, it has low accuracy in differentiating the faults or precisely
locating them [26].
The simulation of the PV system can be done by the use of standard simulation tools
(such as Matlab, for example), where measured irradiance and cell temperature are given
as input variables [27]. The output of such simulations, in most cases, is the power output
of the DC part of the system.
This is currently the main monitoring method used by PV park operators due to two
main reasons: The convenient fact of monitoring only one parameter, and the relation
between DC power output and PR value (out of which PV park operators’ performance is
measured) [26].
Residual Current monitoring Devices (RCDs)
These devices measure the current entering and leaving the PV system through the
current­carrying conductors. By sensing the difference between the two currents, possible
line­ground or line­line faults can be detected [12]. This method is commonly applied on
the inverter level and, if used in a PV farm, is usually applied within the inverter as a
protection unit.
While residual current monitoring devices can be installed in both grounded and un­
grounded PV parks, it is mainly used in ungrounded systems to protect against the ground
and line­to­line faults [28].

Electrical I­V measurements
The current­voltage characterization is the most common method to state the per­

the PV module or array. It is possible to extract parameters that can be compared with
the original datasheet parameters from the I­V curve. However, the datasheet values are

on the instrument used [2].

PV String configuration
The I­V characteristic parameters from a PV module to modules connected in a PV
string can be affected in two scenarios depending on if the modules are connecting in
either series or parallel.
If PV modules are connected in series, the Voc from each module will be added to­
gether along with the series resistance. The Isc in this scenario will be the value from
the module with the lowest Isc. The Imp will be determined where the MPP is. Within the
same type of modules, there can be a difference in the Isc for a PV string connected in
series. This difference in current will be a loss, referred to as the mismatch loss in a PV
string.
If PV modules are connected in parallel, the current from each module is added to­
gether and the Voc will be determined by the module with the lowest Voc.
2.6
Correction of I­V curves
Outdoor PV modules rarely experience what is equivalent to the 1000 W/m2, oper­
ating temperatures on 25 ◦C, and AM1.5G spectrum, as used for STC measurements,
due to constant changes in the angle of incidence (AOI), and water vapor, temperature,
solar elevation, among others [29]. Changes in the spectrum have a direct impact on the
current production. Correction techniques for the translation to STC are constantly under
development and improvement in the PV community. Most of them rely on the techniques
outlined in the IEC 06891 standard [29].
It is, therefore, necessary to correct the raw I­V measurements from outdoors to be
able to compare the PV modules/string performance to each other over time and different
irradiance and temperature conditions.
To correct the outdoor I­V measurements, the most relevant parameters are the op­
erating solar cell temperature and the incident plane of array irradiation, since they are
affecting the performance directly, as could be seen in Figures 2.10, 2.9, and equation
2.7. It is, therefore, important to have high­quality measurements of the environmental
conditions at the time of the I­V measurement.
The European standard IEC 60891:2021 suggests four different procedures for tem­
perature and irradiance corrections to measure I­V characteristics [36]. The four proce­
dures can be used for correcting measured I­V characteristics to other conditions of tem­
perature and irradiance as STC. There are advantages and disadvantages to the different
procedures.
• Correction procedure 1 is best applicable for correct irradiation within ± 40 % of
the target irradiance and is a system of linear equations for current and voltage.
However, knowing the value of Rs beforehand is a requirement.
• Correction procedure 2 is good for irradiance correction larger than ± 40 % to the
target. The correction parameters of the PV device are necessary to know in both
procedures.
• Correction procedure 3 is a correction between the minimum of two I­V curves of
the same PV device.
• Correction procedure 4 can be used when Rs is unknown, and the characteristics
under STC are needed from a single experimental I­V curve [36].
As stated by Baojie Li et Al. [34], none of the procedures is outperforming in any case,
and depending on which degradation factor is impacting the PV module, some procedures

==PAGE:36==
are better than others. For example, in partial shading and under healthy conditions, pro­
cedure 2 is best. Modules that suffer from Rs and Rsh degradation, procedures 1 or 4
are the best methods to correct I­V measurements to a certain irradiance and tempera­
ture when the different procedures are compared to the relative root­mean­square error
(RMSE) of the curve correction.
Baojie Li et Al [34] evaluated the different procedures with measured I­V curves on
healthy modules, and then simulated I­V curves were done using the single diode model.
Different scenarios with variations in temperature and irradiance for different faults were
simulated. They found that procedures 1 and 2 are close overall with a RMSE of 2.8%
and 2.6% respectively, whereas procedure 4 has an RMSE of 4.8%.
In this thesis, correction procedure 1 will be used due to Rs being implemented as
a parameter, requires less parameters than procedure 2, and it has a low RMSE at low

A lower power output of the PV array caused by, for example, non­uniform irradiance

the bypass diodes, appear on the I­V curves as inflection points or steps down the curve.

power output in the PV system. Even small changes not visible directly on the I­V curve
can be detected through this method.

Experimental Procedure
In this section, a review of the performed experiments and the utilized methodol­
ogy is done. The objective of these experiments was to design a condition­monitoring
method able to identify faults from inverter­based I­V measurements. By measuring and
evaluating the I­V curve, the parameters (Pmp, Vmp/Voc ratio, Imp/Isc ratio, Isc, Voc), and
understanding the impact of different failures on these parameters, it would be possible
to determine if a string of PV modules is affected by a failure.
Most of the efforts during this project were performed at DTU’s PV park, mainly due to
the proximity to the PV park and the availability of the equipment and PV modules to make
measurements and experiments. Also, the PV laboratory at DTU was utilized for indoor,
module­level experiments. DTU has planned to refit the PV trackers with new modules,
and it is on these modules that some of the experiments were done.
The experiments performed can be summarized in the following list:
• Indoor characterization of PV modules
• Outdoor I­V measurements on shaded bifacial modules
• Outdoor I­V measurements on bifacial PID­affected modules
• Outdoor I­V measurements on monofacial modules with cracked cells
• Outdoor I­V measurements on bifacial modules with added series resistance
In addition to these experiments, performance data from commercial utility­scale PV
parks provided by European Energy was utilized to test and strengthen the condition­
monitoring method being designed.

3.5.1
Simulated scenario: string­level measurement with added series
resistance modules
In order to be able to understand the impact of added series resistance failures on a
string level, a simulated scenario of 25 modules connected in series was performed. The
goal of this experiment was to extrapolate the results of the module­level measurements
to a situation where several modules are connected. It was expected that the impact of
the same added resistance is lower in the string than the one on the module level. This is
because an increase of 0.5Ωin a module (with a characteristic series resistance of 0.3Ω)
represents an increase of 160% of the series resistance of the system. In a string with
25 modules connected in series, the system would have a series resistance of 7.5Ω, and
therefore, adding 0.5Ωwould only represent an increase of 6% in the series resistance.
To add several I­V curves into a singular string scenario, a horizontal addition has
been performed. The I­V curves obtained from the module­level measurement were used,
and for each point with the same current, the voltages were added. In the case where the
current was not matching, a linear interpolation between the voltage level has been used
to match the same current.
Once the current was matched, the I­V curves of the modules could be added together
in a string. After combining the I­V curves, a newly generated curve is created with its own
set of parameters. The Vmp/Voc ratio of the new curve generated would give, preliminarily,
a sense of the impact of the added resistance on the whole string.

The number of local peaks is then evaluated further to judge if a bypass diode is
activated due to shading or other failures. If only one peak the I­V curve is not affected by
shading and the condition of the string can be evaluated further. If more than one peak,
the string is affected by active bypass diodes and there might be non­uniform shading or
cracked cells. A new measurement is needed to determine if it is consistent (e.g. cracked
cells) or just a temporary shade.
These results showed that a non­uniform shading of a string will have an impact on
the I­V measurements done by the inverter. It was possible to detect a shade of a single
cell within the string. It also showed that the inflection points in the I­V curve will depend
on how much and how many modules are shaded. This is of interest for the condition
monitoring because when the I­V parameters are evaluated a shade will cause misleading
information about the current state of the PV string performance.

4.6
Designing a condition­monitoring method for fault
detection using inverter­based data from I­V curves
It is possible to develop a condition monitoring method by using the results achieved
in this chapter. Such a method could be used to detect failures at a utility­scale PV park
and at DTU PV park.
To perform an effective condition monitoring it is suggested to measure on clear sky
days, since that will give the most reliable results without a fluctuation in the I­V curve
parameters, especially if the investigation is performed over a large area. It can also be
seen that some faults are revealed best around solar noon, avoiding a misinterpretation
of the results due to differences in bifacial gain and a low AOI impact. Furthermore, the
best results will be obtained with multiple strings measured at the same time, to easily
detect discrepancies between two identical strings. It is important that the measurements
are executed at the same instant, or else a variance in environmental conditions can
generate deviations in the measurements.
Therefore, a systematic approach could be used to detect faults and monitor the con­
dition of a PV park, by checking several thresholds for different parameters.
Initial status of PV modules
The results from Section 4.1 can be used in condition monitoring to state which con­
dition should be expected for new modules.
The results showed that, the expected
performance at STC for a module without any failure is in general deviating from the
datasheet stated parameters. This deviation varies for the different manufacturers, but
values around ±3% of the datasheet were measured in this thesis. In general, the Imp/Isc
and Vmp/Voc ratios are higher than the datasheet states.
However, the Vmp/Voc ratio
changes with the irradiance, where the Imp/Isc ratio is steady. It should be pointed out
that these statements are done with measurements at STC, where outdoor measurements
vary more with the weather condition, which the model has to take into consideration.

Fault detection method
Figure 4.27 is showing the different parameters analyzed in the method. The per­
formed steps to analyze each threshold and the meaning of a parameter not reaching the
expected values are explained afterward.
Figure 4.27: Flowchart of the method, indicating the different parameters being analyzed.
The first threshold to analyze is the power output, and it needs to be higher than 0
for the string being checked. When performing several measurements it is possible that
a disconnected inverter or other issue ends up with a power output value of 0. Filtering
this data out would simplify further analysis.
Analysis on inflection points
The second step could be to check if the bypass diodes are being triggered with the
method described in 4.2. If two or more local maxima are detected in the derivative of
the I­V curve (meaning that there is an inflection point in the original curve), it can be an
indication of non­uniform shading, cell cracks, or a module Isc failure.
It was found from the shading experiment in Section 4.2 that with the derivative of
the I­V curve, it was possible to detect non­uniform shade down to one cell. Also, from
the cell crack experiments in Section 4.4, it is possible that a module with inactive cells
triggers the bypass diodes
This analysis has its limitations because a fluctuation in the curve (or noise) can be
counted as a peak, which might be due to an error in the measurement, low irradiance or
jump right after measured Isc.
Analysis on the Imp/Isc ratio
The value of the Imp/Isc ratio calculated from the datasheet could be used as a thresh­
old, to analyze the condition of the modules. All healthy modules measured outdoors had
an Imp/Isc ratio higher than the one able to be calculated from the datasheet, when the
irradiance is over 800W/m2. A low Imp/Isc ratio can indicate a low shunt resistance due
to cracked cells, or high series resistance.
The cracked cells modules showed that the Imp/Isc ratio should be the parameter
with the biggest difference when compared to a module without cracks. In the case of the

==PAGE:77==
added series resistance, it showed that a high series resistance can impact the Imp/Isc
ratio.
The limitation of this threshold is the variation in the ratio with outdoor measurements,
especially at low irradiance, and if this ratio is degrading in older modules, which was not
investigated in this thesis.
Analysis on the Vmp/Voc ratio
The values for the Vmp/Voc ratio have been observed through the results that it changes
with the current and therefore with Gpoa, because of an increase in the voltage drop. At
1000W/m2 irradiance it is in all circumstances lower than what was measured indoors
and is different from module to module. This shows that a threshold value like this can be
complex to monitor. By measuring multiple strings or having a reference string, a com­
parison of the Vmp/Voc ratios can be made, and a lower value in one string would indicate
an added series resistance, an error in the measurements, or a triggered bypass diode.
However, if there is no reference string, our results indicate that a value under 0.8 for
measurement at 1000W/m2 can indicate a low Vmp/Voc at these modules. This can be
due to an increase in series resistance because of failure in the busbars and interconnect
ribbons or failure in the cables.
The limitation of this threshold is that it varies with irradiance and is module­depending.
If measuring a string with added resistance and under inconsistence irradiance, a higher
Vmp/Voc ratio will be measured due to lower irradiance and that could be enough to not be
stated as a possible failure. This thesis has only done measurements during the summer,
it is not known if the seasons have an impact due to temperature changes.
Analysis on the Isc values
Defining a threshold for Isc is complex, since this value constantly changes, especially
under inconsistent irradiance, and it can change within seconds. From the PID experi­
ment, it could be observed that even within three bifacial modules next to each other
variations during the day were up to 2.5%. It is difficult to state whether that difference
is only due to PID failure or also a difference in the bifacial gain. A high difference in Isc
under high irradiance could indicate uniform shading or soiling. In order to be categorized
as a failing string, it has to continuously have a lower value than other strings.
An average utility­scale park in Denmark can have a size over tens of hectares, so
a big variation in irradiation over the park and a slight change in terrain could cause a
change in the Isc within string measurement. Before Isc can be compared to each other it
is needed to be very certain that the measurements are done within a couple of seconds,
and that the strings being analyzed are actually comparable.
Since it was only the PID and uniform shading experiments that showed a change in
Isc, one limitation to defining this threshold is to determine which failures would cause a
loss in Isc. Since a string might bypass a module with low Isc compared to other modules,
failures in the Isc may be found in I­V curves with inflection points instead, or else the
whole string has to be affected by the failure. The effect on Isc from modules with more
severe PID could have helped tune this threshold, especially how it would react on a string
level.
Analysis on the Voc values
Defining a limit value for the Voc of a string was not possible. Throughout the ex­
periments, it was not found a tendency for lower Voc ratios, besides a slight drop in one
of the cracked cell modules. Both the indoor measured values and the stated Voc in the
datasheet have not been reached when measuring outdoors in any of the experiments.
Since Voc is very sensitive to temperature, it might happen that the modules follow another
PV array condition monitoring based on inverter enabled I­V curve measurements
65
==PAGE:78==
temperature coefficient than the ones stated in the datasheets. It is recommended, there­
fore, to evaluate the Voc values within the different strings when the different modules are
expected to have similar module temperatures.
The limitation of Voc as a threshold is that in a string with 1200 V, one cell in short­
circuit will be difficult to detect: it has to be one or more modules being out. A variation
in the module temperature can be expected in a big utility­scale park, depending on the
size of the park, leading to low Voc measurements in some strings.
However, all of these values need to be evaluated over multiple measurements, since
one measurement below the thresholds can be observed due to several circumstances.
That is why, it is recommended to follow up on under­performing strings by doing several
measurements over time, and checking if it is consistently under the threshold values,
to determine the existence of a fault. Also, this flowchart is only considering the failures
that have been tested throughout the realization of this thesis. Other failures should be
investigated for further developing.
It was not possible to state exact values for condition monitoring because outdoor con­
ditions and differences between modules can vary the value of the thresholds throughout
the measurements. Also, the measuring tools being used can determine if a difference
in a value can be found or not. For example, by not having continuous and simultaneous
measurements, a correction of the measurements has to be done for temperature and
irradiation, and the difference between a failing string and one performing right can be
lost. Furthermore, the degradation of the system should be considered year after year,
since the expected performance of the modules will most probably decrease with time.
This indicates the complexity of doing fault detection algorithms at the utility­scale park
level done by the inverter.

Testing the condition monitoring method on European
Energy’s utility­scale PV parks
The designed mechanism to detect possible underperforming strings was tested us­
ing the available data from European Energy’s PV parks. During the realization of this
project, it was discovered that multiple errors can happen while performing an I­V scan
through the NetECO Huawei platform, which complicated the condition monitoring.
The list below summarizes the different errors faced, threatening the reliability of the
measurements.
• Data from previous measurements was saved and shown again on later measure­
ments (even up to a day later).
• I­V scans for multiple inverters took up to an hour to be finalized.
• In some occasions, I­V scans ended with a partial completion, meaning that some
strings were not measured.
• Huawei system froze several times (without completing the scans and not allowing
to do other scans).
• Division of the I­V curve inflection point at high irradiance and high voltage (the
”knee” point), into two different inflection points.
These errors generated uncertainty at the moment of analyzing the data. Especially,
not knowing the exact moment at which the scans were performed could lead to a mis­
judgment of the irradiance and temperature level at the moment of the measurement. This
becomes critical when evaluating the generated current values. The last error has also
been observed in the I­V scans performed with the Huawei inverter at DTU.

sible due to time constraints. Also, further assessments were planned to analyze those
strings in a remote way, but the inverters were offline at the time they were found inter­
esting to investigate further.
4.7.1
Advantages and disadvantages with I­V monotoring
With the obtained results, and after using the I­V curves as a method for fault detection
and condition monitoring, a summary list of the advantages and disadvantages of this
method could be performed.
Among the advantages of this method:
• It is remote, as the measurements can be performed without being on­site.
• It is fast, since a measurement could be done, in theory, in just a couple of seconds
per string.
• It is possible to calculate the power loss of the string, compared to imaging methods
for condition monitoring.
• It is possible to identify possible failures out of a power loss, compared to MPP
tracking.
The disadvantages of this method are:

• It requires several measurements for a fault to be detected with certainty
• Once a fault is detected, most of the time it is not possible to determine the exact
failure affecting the string. Other methods should be used in addition to determining
it.
• It is not possible to determine which module is affected inside a string.
• Failures affecting the voltage values are hard to determine when measuring a full
string, because of series­connected modules.
Although some other methods might be better at determining the exact failure in a
string, the I­V measurements are still a good first­step approach when monitoring the
condition of a utility­scale PV park.

5
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the capabilities, advantages, and disadvantages of inverter­
based I­V monitoring, for operating and maintaining the condition of a utility­scale PV
plant. This has been done through a state of the art studies and experiments.
Throughout the state of art study in Section 2, possible PV module failures and current
methods to detect those were stated and described.
An indoor I­V characterization with flash test and EL images was executed for 129
new bifacial modules from different manufacturers. No cracks were found in the study of
those modules through EL imaging. The I­V characterization showed that the datasheet
values can be expected within an average deviation of Isc of ­1.8%, Imp of ­1%, Voc ­0.3%,
Vmp ­0.7 %, and Pmp of ­0.7%, when using the Endeas 540XLi flasher. Only Trina modules
showed a positive deviation in Vmp and Pmp. On average, the bifacial factor was 66.7%.
Unfortunately, it was not possible to state what should be expected outdoors when new
modules were connected in a string.
An experiment with non­ and uniform shading was performed, where measurements
were executed with an I­V tracer and the inverter on a string level, to be able to distinguish
between failures and shading. It was found that within a string of 19 bifacial modules, it is
possible to detect a non­uniform shade down to 1 cell through the I­V curve. To detect that
a bypass diode was triggered, the derivative of the I­V curve was calculated and used as
a method for detecting minor inflex points in the I­V curve. It was found that with a uniform
shade of 25% of 5 bifacial modules in a string the Isc was halved. This states that, if a
halved Isc is measured in one string out of several strings, uniform shading could be the
reason.
A module­based experiment was executed to detect the impact of PID on PV modules.
Simultaneous and continuous measurements of three modules, where two suffered from
PID and one did not, were done to address this issue. Out of these measurements, it was
detected that PID­affected modules had a lower Isc value and a reduction in the Imp/Isc
ratio of up to 1%.
To detect cracked cells, a monofacial module­based experiment was executed. Si­
multaneous and continuous measurements of three modules, where two had cracked cells
and one was healthy, were performed. It was found that cracked cells could be detected
in a lower Imp/Isc ratio due to a decrease in the shunt resistance, a reduction in Vmp/Voc
ratio due to an increase in series resistance, and a reduction in Voc. It was found that,
with severe cracks, bypass diodes can get triggered. However, due to internal reflection
within the module at AOI > 30◦the bypass diode was not triggered at all measurements.
To detect high series resistance values, an experiment with three bifacial modules,
where two of them were connected to high­resistance wires, was executed. With simul­
taneous and continuous measurements, it was found that a low Vmp/Voc ratio compared
to a healthy module can be used to detect added series resistance. In this case, it was
also possible to detect through the Imp/Isc ratio, since the added resistance was in the
magnitude of 160 %, compared to the original state. Through simulated string­level sce­
narios, an added series resistance with the same magnitude as the ones from the single
modules, could not be detected through the Vmp/Voc ratio.

==PAGE:84==
After the findings in this thesis, it can be concluded that the I­V curve parameters
Isc, Imp/Isc, Vmp/Voc, and Pmp along with evaluating the curve shape could be used in a
condition monitoring method. Through the experiments in this thesis, it was not possible to
state the use of Voc as a monitoring parameter. Such a method would require multiple and
simultaneous measurements throughout a clear sky day, and a reference module/string.
It was not possible to determine the exact threshold values for each parameter, to discern
faulty modules/strings from healthy ones. However, an evaluation of the parameters can
still be used to identify underperforming modules/strings.
This method was tested at three utility­scale PV plants. It was found that inverter­
based I­V measurements at this scale have a difference in Isc, which is suspected to be
due to string layout, bifacial gain, and/or irradiance disparity within multiple strings over
larger areas. The Voc also had a variation, which was suspected to be due to different
module temperatures within the strings. Three strings were identified as underperforming,
and a possible failure for each of them was mentioned.
It was found that the limitations of this method are the complexity of evaluating Isc
and Voc, and determining exact Imp/Isc­ and Vmp/Voc ratios to discern faulty strings from
healthy strings. When using the inverter­based I­V method, it is necessary to know the
exact time for the measurement and the accuracy of the data received, both for the I­V
data and also the irradiance variations from the front­ and rear sides. The last limitation
found was the fact that an on­site inspection (visual, EL imaging, IR imaging) is required
to identify the fault affecting the module/string entirely.
5.1
Further work
Future research on the development of this condition monitoring method could deter­
mine the outdoor I­V parameters on the string level, with newly outdoor­installed modules
measured with the inverter, and compare them to the indoor measurements. This could
help to understand better and calculate the expected parameters for a new PV site using
similar modules.
Using three identical strings would help develop monitoring thresholds even more
and preferred to install failed modules and investigate the impact on the parameters, by
simultaneously measuring the strings with and without the failures. Further research on
the Vmp/Voc values at different irradiance levels could be worthwhile. This could help to
understand how to obtain an expected ratio of a module when deployed outdoors from
its datasheet parameters. This would help to identify failures related to high series resis­
tance.
An investigation on the rear and front plane of array irradiance could be done to ad­
just the expected value of the Isc on PV parks with bifacial modules. By sensing the front
and rear irradiance, the variation of the generated current throughout a utility­scale PV
park could be understood. In addition, software to perform I­V scans on utility­scale parks
could be developed for better results. The Huwaei software NetECO has, at the time of
the realization of this thesis, limitations regarding the time it needs to perform a measure­
ment and the minimum irradiance level requirement. A system that can perform instant
measurements, at any irradiance, on the inverter for a whole PV park, could be of great
benefit while addressing the condition of the PV modules.
Finally, a deeper investigation into the current mismatch within the cells of a bifacial
module, with an angle of incidence > 30◦, would greatly benefit understanding the com­
plexity of fault detection. This could be used to assess how the condition monitoring would
deviate from an E/W tracker system.
