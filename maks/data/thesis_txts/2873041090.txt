Abstract
New regulations from the International Maritime Organisation encourage shipping com-
panies to reduce their vessels’ greenhouse gas emissions. A simple way to do this is
to reduce the amount of ballast water used by the vessel, as this is excess weight that
a good stowage plan can minimise. RoRo ship stowage planning (RSSP) research has
previously assumed that the cargo weights are known. Yet, in practice, these weights are
rarely precisely known, and shipping companies are often only given rough estimates.
This thesis investigates how uncertain cargo weights affect the stowage plan, specifically
aiming to maximise the amount of cargo loaded while minimising the amount of ballast
water needed to ensure the vessel’s stability and seaworthiness. This thesis uses a newly
developed hybrid-slot representation of the vessel to create a Mixed Integer Program
(MIP) of the RSSP problem. Additionally, a linearisation of the ballast water’s contri-
bution to the vertical centre of gravity is made. A two-stage stochastic programming
model is used to model the uncertainty, allowing for more flexible stowage plans. Two
scenario generation methods are compared, and a scenario reduction method is proposed.
The results indicated a significant weakness in the weight limit for one of the decks for
the vessel tested, and the optimal deterministic stowage plan’s feasibility was sensitive
to changes in the cargo weight. The stochastic model was able to create more flexible
and robust stowage plans, but they were still infeasible in some cases. The results also
showed the need for a heuristic to solve the model, as for diﬀicult test instances, the
model could not find a good solution within a 1-hour time limit. It is proposed that
similar feasibility tests of stowage plans for additional vessels be performed to identify
other weaknesses.

Introduction
A major international transportation mode for freight is maritime transport. In 2022,
maritime transport in the European Union accounted for 67.8% of freight transportation,
emphasising its critical role within the market, not only in the EU but also on a global
scale [1]. Within this sector of transportation of goods, Roll-On/Roll-Off (RoRo) vessels,
or ships, are an increasing part of the maritime transportation modes. This is partly be-
cause the cargo can be easily wheeled onto the vessel without the need for gantry cranes
and container spreaders, which are part of the intricacies and challenges of stowing a
container ship. As the name suggests, RoRo vessels allow cargo to be driven directly
on and off the vessel, significantly reducing turnover time for the vessel. These rolling
cargoes include, but are not limited to, cars, trailers, tractors, and other heavy rolling
machinery. However, even though the cargo might be easier to stow on a RoRo vessel, a
good stowage plan remains complicated to find for the shipping companies. The hetero-
geneity of the rolling cargo increases the complexity of fully utilising the decks in a RoRo
vessel and finding an optimal stowage plan. Furthermore, what mainly determines the
revenue for shipping companies is the amount of cargo transported. Hence, the desired
goal for shipping companies is to maximise the amount of cargo loaded on their RoRo
vessels. However, the shipping companies must simultaneously adhere to International
Maritime Organisation regulations and strive to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions
[2]. What plays a large part in a vessel’s emissions is the speed at which it sails [3]. The
challenge for shipping companies is to maintain a profitable freight operation while also
being energy-eﬀicient. By implementing eﬀicient stowage plans, shipping companies can
maximise the number of cargoes shipped and minimise turnover time in port, which
allows for a slower voyage, thereby reducing the vessel’s emissions.
Contrary to container ships and their stowage plan, RoRo shipping and stowage planning
are a new research area first introduced in 2011 in [4], hence, all aspects of the RoRo
stowage planning problem have not yet been researched. The challenges of finding an
optimal stowage plan arise from multiple factors. RoRo vessels often transport cargo
between numerous ports simultaneously, making it beneficial to minimise the number of
cargo shifts. A cargo shift, or simply a shift, occurs when cargo needs to be temporarily
unloaded to clear a path for other cargo being loaded or unloaded. Since shifting takes
time, it should be minimised or eliminated in a good stowage plan. RoRo vessels must
maintain proper stability when fully loaded to prevent capsizing. When a vessel has
finished loading or unloading at a port, the crew determines the vessel’s stability. Water
is then pumped in or out of the ballast tanks to achieve the desired stability level,
ensuring the vessel is seaworthy. Furthermore, the sheer weight of the cargo can bend
the vessel and cause it to take irredeemable structural damage [5], [6]. However, ensuring
stability for the vessel is time-consuming and might not even be feasible if the stowage
plan is inadequate to begin with.
Uncertainties in cargo weights further complicate
stability requirements, as precise weights are often unknown.

In this thesis, the RoRo stowage planning problem involves determining the optimal
deck utilisation for heterogeneous rolling cargo with uncertain weights, while adhering to
multiple constraints such as ensuring vessel stability and maximum shear and bending
forces, such that the amount of ballast water needed is minimised. Cargo shifts are
eliminated as we will only be testing on single-leg journeys, i.e. from harbour a to b.
From here on, a vessel’s stability will be referred to as its seaworthiness.
The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows. Chapter 2 presents the work so
far done in RoRo stowage planning to identify gaps in current literature. Chapter 3
introduces the RoRo stowage planning problem more in depth, and the vessel and cargo
used in this thesis. Chapter 4 introduces the concepts from stochastic programming
used in this thesis. Chapter 5 explains the seaworthiness requirements for the vessel.
The deterministic and stochastic MIP model is introduced in chapter 6 . In chapter 7, a
computational study is presented, and lastly, the findings are discussed and summarised
in chapter 8 and 9 respectively.

Literature review
This chapter reviews the literature related to RoRo Ship Stowage Planning (RSSP). The
RSSP is a relatively new research area with sparse research. Most research focuses on
modelling the problem and/or developing heuristics to solve the RSSP. Furthermore, the
RSSP is a complex problem with numerous aspects, and most research does not account
for every element. Instead, researchers focus on specific areas, thereby simplifying the
problem. A brief overview of some of the previous research can be seen in Table 2.1.
Stowage optimisation and, in general, maritime transport optimisation have been ex-
tensively studied.
However, stowage planning has mainly focused on containers and
container ships. An early example of this is [7]. The RoRo stowage planning problem
(RSSP) was first introduced in [4], where a mixed-integer programming (MIP) model
was described, along with a heuristic. The RSSP accounted for mandatory and optional
cargo, two dimensions of stability for seaworthiness, the determination of deck heights,
and a lane representation of the decks. They continued their work in [8], where a RoRo
ship routing and stowage plan problem was considered.
In [9], the MIP model from [4] is further refined to include three dimensions of sta-
bility for the seaworthiness of the vessel, longitudinal, transverse and vertical, which
is the current standard for handling RoRo optimisation if considering stability for the
seaworthiness. In [10], a new grid representation of the deck was developed, and they
introduced the 2D RoRo Ship Stowage Planning problem (2DRSSP). They considered
the problem as a 2-dimensional knapsack problem, and found that to eliminate shifts,
the cargo that needed to be loaded and unloaded at the same ports should be grouped.
These articles lay the baseline for RSSP research.
Recently, safety on RoRo vessel has been a concern. In [11], they refer to the increas-
ing concern for maritime safety and propose to improve stowage plans so that safety is
considered. They classify the cargo based on how dangerous the cargo are and find the
minimum distance between such hazardous cargo. They propose a model that tries to
maximise the distance between hazardous cargo in the stowage plan, taking into account
multiple decks. Moreover, in [11], they consider fire safety studies and implement sug-
gested changes in their model to improve said fire safety. Furthermore, they introduced
a new hybrid-slot representation of the decks, which will be used in this thesis. Their
new representation of the decks allows for more precise calculations of the vessel’s sea-
worthiness. Furthermore, additional requirements for specific cargo types, such as cargo
lashing and refrigeration, can be taken into account when creating the stowage plan,
allowing for very detailed and realistic stowage plans to be developed.
Several heuristics have been proposed to solve the RSSP, as the different representa-
tions of the RSSP are challenging to solve within a practical time limit when considering
all aspects of the RSSP. In [4], squeaky wheel optimisation with local search was im-
plemented. [9] implemented Tabu-Search and [10] and [12] implemented GRASP and

ALNS. Furthermore, different genetic algorithms have been implemented in [4], [13], and
[14]. A heuristic is not proposed in this thesis, however, it would be a natural exten-
sion to this work, and future researchers are encouraged to read the research mentioned
above.
Minimisation of fuel consumption has been considered in [15] and [16]. In [15], they op-
timised the vessel’s seaworthiness to minimise the necessary ballast water intake, leading
to a lower fuel consumption and thereby reducing GHG emissions. The levels of ballast
water in the ballast tanks were discretised in this work. In [16], a new model and heuris-
tic were developed to consider the relationship between sailing speed, fuel consumption,
and cargo handling time. Furthermore, they studied how the dynamic arrival of cargo
affects the stowage plan. Their MIP model was too complex and could not be solved for
even toy-size instances. The heuristic was able to find feasible stowage plans, but was
sensitive to changes in the relation between revenue, fuel cost, and cargo handling time.
The uncertainty aspects of stowage planning for RoRo vessels have barely been re-
searched.
In [17], the effects of the uncertainty of cargo availability at the time of
planning for RoRo vessels are investigated. Their heuristic proved able to find good
solutions for small and medium instances, but for large instances, their heuristic could
not converge within their time limit.
To the best of this author’s knowledge, no research has been done to investigate the
effects of uncertain cargo weights for RoRo shipping. Some research has been done to
investigate this for container ships. In [18], a stochastic programming model was de-
veloped. Three different approaches were taken to solve the model. They developed
a robust optimisation model, a chance constraint model and a hybrid neighbourhood
search heuristic to solve the stochastic program. They defined three weight classes for
the cargo. Their tests showed that for small and medium instances, all three methods
were able to solve the problem. For larger instances and vessels, only the heuristic was
able to solve the problem. In [19], they introduce a vessel’s hydrostatic table and stress
moments for container ships. This has recently been included in [20] for RoRo vessels
and will also be used in this thesis.

3
RoRo shipping
In this chapter, first, the basics of RoRo shipping are introduced, and the specifics of
the problems investigated in this thesis are further explained. Then, the vessel and the
cargo used for testing are introduced.
3.1
RoRo shipping
A RoRo vessel is a freight vessel that transports rolling or wheeled cargo. The rolling
cargo allows the vessels to often have multiple decks, and loading is often fast and more
convenient than other maritime freight transportation methods, such as Lift-on Lift-off
(LoLo) ships, e.g. container ships. The cargo is either loaded by driving it onboard
(cars or trucks) or it is loaded by supporting machinery such as terminal trucks and/or
roll trailers [9]. The different decks can often be reached through internal ramps on the
vessel. When offloading, the cargo is quickly rolled off the vessel again. Furthermore,
the cargo can quickly be removed from the nearby area around the vessel, and trucks
can pick up their corresponding trailers and be on their way. As a RoRo vessel can have
multiple loading and unloading ports on one voyage, it is ideal that the cargo does not
block one another and that cargo shifts are minimised. A shift is when a cargo needs to
be temporarily moved or unloaded to make way for another piece of cargo. As shifts take
time, and therefore increase the time the vessels are at port, they are normally minimised
when creating stowage plans and have also been taking into account in other studies,
see Table 2.1. In this thesis, they are not relevant as we assume single-leg journeys.
The cargo transported by a RoRo vessel is typically heterogeneous.
While allowing
heterogeneous cargo is one of the advantages of using a RoRo vessel, it also increases
the diﬀiculty of creating an optimal stowage plan. A RoRo vessel has many different
restrictions, varying across each deck, that limit where a piece of cargo can be stowed.
Furthermore, the cargo may have special restrictions, such as being hazardous, requiring
cooling, or other limitations on where it can be stowed on the vessel. To simplify the
model and lower computation time, these are not handled in this thesis. However, the
model allows for easy modification to handle these as well.
Additionally, the vessel
has to be at a satisfactory level of seaworthiness.
A vessel’s seaworthiness depends
on the longitudinal stability, the transverse stability, and the vertical stability. This is
ensured by evenly distributing the cargo and by using ballast tanks filled with ballast
water. Furthermore, a loaded vessel is exposed to structural forces that can cause it to
be destroyed. Hence, shear and bending must be considered. These requirements will
further be explained in chapter 5. Each vessel has a hydrostatic table, which is used
to determine the seaworthiness of the vessel. A hydrostatic table is a large table with
precomputed values that describes the vessel’s buoyancy characteristics and helps to
determine the stability of the vessel [23].
Creating the stowage plan at a harbour and loading the vessel is a complicated process.
This procedure will now be broadly described, as it is meant to motivate the research

done into automatic stowage planning. The procedure is from DFDS (Det Forenede
Dampskibs-Selskab). The main person responsible for the stowage plan is person a in a
loading terminal oﬀice. Here, person a uses LoadMaster, see Figure 3.1, to place cargo
and create the stowage plan. Person a communicates with people loading the vessel,
where there is also a main responsible on-deck, person b. LoadMaster is a program used
to calculate the stability of a vessel, depending on where the cargo is placed and its
weight. Furthermore, LoadMaster uses input from the measurements of the vessel, i.e.
how low the vessel is in the water, and the hydrostatic table to calculate seaworthiness.
This means person a can try to create a stable stowage plan by distributing heavy and
light cargo evenly across the vessel. However, there is no other software to guide them
in creating an optimal stowage plan and minimising ballast water usage. The loading
personnel know the weight of the cargo, or at least an estimate of it, but this knowledge
is not always utilised to create the stowage plan. Furthermore, not all of the cargo is
ready to be stowed simultaneously. This means that person a could decide to stow a
piece of cargo, before it is ready to be stowed. In this thesis, we assume that all of the
cargo is ready to be stowed when creating the stowage plan1. The stowage plan is thus
created during loading of the vessel and is certainly not an optimal plan. The effect
of this suboptimal stowage plan and the need for a better system for creating such a
plan have been of interest since the RoRo stowage planning problem was introduced in
[4]. Previous work has assumed that the weight of the cargo was known when creating
a stowage plan. However, as this might not be the case, this thesis builds on previous
models and ideas to investigate the effect on the stowage plan when the weight of the
cargo is unknown. Furthermore, the seaworthiness described in earlier works on RoRo
stowage planning is developed to more accurately fit a real-world application and include
a vessel’s hydrostatic table.

3.2
Vessel representation
In this thesis, we consider the RSSP as a single port-to-port voyage. The vessel consists
of multiple decks with fixed deck heights. To represent where the cargo is placed, we use
the hybrid-slot representation, introduced in [21] and further used in [20]. For each type
of cargo, a grid of slots is created for each feasible spot where that specific cargo type can
be stowed on the vessel. This allows for creating cargo type-specific slots exactly where
there is room for that cargo type on the vessel. This is an excellent advantage for RoRo
vessels, as generally the decks are not similar, and usually a deck has something, e.g.
pillars or excluded areas, that obstructs a lane or grids in the lane/grid representations.
Furthermore, the model is not dependent on deciding lane width or grid size, and the
slots can be precomputed. In [4], the MIP-model has to determine lane-width, and in
[10], they predetermine the granularity of the grid before running the model, and find
that it has a significant impact on the running time of the model. One drawback of the
hybrid-slot representation is that these slots will have to be found for each cargo type,
and the complexity of the model therefore quickly increases as the number of different
cargo types increases.

When modelling using the hybrid-slot representation, the slots need to be precomputed.
This is done for every different size or type of cargo. Depending on how many different
types of cargo that need to be loaded, this can, of course, become many sets of slots/a
large list of feasible slots for every kind of cargo. However, these slots only need to
be computed once for each vessel and cargo type and can be saved for future use.
Furthermore, some cargo can be generalised to the same size, i.e. personal cars, and
often what type of cargo needs to be shipped is recurrent, i.e. cars, trucks, secu-boxes.
The different types of cargo handled in this thesis will be presented in section 3.3.
The precomputed hybrid-slot representation also saves more precise information about
each slot, which, depending on what is desired, could be useful when modelling the
RSSP. For example, does this slot allow the cargo to be cooled, i.e., does it have access
to power?
In addition, the representation also enables the precise determination of
the longitudinal, transverse, and vertical centre of gravity for a slot, allowing for more
accurate calculations of the vessel’s seaworthiness. This is used in the seaworthiness
constraints introduced in chapter 5.

3.3.3

Stochastic programming
The goal of this thesis is to examine the effects of the uncertainty of cargo weight on
stowage plans. A way to handle uncertainty in linear programming is using two-stage
stochastic programming. This chapter will introduce two-stage stochastic programming
and other essential terms used in stochastic programming. Furthermore, scenario gen-
eration and reduction are introduced.
Stochastic programming is used when not all information is available when a decision has
to be made. This information is normally parameters such as demand, cost, or arrival
times, see [17]. A simple approach for handling uncertainty is to estimate the missing
information and handle the problem deterministically with this estimation. A typical
method for estimating the uncertainty is to find the average of the values it could be.
Say there are three scenarios for how much a piece of cargo weighs: 1, 2, or 3 tons, then
the average would be 2 t., and a deterministic model could be solved using this value.
This problem is known as the expected value program. However, this doesn’t account
for the uncertainty, and one can only further investigate the problem by conducting a
sensitivity analysis, i.e., what happens to the solution if the average changes. Instead, we
use two-stage stochastic programs, which utilise the uncertainty and different scenarios
to create more flexible and robust solutions.
4.1
Two-stage stochastic programming
Two-stage stochastic programming is a stochastic programming method used when a
given problem can be separated into two stages and some of the model’s parameters are
uncertain, meaning a decision must be made while not all information is available. The
uncertain parameters are represented as discrete or continuous random variables. The
method optimises all possible values the random variables can take and thereby finds
the solution that achieves the best results across all possible scenarios. In the first stage
of a two-stage stochastic program, decisions have to be made while the realisation of
the random variables is not realised. At this stage, we must make the best decision,
taking into account every realisation of the uncertain parameters. In the second stage,
a realisation occurs and the uncertainty is revealed. We then have to decide what to do
given the realisation and the first stage decisions. The first stage decisions are called
here-and-now, symbolising that a decision must be made here and now, even without
knowing everything. The decisions in the second stage are called recourse actions, which
are the actions that you can take after the uncertainty is revealed.
In Figure 4.1 a general scenario tree can be seen. Each scenario represents a realisation
ω of the random variables, and πω is the probability of the scenario happening.

4.2
Scenario generation
A good solution from a two-stage model is mainly dependent on the scenarios. Depending
on which elements are uncertain and what values they can take, the two-stage model
and the number of scenarios quickly increase to exponentially large quantities or even an
infinite number of scenarios. A continuous random variable has infinitely many possible
outcomes, making it diﬀicult to generate a finite number of scenarios such that the two-
stage model can be solved within a feasible amount of time. In this thesis, the weight of
each piece of cargo is uncertain, hence, each piece of cargo is an uncertain parameter. If
RORO Stowage Planning Optimisation
16
==PAGE:24==
each cargo’s uncertainty is handled individually, it would lead to a substantial number
of scenarios. Say each cargo had three possible weights, and 25 cargoes needed to be
loaded. It would result in 325 scenarios, which is an unreasonable number of scenarios
for a solver to handle. The number of scenarios required to represent uncertainty varies
and depends on the problem and its corresponding model.
Different techniques can be used to create scenarios. It is common to utilise experts’
opinions or historical data to create more accurate scenarios. In this thesis, we use two
distinct methods to create scenarios. In both methods, we assume that the number of
cargo and the cargo type are known.
The first scenario generation method involves uniform sampling from the weight interval
of each cargo type, as shown in Table 3.1. To create a scenario, each cargo’s weight is
uniformly sampled from its corresponding weight interval. This method requires no prior
knowledge beyond the type of cargo that needs to be loaded. Although these scenarios
will not accurately reflect the cargo weight, they will test the robustness of the stowage
plans and determine if a precise scenario generation method is necessary.
The second scenario generation method utilises historical data on the differences between
the informed weight and the actual weight of the cargo, as shown in section 3.3. To create
a weight for a cargo of type Truck for a scenario, a random sample with replacement is
drawn from the historical difference between the informed weight and the actual weight
for Trucks. The sample is not drawn from all the data available for the Truck cargo type.
Instead, the historical data is split into quartiles based on the informed weight. This
is done based on the idea that the cargo weight difference is correlated to the informed
weight, see Figure 4.2. For the sample, the informed weight of the cargo is matched to the
corresponding quartile from the historical data. A random sampling with replacement is
then done from the difference in informed weight and actual weight, from the historical
data, within this quartile, which is then subtracted from the informed weight of the cargo.
Sampling with replacement makes no assumptions about the underlying distribution, but
with many samples, the samples will approximate the underlying distribution [33]. A

Seaworthiness
In this chapter, the seaworthiness, or stability, of the vessel and the corresponding con-
straints will be explained. The seaworthiness modelling in this thesis is from [20] and
[19], which further develops previously used stability constraints in [15].
The basics of a vessel’s seaworthiness are draft, displacement and buoyancy. Draft is the
vertical distance between the waterline and the bottom of the vessel (keel). Displacement
is the weight of the water displaced by the vessel and is equivalent to the vessel’s weight.
Buoyancy is the upward force acting on the vessel from the displaced water, and is the
force that makes the vessel float. These three parameters have a significant impact on
the seaworthiness of the vessel and are crucial for its operation. Given a displacement,
the draft and buoyancy can be determined from the vessel’s hydrostatic table as long
as the trim of the vessel is constant. The trim is the difference in the vessel’s draft at
the bow (front) and aft (end). In this thesis, we assume the trim to be 0, meaning we
assume that the vessel is level in the longitudinal axis. This is incorporated into the
constraints and their connection to the hydrostatic table. The vessel is separated into
sections, or frames, where the forces affecting the vessel can vary across these frames.
Hence, the forces are found for each frame.
To ensure the model is not too large, the vessel’s frames and corresponding parameters
are interpolated to 50 frames.
To ensure seaworthiness, the cargo must be placed so that draft, buoyancy, and structural
forces are satisfactory for each frame. To further stabilise the vessel, ballast tanks can be
filled with water. However, as ballast water is essentially extra weight, a good stowage
plan will ensure seaworthiness while minimising ballast water.
5.1
Centres of gravity
The centres of gravity are essential to determine the vessel’s seaworthiness. The centre of
gravity is split into three directions: longitudinal, transverse, and vertical. To calculate
these centres of gravity, the lightweight vessel’s centre of gravity, the cargo’s centre of
gravity, and the ballast tank’s centre of gravity are needed. The lightweight vessel’s
centre of gravity is the centre of gravity for the empty vessel. The cargo’s centre of
gravity is defined for each slot and is defined as the middle of the slot and half of the
corresponding cargo’s height. Hence, each cargo’s weight is assumed to be uniformly
distributed in its slot.
The ballast tanks’ centres of gravity in the longitudinal and transverse directions are
similar. Hence, the centre of gravity for these directions is in the middle of the plane of
the tank. For the ballast tanks’ vertical centre of gravity, this assumption does not hold.
When the tanks are filled with water, the vertical centre of gravity changes depending
on the water level. In [15], the water levels were discretised to overcome this challenge.

Mathematical modelling
In this chapter, the deterministic and stochastic models are introduced for the RSSP.
The models use the seaworthiness assumptions and constraints introduced in chapter 5.
As mentioned, the hybrid-slot formulation was first introduced in [21] and further devel-
oped in [20]. The hybrid-slot model separates the deck into slots distinct for each cargo
type and ensures, with constraints, that two cargoes are not placed in two slots that
overlap. The hybrid slots have to be preprocessed. The strength of this method lies in
its performance eﬀiciency, as preprocessing reduces the number of variables. Further-
more, once the precise placement of each cargo is known, seaworthiness calculations are
more straightforward. This also allows for more safety constraints to be implemented, if
wanted, see [21].
6.1
Deterministic model
The deterministic model for the RSSP problem assumes that the weight of the cargo is
known, and the results from this model are used to compare with those from the stochas-
tic models. The RSSP aims to minimise ballast water needed while also maximising the
amount of cargo stowed. Similarly to other research in RoRo shipping, we assume that
it is always more beneficial to load a piece of cargo than not to load it if it would use less
ballast water. Hence, the primary goal is to load the maximum amount of cargo, and
the secondary goal is to stow the cargo in an optimal way that minimises the amount of
ballast water needed.

6.2
Stochastic model
We will now formulate the two-stage stochastic programming model for the RSSP. The
first stage variables determine the decisions that need to be made before the uncertainty,
such as the weight of the cargo, is known. The first stage variables are ac,s and xc, as
these determine if and where a cargo is placed.
The second stage decisions are the
decisions made after the uncertainty is known. These are all the decisions connected to
the seaworthiness of the vessel. In this stage, the indices for the hydrostatic table are
chosen, and the amount of ballast water needed to reach seaworthiness for the vessel is
found.
The RSSP with uncertain weight could be seen as a multistage model, with each cargo
loading being a stage. When each cargo is loaded, its weight or effect on the vessel’s
seaworthiness is revealed. However, this is not a feasible method for two reasons. Firstly,
the model would be too large to solve within a reasonable computational time. Secondly,
this method would require the effect of each cargo on the seaworthiness to be measurable,
which is unclear if it is. For these reasons, the two-stage model is used instead.
The stochastic model is similar to the deterministic model, but has an additional set,
parameters and decision variables. In Table 6.4, Table 6.5, and Table 6.6 are the set,
parameters and decision variables which differ from the ones used in the determinis-
tic model. Otherwise, the stochastic model uses the same sets and parameters as the
deterministic model. The two-stage stochastic model can be seen in (6.22)-(6.53)

Scenarios

method is uniform random sampling from a weight interval. This method uses no in-
formation from the instances other than the cargo type. Hence, the scenarios are not

7.2.1
Stability of optimal solutions
For each instance, the stability of the optimal solutions, or the best ones found, is
tested in terms of feasibility. This is achieved by fixing the placement but changing
the weight of the cargo.
A model is then solved to find the optimal distribution of
ballast water, such that the seaworthiness and deck constraints introduced in section 5
are satisfied. For each instance, new cargo weight scenarios were created to test if the
optimal deterministic placement would still be feasible. If the deterministic placement
is infeasible with the new cargo weights, a slack model is used, where the seaworthiness,
deck weight limit, and ballast tank capacity constraints are slackened, to determine the
limiting factors. The results for Finlandia can be seen in Table 7.3. 10 scenarios for
each scenario generation method were created and tested. This was done since the two
methods generally create different kinds of scenarios. As scenario generation method 1
generally creates heavier cargo scenarios for lighter instances, and scenario generation
method 2 generally creates lighter cargo scenarios for heavier instances, the effects of
over- and underestimating the weight can be observed. For scenario generation method 2,
all of the tests for each instance were feasible. This means that if the cargo is lighter than
expected, the vessel can still maintain seaworthiness, however, the average amount of
ballast water required to reach seaworthiness is still larger than the deterministic solution
with original weights. Furthermore, if the cargo weight is slightly underestimated, the
solution remains feasible.
For scenario generation method 1, fewer tests resulted in
feasible solutions.
Every instance with ”fewer” cargo pieces was successful in every
scenario. For the instances with ”more” cargo, the tests were rarely feasible. Slack model
tests revealed that the infeasibility was due to the deck weight limit being violated on
deck 1, which is the upper deck on Finlandia. The vessel is otherwise still able to reach
seaworthiness and satisfy the other constraints. In Figure 7.5, Figure 7.6, Figure 7.7, and
Figure 7.8, the stowage plans for the deterministic solution are shown, and Figure 7.9,
Figure 7.10, and Figure 7.11 show the total cargo weight across the three decks for
different tests and instances. For the larger instances, more cargo is naturally stowed
on all of the decks. For deck 1, this results in the weight limit being violated when
changing the cargo weights. Even for the heavier instances, e.g. instance 4, for which
the scenarios from scenario generation method 1 more accurately estimate the total cargo
weight, see Table 7.3 or Table 7.2, the tests were still only feasible 3/10 times. This is
due to the weight limit for the decks almost being reached by the deterministic solution,
see Figure 7.12. Changing the cargo weight significantly impacts the total weight on the
decks, and if these are already close to fully packed, the weight limit is easily crossed.
These weight limit issues are only a problem for deck 1 and for large or heavy instances.
The other decks have a higher weight limit, and for smaller instances, the decks are not
fully packed, which reduces the impact of the cargo’s weight uncertainty on the feasibility
of the problem.

7.2.2
Random Stowage plan
To test the versatility and seaworthiness of the vessel, a series of random stowage plans
were made. To create the random stowage plans, an order of priority for which cargo
types to stow was made. This was done to test whether the order of what type of cargo
is prioritised has an influence on the seaworthiness of the vessel. Three different orders
were created and tested 10 times on each instance on Finlandia. The orders were [Truck,
Car, Heavy Machinery, Secu-box], [Car, Truck, Heavy Machinery, Secu.-box], and [Secu-
box, Truck, Car, Heavy Machinery]. For each cargo, in the corresponding sorted order
of cargo types, a slot was assigned to said cargo from a list of all the remaining feasible
slots. It was then tested whether this random stowage plan could be feasible, and what
the optimal stowage plan would be with only the loaded cargo from the random stowage
plan, i.e. if the initial random stowage plan could be optimised further by moving the
cargo. The results can be seen in Table 7.4. A few tests resulted in infeasible stowage
plans, where cargo had to be moved to make the stowage plan feasible. Again, these
plans were infeasible due to the weight limit being violated on deck 1. Instance 8 had
the most tests resulting in infeasible stowage plans, as this instance has the heaviest
cargo and has the most amount of trucks, heavy machinery, and secu-boxes. This leads
to a higher risk of placing too much heavy cargo on deck 1. This can also be seen for
order 3, where the Secu-boxes are placed first. This is the heaviest type of cargo, and
the random stowage plans violated the deck limit for deck 1 in every test, for instance 8.
The random stowage plans for order 2, cars first, for instances 1-4 have no or very little
difference in average ballast water compared to the optimal stowage plan. Furthermore,
the random stowage plan almost exclusively stowed cars, with no slots being free for other
types of cargo. This indicates that when stowing fewer lighter cargo, the stowage plan
is not important, as the random stowage plan was optimal, or close to being optimal.
Furthermore, the optimal distribution of ballast water is not unique, and there are
multiple different stowage plans, for some instances, having the same objective value, as
shown in Figure 7.13. Further testing was conducted to determine the reason why the
stowage plans were insignificant for some instances. A slack model where the ballast
tanks were required to be empty, i.e., no ballast water was allowed, was tested. The
results indicated that the vessel is not deep enough in the water to attain seaworthiness.
Testing on an empty vessel further verified this. On an empty vessel, i.e. no cargo,
ballast water is still needed to satisfy the stability constraints. In total, 1553.19 tonnes of
ballast water are required to submerge the vessel to a depth satisfying the seaworthiness
constraint. In Table 7.5, the total cargo weight and ballast weight can be seen for order
1, instance 1, and order 2, instance 1 and 2. For all of these tests, the total cargo and
ballast weight is 1553.19 tonnes, which is equal to the amount of ballast water needed for
an empty vessel to be feasible. Hence, for random plans with lighter cargo, i.e., primarily
cars, the stowage plan does not impact the vessel’s seaworthiness or the amount of ballast
water needed to correct it. This is not the case for heavier cargo. For both Orders 1 and
3, several hundred tonnes of ballast water can be saved in multiple of the instances.

Figure 7.16 displays the mean solving time for both types of scenario generation methods.

instances 2, 4, 6, and 8. For scenario generation method 1, the models were able to reach
optimality for 10 scenarios in the other instances, however, when using more scenarios,
a greater number of tests reached the time limit. Similarly, the models using scenario
generation method 2 were able to solve instances 1, 3, 5, and 7 within the time limit.
The time to solve the model was increased by a significantly smaller amount when
more scenarios were used. This could indicate that the scenarios, when using scenario
generation method 2, are too similar, hence, increasing the number of scenarios doesn’t
significantly affect the model.
To test if there is any significant difference between heavy and light instances, a two-
sample t-test is performed on the gaps for each number of scenarios.
This will test
whether there is a significant difference in the mean gap for each instance with varying

is conducted between instances that didn’t reach optimality within the time limit, i.e.,

or very close to zero across instances. Furthermore, scenario generation method 1 does

comparing the gap between these instances for this method. The p-values and the 95%

than for the lighter instance. For instances 6 and 8, only for 30 scenarios was the p-value

7.3.2
Stability of solutions and variation of results based on scenario
quantity
Different numbers of scenarios were tested to determine how many are needed before the
objective value stabilises and has a low variation. A larger number of scenarios increases
the complexity of the stochastic models, see Figure 7.19. For both scenario generation
methods, the solution found is generally better with fewer scenarios, stowing more cargo
and using less ballast water. This is expected as the problem is easier to solve for fewer
scenarios, thereby making it easier to find a solution within the time limit.
In Figure 7.14 and Figure 7.15, the average objective value and the 95% confidence
intervals are shown. It is somewhat challenging to analyse these results, as the time limit
affects the tests such that, for most instances, tests with 10 scenarios are close to being
optimal, and tests with 50 scenarios often result in significantly larger gaps than those
with 10 scenarios. These gaps also appear to fluctuate more, resulting in wider confidence
intervals for the objective value. Ideally, the objective value will increase as the number of
scenarios increases, until it reaches a relatively constant level, indicating that this number
of scenarios is needed to fully capture the uncertainty in the problem. Furthermore, it
is expected that the confidence interval will shrink as the number of scenarios increases.
Contrary to this, for a lot of the instances, the confidence interval expands as the number
of scenarios increases. This could be because, as the complexity of the problem increases,
the gap also increases, and the diversity in solutions found within the time limit increases.
In Figure 7.17, the mean standard deviation across all instances is shown, and Figure 7.18
also shows the mean standard deviation but only across instances 2 and 4. These figures
suggest that for challenging instances, some stability in the solution can be achieved by
using 40 scenarios. Otherwise, the solutions found when using 10 scenarios are stable
with small confidence intervals. Similar seaworthiness tests as in subsection 7.2.1, but
only checking feasibility, were made on the solutions for each stowage plan. The results
can be seen in section A.2. The results show that for instances 6 and 8, the vessel could

Discussion and future work
The estimation of ˆmV,T
t
, which is the parameter for the ballast water’s contribution to
the vertical centre of gravity, used in this thesis, turned out to severely underestimate
the ballast water’s contribution to the vertical centre of gravity. The vertical centre of
gravity changes as the ballast tank gets increasingly fuller. A way to handle this is by
discretising the fullness of the tank into levels. This is what is done in [15]. In this
thesis, an attempt is made to linearise the contribution, but the contribution from the
ballast water to the vertical centre of gravity instead became close to irrelevant for the
model. To estimate the implications of this, the deterministic model was tested with a
different ˆmV,T
t
, and the results can be seen in A.4. In these tests, instead of attempting
to linearise the vertical centre of gravity for the ballast tanks, it was set to a constant
parameter similar to the transverse and longitudinal centres of gravity for the ballast
tanks. In the tests, the contribution of the vertical centre of gravity for the ballast water
is defined as the maximal vertical centre of gravity, i.e. if a tank were full, essentially
overestimating the ballast water’s contribution to the centre of gravity.

When comparing the solutions using the two different ways to estimate the contribution
of ballast water for the vertical centre of gravity, in all but one instance, the solutions
were identical, using the same amount of ballast water and loading the same number of
cargo. The instance that did not result in the same solution was instance 4. However,
as this instance is not fully solved to optimality in both cases the solutions might end
up identical if the model was given more time1.
The two different estimates of ˆmV,T are essentially the two extremes of how much the
ballast water contributes to the vertical centre of gravity. With the estimate used in
this thesis, the contribution is severely underestimated, and with the other estimate
the contribution is overestimated. Since the solutions using the two estimations are the
same, the ballast water’s contribution to the vertical centre of gravity must, in general,
not be significant for the model. This also indicates that the reason an empty vessel
needs ballast water to be seaworthy is not due to the vertical centre of gravity of the
vessel, but rather that some other constraints limit the empty vessel. This could be both
the longitudinal and transverse centre of gravity, if the vessel is not balanced in these
directions when empty, but it could also be the shear force limiting the empty vessel’s
seaworthiness.
As the solutions are the same, we argue that the results from the test are still helpful and
something can be learned from them, however, in the future, a more accurate estimation
of ˆmV,T should be made, and the test should be done again to validate the results.

Testing the stability of the deterministic solutions revealed a weakness in the current
stowage plans for the vessel Finlandia when creating stowage plans for a large amount of
cargo. For large instances, the stowage plans utilised most of the decks. However, since
the deck weight limit on deck 1 is significantly lower than the rest of the decks, this led to
infeasibility if the cargo were slightly heavier than initially thought, and in some cases,
even if the total weight of the cargo was less than the actual total cargo weight. This
may be specific to the vessel tested, Finlandia, however, it emphasises the necessity of
this thesis, and ideally, similar tests should be conducted on other vessels to determine if
this is a recurring problem. Similar tests on other vessels might also reveal other weak-
nesses unique to those vessels. These tests didn’t reveal any other weaknesses, indicating
that the centres of gravities and the shear and bending forces are not limiting factors of
the vessel. Furthermore, the change in ballast water was, on average, around 100 −200
tonnes independently of whether the cargo weight was underestimated or overestimated.
This indicates that, apart from the deck weight limit, the vessel is quite versatile and
able to adapt and become seaworthy even if the cargo weight changes significantly.
The significance of a good stowage plan was investigated in subsection 7.2.2 where ran-
dom plans were compared to optimised plans. The results showed that in some cases,
several hundred tonnes of ballast water could be saved. Furthermore, the results show
that when transporting the right amount of cargo, close to or no ballast water is needed
for the vessel to be seaworthy. The random plans were mostly feasible, however, if heavy
cargo were prioritised and randomly placed first, the plans could become infeasible. This
reaﬀirms the need for a good stowage plan.
First, the stochastic models were tested with both scenario generation methods using
10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 scenarios. For most instances, the models could not be solved to
optimality or close enough to optimality to be useful when using more than 10 scenar-
ios. It was therefore decided to use 10 scenarios in further testing. However, some of
the tests indicated that, if possible, more than 10 scenarios should be used before the
stochastic models fully capture all the uncertainty in the problem. Further testing should
be conducted, increasing the number of scenarios and time limit to fully determine the
optimal number of scenarios to use. The results showed that for some types of instances,
the model was able to solve the problem and stow all the cargo. If the instances were
too large, the model could not solve them within the time limit. For large, heavy in-
stances both scenario generation methods’ solutions turned infeasible often, indicating
that a more precise scenario generation method is needed to ensure that these types of
instances can be solved in the future. This can also be solved by using more scenarios,
but as this significantly decreases the solution quality, it is more recommended to try to
optimise the scenario generation.
The instances the stochastic model was able to solve had positive VSS, indicating that
the stochastic solution performs good compared to the expected value program. How-
ever, for several of the diﬀicult instances the VSS was negative. This is thought to be
due to the stochastic models not reaching a low enough gap within the time limit. Sim-
ilarly the EVPI showed that a little was to be gained for the instances the stochastic
model was able to solve within the time limit, while for the other instances, a lot could
RORO Stowage Planning Optimisation
70
==PAGE:78==
be gained by having perfect information about the cargo weights.
A scenario reduction method was proposed to see if it could improve the results for
the instances. The scenario reduction tests achieved that all instances got a gap lower
than 1% within the time limit. The number of cargo the model was able to stow was
equal to the deterministic model for all instances except for instances 2 and 4. These
instances are the instances with the most cargo. This indicates that the model can not
handle too much uncertainty, i.e. too many uncertain cargo weights. Future work could
investigate the effect of handling the cargo weights for Cars deterministically, thereby
decreasing the complexity of the stochastic model significantly. As the Cars are the
lightest cargo, it is proposed that they have less impact on the vessel’s seaworthiness.
The Cars’ weight could be estimated or set to their actual weight. These solutions can be
compared, thereby further analysing their importance in the stowage planning problem.
The solutions, for instance 8, still had infeasible problems. An analysis of the scenario
reduction method showed that while the method reduced the distance to the determinis-
tic weights, the scenarios it generated were clustered close to each other, indicating that
some diversity in the scenarios might have been lost. Further development of a scenario
reduction method is recommended. This could involve using a different distance measure
or a different technique than clustering.
Since the tests all indicated that the limiting factor of both the deterministic and stochas-
tic tests was the weight limit on deck 1, tests were done increasing this weight limit.
The deterministic model only showed improvement in instance 8, indicating that the
weight limit on deck 1 does not significantly impact the deterministic problem. For the
stochastic tests, the weight limit had to be increased 40% before all solutions found were
feasible. This indicates that the generated scenarios need to be significantly improved
if the tests are to become feasible with the original deck weight limit, or the number of
scenarios should be increased.
Two different ways to generate scenarios were tested in this thesis. Scenario generation
method 1 generated random weights to ensure diversity in scenarios. The idea behind
this method was to ensure robustness and stability in the feasibility of the stochastic
solution, and to compare it with a more specialised method, scenario generation method
2. Scenario generation method 2 utilised historical data to more accurately generate
scenarios based on a historical trend in informed cargo weight. The sample, from the
historical weight difference between informed and actual cargo weight, was sampled
from quartiles. This was based on the idea that the difference in weight increased when
the informed weight increased. This is loosely supported by Figure 4.2, however, to
further optimise this scenario generation method, this relation should be researched fur-
ther. Furthermore, we sampled uniformly from these quartiles such that the sampled
set would estimate the original population, i.e., the quartile. However, this limits the
sampled values to only those represented in the historical data. Instead, one could fit a
distribution to the data. This was attempted, but we were unable to find a distribution
which could be fitted across the quartiles for both Trucks and Secu-boxes. Future work
could involve using a kernel density estimation instead. Additionally, to fully complete
the scenario generation method, some data is needed on Cars and Heavy machinery,

==PAGE:79==
as currently the method generates the weight randomly, similar to scenario generation
method 1. There was no clear difference in the performance of the two scenario gen-
eration methods, though in most cases, scenario generation method 2 used less ballast
water.
In this thesis, the time limit was set to one hour. For the deterministic model this was
long enough for all instances to reach optimality except for instances 2 and 4, which are
large instances with all four types of cargo. In these instances, there are more cargo
than there is feasible space for on the vessel. This makes it diﬀicult for the model to
optimise which cargo should be stowed to minimise ballast water simultaneously. This
diﬀiculty was even more significant for the stochastic tests, where most models could
not reach optimality before the time limit. Furthermore, Finlandia is a relatively small
RoRo vessel with only three decks, and a larger vessel would make the RSSP even harder
to solve. In addition, if the RSSP was extended to also include multiple ports, no cargo
shifts, stochastic arrival times, or other aspects of the RSSP previously investigated by
researchers, see Table 2.1, it is highly likely that a time limit of one hour would not be
long enough time to get any feasible results. Hence, if the stochastic models from this
thesis should be used, the time limit should be reevaluated and likely be increased. If a
higher time limit is deemed impractical, which is highly likely in a realistic setting [39],
we recommend a development of a heuristic that is able to solve the models quicker. A
heuristic using the hybrid-grid representation has, contrary to the other representations,
not been developed yet. If such a heuristic were made, larger instances and vessels could
be tested and investigated to determine whether or not the stochasticity of the cargo
weight affects these.

Conclusion
In this thesis, we investigated how the uncertainty of cargo weights affects RoRo vessels’
stowage plans, with the goal of maximising the amount of cargo stowed while minimis-
ing the ballast water needed to ensure the vessel’s seaworthiness. A new hybrid-slot
representation was used to model the problem. Furthermore, by linearising the ballast
water’s contribution to the vertical centre of gravity, a contribution was made to the
previously developed models. It was discovered that the ballast water’s contribution to
the centre of gravity was insignificant.
Eight test instances were used, each with varying weight and cargo type combinations.
The deterministic optimal stowage plans turned out to be rather sensitive, and changing
the cargo weight made several of them infeasible. This was due to the deck weight limits
on the tested vessel Finlandia. This was the only weakness found in the stowage planning
that might make the stowage plans infeasible. Otherwise, the vessel was always able to
obtain seaworthiness.
A two-stage stochastic model was developed.
The results showed that the stowage
plans were more robust than the deterministic ones.
However, the stochastic model
still struggled to find feasible solutions for instances with a lot of heavy cargo. This
indicates that more accurate scenarios for the stochastic model were needed. It was
found that the deck weight limit had to be increased 40% before all solutions were
feasible. For instances with a large amount of cargo, the stochastic model could not find
a low enough gap within the time limit, indicating that if similar investigations were
made on larger vessels, a heuristic or a higher time limit might be necessary to obtain
reasonable results. Furthermore, to reduce the complexity of the stochastic model, it
was proposed for future research to investigate the effect of the uncertainty of Cars. If
this is effect is not significant, the number of uncertain parameters could be reduced
significantly.
The value of the stochastic program was evaluated, and it was found that in the cases
where the stochastic model was able to find a low enough objective value gap, it was
beneficial to use this compared to the corresponding expected value program. However, if
the instances were too large and complex to solve, the stochastic models could not reach
a low enough gap for the objective value, and the expected value program performed
better. The value of perfect information was also found. It was found that this value
was rather low for the instances where the stochastic model reached optimality, where
the differences in objective values were between 0 and 200. For instances, where the
stochastic model could not reach a good solution within the time limit, the value of
perfect information was much higher.
10 scenarios were proposed to use as the stochastic models could not find a low enough
objective value gap for a higher number of scenarios. Some tests indicated that more
stability in the stochastic solution could be gained if more scenarios were used. Using
more scenarios could also result in more feasible stowage plans.

Two different ways to generate scenarios and a clustering-based scenario reduction
method were proposed. Scenario generation method 1 randomly generated weights for
the cargo from a weight interval, and scenario generation method 2 utilised historical
data and was found to generate scenarios more accurately. However, tests found no
significant difference in the performance of the two methods in the stochastic models.
The scenario reduction method significantly decreased the objective value gaps for the
two largest instances. The scenario reduction method was evaluated, and it was found
that the clusters were not clearly separated from each other. Further research and devel-
opment are recommended to improve both the scenario reduction method and scenario
generation method 2.
Creating a stowage plan and loading a vessel is a complex task that involves many
practical considerations beyond the scope of this thesis. This thesis found a weakness in
the form of deck weight limits in the current creation of optimal stowage plans, which
should be investigated if a stowage plan optimiser should become part of the standard
procedure for RoRo vessels and shipping companies. If the cargo weight is uncertain,
the deck 1 weight limit severely increases the risks of creating infeasible stowage plans.
It is unclear if this is a recurrent weakness for other RoRo vessels or if it is unique to
the vessel used in this thesis. We propose conducting similar tests, as presented in this
thesis, on different vessels to verify whether the findings hold and ensure that optimal
stowage plans remain feasible.
