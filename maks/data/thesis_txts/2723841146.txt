Abstract  
 
Anorexia Nervosa (AN) is an eating disorder characterized by an unnatural low body weight, 
an intense fear of gaining weight and an abnormal perception of weight. Affected people have 
a distorted view of their body highly value controlling their weight. However, the causes are 
largely unknown, and the treatment options are limited. Recent studies have reported that the 
microbiota of AN patients remains distinct from non-eating disorder controls suggesting that 
clinical renourishment does not return the gut microbiota to a healthy state. As relapse rates 
for AN are high, it is tempting to speculate that continued attention to the gut microbiota may 
play a role in maintaining healthy weight after renourishment of patients.  The gut microbiota 
is known to play a fundamental role in promoting homeostatic functions. Emerging evidence 
points to a bidirectional communication between the brain and gut microbiota. This pathway 
is known as the gut-microbiota-brain-axis. Therefore, the impact of the gut microbiota from an 
AN patient in recipient mice was investigated.  
80 BALB/cjTac mice were treated with broad-spectrum antibiotics for 2 weeks to deplete their 
existing gut microbiota. Then the mice were colonized with gut microbiota with either an 
anorexic donor (AN mice, n = 40) or healthy control donor (CO mice, n = 40) weekly for 5 
weeks. Half of each of these mice were fed ad libitum (ad lib mice, n = 40) while the other half 
was starved twice a week for 10 hours (starved mice, n =40). Half of the mice of each four 
groups were female (n = 40), the other half were male mice (n = 40). This results in 8 
experimental groups with each 10 mice. Body weight gain and food intake were measured 
through the FMT treatment. To examine possible effects on the gut microbiota, fecal and 
cecum samples were sequenced with the 16S rRNA method.  
During FMT treatment, there was no difference in food intake between AN recipient and CO 
recipient mice. Female AN mice ate statistically significant less when starved compared to 
female AN mice that were fed ad libitum. This observation did not occur in male mice or the 
CO groups. AN mice refed significantly more within 2 hours after the starvation periods. The 
sequencing results indicates a difference in gut microbiota between FMT groups as well as 
feeding pattern in the fecal samples after FMT treatment. There were also differences in the 
taxonomic composition of samples of AN and CO mice.  
In conclusion, gut microbiota from anorexic patients did not affect the food intake or body 
weight in recipient mice. However, we provide new data regarding the impact of periodically 
forced starvation on FMT receiving mice. In addition, the gut microbiota was compared 
between groups.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Anorexia nervosa  
Anorexia nervosa (AN) is an eating disorder characterized by an unnatural low body weight, 
an intense fear of gaining weight and an abnormal perception of weight. Affected people have 
a distorted view of their body and place a high value on controlling their weight and shape. 
Combining information from several sources, Eric Stice and Cara Bohon (2012) suggest that 
between 0.9% and 2.0% of girls and women and between 0.1% and 0.3% of boys and men 
experience anorexia nervosa during their lifetimes1.  
Many people with AN also suffer from depression and anxiety. A study of more than 2400 
individuals hospitalized for an eating disorder found that 92% had a depressive disorder and 
56% were diagnosed with anxiety disorder2. There is also a link between AN and other 
conditions like obsessive-compulsive personality disorder, borderline personality disorder, and 
obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)3,4.  
AN can cause serious physical complications5. In the chronic phase, nearly every organ 
system can be impacted due to malnutrition and purging behavior, resulting in gastrointestinal, 
cardiovascular, and immunological diseases, as well as in metabolic abnormalities6. Due to 
the health issues and mental stress, people with AN have a higher risk of death, with a 
standardized mortality rate of 5.2 and are 18.1 times more likely to die of suicide7. Men with 
AN are at a higher risk of dying than women, partly due to later diagnosis and social stigmas8.   
1.2. 
Communication between gut and brain  
Gut to Brain Communication  
The gut is often described as our second mind and not without reason. The gut microbiota 
(GM) are the collective microorganisms in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. It includes bacteria, 
viruses, archaea, protists and fungi, as well as the genetic material of these microorganisms. 
Strong evidence suggests that gut microbiota has an important role in bidirectional interactions 
between the gut and the nervous system9. Studies on the gut-brain crosstalk have revealed a 
complex communication system that not only ensures the proper maintenance of 
gastrointestinal homeostasis but is likely to have multiple effects on motivation and higher 
cognitive functions9.  
The bidirectional communication network includes the central nervous system (CNS), both 
brain and spinal cord, the automatic nervous system (ANS), the enteric nervous system (ENS) 
and the hypothalamic pituitary adrenal (HPA) axis.  
Studies conducted on germ-free animals have demonstrated that microbiota influences stress 
reactivity and anxiety-like behavior and regulates the set point for HPA activity10–14.  
==PAGE:9==
2 
 
Microbiota may interact with the gut brain axis (GBA) through different mechanisms. First, 
microbiota can interact with the GBA through the modulation of afferent sensory nerves, as 
reported for Lactobacillus reuteris15. As a result, gut motility and pain perception are altered15. 
Second, microbiota can influence ENS activity by producing molecules that can act as local 
neurotransmitters16. The ENS also represents the target of bacterial metabolites. One of the 
main products of bacterial metabolism are short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), such as butyric 
acid, propionic acid, and acetic acid. SCFAs stimulate the sympathetic nervous system17, 
mucosal serotonin release18 and influence memory and learning process19,20.  
The ability of gut microbiota to influence nutrient availability, as well as the close relationship 
between nutrient sensing and peptide secretion by enteroendocrine cells, suggest that 
microbiota-GBA interaction is possible. This interaction could also occur via the release of 
biologically active peptides from enteroendocrine cells, which can affect the GBA21. For 
example, galanin stimulates the activity of the central branch of the HBA axis. Ghrelin too 
possesses a cortisol-releasing effect in humans, and it is probably involved in the modulation 
of the HPA response to stress and nutritional/metabolic variations22.  
Lastly, microbiota affects mucosal immune activation9. The effects of microbiota on immune 
activation might be in part mediated by proteases23,24. These enzymes are upregulated in 
intestinal-immune mediated disorders and become the end-stage effectors of mucosal and 
enteric nervous damage23,24. Increased concentrations of proteases have been detected in 
fecal samples of patients with irritable bowel syndrome25,26.  
Brain to gut communication  
By studying communication from brain to gut, it has been discovered that different types of 
psychological stressors modulate the composition and total biomass of the enteric microbiota9. 
The microbiota is directly influenced by the brain-regulated secretion of signaling molecules 
in neurons, immune cells and enterochromaffin cells9.  
Communication between CNS effectors and bacteria relies on the presence of 
neurotransmitter receptors on the outer layer of bacteria. Several studies have reported that 
binding sites for enteric neurotransmitters produced by the host are present on bacteria, which 
can influence the function of components of the microbiota27.  
Aside from that, the brain has a prominent role in the modulation of gut functions, such as 
motility, secretion of acids, bicarbonates and mucus, intestinal fluid handling and mucosal 
immune response28. All these functions maintain the mucus layer and biofilm which allows 
individual groups of bacteria to grow in a variety of different microhabitats and metabolic 
niches associated with the mucosa28. Stress induces variations in size and quality of mucus 
==PAGE:10==
3 
 
secretion29. Furthermore, it promotes coeloconic activity by increasing the central release of 
corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF)30.  
The brain might also affect microbiota composition and function by altering intestinal 
permeability, allowing bacterial antigens to penetrate the epithelium and stimulate an immune 
response in the mucosa. Acute stress increases colonic paracellular permeability, involving 
overproduction of interferon-γ and decrease in mRNA expression of tight junction proteins31.  
The brain may also influence immune function through the automatic nervous system (ANS). 
The sympathetic branch modulates number, degranulation and activity of mast cells. 
Consequently, stress related muscle dysfunction leads to an imbalance in tryptase and 
histamine32. Other mast cell products, such as CRF can increase epithelial permeability to 
bacteria, which facilitates their access to immune cells in lamina propria33.  
Dysbiosis of the gut microbiota is characterized by the presence of pathobionts, low microbial 
beneficial bacteria, and low microbial diversity. It has been associated with several diseases, 
such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), asthma, obesity, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) 
and various neuropsychiatric disorders including anorexia nervosa34. 
1.3. 
Gut microbiome in anorexia nervosa  
Estimated, 500-1000 species of bacteria, archaea, fungi, viruses and eukaryotes, are present 
in the human GI system35. Together, the phyla Firmicutes (which includes Lactobacillus, 
Enterococcus, and Clostridium) and Bacteroidetes (which includes the Bacteroides genus) 
account for >90% of the intestinal population in healthy adults36. Actinobacteria and 
Proteobacteria are the next most prevalent phyla36.  
The composition and activities of the gut microbiome, and consequently, those of the bacterial 
metabolites, have been found to be significantly influenced by diet37. The composition of the 
gut microbiome is additionally influenced by host genetics, age, chronic illnesses, 
environmental microbial exposures, ethnicity, infections, medications, physical activity, sleep 
and stress38,39. Therefore, there are many factors that can potentially be the reason of a 
divergent gut microbiome in AN patients compared to healthy individuals40.  
Patients with AN typically have fewer number of total bacteria41. The bacterial composition of 
AN patients is significantly altered in compared to healthy individuals40. Higher amounts of 
Proteobacteria, Coriobacteriaceae and Enterobacteriaceae have been found42,43. AN patients 
have lower amounts of Lactobacillus, carbohydrate fermenter Ruminococcus43, and 
Roseburia, which produces butyrate43,44. Archaea levels in AN patients have been found to be 
elevated in several studies43–46. Methanobrevibacter smithii, a methane-producing archaeon, 
==PAGE:11==
4 
 
is the primary focus of research. Its abundance is thought to optimize calorie extraction from 
a diet with very low-calorie content, enabling the extraction of additional calories45,47.   
In healthy subjects, M. smithii contributes significantly to increasing the effectiveness of 
microbial fermentation45,47. The microbial fermentation results in the formation of SCFAs, 
which feed colon epithelial cells and have an impact on immunological responses and 
epithelial integrity39,48. Levels of total SCFAs are inconsistently reported in AN studies40. The 
production of SCFAs may facilitate the secretion of satiety hormones (peptide YY (PYY) and 
glucagon-like receptor (GLP-1)) expressed by the gut enteroendocrine cells49. This might be 
one of the biological mechanisms that the gut microbiota uses to influence hunger, satiety and 
eating habits, all of which have an impact on starvation. SCFAs, such as butyrate, have been 
proposed to increase serotonin and synthesis in the colon50. Another study confirmed that 
stool butyrate levels have an inversely correlation with depression symptoms43,51. 
When AN patients are admitted to the hospital, their total concentration of branched-chain 
fatty acids (BCFAs) is increased44,52. As BCFAs are byproducts of protein fermentation by 
colonic microbiota and are mostly generated from branched-chain amino acids, an increased 
level suggests enhanced protein fermentation in the gut.  
Furthermore, compared to healthy controls, AN patients had higher amounts of the mucin-
degrader Verrucomicrobia, particularly Akkermansia muciniphila44. A. muciniphila levels are 
associated with weight-loss53. Mucin degradation may compromise the integrity of the gut 
barrier and contribute to gastrointestinal symptoms. Moreover, mucin degradation may also 
have implications for the immune system and inflammation in AN. Mucins play a role in 
modulating the immune response and protecting against infections54. 
Significant differences in gut microbial composition have been reported between people with 
AN and normal or overweight individuals. However, further research is needed to determine 
whether the observed differences are a cause or a result of AN40.  
Three studies have reported changes in gut microbiome during nutritional rehabilitation and 
weight restoration in humans44,51,55. All three studies of Kleiman et al. (2015), Mack et al. 
(2016), and Fouladi (2022) show an increase of microbial diversity during weight restoration 
treatment44,51,55. Despite this increase, the AN group’s microbiota remained more similar to its 
own baseline microbiota than to that of the healthy control groups. The microbiota remains 
distinct to non-eating disorder control suggesting that initial clinical renourishment does not 
return the gut microbiota to a healthy state44,51,55. As relapse rates for AN are high56, it is 
tempting to speculate that continued attention to gut microbes may play a role in maintaining 
a healthy weight after discharge55. This emphasizes the need for a comprehensive treatment 
approach that considers additional factors40.  Further exploring the gut microbiota in individuals 
==PAGE:12==
5 
 
with AN will enhance our understanding of disordered eating behavior, dysregulated appetite, 
comorbid depression and anxiety40. 
1.4. 
Gut hormones and appetite  
Both gut microbiota and the CNS produce various neurotransmitters, neuroactive factors, 
neuropeptides, and hormones with appetite-stimulating (orexigenic) and appetite-suppressing 
(anorexigenic) effects57. 
The hypothalamus acts as the control center for hunger and satiety. Part of the hypothalamus, 
the arcuate nucleus (or, in humans, the infundibular nucleus), allows peripheral peptides and 
proteins to enter the brain through the blood-brain barrier. Inside the brain they directly interact 
with its neurons. These include neurons that co-express peptides that stimulate food intake 
and weight gain, specifically, neuropeptide Y (NPY) and agouti-related peptide (AgRP). Other 
linked neurons are expressing pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC) and cocaine- and amphetamine-
regulated transcript (CART) which inhibit feeding and promote weight loss. Together, these 
neurons and peptides control hunger and satiety, as well as weight gain and weight loss58. 
Leptin  
Leptin is an anorexigenic hormone that acts as a feedback mechanism in the brain, signaling 
to key regulatory centers to suppress food intake, regulate body weight and energy 
homeostasis59,60. It is produced primarily by adipose tissue and provides information about the 
body’s energy stores by binding to its hypothalamic leptin receptors61–64. By binding to its 
receptors, leptin influences the activity of various neurons as well as the expression of 
orexigenic and anorexigenic neuropeptides. Orexigenic peptides include NPY, melanin-
concentrating hormone, AgRP, galanin, orexin, and galanin-like peptide (GALP)64–69. 
Anorexigenic peptides whose expression appears to be modulated by leptin include POMC, 
CART, 
corticotropin-releasing 
hormone 
(CRH) 
and 
brain-derived 
neurotrophic 
factor61,63,64,70,71. 
Furthermore, one of the important mechanisms by which leptin may control food intake and 
body weight is the regulation of ghrelin’s effects on the hypothalamic neurons.   
Ghrelin  
Ghrelin’s impact on energy balance is partially mediated through the hypothalamus72. It 
counteracts the suppressing effects of leptin by modulating the expression of various 
hypothalamic peptides72. Ghrelin increases the activity of neurons expressing NPY, AgRP, 
and orexin73–75 and simultaneously suppresses POMC and CRH-producing neurons76. Ghrelin 
secretion in the stomach largely dependents on the nutritional state, with pre-meal increases 
and post-meal decreases observed77–79. Additionally, ghrelin levels show a daily variation that 
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
appears to be influenced by factors such as age, gender, BMI, growth hormone (GH), glucose 
and insulin77,78,80–87.   
The pre-meal increase in ghrelin levels was shown to correlate with hunger scores in healthy 
humans and initiated meals voluntarily in the absence of time- and food-related cues, serving 
as a short-term meal initiation signal88. Ghrelin might also play a role in long-term regulation 
of energy-balance. Peripheral daily administration of ghrelin in rodents induces adiposity by 
reducing fat utilization89. In humans, circulation of ghrelin concentration is negatively 
correlated with BMI, rising when obese individuals lose weight and decreasing when anorexia 
nervosa patients gain weight. This suggests that ghrelin levels change in response to dieting 
to maintain body weight82,83.  
Finally, ghrelin does not seem to be crucial for the maintenance of energy homeostasis. 
Ghrelin knockout mice have a normal body size, body consumption, bone density, growth rate, 
gastric emptying, food intake, reproduction, gross behavior, and tissue pathology90,91.  
Peptide YY 
The hormone, peptide YY (PYY), is produced by specialized enteroendocrine cells 
predominantly found in the distal GI tract92. PYY is secreted in conjunction with GLP-1 in 
response to food intake. Plasma levels are proportional to the energy content of a meal and 
peak one hour postprandially93.  
The concentration of PYY in circulation increases following a meal and decreases during 
periods of fasting. PYY exerts its anorectic effect by inhibiting the orexigenic NPY and 
therefore, increasing the activation of POMC expressing neurons94. This results in a delay in 
pancreatic and gastric secretion and gastric emptying, as well as in an increase in fluid and 
electrolyte absorption. Unlike leptin, obesity does not lead to a resistance towards the effects 
of PYY95.  
Insulin  
Insulin, produced by the pancreas, is a key metabolic hormone that acts as the first adiposity 
signal96. Upon food intake, insulin levels rise rapidly94. Upon crossing the blood-brain barrier, 
insulin acts as an anorexigenic signal, with NPY and melanocortin systems being identified as 
important downstream targets for its effects on food intake and body weight regulation96. 
Plasma insulin levels vary with positive or negative energy balance. In the brain, insulin 
stimulates anorexigenic α-MSH/CART neurons and inhibits orexigenic NPY/AgRP neurons. 
This leads to an activation of the catabolic pathway and suppression of the anabolic pathway96.  
==PAGE:14==
7 
 
The secretion of insulin by beta cells in the pancreas is regulated by glucose levels. Insulin 
secretion is increasing in response to elevated glucose levels and decreasing in response to 
low glucose levels. Insulin plays a key role in regulating the metabolism of fats, carbohydrates, 
and proteins by promoting the absorption of glucose into fat, liver, and skeletal cells97.  
Glucagon 
The anorexigenic effect of glucagon, resulting in satiety, is primarily mediated through the 
liver-brain axis98. The release of substantial amount of glucagon during meals supports its role 
in ending or inducing postprandial satiety99,100. The anorexigenic action of glucagon starts in 
the liver, which has the ability to detect glucagon levels in the portal vein and transmit this 
information via the vagal nerve fibers to the CNS101. The resulting neural signal is then 
transmitted to the hypothalamus. Glucagon being catabolic acts in opposite to insulin, 
promoting the breakdown of stored energy sources and therefore increasing the blood glucose 
and fatty acid concentrations.  
Glucagon-like peptide-1 
GLP-1 is an anorexigenic peptide hormone that is primarily produced by enteroendocrine L-
cells located in the distal ileum and colon. Upon secretion, GLP-1 delays gastric emptying and 
increases insulin secretion, leading to a decrease in blood glucose levels, while also inhibiting 
the secretion of glucagon94.  
Estradiol  
Estrogens play a significant role in regulating energy balance by modulating food intake and 
energy expenditure102,103. Estradiol activates anorexigenic POMC and CART neurons while 
inhibiting orexigenic NPY and AgRP neurons in the arcuate nucleus (ARC)104,105. Additionally, 
estradiol enhances the appetite-suppressing effect of Cholecystokinin (CCK) peptides 
released in response to food consumption106,107, while reducing the appetite-stimulating 
potency of the hormone ghrelin108.  
Studies using ovariectomized OVX mice, which are depleted of endogenous estrogens, 
demonstrated 
that 
OVX-induced 
hyperphagia 
could 
be 
corrected 
with 
estradiol 
replacement109. Notably, OVX mice that were fasted overnight showed two different responses 
to refeeding109. When non-treated, their refeeding was lower compared to control mice, but 
when treated with estradiol, their refeeding pattern was similar to that of control mice. This 
suggests, that endogenous estradiol is necessary for maintaining starvation induced 
refeeding109.

It is known that the decreased appetite and impaired response to fasting of women with AN is 
associated with an estrogen-deprived state of Amenorrhea (absence of menstrual period)110.  
Cholecystokinin  
Cholecystokinin (CCK) is an anorexigenic hormone that has been implicated in the regulation 
of short-term food intake. The hormone is produced in various regions of the GI tract and is 
also known to be present in the CNS. CCK has been shown to slow down gastric emptying 
and decrease energy intake by promoting feeling of satiety111.

1.5. 
Anorexia nervosa in rodent models  
Several animal models have been developed to study the underlying pathophysiology of AN 
and to explore possible treatment options. The most commonly used model is the activity-
based anorexia (ABA) model, in which young rodents are subjected to restricted food access 
and unrestricted access to a running wheel, usually leading to significant weight loss113. Both 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
male and female ABA rats exhibit hyperactivity and reduced anxiety-like behavior compared 
to control rats in tests such as open field and elevated plus maze114,115. The ABA model has 
been utilized to examine the acute phase of AN but not long-term effects of chronic AN. To 
address this issue, a chronic ABA model has been established where the low body weight is 
maintained after the initial weight reduction. These ABA models have provided insights into 
the common traits of AN such as hyperactivity, low body weight, alterations in the HPA axis 
and cessation of the estrous cycle in females116.     
A recent study investigated the gut microbiota alterations in an animal model of AN117. The 
study utilized an ABA mouse model and characterized gut microbiota alterations using a 
combination of 16S rRNA gene sequencing and quantitative PCR analysis of targeted genera 
or species. Results showed increased levels of Clostridium cocleatum and several 
Lactobacillus species and decreased levels of Burkholderiales compared to control mice117. 
The observed gut microbiota alterations were primarily attributed to food restriction and not 
physical activity. Additionally, several bacterial groups were identified that showed correlation 
with mice body weight, food intake, lean and fat masses, and hypothalamic mRNA levels of 
NPY and POMC117. 
However, the limitations of ABA models are that they do not take into account psychological 
factors and genetic predisposition, leading only to a partial understanding of the 
pathophysiology of AN. To further clarify the role of genetics in AN, a genetic Anx/anx model 
has been developed. This model is based on the discovery of an autosomal recessive 
mutation called anx in mice by Maltais et al. in 1984118. Mice carrying the anx/anx mutation 
develop growth failure, low body weight and neurological motor disturbances, and typically die 
from severe malnutrition between 3-5 weeks of age118. Gene expression profiling in the 
hypothalamus of anx/anx mice have been revealed an enrichment of deregulated genes 
involved in cell death, cell morphology and cancer, as well as alterations in signaling circuits 
related to energy balance, including NPY and melanocortin signaling119. Furthermore, anx/anx 
mice have significantly lower serum leptin levels compared to their normal littermates120.  
To date, only two research groups have investigated the impact of fecal microbiota 
transplantations (FMT) from human AN patients on germ-free mice. However, the results of 
these two studies are inconsistent; Hata et al. (2019) reported that the gut microbiota from AN 
patients decreased body weight in germ-free mice121, while Glenny et al. (2021) found no 
influence on body weight122.  
1.6. 
Preliminary results  
In 2021, Østergaard et al. conducted a study to test the possibility of transferring 
characteristics relating to human AN disease into mice123. This was done by transferring gut 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
microbiota from AN donors by FMT. 48 specific-pathogen free female BALB/cjTac mice were 
colonized with either gut microbiota from AN donors (n = 24) or healthy control donors (n = 24) 
weekly for 5 weeks (FMT 1). Three different AN donors and three different control (CO) donors 
were used. A second FMT period (FMT 2) was conducted. One half (n = 24) were colonized 
with the same gut microbiota as they received in FMT 1 (n(COCO) = 12, n(ANAN) = 12). The 
other half was colonized with the opposite gut microbiota as they received during FMT 1. Non 
germ-free mice were used for this experiment. However, mice were treated with antibiotics 
two weeks before FMT treatment. As a result, the experiment is conducted in microbiota 
depleted mice. This way, the mice keep their natural gut microbiota including bacteria, viruses, 
and fungi, however by depleting them of their gut microbiota helps with the acceptance of the 
FMT. Throughout the two colonization periods, body weight gain and food intake were 
measured. Open field test was conducted before and after the colonization periods to assess 
changes in behavior. To examine possible effects on appetite, serum levels of appetite related 
molecules (leptin, ghrelin, PYY, GLP-1, insulin and glucagon) were measured after FMT 2.  
After FMT 1, AN mice had decreased food intake without impact on the body weight compared 
to the CO mice. During FMT 2, there continued to be a difference in food intake between 
ANAN mice and COCO mice. There were no differences in behavior between AN mice and 
CO mice after both FMT 1 and FMT 2. After FMT 2, AN mice had elevated levels of PYY, 
leptin, insulin and glucagon. Concluding, these preliminary results demonstrates that 
antibiotic-treated mice colonized with gut microbiota from AN donors decrease food intake 
without affecting body weight gain. Furthermore, this study provided novel data regarding AN 
gut microbiota and changes in serum concentrations of appetite-related molecules.  
1.7. 
Aim of this study  
In this study, we aimed to further examine the findings of our previous work on AN disease. 
To establish the protocol of the first study, we repeated key experiments to confirm our earlier 
results and ensure the reproducibility of our findings. In this subsequent experiment, we 
broadened our investigation by introducing male mice to explore the sex differences in the WN 
disease. We also included groups of mice that were starved for specific time periods to further 
optimize our method. By doing so, we aimed to better understand the behavior of AN patients 
and reinforce the effects of the AN disease by periodic starvation.

Human sample collection  
The human AN fecal sample was collected from a female patient diagnosed with restricting-

2.2. 
Animals and Housing  
80 inbred specific-pathogen free female BALB/cjTac mice, aged three to four weeks at arrival, 
were housed in the experimental unit for rodents 16-2, located at Panum, Hovedstaden. The 
facility maintained temperature of 22°C (± 2°C) and a humidity of 55% (± 10%), 12/12-hours 
light/dark cycle (light on from 6.00 o’clock). Upon arrival the mice were earmarked and then 
housed two per cage in Innocage® Disposable IVC Rodent Caging System. The mice were 
housed two per cage to minimalize the cage effect regarding gut microbiota but keeping the 
natural behavior of the mice by not housing them alone. The cages were randomly assigned 
into two feeding groups – ad libitum or “starved” – and two FMT groups – either anorexic FMT 
or control FMT. The cages were provided with Tapvei bedding, a carboard shelter, a bite block 
and a Carfil Nesting Cup. The mice had unlimited access to water. The four ad libitum groups 
(20 cages) had unlimited access to feed, while the feed was removed twice a week for 10 
hours during the dark period for the 20 ‘starved’ cages. The mice received the maintenance 
diet Altromin 1324 feed (Brogaarden, Lyngby, Denmark) consisting of 11% fat, 24% protein, 
and 65% carbohydrates. According to Federation of European Laboratory Animal Science 
Association (FELASA) guidelines124 daily routine health monitoring of the mice revealing no 
listed pathogens was performed.

2.5. 
Experimental procedure  
2.5.1. Antibiotic treatment  
During the first two weeks (week 1-2) all mice were treated with broad-spectrum antibiotic. 
Ampicillin (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) was administered in the drinking water (1 g/L). 
The mice received the water ad libitum. The antibiotic and the dose were chosen as it has 
previously been described to consistently reduce the gut bacterial density125.  
2.5.2. Feces sampling  
One pellet of feces was sampled in a sterile 1.5 mL BRAND® disposable microcentrifuge tube 
(Merck KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany) directly from the anus of the mice on Monday of week 1, 
3 and twice a week Monday and Thursday of week 6 and 7. The samples were put on dry ice 
directly after acquiring, and subsequently stored at -80°C until analysis.  
2.5.3. Fecal microbiota transplant  
Before FMT, the fecal samples from the AN patients and healthy controls were thawed on ice 
and subsequently suspended 1:5 in 50% glycerol. The suspension was divided into 20 1.5mL 
BRAND® disposable microcentrifuge tubes (1.2 mL each tube), 10 tubes per donor and 4 
tubes per FMT day. The aliquots were stored at -80°C until administration.  
At week 3-7 FMT was performed once a week, Mondays, on all mice. 50 µL of suspension 
was applicated in the mouth of the mice with a sterile 1 mL KRUUSE disposable syringe 
==PAGE:22==
15 
 
(Jørgen Kruuse A/S, Langeskov, Denmark). It’s possible that the mice experienced stress as 
the suspension had to be administered by scruffing the mice.  
2.5.4. Euthanasia and collection of blood, cecum and brain  
After week 7, the mice were anesthetized with a mixture of Zoletil and Butorphanol. Heart 
blood was then collected in 1.5 mL BRAND® disposable microcentrifuge tube (Merck KGaA, 
Darmstadt, Germany) and left at room temperature for coagulation and later serum collection. 
The samples were centrifuged for 8 minutes at 8000 G at 4°C. Serum (the supernatant) was 
transferred to a new 1.5 mL tubes and stored at -80°C. Immediately after blood sampling, the 
cecum and brain were collected in sterile 1.5 mL BRAND® disposable microcentrifuge tubes 
(Merck KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany) and stored on dry ice. The mice were then euthanized 
by cervical dislocation and the previous blood drawing while still anesthetized. At the end of 
the day, the fecal samples, as well as the brains, cecum and serum were transferred to a 
- 80°C freezer.  
2.6. 
Experimental Analysis  
2.6.1. Bodyweight and food consumption  
After antibiotic treatment, from week 2-7, the mice were weighed twice a week. The food intake 
was measured twice a week at week 2-7. Additional to the two food weigh-ins, food weight of 
the starving cages was measured after 2 hours of returning the food trays in order to document 
the refeeding behavior of the mice.  
2.6.2. Gut microbiota characterization  
The DNA of the mouse fecal and cecum samples was extracted by using the Bead-Beat Micro 
AX Gravity kit (A&A Biotechnology, Gdynia, Poland, Cat. #106-100-M1) according to the 
manufacturer’s instructions. The pellet together with BS suspension buffer were added to the 
Bead-Beat tube with 1 g Zirconia/Silica 1 mm beads and were incubated at 50°C for 20 
minutes. Then, LSU buffer and Proteinase K were added, and the tube was shaken using a 
bead beater for 60 seconds at maximum power. The samples were incubated again at 50°C 
for 20-30 minutes. Following centrifugation (5 minutes at 12,000 rpm), the supernatant was 
collected and loaded onto Micro AXD columns preequilibrated with K1G equilibration solution. 
The lysates passed through the columns by gravity, and afterwards, the columns were washed 
twice with W1G and W2 wash solution. The columns were transferred into new 1.5mL 
BRAND® disposable microcentrifuge tubes (Merck KGaA, Darmstadt, Germany). The DNA 
was eluted with 120 µL E elution buffer and passed through the column by gravity into the new 
tube. The elution tube containing the Micro AXD columns were centrifuged for 30 seconds at 
5000 rpm. To avoid DNA degradation upon freezing due to strongly alkaline E elution buffer,

5 µL of N neutralization buffer was added to the samples (A&A Biotechnology). The isolated 
DNA was stored at 4°C until library building.

correction of FASTQ was performed using NanoFilt (q ≥ 10; read length > 1Kb). Taxonomy

2.7. 
Statistical Analysis  
Statistical analyses were carried out using RStudio version 4.2.2 (RStudio, Boston, MA, USA) 
and graphs were generated using RStudio. A two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was 
performed for both body weight data and the food intake data. For food intake comparison, 
the accumulated food intake over the course of the FMT period (Week 3-7) of each cage was 
used. For body weight comparison the area under the curve for each individual mouse was 
calculated. If the data of two groups were found to be significantly different, separate one-way 
ANOVA was performed on each group. The random factors considered in the analysis were 
feeding group, sex and FMT group.

The sequencing results are first presented by the Jaccard distance (figure 10). The Jaccard 
distance is a measure of how dissimilar two sets are. Jaccard only considers if certain 
sequences are present or absent, it doesn’t consider the taxonomic composition or the 
abundance of the sequences. The data is presented in a three-dimensional principal 
coordinate analysis (PCoA) using unweighted UniFrac distances. The axes represent the 
principal coordinates that capture the majority of the variation in the UniFrac distance matrix. 
Furthermore, the groups are compared by the Shannon Index. The Shannon Index measures 
the α-diversity, while the Jaccard and Bray-Curtis dissimilarity measure the ß-diversity. α-
diversity refers to the diversity of species within a single sample or individual, while ß-diversity 
describes the differences in microbial communities between different samples or habitats. The 
Shannon Index takes into account both the number of species present and their relative 
abundance. A higher Shannon index indicates greater species diversity and more even 
distribution of individuals among species, whereas a lower index indicates lower diversity or 
uneven distribution of individuals among species. 
In total 167 samples were sequenced, 24 fecal samples taken before antibiotic treatment were 
chosen for sequencing. Specifically, three fecal samples were selected from each of the 
experimental groups, and each of the three samples came from different cages. Before 
antibiotic treatment the mice did not receive any different treatment, which is why we chose to 
==PAGE:33==

collected just after antibiotic treatment, before FMT begin, were sequenced. 79 fecal samples

from one mouse in each cage. Six samples did not have sufficient readability at the chosen

3.6. 
Do FMT affect appetite related molecules? 
Due to a delivery delay of the UPLEX kit for measurements of appetite related molecules, we 
were not able to obtain this data. With the help of the UPLEX kit, the previous study determined 
the level of six previously described hormones – leptin, PYY, insulin, glucagon, glp-1 and 
ghrelin – in the serum of the mice (see 1.6.). As soon as the data is available, we would like 
to compare the hormone levels from this experiment with the preliminary results. In contrary 
to the first study, this time we starved the mice for four hours before takedown, which can 
have an impact on the molecule concentrations. The main comparison would be between the 
two FMT groups to determine if the anorexic microbiota has an influence on the appetite 
influencing molecules. Next, we would compare the different feeding groups. We saw a 
significant difference in earlier microbiota and food intake results, so it is interesting to 
investigate if that difference translates into the molecular level. The intention was to starve the 
mice twice a week in order to reinforce the effect of the anorexic disease, the aspiration is that 
this is confirmed by the hormone concentrations. Furthermore, differences between sexes 
would be interesting, as female and male mice have different hormone levels but got fed the 
microbiota of the same donors. 
4. Discussion 
The aim of this project was to attempt to transfer characteristics relating human AN disease 
by transforming gut microbiota via FMT. Results from a previous study have shown that FMT 
treatment of AN and CO microbiota to female specific-pathogen free mice affect food intake 
and serum hormone levels (see 1.6.). In this current study, half of the mice from both AN and 
CO groups were fed ad libitum while the other half was starved twice a week overnight. We 
hypothesized that starving the mice could aggravate the microbiota effect of the anorexic 
disease on food intake and hormone levels in the serum. Furthermore, as the disease primarily 
affects young women in humans, we wanted to investigate the sex difference by using female 
and male mice.  
First, we looked at the food intake of the various experimental groups. As expected, male mice 
ate on average 13,5% more than female mice, due to their larger body. When food intake 
between ad libitum fed and starved mice was compared, we only reported a biological relevant 
and statistically significant difference in female anorexic mice. Female anorexic mice that were 
starved ate 9,9% less than mice that were fed ad libitum (p < 0,01). This validates our 
hypothesis of the starvation pattern reinforcing the effect of anorexia as only the female 
anorexic mice ate less when starved. We did not observe a significant difference in food intake 
within the female control groups when feeding groups were compared. The results also 
==PAGE:44==
37 
 
demonstrate a sex difference of the anorexic FMT effect. The data suggests that the food 
intake of male mice was not affected by the AN gut microbiota when starved or fed ad libitum. 
This follows our hypothesis that the anorexic FMTs have a different effect on female mice. 
Female mice were more affected by the starvation pattern when implanted with anorexic gut 
microbiota compared to the female mice receiving control FMTs while there was no such 
difference within the male FMT groups. The approach of starving mice to evaluate if it changes 
the effects of FMT treatment is new. So far, it is not possible to conclude from the findings of 
this study that the starvation pattern enhances the effect of anorexic FMT treatment. However, 
it is possible to state that the starvation pattern had a significant influence on food intake and 
gut microbiota. If these differences are related to anorexia remains to be investigated with 
deeper analysis.  
We were unable to replicate findings from the preliminary study which showed that mice given 
AN FMTs ate significantly less than their respective control group123. In this study, all anorexic 
groups ate slightly more than their control group. One possible explanation is that the sample 
size of five cages per group with two mice each were insufficient to produce meaningful 
results. The initial plan was to combine the different feeding groups in case they were not 
significantly different. That way we would have had groups with larger sample sizes. Following 
power calculations based on the preliminary results, the sample size per group needed to 
attain statistical significance of p < 0.05 with a 90% probability is 7. In this context, sample 
size is represented by the number of cages required. In the preliminary study four mice were 
housed per cage. The experiment setup also differed from the previous study. The mice were 
housed in a different stable as well as different cages. In the preliminary experiment the mice 
were caged four per cage, this time to get more detailed results on the microbiota, the mice 
were caged two per cage. In this current study we also included male mice which results that 
male mice were in close proximity of the female mice which could have had an influence on 
their behavior. 
This is the first study to test an anorexic FMT model in which mice are subjected to a forced 
starvation pattern. Our hypothesis is that anorexic gut microbiota hinders mice (and humans) 
to refeed enough calories after a period of fasting. To test this hypothesis, we starved half of 
the mice twice a week overnight. Following our hypothesis, we would have expected that mice 
with AN microbiota refeed less than the control groups. The observations in this study did not 
follow our hypothesis.  
First, we wanted to test how much the starved mice ate within the first two hours compared to 
the mice that had food available to them overnight. In general, starved mice ate about 46,3% 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
of what ad libitum fed mice ate overnight within two hours after being. This however is based 
on only one measurement before FMT treatment while the mice were at their youngest.  
During the course of this experiment mice were starved over eight periods while on FMT 
treatment, which results in eight refeeding measurements. When FMT groups of the starved 
mice were compared, AN receiving mice refed significantly more than CO receiving mice. 
Starved female AN mice ate 8,92% more than the female CO groups and starved male AN 
mice ate 13,74% more than male CO mice. This did not follow our hypothesis, but it is a 
substantial result to inspire further investigation. The behavior shown by AN mice resembles 
other eating disorders like Bulimia nervosa and binge-eating disorder. A detailed examination 
of the enzyme activity in the gut and hormone level might help understand the refeeding 
behavior better.  
Like in our preliminary results123, there was no difference in weight gain within the FMT groups. 
We also did not observe a difference in weight gain between feeding groups. Male mice were 
naturally heavier than female mice. Previous anorexic FMT studies reported inconsistent 
observations on body weight of mice. Hata et al. (2019) previously reported that gut microbiota 
from AN patients impaired body weight gain in female germ-free mice. However, the study of 
Hata et al. (2019) colonized germ-free breeders with fresh fecal matter and measured body 
weight gain on the gnotobiotic offspring which is a crucial difference between the studies121. 
On the other hand, Glenny et al. (2021) reported no relationship between AN-associated gut 
microbiota and changes in body weight gain in germ-free female and male mice, which is in 
accordance with the preliminary results and our study122,123. The mice in the study of Glenny 
et al. (2021) were 8 or 9 weeks of age while ours were 3 to 4 weeks in the beginning. As mice 
reach their sexual maturity at about 6 to 8 weeks of age, this could make a difference between 
the two studies. Despite the fact that there was no correlation between FMT and changes in 
body weight gain, Glenny et al. (2021) and this study report that the gut microbiota of AN 
recipient mice were different from controls. This implies that refeeding human patients 
suffering from anorexia might not be enough as a treatment option, and that FMT or other gut 
microbiota treatment options might be necessary for a full recovery. As elaborated before 
(section 1.3.) previous studies in humans have shown that gut microbiota of AN patients 
remained distinct from healthy control groups after nutritional rehabilitation and weight 
restoration44,51,55. The microbiota remains distinct to non-eating disorder control suggesting 
that initial clinical renourishment does not return the gut microbiota to a healthy state44,51,55. As 
relapse rates for AN are high56, it is tempting to speculate that continued attention to gut 
microbes may play a role in maintaining a healthy weight post discharge55. This emphasizes 
the need for a comprehensive treatment approach that considers additional factors40 in which 
fecal microbiota transplants (FMTs) may offer a viable prospect.

Next in our study, the sequencing data representing the humanized mice’s microbiota was 
examined. We were able to report that the general approach of the experiment worked as 
expected. Prior to antibiotic treatment, the mice exhibited their unaltered mouse microbiota. 
Following antibiotic treatment and prior to FMT, the mice displayed the most reduced 
microbiota diversity due to the antibiotics depleting their gut microbiota.  Following FMT, the 
mice exhibited a humanized gut microbiota with the greatest diversity. We conducted 
additional analysis by analyzing the fecal samples collected after FMT treatment and then 
dividing them into the distinct experimental groups. Upon comparing all the fecal samples 
collected after FMT treatment, we observed a slight segregation between FMT and feeding 
groups. There was no consistent distinction between sexes. However, multiple data points of 
female mice had a far distance from male mice, resulting in a challenging analysis of the 
overall dataset. We had expected a more substantial variation in their gut microbiota due to 
the different food intake of male and female mice. A possible explanation could be that both 
female and male mice received FMT from the same human donor, which could have masked 
any differences between the sexes of the mice. Nonetheless, we still detect some differences 
in gut microbiota between a few of the samples of the sexes, and a difference in food intake. 
Therefore, we divided the sequencing data by sex and were able to identify distinctions 
between certain FMT and feeding groups due to the smaller sample sizes.  
Upon analyzing the female and male mice separately, we were able to discern better 
distinctions within the FMT and feeding groups. Investigating the male mice, we were able to 
observe a distinction between the two feeding groups. The gut microbiota of mice fed 
ad libitum differed from that of mice subjected to a starvation pattern. However, it was not 
possible to observe a clear distinction between FMT groups in male mice. In contrast, upon 
examining the gut microbiota of solely female mice, we were able to detect a clear separation 
between the FMT groups. The gut microbiota of mice receiving AN FMTs were distinct from 
those receiving CO FMTs. This finding suggests that anorexic FMTs have a different effect on 
female mice compared to male mice, as the two FMT groups exhibited greater distinction 
within the female groups. Further analyzing was conducted by dividing the data further. 
We were able to observe variations in the FMT and feeding groups after separating the data 
by sex. Therefore, we divided the data by both sex and feeding groups to investigate the 
differences in FMT groups in greater depth. This led to the comparison of four groups: female 
ad libitum mice, female starved mice, male ad libitum mice and male starved mice. Once 
again, AN receiving mice were compared to CO receiving mice. In all four groups we observed 
a distinction between the two FMT groups. When comparing the differences in FMT groups 
between starved and ad libitum fed mice, we observed that the separation between the FMT 
groups was more noticeable in starved mice. These findings suggest that the starvation 
==PAGE:47==
40 
 
pattern had an impact on the gut microbiota of the different FMTs. However, it remains unclear 
whether the starvation of the mice reinforces the effect of the anorexic disease on the gut 
microbiota of AN receiving mice. Further analysis of the sequencing data and fully analyzing 
it will lead to more comprehensive statistical analysis and provide greater clarity on the 
differences between the gut microbiota of AN and CO recipients.  
We also wanted to compare the difference in microbiota of samples from the cecum and fecal 
samples. We discovered a difference in microbiota when the two sample types were compared 
directly. When only analyzing the cecum samples and examining differences between FMT, 
feeding groups and sex did not yield the same results as the fecal samples. There were no 
observable differences in microbiota in none of the three groups. This could be explained by 
the amount of cecum samples. Instead of 80 (as with the fecal samples), only half of the ceca 
(n = 40) were sequenced. Based on these findings, ceca do not appear to be a good 
replacement samples for of fecal samples. However, for other types of analysis, ceca may be 
a better representation of the mice internal state. For example, measuring SCFA levels might 
be more accurate if measured in the cecum, given that they are produced by the microbiota 
in the colon and large intestine in humans127. Likewise, the evaluation of gut enzymatic activity 
may be more precise if analyzed in cecum samples.  
Due to time issues, sequencing data was only analyzed by visual comparison at initial and 
superficial level.  
In previous findings it was reported that rodents in anorexic studies had higher levels of 
Clostridium cocleatum and Lactobacillus and lower levels of Burkholderiales117. These findings 
stem from a study which used an ABA mouse model, which combines food restriction and 
physical activity, and mimics key features of AN. In contrast, in our current study mice 
microbiota was humanized by FMT and mice were food restricted. In our sequencing results, 
we also found Lactobacillales to be elevated for mice that received AN gut microbiota. This 
conflicts with previous findings that have reported lower abundance of Lactobacillus and its 
product butyrate in human AN patients43. However, we did not observe an elevated level of 
Lactobacillus in the human donor which was used for anorexic FMTs. This indicates that the 
elevated level of Lactobacillus in the humanized gut microbiota in mice might not originate 
from the anorexic gut microbiota of the human donor. The mice gut microbiota has not yet 
been compared to human donors in detail. Furthermore, higher amounts of Proteobacteria, 
Coriobacteriaceae and Enterobacteriaceae have been described in AN patients42,43. 
Coriobacteriaceae seem to have a more consistent higher level in the control groups in our 
study. We did not observe any difference between groups in Proteobacteria concentrations 
and Enterobacteriaceae have not been described in the sequencing results. The abundance 
==PAGE:48==
41 
 
of Bacteroidetes in AN samples is noticeably lower. This has been previously reported44,51,128. 
According to these results, Bacteroidetes levels are lower in ill AN patients and rise after 
weight recovery. The microbiota in patients with AN has been described to shift toward an 
increase of mucin-degrading Firmicutes and Verrucomicrobia and away from the 
carbohydrate-degrading species Bacteroidetes44,51. This shift could increase digestion of the 
protective intestinal wall mucus and further weaken the intestinal wall barrier in the colon, 
allowing greater translocation of bacterial products and components44,129, which might trigger 
immune and inflammatory responses130–132. This shows that the impact of Anorexia extends 
beyond the mental illness. A shift in microbiota may pose a health risk that persists after weight 
gain and nutritional recovery. This suggests that having anorexic patients return to eating 
normally cannot be the only form of treatment and that FMT or other gut microbiota 
manipulations, like probiotics or prebiotics may be an effective option.  
In conclusion, we were not able to show any statistical differences in food intake between AN 
recipient mice and CO recipient mice, and thus were unable to repeat the findings of the 
preliminary study. However, the novel idea that starvation pattern might reinforce the effect of 
AN FMT showed interesting results within the feeding pattern. We observed that the starving 
female AN mice ate significantly less than the ad libitum female AN mice. This observation did 
not occur in males or the control group. The hypothesis that AN receiving mice will refeed less 
than CO receiving mice after a starvation period, did not prove to be correct. Instead, we 
observed significant results that AN receiving mice eat more than CO receiving mice. This 
suggests that the starvation pattern has a great influence on AN mice, which inspires further 
investigation in this topic. In addition, this finding contradicts with the observation that starved 
mice did not show a statistically significant difference in body weight compared to mice fed ad 
libitum, which raises the question if the different feeding groups might have differed energy 
expenditure.  
Our sequencing results suggest a difference between FMT groups as well as feeding patterns 
in fecal samples. In this study we also examined the gut microbiota of the ceca in the mice. 
So far, the results suggest that ceca are not a better sample type than fecal samples regarding 
microbiota. However, in different tests, ceca might represent the internal state of mice better. 
For example, measuring SCFA levels might be more accurate if measured in the cecum, given 
that they are produced by the microbiota in the colon and large intestine in humans127. 
Furthermore, we observed similar taxonomic results than previously described. Especially 
interesting are the raised levels of Firmicutes compared to Bacteroidetes, which has been 
described in human patients. In general, a lot of our findings suggest that established methods 
to help AN patients like weight restoration and nutritional rehabilitation are not a sufficient 
treatment method and that FMT may be an effective option for full rehabilitation.

5. Outlook  
In future steps, we will perform statistical analysis for our sequencing results on the gut 
microbiota and the taxonomic results. As discussed in more detail in section 3.6., the next 
subject we investigate are the appetite influencing molecules in the mice serum. The four-hour 
starvation period before takedown in this study may have affected hormone concentrations 
compared to the previous study (see 1.6.). By comparing hormone levels between the two 
FMT groups and feeding groups, it will be possible to determine the influence of anorexic 
microbiota and starvation on the hormone level. If the results of the two studies are 
comparable, it might support the hypothesis that low power is the reason we were unable to 
reproduce the lower food intake for AN mice compared to CO animals. Additionally, to the six 
hormones of the UPlex kit, we considered analyzing the concentrations of estradiol. Estradiol 
concentrations between female AN and CO groups as well as the difference to male groups 
could give an insight on the fact that anorexia affects mostly female humans. Another 
interesting topic are the short-chain fatty acids (SCFA) concentration. SCFA are the product 
of microbial fermentation mediated by M. smithii among other bacteria. Concentrations of 
M. smithii found to be elevated in AN patients45,47. Its abundance is thought to optimize calorie 
extraction from a diet with very low-calorie content, enabling the extraction of additional 
calories45,47.  If we find further significant findings, there is reason to repeat a similar 
experiment with a bigger sample size of mice and cages. The experimental groups can be 
designed depending on the outcome and differences between FMT, feeding groups and sex.
