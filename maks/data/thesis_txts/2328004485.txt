Abstract  
Day-care centers belong to a vurnable group of people in case of a fire, since they children need 
assistance during the evacuation. In 2009, a day-care centre in Mexico caught a fire, where 49 children 
died. Some of the reason for this major loss of life was due to a staff-to-child ratio on 1:23. After the 
fire1480 day-cares were inspected where it was found that 75% did not perform fire drills. Some 
countries have requirement on mandatory fire drills, however this is not the case for all countries. 
 
The aim of this study was to determine how the staff-to-child ratio in day-care centers influences 
evacuation time for the ½-3 year olds and how active training influences the evacuation time for the 3-
6 year olds. The study involved full-scale evacuation drills with varying staff-to-child ratio in five 
day-care centers. In some of the day-care centers more than one group evacuated. The study therefore 
draws on data gathered during 37 runs for the 3-6 year olds and 22 runs for the ½-3 year olds. The 
evacuation drills were video recorded. Cameras were placed to investigate: Evacuation procedure, 
behavior of children and staff, pre-evacuation time, movement time and total evacuation time  
 
The study shows, that the number of staff in day-care centers and their actions have an impact on the 
evacuation. Active training was also found to influence the pre-evacuation time for children between 
the age of 3 and 6.  
 
Most children, between ½-3 are, not capable of initiating an evacuation by themselves and need 
physical assistance from center staff. This study showed, that 20.5 % of the ½-3 were carried, 50.5 % 
received physical assistance and only 29.0 % began evacuation by verbal message. It was also found, 
that extra time is added to the pre-evacuation when staff members have to lift children from their 
initial position (e.g. a chair) to a position from which they can start the movement. This is because 
staff can only help one child at a time. All children whom were carried were under the age of 18 
month, however not all children under the age of 18 were carried, since not enough staff were present. 
The staff-to-child ratio therefore proved to have an impact on the pre-evacuation time. However this 
study, in the children’s natural environment, showed no direct correlation between an increased 
evacuation time and a decreasing staff-to-child ratio. 
 
The effects of active training were evident when observing children between 3 and 6. The pre-
evacuation time decreased from first to second drill, proving that active training, for this age group, 
can be an important factor in minimizing evacuation time.  
 
Lastly, how well the staff were organised also influenced the evacuations. During most of the 
evacuations one staff member, with three staff members present, one was placed in front, one in the 
back, and one in the middle. The toddlers were very dependent on staff showing which way to walk in 
order to speed up the evacuation, while pre-school children often were asked to hold heavy or self-
closing doors during evacuations where only two staff members were present.

Resume 
Daginstitutioner tilhøre en sårbar gruppe af mennesker i tilfælde af brand, da børn behøver assistance 
under evakueringen. I 2009 brændte en daginstitution i Mexico hvor 40 børn døde. En af grundene til 
dette store tab af liv var bl.a. en normering på 1:23. Efterfølgende blev 1480 institutioner inspiceret 
hvor det blev fundet at 75 % ikke udførte evakueringsøvelser. Nogle lande har krav for udførelse af 
evakueringsøvelser, det er dog ikke et krav i alle lande.   
 
Formålet med dette studie er at fastlægge hvordan normeringen i daginstitutioner påvirker 
evakueringen for ½-3 årige og hvordan aktiv træning påvirker evakueringen for 3-6 årige. Studiet 
omfatter felt studie af evakueringsøvelser for fem daginstitutioner med varierende normering. Flere 
institutioner havde mere end en gruppe der evakuerede uafhængigt af hinanden. Dette betød at i alt 37 
gennemløb for de 3-6 årig og 22 gennemløb for de ½-3 årig blev foretaget. For at indsamle data fra 
evakueringsøvelserne blev de optaget med videokamera. Kameraerne var placeret så det var muligt at 
se: evakuerings procedure, adfærd for børn og voksne, reaktion- og beslutningstid, gangtid og total 
evakueringstid  
 
På baggrund af resultaterne fra dette studie kan det konkluderes, at antallet af personalet tilstede i 
daginstitutioner og deres handlinger har indflydelse på evakueringen. Derudover har aktiv træning for 
børn i alderen 3-6 år betydning for reaktions- og beslutningstiden.  
 
En stor del af børene i aldersgruppen ½-3 år er ikke i stand til at påbegynde en evakuering af dem selv 
og behøver derfor hjælp. 20,5 % af børnene blev båret, 50,5 % modtog fysisk hjælp og 29 % startede 
af dem selv ved mundtlig kommando. En handling der under reaktions- og beslutningstiden tager 
ekstra tid er når personalet skal løfte børn fra hvor de opholder sig (f.eks. en stol) og til en position 
hvorfra de har mulighed for at påbegynde gangtiden, da de ansatte kun kan hjælpe ét barn af gangen.  
 
Alle børn der blev båret var under 18 måneder, dog blev alle børn under 18 måneder ikke båret, da der 
ikke var nok personale til at bære. Normering har derfor betydning for reaktions-og beslutningstiden. 
Der kunne dog ikke fastlægges en direkte korrelation mellem en stigende tid med faldende normering 
ved at se på normering i et felt studie i børns naturlige omgivelser.  
 
Effekten af aktiv træning sås for børn i alderen 3-6 år. Her faldt reaktions-og beslutningstiden når man 
sammenlignede første og anden evakueringsøvelse. Aktiv træning havde derfor en betydning for 
evakueringen af de 3-6 årige.  
 
Organiseringen af personalet havde også en indflydelse på børnenes evakuering. For de fleste af 
evakueringerne var der et personale foran og bagved, samt en i midten ved tre personaler. De små børn 
var meget afhængig af at personalet viste vej. Dette gjorde evakueringen hurtigere mens 
børnehavebørn ofte blev bedt om at holde tunge og selvlukkende døre under de evakueringer hvor der 
kun var to voksne tilstede.

Introduction 
 
Day-care centres are used by a group of people who are considered vulnerable in case of a fire. They 
might need help to evacuate during a fire as they are not capable of getting to a safe place by them 
selves[1]. Beside day-care centres places used by vulnerable people includes elder centres, hospitals, 
prisons and institutions for physically or mentally disabled. In 2004 the performance-based design 
method was introduced [2]. Here buildings can be designed regarding to fire by input from the 
behaviour of the occupants in the building. However the majority of the materials available relate to 
people who are able to evacuate by themselves. The limited information available on vulnerable 
people does not take into consideration the amount of help provided. By exploring how help influence 
the evacuation of children in day-care centres a solid foundation for predicting the egress and secure a 
safe building design.[3]. 
 
On June 5th 2009 the ABC Day-care centre in Hermosillo, Mexico caught on fire [4]. The day-care 
hosted a total of 142 children under the age of 6 and 6 staff. 29 children all under 3 died during the fire 
and an additional 20 children died later of their injuries. Furthermore 75 children and 6 staff were 
injured but survived. This major loss of life is was due to a range of factors as low staff-to-child ratio, 
access to one fire exit and deviations from the regulations even though the day-care centre had passed 
an inspection only 10 days earlier. This resulted in inspections of 1480 day-care centres in Mexico. 
Among other thing they found that 75 % of the day-care centres did not have performed or planned 
any fire drills [4].  
 
In some countries regular fire drills are mandatory but in day-care centres in Denmark fire drills are 
only recommended not required [1]. At a recent study [5] of evacuation in Danish day-care centres it 
was however noted that day-care centres which participated in two evacuation drills showed a 
decreasing  evacuation time for the second evacuation drill.

Few studies have looked at evacuation where help is given. One of those was conducted of the 
university of Greenwich. The ratio between cabin crew at airplanes and passengers were compared 
with the evacuation efficiency [6]. The relationship was based on the AASK databases (Aircraft 
Accident Statistics and Knowledge) were people who had been involved in airplane accidents have 
been provided with information about the evacuation. The study observed that a higher number of 
operational crew might lead to decline in evacuation efficiency. However, not enough data was 
collected to make a clear conclusion. Another study has looked at the influence of the number of 
hospital porters helping bed-bound patients evacuate. The study was based on information from an 
evacuation drill conducted at Rigshospitalet in Copenhagen [7]. This study showed that evacuation 
time decrease with increasing number of porters, however there were no linear proportion between and 
increasing number of porters and decreasing evacuation time and at some point there were no different 
in having more porters.  
 
A survey was made of the National Fire Protection Agency (NFPA) and The Technical University of 
Denmark (DTU) in 2013 to determine the self-preservation capability for pre-school children [8]. 
Teachers from day-care centres and experts in childhood development from seven different countries 
were asked to provide their assessment of children’s self-preservation capability in case of fire. The 
finding showed that a majority of teachers believed that children can understand and follow simple 
instructions by the age of 36 months. They also found that 69% believed children under 24 months are 
able to walk on horizontal planes without physical help and that children by the age of 30 months are 
capable of walking down stairs on their own. 56% of the experts believe further that the children can 
understand and follow simple instructions by the age 30 month, and 44% of the experts meant that by 
the age of 30-month children can walk on horizontal surfaces without any physical support. By the age 
of 36 months the experts believed children are capable of walking down stairs. An earlier study of 
evacuation by day-care centres [9] showed that 79.8 % of the 0-2 years were either carried or in need 
of physical help during an evacuation drill and the last 20.2 % received only verbal commands. 
Comparing this with the 3-6 years only 14.1 % needed physical assistance or was carried.  This 
indicates that small children need more help and therefore it is assumed that the staff-to-child ratio 
must be higher in case of an evacuation.

2.1 
Evacuation theory 
In order to dimension secure buildings according to life safety of the users in case of a fire, it is 
important to understand how humans behave during fire [3].  
 
In 2004 the prescriptive codes in Denmark were supplemented with the performance-based guidelines 
[2], which provides more freedom in relation to building design. The principle of performance-based 
fire safety design says that occupants must have more time available to evacuate than it will take to 
evacuate [10]. In order to secure a building is safe according to fire the relation between the available 
safe egress time (ASET) and required safe egress time (RSET) should therefore be: 
 
ASET > RSET  
(1) 
 
ASET is the shortest time from a fire ignition and until critical conditions from toxic gases, 
temperature, smoke or radiation occurs for occupants.

2.1.1 Pre-evacuation period 
The pre-evacuation period can be described as the time from ignition until the people start to evacuate 
to a safe place [3]. This time can be divided into sub-periods as seen in Figure 2.2. 
 
Figure 2.2: Pre-evacuation timeline. [3] 
The first period after ignition is the warning time as describe the time from the fire ignition until the 
fire alarms start or occupant are exposed to cues from the fire (i.e. seeing smoke, advice of fire by 
others etc.) [3]. The warning time depends on the fire and type of warning system installed in the 
building.  
 
The next period is the decision time where people seek information about the fire and evaluate 
whatever the fire is real or not. They wait to evacuate until they are certain the fire is real. This period 
can be influenced by other people in the building and how they respond to the alarm. This type of 
influence is called social influence [11]. A study was made in a cinema with unannounced 
evacuations, to see the responded from people when the fire alarm went off. It was observed that the 
social influence was increasing with decreasing distance between occupants [11]. 
 
The last period is the reaction time as occupant use to prepare themselves to evacuate by gather 
belongings, find friends etc.

3.4 
Evacuation drill 
Precaution taken before evacuation drills 
To ensure gathering of reliable and comparable data, a lot of consideration was given to parameters 
that might influence the evacuation. In order to minimize some of these following actions have been 
taken: 
- 
In order to make the evacuations realistic but also ensure that staff would not plan a day trip 
on the day of the evacuation, all staff was informed which day the evacuation would take 
place. However, they were not informed of the specific time. They were also asked to be in 
the same areas the whole day 
- 
The evacuation was conducted for the smaller children between 9.30-10.30 and again between 
14.30-15.00. This schedule was chosen since the children were expected to be awake and 
gathered for their daily activities. 
- 
In order to avoid children spending time putting on shoes during some drills and running out 
barefoot during others, staff was asked to let the children either leave shoes on the entire day 
or evacuated without.  
 
The conditions for the alarm during the evacuation were either acoustic sound or verbal warning. 
Since several setups took place in multi-storey buildings where the acoustic warning systems were 
connected to the entire building it was decided not to use the day-cares warning system. A wireless 
optical smoke detector from EnsoNordic was therefore used instead for acoustic sound. At some 
setups they prefer to have a verbal warning in order to avoid scarring the children. Here the words 
following words was announced twice “This is an evacuation drill, please leave the building”.

3.6 
Limitations and errors  
This section describes the errors and limitations when conducting the evacuation drills for this project.  
 
When doing full-scale evacuation drills in children’s national environment and comparing several 
drills there are some limitations. Even throw each detail is planned in advance some limitations cannot 
be predicted. 
 
In order to compare several evacuation drills with different ratios the participants should be doing the 
same things both times when the drill starts. However it has not been possible to have all children and 
staff standing at the same location each time and doing the same activities.  
 
The drills were conducted in the time period from beginning of April to the end of Maj. In this time 
period some children moves from either nurseries to day-care, or from day-care to school.  The day-
cares where therefore not fully occupied and at the same time many children where at holiday due to 
public holiday in Denmark at this time, this lead to a higher staff-to-child ratio than normal. 
 
The drills depends of the staff-to-child ratio and even though it is known how many staffs and children 
there normally are at the day-care centre, there can be children who are sick, vacation or not allowed 
to participated do to video recording. In some other cases children were as well picked up, before the 
second drill was conducted and it was therefore not possible to make the planned ratio. Therefore it 
was not always possible to make the same ratios in all day-cares. 
 
The staff were also more prepared then in a real evacuation, do to awareness of the day. The number 
of children were also known due to the staff-to-child ratio had been adjusted. Therefore the staff did 
not need to use time during the evacuation to check how many children they had and update the name 
list. Since the staff knew it was a fire drill the did not use time checking about the fire was real or find 
out where it was as well as the fire exit also were chosen before the drill.  
 
Some errors as would have been possible to predict before the each drill but was first observed when 
looking at the recordings, they are as follow. 
 
The staff was not always fully aware of how many children the actually had. When planning the staff-
to-child ratio it was based on the how many children and staff member was present. However when 
looking at the recording afterwards it can be seen that the number of children present not always in 
compliance of what was told resulting in a wrong staff-to-child ratio. 
 
Original the idea was to also include the time used from exiting the building and until staff said all 
clear since the staff still have to bring the children to a safe place and ensure everybody were still 
there. However the amount of deviations where to many when they exit the building, some forgot to 
signal that everybody was out, some remembered it in the first drill but forgot it on the second drill. In 
other drills additional staff were present outside in order to avoid the children running out on the road.  
 
Due to the use of verbal warning some errors occur. For some of the day-care centres not all the staff 
could hear the verbal warning due to the high sound level from the children or sound damping rooms.

Ethical considerations   
The project involved camera observations of human behaviour and ethics therefore had to be 
considered.  
 
The Scientific Ethical Committee of region Hovedstaden was contacted in order to determine how to 
get the project approved. Since the project was not categorized as a health scientific research project, 
as defined according to §2, they concluded they did not need to give their approval. The materials send 
to the Scientific Ethical Committee of region Hovedstaden can be seen in Appendix B together with 
their response.  
 
The Danish Data Protection Agency was also contacted. However from the beginning of 2016 the 
department Law and Contracts at DTU has been given the responsibility for approving projects on 
behalf on the Danish Data Protection Agency. Master thesis project do not have to be reported if the 
participants give their consent. However since this project involved minors who are not able to 
provide consent the project had to be reported.   
 
Besides contacting the authorities, DTU BYG has their own codex the project also had to be in 
compliance with [27]. In order to achieve a realistic evacuation and since the participants are children 
some statements in the codex have not been fulfilled completely. The deviation is: 
 
• 
Children did not receive written information, since they are not expected to have the capability 
to read it.  
 
• 
Only the leader of the day-care and/or contact person received a detailed experimental plan in 
order to keep the evacuation drills unannounced. The staffs were only informed which day and 
how the evacuation plan looked, but not the specific time. The material send to the day-care 
centres can be found in Appendix B. 
 
 
• 
The children in the day-care centres are minors and therefore cannot sign the consent. Since a 
total of 300-400 children were expected to participate in the project, it would be challenging 
to gather consent from all parents It was therefore decided, in cooperation with the leader of 
the day-care, that a letter would be sent to the parents informing about the project. If the 
parents did not want their children to be recorded they could inform the staff. The letter sent to 
the parents can be found in Appendix B.  
 
• 
The day-care centres did not receive financial compensation from their participation in the 
project. However, they had the opportunity to get feedback on their evacuation after the last 
evacuation drill was conducted.

Procedure  
In this section some of the common procedure depending on the staff are described. First the general 
procedure for both age groups are described followed by the group ½-3 years and ending with the 
group for 3-6 years.  
 
4.1.1 General  
This sub-section descripts and discusses the procedure as is general for both age groups and are 
depend on the staff.  
Name list 
According to all day-care centres evacuation plan, name list should be brought outside in case of an 
evacuation in order to ensure that all children are save. This was done in most drills. It is a member of 
the staff responsibility to bring the name list. Time used on gathering the name list adds extra time to 
the pre-evacuation time.  
Day-care centres in Copenhagen have a new online system where name list of all groups in a day-care 
is stored. This system can be access from different online portals. Day-care’s centres having this 
system used it on some of the drills however it was more often the old system with pen and paper was 
used.  
It was more common that the groups with the children from ½-3 years forgot the name list then they 
for the groups with the older children. This may be due to that they where more busy by helping the 
children evacuate.  
 
Door 
For 2 of 8 setups it was possible for the children to reached the doorknob in order to open the door and 
start the movement. In both cases the fire exit from the group room was leading directly outside. The 
doors were however locked so the children need help to unlock them. For the remaining setups the 
doorknob was placed in a height as was not possible to reach for the children, so they did not have the 
possibility to start the movement. The children’s pre-evacuation time is therefore highly dependent on 
the staff and their tasks they have to do before opening the door. In most drills the staff first open the 
door when all children was gathered.    
 
Checking rooms 
Some staff member was looking in the rooms next door when collecting the children. However not all 
groups did it right away and when knowing all children was gathered the staff stopped checking the 
rooms and evacuated with the group.  
One group did not check any room before starting to go against the assembly point for counting the 
children. This resulted in two children playing in a room with a closed glass door, only got out 
because they saw that the other children were leaving. 
Three groups did first check the rooms after the children were counted and some were missing.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:31==

4.1.2 ½-3 years 
This sub-section focus on the procedure for the age group ½-3 years. First the choice of fire exit is 
described and it is discussed how the familiarity and staff influence the evacuation. Next is a 
description of how the staff gets the overview of the children during the evacuation.  
Use of regular exit 
The children did in most cases use a fire exit, which the children were familiar with. Two groups used 
the exit they regular use, four group evacuated through a door which are not use regular but the 
children are familiarly with and one group evacuated through an exit the children had never used 
before this drill.  
 
For the two groups who used their regular exit as a fire exit the children had no problem finding the 
way.  
However for one group the movement included a pass of some steps they were not familiar passing 
when the whole group was together. Normally they passed the staircase in small groups. The children 
was either crawling or walking down the stairs however it often took time for the children to start the 
movement down the stairs since they considered how to pass is when they were not supervised by 
staff. In one case a child had problem crawling down the stairs and ending up blocking the way for 
rest of the children. 
For the other group the children had no problem moving to the fire exit however when not supervised 
by staff they deviated from the route. 
 
For the groups using an exit they were familiar with but not used regular the children had no problem 
getting to the exit but when they had to pass through the door they stopped and looked at what 
happened outside. Therefor, they need help either by being pushed, holding hand or physical moved 
by a staff.   
 
For the group using an evacuation route which the children were unfamiliar with, they had problem 
finding way on their own. For the drills were the staff-to-child ratio was lowest no staff was showing 
the way since all staff was helping children down from chairs. This resulted in children standing in 
front of the main exit and waiting until a staff went in front of the group and showed the way.  
Overview of children 
The children in this age were only counted in 3 of 21 drills. At one drill with 21 children present and 
playing in different rooms the staff was making a roll call during the movement time in order to secure 
that they had all. This took long time and in the second drill the staff just counted them.  
In another drill the children were as well spread over a larger area with many possibilities of hiding 
and since the movement is started before all children is gathered one staffs count them at the fire exit 
while waiting for the door to be unlocked. In the remaining drills the children were either gathered 
before starting the movement or playing in smaller areas and therefore the last person leaving the 
room insure that no children were left.  
It could therefore seem that for larger groups or groups spread over a large area, is difficult to get an 
overview of the children and therefore the need for counting is higher. 
 
 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
25 
Organisation  
In this sub-section is a description of how the staff organised them self and the children. The staff did 
not have any common procedure to organise them self and the children during the evacuation. The 
organisation of the staff is divided into the pre-evacuation and movement time.  
Pre-evacuation 
When the evacuation started the staff were first helping the children down from chairs or stopped them 
from playing. Often the children located farthest away from the exit were helped first. The children as 
were going to be carried was picked up as the last. 
 
At all drills the children had to pass a door. However they were not capable of opening by themselves 
in all drills. In some of the other drills the door were open, so the children had the possibility to start 
the movement without the staff. 
 
For the drills with a high ratio one staff walked to the assembly point gathering the children before 
starting to move. At the drills with low ratio all staff was busy helping children resulting in children 
did not know were to go and therefor stopped and looked at the staff. 
 
The group as went directly outside from the group room one staff were going outside and gathered the 
children while the remaining staff ran out with the children. 
 
In two drills the staff tried to organise the children. One tried to get the children to go in a line, 
however the moment they open the door all the children started running and forgot about the line.  
For the other drill the staff tried to get the children to hold hands, both the moment the children had to 
pass the door they let go in order to lean on the wall.  
 
Movement  
For most of the drills one staff was in front of the group showing the way during the movement. 
However for a couple of drills children were in front of the group in the beginning of the movement, 
both ending with children stopping to play so the staff had to use time getting them to move again.  
 
One staff was always in the back of the group ensuring that all children got out. For groups with more 
then two staff present one of the staff opened the door to the exit right away however if only two staff 
were present at the drill the door was first opened when all children was gathered in front of the door.

4.1.3 3-6 years 
This focusing on the procedure for the age group 3-6 years. The sub-section treats the use of regular 
exit and counting the children. 
Use of regular exit 
Most setups used a fire exit, witch were not their regular exit, however only in one setup the children 
had never tried the exit before. Two setups used their daily route to the playground and two used an 
exit they regular use. 
 
Before starting the movement the children gathered in front of the door in the group room in order to 
get instructions from the staff. Even though the children was prepared and knew where to gathered 10 
% of all the children run against their regular exit in the first drill instead of the fire exit. In the 
remaining drills none of the children ran against the regular exit and the majority of the children were 
at the assembly point ready to evacuate before the staff.  
 
Counting the children 
It was a comment procedure that the children were counted during the pre-evacuation time or/and 
again when they passed by a staff going from pre-evacuation to movement time. However it was not 
in all day-care centres that they did it at the same way each time. 
  
One group had in the first drill three staff present so while two staff collected the children one staff 
were available for counting them before they started to move. However in the next drill they were first 
counted during the movement, which resulted in the staff had to count them two times in order to be 
ensure that all children was there and thereby delaying the children’s movement.  
 
At another drill the children were in the first drill, with three staff present, counted in the beginning of 
the movement. This changed in the second drill, with only two staff present, where they was first 
counted in the bottom of the stairs. This resulted in some problems for the staff staying back collecting 
the remaining children and checking rooms since the staff did not know if everybody where out and 
had to go 3 levels down in order to check if everybody was down.  
 
One group did a roll call in the first two drills, this where not done in the two next drills. 
 
A group where the staff-to-child ratio was lowest the children were not counted.

Organisation 
In this sub-section the organisation of the staff and children would be presented. 
 
In the majority of the evacuation drills the staff collected all children in front of the exit from the 
group room or level. Often some children were at the assembly point before the first staff member 
arrived. One staff often went to the assembly point to ensure that the children were making a line and 
started counted them while the remaining staff members was collecting children.  
 
When going from pre-evacuation time to movement time the children where in all drills passing a 
door. For one day-care the door was a heavy fire door. When passing the door in drills with three staff 
on staff was holing the door while the children passed by and there was one staff in the back and front. 
However if only two staff was present either a child was holding the door or the first staff was holding 
the door so the children did not have anyone to follow in the beginning of the movement. Both cases 
could be discussed if it is a good solution. The door is heavy for a child especially if they do not know 
where to stand and if the door closes while children are passing it could result in children getting 
injured. However, letting the children start to move at an egress path they do not know is neither a 
good solution. 
 
The organisation of the staff during the movement was depending on the number of staff present. For 
the drills with three staff members one in the front showing the way, one approximately in the middle 
and one in the back.  
For one group where the children was not organised in the first drill ended with all the slow walking 
children in the back of the group only supervised by one staff, while all the others was rushing out. 
Since the group was travelling from the 3. floor the distance between the two groups was very big. 
The staff in the back tried to call for help during the movement however both staff in the front did not 
hear it. This was change in the remaining drill, so the children as walked slower was distributed 
among the staff during the pre-evacuation time. The staff in the front did also slow down the speed so 
it always was possible to see the staff behind.

Conclusion 
 
For the ½-3 year olds a direct correlation between an increasing evacuation time with decreasing staff-
to-child ratio could not be determined based on a field study in the children’s natural environment. 
This was the case for both pre-evacuation time, movement time and total evacuation time. However, 
by studying each group’s individual evacuation it was found that the children were strongly depended 
on the actions taken by staff which therefore effected the evacuation time.  
 
Children are not capable of starting an evacuation themselves and therefore need to be encouraged 
during the pre-evacuation time. For 50.5 % of the children it was necessary to do so with physical help 
and 20.5 % needed to be carried. In almost every drill it was for example necessary to lift children 
from their initial position (e.g. a chair) and to a position where they had the possibility to start moving 
by themselves or were further carried. Such actions take in average between 2.29 and 3.14 seconds of 
physical contact with each child depending of the type of lifting. In the end this can result in long 
delay in evacuation time if staff-to-child ratio is very low. Staff carrying one child had the possibility 
to physically help other children move in the right direction or hold hands. However, staff carrying 
two children did not have these possibilities and relied fully on verbal assistance. All the children 
carried were under 18 month however not all children under 18 month where carried.  
 
The study also showed that the children followed the reaction of staff as well as their daily routines. 
When a staff member was not in front of the group to show the way, children ran after their clothes or 
stopped during the move toward the assembly point, because they were unaware where to go.  
 
For the children between 3 and 6, the effect of active training was observable especially in terms of 
pre-evacuation time. Not only did the study indicate, that without active training the time increases, 
but it was also found, that 10 % of the children were running against the main exit even though they 
before the drill were told that another exit was going to be used. It therefore seems, that active training 
for this age group might be an important factor in minimizing the evacuation time. 
 
When comparing the two age groups the procedure proved to be very different. The toddlers are often 
in groups of less than 10, and therefore were not counted as often and did not walk in a line, which 
was the case for the older children, whom were in almost all drills counted and lined up before starting 
the movement.  
 
The organisation of the staff was also very different from each day-care and even between age groups 
within same day-care centre. For the toddlers the organisation seemed to depend on how they would 
get to the fire exit. In cases were they had to pass a staircase, at least one member of the staff was in 
the front of the group and one was in the back to ensure that all children exited. The remaining staffs 
were often in the back of the group and helped children start the evacuation, until they had to pass a 
closed door. For the older children the procedure often started with one staff in the front and one in the 
back. For the drills with three staff member present one was also in the middle. The staff in the middle 
was often holding self-closing or heavy doors for the children, however in drills were only two staff 
member were present, children were asked to hold doors, or the first staff member held it, which 
meant that no staffs were in front.

Further work 
With the current study a number of issues have been discussed. However a lot of new questions where 
raised during the project which have not been possible to look at. 
 
o Sleeping children: Since the drill where focusing on the staff-to-child ratio it was avoided to 
have sleeping children involved in the drill since it would as well make a difference.  
It would however have a big impact on the evacuation time especially for the smaller children 
since all children are sleeping either inside or in crypt at a higher level. It could be assumed 
that a child as is just waken up would need more assistance. The children in the crypt are as 
well fastened as would take time to move the strap and lift them down on the ground.  
 
o Increasing travel speed when assisted by staff: In the current project some children were 
assisted by staff when waking down stairs to increase the travel speed. It was however always 
the same children as were assisted and it was therefore not possible to compare a child 
walking speed with and without assistance from staff.  
 
o Type of warning systems: In the initial contact phase some leaders make their concern about 
using their warning system with a recorded verbal voice message since they think the children 
would get scared. Normally a recorded verbal voice message decreases the pre-evacuation 
time, however if the children get scared and hide it would increase the pre-evacuation time.  
 
Beside the evacuation already conducted have a lot of information as where not relevant for the aim of 
this project however still highly relevant when looking at fire safety at day-care centres  
 
o Decreasing walking speed over increasing flights: A couple of the day-care centres with 
children in the age 3-6 years where located at 2 and 3. floor and it would be possible to look at 
if the travel speed was decreasing over a increasing length.  
 
Beside study new information regarding day-care centres it is possible to with the information 
gathered in this project to support already existing theories with more data.

Introduction 
When a child is drop off in a day-care centre parents expected that they are looked after and taken 
care off the hole day particularly if a situation should arise where the child could be in danger as e.g. a 
fire. According to regulations children under the age of 6 years belong to a group where additional as-
sistance is required during an evacuation [1]. In July 2009 A. R. Lárusdóttir made a study where 10 
day care centres participated in evacuation drills here it was observed that 79.8 % of the 0-2 years old 
was carried or need physical help and the last 20.2 % received only a verbal command, where com-
paring it with the 3-6 years old only 14.1 % need physical help or where carried [2].  This shows that 
the small children need more help and therefore the staff-to-children ratio have to be different from the 
older children. Denmark has so fare no demands on staff-to-child ratio, however due to the govern-
ments cut down the ratio between staff-to-children is increased.  
It is therefore interesting to investigate how the staff-to-child ratio influences the evacuation.   
 
2 
Objective 
The aim of the evacuation experiment is to get a better knowledge of how the staff-to-child ratio influ-
ence the evacuation time. In Denmark day care centres is divided into two categories “vuggestue” for 
the 0-3 years old as will be mentioned the younger children and “børnehave” as is for the kids at 3-6 
years old as will be mentioned the older children. Here the ratio is different, since the younger children 
have less self-preservation and therefore more staff is needed. The things as is expected to change 
with different staff-to-child ratio during and evacuation and as have influence on the evacuation time 
is: 
- 
Evacuation procedure 
o 
Pre-movement time 
o 
The flow during evacuation 
o 
The amount of help for each child 
o 
Organization of the staff 
- 
Pre-movement time 
o

Experimental setup 
To collect data 6 different day-care centres have agreed to participate in a number of evacuation drills 
with different staff-to-child ratio. The evacuation drill will be carried out after the individual day-care 
centres emergency plan to achieve a realistic evacuation. At total 30 evacuation drills will be per-
formed in the period from 6th of April 2016 to the 1st of June 2016. Since there are differences in the 
buildings design, evacuation plan and amount of evacuations the experimental setup will be different 
from each day-care centre. At total there would be 8 setups.  
To collect data and ensure that no additional persons is present during the evacuation drills in order to 
obtain the desired staff-to-child ratio, the evacuation would be recorded with a drift innovation x170. 
The recorded data will be analysed with adobe premier pro cc. 
The evacuation will be started either by a verbal warning or an acoustic sound from fire alarms, de-
pending which solution the day care centre prefer. All participants in the evacuation have to be notified 
at the same time in order to analyse the pre-movement time.  
When performing the evacuation the participating people would not be exposed for any fire or smoke. 
 
3.1 
Participants 
The participants in the evacuation drills are staff and children from 6 day care centres. Totally 600 
children and 100 staff are expected to participate in the evacuation drills. Since the children are minor 
the parents have to be informed about the drill, which week the evacuation drill will take place and that 
it is recorded. In case they do not accept their child being involved in the evacuation drill or is recorded 
they must inform the staff at the day care centre.  
The leader of the day care centre would be aware of the day and time the evacuation drill will take 
place. The staff would be aware of the day in order to avoid that they plan any day tour trip and then 
not would be present in the building, they would however not be aware of the specific time. The chil-
dren would be informed that they would take part in an evacuation drill but not be aware of the day or 
time.  
To control the staff-to-child ratio a number of children is brought to the meeting point prior for the 
evacuation drill, this will be noticed in the day care centres protocol as they are not present that they.

Staff

the specified time.  
The leader has to fill out a content regarding they accept to participate and have received both verbal 
and written information and that they have given the written information to the staff and both staff and

Children

Formål med evakueringen  
Formålet med evakueringen er at undersøge hvordan forholdet mellem antallet af personale og børn 
påvirker evakueringstiden. Der vil derfor blive udført et antal evakueringer hvor der er en variation mel-
lem antallet af personale og børn.  
Ud fra evakueringen ville bl.a. ganghastigheder, person densitet og evakueringens procedure blive 
analyseret. 
 
Metode 
Evakueringen udføres iht. daginstitutionens egen beredskabsplan som alle ansatte bør være bekendte 
med. For at opretholde det ønskede forhold mellem personale og børn ville der blive installeret midler-
tidige kamera i institutionen samt at børn der ikke skal deltage i evakueringen blive bragt ud til sam-
lingsstedet inden evakueringen begynder. Evakueringen starter ved et aftalt signal mellem daginstitu-
tionens leder og undersøger (enten benyttes institutionens brandalarm eller en mundtlig alarmering). 
Evakueringen er slut når alle er samlet på samlingsstedet og har givet tegn til at de er ude.  
Hverken børn eller voksne ville i forbindelse med evakueringen blive udsat for røg fra undersøger. 
 
Fordele ved at deltage 
Ved at deltage i dette evakueringsforsøg vil I være med at skabe mere og bedre kendskab til hvordan 
normering påvirker sikkerheden i daginstitutioner mht. brand. Derudover får jeres daginstitution mulig-
hed for at øve jeres beredskabsplan og blive godt rustet hvis uheldet er ude.  
 
Udelukkelse fra eksperimentet 
Undersøgeren må til hver en tid ekskludere en deltager fra projektet hvis dennes opførelse findes 
upassende for de andre deltager.  Endvidere vil evakueringen blive afbrudt hvis en deltager kommer til 
skade. 
 
Adgang til resultatet  
Når evakueringen er fuldført og dataene er analyserede er det muligt at få indblik I resultaterne. Hvis 
institutionen ønsker information omkring resultaterne skal dette angives på samtykkeerklæringen som 
lederen underskriver. Resultater fra evakueringerne kan blive offentliggjort i videnskabelige artikler.  
Kamera optagelserne vil kun blive benyttet til forskning og vil ikke blive publiceret.

I Danmark har det videnskabsetiske komitesystem til opgave at vurdere sundhedsvidenskabelige 
forskningsprojekter.  
 
Ved et sundhedsvidenskabeligt forskningsprojekt forstås projekter, der indebærer forsøg på levende-
fødte menneskelige individer, menneskelige kønsceller, der agtes anvendt til befrugtning, menneske-
lige befrugtede æg, fosteranlæg og fostre, væv, celler og arvebestanddele fra mennesker, fostre og

prøvning af medicinsk udstyr.

den kliniske og den socialmedicinsk-epidemiologiske forskning. Begrebet omfatter, udover forskning 
af de somatiske sygdomme, tillige de psykiatriske og de klinisk-psykologiske sygdomme og til-
