Abstract
Cardiovascular disease is a major cause of death worldwide and includes a range of disorders
affecting the heart and blood vessels. Most cases are due to coronary artery disease, where
fatty or calcified plaque builds up and reduces blood flow in the arteries that supply oxygen-
rich blood to the heart, which can lead to heart attack. Blood clots can also form in the left
atrial appendage (LAA), a small pouch extending from the left atrium, and travel to the brain,
potentially causing a stroke. Accurately capturing the shape of cardiovascular structures from
computed tomography (CT) scans is essential for diagnosing and monitoring abnormalities that
increase the risk of cardiovascular events. While deep learning (DL) has enabled significant
progress in medical image analysis, its application to volumetric data remains computationally
demanding. This thesis presents a computationally efficient DL approach for learning an adap-
tive, global shape representation that captures the geometry and variability of heart structures
in a compact latent space.
The proposed method converts anatomical segmentations into dual octree graphs, which offer
an adaptive and memory-efficient structure that can be configured to fit the anatomical detail
level. A dual octree graph variational autoencoder (VAE) model is introduced, which encodes
3D anatomical shapes into a probabilistic latent space and reconstructs them back to their orig-
inal form. The model focuses computational resources on regions with fine detail and merges
features across multiple octree resolutions, effectively capturing both global shape and local
structure while reducing memory and computational costs compared to other shape encoding
methods. The learned latent space is analyzed to assess how well the model captures anatomical
variation and organizes it in a meaningful, interpretable way.
The model is developed and evaluated using anatomical annotations of the coronary arteries,
four heart chambers (left atrium, right atrium, left ventricle, right ventricle), and the LAA from
the large-scale, publicly available ImageCAS dataset of 1000 cardiac CT scans. The anatom-
ical annotations are divided into two single-class models for the coronary artery tree and the
LAA, and one multi-class model for all four heart chambers. Across all structures, the model
successfully learns and organizes the latent space based on shape differences, grouping similar
shapes in the single-class models and separating different classes in the multi-class model. It pro-
duces accurate reconstructions and generates realistic anatomical shapes for the heart chambers
and LAA, but struggles with the coronary artery tree due to its complex and variable branching.
This approach shows potential for characterizing anatomical morphologies, as well as quality
control of anatomical annotations and identifying unusual or outlier shapes at both the single-
class and multi-class levels.

Introduction
Medical imaging techniques such as Computed Tomography (CT), Magnetic Resonance Imag-
ing (MRI), and Positron Emission Tomography (PET) are becoming increasingly accessible for
generating high-resolution images of the human body. These imaging modalities play a crit-
ical role in diagnosis, treatment planning, and disease monitoring. However, manual analysis
and annotation of such images by radiologists requires many resources, as it is both costly and
time-consuming. Recent advancements in deep learning (DL), particularly convolutional neural
networks (CNNs), are enabling the development of automated solutions that can assist radiolo-
gists or take over parts of the manual process. CNNs have shown to be successful in tasks such as
classification, segmentation, and anomaly detection, making the process faster and potentially
more accurate than when performed by clinicians.
Despite these advancements, training CNNs to process 3D medical images remains challenging.
Volumetric CNNs, designed to learn directly from 3D image data, are particularly computa-
tionally demanding. The memory and computational costs of these networks increase cubically
with the volumetric resolution, often making it necessary to downsample the images to make
the training feasible. This downsampling can lead to the loss of important details, especially
in complex, volatile anatomical structures like the coronary arteries. As a consequence, a CNN
may struggle to accurately model such structures, thereby limiting its usefulness for clinical
applications.
One promising way to address this challenge is by focusing on learning a shape representation of
the anatomical structure, rather than extracting it as a binary voxel grid through segmentation.
A shape representation encodes global shape characteristics, capturing both the geometry and
variability of the anatomical structure in a latent space. This approach can incorporate knowl-
edge about what the shape can look like and the constraints on its structure. Such a latent
space representation can be used for tasks like reconstruction, data synthesis, segmentation re-
finement, or as a shape prior to regularize models and improve performance on challenging cases.
Extracting meaningful shape representations from complex anatomical structures in medical im-
ages is however far from straightforward. Many anatomies, such as the coronary arteries, have
very complicated shapes and patterns that can vary significantly between patients. This creates
a need for methods that can learn implicit representations, which can capture both global and
local shape characteristics in a continuous manner.
1.1
Related work
Volumetric fields, such as signed distance fields (SDFs), unsigned distance fields (UDFs), and oc-
cupancy fields, are implicit representations that are widely used for modeling 3D shapes. These
fields represent a shape as a function that maps 3D coordinates (x, y, z) to either a distance
value to the shape’s surface (SDFs and UDFs), or to a binary value indicating whether a given
point lies inside or outside the shape (occupancy fields). The simplest way to process volumetric
fields is to use volumetric CNNs that sample the field on a uniform voxel grid to make the
representation discrete rather than continuous [36, 30]. The discretization in these grid-based
methods allows the use of standard 3D convolutional architectures, but it comes at the cost of
increased computational and memory requirements.
==PAGE:9==
1.1
Related work
2
These challenges are addressed by neural implicit approaches, which represent shapes as contin-
uous functions rather than storing them as discrete 3D voxel grids. Neural implicit approaches
encode volumetric fields using Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) that map any 3D point to a
distance or occupancy value, which allows for shape representations of high resolution that are
simultaneously memory-efficient [25, 27, 8, 29]. These methods can model entire datasets, rather
than only a single shape like in traditional distance field approaches. For example, Park et al.
[27] introduced the DeepSDF framework that encodes volumetric fields using an MLP in an au-
todecoder architecture that can represent an entire class of shapes. While this approach is more
computationally efficient than grid-based methods, MLP-based methods also present certain
challenges. Firstly, the MLP must be re-optimized for every new input during inference, making
the process time-consuming. Secondly, reconstructing 3D shapes from the learned continuous
volumetric field is computationally expensive, as it requires many MLP evaluations to compute
the volumetric field value at all sampled points, especially when reconstructing shapes at high
resolution (since higher resolution increases the number of sampled points).
While Park et al. [27] used a single global MLP for the entire shape representation, Peng et
al. [29] proposed the ConvONet network that combines grid-based representations with local
MLPs. By using multiple local MLPs instead of a single global MLP, they break the shape into
smaller, localized regions that each have their own MLP. Although their method reduces the
computational cost of the MLP evaluations, it remains limited by the resolution of the grid, and
can therefore still struggle to capture fine shape details.
Wang et al. [34] extend previous MLP-based approaches by using multiple local MLPs, similar
to [29], but represent the volumetric fields using an adaptive octree structure. The octree is a
hierarchical tree that represents the shape at different levels of resolution. Nodes at higher levels
of the octree (coarse nodes) represent low-frequency information (i.e. smoother variations in the
shape) and cover larger regions of space. In contrast, nodes at lower levels of the octree (fine
nodes) represent high-frequency information (i.e. fine details of the shape) and cover smaller re-
gions. This adaptive structure allows the framework to focus computational resources on regions
of the shape with fine details, while reducing the computational costs in regions with less detail.
The octree is processed using a dual octree graph neural network with an encoder-decoder de-
sign, which uses a novel graph convolution operator specifically tailored to the octree structure.
At each octree node, a local MLP then uses the features extracted from the octree to predict
the field value at a given point. Thereby, their method avoids unneccessary MLP evaluations
in less detailed areas regions of the shape, which makes their method faster. As a result, their
approach outperforms existing methods in both accuracy and computational efficiency across
various shape categories.
These neural implicit methods all focus on the task of 3D shape reconstruction of common ob-
ject categories (such as chair, car, lamp) from the ShapeNet dataset [7] or indoor scenes from
the ScanNet and Matterport3D datasets [9, 6]. Additionally, the Dual Octree Graph Network
has been evaluated on human body shapes from the D-Faust dataset [4]. An overview of these
approaches is provided in Table 1.

1.2
Aim of the thesis
In this thesis, we modify the Dual Octree Graph Network method [34] by replacing its original
autoencoder with a variational autoencoder (VAE) architecture, aiming to enforce a more struc-
tured and continuous latent space. The approach is adapted for medical imaging and applied
to various cardiac structures in 3D CT scans from the ImageCAS dataset [38]. Rather than
focusing on shape reconstruction, we analyze the learned latent space to evaluate anatomical
plausibility, shape variation, and assess whether the latent geometry is organized in a meaningful
and interpretable way. The project is conducted as an exploratory analysis of how deep implicit
models can be used in medical imaging to learn latent spaces that encode meaningful geometric
shape properties of anatomical structures. An overview of the pipeline is illustrated in Figure 1.
Figure 1. Overview of the proposed pipeline. Anatomical structures are extracted from
CT scans as segmentations, which are converted to meshes and then to point clouds. These
point clouds are transformed into dual octree graphs and passed through a dual octree graph
VAE that encodes them into a latent representation and reconstructs them. The reconstructed
graphs are converted to signed distance fields and then back to meshes. The shape information
in the resulting latent space, containing all samples from the dataset, is analyzed and explored.

Clinical background
2.1
Human heart
The human heart is made up of several parts that work together to keep blood flowing through
the body. It has four chambers: the right atrium (RA), right ventricle (RV), left atrium (LA),
and left ventricle (LV). Deoxygenated blood from the body flows into the RA through two large
veins, the superior and inferior vena cava. When the RA contracts, it sends blood into the
RV, which then pumps it into the pulmonary artery and toward the lungs to receive oxygen.
Oxygen-rich blood then returns to the LA through the pulmonary veins. When the LA con-
tracts, it pushes blood into the LV. The LV then pumps this oxygenated blood into the aorta,
which carries it to the rest of the body. The myocardium is the thick muscle layer of the heart
that contracts and relaxes to keep blood moving [15].
Figure 2 shows a volume rendering of the heart anatomy, where the four heart chambers, aorta,
pulmonary artery and pulmonary veins are colored according to whether they carry oxygenated
blood (red) or deoxygenated blood (blue).
Figure 2. Anatomy of the human heart, showing its four heart chambers (RA, RV, LA, LV),
myocardium, aorta, pulmonary artery, pulmonary vein, and left atrial appendage (LAA).
2.1.1
Left atrial appendage
The left atrial appendage (LAA) is a small, pouch-like extension of the LA, believed to be a
leftover structure from heart development in the embryo. Figure 2 shows the LAA in relation
to the other heart anatomy. Although its exact physiological role is not fully understood, it is
known to contribute to blood volume regulation and hormone secretion [3].
There is large anatomical variation of the LAA across individuals and is commonly categorized
into four morphological types: chicken wing, windsock, cactus, cauliflower. These names are
based on the shapes the LAA resembles, as illustrated in Figure 3. However, this classifica-
tion system suffers from high inter-observer variability, with clinicians often disagreeing on the
assigned morphology.

The LAA is the most common place where blood clots (thrombi) form in people with an irregular
heartbeat (atrial fibrillation). These blood clots can break free from the LAA and travel to the
brain, making it a risk for causing a stroke. Studies have shown that the shape of the LAA
influences this risk, with certain morphologies (such as the chicken wing) being associated with
a lower risk of stroke compared to others.
2.2
Coronary arteries
The coronary arteries are blood vessels that supply oxygenated blood to the heart. Since the
heart muscle needs a constant supply of oxygen to function, these arteries play a crucial role.
The coronary arteries form a complex branching network, known as the coronary artery tree,
which wraps around the surface of the heart. Smaller branches extend into the heart muscle
to deliver blood directly to the tissue. The two main coronary arteries are the left and right
coronary arteries, which originate from the left and right sides of the aortic root.
The left coronary artery (LCA) supplies blood to the left side of the heart and splits into two
major branches: the left anterior descending (LAD) artery and the left circumflex (LCx) artery.
Together, the LAD and LCx arteries supply blood to the LA and LV. The right coronary artery
(RCA) supplies blood to the right side of the heart and branches into smaller blood vessels
that deliver blood to the RA and RV [23]. Figure 4 shows a volume rendering of the heart,
highlighting the aorta, heart chambers, and coronary artery tree. It also illustrates the LCA
and RCA, as well as how the LCA branches into the LAD and LCx arteries.

and RCA (blue), at the aortic root are indicated. The initial branching point of the LCA is
marked with a yellow dot, where it divides into the LAD and LCx arteries.

2.2.1
Anatomical variation
The coronary artery tree varies significantly between individuals, with many different anatomical
configurations. Most of these variations are considered normal and are typically benign. Com-
mon differences include variations in the origin and course of the coronary arteries. For example,
a coronary artery may originate higher than usual from the aortic root [23], or the LAD and
LCx arteries may arise separately from the aorta instead of branching from a common LCA [37].
The most clinically significant type of coronary variation is coronary dominance (also known
as heart dominance). Coronary dominance refers to which artery gives rise to the posterior
descending artery (PDA), which supplies blood to the posterior (back) part of the heart. The
PDA can originate from the RCA in right-dominant hearts, from the LCx in left-dominant
hearts, or from both arteries in co-dominant cases. Approximately 85% of individuals have right
dominance, 10% have left dominance, and 5% have co-dominance [2]. Figure 5 shows examples
of right dominance (Figure 5a) and left dominance (Figure 5b), in which the branching point of
the PDA is marked.

Assessing coronary dominance is clinically significant, as it determines which arteries are re-
sponsible for supplying critical blood flow to the heart. In right-dominant hearts, a blockage in
the RCA is significant because it disrupts blood flow to the PDA, which affects the posterior
heart. Conversely, in left-dominant hearts, a blockage in the left coronary system is more crit-
ical. Especially left dominance, being less common, has been associated with a higher risk of
cardiac events [19, 26].
2.2.2
Coronary artery disease
Blockages in the coronary arteries are a key factor in developing coronary artery disease (CAD),
a leading cause of death worldwide. CAD occurs when fats, cholesterol, calcium and other sub-
stances accumulate along the inner walls of the coronary arteries, forming buildup known as
plaques. The buildup of plaque leads to a condition (atherosclerosis) in which the artery walls

become thicker and harder. This reduces their ability to transport blood to the heart muscle.
Plaques can be divided into two main types: calcified plaques and non-calcified plaques. Cal-
cified plaques are hardened buildups containing calcium that narrow the artery’s lumen, which
is the space through which blood flows. An example of calcified plaque buildup can be seen in
Figure 6, where the calcifications appear as bright spots inside the artery. By narrowing the
arterial lumen, calcified plaques restrict blood flow to the myocardium, which is the heart muscle
tissue that contracts and pumps blood out into the body. In contrast, non-calcified plaques are
softer and primarily consist of fatty substances. While they do not always lead to narrowing of
the artery, they are at risk of rupturing. When a plaque ruptures, it can result in a blood clot
forming, potentially leading to a heart attack or other life-threatening cardiac events [21].

CAD often develops silently over many years, with no noticeable symptoms until a sudden car-
diac event occurs. Due to the potential for reduced blood flow and blood clot formation, CAD
can lead to serious complications such as heart attacks and heart failure [31]. Therefore, it is
important for clinicians to be able to effectively and accurately identify early signs of coronary
plaque buildup to start preventive treatment and thus reduce the risk of severe outcomes.
2.3
Computed Tomography
Computed Tomography (CT) is a diagnostic imaging technology used to generate detailed im-
ages of the human body, including organs, bones, muscles, and blood vessels. The procedure
involves rotating an X-ray tube and detector array around the body while directing X-ray beams
at the targeted area. As the scanner quickly rotates, it captures multiple cross-sectional X-ray
images, or ”slices,” of the body. These successive slices are then stacked into a three-dimensional
image (volume), making it easier to identify the anatomical structures present in the CT scan.
Different tissues absorb different amounts of the X-rays based on their density. The degree to
which the X-rays have been absorbed are measured in Hounsfield Units (HU). For example, in
contrast-enhanced CT scans, fat has low HU values ranging from approximately [−115, −100]
[20], blood ranges between [30, 45] [10], while bone has high HU values between [300, 1900] [28].
By knowing these common ranges of HU values, radiologists can distinguish between different

2.3.1
Coronary CT Angiography
Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA) is a specialized CT scan used to visualize the coronary
arteries of the heart. It is performed on patients with symptoms of or at risk for developing
CAD, in order to detect blockages, plaque buildup (atherosclerosis), or narrowing of the arteries
(stenosis). To enhance the visibility of the coronary arteries in CCTA, an iodine-based contrast
dye is injected into the patient before the scan. Without contrast, these arteries are difficult
to distinguish because their HU values are similar to surrounding tissues. The dye makes them
appear brighter in the scan, making it easier for the radiologist to identify abnormalities asso-
ciated with CAD [14].
Although CCTA provides high-resolution images of the heart and coronary arteries, the imaging
quality can vary due to several challenges in the imaging process. One major issue is achieving
homogeneous contrast enhancement across the entire coronary artery tree. To do this, the scan
must be timed at a precise delay after the contrast injection to ensure the dye has reached the
ascending aorta. However, even with careful timing, the contrast is often not distributed evenly,
making some parts of the artery tree less visible. This can create imaging artifacts, where ar-
teries may appear disconnected or seem to end too soon. Figure 7 shows an example of this,
where the right branches of the coronary tree lack contrast, making it look like the RCA ends
prematurely.

CCTA scans are also prone to various kinds of motion artifacts. Patients must lie completely
still and hold their breath during the scan to reduce movement-related artifacts. However, even
when patient movement is controlled, the heart’s natural beating introduces another source
of motion artifacts [14, 11]. Additionally, some CT scanners do not capture the entire heart
in a a single rotation (i.e. scan). Instead, multiple sub-scans taken over different heartbeats
are combined to form the final image. This process can introduce step artifacts due to slight
differences in heart position between sub-scans, caused by patient movement and/or different
heartbeats. Also, it leads to uneven distribution of the contrast agent across sub-scans, resulting
in sudden changes in HU values between them. Figure 8 illustrates this effect in a CCTA scan
that has been stitched together by three separate sub-scans. The figure shows how HU values

radiologists, but also for automated analysis methods that need consistent image quality for
accurate results.

Data
Medical datasets are highly restricted due to patient privacy concerns, legal regulations, and the
challenges of getting patient consent. Due to strict data-sharing policies and the risk of patient
re-identification, even in anonymized data, most medical datasets remain within hospitals and
cannot be used for public research. Publicly available medical datasets are usually small and
often have biases in demographics and disease prevalence. Also, due to differences in CT scanners
and imaging protocols across hospitals, it is difficult to merge smaller datasets into a larger,
consistent one. High-quality annotations are also challenging to obtain, as it is expensive and
time-consuming to have radiologists label the data. Despite these challenges, efforts have been
made to create publicly available datasets that can support public research in medical imaging.

Limitations of the ImageCAS dataset
Although the ImageCAS dataset is of high quality in both raw volumes and annotations, it has
several limitations. One key issue is that the CT scanner used for all CCTA images in the dataset
does not capture the entire heart in a single rotation. As a result, multiple sub-scans have been

during transitions between sub-scans can sometimes cause these artifacts to make coronary ar-

step artifacts divide the aortic annulus, causing the LCA to appear as two separate segments
rather than arising from a single common origin on the aorta.

Methods
This chapter presents the methods and theory behind the design of a deep implicit model for
anatomical shape representation. The method was developed through the following steps.
1. Extract segmentations of additional cardiac structures with TotalSegmentator, and
partition annotations into three separate datasets (Section 4.1.1).
2. Convert data from binary label maps into surface meshes (Section 4.1.2).
3. Design and train a Dual Octree Graph VAE model on each dataset (Section 4.6).
4. Evaluate the performance of every model by assessing the quality of the latent space
and the reconstructions (Section 4.7).
4.1
Data preprocessing
4.1.1
Dataset preparation
Extracting additional cardiac labels
In addition to the coronary artery labels available in the ImageCAS dataset, segmentations
of the LAA were extracted following the state-of-the-art method described by Sørensen et al.
[32], and segmentations of the four heart chambers (LA, LV, RA, RV) were extracted using To-
talSegmentator. TotalSegmentator, introduced by Wasserthal et al. [35], is a tool developed for
segmenting a diverse set of anatomical structures in medical images. It uses the self-configuring
nnUNet framework by Isensee et al. [16], which adjusts automatically to the specific attributes
of the given dataset, such as image resolution, spacing, and modality.
The original TotalSegmentator tool (total) performs general-purpose whole-body segmentation,
identifying up to 117 structures in CT scans. This includes a coarse segmentation of the heart
as a single region, along with a few structures adjacent to the heart. For more detailed cardiac
anatomy, the specialized high-resolution task (heartchambers highres) provides accurate seg-
mentation of seven key cardiac structures: LA, LV, RA, RV, ascending aorta, pulmonary artery,
and the LV myocardium. In this work, the cardiac-specific task (heartchambers highres) was
applied to the ImageCAS dataset to obtain segmentations of the LA, LV, RA, RV. Figure 13
shows the obtained labels of the four heart chambers and the LAA.
Filtering of the data
Due to inconsistencies in scan quality and coronary artery annotation accuracy across the Im-
ageCAS dataset (as described in Section 3.2), a filtering process was applied to identify and
address scans and annotations with potential errors or inconsistencies. A series of heuristics
were used to detect scans that might have issues such as step artifacts, over-segmentation, and
under-segmentation. This filtering process resulted in a reduction of the dataset from 1000 scans
to 766 scans.

4.1.2
Data conversion into mesh
To make the anatomical segmentations compatible with the proposed framework in this project,
each sample in the three datasets (introduced in Section 4.1.1) was transformed from a binary
label map into a mesh representation. This process involved extracting a mesh from the binary
label map, scaling it appropriately, and sampling points from its surface. These steps were ap-
plied consistently across all six anatomical structures (coronary arteries, LA, LV, RA, RV, and
LAA). The illustrative figures in this section are based on the coronary arteries, but the same
preprocessing pipeline was applied to all anatomies.
Converting label map to surface mesh
The first step in preprocessing involved converting each anatomical segmentation from a binary
label map into a surface representation. A binary label map, L : Z3 →{0, 1}, consists of voxel
values L(p) that are either 0 or 1, where L(p) = 0 represents background and L(p) = 1 repre-
sents the object (in this case, the anatomical structure). A surface representation refers to the
smooth, geometric shape or outer boundary of the object. To extract this surface of the anatom-
ical structure, an isosurface S = {p ∈R3 : L(p) = 1} was generated from the label map using
the Marching Cubes algorithm [22]. This process results in a triangular mesh, M = (V, E, F)
made up of vertices V, edges E, and faces F, that approximates the smooth surface of the labeled
anatomical structure.
Mesh scaling
After extracting the mesh for each anatomy, all meshes were centered and scaled relative to the
largest mesh within their respective anatomical group. Figure 14 shows an illustration of the
mesh scaling for the coronary arteries.

4.2
Octree representation
To efficiently represent the complex shape of the anatomies (coronary artery tree, heart cham-
bers, and LAA), a structured and flexible data format is needed. While surface meshes provide
a compact way to store the anatomical structures, they do not organize the information in a way
that takes advantage of the shape of the surface. For that reason, the anatomies were converted
from surface mesh point clouds (as described in Section 4.1.2) into an octree representation,
which is a hierarchical and adaptive data structure. Octrees were first introduced by Meagher
et al. [24] and have since become widely used in computer graphics.
An octree is a tree-based data structure used for subdividing a 3D space into progressively
smaller cubic regions called nodes. Each node n in the octree represents a cubic region within
the 3D volume and can be subdivided into eight child nodes (octants). Let T denote an octree,
and let N be the set of all nodes in T . The octree is built from the point cloud P = {pi ∈R3}N
i=1
sampled from the scaled mesh of the anatomical structure. The construction process begins by
defining a bounding box B that encloses all points in P, which represents the root node n0 of

the octree. Starting at the root, the 3D space is progressively subdivided by splitting each cubic
node into eight child octants. Each point pi in the point cloud is assigned to the corresponding
octant based on its spatial coordinates (xi, yi, zi). Each subdivision corresponds to an octree
resolution of depth d, starting from depth d = 0 at the root. The octree resolution at depth
d can then be denoted T d. This subdivision process initially continues until a predefined full
depth dfull is reached, which ensures that the octree has a uniform minimum resolution across
the structure. Due to the uniform subdivision of each node into eight children from d = 0 to
d = dfull, every resolution from T 0 to T dfull has a total of 8d nodes,

Beyond this depth, i.e. dfull < d ≤dmax, further subdivision of the 3D space is data-dependent
and done selectively: only nodes that intersect with the surface of the anatomy continue to be
subdivided until the maximum depth dmax is reached. Hence, an octree configuration is charac-
terized by the two predefined depth parameters, dfull and dmax, where dfull ≤dmax. Increasing
these parameters enhances the resolution of the octree, enabling it to capture finer surface details.
Due to the progressive hierarchical subdivision, the octree adapts its resolution across the 3D
volume. This means areas with fine geometric details (e.g., the thin branches of the coronary
artery tree) are represented at higher resolution in ”fine” nodes, while simpler regions (e.g.,
areas far from the anatomy) are stored at a lower resolution in ”coarse” nodes. This makes the
octree memory-efficient, as it only stores high-resolution data where it is most needed.

4.3
Dual octree graph
For a given octree T d, a corresponding dual octree graph Gd can be constructed by connecting
all face-adjacent octree leaf nodes of different resolutions. Each octree leaf node can have up to
six face-adjacent neighbors, one in each of the ±x, ±y, and ±z directions. In defining the dual
octree graph, the notation follow that of Wang et al. [34]. The dual octree graph is defined as
Gd = {Vd, Ed}, where Vd = {vi} represents the set of graph vertices, and Ed = {ei} represents
the set of graph edges connecting the vertices. A vertex vi is defined as (xi, yi, zi, di), where the
first three components (xi, yi, zi) specify the coordinates of the bottom-left corner of an octree
leaf node, and the fourth component di specifies its depth. An edge eij ∈Ed exists between two
vertices vi and vj if their corresponding octree nodes are face-adjacent.

4.4
Autoencoder
An autoencoder (AE) is a deep learning (DL) model that learns to encode high-dimensional
data into a compact, low-dimensional representation and then reconstruct it as accurately as
possible. By capturing the most important features of the input, AEs can for example be used for
dimensionality reduction, noise removal, outlier detection, and discovering underlying structures
in the data [13].

AEs consist of two main components:
• Encoder: Transforms the input data into a compact, lower-dimensional representation,
capturing its most essential features.
• Decoder: Reconstructs the original data from this compressed representation, aiming to
make the output as close as possible to the original input.
Let X be a dataset, and let x ∈X be an input data point. Assume there exists a low-dimensional
representation z ∈Z that captures the most important features of x. The encoder Eθ : X →Z
and decoder Dϕ : Z →X are neural network functions parameterized by θ and ϕ, respectively.
For any x ∈X, its compact representation is obtained as z = Eθ(x), referred to as the latent
representation, latent variable, or latent vector. The space Z of all latent vectors is known as
the latent space. Conversely, any z ∈Z can be reconstructed as x′ = Dϕ(z), referred to as the
reconstruction or decoded input. Figure 21 illustrates the AE architecture.
Figure 21. Illustration of the autoencoder architecture: the encoder transforms the input x
into a latent vector z, and the decoder reconstructs the input x′ from the latent vector z.
The objective of an AE is to generate reconstructions x′ that closely resemble the original input
x, i.e. x ≈x′. The reconstruction quality is quantified using a function d : X × X →[0, ∞],
where d(x, x′) measures the difference between x′ and x. The loss function for training the AE
can then be defined as
LAE(θ, ϕ) = Ex∼p(x)[d(x, Dϕ(Eθ(x)))],
(2)
where p(x) represents the data distribution. This ensures that the learned encoder-decoder pair
effectively compresses and reconstructs the input while preserving its most relevant information.
By minimizing this loss, the encoder-decoder pair learns to compress and reconstruct the input
while keeping its most important features. The optimal parameters θ∗and ϕ∗of the encoder
and decoder networks are found through the optimization

This process allows the AE to learn a compact representation of the data, focusing on important
features while ignoring redundant information.
The AE is trained to encode and decode the input data effectively, such that a given input x ∈X
is consistently mapped to the same latent vector z ∈Z. However, since the AE does not impose
any structure or constraints on the latent space, two similar inputs xi, xj ∈X (i.e., xi ≈xj) are
not guaranteed to be mapped to latent vectors zi, zj ∈Z that are close to each other. This lack
of structure in the latent space makes the basic AE unsuitable for tasks like data synthesis, as
it is difficult to generate realistic samples that resemble the original data.
4.5
Variational autoencoder
A variational autoencoder (VAE) shares architectural similarities with the basic AE, but is de-
signed with different objectives and is based on variational Bayesian methods. Rather than
mapping an input to a fixed latent vector as in the AE, the VAE maps the input into a distri-
bution. Therefore, the latent space is represented as a set of distributions, rather than a set of
fixed vectors. This approach adds structure to the latent space, encouraging that similar inputs
correspond to similar latent vectors [17].
4.5.1
Prior and posterior distribution
Given an input data point x ∈X, the probability distribution over possible latent variables
is denoted pϕ, parameterized by ϕ. The relationship between the input data x and the latent
variable z can be described using the following Bayesian terms:
• Prior pϕ(z): The prior belief about the latent vector z, independent of the data x.
• Likelihood pϕ(x|z): The probability distribution of the data x, given the latent vector z.
• Posterior pϕ(z|x): The posterior distribution of the latent variable z, given the observed
data x, which contains all knowledge about z after observing x.
Through these probability distributions, a sample that looks like a real data point xi can be
generated with the following steps:
1. Sample a latent vector zi from the prior distribution pϕ(z).
2. Generate the data xi from the conditional distribution pϕ(x|z = zi).
The optimal parameters ϕ∗are those that maximize the likelihood of generating real data sam-

4.5.3
Reparametrization trick
Similar to a standard AE, the optimal parameters θ∗and ϕ∗of the probabilistic encoder and
decoder networks in a VAE are found by minimizing the VAE loss function:

However, the reconstruction loss in the VAE objective (Equation 9) involves an expectation over
a latent variable z, which is sampled from the latent distribution as z ∼qθ(z|x). This introduces
a challenge during training: sampling from a distribution is a non-differentiable operation, and
therefore the gradients with respect to θ, which parametrize the distribution qθ(z|x), cannot
be computed. As a result, standard backpropagation cannot be used directly to update the
encoder. To make it trainable, the reparameterization trick is introduced. Instead of sampling
z directly, the random variable z is expressed as a deterministic function of:
1. The input x
2. The encoder parameters θ
3. A stochastic component ϵ that is independent of θ
A typical choice for the prior distribution is a standard Gaussian, pϕ(z) = N(0, I), which assumes
that the latent variables are independently distributed with zero mean and unit variance. For
the encoder’s approximate posterior qθ(z|x), a common choice is a multivariate Gaussian with
a diagonal covariance structure, often referred to as a diagonal Gaussian:
qθ(z|x) = N(µ(x), σ2(x) I),
(11)
where both the mean µ(x) and standard deviation σ(x) depend on the input sample x. This
diagonal Gaussian form ensures that the latent variables are independent of each other, and their
variances are learned based on the input data. The parameters µ(x) and σ(x) are outputted by
the encoder network, parameterized by θ. Using the reparameterization trick, we then sample
z as:
z = µ(x) + σ(x) · ϵ,
where ϵ ∼N(0, I)
(12)
Now, instead of sampling z directly, we sample ϵ ∼N(0, I), which is independent of the encoder
parameters θ. As a result, the stochasticity remains in the variable ϵ, while z can be computed
deterministically. This reparameterization makes the model differentiable, allowing the encoder
to be trained using backpropagation. Figure 22 illustrates the VAE architecture, highlighting
how the latent variable z is computed using the reparametrization trick.

4.5.4
β-VAE
In traditional VAEs, the KL divergence term in the loss function ensures that the learned latent
distribution qθ(z|x) is close to the prior distribution pϕ(z). However, this can cause the model
to produce a latent space that is too constrained, as it is forced towards the prior. This can
limit the model’s ability to capture complex patterns in the data. To address this, a hyperpa-
rameter β can be introduced to balance the trade-off between the reconstruction error and the
KL divergence term in the VAE loss function. Having this hyperparameter allows for a more
flexibility in the latent space, while still maintaining some regularization to keep the learned
latent distribution close to the prior distribution [12].
VAEs that include this hyperparameter are called β-VAEs. The loss function for a β-VAE is:

Equation 13 reduces to Equation 9, making the β-VAE equivalent to the standard VAE.
For β > 1, the VAE places stronger emphasis on the KL divergence term, leading to a more
constrained latent space. This can cause the model to learn a latent space that closely follows
the prior distribution but may hurt the reconstruction quality, as it restricts the flexibility of
the latent space. Conversely, for β < 1, the reconstruction error is given more weight, allowing
the latent space to become more flexible. This results in a looser fit to the prior, which can lead
to better reconstruction accuracy, but at the risk of a less regularized latent space. Tuning β
can be difficult and requires experimentation based on the task.
A key advantage of the β-VAE over the standard VAE is that it encourages disentangled latent
representations. In a disentangled latent representation z, each latent dimension corresponds
to a distinct, independent generative factor. For example, when training a β-VAE on human
face images, the learned latent variables might independently represent factors such as facial

4.5.5
Generating new data
Since the latent space of VAEs consists of distributions qθ(z|x) that are close to the prior
distribution, new data can be generated by sampling a latent vector z from the prior distribution,
z ∼N(0, I), and then passing this latent vector z through the decoder to reconstruct the
corresponding synthetic sample x′. The generated sample x′ will resemble the training data
but will not be identical to any specific data sample x the model has seen during training, as
the decoder generates new, plausible variations from the learned latent space.

4.6.1
Graph operations
Dual octree graph convolutions.
Wang et al.
[34] introduce a novel graph convolution
operation that takes advantages of the structure of dual octree graphs. Unlike standard octrees,
which organize nodes hierarchically via parent-child relationships, the dual octree graph encodes
geometric adjacency by connecting face-adjacent leaf nodes across different octree levels. The
leaf nodes, which make up the majority of the dual octree graph, have exactly six neighbors in
the ±x, ±y and ±z directions. Thus, the relative positions ∆pij between a node and its neigh-
bors is a fixed-size vector. Therefore, instead of learning a continuous function W = MLP(∆pij)
like in other graph convolution methods, they set W to be a fixed-size weight matrix of 7 rows
W = [W0, . . . , W6], mapping ∆pij (including the center node) to {0, 1, . . . , 6} according to its
relative direction. Node features are augmented with node depth and relative position informa-
tion and gathered into a matrix M, allowing the dual octree graph convolution to be computed
efficiently as a matrix product F = M ×W. This formulation allows for fast graph convolutions
with multi-scale feature fusion, in contrast to previous methods like O-CNN [33], which apply
octree convolutions only across nodes at the same resolution.
Graph downsampling and upsampling. In the encoder-decoder architecture, Wang et al.
[34] use downsampling and upsampling of the dual octree graphs to allow multi-scale feature
learning. Downsampling reduces the spatial resolution by merging the finest-resolution leaf nodes
(i.e. depth d) into their parent nodes (depth d −1), using a shared fully connected (FC) layer
==PAGE:35==
4.6
Dual Octree Graph VAE
28
to combine the features of the 8 sibling nodes into one. Coarser nodes remain unchanged. After
downsampling, the octree depth of the resulting graph decreases by 1. For example, the dual
octree graph in Figure 19d reduces to Figure 19c after downsampling. Upsampling reverses this
operation by expanding coarser nodes back to finer ones using the inverse of the FC mapping,
i.e. increasing the octree depth of the resulting graph by 1. These operations help the network
capture broad shape information at low resolution and add fine details back in as the resolution
increases.
4.6.2
Neural MPU
The decoder of the VAE network outputs a set of learned features for each octree node, forming
an adaptive feature volume. These features are combined into point-wise predictions by the
neural MPU, which converts the adaptive feature volume into a continuous volumetric field.
At any query point, the neural MPU blends outputs from nearby octree nodes using smooth,
spatially-local weight functions. Each node contributes a value through a small MLP Φ(x, Fi),
which takes the query point x and the node’s feature Fi as input and returns a local field value.
The final SDF value at x is a weighted average of these local predictions, where the weights
depend on the distance between the query point and each node. This way, the neural MPU
smoothly combines local information to produce a continuous volumetric field, from which a
surface mesh is extracted using the Marching Cubes algorithm [22].

The encoder takes as input a dual octree graph with depth d = 7, denoted by G7 = V7, E7,
which is constructed from the input point cloud P. This graph is processed through graph
convolutions to extract multi-scale features, and is gradually downsampled in three steps from
depth d = 7 to d = 4. At the end of the encoder, a 1 × 1 convolution is applied to the features
to project them into a latent code of shape (4096, 6). This latent code contains the parameters
of a diagonal Gaussian posterior distribution, i.e. the mean µ(x) and the variance σ2(x), for
each of the 4096 nodes in the downsampled graph at depth d = 4. For each node in the latent
code, the first three dimensions represent the mean vector µ(x) ∈R4096×3, and the last three
dimensions represent the variance vector σ2(x) ∈R4096×3. A latent vector z ∈R4096×3 is then
sampled from this distribution as z = µ(x) + σ(x) · ϵ, where ϵ ∼N(0, I). Before being passed
into the decoder, this sampled latent vector is transformed back into a feature representation
using another 1 × 1 convolution.
The decoder consists of four stages in which the graph is upsampled back from depth d = 4
to d = 7. At each stage, corresponding to a specific octree resolution d, the model predicts
the octree splitting labels Ld: that is, for each node, whether it should be split into eight child
nodes at the next resolution level. This predicted splitting is used to construct the dual octree
graph Gd at the current depth. At the same time, the features extracted during decoding are
passed to the neural MPU, which predicts the SDF values at each octree resolution. As a result,
both an octree and an SDF are predicted for each of the octree resolutions d = [4, 5, 6, 7]. These
predictions are then compared to the ground truth octrees and SDFs from the corresponding
encoder stages, and the differences are used to define the loss functions that guide the training
of the network.

Encoding and decoding data

x′. The better the reconstruction, the lower the Chamfer distance between x and x′ will be, and
vice versa.
4.7.3
Visualizing the latent space
Since the latent vectors have shape (4096, 3), various dimensionality reduction techniques are
applied to project them into a 2D space for visualization. This helps in understanding how
the model organizes and represents different anatomical shapes in the latent space. The main
techniques used are Principal Component Analysis (PCA), t-SNE, and UMAP.
PCA is used to assess whether linear combinations of the latent mean vectors capture mean-
ingful anatomical variation. By projecting the latent space onto the principal components, we
can see if the main modes of variation correspond to interpretable factors like size or various
shape variations. It also shows how much of the variance is explained by the principal compo-
nents, indicating whether the latent encoding is structured or redundant. For example, a strong
correlation between the first principal component and anatomical size would suggest that size
is a key factor captured by the model.
t-SNE is a non-linear technique that focuses on preserving local relationships between data
points. It is used for visualizing clusters or groupings that may exist in the latent space, such
as different heart chamber types. Unlike PCA, t-SNE does not preserve global structure, so the
distance between points that are far apart in the plot may not reflect their true difference in the
latent space.
UMAP is another non-linear dimensionality reduction method used alongside t-SNE to visual-
ize the latent space in 2D. Compared to t-SNE, UMAP better preserves both local and global
structure, making it easier to see whether the model has learned a meaningful organization
within and/or between anatomical classes.
Together, these techniques help assess whether the model’s latent space captures relevant anatom-
ical features, how samples are organized, and whether distinct anatomical classes (for the multi-
class heart chambers model) are separated or mixed.
4.7.4
Clustering in latent space
To quantitatively determine whether the learned latent space Z contains distinct clusters of
anatomical shapes, the k-means clustering algorithm is applied to the latent representations
zi ∈Z. The quality of the clusters is assessed using the intra- and inter-cluster distances, as
well as the Silhoutte score. The intra-cluster distance measures compactness within each cluster,
and is defined as the average Euclidean distance between each point and its cluster centroid.
Lower values indicate tighter clusters. The inter-cluster distance measures separation between
clusters, and is defined as the average pairwise distance between cluster centroids. Higher values
indicate better separation. The Silhoutte score is a combined measure of the intra- and inter-
cluster distances. For a point zi, let a(i) be the average distance to other points in the same
cluster, and b(i) the lowest average distance to points in any other cluster. The Silhouette score

Discussion
6.1
Model design
The performance of the deep implicit model and the quality of the learned shape representa-
tions are closely tied to the model’s design and training setup. The model is designed for the
dual octree graph structure, which compresses anatomical annotations into an adaptive graph
structure. This design reduces computational costs during training, enabling the model to be
trained for 250 epochs in approximately 5 hours using less than 10 GB of memory on an Nvidia
V100 GPU.
The most computationally expensive part of the pipeline is the preprocessing stage, which in-
volves sampling point clouds from surface meshes and computing SDF values. Once this prepro-
cessing is completed, however, the storage requirements for each sample are minimal, taking up
only about 300 KB for the point cloud and 5 MB for the sampled SDF values. These storage and
computational demands depend on the octree configuration parameters, dfull and dmax, which
specify the resolution of the octrees. Higher octree resolutions allow the model to capture finer
details but also increase preprocessing time and memory usage. For example, using dfull = 4
and dmax = 7 gave enough detail for the heart chambers, since they don’t have much fine detail.
But for the coronary arteries, this setting caused visible loss of detail, like branches appearing
too thick or nearby branches being merged, because the resolution wasn’t high enough to clearly
separate close structures (Figure 33). Switching to a higher resolution (dfull = 5, dmax = 8) kept
more details, but it also made the reconstructions more fragmented.
This shows the flexibility of the dual octree graph design: it allows adjusting the level of detail
to match the anatomical structure being modeled while keeping training efficient. By sampling
a fixed number of SDF values from the octree nodes, the model focuses computation on complex
areas with many nodes and reduces it in smoother regions with fewer nodes. This way, the
model captures fine shape features with relatively low memory and runtime costs compared to
other models for volumetric data.
Training the dual octree graph VAE involves balancing three main components: the KL loss
(encoder), the octree loss (decoder), and the regression loss (neural MPU). After around 100
epochs, the model began showing signs of overfitting across all datasets. The octree loss on
the test set started increasing, while the KL and regression losses continued to decrease. This
indicates that the decoder was beginning to overfit the training data, focusing too much on
memorizing the specific node-splitting patterns in the training octrees. Because the octree loss
is simply a binary cross-entropy loss between the predicted and ground truth node split decisions
(split or not split) at each depth level of the octree, even small mismatches in splitting decisions
are penalized, particularly at deeper levels (like dmax = 7), where the number of nodes is large
and variations are more complex. This can cause the model to focus on replicating exact node
splits instead of learning generalizable geometry. If the octree loss starts dominating the total
loss (like it did across all three datasets), the decoder may use the latent code from the encoder
to memorize training splits rather than capture meaningful shape variations, hence reducing
generalization. Balancing the different losses of the model also involves tuning the weighing
factors β of the KL loss and λv of the regression loss.
However, loss curves alone are not
enough to determine whether the model has learned a useful latent space or produces accurate
reconstructions, making the tuning of the training process difficult.

Interpretation of latent parameters

6.2
Interpretation of latent parameters
For each sample, the model learns a latent mean and a latent standard deviation. When en-
coding all samples using only the latent means, the variability contributed by the standard
deviations is ignored. As shown in Figure 26, the distributions of latent means and standard
deviations across all three datasets are generally centered near zero and one, respectively. This
indicates that the encoder aligns well with the diagonal Gaussian prior. For the heart chambers
dataset (Figure 26c), these distributions are much narrower than for the coronary artery (Figure
26a) and LAA (Figure 26b) datasets, meaning there is less spread in the learned representations.
This could suggest that the latent space is underused, especially if large parts of it remain unused.
However, in the multi-class heart chambers dataset, the low variability observed in both the
latent means and standard deviations (Figure 26c) is more likely linked to the anatomical con-
sistency across samples. Since heart chambers tend to have similar overall shapes, the model
does not need as much flexibility to represent them, resulting in tighter latent distributions.
The t-SNE projection of the latent means still reveals clear separation between the four heart
chamber classes (Figure 39a), suggesting that the model captures class-specific structure. On
the other hand, latent traversals along each of the three mean dimensions result in no visible
change in the reconstructed shapes, indicating that these axis-aligned directions do not corre-
spond to interpretable or disentangled shape variations.
A PCA applied to the latent encodings within each heart chamber class revealed more inter-
pretable directions of variation. The first principal component typically corresponded to changes
in overall size, while the second captured differences in orientation (Figure 35). This suggests
that meaningful variation does exist in the latent space, but it is not aligned with the individual
latent dimensions. Instead, it lies in combinations of them, as revealed by PCA. Although the
model effectively captures global structure between classes, finer variation within classes appears
entangled and is not directly accessible through axis-aligned latent traversals.
One possible explanation is that the shared encoder must represent all four heart chambers
within a single latent space, which can make it harder to capture subtle differences within each
class. This may cause the model to focus more on separating the classes than on capturing
detailed variation within them. Training separate models for each heart chamber could help
address this. By focusing on one anatomical structure at a time, the model may learn a more
disentangled and interpretable latent space, potentially resulting in clearer and more meaningful
latent traversals. However, this approach would sacrifice the multi-class aspect of the model, so
the choice depends on whether the primary interest lies in distinguishing between heart chamber
types or in analyzing shape variation within each type.
In contrast, the coronary artery dataset shows higher variability in the distributions of both
the latent means and standard deviations (Figure 26a).
This is also reflected in the latent
traversal, where changing the values along the three latent mean dimensions does lead to visible
changes in the reconstructed artery shapes (Figure 36). However, these changes are not easily
interpretable or isolated to single factors of variation. This suggests that while the latent space
carries meaningful variation, it is still entangled. Hence, the model has not learned a disentangled
representation, despite the use of a β-VAE setup.

6.3
Learned shape representations
A key takeaway from applying the dual octree graph VAE to anatomical structures is that
anatomical plausibility in the latent space is not guaranteed. This is clearly seen in the coronary
artery dataset, where many reconstructions, both of real samples and synthetically generated
ones, are fragmented into multiple disconnected components. This is not anatomically plausible,
as the left coronary artery tree is expected to be a single connected component. On the other
hand, reconstructions for the heart chambers and the LAA are much more anatomically accu-
rate. Even synthetic shapes generated from latent codes appear realistic (e.g. Figure 34). For
the heart chambers, the model often corrects anatomically implausible elements in the ground
truth. In some of the worst reconstructions (based on Chamfer distance), the model actually
produces more plausible shapes than the input. For instance, when part of a chamber is missing
in the ground truth due to limited CT scan coverage, the model fills in the missing region and
smooths out flat cuts (Figure 31). This suggests that the model has learned a strong prior on
what a realistic heart chamber should look like, which it applies during reconstruction. As a
result, the model could potentially be used to correct incomplete annotations or flag inconsistent
training samples by identifying reconstructions that differ significantly from the ground truth,
making it a valuable tool for quality control of anatomical annotations.
That anatomical plausibility isn’t guaranteed is expected, since the model doesn’t include a loss
that enforces connectedness or anatomical correctness. Defining such a loss, like enforcing sin-
gle connected components, is especially challenging for dual octree graphs, both in theory and
practice. Even with such a loss, there’s no guarantee of fully solving the anatomical plausibility
problem, and it may introduce new trade-offs.
Despite this, across all datasets, the model seems to organize the latent space according to mean-
ingful shape characteristics. Samples that are close in latent space tend to be similar in shape
(Figures 37 and 38). Anatomical size is one of the main factors shaping the latent space for
both the coronary arteries and heart chambers (Figures 28a and 28c). For the heart chambers,
this effect is present both within each class (Figure 29) and between classes, even though size
differences between classes were removed during pre-processing by scaling. However, the LAA
dataset shows no clear size-based pattern in the latent space, suggesting the model has instead
learned other shape or structural features.
For the coronary arteries, the model primarily captures topological variation, such as branching
patterns, bifurcations, and global connectivity.
This makes sense, as the largest anatomical
variation across subjects in coronary artery trees lies in the topology, not the precise geometry
(e.g. thickness or curvature). As a result, samples close in latent space tend to have similar
branch configurations (Figure 38a). However, interpolations between samples, even when they
are topologically similar, often lead to fragmented reconstructions, where branches break or
merge unnaturally (Figure 36). In contrast, for the heart chambers and LAA, which have fixed
topology, the model captures geometric differences such as shape, curvature, and size. In the
multi-class heart chamber dataset, the model clearly learns to separate the four chambers in
latent space based on geometric features (Figure 39), even though relative size differences be-
tween classes were removed. This strong separation appears despite the heart chambers having
somewhat similar shapes. Additionally, the multi-class model also shows potential for outlier
detection. Four LV samples appear outside the LV cluster and near the LA cluster (Figure 39a).
These samples don’t seem to be mislabeled, but they likely differ from typical LV shapes. To-
gether, these results highlight two ways the model can help with quality control: (1) by flagging
cases where the reconstruction deviates significantly from the original shape, and (2) by identi-

fying unusual samples based on their position in latent space. Both methods can be useful for
checking anatomical annotations, especially when using automated tools like TotalSegmentator,
by helping to catch shapes that don’t match the model’s learned representation or that stand
out from their class.
Unlike the heart chambers and coronary arteries, the latent space of the LAA is not organized
by size (Figure 28b). Instead, it reflects other shape features, with the nearest neighbors in
latent space having similar shapes in terms of morphology (Figure 37b).
The LAA latent
space shows four clear, well-separated clusters (Figure 40). The samples closest to each cluster
centroid represent the core morphological characteristics of that group (Figure 41). These cluster
shapes resemble characteristics of the four commonly described LAA morphologies (chicken
wing, cactus, windsock, and cauliflower, shown in Figure 3), though they were discovered in an
unsupervised manner without predefined morphology labels. Furthermore, transitions between
clusters are smooth, with interpolated shapes blending features from different clusters while
preserving anatomical plausibility (Figure 42). Together, these findings indicate that the model
has learned to represent the morphological diversity of the LAA in a structured and meaningful
way. This represents a first step toward quantifying the complexity of LAA shapes through
automated methods, rather than relying on the current clinical practice of subjective visual
assessments that is known to suffer from high inter-observer variability.
6.4
Anatomical variation and data quality
The differences in the learned shape representations across the various anatomical structures
are closely linked to the amount of anatomical variation within each structure. Among all the
structures analyzed, the coronary artery tree stands out as the most variable and complex. It
has many possible configurations and is highly volatile in its branching patterns. This is true
even though the dataset was simplified by including only the left part of the coronary artery tree,
excluding the right part. Greater anatomical variation makes the learning task more difficult.
In the case of the coronary artery tree, the variation was so large that the model struggled to
capture and represent the shapes in a meaningful way. The reconstructions of actual samples
were often inaccurate, and the synthesized shapes were completely unrealistic. As a result, the
coronary artery dataset, in its current form, was not well suited for this modeling task. To
improve results, the complexity of the dataset could be reduced further. Instead of modeling
the entire tree structure, it may be more effective to focus on individual coronary arteries, such
as the LAD or LCx artery. This approach could provide a more manageable learning task and
lead to more reliable shape representations.
Lastly, as with any deep learning model, the quality of the input data is critical. If the training
data is flawed, the model’s output will also be affected. This is especially true for the dual
octree graph VAE, where the learned shape representation depends directly on the accuracy and
completeness of the annotations used during training. While the model has shown some ability
to correct individual flawed samples when they are projected into the latent space, it cannot
overcome systematic issues in the data. For example, if many training samples consistently
contain the same type of error (such as holes in anatomical structures where there should be
none) the model will learn these flawed patterns. As a result, these inaccuracies will be reflected
in the latent space and in the shapes the model generates. This highlights the importance of
high-quality, accurate annotations, regardless of whether they are generated by human experts
(as for the coronary artery tree in the ImageCAS dataset), or by automated segmentation tools
(as for the heart chambers and LAA).

Conclusion
In this project, a dual octree graph VAE was developed for efficient and adaptive shape repre-
sentation of anatomical structures. The model was applied to datasets of the coronary artery
tree, heart chambers, and LAA. Across all datasets, the dual octree graph structure provided a
compact and flexible way to represent 3D anatomical shapes. Because the model was tailored
to the octree design, computational resources were concentrated on regions with fine details,
reducing memory and computational costs low while still capturing complex geometry. This
made the approach well suited for anatomical data, which often includes both large smooth
areas and small detailed regions.
The model successfully organized shape information into a low-dimensional latent space that
captured meaningful anatomical variation.
While the latent space was not disentangled, it
grouped similar shapes across all datasets, and clearly separated classes in the multi-class heart
chambers model. Reconstructions were anatomically realistic for the heart chambers and LAA,
with deviations in reconstruction quality or latent space placement often corresponding to in-
complete or inconsistent ground truths. This indicates that the learned latent space can support
quality control by flagging unusual samples or discrepancies between inputs and reconstructions.
In contrast, reconstructions of the coronary artery tree were less reliable, reflecting the model’s
difficulty with highly variable topologies.
