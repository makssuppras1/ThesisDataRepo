Abstract
Over the past years a growing number of wind turbines has been installed. With general trends towards
increasing size, power rating and off-shore distance of wind turbines, the use of fault diagnosis and fault-
tolerant control systems is more attractive, due to the possibility of reduced wear and fatigue of components,
costs of maintenance and down-time. Where fault diagnosis and condition monitoring are used to reducing
costs by avoiding critical failure and planning maintenance of wind turbines, an efﬁcient fault-tolerant
control scheme has the objective of keeping the wind turbine - to a satisfactory extend - operational with
nominal or reduced performance.
This thesis focus on understanding and designing parts of a fault-tolerant control scheme for wind tur-
bines operating in the full load region. A selection of fault scenarios in pitch actuators and rotor speed sensor
are modelled and implemented as step changes in the system parameters. A non-linear wind turbine model
is developed and used in the design and operation of an Extended Kalman Filter in order to estimate system
states and fault parameters based on measurements with measurement noise. Due to the inclusion of system
constraints in the solution of the control problem, a Model Predictive Controller (global MPC) is employed
to carry out the operation during nominal conditions in the full load region. A reduced order linear model is
used in this design to reduce computational effort in the optimisation. Another controller (pre-compensator)
between the global MPC and the plant is designed with the purpose of compensating for pitch actuator faults.
It contains another MPC which minimizes the difference between faulty and healthy actuators. Sensor faults
are simply approached by estimating their size, which corrects the state estimates. Simulations show, that
the system performance in most fault scenarios is improved by the use of the proposed fault-tolerant control
scheme in terms of reference tracking capabilities and reduced tower motion.

In fault-tolerant control schemes it is desired to exploit the knowledge about the faults delivered from the
diagnosis scheme and attempt to compensate their impact on the system during operation. There are different
approaches to designing fault- tolerant control (FTC) systems. Figure 1.3 shows the overall architecture of a

detection, isolation and estimation delivers information about the fault to the controller redesign block. Based

on this information, the controller is redesigned before calculating the input to the plant which is subject to
faults and disturbances. Passive fault-tolerant controllers consist of a ﬁxed control law. Their parameters
and architecture are thereby independent of the occurrence of a fault. As an example, robust controllers can
be used as passive fault tolerant controllers, if they are designed to tolerate the changes in dynamics which
are the outcome of a fault. The passive approach is however said to be conservative, since performance
requirements during nominal operation usually have to be low for such systems (Blanke et al. 2006). Active
FTCs have different solutions for the different types of faults that are considered. However, signiﬁcant
knowledge of the faults is required. This knowledge is delivered by the diagnosis block in the scheme, which
detects, isolates and estimates the fault (Blanke et al. 2006). Fault accommodation implies the existing
controllers adaption to the faulty system in terms of changed control parameters. Control reconﬁguration is
the change in the control law and usage of new input or output signals in order to compensate for the fault
(Blanke et al. 2006), e.g. in fault scenarios where hardware is subject to complete failure. Rotondo et al.
(2012) uses so-called virtual actuators and sensors for reconﬁguration. The idea is to adapt the faulty plant to
the nominal controller by hiding the fault. This is done by reconstructing signals with observers and requires
either analytical or hardware redundancy. According to Maciejowski (1998) model predictive controllers are
good candidates for fault tolerance, since they can easily be adapted to a faulty system, e.g. if an actuator is
stuck, the constraints in the MPC will change. MPCs also have fault tolerant capabilities, even if the fault is
unknown (Maciejowski 1998).
1.2
Motivation
As previously discussed, with trends of installing larger wind turbines in rougher environments, faults
and failures have a large share of the costs and the reliability of such systems. In order to address these
issues, fault detection and condition monitoring systems are already implemented in wind turbines today.
Fault-tolerant control schemes however, provide - in some fault scenarios - the possibility of keeping the
WT operational at nominal or degraded performance. The severity of the fault is undoubtedly essential to
this matter, but in cases where the control scheme is capable of compensating the fault to such an extend,
that down-time can be avoided or maintenance can be postponed, it would be beneﬁcial. Compensating the
fault during operation has several advantages: Firstly, the nominal control objectives can still be (partly)
fulﬁlled. Secondly, additional stresses and loads caused by the fault are reduced and the lifespan of the wind
turbine extended. Lastly, less severe faults will not evolve to severe faults and/or cause necessary shut down.
Reducing the impact of the faults on the wind turbine can therefore increase reliability and feasibility and
make wind farms a more attractive investment, as well as making wind power more competitive to other
sources of electric power production.
1.3
Scope of the Problem and Main Objectives
Lu et al. (2009) outlines that the major source of faults and failure has its roots in the components attached
to the drive train, i.e. the main-shaft, bearings, rotor brakes, blades, generator and gearbox. The gearbox is
the component that is considered most critical when it comes to maintenance. For ﬁxed speed wind turbines
the number of failures in gearbox and blades are signiﬁcantly higher, whereas for variable-speed wind
turbines failures in control and electric subsystems are more likely to occur, such as sensors, pitch system
and electronic components. This thesis only considers faults in pitch actuators and sensors of a variable
speed wind turbine. As described in section 1.1 fault-tolerant control schemes consist of diagnosis and
controller redesign, with diagnosis including fault detection, isolation and estimation. The fault detection
and isolation is assumed to provide the correct information of when and where the faults occur, which is

CHAPTER 2
Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines
This chapter covers some background on wind energy and the physical principles behind horizontal axis
wind turbines.
2.1
Wind as energy source
One of the most essential factors in wind energy is its availability, as wind speed is highly variable in
both time and space. Wind ﬁelds depend on many circumstances such as geography, climate and seasons,
which are essential in the planning of wind farms (Burton et al. 2011). Wind speed is often described in
terms of mean wind speed and turbulence. The hourly mean wind speed variation over a year has been found
to approximate the Weibull distribution. Turbulence is variation in wind speed over small time intervals,
typically less than 10 minutes. It is a result of friction, caused by e.g. buildings, mountains or forests, and
thermal effects, i.e. temperature variation in the air. Due to its complexity, turbulence is often described by
means of statistical properties (Burton et al. 2011). Some wind speed variations are considered deterministic.
Wind shear for instance is a result of the the interaction of the wind ﬂow and the ground surface, which
causes lower speeds at the surface. This phenomenon depends on the terrain over which the wind is passing.
Another deterministic wind speed variation is tower shadow, which occurs when the wind has to pass around
the wind turbine tower structure. Therefore, the wind speed is locally reduced in front of the tower and
the blade will experience a reduced aerodynamic torque and thrust. Both of these phenomena should be
considered if individual pitch control is desired (Burton et al. 2011). These phenomena are important when
designing individual pitch controllers. However, this is not the scope of this thesis and therefore these
phenomena are not considered.
2.2
Principle of operation
The HAWT works on the physical principles of aerodynamics. When the wind ﬂows across the blades of
the wind turbine, it enforces aerodynamic torque, which makes the rotor rotate. Thereby, the kinetic energy
stored in the wind is converted to mechanical energy on the rotor blades and the wind speed decreases, when
passing the wind turbine rotor disk area (see ﬁgure 2.1).
In Burton et al. (2011) it is derived, that by considering the rotor disc as an actuator, which extracts
energy from the wind, a theoretical upper limit of the possible energy extraction during ideal conditions can
be obtained. This is named the Betz Limit and has a value of 59.3%. The rotor is connected to an electric
generator through a drive train, which converts the mechanical energy to electric energy. Figure 2.2 depicts
the nacelle of a wind turbine, which includes shaft, gearbox and generator.

2.2.2
Operation Modes and Overall Control Objectives
Due to the variations of the mean wind speed over time, a variable speed, variable pitch HAWT has
different operation modes. It is designed to operate in an interval of a minimum wind speed (cut-in wind
speed) and a maximum wind speed (cut-out wind speed). However, control objectives are different in this
interval, depending on the actual wind speed. The system has two inputs, generator torque and blade pitch
angle, and two outputs, electric power and rotational speed. It is thereby considered a MIMO system. A
common approach to operating a HAWT is to divide the span of wind speeds, in which the HAWT operates,
into smaller intervals called regions (see ﬁgure 2.5). The operation and control objective for each region is
described in the following.
• Region I: The wind speed exceeds the cut-in wind speed and the rotor rotates at a constant angular
velocity, since the generator torque is slightly increased as the wind speed increases. As a consequence
the electric output power increases slightly.
• Region II: The generator torque is controlled, so that rotational velocity increases linearly until it
reaches the rated speed and power increases.
• Region III: The generator torque is increased so that the rotor velocity is kept constant at the rated
value, the power output keeps increasing due to increasing wind speed.
• Region IV: By controlling the pitch angle of the HAWT blades and the generator torque it is possible
to keep both rotational speed and output power constant until the wind speed reaches the cut-out speed
and the HAWT brakes and shuts down.
Regions I, II and III are called the partial load region because the output power is lower than the rated power.
In these regions it is desired to maximise the power production, i.e. maximize CP at all times. Tracking this

In order to assess the impact of faults and the closed loop performance, a more realistic HAWT model is
used. This model is contained in the simulation tool ’Fatigue, Aerodynamics, Structures and Turbulence’
(FAST). FAST is developed by National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) with the purpose of
simulating loads and stresses on wind turbines. The system properties of the HAWT used in the simulation
are speciﬁed in a FAST input ﬁle (.fst) (see ﬁgure 3.1). Here, it is also possible to choose the degrees of
freedom which are included (see section 3.1). Therefore the user is able to vary the level of model complexity.
A wind proﬁle and the HAWT speciﬁcations are fed into ’Aerodyn’, the tool that simulates aerodynamic
forces on the HAWT structure. These windproﬁles can be both stochastic and deterministic and are contained
in the library TurbSim. ’AeroDyn’ shares information with FAST which is then simulating a time series
response of the system. For testing developed controllers the nonlinear FAST model can be implemented in
Matlab Simulink. Figure 3.2 shows the Simulink model used in this project. The FAST code is embedded
in the green block. Additionally there are blocks containing pitch- and torque actuators, a block with the
controller function, sampling and zero-order hold blocks, and various other blocks.

Model Validation
In order to validate the linear models they are compared to the 5MW reference wind turbine simulated in
FAST. The FAST model is non-linear and contains considerable more degrees of freedom than the linear
models. Approaching this, all three system models should be operating at the linearisation point. Each input,

electric power (Pe) - it is possible to asses how well the linear models represents the non-linear FAST model.
Figure 4.3 shows a step change in torque of 1% of the nominal torque. It can be observed that the power
increases and the rotor speed decreases. Both outputs of the linear models follow the trend of the non-linear
model. However, there is an offset on the rotor speed in steady state. Additionally, it can be observed that
the outputs of the FAST model have signiﬁcant oscillations. This is due to the non-linearities and the large
number of degrees of freedom that are included in the reference model; Flap-wise- and edge-wise bending
of blades and side-side tower movement among others (see table 3.1). A step of 1◦on the pitch angle is

CHAPTER 5
Faults on HAWTs
As mentioned in section 1.2, wind turbines can be subject to faults in different components. In general,
faults can either be soft or hard, while soft faults decrease performance, e.g. a gain on a signal, hard faults
are a complete failure of a component, e.g. a defect sensor. Faults are the primary reason for a change in
a system’s parameters or structure, that leads to a degradation in performance or loss of function (Blanke
et al. 2006). Depending on where in the system the fault occurs, it can be classiﬁed as a sensor fault,
actuator fault or plant fault. Faults can also have different levels of severity. Some faults are fatal for the
system or - for safety reasons - require a complete shut down, others can simply cause a slight decrease in
performance (Odgaard et al. 2013). Based on secondary sources, this aims to describe (some of) the possible
fault scenarios of HAWTs in general. Due to the vast number of components and faults in HAWTs and
limitations on implementation, a reduced number of faults has been selected as possible fault scenarios in
this thesis. Mathematical models for these faults will be described and a short description on how these are
implemented in FAST will be given.
5.1
Fault Scenarios
5.1.1
Plant Faults
• Gearbox
Among the faults that are considered plant faults are gearbox faults. These are often due to failure of
bearings and/or tooth damage (Lu et al. 2009).
• Generator
40% of faults in generators are located in the bearings, but could also occur in the stator (38%) or
rotor (10%). Stator or rotor faults can be caused by a short circuit in the stator or rotor windings or
broken rotor bars, which could lead to imbalance and harmonics in the air-gap, torque pulsation and
efﬁciency loss (Lu et al. 2009).
• Power electronics
Faults in power electronics account for 13% of the total number of faults and are primarily caused by
defect semiconductors in the power electronics circuits, especially IGBT’s (Lu et al. 2009).
• Blades
Due to long term stress and load from exposure to the wind, blades are subject to corrosion and creep
fatigue, which can result in cracks and delamination of the blade structure. Accumulation of dirt, ice,
cracks or manufacturing defects can cause imbalance on the rotor and aerodynamics asymmetry or a
reduction in efﬁciency, due to increased surface roughness (Lu et al. 2009).
29
==PAGE:52==
30

5.1.2
Actuator Faults
• Generator torque actuator
The counter torque on the generator is actuated by a power converter. Faults in this component could
be a change in the dynamics or an offset, due to wrong initialisation of torque estimates. (Odgaard and
Johnson 2013).
• Pitch Actuator
The pith actuator is usually a hydraulic system. Faults can occur as a change in dynamics caused
by pressure drop (leakage) or high air content in the oil, which changes the effective bulk modulus
(Odgaard et al. 2013). An offset on pitch actuation could be due to wrong positioning of the blades.
• Yaw actuator
Another fault scenario is the blockage of the yaw break, which ﬁxes the yaw position of the rotor
(Odgaard and Johnson 2013).
5.1.3
Sensor Faults
• Rotor speed sensor, pitch position sensor and generator speed sensor
The speed sensors are usually encoders, which can be subject to mechanical or electric faults. This can
result in a gain factor on the measurement or a ﬁxed value output. The gain factor is caused by dirt in
the rotating part of the sensor. Similar to the speed sensors, the pitch sensor faults are mechanical or
electrical and cause gain factor or ﬁxed outputs (Odgaard et al. 2013).
5.2

By implementing the faults, some limitations arise, when using FAST. Due to the complexity in
implementing plant faults in the FAST code, this has not been further pursued. However, as the FAST code
does not include actuators and sensors these can be modelled and implemented externally in MATLAB.
This also provides the possibility of implementing faults in sensors and actuators, as an abrupt change in
parameter(s) when the fault occurs (see ﬁgure 5.1). Some faults may be represented in a more realistic way,
if the parameter changes slowly compared to the sampling time.

CHAPTER 6
State and Fault Estimation
State and fault estimation are essential in fault-tolerant control schemes. State estimates are useful
because of the process and measurement noise in the system. To avoid propagating noise through the
feedback loop and for instance causing unnecessary stress of actuators, ﬁltering is required. Furthermore, the
controller developed later in this project requires feedback from unmeasured states (see section 4.2.3). Fault
estimation is an obvious necessity when performing fault-tolerant control. It is not possible for the control
scheme to reduce the effect of faults in the closed loop response, if it has no knowledge of the size of it. This
chapter describes the process of developing a discrete time Extended Kalman Filter (DEKF), to provide the
controller with state and fault estimates. The DEKF was chosen due to its incorporation of non-linearities,
which is necessary, in order to estimate both system parameters and states. Section 6.1 presents the general
description and algorithm for the EKF. The state estimates are interpreted and validated through simulation
results. In section 6.2 the description is augmented to include estimates of faults, which are also validated
through simulations.

CHAPTER 7
Global Model Predictive Controller
This chapter describes the derivation and design of the global model predictive controller. Model
predictive control (MPC) is based on the prediction of a system’s behaviour and calculation of future optimal
control actions over a ﬁnite horizon, in order to meet the control objective. One of the major advantages of
MPC is its capability to include constraints in the optimal solution. This is especially useful when avoiding
saturation of actuators in the plant, i.e. the MPC contains information about the physical limitations of
the actuators and solves the control problem without violating these constraints. That makes it a suitable
candidate for wind turbine control. Usually a receding horizon strategy is used, which is a ﬁxed horizon
that moves with the sampling. The optimal control trajectory is calculated by formulating an objective/cost
function as a quadratic function and ﬁnding the minimum of this within the constraints through numerical
optimization. Quadratic functions are typically well conditioned, smooth and have a unique minimum.
7.1
Control Objectives
The global MPC is designed to operate the wind turbine in the full load region. As discussed in section
2.2.2, the control objective in the full load region is to obtain offset free control between the controlled
outputs - the electric output power and the rotor speed - and the reference. However, this would only be
possible in the case where the effect of wind speed variations can be completely removed. Due to variations
in wind speed and its stochastic nature, it is considered an unknown input to the system, i.e. a disturbance,
which causes the power and rotor speed to ﬂuctuate. The control objectives of the global MPC is thereby to
reduce the variance of the controlled outputs and regulate their mean value to the reference by attenuating
the effect of the disturbance on the outputs. This is achieved by controlling the generator torque and the
blade pitch angle.
7.2

being of too high order (Rossiter 2004). The simpler the model, the less resources are required to solve the

linearisation point and ﬁnd an optimal solution to future control actions. The internal model of the global
MPC is the reduced order linear model outlined in equation 4.52, since this already has an embedded model
of the wind and thereby gives better conditions for disturbance rejection. For optimal reference tracking, this
however requires a perfect disturbance model and a perfect estimate of the disturbance states. Therefore,

CHAPTER 9
Simulation Results
This chapter evaluates the fault tolerant control scheme developed in this project. Only a selection
of all the fault scenarios will be presented (refer to appendix B for the remaining results). A closed loop
system response of 1000 seconds has been simulated in a healthy, faulty and fault compensated condition
respectively for each fault scenario. Further, a delay of 5 seconds between the time when the fault enters the
system and the time when the DEKF estimates the fault have been implemented. This delay is used to obtain
a more realistic scenario, as a detection and isolation scheme would cause a time-to-detect before relevant
action can be taken.
9.1
Actuator Dynamics
Table 9.1 presents the performance indicators during healthy, faulty and compensated operation in the
fault scenario of changes in actuator dynamics, as well as the relative deviation from the healthy scenario is
also presented. Figures 9.1, 9.2 and 9.3 show the ﬁrst 200 seconds of the time response during faulty and
healthy conditions. The most signiﬁcant changes in performance are the variances of the controlled outputs
and the side-side movement. During faulty operation these are considerably increased, which - relative to the
healthy condition - means a decrease in performance of 46%, 10% and 270% for the variance of rotor speed,
power and side-side acceleration respectively. For the fault compensated system the same relative decrease
in performance is 3.9%, 0.56% and 20% respectively, i.e. a signiﬁcantly better performance compared to
the faulty operation. The mean values of the controlled outputs are slightly better in the faulty system and
the pitch travel is lower. This was expected, as the faulty actuator has a slower response (see ﬁgure 9.1).
Conclusively the fault compensation improves the performance. Except for the improved regulation of
controlled outputs, the side-side movement is signiﬁcantly reduced which in turn reduces fatigue of the
tower. Figure 9.2 shows the control signals delivered to the actuators, namely that the reference signals
are within the constraints formulated in the MPC. However, the rate of the pitch reference is violating the
constraints causing undesired wear of the pitch actuator. This is an issue that could have been avoided by
implementing constraints on the control moves in the MPC formulation. Doing this, performance of the
compensated system would however be reduced, since the pre-compensator’s control problem of minimizing
the difference between healthy and faulty pitch signal would have fewer feasible solutions. Figure 9.3 depicts
the fault parameter estimates. Both of the estimates converge to the correct parameters, although the DEKF
is not active until 5 seconds after the fault occured.

9.2
Actuator Gain
A gain on the actuator input also has great impact on performance. The regulation of the controlled
outputs have a signiﬁcant offset of 0.31MW and 0.78RPM from their references during faulty operation.
The fault compensated system only has offsets of −0.057MW and −0.12RPM, which is close to the
performance of the healthy system and within 5% of the reference. This can further be veriﬁed by inspecting
ﬁgure 9.4. Additionally, the side-side and fore-aft accelerations are increased signiﬁcantly during faulty
operation, causing a performance decrease of −380% and −35% respectively. By compensating the fault,
the fore-aft acceleration is improved by 4.2%, relative to the healthy system, and the side-side acceleration is
only diminished by 23%. The compensated system experiences an increase in pitch travel of 4.9% whereas
the faulty system pitch travel decreases 14%. This is a natural consequence of the fault, which is a gain,
reducing the commanded pitch angle on blade 1 (see ﬁgure 9.4). The fault parameter is estimated quickly
after it is detected (see ﬁgure 9.6) and the control signals are within their constraints, yet as with the fault on
actuator dynamics, the rate of the pitch signal is violating the constraints. This is almost exclusively the case
in the moment when the fault is detected, due to the spike on the gain estimate. A way to avoid this could be
to refrain from using the estimate in the compensation before it has converged within an interval around the
true value. To sum up, the fault compensated system improves the system performance, compared to the
faulty scenario. This is especially clear regarding the regulation of the controlled outputs and the reduction
of tower movement. The downside is a 4.9% increase in pitch travel.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The actuator and sensor bias and gain faults were simply modelled as additive and multiplicative faults
respectively and the change in pitch actuator dynamics was modelled as a change in the system parameters,
that is, the natural frequency and damping of the actuator. A constant was representing the fault of a ﬁxed
sensor output. All of the faults were implemented as abrupt changes (step) in the nominal model to a faulty
model at the time instant the fault occurred. Simulations demonstrated, that some faults have signiﬁcant
impact on the operation of the wind turbine. For example, a constant sensor reading of the rotor speed
caused the the rotor speed to increase to approximately 40RPM, far above the rating. In actual wind turbine
systems, this would cause a shut down. A sensor gain fault had more or less the same consequences for the
controlled outputs, whereas the actuator dynamics fault caused a large increase of side-side movement of the
tower structure. This fault could therefore reduce the life span of the structure. Only estimation in the fault
diagnosis block was a part of the scope in this thesis. It was solved by applying an Extended Kalman Filter.
The ﬁlter was initially designed for state estimation using the full model. By investigating the innovation it
was shown that this process was not white. However, this was expected, ﬁrstly, because the internal model is
non-linear, secondly, there is a model mismatch between the internal model and the FAST model, and lastly,
the on-line linearisation is a ﬁrst order approximation. From inspection of the state estimates and states it
was concluded that the accuracy of the estimates were satisfying. By augmenting the system description with
artiﬁcial states of integrated white noise, it was also possible to estimate the fault parameters to a satisfactory
extend. A discrete time Extended Kalman Filter was designed for each fault scenario under the assumption,
that the fault detection and isolation schemes provided correct information of which fault the system was
subject to. A dedicated DEKF improved the parameter estimates in terms of how fast the estimate converged
to the real parameter value and avoiding signiﬁcant bias. Nonetheless, the estimate of a sensor bias was
less accurate and had a clear offset. It was concluded, that this was due to the one or more of the factors
described above.
The fault-tolerant control scheme was completed with the controller redesign block, which, based on the
fault parameter estimates, changed the controller parameters. This was implemented as a ’pre-compensator’
between the global MPC controller and the actuator. Actuator bias and gain faults were accommodated
by deducting the bias estimate or by multiplying by the reciprocal gain estimate on the pitch input signal
calculated by the global MPC. The pre-compensator also consisted of a built in MPC with the purpose of
minimizing the difference between future actuator states, predicted from a reference model, and faulty actua-
tor states, predicted by an adaptive model. The adaption was realized by updating the model with parameter
estimates of the pitch actuator frequency and damping during faulty operation. With a step change in wind
speed, it was shown that the pre-compensator effectively compensated the change in dynamics by calculating
a more aggressive control signal on the faulty actuator without breaking the constraints. Regarding sensor
67

CHAPTER 10. CONCLUSION
faults, no compensation in the pre-compensator was necessary. The DEKF has fault-tolerant capabilities
with respect to sensor faults, because correct fault estimates ensures correct state estimates and thereby
correct information for the global MPC to regulate the output power and rotational speed.
Through simulations with a stochastic wind proﬁle the fault-tolerant control system was tested and
compared to the healthy and faulty wind turbine. A set of performance indicators were formulated in order
to evaluate reference tracking capabilities, tower motion and the use of pitch actuators. It is concluded, that
the fault-tolerant control scheme in general improved the system and attenuated the impact of the different
faults. Estimating and compensating the actuator gain fault resulted in mean values of the controlled outputs
close to the healthy system. Further more the side-side acceleration of the tower increased 23% relative
to the healthy system in comparison to 380% in the faulty, i.e. a signiﬁcant relative reduction. The cost
for the compensation was a 4.9% increase in pitch travel. Variance of the side-side acceleration and the
controlled outputs were also heavily reduced in the compensation of the actuator dynamics fault. It was
clear from investigating the pitch angle, that the fault affected the pitch velocity signiﬁcantly. Estimating the
sensor bias did not improve the performance of all indicators, since the variance of the controlled outputs
were increased. This might be due to inaccurate estimate of the bias. However the rest of the performance
indicators shows improvement, i.e. mean distance to references, variance of side-side acceleration and pitch
travel. An issue with this speciﬁc solution is, that the constraints on control signal rates were violated. In the
case of actuator dynamics fault the issue might be solved by formulating constraints on the control signal
rates. This is expected to result in lower attenuation of the fault. In case of actuator gain, the constraint
violation could be avoided by delaying the compensation until the estimate has converged, since it is caused
by a spike in the parameter estimate.

CHAPTER 11
Future Work
As additions to this solution a fault detection and isolation scheme could be developed. In fact, the
Extended Kalman Filter is already known to have such capabilities. E.g. Caliskan and Hajiyev (1998)
proposes fault detection and isolation by use of the covariance matrix of the innovation process. Other
methods as for instance a CUSUM detector could also be exploited (Blanke et al. 2006). Another expansion
could be to test the built in MPC in the pre-compensator with other faults. This solution only considers a
sub-model of the system, i.e. the pitch actuator, but could possibly be extended to include a larger model or
other sub-models where fault occurs as parameter changes. In this context other MPC methods could also be
investigated, for example non-linear MPC.
As outlined previously, one of the drawbacks in the proposed solution is the violation of constraints on
control signal rate of the pitch angle. Solving this issue would be an obvious improvement, but might also
result in lower performance of the FTC scheme. Additional tuning or use of rate constraints in the MPC
formulations could be a procedure for improvement. Another improvement of the FTC scheme could be to
perform fault estimation with higher order EKF’s or the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF), as these could
improve accuracy and remove offsets in the estimates.
