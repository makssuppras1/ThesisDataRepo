Abstract
Listeria monocytogenes (Lm) is an important human pathogen and the causative agent of
listeriosis, a disease known for its high mortality rate. An infection with Lm generally oc-
curs due to consumption of contaminated food and an alarming increase in the number of
outbreaks of listeriosis has been observed in recent years. Contamination of food typically
happens in the food processing environment where Lm can persist for many years, often
as a result of failure to follow proper cleaning and disinfectant procedures. Moreover,
the extensive use of biocides as disinfectants in food processing environments has led to
the emergence of biocide resistant Lm strains, which is believed to increase their persis-
tence potential. As a result, many studies have investigated the mechanisms of biocide
resistance in Lm, although usually focused on pure cultures. In this project, two mock
Lm communities were subjected to treatment with biocides relevant to food processing
environments, namely benzalkonium chloride (BC) and peracetic acid (PAA). The com-
munities were metagenomically sequenced and, through a gene enrichment analysis,
differences between biocide-free control communities and biocide-treated communities
were investigated. For both communities, changes in the abundance of the seven house-
keeping MLST alleles were observed, though these could not be directly correlated with
an increase or decrease in specific STs. A significant decrease in the abundance of the
high-copy number and emrC-harbouring plasmid pLmN12-0935 was observed in both
communities after biocide treatment, likely due to an increased fitness cost associated
with harbouring of the plasmid. No changes in the abundance of the other known QACs
tolerance genes were observed after biocide treatment, possibly as a result of an exclu-
sivity mechanism amongst these genes. After biocide treatment, a significant increase
in the abundance of Listeria phage associated genes was observed in both communi-
ties which could possibly be explained by prophage induction as a result of competition-
or biocide-induced stress. Contrarily, it was speculated that prophage harbouring could
lead to an increased biocide tolerance. The community treated with PAA showed sev-
eral changes in the abundance of genes associated with the cell membrane after biocide
treatment, which was found to correlate with the current understanding of the mode of
action of peroxides.

1
Introduction
The Listeria genus consists of small rod-shaped, non-sporing Gram-positive bacilli and is
currently comprised of 17 species out of which only Listeria monocytogenes (henceforth
Lm) and Listeria ivanovii (L. ivanovii) are considered pathogenic [1][2]. While L. ivanovii
is mainly recognised for its ability to infect domestic livestock, Lm is an important human
foodborne pathogen and the causal agent of listeriosis [1][3]. Lm was the first named Lis-
teria species to be classified and was originally isolated at Cambridge university in 1924
from the blood of infected rabbits. As the animals exhibited a high number of a specific
infection-fighting white blood cell, namely a monocyte, the bacterium was named accord-
ingly [3]. Since its discovery, Lm has come to be known as one of the most important
foodborne human pathogens due to the high mortality associated with listeriosis and its
persistence in food processing environments (FPEs) [4][5].
1.1
Lm subtyping
Lm is a genetically diverse species which results in a considerable heterogeneity in the
pathogenic potential amongst the different strains [6]. For this reason, several subtyp-
ing methods have been developed that can group strains according to their phenotypic
and genetic characteristics. In 1940, the first method for subtyping of Lm was developed
based on somatic (O) and flagellar (H) antigens, which grouped the species into 13 dif-
ferent serotypes [3]. Out of these 13 serotypes, only three serotypes, namely 1/2b, 4b
and 1/2a, have been associated with 98% of human listeriosis cases [3]. Since then,
many studies have shown that Lm can be divided into at least four phylogenetic lineages
with distinct characteristics out of which the majority of isolates belong to either lineage
I or II [7]. Lineage I shows the lowest diversity amongst the isolates and has the lowest
level of recombination, while Lineage II is the most diverse and has the highest level of
recombination. Furthermore, several studies have shown that Lineage I isolates typically
are overrepresented amongst clinical samples, while lineage II isolates are more com-
monly isolated from food, food production facilities and the environment (figure 1) [7][8].
Recently, a 2019 study identified a new hybrid sub-lineage, HSL-II, which consisted of
strains that were similar to those of lineage II and serotype 4h and that showed hypervir-
ulence [9].
Figure 1: Frequency of clonal complexes and lineages among food and clinical isolates.
Figure adapted from [8]
In later years, with the introduction of whole genome sequencing (WGS), even more dis-
criminatory subtyping methods for Lm have been developed based on single nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs) and multilocus sequence typing (MLST), where a different num-
ber of loci are compared between isolates. For subtyping of Lm, the seven housekeeping
1
==PAGE:12==
gene MLST scheme is often used, which compares the nucleotide sequences of seven Lm
housekeeping genes and designates a sequence type (ST) based on the specific com-
bination of allele variants [10]. For an even higher discriminatory power, core genome
MLST (cgMLST) or whole genome (wgMLST) can be used. Moreover, Lm subtypes are
often reported as clonal complexes (CCs), which are defined as groups of ST profiles that
are highly similar, usually with six out of seven housekeeping alleles being identical [10].
A recent study performed a phylogenetic analysis of 6,633 Lm isolates, and investigated
the frequency at which different CCs were of food origin or of clinical origin (figure 1).
Based on this, CC121 and CC9 have been identified as being strongly associated with
food, while CC1, CC2, CC4 and CC6 are strongly associated with a clinical origin [8].
1.2
Listeriosis
Lm is mainly known for its ability to cause listeriosis in humans, which predominantly af-
fects three well-defined risk groups, namely pregnant women, unborn or newly delivered
infants and immunocompromised adults, such as the elderly or individuals with predis-
posing conditions [2][3]. In pregnant women, listeriosis occurs throughout gestation with
symptoms being typically mild and flu-like or even absent. While the outcome of the in-
fection for the mother is usually benign, intrauterine infection of the fetus can result in
abortion, stillbirth and early-onset neonatal disease [2]. Lm infections in infants can be
categorised as either early-onset neonatal disease or late-onset neonatal disease. Early-
onset neonatal disease is contracted in utero and the infant will be ill either at birth or
within the first day of birth. Furthermore, early-onset neonatal disease is predominantly a
septicaemic illness and has a mortality rate of 30%-60% with a high risk of sequelae, such
as permanent mental retardation, in case of survival [2][3]. Late-onset neonatal disease
manifests in infants 3 to 18 days after delivery and is associated with hospital cross-
infection acquired from contact with an early-onset neonatal case. The disease is mainly
meningitic and the mortality rate is 10%-12% [2]. In immunocompromised adults, listerio-
sis mainly manifests as bacteraemia and central nervous system (CNS) infections such
as meningitis and encephalitis [2][3]. Symptoms typically manifest within 2-70 days after
ingestion of Lm and can include severe headache, dizziness, stiffness of neck or back
and poor coordination [3]. For both adult bacteraemia and CNS infections, mortality rates
range between 15%-30%, even though mortality is significantly lower amongst meningitis
patients below 60 years of age [2][3]. Although the general population is frequently sub-
jected to Lm through food supply, invasive listeriosis outside of these above-mentioned
risk groups is very rare [3]. However, cases have been reported where ingestion of a
large inoculum of Lm has led to gastroenteritis in otherwise healthy individuals [11].
1.3
Pathogenesis
One of the major factors contributing to the pathogenic potential of Lm is its ability to
survive within host cells and evade mechanisms of the innate immune system [12]. A
schematic overview of Lm host cell invasion into a non-phagocytic cell can be seen in
figure 2. Briefly, upon ingestion of Lm, the pathogen traverses the intestinal epithelial
barrier and spreads via the lymph and blood towards the liver and the spleen. In im-
munocompromised individuals it can then cross the blood-brain barrier, while it can cross
the fetoplacental barrier in pregnant women. Thereafter, Lm can be internalised by ei-
ther a phagocytic or a non-phagocytic cell [13]. Internalisation in a non-phagocytic cell,
such as epithelial, endothelial, hepatocyte and fibroblast cells, requires that the bacterial
surface proteins internalin A (InlA) and InlB interact with the host cell surface receptors
E-cadherin (Ecad) and mesenchymal epithelial transition factor (Met) [14]. This induces
uptake of Lm into the host cell through receptor-mediated endocytosis resulting in the
bacterium being trapped in a vacuole [13]. For the uptake of Lm into phagocytic cells,
2
==PAGE:13==
such as a macrophage, the bacterium has evolved specialised mechanisms for surviving
within these cells [12]. Normally, macrophages provide a first defense as part of the innate
immune system by engulfing invading microorganisms into phagosomes and then killing
them through a process called phagocytosis [12]. However, when Lm is engulfed by a
macrophage or endocytosed by a non-phagocytic cell, it is able to escape into the cytosol
of this cell by using the pore-forming toxin listeriolysin O (LLO) to rupture the phagosome
or vacuole [13][12]. Then, in the cytosol, where bacterial growth occurs, Lm can use its
surface-anchored virulence factor called actin assembly-inducing protein (ActA) to medi-
ate polymerization of the host protein actin which assembles into filaments on the older
pole of the bacterium [13]. Thereby, Lm can subvert the host cell’s cytoskeleton and gain
intracellular motility, resulting in the bacterium being propelled intracellularly from one cell
to its neighbours with the help of InlC [2][13]. In the new cell, the vacuole is lysed by the
bacterium allowing the propagation of bacterial spread without contact with the extracel-
lular milieu [12][15].

1.4
Epidemiology and surveillance
Although most cases of listeriosis are sporadic, Lm has also been associated with many
smaller and larger outbreaks as a result of consumption of contaminated food [2]. Several
food groups have been implicated in outbreaks with microbiological and epidemiological
evidence supporting association with food types such as dairy, meat, fruits, vegetables,
fish and complex foods such as many different ready-to-eat (RTE) products [2][16]. The
first recorded outbreak of listeriosis directly linked to a food origin occurred in Canada from
1980 to 1981 as a result of ingestion of contaminated coleslaw [16]. Although improved
control measures in the 1990s have reduced the prevalence of Lm in many food groups,
the rate of listeriosis is still high and more severe cases of the disease are now recognised
as occurring more frequently in smaller outbreaks [17]. Moreover, the European Centre
for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC) has reported an increase in notifications of
both listeriosis outbreaks and sporadic cases in recent years [18][17], while Lm has be-
come the third leading cause of foodborne deaths in the US [19]. In general, Western
countries report 1-10 cases per million of the population per year [2][15]. Probable expla-
nations for this increase in listeriosis cases in developed countries include industrialisation
of food production, normalisation of food refrigeration and an improved healthcare result-
3
==PAGE:14==
ing in an increase in elderly and immunocompromised individuals. As food production is
beginning to become more industrialised in low- to middle-income countries, and the HIV
epidemic is causing an increase in immunosuppressed individuals, Lm is also emerging
as an important foodborne pathogen in these developing countries [15]. In 2017, the cur-
rently largest recorded outbreak of listeriosis to date was observed in South Africa which
was associated with processed meat. Here, 937 cases of listeriosis were identified with a
mortality rate of 27% and with 42% of the infected individuals, from whom the HIV-status
was known, being HIV positive [20].
Surveillance of Lm is an important tool in the control of listeriosis as this information can
be used by public health and regulatory authorities to track pathogenic strains, identify
clusters and outbreaks that are distributed geographically or through time and identify an
individual food product as a likely source for clinical cases [17]. Typically, pulsed-field
gel electrophoresis (PFGE) has been used for genetic typing in surveillance, but in more
recent years cgMLST and wgMLST based WGS data has become increasingly popular
as it provide a higher accuracy and shows a superior discriminatory power compared to
PFGE [21][22]. Moreover, collection and distribution of WGS data from Lm isolates along-
side accompanying metadata provide academia with valuable information for studying Lm
evolution, gene distribution, transmission routes and much more [23][24]. In the US, lis-
teriosis has been a notifiable disease since 2001, while the Centers for Disease Control
and Prevention’s (CDC) ”Listeria initiative” was piloted in 2004 and aims at decreasing
the time from outbreak detection to public health intervention. This is done through inter-
views with patients and subtyping of clinical, food and environmental isolates with PFGE
to identify related disease cases [25]. Since 2013, WGS of clinical, food and environ-
mental isolates has also been employed by state laboratories, CDC, the Food and Drug
Administration (FDA), and the US Department of Agriculture’s Food Safety and Inspection
Service [25]. Furthermore, the US has implemented a ”zero-tolerance” policy regarding
Lm isolates in RTE food, meaning that a detectable presence (≥1 CFU in 25g of sam-
ple) of Lm requires a recall of the food product [7]. Although listeriosis is not a notifiable
disease in all European countries, most countries still have surveillance systems in oper-
ation [26]. Moreover, the European Center for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC),
the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and the European Union Reference Labo-
ratory (EU-RL) for L. monocytogenes collect typing data from human, food, animal and
environmental isolates from public health institutes and food safety and veterinary author-
ities on a voluntary basis. The purpose of this project is to aid in the detection of listeriosis
outbreaks that affect countries across European borders [27].
1.5
Mobile genetic elements
One way for Lm isolates to diversify their genetic pool is through harbouring of mobile ge-
netic elements (MGEs) such as plasmids, transposons and prophages [28]. These allow
for rapid adaptation to changing environmental conditions and can typically be transferred
between bacterial strains, and sometimes even species, lost by the host if they introduce
a significant fitness cost or retained if they provide an advantage for survival [28][29].
Plasmids are extrachromosal DNA with the ability to replicate and partition independently
from the chromosome of their host. They often carry genes involved in virulence or host
cell survival and they are therefore frequently harboured by and transferred amongst
pathogenic bacteria. Plasmids can either be self-transmissible, if they possess all ge-
netic information required for transfer, or they can be mobilizable if they must use the
transfer system of another MGE. While all plasmids carry an oriT site (origin of transfer)
from which transfer initiates, only self-transmissible plasmids harbour tra or mob genes
that are needed to assemble the machinery responsible for transfer [30]. A 2010 study on
4
==PAGE:15==
Lm plasmids found that they all harboured three genes necessary for replication (repA)
and partitioning (repB and repC) as well as the origin of replication and a gene encoding
a DNA polymerase IV [31]. Furthermore, they suggested a classification system of Lm
plasmids based on the variant of the repA gene that the plasmid harboured and found
two separate groups, namely group 1 and group 2. A later study identified two new repA
homologs and proposed that these should represent two additional Lm plasmid groups.
They also found that Lm harbours smaller plasmids whose RepA proteins show low simi-
larity to the RepA proteins identified for group 1 and 2. Moreover, these smaller plasmids
were found to replicate through a rolling-circle mechanism, while group 1 and 2 plasmids
are theta-replicating [28]. The largest Lm plasmid survey to date, a study of 1,921 pub-
lished Lm genomes, found that 54% of all investigated strains carried a putative plasmid,
although the presence of plasmids varied greatly depending on ST. ST1, ST2 and ST4
were found to rarely harbour plasmids (<15% of strains), while ST121, ST5, ST8, ST3
and ST204 were found to often harbour plasmids (>71% of strains). Furthermore, they
found that Lm plasmids ranged in size from 4 kbp to 179 kbp with a median plasmid size
of 61 kbp and that highly conserved plasmids were shared amongst Lm strains that were
isolated from distant countries decades apart [28].
Transposons are DNA elements that have the ability to move from one place in the bac-
terial DNA to another through a process called transposition. They can vary greatly in
size, with the smallest transposons being around 1,000 bp and only carrying the trans-
posase gene that facilities transposition, while larger transposons can harbour one or
more genes such as transposition regulating genes or genes otherwise beneficial to the
host [32]. Inverted repeats (IRs) are located at each end of the transposon and are nec-
essary for transposition, where the transposase proteins bind to the IRs and then to each
other, creating a synapse with the IR ends paired resulting in the excision of the trans-
poson [32]. Conjugative transposons, like some plasmids, contain tra genes that they
use to promote transfer of themselves to other cells and thereby facilitate the spread of
transposon-carried genes [30]. A 2020 study by Palma et al. found that 51% of isolates in
their 91 Lm collection harboured transposons [5]. Moreover, Lm transposons have been
found to harbour resistance cassettes for several important compounds such as cadmium,
arsenic and biocides [5].
Another mechanism by which bacteria can diversify their genetic pool is through bacte-
riophages (phages). The life cycle of a phage can include two different cycles, namely
the lytic cycle and the lysogenic cycle. An overview of these phage cycles can be seen in
figure 3. In the lytic cycle, a phage infects a bacterial cell wherein it replicates and assem-
bles new phages after which the bacterial cell is lysed and the new phages are released
[33]. In the lysogenic cycle, the phage integrates into the bacterial chromosome where its
encoded genes can be expressed and thereby confer new biochemical properties to its
host [30]. These properties can include virulence factors or other properties facilitating the
survival of the host bacterium [33]. Phages in the lysogenic cycle that have integrated into
the host bacterium’s chromosome are known as prophages and they passively replicate
alongside the bacterial DNA during cell division [33]. A prophage can revert to the lytic
cycle in a process known as lysogenic induction often triggered by damage to the bac-
terial DNA [30][34]. Phages can also facilitate the differentiation of the bacterial genome
through phage transduction, which can either be done by generalised transducing phages
or specialised transducing phages [30]. Generalised transduction happens when a phage
undergoing the lytic cycle accidentally packages the host bacterium’s chromosomal DNA
instead of packaging its own DNA. Then, when the mispackaged phage infects a new bac-
terial cell, it will not have the ability to replicate as it has lost part of its own phage genome.
5
==PAGE:16==
Instead, it will deliver the DNA from its previous bacterial host, which can then recombine
through homologous regions and become integrated in the new bacterial chromosome
[30]. Specialised transduction occurs when prophages undergo lysogenic induction and
accidentally package bacterial DNA that flank the phage attachment site [30]. In Lm, a for-
mer study found that there is a high occurrence of prophages amongst isolates belonging
to lineage I and II, while a lower occurrence was reported for lineage III and no lineage IV
genomes were tested [35]. Moreover, the previously described Palma et al. study found
that 56% of strains in their 94 isolate collection carried prophages [5]. Although there is
currently a limited number of studies that have reported lab based phenotypic benefits
for Lm isolates harbouring prophages, a 2021 study by Gray et al. found an upregulation
of prophage genes while investigation the colonisation dynamics of Lm indicating that
prophages may play a role in low nutrient adaption, attachment or biofilm formation [36].

1.6
Lm in FPEs
Lm has adapted to live in both soil as a saprophyte and in the cytosol of eukaryotic host
cells as a pathogen [37]. It is ubiquitous in nature and has been isolated from numer-
ous environmental sites including soil, sewage, water and vegetation, where it can live
off decaying plant material for >2 years [2]. As a pathogen, the main route of transmis-
sion is through the consumption of food products that have typically been contaminated
in the FPE [2]. Due to the ability of Lm to survive well on both dry and moist surfaces,
the pathogen can become established and persist on many specific sites in FPEs where
it has been isolated from numerous locations such as equipment, plant and machinery
(shelving, conveyor belts, slicing machines), condensates and drains [2]. Several char-
acteristics of Lm causes food to be the favoured agent for transmission of listeriosis [37].
The bacterium is a psychotrophic organism, meaning that it can grow in non-selective
media at temperatures between 1 and 45°C and it has even been reported as growing in
temperatures as low as 0.1°C in pasteurised milk during storage [3]. Although the optimal
growth occurs at 30-37°C, this still allows for bacterial growth at refrigeration temperatures
with a maximum doubling time of about 1-2 days at 4°C [2]. At slightly higher tempera-
tures, the growth rate of Lm increases significantly and a generation time of 10-13 hours
6
==PAGE:17==
can be observed at 8°C [3]. Furthermore, Lm can grow in a wide range of food with a
relatively high water activity (aw>0.95) and has been shown to grow in a pH range of 4.3-
10.0 and at relatively high concentrations of sodium chloride (10% NaCl) [3][2][38]. As
spoilage or taints as a result of Lm contamination of food is generally not produced, the
pathogen is also not easily detectable, even when present in high concentration. Com-
bined, this makes Lm a major concern as a postprocessing contaminant of long shelf-life
refrigerated foods [2].
Persistence of Lm in FPEs has not yet been ultimately defined, although the repeated
isolation of the same subtype over a long period in the same FPE has been suggested
[5]. Currently, several lineage II clonal groups have been linked to increased persistence
in FPEs, namely CC121, CC9, CC8, CC101, CC7 and CC204 [5]. The persistence of a
specific Lm strain in an FPE has been observed for up to 10 years and several persistent
strains have been linked to food contamination from farm to fork and have been identified
as playing important roles in listeriosis outbreaks over the last decades [5]. Therefore, it is
of utmost importance to understand the underlying mechanisms that allow Lm persistence
in FPEs for which several contributing factors have currently been identified. Lm persis-
tence in FPEs has been identified as being promoted by food safety noncompliance such
as improper hygiene conditions, failure to follow proper cleaning and disinfectant (C&D)
programs and poorly designed food facilities, leading to failure to eradicate the pathogen
and an increased risk of recontamination with genotypes showing persistence potential
from raw materials or external habitats [5]. Furthermore, specific Lm characteristics have
been speculated and investigated for their ability to increase the persistence potential of
the bacteria. These characteristics include the ability to form biofilms, physiological adap-
tation and tolerance to FPE associated stresses such as high temperature, osmotic stress
and oxidative stress as well as resistance or increased tolerance to heavy metals and dis-
infectants [5][39]. Moreover, recent studies have shown that MGEs are more numerous
among persistent isolates in FPEs and that they support the adaptation and persistence
of Lm isolates in the food industry by contributing to the dissemination of genes encoding
favourable phenotypes [40]
1.7
Biocides and biocide resistance
One of the most critical tools for controlling Lm in FPEs is through proper C&D proce-
dures. As a part of these procedures, a variety of biocides are commonly used for dis-
infection such as quaternary ammonium compounds (QACs), peroxygens, biguanides,
alcohols and chlorine compounds [24]. QACs include biocidal compounds like benza-
lkonium chloride (BC) and didecyl dimethyl ammonium chloride (DDAC) and are mainly
membrane-active agents. The compound is believed to penetrate the cell wall, where it
reacts with the cytoplasmic membrane resulting in membrane disorganization. Follow-
ing this, intracellular low-molecular-weight material leaks from the cell while proteins and
nucleic acids are degraded and finally the cell wall is lysed by autolytic enzymes. QACs
are also believed to promote their own uptake in gram-negative bacteria by damaging the
outer membrane [24][41]. Peroxygens, such as hydrogen peroxide (HP) and peracetic
acid (PAA), are broad-spectrum biocides that work as oxidants by producing free hydroxyl
radicals that can oxidise thiol groups of enzymes and proteins and are believed to inter-
fere with the cellular phospholipid bilayers and cytosolic material [24][41]. However, due
to the complexity of the reaction pathway of peroxygens, the exact mechanism of these
compounds is still not fully understood and it is likely that they inhibit and kill microorgan-
isms by several mechanisms [42]. Biguanides are also associated with cell membrane
damage as a result of phase separation and domain formation of membrane lipids [41].
For alcohols, the main bactericidal action also involves membrane damage and inhibition
7
==PAGE:18==
of DNA and RNA synthesis [24]. Chlorine compounds have oxidizing capacity and are
believed to be multitarget reactors that oxidize the thiol groups to disulphides, sulphox-
ides and disulphoxides and have a deleterious effect on DNA synthesis as a result of the
formation of chlorinated derivates of nucleotide bases [43].
In an effort to control contamination in FPEs, the extensive use of biocides has led to the
emergence of Lm strains showing increased tolerance to these compounds and thereby
possibly facilitating their persistence [24]. The ability of Lm to develop resistance to QACs
with BC as representative is especially well studied and can both be an intrinsic prop-
erty, such as biofilm formation, or acquired, which can be non-specific or specific [44].
Non-specific acquired tolerance mechanisms can include changes in the cell wall or cell
membrane that impede the entrance of the disinfectant agent into the cell, while specific
tolerance mechanisms include genes, typically located on MGEs and acquired through
horizontal gene transfer, that directly confer an increased tolerance or resistance to the
disinfectant. In Lm, four genes that are associated with increased tolerance or resistance
to BC have been identified so far [44]. The first BC-resistance genes to be identified were
presented in a study by Elhanafi et al. in 2010 [45]. Here, they identified the plasmid
(pLM80)-associated BC resistance cassette, bcrABC, which was found to be implicated
in a 1998-1999 multistate outbreak of listeriosis caused by contaminated hot dogs. More-
over, they found that the cassette consisted of three genes all necessary for imparting re-
sistance, namely a TetR family transcriptional regulator (bcrA) with a helix-turn-helix DNA
binding motif and two small multidrug resistance (SMR) genes (bcrB and bcrC) which
encoded a proton-dependent multidrug efflux system [45]. The cassette was also found
to be harboured by an IS1216 composite transposon and the cassette could both be lo-
cated on plasmids or on the bacterial chromosome [45]. The next gene to be associated
with BC resistance was the qacH gene which was first described in 2013 by Müller et al.
[46]. Here, they identified a novel transposon, Tn6188, which was comprised of 5117 bp,
chromosomally integrated and harboured three transposase genes (tnpABC) alongside
genes encoding a putative transcriptional regulator and qacH. The qacH gene was found
to encode a SMR protein family transporter responsible for export of BC [46]. In 2015,
Kovacevic et al. identified another gene associated with BC resistance in Lm, which was
involved in a large listeriosis outbreak in Canada in 2008 [47]. They found that the gene
emrE, which was located on a novel genomic island (LGI1), encoded an efflux protein
that exported BC across the outer cell membrane [47]. The most recent gene to be as-
sociated with increased BC resistance was identified by Kremer et al. in 2017 [48]. In
this study, they identified a small novel plasmid, pLMST6, which was 4378 bp in size and
harboured the gene emrC that encoded a small multidrug efflux pump [48]. Furthermore,
an association between the pMLST6 plasmid and an increased incidence of ST6 listerial
meningitis in the Netherlands was observed, while a later study found a reduction in the
survival of zebrafish embryos infected with Lm strains harbouring pMLST6 compared to
plasmid-cured strains indicating a possible increased virulence capacity [44]. Harbour-
ing of the plasmid was also found to confer decreased susceptibility to amoxicillin and
gentamicin although these results have been contested by later studies [48][44].
Due to the unspecific mode of action by which peroxides inhibit and kill microorganisms,
no studies have yet reported genetic determinants that increase resistance to this group
of biocides [42]. Recent studies have however identified an increased tolerance of Lm
to peroxides, QACs and other disinfectant compounds when the bacteria are present in
biofilms [49][50][51]. Moreover, studies have found that resistance is especially increased
in multi-species biofilms [51] and that the resistance of the biocide-treated biofilms is due
to attributes related to the extracellular polymeric substances of the biofilm and not an
8
==PAGE:19==
intrinsic attribute of the cells in the biofilm [49]. A recent 2020 study by Manso et al. found
that temperature affects the response to oxidative stress in Lm and that cells treated with
peroxides at 10°C showed higher survival than cells treated at 37°C [52]. Furthermore, the
study found an upregulation in virulence gene hly and stress gene clpC during oxidative
stress at low temperatures [52].
1.8
Objective
In recent years, many studies have focused on elucidating the mechanisms of resistance
of Lm to different categories of biocides, especially with a focus on biocides relevant to
the food industry [24][53][54]. While these studies have mainly revolved around whole-
genome sequencing of pure cultures, the novelty of this project is to use a metagenomic
approach, through gene enrichment analysis, to search for genes associated with in-
creased tolerance of Lm isolates in mock communities to two of the most commonly
used disinfectants in the food industry, namely QACs (with BC as representative) and
peroxides (with PAA as representative). Additionally, distribution of plasmids, known dis-
infectant tolerance genes, 7 gene MLST alleles and virulence genes were investigated
before and after biocide treatment.

Materials and Methods
2.1
Lm isolates
Two mock Lm communities were designed based on a collection of 330 isolates, which
were grouped into six different overall isolation sources: RTE food (n=176), FPE (n=117),
animal clinical (n=22), human clinical (n=11), natural environment (n=3) and a clinical ref-
erence strain (n=1). The isolates originated from 10 countries: Norway (n=98), Denmark
(n=96), Switzerland (n=65), Slovenia (n=45), Canada (n=11), Germany (n=7), Italy (n=4),
USA (n=1), England (n=1), Finland (n=1) and an unknown country of origin (n=1). The
raw sequencing data and metadata for all isolates were provided or downloaded from
the European Nucleotide Archive (ENA). Additionally, the minimum inhibitory concentra-
tions (MICs) of BC and PAA for the isolates in the collection were determined by broth
microdilution assays prior to the start of the project.
2.2
In silico characterisation of Lm
The two Lm mock communities were designed based on the phenotypic and genotypic
characteristics of the isolates. Therefore, the 330 isolates were initially characterised in
silico. The raw sequencing data were subjected to quality check using FastQC v.0.11.5
[55], trimmed with bbtools v.36.49 [56], assembled by SPAdes v.3.15.3 [57] and quality
of assemblies were evaluated with Quast v.5.0.2 [58], all incorporated into an in-house
pipeline. Serotypes and seven gene MLST profiles were determined using LisSero [59]
and tseeman [59], respectively, while lineages were defined in the BIGSdb-Lm database
at Institute Pasteur [60]. Whole genome SNP analysis was performed for the total Lm
collection using CSI phylogeny with trimmed fastq as input files and the Lm EGDe (Gen-
Bank accession no. AL591824.1) as a reference genome [61]. IQ-TREE v.2.1.4-beta was
used to infer a maximum likelihood (ML) phylogenetic tree from the concatenated and
aligned SNPs produced by CSI phylogeny under the GTR+G substitution model with ul-
trafast bootstrapping (–ufboot 1000) [62]. The ML tree was visualised and annotated with
iTOL [63]. The Python script ”label_clusters.py” was utilised to rename the taxa labels
of the newick files [64]. Moreover, to verify that all isolates belonged to the Lm species,
blastn was carried out using the Lm-specific hly gene encoding LLO, as a marker. Fur-
thermore, ABRicate was used to screen the isolates for known QACs resistance genes
(qacH (Accession no. HG329628.1 [46]), emrC (Accession no. MT912503.1:2384-2770
[48]), emrE (Accession no. NC_013766.2:c1850670-1850347 [47]) and bcrABC (Acces-
sion no. JX023284.1 [45])) with minimum identification and coverage of 90% and 70%
respectively [65].
2.3
Design of mock Lm communities
From the collection of the 330 Lm isolates, two mock communities were designed based
on their MIC values (BC community) and the previous In silico characterisation (PAA com-
munity). The BC community was designed to include Lm strains that showed increased
tolerance to BC by the broth microdilution assays (MIC ≥1.25 mg/L). As mentioned, all
isolates that showed increased BC tolerance were screened for the presence of the four
known genes associated with QACs tolerance. Of these phenotypically BC-tolerant iso-
lates, five did not carry any of the known QACs tolerance genes but were still included in
the community for potential detection of novel QACs tolerance genes. Their MIC values
were re-tested prior to the experiment to ensure a tolerant phenotype. Regarding the PAA
community, all Lm isolates exhibited MIC values of 60 mg/L by the broth microdilution as-
say and it was therefore not possible to design this community based on the tolerance
phenotype. Instead, the isolates were selected based on the MLST characteristics - at
least one isolate from each ST type from the whole collection was included representing
diverse isolation sources, countries of origin, serotypes and lineages.

For each individual community, ML phylogenetic trees were created with CSI phylogeny
[61] and IQ-TREE v.2.1.4-beta [62] and then visualised and annotated with iTOL [63] as
previously described. The metadata for each community was visualised with Microsoft
Excel. The pangenome for each community was calculated by first annotating the as-
semblies with Prokka [66] and then using the ”.gff” files as input for Roary [67]. The

2.4
Biocide treatment
A graphical representation of the experimental design can be seen in figure 4. Briefly, all
isolates from the two communities were streaked from -80°C onto Tryptone Soya Agar
plates (TSA, Fisher Scientific, Denmark) and grown at 37°C overnight. A single colony
from each isolate was cultured individually in 1.8 mL 1/10 Tryptone Soya Broth (TSB, Ox-
oid) in a 96 deep-well plate (Eppendorf AG, Germany) and grown at 15°C for 48h (±1h).
To ensure that the communities were uniform at the starting time of the experiment, the
OD620 of the individual cultures was measured and adjusted to 0.1 (±0.08). Fifty µL of
the cultures belonging to the BC or PAA communities were mixed together, resulting in
two separate mixes consisting of 71 and 80 cultures for the BC and the PAA community,
respectively. From each mix, 50 µL was removed and serial ten-fold dilutions were pre-
pared. From the appropriate dilutions, 50 µL were spread-plated on TSA and cultured at
37°C overnight. Colonies were counted and transformed to CFU/mL. From each mix, 1
mL was extracted and stored at -20°C for DNA extraction. Henceforth, these samples will
be referred to as ”BC_T0” and ”PAA_T0”. Each mix was diluted to obtain 103 CFU/mL in
1/10 TSB and BC or PAA were added to their respective communities. To ensure treat-
ment with sub-lethal concentrations of BC and PAA, the communities were subjected to
varying concentrations of the biocides. Concentrations of 1.75 mg/L, 2.0 mg/L, 2.25 mg/L,
2.5 mg/L and 2.75 mg/L BC were tested for the BC community, while concentrations of 30
mg/L, 35 mg/L and 38 mg/L PAA were tested for the PAA community. A negative control
without biocide was included for both communities.
For each community, 300 µL from the treated samples with varying biocide concentration
and the negative control were distributed into two 100-well honeycomb plates (five wells
per plate resulting in a total of 10 technical replicates per sample) and incubated in a
Bioscreen C (Oy Growth Curves Ab Ltd, Finland) at 15°C and with automatic measuring
of the OD540 every 30 min. The growth curves were continually observed and once the
growth of a community reached stationary phase, samples were taken with a pipette after
melting the lid over the wells. To collect enough volume for further analysis, samples
from different wells belonging to the same community were pooled. Only samples with
similar growth curves compared to the other technical replicates from the same community
were pooled. One mL of the pooled and vortexed samples was stored at -20°C for DNA
extraction, while 50 µL were used to prepare serial ten-fold dilutions. Then, 50 µL of the
appropriate dilutions were plated on TSA and incubated at 37°C overnight after which
colonies were counted to estimate CFU/mL.

2.5
DNA extraction, metagenomic library prep and sequencing
The bacterial pellets from both communities and biological replicates stored at -20°C were
thawed and total DNA extraction was carried out using the DNeasy Blood and Tissue Kit
(Qiagen, Denmark) following the manufacture’s recommendations. To ensure increased
DNA yield, the DNA elution was performed in two steps. Initially, 30 µL pre-heated to
56°C 10 mM Tris-HCl (pH = 8.5) (Rockland Inc., Denmark) was pipetted onto the DNeasy
membrane, the column incubated at room temperature for 5 min and centrifuged at 10,000
rpm for 1 min. Additionally, 20 µL more Tris-HCl buffer was added followed by incubation
and centrifugation for a total volume of approximately 50 µL eluate. The DNA concen-
tration was measured with Qubit 3.0 Fluorometer using Qubit DNA High Sensitivity Kit
(Thermo Fisher Scientific, Carlsbad, CA, USA). For the construction of the metagenomic
libraries, for each sample, 35 µL of input DNA was used according to the KAPA HyperPlus
PCR-free Kit (Roche Diagnostics) protocol, consisting of enzymatic fragmentation (30 min
incubation time at 37°C), end repair and A-tailing, adaptor ligation (adaptors diluted 20:1
or 40:1 depending on the input DNA) and post-ligation clean-up. The concentrations of
the libraries were measured with Qubit DNA High Sensitivity Kit and the fragment sizes
were evaluated by Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent technologies, Germany). Each li-
brary was normalised to 4 nM following the Illumina Denature and Dilute Libraries Guide
(Document #15048776 v16, Protocol A: Standard Normalisation Method for Mid Output
Kits). Libraries with similar fragment sizes were pooled, denatured and diluted to 1.5
pM for loading in a NextSeq 500/550 Mid Output v2.5 Kit (300 cycles) (Illumina) and se-
quenced on a NextSeq 500 platform (Illumina).

Metagenomic and statistical analysis
The raw sequencing data were initially subjected to a quality check using FastQC v.0.11.5
[55] and trimmed with bbtools v.36.49 [56] in accordance with the in-house pipeline.
Python and Rstudio were used for data handling, analysis and visualisation [75]. All scripts
can be found at the following location:
https://sciencedata.dk/shared/7d6a80244efa40f5e2a657856e2afe69
KMA mapping, normalisation and summarisation
After quality control and trimming of the raw reads, they were mapped to the relevant
databases with KMA mapping and default settings [76]. The KMA mapping was done on
the Danish National Supercomputer for Life Sciences, Computerome 2.0, due to the high
amount of required memory. The trimmed reads from the samples belonging to the BC
community were mapped to the ”BC community”, ”BC pangenome”, ”Plasmids”, ”MLST”,
”Virulence” and ”Disinfectant” databases, while the trimmed reads from the PAA com-
munity samples were mapped to the ”PAA community”, ”PAA pangenome”, ”Plasmids”,
”MLST”, ”Virulence” and ”Disinfectant” databases.
For the KMA mapping, two ”input_wrapper.txt” files were initially created - one for the sam-
ples belonging to the BC community and one for the samples belonging to the PAA com-
munity. The input wrapper files consisted of three tab-separated columns and six rows.
Each row contained the paths to the trimmed fastq files from one of the samples belonging
to the specific community. Column one consisted of paths to the forward reads, column
two consisted of paths to the reverse reads and column three consisted of paths to single-
ton reads. Thereafter, paths to all databases were added to the ”kma_databases.txt” file
followed by specifications on mapping options for the ”kma_wrapper.pl” program. Stan-
dard options were chosen for every database (Options=’-mem_mode -ef -1t1 -apm f -
nf’). Tthe ”kma_wrapper.pl” program was run with the ”input_wrapper.txt” file as input
and the appropriate databases designated for mapping, which resulted in the creation of
a shellscript file for each database being mapped against. These shellscripts were run,
which led to the creation of four files from each shellscript out of which only the ”.mapstat”
files were used for further analysis.
The final step in the mapping process was to normalise and summarise the data. This
was done individually for each database with the in-house scripts ”prepare_input_files.py”
and ”kma_mapstat_summary.py”. These scripts took the previously created ”.mapstat”
files alongside the paths to the directories containing the ”Bacterial community” mapping
results and the specific database mapping results. Furthermore, the multi-fasta file, on
which the specific database was created, was also provided. Running these two scripts re-
sulted in the creation of several tables containing information on central log-ratio transfor-
mations (CLR), Fragments Per Kilobase Million (FPKM), fragment counts, relative abun-
dance and Transcripts Per Kilobase Million (TPM). Only the tables with CLR, FPKM and
relative abundance were used for further analysis.
Metrics for mapping results
For this project, read abundances were predominantly reported as FPKM. The aim of
this metric is to normalise for sequencing depth and gene length. This is important as
sequencing runs with more depth will naturally have more reads mapping to each gene
resulting in a higher read count even though the gene was not more expressed. Addi-
tionally, it is important to normalise for gene length because longer genes generally have
more reads mapping to them than shorter genes, thereby creating a bias. The FPKM
metric is designed for paired-end reads and therefore takes into account that two reads
can map to one fragment and will, in that case, not count the fragment twice. FPKM is
16
==PAGE:27==
calculated by first normalising for read depth by dividing the read counts with the total
number of reads in the sample and using a scaling factor of 1 million. Then, the read
counts are normalised according to gene length by dividing them with the length of the
gene in kilobases [77].
In some instances, relative abundance was used as a metric to describe the abundance of
reads instead of FPKM. Like FPKM, relative abundance also normalises for sequencing
depth and is calculated by dividing the read count with the total number of reads in the
sample. In contrast to FPKM, the relative abundance metric does not normalise for gene
length and was therefore used as a metric for the mapping results against the ”Bacterial
community” databases as these databases were based on contigs and not genes. Like the
”Bacterial community” databases, the ”Plasmids” database was not comprised of genes.
However, the sizes of the plasmids in the database varied greatly (2776bp - 152337bp),
with the smallest plasmids being of sizes similar to those of genes. Therefore, it was
decided to initially analyse both the relative abundance and the FPKM of the mapping
results against the ”Plasmids” database.
In more recent years, it has been argued that microbiome datasets should be considered
as compositional and should therefore also be analysed as such [78]. Compositional
data refers to data that only contains relative information instead of absolute information,
which typically applies to closed data, i.e. data that sums up to a constant [79]. Due
to sequencing machines only being capable of delivering reads up to a specific capac-
ity for the instrument, it is argued that the machines have an arbitrary total imposed by
the instrument. The data received from the sequencing machine therefore describes the
relative abundance of a sequence in an environment instead of describing the absolute
abundance of the sequence in the environment [78]. The specific properties of composi-
tional data precludes it from being analysed by standard statistical techniques in its raw
form and as a result log-ratios of components are often used for studying this kind of data
[79]. As previously described, running the normalisation and summarisation scripts on
the mapping results resulted in the creation of tables where the mapped data had been
centered log-ratio (CLR) transformed. Therefore, to account for the possibility of the map-
ping results against the ”Community” and ”Pangenome” databases being compositional,
the CLR tables were included in the further analysis of the mapping results against these
databases to determine which metric performed better.
Plasmid identification in the Lm isolates
To identify the specific plasmids carried by the individual Lm isolates used to design the
two mock communities, the assembled whole genome sequences of each isolate were
screened against a nucleotide database containing representative Listeria plasmid repli-
cons using command-line nucleotide blast with BLAST+ v2.2.31 in Computerome 2.0 [80].
The plasmid replicon database was created by Schmitz-Esser et al. 2021 and included
repA genes from each of the four Listeria plasmid groups as well as the replication genes
from the small Listeria plasmids pLMST6 and pIP23 [28]. First, a multi-fasta file with rep-
resentative plasmid replicons was formatted for blastn with the ”makeblastdb” program
followed by blastn of each genome against the plasmid replicon database carried out
in ABRicate [65]. The blastn output contained the contig IDs that carried the plasmid
replicons. These contig IDs were collected into a single ”.txt” file and extracted from the
assemblies all at once using the ”awk” command for-loop as individual fasta files. Then,
blastn was carried out with each fasta file containing the contig with a replicon gene against
the previously created ”Plasmids” database allowing 5 hits per contig, which resulted in a
single ”.blastn” file per contig. Thereafter, the ”.blastn” files were manually inspected and
an isolate was assumed to carry a specific plasmid if the percent identity for the alignment
17
==PAGE:28==
was higher than 80% and the breadth of coverage was above 90%. Furthermore, to in-
vestigate if the plasmids carried any of the known QACs tolerance genes, blastn of each
gene against the plasmids in the ”Plasmids” database was carried out in ABRicate [65].
It was hypothesised that some plasmids in the ”Plasmids” database had highly similar
nucleotide sequences but were deposited to NCBI with different names. This hypothesis
was explored for plasmids carrying QACs tolerance genes by aligning them with MAFFT
in Geneious Prime v.2022.1.1. The highly similar plasmids were then accumulated and a
representative name was chosen for them [81].
Visualisation of abundance tables
For both communities, bar plots were created that showed the total FPKM for each sample
mapped against the ”Pangenome”, ”Plasmids”, ”MLST”, ”Virulence” and ”Disinfectant”
databases. These bar plots were created in RStudio with the scripts ”Barplot_BC.R” and
”Barplot_PAA.R”. As input, the scripts took the FPKM abundance tables that contained

Gene and plasmid differences in samples
To investigate the differences between the T0 samples, the Negative control samples
and the Treatment samples in both communities, principal component analysis (PCAs)
were done for the mapping results against each of the databases. This was done with
the R script ”PCA.R”, which took an abundance table as input file and used the R library
”factoextra” to create a PCA plot. For the mapping results against the ”MLST”, ”Virulence”
and ”Disinfectant” databases, the FPKM abundance tables were used as input. For the
mapping results against the ”Bacterial community” databases, the CLR abundance tables
and the relative abundance tables were both used as input and then compared. In the
analysis of the ”Pangenome” mapping results both the CLR and FPKM abundance tables
were used as input and then compared, while the mapping results against the ”Plasmids”
database used both the FPKM and relative abundance tables as inputs.
To investigate the differences between the communities with and without biocide treat-
ment, PCAs were done where only the Negative control samples and the Treatment sam-
ples were included. By omitting the T0 samples from the analysis, it was theorised that
the observed differences would reflect gene and plasmid changes caused by stress from
the biocide treatment and not stress as a result of bacterial competition. This analysis
was carried out with the R script ”PCA_without_T0.R” and was only carried out for the
mapping results against the ”Pangenome” and ”Plasmids” databases. As input, the script
used FPKM abundance tables for both the ”Pangenome” and ”Plasmids” databases and
produced one PCA plot per database per community.
To continue the investigation of the effect of the biocide treatment on the two commu-
nities, the previously made PCAs with T0 excluded were further analysed. The R script
”PCA_without_T0.R” was used to calculate the weight of each pangenome gene and plas-
mid on principcal component 1 (PC1) in their respective PCAs. The genes or plasmids
were sorted according to their weight and these results were exported as tables and anal-
ysed in Microsoft Excel. The 50 pangenome genes with the highest weight on PC1 were
investigated further for both communities. For each gene, Uniprot BLAST was run on the
nucleotide sequence and the protein match with the highest score was noted for each
gene [82]. If no match was found or the percent identity was below 60%, the nucelotide
sequence was designated as an ”Unknown protein”. Based on information collected from
Uniprot.org, all genes were grouped according to their function. If no function could be
found for the gene in Uniprot.org, it was assigned to the ”Unknown function” group. In
total, six groups were created for protein functions: ”Cell membrane”, ”DNA binding or
replication”, ”Listeria phage”, ”Metabolic process”, ”Unknown function” and ”Unknown pro-
tein”. By manually inspecting the FPKM abundance tables, it was investigated whether the
FPKM of the specific genes were increased or decreased in the Treatment samples com-
pared to the Negative control samples. If the FPKM was increased in one of the biological
replicates but decreased in the other biological replicate, the effect of biocide treatment on
the gene was marked as ”Inconclusive”. Finally, the R script ”Barplot_gene_function.R”
was used to visualise the assigned functions of the top 50 highest weighted pangenome
genes for both communities. As input, the script used tables containing information about
the gene functions and the amount of genes where the FPKM was increased, decreased
or inconclusive in the Treatment samples compared to the Negative controls. The script
utilised ”ggplot2” to create bar plots depicting the gene function distribution for each com-
munity.

Identification of plasmids in the Lm isolates
By aligning the whole genome sequences of the isolates to a custom database contain-
ing representative Listeria plasmid replicons and thereafter aligning the replicon-carrying
contigs to the ”Plasmids” database, the specific plasmids carried by the isolates in the
communities were determined. Moreover, the identified plasmids were screened for the
presence of the four known QACs tolerance genes. These results can be seen in Supple-
mentary table 2. Furthermore, by examining the ”.blastn” files, it could be seen that the
contig, on which plasmid pLmN12-0935 was located, had a comparatively high coverage
which indicated that pLmN12-0935 was a high-copy number plasmid.
It was hypothesised that several of the plasmids in the ”Plasmids” database were highly
similar despite being deposited in repositories under different accession numbers and
plasmid names. Plasmids carrying QACs tolerance genes were aligned against each
other and if the alignment indicated that the plasmids were highly similar, their abundance
was accumulated and a representative plasmid was chosen among them.
Plasmids with emrC gene
It was found that the emrC-carrying plasmids pLIS3, pLmN12-0935 and the unnamed
plasmid with accession number ”NZ_CP063384.1” were highly similar and only had
two SNP differences.
Plasmid pLIS3 had a SNP in an intergenic region between a
gene encoding a hypothetical protein hp and the repB gene.
Plasmids pLIS3 and
”NZ_CP063384.1” both had a SNP in gene copG (G →T) leading to an amino acid substi-
tution (L →A). These plasmids were accumulated and plasmid pLmN12-0935 was chosen
as a representative for these three plasmids.

3.3
Biocide treatment of the Lm mock communities
In the biocide treatment experiments, the BC and PAA communities were treated with BC
and PAA respectively and then grown until the stationary phase was reached after which
they were sampled for DNA extraction. To ensure that the biocide treatments were as
close to sub-lethal as possible, varying concentrations of BC and PAA were used. For
each community, two biological replicates were created and for each biological replicate,
two technical replicates were made. Furthermore, each culture was distributed into five
wells per technical replicate leading to a total of 20 replicates for each biocide concentra-
tion and control.
The growth curves of the BC community can be seen in figure 9, where the x-axis de-
picts hours after biocide treatment and the y-axis depicts the OD540 of the community.
For all biological and technical replicates, a positive control without any isolates or BC
was created, alongside a negative control consisting of the BC community isolates with-
out biocide treatment. The positive control showed no noticeable changes in the OD540
measurements throughout the entirety of the experiment, indicating that the experiment
had not been contaminated. For biological replicate 1 (figure 9a and figure 9b) it was ob-
served that the negative control reached stationary phase after approximately 43 hours
and therefore a sample was taken at this time. For biological replicate 2 (figure 9c and
figure 9d) the negative control cultures reached stationary phase after approximately 40
hours and were sampled. After approximately 73 hours, growth curves for the biological
replicate 1 cultures with 1.75 mg/L BC reached stationary phase and were sampled, while
the biological replicate 2 cultures with 1.75 mg/L BC reached stationary phase after ap-
proximately 69 hours and were sampled. For both biological replicates, the growth curves
of the cultures with 2 mg/L BC varied significantly and consequently it was decided not
to sample these cultures. No growth or very delayed growth was observed for cultures
with higher BC concentrations. It was therefore decided to use a BC concentration of
1.75 mg/L as the sub-lethal biocide concentration and these samples were used for DNA
extraction and further sequencing analysis.

4
Discussion
4.1
Mock community design
The aim of the design of the PAA community was to create a mock community that was as
diverse as possible based on genome characteristics, while the BC community was de-
signed to include isolates that showed increased phenotypic tolerance to BC. By compar-
ing the pangenome analysis of both communities, it can be seen that the PAA community
pangenome is both bigger than the BC community pangenome (6,830 genes compared to
5,646 genes) and has a smaller percentage of core genes (31.12% compared to 38.91%),
indicating higher diversity of the PAA community. Furthermore, the higher diversity of the
PAA community compared to the BC community can be seen from their respective phy-
logenetic trees. While the BC phylogenetic tree is based on 43,535 SNPs and 81.23% of
the reference genome is covered by all isolates, the PAA phylogenetic tree is based on
53,195 SNPs and only 77.65% of the reference genome is covered by all isolates. As a
higher number of SNPs indicates high diversity between isolates this again illustrates the
diversity of the PAA community. Moreover, the lower percentage of the reference genome
being covered by all isolates in the PAA community also suggests that the PAA commu-
nity is more diverse. However, it is worth noting that a lower percentage of the reference
genome being covered by all isolates results in more SNPs being omitted in the SNPs
analysis thereby potentially leading to the exclusion of important information [61].
The BC community was designed based on the broth microdilution assays leading to the
exclusion of BC susceptible strains. As it was not possible to measure any differences in
the PAA MIC values for the collection of 330 Lm isolates, the PAA community was instead
designed based on diversity, leading to the two communities being designed based on
different criteria. Although both communities were designed based on logical decisions
and could give insight into the respective biocide treatment, the difference in design criteria
led to a less obvious comparison between the results from the two communities.
4.2
MLST alleles
The distribution of the most abundant alleles in the T0, Negative control and Treatment
samples were investigated for both communities (figure 13). For the BC community, only
minor differences were observed between the T0 and Negative control samples, while
only the eighth most abundant allele was changed when comparing them to the Treat-
ment samples. For the PAA community, only one allele variant had a significantly higher
abundance in the Negative control samples compared to the T0 samples. However, three
new allele variants were introduced amongst the top eight most abundant alleles in the
Treatment samples. Unfortunately, a limitation of the metagenomic approach is that all
alleles in a sample are mixed together and it is therefore not possible to discern which
alleles are derived from the same isolate. As an ST-type is decided by the specific combi-
nation of seven alleles, it is not possible to directly correlate these results to an increase
or decrease in specific STs. They do however indicate that the distribution of STs in both
communities are changing as a results of competition and biocide treatment. These re-
sults were further confirmed in the PCA, where it again could be seen that the T0 and
Negative control samples were more similar than the Treatment samples for the BC com-
munity (figure 15e), while similar biological replicates clustered more distinctly together
for the PAA community (figure 15f).
4.3
Plasmids and QACs tolerance genes
Blastn was used to identify the plasmids harboured by the isolates and the QACs tol-
erance genes carried by the plasmids (see Supplementary table 1 and 2). The analy-
sis showed that no plasmids that were found to harbour QACs tolerance genes, were
43
==PAGE:54==
present in BC susceptible isolates. Moreover, these plasmids were only found in isolates
that were previously classified as carrying the QACs tolerance gene through the initial
alignment of the QACs tolerance genes against the isolate genomes. Additionally, the
QACs tolerance genes and plasmid blastn results correlate with the findings from pre-
vious studies [70]. Collectively, these findings indicate that the approach for identifying
plasmid-harbouring isolates and QACs tolerance gene-harbouring plasmids was success-
ful. However, it should be noted that the approach used in this project is limited in its ability
to identify large plasmids in the isolates. As many Lm plasmids have been found to have
DNA regions with a high nucleotide sequence similarity it cannot be assumed that an
isolate is harbouring a specific plasmid unless both the percent identity and breadth of
coverage are high for the alignment [31][84]. As the provided isolate contigs were as-
sembled from short reads, which typically leads to a higher number of shorter contigs, it
is often not possible to achieve a high breadth of coverage for the large plasmids aligned
against these shorter contigs [85].
Out of the total collection of 330 Lm isolates included in this project, 148 (45%) were iden-
tified as carrying a plasmid. A 2021 large-scale survey by Schmitz-Esser et al. found
that 54% of isolates in a 1,921 Lm isolate collection carried putative plasmids [28]. Sim-
ilar results were observed in a 2021 study by Chmielowska et al., where they found that
53% of a 287 collection of Lm carried plasmids [86]. The percentage of isolates in this
project’s collection that harbours plasmids therefore seems comparatively small. How-
ever, Schmitz-Esser et al. also concluded that the presence of plasmids varies greatly
depending on the ST and lineage of the isolate. As the Schmitz-Esser et al. survey only
included 14 STs and this project investigated a collection where 70 different STs were
represented, this could explain the observed difference in percentage of plasmid-carrying
isolates. Chmielowska et al. additionally found that 40% of the isolates in their 287 Lm
collection were resistant to BC [86], while only 22% of all 330 isolates in this project were
found to be BC resistant. As both emrC and bcrABC are known to be plasmid-associated,
the lower percentage of plasmid-harbouring isolates in this project could be a plausible
explanation for this lower observed percentage of resistance.
The alignment of the plasmids with the known QACs tolerance genes showed that the
emrC gene was plasmid-borne for all seven isolates that were identified as harbouring
this gene. This correlates with previous studies where emrC has also currently only been
found on plasmids [48][44]. Moreover, emrC was found to be present in 2% of all isolates
in the collection, while it was similarly found to be present in 4% of a 287 Lm collection
by Chmielowska et al. [86]. Although these two percentages are fairly similar, the slightly
higher percentage of emrC-harbouring isolates in the Chmielowska et al. collection can,
once more, possibly be explained by the total percentage of plasmid-harbouring isolates
being higher in their collection. In the total collection of this project, it was found that 39
isolates harboured the qacH gene, corresponding to 12% of all isolates and 55% of all
BC tolerant isolates. Chmielowska et al. observed that 85% of the BC resistant strains in
their collection carried the qacH gene, which is notably higher [86]. In this project, all 39
isolates that harboured qacH were found to carry it on their chromosome. Although the
Tn6188 transposon on which qacH is located is generally found to be integrated chromo-
somally [46], two plasmids have been found to harbour a gene closely related to qacH
[70]. However, neither of these two plasmids were found in the 330 collection. In the total
collection, five isolates were identified as carrying the emrE gene and the gene was not
found to be plasmid-associated for any of the isolates corresponding with previous studies
[47][87]. Lastly, 14 isolates in the collection were found to carry the bcrABC cassette out
of which the cassette was harboured on plasmids in 13 isolates and chromosomally inte-
44
==PAGE:55==
grated in the last isolate. This correlates with previous findings, as the bcrABC cassette
has been observed to be both plasmid-based and rarely chromosomally integrated [45].
In the investigation of the total abundance of the 12 samples mapped against the
”Pangenome”, ”Plasmids”, ”MLST”, ”Virulence” and ”Disinfectant” databases (figure 11),
the most variance between samples could be seen for the ”Plasmids” and ”Disinfectant”
databases for both communities. Furthermore, it was observed that the total abundance
of the samples mapped against the ”Disinfectant” database proportionally appeared to
be very similar to that of the samples mapped against the ”Plasmids” database. There-
fore, it was hypothesised that one or more plasmids carrying a gene belonging to the
”Disinfectant” database were responsible for this variance in total abundance between
samples. This hypothesis was further investigated by examining the specific abundance
in FPKM of individual plasmids and disinfectant genes more closely with boxplots (fig-
ure 12). For both communities, it was observed that the small plasmid, pLmN12-0935,
had a noticeably higher abundance than all other plasmids, although it was significantly
decreased after BC treatment. Furthermore, it was observed that the emrC gene had a
noticeably higher abundance than the rest of the disinfectant genes, although it likewise
was significantly decreased after BC treatment (figure 14). It was therefore theorised that
plasmid pLmN12-0935 was harbouring the emrC gene, which was confirmed by mapping
the QACs tolerance genes to the ”Plasmids” database. Furthermore, a 2021 study by
Chmielowska et al. found that plasmid pLmN12-0935 only differed from plasmid pLIS3,
on which emrC was originally identified, by 2 altered bases [86][48]. Through the PCA
analysis of the mapping results against the ”Plasmids” database, it was confirmed that
plasmid pLmN12-0935 had the highest weight by far on PC1 for both communities. More-
over, it could be seen from figure 16c and figure 16d that PC1 accounts for 99.9% and
92.3% of the variance between the BC samples and the PAA samples, respectively. It
was therefore concluded that the emrC-harbouring plasmid pLmN12-0935 was the ma-
jor driver for the differentiation between the Negative control samples and the Treatment
samples in regards to plasmids.
Interestingly, although plasmid pLmN12-0935 was found to carry the QACs tolerance
gene emrC, the abundance of the plasmid was lower in both the PAA Treatment samples
and the BC Treatment samples when comparing them to their respective Negative control
samples. It has previously been shown that plasmid-carrying bacteria can exhibit a de-
layed growth when comparing them to plasmid-free strains, indicating a potential fitness
reduction as a result of harbouring plasmids [29]. Therefore, as it was previously deter-
mined that pLmN12-0935 is a high-copy number plasmid, it was theorised that harbouring
pLmN12-0935 could lead to a fitness reduction for isolates in the highly competitive and
biocide stress-induced environments. Kropac et al. have previously compared the growth
of strains harbouring the highly similar pMLST6 plasmid with the growth of plasmid-free
strains and found no growth differences, indicating that the harbouring of pLMST6 does
not result in a fitness reduction [44]. However, this was only tested with isolates grown
under stress-free conditions. These findings correlate with the high abundance that was
observed for the pLmN12-0935 plasmid in the Negative control samples, where there
was no biocide-induced stress. As of yet, no studies have investigated if harbouring
pLmN12-0935 or a similar plasmid has an effect on the fitness of isolates in competi-
tive and biocide-stressed environments and it is therefore only possible to speculate that
this increased competition and stress could be the cause of the reduced abundance of
the plasmid. However, for the community subjected to PAA treatment, it seems likely that
harbouring the plasmid in a high copy number would not be beneficial, since it has been
shown that the EmrC efflux pump does not have an effect on PAA disinfectants [88].
45
==PAGE:56==
For the BC community, there was generally no observed increase in QACs tolerance
gene abundance, but only the previously discussed decrease of the emrC-harbouring
pLmN12-0935 plasmid (figure 14 (left)). A recent large-scale study of 769 Lm genomes
found that bcrABC and qacH were often harboured by isolates in the same environment
but were never present in the same strain [89]. This correlates with the findings from this
project and could potentially indicate an exclusivity mechanism, only allowing harbouring
of one of these two genes in a single isolate. Otherwise, it could indicate that harbour-
ing more than one QACs tolerance gene simply does not increase QACs tolerance and
is therefore not selected for. As all strains in the BC community were chosen for their
tolerance to BC, and the majority of these strains harboured either bcrABC or qacH, the
isolates would therefore not be able to take up an additional QACs tolerance gene or
would not achieve increased tolerance by doing so, which could explain the unchanged
QACs tolerance gene abundance. Moreover, as all BC community isolates were already
BC tolerant, the decrease in emrC could be due to isolates carrying the pLmN12-0935
plasmid merely being outperformed by isolates carrying other QACs tolerance genes. As
of yet, no studies have been carried out which compare the selectivity for different QACs
tolerance genes and therefore this can only be speculated until more research is done on
the subject. Collectively, these results highlight the need for increased research into the
effect of biocide treatment on bacterial communities and not only pure bacterial cultures.
As bacterial communities are ubiquitous in nature, and thereby better reflect the natural
environments of the bacteria, studying them can give valuable information regarding how
both the symbiotic and antagonistic relationships between them can affect other cellular
functions [90].
4.4
Changes after biocide treatment
For both communities, it was investigated which pangenome genes had the largest effect
on the differentiation between the Negative control samples and the Treatment samples
(Supplementary table 3 and 4). Then, the top 50 genes were all grouped according to an-
notated gene function and it was noted whether the gene was increased or decreased in
the Treatment samples compared to the Negative control samples (figure 17a and figure
17b). For both communities, the vast majority of differentiating genes were derived from
Listeria phages and an increase in the Treatment samples was observed for all these
genes. As previously mentioned, prophages are common to Lm and a possible expla-
nation for this increase in phage genes could therefore be cell lysis due to stress as a
result of the biocide treatment resulting in phage induction. Although the specific effect of
BC and PAA treatment on prophage induction in a bacterial community has not yet been
studied, many stressors, such as antibiotics, UV radiation and temperature have already
been shown to cause prophage induction in pure cultures [91]. This hypothesis is further
supported by a 2014 study by Tian et al. where the effect of copper treatment on a natural
multi-species bacterial community was investigated. Through a metagenomic compari-
son, they were able to observe an enrichment in the phage community after copper treat-
ment, implying that a lytic phage cycle was stimulated by stress caused by a high copper
concentration [92]. In contrast, a previous 2014 study by Casey et al. found that genes
encoding phage proteins were down-regulated in Lm strain 6179 after it was exposed to
the QAC disinfectant ”BZT”. However, they hypothesised that this down-regulation could
be attributed to a less frequent spontaneous prophage induction as a result of BZT caus-
ing a mildly acidic environment compared to the control [53]. More importantly, as this
study solely investigated the effect of BZT on a pure bacterial culture (one Lm strain) and
not on a bacterial community, this could explain the different result. Lastly, as prophages
have already been shown to have the ability to increase survivability and fitness of Lm,
another possible explanation for this observed increase in prophage-associated genes
46
==PAGE:57==
could be that the phages increase host fitness in the presence of biocide [93]. This hy-
pothesis correlates with the findings from a 2022 genome-wide association study on a
collection of 197 Lm strains by Palma et al., where they found an association between
prophage-related genes and a phenotypic tolerance to both BC and PAA [24].
Closer inspection of the top 50 pangenome genes, that are causing the differentiation
between the Negative control and Treatment samples for the BC community, revealed that
the top six genes are derived from isolate LIS08. Furthermore, it was observed that the
genes were closely located and that the abundance was highly decreased for all of them
after BC treatment. As it was previously determined that LIS08 carried the pLmN12-0935
plasmid, it was therefore speculated that the top six genes were located on this plasmid.
Comparing these top six genes to the pMLST6-associated genes previously reported by
Kremer et al. substantiates this hypothesis [48]. This indicates that the biggest difference
between the BC Negative control samples and Treatment samples is indeed the presence
or absence of the high-copy number pLmN12-0935 plasmid.
By inspecting the top 50 most differentiating PAA pangenome genes closer, the previ-
ously identified pLmN12-0935-associated genes were again observed to be present. This
correlates with the previous findings of plasmid pLmN12-0935 being important for the dif-
ferentiation between the PAA Negative control samples and Treatment samples. Further-
more, eight genes were assigned the function ”Cell membrane” out of which three were
increased, three were decreased and two were inconclusive when comparing the Treat-
ment samples to the Negative control samples. Interestingly, on three occasions it was
observed that two ”Cell membrane” pangenome genes mapped to the same protein and
that one pangenome gene was then increased while the other was decreased (or both
were inconclusive). This could indicate that several variants of the same cell membrane-
associated protein are present in the community and that one variant is being favoured
over another in the presence or absence of PAA. As PAA is known to be involved in disrup-
tion of cell membranes and increased cell wall permeability, changes in ”Cell membrane”
proteins in the community after PAA treatment seem plausible [94].

Conclusion
By examining the gene enrichment analysis of the two mock communities treated with ei-
ther BC or PAA, it was found that significant differences could be observed when compar-
ing the biocide-free control communities with their respective biocide-treated community.
For both communities, a change in the most abundant seven-housekeeping MLST alleles
was observed after biocide treatment, although this could not be directly correlated with
an increase or decrease in specific STs. The plasmid analysis indicated that 45% of all
isolates harboured plasmids and that the known QACs tolerance genes bcrABC and emrC
were predominantly plasmid associated. Moreover, in both communities, a decrease in
the abundance of the small pLmN12-0935 plasmid, which harboured emrC, was observed
after biocide treatment. As this plasmid was found to have a high-copy number in the ma-
jority of isolates where it was present, it was hypothesised that the decrease in abundance
was as a result of an increased fitness cost associated with harbouring of the plasmid. In-
terestingly, while a decrease in emrC abundance was observed, none of the communities
showed an increase in abundance of any of the other known QACs tolerance genes after
biocide treatment. As the known BC tolerance genes qacH and bcrABC were never found
to be present in the same isolate, it was speculated that isolates could only harbour one of
these genes or that isolates could not increase their tolerance to BC by harbouring more
than one gene. As all isolates in the BC community were BC resistant and most were
found to already harbour bcrABC or qacH, they would therefore be unable to take up ad-
ditional QACs tolerance genes, thereby explaining the unchanged QACs tolerance gene
abundance. Moreover, it was speculated that the decrease in emrC abundance could be
a result of selection for the other QACs tolerance genes at the expense of emrC-carrying
strains.
For both communities, it was found that genes associated with Listeria phages were espe-
cially responsible for the differentiation between the biocide-free control communities and
the biocide-treated communities. Moreover, the abundance of these phage genes was
increased after biocide treatment. It was hypothesised that this was due to prophage in-
duction as a result of stress caused by the competitive environment and biocide treatment.
Contrarily, it was speculated that the harbouring of prophages could lead to increased bio-
cide tolerance. Finally, several changes in the abundance of genes associated with the
cell membrane were observed for the PAA community after biocide treatment. As the cur-
rent understanding of the PAA mode of action involves disruption of the cell membrane,
it was deemed plausible that PAA treatment would select for certain cell membrane gene
variants over others.
