Abstract
The increasing demands for smaller and eﬃcient power supplies has pushed the switching frequencies
of switch mode power supplies. Power supplies switching in the very high frequency range (30-300
MHz) is now possible, and with these frequencies the passive components are now small enough that
integration on silicon wafers is possible.
In this thesis a design of a VHF class E resonant DC/DC converter switching at 250 MHz is
presented. The converter is designed with considerations to an integrated process. The transistor build
on silicon wafers are presented in models, and a method for describing the parasitic parameters needed
to optimize the design in terms of frequency and eﬃciency is presented. The resulting losses in the
transistors was described and calculated.
The design choices regarding passive components is discussed, and simplistic models of a planar
spiral inductor integrated directly on the silicon is created, for use in the converter.
The inductor models designed did not perform well enough to actually be used in the converter.
This was the results of both purely conducting material in the process, but also the proximity eﬀect of
highly conducting substrate played a large role.
Instead a converter model with MIM-capacitor models and ideal inductors was implemented to
have a an output power of 9 watts with an eﬃciency of ∼55 %. The low eﬃciency is contributed to
the losses in the transistors.

Resumé
Det øgede krav til mindre og eﬀektive strømforsyninger har skubbet grænserne for skifte frekvenser i
strømforsyninger. Strømsforsyninger har i dag skifte frekvenser i det meget høje frekvens bånd (30-300
MHZ), og med så høje frekvenser er det muligt at integrere de passive kombonenter på en silicium
skive.
I denne afhandling præsenteres der et design af en klasse E resonant DC/DC omformer, med en
skiftefrekvens på 250 MHz. Omformeren er designet med henblik på en integreret process. Tran-
sistorerne bygget på silicium præsenteres, og en metode til at beskrive de parasitiske egenskaber
bliver præsenteret. Disse er nødvendige for at kunne optimere designet, med henblik på frekvens og
eﬀektivitet. De resulterende tab i transistorerne bliver beregner og beskrevet.
Valg af og design af de passive kombonenter i kredsløbet bliver diskuteret, og en simpel model for
en planar spiral spole bliver præsenteret, og implementeret i designet.
De modelerede spoler præsterede ikke brugbare resultater. Det skyldes både det tilgængelige
ledende materiale og den lille afstand til den ledende silcium plade som spillede en stor rolle.
I stedet blev en model af omformeren med MIM-kapacitor modeller og ideele spoler implementeret,
designet til en udgangseﬀekt på 9 watt og med en eﬀektivitet på ∼55 %. Den lave eﬀektivitet skyldes
tabene i transistorene.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In recent years large innovations in the technology of cell phones has been seen, and many companies
have thrived in the industry, making many diﬀerent components, units and chips. One of the more
criticized parts of the cell phone is audio reproducing. The market has seen phones branded purely on
their ability to reproduce audio better, louder and more eﬃcient. The idea behind this thesis is exactly
this. Making a product for use in a cell phone, capable of suppling an audio ampliﬁer, eﬃciently.
The purpose is thus to investigate the possibilities in designing such a power supply and the many
diﬃculties involved when making a product for a cell phone. Particularly the troubles arising when
trying to integrate a power supply on chip will be a focus point.
In the ﬁrst chapter an introduction to the problem is given. The diﬀerent technologies will be
brieﬂy touched upon, before moving further to the design of the actual converter in chapter 2. In
chapter 3 and 4 subparts of the designed switch mode power supply (SMPS) are discussed in detail. In
chapter 5 a discussion on the passive components in the integrated process is given, and in chapter 6
simulations of the SMPS are shown, with models of the passive components. Chapter 7 presents a
discussion on the diﬃculties of integrating a SMPS, and the future work needed to fully integrate a
DC/DC converter on silicon.
1.1
Audio Reproducing
Class AB
Since its introduction in the 1950’ies the class AB ampliﬁer has been widely used. It is a combination
of a class A ampliﬁer known for simplicity and a class B ampliﬁer known for its improved eﬃciency, at
the cost of some distortion. The class AB ampliﬁer proved advantages over the previous classes, A and
B, and has been used widely in many types of ampliﬁers e.g. [1–7]. Its functional schematic is shown
in ﬁgure 1.1.

It is seen that the two transistors conduct in each diﬀerent period of the signal. By correctly biasing
the transistors one can achieve a very linear behavior, meaning any crossover distortion can ideally be
removed. This is a highly appreciated feature of the class AB ampliﬁer [4,8]. But it also comes with
the maximum achievable eﬃciency of the class B ampliﬁer for a single sinusoidal signal, 78.5 %, due to
the quiescent current. The eﬃciency is often even lower due to the dynamic behavior of music. This is
an unfortunate inherent factor of the class AB circuitry.
Class D
Addressing the theoretical maximum frequency of the class AB ampliﬁer, a SMPS-way of thinking
was introduced with the class D ampliﬁer.
It uses a pulse-width-modulation (PWM) approach,
converting the audio to pulses, which ensures that transistors conduct fully, and only in their respective
period compared to a class AB ampliﬁer. [9]. Its functional schematic is shown in ﬁgure 1.2.

Ideally the eﬃciency can reach 100 %, and it is thus preferable to the class AB ampliﬁer. It is very
widely used. [9–15].
One of the problems with the class D ampliﬁer is the introduced nonlinearities. The modulation
is generated from a carrier, often implemented by discharging a capacitor. This is more exponential
than linear, and contributes to some harmonic distortion. Furthermore, to prevent any shoot through
in the output stage a dead time between the two transistors is implemented. This dead time also
introduces non-linearities. Many advancements has been made addressing the issues of nonlinearity, by
implementing diﬀerent modulation techniques and feedback loops, and in many implementations total
harmonic distortion below 0.01 % has been seen. [11,16–18].
Another problem with the class D ampliﬁer is the side eﬀects of PWM. In the PWM signal high
currents is necessary to charge and discharge the gate of the output transistors, fast and eﬃciently.
These high currents gives rise to EMI problems, which needs to be either ﬁltered out, or otherwise
improved. In a mobile phone, the EMI is very problematic, due to the closeness of antennas used
for communication. Often the antennas are wound around the speaker to save place, and with a
standard class D ampliﬁer might be rendered useless due to EMI. Ways of improving the EMI has been
researched, most promising is diﬀerent modulation techniques used, such as Sliding-Mode modulation.
At higher switching frequencies however, this is not as eﬀective, and other methods must be used. [13].
Another approach to the above described problems is using a tracking SMPS and a class AB
ampliﬁer, eﬀectively designing a class H ampliﬁer. This of course still introduces problems of EMI
due to the switching in the SMPS, but other approaches can be used to lower the EMI of the SMPS
without aﬀecting the audio.

Class H
A class H ampliﬁer is essentially a class AB ampliﬁer or B ampliﬁer where the power supply is
controlled by the input to the class AB ampliﬁer, often through envelope tracking. The resulting
constant losses due to the quiescent current in the ampliﬁer can now be reduced and ideally 100 %
eﬃciency can be achieved [19]. It has not been used as widely as the class D ampliﬁer [20,21], though
some recent patents on diﬀerent class H conﬁgurations has been seen for headphone ampliﬁers [22] and
actuator drivers for hard disk drives [23]. An implementation of a Class H with a H-bridged Class B
as it output stage showed promising results with THD below 0.1 % and good eﬃciency by using a
hysteresis control [24].
The advantages of the class H ampliﬁer is thus the linearities of the class AB ampliﬁer, with
eﬃciency ideally equivalent to that of the class D ampliﬁer. The problems concerning with EMI can
be approached in the SMPS alone, and using zero-voltage switching resonant power converters can
result in better EMI performance. [25].
The focus of this thesis will be to design the SMPS for use with a class AB ampliﬁer, eﬀectively
making a class H ampliﬁer. The class AB ampliﬁer and resulting audio will not be the focus, only the
power supply.
1.2
Integrated SMPS
In designing switch mode power supplies the key drivers are things as prices, sizes and easy
production. The limits to sizes of the converters today is the passive components needed to store
energy in each cycle of the converter. The size of these components are very closely related to the
switching frequency of the SMPS, and increasing this reduces the sizes. In the recent year there has
been many improvements in switching frequencies, and we are now well into the VHF domain with
converters switching at frequencies from 30-300 MHz e.g. [26–29].
When increasing the switching frequencies, one of the problems arising is the resulting increased
switching losses. In each cycle the energy stored in the capacitor is wasted. To solve this problems,
techniques for making zero voltage switching converters has been introduced. Originally these con-
verters where used in broadcasting radio frequencies. They are known as resonant power converters
and can achieve much higher switching frequencies at lower switching losses due to capabilities of zero
voltage switching (ZVS). Eﬃciencies of 80-90 % have been reported. [30].
These improvements in switching frequencies means that the passive components are reduced so
much in size that they can be produced by methods of photo lithography i.e. on a silicon wafer. The
holy grail is considered the fully monolithic converter. A cross section of a generic silicon wafer with
metal layer can be seen in ﬁgure 1.3.
When integrating converters on a silicon wafer a lot of things becomes diﬀerent than a normal
discrete design. First and foremost the substrate connects the whole chip together. Its very close
proximity to the passive components introduces problems with parasitic capacitance and eddy currents
induced in the substrate, which has conductivities similarly to seawater.

integrated class E converters. For the purpose of limiting this project, focus is kept on the resonant

Limitations to the Models
In the following all simulations will be done with the above characterized model. This characteriza-
tion has been done from simulation, and thus neither extraction of a layout nor a real world test have
been performed. The models, as mentioned in the previous section, has a very low gate-resistance and
very linear gate capacitances, far from that which would be expected from realistic components.
A more precise model could possibly be obatined if a layout extraction was available. Here the
models include most parasitics i.e. capacitance to substrate, resistances in the conducting materials
and more. This however was not possible with the design kit available.
The model available is a HSPICE model level 49, which is often used in high-voltage RF simulations.
According to the Star-HSPICE manual [48] it is an enhanced BSIM3v3 model, especially for use with
HSPICE. The model clearly has some limitations, but is the best available. To validate the model, one
would have to obtain a transistor from the described process, and through a network-analyzer analyze
the transistor, in much the same way as described in section 3.4 - Characterization of a MOSFET.

4.1
Driving the Transistor
To drive a transistor at higher frequencies often proven diﬃcult. Especially in VHF converters
it becomes almost impossible to drive the gate with a normal pulse width signal, as the required
currents to charge the gates in the required time are enormous. Often a sinusoidal signal is preferred,
when increasing the frequency to avoid the large losses generated by the discharge of the input capaci-
tance. [49]. There are several methods to drive the gate, either with a externally generated signal, or
with a self-oscillating circuit. Externally gate drives are used in implementation such as [28,33]. One
self-oscillating gate drive, commonly used in the class E converter is the class E oscillator, described
in [49–51]. This procedure involves making a feedback circuit, through advanced analytic approach,
but limits the duty cycle to 0.5 which makes it unsuitable for the desired design.
Recently though, a new passive gate drive has been introduced in [38] and used in, among
others [29,30]. In design aspects it looks like the class E oscillator, but is quite simpler. It is eﬀectively
a LC-ﬁlter utilizing the capacitances of the MOSFET and introducing a gate-inductor. Looking at
ﬁgure 3.3, shown in section 3.4, we can draw a schematic of the parasitics, and introducing a gate
inductance, Lg, to achieve the desired ﬁlter. The described gate drive can be seen in ﬁgure 4.1.

Chapter 5
Passive Components in CMOS process
To fully integrate a resonant converter on a chip, the choice of components is limited to the process
technology. The capacitors can be implemented either in the poly material of the substrate, or in
between metal layers. These methods will be discussed in 5.2. But ﬁrstly a discussion on inductors
will be presented. A lot of solutions have been presented on designing inductors on chip, some of them
limited in their frequency, other in their achiviable quality. The advantage of the diﬀerent types of
inductors will be shown, and ﬁnally a design method for a planar spiral inductor will be presented, all
in section 5.1.
5.1
Inductors
There is several methods to chose from, when designing an inductor on a chip. From the more
simple ones using a planar spiral [52–55] depicted in ﬁgure 5.1(a), to toroids [56,57], depicted in ﬁgure
5.1(b), and also using magnetic ﬁlms to reduce the required size of the inductors [32,34,58]. When
designing inductors for use in integrated circuits, the main concern is the achievable Q values - the
relation between its inductive value and resistive value, as seen in eqn. 5.1. Normally the AC-losses
in an inductor is primarily from hysteresis losses, and the eddy current induced in the fairly low
conducting magnetic material. In an integrated process, however, the close proximity to the highly
conducting silicon layer results in very high losses from eddy currents induced.

Many ways have been tried to increasing the Q. Yoon et. all showed Q values of as high as 50 at
frequencies of 7GHz, by micro machining the silicon out underneath it, giving an overhang by as
much as 30 µm, but this also results in a very fragile structure not suited for products [55]. Others
have tried diﬀerent shielding methods, where metal is placed in either a solid plate or in a pattern,

over frequency using a solid shield, and using a patterned shield showed to improve the Q value.
However the Q-values was very low, not exceeding 2. Similarly results was achieved by [59], where
several diﬀerent shield-patterns was tested, and all though they showed improved Q, it also limited the
self-resonance frequency of the inductors.

Another option is to use bond wires, normally used to connect the chip with the package, as
inductors. A general rule of thumb for bond wires is 1 nH/mm. The advantages of bond wires is their
high surface area per length, and the possibility of getting further away from the silicon and thus
raise the Q values. However, the inductance is not very controlled, and any circuit utilizing this type
of inductance must be able to handle variations in the inductance. [61]. It is thus not suitable for
resonant converters, where the inductance values is an important parameter in achieving optimum
conditions for the power conversion.
A ﬁnal method of implementing inductors could be a redistribution layer technique (RDL), where
inductors are placed on top of the silicon die. This method can utilize thicker metal (up to 15 µm)
and better materials, such as copper. This method has not been investigated fully for this thesis, but
results with RDL inductors have shown very high improvement with Q values of 30-50, at frequencies
as high as 5 GHz [62,63].
The research in this ﬁeld is in fast development, and is one of the key components to achieving the
full power supply on chip integration. Both in terms of modeling and simulating, but also in diﬀerent
structures given higher Q-values. Gardner et. al. [58] made an overview of on chip inductors, and the
only inductors usable in frequencies above 100 MHz are planar spiral structured air inductors. These
inductors have not shown Q values above 10.

5.2
Capacitors
Depending on the process diﬀerent types of capacitances is available. Most commonly the choice is
between capacitances made in the poly-silicon layer, known as MOS-capacitors, or Metal-Insulator-
Metal (MIM) capacitances.
The poly-silicon capacitor utilizes the thin gate oxide, which is the thinnest dieletric on the substrate,
and thus naturally ideal for making capacitors. However the problem with using the poly-capacitances
is that they are both voltage and frequency variant. As with inductors the proximity to the substrate,
means that the parasitic capacitances is going to be high, which in terms means that a higher frequen-
cies, poly capacitors is going to be rendered useless. The maximum usable frequency depends on the
speciﬁc process, but often these eﬀects are not modeled. Furthermore the poly-material have a higher
series resistance than metal will have, also limiting the uses of the capacitance.
It is thus much better to use MIM capacitors. These capacitances besides having lower equivalent
series resistance due to use of metal, instead of the less conducting poly, are also lifted away from the
substrate, giving smaller parasitic capacitances. Furthermore the MIM caps are much less voltage
dependent, than their poly counterparts which, as mentioned in section 4.1, can help stabilize the gate
drive.
Although layout is not a part of this thesis, layout in terms of capacitances is often done in a ﬁnger
structure, where the same metal intertwine to connect capacitance. When having multiple layers one
can achieve much higher capacitances at smaller chip area placing ﬁnger structure above each other.
The actually gained capacitance from this structure would have to be extracted from the layout. [40].
In the process used, MIM capacitors is available, and a simple two plate structure has a capacity of
0.65 fF/µm2.

Chapter 7
Integration Diﬃculties and Future Work
It has been apparent throughout this thesis that there is still a lot diﬃculties when designing
PwrSoC. In this chapter a list of some of the challenges seen will be stated. In the ﬁnal section a brief
overview of some of the other future work needed before this can be used in a commercial product will
be presented.
7.1
Models and Tools
In chapter 3 - Transistor Design it became apparent that the simulation models available for the
transistor had limitations. The parasitic elements of the transistor were simply not modeled as voltage
variant which would be expected from the real world. A parasitic extraction of the transistor might
have made a better model, but necessary tools to make this parasitic extraction was not available with
the chosen process. A possibly solution to this is ﬁnding RF-models for a given transistors, as these
often include more parastics, and thus more suited for this type of use. Furthermore the models will
have to be conﬁrmed with measurements done with a network analyzer.
Similarly it became apparent that neither the mos-capacitors frequency or voltage dependency was
modeled, and the poly-capacitances available in the process showed excellent performance in VHF
frequencies. As discussed this should not have been the case.
For the inductors no readily available tools could model these, without using excessive time on
modeling. Thus crude estimation such as the one showed in chapter 5 had to be made. As this has no
way of being veriﬁed through the tools available, the estimations validity cannot be conﬁrmed.
7.2
Materials
In the modeling of the inductors the materials to make the spiral inductor was the aluminum-alloy
available in the process. The conductivity of this material is about just over half of that of copper,
and thus does not contribute positively to the performance of the inductor.
If better conducting materials was available in the process, better performances might have been
seen. To do a quick test on this, a simulation where the aluminum was replaced with the best known
conductor graphene. This is highly theoretical, and actually using graphene in a process is not anywhere
near available today. The simulations did show better performance, and the resulting circuit did
oscillate. But the eﬀects of the substrate still limited the performance, and driving the gate with the
self-oscillating gate drive was not achievable. Results of this simulation are shown in ﬁgure 7.1. It is
apparent that other solutions than just the conduction of the materials needs to be found.

7.3
Corners and Temperature
A topic which have not been mentioned is that of corners. In a CMOS process there is large process
variations known as corners. These variations contribute to the performance of the converter. Being a
resonant converter the optimum performance is limited to its components, these corner variation can
aﬀect the performance quite drastically. Measures have to be taken to avoid this problem.
The eﬀects of temperature on the components will also have an eﬀect, an if higher power losses is
released to the substrate, this could signiﬁcantly heat up the converter, and inhibit the converter.
7.4
Inductors
As shown and discussed the inductors that can be made directly on chip is not at a stage where
they can perform well enough for a resonant converter. Other methods for integrating inductors must
be found. One can either chose the PwrSiP method and packaging commercially available inductors
together with the chip, in a single package. Another solution could be the mentioned Redistribution
Layers process (RDL) performed by companies such as Amkor. This would have to be investigated
further.

7.5
Other Future Work
Optimization: Given the right CAD tools a method for optimizing the class E resonant power
converter for better performance could be done. This is both in terms of frequency, resulting size of
transistors and capacitors, and also inductors, and their achievable Q values and more. The parameters
for an optimum design is many, and large precise models would have to developed, to ﬁnd an optimum
for a given process, load and power requirements.
Tracking capabilities: As described in the introduction, the designed SMPS was to be used in a
class H ampliﬁer topology. Due to time constraints and complications in the design of the DC/DC
converter, this has not been investigated. The preferred method of controlling the output voltage of a
VHF SMPS is a burst-mode control scheme, where the converter is constantly switched on/of. This
has been used in applications such as [49,64], and could be possibly be combined with class H envelope
tracking as used in [21].
EMI: One of the reasons for designing an SMPS for a class H ampliﬁer, was to avoid the known
problem with generated EMI from the class D converter. Although sources suggest that ZVS resonant
converter do in fact limit EMI, this of course have to be investigated.
Layout: Finally the layout of this design has not been done. To fully understand the size and power
densities achievable with integrated PwrSoC the full layout would have to be conducted.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The recent advancement made in the VHF resonant power converters has lead to the interest of
integrating a resonant power converter. In this thesis a method for designing an integrated class E
DC/DC converter on a single CMOS-chip has been presented.
The relevant theory for a resonant class E converter was presented, and limitations when going
up in frequencies was discussed. A CMOS process capable of handling the voltages peaks seen in a
resonant converter was found.
An approach to characterizing the transistors in ways necessary for designing any integrated DC/DC
switch mode power supply was presented. Using methods of Z-parameter analysis, which is well known
in radio-frequency implementations, this characterization was veriﬁed by comparing the results of a
known power MOSFET with a detailed datasheet.
This characterization was used to design a 1-Ωunit-transistor, which stood as the a base model to
describe all future transistors. This method was also proven through simulations.
A self oscillating gate drive was described and calculated, based on the characterization made. This
self oscillating gate drive showed to perform as expected.
A method for designing a planar spiral inductor on chip was presented. This method is not the
best available inductor, but currently the only available with relatively easy implementation of models.
The Q factor achieved was as high as 15, higher than what has been reported in the literature from
experimental results, but this may very well be contributed to the model.
Based on the characterized transistors, gate drive and inductors, the class E converter was simulated.
Several diﬀerent approaches was taken to maximize the frequency, and several design of both frequency
and transistors was carried out. The ﬁnal design, however, still only presented an simulated eﬃciency
of ≃55 %. The losses in the gate was quite severe, due to very large voltage peaks.
It was evident throughout this project that the tools and models available for designing power
supplies on chip is a limiting factor to both the validity and also amount of results. To further achieve
results in the ﬁeld of power supplies on chip, these tools and models needs to be expanded.
