Abstract
The wave propagation in ﬁnite periodic double structures is characterized by the at-
tenuation of certain bands of the frequency span.
These bands are determined by
the propagation constant. Most studies focus on the calculation of the propagation
constant in inﬁnite single beams. Hence, the goal of the present thesis is to evaluate
the propagation constant in ﬁnite periodic double beams. The analytical solution of
any ﬁnite system is obtained using the image method. A description of the code used
for the simulations is provided. Simulations are conducted for certain systems. A
compromise between the accuracy of the results and the computation time is required.
Additional measurements aiming to support the simulation results are conducted but
the applied setup failed to evaluate the propagation constant. Thus, the frequency
response is measured to match with the calculated resonances. Comparisons between
the diﬀerent cases are made. Finally, conclusions regarding the method, the algorithm
and the measurements are drawn.
Keywords: Wave propagation, periodicity, propagation constant, ﬁnite double beams,
image solution.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation of study
Periodic structures are found in constructions, ships, railways, etc. Therefore, wave
propagation in these structures becomes a matter of importance. It is seen that, due
to periodicity, complex phenomena occur. Reﬂections shape the total waveﬁeld. Prop-
agation and attenuation zones exist forming pass bands and stop bands respectively.
These zones may easily be evaluated once the propagation constant of the structure
is obtained. The propagation constant of inﬁnite structures is determined by many
researchers. However, in reality, inﬁnite designs are impossible to be found or to be
constructed. Finite structures have attracted less focus and even fewer studies pro-
posed measurements to verify the analytical results. Thus, the goal of the present
thesis is the investigation of the propagation constants in ﬁnite periodic double struc-
tures. The theoretical solution is presented and simulations and measurements are
conducted to support it.

2
Introduction
1.2
Previous works
Brillouin [Bri53] studies the wave propagation in one, two and three-dimensional peri-
odic lattices where he establishes that the solution is given by employing the so called
Floquet’s theorem regarding periodic diﬀerential equations. Gupta [Gup70] and Cre-
mer [Cre73] investigate the case of an inﬁnite periodically simply supported beam.
Both of them arrive to the same conclusion that there exist discrete purely propaga-
tion and attenuation zones. Their ﬁndings conﬁrm the theory that attenuation zones
occur only if the propagation constant is purely real.
Mead [Mea70] describes the
behaviour of an inﬁnite beam with periodic simple supports and rotational springs to
suppress the rotation. He also states that there exist alternating propagation (pass
bands) and attenuation (stop bands) zones. In his model, he observes that the lower
bounding frequency of the ﬁrst pass band coincides with the fundamental frequency of
a single element of the periodic beam. Also, the upper bounding frequency of the same
passband corresponds to the fundamental frequency of a clamped-clamped ﬁnite beam
with the same mechanical characteristics as the inﬁnite one. Finally, he concludes
that it is possible to identify the bounding frequencies of the nth pass band since they
correspond to the nth resonance of a single element and of a ﬁnite clamped-clamped
beam. Mead and Yaman [DJM91], although they do not study a periodically supported
sandwich plate with regards to the propagation constants, they however mention that
there are propagation and attenuation zones. They also comment that there are six
resonances in the ﬁrst propagation zone. The number of the resonances coincides with
the number of the periodic elements of the structure. Nordborg [Nor98] investigates
the propagation constants in a ﬁnite length rail. A rail is assumed to be an example of
a periodic structure since it rests on equally spaced sleepers. His ﬁndings conclude that
two propagation constants exist. Both of them were complex at low frequencies while
at higher frequencies they become either purely imaginary (pass bands) or purely real
(stop bands). When it comes to periodic double structures, Brunskog [Bru03] derives
an analytical solution by determining the dispersion equation of the system.
Not many studies are found in literature that focus on measuring the propagation
constants of a ﬁnite structure. Ohlrich [Ohl78] successfully measures the propagation
constants of a ﬁnite structure. His model consists of a beam and nine transversally
equally positioned beams. The structure is excited longitudinally at one end. In order
to eliminate the reﬂections of the other end, he places a sand termination. He then
evaluates the propagation constants by measuring the receptance of several neigh-
boring elements. His ﬁndings matches with the theory. Sonti and Narayana [VRS06]
measure a simply supported beam consisting of 6 spans. The beam is excited ﬂexurally
by a hammer. Their idea is to measure the mobility of two immediate bays to obtain
the propagation constant. They speculate that their results would be more accurate
for the attenuation zones than the propagation because of the resonance behaviour at
these regions. However, their experimental data do not match the analytical.

1.3 Scope of the study
3
1.3
Scope of the study
The present thesis aims to study the determination of propagation constants in ﬁnite
double structures. The study is focused on ﬁnite double beams periodically joined by
springs. Simulations and experimental measurements are conducted to validate the
results of the analysis.
The present thesis has the following structure.
First, an overview of the theoreti-
cal background is given. Then, the analytical solution for several models is derived,
supplemented by the description of the numerical model. Simulations and measure-
ments are conducted to validate the theoretical models. Their results are evaluated
and analyzed. Finally, the thesis conclusions are presented.

2.1
Bending waves in beams
Bending wave propagation in solids is of great importance. Their signiﬁcance arises
from the fact that they are found very frequently in elastic structures such as beams
and plates, and also because they radiate sound more easily than other types of waves
i.e. longitudinal waves.
The main characteristic of bending or ﬂexural waves is that the wave motion is found
to be perpendicular to the surface of the solid and to the direction of the propagation
(Figure 2.1).

2.3
Image Method
The image method is frequently used in acoustics to describe the reﬂections a sound
wave is subject to. Depending on the number and type of obstacles as well as the dis-
tance from the source, the image sources are selected to account for those reﬂections
[Bru05]. However, this method is not commonly used in structural acoustics. Gunda
et al. in [RG95] solved the cases of a simply supported plate and a sliding supported
plate. In their paper, they transform a ﬁnite plate into an inﬁnite using an inﬁnite
set of images making the spatial FT possible. The boundary conditions are fulﬁlled
by selecting the correct sign for the external force. For a simply supported plate they
assigned a negative sign on the "mirrored" external force. Therefore, the displace-
ment and the bending moment become zero validating the simple support restrictions
(Section 2.1). For a sliding support, the "mirrored" force has the same sign as the
initial one satisfying the corresponding boundary conditions. Regarding clamped and
free end boundary conditions they claim that they are not possible to be determined
employing the image method. These types of boundary conditions involve a near-ﬁeld
term which cannot be represented. However, at high frequencies, this term seems to
be vanished and the free boundary condition can be described as a sliding support and
the clamped as a simple support.
Brunskog in [Bru05] employs the image method to deﬁne the solution for a clamped-
clamped beam. The image solution is now applicable for the case of a clamped support.
His idea is to substitute the clamped supports with simple supports while the "mir-
rored" external force retains the same sign as the initial one. In that way, the rotation
and displacement at boundaries become zero fulﬁlling the restrains a clamped support
establishes.

2.4
Periodicity (Floquet’s Principle)
Assume a periodic structure, that is an inﬁnite set of identical elements. Propaga-
tion of waves is obviously aﬀected by reﬂections. The presence of reﬂections implies
that the transmitted wave is attenuated at some frequencies while for the rest of the
frequencies, the propagating wave is transmitted unattenuated over the periodic ele-
ments. These set of frequencies are called pass bands while the rest, where attenuation
is occurred, are known as stop bands.
Floquet’s principle is used to deﬁne the stop bands and pass bands in periodic struc-
tures. Brillouin in [Bri53] describes the Floquet theorem. He assumes that the 1D
Helmholtz equation for a ﬁeld variable ψ is

Assuming that the propagation constant is a complex number, it can be written as
µ= µre+iµi. The variables µre and µi are known as the attenuation constant and the

respectively, of a wave.
Having established that, it is now appropriate to mention the conditions that de-

• Re {µ} = 0 & Im {µ} ̸= nπ, (n= 0, 1, 2, ...): the wave propagates unattenuated

• Re {µ} ̸= 0 & Im {µ} = nπ: the wave decays (stop band),
• Re {µ} ̸= 0 & Im {µ} ̸= nπ: the wave decays while it propagates.

Chapter 3
Method
A combination of the methods and theory described in Chapter 2 is proposed to study
the present cases. First, the problem is described by means of the bending wave equa-
tion, where the intrinsic features of the media are deﬁned. For ﬁnite beams, the image
solution method is applied to expand the domain of the problem from a part-space to
the entire space. Second, the periodicities due to the reaction forces of the supports
and the external source are found by means of the Floquet’s principle, and they are
included in the bending wave equation (BWE). Then, the Fourier transformation, the
Poisson sum formula, and the inverse Fourier transformation are applied to the new
BWE. Consequently, the displacement equation for the speciﬁc problem is found. Fi-
nally, free wave-modes are obtained by omitting the external excitation which allows
to fully deﬁne the periodic wave motion.
In this chapter, the algorithm used to perform simulations for the studied cases is
presented and discussed.

3.2
Numerical Model
The algorithm for determining the wavenumber kx is originally developed by Brunskog
[Bru03] in Matlab. Some alterations are made in order to ﬁt the analytical dispersion
equations, to improve the computational time, and to get the propagation constant
curves. The algorithm is based on the fact that non-trivial solutions are found when
the determinant of the dispersion equation is zero.
This numerical model approach is used because it is presumed that it can be more
eﬃcient for obtaining the propagation constant for any type of periodic structure, since
the traditional way would be to derive the solution by means of contour integration as
in [Mac80], [Mac79], [Nor98].
3.2.1
Description and Settings
Figure 3.11 illustrates the numerical model used and its functions. The main script is
called main, then it ﬂows to the core function findZ, where the functions disp and
isZ are called. The scripts for the mentioned functions are presented in Appendix A
where one can ﬁnd the Matlab coding of the algorithm. As it is known, any algorithm
can be susceptible to errors, commonly known as false positives.
Figure 3.11: Numerical model block diagram.
As seen in Figure 3.12), the parameters and features of the media are loaded, within
the principal script main, such as the length l, width b, height h, Young’s modulus E,
density ρ, cross section area A, area moment of inertia I and the spring constant K.
The frequency span is deﬁned with a frequency step of 5Hz for all the studied cases. In
Figure 3.12 it is seen that the outcome of findZ are the kx that are the solution to the
determinant equation. However, if more than one kx is found there is the possibility
that they are the same even if they have diﬀerent decimals. To avoid this repetition,

user to set the maximum diﬀerence for these kx to be regarded as diﬀerent solutions
of the dispersion equation. The tolerance factor in the algorithm is tol = 1 × 10−4.
Consequently, false positives are also avoided.

Third, the kxm,n candidates are selected one by one as input for the function disp
(red boxes in Figure 3.14) which contains the determinant equation. This equation
includes the inﬁnite summation(s) that are truncated from −100 to 100. In order for a
kx to be evaluated, it should satisfy the initial condition mentioned in Section 3.1 that
is the determinant to be zero. In other words, there are M × N diﬀerent candidates
for kx that probably result into cm,n=0, so the C matrix is of M × N size which is
always a square matrix since M = N (right part of Figure 3.15). Consequently, the
disp function yields a matrix C of cm,n values for the determinant after inserting each
kxm,n.
Fourth, by comparing the sign of adjacent cm,n, it is possible to detect where/if the
determinant equation went through zero. This means that the suitable kx lies between
these two kxm,n. This procedure is known as zero-crossing detection and is done by isZ
(green box in Figure 3.14). The zero-crossing detection deﬁnes the closer or adjacent
point of a mathematical signal/function sign change from being positive to negative or
viceversa, meaning that the mathematical function has crossed the axis (zero value).

Figure 3.15: disp block diagram. In the left kxm,n is the input of the function and

An example of how the function isZ is executed is given in the following. Using the
matrix C, an element is used as a reference i.e. c2,2 which is compared with c1,2,
c3,2, c2,1, c2,3, c3,3, c3,1, c1,3, and c1,1. When a sign change is detected, the algorithm
assigns the logic true value 1 to both c, the reference and the compared one. Next,
the following c element is used as a reference element, which in this case is c2,3 and
once again it is compared with its adjacent. In general, the reference element ﬁrst
goes all along the row m = 2 from column n = 2 to n = (N −1). Then the next row
m = 3 is evaluated through n = 2 : (N −1). At the end, the "reference-rows" are from
m = 2 : (M −1).
Having all the adjacent elements of the complex array C(M, N) compared within isZ,
the whole searching area M × N is evaluated. The output of isZ forms the matrices
RZ and IZ (Figure 3.14), which elements are either 0 or 1, as mentioned before. In
addition, isZ compares separately the RZ = Real(C) and IZ = Imag(C). In order
for the determinant condition to be fulﬁlled both RZ and IZ must be equal to zero
(RZ. ∗IZ). Then, the ﬁnal value for kx is evaluated.
After evaluating the vector ZZ and while being in a loop-type, findZ calls itself until
it > 0 as seen in Figures 3.11 & 3.14. It is the depth factor, it, that forces findZ
to be repeated. After the ﬁrst call, a new C(M, N) (C1) matrix is created within
the original (C0). For example, if within the initial C0 a sign change is detected,
the code selects the two kx candidates where the sign change occurred and within the
second repetition of findZ the new, smaller searching area C1, deﬁned by these two
kx, is discretized into N −1 segments. These repetitions of the findZ function aim to
improve the accuracy of the algorithm, avoid false positives, and make the code less
time consuming.
Finally, when it ≤0, findZ reinserts the calculated kx into disp in order to check
which of them result into a closer to zero value for the determinant. This step is very
useful because it allows the user to deﬁne threshold for the maximum determinant
==PAGE:51==

value to be evaluated according to the predeﬁned determinant condition, so to be
picked as a ﬁnal kx it needs to be under the threshold. Especially in a two dimensional
algorithm, a sign change in C1 may occur for more than two kx candidates. Following
this procedure, false positives are attempted to be eliminated.
It is important to mention that it is possible to get kx in a simpler and faster way
by calculating the purely propagation and non-propagation zones. Since kx ∈C it
is rewritten as kx = kr + iki. Thus, the purely propagation zones are found when
kr = 0, and the purely non-propagation zones when ki = 0 or ki = π. This alteration
in the procedure is considered as the one dimensional (1D) algorithm which is used to
determine the resonances of the system and is explained in the consequent sections.

not assuming internal damping allowing to state that, the resonances of the system

having a resolution of N = 15000 means that isZ has to compare 15000 values, which

Chapter 5
Conclusions &
Recommendations for future
works
The objective of this thesis focuses on the estimation of the propagation constant of
ﬁnite periodic double structures. First, an overview of the related literature and the
theoretical background are given. The method used to obtain the analytical solution
of certain cases is presented while the numerical model is interpreted. Simulations
and experimental measurements are conducted. Finally, conclusions are drawn and
presented in this section.
The image method is deployed to evaluate the propagation constant in ﬁnite structures.
The advantage of using this method is its capacity to convert a ﬁnite structure to an
inﬁnite which allows the appliance of the spatial Fourier Transform and Inverse Fourier
Transform. This transformation is achieved through the replacement of the supports
found at the ends with their corresponding pair. The majority of the support pairs
is predeﬁned. The theoretical equation for the displacement and thus the velocity,
acceleration and the frequency response of the model, is ﬁnally determined. Therefore,
the image method is recommended to be employed in structural acoustics at least for
the established types of supports.
In general, the algorithm is proved to be not that eﬃcient as assumed. The code is not
==PAGE:84==
66
Conclusions & Recommendations for future works
functioning properly for all cases, especially due to the false positives it returns at high
frequencies. It is faster for inﬁnite cases because of the simplicity of the dispersion
equation. To improve the algorithm, a less time consuming zero-crossing function is
needed, while the resolution N needs to be estimated beforehand to get results in
a more straight forward way. It is noticed that the same resolution factor does not
apply for each frequency. A speciﬁc N value is required for each µ; in other words, in
case there are more than one kx or µ per frequency, each propagation constant needs
a diﬀerent resolution factor to be properly evaluated. A one dimensional algorithm
is developed to reduce the computational time and increase the accuracy at higher
frequencies; however, the zero crossing detector needs to be improved to increase the
eﬃciency at that range. The compromise of having readable results and decreasing
the computational time in the 1D algorithm is accomplished by using a low N and
high it. It is also observed that the algorithm is precise in calculating the resonances
at low and mid frequencies.
Throughout the present thesis many cases are investigated and comparisons are made.
The inﬂuence of the periodic length and the stiﬀness of the springs for an inﬁnite double
beam are studied. Simulations show that stiﬀer springs approach the behaviour of
simple supports by projecting clear pass bands and stop bands. Evident stop bands are
found when the periodic length of the structure is decreased. The attenuation provided
by the structure is larger for a shorter periodic length irrelevant of the spring constant.
As for ﬁnite media, only the resonances of the system are expected to propagate. Due
to the nature of the resonances and the frequency step used in the algorithm, it is
not possible to illustrate this behavior. A single beam case and the corresponding
double beam are compared.
As expected, the resonances for the double beam are
shifted to higher frequencies. It is also found that more attenuation is provided by a
double structure. The inﬂuence of an extra periodic segment is evaluated. Because
of the increased stiﬀness, the resonances are shifted to higher frequencies while the
attenuation is increased.
Finally, it may be outlined that for inﬁnite cases, stiﬀer
springs show similar behavior to simple supports and shorter periodic length provide
more attenuation. Regarding ﬁnite systems, double structures oﬀer more attenuation
than the respective single, while the same conclusion holds for adding periodicity to
the system.
Estimating the propagation constant through experimental measurements using the
current setup is not feasible since the response of the beam is greatly inﬂuenced by the
reﬂections from the ends. The propagation constant is evaluated using the numerical
model. According to the simulations it is only possible to estimate the purely non
propagation zones when Im(µ)=0. Nonetheless, the analytical model considers the
reﬂections in the media while measuring the propagation constant experimentally is
more complicated. Sand terminations at the end of the beams applied in previous
works, are not recommended for measuring µ in ﬁnite cases, since the sand terminations
force the model to behave as an inﬁnite media.
==PAGE:85==
67
Future investigations should focus on estimating µ experimentally by exciting the
beams with either a sound pressure or a line force, while getting the data right before
the ﬁrst reﬂection of the signal. This would lead to well deﬁne the excitation for the
system; the fact that a point force (i.e. shaker) is used is opposed to the analytical
solution, where a pressure is assumed to excite the beams. It is suggested that stiﬀer
springs and more periodic supports are adopted to clearly demonstrate the pass bands
and stop bands. However, by increasing the number of the supports, the dispersion
equation becomes more complex and leads to an increased computational time.
