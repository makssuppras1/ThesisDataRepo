Abstract
1
Introduction
There are over 350 small marinas in Denmark[5]. These facilities, along with 300 odd natu-
ral harbors are overwhelmingly responsible for hosting the 57000+ small pleasure boats that
ply around in the seas of this country[7]. A larger port will often host a complex setup of a
powerful radar, automatic identification systems (AIS) and a dedicated operator to track and
monitor ships within the harbor. Many of these marinas however are usually operated by local
municipalities, sailing clubs, fishermen associations and other volunteer cooperatives. Needless
to say, many of these bodies often lack the resources to host such sophisticated facilities. Thus,
there most certainly is an incentive to encourage the development of a low cost, unmanned,
automated solution that can take up the task of a radar and it’s operator. Such a solution must
run with minimum human intervention, be capable of identifying the kinds of vessels entering or
leaving port (surfers/canoes/motor boats etc) and run reliably at any time of the day, through
the year. It must also be durable and hardy enough to withstand the rough Danish weather, and
marine conditions. Additionally, in keeping with the Danish love for sustainability, a solution
that avoids or minimizes the use of the electric grid would be ideal.
Thus, the ideal solution would fulfill the following requirements.
1. Low cost: This solution should be affordable for small harbors and marinas. A regular
harbor radar tower can cost hundreds of thousands of kroner, and may often require a
dedicated operator.
2. Unmanned: A dedicated radar operator for a marina hosting a handful of boats is quite
a silly proposition. The ideal solution will run with no human intervention.
3. Useful: The solution, while unmanned, must produce usable information that can be uti-
lized to take concrete actions. An ideal solution would publish true and useful information
on an easy to access web portal.
4. Durable: The solution will be mounted in a harbor, and be subject to marine air, strong
winds and gale.
5. Sustainable: The device must avoid making use of the electrical grid, as much as possible.
This project will aim to develop such a solution for a marina in Aarhus, Denmark. The marina
has one inlet that serves as the entry and exit, and is about 60 meters wide at the mouth.

Problem statement and description
This section will aim to condense the problem into a handful of actionable research questions
that can be tackled with far greater clarity. Additionally, existing solutions will be explored,
with the aim of providing greater credibility and novelty to this solution.
2.1
Problem statement
As stated in the introduction, Denmark is home to over 350 small Marinas. Many of these
marinas are operated either by municipalities, or a collection of sailors or fishermen, who finance
these Marinas via annual mooring fees or donations. These marinas thus are non-profit and not
commercial, and therefore often have extremely limited funds[2]. Existing harbor monitoring
systems are often extremely expensive and sophisticated, and often offer capabilities far in excess
of the needs of these marinas, which often only host somewhere between 10-100 moorings. This
thesis aims to create a novel solution that is significantly cheaper than existing solutions while
providing similar levels of functionality, while also having as small an environmental footprint as
possible. This thesis will revolve around the use of algorithms capable of identifying a range of
vessels ranging from surfers to large boats, and the creation of a device capable of collecting data
for, and running said algorithms. The first part of this thesis will revolve around the creation
of said device. As stated before, this section will be kept brief, compared to the second section,
which will revolve around the design of effective algorithms.

==PAGE:6==
2.2
Problem Description
As stated before, many of Denmark’s marinas are community run with a small number of
moorings.
Staff on these marinas, if present, is also often very limited, and is restricted to
mostly volunteers doing administrative work. There is thus a pressing need for a solution that
could continuously track vessels moving in and out of port. Such a solution, if accurate enough,
could be used to estimate the number of unused moorings in a marina at any given time. This
information could be hosted on a highly accessible medium, for example, a web portal, and could
prove especially useful to visitors looking to rent a temporary mooring, as the alternative would
involve calling the marina during business hours for this information.
2.3
Existing Solutions
Large ports and harbors often have sophisticated monitoring systems that combine a variety of
sensors and technologies to accurately monitor vessels entering and leaving port. For example,
the "Navi-Harbour Vessel Traffic Management Solution" by Wärstilä[18] makes use of a range of
radars, automatic identification systems (AISs), marine radios and CCTV cameras to efficiently
manage ports. While the cost of this solution varies on a case by case basis, it’s fair to assume it’s
extremely expensive and completely out of reach (and impractical) for a tiny marina. At the very
least, most existing solutions work by combining radars and automatic identification systems.
AISs rely on transceiver systems mounted on vessels that broadcast their position, speed, heading
and other navigational details. While this system is quite powerful, according to Danish Maritime
Authority, vessels smaller than 15 meters in length, or those with a tonnage lower than 300 tonnes
are not required to mount AIS transceivers[3].
This immediately disqualifies most pleasure
crafts, and thus, this solution is not usable. Moreover, most existing solutions rely on a present
operations specialist to make use of radar and AIS data to track vessels, something that’s out
of reach for small marinas on tight budgets.
Figure 4: An operations specialist making use of RADAR and AIS systems
Existing solutions that cater to small marinas often make use of CCTV cameras to monitor
port premises. It may thus be possible to visually confirm the occupancy of moorings. Some
vendors add a "smart" aspect to this solution by making use of computer vision techniques
to automatically determine occupancy. However, such solutions often perform quite poorly at
5
==PAGE:7==
night or in poor weather. These solutions also often require multiple cameras, which can often
lead to cascading costs. Moreover, the filming of people in public, where they are identifiable
may require their consent in accordance with the guidelines laid down by GDPR. Furthermore,
night vision/infrared cameras are not only expensive, but are also currently subject to export
restrictions due to their use in warfare (more about it in the coming section). Thus, there is
certainly room for improving existing solutions, to better optimize them for the sake of small
ports.
2.4
An overview of the proposed solution
The proposed solution will make use of a mmWave radar paired to a Raspberry Pi 5. This
setup will be powered by solar panels and a battery, thus minimizing the utilization of the
electrical grid. This setup will function as an edge device that makes use of computer vision
based algorithms to accurately track and identify vessels. The use of a radar will further lead
to greater night time/poor weather performance, and lower costs. The total cost of the setup
would be less than 5000 Kr, and if sustainability is not a concern, the cost can further be
shaved down to about 2500 Kr by eliminating infrastructure revolving around solar panels and
batteries. This marks a significant improvement over the tens of thousands of Kr that existing off
the shelf solutions may retail for. The proposed solution also sidesteps other considerations like
potentially violating GDPR regulations. The use of FMCW radars further means that the radar
will produce point clouds, allowing for the use of computer vision algorithms. This is achieved
by converting radar point clouds into usable images, by "smudging" them. The "smudging"
process works by collecting radar point clouds over a 3 second window, overlaying them onto
each other, and displaying them on a 2D image. The two dimensions of the image represent the
X-Z plane of the point clouds, with the Y axis controlling the size of individual points in the
image (with closer points being larger). Furthermore, points are color coded based on the time
the radar receives them, with earlier points taking on a red color, and later points taking on a
green color. This allows for visualising motion, and encodes all the spatial-temporal information
produced by the radar.
Figure 5: The radar smudge produced for a moving boat
Finally, this smudge will be subject to a classification algorithm that will be used to determine
the direction, and if possible, the type of the vessel too.

3.1
Detection related hardware
An ideal solution would work effectively throughout the day, in the dark, and in bad weather.
This immediately disqualifies the use of cameras which would be useless in the dark, but allows
for the use of more exotic sensors, like infrared cameras, RADARs and LIDARs. Each of the
three solutions were explored, however, a radar based solution would be the most effective for
the current use-case. A greater insight into this reasoning is explored below.
1. Infrared Cameras: Infrared cameras work by detecting infrared light instead of visible
light like regular cameras.
Said cameras can pick up on natural infrared light, or an
external infrared beam can be used to light up the surroundings like a flashlight, and
boost detection range. Infrared cameras often have significant military uses, like in UAVs
and face strong import and export restrictions. According to the laws set under ITAR
(International Traffic in Arms Regulation), there are restrictions to importing uncooled
microbolometer-based cameras with a framerate of 9 frames per second and a maximum
resolution of 640 x 480[9]. This is quite a low frame-rate and resolution, and cameras
even of this resolution are quite expensive, going for upwards of 6000 kroner[4]. Moreover,
according to the formula governing signal attenuation γ = kfa, where γ is the specific
attenuation, f is the signal frequency and k and a are coefficients reflecting water content
and fog density, the attenuation of infrared light which is of a fairly high frequency is quite
high, making performance in fog or rain quite poor. This can be compensated by with a
stronger infrared searchlight, but at this point, this would lead to cost overruns, and would
be a very energy intensive solution, making it quite unfeasible.
2. LIDAR: LIDAR (Light detection and ranging) makes use of laser pulses of near Infrared
(NIR) light and measures the time taken for the pulses to be reflected back. By knowing the
time difference between a pulse and it’s return, it’s possible for LIDARs to very accurately
measure the distance of objects. Additionally, by making use of several laser pulses, it’s
possible to gain a very detailed point cloud that can reveal shapes and features of the object
being detected. However, LIDARs like IR cameras operate at near infrared frequencies,
making their performance in poor weather quite unreliable. Additionally, they are more
expensive than RADARs. A Lightwave LW20/C with a detection range of 100 meters
costs upwards of 2600 kroner[10]. A corresponding radar with a similar detection range
will cost a little over 1500 kroner[11]. There will be a tradeoff in fidelity and resolution,
but for the purposes of detecting vessels, the tradeoff is acceptable.
7
==PAGE:9==
Figure 6: LIDARs can produce very detailed point clouds
3. RADAR: There are multiple possible classes of radars that can be used. Most radars
are quite expensive, however the two that do meet the criteria of being cost effective are
Doppler effect radars and Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW) radars.
(a) Doppler effect radars: Doppler effect radars emit pulses of constant frequency, and
measure the time taken for the pulse to return. This can allow the radar to determine
the distance of the object. Additionally, by measuring the increase or decrease in
the frequency of the incoming wave (Due to the Doppler effect), it is also possible
to determine the velocity of an object relative to the radar[1]. Pulse Doppler radars
are usually less complex and much cheaper on the market. An HB100 pulse Doppler
radar can easily be found for less than 100Kr. However, these radars correspondingly
produce much simpler outputs consisting of simple sine waves that can be processed
and analysed to glean speed and distance information. It would however be very
difficult to discern between different types of objects (are we seeing a yacht or a
surfer?).
(b) Frequency Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW) Radars: FMCW radars
differ from Doppler Radars in that they emit a "chirp" of a continuously increasing
frequency.
8
==PAGE:10==
Figure 7: Frequency vs time diagram of a FMCW chirp.
By analyzing the frequency of the reflected signal, it’s possible to know exactly how
long ago the original signal was emitted, thus allowing for a range estimation. Ad-
ditionally, by the virtue of having several antennas, and advanced signal processing,
FMCW radars are capable of producing "point clouds" instead of simple sine waves,
allowing for a much richer pool of information. FMCW radars are often more ex-
pensive, with the TI IWR6843AOP retailing for anywhere between 1500-2000 Kr,
however, the much richer information provided by this radar makes up for it for this
project, in my opinion.

3.4
Power Supply Related Hardware
One of the secondary aims of this project is to minimize or avoid making use of the grid al-
together. Towards this end, A setup that consists of a solar panel that charges a battery was
decided on. The battery is connected to a battery charger too, for the sake of reliability, however,
the use of the battery charger is only reserved for emergencies, when the battery voltage drops
below a certain level. The parts decided upon for this bit are listed below
1. A 200W solar panel setup:
The 2 100W solar panels in question are supplied by
Renogy, and is designed primarily for off grid applications, like motor homes and boats.
Figure 9: A single 100W solar panel.
11
==PAGE:13==
2. A 30A charge controller: A solar panel may generate voltages in excess of what a
battery might safely be able to handle. A charge controller safeguards batteries from such
damage. The charge controller is supplied by Renogy too.
3. A 50 Ah 12V LiFePO4: A Raspberry Pi 5 can consume about 7 watts of power, and
the radar consumes about 5 watts of power. A 50 Ah battery supplying power at 12V can
supply 600Wh of power. Thus, it can run the given 12W setup for 50 hours in ideal cases.
The battery thus is more than enough for providing power through the night. The battery
is supplied by Li-Time.
4. A battery charger: While the aim of the project is to minimize, or avoid making use
of the electrical grid, it would be prudent to nonetheless connect the battery to the grid
via a charger, in case the solar panel setup isn’t enough. This connection occurs via a
voltage controlled relay, which only allows the battery to be charged by the charger after
the voltage drops to a certain level. This ensures that the setup prioritizes solar energy
over the grid, and only makes use of the grid when the battery level is dangerously low.
The charger in question is provided by Noiefevo.
5. Other miscellaneous parts: These consist of a 12V to 5V converter to allow the battery
to power the Raspberry Pi, and various other cables, wires and adapters to connect the
various subsystems.

4.1
Data Collection and Visualization
There were several key challenges encountered while setting up a suitable data pipeline to begin
training models on.
1. The nature of this project means that there is no existing dataset that can be referred to.
This means that the data must be sourced from scratch.
2. Another challenge is the decision to use a radar as the primary sensing device.
The
radar produces sparse point clouds which are often very difficult to interpret in isolation.
The radar thus must be coupled with another device, like a camera, for the sake of data
annotation and verification.
3. Thirdly, the data must be collected from a marina, with all the sensitive electronics involved
being exposed to the elements. This means that a suitable housing for the raspberry pi,
the radar and the third device must be created too.
4. Finally, this setup will be mounted in Aarhus. Thus, a way to allow for remote operations
and control must be devised.
4.1.1
Proposed solution
Given the absence of any existing dataset to work with, it was necessary to create a new setup
with the sole primary aim of data collection. Given the difficulty of interpreting radar data, a
decision was made to combine the radar with an auxiliary sensor, namely, a camera. The two
sensors would be connected to a Raspberry pi. The final "commercial" version of this thesis
would exclude the camera and solely consist of a radar and a Raspberry pi. Data collection

Software solution for the data collection module The software component of the data collection

1. Remote operation and maintenance of the deployed device
2. Synchronization and pairing of radar data with visual annotations

Remote Access and Control: Given that the data collection module would be deployed

was achieved using Tailscale, a mesh VPN that allowed for seamless SSH and file transfer to the
Raspberry Pi, even behind NAT or firewalls. This eliminated the need for port forwarding or
static IPs.
Data Synchronization and Annotation Pipeline: Radar data alone is difficult to annotate
due to its abstract nature. Hence, a YOLOv8 object detection model was used on live webcam
input to detect and classify boats. When a vessel is detected in the camera feed, a 3-second
radar recording is triggered. This temporal window captures the radar data associated with the
detected object.
Radar point clouds are streamed either locally (via serial UART from /dev/ttyUSB1) or remotely
(via a TCP server running on the Raspberry Pi). This stream is continuously parsed, and points
are accumulated until the 3-second window is complete.
Radar Smudging Algorithm: Each radar "smudge" is a 2D image representation of a 3D
point cloud accumulated over a short time interval. The smudge uses the XZ plane (where X is
lateral movement and Z is forward range) to form the image’s spatial axes. The vertical height
(Y) of points is encoded as point size or intensity, while the time of arrival of points is mapped to
color. Older points are typically rendered in blue and newer ones in red, using a linear red-blue
gradient.
The full pipeline converts a sparse, temporally-rich 3D radar point cloud into an interpretable
RGB image.
Example of Smudging Algorithm (XZ plane):
• Points are normalized to fit within a 500x500 pixel canvas.
• Each point is projected onto the XZ plane and plotted.
• The Y dimension determines the size or brightness of the point.
• The color of each point is based on its relative time in the smudge interval, using a Red-Blue
gradient to indicate freshness.

MobileNet
MobileNetV2 is a lightweight CNN architecture developed by Google for performing inference
on lightweight, resource constrained devices (like cellphones, IoT devices etc) [13]. It builds
upon the original MobileNet by incorporating "residual connections" and "linear bottlenecks",
methods that allow significantly lower computational cost without a massive loss of performance.
Architecture Overview: MobileNetV2 uses "depthwise separable convolutions", a technique
that speeds up standard convolution by splitting it into 2 steps:
• Depthwise convolution — applies a single filter per input channel.
• Pointwise convolution (1x1) — combines outputs from the depthwise step.
Additionally, MobileNetV2 introduces:
• Linear bottlenecks — prevent non-linearities from destroying too much information.

==PAGE:21==
• Inverted residuals — promote feature reuse and efficient gradient flow.
Motivation for Use: MobileNets were designed for use in ARM CPUs, like those on the Rasp-
berry Pi, making it an attractive algorithm off the bat. The model’s computational efficiency and
smaller model size make it especially attractive for deployment on embedded platforms like the
Raspberry Pi, where computational and memory budgets are limited. Despite being optimized
for speed, MobileNetV2, in theory, should deliver fair classification accuracy.

EfficientNetB0
EfficientNetB0 is the baseline architecture in the EfficientNet family, introduced by Tan and Le
[17]. It employs a novel "compound scaling method" that uniformly scales network depth, width,
and input resolution, creating a balanced and efficient architecture for image classification.
Architecture Overview: EfficientNetB0 builds upon "mobile inverted bottleneck convolution

power of the overall network by allowing for the recalibration of the importance of individual
channels in the overall network. The compound scaling approach improves model accuracy and
efficiency compared to conventional scaling methods that only increase one dimension at a time.
Motivation for Use:
EfficientNetB0, in theory, usually offers better accuracy than Mo-
bileNetV2 at a comparable or slightly higher computational cost, keeping it viable for the
Raspberry Pi 5. Its efficiency and high accuracy should make it well-suited for radar smudge

Architecture Overview: ResNet50 consists of 50 layers organized into residual blocks that

substantially deeper networks than was previously possible.[6]

NASNetMobile
NASNetMobile is a convolutional neural network architecture discovered via Neural Architecture
Search (NAS), proposed by Zoph et al. [19]. It automates the design of network architectures
by optimizing a search space for high accuracy and efficiency.
Architecture Overview: NASNetMobile builds on the idea of using a search controller to find
the optimal combination of convolutional cells that can be stacked to create a scalable network.
The architecture consists of two types of cells:
• Normal cells — preserve the spatial dimensions while extracting features.
• Reduction cells — reduce the spatial dimensions while increasing feature depth.
These cells are repeated multiple times to form the full architecture, allowing flexible scaling
depending on resource constraints.
Key Features:
• Neural Architecture Search — automates architecture design, leading to optimized
feature extraction.
• Efficient building blocks — use separable convolutions and cell structures to reduce
computation.
• Mobile-friendly design — optimized for deployment on devices with limited resources,
such as smartphones and embedded platforms.
Motivation for Use: NASNetMobile aims to combine the high accuracy of architectures dis-
covered by NAS with efficiency suitable for mobile and embedded systems. It represents a bal-
ance between model complexity and performance, potentially outperforming manually designed
lightweight models.

Architecture Overview: DenseNet121 consists of dense blocks where each layer receives input

lution and pooling to reduce spatial dimensions.
Key Features:
• Dense connectivity pattern enabling efficient feature propagation.
• Bottleneck layers and compression in transition layers to reduce model complexity.
• Improved accuracy with fewer parameters compared to similarly deep networks.
Motivation for Use: DenseNet121’s efficient parameter utilization and strong gradient flow
make it a powerful model for image classification tasks, especially when training data is limited,
as is common with radar smudge images. Its depth and connectivity patterns often yield better
feature extraction capabilities than more lightweight models.

Addendum: Potential cause for the poor left classification perfor-
mance
An overall observed trend in each of the 6 models has been a consistent decline in performance
when detecting vessels traveling to the left, with respect to the radar frame. This can potentially
be explained by the following factors.

to the right (leaving port), than vessels moving left (entering port). The 20-30 meter dis-
tance between the average vessel entering port vs leaving port proves especially challenging
for the radar to easily detect, especially for smaller vessels with poor radar observability
(canoes, windsurfers etc).

Possible solutions:
1. Increase the dataset size for leftward moving vessels. This might help models find
more nuances in detecting vessels moving in that direction. However, the utility of this
solution is question given the markedly poorer radar signature of vessels moving in that
direction.
2. Use a more powerful radar.
The current IWR6843AOP radar is generally suited
towards shorter range detections. The radar has an antenna on package (AOP), implying
the radar is a part of the circuitry. This means that the radar module itself quite small.
A potential replacement radar would be the IWR6843ISK, a similar radar which however
has an external, more powerful antenna allowing for much better observability at range.
It also only slightly more expensive than the existing AOP radar, leading to a minor cost
increase of 100 kr.
3. Mount two devices at the two sides of the inlet. Each device would be optimized
for detecting vessels in one direction. A single unified interface on top of these two devices
could then be used to track vessels moving in both directions.

5
Conclusions and future work
5.1
Conclusion
As it stands, using standalone FMCW radars as the sole tracking device for marine applications
shows some promise. The accuracy of such devices and models however appears to be quite
subject to the size of the vessel and distance from the shore. Thus, it may be prudent to re-
explore this premise with more powerful radars and larger datasets. Additionally, while not
explored in detail in this paper, radar smudge data proved to be quite poor in identifying and
distinguishing between various individual classes of vessels (sailboat/large ship/canoe or surfer).
It was observed that similar types of vessels could sometimes have very different radar signatures,
for example, a wind surfer with the sailed raised up has a much stronger signature than a one
with the sail hoisted down. An FMCW based tracking solution thus might be best suited to
simply keeping track of the number of vessels entering or leaving port, with applications needing
more information being restricted to camera-based solutions.
5.2
Future work
Future work would involve around the casing for the device, the datasets, the radars and potential
other models.
1. Changes to the casing: The current casing does not incorporate any radome, a protective
casing for the radar antenna. It would be prudent to design and produce one, with a case
designed to incorporate it. Suitable materials for radomes often have low conductivity, low
di-electric loss, and a stable di-electric constants. Possible materials include polycarbonate,
teflon, quartz etc. Additionally, the radar produces significant heat, so a better cooling
solution would be a welcome addition.
2. Further exploring sustainability: The idea of using solar panels and batteries for
an off-grid solution was turned down at the present harbor, due to the small size of the
mounting tower. This solution could be further explored in other harbours.
3. Increasing the dataset size: Collecting additional annotated data, possibly a few thou-
sand data points for each class might lead to much better performance.
4. Using other radars: Using more purpose made long range radars might be a better
decision, leading to richer, more detailed datasets and radar smudges.
5. Testing out more models: Given the current framework and data pipelines, it should
be very easy to incorporate more models to find something that better fits the given niche.
