Abstract
In 2020, new limits for sulphur content in the fuel used on board ships were set by the International
Maritime Organization. This regulation aims at reducing air pollution from this industry mainly due to the
emission of SOx. To comply with this regulation, most ship-owners combined the use of heavy fuel oil with a
scrubber. The water resulting from the scrubbing process is a complex effluent and the main contaminants
found so far in scrubber water are metals, PAHs, SOx and NOx in their aqueous forms. The presence of
these contaminants and their impact on parameters such as pH are suspected to lead to damaging effects
on the marine environment. It is difficult to fully assess the potential ecotoxicity of scrubber water on
the marine environment given the fact that scrubbers are only recently installed onboard and because the
chemical composition of scrubber water varies from one ship to another according to the engine load and
to the fuel used. This thesis aims at 1) assessing the baseline toxicity of the scrubber water samples; 2)
testing the Toxicity Identification Evaluation approach to identify the substance and/or the parameter
responsible for the largest fraction of the toxicity; 3) addressing the role of metals in the toxicity of the
samples. The samples were received from two container ships: Scandia Seaways and Ephesus Seaways
operating in the Baltic/North Seas and in the Mediterranean/Adriatic Seas respectively, both equipped
with open-loop scrubbers. The toxicity of scrubber water was assessed by performing a bioluminescence
inhibition test on bacteria A. fischeri and algal growth inhibition test on S. costatum. None of the samples
received from Ephesus Seaways (Italy) caused inhibition towards bacteria whereas EC50 estimates range
from 15% to 50% for the samples received from Scandia Seaways (Netherlands) and the scrubber discharge
water sampled at sea was found more toxic (EC50=15% [7;66]95%) than the one sampled when the ship
was in the harbour (EC50=50% [37;86]95%). The scrubber inlet water sampled at sea for both ships did
not cause algal growth inhibition while EC50 were found equal to 43-94% for the rest of the samples.
Moreover, the scrubber water discharged in the harbour by Scandia Seaways was significantly more toxic
than the inlet water with EC50 values being respectively 43% [39;47]95% and 61%[54;70]95%. The samples
analysed in this report are more toxic towards marine algae than previously measured in other studies. It
was found that pH is a driver for toxicity for the scrubber water discharged at sea for a test concentration
of 90%. The aeration test only caused a significant decrease in toxicity for one out of the four samples
tested. No significant decrease in toxicity towards algae was observed after EDTA addition and filtration.
The metal analysis of the samples highlighted elevated concentrations of vanadium (28-122 µg/L) and
nickel (8-28 µg/L) in comparison with the natural seawater (0.75 µg/L for V and 11.77 µg/L for Ni).
These concentrations are correlated to the engine load. The metal concentrations measured in the samples
are at least one order of magnitude lower than the concentrations found to cause negative effects towards
test organisms in previous studies. Therefore, the toxicity of each metal considered individually might add
up to an overall higher toxicity of the effluent or metals might not be the components causing the toxicity
of scrubber water. The risk characterization ratios estimated for a standard OECD Commercial harbour
ranged from 0.003-1.38 for V, Ni and Cu, thus, depending on the scenario considered, the metal input
from scrubber water in a harbour could cause a risk for the marine environment. These results enhance
the need for further investigation of the chemical composition of scrubber water with a potential focus on
the role of PAHs. In fact, there is limited research on the identification of a substance and/or a parameter
influencing the toxicity of scrubber water. Also, carrying the ecotoxicity tests on a third organism (e.g.
crustaceans) would provide a better overview of the impact of scrubber water on the marine environment.

Background and Aim
The shipping sector is a global industry, in fact, 80% of the goods produced worldwide are delivered
by ships (UNCTAD, 2022). The shipping industry is believed to be responsible for approximately 13% of
global human-made Sulphur Oxides (SOx) emissions (Sui et al., 2020). Sulphur oxides have both health
and environmental adverse effects (irritation of the respiratory system and contribution to acid rains).
Therefore, there is an increasing concern for reducing air pollution from this sector especially focusing on
SOx emissions. The International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL) set
limits for sulphur emissions from the shipping industry within Annex VI, Regulation 14. MARPOL Annex
VI specifically regulates the use of heavy fuel oil because of its high sulphur content.
In 2020, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) set new limits for sulphur content in the fuel
used on board ships to 0.5% m/m (mass by mass) outside Emission Control Areas (ECA) and 0.1% m/m
in ECAs such as the Baltic and North Seas (International Maritime Organization). While the use of low-
sulphur fuel is heavily encouraged, the regulation also allows the use of an Exhaust Gas Cleaning System
(EGCS) combined with heavy fuel oil to comply with the new standards in terms of sulphur emissions in
the atmosphere. Among EGCS, wet scrubbers are currently the most widely used. In 2021, the Marine
Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) published updated guidelines based on MARPOL Annex
VI describing the framework for the use of Exhaust Gas Cleaning Systems concerning the new regulation
(Maritime Environment Protection Committee, 2021).
A wet scrubber is an onboard device aimed at cleaning the exhaust gas from the engine by spraying
the gas with alkaline water. Scrubbers can operate in open-loop (OL), closed-loop (CL), or hybrid (both
OL and CL) mode. In an OL system, seawater is sprayed over the exhaust gas and discharged directly
into the sea. A CL system uses freshwater mixed with an alkaline substance such as caustic soda before
spraying it and recirculating it through the system while a small fraction of the water is discharged to the
sea (bleed-off) (Jalkanen et al., 2021).
The current regulation set by MARPOL Annex VI mainly focuses on the sulphur content of the fuel
used, but there is no harmonized and globally approved protocol for assessing and controlling scrubber
water discharge (Hassellöv et al., 2020) although substances like SOx, Nitrous Oxides (NOx), Polycyclic
Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAH) and metals have been found into scrubber discharge water (Marin-Enriquez
et al., 2023). Multiple studies are raising concern about the potential toxicity of scrubber water discharge
and the possibility of transforming an air pollution issue into a marine pollution one (Kjølholt et al.,
2012). The ecotoxicity of scrubber water discharge has been assessed using the Whole Effluent Toxicity
approach because it enables to consider the combined effects of the substances present in the scrubber
water discharge. Moreover, the Toxicity Identification Evaluation approach is used to analyse the response
of test organisms in order to detect the presence of toxicants before and after the samples are modified
physically and/or chemically (Bureau Veritas, 2020).
This thesis aims to assess the potential ecotoxicity of scrubber water emissions to the marine envi-
ronment. To do so, the following objectives are defined:
• Review of regulation and up-to-date knowledge about ecotoxicity of scrubber discharge water.
• Assessment of the baseline toxicity of the samples.
Page 1 of 42
==PAGE:13==
• Testing the Toxicity Identification Evaluation approach to identify the substance and/or parameter
responsible for the largest fraction of the toxicity of the samples.
• Chemical analysis of the samples to measure metal concentrations.
• Focus on the potential impacts of metal input due to scrubber water in an enclosed area.
First of all, a literature review will be carried out to present regulations in terms of sulphur emissions,
scrubbers, guidelines for scrubber discharge water emissions and up-to-date knowledge about the ecotoxi-
city of scrubber discharge water. Then, the baseline toxicity of the samples will be determined by Whole
Effluent Toxicity tests. This will be done by carrying ecotoxicity tests on two different organisms: bacteria
(Biotox luminescence inhibition, Aliivibrio fischeri, (ISO 11348 , 2008)) and algae (marine algal growth
inhibition, Skeletonema costatum (ISO 10253 , 2016)). Additionally, the Toxicity Identification Evaluation
approach will be used to identify the substance and/or parameter responsible for the largest fraction of the
toxicity of the scrubber water. Moreover, a chemical analysis of the samples will be carried to focus on the
role of metals in the overall toxicity of the samples. Finally, a model based on Lunde Hermansson et al.
(2023) will be used to estimate the potential impact of metals input due to scrubber water discharge in a
harbour. A Risk Characterisation Ratio (RCR) will be estimated based on the Predicted No Effect Con-
centration (PNEC) for a range of metals and on Predicted Environmental Concentration (PEC) obtained
from literature review and data generated in this report.

2
Introduction
2.1
Sulphur Oxides emissions
Sulphur Oxides (SOx) are gases mainly emitted during the combustion of coal and oil because of
the high sulphur content of those materials. SOx are also emitted naturally into the atmosphere during
volcanic eruptions and wildfires. Among SOx, Sulphur Dioxide (SO2) is the most commonly found in the
atmosphere. It is estimated that the shipping industry is contributing, through the burning of heavy fuel
oil, to approximately 13% of global human-made SOx emissions (Sui et al., 2020). Those emissions have
adverse environmental effects. In fact, SO2 is a gas reacting with water and dioxygen once emitted in the
air, contributing to acid deposition in the form of rain, snow, cloud water or particles in the atmosphere.
These acid depositions are more commonly known as acid rains.
Acid depositions impact ecosystems
and have damaging effects on aquatic lakes and river species (Likens and Butler, 2018). Acidification
of water bodies has important consequences on the food web of aquatic ecosystems. For instance, loss
of fish diversity has been observed in areas with a high atmospheric concentration of SOx. Moreover,
acid depositions are responsible for mobilizing aluminium ions in soil and toxic forms of aluminium such
as Al3+ can be found in water streams causing fish mortality (Likens and Butler, 2018). Finally, acid
depositions are impacting the soil chemistry and thus have consequences on terrestrial ecosystems like
forests (Likens and Butler, 2018). SOx emissions into the atmosphere also have adverse health effects such
as lung malfunction and heart disease (US EPA, 2023a).
2.2
Regulation of Sulphur Oxides emissions
Given the objective of improving air quality and reducing emissions globally, the shipping industry
has to comply with limits on the sulphur content of the fuel used onboard. In 1997, The International
Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships (MARPOL) defined the first limit in terms of sulphur
content for the fuel used onboard ships. MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 14 set the sulphur content to a
maximum of 4.5% mass by mass (MARPOL Convention, 1997). The International Maritime Organization
(IMO) has since lowered this limit to 3.5% m/m in 2012 and to 0.5% m/m in 2020 (International Maritime
Organization). By implementing this regulation as early as 2020, the IMO estimates that SOx emissions
can be reduced by 77% in comparison to implementing it in 2025 (Corbett et al., 2016). This stricter limit
will also help increase the health conditions of the population living close to coastal areas. Some regions
have been defined as Emission Control Areas (ECA), as shown in Figure 1 and are subjected to stricter
regulation in terms of SOx emissions to reduce air pollution from ships. The North Sea, the Baltic Sea and
the United States Caribbean area are examples among the list of ECAs designated by MARPOL Annex
VI Regulation 14. Within these regions, the sulphur content of the fuel used on board has been limited
to 0.1%m/m since 2015 (International Maritime Organization).

2.3
Recommendations to reduce sulphur emissions
As mentioned in MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 14, shipowners have two main options to comply
with the new sulphur cap: switch to a new type of fuel or combine the use of heavy fuel oil with an Exhaust
Gas Cleaning System (EGCS). Among alternative fuels, marine gas oil; low-sulphur fuel oil and liquefied
natural gas are the most commonly used.
Marine gas oil is obtained from distillates, which are the components of crude oil that evaporate during
distillation and are condensed from the gas phase to the liquid phase. Marine gas oil has a relatively low
sulphur content of approximately 0.1%, thus can comply with the requirements (Viray-Mendoza, 2021).
However, marine gas oil is currently more expensive than heavy fuel oil.
Low-sulphur fuel oil is usually obtained by blending low-sulphur residues with high-sulphur heavy
fuel oil. Low-sulphur fuel oil complies with the regulation in terms of sulfur emissions, however, the use
of such fuel onboard might require operational changes because the fuel has different physical properties
(e.g lower viscosity) than currently used heavy fuel oil (Alfa Laval, 2018).
Finally, liquefied natural gas is probably the most promising alternative fuel for the shipping industry.
Its very low sulphur content complies with regulation within ECA and it is cheaper than marine gas oil
(Pavlenko et al., 2020). However, the cost of transport and storage (both onshore and onboard) are pointed
out as the main disadvantages of liquefied natural gas (Alfa Laval, 2018). Moreover, it is important to
keep in mind that liquefied natural gas contains a high fraction of methane. Even though it complies with
regulations for sulphur, methane emissions associated with its combustion could be problematic regarding
greenhouse gases emissions (Pavlenko et al., 2020).
Despite the numerous alternative fuels available, combining the use of heavy fuel oil with an EGCS
is the most popular option among shipowners to comply with the regulation. This might be because 99%
of ships use conventional fuel (DNV). In fact, this option is attractive because heavy fuel oil is well-known
and cheap. Moreover, minor operational changes are required to install an EGCS on board (Alfa Laval,
2018).

2.4
Exhaust Gas Cleaning System
When operating in OL mode, the scrubbing process relies on the natural alkalinity of seawater to
remove SOx from the exhaust gas. In fact, seawater contains carbonates and bicarbonate ions capable of
reacting with hydrogen ions, buffering change in pH. The chemical reactions between SOx and seawater
produce acidic ions H+. Even though this surplus of acidity is buffered by seawater’s alkalinity (Kjølholt
et al., 2012), the pH of scrubber discharge water is generally lower than the pH of the inlet water (Ytreberg
et al., 2020).
The water flow rate needed for a scrubber operating in OL mode mainly depends on the targeted
sulphur removal rate, the seawater’s temperature (the lower the seawater temperature, the higher SO2
solubility), salinity and alkalinity (Hassellöv et al., 2020). However, an average seawater discharge rate
of 45 m3*MWh−1 (EGCSA (2012), Lloyds’ Register (2012)) has been defined as a basis for the EGCS
guidelines discharge criteria published by the MEPC (Hassellöv et al., 2020).
2.4.2
Closed-loop mode
Figure 3: Scrubber operating in closed-loop mode, (Lloyds’ Register, 2012)
In 2022, it is estimated that only 1% of ships equipped with scrubbers use closed-loop scrubbers
(DNV). When a scrubber operates in CL mode, freshwater is used to wash the exhaust gas. Sodium
Hydroxide (NaOH) is added to the freshwater and helps adjust the pH to ensure efficient SOx removal.
Seawater is only used to cool down the water circulating within the closed-loop wash water. Then, the
wash water is treated and recirculates through the system. A small portion of the wash water, called
bleed-off, is discharged back at sea to keep the concentration of sodium sulphate ions steady. This is
to avoid early degradation of the system because of the formation of sodium sulphate crystals (Lloyds’
Register, 2012). Flocculation and cyclones are the main technologies used onboard to treat the bleed-off
before discharging it back to the sea. Both of these technologies aim at removing solid particles from
the bleed-off stream. Flocculation involves chemical reactions so the particles aggregate together to form
larger masses of solid particles, the water is then filtered. Cyclones use centrifugal separation to remove
solid particles from the bleed-off stream (Kjølholt et al., 2012).

2.7
Ecotoxicity of scrubber discharge water
2.7
Ecotoxicity of scrubber discharge water
Multiple studies have been focusing on assessing ecotoxicity of scrubber discharge water using Whole
Effluent Toxicity methods. For example, Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023) carried Whole Effluent Toxicity
tests using both OL and CL samples on bacteria (A. fischeri), algae (P. tricornutum) and copepods
(A.tonsa). A concentration of 80% of scrubber discharge water was found to cause 20% inhibition of the
bioluminescence of A.fischeri for OL samples, while the bioluminescence inhibition range was found to be
55-95% for CL samples. CL samples caused algal growth inhibition in the range of 87-100% while growth
stimulation was observed for some OL samples probably due to the high iron concentration found in these
samples. Finally, all copepods (A. tonsa) were found dead after 48 hours of exposure to a concentration
of 50% of CL discharge water. However, significant differences were observed for OL samples depending
on the sampling point (before and after scrubbing), but the average mortality was always below 70% for
both undiluted and 50% diluted samples.
Koski et al. (2017) focused on the influence of metals considering the ecological effects of scrubber
water on coastal plankton. Scrubber discharge water samples were taken onboard a ship sailing in the
North Sea and equipped with an OL scrubber. It was found that a concentration of 30% of discharge
water caused 100% mortality among adult copepods A. tonsa during 24-hour acute toxicity tests. The
concentration-response curve of the mortality of adult copepods with increasing scrubber water concen-
tration was correlated with a decrease in pH and an increase in both vanadium and nickel concentrations.
The main outcome of this study is the surprisingly low Lethal Concentration 50% (LC50) values obtained
compared to single-substance-based ecotoxicity tests performed in other studies. For instance, the nickel
concentration causing 100% mortality of adult copepods was 24 µg/L while the EC50 of nickel after 48
hours of exposure on A. tonsa measured by (Zhou et al., 2016) was 0,149-0,249 mg/L. Similar observations
were made for vanadium with a concentration of 173 µg/L causing 100% growth inhibition of Rodomonas
sp. measured by Koski et al. (2017), while Miramand and Unsal (1978) measured the growth inhibition
of three benthic and three phytoplanktonic species after 9 days of exposure to vanadium and found LC50
values in the range of 0,5-65 mg/L.
The concentrations causing sub-lethal and lethal effects are multiple times lower than the one previ-
ously measured for a single substance (Koski et al., 2017), therefore there is a need for determining the
critical factor responsible for causing the toxicity of scrubber water, and the potential synergistic effects
of metals and PAHs present in the effluent.
Conclusions drawn by Hassellöv et al. (2020) are similar to the study carried out by Koski et al. (2017).
Strong negative responses of test organisms have been measured carrying Whole Effluent Toxicity tests
at exposure concentrations well below the previously known LC50 values for single-compound exposure.
For instance, the LC50 of naphthalene on copepod Oithona davisae was found to be 7.2 mg/L, whereas
naphthalene concentrations found in scrubber discharge water do not exceed 9.47 µg/L (Hassellöv et al.,
2020). Additionally, Hassellöv et al. (2020) mentions the need to focus on characterizing PAHs present in
the effluent to better estimate and understand the ecotoxicity of scrubber discharge water. In fact, most
studies focused on the 16 PAHs listed as priority pollutants by the US EPA, but there might be other
PAHs present in the discharge water contributing to the overall toxicity of the effluent. This emphasizes
the need for quantitative estimates of the contribution of each substance/group of substances to the overall
ecotoxicity of the effluent.

Ecotoxicity assessment methods
2.8
Ecotoxicity assessment methods
2.8.1
Whole Effluent Toxicity
Whole Effluent Assessment has been acknowledged by OSPAR and defined as a method to better
understand the combined effects of known and unknown components of effluents (OSPAR Commission,
2007). Within the Whole Effluent Assessment method, Whole Effluent Toxicity is a tool to assess the overall
toxicity of an aqueous sample based on the response of a test organism exposed to this sample. Whole
Effluent Toxicity is often used to determine the acute toxicity of a given effluent to freshwater and marine
organisms. It is especially useful when considering complex mixtures such as scrubber discharge water
because of the numerous substances present in the effluent. Whole Effluent Toxicity differs from substance-
based approach. In fact, the single-substance approach is based on analytical and chemical characterization
of the samples and therefore only considers substances that can be identified. The overall toxicity of the
mixture is estimated by the addition of each of the individual ecotoxicity of the substances identified in
the mixture (Nielsen et al., 2023). However, it is acknowledged that some potentially harmful constituents
of wastewater or surface water cannot be identified (OSPAR Commission, 2007). This could lead to an
underestimation of the overall toxicity of the effluent as unknown constituents might be responsible for a
large fraction of the observed adverse effects. Moreover, the overall toxicity of a complex mixture might
also depend on reactions among the constituents.
2.8.2
Toxicity Identification Evaluation
Toxicity Identification Evaluation (TIE) is a method aiming at identifying the substance or group of
substances responsible for the largest fraction of the toxicity of a given effluent. This is done by analysing
the response of test organisms exposed to a sample before and after the sample is modified.

sampling locations: harbour (with auxiliary engines running) and at sea (with main engines running);
and in two different sampling points: inlet and outlet of the scrubber. It has to be noted that no water
treatment unit was installed on board the ships. For each sampling location, 1L of inlet water and 3L of
discharge water were collected.
3.2
Growth medium for bacterial and algal tests
The medium used for the algal growth inhibition test was prepared weekly following ISO 10253 (2016).
This medium consists of a mixture of natural seawater (approximate salinity 30 ±5‰) and nutrient-rich
stock solutions. The detail concerning the preparation of the medium is presented in Appendix A.2. The
pH of the medium was measured in the range 7.7-7.9 and the salinity was measured in the range 28-33‰.
These parameters also fulfill the requirements stated by ISO 11348 (2008) therefore it was decided to use
the same medium for the Biotox luminescence inhibition test with A. fischeri.
3.3
Test organisms and culturing conditions
Bacteria A. fischeri used for the bioluminescence inhibition assay were purchased from EBPI (Ontario,
Canada). The samples were freeze-dried and stored at -20◦C. The marine algae S. costatum were obtained
from the Norwegian Institute for Water Research, (NIVA, Oslo, Norway). The algae were kept in a 100
mL glass bottle containing filtered natural seawater (obtained from DTU Aqua, Technical University of
Denmark) enriched with nutrients according to (ISO 10253 , 2016). The solution was continuously aerated
with atmospheric air (with an AQUA NOVA NA-200 air pump) to prevent sedimentation of the algae and
to ensure CO2 transfer to the aqueous phase. The glass bottle was kept under constant light provided by
fluorescent tubes with an intensity of 57-123 (Mean=87.9; SD=26.1) µmol.s−1.m−2 measured by LI-189
Quantum/Radiometer/Photometer at a temperature of 15◦C. Once a week, a small volume of algae was
sampled from the glass bottle, transferred into a 20 mL scintillation glass vial (Meridian Biotechnologies)
and mixed with growth medium prepared according to (ISO 10253 , 2016). The vial was then placed on a
shaker table (MTS 2/4, IKA®, Germany) at 250 rpm, under constant light with a light intensity measured
within the range of 57-123 (Mean=87.9; SD=26.1) µmol.s−1.m−2 and steady temperature of 20± 2◦C.
The content of the vial was used over the week and growth medium was added every two days to ensure
exponential algal growth.

3.5
Biotox luminescence inhibition test with marine bacteria A. fischeri
The methods used for the Biotox luminescence inhibition test with A. fischeri were modified from
ISO 11348 (2008). The high-performance luminescence system Junior LB 9509 Portable Luminator was
used for the measurement of luminescence of freeze-dried A. Fischeri.
The medium used for this assay is prepared as described in Section 3.2, its pH ranges from 7.7-7.9
and its salinity is within the range of 28-33‰. The freeze-dried bacteria were reconstituted by adding 12
mL of medium prior to test start and stored at 20◦C for approximately 30 minutes. The test solutions
were prepared from samples of scrubber water.
For each sample, 5 different test concentrations were
selected and the test solutions were prepared in 10 mL volumetric flasks labeled with the concentration,
the sampling point and the location. The test concentrations are expressed as a percentage of scrubber
water in seawater (volume/volume). The final test concentrations were defined using a factor of 2 between
each concentration value, therefore, the final test concentrations were: 1.8%; 3.75%; 7.5%; 15%, and 30%
of scrubber water. The concentration of scrubber water in the 10 mL volumetric flasks was twice as high
as the concentration tested because an equal volume of test solution and bacteria suspension was mixed
into the test vials prior to measurement. Thus, the corresponding volume of scrubber water added to the
10 mL volumetric flasks was respectively: 0.375 mL; 0.75 mL; 1.5 mL; 3 mL, and 6 mL. Twelve 0.3 mL
glass vials (Thermo ScientificTM SureSTART TM) were placed in a rack: 2 vials for each test concentration
and 2 vials for control containing only medium and bacteria suspension. First of all, 100 µL of bacteria
suspension was added to the vials. The luminescence was measured and recorded as luminescence at t=0
min. Then, 100 µL of each test solution (medium for the control) was added to the corresponding vials
starting with the control vials and proceeding in increasing order of concentration. The luminescence was
measured by inserting the vials in the Junior LB 9509 Portable Luminator after 15 and 30 minutes of
exposure. The data treatment for the measured luminescence values is to be found in Section 3.10 below.

3.8
Metal analysis
A chemical analysis of the samples was carried out using Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spec-
trometry (ICP MS). The metals analysed were vanadium, chromium (Cr), nickel, copper, zinc, arsenic,
cadmium (Cd) and lead. The samples analysed were only those originating from the outlet of the scrubber,
both at sea and in the harbour for the two ships. Moreover, natural seawater (from DTU Aqua, Technical
University of Denmark) was also analysed. The concentrations of metals found in the natural seawater
were considered as background concentrations and were used as a basis to compare with the concentrations
measured in the four other samples from scrubber discharge water. The samples were acidified with 65%
HNO3 to a final concentration of 2% and with 37% HCl to a final concentration of 1% before analysis.
Every sample was analysed three times. Collision mode with Helium was used for all the metals to reduce
potential interference with other ions, however, a no-gas flow mode was used for cadmium and lead.

5
Discussion
The tests performed during this thesis are following recommendations for Whole Effluent Toxicity
assessment and are therefore part of the tests recommended by OSPAR for assessing acute toxicity of
complex mixtures (OSPAR Commission, 2007). Toxicity Identification Evaluation tests are also performed
using US EPA (2007) as a reference. Thus, the acute toxicity of scrubber discharge water is evaluated
using bacteria (A. Fischeri) and marine algae (S. costatum) as test organisms. It is usual to use algae
and crustaceans as test organisms to get a screening of the toxicity of the effluent over three different
trophic levels. However, given the duration of this thesis, it was not possible to carry out experiments
using crustaceans so bacteria were used instead. The Whole Effluent Toxicity tests are performed following
International Organization of Standardization guidelines ISO 11348 (2008) and ISO 10253 (2016). These
standard protocols are originally designed for single-substance toxicity but are recommended by OSPAR
Commission (2007) for Whole Effluent Toxicity. Using standardized protocols allows for easier comparison
with previous experiments performed in other studies.
5.1
Biotox luminescence inhibition test with A. fischeri
In practice, the Biotox test is a convenient screening tool as it enables to generate data on a short
time and it is also very easy to set up. That is the reason why it was used as a first baseline toxicity test
to get an overview of the toxicity of the samples received.
No concentration-response curve could be fitted to the results of the Biotox luminescence test per-
formed with the samples from Ephesus Seaways (Italy). Inhibition levels reached are below 20% for all
test concentrations. The maximum inhibition of the bioluminescence of A. fischeri was also measured
to 20% for a concentration of 80% of scrubber discharge water sampled in the Baltic and North Seas by
Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023). Concentration-response curves could be fitted to the results of the Biotox lu-
minescence test performed with the samples from Scandia Seaways (Netherlands). A significant difference
in luminescence inhibition is observed between the scrubber water discharged at sea and in the harbour
with EC50 values being respectively 15%[7;66]95% and 50%[37;86]95% after 30 minutes of exposure. The
difference in toxicity between the scrubber water discharged at sea and in the harbour could be due to the
difference in engine load when the ship is at berth and when it is sailing at sea. Indeed, it is likely that
concentrations of potential toxicants increase as the engine load is higher. It is for example the case for
some metals (Kjølholt et al., 2012). EC10 estimates are within the range of 1-18% for the four samples
tested in this report while Magnusson and Granberg (2022) found an EC10=23% for OL scrubber discharge
water sampled in multiple locations (Mediterranean Sea, Atlantic Ocean, English Channel) and no EC50
value was reached with test concentrations ranging from 0-40% of scrubber water. Based on EC10 values,
the samples tested in this report are more toxic towards A. fischeri than what was previously found by
Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023) and Magnusson and Granberg (2022).
5.2
Marine algal growth inhibition test with S. costatum
The marine algal growth inhibition test was performed in order to assess the toxicity of the samples
on two different organisms. Marine algae were chosen among other test organisms (crustaceans or fish)
because of the rather short time needed to generate data (3 days).
Page 34 of 42
==PAGE:46==
5.3
Toxicity Identification Evaluation tests
The lowest estimated EC10 values are 13% and 27% of scrubber water discharged in the harbour for
Ephesus Seaways (Italy) and Scandia Seaways (Netherlands) respectively. These results are consistent
with the EC10 obtained after exposing P. tricornutum and D. tertiolecta to scrubber water for 96 hours
which were respectively 34 % and 15% (Magnusson and Granberg (2022)). The lowest estimated EC50
values are 66% [56;78]95% for the scrubber water discharged at sea by Ephesus Seaways (Italy) and 43%
[39;47]95% for the scrubber water discharged in the harbour by Scandia Seaways (Netherlands). Concerning
the scrubber water sampled in the harbour for Scandia Seaways, there is a significant increase in toxicity
between the inlet (EC50= 61% [54;70]95%) and the outlet (EC50= 43% [39;47]95%). It is an interesting
finding strengthening the hypothesis that the scrubbing process is responsible for the ecotoxicity of the
water discharged. Moreover, even though a harbour can be considered as an enclosed area compared to the
open ocean this difference in toxicity enhances the fact that the chemical composition of the inlet water
is different from the outlet water. However, no such result was found for the scrubber inlet and outlet
water sampled in the harbour for Ephesus Seaways (Italy). No full concentration-response curve could
be fitted to the scrubber inlet water sampled at sea in this report (see Figures 11d and 13a). This aligns
with the algal growth inhibition test on S. costatum performed by DHI (2021). In fact, no toxicity was
reported for the scrubber inlet water even for the highest concentration tested, therefore the LOEC value
was set to > 910 mL/L. These results strengthen the hypothesis that the inlet water of the scrubber is not
toxic towards algae as its chemical composition is assumed to be close to natural seawater. Regarding the
scrubber discharge water, DHI (2021) reported a LOEC value equal to 625 mL/L and both EC25 and EC50
> 910 mL/L. Moreover, Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023) reported that a concentration of 80% of OL scrubber
discharge water caused 25-40% of algal growth inhibition (P. tricornutum). Based on those studies, it can
be concluded that the samples analysed in this report are more toxic towards marine algae S. costatum
than what was previously measured. Indeed, four out of the eight samples analysed have caused 50% of
algal growth inhibition for concentrations below 80%.
5.3
Toxicity Identification Evaluation tests
The tests based on Toxicity Identification Evaluation are done by performing the marine algal in-
hibition test with S. costatum using the raw samples. The choice of using S. costatum rather than A.
fischeri was made because no toxicity was observed for the samples received from Ephesus Seaways (Italy)
towards bacteria for the baseline toxicity test. Thus, marine algae were assumed to be more sensitive than
bacteria. For each manipulation, the marine algal growth inhibition is estimated for replicates of the un-
treated sample and replicates of the modified sample according to the TIE test performed (pH, filtration,
aeration, and EDTA addition test). This allows for a comparison between the baseline toxicity (untreated
samples) and the outcome of the TIE test and therefore reveals the potential removal of toxicants after
the manipulation.
5.3.1
pH adjustment
No statistical difference in inhibition was observed for a test concentration of 50% of scrubber water
discharged at sea for both ships. The pH values of the test solutions before treatment were within the
range of 6-6.4 and 7-8.8 after being adjusted (Table8). Khan et al. (1998) and Taraldsvik and Myklestad
(2000) both analysed the influence of pH on the growth of S. costatum and measured the highest division
Page 35 of 42
==PAGE:47==
5.3
Toxicity Identification Evaluation tests
rates for pH ranging from 7.0-8.5 and 6.5-8.5 respectively. Therefore, the pH values measured in this report
before and after treatment are very close to the range of optimal pH for the growth of S. costatum defined
in those two previous studies. It can thus be assumed that for a test concentration of 50% of scrubber
water discharged at sea, the pH is not the main driving factor of the toxicity of the samples tested.
However, a significant difference in algal growth inhibition was observed for a test concentration of
90% of scrubber water discharged at sea for both ships. In fact, algal growth inhibition decreased from
100% before treatment to 59% after pH adjustment, resulting in a decrease of approximately 40% in
toxicity for the scrubber water discharged at sea by Scandia Seaways (Netherlands). Therefore, pH is
one of the driving factors of the toxicity for this sample, however, algal growth inhibition of 59% is still
measured after pH adjustment meaning that pH is not the only parameter responsible for the toxicity of
the sample. Regarding the scrubber discharged water sampled at sea from Ephesus Seaways (Italy), no
inhibition was measured after pH adjustment thus resulting in a complete removal of toxic effects towards
S. costatum.
These results highlight the role of pH on the toxicity of the scrubber discharge water at sea for
which pH values (2.93-3.38) were approximately twice as low as the pH of the scrubber inlet water (7.56-
7.76) for the raw samples. The results obtained with the pH adjustment test are consistent with the
EGCS Guidelines (Maritime Environment Protection Committee, 2021) recommending that the pH of the
scrubber discharge water should be at least 6.5 and that a difference of two pH units is allowed between
the inlet and the outlet scrubber water during manoeuvring and transit (see Table 3). Moreover, Koski
et al. (2017) also found that pH was a driving factor in the mortality of adults A. tonsa with mortality
rates reaching 100% for pH below 6.
5.3.2
Filtration
Globally, the filtration procedure did not have any influence on the algal growth inhibition of S.
costatum for six out of the eight tests performed. This emphasizes the fact that solid particles present in
the scrubber discharge water are not the main driving factor of the toxicity of the whole effluent. However,
a statistically significant increase in growth inhibition was observed after filtration at a concentration of
50% of scrubber discharge water for two samples: scrubber outlet water in the harbour (Ephesus Seaways,
Italy) and scrubber outlet water at sea (Scandia Seaways, Netherlands). This increase in inhibition after
treatment could not be explained.
PAHs are likely present in significant concentrations in the samples received. Indeed, it has been the
case for multiple previous studies on scrubber water (Magnusson et al. (2018); EGCSA and Euroshore
(2018) and Ytreberg et al. (2020)). Moreover, Kjølholt et al. (2012) found that more than 50% of the PAH
are estimated to be associated with particles (>0.45µm) and Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023) also noticed
PAH in particulate form, especially in CL discharge water. These findings align with the fact that high
molecular weight PAHs (more than 3 rings) are more hydrophobic and thus less water soluble than low
molecular weight PAHs (2 to 3 rings) (Linders et al., 2019). The filtration procedure performed in this
report did not have a significant influence on the algal growth inhibition of S. costatum, thus in this
case, the role of PAHs in the particulate form is probably not a driving factor for toxicity. Nevertheless,
inhibitions ranging from 51-100% (Table 9) are measured before and after filtration of the samples for
a test concentration of 90% of scrubber water. This highlights the need for further investigation of the
Page 36 of 42
==PAGE:48==
5.3
Toxicity Identification Evaluation tests
toxicity of the samples. It could for instance be interesting to perform a C18 solid phase extraction on the
filtered test solutions to determine whether the dissolve fraction of PAHs could be responsible for a share
of the toxicity of the effluent. This type of test has been performed by Brunn Rasmussen and Fyhn Poulsen
(2020) on produced water, and non-polar organic compounds such as PAHs were found to be significant
drivers of the toxicity of the effluent tested.
5.3.3
Aeration
It has to be noted that no aeration test was performed with a test concentration of 90% of scrubber
water. Indeed, it was assumed that the samples had already been naturally aerated when used during the
previous manipulations for baseline toxicity and other TIE steps. No statistical difference in algal growth
inhibition was observed for the scrubber outlet water discharged both at sea and in the harbour for the
Ephesus Seaways samples for a test concentration of 50% of scrubber water. This observation could mean
that volatile organic compounds potentially present in the samples are not a driving factor for the toxicity
of the scrubber discharge water sampled onboard Ephesus Seaways. A significant decrease in algal growth
inhibition was measured for the scrubber outlet water discharged at sea by Scandia Seaways (from 21% to
12%). Some volatile or semi-volatile organic compounds present in the raw sample might be responsible
for this decrease in toxicity after aeration. However, the increase in algal growth inhibition measured after
aeration of the scrubber outlet water discharged in the harbour by Scandia Seaways (Netherlands) could
not be explained.
Regarding the compounds found in the scrubber water, PAHs are suspected to play a role in the
toxicity of the effluent and the concentration of PAHs should be lower than 50 µg/L in phenanthrene
equivalent according to Maritime Environment Protection Committee (2021). However, Mackay and Shiu
(1981) and Alaee et al. (1996) estimated Henry’s law constant for naphthalene, phenanthrene, fluorene
and pyrene within the range of 10−6 - 10−4 atm*m3*mol−1. Low molecular weight PAH (2 to 3 rings)
such as naphthalene, fluorene and phenanthrene have the highest Henry’s law constants and are therefore
expected to be more volatile than high molecular weight PAHs (more than 3 rings) such as pyrene in
aqueous solution. Thus, a fraction of PAHs could volatilize from the aqueous solution during aeration
and be responsible for a decrease in inhibition as observed for the scrubber outlet water discharged at
sea (Scandia Seaways, Netherlands). Nevertheless, further assessment of the PAHs present in the samples
could be useful to determine the fraction of PAH likely to volatilize during aeration.
5.3.4
EDTA addition test
The tolerance test with EDTA was performed to avoid potential toxic effects towards marine algae
caused by EDTA rather than the scrubber water. Both concentrations tested resulted in the absence of
algal growth inhibition. Globally, the toxicity of the samples tested at both 50% and 90% of scrubber
water was not changed by the addition of EDTA. However, a significant increase from no inhibition to
22% algal growth inhibition was measured after EDTA addition for the scrubber outlet water sampled
from Ephesus Seaways (Italy). This increase in inhibition could not be explained. The role of metals in
the toxicity of scrubber water will be further discussed in Section 5.4 below.

5.4

5.4.1
Concentrations of metals in the scrubber discharge water
The chemical analysis was carried out to get an overview of the metal concentrations measured in the
samples. Natural seawater was used as a basis for comparison with the scrubber water. The analysis was
therefore used to determine whether the metals measured originate from the scrubbing process. A similar
procedure was performed by Koski et al. (2017) with OL scrubber water collected from Magnolia Seaways
and seawater from Copenhagen harbour.
The chemical analysis revealed elevated concentrations of vanadium (28-122 µg/L), nickel (8.54-28.34
µg/L) and copper (2.4-5.8 µg/L) in the scrubber outlet water samples. These results align with multiple
previous chemical analyses performed with scrubber water. The concentrations measured by DHI (2021)
for the scrubber outlet water of 4 ships using an OL scrubber are respectively 54-160 µg/L for V; 9.7-34

the scrubber outlet water and the natural seawater were: 37-163 times higher for V; 1.5-2 times higher
for Ni and 1.1-2 times higher for Cu. Teuchies et al. (2020) also reported average concentrations of 200

Port of Antwerp, the Scheldt estuary and the North Sea. These concentrations are slightly higher than
the one measured after chemical analysis in this report (Table 12), this could be because one ship used by
Teuchies et al. (2020) was equipped with a hybrid scrubber and therefore running in CL mode in the Port
of Antwerp. In fact, higher concentrations of dissolved Ni and V were measured in CL scrubber discharge
water than in OL scrubber discharge water by Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023). The results of the chemical
analysis presented in Table 12 for lead (below detection limit - 0.25 µg/L), cadmium (below detection
limit - 0.12 µg/L) and zinc (11.22 µg/L in seawater and 8.54-22.2 µg/L in scrubber discharge water) also
align with the concentrations measured by Koski et al. (2017) being respectively: below detection limit
- 0.9 µg/L for Pb; below the detection limit for all the samples for Cd and 3-13 µg/L for Zn. Arsenic
was not detected in any of the samples considered in this report, while concentrations of As ranging from
1.3-1.7 µg/L for the harbour of Copenhagen and 0.8-2 µg/L for the scrubber outlet water were measured
by Koski et al. (2017). Lastly, Cr was found at a concentration of 5.98 µg/L in the natural seawater
used for this report (see Table 12). This is higher than any other previous measurements made by Koski
et al. (2017), DHI (2021) who found concentrations of Cr in the seawater were measured below 1 µg/L. Cr
concentration in the scrubber discharge water was 0.79-0.83 µg/L (12) and in the range of 1.6-2.2 µg/L
(Koski et al., 2017) and 2.1-2.9 µg/L (DHI, 2021).
Given the results of the chemical analysis (Table 12) and the outcome of previous studies (DHI
(2021); Koski et al. (2017); Teuchies et al. (2020); Marin-Enriquez et al. (2023)) it is quite clear that
vanadium and nickel are correlated to the scrubbing process. As shown in Table 2, Kjølholt et al. (2012)
highlighted the correlation between both the sulphur content of the fuel and the engine load to some metals
concentrations. For instance, for an engine running at a high load (respectively low load) with 2.2% of
sulphur fuel, the concentrations of vanadium and nickel were found equal to 180 µg/L (respectively 81
µg/L) and 43 µg/L (respectively 20 µg/L). A similar trend is observed with the outcome of the ICP-MS

5.4.2
Ecotoxicity of scrubber water and the role of metals
The lowest EC50 measured for S. costatum for the scrubber water discharged in the harbour is 43%
[39;47]95% for Scandia Seaways (Table 7). Given the metal concentrations measured by ICP-MS (Table
12), this corresponds to a concentration of 12.1 µg/L [11.0;13.2]95% of V and to 3.6 µg/L [3.3;4.0]95% of
Ni. The lowest EC50 measured for S. costatum for the scrubber water discharged at sea is 66% [56;78]95%
for Ephesus Seaways (Table 6), corresponding to a concentration of 55.2 µg/L [46.9;65.3]95% of V and 14.1
µg/L [12;16.7]95% of Ni. Miramand and Unsal (1978) measured algal growth inhibition after 9 days of
exposure to vanadium and found LC50 values within the range of 0.5-65 mg/L. These values differ by at
least one order of magnitude in comparison with the concentrations of vanadium measured in the scrubber
water causing 50% of algal growth inhibition. Similar results are obtained by Koski et al. (2017) with
concentrations of Ni causing 100 % mortality of A. tonsa being one order of magnitude lower than the
EC50 of nickel on A. tonsa measured after 48 hours by Zhou et al. (2016).
Moreover, Subramanian et al. (1980) tested the toxicity of copper and zinc on marine algae S. costatum
and measured algal growth for concentrations up to 25 µg/L for Cu and up to 75 µg/L for Zn after 48
hours of exposure. Moreover, Candra Pratiwi et al. (2019) estimated that 50% of growth inhibition was
observed for the marine algae S. costatum for a concentration of 1,046 mg/L of Cu. The concentrations of
Cu and Zn measured in the scrubber water samples by the ICP-MS analysis do not exceed 5,8 µg/L and
22,2 µg/L respectively (Table 12). These observations suggest that either the toxic effects of some metals
and other substances present in the scrubber water might add up or that vanadium, nickel, copper and
zinc are not the components causing the toxicity of the scrubber water.

5.4.3
Modeling of metal input in a harbour
The choice of using a seawater discharge rate for Ol scrubbers of 90 m3 ∗MWh−1 instead of 45
m3 ∗MWh−1 is based on the updated version of the Ship Traffic Emission Assessment Model (Lunde Her-
mansson et al., 2023). Also, the choice of using the OECD commercial harbour defined by Van De Plassche
and Van Der Aa (2004) can be discussed because this model is based on the dimensions of Rotterdam’s
harbour which is one of the biggest commercial harbours in Europe, thus the results obtained might not
be representative of other locations in Europe. Furthermore, considering the harbour as a closed system is
a strong assumption that could be discussed case by case depending on the location of the harbour taken
into account. In fact, even if a harbour is an enclosed area, some water exchange with the open sea always
exists. Therefore, the results obtained must be considered with caution.
As presented in Table 13, the concentrations of vanadium, nickel and copper in the harbour are
expected to increase after a year of scrubber water discharge. It has to be noted that the total concen-
trations of V, Ni and Cu expected after a year are higher for the harbour in Italy than for the one in
the Netherlands. This estimate is interesting given the fact that the harbour in the Netherlands is under
stricter regulation concerning the sulphur content of the fuel used (0.1% m/m International Maritime
Organization) than the harbour in Italy (0.5% m/m). In fact, as discussed previously, the presence of
compounds like vanadium and nickel in the scrubber water is directly linked to the sulphur content of the
fuel used onboard. Therefore, given the higher sulphur content of the fuel allowed in the Mediterranean
and Adriatic seas, it is relevant to expect higher concentrations of V and Ni due to the scrubbing process
in the Italian harbour than in the Dutch one.
According to Table 13, concentrations of V, Ni, Cu are expected to be within the range of 1.03-1.78
µg/L (21.5-77.6 kg/yr); 11.74-11.86 µg/L (0-6.3 kg/yr) and 0.12-0.13 µg/L (1.8-2.2 kg/yr) respectively.
It was defined by RIVM C.E. Smit (2012) than the maximum acceptable concentration for vanadium in
freshwater was 3 µg/L. Regarding nickel, European Commission (2006) states that above the threshold of
20 kg/year, the release of Ni in the environment must be reported by the operator of the facility. Finally,
the Environmental Quality Standards for Cu in marine waters is defined as 2.6 µg/L. None of these
thresholds are reached given the estimates of V, Ni and Cu concentrations based on the yearly discharge
rate of scrubber water.
The RCR estimates for vanadium and copper are found below one for the three different PEC values
used. However, the RCR value for Ni was above one for the PEC obtained from the modeling of scrubber
water metal input in an OECD Commercial harbour (Table 15). This can be due to the high concentration
of nickel measured in the natural seawater (11.77 µg/L) in comparison to the previous measurements of
Koski et al. (2017) (<0.79µg/L) and Linders et al. (2019) (0.1-9 µg/L). The natural seawater analysed
for this report was not sampled directly at sea but was distributed to DTU through a pipe system which
might be responsible for the elevated concentration of Ni. Moreover, PNEC values for Ni found in the
literature differ from one another: 8.6 µg/L (Van Sprang, 2015) and 1.53 µg/L (Källqvist, 2007) .
It is also important to highlight that the PEC estimates from Lunde Hermansson et al. (2023) are
obtained from the MAMPEC model. The PEC values estimated by the MAMPEC model are ten times
lower than the ones estimated in this report for V and Cu (Table 15). This might be because the harbour
has been considered a closed system in this report, but water exchange between the harbour and the open
ocean was taken into account by Lunde Hermansson et al. (2023).

5.5
Reflection on potential further assessment
Overall, these results must be interpreted with caution because of the uncertainties behind the as-
sumptions made in this report regarding the modeling of metal input due to scrubber water discharge.
Moreover, scrubber discharge water is not the only source of metal in a harbour. In fact, antifouling agents
and ballast water are also expected to be responsible for some metal emissions in a harbour (Lunde Her-
mansson et al., 2023). However, scrubber water emissions are estimated to represent 98-99% of the total
input of Ni and V in the harbour of Copenhagen (Lunde Hermansson et al., 2023) , whereas Cu input
from scrubber water only represents 1% of the total Cu input in the Port of Copenhagen. Therefore, there
is a need to take all the sources of metals into account to get a broader understanding of the potential
risks caused by metals to the marine environment in a harbour.
5.5
Reflection on potential further assessment
First of all, it would be relevant to perform Whole Effluent Toxicity tests with a third test organism
such as crustaceans.
This would allow for further assessment of the toxicity of scrubber water using
multiple endpoints such as mortality of adults and eggs but also reproduction and larval development.
This was done in some studies (Koski et al. (2017); Magnusson and Granberg (2022)). This thesis only
focused on the acute toxicity of scrubber water but there is a need to assess the long-term toxicity caused
by scrubber water and therefore chronic toxicity tests (e.g. copepod development or sea urchin embryonic
development test (OSPAR Commission, 2007) ) would be needed. This is also needed considering the
potential risk of bioaccumulation of metals due to the discharge of scrubber water.
Regarding the TIE steps performed in this report, it would be needed to perform the filtration,
aeration and EDTA addition tests after pH adjustment of the samples. This could help to identify other
driving factors of the toxicity of the effluent than pH. Indeed, it was shown that for one of the samples, pH
adjustment could reduce the toxicity of the sample by 40% but the inhibition measured after pH adjustment
was still above 50%. However, it is questionable whether EDTA addition could have a significant influence
on the toxicity of scrubber water. In fact, metal cations may already form complexes with other ions
present in the seawater such as carbonate ions, and thus might not react with EDTA.
Moreover, the use of TIE as a screening tool to identify substances and/or parameters responsible
for the main share of the toxicity of the scrubber water is questionable regarding the rather low toxicity
induced by the samples (EC50 ranging from 43-94 %).
Finally, further analysis of the samples could focus on PAHs by carrying out a chemical analysis (Gas
Chromatography Mass Spectrometry) to identify the compounds present in the scrubber water and their
concentrations. The focus could be on the 16 PAHs part of the US EPA priority pollutant list but also
alkylated PAHs. Indeed, alkylated PAHs are expected to be found in crude oil and heavy fuel oil and
could thus be present in scrubber water (Du et al., 2022). Having a broader knowledge of the chemical
composition of scrubber water could only help to determine its toxic effects on the marine environment.

6
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to assess the toxicity of open-loop scrubber water towards the marine
environment. This topic is related to the latest regulations in terms of sulphur emissions to the atmosphere
from the shipping industry and the combination of EGCS with the use of heavy fuel oil. This report also
focused on identifying potential drivers for the toxicity of the scrubber water samples received from two
ships equipped with OL scrubbers, operating in the Baltic/North Seas and the Mediterranean/Adriatic
seas.
The differences in toxicity measured among the samples studied emphasize the complexity of the
chemical composition of scrubber water. For instance, the scrubber water sampled at sea was found more
toxic towards bacteria than the one sampled in the harbour from Scandia Seaways. This finding aligns
with previous studies highlighting that the composition of scrubber water depends on the engine load.
The toxicity of the scrubber water discharged at sea seems to be mainly driven by the pH. However,
filtration and EDTA addition tests did not cause a decrease in inhibition after treatment compared to the
untreated samples while aeration caused a decrease in inhibition for one out of four samples.
This study focused on the role of metal in the toxicity of scrubber water. The chemical analysis
confirmed elevated concentrations of V, Ni, Cu and Zn with a clear link between the scrubbing process
and the presence of vanadium and nickel. The modeling of metal input in a standard OECD Commercial
harbour led to risk characterization ratios from 0.07-0.71 for V; 0.003-1.38 for Ni and 0.01-0.20 for Cu.
This indicates that depending on the scenario considered, metal discharge due to scrubber water could
cause a risk to the marine environment in a standardized OECD harbour. Nevertheless, there is a strong
need for further investigation on this topic taking into account other sources of metal discharged in a
harbour and the water exchange between the harbour and the open ocean.
Given the results of this study, it seems essential to investigate the role of PAHs listed on the priority
pollutants list of the US EPA but also alkylated PAHs. Moreover, further TIE tests could help identify
whether filtration, aeration and EDTA addition could lead to a decrease in toxicity after pH adjustment.
Further baseline toxicity testing on a third organism like crustaceans is also missing in this report. These
additional elements would provide a broader overview of the impact of scrubber water on the marine
environment.
Finally, it would be important to look further into the development and the performance of new
types of fuel. Indeed, considering the global climate challenges the world is currently facing, this would
encourage the shift towards a fossil fuel-free society. Currently, the strong economic incentive to combine
the use of heavy fuel oil and scrubbers rather than shifting to an alternative fuel might slow down this
transition and potentially contribute to damaging the marine environment.

observation (Axioscope 7, ZEISS, Germany) using a counting chamber slide (Thoma, Hecht Assistent®,
Germany). The concentration of algae is calculated by dividing the average number of cell per square in
the counting chamber slide by the volume of a square (2.5*10−7 mL). For instance, each area within a red
square in Figure 18 represents 16 squares of the counting chamber slide. The concentration of algae in the
pre-culture is then used to determine the necessary volume of algae pre-culture in each 25mL volumetric
