Summary (English)
In this thesis we deal with so-called inverse random medium scattering
problem, where the objective is to reconstruct information about back-
ground physical medium using measurements of the scattered field on a
boundary . This type of inverse problems arise in probabilistic models,
where the medium possesses stochastic nature and modelled by a random
function.
This leads to the transition from deterministic point of view to the
stochastic one with the stochastic Helmholtz equation. Moreover, multi-
variable environment results in the probabilistic model with random fields
and stochastic integral equations with respect to n-parameter Brownian
motion. This type of integral equations is treated in the framework of so-
called Ogawa stochastic integral and existence/uniqueness of a solution
is established using this set-up. In the thesis was derived the theorem
2.5, which establishes existence, uniqueness and regularity of a solution
to the corresponding stochastic integral equation.
Then the inverse random medium scattering problem was considered.
Perturbation analysis, under certain assumptions about the background
medium, allowed to reduce the problem to several Fredholm integral
equations. The established theorem 3.1 gives approximation of the sta-
tistical moments of the random scattered field by corresponding integral
equations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the past several decades the field of inverse problems has undergone
tremendous development due to significant scientific effort motivated by
industrial, medical and military applications. In a forward problem the
idea is to estimate evolution of a system with all parameters well-known.
On the contrary, the main goal of the inverse problem theory is, based on
a set of observations, to restore some knowledge about the factors that
caused these observations. A large class of inverse problems are based
on models where the direct process is described by a partial differential
equation (PDE) or a system of PDEs.
Essentially one could think about direct problem as an operator
A : X ⊃U →V ⊂Y,
A : φ 7→f
(1.1)
which maps an internal process φ from a subset U ⊂X to the measure-
ment f from V ⊂Y . We call the equation (1.1) well-posed if operator
A is bijective and A−1 is continuous, otherwise we call the problem (1.1)
ill-posed [CK13]. Then, an inverse problem is a problem of estimating
inverse operator A−1. But usually in applications the equation (1.1) is
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
ill-posed and the inverse A−1 doesn’t exist or doesn’t depend continu-
ously on the measurements f.
The problem of existence A−1 can be
fixed, for instance, by adjusting the domain U and the range V of the
direct operator A. But continuity of the inverse operator is tough to fix,
which means that small errors in the measurements f are amplified by
A−1 and produce unreasonable and practically useless approximation of
φ. Then, for example, one could apply some regularization techniques
as Tikhonov regularization or Truncated Singular-Value Decomposition
scheme and approximate operator A−1 by a family of bounded linear op-
erators Rα : Y →X. We refer, for example, to the book [CK13] for more
details about state of the art techniques and methods of solving inverse
problems and dealing with ill-posed operators.
In particular, scattering phenomenon attracts great interest due to its
prevalence in the nature. As examples should be mentioned scattering
of the sunlight in Earth’s atmosphere, various effects in optics, acoustics,
X-rays and particle physics. Abundance of scattering effects in the nature
attracted attention of scientists and gave a rise to the whole branch in the
inverse problem theory – inverse scattering theory. Examples are inverse
source and inverse scattering problems, where the source or the object
that generates a measured radiated wave pattern is to be determined.
Usually the inverse scattering problems possess nonlinear and ill-posed
nature, i.e. the existence of non-radiating sources for a fixed frequency
[DS82] violates uniqueness, which means that sources cannot be uniquely
determined from boundary measurements; moreover, there is no contin-
uous inverse operator, thus small errors in measured data amplify arte-
facts in the reconstructed image. These difficulties can be avoided by,
for instance, considering so-called pseudo-inverse with the minimum en-
ergy solution, or use multi-frequency data to guarantee uniqueness and
stability of the reconstructed solution [BLT10]. The inverse scattering
problems is very important for many scientific and industrial problems
and, at the moment, provide a basis for modern medical applications
of computerized tomography and imaging, radar construction, geophys-
ical exploration and nondestructive testing [CK13]. The inverse source
problem for wave propagation is considered a basic tool in photoacous-
tic tomography [BGS16], which is a modern state of the art tomography
technique.

In the thesis we deal with the one of the most important equations in
the inverse scattering theory – Helmholtz equation. This equation arise
from the system of Maxwell’s equations
∇× E = −∂B
∂t
∇× H = ∂D
∂t + J
∇· D = ρ
∇· B = 0,
(1.2)
where E is the electric field intensity, magnetic field intensity H, the elec-
tric displacement D, the magnetic flux density B, free current density
J and ρ is the free electric charge density. The system (1.2) describes
the propagation of electromagnetic fields in a space. Under certain as-
sumptions about the background medium and E, H fields properties
one can apply Fourier transformation to the system (1.2) and derive the
Helmholtz equation. It carries the name of the physicist Hermann Ludwig
Ferdinand von Helmholtz for his contributions to mathematical acoustics
and electromagnetics. For more details about derivation of the Helmholtz
equation we refer to, for example, standard references [CK13, Néd01].
In particular, we are interested in and work with stochastic version of
the Helmholtz equation which arises in scattering probabilistic models.
Usually an uncertainty in a mathematical model of direct or inverse prob-
lem can be introduced due to several reasons: complex structure of the
scatterer or incomplete knowledge about it, measuring errors in the sys-
tem parameters and directly introduced randomness by a stochastic phe-
nomenon. The mentioned above reasons lead to the stochastic inverse
problems, where the uncertainty is already assumed at the modelling
stage and incorporated in the mathematical model. This approach al-
lows to express knowledge about the structure of the system in terms of
probability distributions, confidence intervals or likelihood functions and
to gain better insight and predictive power for the system parameters or
internal structure.
In this thesis we deal with the inverse random medium scattering prob-
lem, which is described by the Helmholtz equation in the inhomogeneous
random medium with the known deterministic source function. We as-
sume that the background environment possesses a random nature and is

modelled by the additive white noise. So far we know only a few papers
about inverse random source problems, for instance [BCL16, BCLZ14,
LCL17, Li11], where the authors attempt to reconstruct statistical mo-
ments (mean and variance) of the random source function in the homoge-
neous and inhomogeneous media. It is done by reducing these problems
to solving the corresponding deterministic Fredholm integral equations
applying properties of the stochastic integral.
Our mathematical model of the scattering problem in random inhomoge-
neous medium leads to so-called stochastic integral equation, where the
unknown quantity is a random multivariable function, i.e. random field.
Since we are working in multidimensional set-up with random functions
of several variables, then the standard stochastic calculus with Itô or
Stratonovich integrals are not applicable due to absence of natural order
in Rn. This requires construction of the stochastic integral of a random
function with respect to n-dimensional Brownian motion and investiga-
tion of the corresponding integral equations with the Brownian sheet. We
apply the construction of the stochastic integral from the paper [Shi91]
and use proposed results to prove existence and uniqueness of the solution
to the stochastic Lippmann–Schwinger integral equation.
The outline of the thesis is as follows: Chapter 2 concerns about di-
rect problems. There we introduce deterministic Helmholtz equation in
inhomogeneous background medium, establish equivalence between the
Helmholtz equation and the Lippmann–Schwinger integral equation and
proceed to the random scattering problem with stochastic Helmholtz
equation and corresponding stochastic Lippmann–Schwinger equation.
Chapter 3 is devoted to the stochastic inverse medium scattering prob-
lem, where we reconstruct statistical moments of the background inho-
mogeneous medium applying perturbation analysis. In Chapter 4 we dis-
cuss some practical aspects of numerical analysis of the inverse random
medium scattering problem using the results from Chpater 3. Chapter
5 summarizes the results of the thesis and provides possible directions
for further research work. In Appendix A necessary background in the
Brownian sheet, white noise, stochastic integral equation is provided.
The whole section A.3 is dedicated to stochastic integral equations and
establishing the result about existence and uniqueness of a solution to
stochastic integral equations.

Chapter 2
Direct problem
In this chapter we introduce the Helmholtz equation for an inhomoge-
neous medium and discuss its solution.
We start from deterministic
case, where no randomness in the mathematical model of the scatter-
ing process is assumed, and investigate existence and uniqueness of a
solution. It turns out that the Helmholtz equation is equivalent to the
Lippmann–Schwinger integral equation and instead of finding solution of
the partial differential equation one could estimate a solution of the cor-
responding integral equation. Then we proceed to the stochastic version
of the Helmholtz equation, where the medium is described by a random
function. This transition leads to the stochastic Lippmann–Schwinger
integral equation, where one component has integration with respect to
the Brownian sheet. We study this stochastic integral equation and prove
existence, uniqueness of a solution.

Chapter 3
Inverse problem
Having studied solution to the direct scattering problem (both determin-
istic and stochastic versions) we proceed to the inverse random media
scattering problem. The objective is to reconstruct information about
the background medium q(x), where
q(x) = µ(x) + σ(x) ˙Wx
based on the measurements u(x) on the boundary ∂Br. Since the so-
lution to the stochastic Helmholtz equation (2.6), or stochastic Lipp-
mann–Schwinger integral equation (2.7), is a random function, then es-
timating statistical moments, for example mean and variance, of the
background medium is more desirable than a solution to a particular
realisation of the random filed q(x). We apply perturbation analysis un-
der certain assumptions about the functions µ(x) and σ(x) and reduce
the problem of estimating statistical moments to solving corresponding
Fredholm integral equations.
