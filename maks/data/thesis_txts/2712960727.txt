Abstract
In this thesis, the effects of scaling various components of a soft-wing kite are observed. The question
being posed is whether kites can in fact be scaled to sizes larger than existing systems. The main
objective is to give insight into how the performance of the kite, in terms of power and energy
production, change with system size. This was done using an analytical AEP & power curve model
which could produce results for a range of kite sizes. More specifically the area of the lifting body
and length of the tether are looked at. It was found that increasing the area of the kite had an
effect on efficiency of the kite, in terms of power and energy produced per m2 of kite area. Energy
density and capacity factor increase with size up until a specific kite area. It was also found that
the material density of the kite directly influenced its maximum size, or size of which any increase
would not improve energy yield. The findings highlighted that scaling the kite is plausible and can
bring benefits in terms of efficiency. However, the importance of keeping the kite as light as possible
is also made clear.

Introduction to Airborne Wind
Global warming and climate change are currently the largest threats humanity faces. Instigated by
the copious release of greenhouse gases from fossil fuel generators, we are constantly striving to find
new ways of providing energy from clean and renewable sources. Luckily enough, vast amounts of
clean energy streams are already available on planet earth, waiting to be tapped into. Fundamentally,
all energy comes from our sun, be it in different forms. Atmospheric gases are continuously moving
due to uneven temperature gradients caused by the sun, in an attempt to reach equilibrium. The
kinetic energy of these gas particles can be converted into electricity with wind harnessing devices.
In 2019, 5.4 % of the world electricity was generated by wind [25] (see figure 1), with the potential
to power a far larger fraction than this. Majority of this was produced by ”conventional” horizontal
axis wind turbines (HAWTs). These are the most tried and tested types of wind generators, first
widely used in Denmark around 1900 [24]. Over the years, these rapidly increased in size and scale,
with the largest commercial turbines having power ratings of up to 14 MW and blade diameters of
over 200m [18].
Figure 1: World gross electricity production by source 2019
6
==PAGE:7==
While extremely capable machines, they come with some inherit disadvantages. Modern turbines
are situated in the surface layer of our atmosphere (with highest skin friction), making the wind
speeds quite sensitive to altitude. Modern turbines have a hub height of around 100 m, anything
above this and material costs begin to rise significantly. If one was to increase the hub height to
600 m for example, the wind velocity would increase by a factor of 1.3, & due to the power output
being dependant on the velocity cubed, this would lead to a > 2 factor increase in power output
(as seen in figure 34). Wind speeds at these heights are not only greater, but also more consistent,
giving arise to higher capacity factors. In summary, it can be said that there is an untapped energy
potential in our upper atmosphere.
This begs the question; how can we tap into this stream of energy?
Kites have been used for
hundreds, if not thousands of years [11] for a variety of purposes, but only in recent decades have
they been used to generate electrical power. Limited only by the length of their tethering line, they
can operate at altitudes much higher than traditional wind turbines, giving them access to higher
and more consistent winds. With advancements in micro controllers and materials, kites have now
become a viable way of providing power. Still in its early phases, examples of multi kW (up to 100
kW [15]) kite power systems are in existence, and are aimed to reach commercial markets within
the coming years. While current systems are suitable for small scale use, it is interesting to observe
the feasibility of scaling such a device. This leads us to the area of interest, namely the implications
and plausibility of scaling of soft-wing Airborne Wind Energy Systems (or AWESs), with the intent
of grid implementation. The question being posed is can airborne wind feasibly be scaled to large
areas, and does there exist a limit to how large these systems can get?
2.1
Motivation: Why Airborne Wind Power?
With HAWTs being a tried and tested technology, the question of why a new wind harnessing
method is needed. Kites have a number of potential benefits over more traditional wind generator
types. These include:
• High altitude operation give rise to higher and more consistent wind speeds. Surface friction
with the ground causes wind to decelerate, lowering its power potential. The further away
from the ground the wind generator can operate, the more energy available in the wind. [15].
• High material efficiency: Low amount of material resources required per installed power ca-
pacity. Here, a conventional steel tower is replaced by a high strength tether. An AWES uses
up to 90% less material than that of a conventional HAWT [15].
• Transportability: Smaller-scale kite systems can fit into a standard shipping container, allowing
greater ease of transportation [15].
• Rapid deployment: Kite systems are in a sense ”self erecting” and do not require large and
expensive cranes to setup. This allows them to be deployed quickly and in remote areas [15].
The point of highlighting these benefits is not to shine a bad light on traditional HAWTs, rather to
encourage how the potential of AWESs can be used in conjunction with ”traditional” methods to
increase penetration of renewable energy in the grid.

==PAGE:8==
There exists a lot of literature regarding AWES, building upon the novel work done by Miles L.
Loyd published in 1980 [12]. Much work has been done on various designs, physics and modelling,
however, not a great deal of literature was found on large scale AWES, let alone the physics and
implications of it. In order for AWES to become commercial and wide spread, large scale systems
that can compete with other renewable sources must be viable. This is the main motivation behind
this paper, to shed light on what exactly happens when AWES grows in size.
2.2
Related Work
Many external sources were used as inspiration for this thesis, here some of the most influential ones
are listed, their results briefly discussed and finally how this work was built upon.
• Crosswind Kite Power [12] - One of the first papers ever written on airborne wind energy
which describes the basic equations of motions and upper power limits for kites operating in
different configurations. This is one of the fundamental papers that many papers build upon.
The key finding of the optimal reel-out velocity being equal to 1/3 of the wind velocity is a
fundamental assumptions that is used in this thesis. The power and force equations used in
this thesis are similar, but make use of some slightly different assumptions that build upon
Loyd’s paper.
• Analysis of the Maximum Efficiency of Kite-Power Systems [5] - A paper analysing the max-
imum power potential of a kite not taking into account inertia and gravity of the kite. It
introduces the concept of cosine (elevation) losses and being able to find the maximum effi-
ciency of a kite system using simpler analytical equations, rather than complex simulations.
The derived equation for aerodynamic power in the reel-out phase is used in this thesis, however
it is built upon by taking into account losses from gravity.
• Airborne Wind Energy [17] - A general overview of airborne wind energy.
Introduces the
various types of AWESs and some fundamental equations. Combines many papers across the
airborne field into one textbook.
• Unified engineering models for the performance and cost of Ground - Gen and Fly-Gen cross-
wind [7] - This paper derives analytical equations for the physics and power for an rigid-wing
AWES (slightly modified in this thesis for a soft-wing AWES). A refined drag coefficient is also
introduced, taking into account drag from the tether and the wing. An equation describing
the power required to reel the kite in is also given. A power equation taking gravity effects
is present, which provided inspiration for taking this into account, but a slightly different ap-
proach is used in this thesis. The main takeaways from this paper that are used in this thesis
are the refined drag coefficient and the reel-in power equation, which are used in the AEP
(annual energy production) model.
• Material selection and joining methods for the purpose of a high-altitude inflatable kite [22]
- This paper gives insight into how the stresses and therefore consequences of these stresses
change with kite area. An equation showing how the material area density increases with kite
area is given, and this is built upon in this thesis to find the mass of a scaled kite, given a
reference kite area and area density. This equation is a crucial part of the thesis as the mass
is used to calculate the gravity losses.

==PAGE:9==
• Economics of Pumping Kite Generators [8] - provides a basic overview of the factors effect the
economics of AWES. Shows how various site and system parameters influence LCOE. Many
of the cost equations in the economic model came from or were inspired by this paper.
2.3
Types & Basic setup of AWES
In this section, various types of AWES will be characterised, such as the location of the electrical
generator and composition of the lifting body. Then the fundamental components of a soft-wing
AWES are presented along with its method of function. Some examples of existing systems are also
given.
2.3.1
Types of AWES
When it comes to AWES, there are a few main system characteristics to consider:
• Generator location:
– Ground based generation - here the generator is placed on the ground and is connected
to a spool (either direct or via high speed transmission). The connecting tether is wound
around this spool and unwinds due to the pulling force of the kites (reel-out phase).
Once the tether is fully unwound, the kites enters a low drag mode and is pulled back
in, resetting the tether (reel-in phase). A small amount of energy is spent pulling the
kite back in, but this is relatively small compared to the energy produced in the traction
phase [17]
– Onboard based generation - The generator is driven by a propeller placed on the
wing (or sometimes a lighter than air system such as a helium balloon). The electricity
is transported to the ground station using a cable incorporated in the tether line. Here
the idea is that the propeller encounters much higher apparent wind velocities, both due
to the altitude of the kite but also because of its crosswind motion. [17]
• Wing type:
– Soft-wing kite - made of flexible, lighter materials, similar to a parachute which benefit
from extremely large surface areas for minimal weight.
These are somewhat safer to
operate due to their lighter weight. They tend to have slightly lower power output per
area due to lower lift-to-drag ratios. Soft wing kites tend to expend slightly more energy
in the reel-in phase (compared to rigid-wings) due to their large, drag prone area. The
motion of the kite is controlled by a bridle system attached a control unit. The control
unit pulls on various lines in the bridle system that alter the aerodynamics characteristics
of the kite, causing it to change direction. [17] An example of a soft wing from LEI is
shown in figure 2.

Figure 2: LEI V5.40 soft-wing kite with area of 40m2 [14]
– Rigid-wing - similar to the wing of a traditional aircraft. These tend to be smaller in
area than a soft wing, but have much higher lift-to-drag ratios leading to higher power
densities. These are somewhat harder to operate than soft wing kites, they tend to require
a propulsion system to get the wind airborne, and require more space to land. Hard wings
requires less energy during the reel-in phase, as they can fly almost directly toward the
ground station, resulting in very low tether tension. A rigid wind is controlled by moving
aerodynamic surfaces along its wing span [17]. An example of a rigid wing from Makani
is shown in figure 3.
Figure 3: Makani 600 kW rigid-wing kite at ground station [19]
– Semi-rigid (hybrid) wing - these are a mix between the two aforementioned wing
types. It consists of a kite membrane (similar to a soft kite) with inflatable spars along

the positive characteristics of both soft and hard wings.

Components & Setup
In this exploration, a soft-wing kite with a ground based generator will be the system in question.
Here, the basic components of the system will be described. A soft-wing, ground based generator
system consists of 4 main components (listed items correspond to labelled component in figure 4)
[15])
•1. Ground station, consisting of a generator, high speed transmission, tether spool, control elec-
tronics, ballast / ground mounting system
2. Tether, connects the kite to the ground station
3. Control unit consisting of receiver, line spools to control the kite in pitch, roll & yaw
4. Soft-kite
Figure 4: Basic setup of a AWES
[15]
The kite, once airborne produces lift by flying close to perpendicular with the ambient wind. Usually
it flies in a figure of 8 like motion, while slowly unreeling its tether and producing power, this phase
is called the “reel-out” phase. Eventually the tether reaches its maximum length and the kite goes
into a low lift / drag mode such that it can be pulled in again (reel in phase). This is done so in
a way that the reel in phase consumes far less energy than produced during reel out phase. This
process of reel in / out is then repeated.

2.3.3
Examples of existing systems
• Kite Power Falcon - small scale commercial, 100kW AWES designed to bring wind power
to remote areas. Being able to fit in a standard 6m long container, it can be placed in hard
to access areas quickly and with minimal setup time, ideal for disaster relief. With an AEP of
450 MWh, it can provide energy for up to 150 households [15].
• SkySails Propulsion - kites can not only be used for power generation, but also to provide
traction power for ships. SkySails is an automated kite system used to reduce emissions from
cargo ships by assisting the traditional propulsion method. The SkySails C-320 is a 400m2 kite
that can provide 2MW of traction force, hence reducing the required power of the ship motor
by 2MW, corresponding to 60 −70% of nominal cruise power of a 130m ship ([17], chapter
20).
2.4
Scope
The overall aim of this thesis is to observe how increasing the size (area) of an AWES effects its
performance in terms of power and energy production, as well as other parameters. This will be
done with the following steps:
• Derive the fundamental power equations of a soft-wing AWES using reasonable assumptions.
• Develop an Annual Energy Production (AEP) model incorporating various sources of losses.
• Validating the model to asses credibility.
• Using model to observe scaling effects on system performance.
• Based on findings, come to conclusions about whether scaling of AWES is plausible and the
necessary requirements for this.
3
Model Construction
The AEP model will now be constructed. First, the governing equations for reel-in / reel-out power
will be presented in section 3.3.6 & 3.3.5. Secondly, the various sources of losses that deduct from
the overall power are introduced in section 3.2. The model assumptions, as well as the wind climate
and tether scaling algorithm are then discussed in sections 3.3.1, 3.3.3 & 3.3.4. Power curves for
both phases, as well the time spent in each are then shown. Finally, the power curve, wind climate
and reeling ratios are combined to calculated the various energies spent in each phase. From this
the AEP can be determined.
Once the model is developed, a validation is carried out, making a comparison to other power curves
and AEP values from another analytical model and exisitng AWES. Once validated the parame-
ter study is carried for two kites cases, a lighter and heaver kite, observing various performance
parameters as a function of kite area. The effects of lengthening the tether line is also looked into.

3.1
Power Equations
The governing power equations for kite energy will be now be derived. Unlike a traditional HAWT
which principally has one mode of operation (being power production) and therefore one power
equation, a kite system has two, the reel-in (retraction) and reel-out (traction) phase. Due to this,
two power equations, one for each phase, is needed in order to calculate the energy production over
a given period.
3.1.1

the reel-out phase will now be introduced. This derivation ends up with an identical equation found

of tension force of the tether, T, and the reel out velocity, Vout. It is also assumed that the tether
tension is approximately equal to the total aerodynamic force, Ftot, generated by the lifting body of
the kite.

3.2
Sources of Losses
Equation 10 was an indicator of the maximum potential power of the kite at a certain elevation
angle. In a realistic scenario, there are many sources of losses, all of which deduct from the available
power. The following losses are now introduced:
• Elevation angle losses
• Refined Drag Coefficient (aerodynamic losses from tether & lift induced drag included in drag
term)
• Mass losses due to tether and kite (forces opposing Ftot)
3.2.1
Elevation Angle
The kite acts in an elevated position relative to the ground station, resulting in an angle between the
two. This angle is referred to as the elevation angle, θ. The resultant aerodynamic force acts at this
angle, and can be broken up into two force components, one acting in the horizontal direction and
one in the vertical. The horizontal force component is in fact the only component that contributes
to the mechanical power, and is found by Fcos(θ), where F is the traction force of the kite. The
difference between the resultant and horizontal aerodynamic force is known as cosine losses. Ideally
one wishes to keep this angle small, however, small elevation angles lead to lower altitudes and hence
lower wind resources. One method of keeping this angle small while giving suitable altitude is by
increasing the length of the tether, however increase the tether also increases drag, so there is a
trade-off between the two parameters. Throughout this exploration, the elevation angle will be kept
constant at 30◦. Figure 10 shows how the power decreases with angle. It can be seen for a fixed
angle of 30◦, 65% of the total aerodynamic force is usable for power generation.

3.3
Model Assembly
The aforementioned power equations, sources of losses and a Weibull distributed wind climate are
combined in a model that takes on various system parameters of a AWES. For each kite area, a
relevant tether diameter, power curve, and annual energy production (AEP) is calculated. With
these calculated, various analyses are performed to give insight into how the performance of the
system changes with area. Two different cases, a lighter and heavier kite, are shown in order to
observe how mass plays a crucial factor in the efficiency of the system. Insight into the effects of
lengthening the tether to increase altitude is also given.
Finally, an economic model is used to
calculate the levelised cost of energy (LCOE) for both cases for a range of areas.

3.3.5
Reel-out Power Curve
The reel-out power curve is used to find the power of the AWES at a specific wind speed during
the traction phase. It is found by taking the total aerodynamic force of the kite, subtracting the
gravitational force losses from kite / tether mass, and multiplying the net force with the reel-out
velocity, Vout:
Pout = (Ftot −Fmass)Vout
(28)
Where Ftot is equation 8 and Fmass is equation 25. reel-out velocity, Vout, will be equal to 1/3Vw
throughout. The criteria for the power curve are defined by 3 wind speeds:
• Cut-in - minimum wind speed for which power is produced. No power is produced below this
value. If the force at this wind speed is not high enough to overcome the gravitational force
of the kite/tether, the kite will not produce power.
• Rated - wind speed for which maximum power is produced. The power remains constant after
this point until cut-out.
• Cut-out - maximum wind speed for which power is produced. The system stops producing
power after this value is reached.
As formulated in the model:
• If Vwind < cut-in →Pout = 0
• If cut-in< Vwind < rated →Pout = Pout(Vwind)

• If rated < Vwind < cut-out →Pout = Pout(Vrated)
• If Vwind > cut-out →Pout = 0

possible energy produced at rated power (time frame usually being a year):

other parameters the same. The cut-in / cut-out wind speeds will be 2m/s and 15m/s, respectively,

increasing the length of the tether also increases losses due to gravity, so there is a trade-off. Also,
there is more aerodynamic resistance from the tether for longer length, this drag penalty has been

Findings / Conclusions
In this thesis an AWES AEP model was created taking account various sources of losses in order to
observe how performance parameters changed with kite area. The main findings of the model are
listed below:
• Increase kite area increases rated power and energy production.
• Aerodynamically speaking, larger kites perform better than smaller when keeping the tether
the same length.
• The material density of the kite has a large influence on power density and hence energy pro-
duction. As the kite gets larger, the mass force scales faster than the increase in aerodynamic
force and the power density begins to decrease.
• There exists a kite area where increasing beyond this point will decrease energy production.
The higher the material density, the lower this point occurs.
• Increasing the material density of the kite after a certain value decreases its energy density.
• With no fixed cost, the minimum LCOE of the kite occurs at small kite areas, however with a
fixed cost implemented the minimum occurs at higher areas. After this ideal point the LCOE
increases.
• An ideal length of the tether exists where increasing its length will yield no further energy
increase, but decreasing its length will decrease energy production due to lower wind speeds.
• An AWES with the same power rating as a HAWT will have a lower AEP due to its reel-in
phase.
• AWES has a lower CAPEX and LCOE than HAWTs, when using a simplified economic model
Overall it was found that there are arguments for increasing the size of AWES, such as improvements
in energy density and reduced LCOE (with a fixed cost), however these improvements, and their
magnitude are very much dependant on the mass of the kite, more specifically its material density.
In terms of potential areas for improvement, there are few areas that could improve the model to
make it more true to life, listed below:
• More accurate wind model dependant on kite altitude.
55
==PAGE:56==
• More dynamic model taking into account trajectory and inertial effects of kite motion.
• Implementing a transition phase between reel-in and reel-out.
• More realistic economic model, including fixed cost calculations for given AWES size.
6
Unanswered questions
• When observing the rated power density of the kite (figure 26) , it tended to decrease with kite
area after a certain point. It would be expected that as the power density decreased, so would
the energy density (energy is the product of power and time), however for lighter kite this
was not case. In fact the energy density and capacity factor increased with kite area (figure
30 & 32), which is somewhat counter-intuitive. The reason behind this at the time of writing
is unknown, and could either be due to unexplained effects or a possible error in the model.
Further work could be done to explain the discrepancy in findings here.
• Regarding the energy density of the heavier kite (figure 30) it begins to decrease after 300m2
but the CF (figure 32) decreases first around 750m2. It would be logical that the CF and
energy density would follow a similar trend, since a lower energy density should give rise to a
lower CF. Again, this is unexplained behaviour from the model that could be explored further.
