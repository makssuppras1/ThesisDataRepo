Abstract
Air Foil Bearings (AFBs) oﬀer several advantages for turbo compressors,
such as very low mechanical power loss and clean non-contaminating oper-
ation. They are notably diﬀerent from oil lubricated bearings due to the
compliant inner surface, the compressibility of air and the signiﬁcantly lower
viscosity dictating much tighter clearances.
The rotordynamic properties of the AFBs are sensitive to unbalance and
nonlinear eﬀects, making accurate predictions important. Since traditional
frequency-domain methods have proven incapable of satisfactorily predict-
ing their response, some attention has recently been turned to alternative
approaches relying on time-domain simulation.
Simultaneous mathematical models in time have recently been presented,
and such a model has proven to predict the rotordynamic behaviour of
an industrial three-pad AFB with impressive agreement to experimental
data. In this work, a reimplementation of this model is presented which
reduces the simulation time from days to minutes. This contributes to the
practical usability of time-domain methods in the design phase and allows
for parametric studies.
The model employs the widely applied Simple Elastic Foundation Model
(SEFM) for the foil structure, and hence relies on some extensive assump-
tions and signiﬁcant prior knowledge of the system. The second focus of the
presented work has been to overcome these inherent limitations by introdu-
cing a new foil structure model. A novel aspect of the presented foil model
is the inclusion of the mass. Even though the foil dynamics are negligible,
this is concluded to be a necessary addition for maintaining the simultan-
eity of the model. The coupling to this new model signiﬁcantly increases
the required computational time and is hence only practically possible due
to the prior optimisation of the existing model.
Some preliminary AFB simulation results using the new foil model is
presented, but further work is necessary to prove its validity. Such work
should also judge whether the new model contributes suﬃciently to the
prediction accuracy to be worth the additional computational eﬀort.

Introduction
The purpose of the present project is to enhance an existing bearing prediction
code and furthermore to incorporate an extended structural model.
1.1
Background
An Air Foil Bearing (AFB) is a type of self-acting hydrodynamic bearing that
operates by having a thin ﬁlm of air generated between its rotating and stationary
parts. In this ﬁlm, pressure is generated by viscous shearing as it is also the case in
hydrodynamic oil bearings. Nevertheless, the AFBs are notably diﬀerent from their
oil lubricated relatives due to the compressibility of air and the signiﬁcantly lower
viscosity which dictates much tighter clearances. The compressibility complicates
the governing equations signiﬁcantly and the low viscosity of air greatly reduces
both friction and damping.
While the reduced friction implies a lower power loss and is highly desirable,
the lack of damping is problematic as rotordynamic instability becomes a funda-
mental issue. In order to supply damping and to allow for imperfections such as
shaft misalignments or ovality, a compliant inner surface is introduced using a foil
structure as illustrated in ﬁg. 1.1.
During operation, the foil structure deforms to compensate for e.g. thermal
expansion or misalignment. Furthermore, the corrugated foil’s sliding against the

may additionally be dissipated internally in the foil material, but the majority is
attributed to dry friction in the contact points. This is an important point, since
it suggests that any model expected to describe the bearing dynamics should take
the frictional energy dissipation into account.
In the Ph.d. thesis of Larsen [1] which was defended in March 2015, several
mathematical models of AFBs were presented, including one capable of time-
domain simulations of a shaft-bearing test rig. Per se, time-domain simulations of
such systems are not particular revolutionary, but the general increase in available
computational resources and the simultaneous nature of the new model makes it
an interesting alternative to frequency-domain methods.
The model is, however, limited to viscous-like energy dissipation as it relies on
the Simple Elastic Foundation Model (SEFM) (see section 2.1). Additionally, the
SEFM introduces some rather extensive ”engineering assumptions” regarding e.g.
the number of active bumps and displacement amplitudes.
Larsen [1] also presents a separate and very detailed nonlinear two-dimensional
Finite Element (FE) model (see section 9.1) of the bump foil including a spring
based friction model (see section 8.2). Even though this model has already been
implemented and even validated against experimental data, it is computationally
far too expensive for use in time simulations of the bearing system as a whole.
A natural next step – and the goal of this work – would hence be to mimic the
existing foil model using a simpliﬁed model and subsequently to incorporate this
into the time simulations. Such a model would represent a signiﬁcant contribution
to the AFB research.
1.2
Literature Study
A great part of the theoretical foundation for the mathematical description of ﬂuid
ﬁlm bearings should undoubtedly be ascribed to Reynolds and his 1886 article [2].
Regarding AFBs in particular, the extension of Reynolds’ ﬁndings to encompass
also compressible ﬂuids by Harrison [3] should probably be considered equally
important.
A more practical interest in gas lubrication can be traced back to the mid-1950s,
where especially two applications appeared to which gas lubrication became at-
tractive. One were inertial navigation systems for use during prolonged submerged
operation by nuclear submarines. The second were gas pumps located inside the
radioactive gas circuits of nuclear power reactors [4]. Along with advances within
experimental equipment, such as capacitive sensors, and the emerging numerical
computer solutions as presented by e.g. [5], these applications greatly aided the
development of gas lubrication technology. During the 1960s, also the compliant
type gas foil bearings appeared, among these the AFBs. Later, and up to present

times, AFB technology has evolved signiﬁcantly and has been applied successfully
to a wide range of high-speed rotating machinery.
This includes the air cycle
machines of e.g. Boeing 747 or Lockheed Martin F-16 [6]. More recent eﬀorts at
NASA even strives towards creating a completely oil-free turbine engine [7].
Although time-domain analysis of oil lubricated bearings has been performed
by e.g. Castelli and McCabe [8] as early as 1967, a great part of the published
work on gas bearing analysis is based on perturbation solution methods like the
one suggested by Lund [9]. These methods apply to the frequency-domain and
seek the steady state response by linearising the reaction forces around some static
equilibrium, eﬀectively replacing the entire bearing and ﬂuid ﬁlm with a spring-
dashpot system. This is convenient as it allows for classical rotor analysis to be
applied, but, as pointed out by Lund [9] himself, it leaves behind any nonlinear
response phenomena. This prevents, or seriously complicates, the simulation of any
rotordynamic behaviour involving large amplitude motions, such as the transient
behaviour during start-up.
Some of the ﬁrst to apply these perturbation methods to compliant type gas
bearings were Heshmat et al. [10]. They included the compliance by introducing
a linear elastic function of the ﬂuid ﬁlm pressure directly into Reynolds Equation
(RE), which was then solved using a Finite Diﬀerence (FD) scheme. This way of
introducing the foil compliance has latter been referred to as the SEFM and it has
been extended to include a loss factor by e.g. Peng and Carpino [11]. The SEFM
does however remain a rather rough approximation, and eﬀorts have been made
by among others Ku and Heshmat [12] to create more sophisticated foil structure
models including aspects such as the stiﬀening eﬀect of friction and bump-bump
interaction. Later, Iordanoﬀ[13] developed a detailed analytical mathematical
model for the bump foil stiﬀness which takes into account also the welding of
the ﬁrst bump. Common to these structure models is that the foil is eventually
reduced to radially acting linear spring-dashpot pairs. This is not the case in the
more recent bump foil model by Le Lez et al. [14, 15], as the foil is approximated
using a truss structure with around two Degrees of Freedom (DOFs) per bump.
This shows good agreement both to experiments and more detailed models, but
has to the best knowledge of the author, not been coupled to any frequency-domain
bearing prediction codes.
As practice with AFBs evidence signiﬁcant sub-synchronous vibration [16],
their dynamic behaviour is inherently nonlinear.
Applying any linear stability
analysis will, at best, be able to predict when a rotordynamic instability should
appear, but not whether it will actually be harmful. Even though it is somewhat
misleadingly referred to as an ”onset speed of sub-synchronous vibration”, such
a rotordynamic stability limit is exactly the result of the recent work on linear
stability analysis by Hoﬀmann et al. [17]. While possibly being able to correctly

To cope with this inherent limitation, both Andrés and Kim [16] and Balducchi
et al. [18] have attempted to model the foil as having third-order nonlinear stiﬀness
while neglecting the inﬂuence of the ﬂuid ﬁlm entirely. This gives a Duﬃng oscil-
lator like dynamic behaviour and does indeed reveal multiple frequency responses,
sub- as well as super-harmonic. The authors do, however, slightly disagree on the
validity of their results.
Nonlinear time-domain analysis constitutes a fundamentally diﬀerent approach
to determining the dynamic properties of e.g. AFBs. Stable Finite Volume (FV)
based numerical solutions to the compressible RE exist [19] and coupled to the
SEFM, an eﬃcient FE based method has been presented by Larsen and Santos
[20]. Despite these models being originally intended for frequency-domain ana-
lysis, such models and the rapid increase in available computational resources
have made time-domain analysis achievable within reasonable time frames. All
though frequency-domain techniques are inherently faster as they yield steady-
state solutions directly and hence have no need to march forward in time past
an initial transient period, the time-domain methods do not share the frequency
techniques’ inherent restriction to assumed periodic motion [21].
Le Lez et al. [22] have presented a time-domain analysis in which the FV based
solution of RE from [19] is coupled to the equivalent structural foil model from [14].
Instead of modelling the energy dissipation in the foils as being viscous, friction is
implemented directly using a smoothened Coulumb friction approximation given
by Petrov and Ewins [23]. A somewhat similar model is presented by Lee et al.
[24], in which a beam and bar based structural foil model is coupled to a FE based
RE solution. Here, the Coulomb friction is modelled using a stick-slip algorithm.
Even though [22] and [24] use diﬀerent methods for the spatial discretisation
of the compressible RE, both are approximating the time derivative terms using
backward diﬀerence. It is pointed out by Bonello and Pham [25, 26] that such
approximations inevitably introduce a time-lack between the state variables ren-
dering the model non-simultaneous. This can, to some extent, be made up for by
using very small time steps, but the solution will remain dependent on the time
step size and convergence studies are, strictly speaking, necessary [27]. Bonello
and Pham [25, 26] address this problem by introducing a new state variable ψ = ph
(as in [8]), allowing a true simultaneous solution of all state variables of an FE
ﬂuid ﬁlm discretisation coupled to the SEFM.
Using this method, results from the simulation of an industrial three-pad AFB
have recently been published by Larsen and Santos [27]. This model is, like that
of Bonello and Pham [25], based on an FE discretisation of the compressible RE
coupled to the SEFM, but in [27] the equivalent foil stiﬀness and damping were

1.3
Objectives of the Current Project
The overall objective of the present project is to contribute to the state of the art
modelling capabilities of AFBs. This can be unfolded into two rather distinct sub
objectives:
Optimisation of the time-domain based bearing prediction code presented by
Larsen and Santos [27].
This involves an examination of the underlying
mathematical model to identify unnecessary duplications of eﬀort and a near-
complete rework of the original implementation. This is, to a great extend,
a numerical exercise which is not rather speciﬁc to bearing dynamics. It
does however serve to reduce the required computational eﬀort to tolerable
levels and hence paves the way for industrial application. Furthermore, the
optimisation serves to make extensions practically possible. These eﬀorts are
described in ”Part I Optimisation of existing model”.
Coupling of the optimised time simulation code to a more detailed foil structure
model inspired by the work of Le Lez et al. [14]. Basically, this enhanced
structure model should merely replace the SEFM, but it has proven non-
trivial to achieve this without compromising the simultaneous nature of the
model. The work related to this goal is treated in the ”Part II Coupling to
enhanced foil model” of this report.

Numerical Time Integration
When characterising numerical methods for solving IVPs, two major distinctions
are usually made.
One is between explicit and implicit methods, the other is
between Runge-Kutta (RK) and Linear Multistep (LM) methods.
Since these
are important distinctions when it comes to choosing an integrator code in the
current project, a short presentation of both are given in this section. Also, a list
of available implementations are given.
4.1
Implicit and Explicit Methods
In an explicit scheme, everything needed for steeping forward is known before the
step is actually calculated, that is the formula providing each next step depends on
past information only. In an implicit scheme each new step is given as a, generally
nonlinear, equation that needs to be solved. These schemes have diﬀerent proper-
ties with respect to accuracy, stability and cost, with the implicit schemes typically
having larger regions of stability and higher cost per step. An intermediate class
of schemes also exist, namely the predictor-corrector schemes. These are based
on implicit formulas, but substitute all of the unknown components with explicit
predictions – including a preliminary prediction for the step to be calculated. In
this way, one can potentially avoid the need for solving a system of equations.
It should be mentioned that the advantage of not having to solve an equation
system for explicit schemes only holds in general if the IVP itself is given on explicit
form. It is e.g. demonstrated by [38] that even an explicit RK method requires the
solution of equation systems for each time step if applied to an implicitly stated
IVP.

this for all values of λ with negative real part, meaning that its region of absolute
stability includes the entire left half of the complex z-plane, it is said to be A-stable.
Even though no rigid mathematical deﬁnition of stiﬀness has yet been given
[41], it is clear that a class of such stiﬀproblems exists to which only the implicit
schemes are suitable due to the bounded stability of the explicit ones. Among
the implicit RK variants, methods exist that show A-stability for any order (in
contrast to what is possible for the LM methods to be discussed in section 4.3).
This is an impressive property that makes these schemes highly suitable for both
stiﬀODEs and DAEs. In return for this stability, however, the number of nonlinear
equations to be solved within each step becomes Ns, where N is the original system
dimension and s is the number of stages [42]. Even though techniques to partly
circumvent this problem do exist, this property poses an appreciable challenge in
applying these methods. One such RK method that is actually applied and seems
to be relatively widespread is the ”Radau IIA” (coeﬃcient tables for this is given
by e.g. [43]).

As all of the Adams methods are only conditionally stable in the left half of the
complex plane, they are not particularly well suited for stiﬀsystems. Although
the implicit Adams-Moulton methods already have much larger stability regions
than the explicit Adams-Bashforth methods, another family is better suited for
stiﬀsystems, namely the Backward Diﬀerentiation Formula (BDF) methods. In
fact, one rather pragmatic deﬁnition of the very concept of stiﬀness is given in
terms of the BDFs: ‘stiﬀequations are equations where certain implicit methods,
in particular BDF, perform better, usually tremendously better, than explicit ones’
(Hairer and Wanner [45]). The BDF methods have order corresponding to their
number of steps and for k ≤2 these are A-stable. An order of two is not rather
impressive (backward Euler has order one), but it has been shown that this is the
highest possible order obtainable by a LM method while maintaining A-stability,
the so-called ”Dahlquist barrier” [42].

so-called A(α) stability as introduced by Widlund [46] for 3 ≤k ≤6.

the negative real axis, and is hence a weaker requirement than A-stability. For
k > 6, parts of the negative real axis become unstable implying that A(α) stability
cannot be maintained and making these methods unﬁt for practical use [35, 42].

4.4
Choice of Implementation
Apart from being academically interesting, the primary objective of the theoret-
ical discussions throughout sections 4.1 to 4.3 have been the concrete choice of a
numerical solver implementation. This section holds a brief description of some
available implementations relevant to the project and the considerations leading
to an actual selection.
Historically, the ﬁrst modern software package for solution of IVPs for stiﬀ
ODE systems is probably the Fortran based ”GEAR” package from the early
1970s by Hindmarsh [48]. This was developed at the Lawrence Livermore National
Laboratory (LLNL) from the ”DIFSUB” routine with which the BDF methods had
been introduced in 1968 by Gear [49].
In the early 1980s, GEAR and its numerous variants were reworked and organ-
ised into the rather famous ”ODEPACK” also by the LLNL [39]. ODEPACK is
written in Fortran 77 and comprises the ”LSODE” (Livermore Solver for Ordinary
Diﬀerential Equations) along with ﬁve variations build for explicitly given systems
of ﬁrst order ODEs [50]. The ODEPACK furthermore contains ”LSODI” along
with two other variants capable of handling systems on linearly implicit form (see
eq. (28)) including the case of a singular [M], i.e. DAEs.
The ODEPACK solvers remain a reference point for numerical solution of IVPs
and are still widely used. In fact, SciPy’s ”scipy.integrate.odeint”, which is used
by Larsen and Santos [27] to solve the bearing state space system from eq. (20),
is actually a wrapper for ODEPACK’s ”LSODA” solver. The same is the case
for Octave’s and R’s build-in IVP solvers for ODEs and even commercial software
such as Maple provides ODEPACK interfaces.
In 1982, Petzold [51] created the ”DASSL” code resembling ODEPACK’s LS-
ODI for linearly implicitly given systems, but specialised for DAEs. This later
evolved into ”DASPK” in corporation with the aforementioned LLNL, still focus-
ing on DAEs.
All of the above solvers are Fortran 77 based, but in the mid-1990s the require-
ment to handle continuously larger systems motivated a move to the C language
[52]. These eﬀorts, still at the LLNL, have evolved into the ”SUNDIALS” (SUite of
Nonlinear and DIﬀerential/ALgebraic equation Solvers) package containing des-
cendants of all of the mentioned solvers (an interesting family tree is given by
Woodward [53]). The package is still maintained actively with its most recent re-
lease being dated August 2015 and new solvers are regularly added. Both Fortran
and MATLAB interfaces are also provided.
Apart from the newly added RK based ”ARKode”, all of the solvers in SUN-
DIALS are based on LM methods. The reason for this is presumably historical,
as advances within the RK methods are making these increasingly attractive for
stiﬀsystems. One alternative code implementing such a method is ”RADAU5”.
==PAGE:41==

This is a Fortran implementation of the aforementioned ﬁfth-order, three stage
”Radau IIA” RK method originally given by Hairer and Wanner [45]. It is meant
for systems on linear implicit form (see eq. (28)) with a possibly singular matrix
[M] (which produces a DAE). In fact, it handles DAEs of index ≤3, whereas the
the LM codes can usually handle only index 1.
Another interesting code is the Fortran based ”MEBDFDAE” by Cash [54].
This is using the so-called ”Modiﬁed Extended Backward Diﬀerentiation Formu-
las”, which seek to overcome the mentioned limitations on A-stability of regular
BDFs. This does, however, seem relatively untested and the code oﬀers far fewer
options than e.g. SUNDIALS.
Lastly, as MATLAB is quite widespread at DTU, it is natural to also men-
tion the rather comprehensive MATLAB ODE suite. It is originally written by
Shampine and Reichelt [36] and currently contains ﬁve solvers capable of handling
stiﬀsystems of ODEs: Ode15s, ode23s, ode23t, ode23tb, and ode15i. The four
ﬁrst are written for the linearly implicit form from eq. (28), but have diﬀerent
approaches. Ode15s is based on LM methods and uses either the standard BDFs
or their close relatives, the ”Numerical Diﬀerentiation Formulas” [36]. Ode23s is a
one-step solver based on a modiﬁed Rosenbrock method [36] and is recommended
for crude tolerances only. This is, however, the solver used by Bonello and Pham
[26] to solve a system very similar to eq. (20). Ode23t stands out as it is based
on the implicit trapezoidal rule usually ascribed to the RK family [55] and can
handle only moderately stiﬀsystems. Ode23tb utilises a hybrid one-step impli-
cit RK-trapezoidal-rule scheme ”TR-BDF2” as described by Hosea and Shampine
[56]. This is, like ode23s, recommended for crude tolerances only. The most gen-
eral of the MATLAB solvers is ode15i which is written for the fully implicit form
(see eq. (23)) and based on the BDF methods [36]. According to Shampine [57],
its performance is comparable to that of DASPK.
In terms of performance, a strict comparison would require an implementation
of the speciﬁc problem at hand using every solver. Even if such were actually made,
the results would inevitable be biased by the exploitation of each language’s (C,
Matlab and Fortran) strengths and weaknesses. Any direct and fair comparison is
hence very diﬃcult. Nevertheless, a recent comparison between RADAU5, MAT-
LAB’s ode15s and SUNDIALS’ CVODE using ten test problems from the ﬁeld of
systems biology by Gonnet et al. [58] does provide some insight. Examining these
results, CVODE generally performs better than both ode15s and RADAU5, but
this conclusion is obviously subject to both the speciﬁc problems, solver options
and implementation details.
Apart from performance, the ease of implementing each solver has also been
an important aspect. Since the existing pieces of code were written in Python and
C, using the SUNDIALS package would limit the number of language interfaces.

One of the

banded and full Jacobians along with an interface to LAPACK, sparse solvers using

For any linear solver, a construction of the Jacobian matrix from eq. (48) is
obviously necessary. Ideally, an analytical expression for this should be provided
and, for some cases, it is even critical to success [36]. Even though Bonello and
Pham [26] have actually managed to create such an expression for their system, it
is not a simple task. It would most likely be possible to construct some, at least
approximate, expression for the Jacobian and this would likely result in a signiﬁc-
ant reduction of integration time. No such expression has however been developed
within this project and the integration hence relies on a numerical approximation.

The sparsity pattern of the Jacobian is however easily obtained by studying
the dependencies of eq. (20). Even if the equations are reorganised, the Jacobian
bandwidth is, unfortunately, close to the full system dimension, meaning that
no signiﬁcant advantage can be achieved using a banded Jacobian approximation.
Some tests of the dense solvers have shown the LAPACK ones to be faster than the
SUNDIALS build in ones. This is, however, dependent on the installed LAPACK-
/BLAS distribution and its level of optimisation. For the comparisons presented
in the next section, they are on par.
The included iterative solvers include GMRES (Generalized Minimal RESid-
ual), Bi-CGStab (Bi-Conjugate Gradient Stabilized) and TFQMR (Transpose-Free
Quasi-Minimal Residual) implementations. These are generally dependent on a
preconditioning matrix and according to the documentation [62] they are rarely
successful without. Such preconditioning matrix should be a rough approximation
to the Jacobian, leaving the same issue as for the direct solvers. Benchmarks us-
ing the present problem have however shown that even without a preconditioning
matrix the iterative solvers perform impressively well. For the smallest resolutions
considered, the Bi-CGStab solver gives an overall integration time an order of
magnitude lower than of the LAPACK direct solvers. Furthermore, the iterative
solvers have no need for storing the entire iteration matrix and hence lowers the
memory requirement signiﬁcantly. The memory requirement of a single simulation
is hardly a problem for a modern computer, but the purpose of these simulations
will often be waterfall diagrams. In this case it is beneﬁcial to run several simula-
tions with diﬀerent angular velocities ω in parallel making the memory requirement
important.
Even though the iterative solvers have proven to be very fast compared to
the direct methods, they do introduce an additional level of approximation to
the system. In the programme, the error tolerances (relative and absolute) are
speciﬁed for the time integration. Apart from the error given by the integration
scheme itself, some error will also be contributed from the nonlinear solution.
To keep this nonlinear iteration error from interfering with the local integration
error, the iteration is continued until an error 10 times lower than the speciﬁed
tolerance has been achieved (this ”safety” factor is CVODE’s default). When using
a direct solver, each linear system is solved to machine precision and no further
(reducible) error is introduced. In the case of the iterative solvers, on the contrary,
a third source of error is introduced and needs to be controlled. To keep this linear
iteration error from interfering with the nonlinear iteration error, it is reduced by
an additional factor of 20 (again, this is CVODE’s default factor). Experience has
shown this to be particular important if the time simulation code is used to asses
the rotordynamic stability of a given conﬁguration. One approach to asses the
rotordynamic stability is to initiate a time simulation using a perfectly balanced

The sparse solvers provided by ”KLU” or ”SuperLU_MT” have not been
tested, as no routine for approximating the Jacobian while taking advantage of
sparsity is included in SUNDIALS. In MATLAB, an option is provided to specify
the Jacobians’s sparsity pattern allowing it to be exploited in the numerical ap-

this [63, 64], and some rather sophisticated graph colouring techniques have more
recently been presented [65]. This could be an interesting subject for further work,
as an exploitation of the known sparsity pattern could possibly make the sparse
solvers competitive to the iterative ones.
5.2
Performance
As the architecture of the new code is completely diﬀerent from that of the old
one, the system requirements are balanced diﬀerently between e.g. processor and
memory. Furthermore, the additional layer of approximation introduced with the
iterative linear solvers gives these an increasing competitive advantage for lower
tolerances. Even though this makes a ”fair” direct comparison diﬃcult to make,
some benchmarks are presented in this section.
For the benchmarking simulations, the setup given in table 1 is used. The test
rig data are those of the Siemens foil bearing test rig as described in [66]. The
rotor is dropped from the centre at 13 kRPM and 0.2 s is simulated. This is slightly
less than, but comparable to, simulations of practical value. The benchmarking
simulations have been performed one at the time on a desktop computer with
8 GB of memory, a 3.33 GHz Intel Core i5 processor and a conventional hard disk
drive. Timings of these simulations are shown in ﬁg. 5.3 for both the old code
and the new one using three diﬀerent linear solvers. These are the Bi-CGStab (Bi-
Conjugate Gradient Stabilized) iterative solver denoted ”CVSpbcg”, the LAPACK
direct solver DGETRS denoted ”CVLapackDense” and the internal CVODE direct
solver denoted ”CVDense”. According to the documentation of SciPy’s LSODA
wrapper used in the old code, the default absolute and relative tolerances are set
to 1.490 12·10−8. The methods for evaluating the error are evidently identical, but
a slightly lower value of 1·10−8 has been used in the new code to be on the safe
side.

sion as presented in eqs. (19) and (20). For the results presented by Larsen and

Convergence
The spatial resolution of around 75 nodes/pad used with the old time simulation
code was partly dictated by time constraints. The required computational time
made higher resolutions unfeasible and no sound convergence studies have hence
been possible. A convergence study has been made for the related steady-state
implementation as presented in [20]. According to this, a much higher resolution of
around 900 nodes/pad should in fact be necessary for convergence of eccentricities.
Even with the much lower number of nodes, the time simulation code has however
proven to produce results in good agreement to experimentally obtained data – at
least in terms of frequencies. With the reduced simulation time of the new code,
a natural step is hence to investigate the convergence of the time simulation code
directly.
For this purpose, the conﬁguration also used for benchmarking in section 5.2
is simulated with a gradually reﬁned mesh.

Preliminary Parameter Study
Considering the speed of the new code, a thorough parameter study would be a
natural next step. A potential – and highly desirable – outcome of such a study
would be an elucidation of the causal relationships between the sub-synchronous
vibration characteristics and design parameters. An extensive study would require
a large parameter space, including e.g. pad placement, preload and number of pads
to be sweept. This has not been possible within the time frame of this project,
but some preliminary results are presented in this section.
A main parameter of an AFB conﬁguration is the number of foil pads, and it
would be interesting to study its inﬂuence on the dynamic response of the system.
In order to alter the number of pads, one needs to decide on new leading and
trailing edge angles, θl, θt. For the three-pad conﬁguration, these have been given
from the Siemens test bearing, but no such reference is immediately available
for the one- and two-pad cases. Performing just a few tests, it has been clear
that this conﬁguration is essential for the AFB performance regardless of the pad
number.
It is hence diﬃcult to separately asses the eﬀect of altering the pad
number and that of the varying pad angles. For this work, the pad spacing of 5◦
has been retained for all pad numbers and the simplest imaginable one- and two-
pad conﬁgurations have been used. These are illustrated in ﬁgs. 7.1a and 7.1b along
with the ”standard” three-pad conﬁguration in ﬁg. 7.1c. These pad placements
prevent numerical problems as the gaps and associated pressure discontinuities are
placed in safe distance from the maximum ﬂuid ﬁlm pressures. They are however
likely to be suboptimal and the comparison to the three-pad bearing is hence not
strictly fair.

instability is marked by the gradually growing vibration at around 100 Hz.
Similar waterfall diagrams for the one- and two-pad cases are shown in ﬁgs. 7.4

It should be noted that the foil stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients used for the
case of three pads have been directly reused. This is not unproblematic as these
coeﬃcients are calculated based on a number of assumptions regarding the pad
loading. The bumps carrying the majority of the load in the one-pad case will, as
an example, have to push almost 180◦of bumps in order to slide. In the three-pad
case, this is closer to 85◦. The coeﬃcients are however subject to such signiﬁcant
assumptions and inaccuracy that attempting to calculate speciﬁc one- or two-pad
values would make limited sense without experimental data for validation.

This second part of the report treats the development of an enhanced foil structure

8
Modelling of Friction
Friction, especially the case of dry friction, is a highly nonlinear phenomenon and
various approaches to friction modelling are possible. Since this is a key concept in
the current work, this section presents a discussion on some of the available mod-
els. Three diﬀerent approaches are presented, relying on stick-slip bookkeeping, a
continuous friction approximation and nonlinear springs, respectively.
8.1
The Stick-Slip Approach
As dry friction in the Coulomb model [68] is divided into static and kinematic
regimes, one obvious approach would be to maintain this distinction in the model.
Such a stick-slip model is presented by Lee et al. [24] and has, previously, been
implemented along with an implicit Newmark time integration scheme for the
single bump model to be described in section 9.1. Any structure could in principle
have served as test bench, but this was chosen for comparability to especially the
nonlinear spring model to be treated in section 8.2. A ﬂow chart of this particular
implementation is shown in ﬁg. 8.1.
The details of such an implementation will of course vary, but it is essential
that a stick/slip ﬂag is continuously maintained for each of the friction point nodes.
This ﬂag is used to determine whether a node should be immobilised by imposing
a boundary condition (static friction) or subjected to a dynamic friction force
dependent on the normal force, direction of slip and coeﬃcient of friction.
In the case of sticking, the boundary conditions should be renewed and even
though penalisation or Lagrangian multiplier based alternatives do exist, this typ-

constrained matrix to be inconvenient, this modiﬁcation requires a full re-assembly
of the matrix. For solving-procedures relying on some sort of factorisation/back-
substitution scheme, the modiﬁcation will furthermore require a re-factorisation.
Since the cost of each matrix modiﬁcation depends heavily on the system size,
potentially to the power of 3 [67], the importance of these issues grows rapidly
for an increasing number of DOFs. Since an update of boundary conditions is
triggered if any node changes stick/slip status, also the number of friction points
will be decisive for the eﬃciency of such a method.
In the case of slipping, the reaction forces will have to be calculated for the
friction point nodes and updated dynamic friction forces should be applied to the
right hand side vector. This introduces several diﬃculties dependent on weather a
(quasi-) static or dynamic model (including mass and potentially damping) is used.
In the case of a static model, no inherent sense of time exists, meaning that wave
propagation is not an issue when calculating reaction forces. On the contrary, no
inertia is present neither, meaning that any sudden change in the applied forces
or reaction forces must also imply a sudden change in nodal displacements to
retain equilibrium. Such sudden changes are very diﬃcult to reconcile with any
evaluation of nodal velocities, which are also required.
Results from the implemented dynamic model suggest that, dependent on the
applied viscous structural and/or algorithmic damping, very small time steps are
necessary to prevent the reaction forces from ﬂuctuating signiﬁcantly between steps
– even without a friction model. With the addition of the stick/slip model, the
problem is further ampliﬁed as the abrupt changes of the boundary conditions add
to the ﬂuctuations.
For the tested ≈200 DOF single-bump model with two friction points, the
mentioned considerations regarding computational eﬀort have not proven prohib-
itive. The primary eﬀorts have rather been related to the numerical stability of the
algorithm. Depending on the structure’s wave propagation properties, the applied
load and averaging method, the step-to-step ﬂuctuations of the reaction forces
pose a signiﬁcant challenge for tolerable time steps sizes. Both the dynamic fric-
tion forces applied to the slipping nodes and the allowable static friction forces used
to evaluate the retention of the sticking nodes depend directly on these reaction
forces. This means that any ﬂuctuation easily causes numerical instability. Also

This third method is conceptually related to the nonlinear spring based model
discussed in the previous section, as it is also bypassing the stick-slip problematic

between friction coeﬃcient and slipping velocity µf ( ˙xr) of true Coulomb friction is

nonlinear friction function in order to provide anything but simple viscous friction.

far less pronounced since no abrupt changes are made.

Modelling of Corrugated Foil
While the SEFM provides an extremely simple and widely used structural foil
model, it suﬀers from several rather extensive inherent limitations. This section
begins with a discussion of a very detailed, but computationally heavy, model from
[28] and subsequently a much simpler truss approximation from [14]. Sections 9.3
to 9.5 covers the truss assembly and the inclusion of mass and damping, while
some validation of the model is presented in section 9.6.
9.1
Nonlinear Plane Element Foil Model
The existing two-dimensional bump foil model presented in [28] is implemented
in Fortran code using an isoparamtric formulation of a 4-node plane quadrilateral
element often denoted ”Q4” [61]. The plane model is illustrated with a very coarse
discretisation of 52 lengthwise and one radial element in ﬁg. 9.1. Since the foils
are rather long (axially) compared to their thickness, a plane strain assumption is
used to form the constitutive matrix of the elements. To represent loading from
the top foil, the uppermost point of the bump is either subjected to a point force
P or enforced a certain deﬂection. The model is constrained by horizontal roller
supports along its left edge (d, e) where the actual foil is ﬁxed, and by vertical roller
supports at each bearing housing contact point (a, b). The contact point supports
could prove problematic if included in an actual bearing simulation, since the foil
is thus not allowed to separate from the housing. However, for the applied positive
loads P and downward pointing deﬂections, this is unimportant. Additional bumps
are added to the right, so that only the leftmost bump is clamped.
While the Fortran implementation uses nonlinear large deformation theory and
the model is solved incrementally using a NR procedure, no mass is included and
dynamical eﬀects are hence not considered. This eﬀectively means that the model
can represent quasi-static solutions only.
According to convergence studies [28], around 400 lengthwise elements per
bump and 8 elements through the ﬁlm thickness is necessary for convergence, and
the shown mesh is hence for illustration purposes only. This implies that approxim-
ately 3500 nodes, or 7000 DOFs, are necessary for describing a single bump. At the
time of building the model, it was uncertain whether the geometrical nonlinearity
would have a signiﬁcant contribution. Later, as the predictions have been com-
pared to experimental results (see section 9.6), is has become clear that the bump
foil behaviour does not substantiate the need for the applied large deformation
theory. Even though the nonlinear friction model obviously requires a nonlinear
solver procedure, the remaining structure essentially behaves linearly within the
applied range of deformation (~25 µm compared to a bump height of 0.9 mm).

9.4
Inclusion of Foil Mass
The structural model by Le Lez et al. [14], as presented in the previous section,
provides a set of purely algebraic equations which can be expressed on residual
form as:
{0} = f ({x}) = [K]{x} −{f}.
(61)
Such a system has no inherent notion of time and hence only quasi-static results
can be obtained from its solution. The AFB equation system, on the contrary,
is a set of ODEs and are solved in time as an IVP. If the algebraic equations
of such a static foil model were coupled directly to the diﬀerential equations of
the shaft and ﬂuid ﬁlm, the overall structure of the equation system would no
longer be that of an ODE. Instead, the system would belong to the wider class
of DAEs. This would, in itself, not present an insurmountable obstacle as the
==PAGE:78==
60
9

resulting semi-explicit DAE would be of index 1 only, and numerical methods for
such IVPs are readily available. One example is SUNDIALS’s ”IDA” solver, which
could probably have handled the system.
The main issue arise with the inclusion of a friction model. All of the presented
friction models require some knowledge of sliding velocities and it is indeed diﬃcult
to imagine such a model without this requirement. The problem is that these
sliding velocities would be associated to DOFs governed by algebraic equations
only, implying that no expression for their time derivatives would be present. One
could, obviously, obtain these velocities from a number of previous time steps using
some FD expression. In this project, a lot of eﬀort has in fact been put into various
such approaches with varying success. The fundamental problem is that any such
FD approach would wreck the simultaneous nature of the entire mathematical
model. Even if such a model was made and proved numerically stable (which has
turned out to be very diﬃcult), it would defeat the main advantage of the applied
modelling approach and reintroduce the need for temporal convergence studies.
Le Lez et al. [15] present an admittedly elegant manipulation of the static model
from [14] to form a system of ODEs without introducing the foil mass. It is argued,
as it is widely recognised, that the dynamic eﬀects of the foil has no inﬂuence on
the system and hence that neither mass nor internal damping should be included.
In [22], a coupling of the massless foil ODE to a time simulation code from [19] is
presented. In this coupling process, even though the model is reformulated with
alternative state variables, the problem of missing time derivatives is eventually
encountered and bypassed by introduction of an FD approximation.
In [15], a rough, but impressively accurate, estimate of the lowest natural fre-
quency for one bump is given to be around 9 kHz. Models from this project conﬁrm
this, and even though it actually drops to around 2 kHz for multiple bumps, this
is still way above the frequency range of interest. A rule of thumb given by Cook
et al. [61] suggests that dynamic eﬀects can be neglected if the loading frequency
is less than one-quarter the lowest natural frequency. Considering this, the inﬂu-
ence of foil structure dynamics is undoubtedly of very limited, if any, importance
to the overall rotordynamic response and it would deﬁnitely be reasonable to not
include these eﬀects. This is especially true since the very low mass and relatively
high stiﬀness of the foils imply some very high natural frequencies (> 100 kHz),
and hence pose a challenge to the numerical integration. It is however, a main
conclusion of this project that a truly simultaneous mathematical model of the
bearing system relying on friction cannot be realised without the mass of the foil.

9.5
Inclusion of Internal Damping
With the inclusion of mass in the structural foil model, some very high natural
frequencies are also introduced. This means that vibrations with frequencies far
above those of interest are easily excited. Apart from being non-physical, such
high-frequency vibrations require the numerical integration procedure to lower its
step size signiﬁcantly in order to resolve the behaviour. This is clearly undesirable
and have been a challenge to the project. In reality, the bump-foil strips behave
overly damped as friction – and possibly also air resistance – is dissipating energy
very eﬃciently. Small-scale numerical tests have shown that this behaviour can
be mimicked by the present dynamic foil model subjected to friction alone. It
does, however, become much more stable if at least some internal damping is also
included. For this purpose a damping matrix representing the internal dissipation
of energy should be constructed. This should, ideally, be harsh enough to trim oﬀ
the high-frequency vibrations, while not being dominant compared to the friction
driven dissipation. The latter is especially important, since a main purpose of these
eﬀorts is exactly to eliminate the viscous damping presumption of the SEFM. In
the following, the classical Rayleigh approach and an extended version of this are
discussed.
When modelling the internal energy dissipation of a mechanical system, clas-

From a mathematical point of view, this property makes it a very attractive
damping model in the present case. The domain of interest below approximately
500 Hz should ideally remain undamped, while the natural frequencies ranging
from ~2 kHz and up should be damped out to aid the integration. Subject to
ﬂoating-point arithmetic, however, the α2 damping has proven quite diﬃcult to
implement. This is due to the multiplication of [K] by itself, which entails that the
ratio between its largest and smallest number is squared. The mass matrices of this
project are diagonal and it is possible to achieve very low condition numbers of [M]
by applying a suitable length scaling. The mass matrix will hence serve merely as
a close-to-uniform scaling of the damping matrix [K][M]−1[K] and consequently
have little eﬀect on its conditioning. The stiﬀness matrix, on the contrary, includes
values of very diﬀerent magnitudes, partly because it comprises both linear and
rotational DOFs. The conditioning of [K] can also, to some extent, be improved by
appropriate non-dimensionalisation, but it is diﬃcult to lower it suﬃciently. Even
a stiﬀness matrix conditioning number of 105, considered very sensible for many
cases, implies a conditioning number of the damping matrix in the order of 1010. All
though inversion of the matrix is not actually required in the integration procedure,
such conditioning numbers have proven to make it impossible for the solver to
achieve the requested integration tolerances. This is presumably the reason why
the method has not become more popular in spite of its obvious attractiveness.

10.2
Dimensionless Form
As for the ﬂuid ﬁlm and shaft models, a dimensionless form of the foil structure
equations should be applied to ensure a suﬃciently well-scaled equation system. It
would be straightforward to apply the same length, force and time scales as used
for the existing equations, as this would make the equations directly compatible.
While the time and force scales given by ω and paR2 are tolerable for the foil
structure, the length scale C is far from optimal.
Experience has revealed the conditioning of the mass matrix to be especially
important, which makes sense as this is explicitly inverted. While the elements of
the bump foil mass matrix are all identical, the homogeneity of the top foil mass
matrix from eq. (82) dependents on the element length. This suggests that a length
scale close to this element length would be suitable. Attempts to ﬁnd an optimal
length scale in terms of matrix conditioning using the minimisation algorithms

10.3
Assembled Foil Structure Model
A complete model suitable for time simulation can now be assembled from the
discussed bump foil, top foil and frictional models. For this purpose, a reference
system with three DOFs per node, as illustrated in ﬁg. 10.1 for a two-bump foil
pad, is used. Once the system matrices for this system have been assembled, three
things need to be done:
1. Enforcement of indicated boundary conditions and removal of unused DOFs
2. Coupling of corresponding top and bump foil DOFs
3. Reorganisation of equations to give consecutive top foil vertical DOFs
For a static problem involving only the stiﬀness matrix and a displacement vec-
tor, the enforcement of boundary conditions is usually achieved using the simple
method of ”zeros and ones” [61].
The linear equation system to be solved re-
peatedly throughout the time stepping procedure was presented in eq. (47). Con-
sidering this, it is evident that the ”zeros and ones” method could be applied by
zeroing out the constrained rows and columns of the system matrices to produce
a singular Jacobian matrix (see eq. (48)), and then to continuously enforce zeros
in the state space vector. It is unclear if the singularity of the Jacobian matrix
would pose any issues to the solver, but as no direct access is given to the iteration
procedure, drift-oﬀduring each time step could be a problem. Furthermore, as the
system is to be solved thousands of times, it would be vastly ineﬃcient to retain
the unnecessary equations.
Instead, all three objectives stated above are achieved using a single trans-
formation matrix. Coding the automatic construction of such matrices has been
slightly tedious, but the resulting method is quite elegant. Using this transform-
ation, the unnecessary and constrained DOFs are explicitly removed. The same
transformation links the foil structures by imposing so-called multi-freedom con-
straints to connect the vertical DOF of each bump to its corresponding top foil
DOF. Referring to the example structure in ﬁg. 10.1, DOFs 1, 7 and 13 would
hence be mapped to 19, 22 and 28, respectively. Lastly, the transformation serves

COUPLED MODEL EQUATION SYSTEM
This new equation system maintains the structure of a nonlinear ﬁrst order explicit
system of ODEs and can hence be solved using the same techniques as for the
existing system from eq. (20). In the new model it has been exploited that the foil
height is assumed constant across the axial width, such that only one ˜hc variable
is used per tangential position. In the old code, such variable is included for every
node as it incorporates options allowing for axial variation. For the old system,
this would have reduced the number of state equation by 40–50 %, but here the
auxiliary foil DOFs of the new foil structure model are added. Furthermore, the
circumferential resolution of the new mesh is dictated by the bump pitch and the
requested number of top foil elements per bump. For the foil conﬁguration from
table 2, this is in practice increasing the minimal achievable number of equation
to around 2500. That is, if four top foil elements per bump pitch is used and an
element aspect ratio of three is allowed. At the same time, the new system is
signiﬁcantly more diﬃcult to handle for the solver and thus requires much smaller
time steps.
The required computational eﬀort is, in other words, signiﬁcantly
increased in comparison to the optimised SEFM based code.

Results
In this section some preliminary results from the new coupled model will be presen-
ted. So far, these do not exhibit any enhancement in the prediction accuracy com-
pared to the SEFM based results. In fact, the are farther oﬀ. The code is still
in its development phase and it is expected that additional tuning of the friction
model – and potentially some debugging – will improve this. The presented results
do, however, demonstrate the model to be numerically stable and solvable within
reasonable time frames.
For demonstration purposes, the same simulation as used in section 7 is per-
formed using the new model. This means that the bearing conﬁguration from
table 1 is used, but with a slightly increased bearing A mass eccentricity of
uA = 50 g mm.
The new model requires a more detailed deﬁnition of the foil
structure, for which the standard foil parameters in table 2 are used. Stiﬀness pro-
portional damping is applied to the foil structure with the α1 parameter chosen to
provide ζ = 0.001 at 500 Hz. Coulomb friction is approximated using the model
from eq. (52), where γ4 corresponds to the dynamic friction coeﬃcient and γ5
controls the level of smoothing. The remaining γi values are set to zero. The
coeﬃcient of friction is set to µf = 0.15 for both the top foil and housing contacts
and a smoothing parameter of 1·105 is used.
As the mesh is constructed in an entirely new manner, it is diﬃcult to apply
a mesh identical to that used for the SEFM model. Each pad of the test rig foil
bearing has an angular extend of 115◦which, considering the 33.5 mm bearing
radius and the 7 mm bump foil pitch, should give 9.6 bumps. This is clearly not
possible and nine bumps are used instead. The number of top foil elements per
bump is set to four, which is expected to be the minimally allowable. This gives a
top foil element length of 1.75 mm and hence 38 top foil beam elements per pad.
As a uniform mesh is used, this implies that each pad is modelled as being 0.7 mm
too short. For three pads this gives 117 nodes around the circumference and, if the
ﬂuid ﬁlm element aspect ratio is limited to three, 819 ﬂuid ﬁlm nodes per bearing.
For the old SEFM model mesh, this would have implied an overall number of 3284
state equations. As already mentioned, a signiﬁcant saving is achieved for the
coupled model by using only one hc variable per circumferential division. The new
foil structure, on the other hand, adds 576 equations per bearing, meaning that
the overall system dimension becomes 2798.
It is evident from the walk-through of the mesh construction that the obtainable
mesh conﬁgurations are much more limited for the new model. A coarsening could
be achieved either by halving the number of top foil elements per bump or by
allowing a higher aspect ratio to give fewer elements in the axial direction. The
presented conﬁguration is however considered close to minimal.

Conclusion
13.1
Part I
The ﬁrst project objective was to optimise the AFB prediction code presented
by Larsen and Santos [27]. This has resulted in an almost complete rework of
the code using Python for preparatory functions and C for the computationally
heavy parts. The time integration is now performed using the modern CVODE
solver and a very fast iterative linear solver, the ”bi-conjugate gradient stabilized
method”, is utilised.
For the coarsest applied spatial resolutions, the new implementation has been
demonstrated to be at least 150 times faster than the existing one. This improve-
ment has made convergence studies practical and especially the nonlinear eﬀects
have shown to converge slowly. In this sense, it is equally important that the solu-
tion time of the new implementation scales with the system dimension to a power
of approximately 1.8. This number was around 2.4 for the old implementation. At
the same time, the demand for memory has been lowered signiﬁcantly, meaning
that parallel simulations, or simulations with much higher spatial resolution, have
become possible.
While the reduced simulation time is mainly a result of numerical optimisation,
it contributes to the practical applicability of nonlinear time-domain analysis and
hence to the AFB research. Especially in the case of AFBs, time-domain methods
are capable of describing rotordynamic phenomena inaccessible to the frequency-
domain methods. Nevertheless, their application in the design phase has hitherto
been inhibited by the computational expense. Running the new implementation on
a laptop, a waterfall diagram showcasing the rotordynamic response of a bearing
conﬁguration can be obtained in around half an hour. This makes such analysis
a much more operable alternative – or supplement – to existing frequency-domain
methods.
A few results obtained using the new model has also been presented. It is
shown that the sub-synchronous vibrations are symmetrically aligned around the
1
2x frequency, and that they do not change qualitatively with the number of pads.
It is furthermore suggested that the appearance, bifurcations and disappearance of
the sub-synchronous components could be related to the crossing between natural
frequencies and the 1
2x frequency.
13.2
Part II
The second objective of the project has been to overcome the inherent limitations
accompanying the applied SEFM. While the SEFM is widely used and has shown
==PAGE:112==
94
13
CONCLUSION
to provide reasonable results, it relies on some rather extensive assumptions and
requires signiﬁcant a priori knowledge of the system.
In this project, a dynamic foil structure model has been created based partly
on the work of Le Lez et al. [14].
It incorporates a friction model and hence
circumvents the requirement for a viscous dissipation approximation.
Also, as
the foil structure is actually modelled, no assumptions regarding the number of
active bumps are necessary. While the dynamic foil model per se is not particularly
sophisticated, the necessity of including the foil mass is a key ﬁnding of the project.
Even though it is widely recognised that the foil dynamics are negligible, it has
been shown that any massless foil model including frictional eﬀects will inhibit a
simultaneous solution of the state variables. As the simultaneity of the present
model is a key feature, any foil model to be incorporated should obviously allow
this feature to be maintained.
The dynamic foil structure model has successfully been coupled to the ﬂuid ﬁlm
and rigid shafts models, producing a pioneering AFB model. A few preliminary
results have been presented which indicate the foil structure model to behave
overly soft and to introduce too much damping. No experimental results have
been available for an isolated validation of the foil structure model within the
excitation frequencies of interest, but the remaining comparisons show reasonable
agreement. Currently, the issues are primarily ascribed to the eﬀective stiﬀness of
the structure, which should be validated through comparison to the SEFM based
model with equivalent stiﬀness.
Computationally, the new model is considerably more expensive than the op-
timised SEFM model even for comparable spatial resolutions. The presented pre-
liminary results are however not yet suﬃcient to judge whether this cost can be
justiﬁed. Additionally, the new model can not be used with resolutions as low as
for the SEFM based model which prohibits the very fast low resolution screening
simulations possible with the SEFM based model.
13.3
Future Work
At least two possibilities for further optimisation of the code have not yet been
exploited. If maintaining the assumption of constant axial foil deformation, the
system dimension could in fact be reduced by 40-50 % by using only one hc variable
per circumferential position. As the simulation time currently scales by 1.8, this
could potentially reduce the simulation time by a factor close to 3.5. Secondly, it is
very likely that supplying an analytical Jacobian, or merely a rough approximation,
would speed up the simulation considerably. This is no easy task, but some clues
are given in [25, 26].
A possible extension of the model – relevant to both the SEFM and dynamic
foil structure based versions – would be a shell based top foil model. Such a model
==PAGE:113==

Future work

has been created and is currently being used by Bo Bjerregaard Nielsen from the
research group. Including this would eliminate the approximate factor of four on
the top foil stiﬀness and allow for the inclusion of several independent bump foil
slices – as it is actually the case in the modelled second generation AFBs.
An obvious continuation of the present work would be to make use of the
very fast prediction code to conduct a thorough parameter study. This could,
potentially, provide new insights on the causal relationships between AFB design
parameters and their subsequent rotordynamic behaviour. Such insights would, in
many aspects, be of higher value than the code itself.
Lastly, the presented results from the coupled model are very limited and the
model still requires some adjustment.
To clarify how the enhanced foil model
is actually contributing to the accuracy of the predictions, further analysis and
comparison to experimental data are required. It is possible that the remaining
details of the experimental waterfall diagrams could be replicated, but another
possible conclusion would be to simply maintain the SEFM.
