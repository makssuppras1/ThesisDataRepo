Abstract
This thesis investigates how the design of streetscapes shapes people’s transport mode
choices in the Greater Copenhagen area. By combining advanced analysis of Google
Street View imagery with a multinomial logit model, the study assesses whether visually
derived clusters of streetscape types provide additional explanatory power beyond tra­
ditional factors such as population density, distance to station, and socio­demographic
characteristics. Case studies of selected station areas reveal considerable variation in
both the built environment and travel behaviour, but also show that the relationship be­
tween streetscape clusters and mode choice is not always straightforward. While urban
clusters tend to support walking, cycling, and public transport use, car use remains sig­
nificant even in some dense areas, suggesting the influence of other factors such as trip
distance and accessibility. The MNL models indicate that adding streetscape cluster vari­
ables leads to a modest improvement in model fit, and that these clusters partly overlap
with existing explanatory variables. Overall, the findings highlight the value of integrat­
ing micro­scale visual information into mode choice models while also acknowledging the
continued importance of classic determinants. The study concludes with a discussion of
limitations and directions for future research, including the need for more detailed route­
based analysis and comparative studies in other metropolitan contexts.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Cities around the world are facing escalating challenges related to sustainable mobility,
liveability, and public health. Increasing urbanisation, congestion, and emissions have
prompted planners and policymakers to search for new strategies to encourage walk­
ing, cycling, and public transport use, and to reduce car dependence. While decades of
research have established clear links between the built environment and transport mode
choice, most existing studies focus on broad, macro­scale characteristics such as density,
land use mix, and proximity to public transit. However, recent advances in digital map­
ping, computer vision, and the availability of street­level imagery now make it possible to
quantify and analyse the visual street­level aspects of the built environment, commonly
referred to as streetscape design. There is a growing recognition that the everyday vi­
sual and physical qualities of streets such as greenery and sidewalk presence, may play
a significant, and potentially underappreciated, role in shaping people’s travel decisions.
Despite the promise of these new methods, significant gaps remain in our understand­
ing of how streetscape features influence mode choice, particularly when compared to
more traditional built environment measures, and how these relationships play out across
different types of urban contexts.
1.2
Scope
This thesis investigates the relationship between streetscape design and transport mode
choice in the Greater Copenhagen area, defined by the boundaries of the zonal system
used in the transport model: Copenhagen Greater Area Model for Passenger Transport
(COMPASS). The geographical boundary can be seen in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Geographical boundary of the Greater Copenhagen area (Paag et al., 2019).

The study combines travel survey data with image­based measures of the built environ­
ment, using segmented Google Street View (GSV) images to capture detailed streetscape
characteristics at the origins and destinations of individual trips. The analysis is limited
to trips that both start and end within the metropolitan area, with a special focus on five
diverse station areas: Glostrup, Hillerød, Stenløse­Egedal, Østerport, and a combined
group of small towns (Viby Sjælland, Havdrup, Gadstrup, Lille Skensved, and Borup).
The research focuses on home­activity and activity­home trips made for both work and
leisure purposes. While the study is comprehensive within its spatial and methodological
boundaries, it does not include the quality of streetscape environments along trip routes
or capture longitudinal changes over time.
The learning objectives of the study can be seen in the Appendix in A.1.
1.3
Research Question
The thesis is guided by the following research question:
What is the relationship between streetscape design and transport mode choice in the
Copenhagen metropolitan area, and which characteristics are influencing people’s travel
behaviour?
1.4
Study Design and Analytical Approach
To address this question, the study adopts a multi­step, quantitative research design.
First, semantic segmented GSV images from within a 1km radius of each relevant sta­
tion area will be used to classify key streetscape elements. The resulting data are then
used in a cluster analysis to identify distinct types of urban environments based on their
streetscape composition. Then individual trip data from the Danish National Travel Sur­
vey are merged with the streetscape clusters at trip origins and destinations, along with
additional explanatory variables.
In the second stage, a case analyses of the five selected station areas are conducted to
describe and compare streetscape clusters and associated travel behaviours.
In the third stage, multinomial logit (MNL) models are estimated to analyse how both
classic variables and the streetscape clusters influence mode choice. The performance
of models with and without streetscape cluster variables is compared to assess the added
value of detailed streetscape information.
This integrated approach enables the study to move beyond simple descriptive associ­
ations, isolating the potential effects of streetscape design on sustainable transport be­
haviour while also accounting for other factors that might influence the results.

2
Literature review
Numerous studies have sought to identify what determines individuals’ choices of trans­
port mode, drawing from a wide array of factors including socio­demographics, personal
attitudes, and broader societal trends. While these classic determinants remain impor­
tant, there is a growing recognition in the literature that the built environment plays a role in
shaping how people move through cities. Traditionally, research has examined these built
environment influences at the macro scale (for example, considering factors like density
or proximity to transit at the neighbourhood or city level). However, recent work increas­
ingly emphasises the significance of microscale characteristics, which are the features of
streets as experienced at eye level. This is often referred to as streetscape design. This
shift has been enabled by advances in data and methods, particularly the use of GSV
and computer vision, which allow for large­scale and objective measurement of the street
environment.
Accordingly, this review narrows its focus to the built environment. Particularly streetscape
design and its role in shaping transport mode choice will be the focus. The chapter ad­
dresses: (2.1) the main factors influencing mode choice, (2.2) the role of streetscape
design in shaping these choices, and (2.3) how GSV­based analysis is advancing the
field.
2.1
Determinants of Transport Mode Choice
Classic determinants of transport mode choice include trip distance, car ownership, in­
come and accessibility to high­quality public transport.
Empirical research in Copen­
hagen demonstrates that proximity to high­class stations (such as metro or suburban rail),
high service frequency, and perceived safety at stations are all positively associated with
choosing public transport over private car. Socio­economic factors, including income and
gender, also play a significant role: lower­income residents and women are more likely
to choose public transport or active modes, while car ownership strongly predicts car use
(Ingvardson & Nielsen, 2022).
Başaran et al. (2025) expand this perspective by focusing not on origins and destinations,
but specifically on the characteristics at the home and activity ends of each trip. Analysing
travel survey data and station audits from the Greater Copenhagen area, they found that
the influence of transit­oriented development (TOD) characteristics varies depending on
whether it is present at the home or activity end. In particular, higher TOD scores at the
activity end of a trip significantly increased public transport use, while perceived safety at
both trip ends also encouraged more people to choose public transport. Their findings
suggest that enhancing TOD and safety at areas close to stations is crucial for encourag­
ing public transport use.
While the previous studies highlight classic determinants and station­level factors, it is
worth noting that some research looks at the neighbourhood scale. For instance, activity­
based modelling has shown that density, land use mix, and the overall form of neighbour­
hoods can shape travel behaviour in complex ways. Interestingly, similar levels of walking
or public transport use can result from quite different neighbourhood patterns and daily
routines (Liu et al., 2021). This suggests that to fully understand mode choice, it is useful
to combine insights from both neighbourhood­ and street­level research.

Wider reviews, like Elzeni et al. (2022), remind us that walkability and mode choice are
shaped by a complex mix of social, spatial, visual, and climatic factors at both the neigh­
bourhood and street level. Supporting this, Li et al. (2023) find that neighbourhood­scale
features such as population density and distance to metro stations remain major drivers
of public transport ridership across different modes, even as research increasingly zooms
in to the street level.
Mouratidis et al. (2019) find that walking is consistently rated as the most satisfying travel
mode, while car commuting is the least satisfying. However, their results show that the
higher satisfaction reported by residents of compact neighbourhoods is explained by their
ability to make shorter trips and thus use more active modes. This highlights how urban
form shapes both mode choice and travel satisfaction primarily by influencing trip dis­
tance and the feasibility of different transport modes, rather than having a direct effect on
satisfaction itself.
2.2
Streetscape Design and Mode Choice
Looking more closely at the street itself, recent research finds that what you actually ex­
perience at eye­level can be just as important as neighbourhood­scale factors. Capitanio
(2019) shows that pedestrians in Tokyo often walk farther just because certain streets are
more attractive and comfortable, even if this means taking a detour. While this study did
not measure each street feature directly, it suggests that things like greenery, benches,
and overall visual appearance matter.
Koo et al. (2022) use computer vision and GSV images to pull out features like street trees,
width of sidewalks, and scales ofbuildings across Atlanta. They find that these street­level
features can have a stronger link to walking than neighbourhood­wide factors, showing
how much the direct environment shapes behaviour. Still, some street features are closely
tied to broader patterns in the city, like ’building­to­street­ratio’ and employment density,
so the two scales often interact.
Lu et al. (2018) add an interesting angle for cycling: GPS data from Hamilton, Canada,
shows that cyclists often choose longer routes than necessary, likely preferring certain
“dominant” routes for comfort or safety. While the study did not directly measure streetscape
features, it suggests that aspects of street design or perceived safety might explain these
detours, indicating the need for more detailed analysis of the physical environment when
understanding cycling behaviour.
Zhang et al. (2024) provide further evidence that streetscape characteristics play a sig­
nificant role in shaping cycling behaviour. Their study from Xiamen, China, found that a
combination of accessible, visually appealing, and well­designed green elements along
streets was linked to higher cycling volumes, reinforcing the idea that the quality of the
street environment can help encourage active travel.
Both walking and cycling studies agree: comfort, safety, and how a street looks and func­
tions at ground level, can be just as influential as neighbourhood context.
2.3
Google Street View in Streetscape Analysis
In the past, measuring how walkable or cycle­friendly a street was relied on in­person
visits, which were time­consuming and subjective. Now, GSV and deep learning make it
possible to objectively measure street features quickly and at a large scale. For example,
Koo et al. (2022) and Nagata et al. (2020) both used GSV images with computer vision to
study streets in Atlanta and Tokyo. Their research found that features like greenery, width

of sidewalks, and enclosure are closely linked to walking behaviour, even if their models
can not capture every detail of real life.
Huang et al. (2023) took this further in Fukuoka, Japan, combining GSV and virtual reality
surveys. They discovered that natural elements such as trees, grass, and water make
streets seem much more walkable, while more buildings and parked cars have the op­
posite effect. Their approach shows the value of pairing automated image analysis with
people’s actual perceptions.
Finally, in Copenhagen, Qi (2024) applied GSV­based methods to map street greenery
across the city using the Green View Index (GVI). Interestingly, Qi found that Copen­
hagen’s GVI is lower than in some other European cities, suggesting there is still room to
make the city’s streets greener and even more inviting for walking and cycling. However,
Zhang et al. (2024) suggest, focusing only on the quantity of greenery may not be suffi­
cient, since the quality and diversity of green elements are also important for promoting
cycling and walking.
In summary, the literature increasingly shows that the built environment affects how people
choose to travel, and this happens at many different scales, from broad neighbourhood
features down to the details of individual streets. While classic factors still matter, recent
studies have found that streetscape features such as greenery, safety, and how attractive
a street looks also play a key role in mode choice. Thanks to new tools like GSV and
computer vision, researchers can now measure these small­scale features more fully and
objectively than before, creating new opportunities for both research and policy.

Methodology
This chapter details the methodological framework and analytical techniques needed to
conduct the cluster analysis and to develop MNL models.
3.1
Clustering
A range of statistical and machine learning techniques are commonly applied to explore
structure and patterns in multivariate datasets. Preprocessing steps, dimensionality re­
duction, and unsupervised learning methods each serve distinct but complementary roles
in this process. The following sections provide a general overview of key methodological
components. These techniques are frequently combined in research to facilitate mean­
ingful pattern discovery and interpretation in complex data.
3.1.1
Standardisation
Standardisation is a widely used data preprocessing technique in multivariate analysis.
This is often performed using a z­score transformation, which rescales each variable so
that it has a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. The z­score for each value x
is calculated as:
z = x −µ
σ
(3.1)
where µ is the mean of the variable, and σ is the standard deviation. This step is essential
when variables have different units or scales, as it ensures that each feature contributes
equally to subsequent analyses. Standardisation is particularly important for methods
based on distance calculations, such as clustering and principal component analysis, as
it prevents features with larger numerical ranges from dominating the results.
3.1.2
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a method for dimensionality reduction in complex
datasets. PCA projects the original variables onto a new set of orthogonal axes, known
as principal components, each of which accounts for a portion of the total variance in the
data. The principal components are ordered so that the first captures the greatest amount
of variance, with each subsequent component explaining the next highest variance un­
der the constraint of being uncorrelated with the previous components. By selecting a
subset of these components, PCA facilitates data visualisation, noise reduction, and the
identification of underlying patterns, while minimising information loss (Vlachos, 2016).
3.1.3
K­means Clustering
Clustering is an unsupervised learning approach that aims to partition a dataset into
groups, or clusters, such that observations within the same cluster are more similar to
each other than to those in different clusters. K­means is one of the most commonly used
clustering algorithms, due to its computational efficiency and simplicity. The K­means al­
gorithm assigns each data point to the nearest cluster center and updates cluster centers
iteratively to minimise the sum of squared distances within clusters. This method is sen­
sitive to variable scaling, which is why standardisation is typically applied beforehand (Jin
& Han, 2016).
3.1.4
Within­Cluster Sum of Squares
A central consideration in cluster analysis is how to determine the appropriate number
of clusters for a given dataset. One widely used approach is to assess how compact or
cohesive the resulting clusters are as their number increases. This is commonly done

by calculating the within­cluster sum of squares (WCSS) for different numbers of clusters.
The WCSS provides a measure of how closely data points in a cluster are grouped around
their centroid. As the number of clusters increases, the WCSS typically decreases, since
each cluster can represent its data points more precisely. By analyzing how the WCSS
changes with different cluster counts. This is often visualised with an “elbow plot”, which
can help to identify a point where adding more clusters results in diminishing returns in
terms of improved cluster compactness. This point can then be used as a guideline for
selecting the most suitable number of clusters for further analysis.
3.1.5
Silhouette Score
To assess the quality and validity of the resulting clusters, the silhouette score can be
employed as an internal evaluation metric. The silhouette score quantifies how well each
data point fits within its assigned cluster compared to other clusters, thereby providing
an indication of both cluster cohesion (how similar an object is to its own cluster) and
separation (how dissimilar it is from objects in other clusters).
Formally, the silhouette score for a single observation is defined as:
s(i) =
b(i) −a(i)
max{a(i), b(i)}
(3.2)
where a(i) is the mean distance between the observation i and all other points within the
same cluster, and b(i) is the lowest mean distance from i to all points in any other cluster.
The resulting silhouette value ranges from ­1 to 1, where a value close to 1 indicates that
the observation is well­matched to its own cluster and poorly matched to neighbouring
clusters, while values near 0 suggest overlapping clusters, and negative values imply
possible misclassification (Rousseeuw, 1987).
3.2
Multinomial Logit Model
The multinomial logit (MNL) model is a widely used discrete choice model for analysing
situations in which individuals select one option from a set of mutually exclusive alterna­
tives. In transport studies, the MNL model is commonly applied to study mode choice
behaviour, where the probability of choosing a particular transport mode depends on the
characteristics of the alternatives as well as the individual.
The MNL model is based on the principle of random utility maximisation, where the utility
Uni of alternative i for individual n is specified as:
Uni = Vni + εni
(3.3)
Here, Vni is the observed (systematic) component of utility, typically specified as a linear
function of explanatory variables, and εni is the unobserved random component, assumed
to be independently and identically distributed with a Gumbel distribution.
The probability that individual n chooses alternative i from the choice set J is given by:
Pni =
exp(Vni)
∑
j∈J exp(Vnj)
(3.4)
The systematic utility Vni is typically modeled as a linear function:
Vni = β0 + β1x1,ni + β2x2,ni + · · · + βkxk,ni

where xk,ni are explanatory variables, and βk are parameters to be estimated.
Model parameters are typically estimated using maximum likelihood estimation. The MNL
model assumes the property of independence of irrelevant alternatives, which implies that
the relative odds of choosing between any two alternatives are unaffected by the presence
or attributes of other alternatives in the choice set (Train, 2003).
Several complementary criteria are commonly used to assess and compare MNL models:
• Log­likelihood at convergence: This value indicates how well the model explains the
observed choices, with higher values reflecting better fit to the data.
• Pseudo­R2 statistics: Measures such as McFadden’s pseudo­R2 express the pro­
portional improvement in model fit compared to a baseline or null model, facilitating
straightforward comparison across models.
• Information criteria: The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Informa­
tion Criterion (BIC) penalise models that are too complex, helping to prevent over­
fitting and support the choice of models that remain simple and effective.
• Likelihood ratio tests: For nested models, likelihood ratio tests can be used to de­
termine whether adding additional variables significantly improves model fit.
• Interpretability and significance: Beyond statistical metrics, models are also com­
pared based on the significance and plausibility of estimated parameters, as well as
their ease of interpretation for policy and planning purposes.

4.2
The Danish National Travel Survey
The Danish National Travel Survey, known in Danish as Transportvaneundersøgelsen
(TU), is an interview survey that collects data on the travel behaviour of the Danish popu­
lation. Participants document details about their journeys from the previous day, such as
origin, destination, transport mode, and trip purpose. Demographical and socioeconomic
information of the participants are included as well.
Interviews are conducted daily, ensuring representation across geographies and various
demographic segments. Additionally, the observations are weighted to reflect a represen­
tation of the Danish population throughout the year (Christiansen and Skougaard, 2015).
In this study, TU data on a trip level will be used. A trip corresponds to a travel from A to
B. Trips can however consist of multiple modes of transport eg. starting a trip by cycling
to a train station, then continuing the trip by taking the train. In this study, the chosen
transport mode for a trip will be considered to be the mode that accounts for the longest
travel distance on that trip.
TU interviews in the years 2016­2024 will be considered in this study. Those years are
included to ensure a sufficient number of trip observations, while avoiding reliance on
data from too far back in time, where the streetscape characteristics might have been
considerably different. The dataset will also be limited to trips that have both their origin
and destination geographically located in the Greater Copenhagen area. This filtering
has been necessary for calculating generalised travel times (GTT) for the mode choice
model, which will be described more in detail in Section 4.4.4. Only activity­home or home­
Exploring the relationship between streetscape design and transport mode choice
13
==PAGE:22==
activity trips will be included in the dataset. This restriction allows for a clearer assessment
of how residential streetscape characteristics and urban form influence transport mode
choice and ensures consistency in the interpretation of built environment variables. The
observations where the chosen modes are not either walk, cycling, car or public transport
will also be removed. Walk trips longer than 10km, cycling trips longer than 20km and
car or public transport trips shorter than 500m will be removed as well. Observations with
missing values for the variables used in this study, have also been filtered out. This leaves
a total of 44,857 observations from TU.
4.3
Copenhagen Greater Area Model for Passenger Transport
Greater Area Model for Passenger Transport (COMPASS) is an activity­based transport
model that covers the metropolitan area of Copenhagen (Paag et al., 2019). In this study,
COMPASS serves multiple purposes. Primarily, it has been used to calculate generalised
travel times, essential for assessing accessibility and transport mode competitiveness
across different urban settings. Additionally, COMPASS includes synthetic population
data, enabling calculations of population densities within various zones throughout the
Greater Copenhagen area. These population density metrics serve as explanatory vari­
ables within the mode choice model developed for this research.
4.4
Data Preparation and in­between Analysis Steps
Some data preparation and in­between analysis steps have been necessary to be able to
conduct the analyses themselves. These will be described in this subsection.
4.4.1
Defining Streetscape Indicators
In this study, the proportion of the pixels for the GSV images for relevant object categories
will be used as the variables for the cluster analysis, and will act as the streetscape indi­
cators. Thus, the following object categories have been left out: NaN, Pole, Bicycles and
Person. The NaN category has been left out, since it represents the lack of any identified
object categories. The combination of a low mean and standard deviation, as seen in
Table 4.1, has deemed the Pole category irrelevant, and not able to add any meaning­
ful aspect to the cluster analysis. Person and Bicycle have been left out of the cluster
analysis because they mainly capture the number of people or bikes visible at the ex­
act moment the GSV image was taken, which can vary randomly and does not reliably
represent the physical or design characteristics of the streetscape. Including these could
introduce noise rather than meaningful differences between street environments. In con­
trast, Car has been kept in the analysis, but grouped together with Road as a Roadtraffic
category. This combination provides a better measure of how much space and infrastruc­
ture is dedicated to car use, which is a key feature of the built environment. Car and Road
together reflect whether the street is designed primarily for motor vehicles, which is much
more stable and meaningful for distinguishing different types of streetscape and under­
standing their influence on mode choice. The Roadtraffic category has been calculated
as follows:
Roadtraffici = xCar,i + xRoad,i

where xCategory,i denotes the image area covered by the Car and Road category in GSV
image i.

Vegetation category in the cluster analysis. This is because, while each of these cate­
gories represents different types of greenery, they all contribute to the overall amount of

Dimensionality Reduction
To simplify the dataset while retaining most of its variability, Principal Component Analysis
(PCA) was applied as a dimensionality reduction technique prior to clustering, where the
decision about the number of used components was made. Prior to this standardisation
was applied on the variables.
As seen in Figure 4.5, the explained variance decreases gradually across components.
Since all variables are proportions that add up to 100%, the dataset is considered compo­
sitional. This means that only six out of the seven variables are actually independent, and
the last one can always be determined from the others. As a result, the total explained
variance from PCA reaches 100% after six components, and the seventh component does
not add any new information.

4.4.3
Clustering of Travel Survey Data
After each GSV image has been assigned a cluster, the individual trips from the Danish
National Travel Survey can be assigned. This has been done using the geographic infor­
mation system software QGIS, by assigning the origin and destination coordinates from
the trips to the assigned cluster of the GSV image that is closest by. Origin and destination
coordinates will only be assigned to a cluster if it is located within an euclidean distance
of 200m from a GSV image. This will ensure that trip coordinates will not be assigned
clusters in areas far away from the nearest GSV image, even though the trip coordinates
are within 1km from a train station.
Since the individual trip coordinates from the travel survey data are highly sensitive, the
team behind TU have sent over 800,000 coordinates covering the whole Greater Copen­
hagen area which contains actual trip coordinates, but also many ”pseudo coordinates”,
which have no connection to the real survey data. All of these points have been assigned
a cluster (if they live up to the previously stated requirements) and sent back to the team
behind TU. They then returned the survey data with the assigned clusters. This way,
the origin and destinations of the trips was assigned a cluster, without exposing sensitive
information about the specific trip coordinates of the survey participants.

Results
In this chapter, the results of the analyses are presented. The first section reports on the
outcomes of the cluster analysis, including the determination of the optimal number of
clusters and interpretation of the resulting cluster solutions. The second section contains
the findings from the case analysis, in which five selected areas were examined regard­
ing their streetscape clusters and corresponding transport mode choices. Finally, the third
section presents the results of the mode choice models, comparing a Base Model con­
taining standard explanatory variables for transport mode choice with a Cluster Model that
incorporates streetscape clusters as additional explanatory variables. This comparison
assesses whether the inclusion of streetscape clusters improves model fit and effectively
isolates their specific influence on observed transport behaviour.
5.1
Cluster Analysis
To identify patterns in the streetscape characteristics of the study area, a cluster anal­
ysis was carried out using the k­means algorithm. The aim was to group similar street
environments based on the relative proportions of the elements derived from the GSV
segmentation data. As described in Section 4.4.2, PCA was used as a dimensionality
reduction technique, and the first three principal components were selected for use in the
clustering to ensure the most important variation in the data was captured while minimising
noise.
Determining the appropriate number of clusters is a crucial step in the analysis. To support
this decision, two commonly used approaches were applied: the elbow method, which ex­
amines the within­cluster sum of squares (WCSS) as a function of the number of clusters,
and the silhouette score, which assesses the compactness and separation of the resulting
clusters. The elbow plot was used to visually identify points where adding more clusters
results in diminishing improvements in model fit, while the silhouette score provided a
quantitative measure of clustering quality.
Based on these criteria, several potential solutions were identified for closer examination.
For each candidate cluster solution, the clusters were interpreted and assigned descriptive
names based on the mean values of their streetscape features. This approach allowed
for a qualitative assessment of how well each solution captured distinct types of street
environments in the dataset, and made it possible to communicate the nature of each
cluster in a transparent and intuitive way.
In the following subsections, the results of the elbow and silhouette analyses are pre­
sented, followed by an interpretation and naming of the cluster categories for each candi­
date solution. The section concludes with a justification for the selected number of clusters
to be used in subsequent analyses.
5.1.1
Elbow Plot Results
To determine a suitable number of clusters for the streetscape classification, the WCSS
was calculated for cluster solutions ranging from 1 to 20 clusters. As shown in Figure 5.1,
the WCSS decreases sharply as the number of clusters increases from 1 to around 4–6,
after which the rate of decrease gradually flattens out. This is consistent with the typical
“elbow” shape, where adding additional clusters results in diminishing returns in reducing
WCSS.

complexity. This visual “elbow” supports the adoption of a 4­cluster solution as a good

5.2
Comparing Sample Characteristics Across Clusters
The summary tables for activity­end and home­end clusters (Tables ?? and ??) offer
valuable insights into the composition of the dataset and highlight systematic differences
between clusters across a wide range of variables. Not only do they document the vari­
ation in socio­demographic, spatial, and trip characteristics within the sample, but they
also provide important context for interpreting the results of the mode choice models.
A key pattern evident in both tables is that the streetscape clusters are closely intertwined
with many of the classic explanatory variables used in transport modelling. For example,
Urban clusters—whether at the activity or home end—are consistently associated with
higher population densities, a much greater share of trips located within central Copen­
hagen (CPH/FRB), shorter distances to the nearest station, and lower rates of car access.
These attributes are all recognised as important determinants of sustainable travel be­
haviour. In contrast, Suburban and Pathways & Shorelines clusters are more prevalent in
peripheral areas with lower densities, higher car ownership, and generally less favourable
conditions for public transport or active travel.
Other sample characteristics also show meaningful variation across clusters.
For in­
stance, the Urban clusters have a higher proportion of younger adults (18–30 years),
a lower proportion of elderly (66+ years), and a somewhat larger share of work trips. In
Green area clusters, the age profile is more balanced, and education trips are more com­
mon. These patterns reflect the demographic sorting and spatial organisation of the re­
gion, and underline that streetscape clusters are not independent of the population groups
or trip types observed within them.
Importantly, these cross­cluster relationships have implications for model estimation and
interpretation. The cluster variables capture the combined effect of multiple aspects of
the built environment, some of which may overlap with what is measured by variables
like density, station proximity, or car ownership. As shown in the MNL model results,
the inclusion of cluster variables often reduces the explanatory power or significance of
traditional variables. This suggests that cluster variables may subsume or “soak up” some
of the explanatory variation, offering a more nuanced or holistic representation of the local
environment, but also requiring care in attributing causal effects.
The comparison across the tables also reveals that while cluster types are useful for
summarising the diversity of the built environment, they must be interpreted as part of
a broader web of factors that shape travel behaviour. The clear interrelationships be­
tween clusters and key explanatory variables underline the necessity of controlling for
both in multivariate analyses.
In sum, the observed patterns reinforce the analytical strategy of jointly considering both
detailed streetscape clusters and classic transport variables in explaining mode choice.
Together, they capture both the distinctive qualities of urban environments and the demo­
graphic and spatial realities that condition mobility in the Greater Copenhagen area.

5.3.2
Glostrup
Glostrup is located in the inner western suburbs of Copenhagen, served by S­trains and
several bus lines. Its surrounding land use is characterised by a mix of commercial, and
residential zones, creating a distinct blend of suburban and urban forms. The catchment
area of the streetscape clusters for Glostrup can be seen in Figure 5.10.

Figure 5.10: Catchment area of streetscape clusters in Glostrup.
At the activity end, 58% of trips end in Suburban clusters and 34% in Urban clusters, while
Green area (2%) and Pathways and Shorelines (6%) are less represented. This indicates
a spatially mixed environment. In terms of mode share, car usage (56%) is well above
the regional average, with walking at 22% and cycling at 16%. Notably, the relatively high
share of Urban clusters may help sustain these active modes, even within a relatively high
suburban context. The median trip distance is just 3 km, suggesting many trips are local,
which likely supports the elevated walking and cycling shares.
At the home end, Glostrup residents are distributed across Suburban (46%) and Pathways
and Shorelines (30%) clusters, with 21% in Urban clusters and only 4% in Green areas.
The mode split for residents is 51% car, 29% walking, 13% cycling, and 8% PT, further
emphasising the resilience of active travel in mixed environments. The relatively short
mean and median trip distances reinforce the potential for non­car travel, even in areas
that do not fully meet classic urban design criteria for walkability or density.

Hillerød functions as a regional hub in North Zealand and has an end­station for the S­train
network. It is also the centre of a local rail­network connecting neighbouring towns and

urban destination profile, Hillerød exhibits the highest car mode share (64%) among the
case areas. Active modes are underrepresented (8% walking, 13% cycling), even as PT

both long­distance commuters and visitors from surrounding towns.

5.3.7
Overall Reflections
While the case analysis provides valuable descriptive insights into the distribution of street­
scape clusters and transport mode shares across the five station areas, it does not reveal
a simple or universal relationship between the built environment and travel behaviour.
For example, Hillerød displays a high share of car trips even though many activities are
located in urban clusters, contradicting the expectation that urban form alone strongly
favours active and public transport. Similar inconsistencies can be observed in other ar­
eas, suggesting that streetscape composition is only one piece of a more complex puzzle.

==PAGE:44==
These findings highlight the need to consider a wider array of explanatory variables such
as trip distance, travel time, socio­demographic factors, and overall accessibility when
analysing transport mode choice. In particular, neighbourhood­scale contexts and net­
work structures may play a decisive role.
To address this complexity, the following section introduce a quantitative mode choice
modelling approach. This enables the simultaneous analysis of multiple factors, helping
to isolate the specific influence of streetscape clusters while controlling for other relevant
variables. By moving beyond descriptive comparisons, the mode choice model aims to
clarify how different elements of the urban environment interact to shape sustainable travel
behaviour in the Greater Copenhagen area.

Model Comparison
Both the Base Model and the Cluster Model were evaluated using several standard model
comparison metrics. The Cluster Model achieves a slightly higher final log­likelihood at
−32,241.66 compared to the Base Model −32,360.68, indicating a marginally better fit
to the observed data. The Cluster Model also shows a modest increase in explanatory
power, with a pseudo­R2 of 0.418 compared to 0.416 for the base model. In terms of
information criteria, the Cluster Model has a lower AIC value (64,665.32 vs. 64,855.36),
suggesting an improved balance between fit and complexity. However, the BIC is slightly
higher for the Cluster Model (65,458.05) than for the base model (65,439.01), reflecting
the penalty for its greater number of parameters (91 vs. 67). The likelihood ratio test is
also in favour of the Cluster Model, further supporting that it significantly improves the fit:
LR = −2(−32, 360.68 −(−32, 241.66)) = 238.04
(5.1)
Since it follows a chi­square distribution with degrees of freedom equal to the difference
in the number of parameters between the models (91 ­ 67 = 24) and the critical chi­square
value with a significance level of 0.05 with 24 degrees of freedom is around 36.42, the
model fit is significantly improved when the streetscape cluster variables are included.
Overall, while the Cluster Model provides a statistically better fit to the data, the improve­
ments over the Base Model are limited, and the added complexity should be weighed
against the relatively modest gains in explanatory performance. The results of the model

Parameter Estimation Results
The estimates for GTT are negative which is expected. A trip alternative should be less
attractive when it is costly and time consuming. The estimate for public transport is very
high (at −0.006) compared to the other modes. This is explained by the fact that access
and egress times are not included in these calculations. Instead, the distance to nearest
station should reflect this aspect. However, this will not account for whether there are
good bus connections close to the home or activity end.
Men are significantly less likely to use public transport or walking compared to women.
Whether a person owns a bicycle is unsurprisingly significant for the use of bicycle. When
a household has access to at least one car, then the likeliness of using the other transport
modes decreases. This effect is strongest for public transport indicating that a when
people get access to a car, a bigger proportion of public transport trips is substituted
compared to walking and cycling trips.
During Covid, the probability of choosing bike and walk increased significantly, while the
probability of choosing public transport decreased significantly. After Covid, the positive
effect remained for bike and walk, with no effect estimated for public transport. Compared

dense area might have a greater demand for attractive bike lanes, and good public trans­
port options. In these models, it does make sense that public transport is more significant
in the activity ends compared to the home ends. This asymmetry may reflect that individ­
uals live in a fixed location with fixed public transport options available at the home end.
In contrast, activity ends can vary substantially from trip to trip, with differences in PT
accessibility, last­mile walkability, and overall network connectivity, making them a more
critical determinant in the decision to use public transport.
Trips ending in Copenhagen or Frederiksberg are much more likely to be done by bike,
PT, or walking, which aligns with the high quality of urban infrastructure and lower car
accessibility in central areas. The effect is consistent and strong across all three modes.
At the home end, the effects are weaker and generally insignificant except for biking in the
Cluster Model, where there is a modest positive effect. Again, this highlights that urban
context at activity ends matters more than the context at home ends for mode choice.
Shorter distances to the nearest station strongly increase the likelihood of choosing public
transport, especially at the activity end. However, in the cluster model, the significance
of many distance variables becomes insignificant. This is due to high correlation with the
‘No cluster’ trips, which are all located at least 1km from a train station and the variables
for distance to nearest station below 1km.
In the Cluster Model, the effect of density at home end for walking is no longer statistically
significant, even though it was significant in the Base Model. This is not because it is
strongly correlated with the new streetscape cluster variables since there is no high cor­
relation between them. However, the clusters and home density may still describe some
of the same things. For example, both relate to how urban or suburban the home area is,
but the clusters also capture more details, such as how green or built­up the area looks.
Because the clusters include information about the streetscape, the model may rely more
on them and less on the simple density measure. This can make the density at home end
look less important for walking, even if it still matters. In this way, the new cluster variables
may “take over” some of the explanatory power that the density at home end had in the
Base Model. This suggests that using visual streetscape types can be a stronger way
to capture the effect of the built environment on travel mode choice. However, this does
complicate the objective of isolating the streetscape effects but may instead show that
streetscape design might contribute more to the explanation of transport mode choice
than neighbourhood­level effects. This correlates with the findings of Koo et al. (2021),
where characteristics at street level had a stronger link to walking than neighbourhood­
wide factors.
In the Cluster Model, the Green area cluster was chosen as the reference category be­
cause it represents the most natural and least urbanised environment among the identi­
fied cluster types. Using the Green area cluster as the baseline allows the effects of more

the presence or absence of urban features affects the likelihood of choosing sustainable

features in shaping sustainable mode choices, but also suggests that the specific mech­

In general, when the streetscape cluster variables are introduced in the model, there
are notable changes in the estimates and significance of the neighbourhood­scale vari­
ables, such as density, distance to station, and centrality (e.g., Copenhagen/Frederiks­

the clusters capture much of the variation previously explained by traditional geographi­
cal measures. In contrast, the effects of socio­demographic characteristics such as age,
gender, car access, bicycle ownership, and income, remain stable and robust across both
the base and cluster models. This indicates that while the built environment description is
improved by the inclusion of clusters, the influence of individual and household attributes
on mode choice is largely unaffected.
In summary, the results from the multinomial logit models provide strong evidence that
both traditional geographical factors and detailed streetscape characteristics play a sub­
stantial role in shaping transport mode choice in the Copenhagen metropolitan area. The
introduction of visually­derived streetscape clusters in the models offers a more nuanced
understanding of how the built environment influences travel behaviour, most notably by
capturing aspects of urbanity, greenery, and infrastructure quality that are not fully repre­
sented by classic measures like density or distance to transit.
The findings reveal important mode­specific patterns: for walking, built­up environments
at the home end increase the probability of choosing this mode, while green areas at
the activity end serve as especially attractive destinations, drawing walking trips from
a wide range of home cluster types. For public transport, the urban character of the
destination is a key driver, underscoring the role of dense and well­connected areas in
attracting PT trips. For cycling, local conditions at the home end, particularly in suburban
and Pathways/Shorelines clusters are more relevant, suggesting that cycling is supported
by features in the immediate residential environment rather than at the destination.
Importantly, the inclusion of cluster variables diminishes the explanatory power of several

5.4.3
Descriptive Associations Between Clusters and Explanatory
Variables
Taken together, these patterns suggest a complex interplay between the cluster variables
and the more traditional built environment and socio­demographic indicators. To provide
further insight into these relationships, the following section presents a more detailed
descriptive overview of how the clusters correspond to the main explanatory variables
used in the models. This helps clarify to what extent the clusters capture unique aspects
of the built environment, or if they tend to overlap with existing determinants of travel mode
choice.
The descriptive tables of sample composition across the different activity­end and home­
end clusters as seen in Table 5.9 and Table 5.10 provide important context for interpreting
the results of the MNL models. It is evident from these summaries that the streetscape
clusters are not independent of the classic explanatory variables used in the mode choice
models, but rather reflect a wider set of built environment and demographic characteris­
tics.
For example, Urban clusters are characterized by higher densities, more frequent location
within central Copenhagen, better proximity to stations, lower rates of car access, and a
higher proportion of younger adults. In contrast, Suburban and Pathways & Shorelines
clusters are associated with lower densities, more peripheral locations, higher rates of
car ownership, and slightly older populations on average. These differences are not just
theoretical, they are clearly visible in the descriptive statistics, and are mirrored in the
patterns of transport mode use.
This interrelationship helps to explain several findings from the MNL models. The inclu­
sion of cluster variables often reduced the significance of more traditional variables such
as density, distance to station, or car access. In effect, the cluster variables aggregate or
”bundle” a range of environmental and accessibility factors that have established links to
travel behaviour. As a result, the coefficients on the clusters reflect not only the physical
streetscape, but also the associated socio­demographic and locational context.
It is important to note, therefore, that while streetscape clusters provide a richer and more
holistic measure of the local environment, their explanatory power is partly shared with the
other classic variables. The overlap between clusters and traditional predictors highlights
both the value and the complexity of incorporating visual streetscape measures into mode
choice models.
In summary, the comparison of cluster composition with other explanatory variables con­
firms that built environment characteristics rarely operate in isolation. Rather, they are
embedded within a broader urban, social, and spatial context. Recognising these inter­
dependencies is crucial when interpreting model results and when considering the policy
implications of urban design interventions aimed at promoting sustainable travel modes.

6
Discussion
This study set out to explore how streetscape design is linked to people’s choice of trans­
port mode in the Greater Copenhagen area. By combining advanced image­based anal­
ysis with traditional MNL modelling, the study aimed to uncover both descriptive and pos­
sible causal relationships between the built environment and sustainable travel patterns.
It also looked closely at how useful and reliable streetscape measures are, compared to
more traditional variables, for explaining travel behaviour. The results of these analyses
are discussed in this chapter.
6.1
Discussion of Results
The analyses revealed nuanced and sometimes unexpected relationships between street­
scape clusters and transport mode choice. Descriptive case studies of five diverse station
areas: Glostrup, Hillerød, Stenløse­Egedal, Østerport, and a combined group of small
towns, showed a big variation in streetscape cluster composition and mode shares. How­
ever, the connection between specific streetscape types and mode choices was not al­
ways direct or universal.
For example, areas dominated by urban clusters, such as Østerport, had high levels of
walking, cycling, and public transport use. This pattern was expected and aligns with
international evidence about dense urban areas. In contrast, Hillerød displayed high car
mode shares despite many activities being located in urban clusters. In the suburban and
small­town cases, car dependence was prevalent, but shares of walking and cycling were
still high, particularly for short local trips.
These findings indicate that while streetscape clusters contribute to shaping travel be­
haviour, they are only one part of a larger system that includes trip distance, accessibility,
urban function, and socio­demographic factors.
The MNL models allowed for a more detailed assessment of the independent effect of
streetscape clusters on mode choice, while controlling for other factors such as GTT,
socio­demographics and proximity to stations. Adding cluster variables to the models
led to a small improvement in model fit. However, many traditional variables remained
stronger predictors of mode choice than the detailed streetscape clusters.
The findings also suggest differences in how streetscape composition at the home end
and activity end influences different modes. Living in urban environments increased the
likelihood of walking trips. In contrast, the Green area cluster at the activity end attracted
walking trips from a wide range of home locations, highlighting the appeal of green areas
for walking. For public transport, the Urban cluster at the activity end increased the like­
lihood of choosing PT, which is likely due to better service and accessibility. For cycling,
the effects were weaker, but there was some indication that Suburban and Pathways &
Shoreline clusters at the home end supported cycling.
Since individuals generally have only one home but visit many different activity destina­
tions, the built environment at the home end is then assumed to have a more persistent
and lasting influence on travel behaviour, whereas the effect of activity­end clusters is
more variable and context specific. However the case analysis also showed that a place
like Hillerød has many trips where people travel relatively long distances to reach their
Exploring the relationship between streetscape design and transport mode choice
47
==PAGE:56==
activities. The overall cluster mix of Hillerød could be substantially different from a neigh­
bouring city where many of the travellers has their origin. This asymmetric travel pattern
could indirectly influence the observed relationships between clusters and mode choice
in the results.
When cluster variables were included, some classic built environment variables such as
density and distance to station became less significant. This suggests that the streetscape
characteristics may capture certain aspects of urban form not reflected in traditional mea­
sures. However, the overall effect of clusters remained modest, indicating that visual
streetscape features are relevant but not the only drivers of travel behaviour.
These results could potentially have been different if other decisions were made in the
analyses. An example is the decision to use the segmented object categories directly
from the GSV images (except Vegetation and Roadtraffic). Indicators could have been
defined by concepts such as openness vs. closedness or sidewalk­to­road ratio. Different
indicators could have resulted in clusters that showed stronger relationships with mode
choice.
Other explanatory variables could have been used in the MNL models. Headway for
public transport services was not included in the model, which could help in isolating the
streetscape cluster effects. An example is if some of the effects from the Urban cluster
was caused by a higher frequency of trains.
6.2
Limitations
While this study gives new insight into how streetscape features relate to transport mode
choice in the Greater Copenhagen area, it is important to recognise some key limitations.
Only the streetscape characteristics at the origin and destination of each trip are included
in this study. The streetscape design along the actual routes taken during trips has not
been considered, as route information was not available for the observations. This is a
significant limitation, because the streetscape environment experienced along the route
could be quite different from that at the starting or ending point of the trip, and may have
a greater influence on mode choice.
The study uses a cross­sectional approach, which means all the data was collected at
one point in time. Because of this, it is not possible to say for certain that streetscape
characteristics actually cause people to choose certain transport modes. We can only
observe that they are related. Any changes in infrastructure or urban development that
happen later could affect these relationships in ways this study cannot capture.
The cluster analysis is based on visual data from GSV images. While this method is
objective and systematic, it might not fully capture how people actually experience their
surroundings, such as their sense of safety, comfort, or the overall atmosphere. The
images also show the street at just one moment in time, so they might not reflect seasonal
changes or temporary events.
By grouping many different image categories into just a few types of clusters, the analysis
simplifies the diversity found in real urban areas. These broad cluster types were chosen
for clarity and interpretation, but they might not cover all the unique qualities of the re­
gion’s neighbourhoods. They were also clustered for practical reasons, since it is limited
how disaggregated you can study TU data without its geographical information becoming
sensitive.

6.3
Future Work
This study has contributed with new insights into the complex relationship between street­
scape design and transport mode choice, but it also highlights several opportunities for
future research.
First, an obvious suggestion for future work is to incorporate information about the street­
scape environment along the entre route of each trip, not just at the origin and destina­
tion. Having access to actual route data, for example from GPS data, would enable a
much more detailed assessment of how the quality and character of streets throughout
the journey influence mode choice. This could help clarify whether the visual and physical
qualities of the streets themselves play a larger role than the environment at the origin and
destination locations.
Another interesting insight would be to look at the access/egress trip segments to/from
public transport services and specifically whether attractive streetscape design influences
people’s willingness to walk further distances to public transport options.
The study could be expanded to investigate other metropolitan areas, both within Den­
mark and internationally, to examine whether the patterns and relationships observed
in this geographical context hold true in different urban contexts.
Comparative stud­
ies across cities with varied urban forms, transport networks, and policy environments
would help clarify whether the findings are general and identify context­specific factors
that shape the role of streetscape in transport mode choice.
Lastly in hindsight of the finished analysis, it could have been interesting to remove some
of the original explanatory variables after the cluster variables were introduced, to see
whether the clusters are able to completely replace other variables, and maybe even
have a better model fit.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to investigate how streetscape design influences transport mode choice
in the Greater Copenhagen area, addressing both descriptive and causal relationships
between the built environment and sustainable mobility. The study aimed to move beyond
traditional measures of urban form by integrating streetscape clusters derived from GSV
images with trip­level travel survey data and classical mode choice modelling.
The analysis revealed that while streetscape composition is associated with travel be­
haviour, its effects are complex and context­dependent. The descriptive case analysis
across five diverse station areas showed significant variation in both streetscape environ­
ments and mode shares, but did not reveal a straightforward or universal link between
cluster types and mode choice. Instead, the findings highlighted that streetscape charac­
teristics at both home and activity ends interact with a wider set of factors, including trip
distance, urban function, accessibility, and socio­demographics.
The MNL models provided further insights by quantifying the independent effects of street­
scape clusters, alongside classic explanatory variables such as generalised travel time,
population density, car and bike access, and individual characteristics. Adding streetscape
clusters to the models led to a small but statistically significant improvement in model fit.
However, traditional variables, especially those reflecting personal mobility resources and
demographic attributes, remained the strongest predictors of mode choice.
The results pointed to important asymmetries in how streetscape design at the home end
and activity end influences different travel modes. Urban environments at the activity end
were found to encourage public transport use, while green areas attracted walking trips
from a variety of home locations, underlining the appeal of green destinations. For cycling,
the immediate environment around the home was somewhat more important, though the
effects were generally weaker.
Importantly, the inclusion of visual streetscape clusters in the models reduced the ex­
planatory power of some traditional built environment variables, suggesting that visual
characteristics capture additional, finer­grained aspects of urban form that standard mea­
sures like density or proximity to stations may miss or vice versa. Still, the effect of these
clusters remained modest in comparison to other variables.
Overall, the findings underscore that while streetscape design does play a role in shaping
travel behaviour, it cannot be considered in isolation. Instead, sustainable mode choice
emerges from a complex interplay of urban design, accessibility, personal characteris­
tics, and wider network structures. For policy and planning, this highlights the need to
consider both macro­ and micro­scale built environment features, and to recognize that
interventions at the street level may have different impacts depending on location and
mode.
This thesis contributes to the growing body of research integrating advanced digital meth­
ods into transport analysis, and demonstrates the potential of combining visual urban data
with traditional travel survey and modelling approaches. The results support the argument
that future efforts to promote sustainable mobility should pay attention not only to the over­
all structure of urban areas, but also to the specific qualities of streetscapes experienced
at both origins and destinations of trips.
