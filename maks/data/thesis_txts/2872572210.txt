Abstract
Three experiment were done to investigate the effectiveness of different parameters to
increase strength of a Al-Si binary alloy. Systematically varying the silicon content, mould
material and alloying element. Cylindrical cast were done and analyzed by collecting cool-
ing curves, hardness measurements, strian-stress curves. While copper alloying showed
the best effects on the overall strengthening of the Al-Si binary alloy, zircon sand showed
great promise in effecting cooling over greater distince inside the cast. Cerabeads showed
the best homogeneous solidification of the cast due to slow cooling rate.

Introduction
Sand casting involves the process of pouring molten metal into a mould made from a sand
mixture. With today’s automation equipment, sand casting has become more suitable
for high-volume productions. Sand castings typically have a rough surface, often with
surface impurities and variations. The dimensional accuracy and surface finish of the
casting depends on the type of sand and the moulding process. Sand core inserts can be
used to form the internal shape of hollow parts in a casting [1].
The microstructure of as-cast aluminum alloys depends on many different variables: the
alloy composition, the pouring time, the solidification time, melt temperature, and hydro-
gen formation, to name a few. The typical microstructure comprises primary aluminum
crystals surrounded by an eutectic mixture. In hypereutectic Al-Si alloys, primary silicon
crystals may form before the eutectic. The composition of the eutectic depends on which
alloy system is cast. In hypoeutectic alloys, it is common to refine the size of the aluminum
crystals by grain refinement for applications requiring good mechanical properties [1].
In sand mould cast Al-Si alloys, it is often common to use a treatment called eutectic
modification to change the eutectic silicon from a coarse, platelike morphology into a
fine, fibrous morphology. This is commonly achieved through trace additions (<400 ppm)
of specific elements. These additions are usually made in the form of master alloys.
Industrially, strontium has become the most popular modifier because it is more durable
and longer lasting than the other standard modifier [1].
Microstructure is a principal determinant of the mechanical properties of Al-Si alloys, pri-
marily through the effects of eutectic formation and grain structure. The formation of a
refined microstructure can enhance the ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and ductility of
cast alloys. Callegari et al. discuss how microstructural aspects, including phase mor-
phology and the presence of intermetallic compounds, can be optimized by the addition of
specific alloying elements [2]. Furthermore, manipulations such as strontium addition can
refine the silicon morphology, leading to significant improvements in mechanical prop-
erties by promoting a more equiaxed grain structure, which is beneficial for mechanical
performance [3].
The cooling rate during the casting process is another critical factor that profoundly affects
the mechanical properties of Al-Si alloys. Research indicates that increasing the cooling
rate during solidification yields finer microstructures and fewer secondary phases within
the alloy. For instance, Tang et al. demonstrated that an increased cooling rate from 0.22
to 7.65 K/s not only raised the UTS from 146.3 to 241.0 MPa but also significantly improved
elongation [4]. Additionally, Kuo emphasized that variations in cooling rate lead to differing
porosity levels and overall microstructural characteristics, which directly correlate with the
mechanical properties of the castings [5].
Moreover, alloying elements such as manganese and iron play a crucial role in the de-
velopment of intermetallic phases, which can either enhance or detract from the alloy’s
strength. Shabestari highlighted that the presence of iron and manganese could lead
to the formation of detrimental intermetallic compounds that negatively affect mechani-
cal properties, particularly ductility [6]. Conversely, controlled additions of these elements
can contribute to strength by forming stable precipitates that hinder dislocation movement
[7].

In addition to elemental modifications and cooling strategies, innovative approaches such
as mechanical mould vibration have been employed to modify silicon morphology, aim-
ing for improved mechanical properties by producing finer eutectic silicon structures [8].
This technique, along with traditional methods, suggests a multidisciplinary approach to
enhancing the properties of Al-Si alloys.
This study focused on the experimental investigation of changes to the cooling curves,
cooling rates and mechanical properties of as-cast Al-Si alloys in sand, with an empha-
sis on modifications to the sand mould through sand selection and an investigation into
alloying elements.

2.1
Microstrusture
The microstructure of Aluminum Silicon Strontium (AlSiSr) hypoeutectic alloys exhibits
distinctive characteristics that arise mainly due to the influence of strontium as a modifier
during alloy solidification. Strontium is known to alter the morphology of eutectic silicon in
Al-Si alloys, transitioning it from an acicular or plate-like structure to a more desirable glob-
ular or fibrous form. This modification significantly enhances the mechanical properties
of the aluminum matrix.
In a typical unmodified AlSi hypoeutectic alloy, the microstructure consists predominantly
of dendritic primary aluminum grains and significant, needle-like eutectic silicon phases
distributed throughout the aluminum matrix. The primary α-aluminum phase serves as a
matrix within which the eutectic silicon is embedded, creating a network that can lead to
brittle behavior due to the extensive sharp edges associated with the needle-like silicon
crystals [9] [10]. This configuration results in lower ductility and toughness, making the
alloy susceptible to cracking under mechanical stress [9] [11].
The addition of strontium modifies the microstructure of these alloys effectively. According
to research by Fracchia et al., [12], strontium promotes a transformation in the silicon from
a coarse, plate-like form to a rounded, spheroidal shape. This change reduces the stress
concentration factors that exist around the sharp corners of the acicular silicon, thereby
improving the alloy’s tensile properties and elongation [12]. Furthermore, the formation
of a finer eutectic structure contributes to improved fatigue performance and enhanced
corrosion resistance of the alloy [11].
Khalaf [13] notes that controlling the solidification process, including cooling rates and
initial pouring temperatures, is essential in achieving the optimal microstructure following
strontium addition. Lower cooling rates can lead to greater segregation phenomena and
coarsening, which may negate the benefits of strontium modification. Conversely, if solid-
ification is managed correctly, a fine and well-distributed microstructure can be obtained.
This scenario is further supported by Ding et al. [14], who highlighted the importance of
solution treatment to optimize the microstructure and mechanical properties after stron-
tium modification.
Typical findings of microstructure analysis in strontium-modified AlSi hypoeutectic alloys
include the observation of a fibrous or globular silicon morphology within a primary alu-
minum matrix characterized by a more uniform distribution of silicon particles. This mi-
crostructural evolution is crucial as it enhances both the mechanical properties and the
castability of the alloy, making it more suitable for various industrial applications, particu-
larly in the automotive and aerospace sectors [15].
In summary, the microstructure of AlSi hypoeutectic alloys modified with strontium shows
significant improvements over their unmodified counterparts, primarily by altering the mor-
phology of the silicon phase from acicular to globular. This transformation contributes to
better mechanical performance, including higher ductility and toughness, providing a sub-
stantial advantage for engineering applications in demanding environments.

Solidification Theory
When solidifying hypoeutectic or hypereutectic alloys, the first solid to form is a single
phase which has a composition different to that of the liquid. This causes the liquid com-
position to approach that of the eutectic as cooling occurs. Once the liquid reaches the
eutectic temperature, it will have the eutectic composition and will freeze at that temper-
ature to form a solid eutectic mixture of two phases.

based on its volume and surface area. It states that the solidification time is proportional
to the ratio of volume to surface area, raised to the power of a constant. This means that
a casting with a larger volume-to-surface area ratio will take longer to solidify than one
with a smaller ratio.

2.3
Strengthening Mechanics
Aluminum alloys, particularly aluminum-silicon (Al-Si) systems, exhibit exceptional strength-
to-weight ratios and corrosion resistance, making them favorable for various applications
in the automotive and aerospace industries. The mechanical properties of Al-Si alloys
can be significantly manipulated through different strengthening mechanisms, including
solid-solution strengthening, precipitation strengthening, and grain refinement, as well as
through the control of microstructural features during processing.
1. Solid-Solution Strengthening One of the primary mechanisms for increasing the strength
of aluminum alloys is solid-solution strengthening. This process involves the addition of
elements such as silicon, magnesium, and copper to the aluminum base. These alloying
elements dissolve in the aluminum matrix and cause lattice distortions due to their size
and atomic radius differences. As stated by Callegari et al. [2], solid-solution strength-
ening leads to an increase in yield strength by hindering dislocation movement, which is
crucial for the alloy’s overall strength.
The effectiveness of solid-solution strengthening is influenced by the concentration of the
solute atoms and the specific alloying elements. In Al-Si alloys, higher silicon content
tends to refine the microstructure and enhance strength, as the solute atoms create ob-
stacles that inhibit dislocation slip during deformation.

2. Precipitation Strengthening Precipitation strengthening is another essential mechanism
where heat treatment plays a significant role. Certain aluminum alloys, particularly those
containing copper and magnesium, can undergo a heat treatment process called solution
heat treatment, followed by aging. This process allows for the formation of precipitates,
which strengthen the material by obstructing dislocation movement.
While Al-Si alloys mainly benefit from solid-solution strengthening, the incorporation of
magnesium can lead to precipitation hardening in some cases, as noted by Suárez et
al. [10.1590/s1516-14392012005000103]. The alloy’s properties can be tailored through
appropriate heat treatment schedules, where controlled temperatures allow for the nucle-
ation and growth of fine precipitate phases, ultimately enhancing the yield strength and
hardness.
3. Grain Refinement Grain refinement is another critical factor affecting the strength of
aluminum alloys. A finer grain structure leads to improved mechanical properties due to
the Hall-Petch relationship, which states that smaller grains increase the yield strength of
materials. Kuo illustrated the significance of controlling the cooling rate during solidifica-
tion; variations in cooling rates can significantly influence grain sizes and, consequently,
mechanical properties [5].
Rapid cooling can refine the microstructural features, contributing to improved tensile and
compressive properties. Studies indicate that managing solidification rates can effectively
control grain structure and size, leading to improved mechanical performance in cast alu-
minum components.
4.
Influence of Processing Conditions The mechanical properties of aluminum alloys
are not only dependent on the alloying elements but also on the processing conditions.
For instance, the pouring temperature and cooling rates during casting significantly influ-
ence the microstructure and, consequently, the performance of Al-Si alloys. Ahmad et al.
[10.4028/www.scientific.net/amm.315.856] emphasize that lower pouring temperatures
yield finer microstructures with reduced defects, thus enhancing mechanical properties.
Additionally, increasing the cooling rate leads to a reduction in secondary phases, as stud-
ied by Tang et al. [4], which positively affects the ultimate tensile strength and elongation
of the alloy.
5. Applications and Demands As highlighted by Javidani and Larouche [10.1179/1743280413y.0000000027],
these strengthening mechanisms are crucial for meeting the demanding requirements
in applications, especially in internal combustion engines where mechanical fatigue and
thermal stability are vital. The interplay of solid-solution strengthening, precipitation hard-
ening, and grain refinement ensures that aluminum alloys maintain their performance
with reduced weight, contributing to improved fuel efficiency and overall performance in
demanding environments.

3.1
Alloys Preparation
Al-Si binary master alloy was prepared by melting high-purity Al (99.97%), high-purity Si
(99.998%) and ’Al-10Sr’ master alloy in an induction furnace at 750 ◦C. The melt was
degassed using gas purging with nitrogen for at least 30 min. After removing the sludge
from the surface, the melt was poured into a steel mould to produce a cast bar. In the
procurement of alloys with different Si contents (2.5 wt% Si, 5.5 wt% Si, 7.5 wt% Si,
10.5 wt% Si, 12.5 wt% Si) the master alloy bar was sectioned and diluted with more of
the same high-purity Al. The chemical composition of the samples was measured by
AMETEK Materials Analysis Division, summarized in Table 3.1.
The unused material from the silicon experiment (explained in section 2.6) was collected
and remelted for the procurement of an Al-7.5Si binary master alloy used in the sand and
alloying investigations, sections 2.7 and 2.8 respectively.
For the procurement of the Al-7.5Si-4Cu and Al-7.5-0.3Mg high-purity copper (Cu) and
magnesium (Mg) master-alloy were added to the molten Al7.5Si base at 750 ◦C. Gentle
stirring was performed for approximately 3 minutes to promote homogeneity. The melt
was then held for an additional 2 minutes to allow for dissolution before being skimmed
and poured.

3.3
Compression Testing Procedure
Before the compression tests, the 30 mm high cylinder disks
were machined into standardized right cylinders with both
height and diameter equal to 25 mm, using facilities at the
DTU mechanical workshop. This was done to ensure con-
sistency across all tests. The samples were tested using
presses at DTU facilities, where prior to testing, a thin layer of lubricant (RHENUS 125)
was applied to both faces of the specimen to minimize friction between the sample and
the piston head. The test consisted of lowering the piston head at a rate of 1 mm/s un-
til a maximum load force of 155kN was reached. Force and displacement data were
logged throughout the test and later used to generate stress–strain curves for further
analysis.[iso4506_2018].
3.4
Brinell Hardness Testing Procedure
Hardness testing was performed according to the ISO 6506 standard using the Brinell
method, following the 600HBW5/30/10 procedure. A 5 mm diameter steel ball was pressed
into the surface of the specimen under a load of 30 kg (294 N) for a time of 10 seconds.
On each sample, two indentations were made on each side (total of four indentations),
and the diameter of each indentation was measured manually in two perpendicular direc-
tions using a ruler with a resolution of 0.1 mm; the average of these measurements was
then used for the hardness calculation [iso6506_1_2014].

3.5
Microstructure Analysis
The 10 mm disks were embedded in plastic with the cross-section facing up so that the
solidification microstructure could be analyzed. The face was ground and polished using
a standard metallography polishing procedure. The samples were first ground using 320#
grit silicon carbide paper with lubrication to prevent overheating and ensure smooth ma-
terial removal. This was followed by successively polishing with 9 µm and 3 µm diamond
polishing solution with a force of 40 N for 3 min and 25 N for 5 min, respectively. After each
stage of grinding or polishing, the samples were thoroughly rinsed with water to remove
residual abrasive particles and prevent contamination between steps. Final polishing was
performed using OP-S NonDry colloidal silica suspension to achieve a mirror-like surface
suitable for optical microscopy. After polishing, all samples were cleaned with ethanol to
remove any remaining polishing residues before examination.
The metallographic microstructure of the samples was observed with a Zeiss Axiolab 5
optical microscope equipped with an Axiocam 212 color digital camera and Zen core 3.5
software for Windows. All scale bars were calibrated using the software.
3.6
Experimental Silicon Content Survey
Silicon is the primary alloying element in Al-Si casting alloys, strongly influencing solidifi-
cation behavior, microstructure, and mechanical performance. At lower silicon contents,
aluminum alloys exhibit a more ductile matrix with limited castability, while increasing sil-

12.5 wt%. The goal was to investigate a range of hypoeutectic Al-Si alloys—those with
silicon content below the eutectic composition (approximately 12.6 wt%).

Discussion
5.1
Silicon Survey
The intended silicon concentrations in the cast aluminium alloys were 12.5%, 10.5%,
7.5%, 5.5%, and 2.5% by weight. However, analysis of the final compositions showed
slight deviations from these targets. As shown in ??, the actual silicon content in each
alloy was close to the target value, though not exact. The largest deviation occurred in the
AlSi12.5 alloy, where the measured Si content was 11.89% instead of 12.5%. Similarly,
the AlSi2.5 alloy reached 2.17% Si instead of 2.5%. These differences are relatively
small and are likely due to minor losses during alloying and melting, such as oxidation or
sampling uncertainty.
Despite the deviations, the overall trend in silicon content was maintained across the
series, supporting the intended comparative analysis. Sources of error could be linked
to melt homogeneity, loss of silicon during melting, segregation, or the sampling location.
These deviations do not affect the overall survey, where the goal was to explore different
hypoeutectic Al-Si compositions. Thus, these deviations were accepted as the alloys
under investigation.
These small deviations in silicon concentration, while not ideal, were still sufficient to ob-
serve trends in microstructural development across the alloy series. One notable obser-
vation was the increased formation of brittle, nettle-like eutectic silicon phases in alloys
with higher silicon content.
In particular, the AlSi12.5 and AlSi7.5 samples exhibited a pronounced presence of aci-
cular or plate-like eutectic Si structures, which are known to negatively impact ductility
and toughness. This aligns with expectations, as higher silicon content promotes more
eutectic solidification, often in coarse and interconnected morphologies when unmodified.
Conversely, alloys with lower Si content, such as AlSi2.5 and AlSi5.5, displayed signifi-
cantly less of these unwanted phases. The microstructure in these alloys was more dom-
inated by the primary aluminium matrix with smaller and more rounded silicon particles,
which are generally more favorable for mechanical performance.
Overall, the microstructural evolution observed supports the compositional trend and re-
inforces the importance of tightly controlling silicon levels and applying modification tech-
niques where necessary to suppress the formation of these brittle phases.
In general, alloys with higher silicon content solidified over a slightly broader temperature
range and showed earlier initiation of eutectic transformation. For instance, the AlSi12.5
alloy exhibited a noticeable eutectic plateau starting at approximately XXX ◦C, whereas
the AlSi2.5 alloy displayed this feature much later, around XXX ◦C. This trend aligns with
expectations from the Al-Si phase diagram, where increasing Si content lowers the liq-
uidus temperature and shifts the eutectic point upward in the cooling sequence.
Solidification times varied modestly between the alloys. The higher-silicon alloys tended
to solidify more slowly due to a larger fraction of eutectic liquid, which releases latent heat
over a longer duration. This extended solidification range can promote the formation of
interconnected eutectic silicon phases, as observed in the microstructure.
In some samples, a brief temperature arrest or recalescence was noted during the eutectic
stage, particularly in the AlSi7.5 and AlSi12.5 alloys. This indicates delayed nucleation

Overall, the cooling curve analysis supports the metallographic findings. Alloys with faster

morphologies, while those with slower solidification and earlier eutectic formation tended
to develop more of the unwanted nettle-like structures.
5.1.1
Hardness Testing
Hardness measurements were performed on each alloy composition using the Vickers
method. The results revealed a clear linear relationship between silicon content and hard-
ness: AlSi12.5 showed the highest hardness, while AlSi2.5 was the softest.
This trend aligns with expectations, as increased silicon content contributes to a greater
volume fraction of hard silicon phases in the microstructure. Both primary and eutectic
silicon act as reinforcing phases within the aluminium matrix, increasing resistance to
plastic deformation. The results also complement the findings from the cooling curves
and microstructure analyses, where higher silicon alloys exhibited more extensive eutectic
formation and a greater presence of brittle, acicular silicon structures.
Overall, the hardness data supports the compositional gradient designed in this study and
reinforces the link between alloying, thermal behavior, and mechanical response.

with the microstructural observations—where higher-silicon alloys exhibited a denser net-

5.2
Thermal Analysis
The solidification process is often accompanied by the release or absorption of heat, which
is reflected in the cooling curve. From the cooling curve and its first-order differential
curve, three key temperatures can be identified: the nucleation temperature (TN), mini-
mum temperature (Tmin), and growth temperature (TG).
The nucleation temperature (TN) is defined as the point where the slope of the differential
curve increases abruptly. This is the temperature at which nucleation begins, and during
this phase, the latent heat of solidification is released, causing the rate of temperature
reduction to slow down. As the nucleation rate increases, the latent heat of crystallization
also increases. The temperature at which the released latent heat of solidification equals
the heat dissipated by the sample is defined as the minimum temperature (Tmin), at which
the rate of temperature reduction approaches zero.
As the solidification process continues, additional latent heat of solidification is released,
which leads to an increase in temperature. The growth temperature (TG) is reached when
the rate of temperature increase approaches zero, as the latent heat of solidification is
balanced with the heat dissipated during the cooling process.
The difference between the calculated nucleation temperature and the measured nucle-
ation temperature is known as the nucleation undercooling (∆T). The calculated nucle-
ation temperature was determined using an Al-Si phase diagram for each alloy concen-
tration. The results for the Al-Si binary alloys are presented in Table 5.1 and Table 5.2,
respectively.

5.3
Hollomon Fitting of Stress–Strain Curves
To describe the plastic deformation behavior of the samples, the true stress–strain curves
from the compression tests were fitted using the Hollomon equation:
σ = K · εn
(5.1)
where σ is the true stress, ε is the true strain, K is the strength coefficient, and n is the
strain hardening exponent.
The fitting was performed using MATLAB, based on a custom script. Only the plastic
region of the true stress–strain curve was used for fitting. The transition from elastic to
plastic behavior was identified by the deviation from the initial linear region. A logarithmic
transformation was applied to the selected data:
ln(σ) = ln(K) + n · ln(ε)
(5.2)
A linear regression was then used to extract the values of K and n. The coefficient of
determination (R2) was used to evaluate the quality of each fit.
