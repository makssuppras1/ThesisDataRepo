Abstract
The increasing use of exposed structural timber elements in high­rise and complex build­
ings raises a concern in the fire safety community. In an attempt to verify the safety of such
structures and their occupants, engineers can use the aid of computational fluid dynam­
ics (CFD) to predict the fire propagation in compartment fires. This thesis investigates the
smoke filling at the early stage of a ventilation­controlled compartment fire. The aim is to
highlight the importance of studying occupant safety both in fire testing and when utilizing
performance­based design, such as applying CFD modelling. Fire Dynamics Simulator
(FDS) was used as computational model and the reference model was conducted by RISE
Fire Research in 2020. The exposed timber elements in the test consisted of one glulam
beam, CLT ceiling, and two opposite CLT walls.
The CLT­exposed FDS­model was successfully validated by comparing the temperature
development output to the fire test data. A corresponding model of solely concrete ele­
ments was then used to investigate whether the exposed CLT had an effect on the smoke
filling. The output could reveal that the exposed CLT had little impact on the smoke fill­
ing. The smoke was mainly generated by the fuel content, and FDS could successfully
predict the time to critical conditions. 4 minutes and 24 seconds after ignition, the smoke
layer height was measured to be 1.8 m and 1.9 m in the fire test and the FDS models,
respectively. It is concluded that the methods used lead to promising results on both
temperature development and smoke filling, while emphasizing the urgency to verify the
safety of people. Future work on this study and further development in areas of CFD and
FDS is necessary, as the study is built on several methods and approximations that are
not validated by FDS developers.
Keywords: compartment fires, pre­flashover, smoke filling, occupant safety, CLT, CFD,
FDS

1
Introduction
The environmental impact from the building and construction industry has reached a
record height and keeps increasing post­COVID according to the UN Environment Pro­
gramme [1]. The UNEP states that the building industry with its activities and material use
represents almost 37% of the global energy­ and process­related CO2­emissions. Among
these statistics the production of conventional building materials, such as concrete, steel
and aluminum, represents 4% of global energy use and 6% of global emissions. The UN
urges a decarbonization of the building industry by 2050 to meet the Paris Agreement.
When comparing concrete and timber building components, the timber components show
a significantly lower environmental impact [2]. Several nations have therefore answered
UN’s recommendations by increasing the use of timber­based materials. Although tim­
ber is a traditional building material in several cultures, the use of laminated­ and cross­
laminated timber elements (CLT) have increased in recent decades for complex­ and high­
rise buildings. The use of exposed timber have been most welcomed by architects as the
wood linings are regarded as architectonic and appealing. Additionally, timber products
and linings are proven to have a positive impact on occupants’ psychological health [3].
However, there are not only positive aspects of using timber. Fire safety is a concern
regarding the reinstatement of timber structures and design involving exposed timber lin­
ings. The exposed surfaces, whether interior or exterior, imply a great risk concerning fire
spread. Destructive and deadly city fires have initiated the concepts of fire safety regula­
tions in recent centuries. For many years the regulations restricted the use of timber and
other combustible materials in buildings, and these have evolved into today’s prescriptive
codes. Worldwide, the national prescriptive codes have shown a tendency to decrease
the restrictions on the usage of timber structures, even in high­rise buildings [4]. Not only
are higher buildings allowed to be constructed with combustible materials, the codes also
allow a greater use of exposed timber surfaces.
To many fire engineers and scientists the reinstatement of combustible materials in build­
ings is concerning, and they argue that safety should not be compromised by environmental­
or architectonic­based arguments. In recent years there has been a trend to build designs
that are not validated through sufficient research. The ultimate concern is the idea of even­
tually repeating historical city fires and endangering human lives. A general argument to

2.2
Compartment fires
The theory regarding compartment fires and enclosure fire dynamics will be presented
in this section in order to understand the mechanisms of smoke filling. Smoke filling is
significant for compartment fires as the ventilation is limited through openings and the
enclosing boundaries affect the fire properties and the room’s environmental conditions
[15]. Smoke and toxic combustion products causes irritation and reduced visibility, and
can ultimately lead to poisoning or death. It is therefore important to investigate the de­
velopment of the fire, especially early stages, in order to understand the smoke filling of
the compartment. The duration of a compartment fire and its development can be divided
into four main stages: incipient, growth, fully developed, and decay. The fuel load, open­
ings, and boundary linings determine the properties of each stage. Figure 2.2 presents a
typical time­temperature graph for a compartment fire and its four stages.

The stages are described as follows:
• Incipient stage: Piloted­ or auto­ignition of an object inside the compartment.
• Growth stage: The fire spreads from the ignition object and grows as more energy
and combustion products are released. More items within the compartment ignites,
and a hot gas layer is generated close to the ceiling while its temperature and thick­
ness gradually increases. The growth stage is governed by the fuel as there is
usually sufficient oxygen within the compartment at this stage (fuel­controlled fire).
• Fully developed stage: With an approximate temperature of 500­600°C [17] the
hot gas layer has transferred sufficient heat to ignite all combustible content inside
the compartment. This stage change is known as flashover. After flashover the fire
is fully developed, also referred to as steady state, and the fire reaches its peak heat
release rate (HRR). This stage is governed by the openings, fuel, and compartment

2.3.1
Ignition and pyrolysis of wood
In the first fire stage up until ignition, the wood surface vaporizes when exposed to suffi­
cient heat. Heat transferred in the form of radiation or convection decomposes the solid
fuel, and when the gaseous fuel reaches ignition temperature at approximately 400°C
[25], or correspondingly a critical heat flux of approximately 12 kW/m2 [26], the wood ig­

wood. As the gases continues to burn, in addition to vaporized fuel, the decomposition of
the wood also produces a solid carbon­rich residual known as char [27]. As the burning
continues into the wood a charring layer is formed at the exposed surface. The thermal
decomposition of wood under limited oxygen supply and formation of char is better known
as pyrolysis, see Figure 2.7.

2.3.2
Delamination
As the fuel in the compartment is burned out, the fire duration is governed by the burning
of the CLT surfaces. Auto­extinction has been observed in post­flashover stages of fully
developed fires [26]. One important measure to obtain auto­extinction is to mitigate the
occurrence of delamination. Delamination is the mechanism where the outer, charred
lamella of a CLT element falls off due to failure of the adhesive. As the char layer falls
off, new unburnt wood is exposed and can initiate a new combustion process, feeding the
compartment fire. Delamination and aspects included in the decay phase of a fire will not
be investigated in this study as it is limited to early stage fires.

2.4
Fire modelling
2.4.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is a method used to computationally solve problems
of fluid flows and predict its nature by numerical iterations [32]. CFD uses the laws of con­
servation which controls the fluid motion, such as mass, momentum, and energy. Navier­
Stokes equations are partial differential equations which provide an analytical solution
to fluid dynamics and the related laws of conservation. As all cases involving fluid flow
are different, there is an issue in providing a general solution to fluid dynamics [33]. The
domain in CFD is based on a simple box geometry, and given the boundary conditions
of this geometry, computers are able to provide a general solution to the Navier­Stoke
equations.
The domain is divided into smaller boxes, called cells, and the combination of the cells
is called a mesh or grid. By the means of iteration the computer uses the equations and
the known boundary conditions of the mesh’s edges to balance the cells in the model.
The conditions within each cell, such as velocity and temperature, is uniform and only
changes with time [34]. As all neighbouring cells interact with each other, the model is
able to visualize and provide wanted output of the fluid flow. The accuracy of CFD models
depends on the user input, and the model must be validated by for example experimental
studies.
2.4.2
Fire Dynamics Simulator
Fire Dynamics Simulator (FDS) is an open­source program developed by the National
Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in the USA [11]. FDS is a CFD model which
is designed to simulate thermally­driven fluid flows in buildings [34]. Its main focus is
to predict the transport of smoke and heat from fires. The program numerically solves
Navier­Stokes equations correlated to turbulent fluid flow by using Large Eddy Simulation
(LES) as mathematical model. By using LES and finer grids, exact properties related to
time and geometry can be derived in the model. LES uses simplified combustion equa­
tions with a so­called ”low Mach number”, meaning that the gases move with a low speed
velocity. The lowest mach number that can be validated in FDS is approximately 0.1,
corresponding to 34.3 m/s. By that, LES operates within the atmosphere surrounding the
fire in a compartment, and calculates the mixing of combustion products and gaseous
fuel caused by turbulent fluid flow. These calculations provide the burning rate of the fire,
which is then used to predict the spread of hot gases and smoke at low speed motion.
As mentioned, the domain is divided into cells with uniform conditions changing over time.
The cells consist of rectilinear volumes defined by a three­dimensional coordinate system
(X, Y, Z), resulting in a rectilinear grid. Obstructions, which are objects placed in the

Pyrolysis and combustion parameters
Pine and water are the only reacting materials as indicated in the following chemical re­
actions:
pine −→char + pyrolyzate (cellulose) + water
water −→water vapor
As indicated in the reaction schemes, pine undergo one single reaction. After that, water
as by­product then undergo one single reaction when turning into water vapor. Pyrolyzate
is the reactant fuel in the model which has the chemical structure of cellulose.
The combustion model is based on a single­step, mixing controlled reaction. This means
that the heat release will be based on a single gas­phase reaction of cellulose as a result of
the pyrolysis process of pine. In solid­phase the wood undergoes a heating process which
results in the production of the reactant gaseous fuel, char, and water. The surface of the
wood will then undergo the gas­phase reaction and produce heat, various by­products,
and water vapor.
FDS does not have a fully developed pyrolysis model for the burning of wooden mate­
rials. The approach is not validated by the FDS developers, meaning that the pyrolysis
model set­up must mainly be based on approximations with cone calorimetry and TGA
experiments. The main challenge is then to determine material data and pyrolysis reac­

4.4
Reliability and possible errors
Sources of errors and the methods’ reliability have been discussed throughout the thesis.
The most interesting aspects which could affect the results and output are summarized in
the following list.
Grid sensitivity study:
• There should be established criteria to what defines an acceptable deviation in the
output between various grid resolutions in order to conclude an optimized grid cell
size. The topic should be investigated further, especially in the case of complicated
fuel load and its distribution such as in this study. The input used in this study is
highly inaccurate which makes it difficult to conclude an appropriate grid resolution.
Fuel load:
• The fuel load is simplified in both geometry, growth rate, and heat release. Each
furniture’s HRR is based on a ratio between its calorific value and the total HRR
of the fuel. The optimal solution would also here be to determine the chemical
components and their reactions, rather than having reactions based solely on pine
burning.
• the geometry of the furniture as a collective fuel load in the four fires is too much
of an approximation, and the height differences cause inaccuracies for the plate
thermometers especially.
Pyrolysis and combustion:
• The kinetic parameters for the pyrolysis reaction rate are based on unknown A and
E constants, and a more accurate yet time­demanding approach would be to use
a reference TGA of CLT or pine. The constants could then be validated in a corre­
sponding FDS model.
• FDS only calculates a single reaction among the combustible gases. In reality these
gases undergo thousands of reactions, and the chemical processes are therefore
highly simplified.
• The relative humidity from the test is 89% and modelled with the default value of 40
% as two air species would make it impossible to use the simple chemistry model.
• It is limited how well FDS takes the heat contribution into the room into account.
This will affect the temperature measurements.
• In reality there will occur more charring at the lower parts of the timber panels due
to higher oxygen levels. As charring is poorly modelled in FDS it is limited how well
this will be taken into account by the model. This could affect the fire propagation
and smoke generation, but to a limited degree as it is more critical for longer fire
duration.
The effect of exposed CLT surfaces in early stage compartment fires
63
==PAGE:76==
Materials:
• The glulam beam and column are not included, which can also affect the output.
• Gypsum is not added to the openings as they are in the test.
• The adjusted thicknesses of the material layers in order to match the grid cells are
much thicker in the FDS models compared to the fire test. This may change the
thermal inertia among other factors, but the differences probably have too little effect
on the development of the early stage compartment fire.
• The materials are assumed to be perfectly homogeneous in FDS, which is not the
case in reality.
Extinction in CLT FDS­model:
• The suppression of the fire immediately after flashover must be investigated and ex­
plained to closer detail. The reason for the burning only to continue at the openings
must be explained beyond assuming oxygen­starvation in the rest of the compart­
ment.
Time of flashover:
• The radiative heat flux and critical heat flux does not occur at the exact same time.
A study to optimize this would be necessary. This could be the main reason for the
flashover and its peak temperature to occur earlier for the FDS models compared
to the fire test.
Smoke filling:
• The method of studying the video footage from the fire test bear some inaccuracies
due to poor video quality and limited camera angles. Therefore, the estimated time
of full ignition at 4 minutes have great uncertainties.
• The comparative photo­analysis in Figure 4.23 is not optimal. The overall good
results on the smoke filling study is questionable.

5
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the possibilities of modelling an early stage compartment
fire with exposed CLT surfaces. Through this work the thesis has emphasized the impor­
tance of studying the critical conditions for the safety of occupants. By studying alternative
usages of FDS and the limitations of these, it is clear that CFD modelling is still underde­
veloped in order for the performance­based codes to rely on it.
In the validation process the thermocouple trees in FDS showed good correspondence
with the fire test measurements. The plate thermometers revealed poorer results, mainly
due to the localized placement of the measurement devices. When ignoring the fire sup­
pression after flashover, and the resulting drop in HRR, it can be concluded that the FDS
models serve their purpose for the early stages of the compartment fire. The acceptable
results achieved for the concrete FDS­model reflects that the suppression in the CLT­
model is not caused by errors of the input file, but rather FDS’ inability to compute with a
large HRR. As the temperatures are also affected by the geometry of the fire vents and
the unpredictable nature of fire development, it can be concluded that the FDS model is
validated when compared to RISE’s fire test.
The study of the smoke filling showed that the exposed CLT had very little contribution to
the smoke filling of the compartment. This reveals that the smoke gas layer is generated
by the fuel and not the combustible surfaces, as the concrete and CLT models had the
same output. Hand calculations with Zukoski’s plume model deviated with 11 seconds
from the FDS models, not a completely inadequate result. As the heat release rate for the
FDS models and the hand calculations are based on approximations and many assump­
tions, it is difficult to conclude if the FDS modelling could accurately predict the smoke
filling. Both the concrete and CLT FDS­model had 24 seconds until critical conditions
when only simulating the growth phase after full ignition was reached. Compared with the
reference fire test, the output on smoke filling from the CLT FDS­model was satisfactory.
Also here there are approximations made when observing the video footage of the test.
The fire test only deviated with 10 cm from the FDS predictions on the smoke layer height,
Z. When taking the incipient phase into consideration, the smoke layer height at 4 minutes
and 24 seconds was 1.8 m and 1.9 m for the fire test and CLT FDS­model, respectively.
It is concluded that the study has successfully investigated the fire properties of an early
stage compartment fire by the means of FDS modelling. However, the study had to endure
a lot of guesswork and execution of modelling methods which are neither validated nor
commonly used in the FDS community. The thesis have nonetheless highlighted the
importance of continuing the development of proper fire simulation tools to be used with
performance based codes among fire safety engineers. All things considered, the smoke
filling of the compartment was successfully predicted by a validated FDS model.

5.1
Future work
The first initiative for future work would be to continue the iteration and optimization of
the grid sensitivity study and radiative heat flux study. These are not complicated tasks,
although they do require time and sufficient computer power.
One of the biggest challenges of this study was to model the distribution and magnitude
of the fuel and its heat release. A future study could involve different ways to model the
fire and its spread, such as a radically spreading fire in FDS. One could also try to create
obstacles with surface boundaries which resemble the geometry of the furniture. A minor
attempt was made to achieve this, but was dismissed as the fire vents on the floor was
less time consuming in the modelling process.
It would be an interesting study to use a reference fire test that was equipped with a gas
burner or one plume fire source. The furniture and wood cribs that is often used in fire
tests contribute to great challenges for the CFD­modelling engineer, as experienced with
this study. Gas burners would reduce the number of approximations and assumptions in
relation to the fuel load. This would leave more room for optimizations of the FDS output,
and possibly minimize the number of deviations and errors in the validation process. Lo­
cal temperatures could especially improve, as already stated in regard to the geometry of
furniture and localized plate thermometers.
With a simpler fuel set­up there could also be room to investigate the fire and temper­
ature propagation into the CLT­panels during a fully developed fire. This way the study
could go in depth to what happens to the laminated timber, while studying all of the fire
phases of the compartment fire. In addition to this, the use of a reference TGA could
optimize the accuracy of the kinetic parameters in the pyrolysis model.
Lastly, a proper method to estimate the incipient phase should be investigated in FDS.
The way FDS­fires undergo steady growth without considering the time to achieve ignition,
results in output which must be adjusted by the user.

A.1.4
Discussion and conclusion
The CLT FDS­model results show that the differences in output for both the thermocou­
ples’ average temperatures and the HRR is at 4 % or less between δx = 0.10 m and δx =
0.05 m. It is important to keep these deviations as small as possible considering the high
uncertainties in regard to the input parameters of the model. This includes the pyrolysis
model, fuel load and its distribution, burning­ and growth rate. It is difficult to conclude
what an appropriate deviation percent­wise should be. FDS developers and competent
FDS­users have stated that since each case is different, and the input data of various
accuracy, it is hard to establish an acceptance criteria for the output deviation. For this
study, less than 4 % is concluded to be of sufficient accuracy, but should be investigated
in future work.
In addition to this the computation time for the simulations are considered. The simula­
tion time vastly increases after δx = 0.10 m for both the CLT­ and Concrete FDS­model.
It is found unnecessary to increase the fineness after this stage, considered the small
deviation of output for the finer resolutions anyway.
The results from the Concrete FDS­model show a lower average deviation compared
to the CLT output. The computation time is much lower and stays under 24 hours per
simulation.
It is concluded that δx = 0.10 m should be used as grid resolution. The resolution of the
Concrete FDS­model may be of a higher resolution as the output shows that δx = 0.15 m
should be sufficient. However, it is desired to keep the two models of CLT and concrete
with as similar input parameters as possible. Both models are set up with a grid resolution
of δx = 0.10 m. This finalises the grid sensitivity study.

A.6.2
Discussion and conclusion
The CLT FDS­model shows promising results when comparing the set time to ignition
and the actual time of reach critical heat flux. Fire 2­1 and 2­3 show a delay of 37 and 92
seconds, respectively. This could be caused by a timer delay and limited fire intensity of
the ignition source. Other input parameters for these matters have been investigated, but
not adequately enough to draw a conclusion on what the timer and fire intensity should
be. This problem should be studied in future work.
As expected, the concrete model has a slower fire growth and hence a delay in the in­
crease of radiative heat flux. The non­combustible surfaces result in a reduced total HRR
compared to the CLT FDS­model and RISE’s fire test. To complete the comparative anal­
ysis of the smoke filling the models are kept with similar input parameters, and the delays
are therefore ignored.
It is concluded that the FDS­models reaches the critical heat flux of the fuel content within
reasonable limits. An optimization should be performed in the future for the timer and
fire intensity of the ignition source, Fire 1. As the CLT and concrete model contribute
differently to the radiative heat flux, the delays of the Concrete FDS­model are ignored.
