Abstract
Tis thesis deals with the problem of laterally loaded non-slender piles, with the speciο§¹c
aim to evaluate the capability of 3D ο§¹nite element modeling in capturing the monopile
behavior using the hardening soil model. Te success of the numerical modeling is eval-
uated by comparison against similitude small scale centrifuge models of the problem,
performed in DTU in 2018. Te study presented in this thesis focuses on several key as-
pects, among which: (i) representation of the pile using solid elements, beam theory, and
shell elements, (ii) the constitutive model and its governing parameters. (iii) the contri-
bution of vertical shaft shear stresses which creates reaction moments (a feature relevant
to non-slender piles).
Te thesis compares the numerical results with a centrifuge experiment and further ex-
tends the analysis to other monopile conο§¹gurations. Within the investigation, the soil
pile interaction is characterized using numerically extracted p-y curves, which are com-
pared with commonly used API (2000) p-y curves. Various normalization of the results
are considered in an attempt to identify the controlling parameters.
It was found that the API (2000) p-y curves provide an excessively stiff response when
compared with the 3D simulations. Replacing API (2000)βÄôs subgrade modulus with that
basedonE50 (oneofthehardeningsoilmodelparameters)providedamuchbetteragree-
ment. Te normalization of the overall pile response with E50 at depth of one pile diam-
eter, led to a similar normalized load-displacement result for different pile diameters, at
least at the early stage of loading which is governed by stiffness and not failure. Te con-
tribution of the developed (reaction) distributed moment due to vertical shear stresses
was rather minor in the considered analyses, possibly due to the use of a low friction
angle for the interface of the pile and the soil, contributing up to 3% of the resistance. Fi-
nally, simulations of a real size monopile were performed with the aim to produce results
that may be used for future design.

As the worldwide demand for alternative renewable energy increases so does the de-
mand for an improved design solution for laterally loaded piles which are often used as
an offshore wind turbine foundation. Offshore wind turbines are generally subjected to
two main lateral loads. Te ο§¹rst is due to the wind which is applied mostly at the top
of the wind turbine structure, creating relatively high load eccentricity on the founda-
tion. Te second load is due to sea waves. Tese loads are transferred to the foundation
system (monopiles) which provide resistance (reaction) to these combined loads. Today,
monopiles are the most common support structures for wind turbines, accounting for
approximately 80 percent of the foundation systems. A typical monopile is composed of
a hollow steel cylinder with a large diameter, ranging between 4 to 8 m, and a small slen-
derness ratio (embedded pile length to diameter, L/D) typically between 4-8 (Murphy
et al., 2018).
In the past two decades, signiο§¹cant efforts were invested in optimizing the design
of offshore wind turbine foundations using centrifuge experiments (e.g., Klinkvort and
Hededal, 2014; Georgiadis et al., 1992) or different loading ο§¹eld tests (e.g., Murphy et al.,
2018; Byrne et al., 2019; Page et al., 2019). Tis was done with the understanding that the
design of slender piles, using the traditional method of p-y curves (such as API guide-
lines) to describe the soil-pile interaction, is not necessarily suitable for large diameter
monopiles, as it was originally calibrated using relatively long piles with a small diame-
ter. Hence, the p-y curve method ignores the soil resistance developed due to the cross-
section rotation leading to the development of vertical shear stresses along the pile (small
in slender piles but signiο§¹cant in large diameter piles). As a consequence, the traditional
design approaches lead to non-economic, over conservative design, especially when the
friction between the pile and the soil is not small.

2.2
Methods of soil-pile interaction analysis
Generally, the lateral behavior of piles may be analyzed using two models (Fleming et al.,
2009). Te ο§¹rst, and a more common one, is the use of a load transfer function or p-y
curve. In this approach the generalized Winkler assumption is used, in which the soil
response at a given depth is independent of the soil response at other depths. In the
Winkler classical model, this is represented as individual linear support springs along
the pile ignoring the potential resistance of the shear deformation between the springs.
In the more general context, a load-transfer function is used (named p-y curve) which
represents the nonlinear soil reaction p versus the lateral pile displacement, y, at a given
depth. Tis ο§¹rst approach is an approximated technique as it does not capture the con-
tinuum nature of the soil. Te second approach is to capture the continuum nature of the
soil, either through direct analytical derivation (elastic solution such as those of Poulos
and Davis (1974)) or using the ο§¹nite element or ο§¹nite difference method. Te current the-
sis uses the ο§¹nite element method to simulate the pile behavior but establishes p-y curves
from the response of the soil around the pile. Comparison against the commonly used
p-y curves of API (2000) is presented in the thesis. Te p-y curves of API (2000) are essen-
tially based on the comprehensive work of Reese et al. (1974) with the alternative form of

Issues with traditional p-y curves
One of the main differences between conventional piles and monopiles is the contribu-
tion of vertical shear stresses to the behavior of the piles. Te vertical shear stresses can
add an external distribution moment to the overall loading of the pile. In this thesis, it is
denoted by m(z). For conventional piles the soil resistance moment, m(z), is relatively
small (if not negligible) due to the small arm (associated with the small diameter). For
large diameter monopiles, this may be of importance. Currently, this aspect is not well
covered within API standards, and in fact it is ignored by the p-y method. Moreover, the
traditional p-y approach was found to be unreliable for the design of wind turbine pile
foundation systems, as it underestimates the soil resistance (this was shown by Byrne
et al. (2019), for example). Terefore, in the last two decades, a signiο§¹cant research effort
has been placed to develop improved design methodologies using ο§¹eld/centrifuge tests
that are veriο§¹ed with advanced computer models.
One of the major research efforts into this topic is the PISA project (Pile Soil Analysis)
- a joint industry research project by Byrne et al. (2019), established in 2013. Te PISA
project is aimed to develop an improved design method for laterally loaded monopiles,
to answer the limitations and drawbacks of the traditional methods (p-y method), when
designing offshore wind turbines. Te project involved a range of theoretical studies,
numerical analysis, and ο§¹eld testing, on sands and clays. As an outcome of the project, a
number of papers were published for describing the research results and the new design
methodologies.
As an example, in Taborda et al. (2019), the calibration of sandy soil model parameters
from laboratory and ο§¹eld investigation, and the use of an advanced constitutive model
are emphasized and detailed. Tis data was implemented into a global 3D FE model.

Methodology and tools
Tis chapter presents the methodology of the work and the tools involved in its applica-

Te thesis aims to replicate the observed behavior of laterally loaded piles as obtain in
the centrifuge small scale similitude experiments of Hellesen (2018), using ο§¹nite element

the stressing and response in the FE model. Tis is achieved by a few stages:

4. Detailed investigation of the pile response and the interaction forces between the soil
and the pile (in terms of p-y curves and m(z) distribution).

totype and up to a real monopile characteristic).

Chapter9
Summary and conclusions
Monopilesarecurrentlythepreferredfoundationsolutionsforwindturbines. Teydiffer
from conventional piles due to their large diameter (up to 8m) and relative small L/D ra-
tio. Consequently, they behave differently than conventional piles and therefore require
research on the topic.
Tis thesis has dealt with the problem of laterally loaded monopiles and focused on
numerical FE simulations of previously performed centrifuge test in DTU, and the exten-
sion of the investigation to more realistic dimensions. Besides the literature review and
methodology, the work of the thesis was presented in 7 main chapters. Chapter 4 dealt
with the calibration of the constitutive parameters of the hardening soil model using tri-
axial test data of Fountainbluea. Te processes involved a regression analysis to deο§¹ne
the stress dependent stiffness parameters and strength parameters. Te calibrated pa-
rameters were then used in an independent simulation of a triaxial test (using the Soil-
Test option) and the results were compared with those from the test. Overall, a very good
agreement was observed supporting the use of the model for the centrifuge test simula-
tions.
Chapter 5 dealt with the two-sections test pile representation using various methods,
amongwhichshellelementrepresentation, Euler-Bernoullibeam, fullcrosssectionsolid
element representation, and a mixed element solid representation It was shown that the
full cross section representation with an equivalent YoungβÄôs modulus reproduces well the
two-sections Euler-Bernoulli (analytical) beam, better than the other approaches. Con-
sidering that the thesis uses a speciο§¹c feature in Plaxis of volume integration, suitable
for solid elements but not for shell elements, it was decided to use the two-section full
cross section representation as to the chosen model for the rest of the thesis.
Chapter 6 presented the FE model aiming to replicate the centrifuge test, together
with a description of the boundary condition and the selected mesh (following a mesh
sensitivity analysis). Te simulation results are compared with the centrifuge model in
