Abstract
This thesis explores the Clustered Traveling Salesman Path Problem and presents a
solution involving the use of a clustering method applied beforehand. Methods for ob-
taining acceptable clustering are presented, focusing on finding the right parameters used
in the clustering algorithm and adjusting cluster sizes. A solution for the total prob-
lem is found by reducing the problem complexity and solving each cluster as a separate
problem. Methods for calculating lower bounds of varying computational complexity
are introduced, utilizing Minimum Spanning Trees and the Open Traveling Salesman
Problem, the latter of which produces a tighter bound at the expense of increased com-
putation time. The upper bounds are determined using the Open Traveling Salesman
Problem again, but with feasible edges obtained through greedy heuristics. The Lin-
Kernighan-Helsgaun heuristic is also used for upper bounds and creates tighter bounds.
A case study is presented for a postal route on Bornholm with 23,398 addresses after
the removal of duplicates. The best solution obtained offers a total route of 1,495,168
meters with a 2.6% gap compared to the lower bound, completed in 19.88 minutes. Com-
parison with a previous solution shows that the best solution is 1.32% shorter, with an
improvement in computational time of 13.55 minutes.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
When looking at high-density vehicle routing problem (VRP) in a real life scenario like
delivery and pickup industries, it can be necessary to visit a large number of addresses.
Creating a big route that connects all addresses together is very impractical on its own,
especially when dealing with potentially tens of thousands of addresses.
To address
this challenge, it is beneficial to cluster addresses before forming a major route. This
approach ensures that the route covers a defined area before moving on to the next,
resulting in manageable and focused delivery zones. By assigning specific clusters to
individual delivery personnel, consistency is maintained in service areas on a daily or
weekly basis, improving operational eﬀiciency.
In this thesis, the Clustered Traveling Salesman Path Problem (CTSPP) at a large
scale is addressed. This problem is a variation of the classic Traveling Salesman Problem
(TSP), which seeks the shortest cycle through N nodes, visiting each node exactly once.
The addition to the original problem is that all nodes grouped into clusters, and all nodes
within a cluster must be visited consecutively before moving to the next cluster. Unlike
the traditional TSP that seeks a Hamiltonian cycle, CTSPP aims to find a Hamiltonian
path. The problem is tackled by breaking it down into manageable clusters, each cluster
in solved individually and corresponding sub-problems are solved. The solutions to these
sub-problems are integrated to construct a coherent route.
The primary emphasis of this project is to build on a previous project in [Nil23] and
examine the part of the algorithm responsible for grouping customers into clusters, and
identify techniques that can improve its performance. The focus of these two projects
are completely different. In the previous project, the clustering was fixed throughout
the whole project and the emphasis was placed on adjusting how routes were formed,

finding points that were good candidates for endpoints and how to improve the solution
in these ways. This project will not try to change the routing as much but will focus on
how is best to cluster the customers before the routing process is performed with as low

• What are the most effective clustering or districting techniques for this project,

• What are the key factors to consider when designing a clustering or districting

• How can implementation of new clustering or districting methods improve the

• Is it possible to design an iterative approach that adjusts clusters based on the

The project took a change of direction as it was carried out. The data provided
was not as accessible as expected, thus a significant part of the project was spent on
improving the routing process to make it usable for whatever clustering applied as well as
observing and experimenting with a clustering method that could possibly improve the
previous best solution provided by [Nil23]. Still, the current project draws inspiration
from the previous project in many ways.
The thesis is structured as follows. First, Chapter 2 provides a review of relevant
literature on routing, clustering and districting problems. Next, a problem description is
presented in Chapter 3 with a small example to explain the problem in a simplified way.

Traveling Salesman Path Problem (CTSPP) are explained.

in Chapter 7, they are discussed and summarized.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
This chapter provides a review of the existing literature relevant to the research topics
addressed in this thesis. It begins with an exploration of the Traveling Salesman Problem
(TSP), detailing both the Lin-Kernighan-Helsgaun (LKH) algorithm and the Concorde

cated solution algorithms and a method that transforms the CTSP into a standard TSP

districting and clustering techniques and a detailed examination of the primary cluster-

2.1
Routing problems
The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) is one of the most well-known optimization
problem, and it has received extensive attention from researchers in supply chain man-
agement, operations research, computer science, and other fields. The objective of the
TSP is to determine the shortest path through N nodes that starts and ends in the same
city and visits each node. The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) is a widely studied
combinatorial optimization problem in fields like supply chain management, operations
research, and computer science. It shares a close relationship with the Hamiltonian cycle
problem, which assesses whether a specified graph can support a tour that visits each
node exactly once and is classified as NP-complete. The TSP builds on this by seeking
==PAGE:14==
2.1 Routing problems
4
the least costly Hamiltonian cycle, making it NP-hard due to its optimization nature
[SS19].
Given the computational challenges of the Traveling Salesman Problem, researchers
have developed various algorithms and solvers to find eﬀicient solutions. Among the most
notable are the Lin-Kernighan-Helsgaun (LKH) algorithm and the Concorde solver. The
LKH algorithm, an effective heuristic method based on the Lin-Kernighan heuristic, has
been improved significantly by Keld Helsgaun to handle very large instances eﬀiciently,
often producing near-optimal solutions [Hel09]. Concorde is recognized as the state-of-
the-art exact solver for the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), known for its capability
to solve the TSP to optimality. Widely regarded as the best-performing exact TSP
solver available, it has been successfully applied to a broad range of real-world problems
[HS14]. These tools exemplify the ongoing effort to provide effective solutions to the
complex Traveling Salesman Problem.
An extension of the TSP is called the Clustered Traveling Salesman Problem (CTSP),
which was originally proposed by Chisman in 1975 [Chi75]. In the CTSP, the cities are
divided into clusters and the cities of each cluster must be visited contiguously. The goal
of the CTSP is to identify a Hamiltonian circuit with a minimum cost over all vertices,
where each cluster’s vertices must be visited in order [LHW22].
Several dedicated solution algorithms exist for solving the CTSP, ranging from exact
methods to heuristic and metaheuristic strategies. One of the earliest approaches is
the branch-and-bound algorithm proposed by Chisman ([Chi75]) in 1975, which focuses
on solving the integer programming model of the CTSP exactly. [LPQ97] introduced
a tabu search algorithm, a type of heuristic method, specifically designed for scenarios
where clusters must be visited in a fixed sequence. Additionally, [MSD13] improved
GRASP (Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure) algorithms by integrating
various path-relinking strategies, expanding beyond the traditional GRASP method with
six new heuristic variations. These algorithms collectively highlight the diverse strategies
used to tackle the computational challenges presented by the CTSP.
[LHW22] explores a method for solving the Clustered Traveling Salesman Problem
==PAGE:15==
2.2 Clustered Traveling Salesman Problem
5
(CTSP) by transforming it into a standard Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and solv-
ing it with traditional TSP solvers, both exact and heuristic. By adding large artificial
costs to inter-cluster edges, the authors ensure that CTSP restrictions are followed, al-
lowing the TSP solvers to perform competitively against specialized CTSP algorithms.
The study finds that the heuristic TSP solvers often outperform exact methods, offering
faster and more eﬀicient solutions that are practical for large-scale applications. It also
emphasizes how flexible and eﬀicient these methods, especially the heuristic ones, are in
solving optimization problems.
The Clustered Traveling Salesman Problem (CTSP) has led to several variations,
including the Ordered Clustered Traveling Salesman Problem (OCTSP) and the CTSP
with d-relaxed priority rule (CTSP-d). In the OCTSP, clusters must be visited in a pre-
specified order, and the end vertices of the clusters are unspecified [Ahm14]. CTSP-d
adds flexibility by allowing a vehicle to visit locations from nearby priority levels before
completing all visits at the current highest priority. This rule enables more adaptable
route planning that can better balance urgency with cost eﬀiciency [Phu+18].
The study by [SS08] explores the application of the shortest pre-Hamiltonian path
problem, a variant closely related to the Traveling Salesman Path Problem (TSPP). The
TSPP is an optimization problem where the objective is to find the shortest possible
route that visits a sequence of specified cities or nodes and ends at a different specified
city without the requirement to return to the starting point. This consideration is crucial
for large-scale logistics, where eﬀicient routes connect delivery points within designated
zones. By using mixed-integer linear programming (MILP), the authors create a robust
framework that simplifies routing decisions, enhances operational eﬀiciency, and provides
a scalable solution for extensive delivery networks.

2.3
Districting
Districting is the problem of gathering small geographic areas, known as basic units
or basic areas, into larger geographic clusters, called as districts or territories. This is
done in such a way that the districts or territories meet the applicable planning criteria.
Customers, streets, and zip code areas are common examples of basic units. District-
ing is also known as territory design, territory alignment, zone design, or sector design
depending on the context. Balance, continuity, and compactness are three important
criteria. Chapter 25 of Location Science goes into detail about districting problems such
as the major areas of application for districting problems, typical criteria used in district-
ing problems and methods to quantify them and different solution approaches. Many
different districting solution approaches have been proposed in the literature over the
years. Some approaches make use of a mathematical programming model, while others
rely solely on heuristics [KR19].
[HES+65] presented the first mathematical programming approach for a center-

seeking political districting problem.
However, because of its NP-hardness, the pre-
sented formulation is only practical for instances with a few hundred basic units, which
is rather small for typical sales districting problems. [SRC11] proposed integer quadratic
programming models to solve a districting design problem. The problem is to partition
units in a district, meeting criteria such as compactness, connectivity, and balance based
on customers and product demand. The models enable an exact solution framework us-
ing branch and bound and a cut generation strategy. Constraints are relaxed, and an
integer relaxation is solved using branch-and-bound. An easy separation problem is used
to find unconnected districts. Violated constraints are added, and the process repeats
until no more cuts are found. The algorithm ends when an optimal solution is reached.
The results indicate that the quadratic models can solve larger instances and converge
in fewer iterations than the linear models.
Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP) has become one of the
most popular approaches to solving districting problems in recent years [KR19]. [RF09]
were the first to use GRASP to solve a commercial districting problem with multiple
constraints. They developed a reactive GRASP that builds territories incrementally and
explores reinsertion and neighborhood swapping to improve the solution. The method
also features a reactivity function that adjusts the GRASP quality threshold parameter
for accepting solutions from a restricted candidate list. The algorithm’s effectiveness
was tested on data sets ranging from 500 to 2000 basic units, and it was discovered to
be very robust in a variety of scenarios, providing significantly better solutions than
existing methods.
[Ste+15] presented an optimization-based model for improving the healthcare system
in Parana State, Brazil by aggregating health services into micro regions. The model
aims to maximize population homogeneity in microregions, maximize the variety of
medical procedures offered, and minimize inter-microregion distances. An integer-coded
multi-objective genetic algorithm is used to optimize the model, resulting in significant

2.4
Clustering
Clustering, is the process of dividing a dataset into subsets, or clusters, where objects
within a cluster are similar to each other but dissimilar to objects in other clusters.
Clustering methods can produce different clusterings on the same dataset, depending on
parameters or initializations. This process, performed by clustering algorithms, helps
in uncovering hidden groups within data, contributing to valuable insights [HPT23].
Generally, the main clustering algorithms can be divided into the five following different
categories, partitioning-based, hierarchical-based, density-based, grid-based and model-
based. Each type of algorithm has distinct characteristics and is chosen based on the
specific requirements of the data and the clustering task at hand.
Partitioning-based algorithms create clusters by initially dividing data into partitions,
where each partition represents a cluster, with each object assigned to exactly one cluster.
Clusters must each contain at least one object. Some examples of partitioning algorithms
include k-means and k-medoids, among others. These algorithms quickly establish initial
clusters and refine them iteratively [Fah+14]. They are especially well suited for small
to medium-sized datasets with spherical-shaped clusters [HPT23].
In hierarchical-based algorithms, data is arranged in a hierarchical tree structure,
visualized by a dendrogram. Clustering can be either agglomerative (merging smaller
clusters into larger ones) or divisive (splitting one large cluster into smaller ones), and is
irreversible once a merge or split is done. Examples include BIRCH and CURE [Fah+14].
For density-based algorithms, a cluster is defined as a connected dense component
that can grow in any direction that the density leads to. Therefore, density-based algo-
rithms can identify clusters based on regions of high density, allowing for the discovery
of clusters with arbitrary shapes and providing natural outlier resistance. Examples in-
==PAGE:19==
2.4 Clustering
9
clude DBSCAN and OPTICS, which are particularly effective at identifying non-linear
cluster shapes and filtering out noise or outliers [Fah+14].
Grid-based methods divide the space into grids, calculating statistical values for each
grid. It is fast because it processes the entire dataset only once, focusing on grid-level
data instead of individual points, which can be beneficial for large datasets. Examples
include Wave-Cluster and STING [Fah+14].
In model-based algorithms, clusters are formed based on how well the data fits a
predefined mathematical model, often based on probability distributions. This category
includes statistical models like MCLUST and neural network-based approaches like self-
organizing feature maps, which learn by adjusting the weights of the network to best
match the data. These methods are effective for tasks requiring numerical input and
can handle outliers effectively [Fah+14].
2.4.1
DBSCAN
Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) is a data clus-
tering algorithm introduced by Martin Ester, Hans-Peter Kriegel, Jörg Sander, and
Xiaowei Xu in their 1996 paper [Est+96].
DBSCAN is a data clustering algorithm
that groups data points together based on their density in a given space, as the name
suggests, placing it within the category of density-based methods [Sch+17]. DBSCAN
calculates the distance between points in space using Euclidean distance measures. The
algorithm operates with two main parameters: ϵ, which sets the neighborhood radius
around each point, and MinPts, the minimum number of neighbors required within this
ϵ neighborhood [Ner+18]. DBSCAN offers several benefits compared to other clustering
algorithms, it doesn’t need a predetermined number of clusters, it treats outliers as noise
instead of assigning them to a cluster, and it’s capable of identifying clusters of arbitrary
shape, allowing for a more flexible and comprehensive clustering analysis. Additionally,
it remains eﬀicient even when applied to large spatial databases [Est+96]. However, a
drawback is that users face the diﬀicult challenge of selecting parameter values that lead

to the identification of meaningful clusters [HKP11].
2.4.2
K-Means
In [Mac67], James MacQueen introduced the term k-means to describe a method for
partitioning an n-dimensional population into k sets, a concept that has become a cor-
nerstone in the field of data clustering. K-means is one of the most well-known clustering
techniques and falls under the category of partitioning clustering methods. When using
k-means, objects are organized into different clusters. Each cluster is represented by a
central point, known as the centroid. This centroid, which can be defined in various ways
such as by the mean or medoid of the objects or data points within a cluster, serves as
the representative observation. Consequently, each object is placed in the cluster whose
centroid it is nearest or most similar to [HPT23]. In k-means clustering, the conven-
tional practice is to utilize the Euclidean distance metric. The utilization of Euclidean
distances results in the formation of clusters that exhibit a nearly spherical shape. There-
fore, k-means clustering is most effective for observations that tend to exhibit spherical
cluster shapes. The basic iterative implementation of the k-means clustering algorithm
offers several advantages, such as its straightforward implementation and guaranteed
conclusion [Sre22]. A few downsides of using k-means include the need to pre-determine
the number of clusters, making it tough to identify the optimal k, and diﬀiculties in
handling data with outliers [Gor11].

AMCS is a software company recognized as a market leader in Performance Sustain-

To address this problem effectively, the thesis explores a variation of the traditional

into clusters, with all vertices within a cluster being visited consecutively, and the final
solution forms a path rather than a cycle. The overall problem is deconstructed into
manageable subproblems or smaller routes that can be optimized individually.
This deconstruction and creation of smaller routes is based on clustering the dataset.

4.1
K-means
K-means is a clustering method that partitions n observations into k clusters and guar-
antees that the observations in each cluster are the closest to the centroid of that cluster.
The centroid is the mean of the observations belonging to the cluster. K-means cluster-
ing is considered optimal when it minimizes the differences within each cluster, making
the data points as similar as possible. It is also optimal when it maximizes the differences
between different clusters, ensuring that all clusters are distinct [Sre22].
The k-means algorithm involves the following steps: (1) Randomly choose k points to
serve as the initial centers of the clusters. (2) Assign each data point to the cluster whose
center it is nearest to, based on a certain measure of similarity or distance. (3) Compute
the centroid, or average, of all points within each cluster, which gives the algorithm its
mean aspect. (4) Form k clusters with a predefined number of k. (5) Repeat step 3,
continuing the process until the allocation of points to clusters does not change from

at which point additional iterations no longer produce better or different clustering
outcomes.

DBSCAN
The key concepts in DBSCAN are core points, border points, and noise points, which
are classified based on two parameters. The first is ϵ (epsilon), which is the maximum
radius of the neighborhood around a point. The second parameter is µ (MinPts), the
==PAGE:26==
4.2 DBSCAN
16
minimum number of points required to form a dense region. The following explanation
provides a structured overview of the DBSCAN algorithm [Ram+10]:
• The neighborhood of a point p, denoted as Nϵ(p), is defined as the set of points
within radius ϵ from p:
Nϵ(p) = {q ∈D|dist(p, q) ≤ϵ}
where D is the dataset and dist(p, q) is the distance between points p and q.
• A point p is classified as a core point if the number of points within its ϵ-neighborhood
is at least µ:
|Nϵ(p)| ≥µ
• A point p is directly density reachable from point q if p is within the ϵ-neighborhood
of q, and q is a core point.
• A point p is density-reachable from a point q if there is a chain of points p1, p2, ..., pn
such that p1 = q, pn = p, and each pi+1 is directly density-reachable from pi.
• Two points p and q are density-connected if there is a point o such that both p
and q are density-reachable from o with respect to ϵ and µ.
• A cluster X is a non-empty subset of the dataset such that for every pair of points
p and q in X, if p is density-reachable from q, then q is also density-connected to
p.
• Border point is a point that is not a core point but is in the neighborhood of a
core point. It belongs to the cluster of that core point but does not have enough
points in its neighborhood to be a core point itself.
• Noise point is a point that is not in the neighborhood of any core point. It does
not belong to any cluster.

Minimum spanning trees
Given an undirected graph G = (V, E), where V represents the vertices and E represents
the edges, a tree is defined as a connected graph with no cycles, and a spanning tree is a
specific type of subgraph within G that not only is a tree but also includes every vertex
of G, ensuring comprehensive coverage. The minimum spanning tree (MST) problem
aims to identify a tree within an undirected graph G = (V, E), where the goal is to
minimize the total cost. The cost is calculated as the sum of the values (weights, costs,
or lengths) assigned to each edge, denoted by w. For a spanning tree to incorporate
all n vertices of V , it requires exactly n −1 edges, striking a balance that connects all
vertices while maintaining the tree’s structure without any cycles [LC09].

5.3
Lower bounds and upper bounds
Upper bounds (UB) and lower bounds (LB) play a crucial role in optimization, and are
used to estimate the feasible range where z∗, the optimal solution value, can be found.
They are particularly important in problems where determining an exact solution is
computationally expensive or impractical.
The upper bound (UB) is the maximum
possible value that z∗might reach, while the lower bound (LB) is the minimum possible
value.
By tightening these bounds, raising the lower bound and lowering the upper
bound, the feasible region where z∗may exist narrows.
LB ≤z∗≤UB
(5.1)
In minimization problems, similar to the one addressed in this thesis, lower bounds
approximate the optimal solution from below, indicating the minimum value z∗that
could be achieved.
However, these lower bounds are not necessarily feasible for the

master problem, they provide an idealized baseline rather than practical solutions. In
contrast, upper bounds generate feasible solutions to the master problem and represent
the highest known solution value achievable within these constraints. In maximization
problems, the concept is reversed. The upper bound approximates z∗from above, rep-
resenting an idealized upper limit for the objective value. Lower bounds, in this case,
represent feasible solutions, approximating z∗from below. The interaction between the
lower and upper bounds helps reduce the solution space, leading to the optimal value.

5.3.2
Open Traveling Salesman Path Problem
An alternative method of finding a lower bound is to solve the open traveling salesman
problem for each cluster individually. Initially, similar to the MST approach, a distance
matrix for each cluster was created using the Euclidean distance. Next, a dummy node
with a distance of zero to all other nodes is added to the distance matrix, as described in
Equation 4.12. This technique of incorporating a dummy node allows for all vertices in
the TSPP to be potential starting and ending points. The Concorde solver is then used
to solve the TSP for each cluster, using the updated distance matrix as an input. The
output from the solver includes both the route and the associated cost, which in this case
is the distance. To manage the dummy node, it is essential to remove the first vertex
from all formed routes and decrement all indices by one. This adjustment generates
the final routes for each cluster. Since the distance from the dummy node to all other
nodes is zero, no adjustments are necessary. When the total distance from all clusters is
gathered, a lower bound is established. However, similar to the MST lower bound, this
does not represent the complete lower bound, as the routes within the clusters are not
connected.
When the routes are created like this, the end points can end up anywhere in the
cluster. There is no guarantee that the end points of the routes are in a convenient place
in regards to connection with other routes. Endpoints of a route can end up near the
center, in the clusters edge or somewhere there in between. Figure 5.2 shows an example
of an OTSP for cluster number 37 in the best solution found in the case study of the
CTSPP

To establish the lower bound for the complete problem, intercluster edges must be in-

to achieve the lowest possible cost, a minimization problem is formulated to minimize
the cost of intercluster connections. A distance matrix with minimum pairwise distance
between all vertices within all clusters is constructed. The essential step involves calcu-
lating the single linkage distance between any two clusters Ci and Cj, as described in
the following equation:

5.3.4
Intercluster edges for upper bound
To construct a complete route where a feasible solution is achieved, several strategies
may be employed. Given that each cluster is already optimized with a solution from the
OTSP, the primary task involves connecting the individual routes within each cluster to
form a unified route. Each cluster Ci contains an open ended route defined by the OTSP,
which guarantees an optimal internal path among its two endpoints. A straightforward
and effective method to begin this process is the implementation of a basic greedy
heuristic.
The greedy heuristic approach initiates at a specified terminal point. The first step
involves identifying the nearest cluster route in terms of Euclidean distance from this
terminal point and establishing a connection.
The distance between any two points
(xa, ya) and (xb, yb) is computed using the Euclidean formula:
d =
√
(xa −xb)2 + (ya −yb)2
(5.4)
Subsequently, the heuristic continues by locating the next closest route from the
current endpoint and connecting these via an edge. This procedure is repeated iteratively
until all clusters have been visited and interconnected. This strategy ensures all clusters
are seamlessly integrated into a single route and offers a practical and eﬀicient solution
to the problem.
5.3.5
Upper bounds LKH
The Clustered Traveling Salesman Path Problem (CTSPP) is effectively solved using
the Lin-Kernighan-Helsgaun (LKH) heuristics. To ensure the LKH algorithm progresses
through each cluster sequentially, the distance matrix is strategically updated by adding
==PAGE:43==
5.4 Clustering
33
a high penalty constant M to the distances between nodes i and j that are not in the
same cluster, as specified in Equation 4.9. This modification compels the algorithm to
complete all nodes within the current cluster before transitioning to the next. Once
all nodes in a cluster have been visited, the algorithm selects an edge with a significant
penalty to move to the next cluster. This process is repeated until all clusters are visited.
Ultimately, the added penalty costs are subtracted from the total distance to produce
the final routing distance.
Initially, the problem was modeled as a CTSPP with a fixed starting point and
an open endpoint, detailed in Equation 4.13. However, due to the asymmetry of the
distance matrix and the complex nature of the problem, this approach did not yield a
solution within a reasonable timeframe. Consequently, the problem was reformulated
as a Clustered Traveling Salesman Problem (CTSP), creating a cycle rather than a
path. This modification employed a symmetrical distance matrix, still incorporating
inter-cluster penalties as previously described.
To reduce computational demands, a critical parameter of the LKH solver, ‘RUNS‘,
was adjusted from its default value of 10 to just one. This change prioritizes obtain-
ing a initial heuristic solution rather than striving for iterative improvements, thereby
significantly decreasing computational time and ensuring consistent benchmarks across
tests.
The LKH algorithm is well-known for its effectiveness in handling large-scale TSPs.
It is particularly valued for its ability to deliver high-quality solutions within reasonable
computational times, making it a preferred heuristic in complex routing challenges like
the CTSPP.

Clustering
The methods outlined in previous chapters are based on the assumption that the data has
already been organized into clusters. There are numerous strategies for partitioning the
data, one of which involves employing a clustering algorithm. Clustering algorithms are
==PAGE:44==

methods used to group a set of objects in such a way that objects in the same cluster are
more similar to each other than to those in other clusters. The selection of a particular
method for splitting the data into distinct parts is inspired by the specific characteristics
and attributes of the data provided. For this project, the clusters correspond to segments
of a comprehensive route, with each segment being visited before proceeding to the next.
The strategy applied in this project involves utilizing both DBSCAN and k-means
clustering algorithms. Initially, the data was represented visually on a graph, enabling
the identification of key observations. It became apparent that certain areas were highly
dense, whereas others were notably sparse. The dense regions vary not only in size but
also in shape, as one would typically expect in a dataset of real-life addresses. For these
reasons, it was decided to use the DBSCAN algorithm on the data for the clustering.
DBSCAN is effective for segmenting the data into high-density core areas and identifying
outliers as well.
When using DBSCAN, it is critical to choose ϵ based on the density of the provided
data. Specialised methods can assist in determining the appropriate ϵ value. [Est+96]
presents a heuristic method for determining the parameters ϵ and MinPts based on
the density distribution of the dataset. This heuristic utilizes the sorted k-dist graph
to identify a threshold point that defines the ”thinnest”, i.e.
least dense, cluster in
the database, helping to set appropriate parameter values. The proposed interactive
approach suggests computing and displaying the 4-dist graph for the database, allowing
users to estimate the percentage of noise. From this estimation, the system derives a
proposal for the threshold point, which can be accepted or adjusted by the user. The
4-dist value of the threshold point is then used as the ϵ value for DBSCAN. It is men-
tioned that experiments indicate that k-dist graphs for k > 4 do not significantly differ
from the 4-dist graph and require more computation. Therefore, the parameter MinPts
is eliminated by setting it to 4 for all databases (for 2-dimensional data).

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
The purpose of this thesis was to explore whether it would be possible to create a well-
designed clustering that would improve the cost of transportation routing. In this thesis,
three research questions were addressed:
• What are the most effective clustering or districting techniques for this project,
and what are some potential limitations to using these strategies for transportation
routing?
• What are the key factors to consider when designing a clustering or districting
strategy for transportation routing?
• How can implementation of new clustering or districting methods improve the
results in [Nil23]?
• Is it possible to design an iterative approach that adjusts clusters based on the
obtained routing?
To answer the questions above, this study found that a well-designed clustering
strategy can lead to decreased transportation routing costs. This was observed when
the routing process used a fixed clustering from a previous project [Nil23] and compared
it with the clustering obtained in this project. Key factors in designing a clustering
strategy for transportation routing include creating clusters based on the given data. Is
the data dense in specific regions and sparse in others? Is it of a particular shape? Once
these factors are considered, an appropriate algorithm can be selected. It is also crucial
that the clusters are of acceptable sizes to balance computational time.
==PAGE:70==
7 Discussion
60
Visualizing the given data was an essential part of how the clustering algorithm
for this study was chosen. The island of Bornholm has dense regions in some places
of arbitrary shapes but also has sparser regions in other areas. These considerations,
combined with an exploration of the literature, suggested that DBSCAN might be a
suitable clustering method for our data. Of course, other clustering methods could also
be appropriate, but the DBSCAN seemed the most relevant.
To obtain the best clustering possible when an algorithm or method has been chosen,
it can be very important to estimate the parameters used as input and adapt them based
on the given data. When using a clustering algorithm like DBSCAN, many different
outcomes can be obtained with different pairings of parameters ϵ and MinPts. This
study analyzed significant variations in cluster size, the number of outliers, and the
overall balance of the clusters with different parameter combinations. By examining
this closely, we can get an idea of which parameters produce acceptable clusterings and
which do not. By filtering out some extreme cases, the goal is to achieve the best possible
clustering for the data.
31 different parameter combinations were considered because it was not clear that
only one combination would be the best.
By testing these 31 combinations, it was
expected that some would produce outstanding routing with low costs due to good
parameter tuning. The 31 different parameter combinations produced clusters where
the largest cluster contained between 6,200 and 6,300 data points. For clustering alone,
this size is not typically a concern, but in our case of solving for the best routes, these
amounts of data points can considerably delay the project.
More than 12 hours of
computational time to solve the OTSP for one cluster was not considered acceptable for
this project, so further clustering was necessary.
The step of re-clustering large clusters was purely based on the high computational
time, which is why k-means, a straightforward, well-known, and accessible clustering
algorithm, was used.
This simple method significantly improved the computational
times. To put that into context with the 12-hour computational time mentioned earlier,
solving the OTSP for all clusters in each clustering now took at most 45 minutes, with
==PAGE:71==
7 Discussion
61
the fastest being solved in 14 minutes. At this point in the study, clusters of acceptable
sizes that did not use up too much computational time were established. Then further
work and analysis of routing could be performed effectively with no obstacles. The re-
clustering approach could be elaborated in several other ways, and might be something
to consider in future research.
A feasible range for finding an optimal solution for the CTSPP was identified for all
31 clusterings. As explained in the thesis, this was achieved by calculating the lower
and upper bounds. The approach involved trying different lower bounds to see how they
performed and comparing the results with various upper bounds. It can be seen from
all 31 observations that when calculating the lower bounds for the CTSPP, the MST
solution can be computed quite fast compared to the OTSP. However, the OTSP provides
a much tighter bound. This is because the routes of each cluster in the solved OTSP
are feasible solutions that can create a comprehensive route if appropriate inter cluster
edges are added, unlike the MST, which is not feasible for the final solution. When
the upper bounds were calculated, the LKH heuristic for the total CTSPP provided the
tightest upper bound solution and was also faster than using the OTSP combined with
a greedy heuristic for inter cluster edges.
The best solution among the 31 observations had the lowest total routing cost and
the tightest optimality gap of 2.6%, which suggests that the solution obtained using the
LKH heuristic is likely to be near-optimal, given its relatively small size. If this small
gap is acceptable, then the LKH is an effective way to compute the total route after the
right clustering has been found.
Since one purpose of this project was to see if implementing new clustering methods
could improve the results in [Nil23], all bounds were calculated using the fixed clustering
from that project. All aspects were compared to our best observation. The fixed clus-
tering performed worse than the best solution, both in terms of the total route distance
and total computation time. Additionally, the gap where the feasible solution is found
reaches 6% for the previous project, so the that solution cannot be determined with as
much certainty as it can for the best solution.
==PAGE:72==
7 Discussion
62
When comparing the clusterings of the two projects, the size and number of clusters
are notable factors.
The previous solution has 26 more clusters than the clustering
obtained in this project and also includes more large clusters. One of the most significant
factor is how the algorithms cluster the data. With close examination of how the clusters
differ, it can be seen that k-means sometimes forms a cluster with data points from both
dense and sparse areas, whereas DBSCAN only includes the dense areas and excludes
the sparse areas as outliers.
It can be seen that compared to all 31 observations, the previous clustering is always
the slowest or second slowest to solve all types of bounds. This indicates that the new
clustering is better overall when considering computational times.
When examining some of the routing results seen in Appendix D, a solution can
be found that has cluster features similar to our best solution but still has much worse
results in the upper bound solution. It has 83 clusters compared to 84 in the best solution.
The smallest cluster is slightly larger, with 48 data points compared to 45. The average
cluster size is very similar, 281 compared to 278. Additionally, the largest cluster is
smaller than that of the best solution, 1,019 compared to 1,275. When examining the
distribution of the cluster sizes, there is not much difference. There is no clear and
obvious reason for this at the current stage, and further detailed exploration would be
needed to find the answer.
As mentioned earlier, there is potential to improve routing with a well-designed
clustering method. However, the project faces certain limitations, including the time
required to obtain results and the limited number of clustering methods tested. Future
research could expand on this by exploring additional clustering methods and their
various implementations.
Additionally, this thesis did not explore the design of an
iterative approach that adjusts clusters based on the obtained routing, which could
potentially offer deeper insights into the Clustered Traveling Salesman Path Problem
(CTSPP).
