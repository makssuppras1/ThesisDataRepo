Abstract 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the effect of internal curing on the frost 
resistance of concrete. 
This is done through two separate investigations. One focuses on the investigation of ice 
formation in air voids while the other investigates the effect of using SAP to create air 
voids and provide internal curing in concrete. 
In the investigation of ice formation in air voids, test specimens were observed during 
cooling. This was done in attempt to document the initial ice formation within air voids 
created by using either SAP or AEA as an additive. Initially through a microscope setup 
which was adapted into a camera setup focusing only on investigating how ice in air voids 
created by addition of AEA is formed. 
When the images from the camera setup were observed, it was discovered that the liquid 
water in the test specimen was drawn towards the cooled surface, where the solidification 
was initiated. The solidification process dried out the opposite surface and the observed ice 
propagated. The propagation of the ice started at the edge and grew into the artificial air 
void created by the specimen surface and the object glass. The observed mechanisms was 
very similar to the one described by microscopic ice lens growth theory. 
In the investigation focusing on the effect of using SAP as an additive to create air voids 
and provide internal curing in concrete, different test specimens were made to make the 
effects of the additives apparent.  
In the test specimens two different dosages of SAP was added, with four different size 
fractions, a test specimen series containing only AEA as well as a mixed series with AEA 
and three of the size fractions of SAP also used in the SAP test specimens. Finally, a 
Reference specimen series was made with no additives. 
The test specimens were used in a standardized freeze-thaw experiment according to the 
DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 Testing Hardened Concrete – Part 9: Freeze-thaw resistance. The 
standardized test exposes the test specimens to 56 freeze thaw cycles with temperatures 
varying from -20 0C to 20 0C within 24 hours. During the 56 days of exposure the scaled 
material as an effect of frost damage is collected and weighed. This test indicates the frost 
performance as an effect of the different additives. 
In order to more effectively analyze and evaluate the observed differences a supplementary 
Air void analysis was performed by the Danish Technological Institute. The Air void 
analysis was performed according to DS/EN 480-11:1998.  
 
==PAGE:4==

The air void analysis revealed that the expected absorption of the SAP was significantly 
higher than expected. This altered the w/c ratios significantly, the difference in w/c ratios 
made it impossible to make direct comparisons and observe internal curing effects.  
When the effects of internal curing and the effect of the altered w/c ratio are considered it 
is noted that because the high dosage SAP specimens follow a different displaced 
exponential line compared to the low dosage SAP specimens it indicates that the sum of 
effects caused by internal curing and altered w/c ratio lower the amount of scaling. Due to 
the fact that the w/c ratio is slightly altered between these two test specimen series it is not 
possible determine the exact effect of internal curing. However, as there is only a slight 
difference in the w/c ratio between the two sample series the effect of lowering the w/c is 
not expected to be major. Therefore, it is possible to acknowledge that the positives of 
introducing internal curing most likely outweighs the negatives with regards to salt frost 
scaling.  
The freezing cabinet did not follow the predefined temperature cycles in the standard. It 
reached -12 0C when it should have reached -20 0C. This means that comparing the results 
directly to results from other projects is not possible. But comparisons between the 
specimens in this project is still possible because they have all been similarly exposed to 
the temperature cycles. 
The measured air content in hardened concrete for both SAP dosage series are within 1% 
of each other and the mixed series containing both SAP and AEA are within 1.5% this 
could be accounted for just by variation in the natural air content in the concrete. But it is 
noticeable that both samples with SAP have less total variation than the mixed series with 
SAP and AEA added, even when the pure SAP samples have 4 specimens and the mixed 
series only has 3 specimens. This indicates that it is easier to control the air content 
through the addition of SAP compared to AEA, which is part of the incentive for using 
SAP to create the air voids.

1 Introduction 
 
In the modern field of construction, concrete is one of the most popular materials. 
Furthermore, the cost of renovation of existing concrete structures is ever increasing. This 
is due to two factors: One is the increasingly rate of structures built, leading to an ever 
increasing number of concrete structures in need of renovation in the future. The second 
factor is the age of the existing concrete structures. Many structures were built having less 
or insufficient knowledge on concrete deterioration mechanisms, as well as poorly 
designed or poorly executed concrete mixes.  
 
Frost action is one of these concrete deterioration mechanisms and it is investigated in this 
project. Researchers do not agree on the mechanisms of frost action. It is however widely 
accepted that frost damage consists of two types of damage to the concrete: internal frost 
damage and salt frost scaling damage. The latter is investigated throughout this project. 
 
In the 1940’ Powers showed that there exists a correlation between air content in the 
concrete and resistance towards frost damage (Powers 1945). This led to the definition of 
the spacing factor, which is defined as the maximum distance to the surface of an air void 
in concrete (Powers 1949). Today the air content in concrete as well as the spacing factor 
is widely accepted as criterions to document if a concrete is frost resistant.  
 
In modern construction, the most popular way to ensure an adequate factory air content as 
well as spacing factor in concrete, is to add a chemical air entrainment agent (AEA) to the 
concrete mix. However, the addition of AEA has several disadvantages: 
 
 The air content is not clearly correlated with the dosage of AEA rendering it 
difficult to aim for precise amounts of air contents. 
 The air void system in fresh concrete is unstable and could lead to a loss of air 
content before hardening. 
 
Because of these disadvantages, it often leads to more air being entrained during 
production in order to ensure the fulfilment of the criteria for air content in both fresh and 
hardened concrete. This is a problem as the compressive strength of concrete decreases 
when the air content increases, and the properties with regards to durability are altered as 
well. 
 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the effect of using super absorbent polymers 
(SAP) as an additive to create of air voids in concrete on salt frost scaling deterioration. 
This is done by adding dry SAP to the concrete mix. These particles then absorb water and 
swell. During the concrete hardening the SAP releases water and shrinks, hence rendering 
the water available for hydrating the concrete internally. Due to this, air voids are created 
of a specific size based on the particle size of the dry SAP. This means that it is possible to 
==PAGE:8==
DTU Civil Engineering 
 
 
8 
design a mix for a specific air content based on the dosage of SAP as well as the particle 
size of the SAP knowing the absorption. 
 
Hence using SAP as a replacement to the chemical AEA can lead to a more optimised air 
content which results in a minimised reduction of the compressive strength of the concrete, 
and more control over the properties related to the durability of the concrete. Furthermore, 
the use of SAP for creation of air voids in the concrete also changes the curing conditions 
as a by-product. 
 
When SAP is added to the concrete mixture, to create air voids, additional water is added 
to the mixture and absorbed by the SAP. This water is available to the concrete later in the 
hydration process, in this way the SAP should acts as a way of providing internal curing. 
When curing conditions are altered, it changes the microstructure of the concrete, and the 
microstructure is very important to the frost resistance of the concrete. Therefore curing 
effects could be visible in the results, not directly by observing microstructures, but by the 
results from air void analysis paired with the scaling results. 
 
Previous experimental studies regarding the use of SAP for prevention of frost damage 
indicates  that air voids created using SAP are working as regular air voids in correlation to 
durability (Laustsen et al. 2013).  
 
In this project the air voids created using SAP are investigated. This is done by 
investigating ice formation in air voids created using both AEA and SAP as an additive. 
The investigation of ice formation is based on a previous project (Søbye 2016). 
Experiences in the previous project has been utilised in order to create as test setup where 
it is possible to monitor the formation of ice using a customised optical microscope. 
 
Furthermore, the effect of using SAP as an additive for creating air voids on the frost 
resistance of the concrete is investigated. This is done by conducting a freeze-thaw cycle 
experiment according to DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 Testing Hardened Concrete – Part 9: 
Freeze-thaw resistance standard  using different sizes of SAP as well as different dosages 
(DS/CEN/TS_12390-9 2006). The purpose of the freeze-thaw experiment is to investigate 
any correlation between internal curing and frost durability of the concrete using the SAP 
particles to ensure internal curing.

2 Theory 
In the following section the different theories related to salt frost scaling will be presented. 
The sections will focus on subjects and theories relatable to the focus of this project, with a 
special interest in the effect and role of SAP. 
2.1 Salt Frost Scaling 
There are several forms of damage and degradation that happen to concrete. Salt frost 
scaling is one of them. Salt frost scaling as a mechanism is a superficial attack, and 
therefore not that critical by itself, but when it occurs, several other types of damage and 
degradation are accelerated, and can become very critical. For example it reduces the cover 
layer of the concrete so ingressing chlorides or a carbonation front reaches reach the 
reinforcement faster. 
Salt frost scaling can be defined as damage to the surface caused by freezing of a saline 
solution on the surface of the concrete. During temperature variations, the salt frost scaling 
causes progressive damage to the surface by removing and disintegrating the cement paste 
in the concrete.  
The damage on the surface of concrete surfaces caused by salt frost scaling can be divided 
into two main parts, damage caused by internal crystallization and damage related to the 
presence of the saline solution on the concrete. Both internal crystallization and the effect 
of the presence of salt will be described in the following section.

2.1.1 Internal Crystallization 
The theory of internal crystallization describes the effect of having crystals forming inside 
a concrete material. In most, if not all, cases this crystallization will be the forming of ice. 
When ice forms inside concrete, or internal crystallization happens the damage caused can 
be divided into three different mechanisms, hydraulic pressure, microscopic ice lens 
growth and osmotic pressure. 
Hydraulic Pressure 
Hydraulic pressure is formed because ice has a 9% larger volume than the volume of liquid 
water from which it was formed, when freezing of water occurs in a porous material, like 
concrete, this creates an increase of pressure within the concrete pore fluid around the 
forming ice. It was suggested by Powers (Powers 1949), that this increase in pressure 
within the pore fluid could be mitigated by lowering the average distance between the air 
voids within the concrete, and thereby also increasing the amount of air voids in the 
concrete. This is illustrated in Figure 2-1 below. 
 
Figure 2-1 - (Mehta & Monteiro 2006): Hydraulic pressure caused by formation of ice in the capillaries 
However, it was discovered through research of the volume change as a function of 
temperature of samples with varying amounts and configurations of air voids that the 
hydraulic pressure does not adequately account for all of the observed volume change 
during cooling. Therefore a new theory was formed.

Microscopic Ice Lens Growth 
This theory is the effect of microscopic ice lens growth, which is pressure when crystals 
are formed within a porous material. The fact that there is a crystallization pressure was 
discovered when it was noticed that a porous body was damaged when a liquid that does 
not expand during solidification was transformed into solid inside the porous body as 
described in (Valenza & Scherer II 2007). 
During the forming of ice crystals within a porous body, pressure is formed. The pressure 
is formed because of thermodynamics. 
When ice crystals form, the pore fluid around the forming ice is affected by the 
solidification of water. The pore fluid exerts pressure on the concrete in one of two ways. 
The water solidifies at the edge of an air void drawing the liquid pore fluid from the 
adjacent capillary system towards the forming ice crystal. An illustration of the mechanism 
can be seen in Figure 2-2. 
 
Figure 2-2 - (Mehta & Monteiro 2006): Microscopic ice lens growth in an air void 
The amount of pressure microscopic ice lens growth creates, depends on the micro 
structure of the concrete. This means that the accumulation and relief of pressure during 
microscopic ice lens growth is very dependent on the microscopic pore structure. An 
illustration of the dependency of the size and shape of the capillary system can be seen in 
Figure 2-3 below which illustrates the pressure mechanism during forming of an ice 
crystal.

Osmotic Pressure 
The mechanisms in Osmotic Pressure are very similar to the mechanisms for microscopic 
ice lens growth. However, the movement of water is due to local salt concentration 
gradient in the connected capillary system. This is illustrated in Figure 2-4 below. 
 
Figure 2-4 - Concept drawing explaining osmotic pressure 
This means that the osmotic pressure is pressure formed when the water that is connected 
through the capillary system moves towards the higher concentration (Mehta & Monteiro 
2006). When ice initially is formed in the capillaries and because salt lowers the freezing 
point of water, the concretion of solutes is higher near the ice crystals, creating a locally 
higher concentration of salt. This creates movement of liquid water towards the area 
around the forming ice.

2.1.2 Glue Spalling Theory 
A new mechanism, glue spalling, was formulated as the primary cause of salt frost scaling 
(Valenza & Scherer II 2007). It was suggested to attempt to account for all the 
characteristics of the salt frost scaling mechanism. Glue spalling is currently used to 
decorate glass surfaces with a uniformly shallow surface. The technique utilizes the fact 
that different materials have different thermal expansion coefficients. The technique 
consists of sandblasting a glass surface and spreading a layer of epoxy on the blasted glass 
surface and then allowing it to harden and bind to the glass surface. The temperature of the 
composite material is then lowered. When the temperature is lowered, the epoxy contracts 
significantly more than the glass, therefore the epoxy exposes the glass surface to tensional 
stresses. This tension on the glass surface makes the glass fracture at regular intervals, and 
consequently break “islands” of the glass off, creating the wanted decorative scalloped 
surface. The described technique is illustrated in Figure 2-5 below. As concrete is also very 
susceptible to tension this can translate into the situation of a saline solution freezing on 
the surface of concrete. 
 
Figure 2-5: (Valenza & Scherer II 2007): Glue Spalling mechanism 
This glue spall mechanism used for making decorative surfaces on glass is very similar to 
the salt frost scaling mechanism according to (Valenza & Scherer II 2007).  The ice acts as 
the epoxy binder and the concrete is the glass. The proposed glue spalling mechanism 
supposedly accounts for all characteristics of salt frost scaling.

2.3 Super Absorbent Polymers 
SAP is a term used to define a group of materials capable of absorbing a large volume of a 
designated specific substance, which in this case is water. The ability of absorbing large 
volumes can be exploited for creation of air voids, when SAP is mixed in concrete. As 
SAP is mixed in the concrete, it absorbs the pore fluid and swells. During the curing 
process of concrete, the absorbed pore fluid in the SAP is released and the SAP shrinks to 
approximately original dry size. This is illustrated in Figure 2-7 below. 
 
 
Figure 2-7 – Concept of SAP used in concrete for creation of air voids 
SAP is produced by one of two different processes, either a bulk solution polymerization 
process or a suspension polymerization process. For this project, SAP produced using the 
suspension polymerization process is used. During production of the SAP using the 
suspension polymerization process a surfactant is used (Hasholt et al. 2015).

2.4 Curing 
When concrete hydrates water becomes physically and chemically bound. For w/c ratios 
0.42 or higher there is theoretically enough water for full hydration, assuming a closed 
system. When concrete is cast, there is often evaporation of water from the concrete. It has 
become commonly recognized that it is necessary to supply additional water to avoid 
surface problems, as this area is the most exposed to air, and thereby evaporation of the 
water from within the concrete. 
As concrete hydrates, more solid material is formed. The transport properties of concrete 
rely on the capillary system as well as microstructure. Therefore, a higher degree of 
hydration leads to slower transport properties. This can be both positive or negative 
depending on the situation, but faster transport will make the concrete more vulnerable to 
external attack mechanisms such as carbonation, chloride ingress and several others. 
Therefore, it is important to apply appropriate curing conditions to fresh concrete. 
There are several different solutions for curing of concrete currently in use. Some of the 
most popular solutions are sealing the surface to avoid evaporation, saturating the surface 
or even submerging the concrete in water.  It is also possible to supply the concrete with an 
internally available source of water. This is referred to as internal curing. Internal curing 
can be done in several different ways, but in general it is done by allowing a material that 
is part of the concrete mix to absorb extra water, which will then be available to the 
hydration process at a later stage. Usually this is done by using an absorbent type of 
aggregate. 
In (Bentz & Stutzman 2006) and (Lura et al. 2007)  the effect of different curing conditions 
is investigated. The concrete behavior is analyzed for sealed, sealed/saturated, saturated 
and finally internally cured concrete.  
The investigations analyze the effects of the curing conditions on the properties of the 
concrete. They mainly focus on the microstructure, hydration and pore structure. They do 
this through the analysis of experimental work. 
Internal curing and the effects of it was evaluated by observing the following: 
- 
Internal relative humidity 
- 
Degree of saturation 
- 
Sorption isotherm 
After analysis and observation of the characteristics above it was concluded that the 
primary consequences of internal curing are: 
Availability of additional water and high water activity increases the hydration in internally 
cured concrete samples with a low w/c ratio. Autogenous strain is lowered because of the 
==PAGE:17==
DTU Civil Engineering 
 
 
17 
additional water added that is available in the capillary pores once the water for the 
hydration process has been used. This means that there is less tensile stress developed and 
self-desiccation is also reduced. Consequently, there is less risk of early-age cracking and 
damage in the pore structure when using internal curing. 
And the secondary consequences are: 
The mechanical properties are improved, especially at later ages. And internal 
microcracking is avoided in concretes with a low w/c ratio. However, because the 
absorbent aggregate most probably has lower strength than the hydrated concrete the more 
of the absorbent aggregate is added, the less beneficial and more detrimental to the 
mechanical properties it will be. So, if it is possible to add very little absorbent aggregates 
with a large effect, this would be the best way. Self-induced stress and cracking will be 
reduced because the autogenous shrinkage is lowered, this also minimizes the risk of 
internal microcracking. Potentially there is better durability as an effect of the internal 
curing, at least short term, because of the denser and less permeable surface and concrete. 
In (Bentz & Stutzman 2006) it is mentioned that the curing conditions affects the transport 
properties to a very high degree. It is mentioned that the microstructure is very dependent 
on the curing conditions, mainly because the curing conditions determines the availability 
of water for the hydration process. This means that the amount of water available to each 
cement grain determines the degree of hydration the individual cement grain can obtain 
and there by the density of the concrete structure in that local area. So, the cement grains 
can form a very closed microstructure even if they do not obtain full hydration, this 
scenario is shown in Figure 2-8 below. 
 
Figure 2-8 – Locally densified areas of cementitious material due to parts of the concrete having no access to water 
A closed microstructure as shown above, where the main part of the hydration process 
happens so that it forms a closed microstructure would also influence both the ingression 
and transport properties of the formed concrete, and possibly an effect on the frost 
resistance of the concrete, as this is related to both the ingression and the transport 
properties.

The purpose of the experiment is to observe the formation of ice in the open air voids of

The previously developed custom built microscope developed is used for the setup of the 
experiment. The custom microscope consists of an objective with 50x magnification along 
with a body tube with a camera attached to it. According to (Søbye 2016) 1 pixel equals 
124 nm. The focus is controlled using a mechanical adjuster which is adjusted using an 
electric LEGO Mindstorms engine. This is illustrated in Figure 3-7 below.

investigated in (Søbye 2016). The focus was adjusted by rotating the mechanical knob by 3 
degrees per step. This resulted in a movement of the microscope of 1.309 µm. Capturing 
image series of the test specimens was done manually by capturing one image and then 
adjusting the focus of the microscope by using the LEGO engine to rotate 3 degrees and 
then capturing a new image. This procedure was repeated until a satisfied series of pictures 
were obtained.

3.3.2 Freezing Setup  
The freezing device for this experiment was custom built. In (Søbye 2016), a Peltier 
element was used to freeze the test specimen. The result of this was that the test specimen 
was cooled from the opposite side of the microscope, hence leading to that ice formation 
also initiated at the opposite side of the test specimen. This lead to difficulties in 
monitoring the very initial ice formation in the air voids of the concrete, which was the 
focal point of the investigation. 
The purpose of the new custom built freezing device was to ensure cooling from the same 
side as the microscope. This would make it possible to monitor the initial ice formation. To 
ensure this, a Thermojet precision temperature cycling system capable of producing air 
flows with a temperature of down to -85 oC. The concept of the air freezing device was to 
produce an air flow across the test specimen while monitoring it with the microscope. The 
temperature of the air flow was then controlled using the Thermojet. The test specimen 
was insulated with Armaflex on the backside as well as Armaflex was used as insulation to 
stabilize and control the air temperature in front of the test specimen. Temperature gauges 
were placed on both sides of the test specimen to monitor the temperature development of 
the concrete and screws were fitted in insulation beneath the test specimen for support. 
This was done in order to prevent movement of the test specimen during freezing due to 
thermal expansion or shrinkage of the Armaflex. A concept drawing of the freezing device 
is shown below in Figure 3-9. The full test setup is shown in Figure 3-10.

3.4 Image Processing 
After the images from the test have been captured the image series is merged together by a 
MATLAB script. Because the focus of the optical microscope is so sensitive to movement, 
it only focuses on a small part of each image at a time, so by moving the focus through the 
test specimen all parts of the surface should in theory be in focus at some time during the 
process. The MATLAB script processes the image and identifies which pixels are in focus, 
and the areas from the individual image that is in focus will be utilized to construct an 
image of the entire surface of the specimen where all the areas are in focus at the same 
time. The MATLAB script also creates several topology plots from which the depth 
variations of the surface can be estimated.

3.6 Discussion 
During the investigation of ice formation in an air void it was discovered that the test setup 
and the performance of it was very important in order to be able to clarify what happens 
when ice forms. This led to the development of the test setup, it changed from a 
microscope setup to a camera setup to avoid the limitations of the custom built microscope. 
The change made it possible to observe the formation of ice.  
In the final setup, ice formation was observed on the specimen surface underneath the 
object glass. In reality, the ice formation did not happen within a natural air void in the 
concrete, but in the large artificial air void created by the space between the object glass 
and the test specimen. During cooling the water seemed to be drawn towards the colder 
surface due to the temperature gradient, where it was frozen and the amount of solid water 
accumulated. This theory was confirmed by also monitoring ice formation on the surface, 
while cooling from the opposite side of the test specimen. During this test, no ice 
formation was noted on the monitored surface. 
The observed behavior during cooling and solidification of the water on the specimen 
surface could be related to the concept of microscopic ice lens growth in the large artificial 
==PAGE:40==
DTU Civil Engineering 
 
 
40 
air void drawing water from the specimen through the connected capillary system to the 
coldest side of the specimen. The mechanism can is illustrated in Figure 3-30. 
The figure shows the process of water movement through the specimen which was 
observed during the cooling, and the numbering below shows the time frame within the 
cooling process, 1 being the very start and 5 being almost at the end. 
To confirm that the water had moved towards the cooled surface, the test specimen from 
test A in the camera setup was removed from the test setup immediately after cooling was 
stopped, sealed and the cooled side was observed to confirm that it was wet. This was 
confirmed. This is presented in Figure 3-31. 
 
Figure 3-31 - Picture showing the water on the cooled surface from test A, both sealed and unsealed 
When the entire observed behavior is considered it would be expected that within an actual 
air void the ice would form at the edge of the void and draw water towards it from the 
connected capillary system and the ice would grow into the void space. This behavior is 
reminiscent of the mechanisms related to microscopic ice lens growth.

3: Liquid water enters the edge of the artificial air void 4:Water begins to solidify  
5: Solidification continues and liquid water is drawn towards the forming ice

3.7 Future research 
For future research, it would be beneficial to exclude using an object glass, and thereby 
making an artificial air void but rather observe an actual air void. Future research should 
also consider that the cooling direction has a significance, and it is necessary to observe the 
cooled surface to ensure observing the initial ice formation. A microscope with the ability 
to observe a larger area and less sensitivity towards movement would also be preferable.

4.1 Test Method 
The frost scaling test is conducted according to the DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 Testing Hardened 
Concrete – Part 9: Freeze-thaw resistance standard (DS/CEN/TS_12390-9 2006).  
The only deviations from the standard are following: 
- 
Curing conditions. Samples will be sealed after demolding in order to benefit from 
internal curing. If the standard was followed internal curing would have none to 
little effect. 
- 
Cylindrical test specimens instead of square. Which is allowed according to 
DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 as long as the thickness is the same, as well as DS 2426 
which also allows for three test specimens instead of four. 
- 
Scaled material will be removed from the samples by rinsing with demineralized 
water into a filter instead of brushing.  
The Swedish Standard SS 13 72 44:2005 (SS 13 72 44 2005) is very similar to the 
Eurocode standard, but it contains an evaluation tool to estimate the degree of the frost 
scaling damage, and whether it is acceptable, this tool will be used in the evaluation 
process.

4.4 Preparation of SAP 
In the following section, the steps taken to prepare the SAP material for use in the test 
specimens will be described. The SAP material was received as a bulk material, therefore it 
was necessary to separate the material into different size fractions, and based on previous 
experiences with the SAP material (Laustsen 2011) it was necessary to wash off any 
remaining surfactants from the manufacturing process of the SAP material. The 
preparation was performed by a sieve analysis and washing the SAP with ethanol, both 
processes will be described in the next sections.

4.4.2 Washing of SAP fractions 
The SAP particles needed to be washed to remove residual surfactants on the particles left 
behind from the manufacturing process. The fact that surfactants remain on the SAP 
particles was previously discovered in (Laustsen 2011). The surfactants needed to be 
removed to prevent the SAP material from developing foam when they come into contact 
with the water in the concrete mix. The method used for washing the SAP particles is also 
found in a previous project in the present project the washing is considered as a material 
preparation rather than a separate investigation. The washing of the SAP particles was 
performed according to best practice and the previously described procedure from 
(Laustsen 2011). 
 
In the previous report, it was determined that the mass of surfactant in 50 ml of fluid used 
for washing could be reduced from 2.82 g/50 ml to 0.01 g/ 50 ml by washing the SAP 
particles in 99.9 % ethanol 5 times. In the current project the SAP particles were only 
washed 4 times resulting in a theoretical reduction from 2.82 g/50 ml to 0.04 g/ 50 ml 
stated in (Laustsen 2011) figure E.3 which was evaluated as being sufficiently clean for 
use in the concrete casting. 
 
The washing of each individual particle size fraction of the SAP particles was performed in 
the following manner, all the steps below were performed inside a ventilated cabinet in 
order to prevent evaporation of the 99.9 % Ethanol into the laboratory: 
 
- 
The particle size distribution of SAP was put into a 2000 ml beaker 
- 
2 ml of 99.9 % Ethanol per gram of SAP was poured into the beaker 
- 
The mixture was stirred until it was homogeneous 
- 
The beaker was left in order to allow the SAP particles to settle to the bottom of the 
beaker, separating the 99.9 % Ethanol and the SAP particles. 
- 
Parafilm M ® was put on top of the beaker to avoid evaporation of the 99.9 % Ethanol 
while the SAP particles were settling. 
- 
When settled, the 99.9 % Ethanol containing the surfactants was decanted from the 
beaker, attempting to waste as little SAP particles as possible. 
 
The process described above was repeated 4 times for each size fraction of SAP particles. 
 
After the last wash, as much of the 99.9 % Ethanol as possible was decanted from the 
beakers, and the remaining ethanol was allowed to evaporate in the ventilated cabinet for 
approximately 24 hours. When this was done, the samples were put into a ventilated oven 
at 50 0C to expose the SAP particles to as little humidity as possible. In the oven, the last 
Ethanol was evaporated allowing the particles to return to their original state.

4.5 Casting Procedure 
The cylinders for the test have been cast with a diameter of 150 mm and a height of 300 
mm. The Zyklos ZZ 75 HE/0 mixer was used for all castings, and can be seen in Figure 4-5  
 
Figure 4-5 – Zyklos ZZ 75 HE/0 concrete mixer 
In the experiment, some of the test specimens were cast from concrete with Super 
Absorbent Polymers added to the concrete mix. In the project two different dosages of 
SAP material was used, either 1 ‰ or 3 ‰ of the cement weight in the concrete mix. Extra 
water was added to the mixture to let the SAP particles absorb it during the mixing 
process, based on previous experiments 12.5 g of water was added for each gram of SAP 
(Laustsen et al. 2013). 
The SAP material used was divided into four different size fractions, the fractions are 75-
90 µm, 90-125 µm, 125-150 µm and 160-180 µm. 
Other test specimens were cast with an Air Entrainment Agent in the mixture, the product 
used in this project is MapeAir 50 ®, it was added according to the recommended dosage 
stated by the manufacturer. The dosage used was 0.5 ‰ of the cement weight for samples 
with no SAP, and 0.25 ‰ for samples with SAP. 
Finally, reference samples were cast as well. They only contain the concrete used for all 
the other samples, containing no added SAP or AEA. 
In order to ensure that the concrete mixture was uniform all the dry materials was dry 
mixed for 1 minute before water was added, then the water was added and the concrete 
was mixed for 3 minutes, after this the mixer was opened and it was manually ensured that 
==PAGE:55==

55 
the mixing blades would have contact with all of the concrete, by scraping all the concrete 
from the edges of the mixer into the middle and it was mixed for 1 additional minute. 
The dry mixing was necessary because of the water absorbent SAP materials. It was 
necessary to have the SAP particles distributed uniformly before water was added.

4.7.1 Fresh concrete air content and slump 
During the casting process the slump and fresh air content was measured according to EN

4.7.2 Thicknesses of test specimens 
Prior to gluing, the thickness of each test specimen was measured. According to 
DS/CEN/TS 12930-9 each test specimen was required to have a thickness of 50 mm ± 
2mm using a caliper. The measurements were made and the thicknesses varied between 
50.2-53.3 mm which was marginally outside the standard. Thicknesses for all samples can 
be seen in Appendix F. 
It is noted that a series of the test specimens does not meet the requirements for the 
thicknesses according to DS/CEN/TS 12930-9. These test specimens are: Ref/2, A/75-
90/1, A/1/160-180/2, 1/75-90/1, 1/75-90/2, 1/125-150/2, 1/160-180/4 and 3/90-125/2 with 
the largest deviation surpassing the requirements being 1.3 mm for test specimens 1/160-
180/4 and 1/75-90/2.  
These deviations were caused by the precision of the tool available for cutting the test 
specimens. All these test specimens were accepted for testing due to several practical 
reasons: 
 It was not possible to adjust the thickness after cut without risking unnecessary 
damage to the test specimens. 
 Time frame for the project did not necessarily allow for repeating the 28 days 
process prior to gluing for several different concrete mixes. 
 A repetition of the procedure including cutting the test specimens would not 
guarantee a more precise result due to the precision of the cutting tool available. 
A difference in the thicknesses of the different test specimen can have an influence on the 
results as a larger or thinner thickness resulting in a larger or smaller mass, which then can 
lead to a difference in the rate of temperature change for each test specimen.

Based on this the investigation was continued and it was discovered that most likely the

The fact that the test specimens has not been exposed to the temperature cycles defined by

In section 4.7 as well as in Appendix F the thicknesses of the test specimens after being cut 
was presented. In the test standard, it was defined that they were allowed to have a 
thickness of 50 ± 2 mm. Some of the specimens deviated from this demand by up to 1.3 
mm, but it was assessed that this deviation had no effect on the scaling results, and 
correcting it would potentially ruin the surface of the test specimens. It was also noted that 
no correlation could be observed between the thicknesses and the scaling results. 
In section 4.7, it was noticed, that the condition of the surface is critical for scaled material 
during the freeze thaw experiment. Imperfections and unevenness of the concrete surface 
has a very large effect. The salt frost scaling will then initiate in the imperfections and 
propagate to the neighboring surface area, resulting in a larger amount of scaling than 
otherwise. The imperfections that would have been relevant to consider and try to 
minimize in this project are the following:

were exposed to the cycles of temperature variation, and the degree of surface damage 
increased, this would also lead to differences in the thickness of the freezing medium. It 
would become thicker in areas that had already been damaged and less thick in relatively 
undamaged areas of the concrete surface.

In the air void analysis results section the air content was plotted against the SAP dosage, 
by doing a calculation of how much air was added by the two different dosages of SAP it 
was discovered that the SAP particles did not absorb 12.5 g water per gram as was 
expected (Laustsen 2011) but twice as much. This means that the SAP particles have 
altered the w/c ratio from 0.45 to 0.437 for the 1 ‰ dosage series and 0.412 for the 3 ‰ 
dosage series.  
When the w/c ratio is lowered a better resistance to salt scaling will be expected, this has 
been confirmed by previous experimental work illustrated by Figure 4-20. 
 
Figure 4-20(Penttala 2005): Figure showing the correlation between scaling in g/m2 and w/c ratio, for air contents 
varying from 1-8%  
From the figure, it can be seen that in general a lower w/c ratio will result in a lower 
amount of scaling, this is most prominent for lower air contents but valid for all air 
contents between 1 and 8 %. This range is the relevant range for the specimens in this 
project. 
The reason that the lowering of the w/c ratio has a positive effect on the amount of scaled 
material could be attributed to the fact that there is less water in the concrete and therefore 
less water to freeze and create movement within the capillary system of the concrete. A 
lower w/c ratio will also generally result in a higher strength. 
This means that the 3 ‰ SAP dosage series should be more influenced by the lowering of 
the w/c ratio compared to the 1 ‰ SAP dosage series, but both should exhibit lower 
scaling values then if they had been made with the intended w/c ratio of 0.45.

Curing should influence the capillary structure of the concrete and thereby its transport 
properties and its ability to relieve pressure from forming ice within the concrete. If curing 
influences the transport properties, it should be visible as systematic differences in plots of 
the scaling against the air content or spacing factor. 
Introducing internal curing results in two different influences on the concrete related to 
frost scaling: 
- 
Internal curing increases the degree of hydration and consequently more solid mass 
is formed within the concrete. As a result of this there is less room for water, 
consequently leading to less water within the concrete to freeze. This lowers 
potential pressure from forming ice within the concrete. This lowers the likelihood 
of salt frost scaling to occur. 
- 
According to (Bentz & Stutzman 2006) depercolated cement paste microstructures 
form very specific curing conditions. This micro structure has previously been 
illustrated in Figure 2-8. This consequently leads to a lower permeability. A lower 
permeability makes it harder for water to enter spaces enclosed by the local 
microstructure with low permeability. The enclosed spaces between the partially 
hydrated cement particles function as air voids on a capillary. However when 
internal curing is introduced the specific conditions described required to create this 
microstructure are no longer present. As the internal curing will not partially 
hydrated cement particles. As such the introduction of internal would in this 
manner increase the amount of scaled material observed. 
The results from the actual test show systematic tendencies but only indications, no well-
defined system can be observed. The indicated tendencies are: 
- 
1 ‰ SAP dosage specimens exhibits more scaling, higher spacing factor and lower 
air content compared to the 3 ‰ SAP dosage specimens. 
- 
When the accumulated scaling after 56 days is plotted against the spacing factor, 
there seems to be a tendency towards an exponentially increasing amount of scaling 
as the spacing factor is increased. The A/0 specimens and the Ref specimens form a 
third displaced exponential line. The 3 ‰ SAP dosage specimens line seem to be 
displaced towards the right when compared to the 1 ‰ SAP dosage specimens.  
- 
When the accumulated scaling is plotted against the air content there seems to be a 
tendency towards an exponentially decreasing amount of scaling when the air 
content is increased. 
When the effects of internal curing and the effect of the altered w/c ratio are considered it 
is noted that because the 3 ‰ SAP dosage specimens follow a different displaced 
exponential line compared to the 1 ‰ SAP dosage specimens it indicates that the sum of 
effects caused by these factors lower the amount of scaling. Due to the fact that the w/c 
ratio is slightly altered between these two test specimen series it is not possible determine 
==PAGE:78==
DTU Civil Engineering 
 
 
78 
the exact effect of internal curing. However, as the difference in the w/c ratio between the 
two samples is only 0.025 the effect of lowering the w/c is not expected to be major effect 
based on Figure 4-20. Therefore, it is possible to acknowledge that the positives of 
introducing internal curing most likely outweighs the negatives with regards to salt frost 
scaling.  
In the testing procedure for the freeze thaw experiment it is defined that the results from 
the test are not acceptable if the scaling is accelerating. In general, the results from the test 
show that they are reaching a level and stabilizing at that level. This may be the actual 
behavior of the test specimens, but it might also be because during the temperature 
exposure the time between scaling tests become 14 days compared to the 7 days at the 
beginning. This means that the time between the liquid on top of the sample is replaced 
doubles during the test. And if this liquid becomes less effective on the samples during 
exposure this may have a contributing effect to the fact that the scaling results are not 
accelerating. Currently very little is known about the effect of the freezing medium or 
liquid on the samples, and therefore it can be hard to evaluate what is and is not relevant to 
consider with regards to the test method and freezing medium. 
In the theory section osmotic pressure is described, it explains that the differences in salt 
concentrations in solutions in the connected capillary system within concrete is the driving 
force behind water movement and pressures. In the test standard, the freezing medium is 
3% NaCl solution. In the results it was noted that surface damage and indentations had a 
very large effect on the amount of scaling. This could be because when there is surface 
damage and indentations there are also micro cracks in the area, which allows the transport 
of the freezing medium into the concrete to happen faster and deeper into the concrete with 
less resistance. That the amount of damage is dependent on the thickness of the freezing 
medium on top of the concrete is also illustrated in the glue spalling theory presented by 
Valenza and Scherer. When the stress in the glass which is the equivalent of the concrete is 
calculated, it is directly related to the thickness of the epoxy, or the freezing medium. The 
thicker the freezing medium is the larger the stresses experienced by the concrete and 
therefore more damage (Valenza & Scherer I 2006)

4.8.1 Suggestions for improvements 
If another test using SAP is going to be designed the following focal points and 
improvements should be taken into consideration: 
A better saw that cuts the samples with a smoother final surface. 
If the effect of the different SAP fractions on the frost resistance of concrete is still the 
focus, it would be beneficial to make the difference between the sizes of the different SAP 
fractions larger and add a smaller amount of the SAP material, in order to make the amount 
of scaled material bigger, and thereby the variations in the sample series less, and thereby 
more reliable.

5 Conclusion 
One objective was to observe if air voids created by addition of SAP acts similarly as air 
voids created using a chemical AEA with regards to frost action. This was attempted 
investigated using an optical microscope to monitor ice crystal formation in air voids.  
Initially a microscope setup was attempted, but because of difficulties the approach was 
adapted and ended up being a camera setup. The focus of the experiment shifted towards 
investigating how ice in an air void is formed. 
This was done by cooling the specimen from both the observed surface as well as the 
opposite while monitoring the temperatures and capturing images during the cooling 
process. 
When the entire observed behavior of ice formation was considered it would be expected 
that within an actual air void the ice would form at the edge of the void and draw water 
towards it from the connected capillary system and the ice would grow into the void space. 
This behavior is reminiscent of the mechanisms related to microscopic ice lens growth. 
This shows that the direction of cooling is necessary to consider when designing a test 
setup with the purpose of observing initial ice formation in air voids in concrete. 
For future research a solution without an object glass, to avoid making an artificial air void 
and a less sensitive microscope with a smaller magnification would be recommended. 
Another objective of the report is to investigate the correlation between internal curing and 
the frost resistance of concrete. 
The internal curing was supplied by using SAP as an additive to create air voids and ensure 
internal curing. The SAP particles were added in either 1 ‰ or 3 ‰ dosages, four different 
SAP size fractions, 75-90 µm, 90-125 µm, 125-150 µm and 160-180 µm, were used. Test 
specimens containing AEA as well as a mix between SAP and AEA were also used. 
Finally a reference series containing no additives was made.   
A frost scaling test according to DS/CEN/TS 12390-9 Testing Hardened Concrete – Part 9: 
Freeze-thaw resistance standard was conducted on all the test specimens. By comparing 
the performance of the different test specimens the effects with regards curing and scaling 
could be evaluated. In order to more effectively analyze and evaluate the observed 
differences a supplementary Air void analysis was performed by the Danish Technological 
Institute. The Air void analysis was performed according to DS/EN 480-11:1998.
