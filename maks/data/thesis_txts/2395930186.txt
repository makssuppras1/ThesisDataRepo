Abstract
Stroke claims millions of lives worldwide every year and often leaves survivors with
some form of hemiparesis or hemiplegia. Only few patients with initially severely
impaired motor functions recover completely despite directed physical therapy.
Motor imagery (MI) training has shown to activate motor pathways and to pro-
mote neural reorganization. Brain-computer interfaces (BCI) can be used to decode
MI activity and provide feedback to the patient, possibly through functional electri-
cal stimulation (FES) of the corresponding muscles, enhancing the learning process.
Such systems show promise to lead to new forms of supplementary neurorehabilitation
therapy.
In an eﬀort to replace state-of-the-art methods like the Common Spatial Pattern
(CSP) algorithm and conventional machine learning models, this project examines
the possibility of using deep learning for MI classiﬁcation. The suggested system uses
convolutional neural networks (CNN) for temporal and spatial ﬁltering with fully-
connected or long short-term memory (LSTM) sequence classiﬁers. The approaches
are trained and tested on the publicly available Physionet MI dataset.
The selected global classiﬁer reaches 80.10%, 69.72%, and 59.71% mean accuracy
for a dataset with two, three, and four classes respectively.
This competes with
commonly observed accuracy values in related studies and outperforms the results
of related works on the same underlying data. Further investigation shows that only
short input segments are necessary for a relatively high accuracy and that the accuracy
can be further improved by adapting the classiﬁer to single individuals.
Deep learning should therefore be further considered as an alternative for EEG
classiﬁcation tasks in BCI research. However, to create a system, that can fulﬁll the
purpose of real-time feedback in MI training, the presented approach needs to be
examined and improved further.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter deﬁnes the scope of this project and motivates the approach in light
of the current research. A problem related hypothesis is formulated and a course of
action to examine the hypothesis deﬁned. Furthermore, the basic structure of the
thesis is outlined brieﬂy.
1.1
Motivation
With around 17 million incidents per year, stroke is a health issue worldwide. With
6.5 million deaths, it is the second most frequent cause of death and the leading cause
for adult disability [54]. While eﬀorts are necessary to control risk factors in order
to prevent strokes from happening, it is equally important to improve treatment and
post-stroke rehabilitation and thereby reduce the impact on the patients’ lives.
The interrupted blood ﬂow to the aﬀected brain areas caused by either a rupture
(hemorrhagic stroke) or an obstruction (ischemic stroke) of a blood vessel irreversibly
damages parts of the brain as a result of deﬁcient oxygen supply. The neurological
consequences can be vision problems, slurring speech, change of personality, and very
often one-sided paralysis. Current rehabilitation eﬀorts focus on physical therapy and
encouraging continuous training of the impaired body parts [33]. The main objective
is to restore the patients independence in daily activities.
Physical therapy has shown to support recovery of damaged neurons and promote
neuroplasticity, i.e. the reorganization of the nervous system to restore lost motor
functions by remapping them to healthy areas of the brain [14, 48]. It is believed that
visual and sensory feedback during training further promotes this behavior which is
why methods to enhance feedback are sought for.

==PAGE:14==
1.
Introduction
Stimulating muscles with a functional electrical stimulation (FES) device in order
to trigger desired movements has shown to be a promising feedback mechanism in
previous research [11, 12]. Furthermore, motor imagery (MI), i.e. the imagination
of body movements has shown to activate motor pathways in the brain similarly to
the actual execution of movements and therefore shows promise to be able to act
as substitute exercises in situations where there is no residual motor function, or to
prevent exhaustion [27, 31, 49].
The successful combination of these two approaches, namely the MI signal de-
tection through an EEG-based brain-computer interface (BCI) and real-time FES of
nerves to trigger the corresponding muscle responses could lead to new and improved
ways of neurorehabilitation therapy [22, 29]. Triggering the actual muscle movement
automatically, however, requires a reliable and robust signal classiﬁcation system.
Due to the complicated data acquisition procedures and high inter-subject vari-
ability, the signal processing in related research is mostly utilizing specialized feature
extraction or manual feature selection and conventional machine learning algorithms
like support vector machines (SVM) for classiﬁcation of MI signals.
Deep neural networks (DNNs), which have gained popularity in many ﬁelds over
the recent years, have not yet gotten a lot of attention as BCI signal classiﬁcation
tools. The reason may be that they require large amounts of data for training, which
would take a long time to record.
Nevertheless, the growing amount of available
data in the ﬁeld could soon allow training of large enough networks to extract very
generalized features without requiring specialized algorithms and extensive domain
knowledge.
1.2
Hypothesis
This work will investigate the hypothesis that given enough training data, DNNs can
be used as an end-to-end classiﬁcation solution in MI-BCI research. They are assumed
to render unnecessary the specialized algorithms like artifact rejection techniques and
common spatial pattern (CSP) approaches, which are currently the most commonly
used to achieve state of the art classiﬁcation results.
With the ever increasing ability of computer hardware and increasing amounts of
available data, it is expected that DNNs will also gain relevance in this ﬁeld of study.
Given their theoretical ability to detect arbitrarily complex patterns in data, they are
believed to be able to outperform all previous approaches given an inﬁnite supply of
training data and a suitable model architecture.

Aims and Objectives
1.3
Aims and Objectives
The aim of this project is to devise a deep-learning based approach to the problem
of MI-BCI classiﬁcation, which performs successful classiﬁcation at the presence of
large enough training data.
The model should at ﬁrst be designed as an oﬄine
system, while the real-time applicability should afterwards be discussed considering
the results. This work particularly aims at
... ﬁnding a suitable neural network architecture to perform the EEG classiﬁcation
task,
... discussing the eﬀects of certain hyperparameters on the model performance and
ﬁnding a good parameterization, and
... investigating the possibility of adapting a user-independent global model in
order to account for subject-speciﬁc diﬀerences.

This thesis is organized as follows:
Chapter 2 provides the clinical background about the central nervous system, neural

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter provides a brief overview of the biological, medical and technical pre-
requisites of this work. The following sections contain an introduction to the basic
anatomy and physiology of the human central nervous system (CNS), the pathophys-
iology and epidemology of stroke, and fundamentals of BCI systems and EEG.
2.1
Neuroanatomy and Neurophysiology
2.1.1
The Neuron
The basic building block of any nervous system is the neuron[1]. Its main purpose
is to transmit an electrical signal from one location in the body to the other. While
neurons located in the brain typically only bridge distances of few micrometers, there
are single neurons whose axon reaches from the spinal cord to muscles in the foot and
therefore reaching of lengths of more than one meter. The vast majority of neurons
in the human body is situated in the brain, which contains about 100 billion neurons
[1].
Figure 2.1 depicts the basic anatomy of a neuron in the peripheral nervous system.
It consists of the cell body, which the dendrites lead oﬀfrom and form connections
to other pre-synaptic neurons through synapses. Each neuron can typically receive
connections from many other neurons, which may both be inhibitory or excitatory
[1]. The sum of these incoming connections decides about if the neuron is triggered
or not.
When the neuron gets triggered, an impulse is transmitted through the axon. It
is surrounded by glial cells called the myelin sheath, which isolates the neurons as

2.1.2
Neurotransmission
As previously described, a neuron typically receives its activation through synapses
from other neurons. These unidirectional connections involve neurotransmitter molecules
being released into the synaptic cleft from the axon terminal on activation. The synap-
tic cleft describes a 20-30nm narrow gap between the pre-synaptic axon terminal and
the post-synaptic dendrites of the receiving neuron [48]. The transmitters released
into the synaptic cleft are then picked up by matching receptors at the dendrites.
The cell body maintains a certain rest potential of -60 to -70mV with respect to
outside of the cell membrane. This is caused by a higher concentration of K+ and
Cl−ions inside the cell, while Na+ and Ca2+ ions are kept outside of the cell [7].
The reception of neurotransmitters at the dendrites causes local ion channels to open
resulting in a partial depolarization. When suﬃciently many receptors are activated,
a threshold potential is reached causing a sudden complete depolarization of the cell
followed by a repolarization. This impulse called action potential is depicted in Figure

2.1.3
The Brain
The human CNS is regarded as the "most complex living organ in the known universe"

2.1.5
Motor Imagery (MI)
The term MI is referred to as the "mental execution of a movement ... without any
peripheral (muscle) activation" [31]. That is, the movement is imagined by a subject
without actually being executed. Several studies [27, 31, 49] suggest that the brain
regions, which are activated during motor execution (ME) and MI show signiﬁcant
overlap. It is shown that the primary sensorimotor cortex is also used in MI and the
authors of [49], among others, suggest explicitly that MI and ME share certain brain
circuits. Based on this research, it was shown in [27] that training using MI in healthy
subjects can actually improve motor performance.
In their review of studies, the authors of [8] conclude, mental practice of motor
functions proved beneﬁcial when combined with conventional physical therapy. They
emphasize that positive eﬀects in quality and range of movement of the respective
limbs were observed, as well as an increased ability to transfer improvements in trained
skills to diﬀerent tasks.
This shows promise for applying MI training in rehabilitation after stroke to re-
learn basic movements in paretic extremities. MI-based training oﬀers the advantage
that it can be begun already when there is only little or no residual motor function
due to paresis. Furthermore, MI does not involve strain on the muscles and therefore
prevents muscle fatigue.
2.2
Stroke
2.2.1
Pathophysiology
According to the deﬁnition by Sacco et al., stroke describes "a neurological deﬁcit
attributed to an acute focal injury of the CNS by a vascular cause" [42].
Generally, stroke can be divided into two forms: ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke.
In an ischemic stroke on the one hand, brain lesions are caused by infarction of blood
vessels, which interrupt the oxygen supply of the aﬀected regions. Hemorrhagic stroke
on the other hand is caused by the rupture of blood vessels and consequently bleeding
into surrounding brain tissues, which causes damage through compression [13].
10
==PAGE:23==
2.2.
Stroke
Within minutes, irreversible cell death happens in the "core" of the aﬀected brain
regions as a consequence of the oxygen under-supply. Emergency treatment focuses
on the "penumbra" around, which temporarily shows no function due to the under-
supply, but morphologically remains intact, and can be saved by quickly restoring
and stabilizing the blood pressure in the surrounding brain tissues [13].
As a result of the brain damage, symptomatic disabilities usually occur following
the incident. These can be (mostly one-sided) paralysis or problems with coordinating
movements, sensory disturbances, problems with speaking or understanding speech,
cognitive and memory impairments, or emotional disturbances [33]. Which symptoms
occur, as well as their exposure, varies from patient to patient and is highly dependent
on the severity of the stroke and the aﬀected brain regions.
2.2.2
Epidemology
Stroke has grown to become a major health problem. The World Stroke Organiza-
tion reports around 17 million stroke incidents and 6.7 million deaths annually in
consequence of stroke worldwide [54]. While ischemic strokes are signiﬁcantly more
common with 87% vs. 13% of all incidents, hemorrhagic strokes show a higher mor-
tality with 50% vs. 25% during the ﬁrst 90 days in a study by Andersen et al.[3].
As a result of the brain damage induced through the stroke, up to 75% of stroke
patients are impaired in their ability to perform basic activities of daily living (ADL)
in the time directly after the stroke, as reported in the Copenhagen Stroke Study
[20]. Another community-based study by Bonita and Beaglehole [6] reports that 88%
of the patients experienced some form of hemiparesis at stroke onset.
Through rehabilitation eﬀorts and natural recovery, this disability can be lessened
in most cases. The mentioned studies revealed that of the surviving patients only
around 56% [20] and 62% [6] respectively retained some form of disability. Almost
all surviving patients, however, experienced at least an improvement in their motor
functions during rehabilitation.
The study presented in [21] further suggests that the best ADL function is reached
within 8 to 20 weeks after stroke onset. This duration is found to correlate strongly
with the initial severity of the stroke [6, 20]. The authors of [21] further conclude that
neurological or functional improvement after the ﬁrst ﬁve months can generally not
be expected, which emphasizes the importance of eﬀective therapy during the acute
phase.

2.2.3
Therapy and Rehabilitation
Even though part of the brain damage caused by most strokes is irreversible, as
described in section 2.2.1, a certain degree of recovery from the initial impairments
at stroke onset is generally expected. Today’s rehabilitative therapy eﬀorts focus on
achieving the highest possible degree of independence for the patients in basic ADL
functions.
Experts agree that the most important part of stroke rehabilitation programs is
a "carefully directed, well-focused, repetitive practice" [33]. This usually involves
continuously encouraging and assisting the patient to perform tasks of gradually in-
creasing complexity, that involve the stroke impaired limbs or body functions. In case
of total paralysis of a limb, these may start with passive movements, i.e. a therapist
moving the aﬀected limb rather than the patient executing the movement.
2.2.4
Neural Plasticity and Stroke
In recent decades, the expert opinion changed from considering the adult brain to be
more or less ﬁxed to acknowledging that reorganization of neural circuits and in some
cases even the limited creation of new neurons do take place in adults [14].
While some neurons around the center of the stroke may recover and regain func-
tionality, irreversible cell death in the core region of the stroke can usually not be
avoided. Nevertheless, patients may be able to recover the skills associated to the
lost brain region through reorganization of the neural circuits. Theories referenced
in [48] suggest, that the "synapses are modiﬁable by frequency of use, to establish
preferential conduction pathways".
In many ways, post-stroke rehabilitative therapy can be compared to learning new
skills after a sudden loss of the ability to perform them. The repetitive loop of trial
and observation of the outcome through sensory and visual feedback induces neural
plasticity and strengthens brain circuits that support the desired eﬀect [14]. After the
loss of functionality in the brain region, that was previously involved in the execution
of a task, constant training can trigger a remapping of the required circuits to other
healthy regions, which is utilized in stroke rehabilitation therapy.
2.3
Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)
Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs), in a broad sense, are systems which establish a
direct (possibly bidirectional) communication between the brain and external devices.

==PAGE:25==
2.3.
Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)
Consequently, the desired output or action is only controlled by cerebral activity. This
project focuses on the unidirectional communication from the brain to a receiving
device. The central problem therefore lies in measuring brain activity and from these
measurements determine the origin or purpose of the underlying activity.
Diﬀerent neural imaging techniques are used for BCIs including electroencephalog-
raphy (EEG), magnetoencephalography (MEG), electrocorticography (ECoG), func-
tional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) or near infrared spectroscopy (NIRS). They
diﬀer widely in signal quality and temporal, as well as spatial resolution, but also in
terms of portability, cost and invasiveness. In the course of this project, only EEG-
BCI systems are considered, as they are most often used in BCI research and there
is a large amount of data available.
2.3.1
The Electroencephalogram (EEG)
Due to its relatively low costs, non-invasive nature, and high temporal resolution, EEG
is the most widely chosen method for BCI research. It consists of electrodes, which are
placed on the subject’s scalp. The measured potentials need to be ampliﬁed, ﬁltered
and recorded for examination by highly sensitive equipment, as the EEG signal is very
low in amplitude. The conductivity between skin and electrodes can be improved by
applying special conductive pastes or gel [7].
The number of electrodes and their position depends on the application, but the
international 10-20 system shown in Figure 2.5 provides a commonly accepted frame-
work for electrode placement to ensure comparability of recordings. It provides a grid
of named electrode positions, the distances of which are normed 10 and 20 percent
steps. The 10-10 extension extends this system to incorporate a higher number of
electrodes [35].
2.3.2

ﬁelds around neurons which sum up in response to signiﬁcant activation in one brain
area. The resulting ﬁeld is then measurable by the sensitive electrodes on the scalp
[9].
The signal quality of an EEG is generally heavily degraded by noise and attenua-
tion through the skull and other layers that lie between the origin of electrical activity

2.3.3
EEG Rhythms
The activity in the brain is dominated by rhythmical behaviour, which is why diﬀerent
frequency bands are deﬁned to classify EEG events happening at diﬀerent frequencies.
The commonly used band deﬁnitions are delta (2-4Hz), theta (4-8Hz), alpha (8-12Hz),
beta (15-30Hz), and gamma (30-150Hz) [9].
Frequency related activity in an EEG likely originates in neural oscillations [9]
as a result of periodic activation of a brain circuit. Activity in large neuronal pools
that span over diﬀerent brain areas mostly results in low-frequency, high amplitude
oscillations due to longer feedback loops and more involved neurons. Local activ-
ity in smaller neuronal populations, in turn, results in higher frequency waves with
smaller amplitude [32]. Figure 2.7 shows examples of such brain rhythms of diﬀerent
frequencies.
The brain often responds to events with a time-locked event-related potential
(ERP). These are notable increases in the measured EEG potential in response to a
stimulus over a short period [39]. While these increases are often subtle and therefore

3.1
Overview over BCI applications
The area of BCI research has gotten increased attention over the past years. While
it was long considered infeasible to read thoughts and commands through diﬀerent

tasks only through brain activity.
BCIs were successfully used to control an electric wheelchair [15]. Furthermore,

Using an invasive ECoG-BCI and an FES device, Bouton et al. [41] were able to

with spinal cord injury. The patient successfully performed tasks such as grasping,
pouring and transferring objects, despite severe paralysis.
There exists a large interest in using motor imaginary (MI) training and brain-
computer interfaces (BCI) in stroke rehabilitation. In a study by Pichiorri et al. [40],
supplementary MI-BCI training with visual feedback has resulted in a lager functional
improvement than conventional therapy in a control group.
With FES devices showing strong evidence of improving motor function in paretic
limbs [55], eﬀorts are made to combine these approaches to lead to BCI administered

3.2
MI-BCI classiﬁcation
Extensive research focused on improving the classiﬁcation of EEG signals during
MI tasks of upper limb movements [22, 23, 26, 29, 38, 40, 45, 50, 52], lower limb
movements [11, 12], or a combination of both [25, 28].
For feature extraction, the common spatial patterns (CSP) approach, or a varia-
tion thereof, is a popular choice [12, 23, 28, 29, 53]. Other works rely on principal or
independent components as features [11, 52] or employ specialized methods or hand-
crafted features [26, 38]. Recently, features learned by artiﬁcial neural networks [5,
25, 44, 45, 50] were ﬁrst used for MI brain signal classiﬁcation.
Classiﬁcation of the extracted feature sets is mostly performed by supervised ma-
chine learning algorithms like support vector machines (SVM) [28, 52], Bayesian clas-
siﬁers [11, 12], or artiﬁcial neural networks [5, 25, 44, 45, 50]. In most cases, these
depend on high-quality features and extensive preprocessing like ﬁltering, artifact
removal, and trial rejection to ensure good results.
Only little research exists in devising end-to-end deep neural network architec-
tures, which learn usable features for classiﬁcation themselves instead of having to
rely on features created from extensive domain knowledge. The authors of [50] and [5]
successfully used convolutional neural networks (CNN) on structured time-frequency
EEG signals to perform classiﬁcation at competitive accuracy. Finally, the authors
of [45] and [44] proposed a DNN setup, which does not require any time-frequency
analysis beforehand, but takes raw EEG signals as input.

Principal component analysis (PCA)
A common challenge in machine learning, and data analysis in general, is the problem
of high dimensional data. Even though a sample might contain a lot of features,
these are often highly redundant, which opens possibilities to reduce the number of
dimensions without loosing too much information [46].
One tool for this dimension reduction is the principal component analysis (PCA).
It provides a linear transformation of the data’s coordinate system, such that we
obtain a set of new orthogonal coordinate axes that are ordered by the directions
of highest variance in the underlying data. This means that correlated axes in the
original coordinate system will inﬂuence the same principal components.

4.2.5
Recurrent neural networks
The previous considerations have been focusing on conventional feed-forward neural
networks.
These imply strict constraints on the format of the input data (a N-
dimensional vector) and the ﬂow of information inside the network.
In order to capture time-dependency in a feed-forward neural network, one would
typically align all samples along one dimension of the input. This means that M time
steps of N values would be mapped to a long N × M-dimensional input vector. This
increases the number of weights that need to be trained drastically.
Recurrent neural networks aim to resolve this by assigning neurons an internal
state h that is preserved between samples and therefore enables them to analyze
sequences. The activation of a neuron then depends on the current input and the
internal state, which is aﬀected by previous activations.

the course of this work is the LSTM network proposed by Hochreiter and Schmidthu-
ber [19]. It has proven to be especially eﬃcient in capturing long-term dependencies
compared to other recurrent architectures [16].

4.2.6
Convolutional neural networks
In general, the number of trainable parameters in an artiﬁcial neural network is
the determining factor for the training time of the network. Additionally, the more
parameters there are in the model, the more it will be prone to overﬁtting. This term
describes a situation where the trained model performs very well in representing the
data it was trained on, but not on new data from the same corpus.
A problem arises with conventional neural network architectures, where the num-
ber of weights in the ﬁrst layer is not only determined by the number of neurons, but
also by the dimension of the input layer. Therefore, high dimensional inputs (e.g.
images) require many weights as each input value is connected to each neuron in the
ﬁrst layer.
Convolutional neural networks (CNN) aim to resolve the problem of high input
dimensions for data with a grid-like structure [16]. The term grid-like refers to time
series (1-D grids), images (2-D grids), or higher order data like videos (3-D grid),
where adjacent data-points (e.g. subsequent time samples, neighboring pixels in an
image) expected to be more correlated than distant points in the input data.

4.2.7
Overﬁtting and Regularization
The more parameters there are in a neural network, the more it is prone to overﬁtting
on the training data. Overﬁtting describes the situation where a model performs very
well on the data it was trained on, but generalizes poorly to any new inputs. This is
a general problem in machine learning, but it is especially evident in deep learning
applications as the models tend to have many free parameters.
A natural means to reduce overﬁtting is to increase the amount of training data.
Of course, getting more data is often expensive or time-intensive, which is why meth-
ods have been devised to reduce this problem and prime the model towards better
generalization.
Regularization
The general idea of regularization is to extend the cost function C(W; X, y) with
a penalty term Ω(W), which depends on the current weight conﬁguration W. The
regularized cost function becomes
eC(W; X, y) = C(W; X, y) + αΩ(W),
(4.28)
where α is a parameter to control the impact of the penalty term Ω. In machine
learning applications, the term Ωis usually chosen as a matrix norm of the parameter
matrix W.
One such norm is the 1-norm, which computes the maximum column sum of the
parameter matrix W deﬁned as

Another form of regularization, which is commonly applied for LSTMs, is dropout
regularization. It is based on randomly deactivating a ﬁxed ratio of all neurons in a

In practice, regularization is an eﬃcient tool to prevent overﬁtting. The additional
dependence of the cost function on the parameters of the network introduces rules for
training that balance with the urge of perfectly representing the training data. This,
in turn, leads to better generalization.
Data augmentation
Another measure, which can prevent a neural network from overﬁtting is data aug-
mentation. This refers to the concept of distorting the input data in a sensible way
and thereby retrieving multiple samples that are created from the same underlying
input sample.
The techniques have to be chosen according to the application and what shall be
achieved through them. The applied operations should correspond to variations that
might occur in reality [34]. With images as input data, random translations, rotations,
or zooms are common techniques. These can also help achieve independence of the
position, angle, and distance of the target in an input image [16].
With time-series data, such transformations are often not feasible or do not serve
the purpose of the application. In the context of EEG data, it is for example essential
that data from the used electrodes is not randomly mixed together. Low-power addi-
tive noise, however can be beneﬁcial for the generalization performance of a classiﬁer.

5.1.3
Training and Validation Data
As the training process of deep neural networks can be very time consuming, the
overall objective was to yield a model that is as subject-invariant as possible. That
way, it would not be necessary to repeat the entire training process for every new
subject, which, apart from the additional time, would also require an acquisition of
data of that subject beforehand.
In order to measure the performance with respect to this goal, the recordings were
divided into training and validation data by subjects, such that a model was never
trained on trials from a subject, that is also present in the validation set.
Figure 5.2 illustrates this procedure. As a ﬁrst step, the subjects were divided
into training and test groups. Then all trials from all subjects of the training set were
shuﬄed. This ensures a smoother training procedure as batches of adjacent trials are
more representative of the overall statistical distribution of the data.
The models were trained using a mechanism called generator training. It is per-
formed by passing a generator function instead of the training data to the training
algorithm.
This generator function runs an inﬁnite loop, which produces batches
of training data as long as the ﬁtting algorithm requests new ones. In this speciﬁc
implementation, the generator constructs batches by randomly selecting trials out of
the data of all training subjects to which random noise is applied for augmentation
(see 4.2.7).

As there is no algorithmic procedure to obtain a suitable architecture for a DNN, the

inspiration was drawn from [5, 45, 50], where deep neural networks were trained on

CNNs are a favorable method for high-dimensional data, as they contain relatively few
trainable parameters compared to the number of data points in their input. In turn,
this increases the number of hyperparameters, which have to be chosen beforehand
and remain ﬁxed throughout the training process. CNNs are usually built by stacking
multiple convolutional and pooling stages [16, 34]. The number and order of these
stages has to be chosen as well as their kernel/pooling dimensions.

Chapter 7
Discussion
This chapter discusses the relevance of the obtained results with respect to related
works and the general state of research. Furthermore, it makes the connection to the
actual application of a similar system.
7.1
Relevance for clinical applications
The results from section 6.1 show that oﬄine classiﬁcation for the two, three, and
four class data can be performed at global accuracy values of 87.98 %, 76.61%, and
65.73% respectively. Decreasing the amount of input data in favor of a lower response
latency lowers the global accuracy to 80.10%, 69.72%, and 59.71% respectively.
While for the two class case, one false prediction in ﬁve trials may be acceptable,
increasing the amount of classes degrades the accuracy to a level which may not be
acceptable in actual implementations for stroke rehabilitation therapy. In order for
such a system to be viable in clinical practice, it is necessary to ﬁnd ways to improve
the system’s performance.
This might, however, be limited by the low SNR and
spatial resolution of EEG recordings.
The only alternative is to keep the MI training paradigm simple, that is to use
fewer distinct movements to train. Two diﬀerent movements per session, preferably
on diﬀerent sides of the body, are more likely to result in a reliably working system
than many diﬀerent or very similar movements on the same side.
Furthermore, the results of section 6.4 have suggested, that some subjects might
be more able to perform MI than others are. Although evidence suggests, that MI
features like controlling the SMR can be learned by repeated training [4], it is possible
that this kind of training will not be applicable to some patients.

Discussion
In order to obtain optimal results, it is recommended to use transfer learning on
single-subject data to adapt the system to every new patient. This would require a
short initial training session, but can remarkably increase the performance for single
individuals, as was shown in section 6.5. For a smaller part of the subjects, this
process yielded next to perfect classiﬁcation results for the two class problem.

Comparison to other works
The ﬁrst measure of judging the overall performance of the presented models shall
be a comparison to other works that used the same underlying data as this work.
Unfortunately, not a lot of attempts have been made to use the Physionet recordings
for MI classiﬁcation compared to popular data sets like the BCI Competition data.
Therefore, it is neither guaranteed that the presented works represent what could be
accomplished at the current state of BCI research, nor can the completeness of the
list be guaranteed, despite thorough research. Table 7.1 states the maximum overall
average accuracy values from other works performing similar MI classiﬁcation tasks

Furthermore, the number of utilized EEG channels is stated along with informa-
tion on if there existed one global classiﬁer or if an individual classiﬁer was trained for

which used three seconds of MI as input. Two classiﬁers, one with all 64 available
channels and one with a selection of 14 channels (the same as in [24]), were listed for

The accuracy of the presented global model is in line with the maximum perfor-
mance of the presented alternative works, while the subject-speciﬁc classiﬁer exceeds
it signiﬁcantly. Apart from the superior performance, it is important to consider, that
validation of the global classiﬁer was performed on completely diﬀerent subjects than
training. The trained classiﬁer could therefore not make use of any subject-dependent
diﬀerences, as it could with per-subject training.
In a global scope, comparing the results to other works presented in 3 that operated
on diﬀerent data, an average accuracy of close to 90 % is usually reached in a two-class
MI classiﬁcation task.
Disregarding if single methods are slightly superior or inferior, the simple end-to-
end nature of the presented system, the possibility of adapting it to single subjects,
and the competitive results show, that deep learning models are an alternative worth
considering, given enough available data.

7.3
The path to a real-time BCI-FES system
Apart from reliable classiﬁcation, the key to combining an MI-BCI system with a
FES device for muscle stimulation as feedback is a short latency between the related
brain activity and the corresponding muscle contraction. This will likely increase the
perception of correspondence of the two events and induce learning processes and
neural plasticity.
The results from section 6.2 have shown, that already with a short segment of
about one second of input data from the onset of an MI task, a comparatively reliable
class prediction can be obtained.
In the prototyping environment of this work, all samples of a sequence have to be
provided at the same time in order to obtain the result. Creating an eﬃcient real-time
system would require further optimization. As the ﬁlters of the CNN are chosen such
that they represent linear ﬁltering in the time domain, a shift register architecture
could enable the system to save previously computed values and only feed in new
measurements opposed to providing all the past samples at each time step.

7.
Discussion
Generally, the results of section 6.3 and section 6.2 suggest that the classiﬁcation is
by a large part based on information from the start and the end of the MI movement.
A sustained movement does not seem to contribute to the classiﬁcation much.
Section 6.2 has shown, that already at short input segments, the classiﬁcation
accuracy almost saturates at a fairly high level.
These segments, however, were
always drawn from the beginning of the trial. The results from section 6.3 show, that
the accuracy is highly dependent on the time the sample is drawn relative to the cue
and is highest at the start and end of a movement.
In principle, RNN architectures like LSTMs appear much more suited for real-time
predictions like these as they provide the possibility of sequence output in response to
an input sequence. Unfortunately, with the proposed CRNN structure, the classiﬁca-
tion results were much less reliable than the CNN classiﬁcations to begin with. This
was shown in section 6.1. Nevertheless, it may be possible to devise a more suitable
RNN architecture, which can then be used for online classiﬁcation.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
One prerequisite for being able to use MI training with BCIs in stroke rehabilitation is
a system that is enable to decode the MI EEG signals and translate them into move-
ment classes. This thesis presented an end-to-end deep learning approach intended to
replace large parts of the signal processing pipelines that are currently widely applied
in MI-BCI classiﬁcation tasks.
Two diﬀerent models were developed, trained, and evaluated on the publicly avail-
able Physionet EEG data. The ﬁrst model only used convolutional and fully connected
layers, while the second included a recurrent LSTM layer. The latter was discarded
due to long training times and inferior classiﬁcation performance. The achieved clas-
siﬁcation rates of the best model for 3s of EEG input data were around 80% for two
class data and around 60% for four class data.
Deeper insight into the results from sections 6.4 and 6.5 has shown that classiﬁer
performance is strongly dependent on the EEG properties of individual subjects.
In order to improve the per-subject accuracy it is recommended to adapt a global
classiﬁer to each individual through transfer learning, which yielded an up to 9.3%
increase in global average classiﬁcation accuracy.
The global average classiﬁcation results suggest that the classiﬁer performance can
compete with other methods like FBCSP and SUTCCSP. In fact, the classiﬁer out-
performed all other works presented in section 7.2, which operated on the same data.

the global validity of these results, but there is strong evidence that DNNs provide
an opportunity to further increase the state-of-the art classiﬁcation performance.

case in a real-time application showed to be more challenging, as time-invariability of

Chapter 9
Future Work
This work has shown that DNNs can help to obtain classiﬁcation results of MI-EEG
data, which can compete with state-of-the art results of alternative techniques. As
this work has a prototyping, introductory character, it did not go into detail for a lot
of aspects where further investigation is necessary or improvements are possible.
Improved deep learning models
First of all, the model architecture was based on previous works and a trial-and-error-
approach. That being said, there is a high probability that better architectures exist.
The next to inﬁnite possibilities of building DNN models make deep learning a very
ﬂexible framework, but it still requires manual ﬁne-tuning in order to obtain optimal
results. Especially as deep learning is a young and quickly evolving domain, new
methods and techniques may arise that solve problems, which limit the quality of the
results that were observed in the course of this work.
Recurrent DNNs seem the method of choice for real-time time series classiﬁcation
problems like a BCI-FES system. The LSTMs used as part of this work did not provide
as good results as their conventional feed-forward counterparts. Nevertheless, more
eﬀort should be put into investigating diﬀerent recurrent DNN alternatives.
Real-time application
This thesis presented an oﬄine system, which operates on already recorded data.
If such a system is intended to be used in stroke rehabilitation together with an
FES device for real-time muscle stimulation, several points have to be considered
61
==PAGE:74==
9.
Future Work
apart from the necessity of reasonably fast hardware to execute the underlying model
operations in real-time.
The results have shown, that with the current architecture, the classiﬁer mostly
reacts to the start and end of an MI trial. This entails a severely degraded classiﬁca-
tion accuracy between these events. The output of such a classiﬁer, would therefore
have to be post-processed before using the result as input to a FES device.
Finally, almost all MI-BCI classiﬁcation research is based on data from healthy
subjects.
It is questionable, if stroke patients with damaged areas in the motor
cortex are 1) equally able to perform MI, and 2) produce similar EEG features while
performing MI. Larger studies have to conﬁrm that EEG recordings of MI training can
be decoded by EEG classiﬁcation tools at a comparable accuracy to healthy subjects.
Improving and extending training data
A crucial limiting factor in deep learning applications often is the amount and quality
of training data.
This work’s results have shown that diﬀerent subjects perform
diﬀerently at MI tasks. Several data preprocessing steps like artifact rejection and
sorting out bad trials might lead to a better ﬁt of the model, but have not been
applied during the project.
With respect to the amount of data, there is little chance that there exist or will
exist signiﬁcantly (by orders of magnitude) larger databases of MI recordings than the
one used in this work. It is expected that the equipment, experimental paradigm, and
experiment environment severely inﬂuence the EEG signal characteristics. Therefore,
a model obtained from data in one experimental setup is not likely transferable to
another setup.
This capability could, however, be reached by combining data from many diﬀerent
setups, such that the DNN also learns to model the diﬀerent disturbances and learns
to generalize between setups. For this to succeed, a methodology has to be devised,
that accounts for diﬀerent sample rates and possibly channel conﬁgurations to be able
to combine data recorded with diﬀerent parameters.
After all, despite regularization techniques, batch learning, and data augmenta-
tion, the best measure to reduce overﬁtting and allow for longer training times, is
to increase the amount of training data.
This can lead to the discovery of ﬁner
patterns in the data and ultimately to an increased overall performance and better
generalization.
