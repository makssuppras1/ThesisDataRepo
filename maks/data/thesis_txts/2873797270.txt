Abstract
The new JT-60SA tokamak is currently in the process of entering its second operating phase,
at which point the team will be operating with a power input ceiling of 19 MW. Their diverter
configuration(s) will allow for a cooling of at least 10 MW
m2 , but the question remains whether
the heat load will exceed this limit.
To examine whether this cooling limit might be exceeded, we employed the drift-fluid nHESEL
model. This model can be used to simulate outer mid-plane plasma edge phenomena, without
the use of effective diffusion factors. This stands in stark contrast to many other existing mod-
els, which reduce turbulence to effective coefficients, thereby lowering the accuracy of edge
phenomena modelling. To allow for a sweep of the JT-60SA operating parameter space, a wrap-
per was developed for the nHESEL model. This was then deployed in conjunction with a model
for coupling the outer-midplane domain of the nHESEL model to the downstream diverter(s).
It was found that the predicted operating parameters, inferred from the 19 MW input power,
will not exceed the cooling limit of 10 MW
m2 for their planned 19 MW configuration(s), but that
their future 33 MW configuration may exceed one or more limits. Within the examined para-
meter space, it was found that the heat load for the 19 MW case may reach values of ∼2.3 MW
m2 for
a dual-divertor configuration, and twice this value for the corresponding single-configuration
case. Depending on the contribution of ions towards this heat load, values of ∼4 MW
m2 may be
reached for the dual-divertor case, and twice this value for the single-divertor case. For the 33
MW case we may reach heat loads of ∼9 MW
m2 for the single-divertor case, and > 15 MW
m2 when
including the ions. The dual-divertor cases are expected to have heat loads of 50% these values,
or above.

Introduction
Fusion energy; it has long been a dream and a sort of modern holy grail. From an abstract
monolith of power found in children’s comics[1], to the wildly optimistic Mr. Fusion[2], to far-
future sources of energy and propulsion in books[3] and games[4][5], it is an idea which has
captured the minds of many, and for good reasons.
Over the last 100 years, many different ideas and concepts have been explored[6], such as
cold fusion[7] with its unfortunate controversies, to inertial confinement fusion[8], the stellar-
ator[9], and the planned and in-construction DEMO[10] and ITER[11] tokamak-style reactors.
Although solar and wind, combined with improvements in energy storage technology, accounts
for an ever-increasing amount of our current-day energy supply[12], we still lack a stable base-
load power supply not tied to fossil fuel sources, one which does not require mining and re-
fining of radioactive isotopes, and one that is not reliant on local geography or environmental
conditions. This is the promise of fusion energy: to provide a reliable source of power, no mat-
ter the weather or season, no matter the location, which does not poison our environment and
people.
In the present day multiple different types of reactor designs are in development, with the main
differences being the confinement types, and the types of fuel utilised. These confinement
types consist of the previously mentioned inertial confinement, magnetic confinement, and
for the sake of completeness: gravitational confinement. Among these three approaches, we
will be focusing on magnetic confinement - the confinement type utilised in reactors such as
stellarators and tokamaks.
A popular fuel combination is deuterium and tritium[13], both isotopes of hydrogen, and can
combine as
D + T →He4(3.5 MeV) + n(14.1 MeV).
Here, deuterium is a stable isotope which can be extracted from seawater, and tritium is radio-
active, but can be bred in the reactor by bombarding lithium with neutrons. Alternatively, one
can use D + D which can be used to create tritium as
D + D →He3 + n
D + D →T + p,
or D + He-3 which only produces charged particles
D + He3 →He4 + p.
To extract the energy from a fusion reactor, such as a tokamak, and convert it into usable
electric current, we would have to either convert the radiation from the fusion reactions directly
into energy[14] or make do with a more traditional and rather reliable approach. This approach
would of course be to heat up a cooling fluid using the energy released, and then use that fluid
to run a turbine[15]. This is the current plan for DEMO.
To withstand endure the heat load released from the fusion reactions and to clean the plasma,
reactors commonly employ divertors, areas on the reactor walls which the plasma is diverted
unto[16]. These divertors require plenty of cooling and have a maximum heat load they can
endure based on their design, which is the focus of this project: Using simulations to examine
how high the heat-load is on the divertors in the current largest and newest JT-60SA experi-
mental research reactor, located in Naka, Japan, and whether they will be able to endure the
operational parameters decided upon, given their cooling capacity of 10-15 MW
m2 [17].
Due to the reactor only being a few years old, having achieved first plasma on October 23
2023[17], not a lot of data currently exists on its operational modes. The heat loads on the
divertors are therefore not well-known, and predictions are sorely needed to inform future
operations.

2
State of the Art
All simulations conducted during this project were preformed using the nHESEL model[19],
to simulate a small region at the plasma edge, at the outboard midplane. This model is a
2D 4-field energy-conserving fluid numerical model based upon the BOUT++ framework[20],
made for the simulation of low-frequency edge-instabilities in fusion plasmas, which includes
the simulation of neutrals but not impurities. The nHESEL model is built upon the HESEL
model[21][22], which is short for ”Hot Edge SOL Electrostatic” model, where SOL is short
for Scrape-Of-Layer (area outside the separatrix also known as the Last Closed Magnetic/Flux
Surface abbreviated as LCMS or LCFS).
The main advantages to this model, relative to other more common effective diffusion models
(such as the SOLPS-ITER[23] fluid model or EDGE2D-EIRENE[24] fluid-Monte Carlo model)
are that does not rely on effective diffusion and convection coefficients such as the turbulent
diffusivity[25].
Given that it has been shown that effective coefficients for diffusion and convection are not
sufficient to describe SOL phenomena[26], the model has a major advantage when studying
the physics at the SOL of a hot plasma.
However, the nHESEL model does not include the full device geometry, which is a limiting
factor. It is only possible to simulate a small 2D domain at the device midplane, meaning it is
unable to provide information about other important parts of the reactor, such as the divertor
region. Since the model only simulates a small region it is possible to simulate with a far higher
resolution, for a longer time, or in a larger parameter space, than other models. It can also be
used to study the transition between confinement modes[27].
To sum up, the nHESEL model is the model of choice if you wish to study turbulent edge-
phenomena in reactors, confinement modes, or the time-resolution of blobs[28]. If you instead
wish to study the plasma in a larger geometry such as ITER or JET, or include impurities, you
would instead choose other effective diffusion models such as the SOLPS-ITER model[29].
Multiple other codes exist, such as the CUTIE, FINDIF, and PROCTR fluid codes, the MOCA
Monte Carlo code, or the EUTERPE gyrokinetic code[30]. Benchmarking efforts have and are
also being made[31], to compare the output of different codes such as SOLPS and EDGE2D-
NIMBUS to each other.

Theory
In the following sections, the two-fluid equations and Braginskii equations will be introduced,
followed by the drift-fluid nHESEL model.
This will be followed by definitions of derivative quantities, which can be extracted from a
nHESEL simulation. We will then proceed with a description of the transformation from outer
midplane heat flux (upstream), to divertor heat flux (downstream), with its dependencies. The
general method for calculating the total heat loss over the last closed flux surface will also be
covered at the end.
3.1
Fluid Equations & The nHESEL Model
As previously mentioned the nHESEL model is an energy-conserving 4-field model based on
BOUT++, which utilises a 2D slab geometry for its simulations. This simulation domain can be
seen in figure 2. Note that the y and z axis names are swapped internally in the code, and as
such z and y may be swapped for results in later sections. For now though, we are sticking to
the original y and z naming for the sake of introducing theory.

geometry. Note that the directions of the y- and z-axis are interchanged internally, and as such
two coordinate systems are presented: an internal and external.
3.1.1
Basic Quantities
Before introducing of any models, we will cover some of the basic quantities which we use to
describe our plasma.
To begin with, our plasma is assumed to consist of two species of particles: ions and electrons.
We also assume that we are working with a deuterium plasma, limiting ourselves to single-
ionization. As such we define n [m−3] = ne [m−3] = ni [m−3].
Each species in this plasma have a mass me and mi, as well as a charge e−and qi for each species.
Impurities can modify the effective charges.

The Two-Fluid Equations
The model is based upon the Braginskii equations[32], a closed-form set of equations consist-
ing of a continuity equation, a momentum conservation equation, and an energy conservation
equation for closure, given fields for the pressures and densities. The open-form equivalent
of the Braginskii equations is known as the two-fluid equations[13], which only consists of
the continuity equation and momentum conservation equation. These are given as (excluding
Maxwells equations)

A few assumptions are made during the derivation of the model. Mainly that:
• The plasma can be described as two separate fluids interacting with each other.
• The two fluids interact with each other due to collisions/friction.
• The fluids are always in local thermal equilibrium, meaning the local velocity distribu-
tions are always Maxwellian.
• The plasma can be treated as being neutral on length scales larger than the Debye length,
but not below. It is quasi-neutral.

3.1.3
The HESEL & nHESEL Models
To begin with, it must be noted that the nHESEL model is a direct continuation of the HESEL
model, and uses the exact same equations, but with an additional source term on each equa-

The main difference between the previously the aforementioned Braginskii equations, and the

drift-fluid equations. Not two-fluid equations.

ing grids and timesteps; getting the location of the LCFS or wall; or converting from Bohm
normalisation to SI units.
Retrieving raw data is done using the ’collect’ function[47] from the BOUT++ framework,
which reconstructs the simulation domain that has been split up for multithreading purposes.
However, this operation can get rather expensive in terms of overhead, meaning we would like
to minimize the amount of times we load raw data.
A simple way of doing this would be to load everything once, and then simply keeping it in
memory. Unfortunately, this method quickly encounters another issue. With the amount of
fields and the size of the domain, as well as the amount of outputs written, things get rather
expensive from a memory perspective. Even with the large amounts of RAM for each node on
the Sophia cluster (128 GB per node, 256 GB per FAT node), it is possible to run out of memory
by using this method - in which case it will fail. Especially if there are more threads running on
the node at a time, each taking up resources.
At this point, we face an optimisation problem. We need to minimize the amount of times we
load data, reducing CPU time, but also minimize the RAM usage.
Process Optimisation
There are multiple ways we could optimise for CPU usage and RAM usage. One top-level
method for RAM optimisation is to load the data in time slices, processing each slice of time
individually by taking the mean or sum along one or multiple axes, and then joining the data
afterwards.
The slicing by itself allows a flexible reduction of memory usage, at the cost of more overhead
from the ’collect’ function. Then there is the axis reduction. Simply taking the mean or sum
along a single axis would, in case of our simulation parameters, reduce the data size by a factor
of 512. Quite a significant change!
These two methods are effectively illustrated in figure 8.
First we iterate over slices, then over variables within each slice, and during which we reduce
our data size by collapsing one or more axes. Once all slices have been processed, the data are
combined again.
These two methods by themselves may be sufficient, and probably are, as we should be able to
keep everything in memory at all times due to the slicing method. To allow for greater slice
sizes, and therefore less overhead, it would be prudent to add one more optimisation.

This last optimisation is related to how the non-reduced raw data and derived quantities uses
significantly more memory than the reduced quantities.
The optimisation involves counting the amount of times a set of data is required - be that for
calculating another variable, or simply by itself - and then only keeping the data in memory for
as long as needed. As soon as a variable is no longer needed, we free the memory. We effectively
simulate how the data processing is going to be performed, before actually performing it.
This is achieved by keeping an internal dictionary of dependencies as defined in section 3, and
recursively constructing dependency trees for each variable as shown in figure 9. For each
tree we traverse, we count each occurrence of a given variable we encounter, and if a variable
we have already encountered in one tree repeats in another, then we stop going down that
branch. This results in a count of how many times each variable is involved in any process, and
is indicated by the white ’Initialize variable counters’ and beige ’Variable counters’ nodes in
figure 8.
The actual implementation of this optimisation can be found in the following section, about
the ’dataFetch’ method.
4.4
dataFetch Method Implementation
The dataFetch function is a method meant to act like a black box, where the inputs are a list of
variables, and optionally ranges for x or t. Given inputs the method then handles the retrieval
as well as calculations of these variables, and returns them in the order asked for. As they
are also processed in this order, the memory usage and time to process each variable may vary
depending on the order of variable inputs.
Adding a variable such as q⊥,i with many dependencies will for example require the retrieval
and calculation of a significant number of variables up front, but it will then be followed by a
quick retrieval of the remaining variables as we can simply reuse them.

Dependent Variables
The dependent variables are treated one at a time. For each dependent variable we go through
multiple steps.
The first step is checking whether the variable is already present in temporary storage. If it
is, great! Then we can skip the whole calculation process by loading from temporary storage.
If not, then we have to call the ’calcVar’ method, which handles calculating any dependent
variables. More on that at the end of this section, and in figure 11.
After this, we reduce the variable counter for this variable by one, and then check if the count
for this variable has reached zero. A count reaching zero indicates that the variable is no longer
required for any further operations.
If for example we just retrieved a variable from temporary storage and the count is now zero,
then we would remove the variable from storage - no need to keep it around any more! If on

the other hand we just calculated the variable and the count is still above zero, then it means
we need the variable for some operation somewhere down the line. We therefore add it to
temporary storage and move on.
This finishes by appending the retrieved data to a list, and moving on to the next dependent
variable, until we have gone through all dependent variables. We then move on to the inde-
pendent variables.
Independent Variables
The independent variables are treated in a slightly different manner than the dependent vari-
ables. Instead of loading each one at a time, we first check whether they are present in storage
and whether the t- and x-indices are the same as last time they were loaded. If they are indeed
the same, great, no need to load them anew. If they are not, then we mark the variables for
reloading.
After this loop of checking independent variables, we load all variables which are either not
present, or those which were for different ranges of t and x.
These variables are then woven into the already-present list of independent variables, resulting
in the variables being arranged in the requested order.
Finally, we decrease all counters of all independent variables, and discard those whose counters
have reached zero, as they are no longer needed.

Before moving on from the simulation workflow to the simulation parameters, it would be
prudent to cover the exact methods by which we are going to estimate the perpendicular heat
flux q⊥, for Ploss in eq. 54.
We are going to cover three different methods, which will be compared throughout this docu-

It should also be noted that since our expression for the heat flux only covers the turbulent flux

the output with a diffusion correction factor of 1.1. This is based upon data from A.H. Nielsen,
which demonstrates that up to ∼10% of the flux comes from diffusion, and since this is already
a rough measurement we simply add 10% on top of our values for a quick correction.

Now that we have established a simulation model, a workflow using this model, the general
parameters we run this model with, and a downstream projection method, we just need to
figure out which temporal data range we can utilise from our outputs. We need to, in other
words, examine the different convergences of the nHESEL model for our setup.
We have two types of convergence which we need to examine. The first one is how long it
takes before our turbulent behaviour occurs, from the initial perturbations. The second one is
how much data we need to include before the values of our quantities Ploss, DFOL, and PFOL
converges towards a singular value. This will be covered in the aforementioned order.

Now that we have everything from simulation parameters, to workflow scripts, and a working

Unfortunately, the exact plasma pedestal parameters in the JT-60SA reactor are not known, so
we must first estimate them. Fortunately we do know the amount of energy they plan to inject
into the reactor. Since this energy has to escape somehow, we can simply match the total loss
across the LCFS to their input power. This approach should then provide us with a range of
their possible operating parameters.
The disadvantages of this method are that it is not extremely accurate, as some heat may radiate
out instead of being transported by the plasma, and that we have multiple parameters to search
for, slowing down the investigation unless the previously-described workflow is integrated into
an optimisation algorithm. Even then, we will still have multiple combinations resulting in the
same output.
In our case, we will do a very simple type of search: a linear search with constant density, where
we assume T = Te = Ti for simplicity. During the beginning of the second operating phase of
JT-60SA they have an upper limit of 19 MW input power[17], with 16 MW being neutral beam
injection, and 3 MW being electron cyclotron resonance heating. For the sake of including
some tolerance we will increase this to 20 MW power.
The temperature range we will be searching is [100 eV,2000 eV] with a step size of 50 eV,
n = 3.5e19 m−3, 1000 timesteps, and five repeated simulations for averaging. Given that we
are going to obtain data for a larger temperature range, we may as well also examine how the
FOLS, S value, fx value, as well as qD,max change according to temperature.

Conclusions & Outlook
12.1
Conclusions
To begin the end, let us begin by restating our goal. We wished to examine whether the new JT-
60SA experimental research reactor will be able to endure the operational parameters decided
upon, given their cooling capacity of 10-15 MW
m2 .
In the process of examining this, we ended up having to implement multiple methods for cal-
culating the power loss across the LCFS in section 5, as well as implementing multiple different
methods for the calculation of downstream projection parameters in section 8.
We then proceeded to introduce our simulation parameters, as well as some example profiles
from the simulations. From these results, it was observed that the implemented methods for
Ploss, based on the perpendicular heat flux, generally produced a higher value than simply
taking the integral of the parallel heat flux. This may indicate that the assumption about the
midplane being representative for a 60°area centred at the outer midplane may overestimate
the heat flux, and therefore not be completely true.
Downstream Projection Implementation
We then moved on to the actual implementation of the downstream method.
During this we found out that the PFOL value, and as such also DFOL value, may not neces-
sarily lie inside the exponential shape of the heat flux profile, as can be seen in figure 20. This
was due to a background heat flux being present.
We also found that the fx value can change significantly - from being ill-defined at ranges below
∼1 cm, with a value of ∼2, to peaking at a value of ∼8.5 at ∼9 cm, according to our method.
Examining the three different methods for determining the heat spreading factor S, we found
that the first diffusion-based method failed, further reinforcing the reasoning behind not redu-
cing the turbulence to an effective factor.
We also found that while the fitting method produced results multiple orders of magnitude
above the expected ranges, the numerical method did not, and resulted in values of S on the
cm scale. The exact values varied with the dataset though.
Convergence Tests
We then performed a convergence check, examining when we could start using our data based
on our input parameters, and how many timesteps were required before reaching sufficient
convergence. The numbers we found indicate an absolute minimum cut-off of 75 timesteps,
and a more reasonable timestep of 100 timesteps. Ideally, the amount of timesteps should be
higher than both, perhaps by a factor 2, but these values serve as a minimum. For the required
amount of timesteps for convergence, we found that the inclusion of at least 600 timesteps are
required. We therefore found that the total amount of timesteps to run our simulations for to
be at least 700, or ideally above this.
Temperature Sweep
We then moved on to our first large-scale data collection, in the form of a temperature sweep
from 100 eV, to 2000 eV, at a density of 3.5e19 m−3. The first obvious result was that the
simulations begin to break down around 1000 eV, for this density, and that data collection
above 1500 eV becomes increasingly difficult with the given input parameters.
With regard to Ploss it was found that the LCFS method for Ploss calculations drastically under-
estimates the total heat loss compared to the max and maxMeans methods, with the maxMeans
method being exclusively from that point on.
It was also found that the electrons actually contributed more to the perpendicular energy
transport across the LCFS than the ions at higher energies, with the ions contributing slightly
Page 65 of 85
==PAGE:71==

Outlook
more at lower energy. This is likely an artifact of the calculational method, but an interesting
result nonetheless.
It was also found that the 20 MW energy loss was present at ∼550 eV.
For the qD,max values it was found that we did not exceed the cooling limit, at least for the
line-search values, using 20 MW for the input power.
Having established this, and a point to search around, we then moved on to the parameters
PFOL and DFOL, as well as fx and S within the applicable range of 100 eV-1000 eV.
For PFOL we found that it decreased as the temperature increased, indicating that the afore-
mentioned background field may become less significant compared to the exponential profile
near the LCFS. The value at 100 eV differed depending on whether the ions and electrons were
both included, or only one of them. The overall range for PFOL was ∼3.2 cm - 2.0 cm for the
electrons only, and ∼2.2 cm - 0.8 cm for the ions only. The combined ions and electrons range
was in-between these ranges.
Meanwhile the DFOL values were rather consistent, only deviating slightly from the mean
value of ∼4.5 cm.
The S contributions curves, together with the fx curves, both showed the same behaviour and
shape as the PFOL curves. The reason for this most likely lay in their dependency on PFOL.
The S values for electrons only were found to be in the range ∼3.7 cm - 2.2 cm, and the ions in
the range ∼3.0 cm - 1.4 cm, with the combined values once more being in-between these two
ranges.
The fx values were found to be in the range ∼3.6 - 3.0 for the electrons only, and ∼3.2 - 1.7 for
the ions, with the combined once more being in-between.
These values all contributed to the shape of the qD profile sharpening at the temperature in-
creased, further increasing the qD,max value than compared to the case where a constant value,
such as the mean, had been utilised.
Grid Sweep
A grid sweep was then finally done with the (T,n)-combination of (550 eV, 3.5e19 m−3) as a
basis. From this it was found that within the searched range of 450 eV-850 eV and 3.0e19
m−3-5.0e19 m−3, we did not reach the cooling limit of 10 MW
m2 for any parameter combination
based on a heat loss of 20 MW, but we do expect to reach medium-to-high heat loads on our
divertor(s), depending on the exact configuration. This was the case for both electrons as well
as electrons & ions.
When increasing the input power to 33 MW, for testing possible future cases, it was found that
the cooling limit of 10 MW
m2 was still not reached for the electrons-only case, but that both limits
of 10 MW
m2 and 15 MW
m2 were broken when including the ions.
12.2
Outlook
Simulations
With regard to the simulations, it is evident from the radius error, that the parameters should
be double-checked in the future, as the radius of different tokamak configurations may change
when it is upgraded. Therefore all parameters should be checked before any more simulations
are performed, to ensure consistency with real-world configurations.
It should also be noted that we have not simulated using neutrals, effectively using the HESEL
code, not the nHESEL code, which may affect the results. A comparison should be made for
future simulations, to quantify the effects of this.
It could also be interesting to examine L-H transitions at the edge, now that we have a pipeline
for nHESEL simulations.

==PAGE:72==
12.2
Outlook
The line-search was also done using a brute-force method. A more efficient, and precise method,
would be to wrap the nHESEL pipeline with a root-solving algorithm. Any of the SciPy al-
gorithms should suffice for this purpose.
Cooling Limits
With regards to the cooling limits, it is evident that we did not exceed any of them for the
simulated parameter space. We did find though, that for the 33 MW case we may come close to
exceeding the 10 MW
m2 limit, and as such this should be examined further. Possible by extending
the search area.
Ploss Methods
All methods for Ploss become imprecise, especially at low temperatures, as turbulence becomes
increasingly less relevant compared to the diffusive background flow. This is a very very good
reason why the diffusion should be added to the code and calculations. At low temperatures
the results become imprecise, and it is unclear whether the diffusion correction is sufficient.
They become sufficiently accurate at any temperature above a few hundred eV, although we
remain somewhat unsure about the diffusion correction even then. If this is implemented, then
remember to change the default diffusion correction in all scripts from 1.1 to 1, as it would no
longer be needed, and is a pretty rough approximation anyway.
s(x) & R(s) methods
With regards to the s(x) and R(s) functions utilised in the downstream projection, it is evident
that the background mapping resulted in jagged functions and position inaccuracies, which
had to be smoothed out using a Gaussian filter. While this did help significantly, the drop in fx
values at low distances clearly indicates that this is not sufficient.
A better method may be to instead interpolate values for the background flux-grid, as was done
for the final grid sweep. While this may not result in exact values for the magnetic flux, it would
remove the positional inaccuracies, thereby improving the resulting data. This may even lead
to not even having to filter the data.
nHESEL Processing Code Improvements
Finally, let us talk pure code. This section is primarily intended for the future, outlining im-
provements and fixes the nHESEL processing code could benefit from, to prepare it for proper
use. While the code does work, it could work better.
Let us begin with a major improvement, that could be made. We could and should imple-
ment the diffusion into the heselProcessing object, and recalculate the Ploss as well as qD,max
values using this new method, as the 10% correction was simply an estimate used due to time
constraints. This can be implemented through a three-step process, by updating the hardcoded
internal variable dependencies, writing the calculation methods themselves, and finally adding
them to the calcVar function to make them internally callable.
We then have a single bug which I lacked the time to fix at the point of writing. This bug results
in an error when calling the same lower and upper x-indices for any variable, but should be
easily fixable. The same bug has already been fixed for the t-indices. Until it is fixed, it is
impossible to call a single x-slice for the domain. Instead one has to extract a double-width
slice, and then cut it in half.
Another improvement which could be easily made, is related to the way all the scripts currently
work. They all they use a ’paths.py’ file, located in a different folder than the script folder,
which is imported at the start of every script. This should be updated so that the paths.py
file is in the same folder as the scripts, enabling easier ’installation’ of the scripts for different
users, or always in the same location relative to the scripts folder. However, the file would still
need to be updated with the paths manually, unless a proper installation script is written. This
script would need to create the different required folders, and update the paths file, all relative
to its own location or a specified one. This could be included in a general nHESEL installer in
the future.

12.2
Outlook
Now for the biggest issue. Currently, some of the scripts are still dependent on the raw data,
instead of the processed data, due to time-constraints. While this is not a significant issue
for smaller simulation cases, it does pose a problem as the amount of raw simulation data
increases. In my case, each simulation resulted in a folder of size ∼25 GB. Multiply this by
∼1000 simulations over half a year and... You get a lot of data. This can be countered by
simply having the required storage available, but as reducing the data along just one axis can
reduce this number by a factor 512 (in my case), this is quite a significant difference, and makes
handling the raw data more cumbersome than simply reducing it.
Therefore, some time should definitely be spent ensuring complete independence from raw
the presence of raw data, as this is still checked in some scripts; instead, the scripts should
consistently refer to the processed data first in all cases. The raw data should ideally only be
for backup, in case the processed data are missing or need to be recalculated. Currently, partial
independence is implemented and tested, but full independence has yet to be tested. It may
already work, but due to time constraints this has not been examined properly. Also, due to a
lack of time for refactoring, the presence of raw data is checked in multiple different scripts.
Overall though, the foundation is there, and is ready to be built upon.
