Oﬀshore wind turbines and seismic load: Design Evaluation
by Theodoros Pardalakis
The current MSc project focuses on the design evaluation of a reference, 10MW jacket
supported oﬀshore wind turbine, that is subjected both to the conventional environ-
mental loading (wind and waves forces) and the earthquake-induced excitations. More
speciﬁcally, the main scope of this study is to investigate the numerous load cases that
are prescribed by the current state-of-practise (design codes and technical standards)
accounting at the same time, for the earthquake hazard and the relevant load cases. To
this end, site-speciﬁc parameters for the multi-hazard environment (waves and earth-
quake exposures) are appropriately chosen and the oﬀshore wind turbine is analysed
in the time domain with the use of the Aero-elastic-servo code HAWC2 accounting for
the entire set of the code-prescribed design load cases. A thorough design assessment
of the reference oﬀshore wind turbine was carried out, which revealed the govern-
ing design loads that also allowed to make a comparative evaluation of the diﬀerent
hazardous sources and their eﬀects on designing these critical infrastructures. It is
shown that earthquake loading may cause a signiﬁcant increase of moment resultant
demands for a group of diﬀerent ground motions. More speciﬁcally, it is proven that
the seismic loads are design driving for the tower structure, while the impact from
the aerodynamic damping became also apparent. As far as the blades are concerned,
although there was a slight inﬂuence by the seismic loads, it was shown that it was less
signiﬁcant. Finally, a comparison with a wide range of design load cases substantiates
the need for an accurate seismic assessment in seismic-prone areas.

1.1
Climate change and wind energy
In recent years, climate change is on the spotlight among the scientiﬁc community
and consequently the focus on new ways of controlling and eventually reducing the
greenhouse gases is revitalized, as it is considered one of the major key factors. The
heat-trapping nature of carbon dioxide and other gases was initially demonstrated in
the mid-19th century and there is no doubt that increased levels of such elements result
in what is known as global warming [40]. As it can be depicted from Figure 1.1, the
global temperature has been increasing since 1960 approaching the threshold of 1.5 ◦C,
according to the Paris agreement in 2015 [56]. The study of the future climate change
and its potential impacts has been discussed in various studies always pointing out
the importance of this phenomenon [27],[14]. The above combined with the frequent
wildﬁres, drought and high electricity demand, result in an steadily increasing demand
for more and better energy infrastructures.

By identifying the possible eﬀects of climate change and thus, the need for change
regarding the current energy practices, polices towards averting the aforementioned

(like sun and wind) and the reduced use of energy from fossil fuels, in order to limit
the emission leading up to the global warming (ﬁg. 1.2), [57].

Nowadays, several energy solutions based on the use of renewable sources such as
solar, hydro, geothermal and wind, are implemented. A favorable trend towards the
renewables is observed as both the worldwide investments and the scientiﬁc interest
regarding the alternative energy sources, has increased signiﬁcantly. This results in a
better understanding and an improved eﬃciency of the renewable based energy tech-
nologies [58]. In light of that, wind energy seems to be one of the most promising
energy sources which can be also understood by the steady increase in installed ca-
pacity worldwide (ﬁg. 1.3). Starting with a rather shy presence back in 2001 with
only 23,900 MW and following a gently increase until 2006 with 73,957 MW, the ﬁrst
turning point appears in 2007, with an increase of 64 %. From then on wind energy
follows a steady increase until 2017 when the wind energy reaches 539, 581MW in
global installed capacity.
It is also worth mentioning, that many of the previous wind turbines now reach their
limit of operational lifetime (20-25 years [20]) and new strategies for their replace-
ment start to take place, which can eventually lead to an even more optimized wind-
harvesting for the next generation turbines [43]. However, during its lifespan, a wind
turbine can produce 47 times more energy than it consumes [11], a fact that makes it
even more appealing as a concrete alternative against the fossil-fuels power plants.

1.3
Background
Wind energy is a rather demanding topic -from a structural point of view- which also
makes it an expensive investment with a risk of failure that can turn the whole project
unproﬁtable. Prior to the further investigation on the previous studies, it is essential
to introduce the reader to the basic principles of the structural identity of a wind
turbine and its response under a seismic event.
The wind turbine constitutes a high-rise and slender structural system with a con-
centrated mass on the top, hence the lateral forces and deformations due to wind and
seismic excitations can be proved critical for such a structure. However, the support
structure indicates a long vibration period (e.g. 1.5-12 s) which lands on the low
intensity part of strong earthquake motions (ﬁg. 1.5).

Therefore, the structural behavior of such a system can be characterized as "earthquake-
proof" from the most destructive part of earthquake excitations. Furthermore, the
increased damping during operation (up to 5% of the critical damping [31]) can further
contribute to the dissipation of excessive vibrations due to ground motions. It should
be noted, however, that such damping levels can be observed only during the operation
of the wind turbine, as in parked conditions the damping is limited to approximately
1% due to lack of aerodynamic damping.
On the other hand, a cantilever-type conﬁguration -such is a wind turbine- lacks in
terms of signiﬁcant redundancy, hence the mechanism for the force distribution which
in turn contributes to the relief of the high stressed parts of the structure, is acutely
limited.
This can result in local damages in critical areas of a wind turbine (i.e.
tower, foundation) due to seismic excitations, which inevitably can compromise the
structural integrity as a whole. The latter, in combination with the steady increase
in wind energy investments, in seismic prone areas, raise the interest and concern
regarding the understanding of this interaction (wind, wave and earthquake loads).
1.4
State of the art
The seismic loads are usually assessed either via response spectra or by the time-
history representation of ground excitations. The former is associated with the fre-
quency domain analysis (FDA), while the latter with the time history analysis (THA).
The THA is considered as a more analytical and accurate method due to the fact
that the structural behavior can be simulated with respect to every time specimen1.
Additionally, it enables the ability to use the calculated structural and aerodynamic
damping. In contradiction, the FDA lacks the ability of a proper representation of the
system’s damping as it is deﬁned based on the response spectrum, which is usually

Moreover, another important factor for the evaluation is the magnitude of the impact
that a seismic load can have on a wind turbine. Based on diﬀerent studies, this is
closely associated with the actual size of the turbine used in the simulation procedure.
Indeed, a preliminary study on a 38m tower has indicated that the hydrodynamic and
aerodynamic loads are more critical than the earthquake induced ones in less seismic-
prone areas [8]. However, the fact that the investigated model is rather small compared
to the actual size of a wind turbine, can lead to diﬀerent conclusions.
The need for a better understanding of the seismic loads acting on wind turbines is
increasing, especially due to the fact that new wind farms start to appear in seismic
prone areas [54]. Remaining in that frame, a proper investigation on how to capture
all the loads acting on wind turbines is important, due to the lack of building codes
provisions for wind turbines [21]. This includes also the lack of information in terms
of design against seismic loading. In respect to that, the simulations are continuously
improving in terms of accuracy especially under the prism of a THA.
Furthermore, in order to simulate the dynamic and structural behavior of a wind
turbine in a more realistic way it is essential to understand the inﬂuence that the
load coupling during an extreme event can possibly have to the whole response of the
structure. For instance, in Asareh et al. [6], the 5 MW NREL reference turbine was
analyzed under the prism of twenty two diﬀerent ground motions [29]. As the main
scope was to investigate the tower response, it was observed that above 20m height,
the tower starts to show respond in a nonlinear manner. In addition, the importance
of an earthquake motion to the structural demand of the wind turbine has been also
highlighted through an extensive experimental study of 99 earthquake motions and
various turbines and operational conditions which was conducted from the University
of California at San Diego [16].
Once more, it became evident, that due to the
complexity of the wind turbine structure, it is necessary to advice diﬀerent guidelines,
for the design decisions than the building codes, which still lack the introduction of
such type of structures. Relevant guidelines are provided by Det Norske Veritas and
Germanischer Lloyd (DNV-GL), according to which it is required the wind turbines
to remain elastic and sustain no damage during a seismic event [17]. This comes in
contradiction to the standard building codes, where the structure is designed in a way
that the earthquake energy is dissipated in speciﬁc areas of the structure through large
inelastic deformation. In order to have an even more realistic representation of the
structural response, a fully developed Finite Element (FE) model is considered to be
a more accurate approach than simpliﬁed models that simulate the blades as lumped
masses. Adding to this, a time domain analysis on a 70m tall wind turbine excited
by an actual strong ground motion showed that the results between the two models

loads the design driving ones. The impact of the foundation type was also stated,

were also arised from studies on the 5MW NREL reference wind turbine [47], [46].
Further investigation has also been made from Smith et al. [52], where the design
strategy was assessed, for a case where the seismic loads are combined with wind
and operational loads.

either were coincided or were closely-spaced, which in return resulted in instabilities.
For that reason, an analysis placed in a similar domain, should also investigate the
operational frequencies in respect to the ground motion characteristics.

al. [31], where the tower base moment and the tower top displacement were increased

again the 5MW NREL reference wind turbine, while the whole analysis was conducted
through the HAWC2 servo-elastic code. Moreover, a fragility analysis was also con-
ducted in order to further justify the importance of the seismic loading to the design
and assessment of the wind turbine. Similar conclusions were also obtained from Mar-

1.5
State of practise
As it has already stated, wind energy is a ﬁeld that attracts steadily increased interest
among diﬀerent countries which has placed wind technology in the focus of the research
community. However, more knowledge and research is required, in order to be able to
comprehend the behavior of the wind turbines in both structural and design terms.
Moreover, the fact that wind energy investments are increasing in seismic prone areas,
a framework for the design procedures of wind turbines is essential. Consequently, in
the following lines an analytical overview of the existent regulatory frame, both codes
and standards, is being made.
1.5.1
Current regulatory framework
The European Commission, aiming to face the common structural challenges among
the infrastructure, conducted the structural Eurocodes and more speciﬁcally the Eu-
rocode 8 (EN 1998-1) [18] which refers to rules regarding earthquake-resistant design
of buildings. Approved also by the European Committee for Standardization (CEN),
it contains the common framework for Europe, in terms of structural design. Addi-
tionally, the Eurocode 8 introduces Frequency Domain Analysis (FDA) methods, but
the special structural response of a wind turbine due to the nature of the environ-
mental loads and the interaction of the structural members and their corresponded
frequencies (eg. tower, blades, control system), makes wind turbines diﬃcult to be
modelled under that analysis frame.
Moreover, the International Organization of
Standardization (ISO) provides prescriptions for oﬀshore structures in the oil and gas
industry (ISO 19901-2) [26]. Finally, under the prism of general structural design
codes, the International Building Code (IBC [25]) and its reference to ASCE 7-10
[5], are also applied in cases of earthquake-resistant design, mainly in U.S.A. as the
reference building code.
On the other hand, wind turbines are not included among the diﬀerent structures
in the Eurocode 8 and their special structural response, as well as the nature of the
environmental loads, are diﬃcult to be deﬁned. With that in mind, the International
Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), released in 2005 the International Standard for
wind turbine design requirements. Since 2005, several revisions and amendments have
been made until the ﬁnal editions for both onshore (IEC 61400-1) and oﬀshore (IEC
61400-3) conditions wind turbines [15], [12]. Currently, the IEC code requires that in
seismic prone areas the earthquake loading should be combined with the environmental
loads (wind and wave), which is also a reason why it is the most widely recognized
set of standards among Europe and U.S.A. It further states that seismic loads can
be calculated in either the time domain or frequency domain and must include a
suﬃcient number of modes for the analysis. In respect to the wind turbines they

Finally, DNV-GL is another provider of frameworks and guidelines and more speciﬁ-

included as part of the load calculation. It also includes earthquakes among diﬀerent
environmental conditions which must be considered for design, while the design loads
are once again calculated based on superposition of the individual loads. A selection
of design load cases are provided for that reason. On the other hand, this code also
fails to provide the appropriate methods for seismic analysis on turbines, while all
the aforementioned requirements, similar to the IEC standards, apply only to regions
with seismic activity. However, several wind farms are located in regions that there is
no immediate risk from earthquakes, but could sustain signiﬁcant damage if a seismic
event occurred.
To sum up, it can depicted that there is lack of prescriptions regarding the earthquake
assessment in wind turbines and the interference that a seismic action can induce to
the structural design and is clear that more guidelines are needed to be conducted in
order to properly simulate a multi-hazard environment and the corresponding coupling
among the diﬀerent loads in order to obtain accurate design loads. More speciﬁcally,
the challenges as these were resulted from above, can be summarized in the following:
• Many codes that are currently advised are not developed explicitly for wind
turbines, but for simpler and more conventional structures.
• Wind turbine design incorporates diﬀerent challenges that are not usually faced
by simpler building structures. Such challenges can include local buckling of
the tower and dynamic resonance under both seismic and operation loading.
Therefore, appropriate guidelines should be provided in order to adequately
represent a wind turbine under an environmental and seismic hazard.
• The design evaluation of a wind turbine under a multi hazard environment is
still to be fully investigated, while in combination with the lack of appropriate
guidelines, the appropriate design assessment under seismic action turns the
calculation of design loads in a topic with increased research interest.
According to the IEC 61400 when a Time History Analysis is implemented, it is
essential to include a wide range of time-history simulations. Finally, a combination
of the IEC 61400 and Eurocode 8 under the time domain is achieved along the lines
of this project.

Chapter 3
Aero-servo-elastic simulation
The response analysis of the wind turbine which is imposed to a multi-hazard envi-
ronment including wind, wave and earthquake loads, will be based on a time-history
analysis, as this has been already justiﬁed in terms of reliability in Chapter 1. The
entire analysis will be handled by using the HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine
Code, 2nd generation) aero-servo-eastic code, which was fully developed by DTU
Wind Energy Department.
3.1
HAWC2 - Aero-servo-elastic code
The term aero-servo-elastic stands for the aerolasticity theory that HAWC2 is based
in order to conduct time history analyses on wind turbines. The HAWC2 framework
manages to couple all the diﬀerent loads that are acting on a wind turbine. More
speciﬁcally, the synergy between the elastic and inertia forces with the aerodynamic
forces can be fully integrated through HAWC2. However, additional loads that can
be emerged in an oﬀshore environment such as the wakes in a wind farm, turbine
control operations and extreme events like earthquakes, can be further complicate the
coupled loading system. HAWC2 is able to perform a time history analysis by taking
into account the entire loading interaction of a multi-hazard environment in a precise
and reﬁned manner.
3.1.1
Multi-body dynamics theory
Multibody system dynamics (MBD) is an important aspect of computational dynam-
ics a topic which in general deals with kinematics and dynamics of rigid and ﬂexible
systems, ﬁnite elements methods etc. including also non-linear dynamics approaches.
Applying this theory on a wind turbine, HAWC2 discretizes the entire structure in
individual and independent ﬂexible bodies which are coupled together oﬀering an in-
creased accuracy in the response analysis. The coupling between the diﬀerent bodies
is achieved by applying constraints which can either ﬁx any relative position between
the connected bodies or restrain speciﬁc degrees of freedom (DOFs). By introduc-
ing such constraints it is possible to simulate the overall motion of the system which
eventually can be separated in rigid and elastic motions. Regarding the former one,
the increased motions due to the blades’ rotation, can easily be coupled with the
corresponding elastic deformation due to the imposed loads.
To sum up, in order to build the structural model of the DTU 10MW RWT turbine,
the following main bodies are introduced.

Scheme
In the following chapter an analytical description is made on the basis of the selected
Design Load Cases (DLCs) and the reasons why these were selected. Additionally,
a comprehensive description is being made regarding the calculation of the design
loads according to the Standards prescriptions (IEC 61400, EN 2008-1). Finally, the
structure of the analysis scheme is described with a detailed depiction of each load
scenario.
The diﬀerent load scenarios exist under diﬀerent conditions (e.g. normal production
without faults, parked state, emergency shut down etc.) and they are decomposed
into diﬀerent load cases, DLCs, each of them with diﬀerent characteristics.
For
convenience, for the rest of the study, each load scenario will be denoted as "DLC
#x" and each load case as "DLC xx".
5.1
DLCs Description and Selection
5.1.1
Selection
As the scope of the current project is the design evaluation of a wind turbine, the
ﬁrs step is to decide the corresponding DLCs for the analysis. In the latest draft of
IEC, there is a new reference, in which two diﬀerent DLCs are presented, where the
earthquake is for the ﬁrst time introduced as a part of the analysis with a respective
DLC.
The ﬁnal selection of the diﬀerent DLCs was decided based on a comprehensive study
which aims to identify the most critical DLCs that may be aﬀected due to the ad-
ditional seismic loading. Based on the earthquake assessment as this refers to the
last revised version of EN-61400, the earthquake loading shall be superposed with the
operational loading which results as the higher among the mean loads during normal
power production, loads during emergency stop and during idling or parked condition.
Based on the principle that the total analysis shall be based on design demands, the
ﬁnal seismic assessment will take into consideration the corresponding scenarios, DLC
#1, #5 and #6.
Regarding the parked condition (DLC #6), it is expected to result in increased struc-
tural responses due to the lack of aerodynamic damping and hence it is of high interest.
==PAGE:42==
32
Chapter 5. Design Load Cases and Analysis Scheme
More speciﬁcally, if the top of the tower experiences a perturbation in the wind direc-
tion, the out-of-plane velocity component of a blade section will be reduced according
to the BEM theory. This will further decrease the Angle of Attack (AoA), resulting in
a reduction of the lift and drag forces. Correspondingly, the thrust force is reduced,
acting as a resistance to the downwind motion. In addition, when the tower top per-
turbation is against the wind direction, the AoA and the thrust force will increase, but
it will still behave as a resistance force to the initial disturbance. To sum up, when
the airfoil is stalled, meaning the blade is stalled, the aerodynamic damping can be
lower or even negative which consequently leads to an additional energy to the tower,
meaning increased loading [34]. Therefore, in order to ensure that the wind turbine is
under the design prerequisites, seismic excitations will also be applied for the parked
state i.e. DLC #6.
Moreover, according to the latest version of the IEC-61400 the DLCs in which the
earthquake assessment is referred, only two are mentioned. DLC 1.8 and DLC 6.7
are both refer to a situation in which seismic event occurs in parallel with a grid loss.
Although both of the cases refer to Normal turbulence, the former is related to normal
power production, while the later to a parked state (standing still or idling). Based on
the preliminary assessment of the investigation of critical DLCs, there are indications
for higher moment demands in the situations where ETM has been implemented [61].
For instance, DLC 1.3 seem to be more demanding in terms of loading response than
the corresponding DLC 1.6. This is mainly due to the increased turbulence model.
It should also be noted, that these indications are based on studies that take into
consideration only the wind and wave loads.
Due to lack of research in this ﬁeld, DLC #2 and DLC 7.1 will additionally be in-
vestigated as the ﬁrst one refers to power production during special conditions (e.g.
extreme pitch) and the second one is related again to parked condition. During power
production, it is possible to have occurrence of fault which can lead to increased mo-
ment demands. More speciﬁcally, situations where there is extreme pitching in one
of the blades, while the remaining two are still operating can have an impact on the
overall structural response. Moreover, when there is a failure in the turbine safety
system, which may occur abnormal large yaw error, the resulting moments at the
jacket mudline and the tower bottom are increased and comparable with the corre-
sponding ones from DLC #1. To sum up, a representation of the selection structure
is illustrated in Figure 5.1.

5.2.2
Acceleration thresholds
First and foremost, it should be stated that during the investigation of the analysis
framework, it has been observed that the introduction of a strong ground motion
can result in increased accelerations at the tower top which is also captured by the
control system.
However, the controller has been tuned based on an acceleration
ﬁlter which takes into account the acceleration at every time instant and compares
it with a predeﬁned threshold that is usually set by the manufacturer. Due to lack
of actual data from the industry, this threshold has been deﬁned equal to 1.8m/s2,
which represents a realistic value as well. If the reference value of the acceleration is
over-passed, then the control system triggers the turbine into emergency shutdown.
However, for the scope of this study, two diﬀerent thresholds have been applied, one
that corresponds to the actual level and one that is increased to 15m/s2, in order to
investigate the resulted time-series of the structural response. This is done because
when the emergency system is active, the time-series reaches zero values, as the real
representation of the behavior after the emergency incident cannot be fully captured.
In addition, a comparison is made both between the two thresholds and with the
corresponding analyses that do not include seismic actions, in order to clarify the
eﬀect of such diﬀerence in threshold and if the ﬁnal design loads are aﬀected.
Controller
The DTU 10MW wind turbine was controlled by variable speed pitch regulated con-
troller, which was implemented in the basic DTU wind energy controller [39]. In order
to avoid obtaining the wrong conclusion from the transitional period in the simula-
tion, transition response should not be considered. In this study, the ﬁrst 100s was
regarded as the transition period and were discarded.

Bending moments in power production with no occurrence of

Beginning in a similar way to the corresponding study of the tower, in ﬁgure 6.24 are

the ﬂapwise and edgewise moments ﬂuctuate around zero in a similar to sinusoidal
patter. Justiﬁed by the fact that the emergency shutdown is triggered, which turns
the blades into idling mode and as a result is a minimum loading acting to the blades.
Foremost, both the ﬂap and edge wise moments are not signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the
seismic action. This was expected based on previous studies on relevant matter [23],
[53].
Similarly to the above, in ﬁgure 6.25 the corresponding responses are illustrated. This
time, the DLC 1.4 is under investigation, which can result to increased blade moments
as the wind proﬁle is deﬁned also by extreme coherent gust with direction change [41].
In respect to the above, both ﬂapwise and edgewise moments seem to not get aﬀected
by the earthquake, while the former ones remain bigger than the latter. This was
expected, because in the edgewise direction the gravity loads are the governing ones
which are rather smaller that the corresponding ones that act on the ﬂap of the
blade. Furthermore, this time, in the case where normal threshold is applied, both
responses (ﬂapwise and edgewise) seem to increase their base value of ﬂuctuation
after approximately 300s.

the aerodynamic damping, which here may become negative and potentially lead to
aerodynamic and structural instabilities3 [23].

Conclusions and Further Research
7.1
Conclusions
The main purpose of this project was to conduct a design evaluation on a refer-
ence oﬀshore wind turbine under a multi-hazard environment and to investigate the
earthquake impact on key design parameters in order to obtain a general framework
regarding critical design loads cases.
The design evaluation was based upon a comprehensive study on a number of dif-
ferent load cases according to the latest state of practise. The diﬀerent load cases
considered, aim to capture a wide range of a wind turbine’s operational states. More
speciﬁcally,the normal power production, the power production with occurrence of
fault, the parked state with and without fault conditions and the emergency shut
down scenarios were analyzed.
In terms of the reference models used in this study, the modiﬁed InnWind reference
jacket and the DTU 10MW wind turbine were considered.
The simulations were
carried out by means of the aero-servo-elastic code HAWC2, while the entire analysis
was conducted in the time domain.
This approach, even though computationally
laborious, captures realistically the structural response and its interaction with the
ground motion. Additionally, the inﬂuence of a seismic action to the overall structural
response was investigated thoroughly through various response time-series for the
tower, the blades and the foundation.
As far as the tower is concerned, it became apparent that a seismic action can have
a great impact on the moment response. The tower bottom was considered as the
critical design point and the simulations carried out revealed a rapid increase of the
peak response when the seismic action occurred. Moreover, the diﬀerences between
operational and parked state were highlighted and the contribution of the aerody-
namic damping was identiﬁed. The dynamic response, in terms of accelerations and
displacements, was demonstrated through the sensitivity analysis, which pointed out
the great importance of the diﬀerent characteristics in terms of both seismological and
strong ground motion parameters. Finally, the extreme accelerations and how these
can trigger the turbine into emergency shut down were also demonstrated based on
the diﬀerent ground motions.
With reference to the blades, based on the time history assessment, the blade root
moments were not greatly aﬀected by the initiation of the seismic action. By conduct-
ing an analysis on both the operational and parked states the conclusion was reached,
that the seismic action is dissipated almost completely before it starts aﬀecting the
==PAGE:75==
7.2. Further Research
65
blades. From a structural point of view, the blades have been proved to withstand
the seismic loading.
In relation to the jacket foundation, the analysis of the axial forces indicated a steep
increase in the ﬁnal response which in turn can be translated in an increased over-
turning moment at the seabed. However, the soil interaction has not been taken into
account which can increase the level of uncertainty of such an analysis.
The second part of the present study involves the calculation of the design loads for
both the tower and the blades, in order to identify the critical design load cases. Dur-
ing normal power production it was shown that, DLC 1.8 is the most critical for the
tower, while for power production with fault conditions, DLC 2.1 was the governing
one. In addition, when the turbine was in parked state, DLC 6.2 was identiﬁed as the
most critical. However, when the parked state was combined with fault conditions,
DLC 7.1 was proven to be the design driving case, while in the situation of an emer-
gency shutdown, described by DLC 5.1, the design loads govern the entire spectrum.
On the other hand, the blades obtained the maximum responses during DLC 1.4,
2.1 and 6.2 for the corresponding scenarios, but DLC 6.2 was identiﬁed as the most
critical among all.
To conclude, this study showed that the integration of seismic action to the design
evaluation of a wind turbine can be considered as design driving for the tower, how-
ever, the same does not hold true for the blades.
Undoubtedly, there are further
developments that can be implemented during the design evaluation process, but the
variety of critical design load cases that were highlighted in the current thesis, aims
to provide a guidance for a preliminary design and optimization.
7.2
Further Research
As far as the diﬀerent topics presented in the current study are concerned, some indi-
vidual aspects have had to be disregarded, considering the need to align the research
in a speciﬁc direction. Nonetheless, in order to capture the general scheme, some
thoughts on future work will be presented in the following lines.
In the manner of the diﬀerent design load cases investigated herein, the probabilities
among the diﬀerent extreme events and operational states have not been taken into
account. Thereafter, it would be of great interest to apply the design evaluation into
a probabilistic framework, in order to adequately capture the possibility of occurring
of each extreme event and operational state.
As it has already been addressed, the jacket foundation was considered to be fully
ﬁxed at the seabed with a rigid foundation.
However, this approach neglects the
soil-structure-interaction which can potentially have impact on the seismic response
in terms of moment and axial force demand. In addition, the jacket foundation was
investigated in terms of axial force response but both the dynamic and structural
analyses did not reach their full potential. A further investigation on the structural
response spectra could lead to interesting observations regarding potential resonances
as well as the calculation of design loads in terms of overturning moment at the base
of foundation can lead to new design aspects.
In several occasions during this study it has been addressed that wind turbines are
complex structures and that should be treated in a similar way. More speciﬁcally, the
==PAGE:76==
66
Chapter 7. Conclusions and Further Research
diﬀerent structural members can dynamically interact which may result to instabilities
and failures during operation. Although the focus on this study has been focused on
the structural response of the turbine, the dynamic behavior during a seismic event
is also an interesting aspect. Higher modes may be activated which can potentially
aﬀect the structural integrity of the turbine as a whole. It is worth mentioning, that
a more thorough analysis on the moment response, in sections closer to the tip of the
blade, may lead to important observations regarding the actual impact of the seismic
event on the blade’s structural and dynamic response.
As a ﬁnal note, it should be addressed that fatigue considerations as well as ageing and
deterioration of the wind turbine, could potentially evolve new design requirements,
while the impact of seismic events on that matter is yet to be investigated.
