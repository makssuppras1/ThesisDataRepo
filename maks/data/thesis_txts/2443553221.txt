Abstract
Evaluation of the meteorological conditions leading to high vertical wind
shear under oﬀshore conditions
Preliminary analyses have shown an increase of the frequency of high vertical wind shears
at the US East coast when compared to those observed at the North Sea. The aim of
this study is to analyze the high vertical wind shear events in the area where one of
the ﬁrst US large-scale oﬀshore wind farms will be installed. The analysis of those high
wind shears events is performed in terms of their frequency, including an analysis on the
meteorological conditions that leads into their formation. The possible impact on the
future wind farms located on that area is also analyzed. Lidar and other meteorolog-
ical measurements at the Air-Sea Interaction Tower (ASIT), which is located at 3 km
South of Martha’s Vineyard Island, were used in addition to nearby buoy measurements
and ERA5 reanalysis data. ASIT measurements showed that high vertical wind shears
were observed for South-Westerly winds within a limited range of positive bulk Richard-
son numbers 0 - 0.1. Buoy measurements and ERA5 data showed that most of these
events occurred under relatively homogeneous South-Westerly winds along the US East
coast. Such conditions are the result of the presence of a strong high pressure system
(Bermuda-Azores high) over the North Atlantic basin and low pressures over the land
at the East of the US, which result in warm winds from lower latitudes ﬂowing over the
cold water of the Labrador current. The eﬀect of high vertical wind shear on the power
density is not large (1 % lower than using the wind speed at hub height). However, the
high wind shears might have a stronger eﬀect on the loads of the turbines.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During the last decades renewable energies have shown a high development, boosted
because of the need for producing energy with a low impact on the environment. They
have also demonstrated to be competitive with conventional fossil based electricity gen-
eration (Stolpe et al., 2014). Among renewable energy sources, wind energy is the one
with the highest global installed capacity with 539 GW at the end of 2017, being China
(188 GW), US (89 GW) and Germany (56G W) the three countries contributing the
most (GWEC, 2018).
Oﬀshore wind energy in particular has shown a high growth during the last years with a
cumulative rate of installed capacity of 2.4 GW/year for the period 2012-2017 (EWEA,
2014; GWEC, 2018). Nevertheless, it only represents 3.4 % of the global wind energy
capacity (18 GW) (GWEC, 2018). Although there is a relatively low installed capacity
of oﬀshore wind energy, when compared to onshore wind energy, it is expected that
the former will have a more important role in the next decade (GWEC, 2018). This is
because oﬀshore wind farms generally produce more energy than onshore wind farms of
the same capacity due to their exposition to higher wind resources, presenting capacity
factors higher than 35% in many cases (Rodrigues et al., 2015).
Further, the trend
of new oﬀshore wind farms point towards wind farms with a higher average installed
capacity than previous wind farm projects, this capacity increased from 337.9 MW in
2015 (EWEA, 2016) to 493 MW in 2017 (WindEurope, 2018). Also, in general, fu-
ture wind farms, which are under development, are planned to be located at a farther
distance from the coast and installed in deeper waters than the current wind farms,
where generally wind resources are higher (WindEurope, 2018; Rodrigues et al., 2015).
Finally, the development of the wind industry has reduced the levelized cost of energy

of wind farms (IRENA, 2018) from 140 €/MWh to 100 €/MWh for the period 2015-2017.
Most of the oﬀshore wind farms are located in Europe, speciﬁcally in the North Sea with
the UK, Germany and Denmark leading the capacity installed (GWEC, 2018). Despite
the high installed capacity of wind farms in the US, there is currently only one opera-
tive oﬀshore wind farm located in the Rhode Island State. This oﬀshore demonstration
project started in 2016 and is composed by 5 wind turbines of 6 MW. Recently, some
States of the US East coast have deﬁned targets for the installation of future oﬀshore
wind farms, accounting for approximately 8.4 GW of total capacity installed (GWEC,
2018). Among those states are the Rhode Island and Massachusetts; both have released
two big commercial Wind Energy Areas (WEAs) for the development of wind farms.
One is located in the state of Massachusetts and the other one is shared between both
states. Both WEAs are located at the South-West of Marta’s Vineyard island. In the
case of the WEA at Massachusetts, a wind farm of 800 MW is under planning stage
(BOEM., 2018) and is expected to be one of the ﬁrst large-scale oﬀshore wind farms in
the US.
Before the development of a wind farm project, it is necessary to perform an accurate
estimation of the energy that will be produced by the wind farm. As stated on the ﬁrst
version of the IEC 61400 standard (IEC, 2005), the traditional method used for mea-
suring the wind resources for a wind project involves the installation of a meteorological
(met) mast where wind measurements at hub height are collected. Current wind turbines
can have hub heights larger than 100 m, therefore collecting wind measurements using a
met-mast has become a more diﬃcult task due to the cost and technical restrictions of
installing a tall tower. Therefore, wind measurements collected using met-masts higher
than 100 m are not very common (Newsom et al., 2015; Mikkelsen, 2014; Kim et al.,
2016). This situation is even more extreme under oﬀshore conditions where prices for
met-mast installations are higher, making those measurement even more scarce.
Since the last decade, ground-based proﬁling lidars have started to be used in wind
energy (Pe˜na et al., 2015; Kang et al., 2017; Rehman et al., 2018). Detailed measure-
ments of wind speed and wind direction at diﬀerent heights over the swept rotor area
can be obtained with these instruments and are proven to be an alternative to the use of
met-masts. One of the ﬁrst sources of oﬀshore wind measurements in the US East coast
corresponds to lidar measurements collected in the Air-Sea Interaction Tower (ASIT)
(AWS-Truepower, 2017) (see details of these measurements in chapter 3). This is an
important source of information for the understanding of the wind resources that could

be present in the WEAs.
The energy produced for a wind turbine depends on kinetic energy ﬂux which exists
over the whole rotor area (Wagner et al., 2011).
Since some current wind turbines
have rotor diameters of 160m, wind speed at hub height could not be representative of
the kinetic energy ﬂux over the whole rotor area. Factors like horizontal and vertical
variation of wind speed (wind shear), vertical variation in wind direction (veer) and fast
wind speed variations (turbulence), inﬂuence the performance of wind turbines. The
deviation between the expected and real power production has been shown to be related
to the above factors (Wagner et al., 2011; Albers et al., 2007; Morten et al., 2015; Morten
and Roar, 2017). Among them, wind shear is one of the most relevant (Wagner et al.,
2010b). Due to its importance, the estimation of the Rotor Equivalent Wind Speed
(REWS) was included on the new version of the IEC 61400-12-2017 to consider the
wind shear eﬀect into the power performance measurement (IEC, 2017).
Previous studies have shown that high wind shears at the area close to the WEAs occurs
with a higher frequency than those observed at the North sea (Gandoin, 2018). Con-
sidering the high capacity of wind farms which are under design in this new area, it is
important to take into account this phenomenon in order to make an accurate estimation
of their energy production. This study aims at investigating the characteristics of the
vertical wind shears present on the US East coast, particularly for the ASIT location.
In addition, this study tries to ﬁnd the conditions on when high wind shear events occur
and the reason why. This study is divided in the next chapters: Chapter 2 describes
backgrounds and method including a description of the study area. Chapter 3 describes
the data used in this study. Chapter 4 includes the pre-procesing applied to the data
sets used. Chapter 5 shows the results of the study. Chapter 6 includes a discussion for
the main ﬁndings as well as some considerations for future activities at the study area.
Finally Chapter 7 presents the main conclusions.

In terms of atmospheric stability, three states can be identiﬁed: Unstable, Stable and

eddies, vertical motion is increased and under these conditions, the air close to the sur-
face, is at a higher temperature than the air at higher layers. Finally, neutral conditions
are known for presenting an adiabatic atmospheric layer, with no convection, and the
vertical motion is neither enhanced nor suppressed (Archer et al., 2016).

Wind lidar measurements
A pulsed Windcube V2 was installed at ASIT. This type of lidar has been used in many
studies related to wind energy (Matthew et al., 2012; Giyanani et al., 2015; Wagner
et al., 2010a; Kim et al., 2016; Morten and Roar, 2017; Gryning et al., 2017; Courtney
and Hasager, 2016). It measures wind speed, wind direction and vertical wind speed
(w) from 40 up to 200 meters of height over 12 simultaneous heights.
Wind lidars emit a beam of light into the atmosphere. The light is scattered by the
aerosols in the air. The lidar measures the Doppler eﬀect of the backscattered light,
which is proportional to the wind speed projected in the beam direction (Vr).
In the case of a Windcube, the light is emitted in pulses with a duration of some hun-
dreds of nanoseconds. These pulses are emitted upwards along a cone, with a cone angle
γ as shown in ﬁgure 2.5. Four beams are produced in order to be able to reconstruct the
horizontal and vertical components of the wind speed (u, v and w). In the particular
case of Windcube V2, a ﬁfth beam is emitted to the atmosphere perpendicularly.
The wind measurements are limited to the strength of the signal detected from the pho-
todetector of the WindCube. If the signal is lower than the threshold deﬁned by the
developer, then no data is collected. A measurement of the strength of this signal is the
Carrier to Noise Ratio (CNR), which is the ratio between the mean radio frequency sig-
nal power to the mean noise power (Suomi et al., 2017; Matthew et al., 2012). The main
factors that aﬀect the CNR are the atmospheric relative turbulence, relative humidity,
aerosol backscatter and precipitation (Suomi et al., 2017; Wagner et al., 2011; Matthew
et al., 2012). In order to optimize the wind measurements along the range of height of
interest, the focus distance of the Windcube can be adjusted. Normally the height of
interest is coinciding with the hub height of the current wind turbines.
Each 10 minute average wind measurements recorded by the Windcube is associated
with a percentage of availability, which is a function of the values of CNR obtained
during this period. In this study, wind measurements with 100 % of availability will
be considered, which are obtained when the CNR for all measurements during the 10

2.8
Low Level Jets
Low Level Jets (LLJ) are characterized by a high wind shear in the lower part of the
surface boundary layer and a maximum in the wind speed proﬁle between 100-500 m
(Baas et al. 2009). This events have been studied mostly over land, where nocturnal
LLJ are common to occur under stable condition.
The mechanism for the LLJ formation is detailed by Emeis. (2013). This mechanism
is characterized by a sudden transition of a ﬂow from unstable to stable atmospheric
==PAGE:25==
Background and methods
15
conditions. The high wind shear underneath the LLJ depends on the vertical tempera-
ture gradient of the sub-jet layer and is reached when the ﬂow is under a large synoptic
pressure gradient (Emeis. 2017). The change from unstable to stable conditions exper-
imented by the ﬂow, leads into an increment in the wind speed because the frictional
force is reduced. Under this conditions, the wind is mainly under the inﬂuence of the
pressure-gradient and Coriolis forces. Figure 2.6 (left top and bottom) shows an example
of the balance forces during the LLJ formation. In ﬁgure 2.6 (left top) the balance of
forces when the ﬂow is under stable atmospheric conditions can be observed. Under this
conditions the frictional velocity acts in the opposite direction to the resultant force.
Once the ﬂow is under stable conditions (ﬁgure 2.6 left bottom) the friction force is re-
duced, which leads into an increment in wind speed and a turning in the wind direction.
Figure 2.6 (right) shows the suitable synoptic conditions for the LLJ formation, which
are normally observed at the edges of a high-pressure system (Emeis. 2013).
Figure 2.6: Left top: Balance forces before the LLJ formation. Left bottom: Balance
forces during the LLJ event. P, C and F are the pressure-gradient, Coriolis and friction
forces respectively.
L and H represents the low and high pressure systems and the
thin lines are isobars.
Right: Favorable synoptic conditions for the LLJ formation
represented by the shaded area. Source: Emeis. (2017).
In this study a LLJ analysis for the ﬁrst 200 meters of height was carried out using the
methodology deﬁned by Baas et al. (2009). Under this methodology a LLJ is deﬁned as
the lowest maximum observation in the wind speed proﬁle that is at least both 2 ms−1
and 25% faster than the next minimum above (ﬁgure 2.7). This methodology classiﬁes
a wind proﬁle as a LLJ if the wind proﬁles observed in the last and next 30 minutes
satisfy the criteria for a LLJ as well. An additional criteria was included in this study,
which states that the lowest maximum has to be higher than 6 ms−1 in order to avoid
LLJ of low wind speeds.

ERA5 reanalysis
Synoptic scales is a term used in meteorology referring to weather systems in ranges from
hundreds of kilometers to several thousands of kilometers (AMS. 2018), normally local
conditions are aﬀected by synoptic variations. Synoptic conditions can be represented
by reanalysis which corresponds to global models of several atmospheric and ocean vari-
ables. These models are made using data collected from several sources such as satellite
images, radiosonde and weather stations. These measurements are used together with
Numerical Weather Prediction models for simulating continuous information in time and
space of several atmospheric variables (Bailey. 2016). Reanalysis data are characterized
by covering long time periods and representing extended areas, and they have been used
in several studies of wind energy (Rose and Apt., 2015; Kim et al., 2018; Torralba et al.,
2017).
In this study synoptic conditions were studied using the reanalysis ERA5 (C3S 2018).
ERA5 was developed for the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts
(ECMWF) and it was released in 2017. This dataset corresponds to a new generation

4.1
Completing time series of Water Temperature at ASIT
Twater is one of the variables with the highest amount of missing data for the two winter
periods. In this study, the Rib was calculated by setting SST = Twater. Therefore Twater
measurements are needed for computing Rib. Since it is expected that stability would
have a seasonal variation, an alternative for completing the time series of this variable
was considered in order to include a stability analysis during winter.
Approximately 1.6 km to the North-East of ASIT, a submarine node is mounted in the
sea bed recording water temperature measurements 12 m below the sea level every 20
min. This submarine node is property of the Martha’s Vineyard Coastal Observatory
and their water temperature measurements were used to complete the time series of
water temperature at ASIT.
A holdout Cross Validation was carried out to complete the time series. In order to
do that, water temperature measurements at the submarine node from December 2016
to June 2018 were split into a training and testing period. During the training period,
a primer order lineal model was ﬁtted in order to estimate Twater at ASIT from the
water temperature measurements at the submarine node (ﬁgure 4.3 left). Finally the
lineal model was evaluated using data from the testing period and compared to the data
recorded at ASIT (ﬁgure 4.3 right). In the training and testing period, 27601 and 3066
records for ﬁtting and evaluating the model were used respectively.

of the total cases). These cases might be associated to the fact that SST at the Mid-

This gradient of SST could be the responsible that winds blowing from this direction
are characterized for driving warm air masses to the ASIT location. Nevertheless not
all the cases when wind is blowing from the direction of the Mid-Atlantic Bight leads
into positive values of Rib. A 26 % of the negatives values of Rib were found when wind
was blowing from this direction (equivalent to 2617 of the total records).

6.1
High wind shear events
As it was showed in ﬁgure 5.14, the mean synoptic conditions when high wind shears
were observed at ASIT are related with a strong Bermuda-Azores high located at the
middle of the North Atlantic basin. In addition to that, low pressures were observed
over the land of the US East coast. Under those conditions winds from lower latitude
(around 30°N) blows Northward along the US East coast. This ﬂow allows the movement
of warm air masses into the Mid-Atlantic Bight, and once they ﬂow over the relatively
cold waters of the last remaining of the Labrador current, stable conditions are produced
along the whole Mid-Atlantic Bight.
Two representative cases when high wind shears were observed during winter and spring
are analyzed in sections 6.1.1 and 6.1.2 respectively:
6.1.1
High wind shear event: 09/02/2018 - 13/02/2018
In the case of the winter example, during the 9th of February of 2018 a land high pres-
sure system was over the East of US producing winds blowing from land to sea, which
leads to unstable conditions (Figure 6.1 Top left). During that day the high pressure
system moved Eastward and around midnight of the 10th of February it merged with the
Bermuda-Azores high (Figure 6.1 Top right). This allowed the northwards ﬂow of low
latitudes winds producing stable conditions at the Mid-Atlantic Bight, which leads to
an increment of the wind shear during the ﬁrst hours of the 10th of February (ﬁgure 6.2
Top). This conditions persisted until around 10:00 in the morning on the 11 of February
when wind direction at the North part of the Mid-Atlantic Bight changed due to the

Canada (ﬁgure 6.1 Middle right). This event led to North winds at ASIT (ﬁgure 6.2
Bottom) and a reducement of the wind shear and ∆θ during most of the morning of
that day. At the afternoon (around 14:00) the high pressure center over Canada moved

6.1.2

Similarly to the winter event, high wind shears were observed at ASIT when high pres-

midday of the 14th of June (ﬁgure 6.3 Top right and middle left) where high wind shears
were observed at ASIT. Around 18:00 of the 14th, a change in the wind direction due
to the presence of low pressure over the US East coast, leading to North-Westerly winds
at ASIT. Those conditions were observed during the next day with unstable conditions
and low wind shears over the Mid-Atlantic Bight. During that day, Easterly winds were
observed in ASIT until midday, when wind direction changed to South-Wester direction.
In both examples it was possible to observe that the high wind shear events were found
under similar conditions to those described by Emeis. (2013) for the formation of the
LLJ. In this case the sudden change of the ﬂow from unstable to stable atmospheric
conditions occurred by the interaction between the Gulf Stream and the last remaining
of the Labrador current, in addition to the presence of a strong Bermuda-Azores High
over the North Atlantic Sea.
In both cases it can be observed that high pressure systems over the land avoided the
ﬂow of warm air from latitudes around 30 ° into the Mid-Atlantic Bight. Under those
conditions, cold air masses blowing from land to sea were observed, leading to unstable
conditions in this area.

SST diﬀerence, as is the case of the Mid-Atlantic Bight.

6.2
Recommendations for future works
High wind shear estimated by ERA5 using 10 and 100 m wind speeds showed to be
present in the area of the Mid-Atlantic Bight and where one of the WEA is located
(Figure 5.19(b)). Nevertheless, it is important to verify the existence of the high wind
shears by using in-situ wind measurements. Since the spacial resolution of ERA5 is
coarse (0.25x0.25° ∼25x25 km), it could not be representative of the local conditions
presented at the other WEA’s locations. One example of this, was the deviation between
measured wind shears and those simulated by ERA5 at ASIT. This can be attributed
to the fact that ERA5 is not able to represent the local conditions at ASIT, which is
located at 3 km of the Martha’s Vineyard Island.
Analysis of REWS at ASIT showed that the main diﬀerence between REWS and Uhub
was observed under high wind shear events (Figure 5.21). Figure 5.10 showed that high
==PAGE:71==
Discussion and perspectives
61
wind shears were common for wind speed in the range from 10 ms−1 to 18 ms−1. Nor-
mally under this range of wind speed, wind turbines are under blade pitch control. Wen
et al. (2017) showed that power losses could be produced for high vertical wind shear
when the wind turbine is in blade pitch control. In addition, gearbox bearing failures
were related to events of high wind shears (Gould and Burris. 2015). Those aspects
should be considered in order to reduce operation and maintenance costs as well as
making an accurate estimation in the energy produced by the wind farm.
The comparison of the wind proﬁles measured at ASIT with those simulated by power
law showed that most of the wind proﬁles at ASIT are not well represented by the
power law, specially for the high wind shear events. This could be relevant to take into
account because power law is widely used for simulating the vertical wind shear over a
wind turbine (Morten et al. 2015). The eﬀect of high wind shears in the loads of the
wind turbine should be considered using similar wind proﬁles to the ones measured at
ASIT.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
High vertical wind shears were observed with a high frequency at ASIT, which is rela-
tively close to the location where a considerable number of oﬀshore wind farms are being
planned. These high wind shears showed a higher frequency than those observed at some
locations in the North Sea, where currently most of the installed capacity of oﬀshore
wind farms is located. Most of the high wind shears were found under South-Westerly
winds and when Rib was in the interval of 0 - 0.1. High wind shears were more frequent
during spring and summer months. February showed a higher frequency in high wind
shears compared to the rest of winter and autumn months.
High wind shears at ASIT were associated with a strong Bermuda-Azores high and low
atmospheric pressures over the land of the US East coast. Under these conditions, rela-
tively warm air masses from latitudes of 30°N ﬂows northward along the coast, leading
to stable conditions in the Mid-Atlantic bight once they ﬂow over the Labrador current.
In this conditions the ﬂow experiments a sudden change from unstable (over the Gulf
Stream ) to stable condition (observed at the Mid-Atlantic Bight), which are the main
conditions for the LLJ formation. Since LLJ events are characterized for having a high
wind shear below the maximum wind speed of the LLJ, it might be expected that most
of the high wind shear events should be associated with LLJ. Nevertheless, in this study
a considerable amount of high wind shear events were not associated with LLJ events.
It might be possible that some LLJ events were not able to be identiﬁed because only
wind measurements up to 200 m were available.
Wind shears obtained from reanalysis ERA5 showed a relatively high frequency for high
wind shears at WEA’s location. However, wind shears simulated at ASIT did not had
a good agreement with those measured by the Windcube. One possible explanation
62
==PAGE:73==
Conclusion
63
could be the fact that the spatial resolution of ERA5 is too coarse for representing the
local condition of ASIT. In general, wind speed, SST and air temperature measurements
from buoys located at the Mid-Atlantic Bight showed a good agreement with variables
of ERA5. However it is important to verify the existence of high wind shears at the
WEA’s location by wind measurements.
The eﬀect of the wind shears in the power density estimation was not signiﬁcant, show-
ing a diﬀerence lower to 1% when comparing power density estimated using REWS and
U110. The highest diﬀerence between REWS and wind speed at 110 m was observed
when wind shears higher than 0.05 were observed. The high wind shear eﬀect might
have a stronger eﬀect on the loads of a wind turbine, which is something that should be
considered in the design of the wind farms.
Most of the wind proﬁles measured at ASIT were not similar to the ones obtained by
the power law, especially when high wind shears were found. This should be taken into
account for the loads estimation of the wind turbines.
