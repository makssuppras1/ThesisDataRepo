Abstract
Modern day software deﬁned radio (SDR) oﬀers a simple and ﬂexible way to design
and implement digital radar systems. This project, which is made in collaboration
with Forsvarsministeriets Materiel og Indkøbsstyrelse (FMI), focus on the choice
of pulse compression waveform when implementing a radar cross section (RCS)
measurement radar on a SDR platform. The project is split into two main parts.
The ﬁrst part consist of a litterature study of excisting waveforms used for RCS
measurements. In the second part the most promising waveforms are simulated to
test their resistance to 1) limited bandwidth, 2) quantization errors and 3) Doppler
frequency shift.
An alternative waveform using Quadrature Phase Shift Keying (QPSK) to encode
two Golay complementary codes has been tested. It is shown that for most practical
cases this waveform is not a viable option.
Alternatively the two codes can be
transmitted on two seperate pulses, which is shown to be well suited for RCS
measurements.

Introduction
1
Introduction
The radar cross section (RCS) is an important parameter which describes an objects
interaction with electromagnetic waves.
It is included in the radar equation to
represent the magnitude of the returned echo signal, and is essential to consider
when designing a radar system.
Forsvarsministeriets Materiel og Indkøbsstyrelse (FMI) have in many years used a
self-developed radar to perform RCS measurements on military ships and aircrafts.
The radar was built in a time where digital signal processing could not handle the
large amount of data generated. It is therefore a mixture of an analog and digital
radar. The analog part takes care of many of the deﬁciencies the digital technology
had when the radar was built. Today FMI are looking to replace the radar with
a newer system and are in that context investigating the possibility to built an
entirely digital system using software deﬁned radio (SDR)
The purpose of this report is to ﬁnd pulse compression waveforms well suited for
RCS measurements on a digital SDR system. The report is split into two main
parts. The ﬁrst part is a theory section and a literature study of existing wave-
forms used for RCS measurements (section 2). A number of the waveforms with
favourable properties will be chosen for simulation in part two. The second part of
the report will focus on simulating how the chosen waveforms perform under various
constraints and eﬀects (section 3 & 4). This will include limiting the bandwidth,
limiting the number of bits available and introducing a Doppler shift.
Section 2 will present some basic radar theory and introduce pulse compression as
well as software deﬁned radio. In section 2.7 diﬀerent waveforms are studied and
judged based on their eﬀectiveness for high quality RCS measurements.
In section 3 the simulations and used parameters are introduced. The software

[11]
• Specular scattering: Scattering depending on the targets surface material and

• Diﬀraction: Scattering from edges, tips and other discontinuities.
• Multiple bounce: Reﬂections among target elements at oﬀset angles

A simple unmodulated pulse is easy to implement, but the performance can be
greatly improved by modulating it in either amplitude, frequency or phase.
In
modern day, with digital radars and SDR, it is easier than ever to transmit and
receive modulated pulses. The added complexity is a much smaller problem than
it used to be.

together with some of the main reasons to use pulse compression. Section 2.5.2 goes

2.5.1
Unmodulated pulse
A simple pulse with constant amplitude and frequency is a good reference point to
the more complicated waveforms. Inevitable you would want your compressed pulse
to perform better than the simple pulse in the given application. The bandwidth
of a constant pulse is approximately the inverse of the pulse length B ≈1
T [2]. So a
simple pulse can be short with a large bandwidth or it can be long with a smaller
bandwidth. The Range resolution achievable with a constant pulse is determined
as
∆R = cT
2 ≈
c
2B
(26)
where T is the pulse length. So a shorter pulse means a better ∆R. A long pulse
is desirable in applications where the range is large. This is because the achievable
range is determined by the total pulse energy. As the pulse gets shorter the peak
power need to increase, to keep the energy constant (E = Pt)[2]. Larger peak
power necessitate added complexity and price of the hardware.
When the resolution of a short pulse and energy of a long pulse is desired, pulse
compression can be utilised. Pulse compression is performed in the matched ﬁlter
where the pulse is compressed to a width of 1/B. The pulse compression ratio
is deﬁned as the long pulse width T, to the compressed pulse width Tc, or T/Tc
[2]. Since B = 1/Tc the ratio is often written as the time bandwidth product BT.

Bandwidth considerations
The bandwidth of a phase modulated pulse is determined as one over the subpulse
width, which is also equal to the symbol rate, B = 1/tb = fb. It is this bandwidth
which determines the range-resolution of the transmitted pulse. In the time-domain
an ideal phase code consist of square waves with instantaneous amplitude rise times
(see ﬁgure 8). These square waves lead to a sinc shaped frequency spectrum where
the spectral side-lobes decays rather slowly at 6dB per octave [7]. If the spectral
side-lobes are unaccounted for they can lead to problems in both the transmitter and
receiver and the transmitted signal might not meet the spectral emission regulations.
Figure 9 shows the magnitude spectrum of an 25-element polyphase P4 code with

the the phase code. In the case of perfect square waves the the eﬀective bandwidth,

2.7.1
CW waveform
A continuous wave (CW) waveform is often used for indoors RCS measurements
due to its simplicity. The technique is to transmit a constant pulse and measure the
complex return signal at the receiver. The received signal consist of contributions
from the target, environmental scatterers and transmitter leakage. To achieve a
reasonable dynamic range the radar can use CW-nulling or pulse gating.
With CW-nulling the backround clutter from the environment is measured ﬁrst
without the target present. In the receiver a nulling loop creates a signal which
is equal in amplitude but opposite in phase to the background signal [17]. Hence
the background signal is cancelled and only the target signal should, in theory,
be measured. The technique requires stability in the environment which is why it
is only suitable for indoor measurements and is often done in anechoic chambers.
One complication with CW-nulling is the shadow eﬀect which can be an issue when
27 of 101
==PAGE:34==
Literature study
Theory
target support structures are used. If the nulling is done with the support structure
present then a shadow will be cast on the backwall of the facility. When the target
is placed on the support the shadow is changed which in turn means that the post-
mounted background is diﬀerent from the pre-mounted background [13].
A gated CW system is similar to a pulsed system. In this system the received
energy is gated to eliminate unwanted clutter. The main diﬀerence is that the CW
gated system use a narrow bandwidth receiver compared to a pulse radar [18].
2.7.2
Pulse waveform
For pulsed waveform the energy is transmitted in bursts with the receiver turned
oﬀ. The desired signal can be selected by turning the receiver on at the appropriate
time. In this way the undesired clutter is rejected prior to reception.
A train of pulses of duration T and repeated every Tp seconds, results in a discrete
spectrum. Every spectral line is separated in frequency by 1/Tp. The envelope is
sinc shaped and the ﬁrst spectral null is at 1/T. A wideband system use suﬃcient
bandwidth to include the majority of the spectral lines.
This ensures that the
output of the receiver is pulsed. In contrast the output from a narrowband receiver
is continuous wave since it only includes the central line. A narrowband receiver
is subject to signal losses proportional to the duty cycle which is why it is mostly
suited for short-range applications [13] .
2.7.3
Stepped - frequency waveform
In a step-frequency radar a series of pulses are transmitted, each with an increase
in frequency to the one prior. In this way the bandwidth of the system is increased
which in turn results in a better range resolution than a single frequency pulse

The ideal simulation take advantage of the fact that the matched ﬁlter output

function of the modulated pulse. The function which performs the ideal simulation
is called idealsim and a ﬂowchart can be seen in ﬁgure 23. Besides performing the

ideal. In that case it will perform an autocorrelation or cross correlation depending
on the type of input. So despite its name the function idealsim is often called even
if the simulation is not ideal.

samples and the signal g can be approximated by Ng samples, their correlation
requires a total of Nmin = Nf + Ng −1 samples to perform a linear correlation [9].

Golay QPSK considerations

challenges of using QPSK modulation will be presented.

many phase coded waveforms, but some codes like P(n,k) codes [23] are designed
to be more resistance to the bandlimiting eﬀects at the cost of ∆R.

4.6
Errors in RCS measurements
In this section the error in RCS measurements due to waveform choice is considered.
It is assumed that only the total RCS of the target is of interest and not the RCS
pattern. If only the targets combined RCS is of interest then there is little reason
to process more than a single sample per range gate. The measured RCS in all the
range gates that the target span over can be summed to get the total RCS.
As an example consider a target consisting of three scatteres, resulting in the RCS
pattern shown in blue in ﬁgure 46. In this ﬁgure 5 range gates have been named
A to F. The ﬁrst scatterer is in the middle of range gate B and has a RCS of 0.5.
The second scatterer is right in between range gate C and D and has a RCS of
1. The last scattere is in range gate E with a RCS of 0.5. The combined RCS
of the three scatterers is 2. It is seen if the middle of each range gate is sampled
then the peaks of scattere 2 and 3 are missed. This is not a problem when the
matched ﬁlter output for a point target is triangular with no sidelobes. In this case
the combined RCS can be determined by summing each sample in range gate A to
F. This gives exactly 2 in the ideal case. If the matched ﬁlter output of a point
target is not triangular or if it has sidelobes then there will be an inaccuracy in the
measurement.

Conclusion
6
Conclusion
This project was splitted in two main parts.
The ﬁrst part was a theory and
litterature study of existing waveforms well suited for radar cross section (RCS)
measurements.
The second part focused on simulating the performance of the
waveforms under the constraints which are present when the radar is built using a
modern software deﬁned radio (SDR) platform.
Eleven waveforms were investigated in the ﬁrst part of the project. Hereof 7 were
chosen to be simulated based on their attractive properties.
The chosen wave-
forms were 1) Maximum length sequence (MLS), 2) Frank polyphase code, 3) P4
polyphase code, 4) Interleaved Golay codes, 5) QPSK Golay codes, 6) Linear fre-
quency modulation (LFM), 7) Hamming windowed LFM.
In the second part of the project 4 tests were performed, where at least 3 had to be
passed for the waveforms to be considered to be well suited for RCS measurements
using an SDR platform. The 4 tests were based on the hardware speciﬁcations of
the SDR platform Ettus X310.
• Test1: Achieve a PSLR larger than 40 dB with only 4 I/Q bits available.
• Test2: Achieve a PSLR larger than 40 dB with only 8 I/Q bits available.
• Test3: Achieve a PSLR larger than 40dB and an error less than 5%, when the
bandwidth is limited to 160 MHz.
• Test4: Achieve a PSLR larger than 40dB, when the Doppler frequency shift
is 7200Hz.
Four of the simulated waveforms passed at least 3 of the tests. The waveforms were
1) MLS, 2) Hamming windowed LFM, 3) Interleaved Golay, 4) QPSK Golay. The
96 of 101
==PAGE:103==
Conclusion
two best performing waveforms were the interleaved Golay codes and the Hamming
windowed LFM. The Hamming windowed LFM is to be recommended in applica-
tions where a good range resolution is required. The interleaved Golay pulses are
recommended when high precision RCS measurements are needed with a low error.
Using QPSK modulation to transmit two complementary Golay codes on a sin-
gle pulse were considered. In practise it is only possible when the phase can be
recovered. Phase recovery can be performed for a single scatterer, therefore the
modulation type is only feasible when measuring on a single simple scatterer. An
experiment using QPSK modulation was performed on a ﬂat whiteboard target. It
was demonstrated that in this simple case the phase can be recovered and QPSK
modulation can be used. It should not be used when measuring on complex targets.
Future work
The next logical step will be to perform RCS measurement tests on real targets.
This is to verify the simulation results and to discover other potential challenges
or uncertainties which has not been explored in this report. The recommendation
for FMI will be to use interleaved Golay codes, since they perform high quality
RCS measurements for missile trackers or other radar systems which uses large
range gates. When further tests have been performed a new SDR radar can be
implemented, for RCS measurement purposes, using interleaved complementary
Golay codes.
There exist many other waveforms with attractive properties which could be worth
exploring. It could for example be nonlinear frequency modulation or P(n,k) codes
which has been mentioned shortly in the report. Another approach would be to
design a custom waveform using gradient descent or other optimisation algorithms
to get the properties which are attractive for the application.
