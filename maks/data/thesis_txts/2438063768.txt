Abstract
Alginates are carbohydrates composed by guluronic and mannuronic acids and are dis-
tributed in the cell walls of brown seaweeds.
They are widely used in the food and
pharmaceutical industry due to their thickening and gelling properties. Alginates market
size is increasing due to a higher demand in food and beverages, derived from an increase
of global population.
The classical extraction techniques of alginate involve hot alkali treatments, which use chemi-
cals and are water intensive. In the present study, a milder extraction using enzymes to extract
alginate and oligosaccharides from brown seaweed S.latissima was proposed as an alternative.
Different enzymatic treatments were investigated. First, it was conducted an extraction based
on a single Cellic CTec2 incubation at 30 °C, pH 6 for 48h. Subsequently, it was evaluated
the addition of a second incubation with different concentrations of alginate lyase under
different conditions. It was also considered an extraction in which after a CTec2 incubation,
a second incubation without alginate lyase followed.
From the parameters assessment, it was determined to perform a comparison between a
second incubation without alginate lyase and with 1 u/ml of alginate lyase at the same pH
and temperature. The recovery of alginic sugars was found to be 62.71 % of guluronic and
40.80 % mannuronic acid with a second incubation at pH 7.5, 30 °C for 16 h. When 1 u/ml
alginate lyase was added, alginic sugars recovery was found to be 92.88 % of guluronic and
49.32 % of mannuronic acid at pH 7.5, 30 °C for 5 h. Regarding the molecular weight, high
molecular weight alginates resulted from the two-step incubation process without alginate
lyase, whereas a two-step incubation process with alginate lyase lead to low molecular
weight alginates.
It was also investigated if the addition of CTec2 could release some nitrogenous compounds
from the seaweed biomass. The results showed that CTec2 alone allowed only a 0.62 %
nitrogen-containing compounds to be released.
The liquid fraction resultant after the CTec2 treatment was evaluated as a yeast growth
medium with three different yeasts: C.jadini, S.cerevisiae and Y. triangularis. All strains were
found to grow on this medium, which would make it suitable for single cell protein.

Introduction
Marine macroalgae, or also called seaweeds, are a wide variety of photosynthetic organisms
that inhabit the littoral seabed [1]. They are primary producers in the ocean food webs as
they have the ability to transform the energy from sunlight into organic matter [2]. Moreover,
in some countries, such as China or Japan, macroalgae have been part of people´s diets
for centuries as they can be used as direct human consumption. However, their global
commercial exploitation is only a few decades old. After the Word War II, concerns arose
due to the possibility of an insufﬁcient protein supply due to a rapid increase of the world
population [3]. Nowadays, macroalgae are receiving an increased attention not only as
protein source substitution but as a renewable source for the production of different products
within food, pharmaceuticals or biofuels industry [4].
Macroalgae can be classiﬁed into three different taxonomic groups based on various prop-
erties and morphological features: red algae (phylum: Rhodophyta), green algae (phylum:
Chlorophyta) and brown algae (phylum: Ochrophyta) [3]. The latter is the focus of this
study as it is a rich source of alginates [5].
Alginates are carbohydrates that are present in substantial amounts in brown seaweed´s
cell wall. They are commercialized due to their great ability of forming highly viscous
solutions and they are mainly used as thickening agents, drug carrier or stabilizers in food
and pharmaceuticals.
The global market size of alginate was valued at US$ 624.0 million in 2016 [6]. In the
following years the alginate market expects an increased demand, due to the increasing
consumption of food (beers, yoghurts) and pharmaceuticals [6].
So far, the alginate extraction techniques, include hot alkali treatments, which are considered
both energy and water intensive [9]. Moreover, chemicals are used to precipitate or solubilize
alginate, inﬂuencing the functional properties of the remaining cell wall components and
aggravating the waste disposal.
The aim of this study is to analyse the potential alternative of using an enzyme or a
mixture of them to extract alginate from brown seaweeds.
This would mean a more
environmental friendly process, in which the enzymes could selectively extract the alginate
and would preserve the rest of the cell wall. Hence, the remaining carbohydrates and cell
wall components can be further used as valuable sources for other applications. However,
this approach is quite challenging, given the fact that the complexity of the structure and
composition of the cell wall of seaweeds may inhibit or partially lower the enzymatic
extraction.

1.1
Brown seaweed
Brown seaweed are a type of macroalgae, which are abundant in nearshore coastal waters
with low temperatures and suitable substrate for attachment as could be a rocky surface.
They are a large and diverse class of marine multicellular and photoautotrophic organisms,
divided in 19 orders, 250 genera and 1500-2000 species [10]. They belong to the phylum
of Ochrophyta, which forms one of the eight major phylogenetic groups of eukaryotes.
In fact, their origin is suggested to come from a symbiotic relationship between a basal
eukaryote and antoher eukaryotic organism as they have chloroplasts surrounded by four
membranes. A photosynthetic pigment, fucoxanthin, can be found in their chloroplasts and
it is responsible for the olive-brown colour from which the name derives [11].
The cells of brown seaweed are surrounded by a carbohydrates-rich cell wall with alginate
as the main polysaccharide. In the present work, the brown seaweed S.latissima was used for
the study of an enzymatic alginate extraction.
1.1.1
Saccharina latissima
Saccahrina latissima (S.latissima) is also known as Laminaria saccharina and is commonly found
in the northern Paciﬁc Ocean and the northern Atlantic Ocean at depths from 8 m to 30 m.
Exceptionally, it has been found at a depth of 120 m in warmer waters of the Mediterranean
Sea and Brazil [12].
Figure 1: Saccharina Latissima [13]
S.latissima (Figure 1) is around 3m in length and with a claw-like holdfast. The stipe is small,
smooth and ﬂexible and with a tongue-like appearance due to the undivided laminated
blade.

Structural carbohydrates: Alginate, fucoidan and cellulose
The structural carbohydrates are forming part of the cell wall, building a network that
provides the natural structure and corresponding ﬂexibility that brown seaweed has.
The most abundant structural carbohyrates in brown seaweed are alginates. They are found
in the cell wall as a mixture of calcium, potassium and sodium salts of alginic acid. They are
linear polymers made of two monomeric uronic acids, (1→4)β-D-mannuronic acid (M) and
(1→4)α-L-guluronic acid (G) arranged in blocks along the polysaccharide chain. The blocks
are composed by consecutive M residues (MMMM), consecutive G residues (GGGG) or
alternating M and G residues (MGMGMG). The M-block does not gel in presence of divalent
cations, while G-block forms junctions with calcium (Ca2+) connecting two antiparallel
chains and, therefore, increasing the gelling mechanical strength. The ratio of guluronic
acid (G) to mannuronic acid (M), as well as the length of each block, varies depending on
the natural source and has a great impact on the physico-chemical properties of alginate.
Alginates with low M/G ratios tend to form dense and brittle gels, while high M/G ratios
lead to more elastic gels [9]. The molecular weight is another parameter of alginate that
should be considered. A commercially available sodium alginate has a molecular weight

molecular weight. Generally, a high molecular weight is desired as it enhances its physical

Hence, the M/G ratio, the sequence G-block length and the molecular weight affect directly

Sulfated fucans are sulfated structural polysaccharides containing α-L-fucosyl residues.
They may be found either in the form of homopolymers, called fucans or as heteropolymers,
termed fucoidans. The most common term for these polysaccharides is fucose-containing
sulfate polysaccharides (FCPSs)[16]. The brown algal FCSPs consists of branched polysac-
charides, ranging from high-uronic acid, low-sulfate-containing polymers with signiﬁcant
proportions of xylose, galactose and mannose, to highly sulfated fucans. Depending on the
species their structure variates as well as their arrangements with the sulfate groups. Thus,

Cellulose is present in a very low content in brown seaweed compared to other higher plants.

Storage carbohydrates: Laminnarin and mannitol
The storage carbohydrates appear in brown seaweed, when there is a high photosynthesis
activity and there is a lack of nitrogen availability. In this way, the storage carbohydrates
content is variable according to season and spatial variations.
Laminarin is a low molecular weight storage carbohydrate β-glucans (approximately 5

β(1,6)-intra-chain branching. There are two different types of laminarin chains (M or G),
which differ in their reducing end. M chains end with a mannitol residue, while G chains
end with a glucose residue as a reducing end (Figure 5, [18]).

1.2.2
Proteins
Brown seaweeds have a content of proteins around (8-23% w/wDM) [14], which is lower than
the protein content found in the red and green seaweeds (10-47% w/wDM) [14]. Despite
their lower content, they have been found to be bioactive and exhibit antioxidative, antihy-
pertensive, anticoagulative, antitumor and immune-stimulative properties [19]. They can
also substitute animal proteins in foods and still present high nutritonal value. Furthermore,
they can also be used as natural coloring agents. In this way, their extraction from seaweed
is being extensively studied ([20],[21]).
1.2.3
Lipids
Lipids in brown algae are quite negligible when compared to other algae. They are typically
less than 5% w/wDM [22]. As laminarin and mannitol, lipids also have the function of
energy storage.
The lipids are being used in transesteriﬁcation reactions in order to produce bio-oil and in
this way replace the fossil fuels. Most of the research has focused on microalgae. However,
as it is quite challenging to harvest and cultivate microalgae, there is a growing interest in
using macroalgae. Although the majority of the macroalgae have a low content of lipids as
S. Latissima, some brown seaweed species have been found to be able to reach 20-30% dry
w/wDM [23] of lipids.

Minerals

1.3
Potential of carbohydrates
Seaweeds grow fast with a high photosynthetic efﬁciency, great CO2 ﬁxation, low lignin
content and high content of carbohydrates. Accordingly, they have a wide variety of different
applications.
The extraction of alginate, which is the most abundant carbohydrate, has been chosen as the
main goal of this study. However, the cell wall contains more valuable products which are
not destroyed when using enzymes. Hence, the side streams from the enzymatic extraction of
alginate have the possibilty of not being disposed as waste but used in different applications.
The following sections 1.3.1 and 1.3.2 describe the potential of the alginate and the other
valuable carbohydrates obtained in the side streams, respectively.
1.3.1
Applications of alginate
Alginate is one of the main constituents of the cell wall of brown seaweed and is a biological
material that can be used in a wide variety of applications due to the different properties
that it can offer. Alginate´s structure is similar to the macromolecules of the human body,
indicating that alginate has biocompatibilty with the human body. Furthermore, due to
the cross-linking between G-blocks and divalent cations (Ca2+), a gel consistency can be
achieved. In this way, it can be used in the pharmaceutical industry as a wound healing
providing a moist wound environment, as replacements or treatments of tissues and as drug
or protein delivery controlling and enhancing the release. In the textile printing industry,
alginate can be used as a dye and in the paper industry is used as surface sizing. It can also
be used in the food industry as stabilizers or thickeners and as biocatalyst immobilizers,
trapping the enzymes. Moreover, alginate is able to adsorb heavy metals, allowing in this
way the removal of toxines from wastewaters [15].
The global market value for alginates in 2015 was estimated to be US$ 339 million/year[9]
and in 2016 its demand almost doubled being the global size market US$ 624 million [6].
This increase in the demand is due to the higher consumption of foods such as ice-creams,
beers and yoghurt and is also increased due to its recognition by the FDA and European
Commission.

mannitol which are also used in the industry for a wide variety of applications.
Recently, a great interest for fucoidan has raised since it has been discovered to have dif-
ferent applications in the pharmaceutical industry [24]. Fucoidan is also a biocompatible

1.4
Common methods for the alginate extraction
To extract the alginate, the seaweed is chopped into pieces and then stirred for 2 hours
with an alkali hot solution, usually sodium carbonate. A thick slurry is obtained, where the
cellulose is not dissolved and alginate is dissolved as sodium alginate. The solution is too
viscous to be ﬁltered, so previously is diluted with a very large quantity of water and a ﬁlter
aid such as diatomaceous earth is added. In this way, a ﬁltration is forced with air into the
extract while is being diluted with water. The ﬁlter aid holds most of the ﬁne particles, so that
the ﬁlter does not clog. The diluted extract is left for several hours while the air is displaced
to the residue. Then, the mix of this air bubbles and residue are removed from the top and
the solution left is withdrawn [29]. Basically, the alginate extraction protocol is divided into
ﬁve steps: acidiﬁcation, alkaline extraction, solid/liquid separation, precipitation and drying
[29]. The next step is the precipitation of alginate from the ﬁltered solution, either as alginic
acid, calcium alginate or by using directly ethanol. In this section, the different common
methods to precipitate the sodium alginate are discussed as well as an evaluation of the use
of reactive extrusion instead of batch for the alkaline extraction.

1.4.1
Alginic acid process
An acid (generally HCl) is added to the ﬁltered extract forming an alginic acid gel which has
to be separated from water. Due to this gelatine consistency, ﬁltration is not possible, hence
ﬂotation is used instead. The solution contains around 1-2 % of alginic acid and 98-99% of
water. To reduce the water content, the gel solution is centrifuged in a basket-type centrifuge.
In this way, the solids content is increased to a 7-8% and can then be squeezed in a screw
press. Thereafter, the alginic acid and the remaining water are mixed with alcohol (ethanol
or isopropanol) to give a 50:50 mixture of alcohol and water. Then, solid sodium carbonate
is added until the desired pH is reached. Once the sodium alginate has precipitated, the
paste can be extruded as pellets and then can be dried and milled [29].
1.4.2
Calcium alginate process
The addition of a soluble calcium salt, such as calcium chloride, to the ﬁltered extract forms
solid calcium alginate. If the calcium salt and the ﬁltered extract are mixed carefully, the
calcium alginate can be formed as ﬁbres, while bad mixing gives a gelatinous solid. The
ﬁbers are preferred as they can be easily separated by sieving and washing with water to
remove the excess of calcium. Then, it is stirred in dilute acid and converted to alginic acid,
which mantains the ﬁbrous characteristics of the calcium alginate. This form of alginic acid
is resistant to be squeezed in a screw press. The screw press squeezes gradually in order to
ensure that the material does not move out of the press. The product obtained after pressing
contains around a 20-25 % of alginic acid and is mixed with sodium carbonate, until the
desired pH is reached. After the paste of sodium alginate is obtained, it can then be extruded
as pellets, dried and milled [29].
1.4.3
Ethanol route
The addition of ethanol to the the ﬁltered solution until a volumetric proportion of 1:1, will
cause the immediate precipitation of sodium alginate under stirring. Then, the insoluble
polymer is separated and it is washed with ethanol by soxhlet during 100 hours [30]. Finally,
the puriﬁed sodium alginate is obtained by drying the polymer at room temperature under
vacuum until the amount of mass is constant.
1.4.4
Alkaline extraction: Reactive extrusion instead of batch extrusion
Alkaline extraction is one of the main steps of the extraction of alginate and it can be
conducted either by batch rector or reactive extrusion. In both cases, the alginate is ﬁrstly
acidiﬁed with acid sulfuric. Then the batch alkaline extraction is soaked in sodium carbonate,
whereas the reactive extrusion is conducted by a corotative twin-screw extruder being the
sodium carbonate pumped externally. After, centrifugation is performed for separating the
liquid from the solid. For the precipitation of sodium alginate, sulphuric acid is added. The
product is then pressed, heat-dried and milled. The purity of alginate has high values as
95% or even higher and they are maintained similar for both extrusions [31].

There are several advantages of reactive extrusion over batch extrusion. Firstly, the reaction
time is decreased from one hour to just few minutes. Secondly, the extraction yield for
reactive extrusion is more than 15% higher than batch extrusion. Thirdly, the water and
reactant consumption are reduced to less than half for reactive extrusion. Lastly, the reactive
extrusion is a continuous process, which can be an attractive reason for economical and
environmental purposes.
However, the main disadvantage of reactive extrusion is the equipment cost, which is higher
than for batch. This could be a great disadvantage for a large-scale production.
1.4.5
Drawbacks of the common extraction alginate methods
The classical extraction techniques have different drawbacks that makes them expensive and
not environmentally friendly. The hot alkali treatments used for the chemical extraction
have been found to modify the physco-chemical properties of alginates as well as having the
risk of being toxic or poisonous for food and pharmaceutical applications [9]. This implies
an increase in the number of puriﬁcation steps and converts them in an energy intensive
extraction process.
In addition, when the alginate dissolves as sodium alginate, the solution is too dense to be
ﬁltered, so the solution has to be diluted several times using large quantities of water. Fur-
thermore, the seaweed residuals can clog the ﬁlter, thus ﬁlter aids must be used, increasing
the overall cost of the process.
To overcome those difﬁculties and avoid the use of chemicals, there is a need for a milder
alternative way of extracting the alginate from brown seaweed.
1.5
Alternative way: Enzymatic mixtures
One of the most promising alternatives for the extraction of alginate is the use of enzymes.
They allow a targeted selective extraction of the product at mild conditions preserving the
remaining carbohydrates of the cell wall [9]. Furthermore, the use of enzymes implies a
reduction of chemicals and thus it could be considered as a more sustainable and environ-
mentally friendly process.
The enzyme-assisted extraction technique consists in the rupture of the cell wall by the
enzymes, in order to release the desirable compounds from the cell. There are some recently
reviewed studies [9] that indicate that depending on the enzymes that are used for the
extraction, different properties can be acquired by the alginate. This is due to the fact that
depending on the amount and sequence of the M and G residues, the properties of the
alginate can be gelling, water-binding or immunogenic.
There are already some enzyme-assited studies that have been performed in order to mainly
extract anticoagulants, antioxidants and glucose from brown seaweed (Sections 1.5.1 and

1.5.3
Extraction of sodium alginate by an enzyme-ultrasonic method
There is still a few research focused on the extraction of alginate by enzymes. Most of them
are focusing on the use of alginate lyases to obtain alginate oligosaccharides that can also
have a numerous applications within biotechnology [35]. However, one of the studies [36]
combines the use of the cellulase with ultrasound power for the extraction of alginate from
Laminaria japonica. They analyzed different conditions and considered different factors as
pH, temperature, enzyme dosage, ultrasonic power and enzymatic hydrolisis time. Due to
the high number of parameters, they performed a L18 (35) Orthogonal test. This consists in
a tool of experiments design, which through a statistical method the optimized parameters
are found. In this way, the highest yield has been obtained at a pH=4, temperature 50 °C, an
enzymatic dosage of 8% (cellulose 5%(w/v) and protease 3%(w/v)), ultrasonic power of 160
W and an enzymatic hydrolisis time of 3.5 hours.
The average extraction ratio (amount of sodium alginate:amount of biomass) at the optimum
conditions has been found to be 23.1%, while for the traditional techniques is generally
between 17 to 19%. Hence, the enzyme-ultrasonic method has higher extraction ratio.

Materials and Methods
2.1
Preparation of samples
2.1.1
Seaweed Biomass
The biomass is seaweed S. latissima, grown in the coast of the Faroe Islands during May 2017.
After being harvested the seaweed was kept over night in saline water and then frozen. The
biomass was then shipped to DTU and was freeze dried to remove the moisture. Before
using it as a reagent, the seaweed was ground with a hammermill of 1mm sieve.
2.1.2
Buffer
The buffer was a solution that was added to the homogenized biomass to maintain the pH
stable while the enzymatic treatment occured. Depending on the pH, different buffers were
selected. In this study, the treatments investigated were carried out at pH 6, pH 6.3 or pH
7.5. The buffers applied were 0.05 M citric acid and 0.1 M sodium phosphate buffer [37] for
both pH 6 and 6.3, while a buffer of 0.5 M sodium phosphate dibasic and 0.5 M sodium
phosphate monobasic [38] was used for pH 7.5
2.1.3
Enzymatic treatment
The objective of using enzymes is to tear down the cell wall of the brown seaweed to liberate
polysaccharides such as alginate. However, the rupture is desired to be as soft as possible,
meaning that the enzymes chosen, should loosen the cell wall to release the valuable com-
pounds without breaking down the compounds itself. If the enzymes action is too aggressive,
it can lower the molecular weight and simply form monosaccharides.
For the present study, the enzymatic treatment was performed by adding Cellic CTec2
(Novozymes, Denmark) to a ﬁnal concentration of 5% E/S (Enzyme/Substrate in % by
volume) and four different alginate lyase concentrations (Sigma-Aldrich, Germany) 0, 0.1, 1
and 3 units/ml. The storage temperature of both enzymes was between 2-4 °C to avoid their
degradation or any alteration in their activity.
Cellic CTec2 is a mixture of cellulases, β-glucosidases and hemicellulases prepared by
Novozymes [39], which are able to degrade cellulose into fermentable sugars. All the
samples were treated with an incubation of CTec2 for 48 hours at 40°C and pH 6 with an
agitation of 100 rpm.
After the CTec2 treatment, it was decided to perform a second incubation in which alginate
lyase could be added or not. Alginate lyase has been found to degrade alginate by cleaving
the glycosidic bonds that bind the different mannuronic and guluronnic units that form
the alginate itself [40]. In this study, the addition of alginate lyase as a second incubation
for achieving a smooth cleavage of the cell wall was studied. The different conditions and
parameters that were investigated were obtained from a screening design. All conditions
were incubated at 100 rpm.

Freeze drying
Freeze drying was performed for the removal of water molecules that could be present in
the samples. In this way, frozen solvents were removed by sublimation while the water
molecules were removed by desorption, so being released by the surface [41].
Sublimation is a phenomenon that consists in the phase transition from solid to vapour,

without passing through the liquid state. The sublimation occuring in the freeze dryer can

Puriﬁcation of alginate
The alginate obtained in the experiments was puriﬁed by solubilisation or precipitation,

To the solid residues left after the enzymatic treatment, 0.5 M sodium carbonate (Na2CO3)
was added in order to solubilize the alginate contained and form sodium alginate. The
conditions used for the solubilisation were a temperature of 60°C and an incubation time of
2 hours while being mechanically stirred at 100 rpm [30]. The volume of sodium carbonate
added depended on the amount of solid residue, being in this case, 40 g of solid resiude/L
of sodium carbonate [30].
For the precipitation of the alginate obtained in the liquid fraction, calcium chloride (CaCl2)
was used. The liquid fractions were thereby mixed with a solution of a 2% of CaCl2 and left
to settle in the fridge for 2 hours [42].
After the addition of Na2CO3 and CaCl2, the solutions were centrifuged and freeze dried.

Analysis
Different analysis were performed to quantify the carbohydrates, proteins as well as deﬁne

2.3.1
Carbohydrates determination
The NREL protocol [43] uses a two-step acid hydrolisis to brake down the polymeric forms
of carbohydrates from alga into monomeric units. The carbohydrates can then be quantiﬁed
by anion exchange chromatography.
According to NREL, the ﬁrst step of acid hydrolisis, is weighing 25 mg of the freeze dried
samples and adding 250 µl of 72% (w/w) sulfuric acid vortexing it to mix thoroughly. The
samples are then incubated in a water bath at 30 °C for 1 hour. The second step is to add
7 ml of MiliQ water, in this way the acid is diluted to 4 % (w/w). Then, the samples are
autoclaved at 121°C for 1 hour. After completion of the autoclave, the samples have to cool

==PAGE:24==
2
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Technical University of Denmark
down until room temperature is reached. Thereafter, it can be considered that the acid
hydrolisis was performed.
In the acid hydrolisis of the samples of this study, some modiﬁcations to the protocol were
implemented. The solid residues of the samples follow the two-step acid hydrolisis proposed
by NREL. However, the autoclave time has been reduced to 40 min as it was found that is
sufﬁcient for the determination of carbohydrates [34]. For the supernatant only one step of
hydrolisis with 4% (w/w) sulfuric acid has been considered and the autoclave time is kept
to 1 hour as the protocol indicates.
Once the acid hydrolisis has been performed, the hydrolyzed samples need to be neutralized
for being able to run in a anion exchange chromatography column. Therefore, 0.5 ml of
5N sodium hydroxide are added per ml of hydrolyzed sample and is diluted 50 times. The
diluted samples are then ﬁltered with a nylon membrane (0.22 µm) and inserted in speciﬁc
liquid chromatography vials. The samples are then ready to be processed by the anion
exchange chromatographer.
Anion exchange chromatography is a method used to separate molecules based on their
charges. In this way, anion exchange consists of a postively charged resin with afﬁnity for
molecules that have a negative charge [44].
The anion exchange chromatography used in this study was high-performance anion-
exchange (HPAEC) system equipped with a pulsed amperometric detection, a Carbopac
PA-1 column and a Carbopac PA-1 guard column maintained at 25°C [45]. The column injects
10 µl of the preﬁltered sample by using an autosampler. Identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation of
the hydrolisis products was done based on mannitol, fucose, galactose, glucose, mannose,
xylose, guluronic and mannuronic acid standards.
2.3.2
Protein determination: Kjeldahl analysis
The Kjeldahl method has been used for longer than 100 years to determine the nitrogen
content in a material. It is an ofﬁcial method and is described in different normatives such
as AOAC, USEPA, ISO or DIN [46].
The Kjeldahl procedure followed in this study involves three major steps:
1. Digestion. The protein is destroyed by adding 20 ml of sulfuric acid to the solid
sample and boiled at 440 °C. The destruction is catalyzed by two Kjeltabs, which are a
mixture of copper (II) sulfate, potassium sulphate and titanium dioxde. The copper
and titanium compounds catalyze the destruction and reduction of the N-compounds
and the potassium sulphate raises the boiling point of the digested mixture. In this
way, the organic nitrogen is converted into NH4+, CO2 and H2O. Once the digestion
ﬁnishes, the digested samples switch colour to green.
2. Distillation. Before performing distillation, the samples are cooled down by adding
60 ml of water. After their cooling, the NH4+ formed during digestion is going to

be separated from the liquid mixture. Therefore, the cooled samples are taken to the
distillation unit where the pH is raised by adding 10 ml of NaOH. The liquid NH4+
will be transformed into NH3 gas. The gas is then condensated and collected into
a ﬂask. This collector ﬂask contains a prepared solution with 40 ml of HCL and 7-8
drops of ACAndersen indicator. After 4-5 minutes of distillation, the collector ﬂask is
removed and taken to titration.
3. Back titration. The ammonia dissolves in the HCL solution and reacts. The excess
of HCL in the collector without reacting can be back titrated with 0.1 M of sodium
hydroxide and by difference the amount of ammonia is calculated. The titration is
ﬁnished when the colour of the solution switches from violet to green.
In order to perform the calculations, it has to be considered the determination of molar
ratio and the average conversion factor from nitrogen-to-protein. In this way, the molar
ratio is calculated by adding 20 ml of 0.1 M HCL in a ﬂask with 4-5 drops of AC Andersen
indicator and titrating with 0.1 M NaOH for color wrapping from violet to green. The
average conversion factor was considered to be 5, which was described in the literature [47]
as a global macroalgae nitrogen-to-protein factor.
2.3.3
Molecular weight determination: Size exclusion chromatography
Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) is an analytical technique that separates molecules by
size based on their elution from columns ﬁlled with a porous gel. In this way, the smallest
compounds in the sample mixture can enter into all the pores and have a long elution time.
The medium size molecules enter in some of the pores and then they elute accordingly to
their size. The macromolecules are not retained in the columns, so they are the ﬁrst being
eluted [48].
The molecular weight was then calculated by comparing the ones of the samples with
standards of known size.
The chromotagrapher used in this study for perforimg the SEC analysis wass Ultimate 3000
Pump (Thermo Fischer). Speciﬁcally, this analysis was carried out by adding 10 mg of
sample in 500 µl of NaNO3 buffer and then ﬁltering with a nylon membrane (0.22 µm) and
inserted in SEC vials.

2.4
Design of experiments
The goal of the experimental procedure was to try to ﬁnd the conditions which have a
higher yield, purity and if possible molecular weight of alginate. The fact is that there are
different variables that could be manipulated, and this meant that the possible combinations
of experiments that could be carried out for determining the optimal conditions could be
millions.
The design of experiments (DOE) is an optimization approach, in which the variables that
most inﬂuence in our response (yield, purity and molecular weight) are determined by
setting the approximate response to the desired response [49]. Thus, by using a statistical
method it can be concluded which variables have more effect towards the desired response.
There were different variables that could affect the reactions of the study as it could be the
pH, temperature, duration of enzymatic incubation, enzyme dosage or enzyme addition
frequency.
The software JMP provides the necessary mathematical modelling to carry out this optimiza-
tion. From the different statistical models available Screening design was chosen. Screening
design is a model that allows graphical design diagnostics and statistically limits the number
of experiments to be done in order to test the inﬂuence of the different parameters.
A ﬁrst screening is going to be carried out to have consistent experiments with the most
relevant parameters.
2.4.1
DOE screening
A DOE was performed to determine which parameters inﬂuence more in the extraction
process when alginate lyase is added as a second step.
From the different variables that inﬂuenced the enzymatic treatment, the selected variables
used in the Screening design model were pH, temperature, amount of alginate lyase and
enzymatic incubation time. The pH values were chosen accordingly to the enzyme activity
range reported in Sigma Aldrich fact sheet and in article [50]. Regarding the temperature,
the active alginate lyase is between 30 to 50 °C. However, since a mild rupture of the cell
wall was desired and alginate degrades over 40 °C, the temperatures were kept lower than
40 °C. Furthermore, the lower the temperature and incubation time, the lower the amount of
energy consumed and consequently more economical the process will be. For the amount of
enzyme, two possibilities have been investigated; one where only Cellic Ctec2 is added (0
u/ml of alg.lyase) and another where different concentrations of alginate lyase are added.
With respect to the times used, four different enzymatic incubation times were tried. The
inputs for each parameter are shown in Table 4.

2.5
Yeast cultures
The liquid fraction obtained after the CTec2 treatment was found to contain mainly mannitol
but also lower content of other sugars. It was decided to assess the potential reuse of this
side-stream as a yeast growth medium. For doing so, three yeasts were tested; Cyberlindnera
jadinii (CBS 2160), Saccharomyces cerevisiae (CBS 1173) and Yamadazyma triangularis (NNRL
Y-5714).
The yeast cultures evaluation was performed with the following steps:
1. The three yeasts were taken out from the freezer where its strains were mantained in 30
% glycerol at -80 °C. Then, they were plated in the solid Yeast Peptone Dextrose (YPD)
agar with a composition of 20 g/L bacteriological peptone, 10 g/l of yeast extract, 20
g/L of glucose and 15 g/L of agar [53]. They were incubated for 48 hours at 30°C.
2. To evaluate and compare the growth performance of the side-stream, the three selected
yeasts were inoculated not only in the liquid side-stream but also in the liquid media of
Yeast peptone dextrose (YPED) and of Yeast peptone mannitol (YPM). YPED medium
was prepared following the commercial recipe, which consists of 10 g/L of yeast extract,
20 g/L of peptone and 20 g/L of glucose [54]. YPM medium was modiﬁed from the
commercial one, resembling it to the concentrations of YPED. In this way, its composi-
tion was of 10 g/L of yeast extract, 20 g/L of peptone and 20 g/L of D-mannitol. Both
are sterilized at 121 °C for 20 min before the inoculation.
3. The growth of the yeasts in the different mediums was followed by means of cell
optical density. They were tracked during 5 days and they were incubated at 30 °C
with a continuous agitation of 150 rpm. The optical density (OD) of the samples
was measured by a spectrophotometer. The model used was Shimadzu UV-1280 at a
wavelength of 600 nm.

Proteins are also a signifcant component in S.latissima, that has been reported in literature

Mannitol valorisation
The liquid fractions of all the enzymatic treatments yielded high recoveries of mannitol.
Mannitol has been found to be used as component of cell growth media. However to be
used as yeast growth medium, the liquid fractions need to have sufﬁcient amount of sugars.
Therefore a yeast growth assessment was decided to be conducted in the liquid fraction

mainly mannitol (3.65 g/L), but also lower amounts of sugars such as glucose, galactose or
fucose that could be a potential carbon source for yeasts.
The growth assessment tested three different yeasts:
• Cyberlindnera jadinii. It grows well on pentoses and generally is used as animal
fodder and dietary supplement for humans [57].

Baker´s or Brewer´s yeast as it is used for baking bread and for brewing beer [58].

and in different pentoses such as xylose or arabinose. It has been found to be an
opportunistic pathogen in lung tissue [59].

Discussion
4.1
Enzymatic treatment assessment
In the literature few studies have actually tried to use enzymes to recover high molecu-
lar weight alginate [36]. However there already some that have tried to extract alginate
oligosaccharides with potential bioactivities [60] and there are others [61], [62] that believe
that enzymes could enhance alginates recovery due to theirs speciﬁcity and simplify like
this the puriﬁcation step. In the present study various enzymatic treatments in S.latissima
were conducted with the main purpose of extracting alginate with high recovery yields and
high molecular weight.
First it was tried to slightly cleave the cell wall by an incubation with Cellic CTec2. By
applying it, the alginic acids, guluronic and mannuronic acid were recovered in a 12.51 %
and a 10.63 %, respectively. The rest remained in the solid residue. Due to this low yield,
it was decided to add another enzyme as a second step in the process, which could help
releasing more alginate. It was thought to apply alginate lyase, which is the enzyme breaking
down directly the alginate. However, the idea was to add it in low concentrations so that
it would open up the interveawed matrix in which alginate is the main constitutor. It was
decided to apply alginate lyase at different concentrations and at different conditions to
study which set of parameters yielded the highest recovery of alginate as well as preserved
the high molecular weight. It was also studied the case, where no alginate lyase was added
to the CTec2 treated biomass, but still was subjected to a second incubation with different
conditions.
In order to identify which conditions were inﬂuencing a higher yield and molecular weight
of alginate, a screening design was performed in JMP Software. This determined that if
alginate lyase was to be added, the conditions which were enhancing the molecular weight
were a concentration of 1 u/ml alginate lyase, 30 °C, pH 7.5 and 5 hours. By contrast the
experiments carried out with a second incubation step without the addition of alginate lyase
resulted to have higher molecular weight also at 30 °C, pH 7.5 but 16 hours instead of 5
hours. The alginic sugars recovery percentages in this two cases were 92.88 % guluronic and
49.32 % mannuronic acid for the case where 1 u/ml was added and 62.71 % guluronic and
40.80 % mannuronic acid for the case where no alginate lyase was added.
Even so the alginate was recovered in a higher yield when 1 u/ml alginate lyase was added,
it has been demonstrated in the SEC results that this is mostly alginic oligosaccharides
and so low molecular weight alginate. When no alginate lyase was added but a second
incubation at pH 7.5, 30 °C for 16 hours was performed, the recovered alginate presented
high molecular weight.
Hence, if high molecular weight alginate is the desired product, then the process selected
would be the two-step enzymatic treatment with CTec2. However, it has to be considered
that with alginate lyase, higher yields of alginate were recovered and even if they were

4.1.1
M/G ratios
Alginate is composed by a sequence of mannuronic and guluronic acid which can vary over
a wide range. They have the ability of forming high viscous gel solutions. The gel formation
is tightly related to the ratio of guluronic to mannuronic acid (M/G). The higher the content
of guluronic the more rigid the gel results and in the same way the lower its content the
more elastic and weak the gel will be. The M/G ratio is an important factor as it determines
the quality, mechanical strength, porosity, molecular weight and uniformity of alginate gels.
By contrast, the ratio depends on the extraction method, the seaweed growth conditions and
the resulting content of guluronic and mannuronic residues [9].
To the two incubation steps treatments selected after the DOE analysis, the M/G ratios
have been found to be 0.531 when alginate lyase was added (1 u/ml) and 0.651 when no
alginate lyase was added. This M/G ratios denote that when alginate lyase was added more
G-residues were extracted. In this way, if gels were to be formed, when alginate lyase was
added they would be brittle and dense gels, whereas when no alginate lyase was added they
would be more elastic gels.
4.1.2
Puriﬁcation of alginate
Once the alginate was enzymatically extracted to the liquid fraction, the next step was
to separate it from the remaining compounds. In this way, alginate was precipitated and
converted into a reﬁned powder. The idea would be to purify it until it could be packaged
and commercialized.
Some reviews supported the idea of using ultraﬁltration or microﬁltration membranes in
a cross-ﬂow mode. They were aiming oligosaccharides so fractionation of alginate was
being performed by gel chromatography [65]. However, membranes cannot be used for high
molecular weight alginates and moreover membranes are expensive and can clog easily.
In this study, it was carried out a puriﬁcation step which was found to work only when
high molecular weight alginates are targeted. The puriﬁcation consisted in the addition
of 2% CaCl2 to the liquid fraction and then it was left to settle in the fridge for 2 hours.
After settlement, the precipitate that would contain the calcium alginate was centrifuged
and freezed dried. Although the proposed puriﬁcation uses a chemical, it has been reduced
from a concentration of 10% CaCl2 [66] used in chemical extractions to a 2%. Furthermore,
the number of puriﬁcation steps would be reduced, as well as overcoming the water waste
issue presented by the chemical process.

4.2
Seaweed bioreﬁnery
The bioreﬁnery concept consists in a perspective in which high value added products produc-
tion comes along with the complete utilization of the biomass. In other words, bioreﬁnery
is the integration of different biomass conversion processes to produce high value added
products and energy on the same facility. Advantages of bioreﬁnery are that is a zero waste
approach, that it can develop sustainable industry economy and it can minimze the impact
in climate change [67].
Seaweed has the potential to be a valuable feedstock for bioreﬁnery due to its composition
and moreover is a 3rd generation bioresource as it does not have food or feed competition
[68]. Depending on the type and species, different components can be extracted. In the case
of S.latissima alginate, fucoidan, laminnarin, monosacharides as well as proteins and lipids
could be potentially extracted and used in the industry.
Seaweed bioreﬁnery applied to the case of S.latissima is shown in Figure 23. The polysaccha-
rides such as alginate can be used not only in food industry but also in the pharmaceutical or
cosmetic industry. Fucoidans and laminarin can be used as biological components providing
health beneﬁts. Glucose and mannitol can be used mainly in food industry as yeast growth
media or sweeteners.
Regarding the energy storage carbohydrates of S.latissima (laminarin and mannitol), they
can also be used as feedstock for biofuels, such as biogas, bioethanol or biodiesel.

4.3
Chemical vs Enzymatic extraction
The chemical extraction follows simple chemistry principles, in which alginate can be pre-
cipitated straightforwardly. The process is cheap and well-established in the industry (ISP
Alginates UK Ltd Waterﬁeld or Danisco Cultor).
So why should we try to compete with an enzymatic alternative?
The chemical extraction consists in converting the insoluble alginate in soluble sodium
alginate. Then it is precipitated in either calcium alginate or alginic acid, depending if
calcium chloride or acid chloridric is added, respectively, and after they are converted again
to sodium alginate. However this time in a mixture of alcohol and water solution in which
sodium alginate does not dissolve. This simple process leads to three different difﬁculties:
the water supply, the product colour and the waste disposal [29]:
• Large amount of water is used in the dilution of the initial alkaline extract in order to
reduce its viscosity and be able to be ﬁltered. Thereby a reliable water supplier is a
necessity for a chemical alginate factory.
• If the seaweed used is highly coloured such as Ascophyllum, the resultant alginate
extract could also yield in a dark product and thus limiting its value to technical
applications. In this way, many times the seaweed is ﬁrstly soaked in a formalin
solution that will bind the pigments to the cellulose and so most of the colour is left
behind when the alkaline extract is ﬁltered.
• Regarding the waste generated, it can be differentiated between waste waters and solid
wastes. Waste water contains calcium and acid, they can either be cleaned and pumped
into the sea or investigate a way of recycling it within the factory. For its part, the solid
residues need also a sustainable disposal. There have been some attempts of using it as
feedstock for the production of bioethanol but it does not seem economically feasible.
To overcome those difﬁculties and non-sustainable problems associated to the chemical
extractions, the enzymes come into play. Enzymes can be speciﬁcally selected to digest the
glycosidic bonds present in the seaweed material and increase like this the overall porosity
of the seaweed cell wall. This will facilitate the release of the desired biological compounds
from the interweaved matrix. In this way, enzymatic treatments convert insoluble compounds
from the seaweed´s cell wall to soluble in aqueous medium. This feature would greatly
reduce the amount of water required during the extraction and their speciﬁcity simpliﬁes
the puriﬁcation steps as only the enzyme would be introduced and it can be re-utilized as
animal feed or even food supplements (cellulases and lyases are food-friendly) [69].
Moreover, as enzymatic extractions do not use organic solvents, they do not have issues with
dangerous or toxic discharges [70]. This characteristic together with the fact that their are
not aggressive extraction conditions that could deteriorate the cell wall, causes that all the
components of the cell wall can be used in the bio-integration and waste zero concept of
bioreﬁnery.

Nevertheless, enzymatic treatments have also their weaknesses. On the one hand, enzymes
are expensive [70] and for an enhanced use of them, their optimal experimental conditions
should be determined. This means that the enzymatic processes demand adequate oper-
ational enzymatic conditions to be able to extract the desired compounds. Consequently,
the incubation time, temperature, pH and enzyme/substrate ratio concentration should be
variables to optimize in each speciﬁc product that wants to be extracted.
On the other hand, a non-chemical puriﬁcation process has yet not been found and so the
precise purity of the ﬁnal alginate powder has yet not been determined and thus cannot be
compared with the one obtained in the chemical process.

Conclusions and further work
5.1
Conclusions
Alginate has an increasing demand due to the increased requirement of food and pharma-
ceuticals derived from a global growth of the population. The traditional extractions of
alginate from the cell wall of macroalgae use chemicals, large amounts of water and have
waste disposal challenges. Therefore, there is a need for a more environmentally friendly
alternative. Enzymes could facilitate the extraction of alginate and preserve the remaining
carbohydrates of the cell wall. In this way, all the seaweed biomass could be used for other
applications, enabling the bioreﬁnery concept and leading to a more sustainable process.
In the present study, the enzymatic alginate extraction from S.latissima was investigated
with two different approaches. The ﬁrst approach was to perform a single incubation with
Cellic CTec2. This extracted only 12.51 % of guluronic and 10.63 % of mannuronic acid.
Hence, a second approach was carried out, in which the addition of alginate lyase was
studied as a second incubation of the process. A screening design was performed to target
which conditions were the optimal for alginate´s extraction. The results showed that if high
molecular weight alginates were the desired product then the two-step incubation process
without alginate lyase, pH 6, 30 °C and 5 h would be the optimal process with recovery
yields of 62.71 % of guluronic and 40.80 % of mannuronic acid. If low molecular weight
alginates were the product of interest then a two-step incubation process with 1 u/ml of
alginate lyase, pH 6, 30 °C and 16 h would be the process selected with recovery yields of
92.88 % of guluronic and 49.32 % of mannuronic acid.
The addition of CTec2 was also studied for protein extraction. However, it was showed that
it was not able to release a large fraction of nitrogenous compounds in the liquid fraction. In
this way, most of the protein was left in the solid residue.
As a ﬁnal assessment, the reuse of the liquid fraction after the CTec2 treatment was inves-
tigated as a growth medium for three yeasts (C.jadini, S.cerevisiae and Y. triangularis). The
growth tests concluded that all yeasts tested could consume the sugars contained in the
liquid fraction, which were mainly mannitol, glucose, galactose and fucose.

5.2
Future perspectives
In general the alginic sugars have been recovered with low yields with CTec2 and alginate
lyase extractions. Moreover, the addition of alginate lyase lead to low molecular weight
alginates. The evaluation of other types of enzymes such as fucoidanases, which will cleave
fucoidan instead of alginate, should be studied. They could improve extraction of higher
molecular weight alginates.
The use of a ﬁlamentous fungi should also be investigated as it could be an alternative to the
use of commercial enzymes. They are known to produce a range of extracellular enzymes as
a survival strategy when they face a lack of nutrients [72]. An advantage of the ﬁlamentous
fungus is that they can attach to the algal substrate more tightly than enzymes and can
spread more efﬁciently over the algal surface.
In this study, a screening design was performed to determine the parameters inﬂuencing
the extraction of alginate, when alginate lyase was added. They resulted to be a signiﬁcant
amount of variables and combinations to account for, causing challenges in the interpretation
of their interaction. In this way, it should be investigated another screening design studying
only two parameters, providing more conclusive and clear results.
In the carbohydrates analysis section, a loss in the recovery of the sugars was observed due
to a heterogenous destruction by acid hydrolisis. Therefore, a parameter evaluation of the
variables involved in acid hydrolisis should be performed to avoid the destruction of sugars
in their radicals.
