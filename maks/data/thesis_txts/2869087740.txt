Abstract
Wind energy has emerged as a cornerstone in electricity production. By 2030, it is predicted,
within the European Union, that the main capacity will be from onshore wind turbines. Noise
generated by wind turbines is a critical barrier to setting up new wind turbines near urban areas.
Understanding aeroacoustic noise from the turbine blades, is therefore an area of importance
for wind turbine manufacturers.
The aim of this project was to model the flow past a wind turbine airfoil, namely the NACA-0012,
at a Reynolds number of 3 × 106 and analyse the aeroacoustic self-noise to identify the effect,
which vortex generators and various angles of attack have on noise levels.
The hybrid approach was used in tandem with computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to predict
self-noise, applying the Farassat 1A solution to the Ffowcs Williams-Hawkings equations.
The force, pressure and skin-friction coefficients from the steady-state RANS k −ω SST result
was in accordance with experimental data and XFOIL, validating the numerical model. The
RANS models were then switched to the transient k −ω SST Improved Delayed Detached
Eddy Simulation (IDDES) model.
Analysis of the surface pressure power spectral density (PSD) spectra and the 1/3-octave band
sound pressure level (SPL) spectra, displayed broadband noise at non-zero angles of attack.
Increased angles of attack resulted in the self-noise levels increasing in the low frequency range
and decreasing in the mid frequency range. Both findings were in agreement with the empirical
BPM model.
The addition of vortex generators prompted a shift in the self-noise to higher sound pressure lev-
els in the mid and high frequency ranges. Increasing angles of attack caused a slight frequency
decrease of the highest sound pressure levels in the mid frequency range. The self-noise spec-
tra were fairly tonal at low angles of attack, but showed a trend to broadband at higher angles
of attack. These findings identified the effect of vortex generators on airfoil self-noise.
Opposite, at zero angle of attack without vortex generators, the tonal noise was attributed to
laminar vortex shedding, normally seen at lower Reynolds numbers. The results indicated, that
the IDDES model was not suited to model such flows. A main limitation was identified to be the
switching behaviour due to the shield function.
To confidently expand the analysis of SPL and PSD spectra, increased data sampling is needed,
as the spectra exhibited poor data resolution at low frequencies, due to high computational
costs. This would also increase the flow through-times required for further verification of the
change to periodic boundary conditions in an attempt to trigger vortex dissipation.
Expanding on the analysis of this project, investigation of a wider range of operating conditions
is necessary to account for real-world variations, in the flow fields which wind turbines operate
under.

1
Introduction
As the global energy sector transitions from fossil fuels to sustainable energy sources, wind
energy has emerged as a cornerstone of electricity production and, in 2023, accounted for
54% of the electricity supply in Denmark. Of the total wind energy production in 2023, onshore
wind turbines accounted for 56%, while offshore installations contributed 44% [1]. Onshore
wind energy production has also increased at a global level. The global cumulative capacity of
onshore wind energy increased more than fourfold during the 2010 to 2023 period, from 178
GW to 944 GW. During the year 2023, only the capacity of solar photovoltaic (PV) installations
was globally greater than the capacity of onshore wind installations [2].
The levelized cost of energy (LCOE) is a measurement used to assess and compare alternative
methods of energy production. LCOE can be thought of as the average total cost of building
and operating an energy producing asset per unit of total electricity generated over an assumed
lifetime [2]. During the period 2010-2023, the global weighted average LCOE of onshore wind
energy was lower than that of offshore wind energy [2]. It must be noted, that geographical and
time specific differences in LCOE are lost, when the global weighted average LCOE is calculated
for a time period like 2010-2023. Despite cost increases experienced in the time following the
corona pandemic, onshore wind remains the cheapest option together with solar PVs for new
electricity installations in most countries. Furthermore, the long-term expectation is, that LCOE
from wind (both onshore and offshore) will continue to fall [3]. Within the European Union it is
projected, that the installed wind power capacity will reach 393 GW in 2030, of which the majority
(310 GW) will be onshore, also in most Member States with a coastline [4]. Together, this
demonstrates the continued relevance of onshore wind energy. Still, the placement of onshore
turbines near urban areas has raised concerns about noise exposure in the development of
new turbine sites. Noise is a recognized stressor to the human body, which, depending on the
exposure, can result in several unwanted physiological effects [5].
In wind energy, there are two main sources of sound, mechanical sound and aerodynamic
sound. Whereas mechanical sound is created by the components of the wind turbine, aerody-
namic sound is created by the movement of the wind over the turbine blades. The phenomena
underlying the aerodynamic sound are complex fluid dynamic interactions between the blade
surfaces and the air itself. These phenomena are challenging to characterize, making them
difficult to understand and mitigate and therefore an area of great interest for wind turbine man-
ufacturers. Of aerodynamic noise, blade self-noise is a major contributor and is an area of
extensive research within wind turbine blades. Blade self-noise arises from the interaction be-
tween an airfoil and turbulence in its boundary-layer and wake. When the incoming airflow is
smooth, self-noise becomes the dominant noise source [6].
The field of aerodynamic sound, also known as aeroacoustics, originated in 1952 with Sir James
Lighthill’s work on aerodynamic sound. His theory, known as Lighthill’s Acoustic Analogy, pro-
vided the foundation for understanding sound generation by flow, and it remains a cornerstone
in modern aeroacoustic research [7]. Building on this theoretical framework, subsequent em-
pirical studies, such as the development of the Brooks-Pope-Marcolini (BPM) model by Brooks
et al. [8] and theoretical extensions of Lighthil’s work by Ffowcs Williams and Hawking, have
yielded advancements in the ability to predict self-noise emissions based on airfoil geometry
and flow conditions.
One approach to modifying airfoil aerodynamics and potentially altering noise emissions, is the
use of vortex generators (VGs). Vortex generators are small aerodynamic devices installed
on airfoils to delay flow separation and improve aerodynamic performance. While vortex gen-
erators improve wing performance, they also introduce additional flow structures, which can
Page 1 of 90
==PAGE:20==
significantly influence aeroacoustic characteristics. Understanding these interactions is crucial
for developing strategies to mitigate noise emissions, while maintaining aerodynamic efficiency.
More specifically, it is essential to characterize the generated vortices and their role in self-noise
generation, with the goal of understanding their effects.
The aim of the this study therefore to model the flow past a wind turbine airfoil, namely the NACA-
0012, and analyse the aeroacoustic self-noise to identify the effect, which vortex generators and
various angles of attack have on self-noise levels.
Numerical modelling of the flow past a wind turbine airfoil and analysis of the aeroacoustic
sound will be conducted to identify the effect, which vortex generators and various angles of
attack have on the generated vortices and hence on sound levels. Building on Ffowcs William-
Hawking’s equation of aeroacoustics, a transient numerical model will be set-up to investigate
the aeroacoustic characteristics of a simplified wind turbine airfoil, specifically the NACA-0012.
The model will be used to predict noise emissions from the airfoil both with and without vortex
generators. Additionally, the effect of angles of attack of 0◦, 5◦, 10◦and 15◦on noise levels
will be analysed to distinguish between the self-blade noise generated by the vortex generator
vortices and due to the angle of attack.

2
Theory
To model and analyse the flow physics past an airfoil and model transient flow phenomena,
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is used. Accurate modelling of such flows requires a foun-
dational understanding of fluid mechanical principles, including the fundamental conservation
laws governing mass, momentum and energy. Additionally, a detailed understanding of turbu-
lent flows and boundary-layer characteristics is needed to address the turbulence closure prob-
lem and select an appropriate turbulence model, critical for accurately capturing high Reynolds
number flows around the airfoil. The performance of airfoils is characterized by analysing the
lift, drag, pressure and skin-friction coefficients.
Aeroacoustic theory, needed to calculate the noise emissions as a result of the flow-induced
forces, employs the Farassat 1A solution to the Ffowcs Williams-Hawkings equations, which
expand the Lighthill analogy to quantify noise emissions. Implementing the aeroacoustic equa-
tions in a transient CFD simulation to identify noise levels and sources, is known as the hybrid
method in computational aeroacoustics (CAA).
This theory section will outline the laws, derivations and principles, which form the basis of the
numerical flow model and subsequent aerodynamic and aeroacoustic analyses carried out in
this project regarding the NACA-0012 with and without vortex generators (VGs).
2.1
Assumptions
The flow in this study is modelled under several simplifying assumptions, commonly applied in
aeroacoustic and fluid dynamics simulations.
For the aerodynamic flow, the following assumptions have been made: First, the flow is as-
sumed to be incompressible, a valid approximation, when the flow velocity is less than 30% of
the speed of sound [9]. Second, the fluid is treated as Newtonian, with constant viscosity and
no temperature variations, ensuring that viscous forces are negligible except near solid bound-
aries, where the no-slip condition is imposed [10]. Third, at high Reynolds numbers, viscous
effects are assumed negligible throughout the flow field, except in the boundary-layers near
the airfoil surface, where they are crucial for capturing the boundary-layer development [11].
Finally, external energy sources are excluded, and temperature variations are neglected.
For the aeroacoustic calculations, the following assumptions have been made: The incom-
pressible flow assumption implies, that density fluctuations, which generate sound waves, are
primarily driven by pressure fluctuations [12]. The resulting sound waves are assumed not to
feed back into the flow generating them, consistent with the weak acoustic feedback observed
in low Mach number flows [13]. Furthermore, the noise propagation is considered free-field,
meaning sound waves are neither reflected by surfaces nor absorbed by them [13].
2.2
Conservation Laws
In fluid mechanics, the primary governing principles are the conservation laws of mass, mo-
mentum and energy. These laws provide the foundation for analysing fluid behaviour and can
be expressed in two main forms: integral and differential [9].
The integral form of these equations, typically used for derivations, describes the conservation
principles over a control volume, accounting for the fluxes of mass, momentum and energy
across the boundaries of the control volume. In the integral form, conservation laws are applied
over a control volume, where the net flux of mass, momentum or energy across the control
volume boundaries must balance any changes within it [9]. For transient analyses or scenarios,
Page 3 of 90
==PAGE:22==
where localized flow details are critical, the integral form may not provide sufficient resolution,
necessitating the use of the differential form.
The differential form of these equations of motion are coupled with non-linear partial differential
equations for the dependent flow-field variables of density, velocity, pressure, temperature etc.
The differential equation form of conservation laws is obtained by applying Gauss’ divergence
theorem to the surface integration term of the integral equation form. The differential form of
the conservation laws are often used in literature.
Below, the conservation laws will be presented in the differential form, whereafter the assump-
tions of the flow field analysed in this project, seen in Section 2.1, are applied, simplifying the
governing equations. The simplified governing equations will be applied in the simulation set-up
of the numerical models in Section 3.1.

2.3
Turbulent Flows
Turbulent flows are ubiquitous in engineering and natural systems, but their inherently chaotic
and non-linear nature makes them exceptionally challenging to model and solve using the
Navier-Stokes equations directly. While the equations theoretically describe fluid motion, solv-
ing them for turbulent flows is computationally prohibitive for most practical applications [15].
To address this, the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations provide a simplified
framework by averaging the fluctuating turbulent components over time. However, this averag-
ing introduces additional unknowns, resulting in what is commonly referred to as the turbulence
closure problem.
This section outlines the derivation of the RANS equations, discusses the resulting closure
problem and provides a foundation for understanding laminar flow transition to turbulent flow,
boundary-layer separation and the turbulent boundary-layer theory, including the bursting pro-
cess, leading to the requirements of turbulence modelling approaches detailed in Section 2.5.

2.3.2
Boundary-Layer Theory
Boundary-layer theory is a fundamental concept of fluid mechanics describing the behaviour
of fluid flow near solid surfaces. According to the no-slip condition, the fluid velocity at the
surface of a solid body is zero [9]. As the distance from the surface increases, the velocity rises
until it reaches the free-stream velocity, U∞, which represents the undisturbed flow far from
the surface. The region, where this velocity transition occurs, is called the boundary-layer. In
fluid dynamics, boundary-layer analysis provides insights into both laminar and turbulent flow
behaviour as well as the transition region.
In a laminar flow over a smooth surface, viscous forces dominate, resulting in a smooth or-
derly motion. However, as the fluid flows over a surface, the Reynolds number, being directly
proportional to a distance, increases as seen in Equation 2.11
Re(x) = U∞x

where U∞is the flow velocity, ν is the kinematic viscosity and x is a characteristic length in the
stream-wise direction such as the length from the leading edge [15].
The increasing Reynolds number means, that the inertial forces of the flow become more dom-
inant over the internal viscous forces. Beyond a critical Reynolds number, Recrit, the inertial
forces trigger instabilities in the laminar flow, which can no longer be contained by viscous
effects, leading to the transition to a turbulent flow. One of the primary mechanisms respon-
sible for this transition in the absence of significant external disturbances, is the amplification
of small disturbances within the boundary layer, known as Tollmien-Schlichting (T-S) waves.
These waves manifest as stream-wise oscillations, which grow under the influence of adverse
pressure gradients and eventually break down into turbulent structures [15].
Noticeable differences between the laminar and the turbulent boundary-layer exist. A fully tur-
bulent boundary-layer has a higher surface shear stress, τw, than the laminar boundary-layer
[9]. The increase in friction occurs, because the turbulent fluctuations produce more wall-normal

near the wall in the buffer layer 5 < y+ < 70 [15]. The third flow region is the logarithmic layer,
y+ > 70, where turbulence dominates the momentum transfer [15]. The logarithmic layer region
is also characterised by turbulence intensity and production, but to a lesser degree, since it has
a smaller velocity gradient compared to the buffer layer [18].

turbulent structures, with energy concentrated in low-frequency motions [19]. Moreau et al.

Bursting Process
The bursting process describes the mechanism of larger coherent structures sweeping into the
boundary-layer from the mean flow, breaking up into smaller coherent structures, which are
then ejected from the buffer layer, resulting in the ejection and sweep motions [15]. The major
contributions to the Reynolds shear stress comes from the ejection and sweep motions of the
bursting process, although the interaction motions can also contribute to the Reynolds stress
[15].
According to Nychas et al. [21], fluid ejections originate at the wall distance 5 < y+ < 50 for a
smooth-wall surface. This is within the buffer layer, where turbulence production is strongest.
The ejected fluid from the bursting can reach a wall distance, y+, of 80 to 100 [15, 21]. On the
other hand, Corina et al. [22], reported that the quasi-cyclic bursting events occur at a higher
wall distance range 5 < y+ < 70. The two studies were conducted at a Reynolds number of
900 and at 20 000 and 50 000, respectively, suggesting that the phenomena associated with the
bursting process and its y+ location exhibit some Reynolds number dependency. However, in
both studies the bursting events occur within the buffer layer. This could be expected, since the
wall y+ itself is also Reynolds number dependent. It is important to note, that these observations
were made in a pipe flow, which may influence the dynamics compared to other geometries,
such as airfoils. As the measurements were taken in a pipe flow, their use in describing the flow
around an airfoil must be approached with caution, because the geometric and flow conditions
differ significantly. The adverse pressure gradients and possible boundary-layer separation will
likely alter the turbulence structure and bursting behaviour compared to the turbulent flow in a
pipe. Nevertheless, the results provide valuable insights into near-wall turbulence and ejection
dynamics within the turbulent boundary-layer, which may influence the logarithmic.

2.5.1
Turbulence Energy Models
Three widely used turbulence energy models are the k −ε, k −ω, and k −ω SST models.
The k −ε model, often employed in industrial applications, provides a foundation for turbulence
closure, but struggles in boundary-layer flows [14]. To overcome this, the k−ω model introduces

the best suited model for external airfoil flows and was used for this project. Below the basic
formulation for the selected turbulence energy model is presented, and the assumptions and
accuracy for the application in the flow of this project is discussed. Further details about the
k −ε and kω models can be found in Appendix A.

2.5.2
Scale-Resolving Simulation Model
Unlike the turbulence energy models, that are based on the RANS-equations, the scale-resolving
simulation models solve the large coherent structures and model the small-scale coherent struc-
tures. These are the Large-Eddy Simulation (LES) and Detached Eddy Simulation (DES) mod-
els.
DES is a scale-resolving model, that continuously switches between RANS mode and LES
mode to resolve unsteady large-scale turbulent structures [14].
The LES model was initially considered as the transient model for this project, however it was
abandoned due to feasibility considerations, detailed in Section 6.3.2. Further details about the
LES model are found in Section A.3.
The DES model is a Hybrid RANS-LES method (HRLM). The aim of HRLM is to combine the
computational efficiency of RANS models with the scale-resolving accuracy of LES to improve
modelling of complex flow phenomena at high Reynolds numbers within feasible computational
effort [26]. In DES, boundary-layers and irrotational flow regions are modelled using RANS,
while unsteady separated regions with sufficient grid resolution are treated with LES, effectively
combining the strengths of both approaches [14].

2.6
Aeroacoustics
The aeroacoustics considered in this project will be limited to blade self-noise. This is the noise
generated from the interaction between the airfoil and turbulence in the boundary-layer and
turbulent wake interactions.
The theoretical foundations of aeroacoustics are rooted in Lighthill’s acoustic analogy, which
reformulates the Navier-Stokes equations to model the sources of sound in turbulent flows [12].
Building on this, the Ffowcs Williams-Hawkings (FW-H) equation extends Lighthill’s equation to
calculate noise from moving surfaces [13]. Practical implementations, such as Farassat’s For-
mulation 1A, simplify the FW-H equation for computational fluid dynamics code applications,
called the hybrid method, enabling the prediction of noise from complex geometries and un-
steady flows in the far-field [13, 14].
In addition to the theoretically derived equations, Brooks et al. [8] developed an empirical aeroa-
coustic model to predict noise levels of airfoils.
This section will present an overview of the theories and apply the assumptions for the project
to simplify the theoretical equations. Furthermore, the empirical model and the characteristics
of tonal and broadband noise are presented.

2.6.4
The BPM Empirical Aeroacoustics Model
The Brooks, Pope and Marcolini (BPM) model is a widely used empirical method for predict-
ing airfoil self-noise, particularly for applications such as wind turbines and aircraft [8]. Devel-
oped based on extensive wind tunnel experiments, the BPM model provides a framework for
estimating noise contributions from five distinct sources. These are turbulent boundary-layer-
trailing-edge noise, separation stall noise, laminar-boundary-layer-vortex-shedding noise, tip
vortex formation noise and trailing-edge-bluntness-vortex-shedding noise [8].
Unlike theoretical models, such as the Ffowcs Williams-Hawkings (FW-H) equation, which re-
quire detailed flow simulations and computational resources, the BPM model offers a semi-
empirical approach based on measured data, making it suitable for rapid assessments. How-
ever, its validity is known to be restricted due to its reliance on simplified assumptions about
turbulent flow behaviour [29]. The restrictions of the BPM model are attributed to the tuning pro-
cess of the model, based on measurements of the NACA-0012 airfoil shape only, making the
model potentially less accurate, when using other airfoil shapes [29]. As this project focuses
on the NACA-0012 airfoil, the mentioned short-comings of the BPM model are therefore not
expected to be of great influence.
In the context of this study, the BPM model serves as a benchmarking tool to validate CFD-
based noise predictions and to asses the contribution of trailing-edge noise at various flow
conditions. The program NREL AirFoil Noise (NAFNoise), created by Moriarty et al. [35], is
used for the BPM model noise calculations.

2.6.5
Tonal and Broadband Noise
Tonal and broadband noise are used to describe distinct differences in noise emissions. Tonal
noise is characterised by the periodic fluctuation of the acoustic pressure at a specific frequency,
focusing the energy within a narrow frequency range, yielding power peaks and causing a hum-
ming at steady pitch [36]. Tonal noise is the result of vortex shedding of the laminar boundary-
layer [37]. Broadband noise is due to pressure fluctuations at random frequencies, causing
the energy to be distributed over a wide range of frequencies, with no distinct energy peaks at
specific frequencies. It arises from the interaction between the trailing-edge and the turbulent
boundary-layer, and is often described as a swooshing noise [36, 37]. The spectral character-
istics of tonal noise are seen as discrete spikes, while broadband noise is uniformly distributed.
2.7
Computational Fluid Dynamics
In computational fluid dynamics (CFD), numerical methods are essential to solve the governing
equations of fluid flow. This section provides an overview of the numerical methods, spatial and
temporal discretization schemes, meshing strategies, transition modelling and computational
aeroacoustics techniques applied in CFD simulations. These concepts form the foundation for
understanding the physical models and computational approaches used for the steady-state
and transient simulations carried out in Section 4 and 5.
The CFD software Simcenter Star-CCM+ can simulate internal and external fluid flows across a
wide range of flow regimes and for a variety of fluid types. It solves the conservation equations
for mass, momentum and energy in both incompressible and compressible fluid flows [14].
Detailed simulation parameters and set-ups used in this work will be provided in Section 3.
2.7.1
General Numerical Methods and Schemes
Numerical methods in CFD approximates a solution by discretizing the flow volume into smaller
domains or cells and then solving the governing differential equations numerically in the dis-
cretized domains in space and time, producing results at discrete grid points or cell centroids
[38]. The accuracy of these numerical solutions is dependent on the spatial and temporal dis-
cretization schemes as well as the quality and resolution of the computational mesh [38].
The physical phenomena are often idealized or approximated, as described in Section 2.2.
This simplifies the governing equations thereby lowering the computational requirements and
solution time, without affecting the accuracy of the mathematical representation substantially
[38]. The idealizations need to be introduced with caution, so as not to influence the solution
too much. Therefore, idealizations must be understood to avoid simulated physics that are not
representative of real world effects.
The governing equations are discretized throughout the volume mesh, typically using the finite
volume method (FVM), converting the differential equations into integral forms, which can be
solved within each cell volume. The FVM approach is widely used in CFD due to its conservation
properties and flexibility in handling complex geometries [39].
For steady-state RANS simulations, only the spatial discretization needs to be considered. For
transient simulations, both spatial and temporal discretization is needed, when stepping forward
in time. Below, the discretization methods and schemes, applied in this project, are presented.
Spatial Discretization Schemes
In CFD simulations, the accuracy, stability and computing cost of the solution are all directly
impacted by the choice of spatial scheme [14]. In turbulence modelling approaches, like De-
Page 19 of 90
==PAGE:38==
tached Eddy Simulation (DES), proper discretization ensures, that flow gradients, diffusion and
convection from the governing equations are adequately captured [40]. The default spatial dis-
cretization method for DES in Star-CCM+ is the Hybrid-BCD (Bounded Central Differencing)
scheme [14]. This scheme combines Bounded-central differencing and second-order upwind
differencing schemes for the system. For numerical stability and robustness, the first differenc-
ing method is used in near-wall regions, which are dominated by RANS. This hybrid approach
reduces numerical dissipation and improves the resolution of turbulent fluctuations in LES re-
gions [14, 40]. It offers a balance between the numerical stability of upwind schemes and the
accuracy of central differencing [14].
The discretization scheme used in the steady-state RANS simulations carried out in this project
was the second-order upwind differencing scheme.

cretization [14]. This scheme approximates the temporal derivative of the unsteady term, using
the solution at the current time step, t, along with the solutions from the two preceding time
levels, t1 and t2. The formulation of the one-sided second-order backward differentiation for

relevant flow dynamics.
Solution Algorithms
In Star-CCM+, there are two available flow solvers, the coupled and segregated solver. The
coupled flow solver solves the momentum and continuity equations simultaneously. This typi-
cally provides better convergence behaviour for compressible and high-speed flows [14]. The
segregated flow solver solves the momentum and continuity equations in an uncoupled manner
by linking the momentum and continuity equations with a predictor-corrector approach, which
is suitable for constant density flow and has the advantage of requiring less memory and hence
computational resources [14]. The choice between segregated and coupled solvers depends
on the requirements of the CFD simulation, with coupled solvers offering better convergence for
compressible flows, while segregated solvers provide computational efficiency for low-speed,
incompressible cases.
As pointed out in Section 2.1, this project assumes incompressible flows, and therefore the
segregated flow solver has been selected.
2.7.2
Meshing Models
Grid discretization involves dividing the computational domain into smaller elements or cells,
forming a mesh. This discretized representation allows the governing equations to be approxi-
mated numerically within each cell [38].
Page 20 of 90
==PAGE:39==
Numerical methods approximate the continuous variables at the centroid position within each
cell. Thus, the grid resolution, being the size and shape of the cells, influences the calcula-
tions. A finer grid improves accuracy, but increases computational cost. To ensure trustworthy
numerical solutions, they must be tested for grid independence by refining the mesh, until the
results are independent from the grid resolution. The mesh convergence study conducted in
this project is outlined in Section 3.
The balance between computational efficiency and solution accuracy often requires grid refine-
ment in regions of high gradients, such as near boundaries or in turbulence-dominated flows
[38].
To discretize the computational domain, Star-CCM+ offers several meshing techniques. Each
mesh type has its own strengths and weaknesses. After a mesh has been generated, it can be
validated based on mesh quality and validity parameters. Although the mesh quality does not
guarantee a suitable solution of the numerical system, it is an essential step, in order to ensure
the solution is not affected by the quality of the grid system [14].
Often, a base mesh is applied in combination with a prism layer mesh to discretize the fluid
domain volume. Below, the two meshing models used in this project, polyhedral mesh and
prism layer mesh, will be explained. The advantages and limitations of the polyhedral mesh is
compared to the trimmed mesh to explain, why the polyhedral mesh was chosen as the base
mesh for this particular project. The validity parameters will be used to verify the theoretical
reliability of the generated mesh in Section 4.
Polyhedral Mesh
The polyhedral mesh is based on the tetrahedral mesh, which offers a simple solution and uses
the least amount of computation time [41]. The reason for processing the already generated
tetrahedral cells into polyhedral cells, is that tetrahedral cells are not well suited for use with
complex geometries [41]. To generate the polyhedral mesh, Star-CCM+ calculates the initial
cell count from the underlying tetrahedral mesh, consisting of four-sided triangular pyramids.
The polyhedral cells typically have an average of 14 cell faces. This mesh process makes the
polyhedral mesh efficient to build and requires no more surface preparation than the equivalent
tetrahedral mesh. The polyhedral mesh contains approximately one-fifth the number of cells of
a tetrahedral mesh for a given starting surface, thus requiring fewer computational resources
[14]. Hence, the polyhedral mesh offers a computationally-efficient and accurate solution for
complex flows, because the many cell faces allow for computations in multiple directions out of
the cells, which improves calculations for complex and turbulent flows [14].
The polyhedral mesh has been used as the base mesh throughout this project. This is due
to the expected high pressure gradients for simulations of airfoils at high angles of attack and
airfoils with VGs. These regions require a fine mesh to calculate the flow parameters in high
resolution without having to generate too many cells, which would overload the available com-
puter resources. In addition, the polyhedral mesh offers the advantage of allowing the volume
to grow uniformly, whereas a trimmed mesh volume always grows cubic. This allows the user
to refine the mesh close to the geometry and at expected high pressure gradients and to reduce
far-field resolution, where the ambient flow is not affected by the geometry-controlled flow char-
acteristics. It can be done without a sudden jump in mesh resolution, which could interfere with
the numerical calculation. On the downside, the unstructured nature of the polyhedral mesh can
result in cell faces not aligned with the flow direction, which can introduce numerical diffusion
[14]. The numerical diffusion can affect the accuracy of the solution, especially for regions with
steep flow gradients, if the mesh resolution is too coarse.
Page 21 of 90
==PAGE:40==
Trimmed Mesh
The trimmed volume mesh consists of predominantly hexahedral cells, which may be trimmed
to follow the boundary of surfaces. Like the polyhedral mesh, it yields a computationally effi-
cient and accurate solution. The trimmed mesh is considered suitable for external flows, as its
structured cell layout often translates into faster convergence and lower solver computational
requirements [14]. In addition, the trimmed mesh cells use the Cartesian coordinate system and
can therefore align with the direction of the flow. This further allows the generation of anisotropic
cells elongated in a desired direction [14]. On the downside, the trimmed mesh is restricted to
a cell growth with multiples of two. This may lead to abrupt transitions in cell sizes, especially in
regions of high flow gradients, affecting solver performance and accuracy, especially in regions
with complex flow characteristics [14]. Therefore, the trimmed mesh has been deselected for
this project.
Prism Layer Mesh
Prism layers are a fundamental aspect of mesh generation for simulations involving wall-bounded
flows, such as the external flow over an airfoil. These layers consist of structured, orthogonal
cells adjacent to wall surfaces, which accurately capture the steep velocity and pressure gra-
dients as well as other flow characteristics present in the boundary-layer. This capability is
especially critical in external flow simulations, where resolving boundary-layer dynamics signif-
icantly impact flow features such as separation points, pressure distribution and aerodynamic
forces [14].
A critical metric for evaluating boundary-layer resolution is the dimensionless wall distance, y+.
This parameter is used to determine, how well the mesh resolves the near-wall region in turbu-
lence models, including RANS and DES. Achieving an initial cell height corresponding to y+ < 1,
ensures that the viscous sublayer is directly resolved. This is critical for high-fidelity simulations
[14]. For coarser meshes, where y+ > 30, prism layers implement wall functions, that aim to
bridge the gap between near-wall physics and the bulk flow. Star-CCM+ recommends using
10–20 prism layer cells in the cross-stream direction within the boundary-layer to accurately
capture wall-bounded turbulence profiles [14, 42]. An insufficiently resolved boundary-layer
can lead to inaccurate predictions of drag and lift as well as errors in identifying separation
and reattachment points. Using prism layers ensures, that the solver captures the steep gra-
dients normal to the wall, significantly improving the predictive accuracy of wall-bounded flow
simulations [14, 42].
Prism layers also reduce numerical diffusion near the wall. Numerical diffusion is a discretization
error, that smears discontinuities and steep gradients in a finite volume advection scheme.
Numerical diffusion is minimized, when the flow is aligned with the mesh. Therefore, the use of
prism layers in the base mesh enhances accuracy as a result [14].

2.7.4
Transition Modelling
As detailed in Section 2.3.2, flow over the airfoil begins as laminar flow and transitions to turbu-
lent flow with an increasing local Reynolds number. This transition process is often overlooked
by the RANS turbulence models, but transition modelling is critical for high Reynolds number
flows, where a transition from laminar to turbulent flow impacts boundary-layer characteristics,
aerodynamic forces and overall flow behaviour [14]. Therefore, incorporating a transition model
is needed to account for this effect [43].

alignment with physical flow characteristics [14]. This is particularly relevant for the NACA-0012
airfoil, where accurate transition prediction is essential at high Reynolds numbers.
The γ −Reθt transition model does not account for cross-flow instabilities, which causes cross-
flow induced transition, and is typically seen on swept airfoil, sickle wings and inclined prolate
spheroids, all of which are known test cases for cross-flow transition of the boundary-layer
[44]. A correction term proposed by Langtry et al. [44] accounts for the cross-flow effects in
the γ −Reθt model. The term was specifically designed to model the instabilities caused by
span-wise pressure gradients in swept wings [44]. For the unswept NACA-0012 airfoil, used
throughout this project, the cross-flow is not expected to influence the boundary-layer transition
[44]. However, for the unswept airfoil at high angles of attack, span-wise components and sep-
aration effects could lead to three-dimensional boundary-layer behaviour. The chaotic nature of
a separated boundary-layer may lead to cross-stream disturbances, although they do not align
with phenomena, which the cross-flow term was designed to address [44]. If the flow remains
largely symmetric and dominated by stream-wise effects, the cross-flow term may not signifi-
cantly contribute or improve the accuracy of the simulation. However, if span-wise variations
are present at high angles of attack or for airfoils with VGs, the cross-flow term could improve
the accuracy of the simulation. The additional source term has therefore been added to the
numerical simulations used in this project.
In summary, the transition model provides a solid framework to improve accuracy in capturing
the transition effects of high Reynolds number flows, contributing to more accurate predictions
of boundary-layer behaviour and aerodynamic performance.

Computational Aeroacoustics
Computational aeroacoustics (CAA) is a subset of aeroacoustics, which utilizes numerical meth-
ods to model and simulate noise generation from fluid flows. Noise sources arise due to in-
teractions between the fluid and solid surfaces, as well as turbulent flow structures, leading
Page 24 of 90
==PAGE:43==
to pressure fluctuations and the propagation of sound waves [12]. The foundation of CAA is
based on Lighthill’s inhomogeneous wave equation, which provides insight into aerodynamic
noise generation mechanisms [12].
Two primary approaches are commonly employed in CAA in order to predict far-field acous-
tics: The direct simulation method solves the compressible Navier-Stokes equations to capture
sound wave propagation directly. The hybrid method utilizes solutions such as the Farassat 1A
form of the FW-H equations to compute the acoustic field separately from the aerodynamic field
[14].

pressure caused by a sound wave. The noise signature from the sound waves exhibit a cor-
responding noise spectra showing the intensities of the sound pressure level over a range of
frequencies [14]. The sound pressure level (SPL) quantifies noise intensity as

Summary
Key takeaways are summarized below and outline the theory relevant for the simulation set-up
of a steady-state validation simulation and the set-up of the following transient aeroacoustic
simulation and analysis.
The aerodynamic flow is assumed to be an incompressible Newtonian fluid where viscous ef-
fects are significant only within the boundary-layers.
The continuity equation ensures mass conservation under the incompressibility assumption,
while the Navier-Stokes equations govern momentum conservation. Averaging the Navier-
Stokes equations leads to the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations for turbu-
lence. The averaged and fluctuating fluid components lead to the turbulence closure problem.
Page 25 of 90
==PAGE:44==
The k-ω SST RANS and Improved Delayed Detached Eddy Simulation (IDDES) turbulence mod-
els are applied to close the system of equations. The choice of turbulence model is a balance
between computational efficiency and accuracy in capturing complex turbulent structures.
Boundary-layer transition from laminar to turbulent flow occurs, when the inertial forces within
the flow exceed the viscous forces. This is characterised by the Reynolds number. A turbulent
boundary-layer has a higher momentum exchange near the surface, causing the boundary-
layer to remain attached in an adverse pressure gradient. The location of transition has profound
effects on the force characteristics of an airfoil. The turbulent boundary-layer consists of distinct
regions, which can impact the fluid flow and the aeroacoustic sound due to the bursting process.
Aeroacoustic calculations rely on the weak acoustic feedback assumption applicable to low
Mach number flows, with free-field noise propagation considered, neglecting reflection and ab-
sorption effects. Noise emissions, predicted in the far-field using Farassat’s formulation 1A
solution to the Ffowcs Williams-Hawkings equations, enables the estimation of noise contribu-
tions from aerodynamic loading, which may be either tonal or broadband. The Brooks, Pope,
and Marcolini (BPM) empirical model is used to provide a comparison of the aeroacoustic model
results.
In numerical simulations, such as those conducted using the Star-CCM+ software, the mesh is a
critical step. The polyhedral mesh with prism layers may be used to ensure accurate boundary-
layer resolution while maintaining computational efficiency. For temporal discretization, an im-
plicit second-order backward differencing scheme is adopted to ensure numerical stability. The
transient simulations apply a second-order backward facing scheme, where the Courant num-
ber is considered to ensure a proper time step size.

To simplify the analysis and allow easy scaling of the results, the validation and subsequent
simulations were non-dimensionalized, ensuring the Reynolds number depended only on the
Page 30 of 90
==PAGE:49==
kinematic viscosity. This approach guaranteed compliance with the incompressibility condition
U∞< 0.3M. Non-dimensionalization is crucial in fluid dynamics, as it allows for comparisons
across different flow conditions and aids in identifying dominant forces within the system [9].
Non-dimensionalization involves the transformation of the governing equations by removing
physical units, making it possible to describe flow characteristics using dimensionless variables.
This technique is widely applied in systems described by differential equations, such as the
Navier-Stokes equations, to generalize results and facilitate their application across varying
scales [9]. Based on independent scales, the dependent scales can be non-dimensionalised.
For the Navier-Stokes equations, the dependent scales are the velocity, time and pressure
scales. These scales may be non-dimensionalized, using the independent scales density, the
chord length and the reference velocity.
The primary objective of non-dimensionalization in this study was to simplify the governing
equations by normalizing key parameters to 1, thereby enhancing numerical efficiency and fa-
cilitating the interpretation of results. Once the simulation results were obtained, they were be
re-dimensionalized to their physical scales for post-processing.

3.3
Simulation Mesh Generation
A polyhedral volume mesh was employed for the computational domain of the airfoil with and
without VGs, due to its accuracy in handling complex flow structures compared to other meshes
[14]. In the initial validation simulations, there were problems with divergence. Since the flow
past an airfoil is dominated by stream-wise characteristics and is dependent on the resolution
of the boundary layer, the solution problems could be attributed to the triangular surface mesh.
This parameter was therefore changed to quad-dominant, which resulted in better cell alignment
in the principal curvature direction compared to triangles giving a high-quality boundary-layer
resolution [14]. This resolved the divergence issues.
The volume growth rate governs the rate of transition from the defined custom cell sizes to the
base mesh. To capture the near field flow properties properly, Star-CCM+ recommends setting
this value between 1.05 and 1.15 [14]. In this project, the volume growth rate is therefore set
to 1.1. To increase the surface mesh resolution, in an attempt to avoid new divergence issues
as a result of the surface mesh, the surface growth rate was set to ”Slow”. The surface growth
rate specifies the maximum rate of face edge transition, meaning the edge of the neighbouring
cells cannot grow quickly [14].
For this study, 50 prism layers were employed, with a near-wall thickness of 4.28 × 10−6 m
and a total thickness of 5.0 mm. These parameters ensure adequate resolution of the turbulent
boundary-layer for the NACA-0012 airfoil to accurately capture near-wall effects. The number
of prism layers and the near wall thickness ensure, that the non-dimensional wall distance is
y+ < 1, allowing for a fully resolved boundary-layer [14].
To enhance mesh accuracy near the airfoil surface, volume and surface refinement zones were
applied to critical regions such as the leading and trailing edges, where steep pressure gradients
and separation effects were anticipated. The applied mesh refinements focused on achieving
a balance between computational cost and solution accuracy. In the case of the airfoil with
VGs, additional volume and surface refinements were made downstream from the VGs. The
resulting mesh configuration consists of approximately 8.95 × 106 cells for the airfoil without
VGs and approximately 10.31×106 cells for the airfoil with VGs. The details of the default mesh
settings used in the simulations are summarized in Table 3.3. The resulting meshes can be
seen in Appendix D.

The objective of mesh analysis is to determine the most appropriate mesh configuration to

3.5.1
Mesh Convergence Study
As the cell refinement increases, the discretization error decreases, increasing the simulation
accuracy until a point of diminishing returns relative to computational cost [38]. Therefore, a
balance of accuracy and computational cost must be found.
The mesh convergence was carried out at Re = 3 × 106 at α = 15◦. The high angle of attack
was chosen to ensure, that mesh convergence was achieved during the most unstable flow
conditions. At higher angles of attack, the flow over the airfoil becomes increasingly complex
due to phenomena like boundary-layer separation, leading to adverse pressure gradients and
vortex shedding. These complex flow features are sensitive to the mesh resolution, which is
why performing the mesh convergence study at α = 15◦ensures, that the mesh is capable
of capturing critical aerodynamic phenomena with sufficient accuracy. This further ensures,
that the numerical model is robust and reliable over the entire range of angles of attack. The
evaluation was based on the numerical results from Star-CCM+ simulations using the RANS

4
Steady-State Validation
This section will describe the results of the steady-state RANS simulations used to validate the
results with comparable experimental data.
The validation was based on the average force coefficients (lift and drag) as well as pressure
and friction coefficients. The coefficients were obtained directly through Star-CCM+ reports.
These were then compared with external experimental data and results from the widely used
program XFOIL. The numerical calculations were carried out at Re = 3×106 for angles of attack
α = [0◦, 4◦, 5◦, 6◦, 8◦, 10◦, 12◦, 14◦, 15◦].
4.1
Validation against External Experimental Data
Validation of the steady simulation was carried out by comparing the force, pressure and skin
friction coefficients with experimental data by Gregory et al. [48] and Abbott et al. [49], respec-
tively. For unavailable experimental data, the numerical results were compared with the results
from XFOIL [50] at the corresponding Reynolds number.
Gregory et al. [48] carried out several wind tunnel experiments of a NACA-0012 airfoil with a
0.76 m chord and a span of 2.7 m at several angles of attack and Reynolds numbers. The lift
forces were obtained through integration of the pressure distribution of the centre section of the
model measured at 43 static pressure holes. The drag was obtained from measurements of
total-pressure, loss in the wake and 2 static-pressure tubes located 0.1 chord downstream of
the airfoil trailing edge. According to Gregory et al. [48], the measurements of lift and drag are
integral processes, and experimental errors from individual readings tend to be averaged out
statistically.
The experimental data from Abbott et al. [49] were obtained from experiments carried out over
a model with a chord length of 0.696 m and a span width of 0.9144 m, corresponding to the width
of the test section. The lift was measured by integration of pressures representing the reaction
on the floor and ceiling of the tunnel. The drag was obtained from wake measurements. This
method allows for the forces to be obtained from such measurements with a high degree of
accuracy [49].
Below, the force coefficients (lift and drag) from the numerical results of this study are com-
pared to the external experimental data. Furthermore, the pressure and skin-friction coefficient
distributions are compared to the external experimental results obtained by Gregory et al. [48].
4.1.1
Force Coefficients
Figure 4.1a displays the relationship between the lift coefficient, CL, and angle of attack, α, for
the RANS numerical results, XFOIL and the experimental data described previously. It is clear,

the experimental results. At increasing angles of attack, it is seen, that the numerical results
still follow the Gregory et al. [48] and Abbot et al. [49] and also, that all results deviate slightly
below the results from XFOIL. At an angle of attack α = 0◦, the lift coefficient, CL, is expected
to be near zero. These experimental benchmarks provide a target for assessing the accuracy
of the numerical simulations.

RANS numerical results of the NACA-0012 without VGs compared to the experimental data.
From the figure, it is clear, that the numerical results estimate lift and drag coefficients well

5
Results of Transient Simulations
This section will describe and analyse the results of the transient IDDES simulations of the
NACA-0012 airfoil with and without vortex generators (VGs). For each case, a simulation was
carried out at varying angles of attack to allow for a comparison of the self-noise emission
between the airfoils.
The flow models applied by the steady-state models in Section 4 were switched to the transient
flow models. In addition to the steady-state simulation conducted according to the set-up in
Section 3, the airfoil surface pressure over the chord-length at the mid-span was measured at
each time step. The power spectral density (PSD) spectra of the fluctuating surface pressure
were generated to provide insights into the flow characteristics and coherent structures, that
influence the aeroacoustic behaviour of the airfoil due to pressure fluctuations, both with and
without VGs. The PSD spectra were generated at 0.95C, downstream of the VGs and near the
trailing-edge, to capture relevant flow structures.
An acoustic receiver was placed in the midspan plane 1 m from the trailing edge of the airfoil
perpendicular to the chord line on the suction side of the airfoil. The aeroacoustic pressure
fluctuations from the loading noise were calculated over time and propagated to the far-field
using the hybrid method, specifically the Farassat 1A formulation to the FW-H equations. The
1/3-octave sound pressure level (SPL) spectra were obtained using a Fast Fourier Transform
(FFT) to analyse noise levels and their corresponding frequencies.
The computed noise levels from the numerical models were compared to the experimental
data from Brooks et al. [8]. Since their experiments were conducted on a NACA-0012 airfoil,
the BPM model used in NREL AirFoil Noise (NAFNoise) is inherently calibrated to reproduce
these experimental results for that airfoil. However, because the maximum chord length in the
experiments was only 0.38 m [8], NAFNoise was employed to apply the BPM model’s scaling
laws to adjust the noise predictions for the chord length used in this study (1 m).
A scaling study was carried out on the surface pressure PSD spectra. The spectra should obey
a power-law behaviour, fn, due to the turbulent energy cascade [37]. According to Hwang et al.
[54], no universal scaling law exists for the anisentropic surface pressure fluctuations, although
the predicated scaling of f−5 by Blake [55] is generally accepted [37, 56]. The scaling law has
been highlighted in the numerical result PSD spectra, where observed.
The theoretical resolvable frequency was calculated using the Nyquist criterion. However, as
mentioned in Section 2.7.5, this does not consider the mesh cut-off frequency. Figure 5.1 dis-
plays an example of the entire sampled PSD spectrum from the numerical models. As seen in
the figure, data at very high frequencies, observed in the PSD and SPL spectra, were found to
exhibit random fluctuating behaviour, reminiscent of numerical noise. It was therefore deduced,
that frequencies above about 3000 Hz were beyond the mesh cut-off frequency of the mesh and
could be excluded from the results shown in subsequent PSD and SPL spectra.
The transient simulation results for the NACA-0012 airfoil with and without VGs at angles of
attack of 0◦, 5◦, 10◦and 15◦, are examined below.

side, as expected for a symmetric airfoil at this angle of attack. The spectra exhibited distinct

Comparison of Sound Pressure Level Results
The 1/3-octave band SPL of the numerical results, at all analysed angles of attack, were com-
pared in Figure 5.26. In the case of the airfoils without VGs, it was seen, that increased angles
of attack resulted in increased sound pressure levels in the frequency range from 20 Hz to about
200 Hz. This was in agreement with experiments carried out by Bahr et al. [61] over a NACA
63-215 airfoil at an estimated Re = 2.95 × 106. They reported, that, at higher angles of attack,
increased levels of low frequency noise were recorded. In the mid frequency range from 300 Hz
to 1000 Hz, disregarding the tonality of the α = 0◦airfoil, the numerical results from the α = 5◦
displayed the highest noise level, whereafter the airfoil at α = 10◦and finally α = 15◦had the
lowest noise level. This showed the inverse sound pressure level relation to the angle of attack
compared to the observed relation at low frequencies.
Due to above findings, it was found relevant to compare the PSD spectra for the airfoils without
VGs. Throughout the results of the numerical models, at non-zero angles of attack, the power
density on the suction side of the airfoil was consistently higher than on the pressure side. It was
seen, that, with increased angles of attack, the magnitude of the power density on the suction
side increased, while the magnitude on the pressure side decreased. This may be attributed to
a power displacement towards the suction side taking place at increasing angles of attack. The
observation aligns to a certain degree with the prior SPL observations.
Similar trends were not seen in the case of the airfoils with VGs due to little or no data in the
low frequency range. In the mid frequency range, the airfoils with VGs exhibited higher sound
pressure levels than the corresponding airfoils without VGs. This suggested, that the effect of
adding VGs to the NACA-0012 airfoil generally causes a shift in the frequency spectrum from
the low to mid frequency range. The notable exception to this observation was the airfoil at
α = 0◦without VGs. The low frequency trend was also not observed in the PSD spectra for
airfoils with VGs. At α = 0◦and 5◦, the pressure side displayed higher power densities than the
low to mid frequency range. However, at α = 15◦, the suction side displayed the highest power
density.
Figure 5.27 compared the SPL for the airfoils with and without vortex generators at α = 5◦and
15◦, respectively. As previously described as a frequency shift, the VGs yielded a higher SPL
from about 300 Hz. At α = 5◦, the shift was seen as a tonal spectrum with a frequency peak
at 400 Hz. At α = 15◦, the spectrum remained largely broadband, although two distinct bumps
occurred at 160 Hz and 320 Hz, suggesting the latter was a second harmonic. In addition, a
peak was seen at 507 Hz, generated by the airfoil at α = 0◦with VGs. These three observa-
tions suggested, that the noise range with the highest SPL slightly propagated towards lower
frequencies with increasing angles of attack, within the mid frequency range. In Figure 5.26b,
the somewhat tonal spectra at α = 0◦and 5◦, compared to the largely broadband spectrum at
α = 15◦, may further indicate, that increasing the angle of attack causes the self-noise of the
airfoils with VGs to go from fairly tonal to fairly broadband.
Aeroacoustic experimental results by Brooks et al. [8], over an untripped NACA-0012 airfoil
with a sharp trailing-edge and a chord length of 30.48 cm at α = 0◦, 5.4◦, 10.8◦and 14.4◦, were
carried out at 71.3 m/s, corresponding to a Reynolds number of 1.5 × 106 [8]. At α = 0◦and
5.4◦, the results were broadband with a maximum sound pressure level at about 3 kHz. The
sound pressure level of the airfoil at α = 0◦was the lowest at 47 dB. At higher angles of attack,
α = 10.8◦and 14.4◦, the results were tonal with maximum sound pressure levels of 74 dB and
84 dB at 3 kHz, respectively. These are the external experimental results, which in part were
used to create the empirical BPM model applied by NAFNoise. This shows, that the BPM model
was defined based on the results of many airfoil chord lengths and tripping methods. As the
BPM model was calibrated on these results, the model encompasses a wide range of flow

6
Discussion
This section will discuss the key findings, challenges and considerations related to the aeroa-
coustic simulations of the NACA-0012 airfoil. The credibility of applied assumptions, the validity
of the used transient turbulence model and the feasibility of an alternative turbulence model are
discussed. In addition, the numerical method is discussed to provide insights to the credibility
of the results and to consider an alternative approach.
6.1
Discussion of Results
The tonal noise spectrum of the airfoil at α = 0◦was found to agree with spectra characteristic
with laminar boundary-layer shedding. This was supported by a Strouhal number, correspond-
ing to laminar boundary-layer shedding of 0.2. At this high Reynolds number, the boundary-layer
of the airfoil should have been fully turbulent, resulting in a broadband spectrum and no coherent
vortex shedding, corresponding to the results from Brooks et al. [8] and NAFNoise predictions.
The results for the airfoil at α = 0◦without VGs could therefore be deemed an outlier. The
source-of-error was expected to be the limited capability of the transient model to accurately
capture the flow-field. This is further discussed in Section 6.3.1.
Unlike the airfoil without VGs, no patterned behaviour was determined for the SPL spectra
or PSD spectra of the airfoils with VGs. Due to limited sampling of the low frequency range,
comparison between airfoils with and without VGs was limited to the mid and high frequency
range. This limitation was particularly significant, as a trend in the low frequency range of the
airfoils without VGs had been identified, and further investigation into the impact of VGs in this
frequency range, could have provided insights.
6.2
Discussion of the Aeroacoustic Approach
While the hybrid method used in this project may be suitable for the theoretical and computa-
tional modelling of noise, some of the simplifying assumptions given in Section 2.1 may not fully
represent real-world conditions.
It was assumed, that the flow was incompressible. While this assumption is considered valid
for flow velocities below 0.3M, it also has effects on the aeroacoustic equations. The incom-
pressible flow assumption implies, that the sound waves are not generated by compressible
flow fluctuations, but are driven by pressure fluctuations. This limited the aeroacoustic informa-
tion captured by the FW-H hybrid model [29]. As seen in Section 2.6, the assumption reduces
the density driven Lighthill stress tensor, leading to the quadrupole term to be considered neg-
ligible. Martinez-Lera et al. [62] studied a CD airfoil at Re = 1.6 × 105 and reported, that the
quadrupole source term could contribute to high frequencies. Looking at the 1/3-octave band
sound pressure level spectra in Section 5, the numerically modelled sound pressure levels in the
high frequency range were consistently lower than the empirical NAFNoise results. Although
many other contributing factors could be the reason behind the under-prediction, one could be
the neglection of the quadrupole source term.
It was further assumed, that the sound waves would propagate in a free-field. This assump-
tion is considered reasonable, if the sound sources, such as wind turbine blades, are in areas
without surfaces, that can reflect sound waves. However, the presence of mountains or large
buildings, such as high-rise buildings and warehouses, will offer surfaces, from which sound
waves can be reflected. It should therefore be considered, whether such conditions are close
to the sound source, before the assumption is deemed credible. The free-field assumption
further implies, that the generated sound waves propagate through a homogeneous medium.
Page 79 of 90
==PAGE:98==
Air is considered a homogeneous fluid, and, according to the American Meteorological Society
[63], a homogenous atmosphere can be assumed to have a height of 8 km. The homogeneous
assumption is therefore considered valid in wind turbine applications.
6.3
Discussion of the CFD Approach
Below, the considerations to the approach, taken in the CFD software Star-CCM+, are consid-
ered. Potential beneficial changes to the simulation procedures are discussed with the main
focus of achieving vortex break-up due to dissipation.
6.3.1
DES Blending between RANS and LES
One of the key challenges encountered, was the lack of break-up of the vortices generated by
the vortex generators. Due to the high Reynolds number and strong vorticity, it was expected,
that the vortices would show velocity fluctuations and dissipate. Another encountered challenge
was the observed coherent structures on the airfoil at α = 0◦. These were attributed to eddies
seen at much lower Reynolds numbers and were not expected in the results. Both deviations
from expected behaviour could be attributed to the chosen k −ω SST IDDES model and the
RANS to LES transition problem.
The Delayed Detached Eddy Simulation (DDES) model incorporates a shielding function to
protect the RANS region, particularly in the boundary-layers, from premature transition to LES
[14]. This approach often results in the so-called grey area problem, where the transition from
RANS to LES occurs too slowly, leading to delayed turbulence development and compromised
accuracy [64]. The DES turbulence models in Star-CCM+ are defined, so boundary-layers and
regions of irrotational flow are solved using the RANS model [14]. In the case of the airfoil without
VGs at α = 0◦, the flow was highly irrotational. The risk of such a scenario is, that the RANS
solver dominates the transient IDDES model in most of the flow field and thus overestimates
the turbulent viscosity, νT [65]. As the Reynolds number is dependent on the viscosity, the
overestimated νT corresponds to locally solving the flow at a lower Reynolds number. Figure 6.1
shows the Reynolds number calculated by the effective viscosity for the modelled airfoil without
VGs at α = 0◦. It is clear, that the local Reynolds numbers are high at the stagnation point and
near the wall at the airfoil leading-edge. In Section 4, the laminar to turbulent boundary-layer
transition was determined to occur at about 0.4C. This corresponds to a significant decrease
in the local Reynolds number from the defined Re = 3 × 106 in the near-wall region. At the
trailing-edge of the airfoil, the local Reynolds numbers are seen to occur in the near-wall regions
and adjacent to the blunt trailing-edge with regions of low Reynolds numbers further from the
surface. This local manipulation of the flow may explain the vortex shedding characteristics of
flows at lower Reynolds numbers observed at α = 0◦.
The high viscosity leads to an artificially rapid turbulent kinetic energy decay [65]. The lack of
resolved turbulence in the wake of the airfoil at α = 0◦suggested, that the IDDES model was
not designed for such flows, and that the shielding function maintained RANS-like behaviour
across the domain. This was apparent, as the DES methods have been designed to model
vortex shedding in a massively separated flow, where the grey area is small [14, 64]. Hao
et al. [64] addressed the grey area issue over a backward facing step by formulating a fifth-
order Adaptive Dissipative Compact Scheme (ADCS). They concluded, that ADCS reduces
numerical dissipation in the grey area and decreases the eddy viscosity. While ADCS is not
readily available in Star-CCM+, the scheme shows potential for application in the models of this
project, as it facilitates accelerated transition from RANS to LES [64, 27].
A potential solution to the dominant RANS effects, is the use of the Synthetic Eddy Method,

6.3.2
LES Feasibility
In the initial phase of this project, the Large Eddy Simulation (LES) approach was considered
to resolve the large-scale structures of turbulent structures around the NACA-0012 airfoil with
VGs and at high angles of attack. As mentioned in Section A.3, LES can offer high fidelity
for such simulations by directly resolving the larger turbulent eddies, while modelling only the
smaller scales [11]. However, the high computational requirements rendered it impractical for
this study. The reasoning for this could be divided into three parts: First, according to Pope [11],
to accurately capture the turbulent flow, LES requires a mesh resolution fine enough to resolve
80% of the turbulent kinetic energy. Despite reaching a mesh containing 35 × 106 cells, the
resolution remained insufficient, and further adjustments to the mesh near the VGs and wake
region would have been necessary to fully resolve small-scale instabilities. Increased mesh
resolution quickly proved computationally infeasible. Consequently, the attempt to implement
LES was abandoned. Second, the solver requires a Courant number of one in the flow-field
for aeroacoustic modelling to resolve the frequency spectrum [14]. This resulted in very small
time steps, further compounding the computational burden. Third, to obtain valid aerodynamic
and aeroacoustic results required several flow-through runs of the simulation, also discussed
in Section 6.3.5. Given the combination of the fine spatial resolution, the small time step size
and the desired simulation duration, the computational cost exceeded the realistic user access
to the computational resources of the DTU HPC cluster.
In summary, while LES is theoretically well suited for capturing the intricate turbulence and
aeroacoustic phenomena of the flow it was unfeasible for this project within the available com-
putational resources. As a result, it was determined, that, if LES was to be used, the Reynolds
number of the project would need to be lowered significantly, as the computational costs scale
proportionally to the Reynolds number, as seen in Section 2.7.3.
6.3.3
Numerical Diffusion
Related to the key challenges in Section 6.3.1, numerical diffusion was also an area of concern.
Numerical diffusion is a discretization error, that smears abrupt flow changes and steep gradi-
ents and is influenced by the grid resolution [14, 66]. The polyhedral mesh used in this project
can have cell faces, that are not aligned with the flow, which can cause numerical diffusion.
Prism-layers are aligned with the wall-bounded flow, mitigating numerical diffusion near the ge-
ometry surface [14]. Extending the prism layer and adding more prism cells, could have proved
beneficial. Additionally, it was mentioned in Section 2.7.2, that the trimmed mesh aligns with
the direction of the flow, which could have led to a reduction in numerical diffusion. Ultimately,
it was thought, that the accuracy of the numerical modelling of unsteady flow structures from
the VGs and the airfoil at high angles off attack would benefit from the unstructured grid offered
by the polyhedral mesh. Initial attempts at achieving convergence in the steady-state RANS
simulations, using the trimmed meshing model, failed, further adding to this notion.
As the numerical diffusion rate is influenced by the grid resolution in specific regions of the com-
putational domain, Cadiex et al. [66] suggested, that the numerical diffusion could be applied as
an adaptive mesh refinement criteria. An adaptive mesh refinement strategy could have proved
beneficial to the transient simulations, by improving mesh resolution in critical flow regions [14].

6.3.4
Flow-Through Limitations
An additional limitation in the project was the restricted simulation time, which was particularly
clear in the airfoil cases with vortex generators.
The consequence thereof, was limited data in the low frequency range in both the PSD and SPL
spectra. In the PSD spectra, the low data density resulted in ill-defined pwelch distributions,
increasing the uncertainty of drawn observations regarding the power spectral density at low
frequencies. The low resolution is a direct result of an insufficient number of flow-through times,
limiting the sampling of large scale coherent structures.
For transient flow analysis, Star-CCM+ recommends approximately five flow-through times in
order to reach convergence, whereafter the data sampling can be carried out over another five
flow-through times [14]. Due to computationally costly simulations, this was only achieved for
some of the transient simulations. Due to the attempts to trigger vortex break-up, the flow-
through times of airfoils with VGs were very low. Seldom, the simulations reached the recom-
mended convergence prior to the data sampling. This, however, was not the case for the airfoils
without VGs. Table 6.1 shows the number of flow-through times for each of the transient sim-
ulations carried out in Section 5. The reason for the variance in flow-through times across the
cases, is due to the fact, that the models with VGs were carried out with a time-step reduced
by a factor of ten. This change was carried out within Section 5 in an attempt to trigger vortex
dissipation. The low time step size, in combination with the late implementation of the periodic
side plane boundary conditions, was expected to be the reason why the effect of the changed
boundary conditions was not seen.
6.3.5
Side Walls of Simulation Domain
The expected effects of the symmetric and periodic boundary conditions were detailed in Section
3.2. It was expected, that the periodic boundaries would increase the vortex dissipation and
trigger break-up. The effect of the change to periodic boundary conditions, in the simulations of
the airfoil with VGs, were seen to be minimal. As described in the prior section, the number of
flow-through times is the expected reason why, the effect of changing the boundary conditions
was seen to be minimal. A potential solution to this, would have been to increase the time step
and accept a higher Courant number to quickly increase the number of flow-through times. To
validate, if the change to periodic boundary conditions had the desired effect, the measurement
probes should display large velocity fluctuations over time, and clear vortex break-up in the
instantaneous isosurfaces of the Q-criterion should be visible, contrary to the obtained results.

6.4
Summary
In summary, two of the considered problems are seen as the main sources of uncertainty re-
garding the quality of the results.
The first consideration is the lack of understanding of the switching criteria of the applied ID-
DES model, which may explain the presence of coherent structures typically observed at lower
Reynolds numbers on the airfoil without VGs at α = 0◦. A potential solution could have been
to examine the effect of the convection scheme or to apply a different turbulence model, as
DES models are designed for massively separated flows, such as airfoils at high angles of at-
tack. An unexplored option was to apply the Synthetic Eddy Method to generate large coherent
structures into the flow, potentially triggering the switch to LES.
The second consideration is the change to periodic boundary conditions. It was determined,
that the symmetric boundary conditions could have been responsible for the absence of the
expected flow characteristic. The change to periodic boundary conditions for the airfoil with
VGs was not tested at a higher time-step in order to validate the change in flow characteristics
with the new boundary conditions. The omission of this was due to another assumption, that
the lack of vortex break-up was primarily caused by to a high degree of numerical diffusion. The
time-step had therefore been lowered to decrease the Courant number, resulting in insufficient
flow-through times.

7
Conclusion and Recommendation
The aim of this project was to numerically model the flow past a wind turbine airfoil and analyse
the aeroacoustic characteristics. The study specifically examined the impact of vortex gener-
ators (VGs) at different angles of attack on self-noise generation of a NACA-0012. The hybrid
approach was used in tandem with computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to predict self-noise,
applying the Farassat 1A solution to the Ffowcs Williams-Hawkings equations.
The lift and drag force coefficients (CL, CD) and the pressure and skin-friction coefficient dis-
tributions (CP , CF ) were compared to external experimental data to validate the steady-state
numerical model for the airfoil without VGs. The validation showed a strong agreement with
experiments in terms of CL, CD and CP , while CF showed reasonable agreement
The power spectral density (PSD) analysis revealed, that the power density of the suction side of
the airfoils without VGs was higher than the pressure side. With increasing angles of attack, the
low frequency power density increased on the suction side, while it decreased on the pressure
side, displaying a power shift across the airfoil. The sound pressure level (SPL) spectra of the
airfoils without VGs, at non-zero angles of attack, were broadband. With increasing angles of
attack, the SPL in the low frequency range increased, while the SPL in the mid frequency range
decreased, in agreement with the empirical BPM model calculated by NAFNoise.
The addition of VGs prompted a distinct shift in the aeroacoustic characteristics. The VGs led
to higher sound pressure levels in the mid and high frequency ranges compared to the airfoils
without VGs. The results of the airfoils with VGs showed, that operating at α = 15◦would
yield the lowest maximum SPL value spectrum of all airfoils with VGs, and would therefore be
preferable, when only considering noise levels.
The NACA-0012 airfoil without VGs at α = 0◦generated coherent laminar boundary-layer shed-
ding vortices, resulting in tonal noise, significantly exceeding the SPL of all other airfoils, with
and without VGs. This was deemed to be an outlier because the transient IDDES model is not
suited for such flows.
Comparison of the airfoils with VGs at increasing angles of attack showed a slight frequency
decrease of the high SPL levels and a self-noise spectrum change from fairly tonal to fairly
broadband. The numerical results in the low frequency range of the airfoils with VGs had a
limited data density, due to insufficient flow-through times. Further work on this project should
therefore focus on increasing the amount of sampled data.
Challenges regarding the applied IDDES model need to be investigated and effects on flow
characteristics validated. Improved switching between RANS and LES, by mitigating the effect
of the shielding function, could improve artificially decreased Reynolds numbers. The numer-
ical model did not capture expected break-up of the vortices generated by the VGs. Attempts
to trigger break-up were not successful, in part due to computational costs. To improve the
numerical model, further studies into the effect of changing the boundary-conditions from sym-
metric to periodic should be considered over an increased number of flow-through times. In
addition, low mesh resolution could have led to numerical diffusion, contributing to the lack of
vortex break-up due to dissipation.
To apply these findings for wind turbines and to account for real-world conditions it is necessary
to evaluate the impact, that VGs have on the aeroacoustics in different flow-fields. Further
studies should therefore investigate self-noise generation across various Reynolds numbers
and chord lengths to ensure the findings of numerical modelling can be translated to conditions
met by wind turbines.
Page 85 of 90
==PAGE:104==
Noise generated by wind turbines is a critical barrier to setting up new wind turbines near urban
areas. Understanding aeroacoustics generated by the turbine blades is therefore an area of
importance for wind turbine manufacturers.
Main Take-Aways
1. Confirming existing literature for airfoils without VGs:
• Generated self-noise at non-zero angles of attack are broadband.
• Increase of sound pressure levels occurs in the low frequency range with increasing
angles of attack.
2. New findings for airfoils with VGs:
• Addition of VGs causes an increase in sound pressure levels in the mid frequency
range.
• Increasing angles of attack causes a slight frequency decrease of the highest sound
pressure levels in the mid frequency range.
• Self-noise spectra are fairly tonal at low angles of attack, but fairly broadband at
higher angles of attack.
3. Further work:
• Expand the analysis of airfoils with VGs to the low frequency range via increased
data sampling.
• Study the effect of periodic boundary conditions on VG vortex dissipation to improve
model accuracy.
4. Points to consider:
• The applied transient k−ω SST IDDES model is not suited for the low angle of attack
NACA-0012 models.

The k −ε turbulence model is widely used in industrial applications. The primary shortcoming

that the relationship between Reynolds stress and the mean rate of deformation is considered
uniform in all directions. Such an assumption significantly limits the performance of the model
in cases involving large adverse pressure gradients, curved boundary-layers and unconfined
flows [14].
While the k −ε model performs adequately for linear shear layers and recirculating flows, its
application is primarily confined to flows, where Reynolds shear stresses dominate or where
pressure gradients are relatively small. At high angles of attack, the flow over an airfoil of-
ten experiences high adverse pressure gradients, flow separation and complex boundary-layer
characteristics. The standard k −ε model struggles to capture these phenomena accurately.

A.3
Large-Eddy Simulation (LES) Models
Large-Eddy simulation (LES) applies the governing equations to directly compute the large scale
coherent structures of the turbulent flow and only model the small-scale coherent structures [11].
Solving only the large structures is advantageous, because they contain most of the turbulent
kinetic energy, accounting for the majority of momentum transfer in turbulent flows and are
primarily responsible for turbulent mixing [67]. Each solution variable is decomposed into a
filtered value and a sub-grid scale (SGS) component, representing the large and small turbulent
scales, respectively
φ = ˜φ + φ+
(A.8)
where φ represents a fluid flow variable, ˜φ represents the filtered (large-scale) component and
φ+ is the modelled SGS component of a fluid flow variable [14].
Spatial filtering removes smaller eddies and thereby reduces the range of scales, that must be
resolved [14]. LES filtering can be either explicit, where a filter function is applied to discretize
the Navier-Stokes equations, or implicit, where the grid resolution determines the resolvable
and modelled scales. In Star-CCM+, implicit filtering is used, requiring a grid resolution capa-
ble of capturing the energy-containing range, which is often influenced by geometry-specific
turbulence [14]. Implicit filtering takes full advantage of the grid resolution and is, in general,
computationally less expensive than explicit filtering [14].
As mentioned, the small coherent structures are not numerically resolved, but are instead mod-
elled using a sub-grid scale (SGS) model. According to Kolmogorov’s similarity theory, small-
scale turbulence is statistically isotropic and homogeneous at high Reynolds numbers [68]. This
Page 2 of 23
==PAGE:111==
theory allows SGS models, often based on the Boussinesq approximation, to accurately rep-
resent small-scale effects with fewer assumptions than RANS models, which must model all
turbulence scales [67, 14].
While LES offers significant advantages for capturing unsteady large-scale turbulence, it re-
quires fine a grid resolution in energy-dominant regions, making it computationally expensive.
To accurately capture the turbulent flow, the model requires a mesh resolution fine enough to
resolve about 80% of the turbulent kinetic energy. This means, the resolvable coherent struc-
tures are within the inertial subrange, where turbulent energy cascade takes place. However,
LES forms the foundation for hybrid models like DES, which aims to balance computational cost
with modelling accuracy [26].
Because LES solves the large scale unsteady motions directly, LES is more accurate in simulat-
ing transient flows with large-scale unsteadiness, such as flows involving unsteady separation,
large coherent structures and vortex shedding [11]. These flow characteristics are expected,
when simulating an airfoil at a high angle of attack or with VGs.

D.4
Mesh Quality and Validity
Below, the quality metrics for the mesh of the NACA-0012 airfoil without vortex generators are
assessed to identify and mitigate potential issues, which could affect the stability and accuracy
of the numerical solution. To accompany the assessment of quality standards, relevant plots
are generated and are seen in Figure D.12.
Cell Skewness Angle
The cell skewness angle, shown in Figure D.12a, measures whether the mesh cells allow proper
diffusion of physical quantities, such as momentum and energy, without introducing numerical
instability. Skewness angles below 85◦are generally acceptable for CFD simulations, while
angles exceeding 85◦are problematic [14]. At 90◦or greater, cells often exhibit concave ge-
ometries, which can lead to convergence issues due to non-physical numerical diffusion [14].
The skewness angle of the generated mesh is displayed in the figure and confirms, that the
majority of cells in the mesh exhibit skewness angles well within the acceptable range, ensur-
ing minimal risk of diffusion-related inaccuracies between cells. However, it is clear that some
cells exceed the acceptable skewness angle, but are still below 90◦, meaning these cells are
not concave. There are 2361 cells exceeding the skewness angle of 85◦, which is 0.026% of the
total cells in the mesh. Due to the low number of outliers beyond the 85◦threshold, the mesh is
validated as robust.
Face Validity Metric
The face validity, shown in Figure D.12b, evaluates whether the face normals point outward,
away from the cell centroid. In a cell with a bad face validity, one or more of the face normals
point inward towards the cell centroid [14]. A face validity of 1.0 means, that all face normals
are correctly pointing away from the cell centroid. Values below 1.0 mean, that some of the cell
faces have normals pointing inward, indicating some form of concavity. Values below 0.5 signify
a negative volume cell. Cells with a face validity below 1.0 are considered bad. Poor cell quality
is likely to affect both the robustness and accuracy of the solution. The figure demonstrates,
that the face validity metrics of the mesh are 1.0, with no evidence of negative or severely
concave cells. This finding confirms the mesh’s geometric integrity and suitability for accurate
simulations.
Cell Quality Metric
The cell quality metric, shown in Figure D.12c, assesses the overall geometric regularity of the
cells. Generally, flat cells with highly non-orthogonal faces have a low cell quality [14]. A cell
with a quality of 1.0 is considered perfect. A cubic cell is an example of such a perfect cell, but
other polyhedral cell shapes can also have a cell quality approaching 1.0. A degenerate cell has
a cell quality approaching zero. Cells with a face quality less than 1 × 10−5 are considered bad
[14]. Poor-quality cells can introduce significant errors into the solution, particularly in regions
of high velocity or pressure gradients. The cell quality metric displayed in the figure illustrates,
that the majority of the cells in the mesh are high quality with some cells approaching the critical
threshold of 1 × 10−5 with the lowest cell quality metric of 2.0 × 10−3. This demonstrates, that
the mesh is free from significant degeneracies or irregularities which could compromise solution
accuracy.
Volume Cell Metric
The volume cell metric, shown in Figure D.12d, measures the ratio of the volume of a cell to that
of its largest neighbour. Abrupt changes in cell volume can introduce numerical instability and
inaccuracies, particularly in regions with sharp gradients. Cells with a volume change of 0.01 or
lower are considered bad cells [14]. The figure shows, that the volume predominantly transitions
within acceptable limits. This ensures, that the numerical solvers can handle variations in flow
properties without encountering instability. As seen, some cells have a volume cell metric below
Page 21 of 23
==PAGE:130==
the lower limit and are considered bad cells. Only 1291 cells, corresponding to 0.014%, have
a volume change below 0.01. Because of the low percentage, the general mesh quality is
considered good based on the volume celll metric.
Chevron Quality Indicator
The chevron cell quality is shown in Figure D.12e. Chevron cells are pairs of thin slender cells,
which meet at a common face at an angle, such that the line joining the cell centres does not
pass through the common face. Chevron cells are marked as 1.0 and every other cell as 0.
Chevron cells are considered undesirable cells, as they disrupt the accuracy and robustness of
convective flux calculations [14]. The figure shows the chevron quality indicator. It is confirmed,
that there are no chevron cells present in the mesh, ensuring that convective fluxes are not
