Abstract
Lipopolysaccharides (LPS) are the molecules structuring the outer membrane of gram negative
bacteria. They exert a toxic effect from their Lipid A moieties with the capacity of triggering
an immunological host response. Sepsis is a high-mortality systemic disease caused by auto-
ampliﬁcations of the signalling molecules proinﬂammatory cytokines essential to the innate
immune system of the host. High systemic concentrations of LPS molecules can provoke sepsis.
Despite importance, no treatment targeting speciﬁcally sepsis is available. Developing sepsis
targeting drugs require an understanding of involved immunological host responses. Rendering
similar responses to what is observed in human, Göttingen minipigs have potential to serve as
great candidates for animal modelling. Suggestively, an improved approach of detecting LPS
in the circulation is very important for the progress of sepsis treatments. Further a hypothesis
states, that low-grade systemic inﬂammations caused by gut-derived LPS molecules, termed
metabolic endotoxemia, can be linked to metabolic diseases and dysfunctions, e.g. type II
diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular diseases. The investigation of this hypothesis requires
efﬁcient LPS detection methods. Nowadays, there is no well-tested reliable LPS detection
assay due to limitations in the predominating method Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL). This
study tested the limitations and a suppression pattern associated with sera components was
concluded. In relation, a novel and optimised detection method exploiting TLR-4 stimulus upon
the presence LPS were examined. The assay did not completely show optimal results but it
showed potential, thus further optimisation and implementation steps could be substantial for
LPS detection. LPS molecules injected into Göttingen minipig hosts showed clear indication of
immunological host responses in the manner of increased leukocytes, systemic concentration of
proinﬂammatory cytokines and acute phase proteins, and regulation changes in immunological
host gene expressions. Deﬁning a dosage of LPS in relation to a route of administrated requires
a balance between insufﬁcient host responses and toxic effects with fatal consequences for the
animal. An example of a balanced and efﬁcient dosage compared to both intravenous (i.v.) and
intramuscular (i.m.) injections of LPS is here concluded. Immunological assays linked multiple
systemic inﬂammatory biomarkers to different time-points in a time frame of 24 hours post
an injection of LPS. An increased understanding of circulating LPS molecules in Göttingen
minipigs was achieved and subsequently LPS detecting models and comparative studies of
systemic and local responses can be constructed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Lipopolysaccharides (LPS) is an essential part of the outer membrane structure of a gram-
negative bacterium. Popularly, the molecule is as well termed endotoxin and it is well-known
for its ability to trigger multiple immunological responses in a host. LPS serves as an important
molecule in innate immune response research models (Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009; Parker and
Watkins, 2001). The toxicity varies between different gram negative bacteria and not all subunits
of the molecule is signiﬁcantly conserved (Gnauck et al., 2016b).
It induces multiple biomarkers in a broad range of species. LPS challenge experiments increase
the understanding of the different immunological processes following local and systemic inﬂam-
mation (Seeley et al., 2012).
There is a need for reliable methods for detecting LPS in biological ﬂuids. In particular, detecting
or quantifying LPS in plasma or serum is challenging. Different trends have been associated
with endotoxin detection, but the most implemented method is the Lumilus Amebobocyte Lysate
(LAL) assay. The technique measures endotoxin in the circulation by exploiting clot forming as
a result of activation by endotoxin. A consuming aspect of the detection method is the contami-
nation from different parameters en sera (Dullah and Ongkudon, 2016). The current available
assay suffers from an inhibition by components in serum samples which create a unreliability in
detection results.
LPS induces the production of proinﬂammatyory cytokines. These proinﬂammatyory cytokines
are reliable biomarkers for indicating the presence of bioactive LPS (Medzhitov and Janeway,
1997). A variety of proinﬂammatory cytokines are induced by recognition of LPS including
tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and interleukin-6 (IL-6). The cytokines are signalling
molecules that in turn induce secondary responses, including the acute phase response in the host
by the induction of acute phase proteins. The acute phase proteins as well serve as signiﬁcant
biomarkers, especially serum amyloid A (SAA) and C-reactive protein (CRP) in humans and
pigs (Llamas Moya et al., 2006).
Local inﬂammation mediated by LPS exposure is likely to progress into a systemic inﬂammatory
response. A systemic inﬂammatory response induced by LPS is a result of the innate immune
response. A major element of the innate immune system responses to LPS is the secretion of
proinﬂammatory cytokines by neutrophils in the blood stream upon the encounter with LPS,
representing the direct system response (Beutler and Rietschel, 2003). Macrophages can as
well express the proinﬂammatory cytokines if LPS is present in the tissue. If these cytokines

reach the circulation it will be at a delayed subsequently time-point causing a secondary host
response. This indirect systemic response is interesting to distinguish from the direct response
in the manner of understanding LPS derived host responses. This understanding can attribute
to establishing a sepsis inhibiting treatment (Vinther et al., 2016). LPS is suggested to trigger
both a local and systemic responses arising at different time-points. In relation, variations in
LPS administrating routes could be suspected to create different response patterns (Seeley et al.,
2012).
Sepsis is an acute disease stage caused by drastically induced systemic inﬂammatory responses
and is a major type of critical illness. A quantitative world-wide study has demonstrated that
29,5% of intensive care unit (ICU) registered patients suffered from sepsis (Vincent et al., 2014).
Along with related syndromes i.e. severe sepsis, systemic inﬂammatory response syndrome
(SIRS), and septic shock, sepsis contributes signiﬁcantly to the mortality and morbidity in the
ICU (Martin et al., 2003). Despite this, no sepsis-speciﬁc treatment is available. An improvement
of pharmaceutical development in sepsis related treatments is needed, however there is a serious
limitation to efﬁcient clinical trials on drugs targeting sepsis in the ﬁeld. This issue is due to the
fact that the LPS molecule itself is difﬁcult to quantify in biological ﬂuids (Parker and Watkins,
2001; Cohen et al., 2015). Metabolic endotoxemia is a syndrome caused by gut-derived LPS
present in the blood circulation. Potentially, this condition could attribute to a constant low level
of LPS in the blood circulation of the host. A hypothesis of LPS migrating from the gut into the
blood stream causing a low-grade systemic inﬂammation will be considered and discussed in
this thesis (Gnauck et al., 2016b).
The LPS challenged Göttingen minipig model is potentially of great importance for understand-
ing and mimicking immune host responses similar to human (Parker and Watkins, 2001). The
model can furthermore serve as an applicable tool for various host response analyses. It is
essential to conclude an efﬁcient relation between dosage and the speciﬁc route of administration
when using pig models for LPS challenges. In combination with a novel LPS detection method
exploiting TLR-4 stimulus meaningful research for various challenges such as developing sepsis
treatments and linking metabolic endotoxemia to diseases including diabetes could be conducted.
The importance of sufﬁcient testing, validation, and implementation of this model in combination
with the reporter gene assay is very substantial.

CHAPTER 2
Thesis Scope And Objectives
The aim of this thesis is to produce and investigate a comprehensive immunological host response
analysis based on a LPS challenged Göttingen minipigs. The Göttingen minipig shall serve as an
animal model with increased similarities to a human model. Included in the systemic proﬁling
will be a host gene expression quantiﬁcation relevant to the immunological responses following
LPS injections, along with measurements of multiple pro-inﬂammatory cytokines and acute
phase proteins in plasma concentrations, i.e. IL, TNF, SAA, and CRP. A comparative analysis of
expression patterns shall be examined to determine a broad response understanding. The scope is
to be able to relate the results derived from a LPS challenge to an acute phase response. A further
objective of the thesis is to differentiate in route of administration in relation the injected dosage
regarding the observed systemic responses. Thus, a variation in the LPS injection method, i.e.
intravenous (i.v.) and intramuscular (i.m.) injections, will be implemented and applied in the
host response analysis. Finally, existing limitations in experimental endotoxin detection shall
be attempted to be resolved (Gnauck et al., 2015; Fennrich et al., 2016). In regard to the LPS
challenge in Göttingen minipigs, it is an objective of the thesis to apply an engineered optimised
TLR-4 stimulus-speciﬁc reporter gene assay provided by company Euro Diagnostica AB in order
to sufﬁciently detect the injected LPS molecules in the circulation of the host (EuroDiagnostica,
2016).

CHAPTER 3

3.1
Characterization Of LPS On Gram-Negative Bacteria
Bacterial lipopolysaccharides (LPS) are toxic molecules associated with gram-negative bacteria.
Gram-negative bacteria differ from gram-positive bacteria in structural and chemical manners.
Other cellular characteristics contributes to separating them from each other (Tran et al., 2005;
Gnauck et al., 2016b; Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002; Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009), in relevance to
endotoxic capacities of the cells, gram-negative bacteria possesses a outer membrane in extension
to the peptidoglycan layer. The layer is termed periplasm and is a composition that the gram-
positive bacteria do not contain (Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002). The inner cytoplasmic membrane
of the gram-negative bacteria is similar to the membrane of the gram-positive cells. Both are
constructed of a symmetric phospholipid bilayer, as the majority of all biological membranes
(Gnauck et al., 2016b; Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009). In contrast to this, the outer membrane of
the gram-negative cell is not assembled exclusively by phospholipids. The outer components
are traded for a LPS molecule layer. Thus, the outer membrane of a gram-negative bacterium
consists asymmetrically of an inner glycerophospholipid layer that is linked to a layer of LPS
molecules, illustrated in ﬁgure 3.1 (Gnauck et al., 2016b).

the location of LPS anchored by the lipid A in relation to the inner and outer membrane of the bacterium.
(Figure modiﬁed from (Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002)).

LPS molecules cover 75% of the outer membrane of the gram-negative bacteria (Wang and Quinn,
2010). The construction of these lipid-packed membranes creates a low membrane ﬂuidity. This
secondary membrane on the gram-negative cell allows an efﬁcient protection against antagonistic
substances and challenging environments. Furthermore, the membrane reduces permeability
of the cell barrier preventing the penetration of large and hydrophobic compounds (Raetz and
Whitﬁeld, 2002). E. coli is estimated to contain approximately 106 molecules of LPS on the
outer membrane per cell. The toxicity of LPS vary between different gram negative bacteria
(Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009). The following section 3.2 elaborates the synthesis and the general
structure of LPS.
3.2
The Structure And Biosynthesis Of LPS
Lipopolysaccharides as a molecule encompasses a structure composed of three regions, speciﬁed
a lipid A moiety, a core of oligosaccharide (OS), and O-antigen repeats (the outer carbohydrates)
(Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002; Gnauck et al., 2016b; Wang and Quinn, 2010; Beutler and Rietschel,
2003). Lipid A is linked to the inner OS of the core and the outer OS of the core binds to the
O-antigen of the molecule (Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002).
3.2.1
The LPS Molecule
LPS can mainly be divided into two different classes, a smooth(S)-LPS and a rough(R)-LPS
indicated by the structure of characteristics in O-antigen and OS of the given species (Raetz
and Whitﬁeld, 2002; Gnauck et al., 2016b). S-LPS is an intact LPS molecule, where R-LPS
is a trimmed LPS molecule produced by a bacteria with mutations causing defaults in the LPS
assembling. S-LPS is possessed by speciﬁc gram negative bacterium, i.e. Enterobacteriaceae,
Pseudomonadaceae, Pasteurellaceae, and Vibrionaceae. Besides the S-LPS and R-LPS, a
third structure exists. It is characterised by the absence of O-antigens but the presence of
oligosaccharide extensions in the inner OS core, and thereby assembling a molecule structure
deﬁned as lipooligosaccharides (LOS). The LPS structure shows the largest species variation in
the outer core of OS, whereas the lipid A anchor and inner OS core often carry great similarities
from species to species(Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002; Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009). This will be
further explained in the following; subsection 3.2.2, subsection 3.2.3 and subsection 3.2.4.
3.2.2
Lipid A
Lipid A is a phospholipid particularly constructed of two glucosamine molecules. The component
is an anchor-shaped ligand responsible for the toxic effect of the LPS (Raetz and Whitﬁeld,
2002). Lipid A is furthermore the most conserved component of LPS in various gram negative
bacteria (Wang and Quinn, 2010). It is the toxic component in the LPS molecule. Lipid A of
E. coli contains two phosphate groups and six acyl chains composed of either 12 or 14 carbons.

3.2. THE STRUCTURE AND BIOSYNTHESIS OF LPS
7
Production of LPS molecules ensures the bacterial growth and it requires an initial synthesis of
the Kdo2-lipid A complex (Gnauck et al., 2016b), ﬁgure 3.2. 3-deoxy-D-manno-octulosonic
acid (Kdo) is an ulosonic acid essential for synthesis of LPS (Babinski et al., 2002). Due to
species variation, the lipid A synthesis will be elaborated speciﬁcally for E. coli. The Kdo2-lipid
A synthesis involves nine different enzymes(Gnauck et al., 2016b; Wang and Quinn, 2010). The
biosynthesis takes place in the cytoplasm of the gram-negative bacterium where the nucleotide
sugar UDP-GlcNAc is transformed into UDP-2,3-diacyl-GlcN by enzymes listed in ﬁgure 3.2.
Fatty acids are assembled to original structure (Gnauck et al., 2016b). The UDP-2,3-diacyl-GlcN
molecule is hydrolysed into lipid X (Babinski et al., 2002). Following, the lipid X is linked to
its precursor UDP-2,3-diacyl-GlcN, assembling the molecule disaccharide-1-P (Gnauck et al.,
2016b; Wang and Quinn, 2010; Crowell et al., 1986). Phosphorylation of the disaccharide-1-P
constructs the precursor lipid IVA. lipid IVA contains two phosphate groups (Gnauck et al.,
2016b). IVA is structured into Kdo2-IVA complex. Lastly, the hexa-acylated Kdo2-lipid A as
illustrated in ﬁgure 3.2 is assembled.

3.2.3
Oligosaccharide (OS) Core
As previously explained, the OS core is one of the three components of the LPS molecule.
For the purpose of structural functions, the core of OS can be separated into two segments;
an inner core and an outer core. The inner core generally is composed of Kdo residues and
L-glycoro-D-mannoheptose (Gnauck et al., 2016b; Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002). The outer core
shows broader structural differences, arguably due to a more exposed location in regard to
environmental stress factors and pressures from host responses (Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002). The
outer membrane is commonly composed of hexose units and component saccharides (Gnauck
et al., 2016b). The location of the inner and outer core can be observed on model 3.1. The
proximity of lipid A to the inner core can as well be detected. Elaborated, the lipid A-Kdo2 will
serve as an acceptor to the inner OS core assembling the two regions. The biosynthesis of the OS
core and its linkage to the lipid A-Kdo2 both take place in the cytoplasmic surface of the inner
membrane assisted by glycosyltransferases. The binding results in a lipid A-OS core complex.
The synthesis progresses rapidly and sufﬁciently (Wang and Quinn, 2010).
3.2.4
O-antigen
The third and ﬁnal component of the LPS molecule is the O-antigen repeats. The O-antigen is a
repeat unit polysaccharide assembled by repeats of oligisaccharide that generally has between
two and six sugar residues (Gnauck et al., 2016b; Samuel and Reeves, 2003). The biosynthesis
of the O-antigen occurs at the cytoplasmic surface of the inner membrane, comparable to the
synthesis of the OS core (Wang and Quinn, 2010). The O-antigen units are assembled by
glycosyltransferases (Wang and Quinn, 2010). The structure of these O-antigen units vary
signiﬁcantly in different parameters including monomer type, position and stereochemistry of
the linkages. O-antigen repeats can be both be homopolymers or heteropolymers (Wang and
Quinn, 2010).
3.2.5
Export Of LPS To The Cell Surface
The lipid A-OS core complex and the O-antigen repeats are translocated trough the inner
membrane (Wang and Quinn, 2010; Doerrler, 2006). Different membrane proteins are responsible
for the location shift of the O-antigen and ipid A-OS core complex (Raetz and Whitﬁeld,
2002). Both the lipid A-OS complex and the O-antigen will appear the periplasmic surface.
Polymerisation of the O-antigen is catalysed and additionally a ligation of the OS-antigen to
the terminal sugar residues on the lipid A-OS complex occurs. This results in the assembling
of all three regions of the LPS molecule in the periplasm (Wang and Quinn, 2010; Raetz and
Whitﬁeld, 2002). A series of cassette transporters are responsible for transporting the complete
LPS molecule across the periplasm and thus to reach the inner surface of the outer membrane
(Gnauck et al., 2016b). Lastly, an outer membrane complex will attach the mature LPS molecule

to the surface of the outer membrane of the gram-negative bacteria (Wang and Quinn, 2010).
This completes the synthesis pathways of the LPS molecule.
3.3
The Barrier Function Of LPS
As elaborated, the surface of the gram-negative bacteria consists of LPS molecules synthesised
and translocated from the cytoplasm. The location of the LPS molecules on the outer membrane
of the bacteria enhances the ability of the cell wall serve as as selective barrier against toxic
molecules. In regard, LPS attributes to a maintaining a certain cellular rigidity (Wang and Quinn,
2010; Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009). Different interactions between the LPS molecules on the
surface creates a compact, strong, and sufﬁcient barrier function for the cell. It increases the
cell stability and contributes to a reduction in the membrane permeability toward host-derived
antimicrobial compounds (Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009). The interactions include electrostatic
communications derived by magnesium and calcium ions placed on the outer membrane of the
cell and the phosphate groups of lipid A. Further, LPS-LPS interactions in the hydrophobic
regions induced by hydrogen bonding between OS core sugars are promoted. In extension, an
hydrophobic interaction occurs between the lipid A moiety in a LPS molecule and those present
in the adjacent LPS molecules (Gnauck et al., 2016b). It is crucial for the bacteria to adapt to
environmental pressures and immunological responses from the host. To ensure the integrity of
the bacterial barrier, LPS is able to change the structure of its lipid A moiety by an appropriate
acylation. Thus, the lipid A moieties can be altered in the matter of overcoming threatening
environmental changes, such as temperature or pH values (Gnauck et al., 2016b). The following
chapter 4, will elaborate diseases mediated by LPS.

CHAPTER 4
Endotoxemia
Presence of LPS molecules in the blood circulation of a host e.g. resulted by an infection from
a gram-negative bacterium is termed endotoxemia. Endotoxemia is included as pathogenesis
in several different diseases (Munford, 2016). The endotoxic elevation can lead to; systemic
inﬂammatory response syndrome (SIRS), sepsis, severe sepsis, or septic shock (Guzman-Cottrill
et al., 2012). The different syndrome will be deﬁned in the following sections 4.1 and 4.2.
4.1
Systemic Inﬂammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS)
Systemic inﬂammatory response syndrome is an inﬂammatory condition that affects the whole
body of the host. The syndrome is a response to an infectious or noninfectious insult. By
deﬁnition, SIRS require one of the two following from the host (Guzman-Cottrill et al., 2012);
i) A core temperature higher than 38.5°C or lower than or 36°C.
ii) An elevation in the leukocyte count or more than 10% immature neutrophils.
If only one of the requirements are fulﬁlled, at least one of the following must be accompanying
in order for the host to attain the diagnosis;
i) A mean respiratory rate higher than 2 standard deviation above normal for age or mechanical
ventilation for an acute process not related to underlying neuromuscular disease.
ii) A mean heart rate higher than 2 standard deviation above normal for age in the absence of
external stimulus, chronic drugs, or painful stimuli.
4.2
Sepsis, Severe Sepsis, And Septic Shock
Sepsis is deﬁned by the same requirements as SIRS, however with the speciﬁcation of being a
result of a suspected or proven infection (Guzman-Cottrill et al., 2012). Sepsis is a heterogeneous
syndrome causing a lethal systemic inﬂammatory condition (Vincent et al., 2013). Sepsis
is a dominating mortality cause in intensive care units (Parker and Watkins, 2001), and no
sufﬁcient speciﬁc anti-sepsis treatments are available (Cohen et al., 2012). Gram-negative
bacteria infections are predominately the source of endotoxemia. A rapid increase of LPS in the
blood stream can mediate a cytokine cascade. Thus, when a certain threshold of expressed pro-
inﬂammatory cytokines is reached sepsis occurs (Chousterman et al., 2017). The endotoxicity in

4.3
Metabolic Endotoxemia
In addition to the acute elevation of LPS concentration known as endotoxemia, a phenomenon
termed metabolic endotoxemia has become popular in the world of pharmaceutical research due
to its association to metabolic diseases (Munford, 2016; Gnauck et al., 2016a; Boutagy et al.,
2016). The human microbiome consists of approximately 1014 commensal bacteria, whereof
the majority is gram-negative bacteria (Boutagy et al., 2016). Metabolic endotoxemia is by
deﬁnition the translocation of microbiome-associated lipopolysaccharides into the bloodstream
(Neves et al., 2013). It is mediated by the process of the gram-negatvie gut bacteria migrating
from the hepatic portal into the systemic blood stream, thus avoiding detoxiﬁcation steps in the
liver (Gnauck et al., 2016a; Wellen and Hotamisligil, 2016). The chronic elevation of LPS in
the host circulation termed metabolic endotoxemia has a threshold 10-50 fold lower than the
rapidly increased concentration associated with sepsis patients (Gnauck et al., 2016a). As a
result, a chronic low concentration of LPS molecules will be present in the circulation, causing
an endotoxemia host derived from the gut bacteria. A hypothesis has suggested, that this chronic
concentration of LPS in the circulation causes a low-grade chronic systemic inﬂammation (Cani
et al., 2007). This chronic inﬂammation stage has been argued to be linked to metabolic disorders
and associated diseases, i.e. diabetes type II, obesity, and cardiovascular diseases. Research
presents that high fat diets are able to promote metabolic endotoxemia, thus mediating a chronic
low-grade systemic inﬂammation (Gnauck et al., 2016a; Cani et al., 2007).

CHAPTER 5
Immunological Host Response To LPS
5.1
Role As A Pathogen-Associated Molecular Pattern
(PAMP) In The Human Host
In chapter 3, the properties of the LPS molecule on the surface of the gram-negative bacterium
enlightened. It has been established that the lipid A moiety contributes the toxicity of the
molecule (Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009). In addition, lipid A is the responsible ligand for LPS
recognition by the human immune system (Gnauck et al., 2016b). The molecule detected and
presented by an immune cell in the human body is characterized as a Pathogen-Associated
Molecular Pattern (PAMP). LPS serves as a PAMP to the immune system of the host. PAMPs
are prone to be highly conserved parts of the pathogen. the PAMP is commonly an essential part
of the microorganism, thus a region that often does not differ between species (Medzhitov and
Janeway, 1997). Pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) are transmembrane proteins expressed
on an immune cell with the capacity to trigger the immune system upon stimulation from
PAMP. A variety of different PRRs are represented in the human body including the toll-like
receptors (TLRs), NOD-like receptors (NLRs), and RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) (Medzhitov
and Janeway, 1997). As a PAMP, lipopolysaccharides are speciﬁc to interacting with the PRR
toll-like receptor 4 (TLR-4) (Takeuchi and Akira, 2010). TLR-4 is predominately located on
the surface of endothelial cells and immune cells, signiﬁcantly macrophages, lymphocytes in
peripheral blood, and dendritic cells. TLR-4 is composed of an intracellular Toll/IL-1 receptor
domain, a transmembrane domain, and extracellular hydrophobic leucine rich repeats (LRR)
(Gnauck et al., 2016a).
5.2
LPS Recognition In Human Host
As elaborated above, LPS serves as a PAMP of the majority of infectious gram-negative bacteria.
The recognition of the lipid A moiety in the human body will trigger an immune response
(Raetz and Whitﬁeld, 2002). This immunological response activation depends on the interaction
between LPS and TLR-4. A binding requires different assisting proteins (Wang and Quinn,
2010). The following section 5.2.1 will outline the recognition pathway.

5.2.1
TLR4-MD-2 Complex
As previously described, the TLR-4 serves as the PRR to the LPS molecule, meaning it mediates
the recognition of the gram negative bacteria. In the matter of activating an immune response
in the host, the receptor interacts with the glycoprotein myeloid differentiation factor (MD-2),
ﬁgure 5.1. MD-2 will bind to the extracellular region of the TLR-4 forming a heterodimer (Latz
et al., 2002; Gnauck et al., 2016b). MD-2 exists in two variations; a soluble form (sMD-2)
and a membrane-bound form (mMD-2). The sMD-2 is expressed and secreted by endothelial,
epithelial, or other non-immunological cells, where in contrast, the mMD-2 is expressed by
immune cells. Both sMD-2 and mMD-2 are capable of binding to the receptor and form a
TLR4-MD-2 complex, a key component to LPS recognition. In itself, the TLR4-MD-2 complex
will not be able to recognize the present LPS molecules in the extracellular space. The LPS
recognition is dependent on a presenting by the glycoprotein CD14. Similar to MD-2, the CD14
has two forms; a soluble sCD14 and membrane-bound mCD14 (Wang and Quinn, 2010). Both
forms of the CD14 are capable of binding to the LPS molecule and presenting it to the MD-2 on
the dimer complex. Even though this recognition of LPS exclusively presented by CD14 to the
TLR4-MD-2 complex is possible, the binding kinetics is both slow and non-efﬁcient.

5.2.2
LPS-Binding Protein (LBP)
The issue of low afﬁnity in CD14 binding to LPS molecules can be overcome by the introduction
of LPS-binding protein (LBP). LBP is a signiﬁcant constituent for the mediation of LPS recogni-
tion in the host. The protein drastically increases the binding afﬁnity of LPS to CD14 (Wright,
1989; Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009; Wang and Quinn, 2010). LPS molecules in the extracellular
space have an ability to form fatty micelles and thus disguising the lipid A moieties in the center,
ﬁgure 5.1. This limits the binding rate to CD14, given that the lipid A serves as the recognition
site (Wang and Quinn, 2010). This is solved by the inﬂuence of LBP. LBP proﬁciently binds to
LPS monomers, otherwise integrated in the fatty micelles, and LPS aggregates in the extracellular
space. LBP will present the single LPS molecule to both CD14 forms, and thus inducing a high
afﬁnity in LPS signal pathway (Tran and Whitﬁeld, 2009; Wang and Quinn, 2010). In relation,
LBP is responsible for transporting LPS to the CD14 that additionally will present the molecule
to the TLR4-MD-2 complex.

5.3.2
Cytonkines And Acute Phase Proteins
Proinﬂammatory cytokines are characterized as signalling molecules in early disease stages
(Murtaugh et al., 1996). Proinﬂammatory cytokines can mediate the production of acute phase
proteins. The cytokines following a LPS challenge are predominantly expressed in macrophages
and neutrophils in tissue and blood, respectively (Wright, 1997). A type of cytokines are the
chemotactic cytokines termed chemokines. They are responsible for the cell migration relevant
to an inﬂammation. In addition, the chemokines regulate interactions between immune cells
(Sokol and Luster, 2016). The concentration of various cytokines in the circulation will increase
rapidly post the exposure to LPS. Within few hours the high concentration will be cleared
from the blood stream (Chousterman et al., 2017). The proinﬂammatory cytokines function
as well-established biomarkers for an immunological host response, since they are detectable
in the serum concentration. However, their short half-life creates a difﬁculty in measuring
the concentration (Chousterman et al., 2017). Proinﬂammatory cytokine production mediates
systemic effects including the production of acute phace protein. These are proteins difened
by an increase in plasma concentration of >25% (Kushner, 1993). Acute phase proteins are
proteins expressed in the liver upon signalling of cytokines (Kushner, 1993). The acute phase
protein patterns during exposure has been investigated in previous studies (Sun et al., 2014).
During inﬂammation, an either up- or downregulation in the acute phase protein production will
appear. Acute phase proteins can thus both serve as positive effectors, where an increase in the
concentration will occur, or a negative effector, where a concentration decrease will occur. For
the purpose of this thesis, CRP and SAA will be the dominating acute phase proteins of interest.
Both have previously shown upregulation in correlation with LPS, primarily in animal studies of
mice and other small animals (Llamas Moya et al., 2006). CRP and SAA are both determined as
ﬁrst line acute phase protein. This indicates an increase in the concentration within the ﬁrst 4
hours post inﬂammatory stimilus. Furthermore, the concentration increase of the acute phase
protein will peak in the interval of 24-74 hours and then normalize with half-life of 12-18 hours
(Mackiewicz, 1997). Experimentally, both proinﬂammatory cytokines and acute phase proteins
serve as biomarkers for immunological responses (Kushner, 1993).

6.1
Endotoxin Detection Assays
The detection of LPS in plasma concentration is essential to scientiﬁc validation of the association
between metabolic endotoxemia and metabolic disorders i.e. obesity, type II diabetes, and
cardiovascular diseases (Cani et al., 2007; Gnauck et al., 2016a). Multiple endotoxin detection
assays with different approaches have been developed over time (Fennrich et al., 2016). The
most commonly applied endotoxin detection assay is the Limulus amebocyte Lysate (LAL)
assay, exploiting clotting formations upon the presence of endotoxin in a sample (Gnauck et al.,
2015; Dullah and Ongkudon, 2016). Other engineered techniques for endotoxin detection has
been applied in clinical trials, e.g. endotoxin scattering photometry (ESP) and endotoxin activity
assay (EAA), and monocyte-activation test (MAT), but non beneﬁcial enough to outcompete
the LAL assay (Dullah and Ongkudon, 2016; Wong et al., 2016). There are different variations
of LAL assays, and research suggests that the kinetic turbidimetric LAL assay is preferred
over the chromogenic due to better accuracy in endotoxin detection. (Wong et al., 2016).
Advantageously, the LAL assay is recognised to have a high sensitivity. However, the assay
is not applicable for serum samples containing a too high concentration of proteins, bile, co-
factors, β-glycans or lipoproteins (Dullah and Ongkudon, 2016; Boutagy et al., 2016). These
components contributes to sample contamination and thus will tamper the results (Dullah and
Ongkudon, 2016). Given these limitations of the LAL assay, an improved and reliable method
for endotoxin detection in blood is needed in the matter of diagnosing and managing rapidly
enhanced endotoxemia (Gnauck et al., 2016a). New approaches for such detection are being
investigated in the pharmaceutical industry in order to overcome assay obstacles. Amongst them
is the a reporter gene assay constructed around TLR4 assay ready cells (EuroDiagnostica, 2016).
The principle of the assay will be further explained in the chapter 7.

implemented into a column with a collecting membrane by centrifugation. Further puriﬁcation
steps were applied by several washing steps. DNAse was introduced to the membrane and lastly
the sample was eluated. The eluate was denatured at 60°C. Until further experiments the samples
were stored at -20°C.

CHAPTER 10
Discussion
In this chapter the results conducted during the thesis will be discussed and put into perspectives.
In relation to the scope of the thesis, an evaluation of signiﬁcant results will be discussed. Further
optimisations and experimental extension will be suggested for potential future work.
High levels of endotoxemia is known to cause sepsis and sepsis derived syndromes in human.
Progression of the hyperinﬂammatory response during the innate immune system can have fatal
consequences. The mortality of sepsis in early disease stages is caused by an overproduction
of cytokines, popularly referred to as a cytokine storm (Chousterman et al., 2017). As stating
numerous times throughout this thesis, there is nowadays no available sepsis treatment on the
pharmaceutical market. One of the reasons for the lack in sepsis treatments is the issue of not
being not able to detect LPS in a host (Gnauck et al., 2016a). Animal models mimicking human
responses triggered by LPS exposure is currently the most reliable technique for determining the
inﬂuence of the molecule. This is due to uncertainties in endotoxin assays caused by interference
from biological ﬂuids. Another obstacle in detecting LPS is that it after exerting its direct effects
on leukocytes during the short toxic period rapidly proceeds clearance from the circulation.
As presented in the results, the LAL assay has potential to be signiﬁcantly interfered by con-
taminations of different component as proteins, biles, co-factors, β-glycans or lipoproteins in
serum (Dullah and Ongkudon, 2016). Thus, the LAL assay faces major complications detecting
concentrations of LPS in serum. In this thesis a new and improved way of detecting LPS in serum
was tested. The reporter gene assay constructed of engineered TLR-4 assay ready cells was
implemented in the project to activate the TLR-4 stimulating pathway as oppose to exploiting
blood cluttering as seen in LAL assays (EuroDiagnostica, 2016). Throughout the experimental
phase of the thesis numerous obstacles related to the reporter gene assay were encountered.
Unfortunately, troubleshooting in collaboration with the company that developed the assay cells
did not lead to a successful assay despite great effort. Multiple serotypes of E. coli LPS and
luminometers were unsuccessfully applied in the assay. The luminometer seemed to be one
of the dysfunctional parts of the assay since changing the E. coli serotype did not change the
outcome, and further a simple luciferase substrate reaction did not either achieve a ﬂuorescence
standard curve. Changing the apparatus still did not work optimally. In order to troubleshoot
further and to estimate if I was able to activate the TLR-4 signalling cascade including the
downstream transcription factor nuclear factor-κB and proinﬂammatory cytokines, it would be
beneﬁcial to test the gene expression of the above mentioned targets in the assay cell vials. In
this thesis, the cells were stained and conﬁrmed vital, but the conﬁrmation of TLR-4 stimulus

In this thesis, human response to endotoxin was modelled in Göttingen minipigs in relation to a
LPS challenge. As previously explained, the pig has a full set of innate immune effectors similar
to the ones seen in human. Thus, suggestively the responses detected in this thesis mimics the
impact of LPS in a human host. The pigs were thus used as models gor the human response to
endotoxemia. The speciﬁc selection of the Göttingen minipig was ideal due to its well-deﬁned
genetic background and pathogen free health status. (EllegaardA/S, 2018). These pigs improve
the reliability of the results and thereby a justiﬁcation that detected responses are caused by the
LPS injections. Endotoxemia has a triggering effect on immunological responses making these
LPS challenged pigs interesting models for the response patterns. The LPS exposure resulted
in a clear fever response in all LPS challenged subjects in the interval between injection time
and the 12 hours post injection. The fever inducing cytokines are the proinﬂammatory cytokines
that show endogenous pyrogens properties, and these include IL-1, TNF, and IFNs (Netea et al.,
2000). IL-6 and TNF-α was measured in this study and an increase in concentration was observed
responding to the temperature increase. Thus, an early inﬂammation stage in the animals were
observed concluding the activation of the innate immune system. Other than the observed fever
caused by systemic inﬂammation the animals also showed interesting changes in the circulating
total leukocyte count. A clear indication of leukocyte extravasation was detected in the ﬁrst
couple of hours post LPS injection followed by a drastic production increase. The leukocyte
increase initiated after 6 hours is dominated by the neutophil cells, a granulocyte associated
with bacterial infections (Medzhitov and Janeway, 1997). Neutrophiles are essential to innate
immunity. A clear activation of the innate immune system in all animals was concluded from the
CBC. In conclusion, the blood cell data from the pig model accurately mimicked what could be
expected in the ﬁrst 24 hours of induced endotoxemia in human (Wellen and Hotamisligil, 2016).
Other animals could have been selected as subject for the LPS challenge, however choosing a
large mammal model in contrast to rodent models aids overcoming many anatomic obstacles,
and speciﬁcally pigs. Pigs have shown to have more than 80% of their analysed immunological
parameters resembles the immune system of a human (Hau and Van Hoosier, 2003).
Studies have shown that the structural and functional properties of most immune system related
proteins in pigs resemble their human counterparts (Hau and Van Hoosier, 2003). The obser-
vation of a signiﬁcant increase in the production of several inﬂammatory cytokines, i.e. IL-6,
IL-8, and TNF-α, in the time range between injection and 12 hours post injection is another
strong indicator of LPS caused activation of innate immunity. The plasma concentration of IL-6
==PAGE:68==
59
is clearly predominating compared to the other detected proinﬂammatory cytokines in the serum.
It is not new knowledge that endotoxemia induces the immune system in relation to detection of
cytokine production. However, the immunological responses caused by LPS in pig models is
a new and not as well-characterised technique compared to e.g. mice models (Meurens et al.,
2012). The observed responses in the LPS challenge is thus signiﬁcant in the matter of evaluating
similar immunological responses as triggered in the human host. The detected increase in the
acute phase proteins SAA and CRP seem to arise in the two latest time-points. This increase
correlates with the rapid clearance of proinﬂammatory cytokines in the circulation (Llamas Moya
et al., 2006).
Hepatic acute phase proteins are discovered to be drastically induced upon signalling from
cytokines, predominately IL-6. Thus, this induction appear when the inﬂammatory cytokines
stimulate the hepathocytes of the liver. The ELISA results showed a peak in IL-6 concentration
followed by a clearance from the circulation, and lastly a production and secretion of SAA and
CRP. This clearly correlates with pathways ofan innate immune system.
Gene expression analysis can be used as comparative data to the ELISA and CBC results. The
systemic upregulation of the genes IL8, CRP, TNF and both isoforms of SAA showed similar
increase as the measured concentrations of the cytokines IL-8 and TNF-α in serum by ELISA.
So far in this project, activation of the innate immune response has been estimated based on
leukocyte counts and levels of cytokine, and acute phase proteins. Comparable, the results of
the regulations in gene expression was also related to processes of the innate immune system.
The drastic upregulation of mRNA coding for SAA correlates well. Given that the primer for
the IL6 gene did not work properly the dominating increase in plasma concentration could not
be seen at the transcriptional level. Other cytokine producing genes, e.g. IL8, did validate the
systemic increase detected by ELISA. Further, gene expression data from liver tissue of genes
producing acute phase proteins can be correlated with both circulating acute phase proteins
and the systemic gene expression results. SAA were very upregulated in liver samples, and
blood samples, and ELISA concentration at t=24h, the only time-point all sample types have
in common. Due to the necessity of collecting organs, there was only hepatic data from one
time-point. This create a limitation in comparing the results to the other ﬁndings in this study.
However, the acute phase protein detected in the circulation can be assumed to have been secreted
from where they are synthesised (Raynes et al., 2008). The major increase in the expression
of SAA genes in the liver can be associated with the circulation of the protein detected at later
time-points. An increase of the positive acute phase proteins SAA and CRP were detected in
the plasma. However, comparing the two, the level increase of CRP is remarkably lower than
in SAA. The most highly upregulated gene was SAA in agreement to what was observed in
the ELISA assays. Various genes showed a downregulation in relation to producing negative
acute phase proteins. One of the most downregulated genes was TTR in this. TTR is a negative
acute phase protein that can serve as an indicator of inﬂammation in a host (Ingenbleek and
Bernstein, 1999). Newer studies have been evaluating the detection of the TTR downregulation

approach to the downregulation of the gene observed in this LPS study (Campbell et al., 2005).
The fact that the qPCR analysis applied in this thesis detected both up- and downregulated genes
that correlate well with immunologically observations conﬁrms results with a certain reliability.
Together, the three separate immunological host response analyses assembles a comparative
evaluation of the inﬂuence of LPS on the porcine host. From a time perspective all results
correlate with an activation of the innate immune system caused by the LPS injection. In relation
to several other previous LPS challenges, this study showed a homogeneous comparative proof
of innate immunological responses (Sun et al., 2016; Terenina et al., 2017).
The scope of the thesis included an evaluation of response differences and similarities in relation
to route of LPS administration. The study includes two injection methods; i.v. and i.m., compared
in all the three succeeded host response experiments. No parameters in the ELISA, CBC or
qPCR indicated a pattern signiﬁcant for the route of administration. As a matter of fact the results
showed very similar behaviour in all response assays. Very few outliers were detected in this
study illustrating a strong and reliable response. The dosage differences dependent on the route
of administration seems to have little effect on the parameters measured in the present study. The
dosage in i.m. injected pigs was three times larger than the dosage injected intravenously. This
was a precaution constructed due to the severe extent of introducing the toxic directly into the
blood stream. For future references, a different dosage variation or an equalisation would be an
interesting next move. The study required considerations of determining the appropriate dosage.
The data showed, that the chosen dosage was very sufﬁcient in the manner of creating a host
response. Arguably, if the dosage was lowered the same immunological host response would
not have been observed. Primarily, in order to be able to compare data and conclude that the
observed administration patterns were caused by an efﬁcient choice of dosage. Perspectively,
it would as well be interesting for future research to test response patterns after other routes of
administrations e.g. intradermal, intraarticularly or oral administration.
The immune response reported in this thesis, was a systemic inﬂammation. Thus protein con-
centrations and gene expressions were determined in the circulation or in extension by hepatic
gene regulation. Comparing local and systemic responses caused by the LPS challenge could
be another interesting investigation. Tissue samples from the intramuscularly injected pigs was
collected from both the injected muscle and the respective not injected biceps femoris muscle
in the pig. Thus subsequently, a study comparing expressions of the injection site with both a
control tissue and the systemic response results from this thesis could be constructed. Due to
limited time and sample vacancy the muscle tissue study was excluded from the experimental
outline of the thesis. A previous study of a LPS challenge in horses distinguished between
systemic and local responses (Vinther et al., 2016). Based on the same assay principles, (Vinther
et al., 2016) also showed an upregulation of the IL8 host gene 6 hours post a LPS injection. In
addition the gene is even stronger upregulated 8 hours post the injection suggesting that for future
references this time-point could beneﬁcially be included. (Vinther et al., 2016) conducted a

comparison of local and system acute phases by measuring e.g. SAA at different time-points post
an intraarticular and an intravenous LPS injection. Differences was declared between the acute
local and systemic responses. In relation, some of the results could interestingly be compared
to an injection site study of the three i.m. injected pigs in the LPS challenge of this thesis. A
further analysis of a potential LPS caused local acute phase could be conducted.
In conclusion, host responses from the three analyses were successfully compared. In the manner
of optimising the LPS challenge model, an obvious parameter would be to include further time-
points. The time frame changes should introduce more frequent sample collections. Generally,
in this study the cytokines arises at one time-point and are almost cleared from the circulation
at the next. Thus, an extension of time-points in agreement with Vinther et al. (2016) would
be beneﬁcial in order to further determine a more detailed host response. Furthermore, the
LPS challenge has a duration of 24 hours, and at this time-point the production of acute phase
proteins are still increasing. Thus, prolonging the duration of the animal study would create a
more detailed determination of SAA and CRP patterns. In addition, the fact that the expression
rate of SAA gene is still very high at t=24h compared to the initial level could indicate that
the plasma concentration of SAA potentially could peak later than the last time-point. Another
suggestion could be to optimise the porcine IL-8 ELISA kit with polyclonal antibodies to allow
larger differences and thus detect larger amount of IL-8. However, the concentration correlates
reasonably with the level of upregulation in the systemic gene expression.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
The thesis was constructed to obtain valuable comparative data based on an LPS challenge in
six Göttingen minipigs. The challenge was conducted over a duration of 24 hours and relevant
samples were collected in order to apply a broad range of immunological assays. Included were
tissue and blood samples used for different analyses. The analyses encompassed quantiﬁcation
of proinﬂammatory cytokines and acute phase proteins by sandwich ELISA, quantiﬁcation of
expressed host response genes by qPCR, and enumerating blood cell fractions (CBC). The data
from the different assays were compared and discussed further.
The LPS challenge was set up in order to measure the systemic host responses as a representation
of the LPS concentration. The thesis strived to ﬁnd an optimised and successful way of detecting
LPS in biological ﬂuids, speciﬁed serum samples. The available LAL method for detection
is known to be unreliable due to limitations caused by contamination. Parameters in serum
show to interfere with the assay results. This interference was as well observed in this study.
In order to optimise on LPS detection methods, a reporter gene assay based on engineered
TLR-4 assay ready cells was tested. An acceptable optimisation should encounter reliable data
of LPS concentrations in subjected pigs that could be compared the results of the host responses.
The testing and implication of the LPS detection model was of great importance for expanding
the knowledge of LPS responses and thus long term enhance possibilities for sepsis speciﬁc
treatment. Many attempts and alterations were applied in order to functionally measure an LPS
concentration by the reporter gene assay, however no successful outcome was accomplished. In
conclusion the LPS detection of this thesis was not conducted successfully. The results of this
study was instead based on measured host responses that the present LPS molecules activates.
The three host response assays were correlated in order to ﬁnd patterns regarding time-point
and route of administration. All data indicated a clear immunological effect caused by the LPS
challenge. Regulations in immunological host genes correlated with the systemic concentrations
of cytokines and acute phase proteins. The time points of systemic concentration increase
rendered what was to be expected during an innate immune response. The leukocyte count
identiﬁed an inﬂammation that correlated with the temperature increase detected in the minipigs.
The study was designed to allow a comparison of two different routes of LPS administration.
Half of the pigs in the study were injected by i.v. administration and the other half by i.m.
administration. None of the assays conducted in this thesis detected a signiﬁcant pattern
separating the administration methods. Instead all data of the study showed clear and similar host

by this thesis were essential to the progression of a still on-going DTU project. The i.v. and i.m.

efﬁcient relation between the parameters. The study had an important key as pilot project and it
contributed solid ground for the understanding of LPS response for further research.
