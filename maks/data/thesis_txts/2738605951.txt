Abstract  
The human genome contains approximately 20,000 genes, causing number of proteoforms primarily 
due to alternative splicing and, more significantly, post-translational modifications (PTMs). PTMs 
such as phosphorylation, ubiquitylation, and glycosylation allow for rapid and reversible modulation, 
which is crucial for efficient cellular responses to environmental changes. Ubiquitin (Ub) is a small 
76 amino acid (aa) protein that is expressed in all cell types and found in all eukaryotic organisms. 
Ub is most commonly attached to lysine, but some E3 ligases have been demonstrated to ubiquitylate 
proteins via ester-linked ubiquitin at serine (S) or threonine (T) residues. The effects of ubiquitylation 
depend on the type of chain that is formed. Linear ubiquitin chains (M1-linked) are involved in innate 
immunity through NF-кB signaling and autophagy, and their formation is facilitated by Linear 
ubiquitin chain assembly complex (LUBAC). LUBAC is of central importance at the apex of innate 
immune signaling, where it regulates the activation of nuclear factor B-cells (NF-кB) in response to 
various stimuli. TNF signaling is an important part in the regulation of immune responses, inducing 
cell death or survival signals.  
The aim of this study was to identify novel LUBAC substrates and to investigate the role of M1-
linked Ub in their cellular function, through experimental work with Mass Spectrometry (MS), 
immunoprecipitation (IP), Western Blotting, and functional assays.  
The study showed portions of the accumulated M1-linked chains are ester-linked, as they can be 
collapsed in alkaline environment. This finding, lead to investigation of alternative substrates beyond 
proteins, such as lipids, sugars, and nucleic acids. However, these findings suggest that cytosolic 
RNA substrates appear unlikely. Furthermore, the study demonstrated the presences of ester-linked 
Ub chains in the TNF signaling pathway. The study also identified and verified the Ufd1-Npl4-p97 
complex as an interactor of M1-linked Ub. Furthermore, inhibition studies were performed and 
indicated that M1-linked Ub accumulates as p97 is inhibited, highlighting the role of p97 in M1-
linked Ub degradation. However, the data further suggested that p97 functions more as an adaptor, 
as the overall effect on M1-linked Ub accumulation does not vary significantly over time. 
Overall, the findings from this study provide interesting aspects for future research. Future research 
would hopefully lead to insight to the biological functions, significance of LUBAC and M1-linked 
Ub chains.

Resumé 
Det menneskelige genom indeholder cirka 20.000 gener, hvilket forårsager antallet af proteoformer 
primært på grund af alternativ splejsning og, mere signifikant, post-translationelle modifikationer 
(PTM'er). PTM'er såsom phosphorylering, ubiquitylering og glycosylering muliggør hurtig og 
reversibel modulering, hvilket er afgørende for effektive cellulære reaktioner på ændring i cellemiljø. 
Ubiquitin (Ub) er et lille protein på 76 aminosyrer (aa), der er udtrykt i alle celletyper og findes i alle 
eukaryote organismer. Ub er oftest bundet til lysin, men nogle E3-ligaser er blevet påvist at 
ubiquitylere proteiner via esterbundet ubiquitin ved serin (S) eller threonin (T) grupper. Virkningerne 
af ubiquitylering afhænger af den type kæde der dannes. Lineære ubiquitinkæder (M1-bundet) er 
involveret i innate immunitet gennem NF-кB-signalering og autofagi, og deres dannelse er fremmet 
af ’Linear ubiquitin chain assembly complex’ (LUBAC). LUBAC har central betydning for innate 
immunsignalering, hvor det regulerer aktiveringen af ’nuclear factor’ (NF-кB) som reaktion på 
forskellige stimuli. TNF-signalering er en vigtig del i reguleringen af immunrespons, da det inducerer 
celle død eller overlevelsessignaler.  
Formålet med denne undersøgelse var at identificere nye LUBAC-substrater og at undersøge M1-
bundet Ubs rolle i deres cellulære funktion gennem eksperimentelt arbejde med massespektrometri 
(MS), immunpræcipitation (IP), Western blotting og funktionelle assays.  
Undersøgelsen viste, at dele af de akkumulerede M1-bundne kæder er esterbundne, da de kan kollapse 
i alkalisk miljø. Denne observation førte til undersøgelse af alternative substrater ud over proteiner, 
såsom lipider, sukkerarter og nukleinsyrer. Endvidere, tyder data på, at ssRNA-substrater 
forekommer usandsynlige. Undersøgelsen viste desuden tilstedeværelsen af ester-bundne ubiquitin-
kæder i TNF-signalering. Derudover identificerede og verificerede undersøgelse også at Ufd1-Npl4-
p97-komplekset interagere med M1-bundet Ub. Desuden blev inhiberingsundersøgelser udført og 
disse indikerede, at M1-bundet Ub akkumuleres, når p97 hæmmes. Dette fremhæver p97's rolle i 
nedbrydning af M1-bundet Ub. Data antydede yderligere, at p97 fungerer mere som en adapter, da 
på den samlede effekt på M1-bundet Ub akkumulering der ikke varierer væsentligt over tid.  
Samlet set giver resultaterne fra denne undersøgelse interessante aspekter for fremtidig forskning. 
Fremtidig forskning vil forhåbentlig føre til større indsigt i de biologiske funktioner, betydningen af 
LUBAC og M1-bundet Ub kæder.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Post-translational modification  
 
The human genome contains approximately 20,000 genes, causing a number of proteoforms primarily 
due to alternative splicing and, more significantly, post-translational modifications (PTMs)(1). PTMs 
refer to events where an amino acid side chain on a protein is modified. The modification consists of 
either proteolytic cleavage or addition of a modifying group. To this date, more than 400 PTMs have 
been identified, the most common of which are comprised of protein phosphorylation, acetylation, 
methylation and ubiquitylation. PTMs affect a wide range of protein properties such as activity, 
function, localization, stability and interaction, thereby influencing various biological functions, such 
as protein degradation, signal transduction, gene expression, protein interactions etc. (2) PTMs help 
the organism respond to various external stimuli, such as cytokines, pathogens etc. much quicker than 
by de-novo synthesis or degradation. As this process involves the addition or removal of a functional 
group, which is much more efficient than making a new protein. PTMs such as phosphorylation, 
ubiquitylation, and glycosylation allow for rapid and reversible modulation, which is crucial for 
efficient cellular responses to environmental changes.(1)    
  
1.2 The ubiquitin system  
 
Ubiquitin (Ub) is a small 76 amino acid (aa) protein that is expressed in all cell types and found in all 
eukaryotic organisms. Its sequence is highly conserved among eukaryotes and adopts a highly stable 
β-grasp fold. Ub can be conjugated to proteins via aa sidechains in a process called ubiquitylation. 
(3) 
Ubiquitylation can be described through three main step: assembly, binding and disassembly. In the 
first step ubiquitin ‘writers’ assemble the modifications. Secondly, the ubiquitin modifications are 
bound to the substrates, the ‘readers’ (ubiquitin-binding domains (UBDs)) recognizes and transmit 
the signal. Lastly, ubiquitin ‘erasers’ (deubiquitinases (DUBs)) cleave the ubiquitin chains and the 
transmitted signal is completed.(4) For most cases, ubiquitylation causes a signaling event, however 
this is not always the case. In some cases ubiquitylation result in increased protein stability or cause 
a delocalization without any signaling output (4).  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
 
 
Ub chains are catalyzed through an enzymatic cascade utilizing three enzymes, ubiquitin-activating- 
(E1), ubiquitin-conjugating- (E2) and ubiquitin ligase- (E3) enzymes (Fig. 1A) (5). During the 
ubiquitin-activation, the E1 enzyme activates Ub through ATP hydrolysis. An Ub-AMP intermediate 
is formed, as the ubiquitin’s C-terminal glycine is adenylated. This is followed by the formation of 
an Ub-E1 complex, as a thioester bond is formed between Ub and the E1 enzyme. (5,6) During the 
conjugation step, a transfer of Ub from the E1 enzyme to the E2 enzyme occurs, and a new thioester 
bond if formed. Lastly, the ligation step, the E3 enzyme promotes the transfers of ubiquitin from the 
E2 enzymes to the substrate. Here an isopeptide bond is formed between the ε-amino groups of the 
substrate’s lysine (K) residue and the carboxy groups of ubiquitin’s C-terminal glycine residue.(5,6)

to the E1 Ub-activating enzyme through an ATP-dependent reaction. Then the Ub is transferred to an E2 Ub-conjugation enzyme, 
where the Ub then can be transferred to a substrate through its lysine residue. This transfer can occurs in two ways: directly facilitated 
by a RING E3 Ub ligase or through a HECT/RBR E3 Ub ligase, where the Ub is transferred first and then mediate the transfer to the 
substrate. Lastly, a DUB cleaves the ubiquitin chains from the substrate. (B) The ubiquitin code. Depicts the different types of ubiquitin 
chains. (C) Cellular function of ubiquitylation. Ubiquitylation of a substrate can lead to several signaling responses, some of these are 
depicted here. This figure is slightly modified from the original. (3).

The E3 ligases can be divided into three subclasses depending on their domain: Really interesting 
new gene (RING) -, RING-in-between-RING (RBR) -, and Homologous to the E6-AP Carboxyl 
Terminus (HECT) E3 ligases. The RING E3 ligase directly transfers the Ub from the E2 enzyme to 
the target substrate, whereas the HECT binds Ub through a thioester bond before transfer to the 
substrate (Fig. 1A). RBR E3 ligases combines these to aspects, interaction with the E2 through the 
RING domain and binding the Ub by a thioester bond before transfer. (5,7–9) At 600-700 proteins, 
E3 ligases make up roughly five percent of all human genes and confer substrate specificity to the 
ubiquitylation reaction (10). In contrast, Ub activation and conjugation are restricted to eight E1 and 
roughly 40 E2 ligases, respectively (10,11).    
 
Ub is most commonly attached to lysine, but some E3 ligases have been demonstrated to ubiquitylate 
proteins via ester-linked Ub at serine (S) or threonine (T) residues (Fig. 1B) (3). 
Ub can be ubiquitylated itself, forming poly-Ub chains. These chains can vary in length, consisting 
of up to more than ten moieties (3). Here, Ub can be conjugated through an isopeptide bond at one of 
its seven lysine residues (K6, K11, K27, K29, K33, K48, and K63) or the N-terminal methionine 
residue (M1) (Fig. 1B) (3). Ubiquitin can attach to S and T residues, forming ester bonds, and these 
residues can serve as acceptor sites for Ub. This contributes to the formation of heterotypic Ub chains, 
which may include ester linkages. This is particularly relevant for the observed partial collapse of Ub 
chains with base treatment, suggesting the presence of these ester linkages within the chains. (12) 
These chains can be composed of the same linkage type (homotypic polyUb) or different linkages 
(heterotypic polyUb). Moreover, conjugation of Ub onto multiple residues on the same acceptor Ub 
can give rise to branched chains. (13)  
As mentioned before, Ub is most commonly attached to lysine’s, however some E3 ligases have been 
demonstrated to ubiquitylate proteins via ester-linked Ub (14). For example, it has been discovered 
that the E3 ligase HOIL-1 can form ester bonds between ubiquitin and the S or T residues of substrates 
(Fig. 1B) (14). These ester-linkages are able to bind to other substrate types other than protein, this is 
seen in literature where the E3 ligase RNF213 form ester-linkages between ubiquitin and a 
lipopolysaccharide substrate of Salmonella, through its S or T residues (15).

1.3 Chain type-dependent function of polyUb chains 
 
The effects of ubiquitylation depend on the type of chain that is formed (Fig. 1C) (2). K48-linked and 
K63-linked polyUb chains are also referred to as “canonical Ub chains” due to their high abundance 
(up to 90% of all polyUb in the cell and thorough functional characterization)  (5).   
K48-linked chains serve as a degradation signal that marks proteins for degradation by the 26S 
proteasome system (5). Consequently, proteasome inhibition leads to accumulation of K48-linked 
chains (5,16). Proteins decorated with K48-polyUb are recognized and extracted from their native 
environment by Valosin-containing protein (VCP) (17)(18).  
VCP, also known as p97, is a member of the hexameric AAA (ATPases associated with diverse 
cellular activities) protein family. It has two ATPase domains (D1 and D2), and thus catalyzes the 
extraction and unfolding of proteins targeted for proteasomal degradation in an ATP-dependent 
manner. This function depends on several accessory proteins, including ubiquitin-regulatory proteins 
domain 1 (Ubxd1), ubiquitin fusion degradation 1 (Ufd1), nuclear protein localization homolog 4 
(Npl4) and p47. Ufd1 and Npl4 contain distinct UBDs that, together with p97, enable interaction of 
the p97-Ufd1-Npl4 complex to K48-polyubiquitylated substrates. (18–21) K63-linked polyUb chains 
are involved in several cellular processes including endocytosis, DNA repair and signal transduction. 
K63-linked chains are crucial for activating signaling pathways, particularly the NF-κB pathway. 
Several components of the signaling pathway are modified by K63-linked chains such as TNF 
receptor associated factor 6 (TRAF6) and Receptor-Interacting Protein 1 (RIP1). This facilitates the 
recruitment and activation of downstream kinases such as TGF-β-activated kinase 1 (TAK1) and the 
IкB kinase (IKK) complex, leading to NF-κB activation and immune and inflammatory responses. 
(22) 
Atypical Ub chains, non-canonical, includes K6, K11, K27, K29, K33 and M1 linkages, and 
constitute a small fraction of the total polyUb pool. Despite their lower abundance, atypical chains 
play crucial roles in specific cellular processes. (23) Similarly, to K48 chains various non-canonical 
chains are involved in this process in the proteasomal degradation pathway; however, the most 
abundant are K48-linked Ub chains (5,16). Furthermore, K11-linked chains were found to be 
involved in proteasomal degradation and degradation signal in cell cycle regulations, K27-linked 
chains are involved in immune regulation, cell signaling autophagy and DNA repair, whereas M1-
linked Ub chains are involved in innate immunity, through NF-кB signaling and autophagy. (13,24).

1.4 M1-linked ubiquitin signaling  
 
M1-linked polyUb makes up roughly 1% of the total cellular polyUb pool. They are exclusively 
assembled by LUBAC. LUBAC consists of three proteins in a complex: HOIL-1-interacting protein 
(HOIP), Heme-Oxidized IRP2 Ub ligase-1 (HOIL-1) and SHANK-associated RH domain-interacting 
protein (SHARPIN) (Fig. 2B). (7,25) HOIP is a 120 kDa E3 ligase of the RBR family. Due to the 
interaction that occurs between the N-terminal domain and the RBR domain, HOIP is in an 
autoinhibitory state. For activation, HOIP needs to bind either HOIL-1 or SHARPIN. (24) The 
activation of HOIP promotes M1-linked ubiquitin assembly (4).  
HOIL-1 (57 kDa) is another RBR E3 ligase and a crucial component for M1-linked Ub chains, 
through its ability to stabilize LUBAC and enhance the activity of HOIP (24). HOIL-1 can 
ubiquitylate free hydroxyl groups on proteins and non-protein substrates, including itself, HOIP and 
SHARPIN. These can then be extended with M1-linked Ub by HOIP, which result in attenuated 
enzymatic activity of LUBAC (26). HOIL-1 and SHARPIN (47 kDa) form a spherical structure 
through their interaction at the LUBAC-tethering motif (LTM) (Fig. 2B). (24,27).

‘readers’ and ‘easers’. The LUBAC complex (HOIL-1, HOIP, and SHARPIN) assembles the M1-linked chains (green) and attaches 
it to a substrate (gray). The signal is ‘read’ by different UBD and transmitted of various cellular processes. DUBs as OTULUN and 
CYLD-SPATA2 act as ‘erasers’, cleaving the M1-linked Ub chains. B) LUBAC composition and interaction with OUTLIN and

Mutations in HOIP and HOIL-1 in human patients are the cause of LUBAC deficiency, a severe 
inheritable disease characterized by chronic autoinflammation and primary immunodeficiency 
(28,29). Although SHARPIN does not possess catalytic activity, it is required for proper LUBAC 
function, and SHARPIN-deficient (Sharpincpdm/cpdm) mice develop severe chronic proliferative 
dermatitis (30). More recently, human patients with biallelic SHARPIN deficiency present with 
autoinflammation, and immunodeficiency. Furthermore, it has been discovered that LUBAC 
deficiency is a tumor necrosis factor (TNF)  driven disease and treatment with anti-TNF antibodies 
can attenuate autoinflammation.(31,32).  
Additionally, it was discovered that the presences of functional HOIL-1 is a prerequisite for ester-
linked ubiquitylation signals in cells (33,34). The ester-linked ubiquitin chains have been found to be 
involved with signaling pathways for inflammatory mediated protection (14,34), as well as playing a 
role in the control of the Myddosome-dependent signaling process (35). More specifically, it has been 
identified that Interleukin-1 receptor-associated kinase (IRAK) 2, IRAK4 and MyDD88 are substrates 
that are ubiquitylated via ester linkages (36).  
 
As mentioned earlier Ub goes through several step from assembly, signal to disassembly. There are 
ubiquitin ‘writers’ that assemble the modification, ‘readers’ that recognize it and transmit a signal 
and ‘erasers’ that stop the signaling (Fig. 2A).  
In the case of M1-linked Ub LUBAC acts as the ‘writer’, assembling the M1-linked chains. As the 
M1-linked chains are assembled and have been transfered to the substrate, a signal can be ‘read’ and 
then transmitted. The most characterized M1-linked specific UBD ‘reader’ is NF-κB Essential 
Modifier (NEMO). On NEMO, M1-linked Ub is bound the A20 binding inhibitor of NF-κB (ABIN) 
and  ubiquitin binding in ABIN and NEMO (UBAN) domain. This interaction leads to a downstream 
immune signaling response. (4) 
Several other M1-linked specific UBD have been identified, such as DUB A20, HOIL-1, the cellular 
Inhibitor of Apoptosis (IAP) 1 (cIAP1) and cIAP2 etc.. As the signal have been read and transmitted, 
the signal is stopped with the ‘eraser’. In the case of M1-linked Ub, the ‘erasers’ are OTU (Ovarian 
tumor) deubiquitinase with linear linkage specificity (OTULIN) or Cylindromatosis- 
Spermatogenesis-Associated 2 (CYLD-SPATA2). These DUBs cleave the M1-linked chains 
restoring the Ub to this basal state. (4)  
 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
Few DUBs can cleave M1-linked Ub. Ubiquitin specific proteases (USP), such as USP4, USP14 and 
USP21 are able to cleave M1-linked chains, albeit with low specificity. The most specific and 
selective DUBs that has been identified are CYLD and OTULIN. OTULIN exclusively cleaves M1-
linked chains, whereas CYLD can cleave M1- and K63-linked chains with a preference for the latter. 
(4,24) OTULIN can form a stable complex with LUBAC by binding to HOIP’s peptide: N-glycanase 
/ubiquitin-associated UBA-containing or UBX-containing (PUB) domain via its PUB-interacting 
motif (PIM) in the N-terminal region (EEMDYR, aa 52-57) (Fig. 2B). CYLD does not contain a PIM 
and instead requires SPATA2 as a PIM-containing adaptor protein (Fig. 2B). As the DUBs binds at 
the same domain, the binding cannot occur simultaneously. (4,24) 
CYLD knockout has no apparent effect on M1-linked Ub abundance, whereas OTULIN knockout 
mice display a drastic increase. Moreover, loss-of-function or hypomorphic mutations in OTULIN 
are the cause of OTULIN-related autoinflammatory syndrome (ORAS), a life-threatening 
autoinflammatory disease, with remarkably similar clinical presentation as LUBAC deficiency. 
ORAS symptoms are TNF-driven as well, and anti-TNF therapy is the treatment of choice.(4,24). 
This further highlight the involvement of M1-linked Ub in inflammatory signaling.   
 
1.5 Inflammation signaling – Tumor necrosis factor (TNF)  
 
LUBAC is of central importance at the apex of innate immune signaling, where it regulates the 
activation of NF-кB in response to various stimuli. TNF signaling is an important part in the 
regulation of immune responses, inducing cell death or survival signals. When TNF-α binds, a 
signaling cascade begins (Fig. 3).

TNF-α binds to the TNF receptor 1 (TNFR1), which leads to trimerization of the receptor. This results 
in formation of the TNFR1-associated signaling complex I. (37) This complex consists of TNFR1, 
TNF receptor-associated factor 2 (TRAF2) and 5 (TRAF5), TNF receptor-associated death domain 
(TRADD), Src associated in mitosis of 68 kDa (Sam68), and receptor-interacting serine/threonine 
protein kinase 1 (RIPK1). (16)  
TRAF2 acts as a scaffold for two other Ub E3 ligases, c-IAP1 and c-IAP2. Both c-IAP1 and c-IAP2 
promotes autoubiquitylation and K63-, K48-, and K11-linked ubiquitylation of RIPK1 (16). The 
ubiquitylation recruits LUBAC to the complex, resulting in M1-linked ubiquitylation of several 
complex components such as TNFR1, RIPK1, LUBAC and NEMO (37).  
Moreover, LUBAC extends K63-linked chains on NEMO together with M1-linked chains. The 
ubiquitylation of NEMO results in a conformational change in the IKKα/β complex. This 
conformational change allows for phosphorylation IKKα/β and consequently degradation of IкBα. 
As IкBα are degraded, a release and activation of NF-κB occurs. This activates the transcription of 
several genes, which encodes for inflammatory mediators and survival factors. (4)  
Subsequently, as the receptor is activated, the signaling cascade leads to activation of TGF-β-
activated kinase 1 (TAK1), TAK-binding proteins 1, 2, and 3 (TAB1, TAB2, and TAB3). TAB2 and 
3 interacts with K63-linked chains through the NF-κB zinc finger (NZF) domains, which promotes 
activation of TAK1. TAK1 activation, leads to phosphorylated of IKKβ component of the IKK 
complex. For the full complex to become active M1- and K63-linked chains need to bind NEMO. As 
this occurs the IKK complex becomes active, leading to phosphorylation of IκBα, followed by its 
degradation. As IκBα is degraded, NF-κB is activated and its complex (NF-кB, p50-p65 dimer) is 
translocated to the nucleus. In the nucleus NF-кB dimer triggers the pro-inflammatory gene 
transcription leading to immune and inflammatory signal response. (22)

1.7 Aim of the study    
 
The aim of this master’s thesis was to investigate potential M1-linked ubiquitylated LUBAC 
substrates. M1-linked Ub is involved in the regulation of various signaling pathways, primarily in 
immune response and inflammation. However, the potential involvement of M1-linked Ub in other 
cellular pathways and its broader functional implications remain poorly understood.  
Therefore, gaining further insight into M1-ubiquitylated LUBAC substrates and the role of M1-linked 
Ub in cellular processes, will elucidate the biological functions and significance of LUBAC and M1-
linked Ub chains.  
The experimental approach included proteomics for identifying M1-linked Ub-modified proteins, 
followed by validation using immunoprecipitation, Western Blotting, and functional assays.

2.2 Cell culturing     
 
Three cell lines were used for this project: ERT2Cre:OTULIN-/flox MEFs (hereafter referred to as 
OTULINWT MEFs), as well as OTULINWT MEFs immediately after treatment with 100 nM 
4-hydroxytamoxifen (4-OHT) for 5 days (OTULINAcute KO MEFs) or/-two months after 4-OHT 
treatment (OTULINStable KO MEFs). OTULINWT MEFs were cultured in phenol red-free Dulbecco’s 
Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) (Gibco) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 
1% penicillin-streptomycin, GlutaMAX and pyruvate. During and after 4-OHT treatment, cells were 
instead cultured in phenol red-containing DMEM (Gibco) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum 
and 1% penicillin-streptomycin, GlutaMAX and pyruvate. All cell lines were cultured in culture 
flasks at 37°C and were passaged at 90-100% confluence. 
 
2.3 Cell treatments 
 
All cell culture assays were performed at 90-100% confluence.  
The following compounds were used: for TNF stimulation, cells were treated with 100 ng/mL FLAG-
tagged TNF for 0, 15 or 30 min prior to lysis and anti-FLAG immunoprecipitation. Two 15 cm dishes 
were stimulated, and lysates pooled per replicate.  
Proteasome inhibition was carried out using 50 mM MG-132 and p97 inhibition was carried out using 
5 mM NMS-873. Cells were treated in a 12-well plate with either compound for one, two or four 
hours. Co-treatments with both compounds were carried out for four hours.  
All inhibitor assays included a vehicle control (0.01% DMSO for p97 inhibition and 0.5% DMSO for 
proteasome inhibition and double inhibition) and cells were lysed in 2x Laemmli sample buffer (LSB) 
immediately after the indicated timepoints and subjected to SDS-PAGE. 
 
2.4 Affinity pulldown and immunoprecipitation 
 
Two types of pulldowns were prepared in this project: M1-Trap pulldowns for enrichment of M1-
linked Ub and FLAG pulldowns of FLAG-TNF-treated cells. 
For M1-Trap pulldowns the cells were washed in Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and lysed in 1x 
TUBE lysis buffer supplemented with either 5 mM chloroacetamide (CAA) or 5 mM N-
ethylmaleimide (NEM), 1x protease inhibitor cocktail and 1x phosphatase inhibitor cocktail.  
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
 
For FLAG pulldowns, cells were washed in PBS and lysed in 1x Strep buffer supplemented with 5 
mM CAA, 1x protease inhibitor cocktail and 1x phosphatase inhibitor cocktail.  
Lysates were collected and cleared at 4°C for 10 min at 17,000 x g. The lysates were transferred to 
new tubes and 5% was taken as input sample and mixed with 4x LSB. For the M1-Trap pulldowns, 
20 μL of magnetic glutathione beads and 100 μg/mL M1-Trap were added to the remaining lysate, 
whereas for the FLAG pulldowns 20 μL of magnetic anti-FLAG beads were used instead. Both were 
incubated on a rotating wheel overnight (o/n) at 4°C.   
Using a magnetic rack the beads were sedimented, the lysate aspirated, and the beads were washed 3 
times with 1x TUBE buffer or 1x Strep buffer. FLAG pulldown samples were eluted in 50 μL 2x 
LSB and boiled at 70°C for 10 mins, the supernatant transferred to new tubes and subjected to SDS-
PAGE.  
For M1-Trap pulldowns, samples were treated the same way as FLAG pulldowns if they were 
intended for SDS-PAGE. For downstream proteomics analysis, samples were instead washed 3 times 
with PBS to remove all detergent and 5% were used for sample quality control before proteomics 
sample prep. 
 
2.5 On-bead deubiquitylation assays and RNAse A treatment 
 
Enriched M1-linkd ubiquitylated proteins were subjected to on-bead deubiquitylation assays or 
RNAse A treatment following M1-Trap pulldowns.  
Enzymatic deubiquitylation was performed with the following recombinantly produced and purified 
enzymes: OTULIN (M1-linked Ub specific, 1 µM), OTUB1* (K48-linked Ub specific, 5 µM) and 
USP21 (nonspecific, 1 µM). Treatments were carried out in 20 µl 1x DUB assay buffer at 37 °C and 
1400 rpm for 3 hours. The untreated control was resuspended in 20 µl 1x DUB assay buffer without 
DUB. Afterwards, the reaction was stopped by adding 10 µl 4x LSB.  
Ammonium hydroxide treatments were carried out on-bead by adding 20 µl of ammonium hydroxide 
solution (0.5 M ammonium hydroxide in 22 mM sodium bicarbonate buffer) or sodium bicarbonate 
buffer without ammonium hydroxide as a control. Samples were incubated at 37 °C and 1400 rpm 
for 5 to 60 min, followed by the addition of 10 µl 4x LSB. 
For RNAse A treatment, beads were incubated with RNAse solution (5 mM EDTA, 300 mM NaCl, 
10 mM Tris-HCL, 40 ug/mL RNAse A) at 37 °C for 1 hour, followed by the addition of 10 µl 4x 
LSB.

2.6 SDS-PAGE and Western Blotting 
 
SDS-PAGE was performed using 9% polyacrylamide gels cast in-house, or pre-cast 4-12% Bis-Tris  
gradient gels to resolve ubiquitylated proteins. Pre-cast gels were running using 1x NuPAGE™ 
MOPS SDS Running Buffer (50 mM MOPS, 50 mM Tris Base, 0.1% SDS, 1 mM EDTA, pH 7.7, 
0.01-0.09% N,N-dimethylformamide) and self-cast gels were running in 1x SDS running buffer (1.9 
M glycine, 0.25 M Tris base, 0.03 M SDS). All gels were running at 150 V and transferred onto 
nitrocellulose membranes using semi-dry blotting. Self-cast gels were directly transferred using 1x 
transfer buffer. Pre-cast gels were briefly washed in deionized water to remove excess SDS and 
transferred onto nitrocellulose membranes using pre-assembled transfer packs.  
For improved detection of ubiquitylated proteins, membranes were placed in deionized water, 
covered with a sheet of Whatman paper and microwaved for 5 min (high setting). Transferred proteins 
were visualized using 0.1% Ponceau S stain in 5% acetic acid and a photo taken on the Chemidoc 
Imager. Ponceau S was removed with Tris-buffer saline-Tween 20 (TBS-T) (0.2M Tris base, 1.5M 
NaCl, 0.1% Tween-20), followed by blocking in 5% milk solution in TBS-T for 30-60 minutes on 
the shaker, washed 3 times in TBS-T followed by incubation in primary antibody at 4 degrees o/n.         
Membranes were washed 3 times with TBS-T for 5 mins, followed by incubation with secondary 
antibody diluted in TBS-T for 30-60 minutes. Membranes were washed 3 times with TBS-T for 5 
mins. Afterwards, stained proteins were visualized via enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) solution 
and exposure on the Chemidoc Imager. 
 
2.7 Proteomics Sample Prep 
 
Washed beads were resuspended in 20 μL freshly made MS lysis buffer (6M GuaCl, 10 mM TCEP, 
40 MM CAA, 50 mM HEPES pH 8.5) and incubated at 95 °C for 5 min, following by threefold 
dilution with 40 μL of digestion buffer (10% ACN and 50 mM HEPES pH8.5). Samples were digested 
with 50 ng LysC at 37°C and 1,400 rpm for 3-4 hrs. After incubation, samples were centrifuged 
briefly and diluted tenfold with 140 μL of digestion buffer. Samples were digested with 200 ng trypsin 
at 37°C and 1,400 rpm overnight. Samples were acidified with 200 μL of 2% trifluoroacetic acid 
(TFA), then vortexed and centrifuged for 10 min at max speed. The samples were then used for C-18 
Stage Tipping on a SOLAµ 96-deep-well plate.  
 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
 
The StageTip was activated by sequentially washing once with 50 μL of MeOH, 50 μL of Buffer B 
(80% ACN, 0.1% FA) and twice with 50 μL Buffer A' (3% ACN, 1% TFA). In between each wash, 
the SOLA plate was centrifuged for 30 s at 350 g and excess liquid in the bottom of the tray was 
emptied. Samples were loaded unto the columns and centrifuged at 350 g for 4 mins. The columns 
were washed twice with 150 μL of Buffer A ( 0.1% FA) and centrifuged for 2 mins at 350 g in 
between each wash. Samples were eluted twice with 30 μL of Buffer B' (40% ACN, 0.1% FA) into 
LoBind 1.5 mL tubes by centrifugation at 400 g for 2 mins.   
Samples were placed in a Speedvac at 60 °C for 60 mins or until the samples were completely dry. 
Samples were resuspended in 12 μL Buffer A* (2% ACN, 1%TFA) with iRT peptides. Protein 
concentration was determined based on tryptophan fluorescence and approx. 500 ng were injected for 
proteomic analysis. 
 
2.8 MS analysis 
 
Mass spectrometry analysis was performed at the DTU Proteomics Core, Technical University of 
Denmark, based on the following protocol. 
For each sample, 500 ng of peptides were loaded onto a 2 cm C18 trap column (Thermo Scientific 
164946), connected in-line to a 15 cm C18 reverse-phase analytical column (Thermo EasySpray 
ES904) using 100% Buffer A (0.1% formic acid in water) at 750 bar, on the Thermo EasyLC 1200 
HPLC system, with the column oven set to 30°C. 
Peptides were eluted using a 70-minute gradient at a flow rate of 250 nL/min. The gradient began 
with a transition from 6% to 23% Buffer B (80% acetonitrile, 0.1% formic acid) and then increased 
to 38% Buffer B over 12 minutes. The final step involved ramping up to 95% Buffer B over 8 minutes, 
holding at this concentration for 7 minutes. 
The Q-Exactive instrument (Thermo Scientific) was run in a DD-MS2 top 10 method. Full MS spectra 
were collected at a resolution of 70,000, with an AGC target of 3×106 or a maximum injection time 
of 20 ms and a scan range of 300–1750 m/z. The MS2 spectra were obtained at a resolution of 17,500, 
with an AGC target value of 1×106 or a maximum injection time of 60 ms, a normalized collision 
energy of 25, and an intensity threshold of 1.7×104. Dynamic exclusion was set to 30 s, and ions with 
a charge state <2 or unknown were excluded. MS performance was evaluated by running complex 
cell lysate quality control standards. Raw files were analyzed using Proteome Discoverer 2.4 (Thermo 
Scientific) software.

2.9 Database search and data analysis 
 
The mass spectrometry raw files were analyzed in proteome discoverer (2.4). The peptides were by 
matching them to a tryptic in-silico digest of the mouse Uniprot database containing canonical protein 
sequences. N-terminal acetylation, methionine oxidation and di-glycine remnants on lysine were set 
as variable modifications and carbamidomethylation of cysteines was set as a fixed modification. 
The identified proteins were then statistically analyzed in R. All data manipulations were performed 
in the tidyverse framework. Proteins intensities were log2-transformed, filtered for 80% data 
completeness in at least one sample group and missing values were imputed by drawing from a 
downshifted normal distribution with shrunken standard deviation (downshift = 1.8, width = 0.3). 
The cleaned and transformed data were then analyzed by performing a two-sided t-test in limma and 
p-values were FDR-adjusted by Benjamini-Hochberg correction. Finally, the results were visualized 
using ggplot2.

With the previously optimized protocol for maximal accumulation of M1-linked Ub, a mass 
spectrometry screening was set up to identify candidates M1-linked ubiquitylated proteins. In brief,

Overall, few proteins were significantly enriched in OTULINAcute KO samples compared to

Although, LUBAC is required for type I and III interferon signaling (38), it is more likely that the 
proteins instead interact and co-purify with an M1-linked ubiquitylated substrate. The lack of 
identified candidate substrates together the massive increase in M1-linked Ub in OTULINAcute KO cells 
compare to OTULINWT raised the question, whether the chains are attached to a non-protein substrate 
via ester-linked Ub at the base. Since HOIL-1 has been shown to generates such ester-linked chains 
on sugars (36), it is plausible that it could decorate other biomolecules as well and that HOIP could 
extend them with M1-linked Ub (17,36).

3.2 Accumulated M1-linked Ub chains can be partially collapsed by cleavage of 
ester-linked Ub 
 
The isopeptide bond formed between lysine and Ub results in a stable modification that is resistant to 
chemical hydrolysis. In contrast, ester-linked Ub is labile in acidic and alkaline hydrolysis. (14) To 
investigate whether the substrate is not a protein, the enriched cells were subjected to alkaline ester 
hydrolysis. In brief, OTULINWT MEFs were treated with 4-OHT for 4 days and M1-linked Ub 
enriched by M1-Trap affinity pulldown. Ester-linked Ub was cleaved by treatment with 0.5 M 
ammonium hydroxide for 30 min and samples were subjected to Western Blot analysis.  
A substantial collapse of M1-linked Ub chains was observed upon base treatment, especially in the 
higher molecular weight region above 50 kDa (Fig. 6A). This indicates that a large fraction of the 
accumulated Ub chains is ester-linked, further supported by the full cleavage of Ub chains on HOIP 
and HOIL-1 in the ammonium hydroxide-treated sample, both of which are known to contain ester-
linked Ub at the base (14). Furthermore, an observation of increased intensity of the lower bands, 
indicates an increase of shorter chains after base treatment.

3.3 Ester-linked Ub chains are not attached to cytosolic RNA  
 
In their recent research, Kang Zhu et al. presented evidence suggesting that nucleic acids can be 
directly ubiquitylated by the DELTEX family E3 Ub ligase DTX3L (39). It therefore seemed 
plausible that cytosolic RNAs could be modified by LUBAC in the absence of OTULIN. To test if 
cytosolic RNA is indeed a substrate, OTULINWT MEFs were treated with 4-OHT for 4 days and M1-
linked Ub enriched by M1-Trap affinity pulldown. The enriched M1-linked Ub chains were treated 
with either 0.5 M ammonium hydroxide for 5 min, RNAse A for 1 hr or a double treatment of RNAse 
A and ammonium hydroxide and (Fig. 7).  
While ammonium hydroxide efficiently collapsed the M1-linked Ub chains, there was no difference 
in the RNAse A-treated sample, in comparison to the untreated control. Moreover, the ammonium 
hydroxide-treated sample was indistinguishable from the sample that was sequentially treated with 
RNAse A and ammonium hydroxide.  
Based on this, it can be concluded cytosolic RNA is not a substrate of LUBAC.

4. Discussion and future perspectives    
The aim of this study was to identify novel LUBAC substrates and to investigate the role of M1-
linked Ub and their cellular function. In this section, the findings of this study are compared with 
those of literature, together with introduction of future perspectives.  
 
4.1 Investigation of Ester-Linked M1-Linked Ub Substrates and Their 
Implications in TNF Signaling Pathways  
 
Initial mass spectrometry results yielded a limited number of M1-linked Ub LUBAC substrates (Fig. 
5). Based on the lack of identified candidate substrates together the massive increase in M1-linked 
Ub in OTULINAcute KO cells compare to OTULINWT raised the question, whether the chains are 
attached to a non-protein substrate via ester-linked Ub at the base. Literature shows that HOIL-1 can 
conjugate ester-linked ub, which later can be ubiquitylated and extended by HOIP (34). Therefore, it 
was suggested that the accumulated M1-linked Ub chains that were observed are ester-linked, which 
was further verified in this study (Fig. 6A). Several studies suggest that ester-linked Ub interacts with 
other substrates beyond proteins, such as sugars, lipids or nucleic acids (34,36,39).  
Based on this, investigation with cytosolic RNAs as a potential substrates was conducted, through 
treatments with RNAse A. However, based on data it was indicated that cytosolic RNA substrates 
were unlikely (Fig. 7). Although literature suggests a potential for lipid substrates, this avenue is 
challenging given the expertise of our group, as this involves analytical tools such as lipidomics (43). 
Investigation of sugars as potential ubiquitin substrates could also be an interesting avenue to enter. 
Kelsall et al. were able to determine that HOIL-1 can ubiquitylate glucosaccharides, through in vitro 
ubiquitylation assays followed by mass spectrometry and western blotting to verify presence of 
ubiquitylated glucosaccharides (36). For further studies, a similar approach would provide the 
foundation for the investigation of these sugar substrates.  
However, it is still possible that the substrates could be proteins. Given the challenges in identifying 
Ub substrates, due to the instability of ester-linkages, it would be necessary to optimize the mass 
spectrometry protocol to preserve these linkages (44).  
During proteolytic processing, the di-glycine (GlyGly) remnant attaching the Ub to the substrate, 
stays on the substrate, and is used as an identification marker for ubiquitylated peptides when 
processed with mass spectrometry (45). However, because ester linkages are labile and the ester-
==PAGE:43==
 
43 
 
linked GlyGly remnant is prone to hydrolysis, it would be impossible to identify substrates based on 
GlyGly-modified peptides. Enhancing the sensitivity and preservation of ester-linkages between Ub 
molecules or between Ub and substrates, could potentially yield more protein substrate hits. (44)  
 
Additionally, an important consideration is that ester-linkages might predominantly occur between 
two Ub molecules rather than between modified protein residue and the first Ub within the chain, 
leading to branching on the M1-linked Ub chains. To address this, we investigated the presence of 
ester-linkages in known pathways involving M1-linked Ub (Fig. 8).  
When LUBAC is recruited to the TNF complex, formation of M1-linked Ub is promoted and 
ubiquitylation of several components of the TNF complex occurs. Downstream signaling from 
activation of the TNF receptor leads to signaling regulation immune response, through induced cell 
survival or cell death signals. Destabilization of any LUBAC component have severe consequences 
for the formation of M1-linked Ub and in lead TNF signaling. (37) Ester-linked Ub chains were found 
to implicate the immune response through the inflammation signaling pathways (14). More 
specifically, substrates such as IRAK2, IRAK4, and MyDD88 are ubiquitylated via ester linkages, 
and it has been shown how HOIL-1 functionality is necessary for the formation of ester linkages 
(14,33). It has been shown in literature that these ester linkages are employed to various signaling 
pathways, such as the TNF signaling pathway (14)(46).   
 
Based on the finding in this project, it is suggested that ester-linked Ub is present at the TNF signaling 
pathway, corresponding to that of literature (Fig. 8)(34)(46). The ammonium hydroxide treatment of 
TNFR-associated M1-linked Ub chains, suggests that ester-linked Ub is more abundant in the absence 
of OTULIN, despite its inability to cleave that linkage type (Fig. 8). This could indicate that the chain 
branching differs in the M1-linked Ub chains formed by LUBAC.  
Thus, a possibility would be that OTULIN promotes branching of M1-linked Ub chains via ester 
linkages. Therefore, in the absence of OUTLIN fewer branch points would be formed, resulting in 
fewer number of ester-linkages. This could potentially affect the signaling outcome and how efficient 
it is. Further studies would help in elucidating this possibility of signaling outcome based on chain 
architecture. This could possibly also provide insight into the role of ester-linkages in TNF signaling. 
One way to investigate this hypothesis, is by utilizing the already established experimental setup 
(TNF stimulation (OTULINWT, OTULINAcute KO, OTULINStable KO, FLAG-TNF IPs ± NH4OH)) and 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
to analyze the various components of the TNF signaling pathway, where M1-linked Ub chains are 
known to be present (TRADD, TNFR1, NEMO, IKKα/β).  
Furthermore, assessing the differences in NF-кB target gene transcription levels using qPCR assays, 
would make it possible to compare the signaling and transcriptional responses in the different 
conditions. This would require a slightly different experimental setup, where a longer incubation time 
with FLAG-TNF would be required. The results of these experiment would have the potential to help 
elucidate the effects of M1-linked Ub chains architecture and possibly, how ester-linked Ub chains 
influence gene transcription and signaling outcomes. 
 
 
4.2 Identification and verification of p97 interaction with M1-linked Ub       
 
The optimized proteomics experiment revealed the Ufd1-Npl4-p97 complex being pulled down with 
M1-linked Ub. Therefore, the next step was to determine whether p97 binds M1-linked Ub or p97 is 
modified by Ub. This were then validated by M1-Trap affinity pulldown and Western Blotting, 
showing the interaction between M1-linked ubiquitin and p97 (Fig. 9A-B).  
Inhibition studies showed that p97 inhibition leads to an increase in M1-linked Ub across all cell 
lines, including OTULINWT cells (Fig. 11/ Fig. 12). This suggests that p97 has a strong binding 
affinity for M1-linked Ub, as the interaction persists even when p97 is inhibited. Previous studies 
have shown that p97 interacts with the LUBAC complex and a formation of M1-linked chains occurs 
(47–50). This overall corroborate the findings in this project, demonstrating a similar interaction 
between p97 and M1-linked Ub in our experimental conditions. 
However, the results raised the question of whether p97 inhibition leads to an increase of M1-linked 
Ub because the modified substrates cannot be degraded via the proteasome anymore, or whether the 
accumulation of chains is a result of proteotoxic stress.  
p97 is essential for extraction of ubiquitylated substrates from various cellular compartments and 
delivering them to the proteasome for degradation. p97 inhibition is known to induce proteotoxic 
stress signals as due to the accumulation of misfolded proteins and aggregates. (18) M1-linked Ub 
chains are often involved in signaling pathways related to stress responses and inflammation (51). 
This has been seen in cases of degradation of various misfolded Huntingtin proteins (47,48)  and in 
regulation of stress granule protein TDP-43 (49,50). M1-linked Ub is known to accumulate on protein 
aggregates, where it plays a crucial role in their disassembly. By targeting the aggregates for 
degradation, M1-Ub helps maintain cellular homeostasis. (50) This modification is perhaps part of a 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 
 
conserved stress response mechanism, which accumulate in response to various types of cellular 
stress, including DNA damage, mitochondrial dysfunction, and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress. 
If this is the case, the hypothesis of the accumulated M1-linked Ub chains being caused by the stress 
signal, could be possible. The result in this study, does support this hypothesis, as it suggest that p97 
only accumulates M1-linked chains that are targeted for degradation, based on the overall effect on 
M1-linked Ub accumulation does not vary significantly between the inhibitors (Fig. 12).  
However, for certainty follow-up experiments should be made in order to investigate this further. 
This would require a setup that separates the degradation pathways and the stress-signaling pathway 
from one another. Alternative experiment to investigate, if and how M1-linked Ub accumulates as a 
response to various stress factors, could be made. By using stressors such as DTT (ER stress), 
tunicamycin (ER stress), arsenite (stress granule formation) or heat shock (stress granule formation), 
amongst others, the M1-Ub accumulation, and localization can be accessed. (52,53) By comparing 
the responses to those with p97 inhibition, it can be accessed if M1-linked Ub react in a similar 
manner.  
Another potential explanation for the accumulation of M1-linked Ub upon allosteric inhibition of p97 
with NMS-873 (allosteric inhibition), is that the inhibitor causes the steady-state composition of p97 
to change in a way that leads to an accumulation of both p97-associated cofactors and polyubiquitin 
on it (54). This could be the cause of accumulation of M1-linked Ub on p97 that was observed in the 
UbiCRest analysis (Fig. 10). There are several different p97 inhibitors, which either target the ATPase 
domains (D1 and D2) or the allosteric sites of p97 (55). Each impairs the function of p97 by either, 
competitive inhibition of ATP binding or inducing conformational changes that impair p97 function 
(56). However, these inhibitors have also been found to affect the mitochondrial complex I and ATP 
synthase, which result in the loss of oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) capability in the cells (55). 
Therefore, future studies could include comparison of M1-linked Ub accumulation based on the 
inhibitor type.

5. Conclusion  
The aim of this study was to identify novel LUBAC substrates and to investigate the role of M1-
linked Ub in various cellular functions. This was done through various experimental approaches 
including mass spectrometry, immunoprecipitation, Western blotting, and functional assays. The 
purpose was to elucidate further insight in the biological functions and significance of LUBAC and 
M1-ubiquitin chains.  
Initial mass spectrometry experiments identified no M1-linked Ub-modified proteins of interests; 
therefore, the project explored the formation and functionality of ester-linked Ub chains. The study 
demonstrated the ability of collapsing ester linkages in alkaline environments, and it was found that 
a considerable portion of M1-linked Ub chains are ester-linked. As ester-linked Ub are found to be 
bound to substrates beyond proteins, such as lipids, sugars, and nucleic acids, this was explored, 
however the data suggested that the potential for cytosolic RNA substrates appears unlikely. 
Additionally, the role of ester-linkages in known pathways that M1-linked Ub are involved in were 
investigated. This was explored in the TNF signaling, where data suggest that ester-linked Ub are 
present in the TNF cascade. The data further suggests that ester-linked Ub is more abundant in the 
absence of OTULIN, despite OTULINs inability to cleave that linkage type. These findings provide 
interesting aspects to be explored in future studies. This approach could help elucidate the effects of 
M1-linked Ub chains architecture and possibly, how ester-linked Ub chains influence gene 
transcription and signaling outcomes. 
 
Furthermore, through an optimized mass spectrometry protocol the p97 complex was identified as a 
potential interactor of M1-linked Ub chains. This involvement of p97 and M1-linked Ub have been 
seen in other studies, however the interaction between M1-linked Ub and p97 was also confirmed in 
this study. Furthermore, inhibition studies were performed and indicated that M1-linked Ub 
accumulates as p97 is inhibited, this highlights the role of p97 in M1-linked Ub degradation. 
However, the data further suggested that p97 functions more as an adaptor, based on the overall effect 
on M1-linked Ub accumulation does not vary significantly as both p97 and the proteasome is inhibted.  
==PAGE:47==
 
47 
 
Overall, the findings from this study verifies known literature and provides interesting aspects for 
future research. Future research would hopefully lead to insight to the biological functions, 
significance of LUBAC and M1-linked Ub chains.
