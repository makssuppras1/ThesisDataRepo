Abstract
The production of silicon crystals for use in semi-conductors is crucial to the modern
world. Topsil GlobalWafers A/S is one the world’s leading manufacturers of silicon
grown with the float-zone method. The multiple hour long growth process requires
careful control of several parameters and is currently modelled by a set of differential
equations [Wer14]. However, such analytical modelling does not always provide accurate
control which causes crystals to not grow properly, costing both time and money. This
report presents a control method using an ensemble of neural networks in a Model
Predictive Control (MPC) setting which are trained using only observed data with
no domain knowledge required. The networks are trained on and validated against
a simulated float-zone system, proving that the approach can provide accurate and
robust control, and ablation studies are conducted to investigate the effect of network
architectures, control estimation methods, and controller settings. Finally, networks
are trained on historical data provided by Topsil to ensure that the training procedure
allows the networks to accurately learn the real system dynamics, but implementation
is left outside the scope of this project. All code is open-sourced under the MIT License
to encourage reproducibility and further work.

1.
Introduction
Silicon has become one the most critical elements in the modern world.
Its status
as a semiconductor makes it well suited for use in a wide variety of electronics, as
the flow of electricity can be controlled with high fidelity. The first silicon transistor
was invented in 1949 [Zul00]. The combining of transistors allows for advanced logic
circuits to be constructed by combining transistors in different ways. Transistors have
been developed using other materials, such as germanium, but these designs have rarely
left the laboratory, and none have seen more than a tiny fraction of the adoption of
the silicon transistor. The invention of the silicon transistor roughly coincides with
the advent of the Information Age, marked by the digitalization of society. The silicon
transistor has been at the heart of this transformation, allowing for the ever improving
performance of microchips. Already in 1965, Gordon Moore, co-founder of Fairchild
Semiconductor and later Intel, predicted that the number of transistors possible on
an integrated circuit would double every year [Moo06]. He later revised it down to a
doubling every other year, which has largely held true ever since.
Though obviously most known for its role in computers and integrated circuits,
silicon is also used in several other electrical applications such as photo-sensors and
power semiconductor devices, which control the flow of power in both small and very
large scale applications [Sze08].
Common for all applications is that the silicon needs to have very few impurities
for its desirable properties to manifest themselves.
Semiconductors are made from
monocrystalline silicon, meaning that the silicon from which the wafers are cut forms
one single crystal.
Today, two primary methods are used for growing the crystals:
Czochralski and float-zone. Of the two, the Czochralski method is by far the most
common. The silicon from this process is largely used for relatively low-power devices
such as integrated circuits and computers. On the other hand, the float-zone technique,
the focus of this project, produces silicon crystals with fewer impurities, making sili-
con from this process more common in devices with high performance and reliability
requirements [Zul00].

1.1
The Float-zone Process
In simple terms, the float-zone process works by slowly melting a polycrystalline silicon
rod and letting it re-solidify. The polycrystalline rod is slowly lowered through a coil,
which heats the silicon to its melting point. When the material re-solidifies below the
coil, it does so on top of an existing monocrystalline crystal, forming one continuous
crystal. Unlike the Czochralski process, this method is crucible-free, causing to the
resulting silicon to have relatively fewer impurities.
The polycrystalline silicon is typically referred to as the poly or feed material, of
which poly will be used here.
Further, the monocrystalline silicon is often simply
referred to as the crystal, which will also be the case from here on out.
The full melting process, or a run for short, goes through multiple phases, a full list
of which is provided in table 1.1. The melting process is initialized by heating the tip
of the poly. A low diameter silicon crystal is needed on top of which the larger, final
crystal can be formed. When the molten silicon has connected with the starting crystal,
more poly material is slowly melted. When the crystal has reached a sufficient length,
its diameter is slowly increased until it reaches its target size, which is typically 4, 6,
or 8 inches. Growing crystals beyond this diameter presents huge technical challenges
in the float-zone process, so 8 inches is the largest diameter seen at industrial scale.
Once the crystal reaches it target diameter, it is again grown only in the vertical
direction, until it reaches it full length of a few metres, after which the process is
stopped, and the crystal is sliced into wafers and sent along for further processing. The
whole processing takes many hours. Figure 1.1 shows a sketch of the system shortly
after the crystal has reached its full diameter as well as an image of the process courtesy
of Topsil.
The whole process is automated from start to finish. At set time intervals, a number
of variables describing the current state of the system are observed, which are fed to the
controller. These are illustrated on fig. 1.2. The system dynamics are quite finicky and
highly non-linear. Two of the observed variables (the crystal diameter and full zone
height) have predefined target curves which, if followed to sufficient accuracy, result in
a successfully grown crystal.
There are several parameters which can be controlled to get the system to follow
the target values, here among how quickly the crystal and poly are lowered (the pull
rates), the voltage in the generator, and the speed and direction of the rotation of the
crystal and poly. Only the voltage and poly pull rate are determined by the controller,

was both error-prone and labour-intensive. PID controllers have also seen use, but
these present various challenges such as the extensive tuning needed to account for
differences between machines, growth phases, and crystal sizes. They also struggle to
model the slow impact of changing control inputs. These issues will be further explored
in section 2.2.
The current control system used at Topsil instead relies on a model-based approach.
In the early 2010’s, a set of differential equations were developed which describe the
system. When controls are calculated, the historical and present observations are fed to
a numerical optimizer, which uses the differential equations to calculate which control
inputs best make the crystal diameter and full zone height follow their target paths.
This is done continually throughout the process in an MPC manner to always have
recent updates to the control variables.
This is only a superficial overview of the
current controller, which has been refined multiple times since its original development.
For further details, the reader is referred to the original paper [Wer14].
The issue with the current model is that the differential equations represent only an
approximation of the true system. Such an analytical model of the system also requires
tuning to account for machine differences.
MPC’s ability to continually correct for
model inaccuracies allows the current controller to work well for especially the final part
of the crystal growth. However, some of the earlier growth phases before the crystal
reaches its full diameter exhibit much more complicated dynamics, so the inaccuracies
are larger. Often these are too severe to allow for successful control, making the growth
fail. Each failed run wastes both time and expensive poly material.

The Goal of this Project
This project seeks to demonstrate the feasibility of a machine learning-based controller
able to generate control inputs which allows the crystal diameter and full zone height to
closely follow their targets. A controller for the real system will be built and presented,
but implementing and testing it on a real machine is outside the scope of this project.
For this reason, the focus will primarily be on building a controller for a simulated
version of the system to demonstrate the usefulness of the approach. A large emphasis
will be placed on reproducibility by describing design choices and methods to a degree
which allows this report to serve as an engineering guide.
This point is especially
relevant in the context of float-zone’s status in the world of silicon crystal growth.
Because it makes up such a small fraction of the world’s silicon production, relatively

2.
Control Methods
This chapter introduces a number of methods for controlling dynamical systems, such
as the float-zone crystal growth process. The goal is to give the reader an introduction
to a subset of the methods present in control theory.
Namely, the PID controller
and variations of MPC methods will be presented and their strengths and weaknesses
discussed. The result of this chapter is then a ”wish list” of properties for a control
method which guide the decisions behind the final model introduced in Chapter 4.
Hence, most of this chapter is not required reading for the continued understanding of
the report. Only the introduction (section 2.1) and conclusion (section 2.5) are strictly
necessary for the reader to follow.
2.1
Crystal Melting as a Control Problem
To understand what makes certain properties of various control methods desirable or
not, a formal introduction to float-zone crystal melting from a control perspective is
needed.
In general, the state of any control system can be described in terms of dependent
and independent variables. The controller directly sets the independent variables which
in this report will be referred to as control variables and associated with the letter
u. These should be set to achieve the desired values for the dynamics variables, an
observable subset of the dependent variables denoted by the letter x. Another subset
of the dependent variables are the hidden variables which will be referred to with z.
A final subset are the static variables and referred to with s.
These variables are
categorical and carry additional information the system which influence the system but
is not directly a part of it. The growth phase and growth machine number are the two
static variables modelled in the float-zone system here. The crucial distinction between
the dynamics and static variables is that static variables are assumed to be known into
the future. Full lists of the dynamics, control, and static variables are available in tables
3.1, 3.2, and 3.3, respectively.
The letter d is used to indicate the number of variables, or data dimensionality.

To guide the controller, some dynamics variables have an assigned target trajectory.
Dynamics variables with associated targets use the letter r and will also be known as
target variables. As such, r ∈Rdr where dr ≤dx, as the target variables are a subset
of the dynamics variables. In case of the float-zone system, two of the nine dynamics
variables are also target variables (see table 3.1). The targets are calculated ahead of
time and tells the controller what values the variables ideally should have at any given
time. The more accurately the realised values follow the the targets, the better the
chances of successfully growing a crystal.
Because it cannot be assumed that a single observation of the variables constitute
a Markovian system description, a history of observations is kept. The length of this
record is known as the history window and has a length of H time steps, where each time
step has length ∆t. A critical assumption is that the hidden variables will be carried
in the historical information through their interactions with the observable variables.
Further, this history ideally also carries information about the current dynamics of the
system itself, as this can change somewhat over time as machine parts wear and are
replaced, or if there are small differences in the poly material between melting processes.
The goal of a controller can then be formulated as: Given a list of observed variables
over the past H time steps and a list of target values for certain dynamics variables
F time steps into the future, estimate the optimal control values over the next F time
steps which make the target variables most closely follow their targets over that period.
The time step at which control is calculated, or the current time step, is then time H.
Before diving into the world of different control paradigms, some final properties
of the float-zone process need mention. First, everything happens quite slowly in the
melting process where even small changes take minutes if not hours. For this reason,

a controller should not be prone to attempt overcorrections, as changes to the control
input cannot be expected to quickly be reflected in the dynamics variables. There is
also a varying degree of measuring noise on the variables, and because the process is
so slow, the signal-to-noise ratio can be quite high when looking at even relatively long
time sequences up to several minutes. A lot of thought has been put into developing an
analytical model of the system [Wer14]. While this model describes parts of the system
to a fairly high degree, the very necessity of this project indicates that application of
this model in a new controller cannot be expected to achieve notably better results than
what Topsil currently achieve. This constraint, apart from the inherent non-linearity
of the system, rules out methods such as a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) [SLT21],
which otherwise have desirable properties such as the ability to optimize over an infinite
time horizon.
The ability to optimize over longer time horizons is preferable to a controller, as
optimal solutions over consecutive shorter horizons do not in general lead to optimal
solutions on infinite horizons [BGW90]. Naturally, predicting optimal control on longer
horizons will better predict the infinite horizon optimal control, especially on a slow
system like float-zone crystal growth, where the effects of control will only be felt far
into the future.
2.2
The PID Controller
Automated process control has for decades seen application across a large array of use-
cases. Several control methods exist, with the previously mentioned PID controller
being one of the most well known and widely applied ones. Despite the first formal
description of the PID controller being more than a century old [Min22], its simplicity
has meant that it is still widely in use today.
A simple example is cruise control
in a car, where it can continuously adjust engine power to maintain a target speed
despite changes in road conditions, weather, slope etc. The PID controller is in its
basic form model free, which is a significant selling point for many applications, but it
also comes with notable drawbacks. The control is applied based on three terms; the
proportional (P), integral (I), and differential (D). These three terms go into a weighted
sum. Optimal weights here are process specific, hence the required tuning. A modern
version of the PID controller, the PID Neural Network, attempts to solve this by using
a neural network to perform on-line tuning, lowering tuning demands and improving
performance [Din+22].

Another issue is the lack of a control horizon, which means that it cannot take
into account target states beyond the next time step, which, as mentioned, can lead
to suboptimal infinite horizon solutions. This makes the PID controller vulnerable in
systems with slow responses, especially when the measuring noise is high, and it also
prevents the system from taking actions that not only optimize for the next time step,
but also for states further into the future. In the float-zone process, crystals take hours
to grow, making the lack of a control horizon one of the reasons that PID controllers
have fallen out of favour in this setting.
2.3
Model Predictive Control
MPC is one of the most widely applied control paradigms in industrial settings. It
does not refer to one single control algorithm, but rather to a set of control strategies
with common characteristics. As implied by its name, MPC requires a model of the
system dynamics.
Using the current state of the system and control inputs over a
prediction horizon, the model predicts the evolution of the system’s state over the
full horizon. Typically, a numerical optimizer is used to optimize control input w.r.t.
an objective function, which will typically be an integral of the distance between the
predicted and target states over the prediction horizon. The control is recalculated
at intervals at most as long as the prediction horizon. This interval is known as the
control horizon, or recalculation interval, and is determined by model accuracy and
computational complexity, with a trade-off between correcting often for model errors
and limiting calculation time to keep up with the system [Cam+07].
Here we see a stark contrast to the PID controller. Whereas the PID controller is
relatively simple and model free, MPC requires a model of at least reasonable accuracy
over the span of the prediction horizon. However, MPC performs finite time horizon
optimization, allowing it to consider the system state far into the future, which was one
of the major drawbacks of the PID controller for slow systems such as float-zone crystal
growth. The longer the prediction horizon, the less discrepancy there will naturally be
between finite and infinite horizon optimization.
When implementing MPC, the model is naturally the most important piece. It can
either be achieved through system identification, that is learning the system dynamics
empirically, or a mathematical model can be used if equations describing the system to
sufficient accuracy can be derived. Few real systems have the luxury of being linear, but
linear models have for decades been a favourite in MPC implementations. The reason
Page 15 of 75
==PAGE:18==

is simple: Even non-linear systems are typically fairly well approximated by piecewise
linear functions over sufficiently short horizons. Linear models are easily achievable, as
linear regression is fast, trivial, and robust. The speed of linear models means that the
optimization loop is fast enough to allow for short control windows, compensating for
relatively poor model accuracy. For these reasons, linear models have been popular as
far back as the 80’s [ML99].
2.4
Non-linear Model Predictive Control
The field of NMPC is the subset of MPC methods that deals with modelling the system
dynamics in a non-linear fashion. Compared to linear models, non-linear models have
the potential to accurately predict non-linear system dynamics over a longer prediction
horizon, allowing for better infinite horizon solutions.
Topsil’s current automation
method is an example of this, where a system of non-linear differential equations is
used [Wer14]. However, the major shortcoming of mathematical system models is that
complex systems are simply hard to analytically derive accurate models for, hence why
the automation at Topsil sometimes results in system states so far from the targets that
the growth process fails.
The too simple nature of linear models and difficulty in deriving analytical models
has led to extensive research into using non-linear regression models for MPC. One
such type of model is a Gaussian Process (GP), which allows probabilistic modelling of
the state [Bec21]. Unlike their linear cousins, many non-linear regression models can be
unstable once they leave the data manifold on which they were fitted, in turn leading
to NMPC generally being considered less robust [FA02]. The GP does not suffer from
this, and it has the added benefit of estimating the variance of each state feature which
can be used as a proxy for uncertainty. The GP is also fairly easy to tune, having few
hyperparameters. These properties are ideal for NMPC, and GP has been shown to
allow robust and cautious control, especially in low-certainty regions [Koc+04; HKZ19].
However, GPs also posses some properties that make them less suited for certain tasks.
For one thing, they do not learn from data but instead draw the necessary knowledge
from the given data at runtime.
This is done with a kernel function, which gives
the covariances between data points under the assumption that all data is smooth
and follows a multivariate normal distribution with the calculated covariances [BN06;
Wan20].
The most common kernel is the squared exponential kernel, which models
points closer to each other as more strongly correlated. As fig. 1.3 demonstrates, these

modelling assumptions do not hold at all for some variables, especially the poly diameter
and angle, and to some extend also for the crystal pull rate.
Because GPs do not learn from data, they cannot apply knowledge from other runs.
Only the history from the current session would be available (at least without additional
modelling) which naturally challenges the control ability of the system as it moves to
states which have not yet been sufficiently explored.
Artificial neural networks, or neural networks for short, ideally solve many of the
issues exhibited by linear and the hitherto described non-linear models. Neural net-
works are universal function approximators [Cyb89; HSW89], meaning that for any
continuous real-valued function f : Rm →Rn, there exists a neural network which
can approximate f to any desired degree. This property theoretically allows accurate
modelling of arbitrarily complex systems. Furthermore, as long as sufficient empirical
data about a system’s behaviour is available, training a network for system modelling
does not require any domain knowledge. For these reasons, neural networks have been
used in control settings for decades [Hun+92; GEK88], and it is still an area attracting
significant research.
One potential issue with neural networks is their potentially highly complex, irreg-
ular, and over-confident behaviour on data sufficiently unlike the distribution of their
training data [And21]. This risks unstable behaviour in an MPC setting, if a system
states ventures to far from what the network has learned to accurately model. In 2006,
Ahmad and Zhang [AZ06] described the application of neural networks in NMPC us-
ing an ensemble of networks. They found that using the ensemble outperformed using
single networks in both setpoint tracking and, notably, disturbance rejection – a case
under which unstable behaviour more easily rears its ugly head. This should not be
surprising as neural networks in general benefit in their generalization ability when
running in ensembles [HS90].
In fact, neural networks have found applications in a large variety of practical MPC
settings. In 2000, Piche et al. [Pic+00] described multiple such applications, including
controlling a gas phase polyethylene reactor, milk evaporators, and toluene diamine
purification. Back then, available software and hardware did not allow for large neural
networks, demonstrating the capabilities of even small networks when applied to highly
non-linear control problems in an MPC setting.
A modern example is the control
of a diesel engine using a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) based model was used
[HS97; Gor+22]. Under a variety of set-point tracking tasks and varying conditions, the
model lowered both fuel consumption and particle emissions compared to the industry

standard controller. Similar examples of practical applications include improving energy
efficiency in buildings [JK21], steam quality control of thermal power units [WPL20],
and motion planning for autonomous vehicles [Tan+21].
Practical implementations of control methodologies demonstrate applicability, but
they require much work in setting up, testing, and data gathering, hence why much
research focus only on simulated environments. Johari et al. demonstrate the usage of a
hyper chaotic diagonal recurrent neural network in an MPC setting to provide effective
(simulated) Stewart platform control, which is important in several industrial applica-

This array of both real and simulated control problems solved with neural network
based MPC controllers clearly demonstrate that despite their aforementioned chal-
lenges, neural networks can be highly useful in control settings, so long as they are
trained in a sufficiently robust manner. The attentive reader will also notice that the
modern methods all rely on some neural architecture which specifically model sequen-
tial data, which is ideal in the MPC setting, where predictions are made over a whole
window at a time, rather than step by step.
2.5
Summary of Relevant Methods
The previous sections describe three control methodologies – PID controllers, linear
MPC, and non-linear MPC – along with relevant properties of the float-zone system
from a control perspective. A controller needs to effectively deal with
• Highly non-linear behaviour
• Slow responses to control inputs
• Subtle changes in system dynamics over time and between machines
• High signal-to-noise ratio in short time windows
• No known analytical model of sufficient accuracy
• Observable variables do not constitute a Markovian system description
• Some variables do not exhibit smooth behaviour, even when ignoring noise
PID controllers do struggle with multiple of these points, ruling them out. As men-
tioned earlier, they have also already been tried in the industry but were found to be
suboptimal.

dynamics are outside the usual states. However, as the rich literature on the subject
demonstrates, this issue is far from always large enough to prevent effective control.

number of network in each ensemble being denoted with the letter m.
4.1
Feature Engineering
In any data science task, feature engineering should be considered to make the model’s
life easier. Angles are a good example of how feature engineering can be important. If
an angle, θ, is given to a model directly, the model has to learn that 0 and 2π are the
equal, despite them being far removed from each other in the realm of R. Typically
this is solved by replacing θ with two features, cos θ and sin θ, which encode the cyclic
nature of angles such that the model does not need to learn it.
Given this example of common practice, it needs explicit mentioning that it is not
employed here. This is because the two angles present, the poly and crystal angles,
rarely leave the interval [−10◦, 50◦]. If the crystal angle ever were to leave this range
by a significant margin, something would already have gone wrong. The poly angle
will, except for noisy measurements, only have the values 0◦or 45◦, so it is effectively
one-hot encoded. For these reasons, the angles are sufficiently constrained to where the
cyclic effects do not play a major role, and so this encoding has not been implemented.
One piece of common feature engineering which has been applied is one-hot encoding
of the static variables. Each static variable is one-hot encoded individually, and these
encodings are then concatenated to form a full s vector.
Further, all variables bar the static ones are standardized which is a necessity for
useful and stable learning given the vastly different ranges of the different features. It
is done feature-wise using means and empirical standard deviations calculated across
the whole training data set. This also includes the training targets for the networks,
such that both inputs and outputs to the networks are in the standardized domain.
One-hot encoding and standardization are the only two pieces of feature engineering
applied. This is relatively light, but it is important to point out that the ”raw” data
is already heavily processed, as it largely contains values calculated from video feeds,
which in the strictest sense of the word is the only real raw data. This is not the case

for the simulated data, as it comes directly from the simulation and so is strictly raw.
However, it simulates the real data, and so the feature engineering has been kept the

4.2
Network Design
Going back Chapter 2, a wish list was made for properties which needed special at-
tention. These properties influence the design of the network architecture, which so
far has not been considered. One assumption was that a full, Markovian description
of the system is attainable from observed data over the history window. Under this
assumption, a network can be trained to convert the history into a present Markovian
representation of the system, zH ∈Rdz, where dz is a hyperparameter and the dimen-
sionality with which the full system state is modelled. This subnetwork will be called
the history encoder, or Eh for short, with the signature
zH = Eh(Xh, Uh, Sh)
Similarly, a future encoder is employed. This processes future variables by mapping
them into the latent space Rdz, independently of the history. A latent representation is
produced for each time step in the prediction window, and each representation can use
the context of the rest of the window. The future is obviously not observable, so these
representations are not Markovian. The future encoder of the dynamics network, Eu,
and of the control network, Er, vary slightly in their signatures, which are
Zu = Eu(Uf, Sf)
Zr = Er(Rf, Sf)
Finally, the representation of the current state, zH, and the future states are given to
a decoder, Dr in the dynamics network and Du in control network, which produces the
desired output. These are given as
ˆRf = Dr(zH, Zu)
ˆUf = Du(zH, Zr)

This presented formulation of a network is quite versatile, allowing end-to-end modelling
of the data’s sequential nature, while consisting of three blocks whose architectures can
be independently changed to optimize for accuracy and speed. Three architectures will
be presented later in this chapter, which can be used in the different blocks.
What is missing from this formulation are the pre and post-processing steps ap-
plied. Figure 4.1 shows the full architecture of both the dynamics and control network,
including these steps. The preprocessing consists of two operations: Linear interpola-
tion and smoothing, while the post-processing shifts the predictions followed by a final
smoothing operation and linear interpolation.
Linear Interpolation
Linear interpolation is here used to change the number of time
steps in a sequence. The operation, Lt, converts a single-channel time series to t time
steps by linear interpolating between points.
In the preprocessing, L is applied feature-wise to reduce the number of time steps.
The float-zone data has a resolution of six seconds, which is much too low to not
be dominated by noise in a system as slow as here. However, such a high resolution
massively increases the compute needed in the network. Reducing the number of time
steps thus speeds up both training and inference, while loosing little, if any, accuracy.
Two hyperparameters are introduced to control the application of linear interpola-
tion: λh and λf, both of which are real numbers in ]0, 1]. We define
Hi = ⌊λhH⌋
∧

where Hi and Fi are the numbers of interpolated time steps in the history and pre-
diction windows, respectively. Setting either λ = 1 disables the interpolation for the
corresponding window. The decoder outputs Fi time steps which are re-interpolated to
F time steps using LF.
Linear interpolation between two points is differentiable through both points, mak-
ing L differentiable, as long as every point in the input sequence is used. This is the case
when the output has more than half as many points as the input sequence – assuming
the points are evenly spaced, which is the case here. Because the decoder output is of
length Fi and Fi ≤F, LF in the post-processing will be fully differentiable, but this is
not in general the case with LHi and LFi in the preprocessing.
Linear interpolation is implemented on the GPU using PyTorch which both improves
execution speed and allows gradients to be tracked through the operation automatically.

Shifting
The last operator to be mentioned is the shift operator, which in fig. 4.1
has been marked with a humble +. This operator takes as input the final point from
the relevant history sequence after preprocessing and adds it to the output of every
point in the output sequence from the decoder. This makes the decoder predict future
variables relative to their current values. Pilot experiments showed a slight performance
improvement after the addition of the shift operator.
4.2.1
Fully-connected
The fully-connected architecture, also known as feed-forward or Multilayer Perceptron
(MLP), is arguably the most basic type of neural network. It consists of a number of
affine transformations joined by a non-linear activation function, batch normalization
[IS15], and dropout [Sri+14].
As mentioned in section 2.4, fully-connected networks are universal function approx-
imators [HSW89]. Theoretically, then, no other architecture should be needed for any
modelling problem. Training such a model is in practice difficult, as fully-connected net-
works do not explicitly model spatial structure in data, making them struggle with tasks
such as time-series and image analysis. This issue was what allowed the Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN) architecture [LeC+89] to burst into the mainstream in 2012,
when AlexNet handedly outperformed any other model on the ImageNet Large Scale
Visual Recognition Challenge [KSH12].

The major advantage of the fully-connected architecture is that it is easy to imple-
ment and quick to train, so it makes for an effective baseline.
In this implementation, inputs to a fully-connected block are first concatenated
along the feature axis. The time and new feature axis are then flattened. The reverse
operation is performed on the output. For instance, consider then future encoder in
the dynamics network, Eu. The input to this block is

The concatenated and flattened input vector then lies in RFi·(du+ds). The output layer

used for the history encoder, the final layer directly outputs zH ∈Rdz with no reshaping
required.
To simplify experiments, each hidden layer, of which there are l, has the same size,

4.2.2
Transformer
The transformer architecture has seen widespread adoption since its introduction in
2017 [Vas+17], with variations of it achieving state-of-the-art in many different types of
tasks, including Natural Language Processing (NLP), computer vision, and time series
analysis. Its strength in this variety of tasks comes from adding an understanding of the
data’s spatial structure through positional encodings and calculating the importance, or
attention, of each input point to every other point. Crucially, in the calculation of these
attention scores, every point is directly available, which means that the transformer
is much stronger at modelling long-term dependencies than earlier architectures such
as RNNs and LSTMs. This was especially a problem in NLP, where information can
suddenly become important only pages later, seriously challenging older architectures.
NLP was therefore also the original motivation for the development of the transformer,
but it not should be hard to see how its properties are also desirable when it comes to
the float-zone system. Similar to text, which is a sequence of words, float-zone data
is a sequence of observations, and since changes can propagate through the slowly,
long-term dependency modelling is clearly advantageous.
The original introduction was a sequence-to-sequence model, which used an encoder-
Page 34 of 75
==PAGE:37==
4. Methods
Technical University of Denmark
decoder structure. For this task, however, the encoder is the interesting part. The
transformer encoder takes in a sequence of dz dimensional representations and produces
a similar sequence of dz dimensional representations, in which each representation en-
codes relevant information from the rest of the sequence. This is perhaps most intuitive
in the context of NLP, where the meaning of words can change significantly depending
on context. From here on out, transformer will be used to specifically refer to the
transformer encoder, unless otherwise mentioned.
Each data point in the sequence of float-zone data is an incomplete description of
the system consisting only of observable values. The hope is that the hidden variables
can be inferred by their influence on the observable variables over time. The observable
variables have a lower dimensionality than dz. Therefore, in the implementation, an
affine transformation mapping each point in the sequence from the number of input
variables to dz is applied. This transformation is also known as an embedding. Consider
again the future encoder in the dynamics network. The embedding here maps from
RFi·(du+ds) to Rdz. As with most transformer implementations, a positional encoding is
added here. The result is then fed to the transformer, which produces Zu directly.
When used as a decoder, the output is an Fi long sequence of dz dimensional rep-
resentations. These are fed to a single dense layer, which maps the representations to
the desired output dimension, e.g. du for the control network.
The transformer consists of a series of stacked blocks, the output of each being given
as input to the next. Within each block, the self-attention mechanism is employed,
which calculates the attention scores using h heads. The number of blocks is denoted
b. Each block also contains a dense residual layer, the size of which is denoted dt.
4.2.3
Gated Transformer
The gated transformer [Liu+21] is an extension of the standard transformer encoder
architecture introduced to improve multivariate time series classification. The gated
transformer not only explicitly models time dependencies, but also the feature-wise
dependencies. This is done by adding a second encoder, or tower in the nomenclature
of the authors, which considers the sequence of channels with each time step being
a feature; essentially transposing the input. The outputs of The Two Towers [Tol54]
are merged using a gate followed by a dense layer and a softmax function. As such,
the output is, unlike the transformer described before, not a sequence, but a single
probability distribution over classes.

Here, the goal of the gated transformer is not classification, so the softmax is skipped.
Because it does not output a sequence, the gated transformer is suited neither for the
future encoder nor the decoder. However, the output, being a single vector, makes it
well suited for the history encoder, which is how it will be used.
4.3
Training Details
The training of the networks is approached in a fairly standard way. Each batch consist
of a number of sequences containing a history window of Xh, Uh, and Sh and a predic-
tion window containing Uf, Sf, and Rf. Here, Rf does not contain the target values
themselves, but the values of the dynamics values which have assigned targets. The
sequences are sampled by first sampling a random run and then sampling a sequence
of length H + F within the sampled run.
The goal for the dynamics network is to predict Rf given Uf while the control
network attempts to predict Uf given Rf. The loss is calculated before applying LF
(refer to fig. 4.1). The output of the decoder is smoothed being passed to the loss
function. Therefore, the targets were subject to the same smoothing operation.
On the topic of loss, Mean Absolute Error (MAE) is used. Several regression loss
functions exist for time series forecasting, which this learning task closely resembles
[JPJ22]. The most obvious choices are MAE, Mean Squared Error (MSE), and Huber
loss, which uses MSE for small loss and MAE for larger loss, scaled to create a continuous
loss function.
MSE can be unstable because large errors get amplified, which can
cause gradients to balloon. Pilot experiments revealed this to be a significant problem
in this learning task.
However, it punishes small errors less than MAE, which can
be advantageous. Huber loss ideally solves this by mixing the two, but it comes at
the cost of introducing yet another hyperparameter, the Huber δ. Since the loss is
calculated between a smoothed network output and a smoothed target, this advantage
becomes less pronounced, hence why MAE was chosen. Pilot experiments also showed
no improvements using Huber loss over MAE.
MAE also has the nice property that it is given in the same units as the inputs,
opening the door for interpreting the loss as a physical property.
The training is performed using the AdamW optimization algorithm [LH17]. The
only differences between the networks in the ensemble are their initializations and the
sequences sampled. The learning rate follows a one cycle policy [ST19]. This policy
initializes the learning rate at a low level, after which it is raised to a peak followed
Page 36 of 75
==PAGE:39==
4. Methods
Technical University of Denmark
by a a slow reduction to final value much lower than the initial. Figure A.4 shows
a graphing of the learning rate throughout the training. This policy allows fast and
efficient training by not overfitting to early data points, then taking large steps in the
middle of the training, after which it drops to tiny steps to allow squeezing out the final
bit of optimization. The learning rate curve, which was kept constant over all trainings
in the report, is shown on fig. A.4.
Model checkpoints are saved throughout training, but this is mostly to safeguard
against unexpected interruptions. In practice, the test loss keeps falling until conver-
gence, never diverging from the test loss. Not in any of the countless trainings made
for this project has a training shown signs of overfitting, so all models used are simply
those saved after the final batch. Examples of loss curves for different trainings are
available in appendix A.2.

Handling of Constraints
All control variables must fall within defined bounds. For instance, the generator voltage
must of course be positive and is upper bounded by how much power the system can
deliver and manage.
This challenge is common in control scenarios, and there are
multiple ways of handling it.
Neural networks do not naturally lend themselves well to constrained regression,
as the prediction heads in general are unbounded.
One way around this is to use
a bounded activation, such as sigmoid or tanh, as the second-to-last operation in the
network followed by an affine transformation into the bounds of the prediction variable.
This guarantees correctly bounded predictions, but comes at the cost of making the
learning tasks harder. One reason activation functions like sigmoid have fallen out of
favour to alternatives such as Rectified Linear Units (ReLU) and Gaussian Error Linear
Unit (GELU) is that their derivates quickly go to zero when the input strays from 0.
This property means gradients have a hard time propagating back through the network,
especially if applied near the end of the network, as would be the case here, causing
vanishing gradients.
An alternative is to simply cap the predictions to the defined bounds. If the control
variable u is bounded by [ulower, uupper], then the the estimated value, ˆu, is set as

Implementation Details
The code for the project is all written in Python relying heavily on data science and
machine learning libraries such as NumPy and PyTorch.
It has been open-sourced
under the MIT license. While the data from Topsil is not published, all results based
on the simulation can be recreated using the published code.
A major focus of this report is reproducibility, hence the publication of the code.
This focus is also reflected in the code, where significant effort has been made to or-
ganize experiments and make sure that no doubt would exist as to how results were
achieved. This included standard good practice such as saving the configuration with
all hyperparameters in an easily accessible format (JSON was used here). All experi-
ments in this report are configurable from configuration files which are also available
on the repository. Further, detailed log files are produced with each training and con-
troller evaluation with information on hardware, the dataset, the model architecture,
and importantly, the checked out commit of the git repository.
All produced data such as loss values at every batch and states achieved under
different controller settings were saved to allow additional analysis to be performed
without having to rerun the experiments.
The low-overhead pelutils profiler [SH] was always active to quickly identify perfor-
mance bottlenecks while measuring controller speed at the same time. An example of a
bottleneck found was long windows in the training, which seriously hampered execution
speed for the transformer architectures and was the primary motivation for introducing
linear interpolation. All training was done with PyTorch’s Automatic Mixed Preci-
sion (AMP) module.
Deep learning has traditional always relied on 32-bit floating
Page 43 of 75
==PAGE:46==
4. Methods
Technical University of Denmark
point precision, but some operations like matrix multiplication run much faster with
16-bit precision with little precision loss on newer hardware. The AMP module au-
tomatically changes to 16-bit precision for the relevant operations, which significantly
speeds up both training and inference. This general focus on code speed resulted in
code which in general was quite efficient, allowing quicker iteration on both methods
and experiments. The one exception to this is the simulation code, which is sequential
in nature and thus not easy to accelerate using a library like NumPy. This code quickly
proved to be a bottleneck when running the controller, with the simulation taking up
the overwhelming majority of the runtime compared to control calculations. The one
major optimization which was possible was reducing the number of Runge-Kutta iter-
ations to 2 iterations per time step, which was found to be the lowest number possible
without impacting the simulation accuracy in any meaningful way. While still slow,
this took the sting out of the problem. The next major optimization step would be to
rewrite the simulation in faster language like C, but this was deemed too much effort
for the potential gains.
From an architectural perspective, the code was built to be as agnostic to the system
as possible to prevent system-specific rules being spread throughout the code. Instead,
all systems were implemented as extensions to an abstract base class defining a com-
mon system API. This allowed significant code reuse following the DRY (don’t repeat
yourself) principle, while also keeping system implementations clearly separated and
making new environments easy to implement. In fact, early in the project, a simple
simulation which has nothing to do with float-zone at all was implemented to test and
build much of the codebase. This simulation still exists and works in the codebase just
as the float-zone simulation, demonstrating versatility and robustness of both the code
and methods.

Execution Time
In implementation, the controller would operate on a single run at a time. This means
that inference (and backwards propagation in the case of ODN) uses a batch size of
1. With models of the sizes presented here, this will not remotely utilize the power of
modern GPUs. However, since that is how the controller would be used in practice,
the average time to forward a single sequence through the control network will be the
reported benchmark.
Three methods of inferencing through the control network will be used:
• Default PyTorch forward calls with torch.inference mode() active and the con-
trol model set in evaluation mode.
• The same as 1. but with PyTorch’s AMP module enabled. This casts some oper-
ations to 16-bit floating point precision rather than the default 32-bit precision.
On newer NVIDIA GPUs with tensor cores, such as the V100, this can yield
significant speed-ups.
• Compiling the model with torch.compile using the inductor backend.
The reported numbers are averages of 10,000 forward calls on randomly sampled se-
quences. 10 warm-up calls were made prior to each set of measurements. The mea-
surements were performed on a NVIDIA V100 GPU [NVIa] using Python 3.11.4 and
PyTorch 2.2.0 running CUDA 11.8.

6.
Experiments and Discussion
The previous chapter presented the results of the main experiments for this report.
These primarily focused on optimizing hyperparameters for controlling the simulated
system, leaving questions about performance on the real system unanswered.
The
results also present new questions regarding the inherent stochasticity in the training
(namely data sampling and model initialization). The experiments also do not test the
stability of the control approach for targets far from the training data. The goal of this
chapter is to discuss the presented results and make further experiments to attempt to
answer these so far open questions before suggesting next steps to improve the results
of this project.
6.1
Training Variation and Model Selection
One of the main takeaways from the hyperparameter selection results is that while
a specific controller’s performance is known to fairly large accuracy, there is a large
variance between controllers when retraining the the networks, sometimes to an extent
where controllers using individual models handily outperform the ensembles. When a
simulation is available, this is no problem, as multiple networks can be trained and their
control performance measured, after which the best few can be selected for an ensemble.
However, in the case of the float-zone system, testing new models to a sufficient degree is
expensive and time consuming. One number that might indicate how good an individual
model is the test loss from the training. This number only tells how well the model has
learned the system dynamics within the confines of its prediction window and not how
it will perform as a controller where the target trajectory does not look like training
data. If there is a clear correlation between the test loss and control performance in
the simulated case, it should be reasonable to assume that this also holds for the real
float-zone control, allowing the test loss to be used to select the best subset of trained
models.
To test this, Gtn-S is retrained with m = 15 models in the ensemble. Figure 6.1
shows the test loss for the control models plotted against the control errors using DCP

6.2

The controllers have all so far been tested in their ability to grow 8 in crystals. The
simulated training data contains growths of crystals in mostly the 7.5 to 9 in range.
While the dynamics variables in the training data have a tendency to oscillate, the
targets are composed of straight line segments. These oscillations do show up when
using the controller, but they are in general dampened quite well, and the controllers
mostly achieve reasonably low control errors on the target variables.
One of the major questions posed early in the report regarding neural networks
was if they were able to provide stable control when leaving the distribution of the
training data.
As the targets do not oscillate, there is a major difference between
their distributions and the training data’s, proving that they indeed can provide stable
control.

and 6 in crystals, respectively. Gtn-S-LC, Gtn-S, and Gtn-S-BC were all tested on both
of these target sets.

to complete any runs using all control methods, and even that only completed a single
one of the 2,048 test runs with average crystal diameter errors in the 20 −50 mm range

6.3
Managing Growth Phases
The simulated float-zone environment as used here is tuned to mimic the cone phase.
The cone phase, however, is not the only phase for which the controller would be
intended.
The start phase, coming directly before the cone phase, is also of high
interest. As mentioned earlier, the phase is encoded into the model as a one-hot encoded
variable, so ideally, one single model should be able to model all phases with no changes
required. Figure 6.5 shows a float-zone run starting in the drop phase, which is when
the controller is activated, up to the end of the cone phase. Some of the challenges
with modelling different growth phases should be clear from that figure; the earlier
phases are significantly shorter, so having a model for each phase will be limited by a
short prediction horizon. There are also sudden jumps in some variables at the phase
transitions.
This is caused by a change in how they are measured.
All dynamics
variables are calculated based on a video feed of the process, but when the camera
providing the feed changes, such as when transitioning from the pre-cone to the cone
phase, some of the calculations change as well. This means that these calculations are
both machine and phase dependant, but since both are given as input to the model, a
single model should hopefully be sufficient across all machines and phases, begin able
to properly model the phase transitions.
Actually evaluating this is, again, not possible to do properly within the scopes of
this project. What is possible is to train one ensemble using both the drop, start, and
cone phases, and one each for the start and cone phases. While problematic, the loss
should hopefully give some ballpark idea of how well the networks model each phase.

6.5
Future Work
The results presented in this report are, in my estimation, solid and promising for
building a robust system for controlling the growth of crystals in the float-zone process.
Worth mentioning is the largely process-agnostic nature of all the described methods.
Going back to Chapter 2 and Chapter 4, many of the design choices were made to
accommodate the properties of the float-zone system, but none limit the controller to
this system, as would be the case with for instance analytical modelling. For this reason,
the presented control method could be relevant for many control systems, especially in
the wider process industry for systems with traits similar to the float-zone process
which is characterized by its complicated, non-linear dynamics and delayed responses
to control inputs, while also being hard to model analytically – and of course where a
significant amount of data on system is available.
Stepping back to the float-zone system, the very obvious next step towards moving
this research into production is to export a model and code into a format that can be
used within Topsil’s C++ framework. The export itself contains no obvious time sinks,
with PyTorch providing tools for exactly this task [Con]. This also entails rewriting
the MPC setup in C++ and doing sufficient testing, which must be expected to be a
more laborious task.
Apart from the natural next step, a number of changes and additions will be pro-
posed which could help improve performance.

6.5.1
Data
Echoing more or less every machine learning engineer and researcher who has ever
lived, more data would likely make the model better. While obvious, it makes for an
important consideration here; Topsil always collects data from their crystal growth, so
the available data grows over time with no work required. A model trained a year from
now would likely outperform the ones trained for this project. This would make an
iterative approach of continuously retraining models ideal. Data variation is equally
important to data quantity; more data is not better, if it looks too much like existing
data. To increase the variation of future data, one idea is to add a small amount of
smooth noise to the controller – not enough to impact performance, but enough to
subtly increase data variance. The viability of this idea of course assumes that such a
balance could be found. The noise could be generated using a Gaussian process, which
can produce smooth, correlated values over a time sequence.
However, this approach of course only works if the data is any good. As the machine
learning adage goes, garbage in, garbage out. Hopefully the results of this report make
a convincing argument to the reader that the provided data is of sufficient quality to
produce a good controller. There are still plenty of ways to improve it, though. As
mentioned back in Chapter 3, newer data files contain observations of more variables,
such as the rotation of the poly and the crystal. Incorporating these into the training
should allow the models to learn the system dynamics more accurately, but it would
also pose the challenge of dealing with parts of the dataset containing features missing
in other parts. A variety of methods to deal with this exist. A simple way is to add
a binary, static variable for each feature that might be missing, which encodes the
feature’s presence. Alternatively, a model could be trained to estimate missing values,
essentially a case of imputation. Transformers support attention masks which allow for
ignoring certain parts of a sequence. Specifically for the gated transformer, this could
be used to ignore missing features in the channel tower.
A much larger change in the features would be using the raw camera feeds themselves
as inputs to the models. Currently, all dynamics variables are calculated using the raw
feeds.
However, using the feed directly may allow the model to more easily learn
other useful features, which are harder to implicitly learn from the presently available
features.
A model implementing this could have a vision backbone (e.g.
CNN or
transformer based), the features of which are concatenated to the existing features and
given to the model used here. Topsil does not save the video feeds as of now, which
Page 63 of 75
==PAGE:66==

would have to change for this to become a reality in the future.
Finally, more thought could be put into feature transformations. Since the prop-
erties all represent physical quantities, this could be done in a semi-informed manner.
For instance, the squared poly and crystal diameters could be added, which would be
directly proportional to the respective cross sectional areas.
6.5.2
Modelling
As with any project, a balance had to be struck between exploring different avenues
and exploring the selected ones thoroughly. The choices of design and experiments are
a reflection of this. One case of this was which network architectures to explore. Here,
three architectures were implemented. However, many neural architectures suitable
for time series analysis are available.
A more traditional example is the use of 1D
CNNs [Tan+20], whereas other approaches transform the time series into 2D images by
calculating the Gram matrix [Wan+22], which is then fed to a vision model. The blocks
themselves could also be augmented, independently of the selected architectures. For
instance, running identical blocks in parallel and aggregating the results in a ResNeXt-
like manner [Xie+17] could improve performance.
Apart from exploring new architectures, more research into the existing architectures
could be useful. Unless the training objective is carefully designed to prevent it, neural
networks learn correlations rather than causations. We know that in this case, variables
only affect each other in the future, but the networks could be thought to learn that
certain future event correlate with past events and draw wrong conclusions about the
system’s behaviour. Specifically for the transformer, the attention mechanism could
be altered by zeroing out attention scores for how time points attend to future time
points, essentially nullifying these correlations back in time. In general, the field of
active learning contains rich literature on specifically modelling causations which could
be highly relevant to tackling this issue.
One of the artifacts of making predictions over a horizon is that it leads to natural
jumps at the start of each horizon relative to current values. This was strikingly clear
on fig. 4.2 and one of the main reasons for cancelling the idea of using a float-zone
simulation based on a neural network. The loss function used in every experiment was
classical MAE. A more involved loss function could incorporate the derivative of the
predicted series, particularly for the first time step, encouraging smoother transitions.
A practical consideration not addressed in this report is the handling of new growth
Page 64 of 75
==PAGE:67==

machines. The number of machines has been considered a static property, but Topsil is
in the process of adding several new machines. A way of handling this would be to add
a fake machine and assign a random subset runs from all machines to this. This would
teach the network the behaviour of an ”average” machine. New machines could then be
controlled as such until enough data has been collected to retrain the model specifically
for them. This would work nicely in conjunction with the iterative updating of models
proposed in the data paragraph.
The method presented in this report relies on regression models learning system
dynamics. However, this approach forced the exclusion of a large amount of data from
failed runs, robbing the model of knowledge of what control inputs lead to a run failing.
This critically reduces the variation of the data, as the model only learns what control
inputs to give but not what inputs to avoid. This knowledge could be incorporated by
training a classifier (or probabilistic regressor) to estimate whether or not a run will fail
under some control input and only accept control which the classifier expects to not
cause failures.

7.
Conclusion
This project presents a method for building a controller for the float-zone process, a
system characterized by non-linearity, slow responses to control inputs and an unknown
number of hidden variables. The controller uses a neural network ensemble running in
an MPC setting. The networks are trained using historical process data, requiring no
domain knowledge. They are made from blocks which can be independently swapped
out with different architectures. Several architectures were tested, demonstrating the
merits of the transformer [Vas+17], and in particular the gated transformer [Liu+21].
Two approaches of estimating optimal control were also presented. The first directly es-
timates optimal control, while the second optimizes control via gradient descent through
a model of the system dynamics. The second method in general performed the best.
Significant variation in control performance was found between network caused by
training stochasticity. This makes hyperparameter selection difficult, as control evalu-
ation is time consuming in the simulated case and impractical for the real case. This
presents a challenge in expanding control to the real float-zone system. Methods of
estimating models’ relative control performance without using them in control settings
were attempted with no conclusive results. Experiments did prove that the training
successfully taught networks to fit to the real float-zone dynamics, hinting at control
ability.
Experiments were also carried out to test the control performance on target values
outside the distribution of the training data. In general, stable and fairly accurate
control could be achieved as long as the targets where not too far from the training data,
though this was highly hyperparameter dependent. These experiments highlighted the
stabilizing effect of ensemble networks.
While not always controlling as accurately
as the best single models, using an ensemble would provide significantly more stable
control outside the comfort of the training data’s distribution.
In all, the results are very promising for using machine learning based control ap-
proaches in the float-zone system with the proposed methods demonstrating both high
control accuracy and robustness on a simulated version of the float-zone system. Several
paths also exist for further enhancing control ability.

A.1
Smoothing
The smoothing operator smα applies feature-wise smoothing to an input sequence D ∈
RT×d with T time steps and d features (or channels).
This is done by defining a smoothing kernel Aα(T) ∈RT×T.
The dot product
between the t’th row in A and the i’th column in D computes the smoothed value of
the i’th feature at time step t. This leads to the formula for the smoothing operator:
smα(D) = Aα(T) · D
The t’th row in A, at, is calculated in two parts. The first part, a:t, contains the first
t −1 entries, while the second part, at: contains the final T −t + 1 entries.
a:t =
 (1 −α)t−i, i = 1 . . . t −1

at: =
 (1 −α)i, i = t . . . T

Once at is computed, it is divided element-wise by its sum such that all rows in Aα(T)
will sum to 1.
Figure A.1 shows an example of a smoothing kernel and its effect on a noisy data
sequence.
