Abstract
Automatically detecting strokes in video footage can streamline training and enhance the viewing ex-
perience in sports like table tennis. While modern machine learning models are increasingly capable of
analyzing videos, many rely on abstract representations that reduce interpretability. This project pro-
poses using human-inspired perceptual features—such as pose and object detections extracted by Mask
R-CNN—complemented by semantic embeddings from the general-purpose, zero-shot classifier CLIP. The
method is applied to three table tennis matches recorded from near-identical side-view camera angles.
A custom-labeled dataset of 1,635 stroke instances was created, including annotations indicating which
side of the table the players are on, stroke type, body lean, and foot position for these three videos.
From each stroke, structured features of player keypoints, object positions, and CLIP embeddings were
extracted.
Logistic Regression and Random Forest models were trained on these features to classify
stroke types. Prior inspection of the Mask R-CNN outputs indicated that pose-based features were gen-
erally interpretable and reliable, while object detections such as the ball and racket were less consistent,
which corresponded with lower classification performance when these were used. Temporal pose-based
features showed promising results for stroke classification, supporting the hypothesis that human-inspired
perceptual cues offer a strong foundation for video analysis in table tennis. However, CLIP embeddings
provided semantic richness but were not directly interpretable, reinforcing the need for a more diverse
dataset to enable broader generalization and insight.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The field of sports requires extensive training in order to reach higher levels in the competitive arena.
In racket sports, analyzing past performances and those of opponents is crucial for strategy and skill
development.
Video analysis plays a significant role in this process, allowing players and coaches to
break down movements, identify patterns, and refine techniques. In recent years, machine learning has
been increasingly utilized to supplement video analysis for various purposes. A common requirement
among these approaches is the need to extract features from a video that support training, inference, and
analysis.
For example, TTNet[1], a real-time automatic referee system detects events such as service faults and
bounce locations. It uses a U-Net like encoder-decoder neural network to extract spatiotemporal features,
providing player information in the form of a segmentation mask. The same decoded features are used
to find the ball coordinates.
Another task is predicting future strokes which is the purpose of RallyTempPose[2]. A key technical
innovation of RallyTemPose is their dual attention mechanism in the encoder.
The model extracts
skeleton embeddings for each player’s joints using self-attention and applies cross-attention between
players’ skeletons to capture game dynamics and opponent interactions for the prediction.
A primary objective in this domain, and the focus of this thesis, is the detection and classification of
strokes, defined as the action of hitting a ball with a racket. Accurate stroke detection is valuable not only
for training and performance analysis but also for enhancing the viewing experience. A model capable
of recognizing and classifying strokes enables players and coaches to query specific aspects of gameplay,
facilitating skill improvement, strategic development, and overall enriched video analysis.
In addition to training and performance analysis, stroke detection and classification can also enhance the
real-time viewing experience for audiences and aid in statistics gathering. By automatically identifying
and labeling strokes during live broadcasts or match replays, such systems can provide enriched visualiza-
tions, insightful commentary, and interactive statistics that make the sport more engaging and accessible
to both casual viewers and seasoned fans.
While people familiar with table tennis strokes can often recognize and categorize them based on visual
1
==PAGE:8==
cues like player posture and specific movements, machine learning models may instead learn abstract
patterns—such as cross-attention relationships between players’ movements or subtle temporal correla-
tions—that are not directly interpretable or consciously used by humans.
For instance, one study applied SlowFast architectures on the THETIS dataset[3] to classify tennis strokes,
demonstrating the promise of temporal deep learning models[4]. However, such approaches typically rely
on raw video features, which may diverge from the perceptual cues that humans use to identify strokes,
such as posture or object interactions.
When it comes to table tennis, ViSTec[5] is one of the more notable work. Their model leverages a trans-
former model called VideoMae[6], a masked autoencoder trained for video representation learning, and a
graph for contextual learning to achieve state-of-the-art accuracy for stroke classification. However, this
model uses transformer-based features that may not align with how humans perceive strokes. Humans
leverage a combination of object interactions, player postures, and sometimes sound to identify strokes
intuitively. This discrepancy raises an important research question:
Can a model incorporating human-inspired perceptual features—such as object detection and player
posture—detect and classify table-tennis strokes.
This thesis investigates whether a machine learning model that leverages human-perceptible cues can
effectively detect and classify table tennis strokes. To that end, two auxiliary models are employed to
extract these features:
• Mask R-CNN[7]: A state-of-the-art object and keypoint detection model. It can detect the most
important objects in the game scene, such as the ball, rackets, and table. Additionally, the Key-
points detector can find the 2D-pixel position of 17 different joints for both players simultaneously,
enabling posture analysis.
• CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training)[8]: A model trained on 400 million image-
text pairs, capable of zero-shot learning. By using CLIP embeddings the model can infer if the player
is leaning forward, stretching, has their racket hand extended, and much more.
By combining these models, this research investigates whether incorporating human-inspired perceptual
cues into stroke detection offers meaningful information to a stand-alone model, or as complementary
information that could support or enhance existing models based on other types of features.
Since no publicly available dataset with annotated table tennis strokes exists, this project also contributes
with a new custom-labeled dataset. Three table tennis matches from the OpenTTGames[1] dataset were
manually annotated, yielding 1,635 stroke instances. Each annotation includes each player’s side of the
table, stroke type, body lean, and leg position.
This thesis begins by describing relevant theory underpinning the models used in this work, as well as
competing approaches in the literature.
Then, the implementation is detailed, including the feature
extraction and engineering pipeline. The final objective is to evaluate the effectiveness of these features
using classical machine learning classifiers and to compare different feature combinations to assess their
impact on stroke classification performance. Finally, the results are presented and discussed, followed by
a conclusion that reflects on the findings and potential future directions.

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter first outlines general concepts in computer vision, followed by a review of convolutional
neural networks (CNNs), which are a fundamental part of other models.
Subsequently, the chapter explores R-CNN[9] and its development up to Mask R-CNN and the single-
stage YOLO model, to provide context for the choice of detection frameworks in this thesis. The Mask
R-CNN framework is examined in detail, as it is central to the Detectron2[10] system used for pose esti-
mation and object detection in this work.
The second part of the chapter covers transformer architectures and their application in computer vision,
particularly with the introduction of Vision Transformers (ViT)[11] used in the CLIP model. This is
followed by a presentation of CLIP, used for extracting semantic embeddings of relevant parts of the
videos.
Finally, the chapter concludes with a summary of relevant classification and clustering techniques, in-
cluding Logistic Regression, Random Forests, and UMAP, which are used to interpret and classify the
extracted features.
2.1
General
A vast array of computer vision techniques has been developed to artificially make sense of what a
camera perceives. Early methods relied on hand-engineered features like SIFT (Scale-Invariant Feature
Transform)[12], which dominated until the paradigm shifted to deep learning.
It was not until 2012
when CNNs gained prominence through AlexNet[13] that deep learning in computer vision accelerated.
A convolutional neural network is a type of feed-forward neural network designed to process data that
is structured as regular grids, such as images, by using convolutional filters to extract features locally
throughout the grid.
This breakthrough marked a turning point, outperforming traditional methods
by a wide margin.
Many variations of CNNs followed, like VGG[14] which emphasized depth with
smaller filters, and ResNet[15] which introduced skip-connections to tackle vanishing gradients in very
deep networks. Later in 2020, the Vision Transformer (ViT)[11] was developed, which leveraged the
self-attention mechanism from natural language processing to further improve a computer’s competence

at various computer vision tasks.
2.2
Image Classification vs. Object Localization
Image classification and object localization are two related but distinct tasks within the field of computer
vision. Image classification involves determining whether an image contains a specific object. Object
localization extends this task by identifying the location of the object within the image, typically by
creating a bounding box around it and assigning an associated confidence score. Furthermore, object
localization can detect multiple instances of the same object class as well as different object classes within
a single image, making it a more complex and informative task than basic classification.
In practice, object localization models may output multiple overlapping bounding boxes for the same
object. This redundancy is resolved using non-max suppression. This algorithm retains only the bounding
box with the highest confidence score while eliminating others that overlap significantly with it.
To
measure how much two bounding boxes overlap, the Intersection over Union (IoU) metric is used. The
amount of tolerated overlap can be set with an IoU threshold.
In some cases, objects are not detected at all (false negatives) or an object is incorrectly detected where
there is none (false positive). To address this, additional metrics such as precision and recall are intro-
duced.
The recall and precision equations are defined in Equation 2.1 and Equation 2.2, respectively. Precision
expresses the proportion of predicted positive detections that are correct, thereby penalizing false positives
(FP). On the other hand, recall (or sensitivity) expresses the proportion of ground-truth positive instances
that the model successfully retrieves, penalizing false negatives (FN).

2.3
Convolutional Neural Networks
For both image Classification and object Localization, CNNs are suitable.
CNNs work by applying
learnable filters (kernels) to small local regions of the input, sliding across the image to detect patterns
such as edges, textures, and shapes, resulting in a feature map with new grid dimensions. As the network
deepens, more filters are applied, allowing it to learn increasingly abstract features.
When working with images, a color dimensionality is added to the images, representing the RGB color
channels of the image. One filter will slide across all of the channels simultaneously. Each filter produces
a feature, and the number of filters applied corresponds to the number of channels in subsequent layers.
Pooling is a technique that uses a filter with fixed parameters in the input to reduce the height and width
of the feature maps, while preserving depth. Specifically, a pooling filter operates on a feature map with
D channels, applying the operation D times.
This reduces computational complexity, limits overfitting, and makes learned features more invariant to
small translations or distortions in the input. The two most common pooling techniques are max-pooling
and average pooling. The difference between the two can be seen in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: A 4 × 4 image being pooled with a 2 × 2 kernel and stride=2. Standard rounding is
used when the average is a decimal number.

Another important operation is the 1 × 1 kernel. This is also called cross-channel parametric pooling or
feature map pooling. A 1 × 1 convolution kernel does not capture spatial relationships but allows for
mixing of information across channels. A common use of 1×1 convolutions is for reducing computational
load by decreasing the number of channels before applying more expensive convolutions.

height and width of the image.
2.4
Two-Stage Object Localization Models
This section discusses the evolution of Mask R-CNN. Prior object localization models primarily relied
on sliding windows. Sliding windows systematically moved across the image to generate a bounding box,
where an image classification model would detect if an object was present or not. Much like the operation
of filters in CNNs, this window would cover the entire image. As many different window sizes and shapes
were typically used, the method required a lot of computational power.
Two-stage object localization models were developed to address these limitations. They significantly
reduce computational overhead by decomposing the task into two distinct stages:
• Region proposals
• Feature extraction and classification
Mask R-CNN is typically trained on the Common Objects in Context (COCO) dataset[16], particularly
the COCO-80 subset, an image dataset developed by Microsoft. It contains images covering 80 object
categories commonly found in everyday scenes, annotated with instance-level segmentation, bounding
boxes, and object labels. The dataset is widely used for training and evaluating models in object detection,
7
==PAGE:14==
instance segmentation, and keypoint detection tasks. Each object instance is provided with a detailed
segmentation mask and keypoints if available.
2.4.1
R-CNN
Figure 2.3: R-CNN object detection system. Illustration copied from [9].
R-CNN is the first iteration of the two-stage object localization models. It uses a region proposal method
called Selective Search[17], which merges superpixels into candidate object regions based on low-level
features like texture, color, and other similarity cues. As selective search is not scale invariant, all input
images had to be resized to a fixed width of 500 pixels with the height adjusted proportionally to preserve
the aspect ratio.
Once the regions are proposed, typically around 2000 per image, each one is extracted and resized to a
fixed size of 227 × 227 pixels to match the CNN input requirements. Two different warping techniques
were developed to choose from. The first, tightest square with context, adds a padding of 16 pixels around
the object to capture surrounding context. This is the warping technique that was chosen in R-CNN.
The second, tightest square without context, does not add any padding around objects, so none of the
surrounding context is captured. A 4096-dimensional feature vector is then computed for each region by
forwarding it through a CNN with 5 convolutional and 2 fully connected layers.
Each feature vector is then scored using a class-specific linear Support Vector Machine (SVM). Finally,
non-maximum suppression is applied to remove redundant overlapping detections. The R-CNN pipeline
is illustrated by Figure 2.3.
After each region proposal has been scored by the SVM and overlapping detections have been removed
with non-max suppression, a class-specific bounding box regressor is applied to refine the bounding boxes.
For each class, a linear regressor is trained to align ground-truth and predicted bounding boxes.
A key drawback of R-CNN is its computational inefficiency.
Each of the approximately 2000 region
proposals per image is forwarded individually through the CNN, making both inference and training
slow.

==PAGE:15==
2.4.2
Fast R-CNN
Figure 2.4: The Fast R-CNN architecture. The Deep ConvNet is often a pre-trained model, such
as VGG16, replacing the max pooling with an RoI pooling layer. The output of the RoI pooling
layer is fed into two parallel fully connected layers: one for bounding box regression and the other
for classification. Illustration copied from [18].
The inefficiency of R-CNN motivated the development of Fast R-CNN[18], which applies a convolutional
network to the entire input, generating a single feature map. Region proposals—still obtained using
Selective Search on the input image—are then used to extract region features from this shared map using
RoI pooling.
RoI pooling works on each RoI in the feature map, that is, after the convolutional and max-pooling
layers. It first divides the RoI of height h and width w into sub-windows of approximate size h/H × w/W
where H and W specify the desired height and width of the RoI pooling output. Max-pooling is then
applied independently to each channel. This ensures that the output from RoI pooling has a fixed spatial
resolution of H×W, regardless of the original RoI size.
The original Fast R-CNN is initialized from pre-trained networks, such as VGG16. These networks are
modified by replacing the last max-pooling layer with a RoI-pooling layer. The network’s fully connected
layers are also replaced, forming two parallel branches: a fully connected layer for softmax classification
and a fully connected layer for class-specific bounding box regression.
This architecture is shown in
Figure 2.4.
Training is also significantly faster. R-CNN uses a separately warped image of each region, which makes
backpropagation highly inefficient. Because the receptive field can be large, and sometimes covers the
entire image. Fast R-CNN exploits this by utilizing a shared feature map.
In addition, Fast R-CNN uses a multi-task loss on each labeled RoI to jointly train for classification and
bounding-box regression. Further details are omitted, as this is beyond the scope of the thesis.

2.4.3
Faster R-CNN
Figure 2.5: The Faster R-CNN architecture with a Region Proposal Network applied to the feature
map. Illustration copied from [19].
Fast R-CNN improved object detection performance, but still relies on Selective Search to generate region
proposals—a slow and untrainable process. In Faster R-CNN[19] the authors replace Selective Search
with a Region Proposal Network (RPN). The RPN shares the feature map with the detection model,
eliminating Selective Search and making region proposal nearly cost-free.
The RPN is essentially a sliding window that runs over the feature map produced by a CNN backbone
(e.g., VGG16 or ResNet).
For each location, it applies a small network with two branches: one for
object classification and one for bounding box regression. Here, the object classification is binary, and
determines whether the region is likely to contain an object (foreground) or not (background). Even
though the two branches operate independently, during training, the regression loss is only applied to
positive anchors.
The RPN first applies a 3×3 convolutional layer and then splits into two sibling networks, one for bounding
box regression and one for object classification, by pooling the channels produced by 3 × 3 convolutions,
with two separate 1×1 convolutions. Although the RPN operates on small sliding windows, the receptive
field of each window can cover a large part of the input image, enabling it to detect objects of various
sizes. The architecture is shown in Figure 2.5. The RPN places anchor boxes at the center of each
window, then predicts objectness scores and box refinements for each anchor. Anchors have different
shapes, and Faster R-CNN uses nine anchors per position (three scales × three aspect ratios) to ensure
translation invariance and coverage of different object sizes.

==PAGE:17==
2.5
Mask R-CNN
Figure 2.6: The added mask prediction head. Illustration copied from [7].
Mask R-CNN extends Faster R-CNN by adding a third branch to the network that predicts a segmentation
mask for each RoI, in parallel with the existing branches for classification and bounding box regression.
This enables pixel-level object detection, also known as instance segmentation, by predicting a binary
mask for each object in the image.
The mask output requires a much finer extraction of the spatial layout of the object. However, the RoI
pooling layer in Fast R-CNN and Faster R-CNN applies a harsh quantization to the region, which mis-
aligns the extracted features with the original image. This does not affect object localization significantly,
but has a large negative effect on pixel-accurate masks. To address this, Mask R-CNN introduces a novel
layer called RoI Align.
Figure 2.7: Illustration of RoI Align copied from [7].
RoI Align eliminates quantization by avoiding any rounding of the RoI boundaries or bin locations. It
works by dividing each RoI bin into four quadrants and placing a point at the center of each quadrant.
Then it performs bilinear interpolation on the feature map at these precise floating-point locations. The
11
==PAGE:18==
interpolated values are then aggregated—typically by averaging—to compute the final feature value for
each bin. This ensures precise spatial alignment between the RoI features and the original image content.
Each predicted mask is of fixed spatial resolution and is class-specific. However, instead of using a softmax
across all classes for mask prediction, Mask R-CNN predicts a binary mask independently for each class.
During inference, the mask corresponding to the predicted class label is selected, which allows the model
to focus on learning accurate shapes for individual object categories.
Mask R-CNN also introduces minimal overhead compared to Faster R-CNN. The segmentation mask
branch is a small Fully Convolutional Network[20] applied to each RoI, and it adds only a small com-
putational cost. Because of its modularity and simplicity, Mask R-CNN can seamlessly be extended to
other tasks such as human pose estimation or keypoint detection, making it a flexible framework for
visual recognition tasks.
Keypoint estimation
To train for keypoint localization, the model uses an m × m ”one-hot mask,” where all pixels are set
to background except for a single foreground pixel at the joint location. This process is repeated K
times—once for each of the K keypoints. The keypoint detection variant was trained on the COCO
trainval35k split.
2.6
Single Stage Object Localization Models
In contrast to two-stage approaches, single-stage object localization models perform object detection in
a single pass, simultaneously predicting bounding box coordinates and class probabilities directly from
the input image. This design significantly improves inference speed, making such models well suited for
real-time applications.
2.6.1
You Only Look Once

You Only Look Once (YOLO)[21] frames object detection as a regression problem. It directly maps
image input to bounding box coordinates, confidence scores, and class probabilities, without relying on
region proposals, effectively making YOLO a single-stage and end-to-end trainable model. The model is
illustrated in Figure 2.8.

2.6.2
SSD
The Single Shot MultiBox Detector (SSD)[22] builds upon YOLO by leveraging feature maps from mul-
tiple layers of a CNN to detect objects at different scales. SSD introduces default boxes of various aspect
ratios at different feature map location, allowing it to handle objects of diverse sizes. Compared to YOLO,
SSD typically offers better accuracy, while still maintaining high processing speeds.
2.6.3
CenterNet
Instead of directly predicting bounding boxes, CenterNet identifies the centers of objects and then re-
gresses to their sizes. This approach avoids the complexities of anchor box design and leads to more
accurate localization, especially for crowded scenes.
Overall, single-stage models offer a favorable trade-off between speed and accuracy, making them popular
choices in scenarios where low latency is critical. However, for keypoint analysis in videos with subtle
differences, more accurate keypoint detectors are preferred.

2.7
Attention
The attention mechanisms[23] have become an integral part of compelling sequence modeling and trans-
duction models in various tasks, allowing modeling of dependencies without regard to their distance
in the input or output sequences. Transformers relies entirely on attention mechanism to draw global
dependencies between input and output.
2.7.1
Self-Attention
The self-attention mechanism operates as follows:
1. For each word (token), compute a query qi, key ki, and value vi vector by multiplying its embedding
with learned weight matrices.
2. Compute attention scores. For example, the score between the first word and itself is q1 · k1, while
with the second it is q1 · k2
3. Divide the score by √dk where dk is the dimension of the key/query vectors, to stabilize gradients.
4. Apply a softmax function to obtain normalized weights that sum to 1.
5. Multiply these softmax weights by the value vectors, effectively amplifying relevant words and
diminishing less relevant ones.
6. Sum these weighted value vectors to produce the output for the word.
This process is efficiently implemented using matrix multiplication.

stacked in matrix. Then the Query Q, Key K, and Value V matrices by their respective trained matrix,
mentioned earlier.

Multiheaded attention
Multi-head attention improves the model’s capacity to focus on different representation subspaces by
executing the above attention mechanism multiple times in parallel for each embedding. For example,
[23] uses eight attention heads, each with separate learned projections for queries, keys, and values.
The outputs from each of the attention heads are concatenated and then projected back to the model
dimension via a final learned linear transformation, ensuring dimensional consistency and allowing the
model to integrate information across all attention heads.
2.7.2
Model Architecture
The Transformer model consists of an Encoder-Decoder architecture, where both the encoder and decoder
are stacks of multiple identical layers.
14
==PAGE:21==
Encoder
The encoder is composed of a stack of N identical layers. Each layer contains two primary sub-layers:
1. A multi-head self-attention mechanism.
2. A position-wise fully connected feed-forward network (FFN).
Each of these sub-layers is surrounded by a residual connection, followed by layer normalization. The
final output of the encoder stack is provided to each layer in the decoder.
Decoder
The decoder architecture is similar to the encoder but includes an additional sub-layer that performs
multi-head attention over the encoder’s output.
To ensure autoregressive behavior during training, the decoder employs a masking strategy: future posi-
tions are set to −∞before applying softmax, combined with shifting output embeddings by one position.
This ensures that the prediction for position i can only depend on positions less than i, a technique
commonly called causal masking.
Encoder-decoder / cross-attention
In the decoder, cross-attention allows each position to attend over all positions in the encoder’s output
sequence, integrating information from the input sequence into the decoding process.
Position-wise FFN Each encoder and decoder layer also contains a position-wise FFN consisting of two
linear layers with a ReLU activation in between. This is applied independently to each position, with
weights shared across positions but differing across layers.
2.7.3
Positional encoding
Since the Transformer lacks any recurrence or convolution, it adds positional encodings to the input
embeddings to incorporate sequence order information. These encodings are typically based on sine and
cosine functions of different frequencies, making them invariant to sequence length and allowing the model
to attend to relative positions.
2.8
Visual Transformers
Transformers have also been adapted for computer vision tasks, aiming to replace CNNs. However, a
direct application of self-attention to images would require each pixel to attend to every other pixel,
incurring a quadratic computational cost in the number of pixels.
15
==PAGE:22==
To address this, Vision Transformers (ViT) first split an input image into fixed-size patches. Each patch
is then flattened into a vector and passed through a learned linear projection that maps it into a latent
embedding space. This projection creates an embedding of for each path. A positional embedding vector
is then added to each patch embedding, and the resulting sequence is provided as input to the Transformer
encoder. Following the Transformer encoder, a classification head is used to produce the final output.
2.9
CLIP
Figure 2.9: How CLIP learns zero-shot capability. (1) Image–text pairs are encoded by separate
encoders, and a contrastive loss pulls matching pairs closer in embedding space while pushing non-
matching pairs apart. (2) and (3) illustrate how, at inference, text prompts processed by the text
encoder are compared with image embeddings to find the highest cosine similarity, enabling zero-
shot classification. Illustration adapted from [8].
CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining)[8] is a multi-modal model developed by OpenAI that
learns to associate images with their corresponding textual description. The main idea behind CLIP is
to leverage natural language as a flexible supervision signal for image understanding. Unlike traditional
supervised vision datasets, which are constrained by a fixed set of labels, natural language provides
a much broader and more expressive way to define visual concepts. CLIP is contrastive, rather than
predictive. So, rather than generating a textual description or category, CLIP embeds both the image
and the text into a shared embedding space and learns to identify which text is most similar to the given
image (and vice versa) based on cosine similarity.
Just as text-to-text models trained on a wide variety of natural language can perform a wide range of
NLP tasks, such as sentiment analysis, academic assistance, and summarizations without the need for
specialized output heads, CLIP similarly forms task-agnostic behavior in the vision-language domain. For
example, CLIP can perform zero-shot classification by matching an image to a set of textual prompts
like “a photo of a dog” or “a photo of a plane” without having been explicitly trained on those categories.
This was achieved by training on more than 400 million image and text pairs scraped from the internet.
This large-scale and diverse dataset allows the model to generalize across a broad range of visual and
linguistic concepts.
16
==PAGE:23==
CLIP consists of two separate encoders—one for images and one for text—that are trained jointly but
operate independently. In total, 8 different CLIP models were trained, five using ResNet architectures
and three using ViTs. All models employed a Transformer-based text encoder with varying widths, layers,
and heads, all using a causal attention mask. For each textual transformer head, the width depends on the
number of attention heads, with 64 for each. In this project, the lightweight ViT-B/32 image encoder
was chosen, with 12 layers and 12 heads, resulting in a width of 768, along with a textual transformer,
which has 12 layers and 12 heads, resulting in a width of 512. These are projected to a shared embedding
space of 512 dimensions. This model was chosen due to hardware limitations described in section 3.1.
To align image and text representations, CLIP is trained using the InfoNCE loss[24], which is a con-
trastive loss. InfoNCE helps models learn meaningful representations by pulling related things closer
in embedding space, and pushing unrelated ones apart. It enables CLIP to learn by maximizing the
similarity of matching image-text pairs while minimizing the similarity of mismatched ones. This is done
over a batch of image-text pairs, where the model must correctly identify the matching pair among all
other combinations in the batch. By using this contrastive loss, the training can be made significantly
more efficient, since tokens can be processed in parallel.
Importantly, the contrastive loss is applied
symmetrically in both directions: for each image, the model is trained to identify its matching text, and
vice versa.
Given N (image, text) pairs, CLIP is trained to predict which of the N × N possible (image, text) pair-
ings is the correct match. First, the feature embeddings for the images and the set of possible texts are
computed by their respective encoders. Then the cosine similarity between the embeddings is calculated,
scaled by a learned temperature parameter τ, and normalized into a probability distribution with soft-
max. By having both the visual and textual embeddings in the same InfoNCE embedding space, the
model can learn meaningful semantic similarities between them. This is illustrated in Figure 2.9.

2.10.2
Random Forest
Random Forest is an ensemble learning method used for classification and regression tasks. It operates
by constructing a specified number of decision trees during training and outputs the class that is the
mode of the classes (for classification) or the mean prediction (for regression) of the individual trees.
This method enhances prediction accuracy and controls overfitting by introducing randomness in feature
selection.
A core component of Random Forest is bootstrap aggregating (or bagging), where each tree is trained
on a bootstrap sample of the original dataset.
Compared to Logistic Regression, which models a linear decision boundary, Random Forests are non-
parametric and can model complex, nonlinear relationships in the data. However, they are generally
19
==PAGE:26==
less interpretable.
An important hyperparameter in Random Forests is the maximum tree depth
(max depth), which controls how deep each individual decision tree can grow. Setting a limit on tree
depth helps prevent overfitting by restricting the complexity of the model, while leaving it unlimited
allows trees to fully fit the training data, potentially at the cost of generalization. Tuning this parameter
can significantly impact model performance and is often part of the optimization process.
2.10.3
UMAP
UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection), is a non-linear dimensionality reduction tech-
nique used to project high-dimensional data into a lower dimensional space like 2D or 3D while preserving
the underlying patterns and clusters. This enables visual inspections of the data to determine if there
are clustering or meaningful groupings.
The algorithm works by first constructing a graph based on an averaged similarity score between data
points. This makes UMAP unsupervised, meaning it can work on unlabeled data. This is because the
algorithm exclusively handles the points position in space, ignoring any labels. A crucial hyperparameter
is the number of neighbors, as this dictates how much local detail is retained in the low-dimensional
representation. Another important hyperparameter is the minimum distance between points in the low-
dimensional representation, as this specifies how tightly points can cluster together.
Finally, the algorithm optimizes the low-dimensional projections, to the data relationships.
Further details about UMAPs will be omitted, as they are beyond the scope of this thesis.

Implementation
This chapter describes in detail how the project was implemented. To employ the outputs of the Mask

The methodology is organized into a sequence of modular steps:
1. Described in section 3.2: This step involves gathering and annotating the data required for training

2. Described in section 3.3: Next, the annotated data is passed through a Mask R-CNN model to

4. Described in section 3.6: Subsequently, the CLIP model is used to extract embeddings from the

5. Described in section 3.8: Finally, all extracted and engineered features are aggregated into a unified

3.2
Data
To train and evaluate machine learning models for stroke classification in table tennis, a dataset with
annotated strokes was required. At the time of writing, no publicly available datasets contained the
necessary table tennis stroke annotations. This necessitated the creation of a custom-labeled dataset
based on selected video footage.
OpenTTGames provides footage of competitive table tennis games, with five being training videos and
seven being test videos. The videos are all recorded with a single static camera at a near-identical position
with no cuts. Both players are visible, with an umpire in the middle of the frame, behind the table. All
videos are recorded with 1920 × 1080 resolution at 120 fps. This makes the videos suitable for the task,
as each frame, for the most part, contains both players, and since a frame rate of at least 120 fps helps
capture the rapid movements in table tennis.
In this project, the first three training videos were manually annotated:
• Video 1: 12 minutes and 18 seconds
• Video 2: 23 minutes and 55 seconds
• Video 3: 10 minutes and 18 seconds
3.2.1
Annotation
Annotating stroke types in videos can be a time-consuming task. Therefore, it is essential to streamline
the process as much as possible. One of the first steps in this streamlining was to overlay the current
frame number on top of the video.
Examining the data used for the TTNet model reveals an additional opportunity to streamline the
annotation process.
The frame-annotation in their project contains several artifacts.
In addition to
the timestamps marking net hits and table bounces, their data contains numerous frames annotated
with empty event that denote the moments a racket strikes the ball, along with other events. This
significantly simplifies the annotation process, as these events can be inspected instead of manually
reviewing the entire video.
Building upon this streamlined approach to annotation, it is also important to establish a clear taxonomy
for categorizing strokes. Strokes can be categorized into three high-level classes: forehand, backhand,
and serve. These top-level categories comprise more specific stroke techniques, such as loop, push, and
flick. The selection of these specific stroke types aligns with those employed in ViSTec.
Taking advantage of the available stroke timestamps, a script was developed to assign new labels to the
empty events. This script opens a window that plays a few frames, preferably 20 frames on each side
of the timestamp. This range is crucial, as both the swing and follow-through provide valuable context
23
==PAGE:30==
for determining the stroke type.
The script prompts the annotator to input the stroke type, body-leaning information, and leg information
using the predefined codes in Table 3.1, Table 3.2, and Table 3.3. The body-leaning and leg annotations
are not directly related to the objectives of this project, but they add richness to the annotations and can
be used in future projects and use cases. Since this task involves a high degree of repetition, mistakes
are likely. Therefore, an extensive double-checking process was applied.

3.3
Detectron2
Figure 3.2: The 17 keypoints defined by the COCO dataset used for human pose estimation.
After the frames containing strokes were annotated, the Detectron2[10] framework was employed to pro-
cess each frame sequentially. Detectron2, developed by Meta AI Research (FAIR), is an object detection
library built on PyTorch. It contains several object detection models, including an implementation of the
Mask R-CNN model described in section 2.5.
In this project, two distinct models from the Model Zoo were used for each frame: one for object
detection and one for keypoint detection.
Specifically, the object detection task was handled using
the COCO-Detection/faster rcnn R 50 FPN 3x.yaml model, while the keypoint detection relied on the
COCO-Keypoints/keypoint rcnn R 50 FPN 3x.yaml model.
The Detectron2 model for keypoints returns a list of 17 skeletal joint keypoints. The joints are shown
in Figure 3.2. The joints themselves are represented as lists containing the x and y coordinates of the
keypoints, as well as a confidence score. Notably, this confidence score is not a probability bounded
25
==PAGE:32==
between 0 and 1; rather, it reflects the peak activation value from the model’s internal heatmap, which
can exceed 1. This score indicates the model’s confidence in the spatial localization of each keypoint but
should not be interpreted as a probabilistic measure. The model also returns a confidence score for each
list of keypoints, indicating the model’s estimated probability that the detected region contains a set of
keypoints.
The object detection model places a bounding box around each instance segmentation in the frame. Each
bounding box is represented as a list containing the coordinates [xmin, ymin, xmax, ymax], which define
the top-left and bottom-right corners of the box. Each box is also associated with a confidence score
between 0 and 1, indicating the model’s estimated probability that the region contains a specific object
class, in the same manner as the per-instance keypoint scores.
The detectron2 models come with an option to suppress keypoints that are under a certain level of
confidence. Setting this threshold to something large avoids capturing incorrect keypoints. However, this
can lead to missed keypoints in some frames. To avoid this loss of data, this threshold was lowered to
0.01, for both models, practically saving everything detected. The rationale behind this low threshold
is to preserve all potential detections, ensuring no relevant data is excluded and avoiding the need to
rerun inference. Figure 3.3 shows how lowering the threshold yields undesired detections. Both models
sort their detections in descending order of confidence scores, which facilitates the filtering of irrelevant
detections during later processing stages.
The choice of the R-50-FPN (ResNet-50 with Feature Pyramid Network) backbone for both tasks was
driven by the balance it offers between accuracy and computational efficiency. ResNet-50 provides suf-
ficient representational power for extracting rich visual features, while the FPN enhances performance
on multi-scale objects, critical for analyzing sports footage where subjects vary in scale and pose. Both
selected models are trained on the COCO dataset[16], making them well-suited for player analysis and
detection of relevant objects. For this project, the most relevant classes are person (class 0), sports ball
(class 32), tennis racket (class 38), and dining table (class 60), as they are critical to the specific task
being addressed.
During inference, each frame was passed through the object detection model to localize players, followed
by a second pass using the keypoint detection model to extract body joint positions. Although this two-
step process introduces computational overhead, it ensures modularity and leverages highly optimized
and validated models for each sub-task.
Running both models on every frame is computationally expensive, as it effectively requires executing
the entire Mask R-CNN pipeline twice per frame—once for detecting the players and once for estimating
their keypoints. This redundancy could be optimized in future work.
It is worth noting that, although both models are applied to every frame, there is one minor efficiency
measure taken: the bounding boxes for players do not need to be computed by the object detection model.
Instead, the keypoint detection model inherently provides bounding boxes for each detected person as
part of its output.
Each of the two models processes video frames independently and outputs results to separate CSV files:
one for human pose estimation as seen in Table 3.4 and one for object detection as seen in Table 3.5.

Post-processing of Detectron2 Outputs
The purpose of data processing is to organize and convert the data into a format that is easy and efficient
to work with and that will help the model learn. This involves steps such as normalization, feature
engineering, and data structuring.

of the loss w.r.t. the weight wj is proportional the corresponding input feature xj. For derivation, see

If the input values are not normalized, features with larger magnitudes may dominate weight updates,
leading to worse convergence. Since the aspect ratio of videos in this project is 1920×1080, the coordinates
of the keypoints and bounding boxes will vary by the hundreds, leading to an unstable gradient. By
normalizing the keypoint and bounding box coordinates in the range [0,1], this risk can be mitigated,
and training can proceed more smoothly and predictably.

divided by the width and height of the video, respectively, as shown in Equation 3.3. In addition to the

dimensions, which is beneficial when working with videos of varying resolutions. Factors such as position
within the frame and distances to the camera still influence the data. These problems are addressed in
subsection 3.4.3. However, this improves the model’s ability to generalize across videos with different
resolutions.

3.4.2
Organizing The Data
Figure 3.3: A frame showing the detection results from the two detectron2 models with a threshold
of 0.01.
The most essential part of the post-processing pipeline is the assignment of keypoints to the players. Since
the threshold used for the two Detectron2 models is very low, many objects and keypoints are inaccurate,
as exemplified in Figure 3.3. The Detectron2 model will return a list of detections sorted by score for each
frame. This implies there is no temporal information about the keypoints, and hence no knowledge of
who the keypoints correspond to. However, there are a few characteristics of the OpenTTGames footage
that can be exploited to achieve an accurate assignment of keypoints to players.
These include:
• Stationary cameras.
• Umpire location.
Since all of the videos are recorded with a stationary camera from the side, the keypoints can easily be
assigned to players depending on their position relative to a boundary in the video. Two boundaries can
29
==PAGE:36==
be seen around the central cluster of keypoints in Figure 3.4. A natural boundary is the table midpoints,
as detailed in subsection 3.4.3. Since the umpire is located exactly above the table midpoints, the umpire’s
keypoints can be determined based on this information.
Using this insight, a simple rule was developed and applied to assign keypoints to players in each frame.
For each set of keypoints in the list of keypoints sorted by score, the algorithm checks whether the left
hip’s x-coordinate is at least 0.1 less than the table midpoint. If so, assign the keypoints to the left
player, provided no keypoints have already been assigned to that player in the current frame. Conversely,
if the left hip’s x-coordinate is at least 0.1 more than the table midpoint, the keypoints are assigned to
the right player—again, only if no keypoints have yet been assigned to that player for this frame. The
left hip was arbitrarily chosen, and a more natural choice would be the player midpoint, also described in
subsection 3.4.3. The distance of 0.1 is 10% and should capture the umpire’s keypoints while taking up
as little space as possible to ensure it never misses the players’ keypoints. This will not work for videos,
where the umpire is not directly above the table midpoint, as seen from the camera.
Because this boundary is based on the Detectron2 table detection, it remains invariant to changes in
camera position or zoom. This makes the method robust and generalizable across different video record-
ings. Although none of the videos in this project were recorded from behind the players, this scenario
could be addressed by adapting the rule to use the y-coordinate instead of the x-coordinate, effectively
creating a horizontal boundary rather than a vertical one.

global location. Compared to min-max normalization alone, this method reduces noise caused by varia-

of the poses.
Deciding on the midpoint’s location is technically arbitrary, but for intuitive purposes, a balanced position
between the hips was chosen. There are times where the hips are occluded, but since footage is recorded
from the side, in this project it mostly happens when the left player is serving, and since the Mask R-CNN
model is good at estimating occluded keypoints based on the rest of the skeleton, this won’t cause any
problems for the model. Based on Figure 3.2, the keypoints for the left and the right hip correspond to

Although the global position of the player introduces noise when determining the player’s pose, it remains

the players’ positions relative to the table. These two normalization steps are illustrated in Figure 3.5.
Though the camera remains stationary within each video, its positioning, zoom, and angle can vary
slightly across games. Since normalization is based on pixel coordinates, such variation can cause the
same pose to appear differently across videos. Joint positions may start to overlap differently from one
video to another. Therefore, a large and diverse set of video data is needed for a model to generalize
across different camera setups.
To address variation in zoom, joint angles could be utilized instead of relying solely on keypoint position.
This will reduce sensitivity to changes in the absolute distance between keypoints. A similar approach
can be used for the angle between player midpoints and the table midpoint to preserve spatial context
in a scale-invariant way.
In summary, this normalization method ensures that the model focuses on relevant motion patterns while
still incorporating strategic spatial information, which makes the model translation invariant. Further
incorporating angle information will make the model scale invariant. However, full rotation and viewpoint
invariance remain challenging without 3D pose data.

Table midpoint
Given that only one table is present in each frame, the detection with the highest confidence score was
selected. This approach was found to be sufficient as the table is a large and relatively static object in
the frame, making its detection highly reliable.
3.4.5
Ball Data
One of the most telling clues about when a stroke occurs, as well as who is performing it, is the ball.
By analyzing the ball’s position and its trajectory, classification models can more accurately infer both
the timing and the performer of a stroke. For example, if the ball is at the left side of the table, and it
changes direction, the left player has probably performed a stroke. This could significantly narrow down
the model’s decision space. This is beneficial for both Logistic Regression and Random Forest models,
as it provides a strong, spatially grounded indicator that aids in decision boundaries and feature splits,
respectively.
Similar to the table, the ball is a unique object in each frame. Therefore, the detection with the highest
score was chosen. Like the player midpoints, the ball midpoint is also normalized with respect to the
table midpoints. Normalizing the ball’s position relative to the table midpoint enables the model to more
clearly distinguish whether the ball is located on either side of the table or positioned above it, thereby
providing meaningful spatial cues.

Challenges With Ball Detections
Detecting the ball can be challenging, since the ball is small and travels at very high speeds, which causes
significant motion blur. Furthermore, there are a lot of small objects that can resemble the ball. These
problems can make it problematic to use the ball as a feature.
To measure how well the Mask-RCNN model finds the ball, the OpenTTGames dataset is utilized again.
The dataset contain a ball markup.json file, which contains ground truth ball midpoints at specified
frames throughout the video. These can be used to measure how well the Mask R-CNN model is at
finding the ball.
For each frame annotated in the ball markup.json file, the ground truth ball position (midpoint) is
extracted. Since most of the frames have a small gap between them, a linear interpolation on the frames
between the annotations were performed. Specifically, if the gap between frames was less than 120, i.e.
one second, a linear interpolation of all the frames between the annotated ones were linearly interpolated
as ground truth ball midpoints. From these frames, the ball detection predicted by Mask R-CNN with
the highest confidence score is selected. The Euclidean distance between the predicted midpoint and the
ground truth position is then calculated. If this distance is less than or equal to a predefined threshold
radius r, the detection is considered a true positive. If the distance exceeds r, it is considered a false
positive. If no ball is detected in the frame, the result is counted as a false negative. The process is
illustrated in Figure 3.6.
Figure 3.6: Illustration of the ball detection evaluation process. The top-most figure shows a
scene with multiple ball detections and their respective score. From these, the detection with the
highest score is selected, and checked if its within a specified radius of the true ball, as shown in
the bottom-most figure.

of 50 was used based on the assumption that detections within a reasonably small range of the ground

slightly larger or smaller thresholds would likely have produced similar qualitative results. Experiments
were conducted with different confidence threshold, where detection with a confidence score less than
the threshold were suppressed. The threshold yielding the best results with respect to F1 score was no

• True Positives: 162
• False Positives: 470
• False Negatives: 676
• Precision: 0.256
• Recall: 0.193

number of false positives. Even when accepting detections regardless of confidence, the F1 score remains
low. This shows that the current detection approach is not effective for accurate ball localization in our
dataset.

3.4.6
Rackets Data
Racket detections were assigned to players using the same positional logic applied to keypoints, since
there are two rackets in the frame. Instead of the racket’s bounding boxes, the racket midpoints, i.e.
middle of the bounding boxes were normalized with respect to the table midpoint and saved.
To analyze the distances between the racket and the ball, both must be represented in the same coordinate
system. While it is possible to normalize the racket positions relative to the player midpoint — which
could provide insight into whether the racket arm is extended — the wrist joint already serves as a
suitable proxy for this. Therefore, the racket was kept in the same coordinate space as the ball.
3.4.7
Score
Incorporating detection scores as additional features can be valuable, as they provide a measure of
confidence for each part of the combined feature vector. This allows the model to weigh features according
to their reliability.
Including detection scores as additional features may be particularly beneficial for the Random Forests
model. Random Forest can learn to treat features differently depending on their scores, effectively using
the score as a gating mechanism. For instance, if a ball’s midpoint has a low confidence score, the model
can learn to down-weight its influence in decision-making. This is possible because Random Forests are
capable of capturing nonlinear interactions between features, including conditions such as relying more
on a feature only when its score exceeds a certain threshold. In contrast, models like Logistic Regression
apply a linear weighting to each feature and do not inherently model such conditional interactions.
Therefore, while the inclusion of scores may be helpful for both types of classifiers, the impact is expected
to be more pronounced in Random Forests.

3.5
Temporal Features
Temporal features offer a means to model the dynamic nature of strokes by capturing how both player
movement and object trajectories evolve. These features provide a richer representation of stroke me-
chanics than static frames alone and are therefore critical for achieving accurate and robust classification.
Figure 3.8: Keypoints over time for a player during a stroke. On the left, the player’s keypoints
at the moment of the stroke are shown. On the right, the same stroke is depicted with keypoints
at the moment of the stroke, as well as before and after it.
3.5.1
Keypoints Through Time
When classifying strokes, both the player’s pre-stroke movement and follow-through offer key insights.
This was evident during the annotation process, where the frame containing the moment of impact was
often insufficient on its own to determine the stroke type. In many cases, some context from frames
before and after the stroke was necessary. As a consequence, skeletal keypoints were detected and stored
not only at the moment of impact but also across a temporal window that includes frames leading up to
and following the stroke.
By capturing keypoints across this temporal window, it becomes possible to model dynamic aspects of
strokes such as the buildup of momentum, preparatory movements, and the follow-through after contact.
These temporal patterns carry crucial information that a single static pose cannot fully represent. For
instance, certain strokes may involve similar postures at the moment of impact but differ significantly in
how the player approaches or recovers from the shot.
Leveraging the temporal evolution of keypoints allows the model to better capture the sequential nature of
human movement, making it possible to distinguish between strokes that might otherwise appear similar
when isolated to the single frame of ball-racket impact. Figure 3.8 illustrates how a person’s movement
over time is useful for determining the stroke. On the left side of the figure, the keypoint are ordered
in a way that resembles both forehands and backhands, but by looking at the keypoints in surrounding
frames, the movement makes it apparent that the stroke was a forehand.
Since the videos are recorded at 120 fps, an appropriate frame sampling frequency between the frames
36
==PAGE:43==
was chosen to avoid insufficient variation in player pose. That is, the player needs to have moved enough
before the next frame is sampled. This also helps reduce the computational demand.
To decide on how often to sample frames, the high-paced nature of table tennis must be taken into
account, as well as some of the slower phases of the game. There is a slight variation in how long a
stroke, from the stroke initiation to the follow through takes, but during the annotation process, the
average time was estimated to be approximately a quarter of a second. Since that is pretty fast, but
the video is recorded at a very high fps, it was decided that sampling every second frame provides a
reasonable balance, capturing enough motion variation while reducing redundancy. However, serves can
last for more than a second, demanding a window that is sure to capture the entire serve process. This
will cause overlapping windows, which were addressed in section 3.3. A larger window will also include
past and future strokes when placed in the middle of a rally, helping the model learn if one type of stroke
will lead to a certain response, as well as capturing the turn-based nature of the game. But the window
must also not be too large, as including frames that are too far before or after the stroke may introduce
unrelated features, which will confuse the model. This is particularly important for classical models such
as Logistic Regression or Random Forests, which do not inherently model temporal dependencies and

3.5.2
Objects Through Time
Tracking objects moving through time also provides crucial information. By specifically looking at the
ball and detecting the time when the ball changes direction, a clue about when a stroke is happening is
provided. The same goes for the racket, as both the speed and the angle at which a racket moves serve as
strong indicators of when and what stroke took place. In particular, abrupt changes in racket direction or
acceleration often correlate with stroke events. By analyzing these motion patterns, the model can gain a
more nuanced understanding of stroke timing and classification. Additionally, combining ball trajectory
analysis with racket motion creates a richer temporal context for detecting and categorizing strokes.
3.6
Applying CLIP
After the keypoints and bounding boxes have been assigned to their corresponding players, the CLIP
model can be applied.
The motivation behind extracting CLIP embeddings from player and object crops is to obtain rich,
semantically meaningful representations of the visual content in each frame.
CLIP can find image embeddings for the entire frame, but this is not useful for this purpose. Since
embeddings of the whole frame might look radically different depending on the videos, a model that
37
==PAGE:44==
should generalize across videos would struggle to learn consistent patterns. A more suitable approach
would be to consider certain parts of the frame, specifically the crops of the players and objects.
3.6.1
Objects crops
Getting player crops is relatively straightforward, since all of the player and object bounding boxes are
sorted, assigned, and saved in the post-processed file described in subsection 3.4.8. These selected regions
are then cropped and stored in directories organized by video, frame number, and object class.

3.6.2
CLIP Embeddings
The CLIP embeddings were extracted from the crops for both players’ crops for all the frames in a
video. Although the crops will have different sizes, the CLIP preprocessing pipeline resizes them to a
224×224 image, matching the input requirements of the ViT-B/32 model. Before feeding the image crops
into CLIP, each crop is center-cropped and normalized using the standard ImageNet mean and standard
deviation, as required by the ViT-B/32 preprocessing pipeline.

==PAGE:45==
Figure 3.10: Directory structure of the embedding folder.
By extracting these 512-dimensional vector embeddings, the hope is that their structure captures mean-
ingful information about the players and objects. For example, a ball with motion blur might indicate
that it has not yet been struck, as opposed to a clear, sharply defined ball. Similarly, a player leaning
forward while hitting a backhand may yield a significantly different embedding compared to a player
leaning sideways for a forehand shot.
The CLIP model is trained to be a zero-shot classifier, meaning it is a very general model. Therefore,
a lot of information is captured in the embedding, which might not be relevant, such as the color of the
shirt or background.
3.7
Data Augmentation
Once the full table has been constructed, a straightforward horizontal mirroring of the data can be
performed to augment the dataset. The first step involves horizontally flipping the image. Given that all
coordinates are normalized, mirroring a keypoint along the x-axis is achieved simply by replacing each
x-coordinate with 1 −x.
Subsequently, the order of the keypoints must be adjusted to account for the change in orientation. After
flipping, what was originally on the left side of the player is now on the right, and vice versa. Therefore,
the keypoints need to be reordered to match the convention defined in Figure 3.2.
In addition to keypoints, bounding box coordinates must also be updated. Specifically, the xmin and
xmax values need to be swapped to correctly reflect the mirrored box.
Once the keypoints and bounding boxes have been adjusted for the mirrored image, the corresponding
values can be inserted into their respective columns in the dataset. After updating all bounding boxes,
39
==PAGE:46==
the cropped image regions can also be mirrored. These mirrored crops are then used to extract new CLIP
embeddings for the flipped image.
Finally, the label must be swapped. The word ”left” in the label becomes ”right” and vice versa.
Adding mirrored data to the training set most likely confuses the classifier, as there are no videos con-
taining left-handed players, and so the accuracy will probably suffer a bit. However, this trade-off may be
justified, as the inclusion of mirrored samples could improve the model’s ability to generalize to real-world
scenarios, but since this is currently not measurable, no classifier was trained with mirrored data.
3.8
Combining features
Figure 3.11: The composition of the full feature vector. It contains features from the left player,
table midpoints, and the right player.
The spatiotemporal features described in the previous section are combined into a single feature vector,
as illustrated in Figure 3.11. The construction of the full feature vector always begins with the left player.
For each frame within the temporal window, the keypoints, player midpoints, and racket midpoints are
concatenated along with their respective confidence scores. After processing all frames for the left player,
the CLIP embedding of the cropped image of the player taken from the middle frame of the temporal
window is appended. This means that CLIP embeddings for a player were only extracted and appended
for one frame. Next, the ball midpoints are added for each frame in the temporal window, followed by
the table midpoints. The right player’s features are then appended using the same procedure as for the
left player: keypoints, player midpoints, and racket midpoints with scores across all frames, followed by
the CLIP embedding from the center frame.
The final feature vector captures both spatial and temporal information for both players, as well as the
rackets, balls, and table dynamics, simultaneously.
Since it is not known in advance which player performs the stroke, features from both players must be
included in the combined feature vector. Including features from both players in the combined feature
vector also offers two key advantages. First, by incorporating features from the entire frame, the model
gains access to richer contextual information, from which stroke-related patterns can be learned. Second,
the receiving player’s position can also provide cues about the ongoing stroke. In high-speed games like
table tennis, a player must anticipate the opponent’s stroke before it occurs, due to the limited reaction
time and short distance between players. Capturing both players’ positions allows the model to learn
from these anticipatory movements.
40
==PAGE:47==
The implementation of the code includes parameter flags to easily add or omit features. Each keypoint
contains two coordinates, since the third element, confidence score, is discarded. The full feature vector,
with all flags turned on, would have the following structure:
• Keypoints: A single player has 17 keypoints with 2 coordinates each, flattened into a 34-dimensional
vector. One additional dimension is added for an aggregate confidence score, making it 35 dimen-
sions.
• Player midpoint: 2 coordinates plus 1 score →3 dimensions. Total becomes 38 dimensions.
• Racket midpoint: 2 coordinates plus 1 score →3 dimensions. Final per-frame player feature: 40
dimensions.
Given a temporal window of 180 frames sampled every other frame, 91 frames are used (45 before and
45 after the center frame). This results in:
• Per-player temporal features: 40 × 91 = 3640 dimensions
• CLIP embedding: 512 dimensions
• Left player total: 3640 + 512 = 4152 dimensions
Additional features:
• Ball midpoints: 3 × 91 = 273
• Table midpoints: 3 × 91 = 273
Cumulative total: 4152 + 273 + 273 = 4698 dimensions
• Right player features: 4152 (same as left)
Final feature vector size: 4698 + 4152 = 8850 dimensions
3.8.1
Dimensionality Reduction
Dimensionality reduction techniques, such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), were not applied to
the combined feature vector. The decision was based on both the structure and nature of the extracted
features. The skeletal keypoints are already low-dimensional, making further reduction unnecessary.
The CLIP embeddings are produced by a pretrained model that maps visual and textual data into a shared
semantic space. Applying dimensionality reduction to these embeddings risks distorting the semantic
structure and degrading the quality of the representation. Moreover, as there were no computational or
memory constraints in the system, preserving the original feature representations was deemed appropriate.
3.8.2
Missing Data Handling Strategies
Since the frame data depends on the Mask R-CNN model, detections are not guaranteed for every frame.
The model may occasionally fail to detect objects, resulting in missing data. Moreover, the risk of false
41
==PAGE:48==
negatives increases in cases of partial occlusion. In addition, some detections are intentionally discarded
due to low confidence scores.
As mentioned, the threshold used in the Detectron2 models has been lowered to 0.01. This generates
many low-quality detections, which degrade overall model performance. As discussed in section 3.4, this
issue can be mitigated by only choosing the set of keypoints with the highest score on each side of the
table. However, there are cases where the player of interest is not visible in the frame. Due to the small
threshold, the models may falsely identify a player in situations where none exist. As a result, the player
is assigned a false set of keypoints along with the low associated score. These false detections must be
filtered out to avoid degrading model performance. A secondary confidence threshold is therefore applied
to remove such low-quality detections. Since the Detectron2 model is highly reliable at finding keypoints,
this threshold is set to 0.9.
This leads to a sparse dataset. Given this inherent uncertainty, three strategies have been developed to
address missing data, listed below.
Dropping Samples
One strategy is to simply discard a feature vector with missing data. This results in varying degrees of
data loss, depending on the features included. When training on keypoints alone, barely any samples are
lost. Conversely, all training samples are discarded when training on balls and rackets simultaneously.
0-Padding
Another approach is to use a placeholder value for missing data. In this case, zeroes are used as place-
holders. This makes it possible to retain all the training data, at the expense of a noisier feature vector.
Last Known Location
Another approach is to use detections from previous frames. This yields a feature vector that is close to
the truth, particularly when the temporal gap is short and the object remains within the scene. If the
object temporarily leaves the frame, carrying forward its last known location may still be a reasonable
approximation for short periods. However, the longer the gap or the more dynamic the scene, the less
reliable this approximation becomes.
Deciding on a Strategy
When choosing between these strategies, the amount of available data between points of interest has to be
considered. When the temporal window is large or if the entire video is being processed, the last known
location strategy becomes a valid solution. This is often the case during inference or live processing, but
not during training.
This could lead to a combination of strategies, with applying the Last Known Location during inference
and either of the other strategies during training. This would eliminate the risk of having too large gaps
42
==PAGE:49==
during training, while utilizing the Last Known Location during inference.
Another approach is to use the Last Known Location within a range of frames, and then fall back to
one of the other two strategies beyond that range. This offers a balance between temporal coherence
and robustness to long gaps, ensuring smoother predictions in short-term occlusions while avoiding the
propagation of stale or inaccurate information. For example, if a racket is briefly occluded by the player’s
body, using its last known location for a few frames can preserve temporal continuity. However, if it
remains undetected for longer, switching to zero-padding or dropping the sample can prevent using a
location that is too far from the truth.
If the model is being applied to a video, a linear interpolation strategy can also be used. This involves
estimating the missing values by linearly interpolating between the last known and next known detections.
This will lead to better information when the gap of missing data is small and the object’s motion is
relatively smooth or predictable. In such cases, linear interpolation can preserve temporal coherence and
reduce the abrupt discontinuities caused by zero-padding or dropped samples.
Compared to using the Last Known Location, interpolation can provide a closer approximation to the
true trajectory, especially for objects that remain within the frame but go undetected briefly, and was
used in subsection 3.4.5.
In this project, both the training and inference strategies rely on the zero-padding strategy. This is
partly due to the very sparse detections of the ball and rackets, and to keep the strategy consistent across
experiments.
Using zero-padding ensures that the model sees a consistent input structure across all
samples, simplifying the data pipeline and avoiding the need for custom logic to handle missing entries.
It also prevents the loss of rare samples, especially in cases where dropping them would significantly
reduce the already limited training data.
3.9
Stroke Detection
A crucial aspect that has been overlooked so far is detecting strokes. This refers to the model’s ability
to accurately segment the parts of a video that contain an actual stroke. If the model has learned a
good representation of stroke events, the softmax outputs should remain relatively low across and even
across the output classes, during non-stroke segments, and sharply peak during strokes. However, certain
common movements — such as crouching and then standing up — can closely resemble the motion
pattern of a stroke, potentially leading to false positives. Figure 3.12 shows the probabilities for a stroke
occurring.

3.9.1
Game Phases
In table tennis and other racket sports, the game can be split into two main phases, which are the
”Pause” phases and the ”Play” phases. The first of these moments represents the phases in between
rallies, whereas the latter represents the brief periods between strokes in a rally. This approach mitigates
the risk that strokes will be falsely detected in segments where no strokes are actually occurring.
44

Figure 3.13: The same plot as Figure 3.12, using the same model features, but with a model
trained on phase frames as well.
Figure 3.13 shows a significant improvement in reducing false positives, with nearly all the frames in
the pause phase classified as the ”pause” class. By using phase-labeled training frames, the strokes are
thereby segmented more precisely.
Why Two Negative Classes?
The player features used by the model can often look radically different from each other during these
phases. In the ”pause” phase, the players can be positioned, standing, and facing in almost random
directions. Sometimes the players or objects won’t even be present in the frame. On the other hand,
during the ”play” phase, players are typically in a dynamic stance and preparing to return a shot, and
the objects of interest will most likely be present in the frame as well.
Furthermore, by having two different negative classes, representing both phases of the game, the model
will be useful for a more diverse set of use cases. One such use case is counting points, discussed in
section 4.6
Annotating Game Phases
Annotating frames belonging to the two phases is relatively straightforward. The first key observation
is that a pause phase consistently occurs before a serve. The second key observation is that a stroke
followed by a non-serve stroke typically indicates a play phase. With these observations, an annotation
between two frames containing a stroke, with the latter not being a serve, was added as a ”play-frame”.
Conversely, an annotation between two frames containing a stroke with the latter being a serve was added
as a ”pause-frame”, with the first frame signifying the end of a rally.

pause frames in these periods. The tactic is, if the distance between the strokes in the pause phase is
greater than 1000, an annotation in the middle labeled as the pause is added as usual. In addition, two
other annotations, 400 frames before and 400 frames after, are labeled as pauses as well. All numerical
values have been rounded up using the ceiling method. This means that any fractional value has been
rounded up to the nearest whole number. This practice is illustrated in Figure 3.14.

Chapter 4
Results
This chapter presents the results obtained from training on the features and techniques described in
previous sections. To obtain an idea of how well the models perform, a leave-one-video-out cross-validation
has been performed, as well as a visual inspection of a video with a model trained on the full dataset. All
of the models used have been trained and tested using the zero-padding missing data strategy explained
in subsection 3.8.2.
4.1
Cross-validation
To ensure that a model performs well across different subsets of the data, cross-validation is a widely
used technique. Since there are three annotated videos, three different training and test splits can be
used, resulting in three folds of cross-validation.
This simple fold structure is not balanced because the videos contain different amounts of annotated
strokes.
In each fold, two of the videos are used for training while the remaining one is used for testing. This setup
allows for evaluating the model’s generalization ability across different gameplay styles, camera angles,
and player behaviors captured in each video. Rotating the test video across all three possibilities ensures
that the model is not overly specialized to a specific player characteristic.
No separate validation set is used in this setup. Given the limited number of annotated videos, reserving
additional data for validation would reduce the amount of training data too severely. Moreover, since no
extensive hyperparameter tuning is performed, using the full training portion of each fold for model fitting
is reasonable. Model performance is instead assessed across folds through this rotation-based evaluation.
However, since the videos vary in length and stroke density, the train-test split will differ between folds,
which causes a distribution shift. This imbalance should be kept in mind when interpreting performance
metrics. One fold may contain more diverse strokes or longer rallies, affecting how well the model learns
to generalize.

4.2
Clustering
Linear models such as Logistic Regression rely on features to be linearly separable, hence why well-defined
clustering in the data is ideal. To get a visual understanding of the high-dimensional feature vectors used
for classification, a UMAP was applied to different combinations of the feature vector. However, as the
dimensionality of the feature vectors increases, the UMAP struggles to preserve meaningful separations
between clusters.
Figure 4.1: A 2D UMAP of the 512-dimensional CLIP embeddings. The colors and shapes of the
data points do not match other UMAPs in the report.
Figure 4.1 shows a UMAP of the CLIP embeddings for every stroke and phase in all three videos. The
UMAP is able to show some clusters, but the clusters are not clean, i.e, they contain multiple class labels,
which can degrade a classifier’s performance.
Since CLIP is trained on a very diverse dataset with natural language and outputs a high-dimensional
embedding space for the image features, an image of an individual standing upright should be close to,
but fairly separated from, an image of the same person leaning to a side. This is further exemplified in
Figure 4.2 by using textual embeddings. But due to the high level of generality in CLIP, many features
related to the image, such as color or background elements, will be captured, even if they are irrelevant
to the stroke.
For instance, if a player in a red shirt is often positioned on the right side, a model
trained on CLIP features could inadvertently overfit to this association. Therefore, the clusters might
just represent different player configurations rather than different strokes, which may hinder the model’s
ability to generalize effectively to unseen videos where these visual cues are absent.
48
==PAGE:55==
Another challenge with using CLIP embeddings arises due to the variation in crop sizes. The player
bounding boxes vary in size from each detection, and so the crops will have different aspect ratios. As
mentioned in subsection 3.6.2, the CLIP model resizes images to 224×224. Although CLIP’s preprocessing
is reported to be fairly robust, extreme aspect-ratio changes or very small crops can still distort spatial
relations and shift the resulting embeddings. Consequently, it is uncertain whether the resizing step has
a negligible effect in this setting. The player crops can have significantly different sizes when affected
by occlusion events, which happen most often during the left-serve, where the player will sometimes
stand behind the table relative to the camera, causing the bounding box to become small and thereby
potentially introducing additional noise into the embedding space.

patterns than intended.
In contrast, analyzing skeletal keypoints offers a more focused and semantically meaningful representation
for stroke classification. Keypoints directly capture the structure of the human body, abstracting away
irrelevant visual information such as clothing, background, or lighting conditions. Although 2D keypoints

Ablation Study

were conducted. Two classifiers were used: a Logistic Regression, chosen for its ability to linearly separate
classes, and Random Forest, selected for its capability to capture more complex relationships.
Both
classifiers were trained on 43 distinct feature configurations.
The experiments relied on scikit-learn’s[26] implementations of both models. In scikit-learn, Logistic
Regression employs built-in early stopping to limit overfitting, whereas random forests were trained
without a maximum-depth constraint, increasing their risk of memorizing the training set.

performance even when using noisy or high-dimensional feature sets. However, this flexibility also makes
it more prone to overfitting, especially in the absence of depth constraints.

4.6
Points
A model that segments a video into strokes and phases can also be used to count points. In table tennis, a
point is typically awarded after each rally, which ends with a pause phase. Therefore, pauses often mark
the end of a rally. The simple rule applied here is to increment a point counter at every serve, as each
serve marks the transition from a pause phase to a new rally. While this method is simple, it introduces
two systematic issues: it adds a point at the beginning of the game (before the first real rally), and it
misses the final point of the match. These two effects, however, roughly cancel each other out.
A more intuitive approach would be to count every beginning of a pause phase as a point, mimicking how
points are usually counted in table tennis and other racket sports. The confusion matrix in section 4.5
shows that pause phases are more frequently misclassified than serves, making serves a more reliable
measure for incrementing the point counter.
There are also false positive serves, which cause false increments in the point counter. To mitigate this
issue, the scoring logic combines both pause-phase and serve detections. Specifically, a point is only
counted if a serve occurs immediately after a detected pause. This is implemented using a flag called
point pending, which is set to true whenever the model enters a pause phase. This flag remains active
until a stroke is detected. If the first stroke after a pause is a serve, the point counter is incremented.
This logic also handles cases where a pause is momentarily misclassified as play—a common confusion
noted in Table 4.6—by waiting for a valid stroke to confirm the transition.
With this approach, a naive point counting system can be implemented; however, it still cannot assign
points to individual players and does not account for re-serves.
4.6.1
Moving Averages
When applying the model to an entire video, the model’s output probabilities may be ambiguous between
two or more class predictions over a short period. During this period, the prediction with the highest
probability might fluctuate, creating prediction jitter across consecutive frames. This is demonstrated in
the first 100 frames of Figure 3.12.
To combat these issues, a Simple Moving Average (SMA) is applied. The idea behind the SMA is to
average the predicted values over a specified number of frames, which helps reduce fluctuations and
provides a more stable prediction.
An SMA works by taking the average of the values in a sliding window of fixed size over the time-series
data. At each frame, the average of the predictions within the window is computed, and this average is
used as the output for that frame. While effective, this method introduces a delay equal to the size of
the window. By applying the SMA, small jitters in the prediction output are significantly mitigated.
To balance smoothness and responsiveness, a window size of 40 frames was chosen—approximately one-
third of a second at 120 fps. This provides smooth predictions with negligible latency.

Chapter 5
Discussion and Future Work
This chapter presents a critical analysis of the results obtained from the stroke detection and classification
framework developed in this thesis. The performance of the system—built upon human-inspired percep-
tible features such as player keypoints and object locations supported CLIP-derived embeddings—is eval-
uated in light of the research question. The discussion first considers the strengths and limitations of the
current approach, including observed challenges in data quality and model generalization. These insights
provide a foundation for identifying areas where the methodology can be improved or extended. Based
on these findings, several opportunities for future work are proposed, focusing on enhancing accuracy,
efficiency, and applicability to real-world settings.
5.1
Findings
The primary objective of this project was to investigate whether a model incorporating human-inspired
perceptible features—such as player posture and object positions, supported by CLIP embeddings—could
classify table tennis strokes without relying solely on abstract video representations. The experiments
on the stroke classification system, incorporating human-inspired perceptible features—such as 2D body
keypoints, object positions, and CLIP embeddings—yielded promising classification results. Additionally,
it demonstrated effective stroke and phase segmentation when applied to an entire video. Cross-validation
and UMAP visualizations showed that certain stroke types formed reasonably distinct clusters, such as
the two serve classes, while some of the more diverse classes, such as phase classes, were present in many
of the clusters and spread more out. This suggests that perceptual cues can encode meaningful patterns
relevant to stroke differentiation. However, the system demonstrated limitations in achieving robust gen-
eralization across all stroke types. The performance varied notably depending on the specific stroke class,
with overlap and confusion between strokes that exhibit subtle pose or spatial differences as seen from the
camera’s perspective, e.g., when the right player performs a forehand with the arm occluded. The abla-
tion study demonstrated that keypoints normalized to each player’s midpoint consistently outperformed
raw keypoints. Introducing a temporal window around the stroke frame also produced a substantial
performance gain relative to using the single stroke frame alone.
62
==PAGE:69==
Contrary to the initial expectation that explicit global context would be helpful, adding the player’s mid-
point normalized to the table midpoint, together with the table midpoint itself, degraded performance
in Logistic Regression. However, the Random-Forest classifier achieved its best accuracy with this con-
figuration. Finally, as anticipated, injecting sparse and imprecise racket- and ball-centroid features led
to step-wise declines in performance; the more of this noisy information that was added, the greater the
drop in accuracy.
5.2
Approach
A significant portion of the project was devoted to feature engineering, particularly regarding skeletal
keypoints.
Early in the process, it became clear that keypoints offered numerous opportunities for
constructing meaningful features, whereas the scope for engineering features from CLIP embeddings
was more limited. Since CLIP already returns semantically structured vectors, the main possibilities for
additional processing would have involved modifications to the input crops rather than the embeddings
themselves. However, due to time constraints, this was not pursued.
An interesting direction not explored was the modification of color information in the input crops. Al-
though CLIP requires RGB input, muting certain channels could have reduced sensitivity to clothing or
background color, potentially enhancing robustness by making embeddings more invariant to color.
Additionally, the potential for fine-tuning CLIP was not utilized. Aligning CLIP embeddings more closely
with the domain-specific captions defined in Appendix B could have encouraged the model to draw subtle
distinctions between visually similar strokes by adjusting the embedding space.
While these opportunities were not explored, the models incorporating CLIP embeddings nonetheless
outperformed baseline classifiers. However, the exact reason for this improvement remains uncertain.
The generality of CLIP embeddings likely captured both relevant and irrelevant visual features, reducing
interpretability and possibly limiting generalization.
As a result, the majority of the project’s effort
focused on engineering and analyzing features derived from skeletal keypoints extracted using the Mask
R-CNN model.
Although the model showed significant improvement using keypoints, midpoints, and table midpoints,
achieving more than double the performance of the baseline classifier, there are some strict limitations
to the model. Since the keypoints are projected to a 2D screen, the full picture of the 3D player pose is
lost. To humans, a 2D projection to a screen such as a TV won’t dramatically disrupt our perception of
the 3D world in which the game is taking place.
While the model can learn meaningful 2D spatiotemporal features for one camera configuration, it most
likely won’t generalize to other camera positions. In table tennis, the camera is often positioned behind
one of the players, and elevated so it looks down—a setup for which the models trained in this project
are unlikely to generalize well.
With that being said, the result indicates that if the model were to get 3D skeletal keypoint information,
it would be able to learn meaningful stroke and phase representations as well, given that the models
trained on the players’ pose information alone were able to classify strokes and phases. Such 3D skeletal
keypoints were not utilized since getting 3D keypoints from a 2D video can be highly inaccurate.
63
==PAGE:70==
Therefore, while the 2D pose-based approach shows promise, its limitations underline the potential value
of integrating reliable 3D pose estimation in future work.
Even though the majority of the focus of the feature engineering was on keypoints, a crucial aspect for
making a more general model was skipped.
Namely, using the angle of the joints.
Angles are scale
invariant and would work great for training a more generalizable model. However, since the initial results
using midpoint normalization and temporal features already met the performance needs of the project,
efforts shifted toward evaluating and interpreting the existing feature set rather than introducing new
representations.
5.3
Temporal and Label Design Strategies
When applying the model to a video, the temporal frame window introduces two subtle yet important
effects.
Firstly, depending on how missing data is handled, the initial frames—equal in length to the temporal
frame window—may result in compromised predictions. With the strategy used in this project, where
missing data is replaced with zeroes, any stroke that happens within these first frames should still be
detectable, though with reduced contextual information. This, however, is unlikely to pose a major issue
if the temporal window is not too long. This project mainly experimented with a temporal window of
about 1.5 seconds, which means that only the very beginning and end of a video may yield uncertain
predictions—a period during which little relevant activity typically occurs.
Secondly, the frame temporal window centers around the frame of interest, requiring a delay for half the
window size, if the model were to be used in real time. This limitation introduces a trade-off between
temporal context and prediction latency, which must be considered in any real-time deployment.
Further development of techniques to improve both keypoint-derived and CLIP-based features was beyond
the scope of this project, but remains an open and valuable direction for continued research.
5.3.1
Phase Classes for Stroke Segmentation
Adding phase classes was motivated by the need to improve stroke segmentation. Although phase classes
are not inherently required for stroke classification, they significantly helped the model distinguish be-
tween strokes and non-stroke frames. Without phase classes, the model exhibited overconfidence during
certain periods, leading to false positives and poor segmentation boundaries. By incorporating phase
classes during training, the model gained the ability to better identify the onset and offset of strokes,
resulting in improved segmentation performance.
5.4
Data Homogeneity
The strong performance observed in this project is likely partially attributed to the lack of diversity
in both the training and test data. All of the videos were acquired from the OpenTTGames dataset,
64
==PAGE:71==
featuring nearly identical camera setups and an identical background across the videos.
While no test video was explicitly included in the training set, the homogeneity of the visual environment
results in highly similar data distributions. This limits the generalizability of the model and reduces the
challenge of the classification task.
Additionally, the dataset lacked important variation; most notably, there were no left-handed players
represented. Although a mirroring of keypoints and crops was implemented, no model was trained with
the mirrored data. Furthermore, this would still not account for videos where only one of the players is
left-handed. This means that the model will most likely have worse performance on left-handed players.
Mirroring was intended to simulate data from left-handed players, thereby addressing potential biases
in the dataset toward right-handed play. This could help the model learn stroke patterns independent
of handedness, improving its robustness when applied to real-world scenarios. Although mirroring of
both crops and keypoints was achieved, no model was trained with this augmented data. Since none of
the annotated videos featured left-handed players, the increased value of training on the mirrored data
could not be evaluated. However, training a model on the mirrored data yields a near-identical proxy for
left-handed play. This may involve a trade-off, potentially lowering test accuracy on the original dataset
while improving robustness on more diverse or unseen players. As the effect could not be measured within
the scope of this dataset, no experiment with mirrored data was conducted, though the capability has
been developed in the pre-processing pipeline and is available for future work.
5.5
Limited Hyperparameter Tuning
The project had minimal hyperparameter tuning, apart from experiments with regularization for Logistic
Regression and max depth for Random Forest; no further hyperparameter tuning was conducted. Since
the primary objective of the project was to explore the usefulness of Mask R-CNN and CLIP outputs,
simple models were sufficient to provide a meaningful proof of concept and for comparisons between the
features. Hence, no further attempts at optimizing a model were conducted.
More extensive hyperparameter tuning, or the use of more sophisticated models, could potentially lead
to improved performance, particularly if the focus shifts from feature exploration to model optimization.
5.6
Future Work
Building on the findings, this section outlines directions for future research that could further advance
stroke detection and classification in table tennis. The proposals aim to explore opportunities to enhance
the system’s accuracy, efficiency, and research if other relevant types of features are useful.
5.6.1
Audio Cues
Table tennis games have a few characteristic sounds, which are also something humans can use to segment
strokes or something that, to a certain extent, experts could use to classify strokes. One example is the
65
==PAGE:72==
very distinct sound that is made by racket-ball impact, or the squeaky sound of the players’ shoes on the
ground, and yells from players and crowds when a point is scored.
OpenAI has developed a model, CLAP[27], that is similar to CLIP connects images to another modality,
but instead of images uses audio. It could be interesting to further incorporate human-inspired perciptible
features into a machine learning model.
5.6.2
TTNet for Ball Detections
Since the ball is a crucial object for the task, a more reliable ball detector could be used as an extra
auxiliary model. Specifically, the TTNet developed a promising ball detector as part of their multi-modal
model. However, since TTNet was trained on the same set of videos, its performance on this data may
be overly optimistic. If their model demonstrates strong generalization to other video datasets, it could
serve as a natural choice for an auxiliary model dedicated to ball detection.
5.6.3
Code
There is a lot of room for improvement in the code, with regards to readability and efficiency, and ease
of use.
To use the model on a new video, several scripts have to be executed as part of the pre- and post-
processing pipeline, with the final inference at the end. Future work includes creating a single executable
that will walk through every processing step, which creates a video with the results as the end, as shown
in Figure 4.6.
With regards to efficiency, the current pipeline utilizes CSV files and inefficient function calls to re-
trieve information from these CSV files, such as ast.litteral. Future work should involve moving to a
more optimized library, such as Parquet. This can reduce the file reading and writing processing time
drastically.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This thesis proposed a model for classifying strokes in table tennis by leveraging human-inspired percep-
tual features such as 2D body keypoints, object positions, and CLIP-derived embeddings.
While state-of-the-art models based on abstract video representations often outperform in raw accuracy,
the findings of this work demonstrate that perceptual cues closely aligned with human intuition can still
yield meaningful and competitive results.
Through a systematic approach that includes pose normalization, temporal windowing, and feature abla-
tion, the study highlighted the value of structured feature engineering. Notably, keypoints normalized to
a player’s own midpoint combined with temporal context proved most effective for stroke classification.
These features enabled better generalization across the six stroke types used in this project. Conversely,
introducing sparsely detected features like racket and ball midpoints tended to degrade performance.
However, this does not mean these features are inherently unhelpful. Rather, their limited contribution
is likely due to Mask R-CNN’s inability to detect these objects with sufficient accuracy in the context of
a fast-paced table tennis game.
The results show that human-inspired perceptual features can provide an interpretable and extensible
foundation for video-based action recognition in table tennis. Moreover, this approach can be used in
practical applications, such as post-match analysis and enhanced viewing experiences, streamlining game
analysis, and improving viewing experiences.
The study also compared two classification methods: Logistic Regression and Random Forest. Logistic
Regression with normalized keypoints over time yielded the best Macro F1 score. Random Forest was
more robust to noisy inputs and achieved competitive accuracy across most feature sets.
