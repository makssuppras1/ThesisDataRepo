Abstract
As the galloping chase towards capturing renewable energy has moved wind turbines
from the stable onshore ground to the harsh conditions oshore, engineers are faced
with the challenge of developing suitable design methods for these changes. The cur-
rent study aims to improve the determination of the soil resistance to driving (SRD) for
assessing the driveability of large diameter monopiles used for oshore wind turbines.
Data from fourteen wind turbines were examined and used as basis for calibrating im-
proved solutions for assessing the SRD. These had all been installed in the southern
North Sea, eight of which were driven in to coarse grained ground and six were driven
in to ne grained soils. Thus, the problem of determining the SRD was treated sepa-
rately for these two ground conditions. Interpretation of measurements from piezocone
(CPTu) tests were utilized for assessing the soil classication and geotechnical charac-
terization, together with laboratory measurements conducted on specimens retrieved
from boreholes at the sites. The improved solutions were developed by performing a
series of driveability back analyses, from which the blowcount output was compared
to the blowcount recorded during the installation of the corresponding monopile. The
study showed that for the coarse grained soils, the SRD should be treated in analogy
with a shallow footing problem at shallow depths and was directly related to the cone
tip resistance recorded by a CPTu at deeper levels. For the ne grained soils, the SRD
was found to depend on the undrained shear strength determined on the basis of CPTu
tests.

Introduction
A statistical overview presented by GWEC (2017) shows that the number of wind farms
installed oshore has increased dramatically over the past ten years. Moreover, it re-
veals that the vast majority of these wind farms are installed in the North Sea. The
global trends show that Asian and North American countries are making large invest-
ments in onshore wind farms. Thus, the knowledge obtained from the oshore wind
farms in the North Sea may become of great value once these markets start moving
oshore.
This thesis deals with the driveability predictions of monopiles, which are the most
commonly used substructures for oshore wind turbines.
More specically, it deals
with the assessment of the soil resistance to driving (SRD) when performing drive-
ability analysis. Currently the predictions are based on data calibrated against eld
measurements obtained in the oil and gas industry (Toolan and Fox (1977), Stevens
et al. (1982), Alm and Hamre (2001)). While these piles do have their similarities, there
is a dierence in scale. The monopiles used for oshore wind turbines commonly have a
diameter of three to four times that of a monopile used in the oil and gas industry. This
scale eect has shown to inuence the applicability of the currently available empirical
analysis methods for predicting driveability (Holm et al. (2017), Anusic et al. (2016)).
The aim of the report is to evaluate these analysis methods by back analysis and on the
basis of that develop improved solutions for driveability predictions of large diameter
monopiles.
Data from two wind farm sites located in the southern part of the North Sea, outside
of the Dutch and British coastlines, was used for examining the problem of performing
driveability analysis herein. These sites were carefully selected because of their respec-
1
==PAGE:42==
tive geological conditions. At one site the monopiles were driven in to predominantly
coarse grained soils. The stratigraphy at the other site was dominated by London Clay
formations. Hence, the problem in concern was examined for coarse grained and ne
grained conditions separately.
To establish an understanding of the geotechnical features of the two sites, all geotech-
nical investigations were examined and reinterpreted herein.
Soil classication and
geotechnical characterization was performed on the basis of piezocone (CPTu) tests.
These were compared with laboratory measurements conducted on specimens retrieved
from boreholes, to determine which CPTu interpretation strategies were best suited for
these sites.
GRLWEAP was used for drivebility back analysis. The program utilizes the one dimen-
sional wave equation solution presented by Smith (1960) to predict the interaction of
the elements included in a pile driving system, i.e. the driving force of the hammer, the
pile and the resisting soil. Technical drawings of the installed monopiles and recorded
hammer energies during driving were used as input to replicate the actual conditions
at the time of installation. Hereafter, the only variable changing was the input of the
SRD.
The SRD determined from the existing empirical solutions were initially used for per-
forming driveability analysis. Blowcount outputs from GRLWEAP were then compared
with the blowcounts from the driving records of the corresponding monopile installa-
tions. This provided an overview of the performance of each method and potentially
served as an inspiration for developing an improved solution. Hereafter a series of at-
tempts were made to match the recorded blowcount by changing the SRD of one pile at
each location. Once a satisfying result was obtained, the improved calculation of SRD
was applied to a number of piles at each site to determine the overall performance.

Background and motivation
According to GWEC (2017) the wind industry is one of the fastest growing industries
in the world at the moment. China is leading the market with a 35% share of the total
global capacity. With a capacity of 188232 MW at the end of 2017 the country is close
to reaching the 210000 MW target for 2020 (IEA (2018)). Asian and North American
countries account for more than half of the total 539123 MW installed by the end of
2017. On the contrary nearly 84% of the total 18814 MW from oshore wind farms
were installed along European coastlines (GWEC (2017)). These numbers present a
unique opportunity for European industry expertise to approach the global market of
oshore wind energy.
Installing wind farms oshore brings advantages such as more stable and greater wind
loads and it minimizes the noise and sight disturbance. However, placing such a slender
structures in water imposes challenges to the design, installation and operation of the
wind turbines.
EEA (2009) compared the total cost per kW for turbines installed
at dierent water depths. Figure 2.1 shows how the cost is divided between the ve
categories displayed to the right of the histogram. Intuitively, the cost increases with
depth, but one of the interesting aspects that this presentation of the numbers reveals, is
the relative increase in foundation cost. A statistical overview presented by WindEurope
(2018) shows that while several foundation solutions have been developed, monopiles
still remained the most common type of substructure installed in Europe in 2017 with
87%.
5
==PAGE:46==
Figure 2.1: Cost breakdown of oshore wind turbine installations as function of water
depth. Source: EEA (2009)
Figure 2.2 shows a sketch of an oshore wind turbine founded on a monopile. Approach-
ing greater water depths means lengthening the distance from where the lateral load is
concentrated to the supporting medium. Resulting in an increasing in dimensions of the
monopiles causing the transition piece to become more cone shaped in order to connect
the turbine with the monopile. Thus, while the main element is unchanged in design
and cost, the substructure is subjected to changes which require rethinking of design
and construction in order to bring down the costs. One of the challenges, is predicting
the driveability of large diameter monopiles, which is the focus of this report.
The sizing of a monopile is decided by the axial and lateral loads imposed on the
structure in the operational stage, the latter usually being the determining one. A
driveability analysis is required in order to determine whether the pile can be driven
in to the ground and to select an adequate hammer. The driving phase is a temporary
condition which is why selecting an over-competent hammer should be avoided, since
it may impose damage to the pile and therefore shorten the operational lifetime.

==PAGE:47==
Figure 2.2: Sketch of an oshore wind turbine structure, including denitions.
Prediction of the driveability of monopiles is commonly performed in the software GRL-
WEAP. It is based on the mathematical solution developed by Smith (1960), where the
wave equation is used to describe the pile driving system. This method is described in
more detail in section 3.1.1. Pile driving is treated exclusively as a vertical or axial force
equilibrium problem and therefore no further comments will be made on the lateral or
7
==PAGE:48==
moment capacity. In the operational stage the axial capacity of the foundation depends
on the static interaction between the soil and the pile. The forces are transferred in the
interface along the shaft and tip of the pile, shown by the shaded areas of the sections
on the right hand side in Figure 2.2. The mechanism is the same during driving, except
that the resistance exhibit by the soil is increased. This phenomenon is known as the
dynamic resistance of the soil and the magnitude is related to the velocity of the pile.
As input to the software the ultimate soil resistance has to be determined for the soil
along the shaft and at the tip. This resistance is referred to as the soil resistance to
driving (SRD). Several empirical formulations have been developed to assess the SRD
of various soils. These are commonly inspired by classical equations for determining
the axial bearing capacity of driven piles and tted to the dynamic problem based on
back analysis of driving records. It was brought to the authors attention that these
empirical formulations of the SRD have not yet been veried to a recognized level for
monopiles with large diameters. Furthermore, that experience has shown that they do
not always suciently reect a realistic behaviour when compared to eld data.
This thesis set out to improve the design methods regarding driveability predictions
of large diameter monopiles. In order to examine the problem the existing methods
of assessing the SRD are evaluated herein and compared to driving records from two
wind farm sites. Hereafter some hypothesis are tested in the aim of nding an improved
method for assessing the SRD. In the following two section the most commonly used
empirical formulations are stated and the sites selected for the study are presented in
terms of geotechnical characteristics.

3.1.1
Pile driving analysis by the wave equation
Smith (1960) developed a numerical solution for the problem of driving a pile in to the
ground, utilizing the one dimensional wave equation to describe the dynamic process
of pile driving. The system is discretized in to a string of weights and springs as shown
in Figure 3.1. The ram (W1) generates a longitudinal wave trough the system, which
provokes the remaining elements to react. The pile block is a relatively elastic element
and thus represented by a spring, where as the pile cap represented by a weight (W2) is
a heavy rigid element protecting the pile. All pile elements are represented by a weight
(W3 to W12) and a spring (K2 to K11), accounting for the gravitational force and the
elasticity of the pile respectively. The main counteracting force is encountered in the
soil elements represented by resisting forces (R3 to R12).
The stroke height and the weight of the ram determines the velocity at impact and the
energy transferred to the capblock causing it to compress. This compression multiplied
with the stiness of the spring becomes the force applied to the pile cap and the ve-
locity and displacement can be found. By repeating this procedure for the adjacent
pile element, the rst resisting force is encountered. This is accounted for when the net
force is determined and causes the downward velocity to gradually decrease as the wave
propagates through the system.
Smith presented the solution in terms of the wave equation including resistance in a
time and displacement dependent dierential equation. Programming of the solution
in a numerical computation however, is restricted by system boundaries and physical
restrains which need to be taken in to consideration, which he also gave his recommen-
dations on.

Conclusion and further research
In this thesis the challenge of performing driveability predictions for large diameter
monopiles used for oshore wind turbines was examined. More specically, the issue of
assessing the soil resistance to driving (SRD) was examined with the aim of improving
the empirical solutions currently available.
Data from two wind farm sites installed in the southern North Sea was used to perform
driveability back analysis. The main criteria for selecting the sites to be included herein
was that there should be one where the geological conditions predominantly consisted
of coarse grained soils and the other of ne grained soils. This was done to address
the issues of determining the SRD for the two goematerials separately. Data from six
and eight wind turbine locations were used for analyses at the sites with coarse grained
and ne grained ground conditions respectively. Soil classication and geothechnical
characterization by piezocone (CPTu) showed that the stratigraphy at both sites was
inconsistent below 15 m to 20 m below seabed. Thus, the driveability back analyses
were only performed down to 20 m below seabed.
Driveability back analyses were rstly conducted using the existing empirical methods
for determining the SRD. Comparing these with the driving records of the correspond-
ing pile installations, it was concluded that non of the methods resulted in a satisfying
prediction of the driveability. Hereafter, new solutions were established for determining
SRD based on the evaluation of the performance of the existing methods.
For the coarse grained soils, two solutions were established. Since a large proportion of
the soil resistance is encountered at the tip when a pile is supported by coarse grained
soils, it became the key factor for calibrating an improved solution. The major break-
through was to establish a limiting value to the unit tip resistance at shallow depths,
85
==PAGE:126==
which was done by treating the pile as a shallow footing until it had penetrated a dis-
tance equal to one diameter below seabed. Below this level the tip resistance was found
to be an average value of the cone tip resistance measured by a CPTu test. While
the two solutions gave satisfying results of driveability predictions for a majority of
the analysed locations, there were predictions which indicated that the shallow footing
limitation could preferably be extended. For further research the limitation of the unit
tip resistance at shallow depths could be examined for a larger data set and even for a
range of pile diameters to get an indication of whether there indeed is a link. Further-
more, the problem of determining the range over which the cone tip resistance should
be averaged for determining the unit tip resistance at greater depths was seen to be
dicult to assess and thus this is also a topic for future research.
For the ne grained soils one calibrated solution for determining the SRD resulted in
driveability predictions very close to the recorded blowcounts during pile installations.
This solution was purely related to the undrained shear strength determined based
on CPTu measurements. In this case the cone factors for determining the undrained
shear strength were calibrated against laboratory measurements performed on speci-
mens retrieved from boreholes located relatively close to the CPTu prole in concern.
Moreover, the cone factors showed to be of magnitudes not commonly recommended
for sti ne grained soils. Hence, this method for determining the SRD may be very
sensitive to the engineering judgement of the undrained shear strength as well as the
data available for evaluating this strength parameter.
Overall the use of quantities directly measured by or interpreted from CPTu tests pro-
vided very good and nuanced pictures of the soil response to driving. The main question
for future research left from this study is how the improved solutions perform at dier-
ent slenderness ratios, i.e. when the pile is penetrated deeper in to the soil or for piles
with dierent dimensions. Furthermore, it is of interest to examine the performance of
the solutions in mixed ground conditions.
