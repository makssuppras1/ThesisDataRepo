Abstract
Deep learning has significantly transformed modern agriculture, especially through its use in
object detection. This technology has been invaluable in solving various agricultural challenges,
especially weed detection, which has attracted significant attention and resources.
However, the effectiveness of deep learning models relies heavily on the availability of abun-
dant annotated data and the assumption that these data samples are drawn from identical distri-
butions. Acquiring and annotating such data is a time-consuming, expensive, and labor-intensive
process. Moreover, variations in lighting conditions and background complexity across different
fields can lead to domain shifts between data distributions, posing a challenge for model perfor-
mance. Domain adaptation techniques could address the impact of domain shifts by transferring
knowledge from an annotated source domain to an unannotated target domain. This reduces the
need for perfectly aligned data and improves model performance in various agricultural settings.
This thesis introduces a novel domain-adaptive multitask object detection framework designed
to address the aforementioned challenges, with a specific focus on identifying weeds, their stems,
and primary veins in grasslands. Building on the CenterNet architecture, the proposed framework
integrates an attention-based, multi-level adversarial learning approach.
To ensure that feature distributions are consistent across domains, the Domain-Enhanced Cen-
terNet (DECNet) model employs adversarial learning modules (discriminators) at both low and
high feature levels equipped with a Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL). These discriminators determine
whether input data belongs to the source or target domains, while CenterNet generates detection
predictions. Prior to passing input feature maps to the discriminators, a spatial attention module
is utilized to focus only on relevant areas, improving domain classification performance. Through
backpropagation with GRL, gradients are reversed, causing the feature extractor to learn domain-
invariant features. Experimental results on agricultural datasets illustrate significant improvements in
performance on the unannotated target domain compared to the baseline model, with a 7.50%
increase in weed detection and a 5.10% increase in keypoint detection.
Index Terms— Unsupervised Domain Adaptation, CenterNet, Weed Detection, Stem/Vein Detec-
tion, Precision Agriculture

DECNet employs two domain discriminators—SAD and PLD—and a GRL
placed between the discriminators and the feature extractor. It also uses a
spatial attention module. The main goal of DECNet is to align the feature

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Precision Agriculture (PA), also known as Precision Farming (PF), involves utilizing technology to
improve farming management strategies, taking into account variations in soil, crops, and different
environmental factors. Through the use of tools like robots, data analysis, and Artificial Intelligence
(AI), PA helps farmers to make informed choices regarding irrigation, fertilization, and pest control,
among others.
PA has become a promising method for improving sustainability in agricultural production, with
a focus on employing autonomous robots for activities like weed management. Robotic technol-
ogy in agriculture has the potential to improve the effectiveness of current farming methods. Agri-
cultural robots typically utilize Computer Vision (CV) based on Deep Learning (DL) for identifying
plants. Nevertheless, the success of these systems relies heavily on the existence of extensive anno-
tated datasets, as highlighted in the work by Güldenring et al. (2021) [43]. The limited availability
of such datasets, especially for grasslands, presents a notable obstacle in this context [83].
In the domain of PA, weed management is crucial due to its significant environmental, eco-
nomic, and social consequences. Weeds compete with crops for vital resources (e.g., water and
nutrients), reducing yields and jeopardizing food security, which results in substantial economic
losses globally, exceeding US $100 billion annually [37]. Additionally, uncontrolled weed growth

Weeds also host pests and toxins harmful to both livestock and humans, highlighting the impor-

balance [5]. Weed elimination can be accomplished through various conventional methods (e.g.,
hand weeding and mulching) or by employing robots equipped with specialized equipment for
accurate weed removal. However, prior to weed removal, it is essential to detect them. Weed
detection presents unique challenges due to factors such as weather effects, different lighting
conditions, and the similarity in appearance between certain weed species and crops. Further-
more, Kriticos et al. (2006) [62] suggest that the types of weeds may differ depending on factors
such as geographic region, crop diversity, growth stage, and soil characteristics.
Object detection methods have emerged as potential solutions to these problems, but their
effectiveness tends to decrease significantly when tested on datasets with different data distribu-
tions. This challenge is also relevant in the context of weed detection, where models trained on
specific data types from one field may exhibit sub-optimal performance when deployed in a differ-
ent field. Nonetheless, gathering accurately labeled data is both labor-intensive and costly, often
requiring the involvement of domain experts. Additionally, it is unrealistic to anticipate having a
suﬀicient number of labeled images for every emerging case requiring analysis.

1.2
Problem Statement
The objective of this thesis is to tackle the problem of domain shift that occurs when an object
detection model, specifically a multi-task model using OBBs and keypoint detection, is trained
on one type of data and then deployed on a different but related type of data. This is relevant
when considering the detection of weeds in grasslands, Rumex obtusifolius L. in this case, where
the models trained on images from certain fields may not perform well when deployed in new
fields. The central problem, therefore, is to explore and evaluate DA techniques that can handle
this domain shift, thereby maintaining high performance when the models are deployed in new
fields without annotated data.

to investigate this issue. The dataset consists of both iNaturalist and RoboRumex domains. The iNat-

in terms of location, background, and growing conditions. Conversely, the RoboRumex domain,

2.2.6
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), or ConvNets, are DL algorithms for image-related tasks,
outperforming FCNNs due to computational eﬀiciency and parameter sharing. Each neuron in a
CNN layer connects to a specific number of neurons in the input layer. Typical architectures include
multiple stages with convolutional layers followed by pooling layers, ending with Fully Connected
(FC) layers. Trainable parameters are present in FC and convolutional layers, while non-linearity
and pooling layers lack them. The CNN learns fundamental features like lines and edges in initial
layers, parts of objects in middle layers, and entire objects in later layers. The key elements of CNNs
are examined below:
Convolutional layer: Convolutional layers apply filters to input images to extract features. Each
filter (F × F × C) scans the input image (Iwidth × Iheight × C) to produce a feature map or acti-
vation map (O × O × K). Key hyperparameters include filter size (F), stride (S), padding (P), and
number of filters (K). They achieve translational invariance by sliding filters across inputs, sharing
parameters spatially. Activation functions (e.g., ReLU) introduce non-linearity after convolution.
Though technically cross-correlation, it is commonly referred to as convolution in NNs. Stride con-
trols how much the filter moves horizontally and vertically during convolution, with larger strides
producing smaller feature maps. Padding adds border pixels to maintain spatial dimensions and
reduce edge information loss. Valid padding shrinks output size, while same padding retains input
size. Finally, zero-padding adds zeroes to input boundaries.
Pooling layers: Pooling layers down-sample feature maps to control computation cost and
overfitting, introduce translational invariance, and improve robustness to noise. Types include max
pooling (selecting maximum values from patches) and average pooling (selecting average val-
ues).
Fully connected layer: The FC layer, located at the end of an NN, operates on flattened input
data (from multi-dimensional feature maps to a one-dimensional array), connecting each neuron
to every neuron in the previous layer.

2.3.1
Oriented Bounding Boxes (OBB)
Oriented Bounding Boxes (OBB) outperform HBBs by adjusting to objects with arbitrary orien-
tations, such as densely packed objects like weed leaves. OBBs are critical in object detection,
particularly for objects that can be freely rotated or vary in size. OBBs can be rotated to any angle,
preserving orientation information [126]. Oriented detection methods typically begin with generic
object detectors and incorporate modules to predict OBBs from HBBs. An illustration of an OBB is
provided in Figure 2.2.
Two primary approaches for representing OBBs are the θ-based 5-parameter method and the
point-based 8-parameter method. In the 5-parameter method, an angle parameter θ is added
to the HBB representation (bx, by, bh, bw, θ), where θ is the acute angle formed by the long side
of the rectangle and the x-axis. In contrast, the 8-parameter method uses the coordinates of the

2.3.2
Challenges in Object Detection
Object detection faces several challenges, including occlusion, truncation, object scale vari-
ability, and illumination changes. Song et al. (2023) [104] address occlusion by proposing a com-
positional model inspired approach, leveraging front and side cues to reduce its impact. Vedaldi
et al. (2009) [122] tackle truncation by incorporating alignment as a latent variable. Chen et al.
(2021) [19] recommend multi-scale feature representation to handle object scale variability, while
Shakeri et al. (2019) [99] propose a method based on multilinear data decomposition to address
illumination changes (e.g., from different weather conditions).
Additionally, the presence of multiple overlapping BBs for the same object is a frequent prob-
lem, which is mitigated by the Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) algorithm. NMS selects the best
BB for each object based on probability scores and overlap thresholds (e.g., IoU ≥0.50). Anchor
boxes, predefined boxes of varying sizes and shapes, are used to address differences in object
sizes and shapes. While anchor boxes are typically fixed during training, Zhong et al. (2018) [147]
propose dynamically learning anchor box shapes during training to adapt to data distribution.

2.3.3
Categories of Object Detectors
Object detectors can be classified into two categories: one-stage and two-stage detectors.
Two-stage detectors (e.g., R-CNN family) follow a process where region proposals are generated
first using reference boxes, and then each proposal is classified and its BB is regressed. These models
achieve high performance but have slower inference speeds.
On the other hand, one-stage detectors (e.g., YOLO, SSD, and RetinaNet), simplify the de-
tection process by directly predicting class labels and BB coordinates in a single pass. This leads
to faster inference times, making them suitable for real-time tasks. However, they may sacrifice
a bit of performance compared to two-stage detectors. More details about CNN-based object
detectors will be provided in Chapter 3.
Another classification for object detectors is based on whether they use anchors, distinguishing
them as either anchor-based or anchor-free detectors. Anchor-based detectors use hyperparam-
eters (e.g., aspect ratio, size, and number) to generate various anchor candidates, which are then
utilized to predict and classify objects. Anchor-free detectors use keypoints instead of anchors to
generate candidates. This approach reduces the computational complexity of the network since
it does not rely on predefined anchor parameters.

Object Detection Metrics
Evaluating the effectiveness of an object detector is essential to understand its capabilities
and limitations. Before introducing the most used performance metrics for such evaluations, it is
necessary to first explore some fundamental metrics and definitions 1.
True Positive (TP): The model accurately predicts the positive class when it is positive. In object
detection, this means the model correctly detects an object in an image.
True Negative (TN): The model correctly predicts the negative class when it is negative. In
object detection, a TN could indicate that the model correctly identifies when there is no object
in a specific location in an image. However, it is not applied in object detection. This occurs
primarily due to the existence of multiple potential BBs that should not be identified.
False Positive (FP): The model inaccurately predicts the positive class when it should have been
classified as negative. In object detection, an FP occurs when the model incorrectly identifies and
locates an object in an image.
False Negative (FN): The model makes an incorrect prediction by labeling instances as nega-
tive when they should be classified as positive. In object detection, an FN indicates that the model
failed to detect an object that actually exists in an image.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) by Goodfellow et al. (2014) [38], revolutionized ML
by improving the generation of realistic images. They involve two NNs, the generator and the
discriminator, participating in an adversarial training process inspired by game theory. This unsu-
pervised learning approach allows GANs to learn the underlying data distribution and generate
new samples without the need for annotations.
The generator, G, takes random noise, z, sampled from prior distributions as input and aims to
produce samples that resemble those from the training set. It accomplishes this by converting the
input noise through a series of NN layers to generate realistic synthetic data.

While GANs are valuable for generating new images, especially in scenarios with limited train-

stability. Mode collapse happens when the generator focuses on generating a limited range of
similar samples and ignores the diversity of the data distribution. This issue emerges due to rea-
sons such as inadequate model capacity or an uneven competition between the generator and
discriminator.
On the other hand, as already explained, vanishing gradients occur when gradients become
insignificant for meaningful learning. This problem emerges when the discriminator becomes con-
fident, resulting in minimal gradients for the generator’s parameters. Finally, training stability is an-
other significant challenge, as the adversarial nature of the training process can lead to oscillations

2.5
Transfer Learning (TL)
Transfer Learning (TL) leverages knowledge acquired from a source task and domain to ad-
dress a related target task and domain [50] (Definition 2.2). Unlike traditional ML methods requiring
abundant labeled data, TL utilizes pre-trained models for different yet related tasks. Before for-
mally defining TL (Definition 2.2), it is necessary to provide definitions for certain concepts, namely
domain, task, and marginal probabilities, in order to describe TL (Definition 2.1) [85].
In TL, instead of training a network from scratch, a pre-trained network from a different domain
or task can be utilized as a feature extractor and adapted for the specific domain and task (head
replacement). Another approach involves fine-tuning a pre-trained model, where not only the final
layer is replaced for classification/regression, but some previous layers are also retrained. Certain
layers may be frozen to prevent overfitting when target task labels are limited, while the rest can
be fine-tuned when target task labels are abundant 2.
Definition 2.1: A domain D is defined by a feature space X and a marginal distribution
P(X), where X = {x1, ..., xn} ∈X . For a given domain D = {X, P(X)}, a task
T = {Y, P(Y |X)} = {Y.η} involves a label space Y and an objective predictive func-
tion η, which can be interpreted as a conditional probability distribution P(Y |X). We can
acquire P(Y |X) in a supervised manner using labeled data {xi, yi}, where xi ∈X and
yi ∈Y [85], [127].
Definition 2.2: Given a source domain DS and its corresponding task TS, along with a target
domain DT and its associated task TT , the goal of TL is to support the learning of the target
conditional probability distribution P(YT|XT) in DT , utilizing information from DS and TS
in scenarios where DS ̸= DT or TS ̸= TT . Only a small number of labeled target examples
are available [85], [127].
Pan et al. (2010) [85] proposed that differences across datasets may stem from domain diver-
gence (Ds ̸= Dt), which includes shifts in distributions or variations in the feature space, or from
task divergence (Ts ̸= Tt), which requires changes in conditional distributions or differences in
label spaces. When two domains differ, they may have distinct feature spaces (Xs ̸= Xt →het-
erogeneous TL) or marginal probability distributions (P(Xs) ̸= P(Xt) →DA). Similarly, in the case
of different tasks, they may have dissimilar label spaces (Ys ̸= Yt) or conditional probability distri-
butions (P(Ys|Xs) ̸= P(Yt|Xt)). Finally, when Ds = Dt and Ts = Tt, the process simplifies to a
conventional ML configuration, where Ds and Dt become the training and test sets, respectively.

2.6
Domain Adaptation (DA)
Domain Adaptation (DA) is a branch of ML focused on addressing the challenge of distribu-
tional differences (domain shift or domain drift) between the data used for training (source do-
main) and the data for testing (target domain) [85]. As already mentioned, DA addresses scenar-
ios where the task remains constant across different settings (same-single task), but the marginal
distributions vary (P(Xs) ̸= P(Xt)) (different, but related domains) [127], [85]. Additionally, DA
focuses on situations where annotated data is exclusively available for Ds. In these cases, the ob-
jective is to adjust the model’s learned representation to address the differences in input distribution
between the source and target domains, while keeping the essential task-related knowledge and
improving generalization in both domains. This essentially means that we can train a model on
one domain (Ds) and then transfer the knowledge to a distinct yet related domain (Dt) without
needing to re-train the model on the new domain (Dt). Definition 2.3 provides the formal definition

16

of DA [4].
In many real-world scenarios, it is common for the training and testing data to come from dif-
ferent distributions, leading to decreased model performance when applied to the target domain
[66], [123]. Tanveer et al. (2023) [111] state that domain shift in robotic vision can arise from differ-
ences in data distribution, lighting conditions, environmental settings, and sensor characteristics
between the source and target domains.
Definition 2.3: Given a source domain Ds with its corresponding learning task Ts, and a target
domain Dt with its corresponding learning task Tt, DA seeks to improve the learning of the
target predictive function fT(·) in Dt by leveraging the knowledge obtained from Ds and
Ts, where Ts = Tt and Ds ̸= Dt [4].
In DA, the approach is considered supervised when all samples in Dt have labels [80], semi-
supervised if only some samples have labels in Dt (balance between the labeled and unlabeled
data) [94], and unsupervised if none of the samples in Dt are labeled [30]. Additionally, weakly
supervised DA addresses the challenge of working with sparse and noisy labels in Dt [24], multi-
source DA involves adapting from multiple Ds to a single Dt, and few-shot DA addresses scenarios
where there are very few labeled examples in Dt. However, the UDA approach stands out as the
most promising among the mentioned categories, with few-shot DA gaining attention in recent
years as well.
Another classification for DA depends on the use of NNs. Shallow DA techniques rely mainly on
mathematical transformations (task-specific alignment methods), while deep DA techniques use
deep NNs architectures.
Furthermore, as mentioned earlier in Section 2.5, DA can be categorized as homogeneous or
heterogeneous, with most models nowadays falling under the homogeneous category [127]. In
the former case, P(Xs) ̸= P(Xt), but the two domains have the same feature spaces Xs = Xt
and dimensions (ds = dt). In this context, domain shift refers to the difference between the source
and target marginal distributions. On the other hand, in heterogeneous DA, Xs ̸= Xt, P(Xs) ̸=
P(Xt) and ds ̸= dt. An example of heterogeneous DA involves using RGB images as the source
domain along with depth images for the target domain, or vice versa.
Finally, DA can be achieved in one step for related domains or through multi-step methods for
domains with less overlap, using intermediate bridges [81]. However, recent research has mainly
focused on unsupervised homogeneous DA. Figure 2.3 illustrates the classification of DA in the
term of TL. Chapter 3 will provide more detailed information on relevant DA methods.

Subeesh et al. (2022) [105] employed the InceptionV3 model in their research to detect weeds

prove the precision of weed spraying. Their model showcased outstanding performance, achiev-
ing an accuracy of 97.70%. In a more advanced approach, Hu et al. (2020) [53] employed
a graph-based model using standard RGB images for weed detection, particularly in rangeland
environments. They introduced multi-scale graphs to capture complex weed features for classifi-
cation. A notable aspect of their method is its capability for semi-supervised learning, achieved

3.3
Domain Adaptation Categories
DL has improved object detection, but it typically assumes matching data distributions and
access to abundant labeled training data, which unfortunately is not always realistic. To tackle
this, deep DA methods have emerged as a possible solution [68].
DA methods can be classified into many categories, including adversarial feature learning,
generative-based approaches, pseudo-label based self-training, graph reasoning, domain ran-
domization, and mean-teacher training [83]. Additionally, a newly introduced category relies on
object detection for DA using transformer-based models, outperforming all other methods in per-
formance. Another category that will not be individually addressed is the hybrid category, which
incorporates more than two elements from the previous categories. The following sections provide
additional details on the aforementioned DA types.
3.3.1
Discrepancy-Based Methods
In this thesis, traditional shallow DA methods will not be discussed, as it has been observed
that the performance improvements in tackling unstructured data with DA techniques have been
achieved mostly by DL-based approaches [66].
However, various shallow discrepancy-based
DA metrics like Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), Correlation Alignment (CORAL) [107], and
Wasserstein distance have also found extensive use in other deep DA methods explored later on.
Discrepancy-based techniques quantify distribution differences using statistical methods, consid-
ering source and target domain features as probability distributions.
Wang et al. (2018) [127] propose a method for deep DA that involves fine-tuning a base net-
work with source data and then transferring its first layers to a target network. The remaining layers
of the target network are randomly initialized and trained with a discrepancy-based loss. Depend-
ing on factors like dataset size and similarity, the first layers of the target network can be fine-tuned
or frozen during training.
Deep CORAL [108] extends the shallow CORAL method to deep NNs. It aligns the feature co-
variance matrices of the source and target domains by minimizing their Frobenius norm difference.
This alignment is integrated into the network as a differentiable loss function (end-to-end training).
Deep CORAL adapts the model to the target domain, by fine-tuning the pre-trained network with
labeled source data and unlabeled target data.
One approach using MMD, introduced by Tzeng et al. (2014) [117], addresses DA tasks by
learning representations that balance discriminative classification and domain invariance. By op-
timizing classification and domain confusion objectives, this network aligns distributions between
the source and target domains. This is achieved through a loss function that combines classifica-
tion loss with the MMD metric, encouraging domain-invariant feature learning. The network utilizes
a CNN architecture with an adaptation layer to achieve DA, making it suitable for both supervised
DA and UDA scenarios.
Long et al. [76] proposed a hybrid method, which combines shallow DA techniques with deep
NNs, improving upon Tzeng et al.’s (2014) [117] previous work. Unlike the earlier method, which
only relies on a single kernel for MMD computation, this approach employs multiple kernels. This al-
lows the method to capture both basic and complex distribution characteristics more effectively,

thereby reducing domain shift while preserving task-specific features. Additionally, this approach
integrates mean embedding matching, which measures the difference between domain distribu-
tions in a kernel space.
3.3.2
Adversarial Feature Learning
The most used methods for UDA primarily revolve around adversarial training. This approach,
while effective in mitigating domain shift, requires modifications to the network’s architecture. Ad-
versarial DA techniques, inspired by how GANs minimize differences between real and synthetic
data, utilize domain discriminators and gradient reversal layers. These components align the fea-
ture distributions of different domains in an adversarial manner, aiming to learn a shared latent
feature representation between the source and target domains.
The main goal is to confuse the domain discriminator (encourage domain confusion) by train-
ing the detector model to generate features that resemble both domains (similar statistical distri-
butions), while the domain discriminator tries to classify these features. Adversarial-based methods
fall into two sub-categories: generative and non-generative [127]. Generative models, learn un-
supervised transformations, while non-generative methods use domain confusion loss to improve
performance.
One notable example in this category is Domain-Adversarial Neural Network (DANN), which
will be investigated further in Section 4.6. Shen et al. (2017) [100] combine discrepancy-based and
adversarial feature learning methods. They improve DANN by including the Wasserstein distance
to achieve domain-invariant feature representations. The Wasserstein metric quantifies the dissim-
ilarity between probability distributions on a specific space by considering the transport cost from
one distribution to another. In their approach they use a feature extractor, domain critic, and dis-
criminator, where the feature extractor minimizes domain differences, the domain critic estimates
Wasserstein distance, and the discriminator predicts class labels.
The method proposed by Tzeng et al. (2017) [116] involves a two-step procedure. Initially, a
source encoder and classifier are trained on labeled data from the source domain to acquire dis-
criminative features. Subsequently, during the adversarial adaptation phase, the target encoder is
initialized with the source encoder’s weights and is trained to produce domain-agnostic represen-
tations. This is achieved by training a domain discriminator to differentiate between the domains
based on the representations, while the target encoder aims to generate representations that are
indistinguishable across domains. During testing, predictions on the target domain are made by
applying the source classifier to the adapted target encoder’s output.
Hsu et al. (2020) [51] proposed a method for DA in object detection that uses two adversarial
discriminators to align features globally and with respect to object centers. Sun et al. (2021) [109]
proposed a technique that adapts a classifier across domains without assuming shared features.
This method employs a combination of techniques to achieve adaptation, including adversarial
training with a domain discriminator and integration of a residual block to identify domain-specific
variances in the source domain classifier. Wang et al. (2018) [87] suggest a multi-adversarial dis-
criminator to improve adaptation, particularly in scenarios with multiple source domains. Their
method ensures alignment of feature distributions, resulting in better adaptation results [111]. Fur-
ther research also integrates a generative approach with adversarial feature alignment (e.g., [48]

3.3.3
Image-To-Image Translation
Image-to-image translation (or reconstruction-based DA) methods convert images from one
domain (source) to another (target) with or without paired data. By modifying the characteristics
of source images, the model improves its adaptation to the new environment.
Bousmalis et al. (2016) [10], use auto-encoders to isolate domain-specific properties (e.g., im-
age statistics) in separate private sub-spaces for the source and target domains. Additionally, they
allocate a subspace for capturing common features shared between the domains. By segment-
ing the space, shared representations remain unaffected by domain-specific ones.
Cycle-Consistent Adversarial Network (CycleGAN), introduced by Zhu et al. in 2017 [152], uses
two generators and two discriminators. The generators learn mappings between domains, while
the discriminators assess the realism of the translations. Through optimization of these components,
CycleGAN aligns the distributions of generated images with the target domains. CyCADA, pro-
posed by Hoffman et al. (2017) [48], merges CycleGAN with feature-level adaptation to align two
domains (synthetic to real) in both generative image and latent representation spaces. It achieves
this by combining cycle-consistent image translation, preserving image content across domains,
with feature-level adaptation, aligning representations learned by task-specific models.
Hsu et al. (2019) [52] use CycleGAN to convert source images to the target domain, creating
a synthetic intermediate domain with preserved source labels. To ensure consistency in feature
distributions, they integrate a domain discriminator with a GRL after the encoder in their detector.
The process involves two stages; firstly, aligning the source and synthetic domains, with similar con-
tent but different appearance, and secondly, aligning the synthetic and target domains, which
share similar appearance but different content. The authors introduce a weighted task loss for
the synthetic domain, which is based on its proximity to the target distribution as evaluated by the
discriminator in CycleGAN.
In their work, Huang et al. (2018) [55], translate entire images and object regions between
domains and different modalities. This method uses discriminator networks for adversarial training
using GRLs. A promising approach in this category proposed by Bousmalis et al. in 2017 [11]. It
focuses on aligning source domain images with the target domain at the pixel level. They use a
GAN, which consists of a generator, a discriminator, and a task classifier, to make source images
resemble those from the target domain while preserving their original content and labels. In this
case, the model can generalize to unseen object classes by separating DA from the task-specific
architecture [22].
Yang et al. (2020) [137] introduce a method for aligning source and target images without
modifying the network. They achieve this by exchanging low-frequency spectral information. Us-
ing fast Fourier transform and its inverse, they shift images between spatial and frequency do-
mains, exchanging low-frequency amplitude parts (global information—illumination and color)
while leaving phase components untouched (local information—edges and shapes). This results
in adapted source images that resemble the target dataset’s appearance. Yaris et al. (2023)
[138] propose a method that adjusts the histogram of each source image to match a randomly

Abramov et al. (2020) [1] apply a similar method using two main strategies. Initially, they align

3.3.4
Pseudo-Label Based Self-Training
Pseudo-label based self-training involves using a detector trained on a source domain to as-
sign labels to images in a target domain. These labels are then used for retraining, often with a
filtering mechanism to minimize errors. In a sense, this method leverages confident predictions from
unlabeled data (target domain) to generate pseudo-labels, which are used to iteratively improve
the training of the predictor through self-taught supervision.
Shin et al. (2020) [101] introduced a method which consists of two phases. Firstly, sliding window
voting is employed to extend confident predictions and label unlabeled pixels. Secondly, easy-
hard classification and hard-to-easy feature alignment use full pseudo-labels for easy samples
and enforce adversarial learning for hard samples. Mattolin et al. (2022) [79] mixed regions of
high-confidence pseudo detections with source images. They also implemented a consistency
loss term to aid in aligning with the target data distribution. Their method gradually refines the
selection of pseudo target detections based on confidence metrics, shifting from loose to strict
criteria during training.
3.3.5
Graph Reasoning
Graph reasoning uses object relationships modeled with graphs to enforce consistent object
relations across domains. Cai et al. (2021) [13] use a variational graph auto-encoder to derive
three latent variables from graph data: semantic, domain, and random. These variables incor-
porate label information unaffected by domain, domain-specific details, and noise, respectively.
Through disentanglement modules, the method learns a representation of the graph data that is
invariant to domain variations and robust to noise. Dhillon et al. (2012) [25], combine supervised
learning with semi-supervised DA to reduce the difference between domains. This approach con-
nects data points from domains through graph construction to infer labels for unlabeled data.
Li et al. (2022) [72] introduce a method aimed at achieving cross-domain object detection
through the transfer of unbiased semantic knowledge via cross-image and conditional graphs.
The framework consists of modeling unbiased semantics in the source domain, establishing con-
ditional graphs in the target domain, and adapting semantic knowledge between the domains.
Vibashan et al. (2022) [124] use graph-based models to improve feature representations in object

detection. They use a graph convolution network to model relationships between object propos-
als. Pairwise labels are generated based on the learned similarities to maximize feature alignment
among instances while minimizing it across different instances.
3.3.6
Domain Randomization
Domain randomization introduces variations in source domain images to create new domains
for training. In Tobin et al.’s study from 2017 [114], they define randomization parameters controlling
various aspects of the images (e.g., object placement and lighting conditions). These parameters
are uniformly sampled in defined ranges to generate synthetic images. The model is then trained
on these synthetic images to generalize to real ones.
Valtchev et al. (2020) [120] generated synthetic images of cats and dogs using the Unity game
engine, with different parameters like textures, lighting, poses, and breeds. These images were then
used to train a classifier. Another straightforward method for achieving domain randomization
involves applying various data augmentation techniques (e.g., color jitter, random flipping, and
noise injection). Nonetheless, this approach is regarded as relatively simple, consequently, it is
employed in conjunction with other methods.
3.3.7
Mean-Teacher Training
Mean-teacher training uses a teacher-student approach to stabilize training and reduce pre-
diction variance by minimizing consistency loss between teacher and student predictions on the
target domain.
Cao et al. (2023) [14] proposed a framework that integrates mean-teacher self-training and
contrastive learning and consists of two detectors that learn from each other by comparing their
features, using pseudo-labels generated from target domain images. Zhou et al. (2022) [148]
employ two YOLOv5 networks in their work. The student learns from labeled source and unlabeled
target images, guided by pseudo-labels from the teacher. Both networks use style-transferred
fake images to learn domain-specific features. The student also minimizes discrepancies between
target images and fake images through a consistency loss term.
In their study, Cai et al. (2019) [12] use both student and teacher models with identical net-
work architectures and shared region proposals. The student model is trained on labeled source
data and unlabeled target data, while the teacher model is updated through exponential mov-
ing average of the student weights. To ensure consistent predictions for target data, three types
of consistency losses are minimized. Region-level consistency aligns predictions for corresponding
spatial locations, inter-graph consistency matches graph structures, and intra-graph consistency
improves similarity among regions of the same class using pseudo-labels from the teacher model.
Ramamonjison et al.
(2021) [90] employed a two-phase technique using a large teacher
model with greater capacity compared to the student model. Initially, the teacher model was
trained on source data and utilized to generate pseudo-labels for target data. The process in-
volved freezing convolutional layers and updating only batch normalization layers in the first phase,
followed by unfreezing all layers for fine-tuning with refined pseudo-labels in the second phase.

3.3.8
Transformer-Based Domain Adaptation
Despite not traditionally falling under a distinct DA category, researchers have been exploring
the transformer-based architecture of DEtection TRansformer (DETR) [16] for DA since its develop-
ment in 2020. Over the past few years, there have been advancements in DA solutions for DETR
(transformer architecture + convolutional backbone) or its variants. Furthermore, these methods
have now achieved State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) results in DA for object detection.
DA-DETR by Zhang et al. (2021) [143], is a UDA object detector that improves the DETR frame-
work by integrating information fusion. It combines a base detector consisting of a CNN backbone
and a transformer encoder-decoder, along with a discriminator and a CNN-Transformer Blender.
The last component combines CNN features with transformer features to ensure feature alignment.
Training involves two losses; supervised object detection loss on labeled source images and adver-
sarial alignment loss on both source and target images using fused features. The model excels in
UDA in scenarios like weather changes, synthetic to real images, and cross-camera adaptation.
Another DA approach based on DETR comes from Yu et al. (2022) [141], who employ a mean
teacher framework with student and teacher models based on Deformable DETR. The teacher
model generates pseudo-labels for unlabeled target domain data, while the student model learns
from both labeled source domain data and pseudo-labeled target domain data. Their approach
includes a multi-level feature alignment approach, aligning image and object features at local,
instance, and global levels. This is achieved through domain adversarial training and domain
queries, using Deformable DETR’s cross-scale self-attention mechanism.
Wang et al. (2022) [130] proposed a method to adapt detection transformers such as DETR and
Deformable DETR to different domains. This approach involves applying two techniques to both
the encoder and decoder. It aims to align global context and token features to reduce domain
differences and bridge local gaps, respectively. Furthermore, the method includes a consistency
loss mechanism to improve feature discriminability.
3.4
Object Detection Models
After 2012, with the development of AlexNet by Krizhevsky et al. (2012) [63], there was a signif-
icant shift towards CNN-based detectors. Since then, numerous such detectors have been devel-
oped. The majority of current DA methods are designed for particular object detection models,
with the highest number of implementations focused on Faster R-CNN, followed by SSD and FCOS.
Therefore, this widely used detectors and their DA versions are briefly described in what follows.
Additionally, for the sake of completeness and the contributions it offers to the object detec-
tion domain, an introduction to YOLO and its DA version is provided, along with a description of
CenterNet, which will be utilized as a base model in this study. Finally, it is important to highlight

that most current research papers focus on DA for tasks like classification and semantic segmenta-

3.4.1
You Only Look Once (YOLO)
You Only Look Once (YOLO) by Redmon et al. (2015) [91], revolutionized object detection by
treating it as a regression problem. The input image is split into an G × G grid, with each grid cell
predicting b BBs along with confidence scores and class probabilities C, by using a single CNN
with FC layers. NMS is then employed to remove duplicates or overlapping BBs. YOLO achieves im-
pressive speed and moderate performance, running at 45 Frames Per Second (FPS) on an NVIDIA
Titan X GPU with a mAP of 63.40% on the PASCAL VOC 2007 dataset. Despite its speed advantage
over two-stage detectors, YOLO sacrifices some performance.
Since its introduction, YOLO has seen several variations, including YOLOv4 and YOLOv5, among
others. These advancements resulted in improvements in speed, performance, and the ability to
detect objects of varying sizes. The latest version, YOLOv10, was developed by Wang et al. in 2024
[125].
DA-YOLO, introduced by Zhang et al.
in 2021 [145], is among the few studies combining
DA techniques with one-stage detectors.
It operates on intermediate and final feature maps
of YOLOv3, using multiple domain classifiers to align image-level features via adversarial training.
Moreover, it employs YOLOv3’s multi-scale detections to extract instance-level features to reduce
instance domain shift. Additionally, it enforces consistency between image and instance-level
domain classifiers to encourage the generation of domain-invariant BBs.
3.4.2
Single Shot MultiBox Detector (SSD)
Single Shot MultiBox Detector (SSD) by Liu et al. (2015) [75], is a one-stage object detector that
does not rely on region proposals or feature resampling, making it faster and simpler compared to
other methods. SSD uses a set of default anchor boxes with different scales and aspect ratios to
detect objects of different sizes and shapes. It achieves object localization and classification in a
single forward pass by adding extra convolutional layers to the backbone network (i.e., VGG-16)
to generate feature maps of decreasing sizes (pyramid representation), instead of using fixed grids.
SSD utilizes a matching strategy based on IoU to assign ground truth boxes to default boxes,
without needing a separate region proposal network. It is trained with a combination of smooth
L1 loss for localization and Softmax loss for confidence. According to the authors, SSD achieves a
mAP of 74.30% on the VOC test dataset with an input size of 300 × 300, running at a speed of 59
FPS on an Nvidia Titan X GPU. When using an input size of 512×512, it achieves a mAP of 76.90%.
Inoue et al. (2018) [57] were among the first researchers to use SSD300 as their baseline de-
tector for DA. Utilizing CycleGAN to generate synthetic data, they improve domain transfer from
a source domain to a target domain while preserving instance-level annotations. By leveraging
pseudo-labeling, they generate annotations for target domain images using both image-level an-
notations and detector predictions. This process is followed by a two-step progressive fine-tuning
procedure on domain-transferred and pseudo-labeled images.

Chen et al. (2021) [18] modified the SSD network with their adversarial feature learning ap-
proach. They used deep features from various layers to learn characteristics that remain consis-
tent across instances, thus removing the need for specific instance-level details. They improved
the network by assigning weights based on diﬀiculty, aligning object patterns using class info, and
enforcing class alignment across layers.
Xu et al. (2019) [135] expand the SSD model with additional branches to distinguish between
synthetic and real smoke images. Through an adversarial training approach with a GRL, they opti-

minimizing domain confusion, and improving smoke detection.
3.4.3
Regions with Convolutional Neural
Networks (R-CNN)
The Regions with Convolutional Neural Networks (R-CNN), introduced by Girshick et al. in 2013
[33], is the first two-stage object detector. Initially, it uses a segmentation technique to propose po-
tential Regions of Interest (RoI), reducing the computational load compared to exhaustive window
testing. These region proposals (2, 000) are generated using the Selective Search algorithm [118].
Then, each proposed region is resized and fed into a pre-trained CNN (e.g., AlexNet) to extract
features, creating fixed-length feature vectors. These vectors are processed by binary Support
Vector Machine (SVM) classifiers (one per class) to classify the regions into objects or background.
Finally, a linear regressor improves the BB accuracy. Despite achieving moderate mAP scores on
PASCAL VOC 2010 (53.70%), VOC 2012 (53.30%), and ILSVRC 2013 (31.40%), R-CNN is slow and
computationally expensive.
Influenced by the R-CNN network, Hoffman et al. (2014) [49] use its fine-tuning technique to
convert their network from classification to detection. They considered classification data as the
source domain and detection data as the target domain. By fine-tuning the CNN layers with
detection data, they learned a general transformation from the source to the target domain. This
transformation was then applied to the classifier weights of classes without data, using nearest
neighbor averaging to estimate class-specific changes. Thus, they could generate detectors for
classes that only had classification data.
3.4.4
Fast R-CNN
Fast R-CNN, developed by Girshick Ross in 2015 [32], builds upon the R-CNN to address its limita-
tions. In contrast to R-CNN, which applies a CNN to each proposed region, Fast R-CNN performs a
single pass of the CNN on the entire image (instead of 2, 000), producing a feature map. Region
proposals are then mapped onto this map through RoI pooling, extracting fixed-length feature vec-
tors. This method reduces computational costs compared to R-CNN. Fast R-CNN also introduces
a multi-task loss function for simultaneous training of classification and regression tasks, removing
the need for SVMs. However, it still relies on Selective Search algorithm.
In their work, Hoffman et al. (2016) [47] introduced a DA object detection method utilizing
RGB-D data. Their approach involves generating region proposals by combining edge maps and

features from RGB-D images. The detection pipeline uses Fast R-CNN, with region proposal scoring
performed using a multi-modal CNN with separate branches for RGB and depth data. The RGB
branch is trained on all classes using RGB data, while the depth branch, initialized with RGB weights,
is fine-tuned on additional classes using depth information.
3.4.5
Faster R-CNN
Faster R-CNN by Ren et al. (2015) [92], integrate a Region Proposal Network (RPN) into its
architecture, removing the need for external algorithms (end-to-end approach). This approach
reduced processing time to just 0.20 seconds per image [92], while achieving impressive mAP
scores of 73.20% on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 70.40% on VOC 2012.
RPN uses a feature map as input to detect potential RoIs. The produced regions (k anchor
boxes) vary in size and aspect ratio. Furthermore, it generates ”objectness” scores for predefined
anchors, indicating foreground/background regions, and adjusts BBs through regression. Then, it
handles anchor points without grid creation by using 1 × 1 convolutions. NMS removes duplicate
proposals based on IoU threshold. RoI pooling is used for fixed-length feature vector extraction,
followed by classification (Fast R-CNN detector) and BB regression using FC layers.
The majority of previous studies on DA for object detection rely mainly on Faster R-CNN. Chen
et al. (2018) [20] were among the first to propose DA Faster R-CNN as a solution to mitigate the
domain shift, focusing on adversarial learning. This approach tackles domain shift at two levels.
Image-level shift involves changes in image style and lighting, addressed by training a domain
classifier to distinguish between source and target domains. Instance-level shift refers to changes
in specific object attributes (e.g., appearance and size), handled by training another domain
classifier to recognize differences at the object level. These classifiers at various levels are improved
through strong consistency regularization, aimed at learning a domain-invariant RPN.
Wang et al. (2019) [129] conducted a study similar to the one previously mentioned, but with a
different approach. They utilized few-shot learning, including only a limited number of annotated
target samples. Their method involved adjusting patch image features to address global domain
shifts, while also aligning object features to reduce appearance variations.
Guan et al. (2021) [40] use Faster R-CNN as their backbone and improve alignment between
source and target features by dynamically adjusting adversarial learning strength based on sam-
ple alignment entropy, while progressively aligning images and instances guided by entropy. They
also incorporate GRLs and domain classifiers for domain-invariant feature extraction.
Saito et al. (2018) [95] propose a method for cross-domain feature alignment that combines
weak global and strong local alignment techniques. They use two domain classifiers, one for global
and one for local features, to minimize domain differences. Additionally, a context vector-based
regularization technique improves stability and detection performance by integrating intermedi-
ate layer vectors with region-wise features.
Li et al. (2016) [73] introduced adaptive batch normalization, a method designed to transfer
knowledge between domains by adjusting the batch normalization statistics. This method sepa-
rates label-related and domain-related knowledge in a CNN, by replacing the statistics of each
batch normalization layer in the source domain with those from the target domain. Zhang et al.
(2019) [142] combine GANs to transform features across domains with cycle-consistent and iden-

utilizing GRLs and domain classifiers to align features at both image and instance levels.
Since the introduction of Faster R-CNN, numerous DA variations have emerged, including
methods proposed by [46], [3], [131], and [21] among others not discussed here. This reflects the

3.4.6
CenterNet
CenterNet by Zhou et al. (2019) [151] is a one-stage detector that represents each object
as a center-point of its BB, making it anchor-free and removing the need for predefined BBs and
post-processing techniques. It uses an encoder-decoder network, building upon CornerNet [65],
to generate a keypoint heatmap, where each peak corresponds to the center of a detected
object.
CenterNet regresses object properties directly from the center location (size, orientation, depth,
and pose). Additionally, CenterNet uses a local offset prediction to correct for discretization errors.
The network is trained with pixel-wise logistic regression with focal loss for the keypoint heatmap and
L1 loss for offset and size predictions. Deformable Convolutions (DC) in the backbone’s upsampling
layers and skip connections (e.g., Hourglass) improve keypoint detection and object regression
accuracy by capturing finer details (expanded receptive field).
However, CenterNet’s ability is limited to HBBs, making it unsuitable for objects with arbitrary
orientations. Pan et al. (2020) [86] addressed this limitation by improving CenterNet to predict
OBBs. They achieved this by introducing a branch to estimate the rotation angle of the BB, which
adjusts receptive fields according to object shapes and orientations and refines predictions by
considering the characteristics of the detected objects. CenterNet++ by Duan et al. (2022) [27],
represents each object as a triplet of keypoints (bottom-right corner, top-left corner, and center).
They employ center pooling, which captures patterns around center keypoints, and cascade cor-
ner pooling, which improves corner keypoint detection by considering both boundary and internal
directions of objects.
Zou et al. (2021) [153] improved CenterNet with an attention module using mean and max-
imum channel values, along with variance information. They changed the L1 loss function with
IoU loss for border and center point regression (scale invariance). These changes result in a 3.50%
increase in mAP on unaugmented test data and a 3.30% improvement with horizontal flipping
augmentation on the Pascal VOC dataset.
Scheck et al. (2020) [97], utilize UDA methods to close the gap between synthetic and real
images. Their architecture consists of three components; a feature extractor, a detection head,
and a DA head. The DA head uses entropy-minimization or maximum squares loss to ensure low-
entropy or low-squared predictions on real images, respectively.
Cao et al. (2023) [15] improved CenterNet by introducing a method that improves sub-kilometer
crater detection performance on Mars through knowledge transfer from the Moon. Firstly, their
model aligns global features using a dual cross-attention mechanism and a domain classifier, and
then aligns local features with a forward- and backward-aware mechanism and another domain
classifier. These components filter out irrelevant features and remove negative proposals.

3.4.7
Fully Convolutional One-Stage (FCOS)
The Fully Convolutional One-Stage (FCOS) object detector by Tian et al. (2019) [113], oper-
ates similarly to semantic segmentation networks and does not require anchor boxes or proposals.
It predicts both the location and class of objects at every pixel. FCOS consists of a backbone
network, a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN), and a shared head for classification, regression, and
centerness prediction. The backbone extracts feature maps, while the FPN generates multi-level
feature maps for objects of different sizes. The head predicts a binary class score, a 4D BB offset,
and a centerness score for each pixel (pixel proximity to the object center). FCOS directly predicts
distances from pixels to BB edges.
During training, FCOS uses per-pixel predictions. Positive pixels are those inside ground-truth
boxes, with regression targets set as distances to box edges. On the other hand, negative pixels
are those outside boxes or exceed the feature level’s range. The loss function includes focal loss
for classification, IoU loss for regression, and binary cross-entropy loss for centerness. In inference,
predicted boxes are derived from regression targets, and the final score is computed by multiplying
classification and centerness scores.
Yoo et al. (2022) [139] transformed continuous offsets into probability vectors for BBs and com-
bined them with backbone features to align features from different domains while considering
offsets. Additionally, they employed global and conditional domain discriminators to avoid neg-
ative transfer and improve feature discriminability. Hsu et al. (2020) [51] used FCOS for center-
aware feature alignment by predicting pixel-wise objectness and centerness maps. These maps
were combined to highlight the discriminative regions of objects over background. They also intro-
duced a center-aware discriminator for adversarial feature alignment.
Zhou et al. (2022) [150] employ FCOS along with two other modules in their DA framework. The
fusion module merges features across different scales to improve object representation, while the
discriminator aligns feature distributions between domains at pixel, instance, and category levels in
an adversarial manner. Their approach addresses both scale variation and category consistency.
3.5
Domain Adaptation in Agriculture
Over the past few years, numerous efforts have been made to tackle the challenge of domain
shift in the agricultural sector. Therefore, this thesis is not unique in encountering this issue in the
domain of PA. However, most of these approaches primarily focus on semantic segmentation,
followed by classification.
To begin with, Zhang et al. (2022) [144] employ a UDA method for agricultural land extraction
from remote sensing images. They use a GAN with a transformer backbone for inter-DA, integrating
a multi-scale feature fusion module to handle different spatial resolution. In the intra-domain phase,
they split the target domain into easy and hard images based on entropy, performing adversarial
training to minimize intra-domain gaps.
Gogoll et al. (2020) [34] expanded CycleGAN for plant classification by preserving semantic
details during image transformation. They utilized two FCNs for segmentation in each domain
and incorporated two generators and discriminators for DA. Bertoglio et al. (2023) [8] further

developed this method by introducing a loss term to minimize the phase difference between real
and transformed images, assuming phase carries semantic information.
Similarly, Vasconcelos et al. (2021) [121] introduce a more straightforward approach to image-
to-image translation for weed segmentation. They modify the low-frequency spectrum of source
images to align with the target domain using the fast Fourier transform and subsequently employ
contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization. By using this method, they achieved improved

Ilyas et al. (2023) [56] improved their model robustness for crop-weed segmentation by increas-

Magistri et al. (2023) [77] present a method for semantic segmentation that adapts models to

employ GANs to synthesize images combining the content of the source domain with the style of
the target domain. Additionally, they use a contrastive loss to preserve pixel-wise details during style
transfer, preventing the loss of small plants and discriminative features. Their approach involves a
joint encoder to extract image features, which are then decoded by task-specific decoders for
semantic segmentation and DA.

4.3.2
Cycle-Consistent Adversarial
Network (CycleGAN)
Another method for creating an intermediate domain, instead of using the HM technique dis-
cussed earlier, involves employing a GAN-based unpaired image-to-image translation approach.
==PAGE:52==
36
4 Methodology
Among the various options available for this purpose, the well-known CycleGAN will be utilized for
this thesis. It is worth noting that, to the best of the author’s knowledge, this is the first instance of
employing CycleGAN for a problem related to weed detection in the field of PA.
As previously discussed, CycleGAN is a modification of GAN designed for unpaired image-
to-image translation tasks. It operates with two generators, GST and GTS, which handle the
translation of images between a source domain, S, and a target domain, T , in both directions.
Additionally, two discriminators, DS and DT , are employed to distinguish whether images belong
to the source domain or to the target domain.
GST transforms images from domain S to domain T , GST : S →T , while DT distinguishes
between real T -domain images and those generated by GST . The objective is for GST to gener-
ate images indistinguishable from real ones. Cycle consistency is enforced through GTS, ensuring
that transformations are reversible, GTS : T →S.
In other words, cycle consistency ensures that after applying transformations GST and GTS

Most traditional metrics for measuring the difference between two distributions rely on param-

tuning. In response, Gretton et al. (2012) [39] introduced a non-parametric loss called the Maxi-
mum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) loss. This method compares distributions in a Reproducing Kernel
Hilbert Space (RKHS), utilizing a kernel function to map original data into this space.
As previously discussed, MMD is employed to align the distributions of source and target data.

the difference in means computed from both the source and target domains. By optimizing the

4.6
Domain-Enhanced CenterNet (DECNet)
The idea of modifying CenterNet’s architecture was influenced by the Domain-Adversarial
Neural Network (DANN), a widely recognized technique for classification. DANN or RevGrad, in-
troduced by Ganin et al. in 2015 [31], operates by generating predictions using features that are
both discriminative for the task at hand (i.e., classification) and robust against changes between
labeled training and unlabeled test data. Essentially, DANN improves the discriminative aspects of
features for the primary task while ensuring stability against domain shifts. The DANN architecture
consists of a feature extractor, a domain classifier, and a label predictor.
Building upon Ganin et al.’s [31] approach is primarily motivated by three key factors. To begin
with, it is a method that can be utilized in any network, although it was initially applied to classifica-
tion. Moreover, multiple studies, particularly in segmentation (e.g., [23]), have observed favorable
results from its application. Finally, and most importantly, it is an approach that is entirely focused
on UDA, which aligns with real-world conditions.
In the primary network structure of CenterNet outlined in Section 4.4, two additional networks,
consisting of a GRL, two domain discriminators and an attention module, are introduced. These

4.6.3
Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL)
The network incorporates a Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL) for adversarial learning in the DA
task. As already discussed, GRL is placed between the domain discriminators and the feature
extractor. Its role is to guarantee the similarity of feature distributions across domains (feature distri-
butions alignment), making them indistinguishable for the domain discriminators. This leads to the
generation of domain-invariant features. GRL lacks trainable parameters, except for the parame-
ter λp, which remains unchanged during backpropagation.
The purpose of GRL is to train a model to simultaneously minimize a task-specific loss (i.e., detec-
tion loss in this case) while maximizing the domain discrimination loss. During forward propagation,
the GRL acts like an identity function, leaving the input unchanged. However, during backprop-
agation, GRL reverses the gradients by multiplying them with a negative scalar, λp. This aims to
maximize domain confusion (Equation 4.19). This allows the feature extractor to ”fool” the discrim-
inator while it distinguishes the features’ domain.

4.6.4
Attention Module
Most adversarial DA methods align representations derived from whole images in both the
source and target domains. However, not all regions contribute equally to this alignment. To ad-
dress this issue, an attention module is introduced to emphasize important regions of the image,
thereby improving generalization across domains.
The attention module, developed based on the work of Woo et al. (2018) [133], is a spatial at-
tention layer placed between the feature extractor’s outputs and the domain discriminators. This
layer provides guidance on where the most informative areas are, helping the discriminators fo-
cus on those regions and improving the alignment of feature distributions. To compute the spatial
attention, the output of the feature extractor undergoes both average and max pooling along
the channel dimensions, followed by concatenation. Subsequently, a convolution layer gener-
ates an attention map, which highlights areas requiring more emphasis. More formally, the spatial
attention map M, can be calculated as follows:

The performance of oriented object detection is evaluated using the mAP metric discussed in

positions considering their spatial proximity and overlap in their respective regions. mAP50:95 is re-
ported for assessing the performance of true keypoint detection across all experiments. OKS can

Projected Object Keypoint Similarity (POKS) by Güldenring et al. (2023) [41] introduces a metric

calculating OKS between the predictions and these projected points. In each experiment, a to-
tal of five metrics will be presented. These include mAP50:95 for POKS concerning stem, vein, true,
pseudo, and all keypoints.
To evaluate the quality of images produced by CycleGAN, the Structural Similarity Index Mea-

images by evaluating their structural information rather than their pixel values. A lower SSIM value,
which ranges from -1 to 1, suggests less structural consistency between the original source domain
image and the intermediate domain’s image, implying a loss of fine details, textures, or edges.

4.8.2
Training Details
Throughout the experiments, specific hyperparameters were set for the training process. All
images were resized to 512 × 512. To enlarge the dataset, various image augmentation tech-
niques were applied randomly, including random flipping, zooming out, color jitter, rotation by
90◦, Gaussian noise, random brightness and contrast adjustments, and image normalization.
The RumexLeaves dataset, consisting of iNaturalist and RoboRumex domains, was randomly
divided into training, validation, and testing sets using ratios of 0.70, 0.15, and 0.15, respectively,
as defined by Güldenring et al. (2023) [41]. However, an incorrectly set flag in the code caused
the validation and test splits of RoboRumex to be swapped during evaluation. As a result, the
validation split in this thesis corresponds to the original test split from [41], and the test split aligns
with the original RoboRumex validation split.
To align with ML practices, the original validation split of RoboRumex will be referred to as the
test split, and vice versa, throughout this thesis. Repeating the same experiments, which involve
approximately 1, 500 hours of training on an Nvidia Tesla V100, would require significant compu-
tational costs and is therefore not considered worthwhile.
The Baseline model was trained using a batch size of 32 and the Adam optimizer, initialized
with a learning rate of 5e-4. Additionally, a scheduler was employed to decrease the learning rate
at epochs 2, 500, 4, 000, and 5, 000, with reductions to 5e-4/2, 5e-4/4, and 5e-4/8, respectively.
The network’s weights are initialized from a normal distribution N(0, 0.001). Regarding the loss
function defined in Equation 4.11, the weights assigned to λcp, λoff, λkp, λkphm, and λobb are
1.00, 1.00, 10.00, 1.00, and 10.00, respectively.
In order to establish a performance benchmark for comparison in the target domain, the Base-
line model was fine-tuned using images sourced from the RoboRumex train split, employing a con-
ventional supervised approach. This model will be referred to as Oracle and will be considered as
the upper-bound performance on the RoboRumex test split. The fine-tuning procedure employed
500 epochs, with a learning rate set at 1e-5.

The training of the model using histogram-matched source images (HM) followed the same

Although in typical research papers, CycleGAN is trained with nearly all of its hyperparameters

remains constant for the first 200 epochs before linearly decaying to 0 over the final 200 epochs.
Weights are initialized using a Gaussian distribution N(0, 0.02).

where λcyc = 20.00 and λid = 0.50, respectively, to improve model control. Finally, the batch
size is set to 1, with each image in a batch randomly cropped to 128 × 128. The model training
with this intermediate domain (Synthetic) followed the same settings as the HM method.
The DECNet model, equipped with SAD, PLD, and the attention module, was trained using
a batch size of 32 (16 for each domain) and the Adam optimizer for both the detection model
and the discriminators. Training lasted 5, 500 epochs, employing a learning rate of 5e-4 for the
detection network and 1e-4 for the discriminators, with a weight decay of 1e-5. A scheduler was
applied to adjust the learning rates, decreasing them at epochs 2, 000, 3, 500, and 4, 500 for both
the detection model and the discriminators.
The detection network weights were the same as in the Baseline model, while the discriminators
weights were initialized using Kaiming normal initialization with N(0, std2) [45]. The weights for
the detection loss components remained unchanged, while λSAD was set to 0.001 and λPLD to
0.0001. The training parameters remained the same when DECNet was trained with CycleGAN-
generated images as the source domain, as well as when it was trained with the GAN-based
approach.
Finally, the model trained with the MMD loss utilized settings similar to ones used for the main
network of DECNet. A modification was made to employ the RAdam optimizer instead of Adam,
as it was noted to achieve slightly improved results. Additionally, the ReLU activation function in
ResNet50 was replaced by Leaky ReLU. The weight for the MMD loss was set to 0.001, and Gaussian
and Laplacian kernels were applied.

5.3
CycleGAN Image Performance for Center-
Net Training
Using CycleGAN to generate a synthetic domain significantly improves the Baseline results. It
produces images with realistic leaf blades, stems, and veins. CycleGAN excels in replicating ob-
jects abundant in the training dataset, such as weeds, sticks, and grass leaves in the background.
This result is predictable as the generator learns these characteristics during training. Conversely,
CycleGAN struggles with images containing diverse background elements like rocks, marble tiles,
or bare ground, which are less common in the training data. Thus, these areas are translated with
more artifacts.
Additionally, the model occasionally exhibits instability during training, leading to termination
of the process. This instability often arises from the model misinterpreting color information from the
target image. Such occurrences are common in CycleGAN training, as acknowledged by its cre-
ators. While restarting training can be cumbersome, it is a necessary step given the unpredictable
nature of the model. Further limitations of CycleGAN will be elaborated in Section 5.7.

5.5
DECNet Results
The experiments conducted in this section aim to assess the effectiveness of the proposed
approach, which involves employing DECNet to adapt to a target domain only with access to
labeled source data. This method is inherently more complex and resource-intensive, as it requires
modifying the model architecture and including two adaptation sub-networks, thereby increasing
the overall model parameters.
5.5.1
Ablation Study: Effect of Domain Adaptation
Weight
During the optimization process, achieving a balance between detection and adversarial
losses is very important. To achieve this balance, λSAD and λPLD are employed, which control the
trade-off between these two types of losses. To understand the impact of adjusting the DA weights,
an ablation study was conducted on the RoboRumex validation split, which also included a closer
examination of how both discriminators operate independently under varying DA weights. The re-
sults on the RoboRumex validation split are presented in Table 5.6.
The ablation study involved increasing the value of λSAD from 0.001 to 10, and for λPLD from
0.0001 to 0.01. The aim of this study was to assess how different values can affect the significance
of the DA loss compared to the detection loss. It was hypothesized that smaller values of λSAD
would minimize the impact of the adversarial loss, while larger values would amplify it, thereby
potentially strengthening DA.
However, the results in Table 5.6, reveal a different relationship. It appears that setting λSAD
to excessively high values significantly reduces the mAP compared to the optimal performance

The key insight here is that the poorer performance associated with larger values for DA might
stem from the accumulation and backpropagation of more incorrect gradients to the model. As a
result of these observations, λSAD is set to 0.001 for SAD, as it achieves the most favorable balance
between the detection and DA tasks.

5.7
Limitations
In this section, some limitations regarding all the approaches will be discussed. Primarily, these
limitations arise from the significant variation in weed and leaf sizes/appearance encountered by
the detector during training compared to those in the RoboRumex test set. Consequently, perfor-
mance degradation occurs as the detector encounters objects smaller than those seen during
training. The effectiveness of the method relies on CenterNet’s ability to generalize to unseen ob-
ject sizes, partially addressed through the utilization of DC.
Moreover, a significant aspect of this thesis involves creating an intermediate domain, which
impacts the effectiveness of the object detector. Particularly for the CycleGAN method and GANs
in general, their training is unstable, time-consuming, and prone to convergence issues, such as
mode collapse. Additionally, they require a considerable amount of data to achieve satisfactory
results.
Another issue arising from CycleGAN training is the potential for the model to initially learn
an incorrect color translation from the target to the source domain. This problem is commonly
encountered, and the only way to overcome it is to restart the training process. Finally, another
issue associated with CycleGAN is its limited ability to minimize the domain shift at the feature
level, unlike discrepancy-based or adversarial learning methods, as its primary focus is only on
style transfer. However, for this particular case study, it appears that this is not a concern, as the
Synthetic model achieves very satisfactory results.
Another drawback of this study is that the suggested DA method was only tested in a single
instance of domain shift in PA. While the results show potential, it is important to explore its effec-
tiveness across different conditions. Furthermore, another limitation regarding the specific dataset
is its novelty, which, in reality, is not a limitation. This is because other researchers have not previ-
ously used it for DA purposes. As a result, there are no existing implementations available for direct
comparisons with the proposed method.

6.1
Conclusion
The detection of weeds in fields and grasslands is crucial for ensuring the safety and productivity
of crops and livestock. Traditional manual inspections are time-consuming and ineﬀicient, leading
to the use of advanced methods such as UGVs and UAVs. These methods have become a focus
of research in weed detection and elimination. However, a performance gap occurs due to the
variation between source and target images from different fields and grasslands.
This thesis explored different DA methods to address both stem/vein and weed detection chal-
lenges in datasets from different grassland environments. In particular, well-known and commonly
used DA methods, including HM, CycleGAN, MMD, and DANN, were compared with a domain-
adaptive CenterNet model.
The proposed approach introduces a multitask method for UDA,
which builds upon CenterNet by incorporating an attention-based, multi-level adversarial learn-
ing strategy.
This strategy employs two domain discriminators at high and low feature levels, each equipped
with a GRL. These discriminators determine whether the input belongs to the source or target
domains, while CenterNet generates detection predictions. Before passing the input feature maps
to the discriminators, a spatial attention module is employed to ensure the model focuses only
on the relevant areas. Through backpropagation with GRL, gradients are reversed, leading the
feature extractor to optimize in a direction that maximizes domain confusion. Consequently, it
learns invariant features across both domains.
Overall, the proposed approach is believed to be the first utilization of DA in weed detection.
According to the experimental results, DECNet-Full demonstrates significant potential for further
investigation, demonstrating improved detection performance in the target domain through the
addition of a second discriminator, as opposed to DANN, and the integration of a spatial attention
module. While DECNet-Full exhibits substantial improvements over the Baseline model and out-
performs most compared methods, except CycleGAN, which shows slightly better OBB detection
performance, it is not without limitations. There may exist more advanced deep DA methods that
could potentially outperform it. Despite these considerations, DECNet-Full emerges as a promising
==PAGE:86==
70
6 Conclusion and Future Work
choice for DA tasks requiring weed and stem/vein detection. In conclusion, the goal of this thesis
is to establish the proposed approach as a new contribution to the field of DA in agriculture.
6.2
Future Work
While the current methods have shown significant improvements in the RoboRumex domain,
there is still room for further development. One possible direction for future research involves utilizing
a dataset sourced from the same origin for the source domain. As previously discussed, the iNat-
uralist dataset was scraped from the iNaturalist website, resulting in training images with variance
and shifts due to originating from different regions worldwide, captured by different cameras, and
under various conditions. Employing a more ”consistent” dataset with minimal variance among
its data points as the source domain could possibly simplify the DA process.
An additional direction for future research could involve examining the utilization of different
GAN-based techniques for unpaired image-to-image translation. The goal would be to produce
higher-quality images with fewer artifacts and less noise, potentially improving both weed and
keypoint detection results.
Finally, another direction for future study could involve using the same DA framework while
replacing the detection model. Currently, CenterNet is the chosen model for detection. However,
to improve performance further, one could consider using other anchor-free detectors, a two-
stage detector like Faster R-CNN, or even a Vision Transformer. Following these modifications, the
keypoint detection, OBB regression, and DA heads could be adjusted to fit the new detection
model.
