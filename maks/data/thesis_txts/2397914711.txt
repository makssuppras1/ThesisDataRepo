Abstract
The effects of temperature on the petrophysical and geological properties of material
rich in organic matter (immature kerogen), originating from the Lower Ghareb for-
mation (depth of 463.2 m) in the Zoharim borehole (Shephela basin, central Israel),
were investigated and compaired with material from other parts of the same borehole.
The material was investigated by laboratory measurements for density, porosity, spe-
cific surface area, pore size distribution, content of calcite, organic matter, carbon
and organic carbon.
For the properties, it was found that they change significantly for temperatures from
a critical temperature of 125 ◦C and up, and for most of the properties, similar trends
was observed for the cores from other parts of the borehole.
The properties seen to increase with temperature was the grain density, porosity, per-
meability, pore size distribution and slightly the content of calcite. The properties
seen to decrease was the content of organic matter (TOC and LOI), specific surface
area and the irreducible waster saturation in the pores.
Most of the properties appears to have a relationship with the content of organic
matter in the material, which decreases with temperature.

Resumé
Det blev undersøgt hvilken effekt temperatur har på de petrofysike og geologiske egen-
skaber af materiale der er rig på organisk stof (umodnet kerogen) fra den nedre del
af Ghareb formationen (dybde 463.2 m ) fra Zoharim borehullet (placeret i Shephela
bassinet i den centrale del af Israel). Resultaterne blev sammenlignet med resultater
fra andre kerner fra det samme borehul.
Materialet blev undersøgt i laboratoriet for dets densitet, porøsitet, specifikke over-
flade areal, fordelingen af pore størrelser, indholdet af kalk, organisk stof og organisk
karbon. For de forskellige egenskaber blev det observeret, at de ændre sig markant
for temperaturer omkring et kritisk punkt på 125 ◦C og opefter, og for de fleste af
egenskaberne blev det bekræftet at kernerne fra andre dele af borehullet, viste lig-
nende tendenser.
Egenskaber der steg med temperaturen omfatter korn densiteten, porøsiteten, per-
meabiliteten, fordelingen af pore størrelser og til dels indholdet af kalk. Egenskaber
der faldt med temperaturen omfatter indholdet af det organiske stof og dermed også
indholdet af organisk karbon, det specifikke overflade areal og det irreducible vandind-
hold i porerne.
For de fleste af egenskaberne ses der en sammenhæng med indholdet af organisk stof
i materialet, som falder med temperaturen.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the background for the project, a short information about
the history of the area where the tests material originates from, and a bit about what
the purpose of the thesis is.
1.1
Background
In the state of Israel, there are many basins containing sequences rich in organic
matter. Several basins are described in [Min13], including the Shephela basin, which
this study is concerned with.
The Shephela basin is part of an extensive upwelling system, the Levant upwelling
belt, which occurred in the Late Cretaceous along the south-east margin of the Tethys
[Mei+14] and is probably the largest occurrence of oil shale that is known to exists in
Israel. It is estimated to cover an area between 1000 −1400 km2 and possibly more
than this. The basin is located in the central part of Israel and is running in a north-
south directed strip, which is around 70 km long and in the range of 5 −20 km wide.
The thickness of the organic matter enriched sequence (the Ghareb and Mishash for-
mation from the Late Cretaceous) in this area varies from several tens of meters and
up to 550 m [Min13]. The location can be seen in Figure 1.1 from [Shi+16].
The material from the organic-rich sequence of the basin, is a brown organic-rich chalk
containing kerogen, which upon maturation may yield hydrocarbons. The TOC for
the material ranges from 1 −19 %, where the content is highest in the lower part
of the Ghareb formation [Shi+16]. The organic-rich sequence is found at a depth of
some hundred meters, thus the sequence was never buried deep enough to expose it
to high enough pressure and temperature for it to mature naturally, and hence it is
an immature source rock [Shi+16].

The material used for this thesis (from the Zoharim borehole) and material from the
Shephela basin in general, have previously been studied in different ways at different

ties [Bis+15].

1.4
Thesis definition
The objective of this project is to investigate and describe how increasing tempera-
tures affects the petrophysical and geological properties of an organic-rich chalk from
the Lower Ghareb formation in the Shephela basin.
The results will be compaired and related to results obtained in projects concerned
with material from the same borehole (Zoharim).
Thus, the main objectives of this thesis are:
1. How does increasing temperatures affect the petrophysical and geological prop-
erties of the material?
2. How does the found results relate to results obtained by others on material from
the same borehole?

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical
background
This chapter presents the theoretical background and calculations of the tests per-
formed or the properties found in the work with this thesis.
2.1
Kerogen
In a source rock, kerogen is the solid, insoluble organic matter which can mature to
generate hydrocarbons.
When the organic matter is deposited and buried with sediments under anaerobic
conditions in a steadily subsiding sedimentary basin, the temperature and pressure
increases. In response to the burial, an evolution of the organic matter happens which
includes the formation of the kerogen.
The evolution of the organic matter have three distinct phases [Sel98]:
1. Diagenesis: The first phase is a biogenic decay of the organic matter, aided by
bacteria and abiogenic reactions. Water, methane and carbon dioxide is given
off by the material, and a complex hydrocarbon is left. This is the kerogen. The
phase takes place in the shallow subsurface where the temperature and pressure
is nearly normal. In this phase, the content of oxygen is reduced, leaving the
hydrogen:carbon ratio largely unaltered.
2. Catagenesis: The second phase is a release of petroleum from the kerogen,
first in the form of oil and later as gas. This phase takes place in the deeper
subsurface where the temperature and pressure increases. In this phase the
hydrogen:carbon ratio decreases and the oxygen:carbon ratio experiences no
significant changes.
3. Metagenesis: The third and last phase is where the last hydrocarbons (mainly
methane) are expelled. This phase takes place deep in the subsurface where the
temperature and pressure are so high the conditions are verging metamorphism.
in this phase the hydrogen:carbon ratio decreases until only carbon is left, now
in the form of graphite.

2.1.1
Maturation of Kerogen
As described shortly above, the maturation of kerogen takes place during the second
phase of evolution: Catagenesis. During this phase the kerogen will first release oil
and later gas.
The generation of oil and gas happens in the temperature intervals termed the oil-
and gas window respectively, and a number of studies places the oil window in the
temperature interval 60 −120◦C and the gas window in the interval 120 −225◦C
[Sel98]. In Figure 2.1 below can be seen the oil- and gas windows [Ped]. The values
are only suggestions and should not be taken for facts! It all depends on the material
at hand.
For this study, the kerogen in the material have not matured and will do so upon
heating.

2.12
Uncertainty
During measurements of all kinds, there will always be some uncertainty or error
related to them. The origin of the uncertainty can be random or systematic.
Random errors are unpredictable, cannot be avoided and does not affect all measure-
ments equally. The quantity of a random error is unknown and can affect the true
value in both negative and positive direction. Thus the random errors are usually
estimated and reported in the form: z = ± δz.
The systematic errors on the other hand, are predictable and measures can be taken
to minimize them in terms of calibrating equipment and machines before use.
Results which are based on multiple measurements can be reported using either their
propagated error (see Section 2.12.1) or their standard deviation (see Section 2.12.2).

3.6.1
Carbonate content through Titration
The determination of the carbonate content through titration is described in [12] and
conducted in the following way:
0.3 g of the crushed bulk material was weight and transferred to a 250 ml conical
flask.
25.0 ml of HCl 0.5 N was added slowly using a burette, and two teaspoons of glass
beads was added.
The solution was brought to the boiling point on a heating plate to remove the CO2.
100 ml of distilled water was added to prevent the solution from burning, during the
20 min boiling. Afterwards, the solution was cooled to room temperature.
5 drops of the indicator Phenolphthalein ans a small stick-magnet was added to the
conical flask and the titration with NaOH 0.5 N-solution started immediately.
The NaOH is added slowly from a burette under constant stirring on a magnetic
stirrer until the color of the solution changes to faint pink, marking the endpoint of
the titration.
The used volume of NaOH is noted for later estimation of the content of calcite
in the sample [12].

3.6.2
Carbonate content through Acid treatment
The determination of the carbonate content (together with the generation of residue)
is described in [09] and was conducted in the following way:
20 −30 g of the bulk sample was weight directly into a 750 ml centrifuge flask of pre-
known weight. ≈50 ml of water was added to wet the sample. 100 ml of CH3COOH
was added slowly to the sample to avoid overflow of the mixture. Another 100 ml

remain. when the solution was free of carbonates, it was centrifuged at 2500 rpm/min
for 10 min, to make the sediments settle at the bottom. The clear supernatant was
siphoned off. The residue is washed with distilled water, centrifuged and siphoned off
several times, until there was no indication of remaining acetic acid. The content of
carbonate was afterwards estimated using Equation (2.17).

3 Methodology
3.7
Determination of TC and TOC at GEUS
The determination of Total Carbon (TC) and Total Organic Carbon (TOC) were
performed by the company GEUS (Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland)
using their LECO CS-200 Carbon/Sulfur Determinator [Cor03].
The description of the process in this report is only for the carbon, though it is
the same for sulfur, which is not investigated in this thesis.
The bulk sample (for TC) and accelerator was loaded into the machine in a small
crucible. The accelerator was added to ignite the sample and oxidize the carbon in
the sample in a short time frame.
The combustion chamber was purged with oxygen to drive off any residual atmo-
spheric gases. Afterwards, the oxygen-flow through the system was restored and the
induction furnace was turned on.
The inductive elements of the accelerator and sample couples with the high frequency
field of the furnace. This coupling generates a heat and this heat combined with the
pure oxygen environment, causes the sample to combust.
During the combustion, the number of elements containing carbon was reduced, re-
leasing the carbon, which immediately binds with the oxygen to form a little CO and
mostly CO2.
The sample gases were swept into the carrier stream and into the infrared cell (IR
cell) where the carbon is measured as CO2, and the gasses flow through the cell.
The CO2 absorbs infrared energy at a precise wavelength within the IR spectrum, and
as the gas passes through the cell, it absorbs some energy which is then prevented
from reaching the IR detector.
Thus the concentration of CO2 (and thereby carbon) was detected as a reduction in
the level of energy that reached the IR detector.
To get TOC, the carbon from the carbonates in the sample needs to be removed.
At GEUS, this is done by dissolving the bulk sample in 2N HCl. For the precise
procedure, the reader is referred to GEUS.
Afterwards, the residual residue was run in the LECO machine, using the same pro-
cedure as for TC.

The tubes for the sample powder were dried in the oven at 110 ◦C overnight and
set to cool to room temperature in a desiccator. Afterwards the tubes and their cor-
responding stopper was weight.
1 −2 g of the samples were added to the tubes and were set to degass at 60◦C for 4
hours, and afterwards cooled in a desiccator. The degassing is done to remove any
gases and vapours that may have become adsorbed onto the surface of the solids after
the general sample preparation and during storage of the powder.
The tubes with sample and stoppers were weight again and the weight of the samples
were calculated.
The tubes with samples (now without the stopper) were placed in the Gimini III
2375 ([Geo08]) for analysis, and a Devar vessel containing liquid N2 is placed in the
machine under the sample.
First the machine measures the free space in the sample tube by adding He, (which is
a non-adsorbed gas) to the tube, while the Devar vessel is raised so the sample tube
is surrounded by liquid N2.
Afterwards the Devar vessel is lowered while the machine prepairs for the next step,
which is the measurement of the specific surface area.
The Devar vessel is raised again and a sufficient volume of adsorbate gas N2 is ad-
mitted to give the lowest desired relative pressure and the adsorbed volume v (Equa-
tion (2.22)) is measured. To achieve multi-point measurements, v is measured for
successively higher P/P0 values, typically in the range of 0.05 −0.3 for N2 gas.

For the estimation method, based on the cleaning in methanol, there are some things
and situations, which would influence the results.
To monitor the weight loss due to the cleaning process in methanol, the piece of Stevns
Chalk was cleaned along with the samples. If the Stevns Chalk, during this cleaning,
gained small particles or salt grains cleaned off of the sample pieces, the correction
for the weight loss would become too small and the salinity overestimated. The piece
of Stevns Chalk could have been cleaned separately to overcome this uncertainty.
If the sample pieces lost material other than salt during the cleaning (small pieces not
seen when unpacking them from the cleaning socks) the resulting weight loss, would
be estimated too high and the resulting salinity would thus also be too high.
If the weight used, have been moved from it spot, it calibration could have been lost
and thus given a weight too high or low, thereby influencing the results.

5.1
The critical temperatures
As described in Section 2.1, it is known that kerogen will mature upon heating. The
critical temperature for this maturation is in the range of 60 ◦C to 120 ◦C for oil and
120 ◦C to 225 ◦C for gas. Above 225 ◦C all the carbon in the kerogen is in the form
of graphite [Sel98].
One thing to notice about the observations from this work, is that the material does
not loose much weight until after 125 ◦C. From 150 ◦C up to 220 ◦C the weight loss is
doublet for each temperature (Figure 4.3). The porosity of the material was also seen
to increase more rapidly from this temperature onward (Figure 4.10). For the TOC
a significant change is also observed around 125 ◦C from where the TOC decreases
more rapidly with increasing temperatures (Figure 4.23). For both the LOI and the
SSA of both bulk material and residue, similar observations was made, with changes
occurring at 125 ◦C (Figure 4.26 and Figure 4.40 respectively). The permeability for
the material was also estimated to have an increasing trend (Figure 4.45) starting at
125 ◦C.
Through all these observations it seems clear that the organic matter in the ma-
terial experiences some changes around 125 ◦C. This seem to be consistent with the
maturation-theory of kerogen described by [Sel98] and thus, 125 ◦C seems to be a
critical temperature for the material studied in this thesis.

5.3 The loss of structural water in the clay minerals during LOI
101
5.3
The loss of structural water in the clay minerals
during LOI
The LOI is assumed to only be a measure of the content of organic matter in the
sample. For the present study of the material from the Zoharim well, the LOI gave
results of a weight loss of 35 −50 %. Previous studies have had results in the magni-
tude of 13 −43 % weight loss for the core from 337 m and 5 −25 % weight loss for
the core from 560 m.
[Sun+09] and others before them, have showed that the clay minerals in a soil sample,
are prone to lose adsorbed and structural water when exposed to the high tempera-
tures of the LOI test (550 ◦C). Thus the LOI recorded for all the three cores studied
at DTU, have not only been due to the burning off of the organic material in the cores,
but also due to the dehydration of the water within the clay minerals, kaolinite, illite
and smectite.
For kaolinite, the dehydration is reported to begin at 500 −600 ◦C and for illite and
smectite (also chlorite which is not present in the studied cores) there is only the loss
of the adsorbed water for temperatures under 550 ◦C.
As LOI takes place at 550 ◦C, the mineral loosing the most weight due to the dehy-
dration should be the kaolinite.
Another thing to mention here is that the starting temperature of the dehydration
of the clay minerals, are much higher than the temperatures used for any of the core-
studies at DTU (temperatures up to 300 ◦C have been used). Thus the heating of
the samples before the testing (part of the sample preparation) and also the amounts
of the clay minerals studied in section Section 4.11, should not have been affected by
this dehydration.

5.4
The method of maturation used and alternative
methods
At DTU the heating of the material during the sample preparation was performed in
the presence and with access of oxygen to the system and samples. Thus the organic
matter in the material will experience some maturation depending on the tempera-
ture.
A maturation-study performed and described by [Shi+17] have used pyrolysis for the
heating process. The core material was placed in the pyrolysis reactor which was af-
terwards flushed with nitrogen gas several times to expel all oxygen from the sample
and the system before the heating. The heating was also performed with no inflow
of oxygen.
[Shi+17] heated their material at two temperatures to represent an early-mature state
and an over-mature state of the rock-material. For the early-matured state (276 ◦C)
the reduction in TOC was seen to be low, only at 3.3 % and for the over-matured
state (377 ◦C), the TOC experienced a very prominent depletion at 32 %.
For the material studied at DTU, the presence and inlet of oxygen during the heating
process, have caused the reduction in TOC to be relativly higher than if pyrolysis
had been used.
For the core from a depth of 337 m the TOC reduction was estimated to be 82.5 %
(material heated to 300 ◦C). For the core from a depth of 463.2 m the TOC reduction
was estimated to be 57.4 % (material heated to 220 ◦C). And for the core from a
depth of 560 m, the TOC reduction was estimated to be 89.2 % (material heated to
300 ◦C).
The compaired TOC reductions clearly shows that the heating method used, makes
a difference when it comes to the content of organic carbon in the material, and the
method which best simulates the conditions in a real reservoir is the pyrolysis.

Conclusion
Effects of increasing temperatures were seen for the petrophysical and geological prop-
erties of the studied material. The most distinct changes were seen to happen around
a critical temperature of 125 ◦C and up. For all the properties except the specific
surface area, similar trends were observed for cores from other parts of the borehole.
Below is listed the observed temperature effects, and also what was found for the
comparison of the three cores:
• A weight reduction of ≈7% for heating to 220 ◦C was found.
• The dry bulk density had an average value of 1.43 g/cm3. When the tempera-
ture increases, there is no significant variation. It increases with depth and the
core from 560 m had the highest dry bulk density.
• The grain density had an average value of 2.5 g/cm3. It increases with temper-
ature and with depth. The core from 560 m had the highest grain density.
• The porosity had an average value of 42.96 %. It increases with temperature,
but decreases with depth, where the cores from 337 m and 463.2 m had the
highest porosities.
• The salinity of the material was determined to be ≈8000 −9000 ppm NaCl.
It’s temeprature dependency was not investigated.
With depth it decreases.
There were seen to be a big difference depending on the method used, where
the estimation from the methanol cleaning gave the highest salinity.
• The XRD on the material determined a content of calcite, apatite, quartz, pyrite,
kaolinite, illite and smectite. When the temperature increases and there is less
organic material, the contribution from each mineral is seen to increase for all
the cores studied at DTU. The core from a greater depth (560 m) was seen to
also contain dolomite and possible traces of siderite.
• The content of calcite was found to be 64.8 % for both the titration and the
dissolution in acetic acid. For the titration no temperature dependency was
seen for any core, where for the dissolution, a slight increase with temperature
was observed.
For the titration, the content of calcite increases with depth,
where it decreases for the dissolution results. This is influences by the presence
of dolomite in the core from the greatest depth (560 m). For both methods the
core from 560 m is seen to have the highest content of calcite.
==PAGE:120==
106
6 Conclusion
• The total content of carbon (TC) had an average value of 19.03 % and decreases
with temperature. For the depth dependency it is seen to be highest for the
core from 463.2 m. For all the studied cores, it decreases with temperature.
• The total content of organic carbon (TOC) had an average value of 11.35 %
and decreases with temperature. It is highest for the core from 463.2 m and all
cores showed a decreasing trend with temperature.
• The organic matter (LOI) had an average value of 48.73 %. It decreases with
temperature and the core from 463.2 m had the highest LOI. All cores showed
a decreasing trend with temperature.
• For the comparison of TOC and LOI, all cores showed a decreasing trend for the
ratio TOC/LOI with temperature, meaning less carbon would be pressent in
the organic material with increasing temperature. The largest drop in TOC/LOI
was seen for the core from 560 m.
• MICP results gave an average value of the pore radius of 0.12µm and an aver-
age value of the irreducible water saturation of 20 %. When the temperature
increases, the pore radius increases and the irreducible water saturation de-
creases. The pore radius is seen to be in the range of 0.08 −0.16 µm for the
three cores studied at DTU. The irreducible water saturation is seen to decrease
with temperature for all three cores.
• The average value of the SSA for the bulk material was found to be 12.10 m2/g
and 23.49 m2/g for the residue. SSA showed decreasing trends with temperature
for both bulk and residue material. For the bulk material, the core from 560 m
showed an increasing trend. For the residue material, both the core from 337
m and 560 m showed increasing trends with temperature.
• For the permeability from BET, the average value was found to be 0.07 mD. It
increases with temperature and was found to be highest for the core from 463.2
m. All three cores studied at DTU showed increasing trends with temperature.
• The thickness of the irreducible water saturation in the pores was estimated to
be in the range of 0.04 −0.06 nanometer. For the three cores studied at DTU,
a decreasing trend was seen with temperature.
• The effective radius of the pores was seen to increase by 33 % for the temperature
increase to 220 ◦C. For the core from 337 m it was 50 % and for the core from
560 m it was 30 % (both of these cores were heated to 300 ◦C.
