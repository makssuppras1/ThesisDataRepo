Summary 
The study of wooden linings effect on critical conditions in enclosure fires sought to give 
an overview of the effects of having a burnable lining material. This was done through 
Fire Dynamic Simulator which uses computational fluid dynamics to solve the situation 
with computer power instead of a real building. 
The chemical reaction and energy output from burning timber are very complex concepts 
and very difficult to incorporate in a FDS simulation. This study has therefore needed to 
simplify a lot of the wood’s real reaction and be very conservative in its approach in order 
to give guiding numbers for smoke filling. 
The scenarios being tested has been a leaky room model that best matches with the 
setup of a Zukoski smoke filling in a leaky room model. Three room sizes and three initial 
fires has made the bases of models in order to test a varied dataset. 
Though the study has not reached a substantial data basis to yield any empirical smoke 
filling formular guidance, it has suggested a method of research for a more substantial 
study which can be made with this recipe. This method could yield a dataset that would 
be able to give a larger dataset for empirical formular deduction. 
The results found in the study shows that the effect of wooden lining in an enclosure fire, 
can correspond to reaching critical conditions in smoke filling of up to 28 % faster than 
without the wooden lining. Different scenarios could also show that a wooden lining had 
no effect on the smoke filling of a room based on whether it got ignited or not. The data 
has been compared to a program called Argos from DBI, which uses a two-zone 
analytical model. This shows a good correspondence between the FDS results and the 
two-zone model comparison from Argos. The largest difference between critical time for 
smoke filling between a model with and without wooden lining is 42 %, which could 
suggest that wooden lining can have a very large impact on designing fire safety 
analytically.

1. Introduction 
Fire safety of buildings is a complex topic. It mainly consists of two areas: Fire safety 
before flashover and after flashover.  
Fire safety after flashover is about structural safety and about the building not collapsing 
while rescue personnel is putting out the fire. This includes the heat degradation of 
structure strength. For example, steel structures that gets soft when exposed to heat is 
a large safety concern to the structural safety in case of fire, which often requires the 
steel to be covered in large amounts of insulation. 
 
The fire safety before flashover is the safety of occupants in evacuation in the event of 
fire before the fire fills the entire room or becomes inhospitable for the occupants. This 
evacuation strategy both have to take into account fire thermodynamics but also building 
geometry and occupant necessities. 
 
 
1.1 Fire safety before flashover 
Fire safety before consists of making sure occupants get out of the building before, they 
get exposed to negative consequences of a fire. 
This includes heat radiation requirements, toxicity requirements, prolonged heat 
radiation requirements, heat of inhaled air and the visibility through the smoke. Listed 
here are some of the requirements 
 
• 
Optical density: OD < 3.33 dB/m (in small rooms) 
• 
Radition intensity: q < 10 kW/m2 (maximum 4 seconds) 
• 
Inhalation temperature: Tinh < 60°C 
• 
Smoke free height above floor level: z > 1.6 + 0.1*H 
• 
Oxygen concentration: Coxygen > 16% 
• 
Carbon dioxide concentration: CCO2 < 4 % 
 
All of these (and more) must in principle be ensured not to be broken in order to keep 
occupants safe during evacuation. This is of course a very time-consuming task to check 
for each one of these in each project. To make the job feasible of being a fire-safety-
engineer, pre-accepted solutions have been made to make areas easier to design. This 
can for example be how large a room can be, how much the ventilation area there must 
be and how much inventory is allowed in the room in order to not create an uncontrollable 
fire. This is much faster than having to go through a complex chemical burn process and 
calculate how much oxygen (for example) will be left in the air at a certain time. 
 
1.1.1 History of accidents 
 
There is a lot of tragic histories of fires in buildings that has either had large 
consequences on human life or structural damage. Here is a list of some different fires 
around the world mainly caused by the usage of wood incorrectly.  
 
==PAGE:7==

The school fire in Chicago, USA of the Our Lady of the Angels school from 1958. The 
school could house 1600 students and there were put effort into the fire safety of the 
outside, which was made of bricks. But the inside of the building was filled with timber. 
The stairs, walls and roofs were all made of wood. Thereby made it possible for the fire 
to spread and make the fire consequential for the entire building. The fire started in the 
basement and ended up requiring a full evacuation, where 95 people did not make it out. 
[1] 
 
The London fire of 1666 was also largely very critical because of the dry wood that was 
the main building material in the city at that time. The death toll was estimated to several 
thousands. This was a fire caused largely by the fire spread through timber framed 
buildings. A fire of this severity was also one of the reasons why fire demands went 
further towards not having large amounts of burnable construction material in buildings 
close to other buildings. [2] 
 
Lastly, there was a fire at The Grand Iroquois Theatre also in Chicago in 1903 where 
scene curtains and wooden lining in the theatre hall caused 575 people to be burned 
alive in about 15 minutes. This building also had focus on fire safety in regard to not 
spreading it to other buildings by have bricks as its outside layer, but it did not have the 
same restrictions on the inside. [3] 
 
 
1.1.2 Dangerous situations but solvable 
Some newer buildings have managed to accommodate the extra safety regards needed 
for this kind of construction. Mjøstårnet is one of the most famous buildings to overcome 
the obstacles of fire safety as it is an 18-story high timber building. The structural 
members are made so large that the charring of the glulam timber protects the rest of 
the member before failure. The safety before flashover is ensured by special sprinkler 
systems and the fact that every floor was designed as its own fire cell. [4] 
 
1.2 Wooden buildings national laws 
Building projects has to follow the national laws of the country where the building is built. 
This includes fire safety demands of each specific project. These fire safety demands 
make demands for evacuation strategies, structural safety and fire spread.  
 
1.2.1 Danish building regulations 
In Danish building regulations there are rules for what surfaces are allowed in each 
situation. Any potential surface material needs a fire categorization within the European 
fire categorization standard EN 13501-1. This classifies materials in categories of 
whether they are combustible, how much smoke they develop, and whether they create 
flaming droplets and liquids. 
The combustible classification is named with A to F: 
 
• 
A1 and A2: Non-combustible 
• 
B, C and D: Ranges from limited to medium combustible 
• 
E and F: Highly combustible 
 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
8

Wood is an organic combustible material and can therefor never reach an A1 or A2 
classification. A classification of non-combustible has been tried to be achieved from 
many wood producers with coatings or impregnations. These initiatives can usually not 
maintain its integrity throughout its lifetime due to wear and tear, the wood has therefore 
not been awarded the classification of non-combustible. The common classification for 
raw wood (since there are different kinds of woods) is D. 
The smoke potential and flamining droplet classification are divided in s1 to s3 and d0 to 
d2 respectively. [5] 
 
• 
s1: No or small amount of smoke 
• 
s2: Moderate amount of smoke 
• 
s3: A lot of smoke 
 
• 
d0: No droplets 
• 
d1: Some droplets  
• 
d2: A lot of droplets 
 
Wood has a moderate amount of smoke production and no flaming droplets, meaning its 
final classification is D-s2,d0.  
 
When making pre-accepted solutions according to the Danish building regulations, inside 
surfaces must at least be B-s1,d0. If the building part is bearing or an integral part of the 
fire safety like a fire section, the demands become A2-s1,d0. Some woods can be 
modified and approved to B-s1,d0 in limited use, but generally wood is not accepted in 
these situations. There is a pre accepted solution where if the inside surfaces are made 
as minimum B-s1,d0, it is accepted if up to 20 % only lives up to D-s2,d2 as a relief to 
the demand, which includes most woods. This does not make the usage of regular 
wooden surfaces easy to apply in buildings, and this is where the issue starts which this 
report will tackle.  
 
1.3 Project goals 
This project started out with a problem in a course at Denmark Technical University 
named “Advanced building design” where a group of 10-12 students had to come as 
close as possible to real project material for a large building project at minimum 50 m 
height. Some of the students of each group was responsible for fire safety of the building 
including imagining the fire scenarios possible in the buildings. This course has run for 
several years but in the year 2021, an increased focus of sustainability made the rounds 
in this course as well. This meant that a lot of the people responsible for sustainability 
was aware of using sustainable materials as the building materials to obtain a high score 
in the DGNB system. This included the usage of wood in a lot of groups’ cases. This 
immediately presented a problem for the fire safety students as the wood is not non-
flammable as other common wall linings is, such as concrete and gypsum. 
 
As described in the sections above this usage can create fire safety issues. One of the 
measures the course would judge the fire safety was the evacuation procedure, the 
smoke filling of rooms, and whether these two factors fit together.

2. Problem of use of wood 
The problem this thesis will approach will be about the usage of indoor wooden surfaces 
in rooms. Wooden material as a lining in a room is commonly known as a fire safety 
issue as it is flammable and can therefore contribute to a building for and enhance its 
severity. 
 
2.1 Spread on surfaces 
One of the issues with usage of wood is the fact that fire spreads on the surface of wood, 
which is expected with most burnable materials, but wood is also one of the few materials 
that is also broadly used as a building material. A wooden wall can make a smaller room 
fire a spreading phenomenon and severely harm the projected safety of a building in 
comparison to using non-burnable linings of rooms. This is an issue that will not be 
tackled in this study. This study’s purpose is not to clear wood of all suspicion of all fire 
safety issues, but hopefully enlighten more of the impact of smoke production issue from 
having a burnable lining.  
 
This spread on surfaces can be calculated with formulars based off the formulars 
described in [8] but in general has an upward speed of 2.5 to 3.5 mm/s and 0.5 to 1.5

3. Theory of wood’s burnability 
3.1 Ignition 
 
Ignition of wood can vary greatly from wood sort to wood sort, and it can vary even 
between the same wood sort depending on the moisture of the air the wood is in. From 
studies [9] [9] the temperature of ignition can be found ranging between 240°C to 360°C. 
There are even studies that find chemical reactions happening in the wood at 200°C.  
The important parameter for this study is the ignition temperature as it is here the heat 
release rate begins. To be conservative this will be set to 225°C in Pyrosim.

4. Method for research 
4.1 Computational fluid dynamics setup and verifying results 
The method of research for this study will mainly consist of computational fluid dynamics 
simulation, as the study will approach different room sizes and different fire sizes to 
assess the problem. Life size experiments would be too expensive for a study of this 
size. 
 
4.2 Computational Fluid Dynamics goals and hypothesis 
Using computational fluid dynamics is a commonly used tool in the engineering industry 
for analytics of situations too large to build in life size just to test. These simulations are 
guiding and are usually setup so conservative results will appear. This is one of the goals 
for using this software: Producing a model that has no chance of underestimating the 
severity of the wood’s contribution to the smoke filling of the room. 
 
What can be expected to happen when simulating a situation like this in CFD instead of 
building it? The wooden obstacles might not have the same surface characteristics as 
wood would have.  
The roughness of the surface that can “catch” the heat in small edges in the surface is 
not there and might not show the real severity of the situation.  
The transportation of fire over the surface might not happen as severely as it would in 
reality. If the intention still is to at least not underestimate the wood, the wood should 
have higher burning values than usually 
 
 
+ Higher heat release rate  
 
+ Lower ignition temperature 
- Smooth surface characteristics 
 
- Spread within the heat creation of the material in the charring wood. 
 
When these factors are considered, the situation will hopefully not be too far from the 
real consequences. 
 
The amount of wood that should ignite will be hard to predict, as it is both a function of 
the initial fire and where the flow of the ventilation will develop. This is one of the reasons 
CFD is used in ventilation calculations too: To predict where wind flows go and where 
there might be issues. From other studies such as [11], the increased heat release rate 
corresponds to a regular developing fire that can lead to a faster flashover. The 
developing significance of the fire is not severely increased before flashover just because 
of the lining.

5. Smoke production and flow 
5.1 Soot production 
Soot is one of the main components of smoke and is an important part of the toxicity of 
smoke. Soot is the result of impure particles from an incomplete combustion. It is a mass 
of carbon particles that reacts during the combustion process. The study [12] describes 
the chemical processes very well, but in short the carbon particles react with oxygen and 
hydrogen atoms in the fuel (in this case wood) and when there is not enough oxygen to 
react, the carbon does not make a full combustion process and thereby creates impure 
particles that raises toxicity and smoke.  
The important part for this study is that it creates smoke that is much darker and harder 
to see through. 
 
The soot production from timber is a complicated case as the soot is not as clear cut as 
a pool fire. Wood releases different gases as fuel during the burning of wood. These gas 
outputs vary over time as seen on Figure 7. This means that the soot production will vary 
throughout the fire. This feature of wood’s burning theory is not implementable in FDS 
and data for it is also lacking. 
 
 
Figure 7: Gas outputs from 2 kg wooden pellets with different air flow. Left 2 m3/h, right 1.1 m3/h 
[13] 
  
From different fire lab experiments in studies as [9] [14], the soot production from wood 
can vary from 0.015 g/g to 0.02 g/g in general. 
 
The chemical reactions do not matter as much in this study, as it cannot be implemented 
in FDS other than in the burner vent reaction. This means that the burner vent will also 
be a wooden fire reaction with at soot yield of 0.015 according to [14]. 
This also means that the model will lack an amount of extra heating from the soot 
particles landing on the wooden surfaces and heating it up more. And it will not depict 
how wood can contribute to making a harmless fire with a regular amount of soot into a 
toxic fire.

5.2 Flow mechanics 
It is important to understand the flow dynamics of a fire to understand why fires are very 
difficult to predict the consequences of. Enclosure fires are generally turbulent flows 
which makes it too hard to calculate on where different temperatures will occur and what 
will get ignited next. 
 
5.2.1 Laminar flow 
Laminar flow is the predictable flow mechanic: It will continue with the same flow 
constantly as the flow can keep up with the amount of material added to the flow. This is 
the flame that has harmonic shape which can be seen on Figure 8 
 
 
Figure 8: Laminar flame [15] 
 
In a CFD perspective this flow is the most desirable as it is the flow that can yield the 
clearest answers as it does not fluctuate because of small eddies.  
 
5.2.2 Turbulent flow 
The turbulent flow is the flow that is going to be encountered in fire simulation, as it is 
much more aggressive, and the material added to the flow is too large for the 
thermodynamic flow to keep up with. This is seen on Figure 9 
 
==PAGE:21==

Figure 9: Turbulent flame [16] 
 
Eddies are the concept that occurs when the flow is too large. The free flow flows so fast 
that it creates high and low pressures close to each. On Figure 10 the low-pressure areas 
behind the building, where the wind flow is not directed, to will try to suck air into it. 
Thereby it sucks these air streams into these small swivels that is seen. In a fire, the 
flames behave the same way because of the high mass flow.

RANS is the fastest method of calculating on turbulent flows. This is because RANS 
averages the flow in a way that it almost looks like laminar flow. It averages the flow over 
time in order to save on calculation complexity. 
 
The FDS software is for fire and is therefore not working with flows that are slow enough 
to justify a RANS simulation, since too much data would get lost from it. The FDS 
software is only able to simulate LES and DNS [18] 
 
Direct numerical simulation is the simulation that is what it says: It calculates everything 
directly and does not skip or average anything. This method is meant for very heavy 
calculations where cells are generally smaller than 1 mm. This study does not have the 
computer power to handle this method. 
 
The middle ground is the Large eddy simulations which is a version of the direct 
numerical simulation which also uses the Navier-Stokes equations but with a size filter 
over it. This means that where the direct numerical simulations calculate everything, the 
large eddy simulations uses a filter to not go too in depth with the turbulence models.  
In layman terms the LES is detailed enough that it can show that the beach consists of 
multiple sand grains instead of just a giant beached shaped rock (RANS), but it is not so 
detailed that it shows the atoms in each sand grain (DNS).

6. Computational Fluid Dynamics Setup 
6.1 Scenario testing 
In order to get a representative data set, a fitting number of scenarios has to be set up 
and tested appropriately. It is known that the time to critical smoke layer height in a room 
is dependent on certain factors: Height of room, volume of room, degree of ventilation of 
the room, and the size of the fire. Therefore, the scenarios should vary in these aspects 
except for one: As the Zukoski smoke filling formular is the main reference point for this 
study, the degree of ventilation should be set as “leaking” in the most realistic way of 
CFD testing. 
 
The scenarios being tested will be of the room sizes 10x5x4 m, 12x10x5 m and, 30x20x7 
m. This is varied number of sizes that should both be able to show whether a tendency 
in a small room (10x5x4 m) also appears in a large room (30x20x7 m), and cover the 
realistic room sizes for these fire scenarios. A 10x5x4 m room is a simplified small 
apartment and a 30x20x7 room could be a larger entrance area in an office building. 
 
The size of the fires will be 200 kW, 800 kW and 2500 kW. These will represent a small 
bin fire, a wood fire and a small pool fire of gasoline. 
As the last varying input of the study, each scenario will have a model with no burnable 
surfaces, a model with a wooden surface wall close to the fire, and a model with the 
ceiling made of wood. This will yield 33 = 27 models to calculate through CFD. This is a 
small number of models in respects to create a representative dataset for tendency 
tracking, but it is the realistic option for a study running through the computer power 
available for this study.

9. Discussion of viability of study 
9.1 Verification of model through reference study 
In order to assess how credible this study and its results are, the model can be verified 
by comparing it to another study. There is a scarcity of studies about the effects of 
wooden surfaces versus rooms with non-burnable surfaces, but a study named 
Experimental Study of Time to Onset of Flashover in Classroom Size Compartment [20] 
has been made where specific situations are set up with both wooden surfaces and then 
with concrete surfaces. That study is mostly about the severity of wood in the long fire 
scenario and how it might affect the chances of a flashover. In that study it is found that 
rooms with wooden surfaces will cause the flashover in combination with a small 
methanol pool fire, while a small methanol pool fire will not cause a flashover. 
 
The way the study of [20] can be used to verify this study is by comparing the behaviour 
towards flashover and if the wood programmed in the CFD model will behave as the 
other model. The same scenario in the study will be set up in CFD and it will be seen if 
it shows the same flashover. 
Behaviour and time to flashover will be analysed and compared by the heat release rate 
throughout the fire. This is a good indicator of whether the timber of this study behaves 
as real timber on walls. The general issues of programming surface texture and other 
complex fire behaviour factors will still show a difference between the two models.

10. Conclusion of study 
10.1 Wooden linings effect 
Wooden lining of a room is still a problematic material in the fire safety aspects. The 
wood might not contribute an extreme amount to the smoke filling of the room but 
worsening every safety aspect considered in a fire. From the simulations it can concluded 
that maximum temperatures in the room have risen, there is a larger tendency for the 
fire to spread in front of other openings, and the visibility in general is worsened. 
While these effects were unknown from the start of the study, the reference case’s results 
were also unknown until simulated. There is a way of predicting the smoke layer height, 
but with simplified formulars like the Zukoski plume spread. This would not give an 
accurate result as reality does not conform to a two-zone model. Visibility, toxicity of 
smoke, and temperature radiation are also factors that cannot be predicted analytically 
yet.    
   
This means that using wooden linings are not necessarily grounds for never being able 
make them into pre-accepted solutions. Just like have a pre-accepted solution of a house 
with straw roofs, means that the building should have a certain distance to other buildings 
because it has a higher chance of spreading fire to another building, because straws are 
highly flammable. The same concept could be used for wooden lining, or just lining with 
the fire rating D-s2,d2 in general, in fire cells. The fire cells could for example have a 
smaller maximum size. In the Danish building regulations, the maximum size for a fire 
cell with one exit is 150 m2, but with wooden lining, the maximum size could for example 
be 75 m2 because of the faster room filling. That would limit the maximum distance to 
the exit and would compensate for the added risk involved with the burnable surface. 
 
The finale effect of the wood essentially means that a new fire mirrors the original fire, 
which just start from 0 kW instead of a constant fictional fire, as the initial fire used in this 
study. This effect is most clear in the medium sized room 12x10x5 m, in the added heat 
release rate curve. This is an interesting correlation that is probably based on the fact 
that the initial fires of 200 kW, 800 kW and 2500 kW is in the size range of what is 
possible to get from an initial wood fire as well. An example wooden chair has a peak 
heat release of 200 to 2000 kW as seen on Figure 41.

10.2 Guiding numbers 
The results found in this study has several areas where it is possible to point at method 
inaccuracies. This includes the discussed wood’s reaction when ignited, when the wood 
ignites, whether the CFD grid is accurate enough, whether there should have been a 
different fire development programmed to give a more a realistic fire scenario, or whether 
the smoke layer height should have been read from Pyrosim in a different way. 
 
The point of this study was to give an overview of wooden linings potential effects on the 
smoke filling of rooms, and the conclusion to that goal is that a fire in a room cannot be 
simplified into the few factors this study simplified it into: The wood could ignite in several 
different ways and create an unpredictable fire spread, it could not ignite at all and not 
contribute to any increased fire at all or it could be between those extremes. The 
conclusion to that would be to conduct more experiments and preferably in real life 
instead.  
 
Similar studies have placed their initial fire in the corner of a room so timber on two walls 
will be ignited and create the most harmful effect. This could have been the approach for 
this study, as it seeks to find the extreme severity of exposed timber. But to also make 
analysing the results easier, this study has kept it to one exposed timber wall. This way 
the results are not interrupted by each surface sending heat flux to each other. 
This might have been able to make the numbers even more critical and put the final 
guiding numbers to even more extremes. But that would have become too over the top 
for safety and would just be on the of the border of setting the fire up for a flashover test 
since the fire would be placed too conveniently to ignite everything.

At the core of the study, it has been about combining a design approach of smoke filling, 
where a fictive fire with a constant heat release rate of a peak heat release rate of a real 
object (the initial fire), is combined with the fire development of another real object (the 
wooden lining). Meaning that a fictive fire is used to make an extra real fire occur, even 
though that the object being lit on fire will never be exposed to a fire like the fictive one 
set up for it. This is a ridiculous starting point, but if the goal is to make an adjustment to 
a design method that is rather rigid setup as the Zukoski room filling is, this seemed like 
the best method to yield a fair answer for considering wood usage possible at all.  
If the design method with Zukoski had to continue as rigid as it is now, the added heat 
release rate from each graph in figure 29 to 31 could just have been added to the starting 
point of the constant heat release rate and then using Zukoski room filling. For example, 
the large room with an initial fire of 200 kW has an increase of heat release rate from the 
wood of about 1000 kW at 586 s (figure 29). Then the Zukoski room filling could be 
calculated from the beginning again with 1200 kW which would shorten the room filling 
to 307 s instead of the 510 s found in Pyrosim. This seems to be a method that would 
be too conservative and way too restrictive of design. The method is already 
conservative enough with assuming the peak heat release rate of the initial fire as 
constant.  
 
10.3 FDS and CFD calculations 
The challenge of programming wood in FDS/Pyrosim was not solved optimally for this 
study. Programming reactions to specific events in a CFD simulation is always a difficult 
task, as calibrating these reactions in reference to a real-life experiment is a task that 
does not have a good solution. The simulation will make an approximation by nature of 
it being structured in cells of a relatively large size compared to the real world, which 
“calculate” on the atomic level. This means that whatever specific event that the reaction 
is set to activate at (like an ignition reaction at a specific temperature event) will be 
mistimed, as the cells will round its answers. And when the reaction contributes to the 
specific event happening in other places (like a heat release rate reaction raising the 
temperature in the rest of the room), the mistimed first event will enhance the mistake 
and the overall picture. 
 
In order to solve this issue, a re-reaction function of the surface could be implemented 
possibly. In this case, it could be a function which would raise the ignition temperature of 
the unignited surface parts instantly after ignition of the surface. After that, the ignition 
temperature would slowly drop to the original ignition temperature over time. Thereby 
the surface would not ignite all of the surface too fast, but the ignition would still happen 
early on in the test. The important part is to hinder a chain reaction of a possible mistimed 
first reaction. This would give the fire growth a more vibrant fire growth, like the fire 
growth seen in the reference study from section 8 with real timber, instead of the 
simulation in Pyrosim which had a close to perfect and unrealistic exponential growth. 
This could have delayed the flashover possibly and the difference between the 20 and 
26 minutes might have been less with such an incorporation. This comes with a new 
downside of the incidents where the ignition should cause a fast chain reaction between 
the ignited and the unignited. 
 
==PAGE:54==

Another solution could be to divide the wooden wall into two layers: A thin charring layer 
and an underlying layer. The charring would react at approximately 200° degrees and 
could be calibrated to match the first pre-flashover of test 8 from [20] seen on Figure 42. 
The charring layer would then burn away and reveal a timber layer with an ignition 
temperature of approximately 260° that would act later and cause the flashover. This 
would require a very specific cell size, the exact size of the needed charring layers 
thickness to cause the pre-flashover ignition seen in the blue box on Figure 42 and then 
burn away at the exact time needed because of its volume. This can yield better answers 
but requires models that takes a lot longer to setup.

11. Further work 
11.1 Life size experiments 
Timber as a material is still rising in popularity and it will be more and more important to 
have results for these phenomena. Therefore, this study area will be funded more in the 
near future. This will mean that there will come larger studies where life size experiments 
can be made. The life size experiments will be much more important as they are easier 
to keep consistent and realistic. Luckily, this is already happening all around the world, 
as it is a part of the global transition to a sustainable civilization. 
The models made in this study can be widely produced by a script and results can be 
plotted neatly, but the issues discussed earlier in the report will still exists, and more will 
probably become noticeable in a broader dataset. Most of these issues will probably not 
have solutions yet for calculating analytically, but the life size experiments will not be 
disturbed by math that the world have not solved yet and failed to incorporate in CFD 
software. 
 
 
11.2 More specific data on one wood species 
Most of this study worked from scattered data of different wood species because all 
parameters for one wood species was not available. That has made this study quite 
inaccurate as it has basically been a mix and match game from the outlet. In order to get 
a more precise picture on exact consequences of wood usage, it should have been kept 
to one clear reference of one species of wood. That way a certain testing method could 
be developed, and all wood species could have their own study. Ideally, the smoke 
detriment from each wood species should have its own values and designing with them 
could become easier. But as the timber industry is hot commodity at the moment and it 
is changing at a rapid pace to keep up with demand, there will surely arise new and more 
efficient wood species that will make new and more narrow studies obsolete. This paper 
can hopefully be a sustainable paper that keeps a broad enough scope to at least see 
some further usage if the timber industry changes a lot. 
 
 
11.3 Further effects 
As written in earlier sections, there are still fire safety hazards besides the smoke filling 
that are problematic for the fire safety of a building design. This includes additional 
chemical and thermodynamic effects as well as fire spread hazards. The additional 
toxicity and radiation can be measured in experiments, but the fire spread hazards 
should be designed with a more practical approach. This could for example be if wood 
is used as lining on a wall with an emergency exit, the exit door could have a safety 
zone of non-burnable material in a certain radius around the door, so the fire has no 
chance of making the door inaccessible for occupant and no chance of spreading out 
of the door when opened. Just like bathrooms are designed with wet areas in order to 
stop water damage, a fire radius could be implemented in regulations in the same way. 
A lot of these design methods are already being worked on and considered for 
regulations, but the innovation should be sped up by more studies on the subject of 
green material usage.
