Abstract
Digital Twin technology is becoming more and more pertinent. Implementations have
started at various speeds across different industries. Digital models are common today.
There are however still missing pieces in determining the best way to transition from dig-
ital models into digital twins. One problem is that the digital models are too large and
computationally expensive. They need to run faster but with the same accuracy. This
can be accomplished with Reduced Order Models (ROMs).
Pump producers could benefit greatly from digital twin technologies. Monitoring and pre-
dicting behaviours of a pump can give operational customer insights. These insights can
be used in the product development phase and lead to new revenue streams.
This project investigates methods to generate ROMs. The methods used are available
through ANSYS or with common engineering tools. There are several methods to gen-
erate ROMs. Multiple have been investigated in the report, but only two are useful. An-
alytical ROMs and DesignXplorer Response Surfaces are successfully made. They are
compared to the original numerical model. Problems and opportunities when making
ROMs will be presented in the report.
A digital twin is implemented in TwinBuilder. It is a good software for working with the
ROMs. From the implementation an overview is made to test the critical functions of the
pump. The digital twin was tested for several load cases and the results matched the
benchmarks to a satisfying level.
The overall findings of the project conclude that response surfaces is a good method to
use, but they can be difficult to produce. Simple models tend to make better response
surfaces. Analytical ROMs are difficult to produce. Their reliability works according to
the level of compliance to the assumptions and boundary conditions. There are other
ROM technologies that are outside the scope of this project. Comparing to them would
be necessary before ranking the best methods for different applications.
The project also concludes that generating a digital twin is possible and could be used
with advantage today in various industries.

Sammendrag
The following is a translation of the abstract into danish.
Digital Tvillinge teknologi bliver hele tiden mere og mere aktuelt. Der er mange projekter
som har opnået forskellige grader af implementeringer. Digitale modeler er normale i
dag. Der mangler dog viden om, hvordan man bedst bevæger sig fra digitale modeler til
en digital tvilling. En af problemerne er at digitale modeler er for tunge at køre, når man
skal vide ting øjeblikkeligt. En måde at løse problemet på er, at lave reduserede modeller.
Pumpe producenter kan have stor gavn af digital tvillinge teknologi. Ved at overvåge og
forudse pumpens opførsel kan producenten få viden om kundens brugsmønstre. Denne
viden kan bruges i udviklingsfasen og lede til nye salgsmuligheder.
Projektet undersøger metoder til at skabe reduserede modeller. Metoder er tilgængelige
igennem ANSYS eller med basale ingeniør kundskaber. Der er flere forskellige metoder
til at skabe reduserede modeller. Flere er blevet undersøgt, men kun to har været brug-
bare for dette projekt. Analytiske reduserede modeller og DesignXplorer Respons Flader
var mulige at lave. De bliver sammenlignet med hinanden og med den originale simu-
lering. Problemer og muligheder ved at lave reduserede modeller vil blive præsenteret i
rapporten.
En digitale tvilling er implementeret i ANSYS TwinBuilder. Det er et godt værktøj til at
arbejde med de skabte reduserede modeller. Fra implmenteringen kunne der skabes et
godt overblik, der tjekkede alle de kritiske funktioner for pumpen. Den digitale pumpe
tvilling var testet for mange forskellige lasttilfælde og resultaterne passer tilfredstillende
overens med andre test resultater.
Overordnet kan det konkluderes, at DesignXplorer Respons Flader er en brugbar teknologi,
men det anerkendes også, at de kan være svære at producere. Simple modeller giver
bedre Respons Flader. Analytiske reduserede modeller er svære at lave. De er mest
gyldige for simple tilfælde og i overensstemmelse med deres randbetingelser og an-
tagelser. Der er andre ROM metoder som ikke var med i dette projekt. Før de bedste
metoder kan bestemmes for forskellige anvendelser skal de andre metoder undersøges.
Projektet konkluderer også at digital tvillinger er mulige at skabe i dag, og at de med fordel
kan bruges i mange industrier.

Preface
I was first introduced to digital twin technology during a short internship in EDR&Medeso
in Norway. I was intrigued by the technology, so I read more about it, and it sparked my
interest. Upon moving to Denmark I applied for a summer position in the danish office of
the company. I was accepted as a summer employee and introduced to a specific case
of generating a digital twin of a wind turbine. During the two months, I managed to create
a digital twin of the wind turbine.
For this project I wanted to use the same software that I was introduced to during the initial
project. I found that there is a lot of basic work to be done in preparing the simulation
models to enter the interconnected simulation network, that proceeds the digital twin.
A motivation for working with this topic was to gain a deeper understanding of digital mod-
els, and what it takes for them to become digital twins. The first project was limited by a
short deadline. I had to provide everything from sensor data/hardware and the mechan-
ical models to the deployment files and the graphical dashboard in just two months. By
narrowing the scope for this project, I hope there will be more time to delve into the topic
and gain a deep understanding of the digital models, which will be necessary to push the
technology forward.
Phillip Krogstrup Kjeldsen
Copenhagen, 2020

1.1
Digital Twin Technology
The context of digital twin technology will be introduced in this section along side remarks
on applications. A more technical definition of what comprises a digital twin will be given
in section 2.1.
1.1.1
The importance of products
Companies provide products. The products can be a broad variety of services, processes,
goods or experiences. All companies have a vision and a purpose to carry out by deliv-
ering their products. The ideal situation for any company is to know its value proposition
perfectly. Most of the time however, there is limited understanding of how the product is
received, used or how it fails. Understanding a product takes many years, and even large
companies can struggle to find the reason for failures in a new line of products or one
they have been selling for years. Even small changes in the supply chain can spread and
change the product through negative synergy and create new failure modes. If compa-
nies do not understand their products, they are behind the competition and they will not
be able to stay relevant to their customers’ needs.
For a company to stay in business, they should strive to understand their product fully.
That is regardless of whether they are offering a software program, a health service, a
manufacturing process or a machine. An obstacle is, that it is surprisingly difficult to
know a product in full. Over the years most companies grow an intuitive understanding
of certain aspects of they products, but not everything is clear or easily measured. So
to increase experience and understanding, simulation tools can be used to see how the
product behaves in different environments.
1.1.2

difficult or impossible to measure. Simulation tools, regardless of their nature, have an

be used in the simulation? Which inputs can give the most information? How are the
outputs presented and how are they validated and verified?
Simulations can be used to apply specific technological knowledge to systems or com-
ponents in order to understand how to engineer them. Simulations allow the future to

1.1.3
Models and Data
Two simulation types are common today. The model-driven simulation and the data-driven
simulation. Traditionally, simulations are built on physical models, which has been the
most popular among engineers for decades. A data-driven approach is becoming more
popular as the world is producing and recording more data.
Model-driven simulations is the classical method of working with a physical system that is
known from models like Hooke’s law, Fourier’s law, etc. When a simulation of a system is
wanted, the model-driven approach doesn’t require the user to have available data (ex-
cept material properties, load scenarios, etc), because the predictions are made from the
theoretical background of the model. The models are usually transparent and the rela-
tionships between parameters are easily detectable. The models however can be difficult
to formulate. It requires knowledge about the physical system, as well as mathematical
equations, to describe the behaviour. Models quickly become very complex, if they are to
incorporate several deviations from the simple, general case. They work really well within
their assumptions, but can become inaccurate if the assumptions or boundary conditions
break down. This is usually the case for specific and complex simulations.
Data-driven simulation is a new approach fuelled by sensor data and information. It re-
quires less understanding of the system and the actual problem, as the data will give away
all insights, that are necessary. The data-driven approach can generate correlations be-
tween A and B, that cannot be seen by the analyst. Some algorithms are more effective
than others. The data-driven method can require a lot of data. Data-driven simulations
are very good at accommodating for high system complexities, but are usually very locally

applicable. A data set is usually divided into a learning set and a validation set to evaluate
the performance of the analysis.
When deciding which approach to take the following questions can be considered: What
is the price of generating the data and how long will it take? What value does the data
add compared to the theoretical model? Is the theoretical boundary conditions plausible?
Is the theory already available or should it first be developed?
For example, it is desired to construct a bridge using data-driven simulations. It is ex-
pensive and dangerous to build several bridges to see when and why they fail based on
different parameters. After enough trial and error, the right parameters can be identified.
A theoretical approach would seem more reasonable here. Data, however, can also come
from simulations. This requires a certain maturity level of the theoretical simulations. They
would need to be verified and validated. If the simulations are valid, then the experiments
can be carried out at a much lower price.
1.1.4
Industry Driven Digital Twin
Digital Twins was first mentioned in 2002 by Dr. Michael Grieves at a conference to
promote a Product Life-cycle Management (PLM) Center in Michigan. Since then, the
term has been used by many to illustrate different aspects of virtual representations of
physical assets. The digital twin Grieves presented would provide interesting insights into
the asset, e.g. operational data, remaining lifetime, performance, usage, etc. Regardless
of whether the physical twin is located closely or remotely, the digital twin can be deployed
to an IoT platform and accessed from anywhere. The digital twin would be a high-fidelity
interconnected digital model with real-time sensor data from a physical twin. Ideally one
should be able to learn everything about the physical twin just from looking at the digital
twin.
Previously, digital simulations changed the way engineers work and approach problems.
Digital twin technology utilizes the simulations and could change the way engineers work
in the future (Grieves 2016).
A motivation for companies to focus on this technology is to gain product insights that
will lead to a better value proposition and an increase in market share and competitive
advantage. The company with the largest market share will gain the most knowledge.
This can reinforce itself, as the competitors will not be able to gain the same insights.
Some companies are also looking into changing their business model. Rolls-Royce are
famous for their innovate business model of ”power-by-the-hour”, where the airlines would
pay Rolls-Royce for the hours they were using their engines. It gave the airlines less to
worry about and Rolls-Royce could focus on making the engine work as long as possible,
while keeping maintenance costs to a minimum.
One issue with new technologies is that it has not been tested before. So details about the
implementations, know-how, when and where it makes sense, is not clear yet. It might not
be worth it to make a digital twin for a small and insignificant problems. The application
is far clearer in large machinery with long spare-part delivery time and expensive down-
time.

1.4
Problems and Digital Twin Solutions
Digital twin solutions can alleviate several problems that are currently difficult to solve.
In this section a few examples will be given of current problems, that can be solved with
digital twin solutions. The following items apply mostly to manufacturing companies, but
not exclusively.
1.4.1
Current Potentials
Manufacturers continuously spend time to understand their products and test them thor-
oughly, before they leave the factory. They have a lot of test setups, where they test the
products in many different aspects. The data is used for optimizing the model or the next
generation of products.
Manufacturers also focus on simulation driven development in the design phase of the
product. Products are expensive to test or for the early designs, engineers are simulating
Creating a Digital Twin of a Pump during the in-operandi situation
5
==PAGE:26==
load cases to make sure the products can withstand certain requirements. These can be
legislative requirements or their own internal requirements.
In regards of monitoring, there are local data driven monitoring systems. A sensor (e.g.
vibration) can recognize predefined patterns, and can predict which type of damage has
occurred. This solution can only diagnose based on symptoms and cannot predict future
events. That is one of the key values that the manufacturer could gain from digital twin
technology. The data used for these monitoring systems have been measured through
damaging a product and recording the response. This can be an expensive approach.
Off-site monitoring requires a lot of sensors, because a sensor is needed for every param-
eter. The amount of sensors needed for this solution can be greatly reduced with digital
twin solutions.
Manufacturers can experience failure during warranty. Manufacturers usually test their
products quite extensively, and can have a really high manufacturing quality. So they
can suspect that the fault is not theirs, but the customers’. The customers may have
been using the product out of specifications or had an accident. Customers can be given
the benefit of the doubt and provided with a new product, but this is expensive for the
manufacturer. If it can be documented that the customer is at fault, they don’t have to
give them a new product. Having a complete data history for a digital twin will solve this
problem. As a bonus the manufacturer would know exactly what happened, and how to
improve the product weaknesses.
Knowing the state of a product is very difficult without knowing the exact lifetime loads
and simulating the corresponding effects. Customers want advice on whether or not the
products can continue in use after overload. It is difficult to say how much of the expected
lifetime is lost. Normally products are designed to work within certain limits. If the limits
are crossed, then most of the data the manufacturer has is irrelevant. With a digital twin
based on the overload, the manufacturer would be able to make decisions on whether or
not the product should keep running and for how long.
This also applies to products that during manufacturing have received a defect. A digital
model could simulate the lifetime cycles before fatigue. If every failure aspect is consid-
ered, the product could be sold at half price or less depending on the lifetime they can
warrant. The continued monitoring of the product will allow the customer to feel safe about
the purchase.

2.1
Digital Twin Terminology
The digital twin (DT) concept was first introduced in 2002 by Dr. Michael Grieves. It was
presented at the University of Michigan during the formation of a Product Life-cycle Man-
agement (PLM) center. The focus on PLM implies that this link would exist throughout the
lifetime of the product. The digital twin concept captures the intentions of the industry 4.0
revolution: That manufacturing, operations and services can move from function-centric
to product-centric (Grieves 2016) (Grieves 2019).
The definition of a digital twin can differ depending on the source of the definition. Digital
twins can alleviate several business pains. Therefore the sources can have a variety of
focuses, requirements and inquiries. There is subsequently a need to define a terminol-
ogy through a literature review. Following is a list of relevant terms, their definitions and
applications.
2.1.1
Digital Twin (DT)
In the beginning Grieves called the concept a Mirrored Spaces Model, which illustrates
the connectivity between the physical and digital twin. The two systems constantly mirror
the changes in the other system with a two-way communication.
A digital twin is a representation of a physical manufactured product, that is fully described
digitally. Ideally any information that could be obtained from inspecting a physical man-
ufactured product, can be obtained from its digital twin and vice versa. This includes
information from micro atomic level to macro level. The digital twin would be linked to the
physical twin for the entirety of the product lifetime as a separate entity and also into the
recycling phase of the product.
According to (Mamasoula 2019) a digital twin is the digital representation of a produc-
t/asset/production line that, through key enabling technologies such as big data analytics,
internet of things, sensors, simulation etc., gives the real-time status of its physical coun-
terpart.
This sums up the definition for this project case of a pump during the in-operandi case,
but does not include a two-way communication.
A digital twin is, for many applications, where simulation meets the real world. A digital
twin can exist without simulations. A key purpose of simulations is to tell us things that
we do not want to, or cannot, measure. So it is a natural implementation in the digital twin
technology, which is also a tool in measuring/supervising things remotely (because we do
not want to/can not measure them on site).
Grieves points out that digital twins have to main functions; interrogative and predictive.
The interrogative function is knowing how the asset is performing right now. The predictive
gives predictions of the future. Examples of interrogative knowledge can be energy usage
Creating a Digital Twin of a Pump during the in-operandi situation

==PAGE:36==
or current loads. Predictive knowledge can be how the asset will perform tomorrow given
the weather or current state. It can also be used for remaining life time estimations, when
considering fatigue.
Subgroups of a digital twin are digital twin instances (DTI), digital twin prototypes (DTP),
digital twin aggregates (DTA), digital shadows (DS) and digital models (DM). These terms
will be defined below. The first three are definitions based on application and the last two
are definitions based on the level of integration. With the integration definitions follows
additional requirements for a digital twin (Grieves 2016).
2.1.2
Digital Twin Prototype (DTP)
A digital twin prototype is made before the actual physical twin is built. It can be seen as
an ultra-high-fidelity multi-physics simulation with detailed geometries of the component.
It assists in designing the physical twin by modelling the effects of pseudo-real loads. This
type of digital twin is the first that is made during the R&D phase.
With a DTP, the synergy of loads can easily be analyzed instead of the cumbersome
task of making individual strength considerations, which might have contradiction strength
requirements. The DTP also gives the user a chance to know of some of the unexpected
failure modes that are present. It is common to learn more about the failure modes of
the product in testing the physical prototypes, but this can be done at a much lower price
digitally.
2.1.3
Digital Twin Instance (DTI)
A digital twin instance is a virtual copy of an actual physical asset. The information in the
DTI is unique to that physical asset. Information contained by the DTI varies between
industry and application, but could contain tolerances from the manufacturing processes,
a bill of materials, defects in the material/geometry, load history, etc. It can contain a list of
all components, previous components, a service record with sensor data current or past
and future predictions. During the manufacturing, the specific tolerances and features
should be added in order to have a real DTI, that is connected and mirroring the physical
asset.
The DTI can be used for predicting damages and monitoring a specific physical asset.
This could be done by factoring in things like the weather, and schedule future operations
based on the expected performance. This will automate controls and help optimise the
utilization of a component.
A DTI is linked to its physical asset for the entire lifetime and should also be used in the
last part of a products life-cycle. It will for instance ease the recycling, if the materials are
known. At the end of the lifetime, the DTI should have all its data transferred to a data
base for developing the future generations.
2.1.4
Digital Twin Aggregate (DTA)
A digital twin aggregate is a collected set of digital twins of all the produced assets (DTIs).
It can be useful when generating an overview to look at tendencies and general problems
of the product. It is not an independent data structure, but is relying on all the DTIs to
send their information. DTAs allow more tools like machine learning and big data analysis
to enter into the manufacturing decision making. DTA can be used by interrogating DTIs
to predict the behavior of a DTI based on the collective behaviour.

tions rely on the level of integration and automation of the data flow. There are according

Centrifugal Pumps
A pump is a mechanical device used, when transporting liquids or gasses (fluids). The
purpose of the transport can be to move heat, to supply energy or to apply pressure to
a system. There are usually moving parts, that mechanically move the fluids through a
guided path or direction.
There are many types of pumps for several applications and many producers of pumps
worldwide. This project revolves around a Grundfos CR pump. It is a centrifugal pump,
that uses a centrifuge to accelerate the water and generate the pressure, that moves the
fluid. The main component is the impeller which accelerates the fluid. The CR pump is a
vertical inline multistage pump. Multi-stages are used to increase the pressure rise over
the pump. Figure 2.2 illustrates, how the several levels of impellers feed each other and
increase the pressure. The CR pump is used in washers, cooling, air conditioning, water
treatment systems, fire extinguisher, boiler feed systems and other industrial systems.
The pump is good for corrosive fluids, because the hydraulic parts are made of stainless
steel or titanium (Grundfos 2020b).

Components
There are several components that make up a pump, e.g. motor stool, base plate, sleeve,
cover, impeller and pretension bolts. The last four are interesting for the project and will
be explained or simulated in the subsequent chapters. To assist the explanation, a few
examples of part requirements will be given.
Sleeve
The sleeve holds the maximum pressure by being the outer barrier between the atmo-
spheric pressure, and the internal pressure generated through the multi-stages of im-
pellers. The sleeve also acts as a contact point for the covers. The covers and the sleeve
defines the pressure chamber as a cylinder with two lids. The axial stress, hoop stress
and fatigue stress in the sleeve should not exceed or approach certain thresholds.
Cover
The cover is also holding pressure and is subject to the same pressure load as the sleeve.
The connection between the cover and the sleeve must be tight, in order to hold the
pressure. Yield stress is also a concern for the cover due to critical stress regions were
high stress can occur at geometrical details. The cover has a complex geometry, which
can give stress concentrations.
Impeller
The impeller accelerates the water. The fluid is sucked in through the center of the impeller
and pushed out through the blades as seen in fig. 2.3. The blades between the hub and
shroud transfer energy and velocity to the fluid to transport it.
Impellers can also be axial. The two impeller types have significant differences in inlet and
outlet diameters. The radial is best at generating a high pressure and low flow, whereas
the axial delivers high flow, but low pressure. Impellers have different amounts of blades.
The optimal amount of blades, depends on the size of the solid particles in the fluid.
Removing the shroud (bottom) can also be useful, when pumping fluids with debris, but
it lowers the efficiency (Grundfos 2020b).
The impeller is welded. The weld has a stress concentration, that makes it a point for fa-
tigue, along with other weld circumstances. The loads on the impeller come from the rota-

Pretension Bolts
Four pretension bolts hold the motor stool to the cover that holds the sleeve, as seen in
fig. 3.1. Appropriate bolt pretension is important, as the system is not pressure tight if
the pretension is to low. The pretension is adjusted according to the expected working
pressure, as this is the main force that it has to counteract. The pressure will try to take
the covers off of the sleeve, and the pretension bolts will hold them to the sleeve.
For the CR pump the pressures can be high, and the bolts can experience plastic defor-
mation during high loads. This can come from high working pressures, high pretension
values or high liquid temperatures that expand the pump housing. This will loosen the
pretension force.
If the sleeve temperature subsequently drops, then the pretension can lose the required
tension force. The connection between the sleeve and cover might not be tight enough.
This can cause the sleeve to slide between the covers. The torques that the cover has to
resist are the full-load-torque of the engine while running, and the breaking torque while
starting up. The breaking torque is higher than the full-load-torque.

Understanding the problems of a pump and the typical failures, are important in order to
know what to monitor. Some pumps run for long continuous periods, others are started
and stopped quite often. It depends on the function of the pump and the owners usage.
The problems relevant for the case is presented below.
Fluid temperature
The temperature of the fluid pumped influences the length of the sleeve, which changes
the stress in the bolts and the cover. The temperature can also change the shaft angle
and decrease the lifetime of shaft bearings. The digital twin should take angle thresholds
into consideration.

The running pressure is the parameter that determines what pretension load should be
applied. The pretension should contain the working pressure inside without plastic defor-
mation. If the pressure is too high, then the pretension values should be adjusted or the
pump shut down. The pressure is monitored constantly to see if it matches the pretension.
High pressure can also cause the shaft to bend and damage the bearings.

Material Models
Deformation hardening happens on a microscopical scale due to dislocations in the crystal
lattice structure. The deformation of the grain boundaries increases the resistance of
deformation and the strength of the material. The deformations are immediately stopped
by the increase in strength. It is the explanation for the stress interval between the yield
stress σy and the ultimate tensile stress σu.
The small micro-deformations are however followed by macro-deformations of the geom-
etry, called necking. The cross-area shrinks and the specimen weakens. Subsequently
the geometry change becomes too large and the stress-strain curve goes down on the
other side of the ultimate tensile stress (Olhoff and Nielsen 2010).

2.4
Fatigue Models
Structures can break at loads that are smaller than the ultimate tensile stress, if loaded
several times. This phenomenon is called material fatigue. It is caused by a cyclic loading
of the structure where the stress is lower than the ultimate tensile stress σu.
The fatigue failure happens after a certain number of load cycles, where the stress fields
has been oscillating between tension and compression or different tensile stress levels.
After a while a crack is initiated which will grow with each cycle. The larger the crack be-
comes, the larger the crack propagation speed as the stress concentration factor changes.
Lastly the construction will experience a brittle fracture, as the stress concentration gets
to large in the remaining material. Apart from the stress field other factors like corrosion,
welding and surface treatments can change the fatigue properties of a construction (Einar
Bardal 2004).
Fatigue fractures can happen suddenly and without warning. The stresses causing fatigue
are below the ultimate tensile strength, and often also below the yield strength. Growing
cracks can be detected by using proper maintenance tools.
For fatigue it is more relevant to look at the principal stress than the equivalent stress. The
equivalent stress is used more in plasticity. The principal stress also reveal whether the
stresses are in tensile or compression. Tensile stress will open the cracks and be worse
from a fatigue point of view.
Fatigue can be characterised in three phases:
1. Crack initiation
2. Crack propagation
3. Complete fracture
The cracks can be initiated at the surface, at geometries with stress concentrations or
internal material errors. Once the crack is initiated fatigue lifetime decreases significantly.
Usually the crack initiation is the most time-consuming phase. Improving the surface
quality or avoiding stress concentrations, can prolong the fatigue life significantly.
In order to prevent failures of mechanical structure there are two strategies. The con-
struction can be built to endure an unlimited number of cycles and have infinite life. The
structure can also be build to withstand a calculated amount of cycles. The status of the
fatigue failure is monitored or a safe failure design can be implemented. This could be a
pressure vessel that cannot explode, but only leaks. The damage can then be repaired or
the structure changed (Olhoff and Nielsen 2010) (Michael Ashby and Cebon 2013). The
two strategies will be presented briefly and some considerations mentioned.
2.4.1
Finite Lifetime
Designing for a finite lifetime must include safety considerations. When the design breaks,
it must do as little damage as possible around it. Several techniques can be used to limit
the damage done in case of failure, called fail-safe designs. The focus here will be on
monitoring when the failure happens.
If infinite lifetime design is not possible or feasible, then the design is made to last a
certain number of cycles. The amount of cycles a specimen can handle can be determined
experimentally by making S-N curves of the material.

2.5
Modeling
A model is a representation of a physical system, that can show the responses of the sys-
tem to certain influences. When a mathematical model has been developed, it can easily
be duplicated with different parameters. A holistic intuition of the problem can quickly be
obtained.
The model representations can take many forms and be more or less accurate. No matter
what type of mathematical approach an engineer chooses, the mathematical models will
always be representations and approximations of the physical problem. Normally two
main categories are considered: Analytical and numerical models (Hattel 2005).
2.5.1
Analytical
Analytical models are usually based on simple mathematical models. Developing the
analytical equations require expert knowledge and can be time consuming. The mathe-
matical solutions are exact and the computational time usually low. Analytical solutions
usually have assumptions and restrictions, that can limit how exact they are, compared
to the physical problem. The assumptions are formulated during the development of the
equations. The mathematical solutions are exact, but if the application to the physical
problem is not good it doesn’t add value. Assumptions can be uniform deformation, ho-
mogeneous material parameters, isotropic behaviours, etc. Analytical models can also

2.5.2
Numerical
Numerical model are based on complex equations and give approximate mathematical
solutions. Numerical methods make guesses, and evaluates the solution or change in
solution from time step to step time. It is not possible to obtain a solution at a given time,
without running the previous time steps. Even though the numerical models have an ap-
proximate mathematical solution, they are usually more physically correct than analytical
solutions, as the assumptions and constraints are less restrictive and easier to work with.
Complex geometries and boundary conditions are also easier to work with by discretiza-
tion/meshing. Numerical methods are often restricted by the computational power and
memory available.

2.5.4
Errors and Residuals
A data-driven approach to make a model is to use a regression analysis. If a data set is
given, a regression analysis can be performed on the data set by assuming that there is
a polynomial fit. There is most likely a statistical distribution in the data set, so even with
a perfect model there will some errors, or differences, between the measured points and
the model value in the independent variable. A regression can be made numerically by
guessing a fit, and then calculating the differences in the measured points and the model.
The differences are called residuals. As the numerical method tries other polynomials, the
residuals will approach the value of error. When the residuals are equal to the error, the
model doesn’t get better. The iterative process can also be stopped when the difference
between two time step residuals is below a threshold.
An example of a regression fit of a first order linear model to a data set is given in fig. 2.12.
A linear model is given by y = a · x + b. The distance between the data points and the
real model is the error and the estimated model is the residual.

2.7
Design of Experiments
Generating a data-driven ROM requires knowledge of the relationship between input and
outputs. This can be achieved by running the model several times for different inputs
and recording the output as a Design of Experiments (DoE). When generating a DoE, the
different input parameters can be placed to optimise the resolution of the parameter at
either the middle or the edge of the space . There are several ways to do this, but two will
be presented below (ANSYS 2019).
2.7.1
Central Composition Design
Central Composite Design (CCD) is a three- to five-level design, which searches the limits.
It makes sure to have some data points with only one varying parameter. This is done
to counteract noise effects, but can also be one of the downsides of this design. The
rotatable design is preferred, because the prediction variance is the same for any two
locations that are the same distance from each other. An example of the five-level sample
distribution of a CCD DoE is given in fig. 2.13.
2.7.2
Optimal Space-Filling Design
Optimal Space-Filling design (OSF) is a way to construct the DoE. OSF is a kind of random
distribution, but with a memory to avoid clustering of design points. OSF is similar to a
Latin Hypercube Sampling.
Computer DoEs are not subject to the same kind of noise as regular physical DoEs. There-
fore can OSF be utilized, in stead of CCD, to obtain a greater resolution across the pa-
rameter span with fewer points. Some of the setbacks of the DoE type is that the corners
of the design space can go uncovered and rely on extrapolation. An example as an OSF
sample distribution can be seen in fig. 2.14.

3.1.1
Geometry, loads and materials
In the pretension simulation half of a pump house is designed. Bodies in the simulation
are base plate, bottom cover, sleeve, top cover, motor stool and two pretension bolts
fastened on the base plate and motor stool. The base plate and motor stool transfer the
pretension force to the bottom and top cover that compresses the sleeve. When pressure
is applied it will push the covers away from the sleeve, but the pretension bolts will hold
it.
The materials defined are from Grundfos and have elastic and plastic properties to capture
permanent deformation. The material models are bilinear isotropic hardening material
models.
The bolts serve to keep a pressure on the gaskets holding the water. The bolts are loaded
with a pretension in the first load step and locked for the rest of the simulation. All loads
are applied as table values and can change between each time step.
The static loads in the simulation are displacements along the symmetry plane of the
pump to make sure that they don’t move.
Forces are applied four places: at the inlet pipe, the outlet pipe, the shaft opening at the
top cover and the shaft hole in the bottom cover. They are to compensate for the pressure
not applied in those holes.
Pressure are applied two places. One pressure is applied at the inlet pipe. The other is
applied at the outlet pipe and the entire internal part of the sleeve. The pressure on the
inside of the sleeve is the same as on the outlet pipe, due to the flow path.
Thermal loads are applied two places. A thermal load is applied to the bolts, assuming
that the pump is in a machine room with an elevated temperature. Another thermal load
is applied to the pump, assuming that it is pumping hot fluid.
All the forces and pressures come from the same source: the fluid pressure.
The numerical model has several inputs. They can be combined into four inputs. The
pressure, the original pretension and the two temperatures. All the original inputs for the
numerical model can be calculated from these four values. By reducing the number of
inputs, the model becomes easier to make into a ROM.

Reduced Order Models
Numerical full order models easily become large and expensive to run. For DT technology,
the models need to run quasi real-time, so the computational time must come down. There
are a number of ways to reduce the computational time of a numerical model.
An important part of reducing the computational runtime, is to preserve the accuracy of
the model. FEA is computationally heavy, so all the methods investigated for this project,
has gone away from FEA. Instead theory or data-driven models have been used. The
numerical FEA has been used to generate the data for the data-driven models.
Methods and techniques to generate ROMs will be introduced and presented in this chap-
ter. After the techniques have been introduced, the ROMs produced for the project will
be introduced. This will be followed by a comparison of the results.
Choices were made in order to simplify the amounts of inputs for the different models.
The numerical models for instance, had many load objects in Mechanical. Most of the
loads depended on the pump pressure, and varied only because of differences in pro-
jected areas. Other parameters like the room temperature, was assumed constant and is

4.1.1
Analytical Solutions
Analytical solutions are analytically developed models. These are based on physical prin-
ciples and have a high level of generalization. The analytical models are often very good
solutions to simple problems. Hoop stress, one-dimensional tensile stress and heat trans-
fer in a plate are examples of simple models, that can be used with a very high degree of
accuracy.
Analytical models usually take a fraction of the time to process and run, but the develop-
ment of the models can take a long time. Developing the models require expert knowledge
within a number of fields to overview all possible sources of error. The models are subject
Creating a Digital Twin of a Pump during the in-operandi situation
49
==PAGE:70==
to more choices from the developer. If the choices are not completely accounted for or
have been made wrongly, it will damage the integrity of the model.
The analytical models often rely on assumptions that remove degree of accuracy, com-
pared to the real life problem. The assumptions and boundary conditions are an important
part of the analytical models. If the models do not comply to the assumptions and bound-
ary conditions, it is probably not precise. For most of the analytical models, it can be very
difficult to ensure, that the conditions around the physical system live up to the require-
ments from the assumptions. In simple applications, where the boundary conditions are
valid, the analytical models can provide high accuracy, and eliminate the need to make
expensive numerical calculations.
Developing an analytical model is usually done in steps. A developer can start with a
drawing to overview the geometry, and mark where the loads are applied. The loads can
then be added, and the unknowns that are being solved for. The relevant theory should
be included to describe the physics in the model. The developer will have to choose
how complex the physics should be described. The number of parameters and solver
method must be chosen. The theory, geometry and loads bring boundary conditions and
assumptions. These assumptions and boundary conditions should be kept in mind, as
they also define the boundaries of application. When the physical equations are formu-
lated, they can be solved for the unknowns. The results can be verified or validated with
other methods or experiments.
4.1.2
State-Space Matrix
When making a State-Space matrix representation, the system in question can also be
sketched, and the equations describing the behaviour determined.
These equations
should be transformed into a system of linear equations, that can be collected as the
A, B, C and D matrices, for the state space representation. With these matrices defined,
other input and state vectors can be used as input, and the dynamic system can be de-
scribed. Assumptions and boundary conditions should also be followed closely, as this
method is subject to the same limitations as the physical models used.
In ANSYS Mechanical, this is a method to characterise continuous linear time-invariant
systems. The state-space representation is described by the calculation of the matrices
for the system. The method can however only be used in a Modal Analysis to find displace-
ments during the oscillations of a structure. The method requires writing an APDL com-
mand snippet with commands and a few settings. The APDL command used is SPMWRITE.
There are requirements to the solver choice and added damping, forces and degrees of
freedom. The command writes a .spm file, that can be imported into Twin Builder with
inputs and outputs.
Since the method is only available for modal analysis systems, which are not used in this
project, the method has not been used. A state space matrix can however, be imported
into Twin Builder without the use of Mechanical. If the user generate the A, B, C and D
matrices by hand, they can be imported into Twin Builder and used as a ROM.

4.1.4
DesignXplorer: Response Surface
DesignXplorer is a parametric study possible in ANSYS Workbench. The drag and drop
workflow is the same as for the 3D ROM, and it connects to the parameter set automati-
cally.
The response surface generation is a data-driven approach to predict outputs based on
inputs. It uses a design of experiments (DoE) of the parameters in the analysis system
(e.g. Mechanical). The parameters are chosen in the analysis system and defined in
Parameter set in Workbench. The parameters are defined with upper and lower bounds.
When performing the DoE, these bounds are used for defining the valid ROM space. New
parameters can also be defined based on other parameters, e.g. angles can be calculated
from displacements.
The DoE is designed based on settings available through the Workbench canvas. The
type of DoE can be chosen from a set of options. Workbench generates the design points
automatically. More points can be added by setting the DoE type to custom and manually
writing design points. Manually generating more design points can be useful for optimi-
sation of the valid ROM space. Workbench can also add more points automatically, until
a certain statistical error thresholds is acquired. The threshold can relate to the reliability
of the response surface or the error of predicted values.
The design points are calculated using the same solver and software used for the regular
analysis, and the computational time is accordingly. After the computations, the design
points have inputs and outputs available for each design point. The data can be analysed
using a set of different response surface types. DesignXplorer creates a response surface
of each output parameter based on all the design points. The methods for generating
response surfaces are Genetic Aggregation, Kriging etc.
For verification of the model, DesignXplorer also makes a response surface without one
design point to calculate the error. This is a small verification set compared to other data-
driven approaches. Normally the learning set is approximately two thirds, and the rest is
used for verification or validation.
The results are presented in a Goodness of Fit diagram with other statistical data. The
response surfaces can then be exported as an .fmu file The FMU is exported as a Mod-
elExchange 2.0 (ANSYS 2019).

5.4.1
Pretension values
The pretension values are checked in two load steps. Load step 9 is evaluated against the
breakaway torque from the start up. Load step 10 is evaluated against the sliding torque
while the pump is running. The pretension values are multiplied with a friction coefficient
and the radius of the pump housing to evaluate if they are below the two torques. The
tests are seen in fig. 5.13.
MUL1 and MUL3 in the figure receives the pretension forces from the pretension response
surface and calculates the torque. The pretensions are shown in the south west graph
along the pretension force from the analytical solution. The torques are seen in the graph
in the south east corner. The torques are then sent to the COMPARATOR block. If the
value is below an undisclosed threshold, the pretension is too low. The thresholds will not
be revealed, but the safety margins for load step nine is 1.5 - 2 throughout the simulation.
The safety margin for load step 10 is above 5 for the simulation. The values compare

Discussion
In this chapter the results and methods will be discussed. Attempts will be made to answer
why some of the results deviated, and what can be done to improve the models. It will
also be discussed what methods are the best for digital twin implementations.
A few things will be discussed in relation to the project objectives and how the expectations

6.1
Simulation Verification
During the project four ROMs were made. The ROMs were attempted verified by com-
paring to the original simulation. The performance of the different ROMs can be ranked
according to how well they were verified. The ROMs for each of the original simulations
will be discussed one at the time. The results from section 4.4 will be discussed.
6.1.1
Pretension Model
There are two ROMs for the pretension model. They showed varying degrees of accuracy.
The response surface gave the best results, as the level of stresses deviated the least.
Analytical ROM
The analytical pretension model was developed and attempted verified against the original
simulation in previous chapters. The model was not verified. In the comparison to the
numerical model, it showed opposite trends when pressure was applied. The bolt stress
dropped significantly in scenario 2 when the pressure was added. This is due to the
negative sign in the equation for the bolt force. The application of the pressure as a
moment equilibrium could be a wrong assumption. This is an assumption that should be
revised for the model. The pressure increased the stress in the numerical simulation.
When the sleeve liquid temperature is applied in scenario 3 the stress increases to higher
levels than the others. A yield stress was not included, but if a bilinear material model
was included, the material might have yielded. The assumption that the forces produce
an equally large force is probably not true. The system is statically indeterminate, so
the forces might distribute themselves differently. There are methods to work with inde-
terminate beams, but they were not implemented here due to time constraints. Other
distributions of the stress were made experimentally as seen in line 52 in appendix A.1.
The numerical stress values were too far away from the original numerical stress. The
numerical simulation might not have shown a high value due to yielding in the bolts.
Response Surfaces Pretension
The response surface followed the same trends as the numerical simulation for two of the
three cases. The response surface showed the max stress in the bolts. This can make
the result volatile, as it does not necessarily reflect the general stress state. The average
stresses might have been better to use. If the max stress is too close to the yield stress,
the response surface can plateau because of the max strength not increasing.
The response surface for the pretension model had unacceptable error estimations. The
cross-validation showed that the pretension response surface was not reliable enough. It
was difficult to generate a response surface from the simulation. Even with the increased
number of design points the cross-validation was still not very good. It will require more

design points and a further study to generate the response surface for the pretension
simulation.
The simulation is complex and the previous steps affect the later steps. This could play a
role in whether a model is appropriate to use in a digital twin. The final results however,
when comparing the angle and the pretension, were close to what was expected from the
data provided for the pump.
The variation between the the left and right bolt pretension was expected to vary, as the
problem is not symmetric. The bottom cover has a different geometry, due to the inlet and

the pressure, but the reason for the insensitivity was not found during this project. The
models have been verified against the original simulation, but a bug in the APDL code

The method of using response surfaces is good to find general tendencies, but can be
too sensitive to the design points, as there are few. Some response surfaces gave really
good fits in the simulation space. The pretension model had more complications than
the cover model in finding a good response surface. This is probably because it is a
more complicated model. The 10 load steps affect the later load steps very much, so the
tendencies can get difficult to see. It is important that there are design points enough.
Proper hardware helps the process, as the simulations will speed up.
Response Surfaces Deformations
The deformations of the top and bottom of the sleeve were promising. The leave-one-out
evaluation was critical for the response surface of the bottom, but that was likely due to
the small numbers of the scale. The global minimum can not be explained, and the outlier
shows that the cross-validation method is not very good, when the results are volatile.
Overall the values matched the expected from the numerical simulation very well. The
values for the angles in the digital twin ran exactly within the range that was specified.
6.1.2
Cover Model
There are two ROMs for the cover model. They both showed good results when tested
and verified.
Analytical Cover Model
The analytical model was very good around scenario 2 but had larger deviations in sce-
nario 1 and 3. The fatigue life time is calculated as a ratio. A low number in the denom-
inator can give a very high value. The difference in the low range can not be trusted as
the numerical value predicts 22000 cycles before failure and the analytical predicts 50.
The assumption in the cover models fit very well to the two other models. The cylinder
compression tank is a good approximation. The mean stress is assumed zero in the
analytical model and this could be a possible extension to the model. If the mean stress
is in compression then it might show a longer fatigue life time. The S-N curve for the
analytical ROM is made from data extrapolated from the numerical model to find a similar
S-N curve, but this might not have been completely correct.
A source of error for the analytical cover ROM was that the thickness of the cover and
other values had to be measured in the numerical model and estimated in order for the
model to run. This could have given unrepresentative values.
The complex geometry of the cover was not considered in the analytical cover ROM. This
is a drawback of the analytical solution and is much easier implemented in the numerical
model and subsequent response surface.

Response Surface Cover Model
The cover model verification was acceptable based on comparison to the numerical model.
The values of the cover fatigue were very similar. They had deviations around the high

response surface should be used for high cycle fatigue only as the accuracy drops for

Overall the results for the cover ROMs were good and satisfactory.
Leave-one-out
This is the method used to cross-validate the reliability of the response surfaces. The
method is however sensitive to outliers as the validation sets are small. In Static ROM
Builder, and other data-driven methods, the validation sets are usually closer to one third
of the total learning points. This would require more data. As the simulations are time
consuming, the large data sets desired were not possible.
The user is not able to choose the leave-one-out validation set. This would have been
nice in order to compare the different response surfaces against the same values.

generation. The values for the life time are slightly lower than compared to benchmarks,
but this is expected since the response surface and ROM are conservative. The imitated
live data can be seen and the criteria for all the time steps.

could then be investigated for fatigue to see how much damage had been received. The
test setup should include strain gauges on the pretension bolts. The pump should be run
to validate the digital twin.
The results will be validated if the two pumps (digital and physical twin) break down at
the same time. The part damages can also be evaluated. That is however outside the
scope of this project. There will most likely also arise new considerations about the digital
twin when the experiments are conducted. What type of damage is the digital twin best
at showing? What are the best use cases for digital twins?

and implement pump curves.

6.2
Thoughts on the Use of Digital Twins
The production of a digital twin is fully possible with the available tools. The digital twin that
has been produced in this project will provide a manufacturer with product and customer
insights. This digital twin can also be used as a test setup to quickly see the effects of the
RPM on the deformation of the top rim of the sleeve. The twin can also be used to show
customers how the pump will react to the environments that they plan to use it in. The
customer can evaluate if he wants to run his pump out of specification, and sacrifice life
time, or find a compromise between operational specifications that will save him life time.
This digital twin can for instance be part of two or more pumps in parallel. The pumps can
alternate between providing a high pressure so they can be changed or serviced at the
same time.
The field of digital twins could be helped along by industry standards and methods. Making
a digital twin is complex. The biggest obstacles seems to still be the ROM generation. The
best ROMs in this project was the cover model. The goodness of fit was very good and
the model was verified with the numerical simulation. This could be because the physics
are more simple. The pretension model was very complex and difficult to work with. The
digital twin might be more accurate if it is built of many small ROMs instead of one big
one. This might increase the computational time, so a compromise should be found.
If a company is not willing to generate ROMs the original simulations can be kept and
used for a digital twin. If a compromise can be made to the real-time requirement, then
the simulations can possibly be run using HPC hardware. The loads can be recorded
and run collectively. Usually this options involves investing in hardware or buying it out of
house.
The choice of building a digital twin, or any other technical solution, should always be
challenged by a question. Is there an easier way to the same?

Conclusion
During the thesis it has been investigated how to make a digital twin from a set of ANSYS
Mechanical models. A prerequisite for making the digital twin is to transform the numerical
models into reduced order models.
Methods for generating ROMs have been shown and investigated. Some are not applica-
ble for this project. Two methods were successfully used in the project: analytical ROMs
and DesignXplorer Response Surfaces. Two reduced order models were made of each
of the original simulations given for the project.
The Mechanical models had some issues to overcome, before response surfaces could
be made. The inputs could not be parametrized in the given format. In order to gen-
erate the response surfaces is a APDL script written to parametrize the table values in
the numerical ANSYS simulation. Design of Experiments and Interpolations are used to
generate response surfaces of the numerical simulations. The methods used are optimal
space-filling design and genetic aggregation.
The validity of the results varied with the complexity of the original models and the method.
The best reduced order model was the response surface of the cover fatigue model. The
results of the model are the closest to the verified values in the original numerical simula-
tion. The pretension response surface was difficult to make as the output parameters had
complex dependencies and high gradients. The more complex the numerical simulation
is the more difficult it is to make a good response surface. Verification and validation is
important tools when making response surfaces.
The analytical ROMs were difficult to make and the results were not satisfying. The ana-
lytical ROMs were not correctly produced and the assumptions were not correct.
It is shown that is it possible to create a digital twin of digital multi-physics models. A digital
twin has been implemented in Twin Builder. The digital twin of the pump show four critical
parameters for the pump in operation. Two pretension bolt forces, the displacements and
the fatigue life time remaining is shown, to illustrate how the digital twin is performing.
Mock-up data flow and pump curves were implemented successfully in Twin Builder, as
inputs to the ROMs.
All objectives of the project was successful. Ideas to other projects that can be done in
line with this project is listed in section 6.4.
