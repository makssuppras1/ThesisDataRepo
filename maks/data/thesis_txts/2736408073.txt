Abstract
In a time of global climate change, microbial conversion and capture of gaseous carbon (e.g.
CH4, CO2, CO) from sources such as industrial blast furnace gases or biomass gasification
has a high potential to simultaneously mitigate greenhouse gas emissions and contribute to
the sustainable production of biofuels and biochemicals. This thesis aims to systematically
develop a mechanistic model for Thermoanaerobacter kivui, as a case study for continuous
gas fermentation. In addition, the model will be used to explore the process design space
for optimal operating conditions and to incorporate the response behavior of the system into
bioprocess development.
Established methods such as sensitivity and identifiability analysis were used to systematically
develop the mechanistic model. In the optimization of the operating conditions, multiple perfor­
mance measures were defined as objectives and later time scale analysis was used to analyze
the stability and response time of selected operating points.
The model result showed a good performance (normalized RMSE < 22%), indicating the correct
description of the main underlying processes (e.g. microbial growth, acetate, and CO2 produc­
tion as well as uptake rates of H2 , CO and CO2) and the identification of the kinetic parameter
set. Sensitivity analysis revealed that CO as a carbon source is highly relevant for biomass
production (δrmse
Ycox =17.17%) compared to the CO2 pathway (δrmse
Yco2x=3.5%). Identifiability analysis
indicates correlated parameters that could not be uniquely estimated together, such as linear
correlations of Ycox and kLaco , ms and Ki,ac , and Kco and qco,max , and nonlinear correlations for
Ki,co and qco,max , Ki,coh2 and qco2,max , and Ki,co and Kco. Consistent with the sensitivity analysis,
kinetic parameters that have a significant influence on the model output such as Ycox and qco,max
had a lower standard deviation (σYcox=2.5% and σqco,max=23.0%) than less sensitive parame­
ters like Kh2 and kLaco2 (σKh2=55.9% and σkLaco2=52.1%). Using multi­objective optimization,
the optimal operating range for carbon­neutral processes was identified with 0.0118 mol/(Lh) as
the maximized space­time yield of acetate. The optimal conditions were found with a gas com­
position of 39:51:8:2 [%] for H2:CO:CO2:N2 and a dilution rate of 0.048 h­1. Time­scale analysis
provided insight into the system response time for several scenarios in the optimal operating
range and revealed sufficiently stable process conditions for the optimal acetate productivity
with τmax = 23.23h.
In conclusion, this study highlights T. kivui as a promising biocatalyst for fixing gaseous carbon
from industrial flue gases, and it demonstrates the advantage of model­based analysis for gas
fermentation development. In addition, the systematic methodology for model development,
system dynamics analysis, and optimization presented here serves as a robust framework that
can be applied to various bioprocess systems, underscoring its potential for improving biopro­
cess development and optimization in various microbial systems.

Introduction
In response to climate change, the transition from fossil to renewable resources for the pro­
duction of chemicals and fuels is a key aspect of mitigating greenhouse gas emissions. As
7­9% of the global anthropogenic carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions are generated during the
production of steel, and the industrial blast furnace gases are rich in gaseous carbon such as
carbon monoxide (CO), and CO2, integrating carbon capture and utilization technologies into
the steel industry is of increasing interest (Novak et al., 2021; World Steel Association, 2021).
Gas fermentations can contribute to a climate­resilient and low­carbon environment by microbial
conversion of gaseous carbon into value­added organic products. The acetogenic bacterium
Thermoanaerobacter kivui has the advantageous characteristics of being able to grow on both
CO and CO2, thus offering flexibility regarding to fluctuating gas compositions in the effluent
gases. Furthermore, T. kivui can be applied as a promising biocatalyst in hydrogen storage,
CO2 capture and syngas conversion due to its high productivities in formate and acetate pro­
duction (Schwarz & Müller, 2020).
As mechanistic models support the formation of a knowledge base and knowledge transfer,
they find application in all stages of bioprocess development from laboratory to industrial scale,
monitoring, control and optimization. The analysis of the model will lead to valuable information
of sensitivities regarding biological and operational factors of the process, thus facilitating to
optimize process performance measures (Mears et al., 2017).
The scientific relevance of this work lies in its new mechanistic model developed for T. kivui
as a case study for other acetogens and gas fermentations. In addition, this thesis integrates
multi­objective optimization and time­scale analysis to bioreactor models, where the latter is
still uncommon but found application in metabolic and chemical engineering. Therefore, this
work is an interdisciplinary study combining the microbial mechanisms, physical and chemical
phenomena in the bioreactor with methods for model analysis and optimization algorithms.
Five main objectives were derived from the problem statement starting with the systematic de­
velopment, calibration, and validation of a mechanistic model for T. kivui in a two­phase gas­
liquid bioreactor. This is followed by an analysis of how variations in kinetic parameters affect
model output. The investigation then shifts to parameter identifiability, assessing the reliability

2.1
Gas fermentation
In a time of global climate change, there is growing interest in developing novel strategies for the
sustainable production of chemicals and fuels. To achieve a climate­resilient and low­carbon
industrial environment, the current challenge is the transition from fossil resources to renew­
able and non­food resources, and the closing of the global carbon cycle to reduce greenhouse
gas (GHG) emissions (Novak et al., 2021; Vees et al., 2020). Carbon dioxide (CO2) is one of
the most emitted GHG. One technology for the fixation of GHG is gas fermentation, in which
microorganisms utilize CO2, CO and CH4 as carbon sources to produce commodity chemicals
or fuels. If the microbial conversion of gaseous carbon from air, industrial blast furnace gas or
gasification of municipal solid waste or lignocellulosic biomass is applied on an industrial scale,
significant amounts of GHG could be captured and dependence on primary carbon sources
such as glucose and starch would be decoupled (Bengelsdorf & Dürre, 2017; Köpke et al.,
2011; Vees et al., 2020).
2.1.1
Gaseous carbon sources
Biomass gasification and industrial blast furnace gas utilization benefit from their abundant avail­
ability as widely accessible carbon sources, coupled with the advantage of more mature tech­
nological infrastructures compared to direct carbon capture methods that remove carbon from
the atmosphere. Their widespread use and maturing processes highlight their importance as
viable pathways for carbon utilization and mitigation.
2.1.1.1
Biomass gasification
Initial interest in biomass gasification was driven by a desire to move from solid to gaseous
carbon sources, as current commercial biofuel production is based on sugars, starches, and
oilseeds. In order not to compete with the food supply, research is underway on second genera­
tion non­edible lignocellulosic feedstocks such as agricultural residues, agricultural processing
by­products and energy crops (corn stover, wheat straw, corn fiber, seed cake, switchgrass,
etc.). Lignocellulosic biomass requires pretreatment to make it more accessible to enzymes,
which can be biochemical or thermochemical (Munasinghe & Khanal, 2010). In the biochemical
pathway, the non­fermentable polymer lignin is removed to make cellulose and hemicellulose
accessible. They are preliminary broken down into glucose and xylose, which can be consumed
by microorganisms. Pretreatment can produce unwanted and toxic substances that inhibit fer­
mentation (Qureshi et al., 2010; Sun et al., 2019).
The advantage of the thermochemical pathway is that by gasifying the lignocellulosic biomass,
even compounds that are not accessible to microorganisms are converted to gas. Therefore, a
higher yield is obtained from the same amount of biomass. The product of gasification is called
syngas and contains mainly H2, CO, CO2 and CH4 (Kumar et al., 2009). The composition
of the syngas depends on many factors, including reactor configuration, operating conditions
(temperature, residence time, and heating rate), gasification medium (air, steam, CO2), and,
among others, biomass properties. For example, steam is preferred as a gasification agent if
the H2 content is to be high (Mishra & Kumar Upadhyay, 2021; Yang & Chen, 2015).
2.1.1.2
Industrial blast furnace gas
In addition to syngas from biomass gasification, a wide variety of syngases are produced in
industrial production processes. The steel industry, which is responsible for 7­9% of global
anthropogenic CO2 emissions, produces a variety of syngas streams such as coke oven gas
(COG), blast furnace gas (BFG), and basic oxygen furnace (BOF) off­gases (World Steel As­
sociation, 2021). The ratio of components in the flue gas depends on the substrate and the
production step. However, they are all CO­rich and contain varying amounts of CO2 and H2.

In addition, small amounts of other compounds may be present that could have a negative im­
pact on the fermentation (Caillat, 2017; Molitor et al., 2016). However, one way to reduce their

2.1.2
Microbial vs. chemical conversion
Franz Fischer and Hans Tropsch invented the metal catalyst conversion of CO and H2 to a mix­
ture of hydrocarbons in the 1920s, called the Fischer­Tropsch (FT) conversion. The process is
well understood and has been commercially used and improved since 1938 (Dry, 2002). The
syngas is passed over a metal catalyst consisting mainly of iron, cobalt and ruthenium at high
temperatures and pressures and is then converted into liquid hydrocarbons. The downside is
that the metal catalysts are sensitive to permanent poisoning by small amounts of contami­
nants in the syngas, e.g. sulfur compounds (Molitor et al., 2016). Furthermore, the CO:H2 ratio
must be kept constant, which is difficult to achieve with industrial off­gas so that cleaning of the
exhaust gases and balancing of the CO:H2 ratio cause additional costs (Dry, 2002).
Comparing the FT process with microbial conversion, fermentation processes have the following
advantages (Daniell et al., 2012; Munasinghe & Khanal, 2010; Perret et al., 2022):
• higher flexibility regarding the composition of gas mixture
• higher tolerance to specific contaminants (within limits)
• one­step conversion of syngas to hydrocarbonate
• milder operating conditions (temperature and pressure)
• high selectivity regarding product formation
• variety of possible products
• lower environmental footprint of process waste
Although there are several benefits within microbial fermentation, there are still bottlenecks
present that need to be overcome such as low cell densities, low space­time yields, limitations
in the gas­liquid mass transfer and high production costs (Munasinghe & Khanal, 2010). Sun
et al., 2019 summarizes several approaches that address some of the drawbacks, e.g., through
modified bioreactors, genetic and metabolic engineering, media design, and process control
strategies.
2.2
Microbial conversion of CO2 and CO
Microorganisms show a remarkable versatility in their ability to utilize different gaseous sources
of carbon (CO2, CO, and CH4). Among them, different groups are specialized for each of men­
tioned the carbon sources. For example, autotrophic microorganisms, such as cyanobacteria,
adapted at using CO2 as the primary carbon source for photosynthesis. In contrast, carboxy­
dotrophic bacteria show specialization in CO metabolism, and methanogens, mainly archaea,
are capable of converting methane to CO2. These diverse groups of microorganisms contribute
to the global carbon cycle and modulate greenhouse gas dynamics in different ecosystems and
environmental niches (King & Weber, 2007; Singh et al., 2010).
2.2.1
Acetogenic bacteria
Acetogenic bacteria, or acetogens for short, are a group of bacteria capable of producing acetate
from CO and/or CO2 and H2 through the acetyl­CoA pathway. The pathway is used not only to
reduce CO2 to acetyl­CoA, but also to conserve energy and assimilate CO2 into cellular carbon.
The acetyl­CoA pathway is also called the Wood­Ljungdahl pathway in recognition of H.G. Wood
and L.G. Ljungdahl, who with their team proposed a scheme for the biochemistry of the pathway

(Ljungdahl & Wood, 1969). It was then further investigated by Wood, 1991, Ljungdahl, 1986,
Ragsdale and Ljungdahl, 1984, Drake et al., 2008 and many others.
Acetogens are obligatorily anaerobic and facultatively autotrophic, growing on both organic sub­
strates (hexoses, pentoses, alcohols) and inorganic substrates such as syngas. Due to their
high metabolic flexibility, these bacteria are widely distributed in nature and have been isolated
from a variety of habitats (alkaline, high­salt, hot environments, soils, sludges, fresh water, ma­
rine sediments, and animal intestinal tracts). Of the more than 100 species found, there are 3
model organisms that have been extensively studied: Moorella thermoacetica, Acetobacterium
woodii and Clostridium ljungdahlii, because most of the species belong to the genera Aceto­
bacterium and Clostrium. (Drake et al., 2008; Schuchmann & Müller, 2014).
2.2.2
Thermoanaerobacter kivui
Thermoanaerobacter kivui is an acetogenic bacterium. Leigh et al., 1981 isolated T. kivui, also
called Acetogenium kivui, from the sediment of Lake Kivu, Democratic Republic of the Congo. It
is a gram­positive, chemolithotrophic, thermophilic and obligate anaerobic bacterium. The cell
morphology of T. kivui is rodlike, non­motile and non­sporeforming, and it often occurs in pairs
of chains. Optimal growth conditions for the microorganism are achieved at a temperature of
66°C and a pH of 6.4, while the temperature tolerance ranges from 50°C to 70°C and the pH
tolerance from 5.3 to 7.3. The primary end product is acetate, which can be produced either
by heterotrophic growth on hexoses and pentoses such as glucose, mannose and fructose, or
by autotrophic growth on syngas (H2, CO and CO2). Due to its high productivities for formate
and acetate production, T. kivui is a promising biocatalyst for future applications in hydrogen
storage, CO2 capture and syngas conversion (Regis et al., 2024; Schwarz & Müller, 2020).
2.2.3

produced through the reduction of two molecules of CO2, where H2 is required as electron

the methyl branch to formate and then stepwise to methyl­tetrahydrofolate (THF).

2.3
Mechanistic model
Understanding the metabolic network and its impact on process dynamics is crucial to estab­
lish a process for T. kivui at laboratory and industrial scales. Mechanistic models integrate
the biological microbial system into the environment of a bioreactor, allowing the dynamics of
substrate utilization, growth, and product formation to be studied under a variety of operating
modes and conditions. As a result, bioreactor models provide insights into cultivation strategies,
performance optimization, and bioreactor design, among other applications.
2.3.1
Definition
Mechanistic models use mathematical expressions to describe the current understanding of a
dynamic system, including its constituent parts and underlying mechanisms (Gernaey et al.,
2010). The mathematical expressions are derived from scientific laws of physics, chemistry,
and biology such as mass, momentum, and heat balances, so mechanistic models are deter­
ministic in nature. This implies that the predicted output of the model is determined by its input
(Fernandes et al., 2013). Because of the complexity of biological systems, mechanistic models
are often complemented by empirical expressions because some existing phenomena are not
yet fully understood. Empirical expressions, such as Monod kinetics for microbial growth and
5
==PAGE:17==

metabolic rates, often simplify and idealize the observations in a data set without knowing the
underlying mechanisms in full detail (Mears et al., 2017). Once a common knowledge base has
been established, knowledge can be transferred and extended to gradually move from empirical
to more detailed, deterministic models (Gernaey et al., 2010).
The increased interest in mechanistic models in recent years has been encouraged by the
regulatory authorities, FDA and EMEA, in the context of Quality by Design (QbD) and Process
Analytical Technology (PAT) guidelines, which can only be practically implemented with compre­
hensive process knowledge. The focus is on using scientific process understanding to identify
factors that impact product quality and quantity. Real­time quality assurance and implementa­
tion of control strategies are designed to both increase manufacturing efficiency and reduce the
risk of poor product quality (Gernaey et al., 2010; U.S. Food and Drug Administration & U.S.
Department of Human and Health, 2004).
Another approach to modeling complex, nonlinear systems is data­driven modeling. Machine
learning algorithms facilitate modeling when extensive process knowledge is unavailable (Yati­
panthalawa & Gras, 2024). In contrast to mechanistic models, the mathematical expressions of
machine learning algorithms don’t represent causal relationships, but rather mappings between
data. Consequently, model parameters have no physical, chemical, or biological meaning. As
the algorithm learns from the data, the quality and variability of the data determines the per­
formance of the data­driven model. Thus, extrapolation into process design spaces that are
new to the model results in performance loss. However, data­driven models are more flexible,
capture the data better, and save time when it comes to developing models (Du et al., 2022).
Hybrid approaches combine the benefits of both the adaptability of data­driven models and the
interpretability of mechanistic models (von Stosch et al., 2014). Whether a mechanistic, hybrid,
or data­driven approach is best depends on the purpose of modeling the application.
2.3.2
Application
The application of mechanistic models in the development of fermentation processes is mani­
fold. Finding stable operating conditions, achievement of desired growth rates and maximization
of productivity are exemplary objectives (Lira­Parada et al., 2021). For this reason, mechanistic
models are relevant in many phases of the process development: Initially, the exploration of
the optimal operating conditions, such as temperature, pH, stirrer speed and pressure, with the
aim of obtaining a highly profitable process, is an important task (Almquist et al., 2014). Later,
when the process is scaled up, a mechanistic model can be a valuable tool for investigation
of the sensitivity to changes in process conditions such as heat and mass transfer rates, since
industrial scale testing can be very expensive. By coupling a control algorithm to a model that
represents reality, different control strategies can be tested before being implemented online
(Craven & Whelan, 2015; Mears et al., 2017). Among other applications, they can also be used
to assess the energy efficiency and operating costs of a process (Dassau et al., 2006; Gernaey
et al., 2010).
2.3.3
Types of mechanistic models
A single living cell is already such a complex system that the mathematical description of it can
only be a very rough approximation. Therefore, the question arises as to how the physical sys­
tem and its mathematical description of it are related to each other. According to Bailey (1998),
developing a model allows us to organize our observations and be able to predict the perfor­
mance of a system within certain limits. Therefore, it is necessary to consider the application
of the model and the problem it should solve to understand the assumptions that the model is
based on.
Fredrickson et al. (1970) and Bailey (1998) classified mechanistic models based on the level of
structure and segregation. One aspect is about the description of biomass. The cells in a biore­
actor are represented as a cell population where each cell can differ in cell age, size, growth
rate and metabolic state (Craven & Whelan, 2015). Segregated models represent some de­
6

gree of heterogeneity of the population in properties and activities while unsegregated models
assume the cells to be identical individuals within the cell culture. Therefore, the entire popu­
lation is described by a single average cell variable. The other aspect is about the description
of the internal metabolism of a cell. Structured models decompose the cell as unit into genetic,
morphological or biochemical attributes (Muloiwa et al., 2020). Thus, the physiological state of
the cell can be described by several parameters such as NADH, precursors, metabolites, ATP
(Goldrick et al., 2015). Unstructured models, on the other hand, consider only implicitly the in­

The most idealized and common approach assumes a homogeneous environment in the biore­

model. If the assumption doesn’t hold, metabolic flux analysis, flux balance analysis or genome
scaled modeling increase the complexity (structured models). Similarly, the average cell as­
sumption sometimes doesn’t hold for dynamic behavior that is more complex than steady state
or balanced growth, such as transient growth or changes in biomass composition. This addi­
tional level of complexity is added by population balance models. Obviously, the most complex
and detailed models are structured and segregated models that come along with high com­
putational costs (Gernaey et al., 2010; Panikov, 2010). In conclusion, defining the purpose
of the model is the first step in selecting the necessary complexity of its cell and population
representation.
2.3.4
Model development
When developing a mechanistic model, it is not only the type of model that is important, but
also ensuring that the outputs of the model are reliable before using model­based predictions to
study or improve a process (Anane et al., 2019). Good Modeling Practices (GMoP) have been
established to minimize the risk of errors during the model building process or in the subsequent
application and interpretation of the model. GMoP are mathematical and statistical tools that
have been integrated into frameworks for the development of mechanistic models (Rkan Sin
et al., 2009). Different frameworks exist in the literature with different methods of validation
depending on the goal of the model (Kroll et al., 2017). The following workflow combines the
frameworks described in Almquist et al. (2014), Daume et al. (2020), and Sin et al. (2008), taking
into account valuable input and considerations.

explore the process design space, test sensitivities, or develop control strategies.
2.3.5
Challenges and future perspectives
Due to the complexity of biological systems, the development of mechanistic models is as­
sociated with a number of challenges. The most obvious and frequently cited aspect is the
enormous amount of time, resources and knowledge required by a systematic development
approach (Gernaey et al., 2010). Another aspect is the need for high quality experimental data
to fit the model and assess its validity and accuracy (Hernández Rodríguez et al., 2019). Despite
the challenges mentioned, mechanistic models have great potential in future process develop­
ment. They offer opportunities in process monitoring and control to increase the stability and
robustness of processes to perturbations and to operate with higher product selectivity. Also
outside the operation of fermentation processes, they have great potential in optimization prob­
lems as well as being a platform for collaboration, knowledge sharing and broadening mutual
process understanding (Craven & Whelan, 2015; Mears et al., 2017).
2.4
Model fitting & validation

quality and performance of the model. If the checkpoints are not satisfactorily met, the model
must be revised, creating an iterative workflow in model development (Step 2­5). The following

2.4.2
Parameter identifiability
The sensitivity matrix is then used to assess the identifiability of the parameter set. One aspect
of the accurate determination of a parameter is that the model output is sensitive to it. If this is
not the case, an infinite number of parameter values would result in the same model output. The
other aspect is that changes in the model output by a single parameter should lead to individual
changes in the model output that are not compensated by another parameter (Brun et al., 2002).
The model structure alone determines whether the model is structurally identifiable, so that
structural identifiability is independent of the quantity and quality of experimental data. Non­

of the iterative workflow described in subsection 2.3.4 (Vora & Daoutidis, 2001).
Non­practical identifiability arises from the information content of the experimental data. It could
be either the amount or the quality of the data or inappropriately chosen time points (Raue et al.,

2.4.3.1
Local vs. global parameter estimation
The goal of any optimization problem is to find the global minimum, a value of θ where the
objective function J is minimized. The difficulty of this task is that the function may have not only
one global minimum, but also several local minima. Since local minima minimize the objective
function within a certain neighborhood, it is difficult to prove that a given potential point is a
global minimum. Based on this idea, optimization algorithms can be divided into two groups:
local and global methods (Kochenderfer & Wheeler, 2019).
Local optimization algorithms start with a predefined starting point in the parameter space and
improve the objective function by iteratively applying the optimization algorithm. The initial­
ization of the starting point can be defined based on measurements, literature, or simply by
guessing. There are several ways to determine the search direction within the parameter space
12
==PAGE:24==
Chapter 2. Theoretical framework
such as gradient, Hessian, or direct search such as the Nelder­Mead or Hooke­Jeeves methods
(Almquist et al., 2014; Nocedal & Wright, 2006). Local optimization algorithms tend to find local
solutions because the algorithm is highly dependent on the initial conditions. One approach
to increase the probability of finding the global minimum is to combine the local optimizer with
Monte Carlo as proposed in (Anane et al., 2019; Nikerel et al., 2008). For small models with few
parameters, this is an easy way to overcome local minima, but methods have been developed
to search the parameter space more effectively.
Due to the inefficiency of local methods with respect to larger, more complex optimization prob­
lems, several methods have been developed to explore the parameter space more compre­
hensively, including common methods such as simulated annealing, genetic and evolutionary
algorithms, particle swarms, and others (Moles et al., 2003). Almquist et al., 2014 provides an
extensive list of different algorithms and their comparative studies. Unfortunately, global opti­
mizers come at the cost of higher computational requirements, slower convergence rates, and
less transparent calculations.
2.4.3.2
Nelder­Mead algorithm
For the purpose of model calibration with few kinetic parameters, the Nelder­Mead algorithm is
a robust method, easy to implement and provides fast results (Kroll et al., 2017). The Nelder­
Mead algorithm belongs to the group of direct search algorithms, which means that they do
not rely on gradients to reach the local minimum. Instead, the algorithm uses a simplex, a
generalized tetrahedron in n­dimensional space, which moves through the design space by
evaluating the objective function at different points. The updating of the simplex is based on a set
of rules, basically by comparing the function values at each vertex and then replacing the highest
valued vertex with another point. Through reflection, expansion, contraction, and shrinking
iterations, Nelder­Mead dynamically adapts its search direction, aiming to converge to a local
minimum without explicit derivative guidance (Nelder & Mead, 1965). Besides the challenge of
overcoming local minima, another disadvantage of the Nelder­Mead algorithm is that it is a point
estimator, resulting in a value for each parameter. Therefore, there is no information about the
output uncertainty of the estimates (Hernández Rodríguez et al., 2019).
2.5
Model applications
Once the model is developed, it can be applied in various ways, all with the goal of better
understanding the behavior of microorganisms in their environment. In the following section,
the focus is on optimization problems with conflicting performance measures and is used to
explore the process design space of optimal operating conditions. The second section focuses
on the analysis of steady­state stability and robustness to changes in state variables based on
the time scales of the system.
2.5.1
Multi­objective optimization problem
Many fermentation processes involve the simultaneous optimization of multiple performance
measures such as productivity, cost, and time, which are often conflicting or competing, making
them difficult to prioritize. A multiple objective problem (MOP) will not result in a single solution
that is the global minimum or maximum with respect to all objectives. Instead, an MOP will be
the set of best possible trade­offs among all objectives, commonly known as Pareto optimal
solutions, where the evaluation including all objectives makes none of the solutions better than
any other. The solutions on the Pareto front have the property that the solution cannot be
strictly dominated by another solution, meaning that none of the solutions can be improved
without degrading at least one of the objectives. Along the Pareto front, the non­dominated
solutions differ in how each objective is valued, but all solutions are acceptable (Coello et al.,
2021; Sarkar & Modak, 2005).
Historically, multi­objective formulations have combined the individual objective functions into
one by using their weighted sum or a ratio, or by selecting one objective function and represent­
ing the others as constraints. This method can cause a loss of information about the relationship
13
==PAGE:25==
Chapter 2. Theoretical framework
between the different objectives, and it introduces subjectivity and bias into the weighting of the
objectives, resulting in an overall inability to capture the entire Pareto solution set. To overcome
these challenges, several approaches have been investigated to generate the non­dominated
solution sets of the Pareto front, including tabu search, simulated annealing, evolutionary algo­
rithms, and neural networks (Luh et al., 2003; Sharma et al., 2024).

2.5.1.2
Genetic algorithm
Genetic algorithms are one of the early methods applied to MOP and are inspired by biological
evolution, using individuals grouped into a population, each representing a point in the parame­
ter space. Each individual is described by its fitness, which is inversely related to the objective
function, so that fitter individuals are more likely to pass on traits when creating the next genera­
tion population. Therefore, each population minimizes the objective function of the optimization
problem. To maintain diversity within a population, the genetic operations crossover and muta­
tion are applied within a population (Kochenderfer & Wheeler, 2019).
The vector­valued genetic algorithm developed by Schaffer, 1985 was an early implementation
of evolutionary algorithms for multi­objective optimization problems. Evolutionary algorithms
appear to be excellent solvers of multi­objective optimization problems because of their ability
to simultaneously process a diverse set of possible solutions (a population) so that the Pareto
optimal solutions can be found in one run of the algorithm, can easily handle discontinuous and
concave Pareto fronts. In contrast, traditional mathematical programming requires multiple runs
to find the solutions of the Pareto front, and the shape and continuity of the solution set can be
a concern for them (Coello et al., 2021).
2.5.1.3
Nondominated sorting genetic algorithm II
The most popular multi­objective evolutionary algirithm to date is the nondominated sorting
genetic algorithm (NSGA) that was proposed and revised to NSGA­II by Deb et al., 2002. The
first version of the algorithm was critized for its high computational costs that came along with
the nondominated sorting of large population sizes. Further, the algorithm was lacking elitims
which on one hand speeds up the performance of a genetic algorithms and on the other hand

prevents the loss of good solutions once they were found. And last, a sharing parameter was
required to be specified to keep the populations diverse. In order to address these challenges,
Deb et al., 2002 made the algorithm more efficient but less parameterized, while at the same
time forcing different solution sets.
Before the main loop of the NSGA­II algorithm starts, an initial random parent population P0 and
an initial offspring population Q0 are generated. Both populations have size N. To generate the
offspring, P0 is sorted based on nondomination (rank 1 is the best level, 2 is the second best,
and so on) and binary tournament selection, recombination, and mutation operators are used
for the next generation Q0.
The main loop is illustrated in Figure 2.4 and has a different procedure where the best ranked
individuals of the t­1 generation are grouped into a result population. This population is the
parent population Pt of the tth generation. Binary tournament selection, recombination and
mutation operators are again used to create the offspring Qt. In the next step, the combined
population Rt = Pt ∪Qt of size 2N is created to ensure elitism.
Therefore, nondominated sorting is applied to the combined population of parents and offspring,
evaluating the fitness of individuals: F1 are the population members with the best solution (rank
1), and are therefore emphasized more than other solution members in the combined popula­
tion.If the size of F1 is less than N, all members of F1 are selected for the next generation Pt+1.
The members of the following solution sets (F2, F3,..) are added until the size N is reached.
In the case that the last solution front Fl does not fit completely into Pt+1, a criterion other than
rank is required, because all members of Fl have the same rank. Hence, the individuals are
sorted using the crowded­comparison operator, which also takes into account the distance to
other solutions. For individuals with the same rank, the operator prefers the solution that is in a
less crowded region, which increases the diversity of the solution set without defining additional
parameters.

2.5.2
Time­scale analysis
Time scales are a characteristic measure of time used to describe a system and its behavior.
The challenge in determining the time scale of a system is that the system’s time scale is not
inherently defined. Therefore, many different time scale approximations exist in the literature
(Wartha et al., 2021). The general idea arises from the question of how a system responds to
small perturbations. The answer lies in the analysis of the eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix,
which describes the behavior of the system in a linearized neighborhood of some point in the
state space. The ordinary differential equation describing the state variables of the model has
been defined in Equation 2.5. When the system is perturbed with a small disturbance, the
change in state x can be approximated by the Jacobian J multiplied by ∆x (Maas & Pope,
1992):

step (Tomlin et al., 2002). The Jacobian matrix is then decomposed into its eigenvalues, whose
inverse of the real part (Re) represents the time scale τi [h] of a mode i, as follows (Klinke &

The sign of the real part of the eigenvalue indicates whether the perturbation on the system will
increase, remain constant, or decrease with time. The perturbation will increase with time if the
system is deflected in the direction of the eigenvector and the corresponding eigenvalue has
a positive real part. If the real part of the eigenvalue is negative, the system will stabilize and
return to the previous state (Maas & Pope, 1992). The scale of an eigenvalue with a negative
real part, on the other hand, determines the amount of time it will take for the system to relax.
In a complex kinetic system, time scales can vary over several orders of magnitude, so the
slowest time scale among the time scales of each state variable determines the overall system
response (Isaac et al., 2013).

tion of metabolic networks (Jamshidi & Palsson, 2008; Klinke & Finley, 2012) and in chemical
engineering (Isaac et al., 2013; Prüfert et al., 2014), but it has not been widely integrated into
applications for bioprocess design. As discussed in subsection 2.5.1, performance metrics,
cost, and time are often conflicting objectives for a bioprocess. Therefore, when incorporated
into the objective function of a process optimization problem, time scale analysis is a valuable
method. Through the definition of a multi­objective optimization problem, the maximization of
selected performance metrics and the minimization of time scales for fast system response
is achieved. For example, this would lead to a solution that avoids operating points near the
washout, because even though productivity is high near the washout, the system response
slows down dramatically. In conclusion, this quantitative analysis can be easily implemented
in process design and development and can help to correctly plan experiments and determine
stable process conditions (Sinner et al., 2020).

Objectives of the Thesis
Gas fermentation is a promising technology of the future that utilizes gaseous carbon substrates
for microbial conversion, thus providing a sustainable alternative to traditional chemical pro­
cesses. Within this landscape, the microbial conversion of industrial blast furnace gas to value­
added products, particularly by acetogens such as T. kivui, represents an important avenue for
research. However, despite the extensive literature on mechanistic models and their applica­
tion to gas fermentation processes, a mechanistic model for T. kivui remains to be developed,
emphasizing the complexity of its metabolic pathway and the need for further research.
The field of mechanistic modeling has adopted various workflows incorporating essential ele­
ments such as sensitivity analysis, identifiability analysis, and parameter estimation into model
development and fitting to ensure model validity and reliability. The utility and versatility of such
models is demonstrated by the abundant literature on mechanistic models. They promote un­
derstanding of microbial behavior, are capable of predicting system behavior under unstudied
operating conditions, and are therefore used to develop, optimize, monitor, and control biopro­
cesses.
Furthermore, the application of modeling goes beyond theoretical constructs and finds practi­
cal use in analyzing and optimizing bioprocesses. Multiobjective optimization techniques have
emerged as a common strategy, allowing the simultaneous optimization of conflicting objectives
and facilitating the design of more efficient bioprocesses. However, the use of time­scale anal­
ysis, which is a potentially powerful tool for understanding the dynamics of bioprocesses, has
remained relatively unexplored. Its limited use in bioprocess design highlights an opportunity
for future research to exploit its ability to predict stability and response times and improve the
efficiency of gaseous fermentation systems.

5.1
Model development
The development of the mechanistic model was started from scratch and the description of
the dynamics of the system was based on a review of the literature. The first ”prototype” of the
model was fitted to the experimental data provided by the cooperation partner at the TU Vienna.
Their expert knowledge of the process improved the description of the underlying mechanisms,
resulting in the reaction scheme presented in Figure 4.1.
5.1.1
Fit model to one set of experimental data
In this section, the focus is on fitting the kinetic parameters of the mechanistic model to a fed­
batch fermentation (experiment 1) where T. kivui is growing on two two carbon sources and the
composition of the gas mixture in the feed 52% CO, 21% CO2 and 24% H2. The remaining 3%
are N2 gas which is inert and therefore isn’t reactive in this system. The output states of the
model are the biomass and acetate concentration as products, the uptake rate of CO and H2 as
well as the net rate of CO2 which is the sum of the uptake rate and the production rate of CO2 .
Being already adapted to the environment, the microorganism starts to grow exponentially with­
out a previous lag phase. The exponential growth phase lasts 20h, then growth slows down,
reaching maximum biomass concentration of 1.54g/L after 38h. In the second half of the fer­
mentation process, the biomass concentration decreases to a concentration of 0.69 g/L. With
an RMSE of 8.42%, the mechanistic model is able to capture the multiple stages of biomass
growth during the fed­batch process accurately. The highest deviations from the experimentally
measured biomass concentration occur at the peak concentration and the subsequent declining
concentrations. The model hereby averages out the two extremes.
In alignment to the biomass concentration, the CO uptake increases exponentially during the
first 20 h, as being the preferred carbon source for growth. Then, after 20 h, the uptake rate
slows down and reaches a stable plateau at an uptake rate of ­0.035 mol/(Lh). The rate remains
constant until the fermentation stops. The mechanistic model can capture the CO uptake rate
well with an RMSE of 12%. Based on the model the uptake rate is constant from 20 h to 80 h
even though CO is still available in the medium.
H2 is used as an energy source, providing electrons for the reduction of CO2 in the WLP. Similar
to the CO uptake, the uptake rate of H2 increases exponentially with the growth of the microor­
ganisms. After 20h, the uptake of H2 remains constant at ­0.018 mol/(Lh) for the next 20h.
When the biomass concentration starts to decrease after 40h, the H2 rate slows down steeply
and even becomes positive, meaning that more H2 is produced than taken up. The model
doesn’t include a term for the production of H2 yet. Still, with a RMSE of 25.2%, the model is
able to capture the exponential increase of the H2 uptake well at the beginning of the process,
but reaches a lower constant rate of ­0.014 mol/(Lh) that remains until the fermentation stops.
The constant rate after 20h is reached because the system is gas­limited by H2 meaning that
all the H2 that is fed into the reactor is immediately taken up by the organism.
Since CO2 is both a compound in the gas feed and a by­product of the oxidation of CO, the
exhaust gas analysis provides only information on the sum of uptake and production of CO2.
The experimental data show that the net rate of CO2 is nearly balanced for the first 40 h, at a rate
of 0.001 mol/(Lh). Similar to the H2 rate, CO2 increases in the second part of the fermentation,
with a higher rate being produced than CO2 absorbed. This state has the highest RMSE of
72.81% because the model predicts a higher rate of production than uptake of CO2 already at
the beginning of the fermentation. The model reaches a net rate of about 0.0097 mol/(Lh) after
20h. Until the fermentation stops, the rate is quite stable, but decreases slightly in the last 40h.

5.1.2
Comparison of experiments based on the local sensitivity analysis
In experiment 1, as described in the previous section, T. kivui consumes both CO and CO2 as
carbon sources. However, the microorganism also has the ability to grow on CO alone, using it
as both a carbon and energy source. Sensitivity analysis is a valuable tool for investigating the
influence of kinetic parameters on model performance under different experimental conditions.
The six fed­batch experiments can be divided into two groups: one with two carbon sources for
experiments 1 and 2, and one with only one carbon source (CO) for experiments 3­6. Figure 5.2
displays the ranking of the parameter sensitivities for the whole process. With a) indicating
experiment 1 with both CO and CO2 and b) experiment 5 with only CO in the gas inlet. A more
detailed analysis is shown in Figure 5.3, which depicts the ranking of parameter sensitivities for
specific states, biomass and acetate concentration, as well as the time­dependent sensitivities
during the different phases of the process.
The ranking for experiment 1 with two carbon sources as shown in Figure 5.2a a) reveals that
the parameter sensitivities range from 0 to 17.5%, where 0% means that none of the model
outputs is influenced by that parameter. The higher the percentage, the more the model output
is influenced by the respective parameter. All influences are normalized and sum up to 100%
in total. Overall the model output is sensitive to all kinetic parameters, but with different effects.
Only the the volumetric mass transfer coefficient for CO2 (KLaCO−2) has no effect to model
output. A possible reason could be that CO2 has higher solubility than CO and is never a
limiting gas throughout the process. In addition, the influence of CO2 may be overshadowed by
other more dominant parameters such as the CO2 uptake rate.
In general, parameters related to CO metabolism such as Ycox (17.7%), qco,max (14.39%), Kco
(10.26%), kLaco (17. 97%) are more sensitive than those related to CO2 metabolism, such as
Yco2x (3.5%), qco2,max (2.91%), Kh2 (0.45%), Kco2 (1.54%), kLah2 (11.76%), and kLaco2 (0.04%).
Based on the mass balance, the carbon absorbed from CO is metabolized to biomass more
than from CO2, making it a very important nutrient. Another aspect is that half saturation and
inhibition coefficients have less influence on model output than parameters directly related to
biological output, such as yield coefficients and uptake rates.
Figure 5.2b shows the ranking of the parameters for the scenario in which T. kivui grows only
on CO, so that all parameters that are correlated with the uptake of CO2 have no effect at all on
the model output. As a result, only Ycox, ms, qco,max, Kco, Ki,ac, Ki,co , and kLaco impact the state
variables, and their sensitivity measures are a bit higher than in Experiment 1. With 26.32%
and 25.9% respectively, the maximum uptake rate of CO (qco,max) and the yield coefficient of
biomass to CO (Ycox) are the most sensitive kinetic parameters.

that the model output is insensitive to the kLa (kLaco, kLaco2, kLah2) while the fermentation is

constant as the gas­liquid transfer becomes a limiting factor.

5.1.3
General model: Fit model to all experimental data at once
With the knowledge that the model is able to capture the main mechanisms of the individual
experiments, and with an understanding of the underlying pathway mechanisms for varying op­
erating conditions, the next objective is to create a general model that is representative of all six
experimental data sets. This general model aims to have a single set of kinetic parameters that
can handle the varying process conditions that were also covered by the different experiments.
The same workflow used to fit the model to one experiment was applied to the general model.
Optimal parameters for individual experiments as well as the generic parameter set fitted to all
six experiments are shown in Table 5.2.
Figure 5.4 illustrates all six experiments, which are divided into three groups based on the
composition of the inlet gases, review Table 4.1. For each case, the fit of the general model
is shown along with the measured data from the experiments. The general model has one
set of kinetic parameters (Table 5.2, column ”General”), but the gas mixture varies according
to each group. With a total deviation of 22.24%, the overall error of the model is highest for
experiments 1 and 2. The general model has a better fit for experiments 3 and 4 with an RMSE
of 18.76% and the best fit for experiments 5 and 6 with a deviation of only 13.83%. Thus, the
experiments in which T. kivui grows on CO alone have an overall better fit than the experiments
with two carbon sources. Since fewer parameters determine the performance of the system, the
sensitivity is higher than in the more complex parallel gas uptake system. When the duplicates of
an experimental setup do not show identical trends (e.g. biomass concentration in experiments
1 and 2, acetate of experiment 5 and 6), fitting the model to the experimental data is challenging
because the model attempts to balance all trends equally. The reason for this is the definition
of the error function, which gives equal weight to the error of the six experiments.
Looking more closely at the state variables of each fit, the biomass concentration has the lowest
RMSE, all below 11% for the three groups. The model captures the different stages during
the fermentation, including the exponential growth phase in all experiments, the differences in
the maximum biomass concentration depending on the gas concentrations in the feed and the
decrease of the concentration after 40h. The highest biomass concentration can be found in
experiment 3 and 4 at around 2 g/L, while in group 1 and 3 the maximum concentration is around

5.1.4

5.2
Numerical simulations
After model development and analysis are complete, the model is used for numerical simula­
tions to explore the process design space. Simulations can be an excellent tool to complement
experiments because of the non­linear and complex nature of the microbial system, and be­
cause the combinations of possible process conditions are manifold. The mechanistic model
has the advantage that it can be applied to areas of the design space where it hasn’t been
developed (e.g. different gas compositions). Linking simulations with experiments has the ad­
vantage of minimizing the time and resources required to explore the design space by reducing
the number of experiments. At the same time, the experimental data can be used to validate
the model and improve the understanding of the underlying mechanisms, thus improving the
performance of the model.
Dilution rate defines the rate at which liquid enters (without cells, acetate, gas) and exits (with
cells and acetate) the reactor. The assumption is that there will be no escape of gas through
dilution. While experimental data from fed­batch fermentations were used to fit the model, the
numerical simulations are run in continuous operation with the goal of achieving high productivity
and maximizing the space­time yield (STY) of acetate. In continuous fermentations, the space­
time yield of a compound is often preferred to the concentration due to varying dilution rates.
With high dilution rates, the concentration of a compound could be low but the productivity
high, resulting in more product than high accumulated concentrations in the reactor with low
productivity.
5.2.1
Optimization of the process design space
In addition to maximizing the space­time yield of acetate, the process must be carbon neutral.
This means that the process should not produce more CO2 than it consumes. If the consumption
of CO2 is even higher than the production, the process becomes carbon negative. These two
goals are contradictory because CO, which strongly promotes microbial growth, has CO2 as a
by­product. One extreme is that the microorganism grows on a H2:CO2 mixture, so the process
is always carbon negative. The other extreme is that the bacteria use only CO as a carbon and
energy source. This results in high growth and acetate production, but also in GHG production.
Acetate production, although based on effluent gas, would not be climate­friendly, especially on
an industrial scale.
Therefore, the optimal operating range is between these two extreme operating conditions.
Ideally, any gas composition of H2, CO and CO2 is possible. In order to always achieve 100%
in the gas mixture in the feed, the inert gas N2 can be added. This allows more variability in the
possible gas compositions. The dilution rate is another important parameter during optimization
as it controls the ratio of CO and CO2 consumption as well as concentration of cells and growth
inhibiting biomass. The impact of a process on CO2 can vary depending on the dilution rate,
because the rates at which the three gases are taken up depend on their concentrations in the
liquid phase.
5.2.1.1
Real scenarios: high and low CO syngas
Biomass gasification produces two common gas mixtures depending on the choice of CO2 or
steam as the gasification agent: high CO syngas (HCS) with a composition of H2:CO:CO2:N2
as 58:30:9:3 and low CO syngas (LCS) as 24:52:21:3. In the first optimization scenario, the
two gas compositions remain stable. However, the effect of the dilution rate on the two main
objectives is evaluated. Therefore, Figure 5.7 shows the STY of the acetate (on the left) and
the net rate of CO2 (on the right) versus the dilution rate.
At a dilution rate of 0.0 h­1 , the space­time yield of acetate is also close to zero for both
syngas compositions. With increasing dilution rate, both space­time yields increase rapidly
to their respective maximum of 0.01229 g/(Lh) at 0.04314 h­1 for HCS and a lower maximum of
0.006238 g/(Lh) for LCS at a slightly faster dilution rate of 0.0451 5h­1. Thus, HCS composition
in the feed results in higher acetate STYs, while LCS has about half the HCS rates. As the

5.2.1.2
Ideal combinations of gas mixture and dilution rate
As shown in the low and high CO syngas scenarios, the gas mix in the feed has a big impact on
the two objectives of maximizing acetate STY while maintaining carbon neutrality. Ideally, any
combination of the four gases can be used to feed. In this chapter, not only the dilution rate is
optimized, but also the gas composition. The result of this analysis is the Pareto front, which is
the set of operating points that are not dominated by any other operating point with respect to
the objective function. The search for the Pareto front is a genetic algorithm that is described
in subsection 4.3.1.
Figure 5.8 shows the net rate of CO2 versus the space­time yield of acetate for the operating
points on the Pareto front. The operating points consider the concentrations of H2, CO, CO2,
and N2 as well as the dilution rate with the condition for the gas mixture that the sum of all four
gases must equal 100%. The dilution rate can range from 0.0 h­1 to 0.5 h­1.
The overall trend of the Pareto front shows that the net rate of CO2 competes with the acetate
STY. Therefore, a negative net rate of CO2 is always associated with a lower space­time yield
of acetate, while a very high acetate productivity is associated with a positive net rate of CO2 .
The Pareto front can be divided into two parts. In the first part, the production of CO2 increases
faster than the space­time yield of acetate, which is represented by a steep linear correlation.
At a CO2 net rate of ­0.021 mol/(Lh) and a space­time yield of 0.0028 g/(Lh), the trend of the
Pareto front changes. This second part is driven by an increase in the STY of the acetate that
is faster than the increase in the CO2 net rate.

5.2.2
Time scale analysis
Another aspect of process optimization is knowing the stability and robustness of the process
to changes in the state variables (biomass, acetate, H2, CO, and CO2 concentrations) or other
deflection such as a stop or change in the inlet gas or of the dilution rate. A process is consid­
ered to be resilient if it can quickly return to its steady state after a perturbation in the steady
state. This system response can also be referred to as the ”characteristic time”, which can be
calculated for each model output based on the eigenvalue analysis of the linearized model.
5.2.2.1
Optimal gas feed composition against dilution rate
In this section, the steady state dynamics of the system is studied in response to process per­
turbations for varying dilution rates. To evaluate the time scales in a steady state, the Jacobian
matrix is decomposed. The characteristic time scale of a mode is the result of the eigenvalue
decomposition. Figure 5.11 shows the time scale of several modes for different dilution rates
while the gas mixture is held constant and contains 39% H2, 51% CO and 8% CO2. The modes
represent the state variables of the model, in this case the concentrations of biomass and ac­
etate, as well as the concentrations of the three gases in the feed. In the left plot, the overall
time scale of the entire system is represented by choosing the largest time scale of the modes
for each operating point, since the slowest reaction in the system will be the rate­limiting step
for reaching a steady state.
At a slow dilution rate of 0.01 h­1, the largest time scale is 71 h, meaning that it takes around
71 h that the system reaches its steady state again after a deflection in the process. In this case,
the slow time scales are caused by the H2 and CO2 concentration. Due to the low dilution rate,
the residence time is very high leading to high cell density and acetate accumulation. Since
acetate inhibits the growth, the biomass starts to decrease leading to lower uptake rates of
CO. For high concentrations of CO, the uptake of H2 and CO2 is inhibited. After perturbing the
system, it takes relatively long time to reach steady state again because of the long residence
time and complex interactions.
The time scale reduces with an increasing dilution rate to 9.07 h as fastest characteristic time

for a dilution rate of 0.185 h­1 . Due to the shorter residence time the acetate concentration

The analysis underscores the utility of time scale assessment as a powerful tool for under­

is also valuable for refining process control strategies. It enables more precise manipulation of
operating conditions to improve productivity, stability, and efficiency in bioprocesses.
In conclusion, incorporating the time scale into the analysis of operating points provides insight
into the stability of the process. The identified operating conditions for a carbon neutral process
with maximized acetate space­time yield were tested to be sufficiently stable and resilient to
process changes, and therefore promising operating conditions for further analysis.

Conclusion & Outlook
The first objective of the thesis was the systematic development, calibration and validation of
a mechanistic model that captures the dynamic behavior of T. kivui in a two­phase gas­liquid
bioreactor. A general model was built to represent the six available fed­batch experiments with
one set of kinetic parameters. The fed­batch experiments differ in the composition of the gas
feed. The model output showed a good performance (normalized RMSE < 22%) capable of
describing the underlying biological mechanisms including the uptake of the three gases as well
as the production of biomass, acetate and CO2. In addition, the model performance indicates
a correct identification of the kinetic parameters.
The second objective was to analyze the sensitivity of the model to its kinetic parameters. The
comparative sensitivity analysis for two experiments, one with CO2 and CO as gaseous carbon
source and one with CO alone as carbon source, provided valuable insights into the interre­
lated importance of the parameters for different operating conditions as well as different stages
throughout the fed­batch fermentation. It was shown that the CO pathway contributes strongly
to the production of biomass, resulting in high sensitivity measures for the parameters correlated
with CO uptake (δrmse
Ycox =17.17%, δrmse
qco,max=14.39%) compared to the CO2 pathway (δrmse
Yco2x=3.5%,
δrmse
qco2,max=2.91%). Process efficiency and stability is improved by knowing the key kinetic pa­
rameters at different stages such as exponential growth and stationary phases in order to make
targeted adjustments.
The analysis of the identifiability of the parameters with respect to the structure of the model
and the available experimental data was the third objective. The results provided insight into the
correlation of kinetic parameters such that linearly correlated parameters such as Ycox and kLaco,
ms and Ki,ac, and Kco and qco,max or non­linearly correlated parameters such as Ki,co and qco,max,
Ki,coh2 and qco2,max, and Ki,co and Kcowere detected. To obtain uniquely identifiable parameter
subsets for their estimation, these parameters were estimated in separate subsets. In addition,
the results of the uncertainty approximation are consistent with the sensitivity analysis. Kinetic
parameters with high sensitivity had a lower standard deviation (σYcox=2.5% and σqco,max=23.0%)
than less sensitive parameters (σKh2=55.9% and σkLaco2=52.1%).
For the fourth objective, the design space of operating conditions was explored and optimized
with respect to the two performance measures of maximizing the space­time yield of acetate
and minimizing the net rate of CO2 with the condition of achieving a carbon­neutral process. The
Pareto optimal solutions provided valuable insight into a set of equivalent operating conditions
for gas mixture and dilution rate, from which four solution sets were selected to cover the entire
Pareto front. The net rate of CO2 was minimized the most (rco2= ­0.0328 mol/(Lh)) with a gas
mixture of 85% H2, 3% CO, 9% CO2, 3% N2 and a dilution rate of d=0.074 h­1 at the cost of
a low space­time yield of acetate. In contrast, the space­time yield of acetate was maximized
to 0.0118 mol/(Lh) while maintaining carbon neutrality with a gas composition of 39:51:8:2 in %
for H2:CO:CO2:N2 and a dilution rate of 0.048 h­1.
In the next step, objective five, the selected solutions of the Pareto front were analyzed with
respect to their response behavior and the stability of the system. Time­scale analysis revealed
sufficiently stable process conditions for optimal acetate productivity with τmax = 23.23h. The
relevance of the method was especially emphasized for operating conditions that had similar
performance in net rate of CO2 and production rate of acetate, while differing in time scale (case
2: τmax = 23h and case 5: τmax = 34h). Therefore, this is an interesting objective in bioprocess
optimization.
In conclusion, the strength of the mechanistic model for gas fermentation with T. kivui is its
systematic and thorough development, ensuring the reliability of the model predictions within

its uncertainty measures. It fills a significant research gap and provides a valuable understand­
ing of the current state of knowledge on gas fermentation with T. kivui as a case study. The
integration of important methods in the model analysis, such as multi­objective optimization and
time­scale analysis, contributes to a broader applicability of mechanistic models in bioprocess
development.
Regarding the first objective, the limitation of the mechanistic model is the representation of the
CO metabolism and the related H2 production. Since the details of the CO pathway, its required
enzymes, their sensitivities, and the connection between CO2 and CO metabolism are still an
open research topic, it is not yet included in the model. As new research becomes available,
the model can be updated and revised. In addition, the model can be extended to correlate the
stirrer speed and aeration rate, which are not yet included, to the kLa for more refined control
and optimization of the bioprocess.
Objectives two and three are highly dependent on the quantity and quality of available exper­
imental data. In order to improve the sensitivity measures and the identifiability of the kinetic
parameters, new experiments could be designed and conducted with the aim of testing operat­
ing conditions where the as yet non­identifiable parameters are expected to have a significant
influence on the output. This would increase the confidence in the parameter estimates.
The identified solutions of the Pareto front are beyond the experimentally studied operating
space. Furthermore, the model has been calibrated using fed­batch experiments, while the
optimizing simulations were run continuously. Therefore, the solutions of interest are so far a
proposal and need to be experimentally validated. Similarly, the time­scales can be experimen­
tally verified by experimenting with process deflections after having reached a steady state.
Finally, time scale analysis can be incorporated into the multi­objective optimization problem.
Making time scale analysis a standard method for bioprocess design and optimization would
have the advantage of designing processes with high productivity, efficiency and stability. The
latter is very relevant for the control strategy of bioprocesses in the context of utilizing gaseous
carbon from industrial blast furnace gases, because their gas mixture could vary. Knowing
the influences of these variations on the process performance allows one to make targeted
adjustments.
