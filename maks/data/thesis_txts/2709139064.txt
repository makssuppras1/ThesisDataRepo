Abstract
To limit global warming to well below 1.5 °C, the energy sector needs to change from the
ground up. To achieve this, the global community needs to mobilise capital at an unprece-
dented scale to finance the clean energy transition. The energy sector has traditionally been
dominated by larger players, while other parts of society only had limited opportunity to
participate. The tokenisation of energy assets represents a novel approach to financing and
investing in energy projects, which aims to democratise the energy sector. Tokenisation is
the process of representing the value and ownership rights of a physical asset using digital,
blockchain-based certificates or tokens. A token-based system is intended to provide liquidity
to energy assets and reduce barriers, by automating legal and transactional processes. This
would allow for a more fractionalised ownership structure of energy projects and thereby
increase the accessibility of energy asset investments.
The aim of this thesis is to shed light on the concept of tokenising energy assets, understand
its potential consequences and opportunities, as well as analyse benefits it could bring to
individual energy assets and energy asset portfolios. To this end, the analysis will be con-
ducted in three steps. Firstly, industry interviews are used to assess the interest and activity
level in blockchain and tokenisation within relevant companies active in the sector. Secondly,
the impact of tokenisation of energy assets on project investor returns is analysed through
discounted cashflow models. Thirdly, the potential benefits that tokenised assets offer for
portfolio management are analysed through the use of Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT).
The results obtained in this thesis allow for optimism concerning applying tokenisation to
energy assets. While the novelty of blockchain technology and the lack of regulation represent
key barriers, interviewed energy experts support the idea of representing asset co-ownership
using blockchain-based tokens. Moreover, the analysis found that tokenisation can improve
energy project investor returns and optimise energy asset portfolios. This thesis was a first
attempt to analyse consequences and implications of using tokens to represent ownership in
energy projects. However, the research presented in this work could benefit from more focused
and expansive work and thus only scratches the surface of what is possible. The growing trends
towards the decentralisation and digitalisation of assets call for further investigations into the
topic of tokenisation, as this could be a unique opportunity to empower citizens to play a
central role in the clean energy transition.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Climate change is arguably humanity’s biggest challenge. Human influence on increasing the
temperature of Earth’s atmosphere is the driving force of past, current and future change
of the planet’s climate [3]. This is fueled by rising concentrations of carbon dioxide (CO2)
in the atmosphere, which today are likely higher than at any point in the past 3 million
years [4]. To mitigate this, the international community presented the Paris Agreement at
COP21 in 2015, which laid the foundation of limiting global warming two well below 2°C and
preferably to 1.5°C [5]. Since our society’s current way of life is prohibitive in reaching these
goals, immediate action in the form of deep reductions in carbon dioxide (CO2) and other
greenhouse gas emissions is required [3]. The International Energy Agency (IEA) developed
a strategy called Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Scenario (NZE), which is aiming to limit the
long-term increase of average global temperatures to 1.5°C. Reaching this goal will only be
possible, if all governments not only design, but especially successfully implement their energy
and climate policies and strategies [6]. As the IEA states, appropriate strategies entail the
"immediate and massive deployment of all available clean and efficient energy technologies".
To achieve this, annual investments in renewable electricity need to triple to limit global
warming to 1.5°C. By 2030, the combined global installed capacity of solar PV and wind
power needs to quadruple, relative to values of 2020 [6].
While awareness about the need for investment is rising, private companies and investors
who have the means to deploy capital are lacking adequate high-quality energy projects
[7]. This is exacerbated by the fact that financing needs and risks differ between developed
and developing countries, making such investments less attractive for private companies and
institutional investors [7, 8]. This however might not be the case for individual citizens or
communities that would like to invest in or co-own energy projects. As has been pointed out in
[3] and [9], an effective and far reaching clean energy transition can only be successful through
awareness, participation and support across society. Today there is an issue with investments
in renewable energy.
Large market players and other financial institutions dominate this
field, due to legal, transactional and financial barriers for smaller entities. More investment
and societal participation is needed for an effective clean energy transition, but there are few
simple, affordable and flexible services offering investment opportunities. The rise of new
digital technologies, such as blockchain, might provide opportunities to innovate. Blockchain
has the advantage that it allows for transactions in a secure, transparent and efficient manner
and most importantly automate legal and transactional processes [10, 11].
1.2
Tokenised Energy Assets - A new Sustainable Investment Class
A blockchain-based approach that could tackle both the issue of increasing investment in
energy projects, as well as allowing people to play a bigger role in the energy transition is
the tokenisation of energy assets. Tokenisation is the process of representing the value and
ownership rights of a physical asset using digital, blockchain-based certificates, which are also
called tokens [10, 11]. These tokens are legal representations of the tokenised asset and can be

traded with other investors [10, 11]. Such a token-based system enables a more fractionalised
ownership structure, allowing for multiple co-owners. This can have benefits both for people
interested in participating in the energy sector, as well as private or institutional investors
that are hesitating to commit capital to illiquid renewable energy projects. By acquiring these
tokens, individual as well as community investors have a low-cost way of purchasing shares in
energy assets. Through this, they will be able to obtain the rights to a part of the financial,
social and environmental benefits, which the tokenised asset will generate throughout its
lifetime. In turn, private or institutional investors can profit from the fractionalisation of
energy assets by diversifying and optimising their portfolios, easier comply with sustainability
requirements and invest smaller shares into projects that they did not find attractive before
the possibility of a more optimal diversification. It is important to mention, that not only
newly developed energy projects, but also already existing energy projects can be tokenised
and thus provide a way for the original asset equity holders to manage their asset exposure.
Tokenisation could have potentially disruptive effects on the energy sector. By tokenising
energy projects, previously illiquid and non-tradable assets can be turned into a more broadly
available investment product similar to securities. These tokens can be used as individual
investment products or combined into energy asset portfolios similar to exchange traded funds
(ETF). Just like ETFs, energy asset portfolios would allow for a broader diversification and
thereby reduction of risk.
Energy asset owners that want to manage their exposure and
diversify their portfolio can do so, by acquiring shares of multiple different energy assets to
combine them into their energy asset portfolios. Correspondingly, institutional investors or
companies can use energy asset portfolios to comply with sustainability requirements and
in the process inject more capital in the energy sector. However, the implementation of a
token-based system that is used to manage and transfer ownership in energy assets would
have significant implications for the energy sector, some of which are explored further in this
thesis.
1.3
Literature Review
Within this literature review, a few aspects relevant to the concept of tokenisation will be
investigated. These topics include alternative approaches to energy co-ownership, blockchain
and tokenisation in the energy sector as well as the application of modern portfolio theory to
energy assets.
1.3.1
Alternative Approaches to Energy Co-Ownership
Since tokenisation is supposed to allow for co-ownership of energy projects, it is important
to investigate already existing alternatives. The main alternatives include energy commu-
nities and energy crowdfunding. The Clean Energy Package (CEP) of the European Union
[12] provides two definitions for energy communities, namely renewable energy communities
[13] and citizen energy communities [14]. According to the European federation of renew-
able energy cooperatives (REScoop.eu), which bases its definition on the definitions shown
in the CEP, an energy community is "a way to ‘organise’ citizens that want to cooperate
together in an energy-sector related activity based on open and democratic participation and
governance, so that the activity can provide services or other benefits to the members or the
local community" [15]. The stated primary purpose of energy communities is the "provision
of environmental, economic and social community benefits for its shareholders or members
of the local areas where it operates, rather than financial profits" [15]. Although the con-

cept of energy communities sounds similar to what tokenisation wants to accomplish, there
are key differences which are highlighted hereafter. Already the primary purpose of energy
communities contains two aspects that tokenisation aims to alter. Firstly, the purpose of the
proposed concept of tokenisation is to provide access not only to local members of energy
communities, but instead to a broader part of society - and ultimately everyone. Secondly,
tokenised assets are intended to be used as investment products and thus, not only societal
and environmental benefits are of importance, but also financial profits. Moreover, according
to REScoop.eu, medium and larger enterprises are barred from controlling or managing the
community and especially for renewable energy communities, the controlling entity must be in
close proximity to the project [15]. This again goes against the idea of allowing co-ownership
of larger projects, since larger projects are typically managed by larger entities.
Another approach to co-financing or co-owning energy assets is energy crowdfunding, which
aims to expand on the fundamentals of energy communities, by removing the locality re-
quirements and allowing for a more international crowd to participate in energy projects [16].
Under the crowdfunding umbrella, there are multiple ways to participate, typically through
loans, bonds, equity and donations [16, 17, 18]. While crowdfunding platforms provide differ-
ent offerings to a broader and more international investor community, the investors of most
crowdfunding platforms are faced with impeded abilities to exit or realise their investment
[17, 18]. The European Commission identified the absence of a secondary market as one of the
key risks for customers of crowdfunding platforms [18]. This issue can also be observed on a
platform that collects information about around 35 green energy crowdfunding projects [19].
The lack of a secondary market is especially relevant for equity-based crowdfunding, where
the investment is intended to be recovered by selling one’s shares to another investor. The
main reasons why flexible or efficient trades between investors are currently impossible are
missing pricing mechanisms, slow or complex settlement processes and investor matchmak-
ing [18]. Like other private equity investments, equity crowdinvestments are unlisted equity
investments, which carry the risk of missing liquidity or non-tradability. A blockchain-based
approach that can mitigate the aforementioned challenges is the tokenisation of energy assets
[20]. The Organisation for Economic Development (OECD) as well as the United Nations
Environment Program (UNEP) state that increased tradability, liquidity and transparency
as well as the ability to automate legal and transactional processes are the key competitive
advantages of blockchain-based products [10, 20].
1.3.2
Blockchain and Tokenisation
Blockchain is a new database system in the form of a distributed records keeping system (or
ledger) which does not depend on a central authority [21, 11]. Its processes are defined by a
consensus algorithm, which allows users that do not need to trust each other to interact with
one another [11]. Blockchain adoption has been growing exponentially over the past years, at-
tracting at its peak around 3 trillion dollars in November 2021 [22], which represented around
7.5 % of global (M1) narrow money supply at that time [23]. With its rapid adoption and
technological development, increasing numbers of new use cases and applications in different
sectors have been explored. Within the energy sector, blockchain applications are not new.
A meta-study of the energy sector has analysed 140 different companies and startups actively
trying to implement blockchain technology in their projects [1]. These applications include
decentralised energy trading, energy metering and billing as well as decentralised security.
While many of the applications focus on the aspect of producing and trading energy, some
projects also look at green certificates and carbon trading. Overall, the main purpose of the
applications highlighted in this study is to facilitate a more intelligent way to manage assets

and their production [1]. However, the applications showcased in this paper do not include
any form of tokenising the equity of energy assets.
While tokens have existed since the creation of bitcoin in 2008 [24], the concept of using tokeni-
sation as a means to finance projects like infrastructure or energy projects is relatively new in
research. In [25], the ability of tokenisation to address market failures that green investment
vehicles are facing today is assessed. In [26], a case study about the first regulation-compliant
energy asset token is presented. Both highlight the fact that due to the nascent nature of the
technology, tokenised infrastructure or energy projects have only been implemented at the
pilot stage. Nevertheless, some prominent examples that rely on real asset tokenisation have
started surfacing since 2015, when NASDAQ launched its distributed ledger platform called
NASDAQ Linq, that allows placement and trades of tokenised assets [27]. A project similar
to NASDAQ Linq was announced in 2019 as SDX, a digital exchange platform of the Swiss
Stock Exchange by SIX [28]. Other examples include Mt Pelerin Group SA, the first company
that tokenised its shares in 2018 [29, 10], or Equisafe who was one of the first companies to
tokenise a real estate project[30]. These use cases are discussed in more detail in section 2.1.5.
1.3.3
Applying Modern Portfolio Theory to Energy Assets
A use case of the tokenisation of energy assets is portfolio management, which can be studied
using modern portfolio theory (MPT). The MPT postulates that asset performance not only
includes return, but also risk [31]. An approach that takes both metrics into account is the
mean-variance analysis methodology which was first proposed by Markowitz in 1952 [31] for
which he received the Nobel Prize. Markowitz’ approach allows not only to rate different
assets based on their risk-return profiles, but also allows to develop the optimal portfolio that
maximises return while minimising risk [31]. Modern portfolio theory has been applied in the
research of energy assets and energy generation planning as an alternative to the traditional
least-cost approach to investment planning, especially by Awerbuch [32, 33, 34, 35]. In his
work, Awerbuch focuses on electricity generation planning and energy security analysis, which
takes into account the risk induced by fuel price volatility [32, 34] as well as operational,
construction, maintenance costs and planning period risks [33]. Moreover, he analyses the
role of wind generation for Scotland’s energy diversity and security [35], and how portfolio
theory can be used to generate portfolios that minimise CO2 emissions [34]. Other studies
analysed electricity generation portfolios on a country or regional level, such as Portugal [36],
Italy [37] or the EU [33, 34]. Moreover, modern portfolio theory has been used to analyse
risk implications of renewable support instruments [38, 39]. In [40], the author focuses on the
investor’s perspective, when applying modern portfolio theory to energy assets. In [40], the
expected net present value (ENPV) per unit of capacity of an investment is used to measure
the return of different energy technologies. This is practical, since the authors based their
analysis on dispatchable baseload generation technologies and assume equal capacity factors.
In this thesis however, the focus is laid on dispatchable technologies (biomass, natural gas)
as well as non-dispatchable technologies (solar PV, offshore wind). Due to this, the approach
from [40] is not viable. Instead, the focus is laid upon measuring asset returns using the
internal rate of return (IRR).

2.1
Blockchain and Tokenisation
The internet is a prime example of how innovation disrupted all parts of society.
In the
beginning, the first version of the world wide web - also called Web1 - was very simple [21].
Consumers were only able to read and search information that was broadcast by companies.
Around the Dotcom Bubble in 2000 at the peak of the early internet hype, the term Web2
was coined, which refers to the front-end disruption of how people interact with the internet
[21, 41]. Web2 now allowed people to produce content and write information on the internet,
as opposed to simply consuming it [41]. Still today, we see ongoing innovation through website
design, software-as-a-service (SaaS), social networks and internet-based monetary services.
Web2 platforms and products all have something in common: they depend on a central player
or authority to grant access and process interactions between their servers, services or their
users [21, 41]. This is where the Web3 innovation comes in, which includes blockchain and
cryptocurrencies. In contrast to Web2, which was a front-end disruption, Web3 is a back-end
disruption [21]. It completely changes the way computers interact with the internet and how
the internet is ’wired’. Today, in order to search for information or to process transactions,
one needs to interact with Google’s servers or use the bank’s portal, who both hold all the
data and authority. Web3 instead proposes a decentralised approach, where people instead of
big corporations own the network. By using their own computers, people can provide services
such as computation for the network, which is remunerated through rewards. There is no
central authority, that manages user queries or transactions and thus also no central entity
that profits from holding all the decision power [21].
2.1.1
The Definition of Blockchain
In short, a blockchain is a new concept of a database, in the form of a distributed records
keeping system (or ledger) which does not depend on a central authority [10, 11]. Blockchain is
commonly seen as a digital ledger technology (DLT). Its processes are defined by a consensus
algorithm, which allows users, that do not have to trust each other, to safely interact with each
other [11]. The term ’chain-of-blocks’ has been first coined in late 2008 by Satoshi Nakamoto -
a pseudonym for an unknown person or group - in their famous white paper about Bitcoin [24].
Within this paper, Nakamoto proposes a decentralised peer-to-peer electronic cash system,
that allows for monetary transactions between two parties, without the need for a central
authority to process the transaction. This system relies on mainly two things [24]:

and in case one wants to participate, they only need a computer that is connected to
the network.

2.1.3
The Definition of Tokens and their Regulation
Before being able to define tokenisation, one needs to understand what tokens are and what
types of tokens exist. According to [21], "cryptographic tokens represent programmable assets
or access rights, managed by a smart contract and an underlying distributed ledger. They
are accessible only by the person who has the private key for that address and can only be
signed using this private key". The European Union defines crypto-assets (or tokens) as "a
digital representation of value or rights which may be transferred and stored electronically,
using distributed ledger technology or similar technology" [48]. In other words, tokens are
digital certificates or a code snippets, that can only be accessed by people with the required
credentials. Bitcoin and Ethereum also represent tokens.
Regulation on blockchain and cryptocurrencies is currently still in development.
Among
governments that are looking at regulating blockchain and cryptocurrencies, the Swiss gov-
ernment is one of the most advanced. The Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority
(FINMA) has developed its own categorisation of tokens, based on their use cases or appli-
cation [49]:
• Payment Tokens: Payment tokens (synonymous with cryptocurrencies) are tokens
which are intended to be used, now or in the future, as a means of payment for acquiring
goods or services or as a means of money or value transfer. Cryptocurrencies give rise
to no claims on their issuer.
• Utility Tokens: Utility tokens are tokens which are intended to provide access digitally
to an application or service by means of a blockchain-based infrastructure.
• Asset / Security Tokens: Asset tokens represent assets such as a debt or equity claim
on the issuer. Asset tokens promise, for example, a share in future company earnings
or future capital flows. Therefore, in terms of their economic function these tokens are
analogous to equities, bonds or derivatives.
Tokens which enable physical assets to be traded on the blockchain fall into the category of
asset / security tokens. Tokens that represent energy assets therefore are under the category

2.1.4
The Definition of Tokenisation
Tokenisation is the process of digitally representing an existing, real (off-chain) asset on a
distributed ledger [11]. For this, assets that exist in reality and not on the chain (therefore
off-chain) are moved onto the chain, by creating tokens that represent these assets [10].
Assets that are tokenised are "moved on-chain" and are represented by tokens, that carry
information and rights of the asset.
This means that not only the data of these asset is
tracked on the chain. Tokens can also carry information about their value, their ownership
and their historical transactions [10, 11]. Especially when considering ownership, tokenising
assets can have real world legal effects, as ownership can now be traded by trading tokens [10].
It is important to note, that physical assets that are tokenised continue to exist in the real
world (i.e. off-chain) [10]. The tokenisation of energy asset refers to moving the information,
ownership rights and value of the energy project onto the chain and represent it using digital
tokens.

2.1.6
The Benefits of Tokenisation
Before considering tokenisation, one should ask themselves, which problem it is supposed to
solve and if there are measurable benefits for implementing it. Tokenisation in itself is nothing
else than a new technological approach to facilitate processes, which may or may not show
benefits compared to the current approach. This is also highlighted by the EU [48] as well
as the Swiss FINMA [49], as they emphasise the importance of technology-neutral policies
for sectors where blockchain and tokenisation could be beneficial. Some of the benefits of
tokenisation are highlighted below [10, 11]:
• Fractional Ownership: Tokens enable fractional ownership of assets and thus enables
investors to acquire small fractions instead of the whole asset. The ownership of to-
kenised assets can thereby divided up between multiple investors, reducing investment
barriers and allowing them to manage their risk better.
• Access to Financing: Companies or projects that for some reason are unable to obtain
funding could use tokenisation as a means to acquire financing from investors. Reasons
why such companies or projects are unable to obtain financing include legal barriers for
retail investors to buy into private companies, minimum ticket sizes for investors, high
legal and transactional costs or a missing market.
• Liquidity: Asset tokenisation can turn previously illiquid assets in liquid and tradable
counterparts. Non-liquid assets suffer under the liquidity-premium investors have to pay.
Tokenisation reduces these illiquidity-premia and thus makes assets more attractive.
Moreover, investors might be scared off from non-liquid assets, since they do not want
to worry about having to find a counterparty, in case the investor would like to sell.
Tokenising assets could therefore be beneficial to assets that traditionally have been
illiquid, such as real estate or similar assets like energy projects.
• Efficiency Gain and Cost Reduction: Faster, more efficient and more transparent
transaction structure through "disintermediation". The automation provided by smart
contracts allows for the removal of costly intermediaries that slow down processes and
transactions. For instance, notarial services can completely be replaced by code that
replaces such services. This reduces costs and increases speed of transactions and the
overall settlement process.

• Democratisation of Investments: Tokens enable fractional ownership of previously
unaffordable assets and thus enables the average investor to invest and co-own assets

• Increased Security: As there is no single point of failure in a blockchain but instead,

2.1.7
Challenges, Risks and Barriers of Tokenisation
A token-based system and on a broader perspective a blockchain or DLT-based system can
provide benefits to certain applications or systems. However there are a number of challenges
or barriers that need to be overcome, in order to make it commercially available. The stated
disadvantages or barriers have been sourced from [10, 21] and [11] where industry experts
provided their insights.
• Lack of Regulation: According to the literature sources, the lack of regulation is
the primary challenge or barrier that blockchain needs to overcome in order to become
commercially viable. The legal risks induced by unclear rules discourages DLT-based
projects and products to move forward. Different jurisdictions have made different levels
of progress to regulating blockchain systems and tokens and thus, diverging and missing
legal rules are the key inhibitor of blockchain expansion into new markets.
• Reluctance to Change Business Practices: The adoption of blockchain-based prod-
ucts or services inevitably leads to changes in the processes of a business. However the
lack of field knowledge or skills and directly tangible benefits as well as considerable
implementation times are currently making blockchain adoption less attractive for busi-
nesses.
• Cybersecurity Risks: By moving certain services and operations on a blockchain, it
is inevitable that they become exposed to cyber-attacks. While blockchain is intended
to be a highly secure technology, new advances in computational power (i.e. quantum
computers) or new attack strategies might be able to abuse certian vulnerabilities. Addi-
tionally, more sophisticated blockchain projects which are based on smart contracts, are
faced with a so-called smart contract risk. Smart contracts provide extended function-
alities to DLTs and therefore also carry more opportunities for cyber attacks, compared
to potential conventional solutions.
• Cost-Benefit Tradeoff: As blockchain applications are still relatively niche, the cost
of moving from a legacy system towards a DLT-based system might be inflated. Because
of this, the benefits a blockchain-based system might provide must significantly outweigh
the costs it would require to set it up, leading to high investment requirements.

2.2.2
Combining Assets to Create Portfolios
Assets like power plants can be combined together to create a portfolio. Such a diversification
strategy wants to make use of the fact, that the performance of assets is not identical over
time. The financial world typically follows the principle of not placing all eggs in one basket
- meaning that one should not invest all of their funds in one asset, but instead spread
the risk across multiple assets with low correlation - which is called diversification.
The
lower the correlation between assets, the higher is the portfolio diversification effect, when
combining assets into a portfolio. For instance, a strategy that involves investments in a
renewable energy company as well as a healthcare company might provide opportunities for
diversification, since the risk of a worsening market outlook within healthcare would not
impact the renewable energy company. The risk presented here is called unsystematic risk
as the source of risk only affects one asset within the portfolio. Portfolio diversification can
be used to mitigate such risk, however diversification cannot avoid systematic risk, which is
a type of risk that affects the whole economy. If the whole economy is suffering, most likely
all asset will suffer from that and diversification is not able to remove that risk.

2.2.4
Minimum Variance Frontier and Efficient Frontier
As part of mean-variance analysis, Markowitz postulates that based on a pool of available
assets, it is possible to determine the portfolio that minimises risk for each given level of return
[31]. Conversely, it is possible to determine the portfolio that maximises return, for each given
level of risk. Both approaches yield the same results, as solutions for each risk/return level
are global optima within the sphere of available assets. The result of both approaches is a so
called minimum-variance frontier (MVF) which represents the set of portfolios that minimise
risk for different levels of return. This means that for portfolios that exist on this frontier,
there is no better way to combine risky assets which would achieve a lower risk for given
levels of return. In other words, if an investor requires a certain level of return, the minimum-
variance frontier provides them with the single best portfolio composition to minimise their
risk. An example of such a MVF is shown in figure 2.3.
As can be seen, there are sections of the MVF which show lower risk levels than the lowest risk
asset. This is possible due to the portfolio diversification effect. Portfolio risk is a function
of asset standard deviations as well as the correlation coefficient between assets (equation
2.6). Combining negatively correlated assets can result in portfolios with lower portfolio risk,
compared to the lowest risk level of included assets. When considering portfolio diversification
effects, it is essential to understand that the influence of a single security on portfolio risk is
driven by the covariance of its returns with the returns of other securities, rather than the
individual variance of said security. The effectiveness of portfolio diversification is dictated
by the correlation among securities.

2.2.5
Optimising Portfolios
Since any portfolio that is located on the efficient frontier is efficient, and efficient portfolios
being portfolios that have the highest return for a given risk level, the global optimal portfolio
of risky assets must be located on the efficient frontier. However, the notion of an ‘optimal’
portfolio does not imply a portfolio with no risk or highest possible returns, but instead
a portfolio that provides the best trade-off between risk and return, given a certain market
environment. In 1964, William Sharpe developed a framework called the Capital Asset Pricing
Model (CAPM) [56], which allows to compare the risk-return profiles of different portfolios,
for which he was awarded a Nobel Prize. The CAPM states that in order to obtain a higher
return than the risk-free rate (i.e. an excess return), one needs to accept a higher risk [56]. A
way to compare the trade-off between excess returns and risk between portfolios is the Sharpe
ratio, proposed by William Sharpe in 1966 [57]. The Sharpe ratio can be calculated for each
individual portfolio to assess the risk-return trade-off individually.
Sharpe Ratio =

through the highest possible slope which lies tangent to the efficient frontier. The CAL that
lies tangent to the efficient frontier is always the line with the highest slope. Moreover, the
point where the tangent line and the efficient frontier meet represents the optimal portfolio.
Following this, determining the optimal portfolio from a given pool of assets can be done by
finding the portfolio that maximises the slope of the CAL, or in other words that maximises
the Sharpe ratio.

3.2
Impact of Token-Based Exit Strategies on Investor Returns
This section aims to answer research question 2, which deals with the impact of token-based
exit strategies on investor returns. An exit or divestment strategy is a strategy of an investor
which is intending to sell part, or all of their equity within a project to another investor. To
model the impact of such transactions, a two-step framework has been developed. The first
step includes the valuation of an example offshore wind project off the coast of Denmark, while
the second step includes the analysis of interactions between three different investors. This
interaction is modelled through so-called secondary and tertiary equity sales. The desired
outcome of the first step are quantitative results that describe the financial performance of
the offshore wind farm. These results are used as input parameters for the second step, where
a conventional exit strategy is compared with two token-based exit strategies. The reasons
why tokenisation is relevant are the increased liquidity and accessibility a token-based system
is presumed to enable. This gives rise to new approaches or strategies for investors to recover
their investment in energy projects.

3.2.1
Step 1: Valuation and Return of Offshore Wind Farm
Within this first step, an example offshore wind farm project is analysed using a discounted
cashflow model approach to determine the asset’s financial performance. For this, methods
of asset valuation presented in [53, 60, 54] are used. A metric that sees major use in asset
and project valuation is the net present value of discounted cash flows, or simply NPV . This
metric is used as a decision-making tool to decide, whether an investment should be pursued or
not. A NPV > 0 indicates a profitable investment and, therefore, the final investment decision
(FID) to pursue this project should be taken. Since the NPV depends on the invested capital,
it is not always practical to use the NPV as a metric for different project sizes. A measure
that allows the comparison of different asset returns while being agnostic towards investment
sizes is the internal rate of return (IRR), which is defined as the discount rate, at which the
NPV of a project is equal to 0. Similar to the NPV, the IRR can be used for decision-making
whether to proceed on an investment or not. For instance, if a company assumes a discount
rate of 10 %, any project with a higher IRR than 10 % would result in a positive NPV.
Therefore, a project with an IRR greater than the company’s assumed discount rate should
result in a FID to pursue the project.
The analysis of power plant returns is conducted
through a pre-tax bankability analysis, which includes annual loan related payments in the
form of loan repayments and interest payments. This approach is taken because it allows
to analyse cashflows paid to equity shareholders and thereby, the internal rate of return of
equity injections into the project.

EBITDA and CFADS
Whenever a project is financed through debt, i.e. the developer borrows money to finance the
project, a bankability analysis is conducted to analyse, whether the project is bankable under
the attainable financing conditions. The analysis typically consists of two parts. The first
part is a discounted cashflow model that determines the cashflows available for debt-service
(CFADS), i.e. the amount of money that is available to repay the loan. The second part
consists of determining the annual installments required to pay back the loan as characterised
by the loan conditions. If the projects generates more cash through its operations than it
consumes through the repayment of the debt, a project is considered bankable. A metric that
is used to analyse this is the so-called debt-service coverage ratio (DSCR) which is defined as
the quotient of the CFADS and the annual loan repayment. A DSCR of ≥1 indicates that
in this specific year, the project generated more cash than it demanded for loan repayments.
Figure 3.2 showcases the general structure of such an analysis. In this case, the project has
been analysed in a pre-tax bankability analysis which focuses on a period of 28 years. The first
three years up until the commercial operation date (COD) represent the construction period
and the remaining 25 years represent the operational period of the offshore wind project.

Secondary and Tertiary Equity Sales
The transfer of equity between investors is modelled based on the secondary equity sales
methodology showcased in [72]. Project developers (primary investors) who take on most
of the project’s risk may look for a way to exit the project, by selling part or all of their
equity. For this, there must be a secondary investor who is ready to purchase the amount the
primary investor wants to sell. The reason why primary investors might want to sell is the
fact that they would like to be remunerated for taking on a higher risk and sell the project at
a premium. This can be achieved by finding a secondary investor who is ready to purchase the
project at a higher price and therefore accepts a lower level of return, since once the project
is built and operating, the risk is significantly reduced. Figure 3.4 shows a simplified cashflow
model that includes the project transaction from the primary to the secondary investor.
Figure 3.4: Cashflow Model of Secondary Equity Sale and Impact on Equity IRR [2]
As can be seen, the project sale acts as a cashflow-effective entry in the cashflow model and
thereby affects the return investors can obtain. The higher the sale value of the project, the
higher the return the primary investor can achieve. In turn, the higher the return of the
secondary investor, the lower the purchase value of the project. Note that in the extreme
case, the secondary investor demands the same return as the primary investor. In this case,
the purchase value enters the cashflow model at its original valuation. The purchase value

energy asset portfolio, a two-step simulation framework consisting of Monte Carlo simulations
of discounted cashflow (DCF) models as well as modern portfolio theory has been developed
(figure 3.6). The baseline for this analysis is a conceptual base case portfolio that consists of
four assets in equal parts (25 % each). These assets include an offshore wind farm, a solar PV
farm, a natural gas power plant as well as a biomass combined heat & power (CHP) plant
- all assumed to be located in Denmark, more specifically in the price zone Denmark-West
(DK1).

In the second step, the resulting asset returns are used as inputs for mean-

as a whole into their portfolios, leading to sub-optimal portfolios. This is because the global
optimal portfolio can only be achieved, when fractions of assets can be traded in an unlimited
liquidity scenario. To model such differences in optimality, the efficient frontiers and optimal
portfolios under different liquidity scenarios are determined. This is done to show that while
tokenising energy assets might bring benefits, it is not necessarily required to tokenise 100
% of the asset’s equity. It is important to mention that, since the liquidity is assumed to
be provided by tokenising energy asset shares, the terms liquidity scenarios and tokenisation
scenarios are used interchangeably throughout this section.
3.3.1
Step 1: Monte Carlo Simulations to obtain Asset Return Distributions
The first step of this two-step modelling framework consists of a post-tax financial analysis
of the specified energy technologies using a discounted cashflow model.
For each energy
technology, 10,000 scenarios capturing electricity price volatility, carbon price volatility as
well as uncertainty within investment and operational costs are simulated using Monte Carlo
simulations. The discounted cashflow analysis is conducted for a period of 25 years from 2022
to 2047 and thus, the assumed lifetime for each energy technology amounts to 25 years. For
all uncertain parameters it is assumed that they are normally distributed and therefore can
be described using the mean and standard deviation of the distribution. The financial model
and the Monte Carlo simulations have been coded in the open-source Python programming
language [73] and processed using the high performance computing (HPC) services provided
by the Computing Center of the Technical University of Denmark (DCC). For the processing,
12 cores with 2 gigabytes of memory per core have been used. The code can be found on the
author’s GitHub page [74].

Chapter 5
Discussion of Results and Methodologies
The overall goal of the thesis was to analyse the concept of using tokenised energy assets as
a new sustainable investment class which aims to improve citizen participation and increase
investment in renewable energy. For this, the analysis was focused on understanding implica-
tions and requirements for a token-based system, while assessing consequences and potential
benefits of applying tokenisation to individual energy assets and energy asset portfolios. The
overall findings presented in this thesis allow for optimism about tokenising energy assets,
not only from the perspectives of interviewed experts, but also from the quantitative analysis
conducted throughout this thesis. The discussion is broken down into three sections, namely
expert interviews, the impact of tokenisation on project returns and using tokenisation as a
portfolio and risk management tool. Each section contains discussion of results as well as the
applied methodology. Since obtainable results directly depend on the methodology, critical
reflection on both aspects are provided.
5.1
Expert Interviews
5.1.1
Results
While the expert interviews showed that all interview partners support the idea of representing
asset co-ownership using blockchain-based tokens, they also provided valuable feedback on
which requirements need to be fulfilled for a token-based system to work. The reasons why
interview partners support the concept are the potential benefits and implications tokenisation
could bring to the energy sector. Indeed, two main arguments for using this technology are
the facts that it allows for much more transparent ownership structures and that it reduces
barriers for investing into assets, which were previously unavailable or non-tradable.
As
stated by the experts, citizen co-ownership of energy assets is an essential part of increasing
the public acceptance of clean energy investments and energy transition measures. This has
also been highlighted in [9] and [80] which state that "all forms of participation can increase
citizens’ sense of ownership and community unity, as well as raise awareness, acceptance and
active support for the energy transition". Despite unanimous support for citizen participation
in the form of co-ownership, only 2 out of 6 interviewed companies are currently enabling
individuals to invest in energy projects. Interview partners state high costs in the form of
legal and transactional expenses as well as time commitments as the reasons why they are
not active in this field. Another key downfall of co-owning energy assets is the complex legal
structure as well as the non-tradability of ownership. The company of an interview partner
has solved some of the aforementioned issues by creating a special-purpose-vehicle (SPV)
inside the SPV of an energy project with the sole purpose of representing and managing
the share of co-owners. However, even in this structure, the non-tradability of co-ownership
shares remains a key problem and could put off other investors from participating.
The general sentiment outlined above creates a unique opportunity for a token-based co-
ownership system, when considering the benefits of a blockchain-based system. As stated
in [10] and [11], the deployment of smart contracts can reduce legal and transactional costs
through automation. The inherent structure of the blockchain allows for an easy transfer of
ownership with low transaction fees, solving the issue of non-tradability [10]. Since tokens
==PAGE:62==
54
Discussion of Results and Methodologies
allow for a fractionalisation of ownership and thus lower investment ticket sizes, investments
are open to a broader part of society. Similar projects that aim to tokenise physical assets
have already been realised in the real world, as shown in previous sections. A key difference
to the other applications however is the question about ownership rights.
The interview
partners have been probed for 5 different rights that a token should provide to the token
holder. Among these, profit rights and equal ownership status have been unanimously agreed
upon, while voting rights, operational decision rights and strategic decision rights have been
contested. In essence, the general opinion was that tokens could provide rights similar to
stocks, which would also follow the Swiss regulatory framework of Switzerland where tokens
tokens that represent physical assets (security tokens) have been declared as securities [49].
The co-ownership structure of a token-based system can be set similar to the characteristics
discussed above and in section 4.1.1. An embedded SPV intended to represent the co-owned
share of the project can be created inside of the SPV that contains the whole project. By
tokenising the embedded SPV, individuals as well as institutional investors now have a low
cost way to purchase shares of the energy asset.
A similar approach that tokenises the
SPV of a project has also been taken in the case of tokenising infrastructure projects in
[81]. Once an individual or institution purchases a token, they obtain certain agreed upon
rights (i.e. rights discussed in section 4.1) and, in case they would like to exit the investment,
they can sell the token to another investor. While a token-based system might provide certain
benefits, implementing this system will require skills that are not available in all organisations.
Companies with previous experience in blockchain might be better positioned for adopting
such an technology.
Despite all the potential benefits discussed in the interviews and highlighted in this section,
there are key issues that might prevent the deployment of token-based systems, most notably
the lack of appropriate regulation and the failure of the regulatory body to provide clear and
decisive regulation on this matter. According to the experts, regulatory issues are not only
relevant for a potential future application of tokenised energy assets, but instead are also
relevant for today’s blockchain applications in the industry. Unless regulatory bodies move
forward in providing clear regulation, the deployment of token-based projects or services
might be too risky. The lack of clear regulation has also been highlighted in [11] as the key
inhibiting factor of blockchain deployment. Additionally, as for all investment products, one
needs to clarify who should be allowed to invest. This is especially relevant for tokenised
energy assets, since energy projects are not only of societal interest, but instead geopolitically
relevant. Because of this, the experts highlighted the importance of an investor identification
system similar to the systems used today. Experts emphasise that the energy sector will
hesitate to adopt such a system unless the above mentioned requirements have been met.
5.1.2
Methodology
As stated before, the aim of the interviews was to better understand interest and activity
levels of industry players in blockchain and tokenisation and how a token-based system might
look like. There are a few potential approaches that can be taken to investigate this, such
as structured, unstructured or semi-structured interviews. Because of the novel nature of the
concept of tokenisation, semi-structured interviews were chosen. Semi-structured interviews
are suitable for interviews that aim to compare answers of respondents while allowing for
open-ended questions and discussions, and are suitable for topics the interview partners do
not necessarily know about in more detail [82]. The questionnaire was purposely developed
so that participants discuss the concept of tokenising energy assets. As expected with the

5.2
Impact of Token-Based Exit Strategies on Investor Returns
5.2.1
Results
Part of this thesis was to test one of the use cases that have been stated by the experts in
the conducted interviews. They stated that nowadays, divestment from an energy project is
typically done using a lump divestment strategy (case 1), where a large part or the whole
project is sold.
This is done since such sales always carry significant transactional costs.
The aspiration of tokenisation is to reduce these costs and enable new divestment strategies.
Considering this, experts proposed to model more individualised divestment strategies, such
as the linear (case 2) and exponential (case 3) divestment cases analysed in this thesis, and
compare them with the conventional lump divestment strategy.
As discussed above, the
divestment strategies have been modelled according to the equity sales approach shown in
[72].
As shown in the analysis, although conventional divestment strategies with defined require-
ments can be used to increase investor returns, token-based strategies are able to further
increase these returns. The conventional divestment strategy is able to yield a higher return
(13.87 % at 100 % divestment) for the secondary investor, compared to simply holding the
project (12.26 %), which falls in line with results obtained in [83]. Considering the assumed
tokenisation costs, the findings shown in section 4.2 highlight significant opportunities to grow
secondary investor returns through the implementation of token-based divestment strategies,
while keeping tertiary investor returns constant.
Compared to the conventional lump di-
vestment strategy, the token-based linear and exponential divestment strategies increase the
secondary investor IRR by around 20 % (to 16.64 %) and 50 % (to 20.77 %), respectively
(100 % divestment case). Despite the inclusion of tokenisation costs, which increase the op-
erational costs of the project by around 6.8 % (excluding debt service), these increases of
investor returns are possible. The sensitivity analysis shows that excluding the tokenisation

costs would be able to yield an additional return of 1.14 % and 1.25 % for the token-based
cases 2 and 3 respectively. In turn, increasing the tokenisation costs to their upper limits
would reduce the secondary investor returns of case 2 and case 3 to 13.22 % and 15.31 %
respectively, leading to case 2 being less profitable than the conventional case 1. Although
the token-based approaches allow for investor return improvements, one needs to acknowledge
the reduced robustness of secondary investor returns against sensitive parameters, as shown
in figures 4.6a to 4.6c. Because of this, the different token-based strategies suffer from a
higher risk, however it is not fully understood as to why this occurs. This calls for a more
detailed investigation into how the risk-return profile of the asset is changed and whether it
is worthwhile for the secondary investor to take on this increased risk.
A key driver for increased returns are early equity sales at a premium. As shown in figure 4.5a,
the more equity is sold at an earlier stage, the lower the required premium, in order to allow
for the higher returns that the secondary investor would like to attain. This is because of the
value these equity sales bring, compared to staying invested in the project. The relationship
can be showcased through the sensitivity analysis performed for the equity share that is sold
in each year. For case 3, the base value is selling 25% of the current equity each year. If this
is increased to 30 %, the secondary investor is able to increase their return from 20.77 % to
22.33 % corresponding to an increase of 7.5 %. Conversely, a less aggressive strategy with a
divestment share of 20 % of the current remaining equity share per year would yield a return
of 18.74 %, corresponding to a reduction of around 10 %. This indicates a potential risk of
not meeting the desired return, in case there are not enough investors willing to purchase the
equity that is offered for sale. Failing to sell the expected share of one’s current equity might
therefore result in lower expected returns.
5.2.2
Methodology
The results of this analysis are only attainable under key considerations. First the approach
of modelling the return of equity sales as taken from [72] does not necessarily represent the in-
dustry standard. Other valuation approaches from the mergers & acquisitions and corporate
and project valuation literature might yield different results with the same input parameters.
Interested readers are referred to [84]. Secondly, throughout this analysis, the assumption
has been made that there are enough interested tertiary investors willing to purchase the
offered equity at the specified price. In reality, this most likely will not always be the case.
Thirdly, the tokenisation commission fee and token service fee have been derived from di-
versified investment products that are currently available today. Furthermore, due to the
novelty of this approach, similar cost estimations for blockchain based products are currently
simply not available. To better represent the actual cost of tokenisation, more detailed es-
timations of tokenising and moving energy projects "on-chain" as well as maintaining the
token infrastructure could provide more accurate results.
Finally, to better compare the
token-based divestment strategies, a more detailed investigation of how exactly conventional
divestment strategies are executed today would allow for more realistic results. While the
experts provided valuable insights during the interviews, the inclusion of further details such
as the required time and cost to organise and finalise such deals would help to better represent
reality. At the time of the analysis however, the author was unable to obtain more informa-
tion from experts or other sources. Because of the above considerations, the results obtained
in this section are not necessarily directly applicable to other energy projects. Nonetheless,
under the assumption that the same input parameters and methodology are used to assess
the impact of tokenisation on project returns, similar results could be expected. Generally it
can be concluded that the fractionalisation and individuality of designing divestment strate-

5.3
Tokenisation as a Portfolio and Risk Management Tool
5.3.1
Results
The purpose of this analysis was to present a use case for how tokenisation can be applied in
the real world. Currently it is not possible to easily trade energy assets and therefore, asset
managers cannot easily adjust their energy asset portfolio. Two stated benefits highlighted
in [10] and [11] are the increased tradability and the increased liquidity, when applying to-
kenisation to real world assets. Therefore, the focus of this analysis was to determine how
tokenising part or all of the equity of energy assets could help finding optimal portfolios. It
is important to note that the analysis was conducted using an arbitrary base case portfolio
as a starting point and thus, results obtained in this thesis should be considered to be more
conceptual, instead of applicable to all energy asset portfolios.
The findings in this section prove to be promising considering the benefits for the analysed
energy asset portfolio showcased in 4.3. The individual asset returns obtained through Monte
Carlo-based stochastic analysis of discounted cashflow models seem to be in line with energy
asset returns showcased in [85].
Using the initial portfolio with the assumption of non-
tradability of assets as a baseline, the stepwise introduction of tokenisation to energy assets
proved to be highly beneficial. As shown in section 4.3, increasing the share of tokenisation
leads to more optimal portfolios and with a maximum of 100 % liquidity to the global optimal
portfolio. While the idea that high liquidity allows for optimal portfolios is not new, it is
necessary to understand how the optimal portfolio changes at different liquidity levels. This
is relevant for asset managers who may want to introduce tokenisation into their portfolio
management practices, but prefer to only tokenise a portion of their assets. In this analysis
case, the highest improvements of a portfolio’s Sharpe ratio were achieved in the first two 25
% increments of tokenisation. Indeed, increasing the liquidity (or tokenisation share) from 0
to 50 % increased the portfolio’s Sharpe ratio by 47.8 %, while increasing the liquidity from
50 % to 100 % only increased the Sharpe ratio by 7.5 %. This highlights the fact that it is
not necessary to tokenise a whole project. Instead, the approach of "as much as required,
not as much as possible" should be considered. It is important to point out, that relative
to typical Sharpe ratios seen with more traditional securities like stocks, the Sharpe ratios
obtained in the results seem very high. For instance, the Sharpe ratio of an internationally
balanced MSCI World ETF portfolio shows an average Sharpe ratio of 0.78 for the period
of 1978 to 2022 [86]. This significant difference between the Sharpe ratios is due to the very
low resulting risk of the global optimal energy asset portfolio, which is around 20 times lower
than the risk of the MSCI World ETF, while showing a similar return [86]. Comparing the
Sharpe ratios of energy asset portfolios with Sharpe ratios of equity ETFs therefore is not
very practical. Nevertheless, similar magnitudes in Sharpe ratios have been obtained in other
types of portfolio analyses of the energy sector, i.e. [38].
Since energy assets today are illiquid, investing or acquiring such assets will always carry the
issue of the liquidity premium [87]. This stems from the fact that there is no central market
place that allows the trade of assets. Instead, acquisition deals are typically done on an over-
the-counter (OTC) or negotiational basis, which lead to higher project prices in the form of
so-called OTC illiquidity premiums [88]. Moreover, since OTC deals are based on individual
==PAGE:66==
58
Discussion of Results and Methodologies
trades, the clearing of the trade might be slower compared to a central exchange [53, 87].
Under the assumption that energy asset owners - like other portfolio managers - would like to
rebalance their portfolio according to their future market expectations, tokenised assets that
are easily tradable could become a game changer. Instead of having to develop new projects or
negotiate with M&A departments of asset owners, ownership could be traded using tradable
blockchain-based tokens.
5.3.2
Methodology
While the results of the energy assets as well as the asset portfolios seem realistic, there are
some modelling shortfalls which could be improved upon, to yield better results. Firstly,
replacing the arbitrary baseline portfolio with a more realistic starting point, i.e. the average
portfolio composition of Danish energy asset managers, would allow to better represent reality.
Secondly, a more sophisticated market clearing model would be beneficial to obtain more
representative revenues of power plants. In the simplified market clearing model used in this
thesis, dispatchable power plants produce energy in hours where the electricity price exceeds
their marginal cost, while non-dispatchable power plants produce according to their hourly
capacity factors. With the inclusion of must-run, ramping and demand constraints, it would
be possible to account for the technical limitations of operating power plants and thus yield
more accurate results. Thirdly, revoking the strong assumption that all uncertain parameters
are normally distributed would be also beneficial for the model outcomes. The assumption
was made in order to simplify the inclusion of stochasticity in the model and, due to the more
conceptual approach of the analysis, this inaccuracy was considered to be neglectable.
While modern portfolio theory (MPT) is arguably the most prominent approach to deter-
mining optimal portfolios, it has been criticised for not accurately depicting reality. Most
notably, MPT assumes a Gaussian distribution of returns, in order to use the standard de-
viation or variance as a measure of risk [53]. This assumption disregards potential extreme
losses which could be accounted for by using asymmetric, fat-tailed measures of risk [89], as
done in the post-modern portfolio theory (PMPT) [89, 90]. The limitation of not considering
extreme values is also relevant for the distribution of asset returns as shown in figure 4.7.
Both natural gas, as well as solar show relatively fat tailed distributions very high maximum
returns, compared to their mean returns of 4.71 % and 9.19 %. In the 10 000 analysed sce-
narios, the maximum return of natural gas and solar assets amounted to 19.55 % and 17.61
% respectively. Another issue of the MPT is the fact that the model uses historical expected
returns and historical asset covariance as input values, and assumes future performance based
on historical values [53, 91, 92]. The Black-Litterman model aims to overcome both above
mentioned shortfalls, by extending the MPT with the inclusion of the views of the investor
or asset manager and thereby counteracting the assumption that historical performance can
be used to describe future performance [91, 92]. Since the latter models were unknown to the
author during the development of the thesis, the MPT was chosen to analyse portfolios.

Conclusion and Future Research
The aim of this thesis was to propose the tokenisation of energy assets as a new way of how
we think about investments in and ownership of renewable energy. To this end, different
aspects of a token-based system were explored and analysed, which were formulated in the
form of three research questions in the beginning of this thesis.
On a collective level, it
can be concluded that the tokenisation of energy assets might indeed bring benefits to the
energy sector and thus, further research would prove to be valuable in understanding potential
benefits in more detail.
Through expert interviews, the interest and activity levels of energy companies in blockchain
technology and tokenisation have been explored. Moreover, the implications and requirements
for a token-based system have been discussed and summarised into key requirements. While
the companies of interviewed experts showed varying interest and activity levels in blockchain
technology, they all supported a token-based system under the assumed beneficial effects. A
key conclusion from this series of interviews is the need for clear regulation. Without a sound
legal framework, experts expressed scepticism regarding the adoption of a token-based system.
The impact of tokenisation on energy project investor returns was analysed using a discounted
cashflow model of an example offshore wind farm. Additionally, investor returns under three
different exit strategies have been explored, using a secondary equity sales approach. While
all three exit strategies showed increasing returns, the token-based exit strategies performed
better compared to the conventional exit strategy. As a result, token-based approaches that
allow for more individualised divestment could be a viable approach to onboard citizen in-
vestors while at the same time increase the returns of energy asset owners. This could give
rise to a new sustainable investment product based on tokenised energy asset shares.
The effect of using tokenisation as a tool to manage and optimise an energy asset portfolio
has been analysed through mean-variance portfolio analysis. Within this analysis, optimal
portfolios for different levels of liquidity / tokenisation were obtained. While these results are
based on an arbitrary base case portfolio, they highlight how a token-based application could
be used to increase portfolio returns while reducing portfolio risk. Asset managers could use
tokenised shares of energy assets to optimise their energy asset portfolio or instead, turn their
energy asset portfolio in a sustainable investment product for citizens. With the introduction
of the EU taxonomy on sustainable investments, companies and financial institutions have
to adjust their activities and portfolios towards more sustainable levels as outlined in the
taxonomy [93]. According to [94], "large financial and non-financial companies that fall under
the Non-Financial Reporting Directive will have to disclose to what extent the activities that
they carry out meet the criteria set out in the EU Taxonomy". This requirement also includes
financial market participants like asset managers. For companies that struggle with achieving
certain levels of sustainable activities, investing in a portfolio of tokenised green energy assets
might be a key solution to comply with sustainability requirements.
Considering the novelty of the concept of tokenisation of energy assets, there are many oppor-
tunities to conduct further research. This can also be seen from the broad scope of this thesis,
which might have provided more in depth and more tangible results, if it were conducted as
==PAGE:68==
60
Conclusion and Future Research
a PhD thesis. From a more philosophical perspective, the question of how the future energy
sector will be structured can be explored. With the rise of digital solutions, market players
might want to consolidate their operations and focus on their core business, as has happened
recently in the German energy sector. In the future, energy companies might have to make a
decision between staying an asset-based company and/or a provider of digital services. More-
over, it would be valuable to expand on the shortfalls highlighted in previous sections. These
include further research in cost estimations of blockchain-based systems, tokenisation costs
and token service fees. In connection with this, comparing the costs to set-up a token-based
system with the conventional method of energy asset acquisitions and ownership transfers
would provide valuable insights about whether cost-reductions could be achieved. A key as-
pect of the energy transition is the participation of citizens in the form of co-owning energy
assets. To better understand the key barriers that are currently preventing a more wide-
spread co-ownership model, a meta-analysis of current ownership models combined with an
analysis as to why so little energy companies currently focus on enabling co-ownership could
give rise to more practical approaches.
As highlighted throughout this thesis, a token-based system requires the cooperation of key
stakeholders, namely the regulator, the asset owners and token investors. Without a well func-
tioning cooperation, we could miss out on an opportunity that could contribute to democratis-
ing the energy sector and mitigate climate change through increased awareness, acceptance
and investments in renewable energy projects.

A.1
Project Finance
Energy projects like other infrastructure projects have certain characteristics that require
special consideration before making the final investment decision (FID). They usually show
long lifetimes (15 - 25 years), very high up-front costs and not specifically high returns, which
can make them a risky environment to invest in or finance. Because of this, such projects
are usually financed with a high amount of leverage (=borrowed money) using the principle
of project finance.
This is especially important for players who are intending to develop
energy projects, but do not have the means to do so. Article [95] showcases, that project
finance "plays an important role in the energy sector - not only in the risky environment of
a developing country, but also in stable, low-risk countries such as Germany", especially for
renewable energy projects. This is because more and more smaller sponsors are interested
in developing their own solar or wind projects, but are lacking the resources in their balance
sheet to do so. In [96], the impact of auctions on financing conditions and cost of capital
for wind projects is investigated. While auctions expose project developers to higher price
risk induced by unhedged electricity prices, the authors state that financing conditions have
improved [96]. While the usage of project finance in renewable energy projects has increased
in the past [97], this improved financing environment could push the adoption of project
finance even more. The information and methodology presented in this section is mainly
sourced from [72] and [53].
In essence, project finance is a way to raise capital for larger projects like energy projects
through the use of different sources of capital. These sources typically include one’s own
equity or borrowing money from a bank. The speciality of project finance is the fact that
the money is borrowed against the expected cashflows of the project, with the project being
used as a collateral - also called non-recourse loans. This is done by project developers (also
called sponsors), in order to reduce their risk in the form of liabilities of paying for unserviced
loan payments. To enable such limited liability, large energy projects are typically not added
to the balance sheet of a company but instead are placed in their own legal entity called
special-purpose-vehicles (SPV). While the SPV is still owned by the developing company, it
is structured to be legally and economically independent, which is also called ’ring-fenced’.
This is done because of two reasons. Firstly, an independent legal structure precludes the debt
lender to pursue the parent company, in case the project does not generate enough cashflow to
service the debt payments. Secondly, the ring-fenced structure mandates, that the cashflows
that are generated by the project are first used to pay of liabilities such as debt, before they
can be paid out as profits to the parent company. This is done to protect debt lenders like
banks against withholding cashflows that would be available for debt servicing. The order
at which cashflows are used to pay off costs, liabilities or investors can be described using a
’cashflow-waterfall’ [72]:
1. Payments that are essential for a project’s operation are covered. These include oper-
ating, fuel or maintenance costs as well as tax payments
2. Other non-essential payments that are linked with the operation of the project, i.e.
==PAGE:76==

3. Interest on debt
4. Scheduled debt principal repayments
5. Payments to debt reserve accounts which are used to cover debt payments in cases of
unexpected cashflow failure
6. Payouts to investors
As can be seen, both debt interest and debt principal repayments are ranked higher than
payouts to investors which guarantees that debt is repaid before profits are paid out.
A.1.1
Capital Structure
As mentioned above, project finance entails finding different sources of capital to fund a
project. The most typical streams are equity and debt. Equity is capital that belongs to
the company pursuing to develop the project. The company needs to decide, whether to
invest into a project or not, based on a profitability analysis for the investment. For this,
a company must estimate its cost of capital, i.e. the return that could be generated, if the
money is invested somewhere else. If a project is not able to provide a return that is higher
than the cost of capital, it is not worthwhile to pursue. Debt is capital that has been borrowed
from another entity to increase the available investment capital. Debt will need to be repaid
over a specific period with added cost of borrowing the money on top - the interest rate or
cost of debt. Companies borrow money, since they can obtain capital with a cost of debt that
is lower than their cost of capital and therefore more investments become worthwhile. The
combined cost of capital of a capital composition can be described using the weighted average
cost of capital (WACC) as shown below:

A.1.2
Project Investor Types and their Investment Lifetime
Throughout the development and operation of a project, different investors with different
objectives and tolerances for risk will be present. One can distinguish between active and
passive investors. Active or primary investors are typically the developer of a project who take
on most of the risk, by being responsible of designing and constructing the project, while at
the same time trying to find sources of debt financing. Since this represents the riskiest period
within a project, the primary investor demands for the highest level of return, to mitigate
that risk. Passive or secondary investors are typically actors who do not want to take on the
risk that comes with the responsibility of developing a project. They usually come in once
the project has secured funding (=financial close), by purchasing equity from the primary
investors at a premium (to cover the higher return expectations of the primary investors).
Both primary and secondary investors might choose to continue holding equity throughout
the lifetime of the project or exit the project according to their preferred exit strategy. This
entails selling equity to a tertiary investor, who prefers to invest into an already operating
project, which represents the lowest risk and thus lowest return opportunity.
A.1.3
Project Finance Process
When discussing project finance, it is important to understand the process. The explanations
below were obtained through the interviews conducted for the thesis, where experts explained
the process. It starts off with the project developer (or primary investor) who is interested
in developing a project. They are responsible for planning and developing the project and
assume all risk. In this time, the developer will approach other (secondary) investors to see if
there are others who are interested in cooperating on this project. Typically, these secondary
investors are not in charge of operating or developing but instead, provide equity to the
project. With or without new investors on board, the primary investor will approach the
bank to ask for debt financing of the project. For a bank to agree on this, they will require
two things: first a PPA off-take agreement with a consumer, to lock in a certain revenue level
and thereby mitigate power price risk. Secondly, the project developers and other potential
co-investors will need to post a guarantee as a security, which the bank will take over, in case
the project is not developed. This is used to further reduce the bank’s risk in the project.
Next, an SPV is created which contains the project and with it also the cashflows that are
generated (ring-fencing). From the day the project goes into operation, the bank needs to
return the guarantees and the project can start earning money to pay back the loans to the
bank. Once the loans are paid back, the project is held by 100 % equity and the bank is out
of the project. Before all loans are paid off, some of the investors that have been brought into
the project as providers of equity are already thinking about an exit strategy, to reduce or
completely remove their exposure in the project. Through secondary or tertiary equity sales,
investors can sell their share of equity to other interested investors (i.e. tertiary investors)
and execute their exit strategy.

A.2.1
Context for Interview Partners
To mitigate the effects of climate change, the energy sector needs to change from the ground
up, by replacing energy technologies along the whole supply chain to induce a transition
towards clean energy.
Currently, ca.
USD 330 bn.
are invested in renewable electricity
annually, however in order to achieve the goals of the Paris Agreement, this number must

The financing gap between current and required

electricity sector have the potential to bridge this financing gap. One such measure that aims
to democratise the energy sector by increasing societal participation, and enable asset owners
to offer their assets as investment opportunities for the general public is the tokenisation of
energy assets. Tokenisation is the process of representing the value of a physical asset using
digital "security tokens", which themselves are tradable shares of ownership of the tokenised
assets and are legal representations of the physical counterpart. One can compare security
tokens of energy assets with company shares that are traded in the stock market, but just
focused on small shared of the individual energy project.
The tokenisation approach should be enabled by leveraging the benefits and applications of
the quickly developing blockchain technology. Security tokens themselves have the benefit of
representing an elegant solution to automate legal and transactional processes and thereby
allowing for significant cost and barrier reductions in asset transactions. Through this, tradi-
tional energy assets that are only accessible to corporations, pension funds or banks become
available to the general public as investment opportunities. The aim of tokenisation is to
provide an alternative means to provide financing for projects and act as a complement to
equity and debt financing. Furthermore, considering the main benefit of using security tokens
to represent small shares of assets - namely the efficient tradability of these tokens - tokeni-
sation could be a novel approach to how companies manage their asset exposure through
adjusting their portfolio using tokenised assets.
A digital token is a digital representation of value or rights which is traded for the purpose
of facilitating access to and tradability of these values or rights. Tokens are created through
an initial coin offering, where the characteristics like value and rights that are attributed to
these tokens are specified and published. Digital tokens are blockchain based and themselves
represent tradable assets. Tokenisation is the process of representing the value of a physical
asset using a digital token.
