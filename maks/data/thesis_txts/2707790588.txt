Abstract
The Petrol Station Replenishment Problem revolves around supplying
petrol to gas stations before they run out of stock.
The problem is
highly complex and diﬀicult to solve, and all publications have chosen to
solve the problem heuristically. This research investigates the problem
by a new perspective, to check if orders can be grouped together using
the ”Can-do and Must-do orders” logic. The problem is not widely re-
searched, but looking at the problem from this perspective has not been
done before. Two heuristic strategies are proposed with respect to the
can-do and the must-do orders. They are tested against a real-life data
set and the inclusion of can-do orders is quantified and measured. The
results showcase that including can-do orders does negatively affect the
solution, both in terms of cost and time. There is some indication that
the two strategies can be mixed together, but further research on the
topic is required for that.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Despite the emerging discussion surrounding sustainable resources, oil re-
mains as one of the widely-used source of energy in the world. The most
obvious examples of oil usage is petroleum or diesel in cars, trucks, ships
and airplanes [1]. But as oil, any other type of fossil fuels is considered
non-renewable„ meaning that their supply is limited and will eventually
run out [2]. Based on recent research, judging by how much oil we are
using today, and what we have in reserve currently, it could only take
around 48 years until we eventually run out of oil [3]. While we might
find some new ways to discover and extract more oil, it would almost
certainly come at a greater cost [4].
Most car manufacturers today are, and justifiably so, rapidly devel-
oping cars that use another source of energy than fuel, mostly being
electric. Nevertheless, fuel and diesel cars make up for about 75% of
newly registered cars in the EU, according to a 2021 study [5]. While it
is hoped that in the coming years, electric cars will own a bigger share
of the market, there is still a huge demand for fuel or diesel driven vehi-
cles. Since fuel isn’t going away in the coming decades, there is a great
need for an eﬀicient usage of fuel. An important element of this is the
transportation and logistics industry.
The need for manufacturers to stay competitive in an ever-increasing
world of customer expectations to maintain profitability paves the way
for operations research and optimization tools, in order to cut costs and
maximize profits.
With the emergence of computer technology, these
techniques have become vital within the logistics industry, in order to
save costs, such as the Capacited Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP), ini-
tially developed by Dantzig and Ramser in 1959 [6]. The VRP has been
developed and branched to other types of problems since, such as the

problem this thesis deals with, the Petrol Station Replenishment Prob-
lem [7].
The Petrol Station Replenishment Problem revolves around planning
petrol delivery to distribution stations, and consists of several layers of
decision-making. These decisions include assigning feasible routes, keep-
ing track of the inventory at the stations, how many trucks are required
to supply the fuel, among many more. As with other routing problems,
the objective is generally to minimize the total travel cost. In this thesis
a more complex variant of the problem is analysed, among other things
involving a multi-day horizon, forecast-based demand, compartmental-
ized truck fleet and station restrictions.
The main topic of the thesis is the term defined as ”Can-do and must-
do orders”. The term describes the daily decision of travelling to a certain
station, based on whether the station has to be visited on the day, or
whether it could be included in the route. There are many factors that
come into play here, for example if a station is visited on a given day
and there is another station close by, it could make sense to include that
station in the daily route, instead of going back to the same vicinity a few
days later, when that station has now become a ”must-do” order. In this
thesis, this decision-making process is quantified, and feasible routes are
created with a mix of can-do and must-do orders. The solution strategy
is then tested on a real-life data set, which involves gas stations around
Denmark. The data and the problem was provided by AMCS, a cloud-
based waste management consultancy, originally from Ireland [8]. It is
furthermore tested against a strategy that only involves must-do orders,
and strengths and weaknesses of the two strategies are compared.
The remainder of the thesis is organized as followed. Chapter 2 anal-
yses the problem at hand, describes the problem parameters and defines
assumptions and KPIs. Chapter 3 discusses the literature surrounding
the topic, and comparisons are made to the methodology developed in
this research. In chapter 4, the problem is defined mathematically us-
ing Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP). The problem’s sets, parameters,
decision variables and constraints are defined here and used for the re-
mainder of the thesis. In chapter 3.2, the two solution strategies are

CHAPTER 2
Problem Analysis
2.1
Problem description
In this research paper, an optimization of the distribution of petrol to
numerous stations throughout Denmark is addressed. This problem, ac-
cording to literature, is called the Petrol Station Replenishment Problem
(PSRP). The problem consists of both routing and inventory decisions,
as it incorporates both the optimal routing of trucks, as well as supplying
petrol stations with products before they run out, i.e. reach a dry run.
The products are stored at a depot, and have to be delivered to petrol
stations before they run out. The scope of the project is over a 30 day
time horizon, where a a daily consumption forecast is utilized to deter-
mine when each station runs out of stock. There are six different petrol
products in general, and within each compartment of the trucks, only one
product can be stored. There are furthermore 59 stations in total, each
equipped with a single underground fuel tank, of a certain product type.
As can be expected, the daily consumption of the station tanks varies
from station to station, depending on the demand of the stations. The
main concept behind this research are the so called can-do and must-do
orders. They can be described by categorizing all supply runs based on
if a customer has to be visited that day, or if he could be visited. It is
therefore the decision to visit a given customer exactly at his dry run, or
sooner. A visual representation of the problem can be seen on figure 2.1

With such a rich problem, there are many things that a supply plan-

• Which stations are visited in each route.
• Which day each station is visited.

A route can consist of one or many stations that a single truck is
capable of visiting on any given day. The decision of visiting multiple
stations during is derived from the fact that it can save the total distance
the trucks need to drive throughout the month. An example of this can
be that a station has to be visited on a given day, but during the trip the
truck can visit other stations within the same vicinity, even though these
station are not at their dry run. By doing so, the trucks avoid having
to go out again the next day, or whenever the dry run of the stations is,
and thus save mileage on the trucks at the same time.
Each route a fuel truck takes can therefore consist of any number of
stations, as long as the capacity of the truck is respected. A truck can
furthermore use multiple routes per day, i.e. return to the depot after one

2.3
Assumptions and Key
Performance Indicators
For a problem as rich as the PSRP, especially the variant being dealt
with in this research, certain assumptions will have to be made, such that
modelling and solving the problem becomes a bit more straightforward.
==PAGE:22==
10
2 Problem Analysis
The following assumptions are thus made and used throughout the entire
research.
• All vehicles are assumed to drive on the same speed, such that they
will have the same travel time driving the same distance.
• Stations can be visited multiple times per day, any time of the day.
• The vehicle compartments are assumed to be equipped with a flow
meter, i.e. the amount in the compartment is known at any given
moment.
• There is enough inventory at the depot to fulfill all demand.
• In each station, there can be only one underground tank.
While the main objective of the problem is to minimize the total
monthly mileage of the trucks, there are certain other indicators that can
be used to verify the solution. If the quality of the solution only depends
on the total monthly mileage, a biased solution can occur. For example,
if stations are grouped together for a trip only based on geographical
location, there might be stations that have very dissimilar consumption
rates, and therefore should not be routed together.
The following KPI’s will therefore be used to verify the quality of the
solution at the end of this thesis.
• Total travel cost.
• Total CPU time of the solution method.
• Total number of stations visited.
• Number of dry runs throughout the planning horizon.
• How far away from the dry run each replenishment is.
The first KPI, and the most obvious, is the travel cost. The main
objective of problems such as this one is almost always to minimize cost.
The cost here is derived from the amount kilometres driven throughout
the whole planning period. The CPU time plays a major role in mea-
suring the performance of such large problems. Since the problem is so
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Assumptions and Key Performance Indicators
11
complex, it is bound to take a significant amount of time to solve. A
key role in minimizing the CPU time is the number of stations visited
at each day. The solution method’s performance will depend heavily on
how many stations and tanks need to be filled over the whole planning
horizon.
The last two KPIs are related to each station’s dry run.
Firstly,
the amount of dry runs is going to be measured. The amount of dry
runs throughout the whole period would ideally be 0. Secondly, it is
important to determine how far away the replenishment day is from its
minimal dry run. The minimal dry run is here defined as the day a tank
runs out given that it has been filled to its maximum capacity in its last
replenishment.
The defined KPIs will play a major role throughout the whole report
and the solution method will be designed with these in mind. At the end
of the report, the final solution is measured in regards to the KPIs.

CHAPTER 3
Literature Review
In this chapter, the literature surrounding the topic of the project is
analyzed. Different sources are used to distinguish the various methods
that have been used to define and solve the Petrol Station Replenishment
Problem. PSRP can be described as a variant of other problems that
have been thoroughly researched throughout the decades, such as the
Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP). The main similarities and differences
between these problems and the PSRP will therefore be discovered, in
order to properly define the problem at hand.
The Vehicle Routing Problem has been a topic of heavy research since
the late 1950s. While its characteristics have evolved since then, in its
purest form it aims to ”serve a set of customers, geographically dispersed
around the central depot, using a fleet with varying capacities.” This is
still to this day one of the most widely research topics within Operations
Research [9].
The classic version the VRP, the Capacitated VRP (CVRP), devel-
ops the least costly delivery routes, starting and ending at a depot, and
visiting each customer exactly once, while respecting the capacity of each
vehicle utilized. Nowadays, the CVRP is often extended by the applica-
tion of real-life complexities, making them immensely more diﬀicult to
solve, but in turn their solution more practical. These complexities in-
clude variation of the capacities (Heterogeneous Fleet VRP), delivering
within certain time windows (the VRP with Time Windows), and var-
ious others. With such rich complexities, solving the problem becomes
harder and harder as the real-life aspects are taken more into context.
Looking at literature overviews on VRPs, it becomes evident that due
to complexity of the models, the heuristic approaches tend to be more
favorable over exact methods [9]. Braekers et al. [9] did a literature
==PAGE:26==
14
3 Literature Review
survey on various VRPs found in the literature, and concluded that over
80% of the literature relied on either metaheuristics or classic heuristics,
while around 17% used exact methods. Similar results can be seen from
Ostermeier et al. [10], where the state-of-the-art framework of Multi-
Compartment VRPs (MCVRP) was studied. There, around 76% of the
literature proposed heuristic approaches, and 17% used exact methods.
While the literature on PSRP is not as widespread as the VRP, several
publications on the topic were found. For the remainder of this chapter,
the PSRP will be defined, its characteristics discussed, and potential
solution methods are explored based on these publications.
3.1
Definition of the PSRP
The term Petrol Station Replenishment Problem is not a uniform no-
tation of the problem across the literature. There are various different
terms used to define similar, or even the exact type of problems. It is
however the term that is most frequently found within the literature
([11], [12], [13], [14]), and is therefore the defined term for this thesis.
Cornillier et al. [14] use the term Multi-Period Petrol Station Replenish-
ment Problem(MPSRP), which, like this research, describes and solves
a PSRP over a certain planning horizon.
Similarly, Triki et al.
[13]
defines the same problem as Periodic Petrol Station Replenishment Prob-
lem(PPSRP). These definitions describe in general the same problem,
with slight differences, but the more simpler term PSRP will be used in
this research, for simplicity reasons.
In the introduction to this chapter, the classical VRP was discussed.
The PSRP does in fact draw some obvious inspirations from the VRP,
but there are some additional complexities that arise when considering
the problem. [13] discuss the similarities and differences between the two
problems. It is worth noting that the PSRP model they propose is of
a more simplicity than the one researched in this report, and therefore
the differences between the two problems become even larger when this
research is considered. The two problems however do share some key
==PAGE:27==
3.1 Definition of the PSRP
15
components, seen below:
• The objective function is typically based on minimizing the total
driven cost, i.e. minimizing total mileage on the vehicles.
• Each vehicle must start and end its route on the depot.
• Each customer can not be visited by more than one vehicle each
day.
• A vehicle can visit multiple customers in its route, but the supplied
goods on the route can not exceed its capacity.
• During the planning horizon, each customer has a prespecified num-
ber of times it will have to be visited.
As mentioned in chapter 2, the two main drivers to the decision mak-
ing of the PSRP are the route planning and the inventory management
of the customers. The above similarities between the VRP and PSRP are
exclusively derived from the route planning aspect of the problem. This
aspect makes sure to select the least costly combinations of customers
each day to form a route, starting and ending at the depot. While this
is of great importance to the problem, the inventory management of the
customers is in its essence the core of the PSRP. Instead of having a
defined daily demand, the PSRP in this report has a daily consumption
rate of each customer, which paves the way for the importance of track-
ing the inventory of the stations. This in turn introduces the concept of
Can-do and Must-do orders, which revolves around the fact that during
the daily planning there will be customers that have to be visited, but
more complicatedly there will be customers that can be visited and will
therefore be included in the route. The main operational constraints that
the PSRP depends on, that differ from the general VRP are listed below.
• Each customers’ inventory must be tracked such that the day of
tank depletion is known.
• Each vehicle utilizes multiple compartments, and in each compart-
ment, only one type of product may be stored.

• Any two-or more customers can be combined to form a route, no
matter if the customers included are not at their dry run on the
given day.
• Some vehicles can not deliver to some customers due to access
restrictions.
While there might be more specific operational constraints that make
each variant of the PSRP unique, these are the generic ones that most
PSRPs have in common.
3.2
Solution Strategies
Through reviewing the literature on the PSRP, it becomes apparent that
solving the problem through an exact solver is not the preferred solution
method. Most of the literature showcases that even solving the problem
for a single day for a single day becomes too complex and time-consuming
to solve exactly ([14], [13], [15]). Therefore, most of the studied literature
here will be based on heuristic approaches.
Triki [15] proposes four different heuristic algorithms to solve peri-
odic PSRP for instances of 38 petrol stations over a planning period of
6 days. The main idea behind all four algorithms is to split the task of
assigning the periodic combinations to the stations from the routing part,
instead of dealing with both tasks simultaneously. The heuristic is there-
fore divided between a combination assignment procedure and a routing
procedure. Triki proposes four different heuristic methods for the combi-
nation assignment, and the first proposed heuristic minimizes the total
demand on each day of the planning horizon. It does this through a sim-
ple mathematical model, and its advantages are its simplicity and ease
of implementation. Its drawbacks are however that it does not consider
the geographical position of the stations. The second heuristic proposed
sets out to minimize the total number of visited stations every day. It
also utilizes a simple mathematical model to do this, and its advantages
and the disadvantages are the same as for the first heuristic. The heuris-
tic aims to solve a relaxed version of the periodic VRP, to minimize the
==PAGE:29==
3.2 Solution Strategies
17
total distance between the depot and the visited stations. Unlike the
first two, this heuristic takes into account more information regarding
the positions of the stations, but on the other hand it is more complex
and therefore harder to solve. The fourth and last heuristic proposed by
Triki is an extension of the third one, where it uses a clustering strat-
egy to group together stations based on the geographical position. This
strategy uses an ever better amount of information, but in turn becomes
more complex. After using any of the combination assignment methods,
the routing procedure is constructed, and the assigned combinations act
as an input for that. For this part, a standard VRP model is used to
construct the routing plan.
Benantar et al [11] propose a detailed six-step solution approach
based on a local tabu search approach to solve the problem. The ap-
proach is set out solve an instance of over 100 customers and 20 trucks.
The algorithm differs from most of the existing work, as it combines
the loading and the routing problems, instead of dividing them into two
different parts.
A Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic [16] is introduced to
explore the solution space, and using defined benchmark the solution is
iteratively improved. The results of the proposed method showed a great
improvement on the real-life results, and furthermore improved similar
problems in the literature.
Albornoz and Villegas [17] deals with a large scale PSRP over a 31-
day period and a customer set of over 100.
They introduce an algo-
rithm which is split into two phases; Route generation and assignment
& scheduling. The route generation phase acts as an initialization phase
where all demand orders are assigned to certain routes and truck types.
Following that, the performed routes are maximized on the Assignment
& scheduling phase using a simple assignment model. The routes are
then sorted based on their priority. The heuristic was tested against a
month of real data and showcased that it outperformed the company
results.
Cornillier et al.(2008) [18] studied a single-period variant of the PSRP
and proposed an exact algorithm to solve instances where the problem
was simplified by limiting the number of visited stations to two. The

CHAPTER 5
Solution Method
As thoroughly detailed in chapter 4.3, a different solution method than
using an MIP model is needed to solve the PSRP. Chapter 3.2 gives an
insight in different solution methods found in the literature. This sec-
tion will in detail discuss two different solution strategies. The first one,
a decomposition heuristic which aims to group together stations based
on a so-called turnover rate, and furthermore incorporate can-do orders
with the must-do orders. The second one is much simpler in its logic, as
it goes only to each customer at its dry run, i.e. it only makes use of
must-do orders.
5.1
Strategy 1 - Decomposition
heuristic
It was discussed in chapter 3.2, that the strategy ”Group-before, route
after” is found to be most appropriate according to the literature. A vari-
ant of the strategy will be utilized in this research paper. A multi-phase
decomposition heuristic is therefore proposed. Sections 5.1.1-4 describe
every step of the heuristic in detail.
The heuristic iteratively plans for one day at a time, for the whole
planning period of 30 days, where t represents the period in the consid-
ered iteration, for T days. The main logic behind the heuristic is being
able to group stations together that are as similar to each other as pos-
sible. By doing so, the route creation becomes more simple, as well as
the total distance can be saved by routing similar stations together. The

5.1.1
Quantification
The first step of the heuristic is to quantify each station such that com-
paring all stations becomes easier. There are several ways to quantify
each station, and using the KPIs from chapter 2.3, the most important
elements of the quantification become evident.
The most straightforward quantification and grouping would be based
on geographical location. Stations grouped together in geographical clus-
ters would therefore always be routed together. While this might save
some mileage looking at a short horizon, over a longer time period it
would not be beneficial to just focus on distance. As some stations take
more time than others to reach their dry run, the trucks would have
to go out much more often to compensate the different depletion rates
of the grouped stations. The quantification of stations used in this re-
search therefore rather tries to incorporate the depletion rate of each
station. This is defined as Turnover Rate and its definition can be seen
in equation 5.1. Note that in the first three steps of the heuristic, the
product dimension is removed from each station in order to simplify the
calculations. This has no effect on the solution, as each station can only
be supplied with one type of product.

5.3
Extended Model
One of the assumptions defined in section 2.3 was that there could be
only one tank per station. In real-life circumstances, this is most often
not the case, as most gas stations offer different types of petrol. There-
fore, in order to validate the solution, the two strategies will be tested on
a data set that includes two tanks per station. There is therefore added
an additional tank for each station, with an alternative product.
For strategy 1, the heuristic has to be tweaked a bit, as the product
dimension needs to be added to the heuristic process, and therefore re-
calculate the groups, replenishment and supply matrices. This is due to
the fact that both stations are filled up at each visit. In terms of the
order generation, the same logic applies here as previously. That is, if
the station has the lowest turnover rate in its group, the tank with a
lower turnover rate at the station is filled up to it maximum capacity,

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
This chapter will discuss and reflect the findings gained in chapter 6.Sec-
tion 7.1 will discuss the analytical findings of the paper, and bring conclu-
sive remarks. Section 7.2 reflects on the various limitations that impacted
the project. Section 7.3 lastly addresses possible further research on the
topic.
7.1
Interpretation of results
The main topic of this thesis has been to critically evaluate the choice of
can-do orders versus their must-do orders. While the total cost was the
main objective of the problem, it became evident that the total driven
kilometres would be a function of the quality of the solution method,
i.e. by minimizing total visits. In order to evaluate the can-do orders
against the must-do orders, two strategies were proposed. The first one
combines can-do and must-do orders by a group formation based on how
often each station gets empty during the planning horizon. The second
strategy takes a more direct approach, and only selects must-do orders.
Upon running the two strategies, it became evident that under flexible
circumstances, the second strategy performs better.
It is worth considering that as strategy 2 only supplies at the dry run,
it assumes a perfect consumption forecast. That means that if certain
changes occur during the month, the strategy could have a hard time
dealing with it, and dry runs might even occur. As the data set has
only a slight variation in the consumption rate, there might be instances
in real life where stations can have a higher consumption than initially
anticipated. Strategy 1 however tries to mitigate this by supplying each
==PAGE:66==
54
7 Discussion
station with the consumption up to the next visit, plus one day. This
both deals with variability in the consumption, but also could more eas-
ily deal with real-life variation that can occur. Strategy 1 therefore has
the potential to be more robust than strategy 2. One of the drawbacks
of strategy 1 is that tanks with a really low turnover rate, e.g. they need
to be replenished every second day, are being grouped together with sta-
tions with a higher turnover rate, and therefore all stations in that group
need to be visited every second day. A good example of this can be seen
on figure 6.3, where station 10435 turns over every 3-5 days according to
strategy 2, but is visited every 2 days by strategy 1.
The assumption of having only one tank per station was defined so
that the design of the solution method would be more simple, and more
easily understood. It was important however to test the method on a
more complex model, therefore the extended model was developed and
tested upon. The results of the extended model indicates that under
more complex scenarios, using only must-do orders becomes more dif-
ficult to solve. Strategy 2 however does perform better, showing that
choosing only must-do orders does in fact perform better. While there
might be some indication that mixing the two strategies together could
be favourable, testing that was out of scope for this thesis, thus no con-
clusive remarks can be made in that regard.
It also an important element to mention that the method’s perfor-
mance relies heavily on the data at hand. If there are a lot of popular
gas stations that turn over frequently, the number of visits will increase.
It was furthermore seen that increasing the number of groups generated,
improved the performance generally. There is therefore room for further
analysis on how it would perform in higher amount of groups. Due to
time, this was out of scope for this research however.
7.2
Project limitations
The biggest limitation of this study was the amount of time it took to
test and verify different strategies. As the strategies proposed could in-

clude many variations that could be tested, the computational diﬀiculty
made it diﬀicult to test many variations.
There are also a few cases where the models do not reach the optimal-

the computer all strategies were tested on. Using a more powerful com-
puter or interface might allow for more testing, and furthermore could
even improve the solution by, at the very least, get much closer to the

A small limitation to the study was the limited amount of literature
there was on the topic. It was mentioned in chapter 3 that there are not
a great deal of sources out there on the Petrol Station Replenishment

variations in each publication. It was therefore diﬀicult to gather initial
knowledge on the problem, and getting an idea for how to approach it
took more time than initially anticipated. On the other hand, this paved
the way for looking at the problem from a new perspective, and try to
tackle it differently than other sources have done previously.
7.3
Further research
As the topic of this thesis is a new perspective on the problem at hand,
there is some room for additional research.
Firstly, as mentioned in section 7.1, the logic behind the group forma-
tion could be explored further. Some stations could be entirely removed
from the group formation and be treated independently, due to their
unique characteristics. One idea could be to take the tanks with the
lowest turnover rate, and keep them out of the group formation. The
order generation of groups would therefore only include stations with a
turnover rate above some set limit. By doing so, one would in general
be combining strategy 1 and 2 together.
Other factors could also be considered when grouping together sta-
tions, such as station location, and the product each station tank is
==PAGE:68==
56
7 Discussion
demanding. Considering other factors however might act as trade-offs
against the turnover rate. One should therefore be careful when selecting
other parameters here, in order to avoid worsening the solution.
The appropriate amount of groups could furthermore be explored.
There was an indication that more groups indicated a better performance
of the solution, and this could therefore be tested further. This can how-
ever vary based on the data set at hand, whereas if there is a low variation
between turnover rates of all stations, lower group amount is likely to be
more favourable.
Secondly, an improvement procedure could be explored. It was seen
that the VRP performed worse on days with 20 visits or higher, and
therefore a look-back or look-look ahead procedures could be conducted.
This could be done by taking the most demanding days and seeing if
some supply visits there could be moved to the day before, or the day
after. One could therefore even out the visit per day over the whole time
horizon.
Lastly, as the VRP was the most time consuming process of the two
strategies, there might be room for improving the VRP, such that it
performs better. While the VRP was simplified to the best of the ability
of the author, there might be some areas where it could be broken down
even more. For example, in the current version it both designs routes
and loads trucks. These two processes could be divided into two sub-
problems, where each problem is performed at a daily basis for the entire
period.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this research paper, real-life instances of the problem of supplying
petrol to stations around Denmark were investigated. The problem, de-
fined as the Petrol Station Replenishment Problem, involves a multi-
horizon and highly complex characteristics, which makes it diﬀicult to
solve. Several heuristic methods have been researched on the topic in
the past, but this thesis takes a new perspective on the problem. The
logic of ”can-do and must-do orders” was investigated, in terms of the
strength and weaknesses of choosing to supply stations earlier than ac-
tually needed.
The problem was firstly defined mathematically, and
experiments were made using an exact method. The results of the exper-
iments showed that the problem is far too complex to solve using linear
programming techniques, thus heuristic strategies were developed.
Two heuristic approaches were proposed and developed, which ap-
proach the problem from two different perspectives. Firstly, a decom-
position heuristic was developed, which aims to group stations together
based on similarities, and furthermore generate orders for the whole time
period. The strategy makes use of the ”can-do and must-do” logic by
combining daily mandatory orders with optional orders.
The second
strategy only makes use of must-do orders, and thus only visits stations
at the day of their dry run. The two strategies were developed such that
the ”can-do and must-do orders” concept could be critically analysed, by
introducing a strategy that only takes must-do orders.
In terms of the existing literature, though limited, there were some
comparisons that could be made. Judging by the mostly related litera-
ture, as well as the own company’s results, both strategies perform quite
well in comparison. While the two comparison sources were different in
==PAGE:70==
58
8 Conclusion
complexity and approach, the initial results showcased that the strate-
gies outperformed the two. It however became clear from the results that
mixing can-do orders with must-do orders could not compete with strat-
egy 2 in terms of performance and quality of the solution. The results
showed that strategy 2 had a 60% better cost, and used a 136% less time
solving the instances.
In order to validate the results even further, the two strategies were
tested against an extended model, whereas a second tank was added to
each station. This meant higher supply demand at each station, and fur-
thermore reflected real-life scenarios better. The results of the extended
model showcased that both strategies had a harder time to solve the in-
stances, and moreover the cost increased significantly, 39.2% and 31.5%
respectively. Strategy 2 however still outperforms strategy 1 significantly
in terms of cost. While further research is encouraged on the topic of the
thesis, the result indicates that focusing on must-do orders will decrease
costs over time.
This thesis takes a new and fresh perspective on the Petrol Station
Replenishment Problem, as well as other similar Inventory Routing Prob-
lems, and gives a detailed overview on how combining can-do orders with
must-do orders can be constructed.
