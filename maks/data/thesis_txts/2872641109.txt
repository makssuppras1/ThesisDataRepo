Abstract 
 
2 
 
 
This study aims to enhance the efficiency of Returnable Packaging Systems (RtPS) 
in “return-on-the-go” models in the food industry. After a literature and empirical 
research were conducted, user acceptance, business viability, logistical complexity 
and sustainability were analysed. It was concluded that one of the main obstacles 
these systems face is the user misbehaviour, since users’ actions often lead to 
inefficiencies that decrease the sustainability potential of the systems. 
To achieve the desired user behaviour, this project presents a toolkit for system 
designers who work in reusable packaging supplier companies. It initially provides 
guidance to examine their current system characteristics and identify hotspots by 
understanding the root causes of these behaviours. Then, it presents a set of 
strategies to address them, as well as Rebound Effects prevention strategies to 
ensure the achievement of the environmental goals. It concludes with an 
implementation and evaluation plan. 
These initiatives support increasing their system’s user acceptance and, therefore, 
its effectiveness from sustainability, business viability and logistical complexity 
perspectives.  
 
Keywords: Reusable Packaging, Returnable Packaging Systems, Return on-the-go, 
Reusable Packaging Systems, Food industry, Circular Economy, Sustainability, 
Rebound Effects, User behaviour, Behavioural change strategies, User acceptance 
and Design toolkit.

Introduction 
 
9 
 
 
 
“In a world increasingly focused on sustainability, the mantra “reduce, reuse, 
recycle” has evolved to become a ubiquitous rallying cry - both for corporates and 
individuals. However, current evidence suggests that while “reduce” and “recycle” 
have progressed significantly in recent years, “reuse” has been lagging” (Returnable 
Packaging | Strategy&, n.d.). 
In the last decades, an urgency for more sustainable practices has become 
increasingly evident. The past ten years (from 2015-2024) have been the warmest 
years on record and this past 2024 was the warmest year ever recorded, with 1.55ºC 
above pre-industrial level, which surpasses the 1.5ºC limit set by the Paris 
Agreement in 2015 (WMO, 2025).  
This ongoing Earth temperature rise is a consequence of resource overconsumption 
and increasing greenhouse gas emissions. One way to measure the current level of 
overconsumption is the Earth Overshoot Day, the date when humanity's demand of 
ecological resources exceeds what Earth can regenerate in a year. It occurred on the 
1st of August in 2024 and for 2025 it is expected to happen the 17th of July. This also 
indicates that the Earth’s biocapacity is insufficient to sustain humanity’s Ecological 
Footprint (Earth Overshoot Day, 2025). 
Denmark presents an even more alarming date for its Overshoot Day, the 19th of 
March. This highlights the country’s high resource consumption and the urgent need 
for circular solutions. The Circularity Gap Report in Denmark reveals that the 
country’s economy is only 4% circular. This means that out of all the materials 
consumed, only 4% make it back into the economy as recycled materials (CGR 
Denmark, 2025). 
One of the main sectors that is responsible for resource overconsumption is the 
packaging business. In Denmark, each inhabitant generates 178 kilograms of 
packaging per year, positioning the country in the 7th position in terms of packaging 
waste generation in the European Union (EU). Therefore, there is clearly a need for 
more resource-efficient and sustainable packaging solutions (Pison et al., 2015) 
(Packaging Waste Statistics - Statistics Explained, 2024). 
Considering all this factors, a key strategy to mitigate the environmental impact of 
this sector is to rethink packaging systems and make them more sustainable. In the 
current economy, materials are taken from the Earth, products are made from them, 
and eventually they end up thrown away as waste. In contrast, Circular Economy 
(CE) is based on the principles of designing out waste and pollution, keeping 
products and materials in use, and regenerating natural systems. In fact, one of 
Denmark’s pathways to support CE is to create a more circular food system.

However, making technology more efficient and implementing circular strategies 
can sometimes lead to systematic responses to these interventions, also called 
Rebound Effects (RE), which can lower CE effectiveness. RE are systemic or 
behavioural responses that occur despite sustainable efforts and result in less 
impact than expected or ultimately higher negative impact (Hertwich, 2005). 
To support the transition to RuPS, this thesis aims to make these systems more 
effective and convenient for all the stakeholders involved in the food sector, while 
anticipating and preventing potential rebound effects. This project is tailored to 
companies that have already adopted a Reusable Packaging System and are looking 
to enhance its effectiveness. Therefore, encouraging their implementation, 
promoting CE, and reducing the environmental impact in the sector.

What is Reusable Packaging? 
Reusable Packaging refers to all packaging that is designed to be used multiple 
times before being discarded or recycled. This englobes a system of containers, 
crates, pallets or other packaging materials, although for the scope of this thesis the 
focus is only on primary packaging or retail packaging, which is the one in direct 
contact with the product (Dillon, 2023). 
Reusable Packaging Systems (RuPS) 
Reusable Packaging Systems are systems that promote circularity by incorporating 
Reusable Packaging. They include from production and distribution stages to 
collection and return ones. They also cover the cleaning and redistribution of RP to 
be back in the loop and be reused as many times as possible. Their aim is to provide 
a more sustainable alternative to single-use packaging systems by reducing waste 
and resource consumption, lowering gas emissions and creating environmental 
awareness. 
Current situation 
Currently, more and more organisations and companies are implementing CE 
strategies. Although implementing this type of RuPS is often challenging, as they 
demand high collaboration between stakeholders, there are major players in the 
food industry that have started using them. Danone, for example, renewed its 
strategic partnership with the Ellen Macarthur Foundation in 2020 and set up a goal 
of scaling Reusable Packaging and collection schemes, developing a Circular 
Economy for the food industry and learning tools to increase CE understanding 
(Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2021a). Also, Carrefour started implementing RuPS by 
introducing refill stations for dried goods like cereal and pasta in some of its 
supermarkets (Robinson, 2024). These examples show how the trend is growing and 
how innovation is required in this sector. 
Furthermore, the European Union (EU) is also prioritizing Reusable Packaging as 
part of its sustainability mandate (see further data about European and Danish 
legislations in A.1 Environmental legislations), which is a sign that not only 
companies are interested in making a change on the system, but also governments

Benefits and challenges of Reusable Packaging Systems 
Benefits of Reusable Packaging Systems 
From the user, business viability, logistical complexity and sustainability 
perspectives, the advantages of Reusable Packaging have been widely recognized, 
making it an increasingly popular choice for businesses and consumers.  
From a user point of view, RuPS offer different benefits such as community 
engagement, as people are working together towards a common objective; job 
creation, since they are new systems with new roles needed; and health 
improvement, since waste and pollution are decreased (Heeremans et al., 2025). 
From the business perspective, RP is often more durable and robust than single-
use packaging, which reduces the possibility of damage during transportation and 
storage. Consequently, companies would save money as the risk of damage or loss 
during these stages is reduced.  
==PAGE:15==
 
 
Initial literature research 
 
14 
 
These systems are also associated with other benefits such as giving the company 
a sustainable image, which can attract more clients and, consequently, create more 
sales (Raible et al., 2024). 
Moreover, although the initial investment of implementing RuPS might be higher, the 
long-term savings can arise through reduced material costs, waste management 
costs, and potential economies of scale as a reuse system is optimized. This leads 
to a higher return on investment (ROI) over time (World Economic Forum, n.d.). 
An example that shows that RuPS are a cost-saving option is a previous study from 
the Ellen MacArthur Foundation that revealed that converting 20% of all plastic 
packaging into reuse models represents a $10 billion opportunity (Ingilizaian et al., 
2019). 
Regarding logistical complexity, by streamlining operations across the supply 
chain, logistics processes could be more efficient, and RuPS might reduce the 
inventory costs (Scherrer de Quadros, 2024) (Manibusan, 2023). 
Lastly, regarding the sustainability benefits, RuPS not only reduce carbon 
emissions but also reduce resource consumption.  
An analysis developed by Zero Waste Europe and Reloop, in collaboration with the 
University of Utrecht, reports that Reusable Packaging produces by far fewer carbon 
emissions than single-use packaging. According to their report, setting a target of 
50% Reusable Packaging by 2030 in the EU could cut CO2 emissions by 3.7 million 
tonnes and save 10 billion cubic metres of water and 28 million tonnes of material 
(Zero Waste Europe, n.d.)  (World Economic Forum, n.d.). 
As for the resource efficiency, the goal of CE is to maintain the value of the products, 
materials and resources as long as possible. By implementing RuPS, businesses 
could significantly decrease the environmental footprint associated with 
manufacturing, transportation, and waste management (Ellen MacArthur 
Foundation, Reuse – rethinking packaging, 2019).  
By implementing RuPS, companies would also help to promote awareness of 
environmental issues among their customers. Consequently, businesses can build 
a more environmentally conscious and compromised community by engaging 
customers and stakeholders on these issues (Manibusan, 2023). 
Challenges of Reusable Packaging Systems 
However, as mentioned before, many businesses are encountering some 
challenges when implementing, developing and maintaining these systems.  
First, from the user perspective, the main challenge is usually the adaptation of 
customers to this system and the behavioural change that it implies. The Bradley &

Corsini (2023) review claims that until RP is socially and culturally accepted by 
consumers, these systems will not be able to reach their full environmental and 
economic potential. Research has shown that between the 22% and 44% of 
returned packaging never reaches another consumer, highlighting significant 
inefficiencies in the current systems. Moreover, 1 out of 4 returned products end up 
being discarded, emphasizing the need for circular returns management practices

Secondly, this system involves a complex logistics network since interaction 
between different stakeholders is needed. This requires an overhaul of existing 
supply chain processes (Scherrer de Quadros, 2024). Consequently, another 
challenge is the scalability of RuPS. This could only be achieved with businesses 
cooperation along the reusable supply chain, as there is a lack of standardization 
and infrastructure along different regions.  
Lastly, these systems are limited by the current and ongoing legislations and 
government policies. However, governments are encouraging the growth and 
scaling of reusable systems with public incentives and more strict environmental 
regulations (Almeida & Milà i Canals, 2025) (Scherrer de Quadros, 2024). 
 
Rebound Effects of Reusable Packaging Systems 
Although Reusable Packaging seems to be the most favourable alternative form of 
packaging, under the wrong circumstances, it can have negative impacts on 
Circular Economy, which are identified as Rebound Effects. Rebound Effects occur 
when improvements in efficiency, effectiveness or sufficiency lead to increased 
overall consumption, which can lower the benefits of environmental practices. 
The rebound effect was originally studied from an energy-economics perspective by 
William Stanley Jevons, who published an essay in 1865 explaining the Jevons 
paradox. His research showed that large increases in energy efficiency led to large 
cost reductions and therefore, large increases in energy demand and consumption. 
Jevons’ paradox only applies when the RE is higher than 100%, meaning gains in 
efficiency have caused an overall increase in consumption (Brockway et al., 2021a). 
However, more recent research highlights that this concept has expanded to include 
systemic behavioural responses to efficiency, effectiveness or sufficiency strategies 
to moderate the overall consumption of products and services. The lack of 
consideration of potential REs triggered by CE is delaying the achievement of CE’s 
full potential (Scott et al., 2024) (C. Castro et al., 2022)  (Guzzo et al., 2023)(Andrew 
& Pigosso, 2024) (Figge & Thorpe, 2023). 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
Initial literature research 
 
16 
 
Sorrell & Dimitropoulos (2008) classifies REs in three categories that can be applied 
broadly: direct, indirect and economy-wide.  
Direct Rebound Effects occur when the increase of efficiency, effectiveness or 
sufficiency of a resource leads to an increase consumption of the same resource. 
Since the scope of this project only considers the interactions within the defined 
system, direct REs are the ones in which the study is focused.  
 
Figure 3: Direct Rebound Effects example 
An example of this, translated to RuPS, could be that if the efficiency of Reusable 
Packaging Systems improves, then, RP prices will be lower. This could result in a 
change of user behaviour, as they could perceive it as something they do not need 
to take care of, resulting in packaging losses and, consequently, in a need for more 
RP production. Therefore, consuming more resources and producing higher 
emissions than estimated when planning the system. 
Another example of a direct RE driven by user’s behaviour is overconsumption due 
to misperceived sustainability of Reusable Packaging. Since RP is perceived as a 
more sustainable option, consumers might mistakenly believe they can be less 
careful when disposing them. Similarly, they could think that because of its more 
sustainable appearance, RP should be recycled after use instead of reused. This 
could lead to an anticipated disposal (therefore, not reaching its environmental 
break-even point (e-BEP)). This behaviour leads to fewer effective return rates and, 
consequently, more RP would be needed to be produced unnecessarily, increasing 
material and energy consumption, as well as the overall environmental impact of 
the system (Cottafava et al., 2021). 
On the other hand, Indirect Rebound Effects occur when the savings due to an 
increase of efficiency, effectiveness or sufficiency are spent on other goods or 
services, increasing overall resource use. In the RuPS context, an indirect RE could 
happen if by increasing RuPS efficiency, companies start saving money and they 
expand other areas of their business, increasing the overall environmental impact. 
Lastly, Economy-wide Rebound Effects are the sum of direct and indirect effects. 
They are related to overall societal improvements that lead to economic changes 
(prices decrease) and to an increase of consumption (Brockway et al., 2021; C. G. 
Castro et al., 2022).

Business models for Reusable Packaging Systems 
The Ellen MacArthur Foundation identifies four RP business models that help 
businesses transition from a linear to a circular system: refill at home, refill on-the-
go, return from home and return on-the-go.  
 
Figure 4: Reusable Packaging business models 
• Refill at home systems consist of the users purchasing the refill of the 
packaging and refilling their RP at home. The packaging can be refill packs or 
concentrates that can be either reusable or single-use recyclable. 
• In refill on-the-go systems, the users bring their own containers to the stores 
or cafes and have them refilled. 
• In the return from home business model, the RP is collected from the 
customers’ homes after the consumption of the food/drink. 
• Return on-the-go business model involves users getting the product in RP 
and returning it at a store or drop-off point, such as a return machine or 
mailbox (Reusable Packaging Business Models, n.d.-a). 
In A.3 Reusable Packaging Systems - Business models analysis, the four different 
business models are presented in more depth. This was key to understand them and 
to create a baseline to compare the business models from the same perspectives. 
The return-on-the-go business model was selected as the most suitable for this 
project, as it aligns closely with mitigating rebound effects, particularly by 
emphasizing user responsibility and behaviour. Therefore, from now on, the focus of 
the study is on Returnable Packaging Systems (RtPS).

Methodology 
 
18 
 
 
 
The thesis follows the Design Research Methodology (DRM) by Blessing & 
Chakrabarti (2009). It was created to fulfil the need for a common methodology in 
design research and to help achieve more rigour, effectiveness and efficiency. It 
claims that by improving research, there will be a better overview for design research 
which would help implement its results in practice. 
This set of tools and guidelines define the framework of the design research and 
improves the chances of obtaining more valid and useful results. They collect the 
complexity and multi-faced facets of design. 
It is divided into four phases: 
1. Research Clarification (RC) 
2. Descriptive Study I (DS-I) 
3. Prescriptive Study (PS)

Methodology

Research Clarification (RC) 
The aim of this phase is to draft a research plan for the thesis that includes scope 
definition of the project and the defined research questions. This stage also covers 
the development and understanding of the current and desired situation, as well as 
the identification of the relevant disciplines and areas involved. 
Descriptive Study I (DS-I) 
The goal of this phase is to better understand the existing situation by doing further 
literature and empirical research and then analyse the results, as well as a 
systematic literature review. The expectation of this stage is to identify the 
challenges in the current situation and define the gap in the existing methods and 
approaches available in the literature to transition to or improve the efficiency of 
RtPS in the food industry. After this phase, the most suitable challenges are selected 
to be addressed and some design requirements for the support are defined. 
Prescriptive Study (PS) 
After gathering and analysing the knowledge gained in the previous stages, in the 
prescriptive study the desired situation is initially defined. The support 
requirements and main functions are detailed and then the support is developed. 
This stage involves initial evaluation and iterations. The last iteration is evaluated in 
the next section. 
Descriptive Study II (DS-II) 
In this last stage, the goal is to test and evaluate the support tool with stakeholders 
from the industry. An evaluation plan including measurable factors and success 
criteria is developed. After evaluation, necessary improvements are identified, and 
a last support iteration is done (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).

Single-use packaging is generating more and more concerns as the need for 
sustainable alternatives in the food industry is increasing. Returnable Packaging 
Systems are a promising solution to this matter, since they promote reuse. These 
systems show high environmental potential while leaving space for improvement 
and innovation. However, there are several challenges related to user acceptance, 
business viability, logistical complexity and sustainability.  
 
 
The scope of this study is to develop a support for system designers from Reusable 
Packaging supplier companies in the food industry to help them transition to, or 
improve the efficiency, of a return-on-the-go packaging system. The study analyses 
the perspectives of user acceptance, business viability, logistical complexity and 
sustainability of these systems. Potential rebound effects are also included in the 
study to ensure that the proposed solutions contribute to reduce environmental 
impact without compromising its sustainability gains. 
The challenges of these systems were defined as follows: 
- 
User acceptance: assesses how likely are the consumers to engage with the 
system, considering the willingness to enter the system, the convenience 
and the ease of use, and their likelihood to behave correctly when being a 
part of it.  
- 
Business viability: measures how feasible is for companies to make the 
transition from a single-use system to a returnable system or improve the 
efficiency of a returnable system. 
- 
Logistical complexity: refers to how complex the management of supply 
chain and distribution processes are, including transportation, storage and 
delivery time.

- 
Sustainability: defines the reduction of resource consumption, waste and 
carbon emissions achieved by using RtPS.

Stakeholder identification 
Returnable Packaging Systems in the food industry involve multiple stakeholders 
with different responsibilities and relationships between them. They were divided 
intro internal and external depending on if they have direct relationships or not with 
the food company that wants to transition to or improve their RtPS.  
The internal stakeholders involve: 
• Food company management employees: oversee developing strategies and 
the implementation, contact with manufacturers, logistics, retailers, etc. 
They ensure the alignment with the company goals and the implementation 
of the regulations.  
• Food company public-facing employees: engage with customers, give 
feedback to management, ensure smooth implementation and consumer 
satisfaction.  
• Washing responsible (if in-house): they are responsible for cleaning and 
sanitizing the RP.  
• Packaging suppliers: company that provides RP aligning to the food 
company’s needs. The designers that would use the support tool to help 
transitioning to RtPS are part of this company.  
Externally, a great variety of stakeholders can be found that interact with the RP in 
diverse points of view: 
• Users: use and return the RP to the system. 
• Food and beverage producer: company that produces the product that the

On these systems, from the user acceptance point of view, the RP collection is 
considered the main hotspot as it depends on the customer usage and 
understanding of the system. Therefore, the main challenge is user’s adaptation to 
this system and the behavioural change that it implies.  
Moreover, from the business viability perspective, scalability is the main challenge 
that companies face due to lack of packaging standardization. This also affects the 
logistical complexity since the infrastructure for collecting, washing, sorting and 
delivery cannot be shared between different companies. In addition, companies are 
unable to track the location and status of the RP, leading to inefficiencies on the

system and loss of inventory during transportation. Another challenge is that the 
absence of strong incentives plus the inconvenience of some drop-off points lead 
to lower return rates.  
From the sustainability perspective, an increase of the system’s efficiency could 
lead to unintended consequences that produce overconsumption, therefore, 
increasing the system’s environmental impact. These consequences, also known 
as rebound effects, are caused mainly by misperceived sustainability from users or 
overconsumption of RP because of prices reduction.  
 
 
After the initial literature review, a further empirical study was done to validate and

the literature review and the empirical study. The outcome of this study is a more

framework.

Data processing 
Before data was analysed, it was processed. The notes and transcripts that resulted 
from the data collection were organised in a table format (see in A.5 Empirical Study 
- Data processing) for each study case. The tables present the insights each 
stakeholder shared related to their system understanding. The systems were 
analysed from four perspectives: user acceptance, business viability, logistical 
complexity and sustainability. An extra area was added regarding system efficiency 
that helped identifying the factors that affect it. Those factors would be further 
analysed to prevent rebound effects due to efficiency increase in the system. 
Data analysis and interpretation 
After the data was processed, the findings were linked to get correlations and shared 
hotspots between the four scenarios. The data from the tables was summarized and 
then interpretated to get a more accurate challenge definition for each of the four 
criteria areas.  
From the point of view of user acceptance, results show that one of the main 
features that users value is the high-quality of the packaging, since they appreciate 
having a similar experience than using normal tableware. They also value being able 
to exchange the packaging if it breaks or deteriorates. However, some reluctance 
was found due to lack of knowledge of the system before the order.  
From a business viability perspective, RtPS add value to the restaurants and cafes 
since RP suppliers try to make the system affordable and food companies can save 
money by lowering the single-use packaging expenses. To increase return rates, 
monetary incentives have been found to be effective, although penalty systems 
seem to have a higher impact. 
From a logistical complexity point of view, the collection of RP does not add 
significant workload when it is properly integrated with the usual business 
operations of the public-facing employees and washing responsible.  However, the 
practice has shown that that is not usually the case. Moreover, storage space has 
also been categorized as insufficient in most of the scenarios. To help with inventory 
management and reduce costs, some companies have introduced RFID 
technologies. However, it can end up adding workload to public-facing employees 
when selling the product. Scalability is also a challenge since it involves business 
collaboration and the need of a shared infrastructure. 
In terms of logistics for the consumers, convenience is key, since most of them 
would not deviate more than 5 minutes to return the RP. Also, users find 
inconvenient to wait on a queue only to return the RP, triggering low return rates. RP 
collection is also usually limited to the opening hours of stores or restaurants, 
leading to potentially time-consuming interactions between users and employees. 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Descriptive Study I (DS-I) 
 
34 
 
Some of the companies tackle this problem by establishing multiple return points 
and smart mailboxes, which offers the user more flexibility.  
Regarding sustainability, users feel more sustainable using RP, and companies 
support sustainability by reducing the consumption of single-use packaging. 
However, overproduction can occur due to low return rates. 
In general, the system efficiency fails due to insufficient user assistance and 
incorrect behaviour from different stakeholders. Public-facing employees do not 
always explain the system to the customers, who then use the RP wrong and keep 
them instead of returning them.  Because of this, the systems gradually turn into a 
refill model. The public-facing employees have also showed inadequate conduct, 
which also leads to RP loss. 
 
Challenge identification from Empirical Study 
The implementation of RtPS faces a main challenge: users are reluctant to transition 
to RtPS.   
One of the main reasons is that the users do not understand the system or do not 
feel encouraged to take part in the system.  This is a consequence of the lack of 
guidance from public-facing employees who, due to the high-paced environment 
they work in, especially during rush hours, do not find the time to explain the system 
to the users. Moreover, the quality of the RP affects the users’ perception of the 
system. 
The return process is also generating reluctance since it is time-consuming for the 
users when it involves interacting with public-facing employees. Apart from that, the 
return process is usually limited by the opening hours of the restaurants and cafes. 
This causes inefficiency in the system, which is translated into low return rates, 
forcing a transition to a refill on-the-go system and therefore, overproduction. 
Moreover, there are ongoing challenges regarding cultural and behavioural 
adaptation since depending on the country, users present different attitudes 
towards RtPS. 
From the public-facing employee’s point of view, the collection of RP can also be 
time-consuming if it is not correctly integrated into the usual business operations 
and, in most cases, there is not enough storage space for the RP.  The public-facing 
employees have also been identified as a source of inefficiencies due to wrong 
behaviour, sometimes leading to loss of RP.  
From the food company’s perspective, scalability is one of the main challenges 
since it involves business collaboration and a need of a shared infrastructure. 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
Descriptive Study I (DS-I) 
 
35 
 
Overall, the main challenges rely on making the system as seamless as possible for 
all the stakeholders involved in the system.  
 
Validation of literature research 
To begin with, user adaptation has been proved to be one of the main challenges 
for successful system implementation. However, these real-case scenarios have 
shown that public-facing employees are not supporting this transition, as they are 
usually not explaining the system to users. Moreover, based on the empirical data, 
users do not need higher incentives. The study cases have shown that users in the 
Danish market are used to using this refund systems (Danish return system), so they 
are not expecting a high incentive in return. In addition, the empirical study 
confirmed that user acceptance is influenced by the quality of the RP. 
From the business/logistics perspective, RP tracking was identified as a challenge 
in the literature research. However, some companies are already addressing the 
situation by implementing RFID tracking systems. In relation to this, RP losses 
during logistics were believed to happen during transportation, but some of them 
are lost because of public-facing employee’s wrong behaviour. Users have 
confirmed reluctancy to the system also due to inconveniences in the return 
process, since it is either limited by the opening hours or by a time-consuming 
interaction with public-facing employees. Furthermore, another of the findings is 
that RP collection is also inconvenient for the public-facing employees as they 
perceive it as extra workload plus storage space is often insufficient. 
Regarding sustainability, the feeling of being more sustainable is one of the main 
motivations for users to participate in the system. In addition, low return rates often 
end up causing overproduction which increases the environmental impact of these 
systems. 
To conclude, considering system efficiency, empirical data has shown that an 
increase of use of these systems often leads to a transition to refill on-the-go 
models. The advantages and drawback of this evolution depend on the systems 
initial goal. Moreover, Rebound Effects appearance and prevention is addressed 
and studied in the Prescriptive Study.

Synthesized findings from the publications: methods and approaches  
Regarding the content, 4 papers were selected as relevant. These are the ones that 
answer the RQ2: “What are the methods and approaches available to implement 
Returnable Packaging Systems that are applicable to the food industry?”. They were 
synthesized as follows: 
The first tool consists of a set of subtle recommendations and suggestions from 
three points of view: design, communication and forming the habit. It assesses the 
challenges of user transition to RtPS, highlighting the importance of robust 
materials and standardization, and user understanding and awareness of the 
system by strong communication.  
Then, the second tool is a set of design recommendations to assess user 
acceptance challenges. It was focused on three RtPS archetypal models. Some of 
the findings are that user acceptance can be improved by having an effective 
communication with users and an effortless returning process. A limitation for these 
guidelines is that they only consider issues that affect user acceptance, but they do 
not contemplate other areas like business viability. Moreover, recommendations 
may only apply to similar models to the three that were studied. 
The third tool gives brief eco-design guidelines that recommend designers how to 
design the packaging, considering seven different points of view. It also analyses the 
user acceptance of RP in different types of food and gives further recommendations 
on how to start into the market, highlighting that it is easier to start with dry foods 
and bread, and that younger customers are more likely to engage with the system. 
Even though the research is mostly focused on supermarkets, the eco-design 
guidelines can be applied to all return on-the-go systems.  
The last tool is a strategic design tool that consists of a classification system that 
includes the Identification of 15 archetypal models of RP systems. It is useful for 
having a better understanding of the different archetypal models and to support 
idea generation for transitioning to RtPS. However, this tool was missing some 
guidelines for users, and it could not be tested on a real-case scenario although it 
presented successful results in an academic setting. 
Regarding the process, all the studies share the same target audience with the goal 
of supporting the transition to RtPS. All the studies did some initial data collection 
from different stakeholders, mostly users. Some of the collection methods were 
interviews, user experience evaluation, questionnaires… However, there is a 
common lack of testing and validation in real-case scenarios from the sector which 
end up with result not being validated.

Synthesized findings from the publications: support format 
From the support format point of view, five literature publications were selected. The 
findings can be synthesized as follows: 
A feature that can be highlighted in the fourth publication, “Mapping & classifying 
business models to replace single-use packaging in the food & beverage industry: a 
strategic design tool” (Mansour et al., n.d.), is its classification of the RP systems, 
which provides a structured way to analyse and categorise them by providing a 
visual classification tool to identify opportunity areas in the market through 
positioning existing offers and identifying gaps. 
The fifth publication is “Barriers to adoption of Reusable Packaging from supply 
chain perspective: multi criteria decision making analysis and industry 4.0 
strategies”. It explains how the industry 4.0 technology can be used by designers to 
enhance the efficiency in reverse logistics. Different barriers presented in tables 
answering what, why, where, when, who and how the issue is addressed. It is a good 
example of how a tool can be tailored towards identifying and describing the barriers 
and how to solve it, based on the pre-defined solutions within the industry 4.0. 
In the sixth publication, “Archetypes to categorise upstream packaging strategies 
for a Circular Economy” (Terzioğlu et al., 2024), ten archetypes of upstream 
packaging strategies are described, and each archetype was explained with 
stakeholder system maps and examples. It provides a clear structure for 
categorizing and comparing the different system approaches.  
Moreover, the “Characterization and environmental value proposition of reuse 
models for fast-moving consumer goods: Reusable Packaging and products by 
Muranko et al. (2021)” provides a reuse models framework to map the behaviour of 
the user, the product and the infrastructure. It describes each of the different reuse 
models and defines the “reuser” behaviour for each of them, how the reusable 
product is, how the infrastructure is organized and who the responsibilities rely on 
and offers some examples for each of the models. Even though the goal of the paper 
is to explain businesses that they can adopt multi-model combinations, the analysis 
and display of the different models is relevant for the scope of this project.  
Lastly, a UN environment programme report: “Turning off the Tap- How the World 
Can End Plastic Pollution and Create a Circular Economy United Nations 
Environment Programme (2023)” emphasizes the need to switch to reuse models 
and identifies the different areas that could be used to scale up the reuse models 
such as the bottled products, the retail and catering, etc. The report display and 
user-friendliness can be used as an inspiration.

Problem Clarification  
After the challenge identification in the Empirical Study, user acceptance was 
demonstrated to be a clear limitation to address for RtPS implementation.  
One of the main challenges regarding user acceptance was the user understanding 
of the system. User misunderstanding or lack of awareness of the system often lead 
to incorrect user behaviour. Currently, user awareness of the system relies mainly 
on public-facing employees. However, this has shown to cause inefficiencies in the 
systems, so one of the goals of the support is to ensure user understanding of the 
RtPS without making it a public-facing employee responsibility.  
Besides the system understanding, achieving correct user behaviour and 
interaction with the packaging are critical objectives to improve the efficiency of the 
RtPS. According to the empirical study, users frequently present undesirable 
behaviours, such as the retention of RP rather than their return. These behaviours 
can be caused by different factors, including a reduced sense of user’s 
responsibility for RP return or an insufficient awareness of the consequences of 
their actions. Behavioural design practices have the potential to guide the user 
behaviour towards the desired one. However, the effectiveness of the interventions 
is limited by the understanding of the root of these behaviours, as well as by the 
possible RE that could arise by implementing design initiatives in the system. 
By addressing user acceptance barriers, the other areas of the system would also 
be enhanced. By designing systems that are intuitive and easy to use, user friction 
is reduced, leading to higher adoption rates, increased satisfaction, and more 
consistent engagement with the RtPS. The sustainability goals would be achieved 
by maximizing the number of use cycles per RP, thereby reducing the need for new 
production, lowering resource consumption and packaging waste, and ultimately, 
minimising the overall environmental footprint of the system. This would directly 
improve the business viability by reducing costs and RP losses, and by improving 
the brand loyalty. Moreover, the correct behaviour from the users can simplify the 
logistical complexity, leading to a more effective collection, cleaning and inventory 
management.

To conclude, the goal of the support can be defined as follows: 
Enhance user acceptance of return on-the-go systems by first, addressing user 
barriers with behavioural strategies and then supporting Rebound Effects 
mitigation by providing identification and prevention strategies. 
 
Desired situation framing 
The main users of the tool would be system designers working in RP supplier 
companies that have already implemented RtPS or want to implement them, and 
are looking to optimize or improve the efficiency, effectiveness and sufficiency of 
their systems. The tool should be open to be used freely by any user interested. 
Therefore, the tool was designed to be simple and intuitive, requiring no skills or 
previous training.  
Since there is no commercial interest behind the development of the tool, it is 
expected to be free of charge. Initially, it will be introduced to some Reusable 
Packaging suppliers, and later, it is expected to be accessed by system designers on 
their own.  
In terms of use, the tool will not be used frequently, only when assessing the need 
for system improvements, during system optimization phases, or when addressing 
rebound effects.

Research questions answers 
Based on the DRM phases some research questions were formulated at the 
beginning of the study to structure it. After the development of the project, they were 
answered as follows: 
• RQ1: What are the key challenges in implementing Returnable Packaging 
Systems in the food industry from user acceptance, business viability, 
logistical complexity and sustainability perspectives, including rebound 
effects occurrence? 
The main challenges related to user acceptance can be divided into two key 
areas: the user understanding of the system and the user misbehaviour. 
Currently, the awareness of the system relies in public-facing employees and 
visual representations on the points of sale, which has been proved to 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
Discussion 
 
63 
 
communicate ineffectively and to cause reluctancy to the users to purchase RP.  
Regarding the user behaviour, the empirical study indicates that users frequently 
present undesirable behaviour, such as the retention of RP rather than their 
return. This can be caused by different factors, including a reduced sense of 
user’s responsibility for RP return or an insufficient awareness of the 
consequences of their actions.  
From the business viability point of view, scalability is one of the main challenges 
faced by the companies due to the lack of packaging standardization, which is 
directly linked to the logistical complexity. The collection of RP has also been 
proven to add workload to the public-facing employees, and the storage space 
has been categorized as a source of logistical problems. It is important to note 
that the incorrect behaviour of users and other stakeholders also affects the 
system’s effectiveness in these two areas.  
In terms of sustainability, the combination of these challenges decreases the 
achievement of the system’s fully sustainability potential, such as the loss of 
packaging or the resource consumption due to ineffective logistics. Moreover, 
Rebound Effects may arise by the increase of the system’s effectiveness and 
therefore generate an overconsumption of resources. 
 
• RQ2: What are the methods and approaches available to enhance 
Returnable Packaging Systems in the food industry? 
Regarding the content, four papers were selected as relevant. The first paper 
consists of a set of subtle recommendations and suggestions from three points 
of view: design, communication and forming the habit, while the second one is 
also a set of recommendations but focused on user acceptance challenges. The 
third paper presents eco-design guidelines on how to design the packaging, and 
even though most of them are related to the physical design of the packaging, 
some of them are related to the system design. The last tool classifies the RtPS 
in 15 archetypal models and supports ideas brainstorming to transition to RtPS. 
From the support format point of view, four more literature publications were 
selected as relevant. The first one presents a classification of RtPS and guides a 
gap identification in the system. The second paper presents barriers in RtPS and 
brainstorms solutions within industry 4.0. The third paper describes the different 
business models and is focused on user behaviour and responsibilities 
distribution amongst the system. Lastly, a UN report emphasizes the need to 
switch to reuse models and identifies the different areas that could be used to 
scale up the reuse models. The last tool identified as relevant in the content is

• RQ3: How to support designers in enhancing Returnable Packaging 
Systems in the food industry by addressing user acceptance and 
sustainability challenges, including rebound effects? 
After answering RQ1 and RQ2 and therefore, identifying the main challenges 
faced in RtPS and the existing methods and approaches to enhance these 
systems, the goal of the support was as follows: 
Enhance user acceptance of return on-the-go systems by first, addressing user 
barriers with behavioural strategies and then supporting Rebound Effects 
mitigation by providing identification and prevention strategies. 
A toolkit of templates was the format that was decided to be elaborated. After 
iterating twice and implementing both external designers’ feedback as well as 
some changes after the completion of the toolkit from the designers of it, a third 
iteration was developed. The set of templates were grouped into five sections: 
“Understanding”, “Strategies”, “Rebound Effects”, “Implementation” and 
“Evaluation”. This constituted the defined complete version of the toolkit.  
Afterwards, a compact version including only the “Strategies” and “Rebound

sections were selected as they add more value to companies that have 
experience in the industry, since they have a better understanding of the user 
barriers and do not need guidance for the implementation and evaluation plans.

Conclusions 
 
67 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
To conclude, the main contributions of this project are first, RuPS business models’ 
hotspots identification: secondly, a deeper analysis of the current challenges of 
RtPS; then, a support for designers from RP supplier companies in the food industry 
to help them transition to or improve the effectiveness of their return on-the-go 
system; and finally, by developing the support, the existing gap in the literature was 
partly filled since the toolkit is targeted to return on-the-go model, it is focused on 
system design and includes RE prevention strategies.   
This set of tools has been defined as valuable by stakeholders from the industry 
since it provided new perspectives and ideation practices to address user 
acceptance challenges and Rebound Effects appearance. Consequently, the toolkit 
is expected to potentially enhance user acceptance and therefore, RtPS 
implementation.  
However, some of the limitations this project had were related to: time constraints, 
therefore, the toolkit only covered user acceptance and sustainability matters 
instead of including also business viability and logistical ones; moreover, because 
of time limitations, a complete digital version of it could not be developed; the lack 
of a high number of participants for evaluation, that limited the conclusiveness of 
the findings; and lastly, the lack of companies involvement, so only the initiatives 
ideation process could be evaluated instead of including also the initiatives’ 
implementation results in the system. 
 
Reflection of the design process  
The process of developing this project has been smooth and consistent. The Design 
Research Methodology has given guidance through all the stages of the project, and 
the research questions have also helped define the different steps of the process.  
The main change of direction was during the DS-I. While the original scope covered 
all business models of the Reusable Packaging System (RuPS), it was narrowed to 
focus on return on-the-go, as the differences between the models prevented the 
toolkit from effectively addressing them all.  
==PAGE:69==
 
 
Conclusions 
 
68 
 
As expected, the beginning of the project was overwhelming due to the amount of 
knowledge required to fully understand the Reusable Packaging Systems field. 
Moreover, the systematic literature review was challenging since it was a new 
process that requires specific ways of filtering papers to achieve the desired results. 
Lastly, another big challenge was the difficulty of contacting with companies from 
the industry that had the interest and time to engage with the project, which was a 
recurring problem when trying to do the evaluation in the DS-II.

European legislations 
To fully frame the market, the legislations on the European territory were 
investigated.  
The European Union (EU) developed some rules to make more sustainable 
packaging and reduce packaging waste. This was meant to be done by lowering the 
use of primary raw materials and fostering the transition to a circular, sustainable 
and competitive economy. The Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation 2025/40 
(PPWR) rules were developed in 2024 and entered into force on 11 February 2025. 
These rules are a key regulation to reduce packaging's environmental impact. 
The main goals of this rules are the following: 
• Prevent and reduce packaging waste through more reuse and refill systems. 
• Make all packaging in the EU market recyclable in an economically viable 
way by 2030. 
• Safely increase the use of recycled plastics in packaging. 
• Decrease the use of virgin materials in packaging and put the sector on track 
to climate neutrality by 2050. 
This new set of requirements cover the entire packaging life cycle, from product 
design to waste handling. It is expected that by 2030, these measures help reducing 
gas emissions and water use while managing the packaging impacts on the 
environment and human health. These rules include: 
• Restrictions on certain single-use plastics, such as pre-packed fruit and 
vegetables weighing less than 1,5 kg and individual portions of condiments, 
sauces, and sugar in hotels, bars and restaurants. 
• Minimising the weight and volume of packaging and avoiding unnecessary 
packaging. 
• 2030 and 2040 targets for a minimum percentage of recycled content in 
packaging. 
• A requirement for take-away businesses to offer customers the option to 
bring their own containers at no extra cost. 
• Minimising substances of concern, including restrictions on packaging 
containing per- and polyfluorinated alkyl substances (PFAS) if they exceed 
certain thresholds. 
 (Packaging Waste - European Commission, n.d.; Regulation - EU - 2025/40 - EN - 
EUR-Lex, n.d.)

Danish regulations and action plan 
Beyond the European PPWR, the Danish government has its own national additional 
regulations regarding packaging. One of the main measures is the Extended 
Producer Responsibility (EPR) for packaging that will take effect on October 1st of 
2025. This regulation aims to reduce packaging consumption by promoting the 
collection, recycling, and reuse of various packaging materials.  
As part of this extended producer responsibility, environmental objectives regarding 
the collection and recycling of packaging materials have been strengthened, 
emphasizing the collective commitment to a more sustainable and environmentally 
responsible approach to packaging (Emballagebekendtgørelsen, n.d.; EPR 
Denmark: Extended Producer Responsibility 2025 – Ecosistant, n.d.; Packaging – 
Legislation - Dansk Producentansvar, n.d.). 
In addition to the EPR, Denmark also developed the National Plan for Prevention and 
Management of Waste (2020-2032) as part of its transition to a Circular Economy. 
The plan focuses on producing less waste and having better use of natural 
resources, enhancing more and better recycling practices, improving biomass use 
and developing a sustainable built environment. 
One of its key points to avoid resource overconsumption is by strengthening circular 
business models such as the reuse of products. This will increase the utilisation rate 
of products and materials.  
This plan also highlights the need to address single-use plastics increased 
utilization, especially in the packaging sector. The plan sets some targets including: 
• Introduce a ban on certain single-use plastics.  
• Reduce the number of specific forms of plastic take-away packaging by 50%. 
• Require recycling of a minimum of 60% of collected plastic waste. 
• Establish a research mission on recycling and reduction of plastic waste.

Some packaging return initiatives in Denmark have already been successfully 
implemented and developed. The most known one is the Danish Return System and 
was approved by the Danish Ministry of Environment to operate the deposit and 
return system. This privately owned, non-profit organisation started in 2002 and, 
nowadays, achieves a 90% collection rate for single-use cans and bottles made 
from plastic, glass, and aluminium. They are responsible for collecting, processing, 
and returning the empty bottles and cans to producers to be recycled. 
Their return and deposit system starts with the producers paying a deposit to the 
organization when sending beverages to shops. Then, consumers get charged the 
pant of the packaging and will later receive a refund if they return it to some returning 
points usually located in the same shops. Essentially, the deposit money flows 
continuously throughout the system, so that it breaks even, rather than making a 
profit. Unclaimed deposit money, however, is kept by the Danish Return System, 
which is used to finance and develop the system. 
Some key success factors were identified from this system: 
• Effective infrastructure: they successfully established a network in which 
private shops, the government and consumers cooperate towards a 
common goal. They managed to develop a system that is beneficial for every 
stakeholder involved in it, apart from themselves. 
• Customer engagement: deposit refunds and cultural participation secures a 
high rate of return on the consumer side. Plus, the flexibility of return points 
makes it much easier for users to return the packaging. 
• Economic benefits: it is cheaper for companies to produce new products 
from recycled materials, so that is another incentive for them to participate 
in the system. Moreover, they do not need to handle the logistics system to 
collect the empty cans and bottles. 
• Environmental benefits: this system preserves the value of the materials, 
saves virgin resources and emits less CO2. For instance, producing cans 
from recycled materials uses 95% less energy than it would if they were 
produced using virgin materials. Moreover, due to the high return rate in 2021 
a 14% less CO2 was emitted per collected can/bottle when compared to 
2020 (State of Green, 2022).

1. Refill at home  
In this business model, consumers refill their containers at home, using refill packs 
or concentrates that can be either reusable or single-use recyclable. Some 
examples of products that can use this approach are cereals, grains, spices and 
seasonings, that can be purchased and used to refill the jars at home. Another 
example of products that rely on this approach are beverages, sold in shape of 
concentrated juices or syrups that can be diluted.

Benefits 
This model allows consumers to purchase larger quantities of refill products,

of going to the store, which makes it more convenient for them. It also gives a lot of 
space for customization approaches, especially in product sizes.  
In terms of cost savings, the production and transport costs are minimized by using 
less material. 
Challenges 
Regarding its challenges, consumer adoption to the new system is crucial, and the 
first step to achieve it is providing them with a refillable container to store at home, 
either delivered by the brand or another source. Another user barrier is that they 
need enough space to store the refill products at home. There is also the risk that 
containers are not cleaned correctly, which is a big concern for hygiene and possible 
contamination.  
From a design point of view, the product must be compatible with multiple container 
shapes and would require standardization or universal designs.  
Also, from the environmental point of view, a full life cycle assessment (LCA) is 
necessary so refills do not create more waste than the traditional single-use model. 
Therefore, it is important to supply refills either without packaging or in packaging 
that is recyclable, compostable, or even reusable itself (Reusable Packaging 
Business Models, n.d.-b). 
Lastly, from the economic perspective, there are initial setup costs, both for the 
company and for the consumers, who needs to do an initial purchase of the refilling 
system. 
Market situation 
Even though this approach is mostly used for cleaning products and soaps, one of 
the biggest companies that is rooting for this approach is PepsiCo, who has 
developed a SodaStream Sparkling Water Maker, which is a home carbonation 
system that includes a machine, a carbon dioxide cylinder and a plastic bottle. 
Then, they offer a range of 60 concentrate syrups for customers to choose from. To 
further minimize packaging waste, PepsiCo also offer a system whereby consumers 
can exchange their spent CO2 cylinders for new ones (Packaging World, 2025). 
 Another big example is Nescafe, who has designed refillable paper backs for coffee 
to enable the refilling of glass jars, extending their lifespan (Packaging World, 2024).

For the consumer challenges point of view, users might find it inconvenient to carry

an embedded chip that interacts with the system, giving information about how 
many times it has been reduced, which products are sold most, or if they are lost, 
who is responsible. Then, customers can return it and get the deposit back, and the 
packaging is sanitized and used by another consumer (MIWA, 2025). 
There is also a UK Refill Coalition, constituted of GoUnpacked, Aldi, Ocado and 
CHEP, that is rooting for an in-store refill system for dry goods at Aldi stores in the 
UK (Packaging World, n.d.).

Hotspots analysis 
The main hotspots found in this business model are mostly related to the container 
logistics. The user needs to transport the RP to the supermarket every time they 
want to purchase their goods, and then store them at home, which might take up 
more space than single-use packaging. Then, they need to clean them and sanitize 
them between uses, which adds inconvenience to the process. Partial refills are 
also hard to manage, since the users might need to transfer the contents to another 
packaging before using it to refill.  
Because of these inconveniences, refill stations must be conveniently placed to 
ensure user’s convenience. In stores, the coordination of the supply chain is 
challenging, as well as setting up, maintaining and cleaning the refill stations (which 
requires water and energy). Therefore, there can be spoilage and waste due to 
inventory inefficiencies.

Benefits 
The main benefit of these systems is the user convenience and adaptability as 
customers do not need to take part in the returning process of the packaging, since 
RP is picked up directly from their homes and the system can be implemented with 
relatively little need for significant behaviour change from consumers. 
Consequently, leading to higher return rates.   
Moreover, the company adopting this model can ensure strong brand engagement

Challenges 
Despite the potential benefits, these systems also encounter some challenges. 
Although consuming and returning from home is often a welcome behaviour change 
for consumers, these systems could be inconvenient for users as they may need to 
adapt to the delivery time of the product, to ensure they are home by that time. 
Moreover, RP providers need systems to handle the logistics of return and cleaning 
of products to deliver the solution, and those systems often have yet to be built. 
Addressing that challenge, and thereby giving consumers the access they need, is 
the top priority for this modality (Inglizian & Ghosh, 2021). 
Furthermore, many refill schemes have struggled as they add costs for both the 
retailer and consumer, as retailers face expenses in setting up refill stations, while 
consumers must invest in reusable containers, making them less popular or 
profitable than pre-packaged goods. Therefore, supermarkets show concerns over 
the consumer appetite for refills during the cost-of-living crisis. As Rob Spencer, 
director of GoUnpackaged, said: “An industry-wide approach will lead to a reuse 
system that works for everyone in the supply chain and make it easier for shoppers 
to engage with reuse via online shopping” (Butler, 2024). 
 
Market situation 
An example of a company that implemented this system is the American 
supermarket company, Walmart, in collaboration with Loop. In 2022 they developed 
a pilot for home delivery service in Bentonville and Rogers, Arkansas, offering an 
assortment of products in reusable containers via Walmart+ InHome (return from 
home). Although the pilot program concluded in 2024, the VP of merchandising for

eliminate packaging and single-use plastic from many of their regular purchases”, 
which shows the clear intention of the company to go towards Reusable Packaging 
Systems (Packaging World, n.d.-a). 
Moreover, a European company that has also implemented this system is Ocado 
Retail, based in the UK. It operates the online grocery platform from Ocado Group. 
It started offering everyday products such as pasta, rice and washing liquid in 
refillable packaging in August 2024. The company claimed that each Reusable 
Packaging could replace up to five single-use plastic items and they were designed 
to be used more than 60 times. Some results of the first months were that the RP 
reached nearly 30% sales share of equivalent single-use products, with the 
packaging return rate increasing week by week as customers hand their packaging 
back with each Ocado purchase (Nott, 2024). 
 
Hotspots analysis 
The first clear critical hotspot area in these systems is related to the collection and 
reverse logistics. This model requires a broad and complex logistics that should be 
managed effectively to keep costs and emissions low. Therefore, existing delivery 
networks should be integrated to prevent extra transportation. 
Another risk area is user’s behaviour, since it should be guided to avoid packaging 
mismanagement. Consumers might not understand the system flow and end up 
mixing the packaging with waste, leading to lower return rates.  
Finally, in the cleaning and sanitizing phase, energy and water consumption must 
be optimized so the environmental footprint of the system does not offset the 
intended benefits. Also, health authorities and logistics providers need to ensure 
that the packaging meets the safety regulations to be used again.

This model is based on customers returning the packaging to designated pick-up 
points (either in-store or a drop-bin) and afterwards, businesses are responsible for 
their cleaning and redistribution. It is especially effective in urban areas with dense 
populations (Huay Neo & Prabhakar, 2024).

Benefits 
These systems offer some benefits to the businesses as the return system is often 
offered by a third-party business as a service, meaning the brand or retailer does not 
need to be directly responsible for the reuse system. The service provider would 
provide the packaging and handle the collection, washing, and redistribution. This 
has worked particularly well for beverages and takeaway food.  
Moreover, standardisation of packaging design can help make reuse affordable and 
feasible for the whole value chain. System costs are lowered if packaging can be 
used across brands or even entire product categories, because storage, transport, 
sorting, and washing can be optimised (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2023). 
 
Challenges 
On-the-go models (e.g. returnable beverage cups) require greater adjustments to 
consumption habits due to the need for consumer travel and education. The 
absence of strong incentives or the inconvenience of some drop-off points may lead 
to lower return rates (Inglizian & Ghosh, 2021). 
Moreover, these systems involve complex logistics as managing the return, 
cleaning, and reuse of packaging involves creating effective systems for drop-off 
points and return shipments. This often requires a complete overhaul of existing 
supply chain processes. Because of its complexity, companies often experience 
lack of real-time transparency as they are unable to track the location and status of

the reusable containers, leading to inefficiencies and potential loss of inventory

Food companies like Starbucks, KFC or Taco Bell, among 30 other companies, 
collaborated in the Petaluma Reusable Cup Project (California), in which they 
implemented reusable cups as the default option for to-go drinks for three months. 
The testing resulted in over 220,000 disposable cups returned in just 12 weeks, 
therefore, reducing the number of single-use cups going to landfills Petaluma 
Reusable Cup Project — Sip, Return, Repeat, n.d.)(Starbucks to Lead First-Ever City-
Wide Reusable Cup Project in California with NextGen Consortium , 2024) 
Moreover, in 2018 Coca-Cola started using universal reusable plastic (PET) bottles 
which can be used across multiple soda brands in Latin America. Consumers return 
empty bottles to retailers who store them and then give them back to Coca-Cola for 
cleaning and reuse. Implementing a universal reusable bottle reduces logistics 
complexity and increases system effectiveness. However, it requires significant 
investment in infrastructure and strong collaboration between the public and 
private sector (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2021b). 
On the other hand, not all the initiatives of these systems have been successful. In 
January 2021 in Oakland, Dispatch Goods launched a reusable container restaurant 
service. However, customer confusion and complicated logistics proved to be the 
source of problems, and the company stopped the program at the end of 2022 
(Hamstra, 2021). 
Another example of this is the collaboration of Tailwind Café in Seattle with a 
Reusable Packaging and logistics company called Reusables.com, that 
implemented returnable metal to-go cups. Workers described the situation as 
overwhelming as they had to explain the return system to every interested customer. 
Moreover, many were hesitant to participate after learning that they could only 
return the cups to Tailwind or the other drop-off location, 6 miles away (Grist & 
Winthers, 2024). 
 
Hotspots analysis  
Some hotspots were identified in these systems. First, for the collection phase to be 
effective, return rates should be high. To achieve so, the drop-off locations should 
be convenient for consumers and the returning system must be clear and easy to 
follow for them. Otherwise, this would lead to higher packaging loss and higher 
==PAGE:89==

replacement needs. Additionally, customer engagement and brand loyalty can be 
improved through deposit/reward schemes. 
In relation to this, as this model relies on multiple return points, this makes it 
complex for the collection and reverse logistics phase to be managed effectively. To 
achieve economies of scale, infrastructure for washing, collecting, sorting, and 
delivery should be shared. Moreover, opportunities for this kind of business model 
include smart technologies used to tag and track packaging, control deposit 
payouts and gain user insights.  
Lastly, another challenge is the lack of packaging standardization. To scale up these 
types of systems, companies should consider standardizing their packaging to 
reduce costs and make the processes of cleaning and redistribution more effective. 
(Grist & Winthers, 2024).

Business Models comparison 
Despite the challenges associated to the implementation and development of these 
systems, the multiple benefits of RuPS make the transition a worthwhile subject of 
further study.  
To assess these challenges, first, an analysis between the four different RuPS 
business models was conducted. Then, the most promising one based on 
predefined criteria was selected to focus the research. 
The hotspots of the business models were synthesized as follows: 
The two refill-based business models face a common challenge: the systems must 
be user-friendly and accessible. Consumers play a crucial role in acquiring 
Reusable Packaging before entering the system, but since this purchase is a one-
time event, it is not a primary focus. Proximity to the store is also of key relevance, 
as the shorter distances make these models more attractive by reducing 
transportation challenges. Additionally, these models are better suited to planned 
purchases since users are less likely to have containers ready for spontaneous 
purchases. This limitation is particularly significant for the refill-on-the-go system. 
Moreover, the user is responsible for the maintenance and hygiene state of the RP, 
which might influence in the overall convenience of the system. In contrast, retailers 
do not have that many responsibilities besides setting up the system and selling the 
products.  
On the other hand, in return-based systems, logistics should be optimized both in 
the distribution and redistribution of RP. In the return from home model, delivery and 
return routes should be shared to lower extra transportation and carbon emissions. 
In the return on-the-go model, logistics should be optimized for packaging 
collection after usage. The lack of business collaboration and, therefore, the lack of 
packaging standardization, makes the RP collection more complex as there is no 
shared infrastructure. 
Moreover, returning conditions should be seamless to users. In the return from 
home model, the delivery time and date (prior to usage) is key to make it attractive 
for consumers. In return on-the-go systems, drop-off points location should be easy 
to access for users to achieve a high return rate. In relation to this, higher return rates 
rely on the user’s understanding of the system, so these systems should really focus 
on customer education and guidance.  
To further analyse the different models, a Service Blueprint tool was done, 
highlighting which stakeholder has the main role in each of the stages. That way, 
critical tasks (highlighted in grey), in which the main hotspots take place, can be 
easily identified.

After this analysis, the difference between the refill and return systems was clearer.  
The refill systems have proven to rely in the consumer’s actions, especially in the 
usage and wash stages. 
In the return from home model, as mentioned before, the logistics play the most 
critical role in the RP distribution, collection and redistribution. In contrast, in return 
on-the-go systems, the main critical step is the RP collection, which depends on the 
customer usage and understanding of the system.  
After this analysis, a further comparison by a weighted matrix was done to see each 
of the model’s potential in the four predefined areas: user acceptance, business 
viability, logistical complexity and sustainability. 
Since the customer behaviour is one of the key points for RuPS to work, it was 
weighted with a 30% of the total. The business viability is represented by 20%, since 
it is vital for the system to be sustained that companies make profit from it. The third 
characteristic is the logistical complexity, with a 10% weight because it’s logistical 
challenges can often be addressed with technological solutions or adjustments in 
processes, making them less critical to address upfront. Lastly, the sustainability 
impact holds the higher weight, 40%, since reducing the environmental impact is 
the main goal of transitioning to RuPS.

From the user acceptance point of view, the refill on-the-go model was considered 
less convenient for users as it demands high consumer involvement. On the 
contrary, the return from home model was believed to have higher acceptance as it 
requires minimal effort from users. 
Then, from a business viability and logistical complexity perspective, the most 
viable model was thought to be the refill at home since, as mentioned before, the 
consumer has the highest responsibility. In contrast, the return from home model 
requires high logistical costs and challenges in collection and redistribution, so it 
was believed to be less viable and more complex logistically. 
Regarding sustainability, although all the option’s environmental potential could 
be limited due to the system’s inefficiencies, the return on-the-go model is the one 
believed to have more environmental benefits. These systems are believed to have 
less environmental impact as they reduce packaging waste by RP collection and 
redistribution, and they reduce resource consumption and carbon emissions by 
avoiding new RP productions.  
To conclude, as sustainability was assigned a higher weight, the return on-the-go 
model was selected to be further analysed in this study. Moreover, according to the 
scope, since one of the key goals is to mitigate the RE, this model presents a 
particular relevant case. Its effectiveness depends mainly on the correct user’s 
behaviour which, if not guided, could cause some unintended consequences.
