Abstract 
Flavonoids are a large group of secondary metabolites, which are naturally occurring in the plant 
kingdom. The interest in flavonoids is rapidly growing due to their antimicrobial effects. Today, 
large-scale production of flavonoids by plant extraction is insufficient to meet the needs of the 
pharmaceutical- and nutraceutical industries.  Although flavonoids are mostly regarded as a 
secondary metabolite from plants, they have also been isolated from various fungal species. One 
very interesting fungal derived flavonoid with promising antimicrobial properties is 
chlorflavonin. The findings of natural produced flavonoids in fungi pose a great step in the 
direction for a more sustainable production.  
Recently, the fungi Aspergillus campestris showed the capacity of producing chlorflavonin. 
However, the exact gene cluster involved in the biosynthesis of chlorflavonin has not been 
elucidated. This study sought out to investigate the involvement of a potential gene cluster, 
identified by (Kjærbølling et al., 2018), in the chlorflavonin production. This is done by 
individually deleting several genes from the potential gene cluster using CRISPR-Cas9. 
The involvement of the potential gene cluster was confirmed through the deletion of the gene 
coding for the PKS hybrid, assigned chlor1, which resulted in a strain with no detectable 
chlorflavonin production. Based on this observation, the remaining genes in the chlorflavonin 
gene cluster was investigated. 
However, the gene deletion using 90mer repair oligonucleotides in A. campestris proved more 
difficult than anticipated. Two main problems presented in this study: the low frequency of 
mutations and the difficulty of identifying the few possible successful mutants. To improve the 
number of mutants the transformation media was changed from the traditional transformation 
media to YES media. This change resulted in the successful deletion of the gene assigned chlor2. 
For easier identification of possible mutants, longer repair templates were designed which 
incorporates the RFP gene. However, due to the failure of the antibiotic selective marker, no 
definitive conclusions could be drawn based on the experiment performed with the longer repair 
templates. 
Finally, four plasmids were constructed with four of the potential chlorflavonin genes. The 
plasmids were transformed into Aspergillus oryzae in the hopes of identifying the isomerase gene 
responsible for the ring-closure of the PKS backbone. Successful transformation and non-
chromosomal expression were achieved for chlor11.

Resume 
Flavonoider udgøre en større gruppe af sekundære metabolitter, som er naturligt 
fremkommende i planter. Interessen for flavonoider er stigende grundet deres antimikrobielle 
egenskaber. På nuværende tidspunkt er industriel produktion af flavonoider via plante 
ekstraktion utilstrækkelig inden for den farmaceutiske- og den fødevaremæssige industri. Selvom 
flavonoider mest betragtes som en sekundær metabolit fra planter, er de også blevet isoleret fra 
forskellige svampearter. En af flavonoider isoleret fra svampe er chlorflavonin, og er en 
interessant type flavonoid med lovende egenskaber. Isoleringen af flavonoider fra svampe er et 
stort skridt i retningen for en mere bæredygtig produktion.  
 
For nyligt er chlorflavonin også blevet isoleret fra svampen, Aspergillus campestris. Dog er den 
nøjagtige genklynge involveret i biosyntesen af chlorflavonin ikke belyst. Dette projekt bestræber 
sig på at undersøge involveringen af en potentiel genklynge, identificeret af (Kjærbølling et al., 
2018), i produktionen af chlorflavonin. Dette gøres ved individuelt at slette flere gener fra den 
potentielle chlorflavonin klynge ved hjælp af CRISPR-Cas9. 
 
Involveringen af den potentielle genklynge blev bekræftet ved sletningen af genet der koder for 
PKS hybriden, tildelt navnet chlor1, der resulterede i en stamme uden detekteret chlorflavonin 
produktion. Baseret på denne observation, blev de resterende gener i chlorflavonin genklyngen 
undersøgt.  
Det viste sig dog at gen sletningen, ved brug af 90mer oligonukleotider, i A. campestris var svære 
end forventet. To hovedproblemer opstod under udførelsen af dette studie: den lave frekvens af 
mutationer, samt at det var svært at identificere de få mulige mutanter der blev lavet. For at forøge 
antallet af mutanter blev YES medie brugt i stedet for det traditionelt benyttede 
transformationsmedie. Dette skift resulterede i en vellykket sletning af genet chlor2. For at 
forbedre identificeringen af mulige mutanter, blev længere reparations templates designet med 
RFP-gener. Der kunne dog ikke drages nogle definitive konklusioner, på baggrund af forsøget 
udført med de længere reparations template, grundet svigt af den antibiotiske selektive markør.  
 
Til sidst blev fire plasmider konstrueret med fire af de potentielle chlorflavonin gener. 
Plasmiderne blev transformeret ind i svampen, Aspergillus oryzae, for at undersøge om en af disse 
koder for en isomerase, der er ansvarlig for ringlukningen af PKS-strukturen. Succesfuld 
transformation og non-kromosomal gen-udrykning blev opnået for chlor11.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Project description 
1.1.1 Project background 
Filamentous fungi are ubiquitous organisms impacting human life in both positive and negative 
manners. They are a rich source of enzymes and bioactive metabolites used within the 
industries of biotechnology, food technology and pharmaceuticals, (Fleißner & Dersch, 2010). 
Filamentous fungi of the genus Aspergillus occupy a large spectrum of habitats, including soil, 
decaying organic materials and indoor environments. The Aspergillus genus currently 
encompasses more than 300 species of filamentous fungi. Species of Aspergillus are known as 
efficient producers of proteins, organic acids, and secondary metabolites (SM). However, only 
a fraction of the secondary metabolites has been isolated and identified, (Fleißner & Dersch, 
2010). Despite the impact of filamentous fungi in our everyday life, detailed knowledge into the 
molecular biology and biochemistry is lacking, and it is only available for few fungi, (Nødvig et 
al., 2015). However, the decrease in cost of whole-genome sequencing is making it more widely 
available, and with projects like the 1000 Fungal Genomes Project, the number of sequenced 
fungi genomes is rapidly increasing (Bertelli & Greub, 2013; Nødvig et al., 2015).  The large 
number of genome sequencing studies shows a myriad of putative secondary metabolite 
pathways potentially hiding future drugs or industrial building blocks, (Nødvig et al., 2015).  
Flavonoids are a large group of secondary metabolites, which are naturally occurring in the 
plant kingdom. The interest in flavonoids is growing because of their antioxidant and 
antimicrobial effects (Austin & Noel, 2003; Babii et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Jubair et al., 
2021). Even though flavonoids are mostly regarded as a secondary metabolite from plants, they 
have also been isolated from different fungal species. One very interesting fungal derived 
flavonoid with promising antimicrobial properties is chlorflavonin. It was first isolated from 
the fungus Aspergillus candidus in 1969, (Richards et al., 1969).  
In a recent study, Aspergillus campestris showed the capacity of producing chlorflavonin, 
(Kjærbølling et al., 2018; Rahbæk et al., 2000). However, the exact gene cluster involved in the 
biosynthesis of chlorflavonin remains to be elucidated. A potential gene cluster encoding the 
genes for chlorflavonin production was presented in 2018, although further investigation to 
verify the candidate cluster is required (Kjærbølling et al., 2018). The complete elucidation of 
the gene cluster and metabolic pathway will enable homologous or heterologous production of 
chlorflavonin and other flavonoid-derived compounds.   
The findings of natural produced flavonoids in fungi pose a great step in the direction for a more 
sustainable production. Today, large-scale production of flavonoids by plant extraction is 
insufficient to meet the needs of the pharmaceutical and nutraceutical industries. Moreover,

polyketide synthesis pathway (PKS). The enzyme chalcone synthase (CHS) catalyses the initial 
step of sequential decarboxylative addition of malonyl-CoA extender units, to a p-coumaroyl-
CoA stater unit. This commits the pathway towards the flavonoid biosynthesis.  
The extension is followed by intra-molecular cyclization and aromatization of the linear 
phenylpropanoid tetraketide catalysed by the chalcone isomerase (CHI). Various downstream 
enzymes in branching pathways modify the aromatic scaffold to produce different compounds, 
including the different subclasses of flavonoids, see Figure 1.2 (Austin & Noel, 2003; Li et al., 
2019).  
 
Even though flavonoids are mostly regarded as secondary metabolites from plants, various 
flavonoids have also been isolated from fungal species (Richards et al., 1969; Smith et al., 2015; 
Wang et al., 2020). Research has shown that certain strains of endophytic fungi have acquired 
the ability to produce different flavonoids through their symbiotic co-operation with host 
plants (Patil et al., 2015; Qiu et al., 2010). Moreover, type III PKSs are also found in several fungi 
species. A genome survey across the fungi kingdom shows the presence of the genes associated 
with the biosynthesis of flavonoids, such as chalcone synthase and chalcone isomerase 
(Mohanta, 2020). The shared genes between plants and fungi are likely a result of convergent 
evolution as fungi and plants share no common ancestors but have evolved similar genes.  
However, some fungal species have also acquired more unique biosynthetic pathways which 
share little or no similarities with the traditional type III PKS flavonoid pathway. This is the 
scenario with chlorflavonin, which through different examinations was found to be synthesized 
via a unique biosynthetic pathway. This is among other observed in A. candidus and A. 
campestris. The fungal-specific pathway is possibly a result of divergent evolution, since both 
fungi share a common ancestor with the fungi kingdom however have evolved two different 
pathways (Burns et al., 1981; Gautam, 2020; Kjærbølling et al., 2018).

1.3 Chlorflavonin 
Chlorflavonin is a flavonoid which was first isolated from the fungus A. candidus in 1969 
(Richards et al., 1969). It was the first fully characterized flavone to be isolated from a fungal 
species, and the first naturally chlorinated flavone to be isolated from any origin. It has been 
shown to exhibit antifungal properties, with very high specificity. It inhibits the growth of 
certain fungal species completely and even at low concentrations, e.g., observed in a study with 
Aspergillus fumigatus a known opportunistic pathogen. However, most fungal species show no 
reaction to the presence of chlorflavonin (Moretti et al., 2013; Richards et al., 1969). The 
mechanism of inhibition of fungal growth remains to be elucidated. 
Beside antifungal properties, chlorflavonin shows a bacteriostatic effect towards 
Mycobacterium tuberculosis. In combination with isoniazid or delamanid, it revealed a 
pronounced synergistic killing effect, resulting in killing a culture of M. tuberculosis, (Rehberg 
et al., 2018). In the same study, the cytotoxic potential of chlorflavonin against human cells was 
investigated, and it showed that chlorflavonin exhibit no cytotoxic effect against two lines of 
human cells, (Rehberg et al., 2018). Together these findings point towards chlorflavonin being 
a promising novel structure in the fight against M. tuberculosis. 
 
The structure of chlorflavonin was elucidated by A. E. 
Bird and A. C. Marshall in 1969, who showed that the 
chemical nomenclature is 3’-chloro-2’,5-dihydroxy-3,7,8-
trirnethoxyflavone, see Figure 1.3 (Bird & Marshall, 
1969). The similarity between the chlorflavonin 
structure and the flavonoids derived from plants is 
evident. An obvious proposal for the biosynthesis is 
therefore a type III PKS (Bisht et al., 2021; Hashimoto et 
al., 2014; Kjærbølling et al., 2018b). However, an 
examination of incorporating radioactive labelled 
cinnamate, acetate and p-coumaric acid in chlorflavonin produced by A. candidus, shows a low 
incorporation efficiency of cinnamate and acetate in chlorflavonin, while p-coumaric acid is not 
incorporated, (Burns et al., 1981). This points towards that A. candidus have evolved a unique 
biosynthetic route to the flavonoid structure. In the same study, a proposal of a possible 
biosynthetic route for chlorflavonin production was presented, see Figure 1.4 (Burns et al., 
1981).

the putative chlorflavonin gene cluster a comparative study was performed, and a homolog 
gene cluster was found in A. candidus. Further experimental work is required to verify the gene 
cluster and the biosynthesis together with linking the different genes to the different parts of 
the biosynthesis, (Kjærbølling et al., 2018).

1.4 Aspergillus campestris 
Aspergillus campestris belongs to the section Candidi, and was 
first described by Martha Christensen in 1982, where it was 
isolated from a sample of native prairie soil (Christensen, 1982; 
Rahbæk et al., 2000; Varga et al., 2007). The species is 
characterized by its restricted growth on minimal media at room 
temperature. Growth on minimal media is illustrated on Figure 
1.6, (Rahbæk et al., 2000; Varga et al., 2007).  
Initial interest in A. campestris was taken, because of the 
unusually large number of secondary metabolites observed. 
Examples of secondary metabolites found was terphenyllin, 3-
hydroxyterphenyllin, candidusin C and chlorflavonin among 
others, isolated from extracts (Rahbæk et al., 2000).  
A. Campestris was the first member of the section Candidi to be whole genome sequenced in 
2017 (Kjærbølling et al., 2018). At the time of Kjærbølling et. al’s study, A. campestris was not 
genetically engineerable as no selection marker for successful gene editing in A. campestris 
existed (Kjærbølling et al., 2018). This changed when the ku70 gene was successfully deleted 
by the research group, Eukaryotic Molecular Cell Biology, at DTU. Ku70 is a key protein in the 
NHEJ repair pathway, laying the groundwork for genetic editing using CRISPR in A. campestris. 
Further work is still required to construct auxotroph mutants for easier selection, instead of 
using antibiotics.

2 Experimental theory 
2.1 Strategies for verification of putative chlorflavonin pathway 
During this study, the experimental execution is performed according to three strategies, to 
verify the putative chlorflavonin cluster. Two of the strategies are based on the deletion of the 
genes in the putative cluster individually using CRISPR-Cas9 in the fungus A. campestris, while 
the third strategy is based on transforming four of the potential genes in Aspergillus oryzae for 
plasmid-based gene expression. The two strategies with CRISPR-Cas9 are followed by double 
stranded break (DSB) repair by homologous recombination (HR). Those plasmids are 
constructed with two crRNA to induce cuts in two different parts of the genes: one directly 
upstream of the genes, and one in the genes themselves.  
The first strategi is using a CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid, which is co-transformed with a 90mer repair 
oligonucleotide. Hygromycin is used as a selective marker for successful transformation. 
Successful mutations are selected through fungal colony PCR.  
The second strategy is using a CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid, which is co-transformed with a longer 
repair template which consists of two longer homology arms and a Red Fluorescence Protein 
(FRP) integrated in between. Several longer repair templates were assembled in plasmids and 
subsequently linearized before transformation. Hygromycin is used as a selective marker for 
successful transformation. Successful mutations are selected using fluorescent light, as 
successful mutation have the RFP gene integrated, instead of the targeted gene for 
chlorflavonin.  
The third strategy is performed using the fungus A. Oryzae. This strategy is designed to indentify 
the isomerase gene in the putative chlorflavonin cluster. The design is based on the 
construction of four plasmids containing the individual genes chlor4, chlor6, chlor7 and chlor11 
of the putative chlorflavonin cluster, see Table 1.1 in 1.3 Chlorflavonin. The genes, including the 
Ptef promoter and Ttef terminator, are cloned into a backbone vector which contains an AMA1 
sequence for non-chromosomal expression. The plasmids are then individually transformed 
into A. Oryzae, which already express the PKS-hybrid. pyrG is used as a selective marker for 
successful transformation.  Table 2.1 lists the strains planned to be constructed in this study.

2.2 CRISPR-Cas9 
Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats, or CRISPR, is a powerful genome 
editing tool which allows for targeting modifications of the genome, by removing, adding, or 
otherwise altering sections of the DNA sequence. The tool is an adaptation of the natural 
mechanism which protects bacterial cells against foreign DNA, such as phage DNA. CRISPR 
refers to the arrays of repeated DNA sequences from bacteriophages found in the genomes of 
prokaryotic organisms. The CRISPR sequences are used to recognize DNA from new infecting 
phages, where a Cas-protein (CRISPR-associated protein) introduce double stranded breaks 
(DSB) at specific sites, making the transcription of necessary genes for the infection impossible 
(Barrangou, 2015; Lodish et al., 2021b) . 
The CRISPR system has been adapted to function in many different cell types, by the 
development of the small system consisting of the Cas9 nuclease and an engineered single guide 
RNA (sgRNA). The sgRNA is composed of two regions, where the first region is a small fragment 
of pre-designed RNA sequence that is 20 nucleotides long. It is also known as the crRNA, which 
identifies the target sequence in the genome. The second region is a backbone RNA, also known 
as the tracrRNA, which is made up of two complementary sequences that forms a double-
stranded hairpin that binds to Cas9, see Figure 2.1 (Jinek et al., 2012; Lodish et al., 2021b).  
The genes for the Cas9 nuclease and sgRNA can then be inserted in a plasmid, which is 
transformed into the target cell. The Cas9 and sgRNA forms a complex, and the crRNA targets 
the Cas9 complex to the specific site of the genome where Cas9 makes a DSB. This is repaired 
via either Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ) or Homologous Recombination (HR), see Figure 
2.1 (Jinek et al., 2012; Lodish et al., 2021b).

When designing the crRNA, the only requirement is that the crRNA target sequence is 
immediately downstream of a Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM). The PAM sequences vary 
dependent on the Cas protein used. The most common Cas protein used is the Streptococcus 
pyogenes Cas9, which has the PAM sequence NGG, (Sander & Joung, 2014). 
To control the gene editing tool, a central step in preparing a microorganism for genetic 
engineering is the deletion of the gene ku70, a crucial gene in the NHEJ repair pathway. With a 
successful ku70 deletion, the favoured DSB repair pathway is HR. When adding specific repair 
templates containing regions of homology up- and downstream of the break, it can be repaired 
through HR. This allows the introduction of point mutations, insertions or deletions, see Figure 
2.2, (Löbs et al., 2017).

A selective marker is required when performing gene editing with CRISPR-Cas9. An 
antimicrobial agent could be used when resistance is included in the CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid, or 
disabling of essential genes like pyrG is a useful selective marker for successful transformation 
(Löbs et al., 2017). 
A third selective marker can also be inserted in the repair template, between the homology 
arms, like a fluorescent protein. This makes for easier selection of successful transformants and 
mutants, eliminating the need for subsequent screening, see Figure 2.2. 
The CRISPR-Cas9 system was adapted for use in filamentous fungi in 2015, (Nødvig et al., 2015), 
where the tailored CRISPR-Cas9 system was used to introduce mutation in six different species 
in two sections of Aspergillus. Modifications were introduced in both components of the CRISPR 
system. Firstly, the Cas9 gene was codon optimized for expression in Aspergillus niger. In 
addition, to the codon optimization, the sequence for SV40-NLS (Simian virus 40 Nuclear 
Localization Signal) was added in the C-terminal to ensure the Cas9 protein is guided into the 
nucleus. Secondly, the sgRNA was imbedded in a larger transcribed synthesized by RNA 
polymerase II, as sgRNA are generally transcribed from genes controlled by RNA polymerase 
III promoters, which are poorly defined in filamentous fungi (Nødvig et al., 2015). The modified 
Cas9, as well as the larger transcript with the crRNA included, are cloned into a backbone 
vector, which contain the AMA1 sequence which promote non-chromosomal gene expression, 
(Aleksenko & Clutterbuck, 1996; Nødvig et al., 2015).  
In a later study, the possibility of using single-stranded 90mer oligonucleotides as repair 
template was demonstrated. This eliminates the need for the construction of complex repair 
plasmids. Using the 90mer oligonucleotide as a repair template, the DSB is repaired through HR 
and the targeted gene is deleted from the genome, see Figure 2.2 (Nødvig et al., 2018).

2.3 USER cloning 
The plasmids constructed during this study are assembled using USER cloning. The Uracil 
Specific Excision Reagent (USER) cloning technique provides a fast, simple and efficient method 
to simultaneously fuse and clone multiple PCR fragments into a vector.  The cloning method 
was first introduced in the early 1990s as an alternative to restriction and ligation-based 
cloning (Geu-Flores et al., 2007).   
The method is based on obtaining DNA fragments which shares 6 to 10 bases of homology, with 
a single deoxyuracil at the 3’ ends. These are amplified by PCR using a polymerase, which 
accepts primers containing uracil, e.g., X7 polymerase. An example of the amplification is 
illustrated in Figure 2.3. The PCR products are called BioBlocks. The BioBlocks are treated with 
USER enzyme(s), which selectively cuts out uracil bases. It leaves the phosphodiester backbone 
intact, and thus creating compatible ends allowing for directional cloning, see Figure 2.3 (Geu-
Flores et al., 2007; Jurate & Vaisvila, 2013). The BioBlocks along with a pre-digested backbone 
vector can be transformed directly into a microorganism, such as E. coli for assembly without 
prior ligation. 
 
 
Figure 2.3: Schematic assembly of three BioBlocks using USER cloning. The BioBlocks X1, X2 and X3 are amplified with primers 
containing an uracil, U, near the 5’ end. The PCR products, the BioBlocks, are mixed with digested USER-compatible vector and 
the mix is treated with the USER enzyme, which generates 3’ overhangs designed to be compatible with each other. The mix 
can be transformed directly into E. coli for assembly without prior ligation. Figure taken from (Geu-Flores et al., 2007).

2.4 Design of Primers for Plasmid construction 
In this study, primers are designed for the construction of three different types of plasmids: 1) 
plasmids containing gRNA for CRISPR-Cas9 mediated gene knock-out in A. campestris, 2)

Analysis by Gel Electrophoresis 
The PCR products were analysed by gel electrophoresis using either a 100 bp ladder (Range: 
100-1517 bp), or a 1 kb ladder (range: 0.5-10 kb). The PCR products were loaded on an agarose 
gel containing 1000X SYBR-Safe, and ran on an EMBI Tec RunOne Electrophoresis Cell at 100V

samples above 1 kb were analysed with 1% agarose gels. PCR products were separated and 
purified, if more than one band were observed.  
Separation and Purification 
The separation of the PCR products was performed by gel electrophoresis, by using a thick 
agarose gel at either 1% or 2% agarose. The agarose gels ran at 100V for 20 minutes, and 
afterwards bands were visualized in a transilluminator. The desired bands were individually 
cut and placed in an Eppendorf tube. The bands were dissolved and purified using GFX Gel Band 
purification kit (Cytiva), by the recommended protocol which is presented in Appendix 3. The 
concentration of the BioBlock was measured using spectroscopy by nanodrop at A280 nm.

3.2 Fungal gene editing  
The recipes for the media and solutions mentioned in this section are presented in 3.5 
Preparation of media and 3.6 Preparation of solutions, respectively. 
3.2.1 Fungal protoplastation 
Both A. campestris and A. oryzae protoplast were obtained as described by (Nielsen et al., 2006) 
with minor changes for A. campestris. The first step is cultivating the fungi. A. campestris was 
plated on minimal media (MM) plates and incubated for 21 days at 25°C. The spores were 
suspended in 50 mL YPD media and transferred to a shake flask, with additionally 50 mL YPD, 
and incubated at 150 rpm and 25°C for 72 hours. A. oryzae was plated on MM plates with uracil 
and uridine, and incubated for 6 days at 30°C. The spores were suspended in 50 mL YPD media 
containing uracil and uridine. It was transferred to a shake flask with additionally 50 mL YPD, 
and incubated overnight at 30°C and 150 rpm.  
After incubation, the mycelia were collected using a sterile Mira cloth and washed twice with 
APB (Aspergillus protoplastation buffer) to remove media residues. The mycelia were padded 
dry, to remove excess fluid and transferred to a 45 mL falcon tube. It was resuspended in 10-
25 mL of filter-sterile 80 mg Glucanex/mL APB (for A. campestris) or 40 mg Glucanex/mL APB 
(for A. oryzae) until the solution had a thick fluid consistency. The mixture was shaken at 30°C 
and 150 rpm for 2.5 hours. Samples were collected and examined under microscope every 30 
min, to observe the progress of the protoplastation. The digested mycelia were collected by 
filtering the mixture with mycelia, enzyme and APB through a sterile Mira Cloth. The flow 
through was collected in a 45 mL falcon tube. APB were added up to a total volume of 40 mL. 
An overlay with 5 mL of a 2-fold Aspergillus transformation buffer (ATB) dilution were made 
very carefully. The flask was centrifuged at 3000 xg (acceleration 9, deceleration 4) for 12 min 
(at speed). After centrifugation, if the protoplastation was successful, a halo was observed just 
below overlay. The halo of protoplast slurry was collected and transferred to a new 45 mL flask, 
where non-diluted ATP was added up to a volume of 40 mL. The flask was centrifuged at 3000 
xg (acceleration 9, deceleration 9) for 12 min (at speed), and the supernatant was discarded. 
The protoplast pellet was resuspended in 1 mL of ATP and transferred to a sterile Eppendorf 
tube. 
3.2.2 Gene editing in Aspergillus campestris 
The fungal gene editing in A. campestris is performed using a CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid. The 
CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid is co-transformed with a 90mer repair oligonucleotide, or a longer repair 
template assembled in another plasmids. This section covers the two experimental strategies 
performed in A. campestris, see 2.1 Strategies for verification of putative chlorflavonin pathway.  
Fungal transformation  
Transformation plates, with and without the repair oligo/template, were prepared for each 
gene. Furthermore, a positive control with the circular pFC332 plasmid and a negative control 
was also prepared for each transformation. In the following section a distinction is made 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
Page 24 of 61 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
between the repair oligo, which refers to the 90mer repair oligonucleotide, and the repair 
template, which refers to the longer repair template with RFP gene. 
Prior to the transformation the repair oligo was diluted in 200 µL MQ water, and the repair 
template was linearized as described in 3.1.3 Vector digestion and plasmid linearization. 
Hygromycin was used as a selection marker. Two layers of transformation media (TM) were 
made in the fungal transformation. The first layer consists of TM with 25 µg/mL of hygromycin, 
and the second layer consist of TM with 50 µg/mL of hygromycin. 50 µg/mL of hygromycin is 
considered 100% of minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC).  
Dependent on the successfulness of the protoplastation, 50 µL to 100 µL of A. campestris 
protoplast was transferred to 15 mL falcon tubes, together with 1 µg DNA. 10 µL of the repair 
oligo or 1 µg of repair template was transferred into the tubes marked with +repair. The falcon 
tubes were placed on ice for 15 minutes. 1 mL of PCT was added, and the tubes were placed at 
room temperature for 15 min. TM with 25 µg/mL of hygromycin was added to the tubes up to 
a volume of 13 mL, and the content was mixed by inversion of the tubes. The content was 
subsequently poured onto a plate. The plates were left at room temperature for a couple of 
hours followed by incubation at 30°C overnight.  The next day, an overlay of 13 mL TM with 50 
µg/mL of hygromycin was poured. The plates were left at room temperature for a couple of 
hours, followed by incubation at 30°C. After 3-4 days, the plates were transferred to 25°C and 
incubated until transformant was observed.

3.2.3 Gene editing in Aspergillus oryzae 
The fungal gene editing in A. oryzae is performed by transforming four plasmids containing the 
genes for non-chromosomal expression, see 2.1 Strategies for verification of putative 
chlorflavonin pathway. 
Fungal transformation  
A. oryzae protoplast was gently mixed with 0.75 µg of the gene plasmids. 150 µL PCT was added 
and mixed by inversion. The mixture was incubated for 10 min at room temperature. 
Afterwards, 250 µL ATP was added and mixed by pipetting. The mixture was plated on 
prepared TM agar plates without additional nutrients. To avoid osmotic stress in the 
protoplasts, the volume of the gene plasmid solution should be below 25% of the total volume 
of the protoplasts-DNA mixture. The amount of protoplast added was determined based on the 
volume of gene plasmid solution added. A positive and negative control was prepared by adding 
the circular pAC572 vector and MQ water to the protoplast, respectively. The plates were 
incubated at 30°C for 6 days.  
3.3 Genomic DNA extraction 
gDNA was used as template for the homology arms for the repair templates, and the putative 
chlorflavonin genes for the gene plasmids. The gDNA extraction was performed using a kit 
developed by the Research group, Eukaryotic Molecular Cell Biology, at DTU. With a sterile 
colony loop, A. campestris colonies was scraped from a plate and transferred together with 500 
µL of breaking buffer and 200 µL Ø2mm glass beads to FastPrep tube. The FastPrep tube was 
placed in a FastPrep at speed 4 for 40 sec. Afterwards, the FastPrep tube was centrifuged at 
10,000 G. 150 µL of the supernatant was transferred to an Eppendorf tube together with 15 µL 
of 5M NaCl and 400 µL of ice-cold 96 % EtOH. The mixture was vortexed and centrifuged for 3 
min at 10,000 G, followed by the discharge of the supernatant and dried at 50 °C. The DNA was 
resuspended in 200 µL of MQ water.

3.4 Production of Chlorflavonin and chemical analysis 
3.4.1 Chlorflavonin production 
Production of chlorflavonin was initiated in order to investigate if the mutants obtained in 3.2.2

colonies had a diameter of ~3 cm. The colonies were extracted via plug extraction. 
3.4.2 Plug extraction and Chemical analysis 
Sterile Eppendorf tubes were labelled according to the gene deleted and the production media. 
With a sterile 1 mL pipette, 4 circles were cut with the broad end of the pipette and transferred 
to the Eppendorf tube.  
The Eppendorf tubes were subsequently delivered to Post Doc. Xinhui Wang who performed 
the chemical extraction and analysis. The chemical analysis was performed by HPLC-MS  using 
a diode array detector. A Poroshell 120 Phenyl Hexyl column (Agilent Technologies) was used 
at 60°C for separation. The mobile phase was composed from 10% acetonitrile (LC-MS grade) 
in Milli-Q water buffered with 20 mM formic acid. Acetonitrile increases to 100% in 15 min, 
staying there for 2 min, before decreasing to 10% in 0.1 min.

4 Results and Discussion 
4.1 Confirming the putative chlorflavonin cluster 
Prior to this study, an experiment with deleting a gene of the putative chlorflavonin cluster in 
A. campestris was performed by Fabiano Jares Contesini. The experiment successfully deleted 
the PKS-hybrid gene, chlor1, see Table 1.1  for nomenclature. The methodology used by Fabiano 
Jares Contesini is the same as performed during this study with 90mer repair oligonucleotide, 
see 3 Methods. This deletion of chlor1 is analysed during this study, since the results were not 
fully investigated at the time with chemical analysis.  
The deletion was achieved using CRISPR-Cas9. A CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid were successfully 
assembled with two crRNA sequences, and transformed into A. campestris. Fabiano Jares 
Contesini screened for transformants with the ∆chlor1 mutation after several weeks of growth 
by using fungal colony PCR. During this study, the screening was reconfirmed by performing 
fungal colony PCR on seven colonies collected from a prepared master plate. The results are 
presented in Figure 4.1.

There are various explanations to why the deletions had not been successful, the four most 
promising being: 1) the Cas9 enzyme is not efficient in A. campestris, 2) the genes are not easily 
accessible to the Cas9 enzyme, 3) the 90mer may be degraded before the DSB is induced and 4) 
the sgRNAs are inefficient.  
First, the CRISPR-Cas9 system is of bacterial origin. The Cas9 enzyme is originally isolated from 
the bacterium Streptococcus pyogenes with an optimum growth temperature of 40°C, hence the 
Cas9 enzyme has higher efficiency at elevated temperatures, (Milner et al., 2020). A. campestris 
optimal growth temperature is 25°C, thus somewhat lower compared to the optimal 
temperature of Cas9. It has been demonstrated that the incubation temperature greatly 
influences the mutation frequency, with up to a 100-fold increase with incubation at 37°C 
compared to 22°C, (LeBlanc et al., 2018; Milner et al., 2020). The Cas9 enzyme used to induce 
DSB was previously optimized for expression in A. niger, (Nødvig et al., 2015), thus the 
effectiveness in A. campestris may be lower compared to A. niger. This decrease in effectiveness 
could explain the present of transformant in the plates both with and without the repair 
template, as a low effectiveness would result in a low number of induced DSB.  
The second explanation is that the genes are not accessible to the Cas9 enzyme. The genes are 
proposed to be part of a metabolic pathway. Chlorflavonin is a secondary metabolite, which are 
normally not continuously expressed. Less frequent expressed genes are usually packed in the 
facultative heterochromatin, which is a more condensed part of the chromosome, (Reyes-
dominguez et al., 2012; Studt et al., 2017). This could explain the lack of induced DSB, as the 
Cas9 enzyme cannot access the genes. This explanation was further investigated by repeating 
the A. campestris transformation using modified YES media instead of traditional TM for the 
transformation. The results of the transformation using a different media is presented in 4.2.3 
A. campestris transformation with a different transformation media. 
The third explanation could be, that as the 90mer repair oligonucleotides are a short sequence, 
there is a possibility they are degraded quickly after entering the cell. Short RNA sequences are 
degraded to prevent the accumulation of unused RNA in the nucleus and cytoplasm. This 
suggestion was investigated by designing longer repair templates which includes larger 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
Page 39 of 61 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
sequences of homolog up- and downstream of the desired gene. The results of the 
transformation with long repair templates are presented in 4.2.4 A. campestris transformation 
with long repair templates.  
The fourth explanation could be the nature of the sgRNA. In some cases, the sgRNA is ineffective. 
The effectiveness of the sgRNA is evaluated by conducting the fungal transformation with and 
without the repair oligo/template, as performed for the experiments presented in this report, 
(Nødvig et al., 2018). However, this explanation is deemed unrealistic as this could be an 
explanation if mutants were absent in some of the transformation plates, but not all. The 
likelihood of all the 16 crRNAs designed, two for each of the eight genes, are ineffective is 
deemed too low for this to be a probable explanation.  
Due to the time restriction of the study, and the slow growing nature of A. campestris, the first 
and fourth explanation was not further examined.  
4.2.3 A. campestris transformation with a different transformation media 
Secondary metabolite (SM) production is very energy-consuming and is usually only initiated 
when advantageous to the microorganism. The chromatin structure has been shown to be a key 
regulator of SM production. Genes placed in facultative heterochromatin are only expressed 
under certain circumstances. The circumstances include nutrient deficiency, heat stress, 
competition between microorganisms, or sometimes certain nutrients need to be present in the 
environment. Several strategies have successfully been applied to “awaken” otherwise silenced 
genes. Methods include variations in culture conditions and co-cultivating the fungi with other 
microorganisms. However, the majority of the approaches are based on genetic manipulation, 
(Reyes-dominguez et al., 2012; Studt et al., 2017). 
To test the suggestion that the Cas9 enzyme is unable to access the genes in the pathway, it was 
decided to perform the fungal transformation using modified YES agar. YES agar is a more 
nutrient-rich agar compared to normal TM. Media recipes is presented 3.5 Preparation of 
media. It was reckoned that it could open the heterochromatin structure into a more 
euchromatin-like structure, and making the genes more accessible to the Cas9-enzyme. The

campestris mutants, using modified YES agar as transformation media. The growth pattern

template containing longer sequences of homology poses a bigger risk for mutations going 
undetected. As the nature of the repair is not fully evaluated, there is a possibility that a 
different repair happens when using the repair oligo. This repair may cause changes in the

be missed, as only a limited number of colonies are screened when using fungal colony PCR.

transferred for the master plate, as happened in the results presented previous in this section.

a selection marker, for insertion in the A. campestris genome instead of the deleted genes.

5 Conclusion and Future Perspective 
This study sought out to investigate the involvement of the potential gene cluster in the 
production of chlorflavonin in A. campestris. The experiments involved individual gene deletion 
using CRISPR-Cas9 together with various types of repair templates and transformation media.  
 
It was found that the deletion of the gene coding for the PKS hybrid, assigned chlor1, resulted 
in a strain with no detectable chlorflavonin production. Thus, the involvement of the gene 
cluster in chlorflavonin production was confirmed.  
The remaining genes, chlor2 to chlor11, were investigated by constructing CRISPR-Cas9 
plasmids for individually gene deletion. Successful CRISPR-Cas9 plasmids were obtained to 
delete the genes chlor2-chlor9. However, the gene deletion in A. campestris with 90mer repair 
oligonucleotides proved more difficult than anticipated, and only the deletion of chlor2 was 
confirmed via fungal colony PCR screening, after a change of transformation media. It was not 
possible to investigate the result of the chlor2 deletion further, as the colonies transferred from 
the transformation plate to the master plate did not exhibit the chlor2 deletion.  
The screening method of using fungal colony PCR was deemed unfavourable, because of the 
likelihood of missing the few possible successful mutant generated. As a solution, longer repair 
templates were assembled which incorporated and RFP gene. However, it was difficult to access 
the success of the longer repair template based on this experiment, due to the failure of the 
hygromycin selective marker. The transformation using longer repair templates needs to be 
repeated, in order to conclude the success of using the RFP gene as selective marker.  
Based on the experiments performed in A. campestris, it can be concluded that more work needs 
to be performed to investigate the effectiveness of the Cas9 enzyme in A. campestris, along with 
the usage of RFP as selective marker. Furthermore, the conditions for chlorflavonin production 
should also be evaluated to enhance the chances to verify successful gene editing.  
Because of the low success for deleting the genes in A. campestris, plasmids were constructed 
including 4 of the 10 remaining genes in the hopes of finding the isomerase responsible for the 
ring-closure. The four gene plasmids were individually transformed into A. oryzae. However, 
successful transformation was only achieved for chlor11. Because of time constrains, the results 
of the chlor11 transformation were not investigated within this study, and are therefore not 
included in this report.
