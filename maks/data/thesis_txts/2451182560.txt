Abstract
Heavy metal contamination can cause both damage to plants and be resolved by plants. In this study, essential
Cu, ranking highly in risk assessments due to its omnipresence and high ecotoxicity, and non-essential Cd, which
is toxic to both plants and humans, are assessed. Plant roots take up Cu and Cd, causing potential threats to
food security through direct detrimental e↵ects on crops and edible plants, and further transfer up the trophic
levels of the food chain. Contrarily, some robust plants eﬃciently retain and translocate Cu and Cd in phytore-
mediation e↵orts. In this paper, the phytotoxic e↵ects of Cu and Cd on transpiration and growth are studied
for the metals individually and in a mixture. The assessment of phytotoxicity in the study is based on the
principle that excess exposure of contaminants leads to reduced growth and transpiration rates, which can both
be monitored. Additionally, the equilibrium passive di↵usive uptake from solution to willow roots and stems is
quantiﬁed, as well as the heavy metal compartmentalization in roots, stem and leaves. Finally, the e↵ects the
climate change variables temperature and atmospheric CO2 concentrations have on phytotoxicity are evaluated.
Stock solutions of the two heavy metals are prepared in 1 g/L, in demineralized water. Diluted solutions of
0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, and 5 mg/L are prepared continuously throughout the experiment, in modiﬁed ISO 8692 nutrient
solution. Willow cuttings (Salix viminalis) and cress (Lepidium satvium) are used as test plants in hydroponic
experiments, by conducting water loss tests for up to two weeks. Inhibitory e↵ects on transpiration rates, weight
change and visual assessment are the main indicators for phytotoxic response utilized in this study. Cress is
used in experiments testing individual Cu and Cd toxicity. Willows are tested similarly, with the addition of a
mixture toxicity, an uptake experiment and climate chamber experiments of Cu phytotoxicity where cuttings
are treated at ambient conditions (19°C and 410 ppm CO2), elevated temperatures (30°C and 410 ppm CO2),
elevated CO2 (19 °C and 700 ppm) and a combined treatment (30°C and 700 ppm). In addition, severed willow
roots and stem pieces are submerged in solution and shaken for 24 h in the estimation of the passive di↵usive
uptake parameters RCF and SCF.
The willow phytotoxicity is in the order of mixture > Cu > Cd, while the phytotoxic e↵ects of a mixture
between Cu and Cd is not signiﬁcantly di↵erent from the phytotoxicity of Cu. The transpiration based log
normal EC50 estimates of the mixture, Cu and Cd are 0.32, 0.35 and 3.3 mg/L, respectively. For cress, the order
of phytotoxicity is also Cu > Cd, and the Cu EC50 of 7.5 mg/L reveals that cress is signiﬁcantly more robust than
willow. The climate chamber experiments reveal that the positive synergistic e↵ects of temperature and CO2
on willows, including increased metabolic rates, increased growth and higher water use eﬃciency, signiﬁcantly
alleviates the toxic e↵ects of Cu, leading to a EC50 of 2.7 mg/L of Cu, higher than the ambient conditions EC50
of 0.92 mg/L, and the combined treatment is the only condition where WUE and growth is not negative at the
highest exposure doses. The total recovery of Cu from solution in the individual Cu experiment is 49% for the
0.2 mg Cu/L dose and 92% for the 1 mg Cu/L dose, while the recovery of Cu from the mixture experiment is
63 % for the 0.2 mg Cu-Cd/L dose and 89% for the 1 mg Cu-Cd/L dose, revealing that the willow control and
tolerance mechanisms are eﬃcient at 0.2 mg Cu/L. Furthermore, the recovery of Cd in the mixture experiment
is 78% and 71%, for the same doses. The order of plant compartment concentrations is roots > leaves > stem
at both 0.2 and 1 mg Cu-Cd/L exposure for both Cu and Cd. The root concentrations increase with exposure,
the leaf concentrations remain stable, while the stem concentrations increase for Cu and decrease for Cd with
increased exposure. The R/W increases from 23151 to 33379 L/kgdw for Cu and decreases from 19738 to 16774
L/kgdw for Cd when exposure increases from 0.2 to 1 mg Cu-Cd/L. The translocation rates R/L increase in both
cases, meaning that more Cu and Cd is retained in the roots at high concentrations. Finally, the equilibrium
passive di↵usive uptake parameter RCF based on average exposure increases from 24693 to 36419 L/kgdw for
Cu and 33198 to 46272 L/kgdwfor Cd. RCF indicates that more Cd than Cu di↵uses into roots. RCF > R/W,
indicating that active plant processes restrict uptake and translocation of Cu and Cd. Finally RCF > SCF by
2 to 3 orders of magnitude, implying a higher Cu and Cd aﬃnity of the roots than the stem.

Preface and Acknowledgement
This master thesis is the ﬁnal project of the 120 ECTS graduate MSc program ’Environmental Engi-
neering’ at the Technical University of Denmark, DTU. The experiments have been conducted at the
DTU Department of Environmental Engineering (Lyngby) and the DTU Risø Environmental Risk
Assessment Facility (RERAF). For Marte Teksum Haugland, the project work has extended over the
period from January 28th 2019 to July 5th 2019, due to her participation in a 5 ECTS course at
DTU parallel to the project. For Ingeborg Kvalheim, the project work has extended over the period
February 4th 2019 to July 5th 2019. This thesis constitutes 30 ECTS per author. Problem deﬁnition,
literature review, experiment execution and writing has taken place with equal contribution from both
group members.
The purpose of the study is to assess the phytotoxic e↵ects of copper and cadmium to cress and willow
trees, in relevance of both food security and phytoremediation implications of introducing heavy met-
als to the soil in a future where the climate is changing and reuse of resources potentially containing
heavy metals is increasing. This is achieved through willow and cress transpiration experiments for
Cu and Cd individually and in a mixture, uptake analyses for assessing active and passive uptake,
accumulation and translocation of the heavy metals, and by the use of the climate chamber facilities
of RERAF to assess climatic e↵ects on phytotoxicity.

Technician Sinh Hy Nguyen, to whom we are ever grateful. Other contributors we wish to thank are
Mikael Emil Olsson for ICP-MS analysis and Zakie Peter Ruru and his team of technicians for their
e↵orts in assuring the functionality of the climate chambers.

Introduction
Biosolids and treated water from waste water treatment plants have historically not been regarded as
resources for further utilization, but as climate change, population growth and urbanization increas-
ingly deﬁnes and a↵ects the human society, the value of these waste products is being reevaluated.
Water and resource scarcity, including phosphorous depletion, is becoming a pressing issue across
the globe, and reuse of resources from waste e✏uents has become increasingly environmentally and
economically beneﬁcial and prioritized under sustainable policy making (Ulrich and Frossard, 2014).
The phenomenon of waste-to-value has become critical and prominent in modern society.
While reuse of resources includes numerous environmental and economic advantages, the land applica-
tion of biosolids and mineral fertilizers and wastewater for irrigation, introduces a new challenge, which
is concentrated introduction of contaminants to the environment, including heavy metals. While com-
binations of heavy metals are naturally present in the soil at non-toxic concentrations for living beings,
their geochemical cycles and biochemical balances have been altered dramatically due to this global
population growth, reuse, industrialization and other anthropogenic inﬂuences.
Their drastically
increasing presence in the environment has led to them being classiﬁed as priority environmental con-
taminants due to their omnipresence, persistence, non-biodegradability and toxic e↵ects on metabolic
activities for both plants and higher organisms (Mclaughlin et al., 2011). Currently, pollution by the
adverse group of heavy metals is recognized as a major public concern because the contaminants from
these waste streams can be introduced to crops and plants via plant uptake. Contamination of plants
poses a potential health risk to both humans, animals and the ecosystems as a whole.
Heavy metals present in contaminated soils are prone to trophic transfer in the food chain, where
they are initially introduced through root uptake from plants. This indicates that initial plant uptake
determines the metal availability and exposure for other living organisms (Morkunas et al., 2018).
The soil is therefore increasingly regarded a major source of metals being transferred in the food
chain through increased amounts taken up by plants (Gall et al., 2015). Thus, the adverse ecological
e↵ects of heavy metals ﬁrst manifest themselves with acutely phytotoxic e↵ects on plants growing on
these polluted soils. The term phytotoxicity involves detrimental deviations from normal appearance,
growth patterns and transpiration schemes of the plants in response to the substance (Mclaughlin
et al., 2011). Overall, understanding plant uptake and phytotoxicity of metals is a crucial measure
in assessing exposure pathways to plants and other organisms, and predicting e↵ects on vulnerable
ecosystems.
The plant realm is however not only at risk in the face of heavy metal pollution, it also has the
potential to cost-e↵ectively, eﬃciently and environmentally friendly resolve the emerging soil con-
tamination issues through phytoremediation. Phytoremediation is a promising and green clean-up
technology where plants and associated microbes reduce the concentration of heavy metals, radionu-
cleides and organic contaminants in the environment (Ali et al., 2013). Fast growing and high-biomass
producing trees such as willow trees can stabilize and extract the heavy metals while preventing ero-
sion and metal leaching, before gradually improving the soil quality for crop cultivation (Vangronsveld
et al., 2009).
The complexity of future challenges posed to both food crops exposed to heavy metals and plants
used for phytoremediation, is increasing with climate change. Anthropogenic climate change is es-
timated to have caused approximately 1°C of the global warming above pre-industrial levels, and

the global warming is likely to reach 1.5°C between 2030 and 2052 if this rate continues (Masson-
Delmotte et al., 2018). In parallel, the global annual mean atmospheric CO2 concentrations have
increased by 45% since the pre-industrial levels, from 280 ppm to 415 ppm as of May 2019 (Oceanic
and Atmospheric Administration, 2019), making the current concentrations the highest in the past
14 million years (Baer and Singer, 2018). Climate change, through temperature rise and elevated
atmospheric CO2, has direct e↵ects on plant productivity, development and function, as identiﬁed by
transpiration, growth and water use eﬃciency (WUE) alterations (Morison and Lawlor, 1999). It is
generally considered that warmer temperatures will increase the transpiration rates while increased
CO2 will decrease the transpiration rates. Naturally, the nature of heavy metal uptake by the plants
is linked to the climatic e↵ects on plant functionality. Increased temperatures leading to increased
transpiration is expected to the uptake of heavy metals while the metabolic rates of the plant also
increase, alleviating some of the detrimental e↵ects. Increased CO2 reduces the stomatal conductance,
leading to decreasing uptake of heavy metals through transpiration. There is however still consider-
able uncertainty about the balance of these processes and the extent and direction of acclimation and
plant response to temperature and CO2 individually, and when interacting.
The increasing presence of heavy metals in soils poses plant species speciﬁc hazards. Furthermore,
assessment of temperature and CO2 e↵ects on readily available heavy metals would give insight and
understanding of heavy metal bioavailability with climate change. Although it is generally considered
that warmer temperatures will increase the transpiration and increased CO2 will decrease it, there
is still considerable uncertainty about the balance and interaction of elevated temperature and CO2
(Morison and Lawlor, 1999). Furthermore, another layer of unknowns is added when the toxic e↵ects
of heavy metals is a part of the system, as the research on heavy metal phytotoxicity under combined
temperature and CO2 regimes is highly limited. Overall, it is crucial to ensure suﬃcient knowledge
of the implications climate change and heavy metal pollution from increased resource reuse have on
future food security and application of phytoremediation technologies.
1.1
Problem Delimitation and Research Objectives
In this study, hydroponical experiments are conducted to assess plant responses to copper (Cu) and
cadmium (Cd) exposure. The essential micronutrient Cu ranks very high in several risk assessments
to plants, trees and the environment, where Cu has the highest overall risk when being compared to
other metals, pesticides, persistent organic pollutants, pharmaceuticals, surfactans and nanoparticles
(Johnson et al., 2017) (Clausen and Trapp, 2017). This high ranking of heavy metals in general, and
especially Cu, is due to their omnipresence combined with their ecotoxicity. Investigating uptake and
phytotoxicity of the non-essential metal Cd is highly relevant with the recently revised EU legislation
on fertilizing products, where the Cd limit values for mineral and organic fertilisers, soil amendments
and growth media are reduced (Council of the European Union, 2018). Eggen et al. (2019) (Norwe-
gian Scientiﬁc Committee for Food and Environment) have also recently conducted an extensive risk
assessment of Cd content in Norwegian phosphorous fertilizers as a result. The e↵ects of exposure and
uptake of Cu and Cd is highly plant species dependant. Therefore Cu and Cd e↵ects on both cress,
generally representing edible plants, and willows, generally representing phytoremediation plants, are
incorporated in the experiments.

2
Background
2.1
Copper Plant Relations
Cu is one of the most abundant metals in the Earth’s crust and functions as a micronutrient, essen-
tial for all life at low concentrations. Cu can pose a threat to plants as the plants take up Cu and
accumulate the excess in their tissues. Both deﬁciency and excess of Cu can have hazardous e↵ects
on plant growth and alter biochemical processes. However, plant tolerance to Cu varies and is greatly
dependant on plant species and genotype (Adrees et al., 2015). Cu in the environment can originate
from both natural and anthropogenic sources. Anthropogenic activities such as mining and industry,
as well as addition of organic or inorganic fertilizers in agricultural soil (liming, sewage sludge, irriga-
tion waters and pesticides) are important sources of Cu accumulation in the environment (Nagajyoti
et al., 2010).
2.1.1
Soil and Plant Occurrence and Distribution
Cu is present in soil all across Europe. Figure 1 shows the distribution of Cu concentrations in the top
soil in the EU. Soil Cu accumulation all over Europe is mostly due to agricultural activities, where
the highest accumulations are observed around the Mediterranean countries, due to high use of Cu
containing products as pesticides, applied in vineyards and orchards (T´oth et al., 2016). The average
Cu concentration in soil for di↵erent land cover classes, are found to be 17 mg/kg, where vineyards
have the highest mean concentration of 49 mg/kg (Ballabio et al., 2018). However, these mean values
are below the EU threshold limit of 100 mg/kg in soil (T´oth et al., 2016).
Figure 1: Spatial representation of Cu in European top soil (T´oth et al., 2016)
The phytotoxicity of Cu is highly dependent on the metal concentration availability in soil.
The
concentration of Cu in unpolluted soil ranges between 3 and 100 mg/kg, while only 1-20% is readily
bioavailable, and the rest is bound to organic matter. The bioavailability is mainly determined by
the growth medium and its physical and chemical characteristics a↵ecting the adsorption process, as
well as the total concentration of Cu, cation exchange capacity (CEC), soil organic matter and clay
content, and soil pH. pH is one of the main variables controlling Cu solubility, mobility and transport,
and thus has a large e↵ect on metal bioavailability. Cu becomes readily available at pH below 6,
9
==PAGE:11==
meaning that the concentration of free Cu2+ increases in the soil solution with a decreasing soil pH,
due to reduced Cu adsorption abilities (Adrees et al., 2015). This also a↵ects the plant uptake of
Cu, which will increase as the soil pH decreases. Another important factor a↵ecting Cu solubility is
the amount of organic matter dissolved in soil solution (Reichman, 2002). Soil organic matter with
high CEC can e↵ectively adsorb Cu, as Cu has a high aﬃnity for the binding sites of soil components
(Yruela, 2009). Organic matter forms complexes with the Cu ions, which decreases the Cu bioavail-
abillity as the plants are unable to absorb large metal complexes (Reichman, 2002).
Cu present in the soil solution is taken up by plant absorption through the root system, where the
rate of uptake is associated with the transpiration and water transport from the soil into the roots.
Initially, Cu is adsorbing on the root surface, due to negative cell wall charges, where it dissociates
from its complex forms before being absorbed by the roots (Adrees et al., 2015).
Generally, the
concentration of essential metals in the plant biomass is stable because it is maintained by di↵erent
control mechanisms on ion uptake and translocation, independently of the external concentrations.
However, at excess external levels, these control mechanisms break down followed by an increased
biomass concentration with increased concentration in soil (Mclaughlin et al., 2011). The roots are
the ﬁrst organs which are in direct contact with Cu, and Cu ions are often deposited in the root
tissues to restrict transport to the leaves. Cu is transported from the roots to the shoots through
the xylem, in complexes formed with amino acids. Because the Cu mobility in plants is limited, the
concentrations in the roots will be greater than the concentrations in the shoots (Adrees et al., 2015)
(Poschenrieder and Barcelo, 2004). As the exposure concentration of Cu rises, the concentration in
the roots will rise proportionally, while the translocation to the shoots is not linearly correlated to
the concentration increase in the roots (Larcher, 1995).
2.1.2
Tolerance and Detoxiﬁcation Mechanisms
Some plant species have developed tolerance and detoxiﬁcation mechanisms at the cellular level to
avoid metal toxicity.
These mechanisms enable the plants to maintain normal functioning in the
presence of high concentrations of toxic compounds (Adrees et al., 2015). Plants have overall developed
two basic strategies for tolerance: metal exclusion and metal accumulation. These defence strategies
are developed to prevent damage within the plant by avoiding accumulation of toxic concentrations in
sensitive parts of the cells. Metal exclusion restricts the uptake and transport of Cu by immobilizing
the Cu in the cell wall, or restricting inﬂux through the plasma membrane.
However, complete
exclusion is not possible because many cations are essential for plant function (Larcher, 1995) (Hall,
2002). Organic acid excreted by the plant can also reduce Cu uptake by forming complexes with
the metal ions outside the roots, preventing it from being taken up (Yruela, 2005). Within the cells,
there is a variety of mechanisms involved in preventing Cu accumulation. One detoxiﬁcation and
tolerance mechanism includes chelation of metals by organic acids, amino acids, phytochelatins and
metallothioneins, which form complexes with Cu to limit Cu uptake in plants (Adrees et al., 2015)
(Hall, 2002). Vacuolar compartmentalization is another mechanism for plant tolerance, which involves
transporting excess Cu into the vacuole where Cu does the least harm to essential cellular processes
(Yruela, 2005) (Hall, 2002).
2.1.3
Phytotoxicity Responses
Toxic heavy metal e↵ects mainly occur due to the metal ions interfering with the electron transport in
respiration and photosynthesis processes, together with inactivation of vital enzymes. This results in
a lowered energy status, causing decreased nutrient uptake and reduced growth (Larcher, 1995). Cu
10
==PAGE:12==
toxicity to plants is an adverse problem in agricultural production due to the negative e↵ects elevated
Cu concentrations have on all stages of plant growth. Phytotoxicity of Cu is highly depending on
plant species, the concentration and bioavailability of Cu in soil solution and exposure time (Yruela,
2009).
Cu is an essential nutrient at optimal levels, and thus required for plant growth and development as
an important constituent in numerous processes within the plant, including maintenance of normal
functioning of enzymes and proteins being involved in photosynthesis and respiration (Adrees et al.,
2015). However, excess levels of Cu is toxic to plants, and is shown to cause a range of toxicity
symptoms. Plants grown in the presence of elevated Cu levels have exhibited reduced yield due to
inhibition of growth, changes in plant morphology, leaf chlorosis, necrosis, inhibition of root and shoot
elongation, root browning, wilting, poor seed germination and interference with photosynthesis and
respiration processes (Poschenrieder and Barcelo, 2004).
Many of the symptoms listed are direct
results of water deﬁciency due to heavy metal stress.
Excess Cu in the leaves leads to increased
stomatal resistance, which causes lower transpiration rates (Poschenrieder and Barcelo, 2004). Poor
uptake of water limits the availability of nutrients, which retards the metabolism and growth. Figure
2 shows the plant dose response to Cu, with concentration in aerial parts against the plant dry
weight. Plant growth is generally optimal at Cu concentrations between 2 and 30 mg/kgdw, while Cu
concentrations higher than this are inhibiting plant growth (Adrees et al., 2015). Chlorosis is caused
by membrane destruction as a result of Cu induced lipid peroxidation, a condition in which leaves
produce insuﬃcient chlorophyll reducing the characteristic green color of plants (Larcher, 1995).
Figure 2: Hypothetical dose response curve of plant dry weight as a function of Cu concentrations in aerial
parts of the plant (mg/kgdw), with included optimal range of Cu concentrations (Adrees et al., 2015)
Root growth is often inhibited before shoot growth, due to the roots being the location of preferential
Cu accumulation when the Cu supply is large. Roots are thus not more sensitive to high Cu con-
centrations than shoots, despite the growth inhibition occurring at the roots ﬁrst. This is explained
by the roots being the plant compartment initially exposed to the Cu concentrations in the environ-
mental medium, and the root concentrations are thus proportional to the external concentrations.
Cu exposure of the leaves requires xylem transport of the Cu taken up by the roots, which is often
highly restricted in comparison to the direct absorption of the roots. Root analysis is therefore often
necessary to be able to provide an appropriate indication of the Cu tolerance of leaves. In fact, up
to 60% of total Cu in roots might be bound to the cell wall fraction and exist in the cell wall plasma
membrane interface (Larcher, 1995).

2.2
Cadmium Plant Relations
Cd is characterized as one of the most toxic heavy metals existing, because it adversely a↵ects the
biological processes of living organisms including human beings, animals and plants. Although Cd is
a non-essential element for plants, is it eﬃciently absorbed by the roots and leaves, leading to a range
of consequences (Kumar and Aery, 2016). Main Cd sources are pesticide and fertilizer use, solid waste
(household, municipal and industrial) and sludge disposal, as well as electroplating, batteries, welding,
smelting and pigments (Wahid et al., 2009) (Hasan et al., 2009). Furthermore, wastewater applied
as irrigation water causes accumulation of Cd in soils. The Cd tolerance is highly plant species and
genotype dependent, as they exhibit di↵erent absorbance, translocation and accumulation properties.
Nevertheless, most species are sensitive to Cd exposure.
2.2.1
Soil and Plant Occurrence and Distribution
Typical total soil Cd concentrations range between 0.1 and 1 mg Cd/kg soil (Six and Smolders, 2014),
while the average Cd concentration in European soils is 0.28 mg Cd/kg soil. The distribution of Cd
in European agricultural soils is visually represented in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Spatial representation of Cd in European top soil (T´oth et al., 2016)
Cd is among the most toxic and mobile heavy metals in soil because the Cd salts are water soluble,
which in turn generates an abundance of highly mobile ionic Cd available for uptake, phloem trans-
port and translocation between di↵erent plant compartments. The phytoavailablity of Cd and all
heavy metals is naturally depending on the physical and chemical properties of the soil and the soil
solution (Wahid et al., 2009). Like Cu, the availability of Cd is depending on Cd concentration, pH,
temperature, redox-potential and the presence of other elements (Hasan et al., 2009). The availability
of Cd is reduced by the competition of Ca ions and at low pH. Furthermore, the bioavailablity of Cd
is proven to decrease with increased dissolved organic matter content in the soil (Prasad, 1995).
Several studies show that the roots is the plant compartment which accumulates the greatest quantities
of Cd, as the roots are in direct contact with the pore water (Smolders, 2001). The root uptake of
Cd increases proportionally to increasing Cd2+ salt concentrations.
This trend of linearity holds

for the environmentally relevant concentration range (up to about 20 mg Cd/kg soil), whereafter the
relationship becomes curvilinear. Cd is taken up by the plant roots and competes with several mineral
nutrients with physico-chemical traits corresponding to those of Cd. These nutrients of similar traits
include K, Ca and Mg, which have a declining presence in the plant when Cd is present. The main
root uptake mechanism is Cd entering the cytosol through Ca2+ channels. In addition, the uptake
of nitrate, which does not share traits with Cd, is also inﬂuenced under the presence of Cd. Because
the roots is the ﬁrst plant compartment being exposed will Cd accumulate here, and this is where
damage is occurring ﬁrst (Hasan et al., 2009). When Cd has entered the plant roots, it is complexed
by ligands like organic acids and phytochelatin and reaches the xylem through the apoplastic and
symplastic pathways (Benavides et al., 2005). Cd is often transported by the same mechanisms as
the essential metalloid nutrients, so the transport occurs both in the xylem and phloem (Hasan et al.,
2009). Generally, the Cd content in plants follows the order of roots > stems > leaves > fruits > seeds.
As such, the Cd concentrations are generally higher in the plant leaves than the fruits and storage
organs, while roots have the highest concentrations. Table 1 shows that the compartmentalization of
Cd between twigs and leaves is highly depending on the soil type, and the uptake is generally lower

2.2.2
Tolerance and Detoxiﬁcation Mechanisms
Plants display a range of mechanisms to mitigate the adverse e↵ects of Cd uptake, including binding
to cell walls, reduced membrane transport, chelation and compartmentalization. Firstly, it is the
physical, chemical and biological binding properties and capacities of the soil which determines the
initial uptake of Cd from pore water, as soil particles with a high aﬃnity for cations reduces the
plant available fraction. Among the extracellular biological defence mechanisms, mychorrizha fungus
enables increased tolerance of its plant host (Benavides et al., 2005). The cell walls of the roots are in
direct contact with the solution, and the binding properties of the cell walls determine the availability
of Cd for further transport and translocation, as bound Cd is immobilized. The cell wall exclusion of
Cd is the most functional defence mechanism of plants.
Extracellular carbohydrates present in mucliage and callose also limit the Cd uptake by chelation.
Chelation is the process when cations and a compound bind into a neutrally charged complex that
moves freely in the xylem of vascular plants. Organic acids and amino acids in the soil are capable
to chelate and bind heavy metals in the soil, making them unavailable for root absorption.
Free
Cd ions can in turn be subject to chelation, which synthesises detoxiﬁed ligand-metal complexes as
phytochelatins, metallothieoneins and organic acids (Wahid et al., 2009).
The concentration and
aﬃnity of chelating molecules and highly selective transport enzymes a↵ects the accumulation rates.
It has been reported that especially phytochelatines are essential for the build-up of tolerance of non-
essential heavy metals, and in particular Cd (Benavides et al., 2005).
13
==PAGE:15==
The detoxiﬁed metabolites can in turn be compartmentalized in intracellular compartments in both
roots and shoots, which is another important tolerance mechanism (Benavides et al., 2005). This
compartmentalization limits the free transport of Cd ions in the cytosol and retains them in a limited
area, e.g.
by storing Cd in the vacuoles to protect the cytoplasm.
Plants partitioning Cd into
di↵erent plant compartments as a tolerance mechanism plays a crucial role in the metal tolerance,
and the sequestration in cellular compartments like the vacuole has been proven. An example is the
transport of phytochelatin-metal complexes into the vacuole by transporter or exchanger proteins.
Recent studies suggest that phytochelatin-Cd complex formation plays a more important role in
detoxiﬁcation than the vacuole sequestration (Viehweger, 2014).
2.2.3
Phytotoxicity Responses
The most frequent e↵ects of Cd induced phytotoxicity are inhibition of root elongation, insuﬃcient
nutrient uptake, perturbation of water relations, suppression of photosynthetic activity, declining
biomass production, and at highly excessive exposure, plant death. Typical visual proof of toxic Cd
exposure is chlorosis of both leaves and stem, leaf rolling and necrosis, root browning and tip-burning,
as well as growth inhibition and red-brown discoloration of leaf margins or veins (Kumar and Aery,
2016). Toxic concentrations of Cd in plants induces disturbance in physiological and biochemical pro-
cesses and have e↵ects on anatomical and development events, as well as molecular and cytological
processes (Wahid et al., 2009). In turn, these disturbances lead to growth and development impair-
ment which both have a negative e↵ect on the yield. Cd uptake from solution is fast, so symptoms
are often prominent within only 48 h of exposure.
Cd is an eﬃcient photosynthesis inhibitor and a negative relationship between net photosynthesis and
transpiration inhibition has been observed in a range of species (Prasad, 1995), because Cd causes
stomatal closure leading to increasing stomatal resistance and reduced presence of CO2 ﬁxating en-
zymes in the mesophyll. These implications are identiﬁed as a main cause for reduced shoot and root
elongation, as well as diminished dry matter production. Moreover, heavy metal accumulation in
leaves is associated with reduced net photosynthesis. Photosynthetic components as light and dark
respiration are both a↵ected by the presence of Cd at toxic concentrations. A positive side e↵ect of
Cd induced stomatal closure is however that the uptake and transport of Cd becomes limited as the
transpiration decreases (Wahid et al., 2009).
Alteration of plant mineral nutrition- and water levels is another result of excess Cd exposure (Wahid
et al., 2009). The nutritional status and Cd uptake is negatively correlated for a range of macronu-
trients and micronutrients. Cd inhibits Fe(III) reductase, which leads to Fe(II) deﬁciency (Nagajyoti
et al., 2010), and observed leaf chlorosis may be due to Fe, Cu or P deﬁciency or reduced Mn trans-
port (Viehweger, 2014). Generally, Cd inhibits uptake, transport and utilization of both metalloid
nutrients and water. Furthermore, Cd inhibits absorption and translocation of nitrate from roots
to shoot, due to inhibited nitrate reductase activity and decreased nitrogen ﬁxation and ammonia
assimilation capacity. Generally, Cd interferes with transport and utilization of many essential nutri-
ents and water by plants. Cd is interacting with the water balance of plants by a↵ecting the plasma
membrane permeability. Diminishing plant growth and vigour (stunting) due to Cd stress is overall
due to this diminished water exchange. Changes in water and nutritional values are overall due to
negative e↵ects on root structure and functionality. Importantly, inhibition of long- distance water
transport leads to a reduction in leaf water content and subsequently to the development of a water
deﬁcit in leaves.

2.3
Copper and Cadmium Mixture Toxicity
Most toxicity studies are conducted with individual metals, while plants and organisms are generally
exposed to a cocktail of metal contamination in the environment. Combined Cd and Cu exposure is
of special concern due to their high abundance and occurrence in soil, combined with their hazardous
e↵ects on human health and damage to plants (Mwamba et al., 2016) (Hakmaoui et al., 2007).
Mixture toxicity e↵ects of Cu and Cd to plants is depending on the species and genotype in terms of
tolerance and sensitivity, as well as the heavy metal concentration in soil. Research has shown that
simultaneous supply of Cu and Cd to plants leads to the metals having an e↵ect on each others uptake
and bioaccumulation (Mwamba et al., 2016). Multiple studies found that the presence of Cu has an
inhibitory e↵ect on Cd uptake and accumulation with elevated Cu and Cd concentrations (Mwamba
et al., 2016) (Abboud and Wilkinson, 2013). On the other hand, Cd is shown to have an increasing
e↵ect on bioaccumulation and uptake of Cu at low concentrations (Mwamba et al., 2016). However,
these e↵ects are highly dependant on the plant species and concentrations present, as well as the
plants tolerance to heavy metals. Plant tolerance was studied by Landberg and Greger (2002) where
the accumulation of Cu is seen to decrease in the presence of Cd and Zn in high Cu-accumulating
plants, while low Cu-accumulating plants show no signiﬁcant e↵ect. However, accumulation of Cd
did not di↵er in either high or low Cu-accumulating plants in the presence of Cu and Zn (Landberg
and Greger, 2002).
The metals are not found to interact within the plant, as the shoot to root
translocation rate is not seen to di↵er between single and combined exposure (Mwamba et al., 2016).
The highest concentrations of Cu and Cd are accumulated in the roots with limited translocation to
the aerial parts (Hakmaoui et al., 2007) (Mediouni et al., 2006). The combined phytotoxic e↵ects of
Cu and Cd to plants are seen to cause growth inhibition, chlorophyll loss, and oxidative stress. In
general, single heavy metals are less toxic to plants than heavy metals in a mixture (Montvydiene and
Marˇciulioniene, 2004), and Cu is found to be more toxic to plants than Cd (Hakmaoui et al., 2007).
2.4
Elevated CO2 and Temperature E↵ects on Phytotoxicity
Elevated temperature conditions and CO2 concentrations directly a↵ect the plant functionality in
the plant-pore water-soil system. Temperature is a main factor a↵ecting the plant growth rate and
development, and thus warmer temperatures and extremes associated with climate change will impact
the plant productivity. The e↵ects temperature has on plants is highly species dependent, as each
species has a deﬁned range of minimum and maximum temperature boundaries where the growth
rate is positive. Furthermore, the response di↵ers throughout the plants development stage. Limited
e↵orts have been devoted to assess the phytotoxicity of heavy metal polluted soils under the inﬂuence
of increasing temperatures (Fu et al., 2018). When temperatures increase, the uptake of water-borne
bioavailable heavy metals will increase as well. However, as intermediate temperature elevations lower
than or equal to the temperature optimum are associated with increased metabolic activity, the plants
are as well more able to cope with the exposure. Thus, the ﬁnal e↵ects elevated temperature has on
toxicity of heavy metals in plants is a trade-o↵between increased uptake as a function of increased
transpiration rates and increased metabolic activity of degradation agents. Li et al. (2012) report
that the increased translocation eﬃciency, photosynthetic capacity and growth lead to increasing leaf
concentrations of Cu, Zn and Fe, while the tuber concentrations of Cd, Pb, Fe, Zn and Cu decrease,
because the tuber growth rate exceeds the increase in uptake rate due to elevated temperatures.
Cornu et al. (2016) found an increase of Cd and Zn in the root and shoot tissue in lettuce, as well as
an enhanced shoot-to-root translocation with increased soil temperature (10, 20 and 30°C). This was
suggested to be an e↵ect of the increased transpiration caused by elevated temperature (Cornu et al.,
2016). In the study by Ge et al. (2016), warming also promoted Cd translocation from root-to-shoots,

==PAGE:17==
and increased Cd and Cu accumulation in rice biomass when elevating mean air temperature by 1°C.
This emphasises that there is no overall tendency in terms of plant response to elevated temperature
regimes.
Elevated CO2 stimulates photosynthetic carbon gain and net primary production on the long term
despite down-regulation of Rubisco activity. Further, CO2 improves nitrogen use eﬃciency and de-
creases water use both on the leaf and canopy scale, while stimulating dark respiration.
For C4
plants, CO2 does no directly stimulate carbon gain under drought events. The result is a stimulation
of yield (Leakey et al., 2009). More research on the e↵ects elevated CO2 conditions have on heavy
metal uptake by plants is needed (Guo et al., 2011). For plants, increased CO2 concentrations lead to
reduced stomatal aperture and decreased transpiration. At ambient conditions, most C3 plants are
CO2 limited, so an increasing CO2 is expected to have a positive e↵ect on plant growth and photosyn-
thetic activity, when nutrients or light is not limited. As the transpiration decreases, metal exposure
often decreases while WUE increases. Guo et al. (2011) state that elevated CO2 leads to lower Cu
concentrations and higher Cd concentrations in shoots and grain of rice and wheat, and indicate that
the distribution of contaminating elements in soil and plants is altered by the inﬂuence of CO2. Duval
et al. (2011) indicate that CO2 alters the distribution of contaminants in the ecosystems, Wu et al.
(2009) report that elevated CO2 increased Cu and Cd concentrations in both the shoots and roots
of rice, while Li et al. (2010) report decreasing or no e↵ect on Cu concentration in rice, while Cd
concentrations was rising. These outcomes are not uniform, and highlight the importance of further
research.

3.1
Willow Experiment
Willows are ideal test plants, due to their ability to grow fast under di↵erent temperature, light, pH
and nutrient conditions. Furthermore, willow trees have minimal di↵erences in the genetic material
throughout the stem, making them easy to propagate by cuttings (Clausen and Trapp, 2017). The
hydroponic willow test uses transpiration as an indicator of the health of the cutting. Transpiration
is an established method to measure toxicity to a plant due to its close relation to growth and photo-
synthesis of the cutting, but an easier and faster parameter to measure. It is based on the principle
that healthy cuttings transpire more and will have increased transpiration over time due to growth.
Exposing cuttings to a toxic solution will result in inhibition of transpiration. The toxicity can there-
fore be measured by this inhibition (Trapp et al., 2004). The willow cuttings are either sampled from
the willow tree forest at DTU Risø or retrieved from a source close to the Lyngbyvej highway in near
proximity of DTU Lyngby. The tree stems are separated into cuttings of approximately 30 cm length
and 1 to 2 cm in diameter. The willow cuttings are placed in buckets with approximately 15 cuttings
in each, with tap water covering 1/3 of the bucket. To ensure that the cuttings are placed correctly
in the buckets, the buds are examined and pointing upwards. The bucket is placed in darkness in
room temperature until roots have developed, usually after 1 to 1.5 weeks. Thereafter, the cuttings
are placed in full light until they have developed at least 10 leaves, usually after 1 to 1.5 weeks (Trapp
et al., 2004).
Four days before the heavy metal solution is added is each cutting weighed and placed in Erlenmeyer
ﬂasks containing 400-500 mL of nutrient solution. All cuttings are grown in modiﬁed ISO 8692 nutrient
solution (see Appendix I). The ﬂasks are sealed with aluminium foil to prevent algae growth, and a
paraﬁlm covering the top to minimize evaporation of water. Each Erlenmeyer ﬂask together with the
cutting, is weighed and placed under artiﬁcial light (Phillips TL-D 36W) to adapt to new conditions.
The cuttings exact location and direction are marked to make sure that they are exposed to the same
light conditions throughout the entire experiment. After 3 days, each plant-ﬂask-system is weighed
again and the initial transpiration can be measured. At day 0, each plant-ﬂask-system is weighed
to determine the weight loss from the day before. This weight loss is used to rank each cutting in
terms of transpiration eﬃciency to determine which toxic concentration the di↵erent cuttings are
being exposed to. The cutting with the highest transpiration is used as a control, the second highest
transpiration is exposed to the highest toxic concentration, the third highest transpiration is given
the second highest concentration, etc. The toxic concentrations are added into the Erlenmeyer ﬂask
together with nutrient solution and the cutting. Each plant-ﬂask-system is then placed back under the
artiﬁcial light at the exact same location and direction as before. Each plant-ﬂask-system is weighed
every day for approximately 1 to 2 weeks to document the changes in transpiration. Throughout the
experiment, it is ensured that no cuttings experience water stress. At the end of the experiment, both
the plant-ﬂask-system and each cutting is weighed individually (Trapp et al., 2004).

Mixture Experiment

3.2
Cress Experiment
The cress experiment is a hydroponic phytotoxicity test with cress (Lepidium Sativum) as the test

submerged in water. After 1-1.5 weeks, the cress is grown and ready for heavy metal exposure. The

3.5
Analysis
In the following section, the main analysis approaches of the study are presented.
3.5.1
Hach Lange Spectrophotometry
Spectrophotometry is a method used to ﬁnd the concentration of a compound in solution by mea-
suring the amount of light absorbed by the sample solution. This is done by passing a beam of light
through the sample and measuring the light intensity as a function of its wavelength. The technique
is based on the principle that every compound absorbs or transmits light at di↵erent wavelengths. By
measuring the amount of light absorbed by the sample at di↵erent wavelengths, the concentration of
the compound can be identiﬁed (Hach Company, 2011).
The Hach Lange LCK-529 Cu trace test contains 20 cuvettes and has a detection range of 0.01 to 1
mg Cu/L and analyses concentration of dissolved and non-complexed Cu-ions in the samples. The kit
is used to analyse the Cu concentrations in the tightly sealed liquid samples of the willow experiment
for Cu.
2 of the 20 cuvettes are used for veriﬁcation of the stock concentrations of 5 mg Cu/L
and 1 mg Cu/L. 1 of the control water samples are analysed and the 17 remaining water samples
from the Cu willow experiment are analysed. The exposure concentrations greater than 1 mg Cu/L
(5 mg Cu/L and 2 mg Cu/L) are diluted to being within the measuring range, and the resulting
spectrophotometer reading is multiplied by the respective dilution factor. The test kit comes with
ascorbic acid which is added in each cuvette together with the sample. The acid reduces Cu(II)-ions in
complexes to Cu(I)-ions. Each cuvette contains bathocuproine disulphonic acid in a microcap which
reacts with the Cu(I)-ions in the sample, creating a orange complex. Firstly, a blank is prepared as
a control solution, and contains 0.5 mL ascorbic acid and 5 mL distilled water, as the water sample
tubidity is low. The blank is placed into the spectrophotometer where the absorbance is measured.
The absorbance from the blank will automatically be subtracted when each samples is subsequently
measured. Each cuvette is in turn prepared, containing 0.5 mL ascorbic acid and 5 mL of the sample.
The latter is injected with a ﬁlter pipette for removal of residuals. The cuvette is sealed with a lid and
mixed with the bathocuproine disulphonic acid in the microcap until complete dissolution. After 5
minutes, the cuvette is mixed again and the solution is checked for air bubbles potentially disturbing
the measurement, before it is placed in the spectrophotometer for analysis (Hach Company, 1998).
The individual cuvettes are analysed 3 times in the spectrophotometer.
3.5.2
Inductively Coupled Plasma - Mass Spectrometry
Before the solid samples can be analysed by Inductively Coupled Plasma - Mass Spectrometry (ICP-
MS), the heavy metals must be extracted by means of microwave assisted acid digestion. 4 stem-,
4 leaf- and 4 root samples from the mixture experiment, and 3 stem- and 3 root samples from the
RCF and SCF experiment, are to be analyzed by ICP-MS. The US-EPA method 3015A are used to
perform extraction of elements by microwave heating (EPA, 2007). All samples must be prepared
before they are ready for the microwave treatment. 3 replicates from each stem sample are made,
where each replicate weighs approximately 0.5 g. 2 replicates from each leaf sample are made, where
each replicate weighs approximately 0.5 g. 1 replicate from each root sample is made, weighing ap-
proximately 0.1 g. The exact weight of each replicate is registered. Each sample replicate is placed
in a teﬂon vessel, and thereafter 9 mL nitric acid (HNO3) (suprapur) 65% and 3 mL hydrochloric
acid (HCl) (suprapur) 30% is added to the vessel. Furthermore, each vessel is mixed by a Vibroﬁx
VF1 Electronic stirrer, while 0.25 mL Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) (suprapur) 30% is carefully and
22
==PAGE:24==
continuously added. Each vessel is sealed and placed in a rotor before it is placed in a Multiwave 3000
microwave oven. The vessels are heated to 175°C in 10 minutes, held at 175°C for 10 minutes before
cooled for 25 minutes. After extraction, each sample is transferred to a 50 mL vial, where Milli-Q
water is added until the sample has a total volume of 50 mL. The liquid samples are now ready for
ICP-MS analysis.
With its ability to simultaneously determine over 70 elements in less than 3 minutes, ICP-MS is one
of the most applied analytical techniques for detecting metals down to the ng/L range. Samples are
introduced to the instrument on liquid form or as dissolved solids in solution. The principle order of
the ICP-MS is that the samples are ﬁrst introduced as aerosols with argon as a carrier gas, before a
plasma dries the aerosols and dissociates the molecules into single-charged ions. The ions are advected
to a quadropole where ions of speciﬁc masses are selected and passed through as a mass ﬁlter, before
the detector registers mass signals for each ﬁngerprinted ion (Thomas, 2013).
3.5.3
Stomatal Conductance
The stomatal conductance is measured in the climate experiment for the doses 0, 0.5 and 1 mg Cu/L.
These doses are chosen to investigate the di↵erence in stomatal conductance for concentrations above
and below the EC50 value found in the individual willow cutting experiment. The SC-1 Leaf Porome-
ter is used to measure the leaves at three locations on the individual cuttings; high, intermediate and
low, and the conductance is measured in the middle of the leaf length each time. The measured leaves
are randomly selected for each measurement. An average of the three measurements is calculated,
representing the conductance for the given cutting. The stomatal conductance is measured three
times during the duration of the experiment: before the cuttings are exposed to Cu solution, two days
after exposure and at the last day of exposure.
Stomatal conductance is a measure of the rate of gas exchange and transpiration through the leaf
stomata. The SC-1 Leaf Porometer by Decagon Devices can measure stomatal conductance within the
range of 0-1000 mmol/m2s2 with a 10% accuracy. The leaf porometer contains a sensor head which
gathers information about vapour pressure and humidity in the leaf. The leaf is placed over an open
chamber in a clip in the upper part of the sensor head. The chamber contains two known conductance
elements in series which monitor the relative humidity. The bottom of the sensor head contains a
desiccant chamber which contributes to the lowering of the ambient humidity to near zero. This
ensures a steep humidity gradient between the leaf and the ambient condition, which is important to
minimize errors in the measurement. Water vapour released through the leaf stomata will travel along
the chamber to the desiccant chamber by molecular di↵usion, creating a relative humidity gradient.
When the vapour ﬂux gradient reaches steady state, the porometer will calculate the conductance
from the di↵erence in relative humidity between the sensors. A smaller di↵erence in humidity will
result in a smaller conductance, indicating that the stomatal aperture is narrowing (Decagon Devices,
2016).

3.6.7
Statistical Analysis
Statistics conducted in this study have been done in Excel by the use of the statistical tools and
data analysis programs embedded in Microsoft Excel, as well as the LN-stat software developed by
Christensen et al. (2009). The main statistics used are F-test, T-test, 95% standard errors, and linear
and log normal regressions.
The F-test is a two-tailed test which checks whether the variance between two populations are sig-
niﬁcantly di↵erent from each other. The test returns a P-value, which explains how compatible the
data set is with the null hypothesis with a 95% conﬁdence. If the P-value < 0.05, the null-hypothesis
is rejected, meaning that the two populations have signiﬁcantly di↵erent variances (Microsoft, 2019).
The result from the F-test is further used in the T-test to deﬁne if the T-test is to be performed with
equal or unequal variance. The T-test is used to determine if the mean values between two samples
are signiﬁcantly di↵erent from each other. The T-test is conducted as either one-tailed or two-tailed,
and if the samples are paired, have equal variance or unequal variance. The T-test returns a P-value
with a 95% conﬁdence, as explained above. If the P-value < 0.05, the null-hypothesis is rejected. A
one-tailed tests checks if the mean of one sample is signiﬁcantly lower than the mean of the other
sample, while a two-tailed tests checks if the means of two samples are signiﬁcantly di↵erent. In this
study, both one-tailed and a two-tailed T-tests are conducted (Microsoft, 2019).
The standard error explains the spread of values in the dataset with a given level of conﬁdence, where
the following equation constitutes the conﬁdence interval:

NRG and NRT are used as input in EC50 estimations.
The estimations are performed as linear
regression by means of Microsoft Excel data-treatment, and log-normal regressions by means of the
LN-STAT statistical program, developed by K. Ole. Kusk (DTU) for dealing with non-linear, log
normal distributed continuous response data (Christensen et al., 2009). The EC50 value estimated by
NRG was calculated for the last day of exposure, while EC50 estimated for NRT was calculated for 72
h after exposure. For NRT, EC50 is estimated when the NRT regression equals 50%, indicating 50%
inhibition. Because the inhibition of growth, as indicated by NRG, will not realistically fall below
50%, the 50% percentile of the data set is used to estimate the EC50 by linear regression in this case.
Because the LN-stat model can not be modiﬁed, LN-regression is not performed on any of the NRG
data sets.

EC50
Table 5 presents the resulting EC50 values for the 3 willow experiments based on NRT (at 72 h) and
NRG (average NRGs are included in Appendix VI). The EC50 values and their corresponding 95%
lower and upper conﬁdence limits are calculated by both a linear and a log normal regression. In

contains results from the log normal regression, the NRT and NRG response curve and the e↵ective
curve ﬁt, as well as estimated EC10 and EC20 values. In the NRT estimates the conﬁdence limits are
close to the estimated EC50, with the linear regression for the Cd experiment as an exception. The
NRG estimates are regarded as poor with highly deviating upper conﬁdence limit in the Cd experi-
ment. The EC50 value based on NRG could not be calculated for the Cu experiment as the weights
of control cuttings were not measured at the start of the experiment. The R2 values for for both
NRT and NRG indicate that linear regression does not suﬃciently address the relationship between
inhibition and exposure concentration, which is not linearly correlated. The poorest correlation is
observed between the growth inhibition (NRG) and exposure concentration. The goodness of ﬁt for
the log normal regression (NRT), suggests that the Cu experiment has the best ﬁt to log-normal
distribution.
Based on NRT, the linear regression and the log normal regression for the Cd willow experiment,
estimate a EC50 of 3.6 mg Cd/L and 3.3 mg Cd/L. This is the highest EC50 and indicates that Cd is
the least toxic to plants. The EC50 from the Cu experiment regressions are 0.33 mg Cu/L and 0.35
mg Cu/L while the mixture experiment has EC50 values of 0.28 mg Cu-Cd/L and 0.32 mg Cu-Cd/L.
Thus, the highest toxicity to plants is observed in the mixture experiment. However, the conﬁdence
limits in the log normal regression in the Cu experiment and in the mixture experiment overlap, and
thus there is no clear di↵erence in toxic e↵ect between the two. Based on NRG, the linear regression
estimate more similar EC50 values between the experiments. The highest EC50 value is estimated in
the Cd experiment, to 0.71 mg Cd/L compared to 0.62 mg Cu-Cd/L in the mixture experiment, but
the R2 is low in both cases, indicating a poor regression ﬁt and high uncertainty.

Climate Chamber Experiment
The following section presents the results from four experiments, where willow cuttings are exposed
to Cu in combination with either elevated temperature, elevated CO2 or combined treatment with
elevation of both variables.

Climate Chamber Monitoring Data
Each climate chamber is programmed to contain a range of parameters, presented in Table 2, to
achieve consistent conditions throughout the experiment.
Each parameter is measured to control
the actual exposure in each chamber during the experiment. The actual exposure measurements are
retrieved from the RERAF database and are presented in Figure 10. The CO2 concentrations are
measured by two di↵erent sensors, the WMA sensor and m˚aler 2 sensor. The WMA sensor is regarded
the most reliable sensor of the two and the actual exposure will therefore be analysed from this sensors
data. The Ambient and "T chambers have CO2 levels above the 410 ppm target, where the CO2

the same in both chambers, is this elevated level not considered problematic for the experiment. The

another chamber with more stable conditions at 24 h. The CO2 level is thereafter seen to be stable at

5
Discussion
In the discussion chapter, the phytotoxicity indicators transpiration and growth are evaluated, as
well as a overall visual assessment to back up the ﬁndings. Furthermore, the important ﬁndings are
interpreted and compared with previously conducted research, while limitations and implications of
the study are presented and discussed.
5.1
Willow Experiment
Single metal toxicity of Cu and Cd to animals and plants is widely studied and useful in determination
of the level of tolerance. However, heavy metal contamination rarely occurs by presence of single
metals, and multiple metal exposure is therefore more relevant regarding toxicity. Cu is chosen due
to its essential function in plant development at low concentrations, while Cd is chosen as element
without any positive e↵ect on plant metabolism. The central objective of the willow experiments is
therefore to compare the phytotoxic e↵ects when willows are exposed to Cu and Cd separately and
as a mixture. Changes in growth and transpiration are the main indicators used in the assessment of
toxicity.
5.1.1
E↵ects on Phytotoxicity Indicators
There is no signiﬁcant di↵erence between the Cu and mixture experiment for either the NRT, growth
rate or WUE indicators, when comparing exposure concentrations (two-tailed t-test).
Contrarily,
growth and WUE for the Cd experiment are both signiﬁcantly di↵erent (two-tailed, t-test) from the
Cu experiment and mixture experiment, and Cd exposed willow cuttings are less negatively a↵ected.
In regards to NRT, there is no signiﬁcant di↵erence (one-tailed, t-test) between the highest and low-
est exposure concentrations between the Cd and mixture experiment. Cuttings exposed to 0.2 mg/L
dose are una↵ected in both experiments, while the 5 mg/L concentration is lethal in both cases. The
accumulated transpiration of the Cd experiment is signiﬁcantly higher (one-tailed, t-test) than both
the Cu and mixture experiment, which plausibly indicates that more Cd is taken up with the transpi-
ration stream, but plant protection mechanisms are as well present and eﬃcient. A main limitation
to proving this statement, is that single Cd concentrations have not been analysed. Nevertheless, the
phytotoxicity of Cd to willows is signiﬁcantly lower than both Cu and the mixture of Cu and Cd.
Cd and Cu toxicity induces growth inhibition and chlorosis in plants (Mediouni et al., 2006). Toxicity
symptoms as chlorosis and necrosis due to excess metal exposure, is observed in the Cu experiment
for the willow cuttings exposed to 1, 2 and 5 mg Cu/L, and the same symptoms are observed on
cuttings in the mixture experiment. Leaf chlorosis is only observed for cuttings exposed to 5 mg
Cd/L in the Cd willow experiment. Root elongation and mass are observed to be clearly reduced,
and the root color is brown, for the same concentrations in the Cu and mixture experiments. Figure
14 shows the di↵erence between the roots of a control willow cutting and a cutting exposed to 5 mg
Cu-Cd/L in the mixture experiment, which are clearly di↵erent both in root mass and color. In the
Cd experiment, the root mass is reduced for cuttings exposed to 5 mg Cd/L, however, no prominent
root discoloration is observed.
A study conducted by Wong and Bradshaw (1982), researching the toxicity of heavy metals to rye
grass, found that Cu had a more severe inhibition of root growth compared to Cd, in accordance
with the ﬁndings in this study. Another research conducted by Mediouni et al. (2006), studied Cd
and Cu toxicity on tomato seedlings, and found Cd to be more inhibiting to growth at an exposure
50
==PAGE:52==
concentration of 0.1 mg Cd/L, compared to the same concentration of Cu, indicating higher toxicity of
Cd at lower concentrations. However, at high concentrations, Cu was more toxic than Cd. The impact
on growth in our study, shows that Cu has a higher toxic e↵ect on average growth compared to the
same concentrations of Cd, as lower growth rates are observed in the Cu experiment. This insinuates
that the studied concentrations of Cu and Cd have been too high to achieve a conclusion similar to
Mediouni et al. (2006), or that Cd toxicity is highly species dependent. The di↵erence in toxicity
between the two heavy metals could be explained by the fact that Cd is a non-essential element for
growth and the plant may therefore exclude the metal to minimize its toxic e↵ects (Mediouni et al.,
2006). Conclusively, the toxic e↵ects present in the mixture experiment are mainly due to Cu and
not Cd toxicity, which is consistently supported by the NRT, accumulated transpiration, growth and
WUE indicators, as well as the visual assessment.

5.1.2
EC50 Interpretation
As the EC50 estimations based on NRG are considered too uncertain, only NRT based regressions
are discussed. The most prominent toxic e↵ect to willow cuttings is found in the mixture experiment
with a EC50 value of 0.28 and 0.32 mg Cu-Cd/L at 72 h, calculated by linear and LN-regression,
respectively. The Cu experiment shows similar toxicity as the mixture experiment, with EC50 of
0.33 and 0.35 mg Cu/L. The 95% conﬁdence intervals were observed to overlap for the calculated
EC50 values in the Cu and mixture experiment. This expresses similar toxicity between Cu and the
mixture, while no synergistically accelerated phytotoxic e↵ects between Cu and Cd are proven. Cd is
the least toxic to willows, with EC50 values of 3.6 and 3.3 mg Cd/L obtained by respectively linear
and LN-regression. The EC50 of the mixture is ten times greater than the single Cd toxicity.
Clausen and Trapp (2017) report a EC50 value of 1 mg Cu-Cd/L after 240 h of an equivalent Cu-Cd
mixture experiment, which is higher than the EC50 obtained in our study.
The same study also
51
==PAGE:53==
reported EC50 values for single toxicity of Cu, with values of 0.9 and 0.4 mg Cu/L after 72 h, and
1 mg Cu/L after 240 h (Clausen and Trapp, 2017), which are generally higher than the ﬁndings of
this study. Lastly, Clausen and Trapp (2017) found a EC50 value of 1.5 mg Cd/L after 240 h when
evaluating single Cd toxicity. The di↵erence between the EC50 values found in our study compared to
this research, may be due to non-corresponding environmental conditions, including light levels, CO2
concentrations, nutrient supply and humidity. Other research shows the same tendency as observed
in this study, where Cu-Cd mixture and Cu exposure exhibit comparable toxicities, while Cd is less
phytotoxic than Cu at comparable concentration ranges (Hakmaoui et al., 2007) (Mwamba et al.,
2016).
5.1.3
Uptake Interpretation
Cu and Cd uptake, accumulation and distribution in the di↵erent plant compartments is investigated
to obtain a better understanding of their phytotoxicity to willows. The Cu and mixture experiments
are included in the uptake analysis. Understanding the heavy metal distribution and accumulation
in the plant biomass is also essential for assessing bioremediation potential and when assessing the
potential risk they constitute of being introduced to the food chain.
The translocation patterns shown in Figure 6 indicate that the Cu and Cd concentrations both are in
the order of root > leaf > stem. Higher accumulation of Cu and Cd in roots is due to the roots being
in direct contact with the solution. Furthermore, roots are also considered barriers of heavy metal
translocation to protect several physiological and metabolic processes in the plants, which explains
why heavy metals are accumulating here (Mediouni et al., 2006). While the stem concentrations are
dependant on dosage, the leaf concentrations indicate that translocation is dosage independent. The
translocation to the leaves is more eﬃcient for Cu than for Cd, an important quality to assess for
phytoremediation e↵orts. The R/W ratios in Table 8 show that more Cu is taken up than Cd, and
that while the root uptake of Cu increases with increasing exposure, the Cd root uptake decreases.
Figure 7, showing the mass distribution (%) between solution, roots, stem and leaves illustrates that
the uptake patterns of Cu and Cd are similar. Only a small fraction is present in the water at the
end of the experiment, and while the 0.2 mg Cu-Cd/L dose has the highest fractions of Cu and Cd
in the stem, the 1 mg Cu-Cd/L doses have the highest fractions of Cu and Cd in the roots.
Previous research shows large variations of Cd translocation to leaves between species, indicated by
the R/L ratio. Gramineae (maize, barley, oat, ryegrass, cocksfoot, rice) as well as Leguminosae (peas,
beans, etc.) are plants which often have a low translocation of Cd, with R/L between 30 and 150.
Oppositely, species as tomatoes, potatoes, lettuce and cabbage have a large translocation of Cd from
roots to shoots with associated R/L ratios < 10 (Mclaughlin et al., 2011). The calculated R/L ratios
for Cd in the mixture willow experiment are 74 to 416, similar to the general translocation pattern in
Gramineae and Leguminosae plants. The R/L value is increasing with increasing exposure concentra-
tion of Cd, indicating that high exposure concentration leads to an inhibited translocation ability to
the leaves. Zacchini et al. (2009) found a R/L of approximately 660 for willows hydroponically exposed
to 5.6 mg Cd/L, which is in line with the increasing R/L for Cd with concentration in this study.
The same translocation tendency applies for the stem, where a lower concentration is observed with
increased exposure. These ﬁndings contradict other studies, where the root and shoot concentrations
increased proportionally to the Cd concentration in the exposure solution (Hakmaoui et al., 2007)
(Mclaughlin et al., 2011). Typical translocation patterns for essential metals is higher translocation
52
==PAGE:54==
with lower external concentration (Mclaughlin et al., 2011). The results in Table 8 are in line with
this statement, where the R/L value for Cu is smaller at the 0.2 mg/L exposure concentration than
1 mg/L, indicating that a higher fraction of Cu is translocated to the leaves at 0.2 mg Cu-Cd/L than
at 1 mg Cu-Cd/L exposure. This indicates phytotoxic conditions for the willow cuttings at 1 mg
Cu-Cd/L, resulting in a restricted translocation from the roots to the leaves. Limited translocation
in plants has also been shown in previous research, and is described as a possible defence strategy by
the plant, to avoid shoot damage (Hakmaoui et al., 2007). The Cu concentration in the stem is seen
to increase with increasing exposure concentration. However, when comparing the Cu concentration
in the stem with the Cu concentration in the roots, a smaller fraction of Cu is translocated to the
stem at 1 mg Cu/L compared to 0.2 mg Cu/L, indicating that the willow trees are also limiting the
translocation of Cu to the stem with increasing exposure concentration. In the study by Adrees et al.
(2015), the Cu concentration in rapeseed was found to increase in both leaves and roots with exposure
concentrations of 3 to 10 mg Cu/L. Furthermore, the shoot concentration in maize and indian mustard
also increased from 58 to 82 mg/kgdw and 80 to 88 mg/kgdw with increased exposure concentrations
from 0.63 to 3 mg Cu/L, respectively. However, the Cu concentration in the plant tissue did not
increase linearly with elevated Cu concentration in solution (Adrees et al., 2015).
The results from the analysed solution after the Cu experiment, show that an average of 49% of the
Cu is recovered in the 0.2 mg Cu/L samples, while the cuttings exposed to 0.5, 1, 2 and 5 mg Cu/L,
have respectively 89%, 92%, 92% and 91% recovery (see Table 6). The recovered fraction in the 0.2 mg
Cu/L concentration group is associated with high internal variability, as emphasised in Section 4.1.4,
which is a plausible explanation to its deviation from the other concentrations. It is evident that the
standard error between cuttings of the 0.2 mg Cu/L group is high. This inter-cutting variability may
be an e↵ect of negligible inhibition, and thus the individual cuttings behaving normally according to
their initial ranking. Another explanation is that plant uptake and translocation of essential metals,
like Cu, is highly dependant on the Cu concentration in the external solution. When a plant is ex-
posed to elevated metal concentrations, the control mechanisms for ion uptake and translocation will
break down, and the Cu concentration in the plant biomass will increase with increasing exposure as a
consequence (Mclaughlin et al., 2011). Thus, the control mechanisms are regulating ion uptake more
eﬃciently at 0.2 mg Cu/L, while concentrations ≥0.5 mg Cu/L are exceeding the regulation threshold,
but remains at ⇡90 % uptake for all doses. Thus, recovery eﬃciency is not concentration depen-
dant at the evaluated doses. The uptake analysis of the Cu experiment is limited to initial and ﬁnal
solution concentrations, and no actual uptake concentrations in the plant compartments are provided.
As both the Cu and mixture experiment solution concentrations have been analysed, the e↵ects the
mixture conditions have on Cu uptake can be evaluated. Landberg and Greger (2002) state in their
research that total Cu uptake and root accumulation of Cu decreases in the presence of Cd (and
Zn) in willow clones with high Cu accumulative properties.
Low-accumulating clones showed no
signiﬁcant decrease in Cu uptake in the presence of Cd (and Zn). Thus, if the willow cuttings used
are highly accumulative, it is expected that the Cu uptake is higher for cuttings only exposed to Cu
compared to both heavy metals. Oppositely, if the cutting has low Cu accumulative properties, the
Cu uptake is higher in the mixture experiment. The total Cu uptake in both the Cu experiment
and the mixture experiment is increasing with increasing exposure. The average recovery of Cu in
the 0.2 mg/L exposure regime is 49(±66)% for the Cu experiment and 63(±27)% for the mixture
experiment. In the 1 mg/L exposure regime, the recovery is 92(±0.6)% in the Cu experiment and
89(±1.7)% in the mixture experiment. The standard error of the recovery is high in the 0.2 mg/L
53
==PAGE:55==
dose, as the cuttings are not strongly inhibited and exhibit normal uptake behaviour. However, the
average recovery in the mixture experiment is higher than the Cu experiment, implying that the
cuttings may be characterized by low-accumulative properties (Landberg and Greger, 2002). In the
1 mg/L dose, the recoveries are not signiﬁcantly di↵erent (two-tailed t-test) and have low standard
errors. They are acting uniformly. Based on the analysed ﬁnal solution concentrations, the presence
of Cd in solution has not a↵ected the total plant uptake of Cu. Because the plant compartment
concentrations have not been analysed in the single Cu experiment, the in-plant distribution can not
be compared, which limits further insight in translocation di↵erences between the single compound
exposure and the mixture exposure.
However, a hydroponic study by Mwamba et al. (2016) on
mixture toxicity between Cu and Cd in rapeseed found a decreasing bioaccumulation of Cd in the
shoots and roots in the presence of Cu. The Cd content in the tissue decreased further with elevated
Cu concentration in solution, suggesting that Cd was outcompeted by Cu for binding on the root
surface when a higher concentration of metal was present (Mwamba et al., 2016). These ﬁndings
show similar tendencies as this study, with decreasing Cd concentrations in the stem and leaves with
elevated exposure. Mwamba et al. (2016) also found Cd to enhance uptake and bioaccumulation of
Cu in the plant tissue of rapeseed, when exposed to 50µM Cd and Cu, but not at 200µM exposure
concentration, compared to single Cu exposure. In our study, higher average recovery of Cu is found
in the mixture experiment compared to single Cu exposure, indicating that Cd has contributed to an
increased Cu uptake, in accordance to the ﬁndings in Mwamba et al. (2016). Furthermore, Mwamba
et al. (2016) found the Cu root-to-shoot translocation rate to increase with elevated exposure, in
compliance with the ﬁndings in this study. The mixture conditions did not a↵ect the root-to-shoot
translocation rate in rapeseed, compared to single metal exposure (Mwamba et al., 2016). The e↵ect
mixture metal exposure have on root-to-shoot translocation can not be quantiﬁed in this study as the
translocation of Cu and Cd are not investigated for single metal exposure.
5.1.4
Limitations, Implications and Recommendations
Plants, humans and the ecosystems as a whole are continually exposed to complex and dynamically
changing mixtures of metals. Contrary to most studies, the willow cuttings in this study are not only
exposed to the contaminants individually, but they are also exposed to a mixture of the metals Cu
and Cd. This creates grounds for comparison of toxicity and uptake responses, in an e↵ort to achieve
insight in interference and combined e↵ects of two known phytotoxic metals.
The willow tree toxicity experiment is done hydroponically and not in soil solution, which would
better resemble natural conditions. The hydroponic test is considered a worst case scenario with un-
restricted bioavailability compared to soil experiments, having a large inﬂuence on the heavy metals
available for uptake (Clausen and Trapp, 2017). The fate of heavy metals in soil is highly dependant
on the soil characteristics in terms of solubility, organic matter content, and formation of complexes,
which are not accounted for in the hydroponic experiment. Furthermore, pH measurements are not
included in the experiment, but will have a large inﬂuence on the solubility and availability of Cu
and Cd in solution. While Cu becomes readily available at low pH, Cd is less available at low pH.
The toxicity of Cu and Cd to willow trees is therefore expected to be pH dependant. By including
pH measurements, one could better understand the inﬂuence pH has on the level of Cu and Cd toxicity.
A plants phytoextraction eﬃciency depends largely on the contaminant present in soil, and are best
suited for remediation purposes when the pollutants exist at relatively low concentrations (Ghosh and
Singh, 2005). The combination of high R/W and low R/L reduces damage to the root physiology and
54
==PAGE:56==
biochemistry, which improves the e↵ectiveness of the plant metal uptake, and are therefore important
indicators when assessing a plants phytoremediation abilities (Zacchini et al., 2009). The high R/W
values found in the uptake experiment show that willow cuttings are eﬃciently taking up Cu and Cd
through the roots, which is a crucial property when phytoremediating polluted sites. However, the
R/W for Cd decreases with increased exposure, implying that the Cd remediation eﬃciency is lower
at the high Cd concentration. The R/L for both Cu and Cd are increasing with increased exposure,
meaning that translocation abilites of the willows is decreasing. At an exposure concentrations of 1
mg Cu-Cd/L, the function of willows as phytoremediators of Cu and Cd is low.
In the agricultural setting and for edible plants, low R/W and high R/L ratios are preferred for the
prevention of accumulation in roots and more importantly, avoidance of translocation to edible leafs
and fruits. This will limit uptake further up the food chain. In a world where agricultural production
is increasingly depending on recycled water for irrigation and sludge application for nutrients, heavy
metal accumulation and translocation is of increasing concern, as it a↵ects food safety and crop
growth. Willow trees are not agricultural plants and heavy metal uptake is therefore not considered
to pose a great risk for further accumulation up the food chain with humans involved. However, if an
agricultural plant exhibits the same uptake, accumulation and translocation properties as willows, it
would be easier to evaluate the risk. It is proven that willows do show similar tendencies as numerous
edible plants, including rapeseed and Gramineae species. The cuttings are less e↵ective translocators
of Cu from roots to leaves at high exposure concentrations, and overall less e↵ective translocators of
Cd from the roots to the stem, with increased exposure concentration. If the same tendency holds
for agricultural plants, it indicates that the risk of Cd accumulation up the food chain is decreasing
with elevated external concentration, while Cu accumulation stabilizes. This implies that the risk of
Cu exposure through the food chain is higher compared to Cd exposure, this is beneﬁcial due to Cd
being very toxic to humans.
5.2
Cress Experiment
Cress is commonly consumed by both humans and animals. Potential heavy metal contamination
taken up by the plant may therefore pose a direct risk for further bioaccumulation through the food
chain. Thus, cress has the potential to function as a representative bioindicator for phytotoxicity in
risk assessment, where assessing cress sensitivity to heavy metal contamination, as well as the level of
exposure where phytotoxic e↵ects occur, is of high relevance. In this study, the single metal toxicities
of Cu and Cd to cress are investigated.
5.2.1
E↵ects on Phytotoxicity Indicators

5.2.2
EC50 Interpretation
For the cress experiment, the NRG based EC50 is not included in the discussion, as the R2 is only
0.30. The 72 h NRT based EC50 for Cu on cress is 10 mg Cu/L and 7.5 mg Cu/L, for the linear and
LN-regression, respectively (see Table 11). This result is in line with the ﬁndings for Montvydiene and
Marˇciulioniene (2004), which reports a cress 2-day EC50 for Cu of 7.6 mg Cu/L, based on root length
decrease. The found EC50 value for cress is generally very high compared to other species. Wong and
Bradshaw (1982) report a 14-day EC50 of 0.02 mg Cu/L for Lolium Pererene in a root elongation test,
while Fargasova (1998) reports a 11-day value of 4.3 mg Cu/L and Wang (1987) report 4-day values
of 2.4, 2.8 and 2.8 mg Cu/L for millet, cucumber and lettuce respectively. A germination experiment
by Pokorska-Niewiada et al. (2018) ranks the LC50 of di↵erent species in the order of rapeseed <
vetch < pea < barley < wheat < oat < rye < carrot < cress. For willows, the EC50 is found to be
0.33 or 0.35, as stated in Table 5, so cress EC50 for Cu is clearly larger than this, making cress more
robust and tolerant. Conclusively, the relatively high EC50 for Cu stated in our study is supported
by previous research, and cress is not sensitive to Cu exposure compared a range of other species.
No trends in the relationship between transpiration and concentrations are deducible for the Cd
experiment due to the low concentration dosages, and consequently, no EC50 is obtained in this
study, despite the standard dosages being increased compared to all remaining experiments. However,
Montvydiene and Marˇciulioniene (2004) report a 2-day EC50 of 26.5 mg Cd/L and Gianazza et al.
(2007) reports an inhibition of 50% for a concentration of 33.7 mg Cd/L, based on growth inhibition
of cress. Wong and Bradshaw (1982) report a EC50 of 1.8 mg Cd/L for Lolium Perenne, which again
is underlining the robustness of cress. Finally, a LC50 of Cd based on germination is ranking the
investigated species in the order of vetch < pea < barley < oat < carrot < rapeseed < wheat < cress
< rye, where only rye exceeds cress in terms of Cd induced lethality (Pokorska-Niewiada et al., 2018).
5.2.3
Evaluation of Cress Experimental Method
As opposed to the willow experimental set-up, which has been validated and proven functional and
reliable in several previous experiments, the corresponding cress transpiration test set-up with germi-
nation boxes is novel and, consequently, not standardized. Therefore, the cress experiment conducted
in this study comes with a range of uncertainties.
Firstly, due to the discussed low heavy metal
sensitivity of cress, the concentrations used in the experiment do not represent the full range of toxic
57
==PAGE:59==
e↵ects indicated by transpiration, where the NRT never falls below 50% in either the Cu or the Cd
experiment. The concentrations required for Cd were evaluated to being too high for safe laboratory
practise in the circumstances of the study. To compensate for the lack of representative dose-response
results, the visual assessment becomes crucial and an inevitable part of the analysis, as especially the
exposed roots were showing visible e↵ects.
A crucial di↵erence between the willow and the cress test is that in the cress test, seeds are used.
The seeds are naturally equipped with all nutrients necessary for the cress germination process, while
the willow cuttings are depending on the nutrient solution from the beginning - an implication which
might inﬂuence the uptake. Another uncertainty associated with the cress seeds, is that the amount
is determined on a weight basis and not by counting, which would ensure that the same exact amount
of seeds is present in all germination trays. This could lead to signiﬁcantly more seeds being initially
present in some of the germination trays. Furthermore, not all seeds were germinating in the prepara-
tion growth phase. In contrast to the willow experiment, which is conducted on an individual cutting
basis, the cress experiment batches comprise a community of individual cress shoots with associated
canopies, which makes the experiments di↵er. As visualized in Figure 8, the initial transpiration rate
at 0 h is uniform for all germination trays in both the Cu and Cd experiment. This creates an equi-
table starting point, which eliminates or strongly reduces the uncertainty related to di↵erent initial
transpiration eﬃciencies. In the willow experiment, this initial di↵erence in transpiration eﬃciencies is
prominent in several of the conducted willow experiments, interfering with the grounds for comparison.
The use of the gridded germination trays makes the experiment an open-system, which generates chal-
lenges in quantifying the true evaporative water losses and preventing loss of volatile contaminants.
Thus, using transpiration as an indicator for toxicity is considered an uncertain parameter due to the
evapotranspiration-related uncertainty from the open system experiment. Blanks were included to
measure the evaporative losses from the trays, where sesame seeds were distributed on the trays to
mimic the cress seed area. This method constitutes a great uncertainty when evaluating the evapo-
ration from the trays, as the sesame seeds distribution area does not necessarily represent the trays
with full-grown cress. The assumed evaporation from the blanks is therefore possibly overestimated.
As mentioned, the use of transpiration as an indicator has proven to be uncertain due to non-
quantiﬁable, but signiﬁcant evaporative losses. Often, cress tests are conducted with root or shoot
elongation as an indicator or as germination experiments, which from literature review appears to be
more prominent, and should therefore be considered included in future equivalent cress experiments.
The growth is estimated on a wet weight basis, and because each germination tray has a great mag-
nitude of roots retaining water, it is challenging to standardize the method so comparable amounts
of external water is removed before weighing.
5.2.4
Limitations, Implications and Recommendations
Overall, the cress test is inexpensive and non-complex, making it a suitable addition to transpiration
experiments for assessing the phytotoxcity of non-volatile compounds, but further method standard-
ization and addition of root elongation as an indicator is recommended. It is also recommended to
conduct parallel germination experiments in the future, to validate the test results by capturing and
comparing the e↵ects on non-sprouted seeds to the method used in our experiment.

==PAGE:60==
Adding another test plant to the transpiration based phytotoxicity experiments has been proven to be
important, as the sensitivity to Cu and Cd is highly species dependant, which may have a substantial
impact on the conclusions of phytotoxicity tests. It is stated that cress is very robust and less sensitive
to heavy metal exposure than a range of other species. This conclusion is also true when comparing
willow and cress, as the results from this study indicate that willows have a signiﬁcantly higher
sensitivity to both Cu and Cd. This di↵erence is important to consider when choosing plants for use
as bioindicators in assessing e.g. the sensitivity to sewage sludge and wastewater land application
in the agricultural setting. It is therefore recommended to use a minimum of three species when
conducting phytotoxic risk assessments to ensure a coverage of the range of plant responses, whereof
the willow and cress transpiration experiments capture a wide range of species speciﬁc e↵ects.
5.3
Climate Chamber Experiment
As concluded in Section 5.1, Cu is signiﬁcantly more toxic to willows than Cd. Therefore, the e↵ect
climate change has on Cu phytotoxicity to willows has been assessed in this study. Temperature and
CO2 are among the environmental parameters relevant for the assessment of climate e↵ects on plants,
as they inﬂuence uptake and fate of heavy metals.
High temperatures are generally expected to
stimulate the plant physiological processes such as transpiration and metabolism, while heat stress is
related to decreasing growth rates. Potential transpiration is proportional to vapor pressure of water,
and with increasing temperatures, the vapor pressure deﬁcit should increase exponentially. Because an
increasing atmospheric CO2 concentration will cause a larger di↵erence in CO2 concentration across
the stomatal aperture, a plant can take up CO2 at the same rate, but with a smaller stomatal aperture
and lower stomatal conductance, which decreases transpiration losses and hence increases WUE while
the growth is boosted (˚Agren and Andersson, 2011).
5.3.1
E↵ects on Phytotoxicity Indicators
Warming alone ("T chamber) reduces the average growth (Table 13) and weight change (Table 14)
signiﬁcantly compared to Ambient conditions (one-tailed t-test). Several mechanisms contribute to
this reduction, as heat-stress increases the leaf-to-air water vapor pressure deﬁcit and increases dark
respiration rates, which increases transpiration and decreases water use eﬃciency (Wang et al., 2015).
The NRT in the Ambient chamber is signiﬁcantly lower than in the "T chamber for the 0.2, 0.5 and 1
mg Cu/L doses (one-tailed t-test). Indeed, the accumulated transpiration in the "T chamber is higher
than in the chambers not exposed to elevated temperatures (see Table 15) and the WUE is lower (see
Table 16) for all doses except the controls, which are probably outliers due to the high individual
variation in the control group (as indicated by the broad 95% conﬁdence intervals in Figure 15). The
leaf-to-air water vapor pressure deﬁcit decrease should lead to increased stomatal conductance in the
"T chamber compared to the Ambient chamber (Ruci´nska-Sobkowiak, 2016), but only the 1 mg Cu/L
dose at day 0 (initial exposure day) and day 2 (after 48 h) in Figure 13 exhibit signiﬁcantly higher
stomatal conductance in the "T chamber compared to the Ambient chamber (one-tailed, t-test). This
misrepresentation is likely due to the high uncertainty associated with the stomatal conductance mea-
surements and the very strong individual cuttings present in the control groups causing high internal
variation. Accelerated plant development, shortened phenological stages and decreasing photosynthe-
sis also potentially contribute to the observed reduced growth, but have not been used as indicators
in this study. The toxic e↵ect of Cu in the "T chamber is evident between the 0 mg Cu/L and the 2

5.3.2
EC50 Interpretation
EC50 is calculated based on the normalized parameters for transpiration rates (NRT) and growth rates
(NRG), which in combination make up the WUE (both shown in Table 17). The EC50 estimation
proves to be sensitive to the parameter chosen. Based on NRT, the order from low to high EC50 is
Ambient < "CO2 < "T < "T and CO2, while based on NRG, the order is Ambient < "T < "CO2 <
"T and CO2. In both cases, the positive combined e↵ects of temperature and CO2 ensures that the
combined treatment has the overall highest EC50. The Ambient conditions are associated with the
highest phytotoxic e↵ects of Cu exposure, as EC50 is the lowest in the Ambient chamber under both
estimation approaches. Finally, the order of the individual "T and "CO2 treatments switches between
the two EC50 approaches. When using NRT, the "T chamber is less toxic because transpiration in-
creases under elevated temperature conditions, and decreases under elevated CO2 conditions. When
utilizing NRG, the growth rate being stimulated under elevated CO2 conditions becomes the domi-
nating feature which makes "CO2 more robust. The strong positive e↵ect of temperature increase on
transpiration potentially overshadows the more modest negative transpiration e↵ect of contaminant
exposure. Meanwhile, the same positive e↵ect of elevated CO2 on growth inﬂuences the conclusions
when using NRT. Therefore, basing EC50 estimations on NRT alone when assessing climate e↵ects
may lead to misleading conclusions. Therefore, adding NRG as a second estimation parameter is
recommended for toxicity assessment in climate experiments.
Conclusively, the study has shown that the combined e↵ects of temperature elevation from 19°C to
30°C and CO2 elevation from 410 ppm to 700 ppm leads to decreasing toxicity of Cu to willows when
using growth and transpiration rates as indicators, which is also supported by the overall signiﬁcantly
higher WUE in the combined chamber, especially for the high exposure concentrations of Cu.
5.3.3
Limitations, Implications and Recommendations
The IPCC state with high conﬁdence that the ﬁndings of climate change e↵ects on crop production
in food, nutrition security and food production systems leads to a greater reduction in global crop
yields under a 2°C increase scenario compared to a 1.5°C increase scenario. Their conclusion is based
on studies on the e↵ects of changes in temperature, precipitation, atmospheric CO2 concentrations
and extreme weather events (Hoegh-Guldberg et al., 2018). The ﬁndings of our study contradict the
IPCC statement, whereas our study indicates a higher productivity under combined elevated CO2
and temperature conditions for willows.
These ﬁndings are remarkable and have the potential to contribute to the public discussion of climate
change induced e↵ects on plant productivity and plant toxicity as an ultimate consequence, for ex-
62
==PAGE:64==
ample in the ﬁeld of crop and food safety in combination with challenges connected with current and
projected future water scarcity. The ﬁndings show that the willows have a higher WUE in a scenario
of elevated temperatures and CO2 conditions, which has the potential to lead to lower agricultural
water consumption in the future. Especially if this conclusion is representative for agricultural plants,
the controversial irrigation related water consumption may decrease in the future. The inter-specie
variation in response to elevated CO2 concentrations has been addressed in Ainsworth and Long
(2005), where it is emphasised that functional types and species response to CO2 varies. It is stated
that young cuttings are more responsive to elevated CO2 than older cuttings, herbaceous species,
grasses, legumes and crops, in terms of e.g. dry matter production and the degree of photosynthetic
acclimation.
Another implication of current and projected water scarcity is the focus on increased reuse of poten-
tially contaminated water sources. The results of this study ﬁnd that the combined e↵ects of increased
temperature and CO2 leads to detoxiﬁcation of Cu to willows compared to ambient conditions, and
again, if this conclusion is representative for a range of both plants and heavy metals, the issue of
heavy metal contamination is less pressing under future climate change scenarios. The ﬁeld of phy-
toremediation also beneﬁts from this knowledge, as potential clean-up will be accelerated at higher
temperatures as the translocation rate increases parallel to the transpiration stream, leading to a
higher accumulation of contaminants (Clausen and Trapp, 2017). The increased growth and uptake
of chemicals due to climate change reduces the time it takes to clean up a contaminated site.
The atmospheric CO2 concentrations of 410 ppm and 700 ppm and temperatures of 19°C and 30°C
used in this study are not representative for all future scenarios of climate change, as the combination
depends both on geographical location and global socio-economical development.
Therefore, it is
recommended that related experiments and modelling e↵orts are conducted for a range of temperature
and CO2 combinations, in correspondence to the IPCC projecting climate change through a range
of representative concentration pathways and likely scenarios.
Other environmental aspects, like
humidity, light conditions, diurnal variation, nutrient supply and wind are also likely to a↵ect the
plant response to heavy metals and are obviously critical in the assessment and interpretation of
e↵ects of increased atmospheric CO2. Therefore, the impacts of temperature and CO2 interactions
should not be considered in isolation. The positive synergistic e↵ect of temperature and CO2 on plant
biomass production and toxicity may have a threshold depending on the degree of both pollution,
temperature and CO2 increase. As concluded by Ainsworth and Long (2005), the extent to which
rising CO2 corresponds to rising average temperature will determine whether the yield stimulation is
signiﬁcant. A ﬁnal recommendation is to combine the experiments with uptake analysis, to assess the
temperature and CO2 e↵ects on heavy metal uptake which is also closely related to phytotoxicity.
5.4
RCF and SCF
Soil-to-plant transfer is one of the major pathways for pollutants to enter the food chain.
The
motivation for the estimation of RCF and SCF based on hydroponic experiments is to determine
the equilibrium partitioning between the liquid phase and the roots and submerged stem fractions.
Because di↵usion processes consistently occur in the direction towards higher entropy, i.e equilibrium
condition, the metals will partition from the liquid phase to the roots and stem fractions in direct
contact with the solution, when the initial concentrations in the biomass is low (Zhang et al., 2008).
Realistic quantiﬁcation of the RCF and SCF thereby contributes in the empirical estimation of di↵usive
plant uptake through direct contact with the solution, being the starting point for further translocation
of heavy metals to the remaining plant compartments. The further translocation depends on the
63
==PAGE:65==
passive uptake quantiﬁed by RCF and SCF, plant defence and tolerance mechanisms, xylem water
ﬂuxes and the masses of the respective compartments, like leaves or fruits (Mclaughlin et al., 2011).
This knowledge is crucial when modelling and assessing the risk associated with Cu and Cd exposure
to plants, humans and the ecosystems.
5.4.1
Equilibrium Passive Di↵usive Uptake Evaluation
Calculated RCFCu range between 14676 L/kgdw and 194297 L/kgdw while RCFCd range between
19696 L/kgdw and 249274 L/kgdw, as presented in Table 19. Consistently, RCFCu < RCFCd when
comparing the two doses internally, indicating that the passive di↵usion from solution to roots is the
highest for Cd exposed roots. The RCFs for the 1 mg/L dose are larger than the RCFs for the 0.2
mg/L concentration, so the passive di↵usion to roots is increasing with increasing exposure concen-
tration. Because the roots have a high contact surface with the solution they are more susceptible
to uptake than the stem cuttings. Thus, the passive di↵usion to the stems, as indicated by SCF in
Table 21, is two to three orders of magnitude lower than the di↵usion to roots, as indicated by RCF.
However, the stem fractions weigh more than the roots, and therefore the accumulated mass uptake
of the metals may nonetheless be higher in the stem fractions than in the roots. The SCFCu ranges
between 332 L/kgdw and 2482 L/kgdw, while the SCFCd range between 528 L/kgdw and 6372 L/kgdw.
Opposite to RCF, the SCF decreases with increasing exposure, meaning that in the stem case, the
di↵usion to the exposed fraction of the stem is higher at the 0.2 mg/L exposure concentration than
the 1 mg/L exposure. Paterson et al. (1990) state that Briggs et. al. (1984) assumed that the con-
tribution of the aqueous phase to uptake by the stem (SCF) was similar to that in the root (RCF),
which has been proven to not be the case in this experiment.
The RCF calculation based on the average solution concentration can be compared to the R/W ratios
from the willow mixture experiment seen in Table 8. While the R/W ratios include both passive
and active uptake, as well as translocated amounts, the RCF only represent the passive di↵usive
equilibrium uptake. The RCF and R/W value for Cu in the two experiments show comparable values
with a R/W of 23151 and RCFCu of 24693 when exposed to 0.2 mg Cu-Cd/L, and a R/W of 33379
and RCFCu of 36419 when exposed to 1 mg Cu-Cd/L, indicating similar ratios between the root and
exposure concentration in solution in the two experiments. R/W is slightly lower, indicating that
a certain amount of Cu is being removed from the roots. On the other hand, the RCFCd is much
higher than the R/W value in the mixture experiment, with a R/W of 19738 and a RCFCd of 33198
when exposed to 0.2 mg Cu-Cd/L, and a R/W of 16774 to a RCFCd of 46272 when exposed to 1 mg
Cu-Cd/L, indicating that a higher amount of Cd is either excluded or relocated by the plant. Overall,
R/W is lower than RCF, attributed to the plants ability to regulate the uptake and partitioning
through active defense mechanisms.
There is a lack of studies where the root or stem concentration factors of metals have been calculated.
Solanki et al. (2018) calculated the RCFCd based on the initial exposure concentration. According
to the study, RCFCd is between 0.59 and 0.86 L/kg after 30 days of exposure to Canna Indica L.,
which were hydroponically exposed to Cd concentrations between 5 and 100 mg Cd/L. The root con-
centrations were reported to reside between 4 and 59 mg Cd/kgdw depending on concentration. Both
the reported RCF and root concentrations are signiﬁcantly smaller than the results of our study. The
tissue concentrations of non-essential elements such as Cd rise almost proportionally to their concen-
trations in soil or solution at low concentrations (Mclaughlin et al., 2011), as exempliﬁed by our study
(see Table 18 and 20). However, as surrounding concentrations rise, this leads to saturation in tissue

==PAGE:66==
concentrations in the stem, which results in declining uptake rates and declining SCF values compared
to lower concentrations. This also applies for the study of Solanki et al. (2018), which operates at
concentrations between 5 and 100 mg Cd/L. The SCF exhibits this saturation behaviour, where the
SCF at 0.2 mg/L is higher than SCF at 1 mg/L.
RCF is in many cases calculated as a ratio between the root concentration and soil concentration.
Since the plant roots and submerged stem fractions in the hydroponics system take up Cu and Cd
easily and directly without physico-chemical inhibition of soil, the calculated RCF and SCF values
are expected to be higher in the hydroponics system than they would be in the soil system. If the
plants were growing in soil, the distribution coeﬃcient, kd, would describe the concentration ratio be-
tween contaminants in the soil and in the pore water between soil particles. This partitioning creates
the grounds for assessment of the potential ions availability for plant uptake (Kim et al., 2005). An
example study for root-to-soil ratio is conducted by Isteniˇc et al. (2012), where an average RCFCu of
1 was found. The study investigated 13 plant species from stormwater treatment ponds in Aarhus,
Odense and Silkeborg. In an e↵ort to make the RCFCu by Isteniˇc et al. (2012) comparable to the
results of this study, the mean, minimum and maximum kd for Cu are retrieved from Mclaughlin et al.
(2011) (See Appendix V), and used to convert the reported average concentration in soil to a newly
estimated concentration in pore water. The minimum, maximum and mean RCFCu of Isteniˇc et al.
(2012) is then 7 L/kg, 82850 L/kg, and 4799 L/kg, for minimum, maximum and mean kd, respectively.
These converted RCFs are relatively comparable to the RCFCu in Table 19, especially if the true kd
values for the soils in the experiment conducted by Isteniˇc et al. (2012) are residing between the mean
and maximum reported kd values by Mclaughlin et al. (2011). The study of Cu bioaccumulation and
translocation in Jathropa Curcas. in contaminated sites by Ahmadpour et al. (2013) indicates an
average RCFCu of 2, based on root-to-soil ratio. Converting this to a root-to-solution based RCFCu
results in minimum, maximum and mean values of 14 L/kg, 165700 L/kg and 9598 L/kg. This interval
encapsulates the RCFCu in Table 19. The soil-to-radish system experiment by Petousi et al. (2014)
gave a root-to-soil ratio of 0.9, which converts to 4319 L/Kg with the mean kd. The wide range of
the kd limits the grounds for comparison, and applying the mean kd in root-to-soil studies gives RCF
values one to two orders of magnitude smaller than the resulting RCF calculations from this study.
RCF and SCF calculations can be interpreted in many ways. The mentioned root-to-soil concentration
factor is commonly used for soil and ﬁeld experiments while three other options are used in hydroponic
experiments as it has not been standardized. In this study, the RCF and SCF are calculated with three
denominators. This is to account for exposure concentrations not being constant up to the sampling
point because of the gradual uptake of Cu and Cd by the plant roots and the occasional reﬁlling of
solution. The three approaches for RCF and SCF computation are the ratio between the root or stem
concentration after 24 h and either the ﬁnal solution concentration, the initial solution concentration
or lastly, the average solution concentration. Using the ﬁnal concentration in solution as denominator
in the calculation of RCF and SCF results in di↵usion coeﬃcients one order of magnitude higher,
compared to the initial or the averaged solution concentrations. Skaates et al. (2005) conducted an
RCF experiment with analogous experimental set-up to this study, where severed poplar and willow
roots were exposed to dieldrin, and used the average concentration in the RCF calculation (Skaates
et al., 2005), to account for the non-constant exposure concentrations. In the study conducted by
Rendal et al. (2010), the averaged exposure concentration and initial exposure concentration are
used to compare the inﬂuence the denominator has on RCF. Correspondingly to this study, using
the average concentration results in lower RCFs than using initial concentrations in the calculations.

==PAGE:67==
Overall, the elected calculation approach inﬂuences the outcome to a higher or lesser extent, and in
turn has a potentially high impact on the estimation of di↵usive uptake in e.g. modelling e↵orts in
risk assessments.
5.4.2
Limitations, Implications and Recommendations
In plant uptake modelling e↵orts, RCF has predominantly been derived empirically from a positive
linear relationship between RCF and the logarithmic octanol-water partitioning coeﬃcient (KOW ),
developed by Briggs et al. (1982). As heavy metals are ionic and not associated with the KOW parti-
tioning coeﬃcient, many current model estimations are generally not suﬃciently developed or adapted
to understanding the fate of ionic compounds and heavy metals. The fate and behaviour of natural
substances such as heavy metals is inﬂuenced by the chemical form of the substance and the local
environmental prerequisites, rather than being explained by property based partitioning coeﬃcients.
The water solubilities of complexes containing metals determine whether they dissolve in the water
column or are deposited and incorporated into sediments. Chemical and biological transformation
processes ensure transformation between insoluble and soluble form, and as such, the metals can tran-
sition between the sediment and water phase, depending on their persistence in the relevant media
(Trapp, 2004). This study provides speciﬁc RCF and SCF estimations for willows at two concentra-
tions and a contribution in the understanding of the di↵usive uptake pattern of metals, which can not
be predicted by the conventional estimation of RCF based on partitioning coeﬃcients.
A main limitation of the experimental setup is that especially the severed roots are quite rapidly
starting to deteriorate, which has limited the experiment to last < 24 h. Due to the short duration
of the experiments, the RCF and SCF may not reﬂect the equilibrium conditions.
There is no
way of knowing if equilibrium has been reached, as there is no continuous response output showing
the stabilization of RCF or SCF, and no repeated experiments to validate the ﬁndings.
A great
uncertainty in the results is evident by the extremely high recoveries calculated in Table 22 and 23,
which reveals a substantial unidentiﬁed source of both Cu and Cd or calculation errors, which is also
a great uncertainty in the mixture experiment where the background metal concentrations in biomass
have not been identiﬁed.

6
Conclusion
The hydroponic willow experiment is conducted to assess the phytotoxicity of Cu and Cd to willows,
both individually and when present in a mixture. The individual assessment found Cu to be more
phytotoxic to willow than Cd, as the toxicity indicators are found to be less inhibited in the Cd exper-
iment than the Cu experiment. Cu has a larger inhibiting e↵ect on willow transpiration compared to
Cd. Willow cuttings exposed to Cd are also found to have a higher growth rate and WUE compared
to Cu, indicating that willow are less sensitive to Cd exposure than Cu exposure. When comparing
the individual toxicity experiments with the mixture experiment, no signiﬁcant di↵erence is observed
in the measured NRT, accumulated transpiration, growth or WUE between the individual Cu exper-
iment and the mixture experiment. Signiﬁcant di↵erence is however found between the individual Cd
and mixture experiment at all parameters. This indicates that the phytotoxic e↵ect observed in the
mixture experiment is induced by Cu and not Cd, at the tested concentrations. This is also observed
by the EC50 estimates, where comparable values are found in the Cu and mixture experiment, while
the EC50 value in the Cd experiment is one order of magnitude higher.
Individual uptake of Cu in willow trees shows a higher recovery of Cu when exposed to 0.5 mg Cu/L
and higher, compared to 0.2 mg Cu/L. This indicates that exposure of 0.2 mg Cu/L does not induce
phytotoxic e↵ect on willow and control mechanisms inside the plant can therefore regulate the Cu
uptake more eﬃciently.
The results from uptake of Cu and Cd in the willow mixture experiment shows that willow trees have
a better ability to accumulate Cu through their roots compared to Cd. The calculated R/W value
shows that the fraction of Cu in the roots increases with increasing exposure concentration in solu-
tion, while the fraction of Cd in the roots decreases with increasing exposure concentration. A higher
fraction of Cu is translocated to the leaves compared to Cd, as seen by the lower R/L value calculated
for Cu uptake. Willows have a better ability to translocate Cu and Cd to the leaves at lower exposure
concentrations. This is observed by the lower R/L value calculated for Cu and Cd when exposed to
0.2 mg Cu/L than at 1 mg Cu/L. The concentration of Cd in the stem is also decreasing with elevated
exposure, while the concentration of Cu in the stem increases with increased exposure. Overall, the
uptake rate for Cu (as indicated by R/W) increases with exposure, while R/W of Cd decreases with
exposure. For both metals, the translocation eﬃciency decreases with increasing exposure.
The cress experiment is conducted to determine the phytotoxicity of Cu and Cd to cress. Cu is found
to have an increasing inhibiting e↵ect on cress transpiration with increasing exposure concentration.
However, no phytotoxic di↵erence is found for cress with elevated Cd exposure, as no signiﬁcant dif-
ference is found between the NRT for the tested concentrations. Phytotoxic e↵ects are also found on
growth rate and WUE for cress exposed to Cu, while no inhibition in either growth rate or WUE
is observed in the Cd cress experiment. As the tested Cd concentrations did not have a phytotoxic
e↵ect on cress, a EC50 estimate for Cd is not obtained. All toxicity indicators therefore show that
Cu is more toxic to cress than Cd for the tested concentrations. Cress is less sensitive to Cu and Cd
exposure than willows.
The climate chamber experiment is conducted to assess how elevated temperature and CO2 conditions
inﬂuence the phytotoxicity of Cu to willows. Elevated temperature conditions in the "T chamber are
found to induce higher transpiration rates, where NRT for the tested concentrations is generally less
inhibited than in the Ambient chamber. The growth rate and WUE is lower in the "T chamber with
67
==PAGE:69==
higher exposure concentrations compared to the Ambient chamber, indicating that willow are less
eﬃcient when exposed to elevated temperature conditions. However, the severity of the high exposure
concentrations is lower in the "T chamber than the Ambient chamber. It is expected that elevated
CO2 conditions would lead to increased WUE, which is a↵ected by increased growth and decreased
accumulated transpiration. However, willow trees in the "CO2 chamber shows no signiﬁcant di↵er-
ence in growth rate, WUE, and NRT from the Ambient chamber. The detrimental e↵ects to willow
appear to be alleviated by the combined e↵ects of elevated temperature and CO2 in the "T and CO2
chamber. Despite no signiﬁcant e↵ects of the elevated CO2 conditions alone on the plants compared
to ambient conditions, the elevated temperature induced increase of transpiration, decrease in growth
rate and decrease in WUE, appears to be largely counteracted by the e↵ects of the elevated CO2 in the
combined chamber. Additionally, the positive e↵ect elevated temperature has on very high exposure
doses remains valid in the combined treatment. The EC50 estimates is shown to be sensitive to the
parameters chosen. However, EC50 values based on both NRG and NRT shows that ambient condi-
tions induced the highest toxicity of Cu to willow, while "T and CO2 induced the least toxic conditions.
RCF and SCF are estimated to determine the passive di↵usive partitioning between the liquid phase
and the roots and the submerged stem fraction. RCF is found to be larger for Cd than Cu, indicating
that a larger fraction of Cd is passively di↵used into the roots, compared to Cu. For both metals,
passive di↵usion increases with increasing exposure. Opposite RCF, SCF decreases with increasing
exposure, meaning that a lower fraction of available metal di↵uses into the stem with higher exposure
concentration. Finally, a larger fraction of both Cu and Cd is di↵using into the roots compared to
the stem, as RCF values are several orders of magnitude higher than SCF values.
These ﬁnding are all relevant in both current and future challenges regarding increased unintentional
pollution and reuse of resources potentially containing heavy metals. Successful risk assessments re-
lated to food security implications of recycled irrigation water and biosolid application on agricultural
land in a changing climate is depending on this type of research. Furthermore, understanding the
internal plant dose-response of heavy metal dynamics and climate change induced alleviation or in-
tensiﬁcation of phytotoxicity is crucial to successfully implement phytoremediation technologies for
cleaning up contaminated sites, which is a pressing and increasing issue.

Appendix V: kd values
The table shows reported kd values for Cu and Cd from (Mclaughlin et al., 2011). The distribution
coeﬃcient (kd) is a measure of the relative distribution of elements between the solid phase and pore
water (water held in pore space between soil particles), and is an important property to consider in
assessing the potential availability of metals to plants. It is stated as the concentration in the solid
phase (mg/kgdw) divided by the concentration in the solution (mg/L). The degree of contamination
and soil aﬃnity to the respective metals determine the size of kd. The values in the Table display a
range of values, as the distribution is a↵ected by e.g soil mineralogy, particle size, pH, salinity, redox
conditions and metal concentrations.
