Abstract 
Since the concept of generating electricity from decomposing organic matter was discovered and 
which has led to the subsequent development of the Microbial Fuel Cell (MFC), research into 
various parameters including electrodes, materials, catalysts, electron transfer has been conducted, 
basically to prove the concept. However, very little progress has been made in the upscaling and 
practical implementation of the MFC technology. This can be attributed to low voltage production 
and low organic removal rate of MFCs.  
This study, therefore, primarily aims to investigate the possibilities of replacing the inefficient 
abiotic anode materials with anaerobic granules (AG) from Upflow Anaerobic Sludge Bed reactors 
by development of proper transition strategies. The study also aims at investigating the conductivity 
of the granules as well as their wastewater treatment and electricity production ability.   
The granules were tested in two periods. The first investigated the conductivity by testing with 
different anodes. The second periods focus on the wastewater treatment and energy by supplying 
organic loads different acetate concentrations and testing the COD removal and coulombic 
efficiency. 
It was found that the granules exhibited electrogenic characteristics in addition to their 
methanogenic behavior. In addition, the conductive nature of the granules is limited especially with 
reduced electrode surface area. The granules also have a high efficiency for treatment of wastewater 
similar to those in the UASB. Energy losses in the systems still need to be addressed in the future 
work.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
Since the discovery of electrical tendencies of decomposing organic matter by Potter, 1911, 
research of that concept has led to the development of the microbial fuel cell (Santoro, Arbizzani, 
Erable, & Ieropoulos, 2017). The emergence of the microbial fuel cell (MFC) has brought new 
vitality and opportunity to the bioenergy community. The research into the MFC has increased 
manifold over the last decades. Research has mainly focused on the anode and cathode materials, 
the process, the possible substrates that can be used effectively as well as the bacterial community. 
However, very little progress has been made in the upscaling of the MFC. This is due to the low 
voltage production and low conversion efficiencies (Premier et al., 2012). Nevertheless, 
investigation into the cathode and anode materials, design of the reactor, bacterial communities as 
well as substrates has yielded encouraging results. The anode chamber has been especially of 
interest as it contains the main driving forces of the MFC: the bacteria and the electrode.  
The electrode used is highly crucial for the success of the MFC as it provides the surface for 
microbial attachment and transfer of electrons. Different kinds of electrodes have been utilized over 
the years since the discovery of the bioelectrochemical process. A review by Sonawane, Yadav, 
Ghosh, & Adeloju, (2016) has shown the advances made in the development of carbon based 
organic and inorganic material as well as 2 dimensional and 3 dimensional electrode designs. 
Problem Statement 
The bacteria in the anode chamber act as biocatalysts in the oxidation of matter – both organic and 
inorganic – to produce current (Logan et al., 2006). The bacteria are generally attached on the 
surface of electrodes to form a thin layer which namely is biofilm. The formation of a biofilm on 
anode could  facilitate the easy transfer of electrons (Givskov et al., 2000). However, the 
availability of electrode surface area may be an issue. 
Limited surface area of 2 dimensional anodes such as carbon/graphite plates and carbon cloth led to 
the development of 3 dimensional anode electrodes. Granular graphite has been used in studies by 
(Moreno et al., 2017) and has been shown to be effective. Carbon nanotubes have also been 
determined as an alternative to 2D electrodes (Li, Cheng, & Thomas, 2017). However, the 
environmental and economic costs may not be feasible. Carbon based electrodes may be generally 
classified as low cost, however, modifications needed to improve their properties for use in MFCs 
may lead to higher cost (Li et al., 2017).  
The ideal electrode should be conductive; corrosion resistant; has viable surface area for easy 
attachment for bacterial biofilm; durable; environmentally friendly and low in cost (Santoro et al., 
2017). To achieve this goal, this study seeks to add existing scientific knowledge by investigating 
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
 
the feasibility of anaerobic granules for use in MFCs, both as bacterial catalysts and electrode 
material. A study by Shrestha et al., (2014) hinted at the possible conductivity of anaerobic 
granules. The presence of electrogenic bacteria in the granule (Morita et al., 2011) also provides an 
incentive for this study. 
State of the art 
Current anode material and biofilm in MFC 
Fossil fuels have been the primary source of energy all over the world, however, that is changing. 
With more awareness about the effects of fossil fuel on climate change, the alternatives are getting 
more attention. The Microbial Fuel Cell (MFC) is one of these alternatives. The MFC oxidizes 
organic matter in the anode to produce electricity (Logan et al., 2006).  This would have been a 
good solution to the world’s energy problem, but there is a catch. Theoretically, the MFC can 
produce up to 1.1V of electricity (Rozendal et al., 2008). In practice, they have only produced up to 
~0.5V electricity (Oh & Logan, 2007). Though studies are ongoing to find methods of increasing 
the electricity production such as stacking and connection of multiple MFC; optimization of anode 
and cathode components (Coates & Wrighton, 2009; Oh & Logan, 2007); some researchers have 
turned to the task of finding equally ‘green’ uses for the MFC.  MFCs are being developed as/for 
biosensors (Chang et al., 2004; Dávila, et al., 2011; Kaur et al., 2013; Tront, et al, 2008); rare/heavy 
metal recovery systems (Abourached, 2014; Huang, et al., 2010; Rodenas Motos et al., 2015) 
hydrogen production (Hu, Fan, & Liu, 2008; Ditzig, Liu, & Logan, 2007; Liu, Grot, & Logan, 
2005); biofuel production (Birjandi, Younesi, Ghoreyshi, & Rahimnejad, 2016); and wastewater 
treatment (Abourached, 2014; Aelterman, Rabaey, Clauwaert, & Verstraete, 2006) among others. 
Wastewater treatment seems to be particularly promising.  
The earth is caught in a never ending loop with wastewater. As long as there are people on the 
earth, there will always be wastewater produced. Various forms of disposal such as aerobic 
treatment, trickling filters and anaerobic digestion exist. The question remains is how effective are 
these forms of disposal. In most developing countries, the practice of unsafe disposal of wastewater 
is fairly common leading to pollution of waterbodies (Monney, et al, 2013). This is mostly due to 
the difficulty in funding the high initial capital investment of the construction and operation of 
treatment facilities (Rammont & Amin, 2010).In addition, traditional wastewater treatment plants 
usually require high energy input and disposal of the sludge which is a byproduct of the treatment 
process(Massoud, Tarhini, & Nasr, 2009) limiting their appeal. Enter the MFC. Studies by 
(Rozendal, et al, 2008) have shown that the MFC has potential as a wastewater treatment system. 
According to Aelterman et al., (2006), the MFC has the ability to oxidize organic matter for energy 
recovery with low energy input and sludge production.

Washout of Bacterial Component in Anode Chamber 
The driving forces behind the performance of the MFC are the anode electrode and the bacteria. 
These are situated in the anode chamber along with the organic matter and substrate (Rahimnejad, 
et al, 2015). The bacteria degrade the organic matter in the anode chamber to generate electrons 
which are transferred across an external circuit to reduce electron acceptors (Rahimnejad et al., 
2015). Running an MFC may, however, lead to the washout of the bacteria from the anode chamber 
especially in the continuous flow systems required for wastewater treatment. Batch-fed systems 
where the substrate solution is supplied and changed after electricity production declines below a 
predetermined value are not suitable for WW treatment due to risk of contamination (Rahimnejad, 
et al, 2011). Continuous flow systems provide a constant supply of substrate solution in order to 
maintain a constant level of electricity production (Rahimnejad et al., 2011). 
Possible solutions to solve the washout problem include increasing retention time and the 
immobilization of biofilms/microbes on the anodes. While increasing the HRT may lead to 
enhanced performance of the reactor through improved organic matter removal and improved 
effluent quality (Ladu & Lu, 2014), it  may lead to higher operation cost due to the longer operation 
time.  This solution pales in comparison with the possibility of growth and immobilization of 
biofilms. 
Development of Microbial Biofilm 
 The bacteria have evolved to combat the problem of washout through the formation of biofilms. 
According to Givskov et al., (2000), a biofilm consist of microbial communities with encased 
extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) that attaches itself to a surface. The EPS acts as the 
immobilization mechanism and also allows for contact and transfer between cell (Givskov et al., 
2000). Synthetic biofilm formation is mostly used when growing pure cultures but it can be used for 
mixed cultures as well (Read, Dutta, Bond, Keller, & Rabaey, 2010). The EPS necessary for biofilm 
formation protects the bacteria through adsorption of toxic substance and acts as a buffer to any 
external shock as well as slow down the diffusion rate of antimicrobial agents (Givskov et al., 2000) 
The biofilm also presents the chance for protection through genetic modification. Microbes in a 
biofilm may evolve in a manner to prevent jeopardizing the biofilm structure through the repressing 
of flagellum growth that weaken and by producing exopolysaccharides that strengthen the structure 
(Garrett, Bhakoo, & Zhang, 2008).  A biofilm usually consist of multiple colonies which develop a 
symbiotic metabolic relationship (de la Fuente-Nunez, Reffuveille, Fernandez, & Hancock, 2013). 
The biofilm also affects the metabolic activities of the microbes. The close proximity provides the 
central located bacteria (usually with limited supply of nutrients) with access through interspecies 
transfer (Davey & O’toole, 2000).  In MFCs, biofilms have been shown to increase electricity 
production. This is seen in the results of a study by (Baranitharan et al., 2014) which showed that 
there was improved voltage production in a palm oil effluent fed MFC after the 7th day of formation 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
of a biofilm. Immobilization in MFCs often involves the development of a biofilm on the anode 
electrode. Immobilization presents several advantages. It prevents washout as well as the possible 
clogging of the part of the reactor by the small cells (Srivastava & Srivastava, 2003). It improves 
the efficiency as well as the economics of running the reactor (Srivastava & Srivastava, 2003).  
Types of Anodes 
The choice of anode also matters for the operation of the MFC as it serves the purpose of biofilm 
attachment as well as electron transfer. Research by Read et al.,( 2010) showed that biofilms formed 
by pure cultures worked well close to the electrode. Immobilization of the bacterial biofilm may be 
aided by the anode chosen. According to Santoro et al., (2017) the best kind of electrode possesses 
the following qualities: conductive, corrosion resistant, viable surface area for easy attachment of 
bacterial biofilm, durable, environmentally friendly and low in cost. Carbon based and metal- based 
materials have been shown to possess some of these qualities (Santoro et al., 2017; Wei, Liang, & 
Huang, 2011). 
Carbon-based anodes 
Wei et al., 2011 shows that carbon based anodes usually exist in three main forms: planar, packed 
and brush structures. The planar structured anodes consist of carbon plates, graphite plates and 
carbon cloth (Wei et al., 2011). Carbon and graphite plates are both usually thin plate sheets. 
However the latter is deemed to be more durable than the former which is slightly brittle. Both 
types may have a smooth or rough surface. Studies by ter Heijne, et al, (2008) have shown that the 
graphite plate with a modified surface produced higher power density than the smooth. Both the 
graphite and carbon plates are relatively stiff and may not be appropriate for use in MFC with 
irregular configurations (Wei et al., 2011).In addition, their relatively low specific area means that 
there is less surface area for the microbial biofilm to attach itself to (Wei et al., 2011). Other 
disadvantages of the above materials include their high cost of production (Wei et al., 2011). The 
carbon cloth was developed to improve upon the carbon plate. It has a relative higher flexibility and 
higher surface area due to its porous nature (Wei et al., 2011). Zhang et al (2010) shows that the 
cost of production of the carbon cloth is higher. 
The packed form of anode is expected to provide a solution to the surface area issue regarding the 
planar structural form. Carbon nanotubes, carbon granules and granular graphite among others form 
part of this solution (Wei et al., 2011). Carbon nanotubes are made of cylindrical shaped carbon 
material and have a large surface area for easy adherence microbial biofilm (Hindatu, Annuar, & 
Gumel, 2017). They are considered to chemically inert and highly conductive (Hindatu et al., 2017).  
Metal based anodes- e.g. Titanium plate/mesh 
Metal also fulfill some of the requirements for anodes. They are relative cheaper, reducing the cost 
of the MFC. Some metals such as stainless steel are durable, corrosion resistant and highly 
==PAGE:12==
 
11 
 
conductive. However, stainless steel in a study by Hou, J., Liu, Z., Yang, S., & Zhou, (2014) 
showed to have poor biocompatibility. Modification through the development of composite and 
nanocomposite forms have shown to improve performance of the metal though. 
In the search for eco-friendlier anode materials, scientists are researching the use of biomass and 
recycled materials. Carbonized biomass such as bamboo charcoal have shown to be feasible as 
anode materials (Zhang et al., 2014). The bamboo charcoal tube was observed to be more 
biocompatible relative to the graphite tube anode. In addition, it had a higher specific area and 
higher power density compared to the graphite tube anode (Zhang et al., 2014) . However, during 
the scale-up testing, Zhang et al., (2014) showed that there was a decrease in the total power 
increment with the increasing addition of bamboo charcoal anodes needed for scale-up. 
In order  to combat the environmental impacts and reduce the high cost of producing anode 
materials for MFCs, Wang, Davidson, Zuo, & Ren, (2011) investigated the viability of recycling 
used rubber tires. The tires were ground up, coated with graphite to make them conductive and their 
performance was compared with that of graphite granules. The crumbled tire granules showed a 
higher specific area (4.5m2/g) compared to the graphite granules (0.3m2/g) (Wang et al., 2011). 
However, Wang et al., (2011) observed that, at a stable voltage of 390mV, the crumbled tire 
granules produced less power than the graphite granules (430mV). In addition, they had relatively 
lower COD removal (85%) compare to the graphite granules (92.8%) (Wang et al., 2011). 
Disadvantages of Current Immobilization on Anodes 
Immobilization has taken place primarily on anodic materials. However high cost of producing 
these anode materials may limit their use and the scale up of MFCs (Wei et al., 2011). The 
acclimatization time of the biofilm also means an increase the length of the setup period (Babauta, 
Renslow, Lewandowski, & Beyenal, 2012). In addition, the environmental effects are myriad.   The 
use of the granular graphite as well as synthetically modified anode material has shown to be 
effective but these add to total cost of construction and operation of the MFC (Hindatu et al., 2017). 
In addition, the synthesizing process as well as disposal method of the anode material may have 
ecological consequences (Hindatu et al., 2017). The disadvantages inherent both in immobilization 
and anodes may be overcome through the development of a less costly, more environmentally 
friendly but equally effective alternative.  That alternative may be found in the use of anaerobic 
granules (AG) for immobilization and possibly even as an anode in the MFC. 
Characteristics of Anaerobic Granules 
Anaerobic granules were first noticed by Dr Lettinga in South Africa whiles investigating the 
anaerobic filter. He observed that the bacteria were able to form granules on their own without the 
aid of inert materials under certain conditions (Mchugh, O ’reilly, Se Mahony, Colleran, & O 
’flaherty, 2003). The granule usually consists of intertwined combination of anaerobic microbes 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
 
with a symbiotic relationship (Mchugh et al., 2003). The structural nature of the granule depends on 
the composition of the influent feed as well as the operating condition of the reactor (Habeeb, Bin, 
Latiff, Daud, & Ahmad, 2011). Generally granules contain these main components: Inorganic 
matter, Microbial cells, and Extracellular polymers (ECP). The inorganic matter which may make 
up 10% to 90% of the granule mainly consists of minerals and ash (Habeeb et al., 2011). The ash 
may affect the density as well as the colour of the granule due to the Iron (II) Sulphide (FeS) 
present (Habeeb et al., 2011). The microbial cell may adhere to the inorganic materials by forming 
aggregates surrounded by ECP (Habeeb et al., 2011). Though widely studied and little understood, 
research has come about with several theories of the formulation of these granules. A review by Pol, 
De Castro Lopes, Lettinga, & Lens, (2004) compares the various theories. 
The Selection pressure theory states that granules formed are influenced by the HRT and the gas 
loading rate (GLR). These may cause the washout of light/ dispersed sludge and prevent the 
removal of the heavier components (Pol et al., 2004). Microbial growth then occurs on these heavier 
components which act as a nuclei for growth mainly consisting of organic and inorganic matter (Pol 
et al., 2004). This increases till a maximum size in reached causing the breaking of parts of the 
granule which then act as growth nuclei for the repetition of the process(Pol et al., 2004).  
Another theory is the growth of suspended solids that are colonized by bacteria. Pol et al., (2004) 
shows that Pereboom suggests that granules are formed from fine particles and colonized suspended 
solids from the influent. The granule size is believed to increase or decrease in size based on the 
biomass present (Pol et al., 2004).  
The  Cape Town Theory suggested that the granule formation was mainly due to the 
Methanobacterium strain which used hydrogen as its energy source and produced all of its own 
amino acids except cysteine (Pol et al., 2004).The presence of ammonium in the influent would, 
however, lead to higher production of these amino acids which would then be secreted as ECP. The 
ECP would then bind the Methanobacterium strain and other bacteria together to form the 
granule(Pol et al., 2004).This theory suggested that systems that did not have hydrogen or did not 
undergo degradation in low hydrogen partial pressure were not feasible for granule formation.  
However, as acetate-fed reactors also produced the granulation effect, this theory did not hold up 
(Pol et al., 2004). 
Aim  
The primary aim of this thesis is to investigate the possibilities for the optimization of the MFC 
through the use of anaerobic granules from UASB reactors. Secondary aims of the thesis include the 
investigation into the characteristics of the bacterial community of the process to better predict the 
outcome of future experiments. In order to do this, these questions must be answered: 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
 
1. Is there the possibility of electricity production from anaerobic granules? 
2. How do reactor conditions and operation affect the electricity production and conversion 
efficiency? 
3. What characteristics are the granules used in the MFC expected to have? 
The answers to these questions will be determined through data collection and analysis from 
experimental work in the DTU Biotech Laboratory.

Chapter 2 
Materials and Methods 
MFC reactor design and operation 
 
 
Fig 1. (A)MFC reactor with granules in anode and ferricyanide solution in the cathode (B) Mesophillic 
granules used in the reactor 
The three MFC reactors used in the study were constructed with polycarbonate as a two chamber 
reactor separated by a cation exchange membrane (CEM). Both the anode and cathode chambers 
were cuboid with dimensions 4 cm in length, 5 cm in width and 5 cm in height with a net volume of 
100ml. Two types of cathode were used in the cathode chamber: a Ti plate with Pt catalyst for 
increase oxygen reduction rate (Logan et al., 2006) coating initially and a graphite plate in the 
second phase of the experiment to decrease the cost of the reactor altogether (Logan et al., 2006). A 
carbon brush, activated in a furnace at 450 °C for 20 minutes was used in the anode chamber. The 
anode chamber was connected to a 500 ml glass bottle by two 50 cm long tubes. The tubes were for 
inflow and outflow of the influent.  The bottle was connected to a 1 L gasbag. Each reactor used a 
fixed external resistance of 1000Ω. 
Three reactors were run as batch systems with medium recirculation under three different 
conditions.  
 Room temperature (22 °C) with domestic waste water (DWW) as the medium (G2) 
 Room temperature (22 °C) with salt based medium (G1) 
 37 °C with salt based medium (G3) 
All three reactors used acetate as the substrate and 450 ml of medium.  The granules used in the 
experiment were obtained from mesophilic reactors run on potato starch influent. 50mM Potassium 
ferricyanide was used as the catholyte. Though it is not environmentally sustainable, it is the 
prefered choice for tests where the processes and occurrences in the anode chamber are under study 
(Freguia, Rabaey, Yuan, & Keller, 2007) . 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
The study was run in two parts. In the first period, different anode configurations were tested with 
the granules to determine their conductivity. In the second period, the efficiency of the bacteria for 
wastewater treatment was tested. 
First period 
200 ml of granule, procured from a lab reactor run on potato starch and previously stored at 4°C, 
were activated by addition of 1g/l of acetate and placed in an incubator at 37°C for a 48 hour 
period. 50 ml of the granules were placed in both the G1 and G2 reactors. Both reactors were 
connected to the medium bottles via the tubes and a pump. The medium was pumped into the 
reactors at a rate of 3rpm. The medium was sparged with pure nitrogen to provide anaerobic 
condition for the bacteria. The operation of both reactors was ran in phases during the first period 
with each phase denoting a specific change to the configuration of the anode chamber.  
The G3 reactor was set up in the same manner as a control with the utilization of the plastic mesh 
and carbon brush during the whole period.

Second period 
500ml of granule, previously stored at 4°C, were activated by addition of 1g/l of acetate daily and 
placed in an incubator (details of incubator) at 37°C for a 48 hour period. 80 ml of the granules 
were placed in all three reactors. The reactors were then connected to the medium bottles via the 
tubes and a pump. The medium was pumped into the reactors at a rate of 20rpm. The medium was, 
also in this case, recirculated to provide better access to the substrate for the bacteria. The reactors 
were operated with the carbon brush and the plastic mesh configuration only of the anode chamber.  
Control reactors 
Two 500mL standard glass bottles were fitted to be airtight with rubber stoppers. Two holes were 
made in the rubber stoppers and tubes were connected through these. The inflow tube for sampling 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 
and sparging purposes and the out flow tube for gas collection. The outflow tube was connected to a 
1.1L gas bag. 
Each bottle was filled with the 150mL salt solution medium which had been autoclaved 
(Certoclave) for 30 minutes to prevent cross contamination. Under sterile conditions in the laminar 
flow workbench, 100mL of granules was added to each bottle. The bottles were sealed and sparged 
with pure nitrogen for 10 minutes to ensure anaerobic condition. Bottle A was placed in shaker at a 
rate of 150rpm at 37°C. Bottle B was placed on a mixer at a rate of 150rpm at room temperature 
(22°C). 
Measurements and Parameters 
Electricity recording 
The voltage across the resistance (1000 Ω) was recorded by a digital multimeter (model 2700, 
Keithley Instruments, Inc,; Cleveland, OH)  every 30 min. The current and coulombs were 
calculated from this information.  
pH and Conductivity 
The anode and cathode solution samples were collected from the reactors using a 10 mL syringe. 
pH values were measured using a pH-meter (PHM 92 with measuring range is pH 2-10 at 
temperature 0°C to 60°C) provided with the suitable electrodes (Gel pH electrode - pHC3105-8, 
Radiometer analytical). The conductivity of the anodic solution was determined using conductivity 
meter. 
Gas Analysis 
For each batch cycle, gas samples were taken at 3 hour intervals during the day (9am to 6pm) 
during the first 72 hours and at 24 hour intervals from the 72 hour mark to the end of the batch 
cycle. Gas chromatography was used to analyze the gas composition in the gas bag using an airtight 
syringe with a luer lock. The N2, CH4 and CO2 concentrations were tested using Mikrolab Aarhus 
a/s. biogas gas chromatograph coupled with a thermal conductivity detector and packed columns for 
compound separation whiles the H2 was tested using Mikrolab GC equipped with a packed column 
for compound separation, main column: column 4.5 m x 3 mm IDMolsieve 5A 10/80 and a thermal 
conductivity detector. A low concentration standard was used from an already prepared standard 
mixture from AGA with volume ratios: 85% N2, 5% CH4 5% CO2 and 5% H2. Nitrogen was 
excluded from the calculations as its presence was only due to the sparging process.  
COD and VFA 
The samples of anode solution were collected via the outflow tube of the reactors using a 10 mL 
syringe. According to the method (Danish Standard DS 217:1991 “water examination.

Granule samples 
Granule sampling occurred after each major change of performance of the reactor: the beginning

were them processed in the following methods: scanning electron microscope (SEM) and DNA

For SEM observations, the granules were washed and fixed according to a modification of the 
method described by Manavi, Kazemi, & Bonakdarpour, (2017): granules were washed with a 
phosphate buffer and fixed with 2.5% glutaraldehyde for 2hrs at 4 °C. Subsequently, the fixed 
granules were washed using 0.10 M sodium cacodylate buffer and dehydrated by successive 
passage through 25, 50, 75, 80, 90, 95 and 100% ethanol. The prepared granules were coated with a 
gold thin layer by sputtering (Emitech K450X, Quorum Emitech, England) and observed with a 
SEM (Tescan Vega II, Tescan Brno s.r.o., Czech Federation).

First Period 
Electricity production 
The G1 reactor (22°C with medium) was run in 8 phases. Each phase was designed to test the 
possibility of granule conductivity. In phase 1(shown in Fig.2A), a lag phase of 4 days was 
observed where the electricity production ranged between 0.0919 and 0.1558V. The lag phase may 
be attributed to the bacteria acclimatizing to the conditions of the reactor as shown in papers by 
Schultz & Kishony, (2013). After this, there was a sudden increase to 0.631V. Maximum voltage 
(0.67) was reached a few hours later after which the electricity production began to steadily 
decrease. The change in cathode solution caused a spike in the voltage but it quickly reverted to 
trend of steady decrease. This phase was operated until the baseline voltage of 0.1V was reached. 
In this stage (shown in Fig.2B), the enrichment of the bacteria in the reactor continues. By attaching 
a new bottle of medium and substrate, an immediate increase to the maximum voltage is observed. 
There was no lag period at the beginning of the second phase as the bacteria were already adapted 
to the reactor conditions. The reactor followed a similar trend to the one observed in the first phase.  
Switching the anode from the carbon brush to the titanium (Ti) rod and plastic mesh showed a sharp 
decline in the electricity production below the baseline voltage of 0.1V(shown in Fig.2C). The 
voltage steadily decreased from 0.00065V to 0.000221V and subsequently began to slowly increase 
to 0.00065 again. This may tentatively indicate the possibility of higher power production with 
longer startup time. The overall low electricity production may be attributed to low surface area 
available for contact and transfer of electrons. 
Both phase 4 and 5 showed the same trend as phase 3: relatively high initial reading followed by 
sharp decline and subsequently a steady but relatively low electricity production. Phase 4 (shown in 
Fig.2D) showed steady electricity production between 0.000317 and 0.000459V. This showed that 
using the Ti rod with the cotton gauze to pack the granules more tightly than in phase 3 led to 
higher voltage production. 
Increasing the available surfaces and connection points by using the Ti tree anode and cotton gauze 
for phase 5 did not yield improved results (shown in Fig.2E). The electricity production though 
steady was lower in comparison with the phase 4. 
Phase 6 and 7 (shown in Fig.2F and 2G respectively) showed a better performance than Phase 5 
with average electricity production of 0.0623Vand 0.0387V respectively. Both phases, however, 
showed rapid decline in the first two hours to 0.005425 and 0.006703V respectively.

Second Period 
Electricity production during Enrichment period 
During the enrichment period, the G1 and G3 reactors were run with the same medium and acetate 
concentration with temperature as the only differing parameter. G1 was run at 22°C and G3 at 
37°C. The G2 reactor was run at 22°C as well but with domestic wastewater and acetate as the 
substrate. From the start, all reactors showed a lag period of approximately 3 days. During this time, 
the G1 reactor produced less than 0.1V electricity; the G2 reactor had less than 0.2V and the G3 
reactor’s electricity production was between 0.3V and 0.1V (Fig. 6A). This lag phase can be 
attributed to the adaptation time of the bacteria to their surrounding conditions (Schultz & Kishony, 
2013). In the G1 reactor, erratic electricity production is observed (Fig. 6B). The highest voltages 
are recorded at the replacement of the old substrate and cathode solution with new. The production 
is observed to stabilize only at voltage below 0.1 with a decreasing trend. In the G3 reactor, the 
electricity production is higher compared to the G1 reactor with maximum voltage of 0.5563V 
occurring at day 26 of enrichment. Though the reactor shows a decreasing trend in electricity 
production, it can be observed from the graph that the time for this occurrence is lengthening (Fig. 
6C). This suggests that given sufficient time, stabilization of the power production in the reactor 
will occur. The differences between the G1 reactor and G3 reactor may be attributed to the 
difference in temperature. Gonzalez del Campo, Lobato, Cañizares, Rodrigo, & Fernandez Morales, 
(2013) showed that increasing the temperature increased the cell voltage produced for an MFC 
seeded with sludge from a wastewater treatment plant and batch-fed with synthetic wastewater. This 
was attributed to the effect on microbial activity in their MFC; a condition reflected in the reactors 
in this study with the G3 reactor having higher voltage than the G1. Grant & Lin, (1995) also 
showed this phenomenon in their work with anaerobic granules in UASB reactors that the improved 
organic load removal from influent wastewater with increasing temperature. The G1 and G3 
reactors were run for longer enrichment periods than the G2 reactor in order to ascertain if they 
would stabilize eventually. 
The G2 reactor is the most stable among the three reactors. After the initial lag period, electricity 
production increased rapidly to 0.614V (Fig. 6A). The drop in voltage between day 4 and day 5 was 
due to blockage of the effluent by the sediment in the wastewater leading to buildup of pressure and 
subsequent leaking of the reactor. This was solved by filtering the WW before use. A new bottle 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
 
was attached after which the reactor resumed working as normal. The high power output may be 
attributed to the presence of microbes in the already present in the domestic WW. Research by 
(Aelterman et al., 2006) using wastewater in the anode chamber showed that power was produced. 
Their reactor using granular graphite and wastewater as substrate showed a power production of 
58W/m3. This may suggest that using the granules provides an extra source of microbes as well as 
relatively simpler method immobilization preventing washout. It would be interesting to know if 
running the wastewater reactor at 37°C would achieve higher power densities than recorded 
presently at 22°C.

Microbial Analysis 
The study started on the premise of the following possibilities that: the conductivity of the granules 
existed either by direct transfer via nanowire or other appendages/pili or by indirect shuttling of 
electrons via other organelles or products of microbial metabolism and that the nature of the granule 
as a mixed culture could contain electrogenic microbes.  
Though time constraints prevented the inclusion of the results of DNA analysis tests conducted, 
inference may be made from the results of the other tests available. However, the DNA test results 
are needed to support or disprove these hypotheses. Commonly known electrogenic bacteria such as 
many Geobacter species, 
Shewanella 
putrefaciens, 
Rhodoferax 
ferrireducens, Clostridium 
butyricum and Aeromonas hydrophila oxidize organic substrates for the production of electricity in 
MFCs (You et al., 2007). These bacteria were initially isolated from mixed cultures. The electrons 
produced from the oxidation are transferred to the electrode through  microbial self-produced 
chemical shuttles; biologically produced nanowires or chemically active redox enzymes (You et al., 
2007). A study by Zhu, Kougias, Treu, Campanaro, & Angelidaki, (2017)  showed that 
approximately 68.3% bacteria and 21.8& archaea made up the microbial community in mesophilic 
granules fed with potato starch. The granules contained high contents of Anaerolinaeceae and 
Syntrophobacter. The Methanobacterium and Methanosaeta genus made up the archaea 
community(Zhu et al., 2017). Another study by (Antwi et al., 2017) showed similarities with the 
microbial community in their study consisting of Anaerolineaceae (16.30%) followed by 
Synergistaceae (9.20%), Methanosaetaceae (8.91%) and Clostridiales_Incertae Sedis XI (6.44%) at  
the family taxonomic level. With the exception of the methanogenic archaea, the other microbes 
functions include the hydrolysis and fermentation of complex subtances into their simpler form as 
well as the supply of enzymes need for the process and a chemolithoautotrophic and heterotrophic 
functions (Antwi et al., 2017). In an MFC fed with potato starch by Tian et al., (2017) showed a 
microbial community with exoeletroogenic bacteria such as Geobacter and Dysgonomonas.  
The granules used in this study were mesophilic granules derived from a UASB run on potato starch 
and may share microbial community characteristics with the above studies. The possible presence 
of the electrogenic bacteria provides insight into the electricity production during the first phase. 
Another avenue for exploration proposed by Dubé & Guiot, (2015)  about the existence of direct 
electron exchange between microbes in the anaerobic granule shown to exist in a relationship 
between acetogens and methanogens. In addition, the structure of the granule provides niches for 
habitation of other microbes which have either competitive or cooperative relationships that reflect 
the syntrophic nature of the granule (Mchugh et al., 2003).

Control Bottle Reactors for Second Period 
This test was to ascertain the methanogenic properties of the granules. The granules kept at 37°C 
cumulatively produced 216ml of Methane and 49.23ml of carbon dioxide over a 50 day period . The 
granules at 22°C produced low methane volume (87.21ml) but more carbon dioxide (86.17ml) than 
the 37°C bottle reactor (Fig.9). The higher methane production in the 37°C bottle reactor may be 
due to the temperature. As the granules are mesophilic, adjustments to temperature are not required. 
The 22°C bottle reactor required that the bacteria adjust to the conditions present limiting their 
functioning and methane production. Grant & Lin, (1995) shows in their study that mesophilic 
granules performed better at optimum temperatures (between 35°C - 40°C) than at lower 
temperatures as reflected here.

Discussion 
First period 
The 22°C reactor ran with only medium and acetate produced the maximum current of all three 
reactors in the first two phases. However, the subsequent anode material tests produced almost 
negligible voltages. The recovery of electricity production was expected with replacement of the 
carbon brush during phase 8 but this did not occur as voltages were below 0.1V. The bacteria may 
have been inhibited by the testing of the anodes and as such metabolic processes may have been 
limited. The 22°C DWW reactor performed much better than the 22°C G1 reactor. This is due to the 
extra source of microbes which enhanced the action of the MFC. 
In the cases where carbon brush was removed (G1 Phase 3 - 8 and G2 3&4), it can be said that the 
lack of voltage production is due to non-conductivity of the granules in opposition to the suggestion 
made by the study from (Morita et al., 2011). However, more tests are required to prove this theory.

The lack of electricity production can also be attributed to the reduced surface area. Rahimnejad et 
al., (2015)stated that adequate surface area is an essential for the proper functioning of the MFC. 
The study by Mustakeem, (2015) states that by increasing the surface area of the anode, electricity 
production can be increased exponentially. 
The use of only the metallic anode (G1 Phase 3 - 8 and G2 Phase 3 & 4) may also have contributed 
to the low production. Metals have been shown to have relatively low bio-compatibility 
(Mustakeem, 2015). Hou, et al (2015) showed that coating with other materials such carbon 
nanotube to make a composite anodic electrode greatly improves the bio-compatibility and 
consequently the power production. However, that is in contrast to the purpose of this section of the 
study which is to reduce the overall cost of the MFC by the reducing the amount of material needed 
for the anode. 
For Phase 4 for both G1 and G2 reactors, it was observed that the G2 reactor performed relatively 
better with highest voltage of 0.0145V compared to that of G1 0.00045V. The performance may be 
attributed to the already existing bacteria in the waste water (Logan, 2009).   
The G3 reactor which acted as control showed that electricity production using only the carbon 
brush provided relatively high voltages (0.39V). However, a longer enrichment phase is suggested 
for stabilization of production. In addition, extra anodic material such as steel as was the case in G1 
and G2 reactors may have enhanced the performance of this reactor. 
The testing of different electrodes was conducted to demonstrate the conductivity of the anaerobic 
granules. Morita et al., (2011) suggested the existence of electron transfer between methanogenic 
aggregates sourced from UASB reactor treating brewery waste. The results of this study indicate 
that, while there may be electrons present from oxidation, the granules may lack the conductivity 
required to transfer them to the electrode. It is also possible that given sufficient adaptation time, the 
voltage may have increased especially in the G2 reactor.  
Second Period 
In this case, the effect of temperature was very apparent between the G1 and G3 reactors. The G3 
reactor achieved relatively high voltages (0.55V) compared to that of the G1 reactor where the 
highest voltage produced was 0.388V. The G2 reactor showed the best performance in the study 
reaching maximum voltage of 0.64V even though it was run at lower than optimum temperatures. 
During organic loading tests, the DWW reactor was efficient in COD removal but had low 
coulombic efficiencies. This also reflected in the COD tests run on samples from G1 and G2 
reactors. 
While high COD removal is of significant value, high coulombic efficiency is preferred as well in 
MFC. As shown by Freguia et al., (2007), the low coulombic efficiency may be attributed to the use 
==PAGE:33==
 
32 
 
of the electrons for biomass growth. It is also possible that micro-organisms that have alternative 
electron accepters may exist in the granules or waste water and take up some of the electrons 
produced (Freguia et al., 2007).With regard to the continued electricity production even after the 
depletion of the COD, Freguia et al., (2007) suggests that there is a possibility of the microbes 
storing the energy produced and slowly releasing it with time. 
This storage of energy may have influenced the relatively lower COD removal experienced during 
the 1.5g/L test. The bacteria may have stored energy from the previous 2g/L test - which provided a 
high amount of substrate - causing the much slower uptake of substrate. 
The production of methane by the reactors was surprising as it was expected that the process of 
electricity production would inhibit the methanogenic function of the granules. However, Kouzuma, 
Kato, & Watanabe, (2015)suggests that methanogens such as Methanosaeta are capable of 
accepting electron via direct cell contact and conductive material possibly explaining the presence 
of methane in the reactor. This may also have acted as a sink for the electrons produced during 
oxidation leading to low coulombic efficiency(Morita et al., 2011).

Chapter 4 
Conclusion 
The study shows the anaerobic granule as a promising method of improving the performance of the 
MFC as a wastewater treatment system. Incorporation of the AGs led to the immobilization of the 
bacteria. The study also showed interesting results that speak to the viability of the use of AGs in 
MFCs: 
 The granules have the ability to exhibit electrogenic characteristics in addition to the 
methanogenic behavior 
 The conductive nature of the granules are limited especially with reduced electrode surface 
area 
The AGs have a high efficiency for treatment of wastewater similar to those in the UASB. 
However, they have lower coulombic efficiency indicating energy losses in the systems. 
 
Future work 
The study done, while impressive, is not exhaustive in its investigation of the use of the AGs in 
MFCs. In the interest of boosting the performance, further attention should be given to the 
following: 
 Investigation into the microbial communities to determine the cause of the electrogenic 
qualities displayed by the granules. 
 Combination of the granule with function-specific cultures to obtain specific product results 
in the MFC 
 A longer enrichment period is suggested for the 22°C and 37°C medium reactors as they 
may show improved performance with time.
