Executive Summary 
 
This thesis has been aimed at understanding the basic concepts of linear elastic fracture mechanics 
(LEFM) and using the concepts to attempt to correlate the results from a test campaign carried out 
on specimens taken from an actual blade with Finite Element (FE) calculations (Based on LEFM) to 
see if such correlations may be made and in future use the FE calculations to predict the crack 
propagation rates.  
This kind of work has been carried out before in several studies. In this instance the peculiarity is 
that the test samples from an actual blade which has seen some operational life have been taken. 
The methodology followed in the study has been used in other work carried out on varied types of 
sandwich composites. 
The various steps followed in the present work are listed below: 
1. Sandwich tear test (STT) on specimens from a real-life blade cross section essentially providing 
da/dN (crack growth rate) curves under fatigue loading for changing energy release rates. 
 
2. FE calculation using Crack Surface Displacement Extrapolation CSDE method were performed to 
calculate the energy release rates at different crack lengths (static analysis). The CSDE method is 
based on Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics approach to crack propagation.  
As no fracture characterization of the specimens in question has been performed, no fracture 
characterization parameters were available and hence a fatigue simulation of the fatigue testing 
using FE was not possible. 
Fracture characterization tests provide relationships between crack growth rate and strain 
energy release rate for a range of mode-mixites. These can be used as inputs to FE calculations 
based on which the crack increment for each cycle is determined. 
 
3. Presentation of the results from FE calculations and experimental results from the Sandwich tear 
tests at predefined crack lengths have been performed and trends which have been observed have 
been discussed.  
 
In Chapter 1, an introduction which provides the basis for the importance of investigating crack 
propagation at interfaces in the sandwich structures for wind turbine blades has been presented. 
 
In Chapter 2, an overview of linear elastic fracture mechanics has been presented and how it is 
relevant in the present work. 
 
In Chapter 3, an overview of different types of tests (that are commonly used) concerning different 
parameters important in studying crack propagation in sandwich structures has been provided. 
 
In Chapter 4 and 5, Sandwich tear test has been described in detail along with the tests carried out 
on the specimens from a wind turbine blade and results obtained during the testing. 
 
==PAGE:4==
 
III 
 
In Chapter 6, the FE modeling using the CSDE approach has been presented along with some 
results. 
 
In Chapter 7, the conclusions including the observed trends in the FE calculations and static and 
fatigue testing has been discussed. 
 
In Chapter 8, limitations in the present study and future work that could be done to highlight the 
fracture mechanics behavior of the interface crack propagation has been presented along with a 
hypothesis and proposed test campaign.

1. Introduction 
 
Wind turbine blades are one of the most severely loaded components of a wind turbine. Along with 
this, they are also one of the main constituents in the wind turbine costs. 
To make the wind turbine blades lighter and bigger for rising energy demands, sandwich structures 
are used extensively as load carrying members in a wind turbine blade design. 
 
Figure 1: Depiction of some important terminologies in wind turbine blades. Image from Maurizio Sala, 
investigating benefits of wind turbine blade retrofit reinforcements (2013-2014) 
As the sandwich structures provide a relatively high stiffness and strength to weight ratio, they are 
very useful in keeping the loads due to the self-weight of the blades low at the same time being able 
to take the external loads due to the wind. 
A generic blade cross section is shown below: 
 
Figure 2: Depiction of important load carrying members of a generic wind turbine blade cross section, Image taken 
from Bent F. Sørensen , John W. Holmes , Povl Brøndsted & Kim Branner, Blade Materials, Testing Methods and 
Structural Design  
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
 
A sandwich panel it consists of three distinct layers which are bonded together to form a load 
carrying section. Two thin sheets of in a laminate, the faces, are adhesively bonded to each side of a 
thick but considerably lighter core. The main benefits of using this kind of lay-up are the high 
stiffness and strength to weight ratios.  
American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) defines the sandwich structure in the following 
manner: 
"A structural sandwich is a special form of a laminated composite comprising of a combination of 
different materials that are bonded to each other so as to utilize the properties of each separate 
component to the structural advantage of the whole assembly." 
The main types of the core are corrugated, honeycomb, balsa and the foam cores. The corrugated 
cores are used in heavy industries like shipbuilding as well as packing industries. The honeycomb 
cores are more expensive and are used in aeronautical industries because of their superior 
performance compared to weight. The balsa and foam cores offer a reasonable performance 
compared to price and are widely used in maritime structures and wind turbine blades. 
Blades may undergo loading/unloading cycles in the order of 100 million in an operational lifetime 
of 20 years. This leads to several types of failures in the blades. 
Some of these failures have been depicted in the picture below: 
 
Figure 3: Depiction of some common types of wind turbine blade failures, Image from Bent F. Sørensen , John W. 
Holmes , Povl Brøndsted & Kim Branner, Blade Materials, Testing Methods and Structural Design 
The Sandwich sections primarily take the edgewise loading of the blade in addition to the 
aerodynamic loads. These sections are generally loaded with repetitive compression tension cycles. 
This repetitive loading may cause diverse types of failures in the sandwich structure. 
 
 
==PAGE:12==
 
3 
 
Some of these failures have been listed below: 
1. Failure or localized yielding at the core of the sandwich material due to tensile, compressive and 
shear loading. 
2. The sandwich interface with the laminate which can also be called a ‘debonding’ of face-sheet and 
core.  
3. Buckling failure of the sandwich structure in the form of wrinkling, shear crimpling and face 
dimpling. 
In the present work, the face-sheet to core delamination is being studied. The wind turbine blades 
are fairly loaded in the edgewise and flap-wise directions. 
 
 
Figure 4: The edgewise and flap-wise loading directions for a wind turbine blade, Source, 
https://gaiasol.org/2016/06/27/biggest-wind-turbine-ever-to-be-built/ 
These edgewise and flap-wise loads transform into compressive loads at the sandwich panels 
which in turn would result in the caving in and out (also known as breathing) of the max-chord 
section of the blades.  
 
Figure 5: Bending due to 3-point loading of a curved sandwich panel. Image from Maurizio Sala, investigating 
benefits of wind turbine blade retrofit reinforcements (2013-2014) 
The figure 5, shows the effect of three-point loading on the compression and tension. Breathing of a 
blade section is exactly the opposite of this where the compression and tension forces in the skin 
result in the bending of the panel producing forces perpendicular to the panel.

The repetitive breathing of these panels in addition to the compressive loads might propagate an 
already existing delamination between the face-sheet and core or initiate a new debonding.

Adhesives joints exhibit complex failure modes due to complex loading and susceptibility to 
manufacturing defects (This excerpt is taken from the DNVGL standard on wind turbine blades (Ref: 
DNVGL-ST-0376).  The following failures are considered: 
1. Adhesive/adherent interface failure: This failure is characterized by the failure at the adhesive and the 
adherent interface, i.e. interface failure. An interface failure is one of weakest failure modes and should 
be avoided. This failure mode is caused by poor adherent preparation and/or incompatible adhesive, 
among other causes. An interface crack may subsequently be loaded by a mix of shear and peeling 
stresses. 
 
2. Adhesive failure: This failure is characterized by the failure at the adhesive, i.e. cohesive failure. 
Adhesives can have a nonlinear behavior with large strains to failure, thus it may experience ductile 
failure. 
 
3. Adherent failure: This failure is characterized by the failure of the adherent, i.e. the adhesive is 
stronger than the adherents.  
The failure of the sandwich interface between the face-sheet and core has been widely studied with 
the fracture mechanics approach. A wind turbine blade sandwich structure is subjected to mixed 
mode loading. A crack/flaw/debond in one part of the blade may not be as problematic as being in 
another part of the blade which is subjected to more critical loads. 
Face-sheet to core debonding is one of the most important failure modes for sandwich structures. 
What makes these kinds of defects more likely in the wind turbine blades is the fact that the 
debonds may be introduced during production, operation or extreme load events like impacts 
during installation or transportation. 
This makes it important for wind turbine operators to continuously monitor the structural health of 
the wind turbine blades in terms of debonding. 
Moreover, it is also a kind of failure which is cannot be visually detected and hence methods such as 
tap test must be used to find if there is a delamination. The tap test method is a Non Destructive 
==PAGE:15==
 
6 
 
Testing (NDT) technique which involves impacting on the surface of a component with a small 
hammer. The excitation of the component produced creates a sound that can be heard from the test 
piece. 
If a defect such as a delamination is present, a duller sound will be heard which due influence on 
the damping of the impact will be caused due to the presence of the defect.  
If the inspector must deduce the extent/presence of defect based on their hearing alone, it becomes 
a very unreliable method in terms of the quality of data it produces. There are however techniques 
where the vibration can be measured and then post processed (based on calibrations) to quantify 
the extent of the damage. 
There are also techniques available and researched using electromagnetic imaging techniques. 
But still, all these require a very thorough schedule of inspection on the wind turbine blades from 
time to time which are costly as well as the wind turbines must be stopped to carry out these tests. 
The inspection is further complicated by the fact that only one side of the blade (outside) is freely 
available. A lot of locations on the inside are still non-accessible.  
Localized failures in the sandwich panels of the blades may cause the entire blades to fail if the 
failures reach a critical size and are present at critical locations. This results in downtime of wind 
turbines in addition to very high costs of blade replacement if possible. 
During the induction of a new blade, manufacturers of the wind turbine and blades must generally 
submit the blades to testing and manufacturing evaluations (along with detailed design 
verifications) to verify if the blades can be manufactured with the specified quality and if they can 
withstand the ‘equivalent’ fatigue and extreme loads. 
To this end, several tests are generally carried out (on the first manufactured blade) as required by 
various wind turbine blade standards like DNV-DS-J102 (Design and Manufacture of Wind Turbine 
Blades, Offshore and Onshore Wind Turbines) and DNV GL -ST-0376(Rotor blades for wind 
turbines). 
These tests include: 
1. Static tests carried out at loads higher than the extreme loads that the blades ‘would’ experience in 
their operational life. 
2. Fatigue tests carried out equivalent loads for a few million cycles that represent the equivalent 
operational lifetime of 20-25 years of the blade. 
3. Post fatigue static tests carried out at the same level as the first static tests. 
In addition to these, manufacturers/designers of blades carry out coupon testing to confirm the 
mechanical properties of the constituent laminates and cores. 
The evaluation is successfully concluded mainly by attributing the non-failure of the entire blade 
during the testing phase along with the supporting analytical and finite element design calculations 
performed on the blade. 
Even after the afore-mentioned testing has been carried out, it is difficult to assess the blade 
damage as the available NDT evaluations are still very limited in detection of a smaller failure. 
==PAGE:16==
 
7 
 
This limitation means that the manufacturers of the blades have to consider design approaches 
which are based either on infinite life, safe life, damage tolerant or a combination of the above. This 
results in bulking up the blade design making the blades heavier and costlier. 
Due to the push on lowering the renewable energy costs, manufacturers and wind park operators 
are constantly pursuing better and more accurate ways to predict and if possible avoid failures in 
wind turbine blades and also optimize the designs with respect to costs. 
Several studies have highlighted that debonding considerably reduces the load carrying capacity of 
sandwich structures (Berggreen et al., 2005).  The effect of debonding on the residual lifetime and 
structural performance of the blade and the wind turbine is also an important field. Studies 
focusing on methods which might be able to help predict the propagation of debonds in sandwich 
structures have been carried out e.g. Kardomateas and Huang (2003). 
Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM) has been quite commonly used to model the debond

first law of thermodynamics states the following regarding the conservation of energy: 
“In a thermodynamic process involving a closed system, the increment in the internal energy is equal 
to the difference between the heat accumulated by the system and the work done by it.”  
Clausius, R. (1850), p. 384, equation (IIa.) 
Griffith in 1920 applied this statement of the law of thermodynamics to cracks. He stated that crack

according to him was the critical condition of the fracture propagation.

The relative opening and sliding displacements may be found from post processing of finite element 
simulations and can be used in the calculation of energy release rates and mode mixity phase

However, in an interface both the strain energy release rate and the phase angle close to the crack 
tip behave in an oscillatory manner (Williams, 1959), see Figure 16. This oscillation is physically 
impossible since it would mean that the upper and lower surfaces of the crack will wrinkle and 
penetrate each other close to the crack tip.  
It is shown that the extent of the oscillatory region is of the order of 10-6 of the crack length 
(Erdogan, 1963). England (1965) determined the distance from the crack tip, which after the first 
‘mathematical’ interpenetration occurs in the order of 10-4 of the crack length. This mathematical 
error needs to be avoided for determination of realistic mode-mixity and strain energy release rate. 
Berggreen et al. (2005) compared different numerical methods for avoiding this mathematical 
oscillatory error, including the Virtual Crack Extension method (Parks, 1974, and Hellen, 1975) and 
the Virtual Crack Closure technique (Beuth, 1996). Furthermore, Berggreen (2005) developed the 
Crack Surface Displacement Extrapolation (CSDE) method for avoiding this imaginary oscillation. 
The CSDE method is schematically presented in Figure 16.  
The CSDE method is based on the observation that the variation of mode-mixity phase angle and 
energy release rate are both linear in the K dominated region with respect to the proximity to the 
crack tip.  
This linear variation is used to extrapolate the mode-mixity phase angle and the energy release rate 
at the crack tip position and avoid the oscillatory part. 
The CSDE method is used in this thesis for determination of the mode-mixity phase angle and 
energy release rate.

3. Types of test methodologies available 
 
There are different types of tests that have been researched over the last decade to approach the 
fatigue and static strength evaluation of sandwich structures with foam core materials used in wind 
turbine blades. 
These tests are primarily to evaluate the mechanical properties of the constituents of a sandwich 
specimen like core and face sheets. Some of these tests have also been used to evaluate 
failures/cracks in the interfaces between face-sheets and cores. 
Some of the most commonly used tests for each have been described below briefly along with the 
possibilities they provide in exploring the materials in a sandwich structure: 
1. Compression test: This is based on ASTM D1621-73 standard and is primarily a test method for 
foam core compressive strength evaluation. This is a static evaluation method and does not 
consider the sandwich structure as a whole. This test method is used by suppliers of foam cores to 
specify the compressive modulus and compressive strength of the specific foam core material. 
 
2. Tensile test: This is based on ASTM D1623-78 standard and is primarily a test method for foam 
core tensile strength evaluation. This is a static evaluation method and does not consider the 
sandwich structure. This test method is used by suppliers of foam cores to specify the tensile 
modulus and tensile strength of the specific foam core material. 
 
3. Block shear test: This test method is used for evaluating the shear strength properties of sandwich 
panels. This is described as per the standard ASTM C273-61. 
 
4. Four-point bending beam (FPB) test:  This test setup makes it possible for controlling the 
transverse force in the outer span hence keeping the shear stress in the core constant. Also, the 
setup ensures that the inner span has a fixed bending moment and no transverse force. This test 
provides a way to calculate the flexural stiffness of the face and core materials of a sandwich panel.

4. Experimental Work: 
 
STT (Static Tear Test) is a method of experimental structural evaluation of the effect of loading on a 
pre-existing crack in a sandwich specimen. 
The pre-existing crack is introduced between the core and the skin. The face-sheet is then pulled 
away from the core on one side to propagate the de-bond. 
This approach has also been used in Ref: [1] and [10]. 
 
In the present experimental campaign, the STT on specimens taken from a section of a wind turbine 
blade has been conducted in static and fatigue loading. 
 
The test campaign was performed at the workshop situated at the Technical University of Denmark 
(DTU) Lyngby campus. 
 
In the STT setup, the sandwich specimen is constrained at both ends in all directions and at the 
center of the specimen in all directions. A complete de-bond is introduced on one side of the 
specimen leaving the other side with a small pre-crack. De-bond for a sandwich is defined as the de 
bonding between the core and the face. 
 
For static test, the skin is pulled apart from the core in the test setup. During the operation, 
displacements in the vertical direction are measured along with the applied loading. 
The loading applied is in the form of a continuously applied vertical displacement on the face-sheet. 
 
For fatigue test, the skin is pulled apart and brought back to original position as soon as a pre-
defined maximum load is reached. This is done at a frequency that is decided based on factors like 
time, resources and how many cycles the study is aimed at. In the present case, a frequency of 2Hz 
was used.

6. Finite element formulation of CSDE 
 
The CSDE method has been implemented in an Ansys APDL routine to calculate the energy release 
rates and mode mixity angles. 
The basis of this modeling approach can be found in Ref: [3]. 
The code was adapted to an STT test setup. 
In this thesis, only a limited part of the code was implemented assuming that the crack only 
propagates at the face and core interface. This assumption is acceptable as in the static as well as 
fatigue test, the same behavior was observed. 
The FE model is prepared with face-sheet and core with the help of Plane183 elements. The 
element size near crack tip is kept at a size of 5microns. The boundary conditions have been 
defined based on the STT setup.

7. Conclusions  
 
In the present work carried as part of the flexible master’s thesis, samples from an actual blade 
which has undergone some operational lifetime have been tested using the STT approach under 
fatigue as well as static loading along with finite element calculations to calculate energy release 
rate and mode mixity phase angle for pre-defined crack lengths under constant loading. 
 
7.1 Discussion on experimental results 
 
The static results on the specimen shows a clear trend of an increase in the required load to 
propagate the crack with increasing crack length. This happens primarily due to the applied load 
being utilized more in stretching the laminate face sheet than opening the crack. 
 
Figure 73: Static test result 
Similar result trends have also been shown in other work carried out on different kinds of core 
and face sheet combinations [10]. 
The fatigue experiments showed that there was significant variation between the samples under 
fatigue loading. 
==PAGE:63==
 
54 
 
 
Figure 74: Comparison of test experimental data 
Please note that the specimens for fatigue testing had varying lengths of initial crack.  
The main concept in the fracture mechanics approach that has been studied is considering how the 
crack would grow based on mode mixites, critical energy release rates and actual energy release 
rate. 
When the result from the static test is observed, it is observed that the load required for 
propagation of crack increases with the increase in crack length. This happens because the mode 
mixity changes with the change in crack length.  
But, when we look at the rate of crack growth for fatigue loaded samples, although a trend of 
decreasing rate of crack growth is observed with the increase in crack length, there still are jumps 
in the rate of crack growth at some locations.  
This could be due to various reasons: 
1. The first reason that comes to mind is that there is incomplete adhesion at the interface at some 
locations and this can be attributed to the manufacturing process variability. 
 
2. The 2nd reason could be that due to the load cycles (during the operational life) or environmental 
degradation, the bonds have different strengths at different locations. This is of course difficult to 
quantify within the scope of this study. Also, since the samples are taken from the same panel, they 
should match in these criteria with each other in these aspects.

8. Limitations and future work: 
 
The current experimental campaign and numerical study suffered from limitations. The most 
important limitations have been highlighted below so that in future, further work may be carried 
out considering the lessons that have been learnt during the present work. 
1. Due to the practical aspects of the timeline and limited availability for the specimens, it was not 
possible to carry out an experimental validation of the material properties for the face sheet and 
core. This makes the comparison with the FE calculations not very accurate. 
This is something that can be carried out in future studies. 
 
2. For the ease of the experimental setup, STT approach was used which although is not an issue but, 
does not provide a direct relation to the compressive loads that the sandwich structures in blades 
experience during operation. To make this correlation possible, further testing could be carried out 
where the vertical load (due to breathing of sandwich sections) is observed under the application of 
a compressive load. 
 
3. Models for the cumulation of damage (in terms of critical energy release rates) for specific mode 
mixites can be made using the fracture characterization approach as carried out in [10] and [14]. 
This relation can then be introduced as an input in the FE calculation to calculate the crack 
propagation based on the calculated energy release rate. 
 
4. The fatigue loading was based on load control. This was the easiest to implement in the available 
test control setup. A more complicated test control setup which involves control of energy release 
rate could be programmed using the correlation with the FE calculation. This could potentially 
provide with more qualitative data on the cycles to damage based on a specific energy release rate. 
 
5. Generally, a higher number of specimens would be advisable so that a good statistical basis for the 
data can be formed which can be used to make the conclusions more reliable using available 
statistical methods. 
A comprehensive study involving the above-mentioned activities could provide a FE based 
approach supported with some experimental testing for the crack growth prediction under 
operational loads for different blade sandwich sections. 
 This potentially can be used as an input in a maintenance program scheduling.

8.1 A proposal based on the difference between the static sample results and fatigue 
sample results. 
 
As discussed in conclusions, higher rates of crack propagation are seen at certain crack lengths 
where the expected trend would be of an exponential decrease in the da/dN curve with respect to 
increasing crack length. 
This observation could be attributed to various reasons at the structural level. 
But adhesive bonds might have failures at a micro structural level too (in its scale) subjected to the 
Mode II and Mode III loading cycles. Due to its scale, effect of such microstructural failures is

specimen variability in results. But, it would be quite interesting to be able to quantify the effect of 
microstructural failure at the structural level.

manufacturing to make them virtually identical to each other.
