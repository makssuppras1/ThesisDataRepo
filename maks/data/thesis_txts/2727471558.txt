Abstract 
This project focus on the study of new ways to ventilate a dwelling. In order to do this, it 
have been necessary to analyse how the normal dwellings are ventilated nowadays and try 
to find a new solution to optimize the ventilation system that suits to the lifestyle of the 
residents of the dwelling. 
The focus will be to see how the air concentration levels of CO2 change by applying a model 
of cascade ventilation respect of a dwelling with normal ventilation and compare the results 
for different dwelling layouts and hosts lifestyles. 
To determine if the introduction of cascade ventilation in family dwellings would be 
successful, different layouts dwellings and family lifestyles have been studied. The results 
reflect how the CO2 concentrations in the bedrooms were so much lower with the 
implementation of the cascade ventilation because of the higher air flow that was flowing 
through this rooms. However, the CO2 concentrations on the living room was similar in both 
cases, so we can conclude that the implementation of cascade ventilation would be 
successful in these conditions. 
To fulfil the previous results, CFD simulations replicating the same model have been carried 
out. The results showed that that the hypothesis of a full mixed air model was not true, 
especially in the living room, where the results of the simulations showed poor ventilated 
and proving that the cascade ventilation would not be as effective as the previous 
experiments had shown.

1. Introduction 
This project has the purpose to introduce the concept of the cascade ventilation and study 
how this would affect in a family dwelling.  
1.1. Motivation 
The main reason to be of this project is to find new ways to improve the ventilation system 
of the residential buildings, trying to find a new solution that provides a more efficient way 
to ventilate a dwelling reducing duct work and simplifying the ventilation system. 
That would be a good solution to try to reduce the energy consumption of the residential 
buildings, since the ventilation system is necessary to guarantee a good air quality inside 
the building and represents a high percentage of the total energy consumption of a family 
dwelling. Furthermore, the cost of housing will be reduced and that would be very useful 
with the global increasing climate crisis especially in the Nordic countries like Denmark, 
where the cost of the energy has increased a lot in the last few years.   
1.2. Scope  
This project will focus in studying the ventilation systems of family dwellings excluding other 
type of buildings such as offices and industrial buildings, maybe the conclusions retrieved 
from this project could be extrapolated for other type of buildings, but this is out of scope for 
this project. 
Also, the study will focus just in the mechanical ventilation of the building, considering that 
the dwelling has not natural ventilation so the influenece of the shape and orientation of the 
building will not be relevant, neither the wind effect. 
The climatization of the dwelling is also out of scope for this project, considering an 
isothermal situation where the temperature is the same inside and outside of the building. 
1.3. Objectives 
The main goal of the project is to prove if the introduction of the cascade ventilation would 
be possible in a family dwelling. The idea is to study the CO2 levels inside the building and 
see if these levels will be compatible with the cohabitation without being a risk for the health 
of the residents.

2. Theorical background 
Ventilation in the design of a residential building is necessary and indispensable to ensure 
the quality of the indoor air, reducing the number of particles in suspension and thus 
reducing the possibility of spreading contagious diseases such as Covid-19. In most 
countries there are regulations governing the operation of ventilation systems in buildings, 
which determine the minimum conditions that must be met by the indoor air in a dwelling. 
In Denmark, all these parameters are stipulated according to BR18 - 22, in particular DS 
447, which deals with ventilation in buildings, specifying the necessary parameters 
depending on the type of ventilation and the use of the building. 
There are different ways to ventilate a room, this can be done naturally through windows 
and openings in the facade, taking advantage of the natural phenomena of convection and 
pressure difference. However, the most common and effective way to ventilate a building 
is through mechanical ventilation, which is based on the injection of outside air through the 
implementation of mechanical fans that drive and extract air from the different rooms of a 
house through ducts that make up the ventilation system. Mechanical ventilation allows 
greater control over the air flow and ventilation rate of the building, which is sometimes 
necessary in spaces where it is of great importance to ensure good indoor air quality such 
as in a hospital, however it also involves an extra cost in energy as the fans in charge of 
moving the air through the ventilation system need constant power supply to ensure proper 
ventilation of the building.  
Most homes have a hybrid ventilation system, which is composed of a mechanical 
ventilation system as mentioned above combined with natural ventilation through windows, 
in this way it is possible to have a controlled ventilation without increasing excessively the 
energy cost of the home, since when using natural ventilation it is not necessary that the 
ventilation system is constantly running.

2.1. Mechanical ventilation 
The mechanical ventilation system of a dwelling consists of a controlled injection and/or 
extraction of outside air into the rooms.  
These systems can be more or less complex as required, there are different types of 
ventilation systems depending on the use of the space to be ventilated. One way is 
mechanical ventilation by overpressure, which consists of a single external air inlet that 
injects fresh air into the room to be ventilated, this produces an increase in the pressure in 
the room creating a pressure differential between the inside of the room and the outside, in 
this way the air inside the room will come out naturally to the outside through openings or 
grilles thanks to this difference in pressure between inside and outside, without the need to 
place a mechanical extraction system consisting of a fan responsible for removing the 
excess air to the outside. This system allows total control over the entry of air into the room, 
which is why over-pressure ventilation is widely used in places such as car parks or fire 
escapes, as in the event of a fire it prevents smoke from entering the room. 
 
 
Figure 1. Over pressurized ventilation system [1] 
 
There is also the possibility of ventilation in the opposite way, without the need of an external 
air injection system, but only with a mechanical ventilation system by depression, with a fan 
in charge of extracting internal air to the outside in a controlled way, causing a negative 
pressure difference with respect to the outside, in this way the external air enters the room 
through openings, contrary to what happens with overpressure. This system allows a 
controlled exhaust of the interior air, this is why it is used in kitchens and bathrooms as it 
allows a controlled exhaust of the pollutants generated in these rooms. 
==PAGE:10==
Pg. 10 
 
Report 
 
 
Figure 2. Underpressurized ventilation system [1] 
 
In most dwellings there are currently mechanical ventilation systems with simultaneous 
injection and extraction, combining the phenomena of depression and overpressure. 
Normally, rooms with sources of pollution and bad smells such as kitchens and bathrooms 
incorporate depression ventilation systems, and in most countries there are regulations that 
require a minimum amount of indoor air extraction flow in these rooms, in Denmark the 
regulations require a minimum extraction flow of 20 l/s in kitchens and 15 l/s in bathrooms 
and toilets, however, unlike other rooms in the house, these rooms are not required to have 
outside air intake. 
In other rooms such as bedrooms and living rooms, according to Danish regulations, fresh 
air must be supplied directly from the outside. For this reason, and to ensure proper 
ventilation, most rooms other than bathrooms and kitchens have both air inlets and outlets 
to the outside. 
In conclusion, it can be stated that in general most dwellings incorporate a balanced 
mechanical ventilation system, with the existence of air injection and extraction openings 
that ensure a constant and controlled flow of both incoming and outgoing air. 
In most of these mechanical ventilation systems, a heat recovery system is incorporated, 
which consists of indirectly bringing the air inlet and outlet flows of the dwelling into contact 
with each other, thus producing a heat exchange between the inlet and outlet that allows 
energy savings in the building's air-conditioning system, especially in situations where the 
temperature difference between the indoor and outdoor air is significant. This is why in 
countries with extreme climates, such as Denmark, the regulations stipulate the mandatory 
installation of these heat recovery systems.

2.2. Cascade Ventilation 
The principle of cascade ventilation presents a significant opportunity to decrease costs 
related to investment and operation. As a result, it is highly appealing for the implementation 
of uncomplicated and reliable ventilation systems in sustainable buildings. 
The fundamental concept involves designing the living room as a dedicated overflow area, 
while only installing supply air nozzles in the bedrooms. This approach optimizes the 
utilization of supply air, allowing for reduced supply air rates while still ensuring satisfactory 
indoor air quality for various floor plan configurations. However, certain uncertainties 
remain, particularly concerning the effective mixing of fresh air from the supply air rooms 
with the air in the living room, especially in specific living room layouts such as those with 
opposing supply air and extract air room doors. Both the results of computational fluid 
dynamics (CFD) simulations and measurements confirm the occurrence of short-circuit 
flows between the overflow openings under many of the worst-case boundary conditions 
examined. 
So, as it stands, the effectiveness of cascade ventilation is intricately linked to the geometry 
of the house in which it is applied, as it can work very well for some house layouts but have 
the opposite effect in others. The key to ensuring that cascade ventilation works correctly 
and has the desired effect is to ensure the correct airflow throughout the house, from the 
air intake point in the bedrooms to the exhaust areas such as kitchens and bathrooms. It is 
therefore important to ensure that the outside air flow is mixed correctly with the inside air 
and flows through the house avoiding recirculation and areas of poor ventilation to avoid 
the concentration of pollutants in certain areas of the house, especially in the bedrooms and 
living room which is where the inhabitants of the house spend most of their time.

2.3. Basic concepts of ventilation 
As discussed above, the main objective of ventilation of a human occupied space is to 
ensure a good indoor air quality by controlling the CO2 levels in the space.  
This is related with the ton of fresh air coming through a space, which must be determined 
by the volume of the space. The ventilation flow or ventilation rate refers to the total airflow 
or amount of outside air that enters and exits a space simultaneously, this value (usually 
given in l/s) is calculated according to the size of the space to be ventilated and the expected 
occupancy of the space.  
This gives rise to the concept of the air renewal rate, which gives information on how quickly 
the air in a space is completely renewed and is calculated as follows. 
 
𝑨𝑪𝑹=  𝑽𝒆𝒏𝒕𝒊𝒍𝒂𝒕𝒊𝒐𝒏 𝒓𝒂𝒕𝒆 (𝒍/𝒔) ×  𝟑𝟔𝟎𝟎 (𝒔/𝒉) ⁄ 𝒓𝒐𝒐𝒎 𝒗𝒐𝒍𝒖𝒎𝒆 (𝒎𝟑) ×  𝟏𝟎𝟎𝟎 (𝒍/𝒎𝟑) 
Equation 1. Air change rate [3] 
The air change rate gives a value in h.1, which refers to the number of times the air in the 
room is completely changed in one hour. This value is very useful to know how well-
ventilated a space and can compare the ventilation of spaces of different sizes directly, 
which is why the air change rate is used as a standard value to determine the ventilation 
needs of a certain space, it is estimated that from an air change rate value of 1 – 1.5 h-1 it 
is considered that the space enjoys a correct ventilation.

3. Dwelling Configuration 
As discussed above, the implementation of cascade ventilation does not work the same for 
all dwellings as the layout of the rooms in the house is crucial in determining whether 
cascade ventilation can be implemented successfully or not. 
3.1. Common family dwellings 
In order to see if the implementation of cascade ventilation would be feasible on a large 
scale as an advancement of current ventilation systems, it is necessary to carry out a study 
on different types of standard family houses of similar size, to see what are the 
characteristics that a house must have in order to be able to implement cascade ventilation 
in it. 
Three types of family houses with different layouts have been chosen to see which of them 
would be the best candidate to implement cascade ventilation, and in which ones it would 
not be feasible. 
Dwelling 1 
Family house 
2 Bedrooms, 1 Bath, Open kitchen + Living room. 
Area: 90 m2 
This house has 2 bedrooms on one half of the house situated on both sides separated by 
one bathroom in the middle, and the other half of the dwelling is composed by a large open 
space where the kitchen and the living room are placed without any separation between 
each other. This concept of an open kitchen sharing the same space with the living room 
has become popular in modern houses in the last few years, especially in Nordic countries 
like Denmark.

As already mentioned, when implementing cascade ventilation, the air intake is through the 
rooms and the exhaust is from the kitchen and bathroom. In this way the incoming fresh air 
flows through the rooms into the corridor from both sides. A part of this air flows into the 
bathroom and exits through the extractor fan, the remaining air flow is distributed between 
the kitchen and the living room and exits through the kitchen extractor fan.

Here it could be the case that the living room would not be sufficiently ventilated because 
of the poor flow it would receive, as most of the fresh air flowing from the hallway into the 
shared space of the kitchen and living room would go directly into the kitchen exhaust fan. 
This could result in the flow of fresh air not reaching the top of the living room, where 
recirculation would occur, producing a poorly ventilated area. Thus, when residents were in 
the living room, dangerous levels of CO2 would accumulate in the upper zone.

Here we have a house with a central passageway leading to 2 bedrooms on one side and 
a bathroom followed by a kitchen on the other. The corridor leads into the living room, which 
is the largest room in the house, making it more difficult to ventilate properly. 
In this case, the outside air would enter through the bedrooms and flow through the corridor 
into the kitchen and bathroom, where it would exit again to the outside through the extractor 
fans located in these rooms. With this behaviour, the ventilation flow would not flow through 
the living room, as the living room is located at one end of the house and is not situated 
between the air inlet and the outlet which is the natural path that the air circulation inside 
the house would follow.  
This is a case of a house in which it would not be advisable to implement cascade ventilation 
as the living room would not be properly ventilated due to poor ventilation flow. In addition, 
the living room is a room where the occupants of the dwelling conduct most of their activities 
within the dwelling and, being a family dwelling, it is the place where the whole family 
gathers. For this reason, being an area where there would be one or more sources of 
pollution constantly, poor ventilation would lead to extremely high levels of CO2 
concentration in this area of the house.

If we analyse the layout of this house, we can see that the bedrooms are located at different 
ends of the house, which means that the air inlets are distributed throughout the house, this 
improves the ventilation flow and helps the outside air to mix better with the air inside the 
house. Another advantage is that the living room area is located in the central part of the 
house, as having air inlets and outlets on both sides means that most of the ventilation flow 
will be through the living room, which means that the air in the living room will be constantly 
renewed, thus reducing the CO2 concentration levels inside the living room. It can also be 
assumed that the air outlets are on both sides of the house, located in the bathrooms and 
the laundry room, which also favours the circulation of air through all the rooms of the house. 
Finally, it can be concluded that the layout of this house favours the phenomenon of 
cascade ventilation as all rooms are spacious and all of them receive ventilation flow.

3.2. Ideal Layout 
After reviewing different types of homes, analysing their advantages and disadvantages, 
the layout of a single-family home of similar size and shape to those previously seen has 
been designed, incorporating all the strong points to ensure that the implementation of 
cascade ventilation has the greatest possible impact thanks to the configuration of the 
house. 
The main conclusion that can be drawn from the previously seen homes is that the air inlets 
and outlets should be on opposite sides so that the air flows from one end of the house to 
the other, passing through all the rooms. This avoids areas with poor ventilation. For this 
reason, the design of the house will have the bedrooms grouped together at one end and 
the kitchen and bathrooms at the other, with the living room in between to ensure that it 
enjoys good ventilation since it is the only room in the house without any air inlets or outlets. 
Therefore, it is crucial for the air to flow from the other rooms. 
Another aspect to consider is how the rooms are connected to each other, that is, how they 
are distributed within the house and whether they are directly connected or not. In order for 
the incoming air through the bedrooms to flow only to the living room and then be able to 
exit through the kitchen and bathrooms to the outside, a layout is chosen where all the 
rooms are directly connected to the living room but not to each other. This ensures that the

promote proper air mixing and avoid areas of poor ventilation. The presence of corridors 
and halls to connect the rooms has also been avoided, as they hinder the circulation of air 
through the rooms.

4. Ventilation’s simulations 
Once the impact of the configuration of the dwelling on the performance of the cascade 
ventilation has been studied, we will proceed to study how it would work in a real case. 
A series of simulations will be carried out in 2 different housing configurations for different 
lifestyles of its inhabitants. The objective is to know the CO2 levels in the different rooms of 
the house throughout the course of the day. 
These simulations will be carried out according to the theoretical principles explained in 
section 2.3. As these equations can only be applied under certain conditions, the 
assumptions for the simulations are as follows. 
 
• 
The dwelling only works with mechanical ventilation. 
• 
The ventilation system works without stopping. 
• 
The dwelling has no air leaks to the outside (Winter case), also there is considered 
no leaks between separate rooms (Just if it’s made on purpose). 
• 
There is a perfect mix of air between the fresh outdoor and the inside air, so the 
CO2 concentration in a single room is constant. 
• 
The only sources of air pollution inside the dwelling are the people. 
• 
The temperature is the same in all the rooms of the house, and the intake air has 
the same temperature as the inside, set at 20 Celsius degrees. 
 
 
The simulations will be carried out with a spreadsheet such as Excel and then CONTAM 
software will be used to verify the results. This software allows the construction of a model 
of the dwelling by defining air inlets and outlets, openings in the interior and exterior walls, 
and sources of pollution.  
In addition, it also allows to set operating schedules for the air inlets and outlets and the 
sources of pollution. In this way it is possible to simulate the life of a family during the 
different hours of the day, establishing different schedules for each of the elements that 
form part of the simulation.

In this case, as mentioned above, the ventilation of the dwelling is exclusively mechanical,

4.3. Simulation conclusions 
When analysing the results obtained from both models, it can be concluded that in general 
cascade ventilation gives better results than conventional ventilation. This trend is true 
regardless of the geometry of the dwelling, as the same behaviour is observed in the two 
models despite the fact that they are completely different concepts. In these we can see 
how in cascade ventilation the CO2 concentrations reached in the bedrooms are much lower 
when cascade ventilation is applied compared to conventional ventilation, as the entire 
ventilation flow of the house passes through these, thus the ventilation ratio is much higher 
ensuring adequate ventilation for these rooms, where the inhabitants of the house spend 
most of the hours. 
Another observable trend in both models is the behaviour of the CO2 concentration in the 
living room, since, if we compare the evolution of the pollutant levels throughout the day 
between cascade ventilation and conventional ventilation, we observe many similarities 
between the two, although slightly lower concentrations are observed throughout the day 
in conventional ventilation with respect to cascade ventilation, the maximum levels reached 
are very similar. This is due to the fact that the ventilation flow into the living room is the 
same in both cases, although in the case of conventional ventilation most of the air enters 
directly from the outside through a fan, whereas in cascade ventilation it flows from the 
bedrooms dragging the pollutants from the bedrooms. 
When comparing the results between the two simulated models, it can be seen that the first 
model has better results in CO2 levels, this was to be expected as the geometry of this 
model was specially designed to favour cascade ventilation. In the first model the living 
room receives at all times the total ventilation flow of the house as it is located between the 
air inlets and outlets, unlike the model based on the open concept, where all the air flows 
through the hall and is distributed among the rest of the rooms, where only a percentage of 
this air flows into the living room. 
It should be noted that the conclusions drawn from the above results are subject to the 
assumptions mentioned at the beginning of the chapter, therefore, these statements are 
true if it is considered that the air in each room is perfectly mixed and the concentrations 
are uniform, otherwise the results obtained could be very different.

5. CFD simulations 
To check whether the results obtained in the previous chapter would be applicable to reality, 
it is necessary to use computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software to replicate a model like 
the one used in the previous simulations and see whether the hypotheses set out above 
are fulfilled. 
The software used for the CFD simulations is FLUENT from ANSYS, this software is based 
on the finite element method which consists of the discretisation of the model through a 
mesh formed by different cells of variable shape and size depending on the model to be 
simulated. 
In this case, we have opted for a simulation of the ideal geometry model presented in 
chapter 3.2, as this model has proved to be the best for the implementation of cascade 
ventilation according to the results of the simulations carried out in the previous chapter. 
The aim of these simulations is to see how the incoming air flows from the bedrooms to the 
rest of the rooms and how it is distributed within the rooms, in this way it is possible to see 
if the air is mixed correctly and to detect areas of poor ventilation or possible recirculation. 
In addition, the software allows to place sources of pollution within the model and to analyse 
how the pollutant is distributed throughout the house.

5.3. Simulation parameters 
With the discretised model, the mesh is exported to Fluent. 
 
Figure 37. Model in Fluent 
Once the model has been generated in Fluent, it is necessary to establish certain 
parameters before carrying out the simulation calculation. The first step is to define the 
properties of the fluid to be used in the simulation. Fluent has a database with different 
compounds with their respective properties, such as air, which will be the fluid used in the 
simulation. 
Subsequently, the boundary conditions of the surfaces that make up the model are defined, 
in this case it is necessary to define the parameters of the inlet surfaces such as the fluid 
inlet velocity, as well as the turbulent intensity and the hydraulic diameter. The inlet 
temperature is also defined, which is set at 20 degrees Celsius, as well as the interior of the 
house, since an isothermal situation is considered throughout the model without thermal 
diffusion. In this case, it is not necessary to define the other surfaces, since by default they 
are considered as impermeable and do not allow the fluid to pass through, and since the 
temperature is the same throughout the model, there is no heat transfer between surfaces, 
therefore, it is not necessary to establish thermal diffusion coefficients. 
==PAGE:56==
Pg. 56 
 
Report 
 
In this case, a steady-state simulation has been carried out, i.e. it does not evolve as a 
function of time as those carried out previously, but rather the software performs 
calculations from initial values until the values converge at a point. This process is repeated 
several times over different iterations until the results stabilise and converge on the same 
values. To ensure the reliability of the results obtained, simulations of up to 1500 iterations 
will be carried out.

5.5. Simulation conclusions 
The results of the CFD simulations show that cascade ventilation is not always the best 
option, as this depends to a large extent on the fact that the air that has to flow from the 
rooms to the rest of the rooms of the house must be mixed with the existing air, otherwise 
there are inequalities in terms of the distribution of pollutants within the house, which could 
produce high levels of CO2 in certain areas, as seen in the results of the first model. 
With regard to the 2 models studied for the cascade simulation, the influence of how the air 
moves from one room to another has been demonstrated, as when comparing these 2 
models with the same geometry and the same ventilation flow, totally different results are 
obtained, hence the importance of the CFD simulations. In this case, better results are 
obtained in terms of pollutant distribution in the second model, where all the air circulating 
in the living room enters through the same point, dragging the pollutant generated by the 
source. In the first model, the air from the bedrooms enters the living room at the ends and 
passes directly through the room into the kitchen and bathrooms without mixing with the 
existing air, resulting in the recirculation shown in the results. However, it is very likely that 
if the simulations were repeated by changing the position of the pollution source, the results 
would be very different, with the first model being the most favourable and not the other 
way round, as is the case with the source located in the centre. 
In conclusion, it is difficult to say which model would be the most favourable as this depends 
on where the sources of pollution are located, in the simulations carried out the best results 
are given in the third model corresponding to conventional ventilation, however, in this 
model there is also the most unfavourable case for ventilation in the bedrooms and if there 
were a constant generation of a higher ratio in both bedrooms CO2 levels harmful to health 
could be reached in a room vital for the development of life inside the bedroom, a fact that 
would be unacceptable. 
Finally, the results obtained in the simulations are subject only to how the incoming air 
interacts with the surfaces and is distributed throughout the house, since, as it is considered 
an isothermal situation, there are no convection phenomena due to temperature differences 
between the outside and inside air, as well as temperature differences between the rooms 
in the house, since if these variables were introduced into the model, the results could vary 
significantly.

6. Conclusions 
After studying the behaviour of cascade ventilation in different types of houses, it is not 
possible to ensure the good performance of this type of ventilation in general, as there are 
too many factors that can influence the ventilation of the house. However, given the right 
conditions, the implementation of cascade ventilation can be an alternative to conventional 
ventilation systems, resulting in large energy savings in the maintenance of dwellings and 
other types of buildings. This could lead to a revolution in the design of ventilation and air-
conditioning systems, and could even lead to a change in the way new buildings are 
conceived, where architecture could be approached to facilitate the implementation of 
cascade ventilation. 
When studying the results of the first simulations with those obtained with CFD, it cannot 
be affirmed that the hypotheses considered in the first simulations are correct, since in 
reality there are many more complex variables than the air change rate and the generation 
of pollutants that influence the ventilation of the dwelling. Unfortunately, it is not possible to 
compare the CO2 pollution results of the first tests with the CFD simulations because the 
first ones are a study of the pollutant levels over time, i.e. they are transient simulations, 
whereas the CFD simulations are stationary simulations where there are no changes in the 
generation sources. Another reason why it is not possible to replicate the scenarios of the 
CONTAM simulations with the CFD simulations is that in the latter it is not possible to 
position two sources simultaneously in the model, as was the case with the first simulations. 
Nevertheless, the CFD simulations serve to observe the behaviour of the air inside the 
dwelling and to see if the conclusions obtained in the previous experiments could be 
extrapolated to reality. 
Finally, it can be concluded that the objectives presented at the beginning of the project 
have been achieved, thus demonstrating that cascade ventilation is not only a revolutionary 
concept, but that under certain circumstances it could be applied to reality. However, it is 
necessary to continue researching this concept in order to find the keys for it to work in a 
general way and to be implemented on a large scale in the near future.
