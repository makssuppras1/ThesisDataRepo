Abstract
Over the past few centuries, methods to predict the behavior of the increasing number of
space objects have evolved. One such method is Angles-Only Initial Orbit Determination
(AIOD), which supports the EU Space Surveillance and Tracking (SST) system by using
smaller telescopes to predict and verify the orbits of space objects. In this project, the
importance of image and input calibration is emphasized. It also evaluates the perfor-
mance of two Gauss methods, a Laplace method, and a Doubler R method. To test the
robustness of each method, three main tests were conducted: varying the interval be-
tween input points, adjusting the number of significant decimals in the input, and testing
the precision of guesses in the Doubler R method. The results indicate that the Gauss
method is generally more stable, while the Doubler R method can yield good results if the
initial guess is accurate.

Introduction
Space-based applications have become an integrated part of everyday life, providing es-
sential services such as communication, navigation, and Earth observation. Our modern
society is fundamentally dependent on the functionality of space infrastructure, making
it crucial to maintain and even strengthen our awareness and safety measures for these
objects.
In recent decades, the number of space launches has increased drastically, particularly
in Low Earth Orbit (LEO). This rapid growth in the number of man-made objects in space,
including satellites and orbital debris, has led to a congested environment around Earth.
The increasing volume of objects poses a growing challenge in maintaining a safe oper-
ating environment for our space infrastructure. Consequently, it is vital to address these
challenges to ensure the continued functionality and safety of our space-based systems.
The number of objects in the space infrastructure has increased significantly in recent
years. As of June 2024, approximately 18,400 satellites have been placed in Earth orbit.
Of these, around 12,550 are still in orbit, but only about 10,000 remain functional. An
additional 50,000 satellites are expected to be launched in the next decade. Besides
these new satellites, rocket parts and other debris also occupy space in these orbits before
re-entering the atmosphere, which is considered a best-case scenario.
The increasing number of satellites and orbital debris heightens the risk of collisions and
the potential damage from objects re-entering Earth’s atmosphere. To mitigate these
risks, an efficient system for Space Surveying and Tracking (SST) of such objects and
providing this information has become more essential than ever.
EU SST is a sub-component of the Space Situational Awareness (SSA) component of
the EU Space Program. It consists of a council from 15 EU member states that have
networked their national assets (radars, telescopes, and lasers) into the EU SST system.
This system provides three important services [1]:
1. Collision Avoidance: Using all available information to detect potential collisions
and generate collision avoidance alerts if necessary.
2. Re-entry Analysis: Generating information about uncontrolled re-entries of space
objects and their risks based on a time frame and location of possible impact.
3. Fragment Analysis: Detecting and characterizing possible in-orbit fragmentation,
break-ups, or collisions.
To conduct these analyses, it is crucial to know the exact orbit of each object. An object
that orbits another body, such as the Sun or the Earth, is referred to as a Resident Space
Object (RSO). This term is predominantly used for Earth-orbiting objects [2].
Observations can be conducted using various instruments and methods, both space-
based and Earth-based. These observations can be carried out using radars, telescopes,
and lasers, each offering multiple methods to determine the orbit from the collected data.
Combining different observation techniques often enhances the accuracy and reliability
of the orbit determination process.
It has never been more important to deploy as many instruments as possible to create
new or verify existing orbit information for the space objects surrounding Earth. The DTU

2
Theory
This section will cover the theory to understand the different methods, how an RSO moves,
and important considerations when taking space images. When capturing a space image,
several factors must be considered. Decisions need to be made about the type of cam-
era and the associated filter to use. Understanding what can cause poor image quality
and how to prevent or at least improve each image is essential. For space images, the
observation mode and the information that can be extracted from the stars in the image
can also be important.
2.1
History of orbit determination
To understand how much easier it is to calculate an object’s orbit with the technology we
have today and the development of methods from the beginning, a brief timeline of key
advancements is provided and shown in Figure 2.1 [3, page 423].
The earliest celestial orbit predictions were formulated before the development of the tele-
scopes and satellites, relying on precise initial orbit or state vectors for subsequent anal-
ysis. Johann Kepler (1571-1630) made in 1627 the first notable orbital determination with
the Rudolphine tables. Using this method, Kepler accurately predicted the conjunction of
Mercury and the Sun in 1631 with a precision of within five hours.
Isaac Newton (1643-1727) revolutionized orbital calculations by releasing his work, Prin-
cipia, in 1687. Within its pages, Newton presented the first practical approach for deter-
mining the orbit of a celestial body based on three observations. It was with this method
Newton successfully showed a way to calculate the parabolic orbit of a comet using just
three observational data points.
In 1744, Leonhard Euler (1707-1783) introduced a comprehensive analytical technique
for orbit determination, closely paralleling Newton’s approach. Euler’s method requires
three relatively proximate observations.
Johann H. Lambert (1728-1779) further refined Euler’s method between 1761 and 1771,
extending its applicability to include elliptical and hyperbolic orbits. This refined tech-
nique, often referred to as Lambert’s problem, remains a cornerstone of orbit determina-
tion methodology to this day.
Pierre-Simon de Laplace (1749-1827) introduced a novel orbit determination method in
1780, solely relying on angular measurements. This approach, while innovative, posed
considerable computational challenges in the absence of modern computing resources.
In 1801, Karl Friedrich Gauss (1777-1855) devised a practical methodology for initial or-
bit determination, notably applied to predict the reappearance of Ceres later that year.
Gauss’s method, utilizing right ascension and declination at three distinct observation
times, simplified orbit calculations compared to earlier techniques. This streamlined ap-
proach is often likened to Lambert’s problem, after the original inventor.
In 1889, Josiah Gibbs (1839-1903) refined Gauss’s method, particularly focusing on de-
termining position vectors at the initial and final observation times. Subsequently, Samuel
Herrick (1911-1974) developed a Taylor-series-based approach to tackle the Gibbs prob-
lem, enabling solutions for short temporal intervals. Such advancements proved crucial
for modern radar systems and the analysis of dense observational datasets.

2.2.2
Circular shape
Most objects in the Satellite Catalogue (SATCAT) from Space-Track are characterized
by nearly circular orbits, meaning the eccentricity is close to zero. Approximately 92% of
these objects exhibit orbits with eccentricity less than 0.1, while 72.4% possess orbits with
less than 0.01. Consequently, many Initial Orbit Determination (IOD) methods presume
circular orbits, which simplifies the geometric and dynamic complexities of the problem
[5].
2.2.3
Influence on the orbit
Various forces influence an RSO’s orbit, leading to potential changes over time [6]. Below
is a list of effects that can impact the orbit of an RSO.
• Gravitational force: Attracts the object towards Earth.
• Atmospheric drag: Reduces the speed of the object.
• Third-body gravitational effects: Influence from celestial bodies such as the sun or
the moon.
• Solar radiation pressure: Exerts a force on the object.
• Tidal forces: Influence from gravitational variations due to the moon and the sun.
• Changes in albedo: Alterations in the reflective properties of the object’s surface.
• Thrusting: Application of propulsion that can accelerate or decelerate the object.
An RSO will typically exhibit a spin around its axis, which can occur in either direction.
This spin can align with the object’s orbital motion, known as prograde spin, or move in
the opposite direction, termed retrograde spin. The Yarkovsky effect is a phenomenon
wherein the spin of an object influences its orbit. If an object has a prograde spin, the
Yarkovsky effect can cause its orbit to expand, while a retrograde spin may result in orbit
shrinkage. In Figure 2.3 are the two spins illustrated [7].

2.2.4
Two-line element
The Two-Line Element (TLE, sometimes called three-line element) is a set of 2-3 lines
containing orbital parameters for an Earth-orbiting object, such as an RSO. In Figure 2.4,
an example describing each section of numbers is provided. The TLE typically consists
of three lines, with the first line (line 0) containing the object’s name and is therefore not
always counted. Additional information, such as alternate names or identifiers like ”R/B”
for rocket bodies or ”DEB” for space debris, may be provided following the object’s name.
Figure 2.4: An example of a TLE for ISS.
Line 1, column 8 denotes the classification, with ”U” for unclassified, ”C” for classified, and
”S” for secret. Columns 10-17 contain the international Designator, indicating the year of
launch, launch number that year, and launch part (e.g., ”A” for the first part). Columns 19-
32 display the TLE orbit time, comprised of the last two digits of the year and the Julian
date (JD) since January 1st of that year.
Columns 34-43 and 45-52 represent the first and second-time derivatives of the mean
motion. Columns 54-61 denote the drag term, which quantifies the object’s susceptibility
to atmospheric drag. This is calculated from drag coefficient CD, the cross section area
A, and the mass m, as B = CDA
m

orbital element calculations, this is usually SGP4/SDP4. Columns 65-68 display the ele-
ment number, indicating the TLE version. This means that most will have a fixed element

Line 2 columns 9-16 contains information on inclination, 18-25 of right ascension of the
ascending node, 35-42 the argument of perigee, and 44-51 mean motion, all in degrees.
In between is in columns 27-33 the eccentricity, without decimals. Columns 64-85 denote

TLE to Keplerian elements
From the TLE, four Keplerian elements can be directly extracted, while one can be calcu-
lated. The inclination (i), right ascension of the ascending node (Ω), eccentricity (e), and
argument of perigee (ω) are directly read from the second line of the TLE. Additionally,
the semi-major axis (a) can be computed from the mean motion (n) using the formula:
a = (µ/n2)1/3. Where µ = G · mEarth = 398.6 · 103 km3/s2 represents the Earth’s gravita-
tional parameter [3, page 107].
2.2.5
Times
Side real-time
To derive the orbit of a satellite or celestial body from observations, recording the time of
each observation is crucial. Solar time, the time we typically use in our daily lives and set
our clocks by is based on the motion of the sun across the sky. A solar day represents
the duration for the sun to return to the same position overhead, equivalent to lying on the
same meridian. This duration, known as a solar day, encompasses 24 hours, from high
noon to high noon.
Universal Time (UT) is established by tracking the sun’s transit across the Greenwich
meridian and is sometimes referred to as ”Greenwich Mean Time” (GMT), representing

from Universal Time by adding one hour for each time zone westward from Greenwich to
the observer’s location.
Sidereal time is measured by the rotation of the Earth relative to the fixed stars, which
compose the celestial sphere. A sidereal day, measuring the duration for a distant star to
return to its original position overhead, equates to 24 sidereal hours. However, due to the
Earth’s orbit around the sun, the sidereal day is slightly shorter than the solar day. Specif-
ically, one sidereal day spans 23 hours and 56 minutes. To offer a different perspective,

Julian date
The Julian day number represents the count of days since noon Universal Time on 1
January 4713 BC (or sometimes in the year 2000). This time scale’s origin is placed in
antiquity to avoid the need to handle positive and negative dates, except for prehistoric
events. The Julian day count is uniform and continuous, unaffected by leap years or
variations in the number of days across different months. Calculating the number of days
between two events involves simply subtracting the Julian day of one event from the Julian
day of the other.
Notably, the Julian day begins at noon rather than at midnight. This decision was made
so that astronomers observing the heavens at night would not encounter a change of date
during their observations [10].

2.2.6
RA/Dec and Lat/Long
Right Ascension (RA, α) and Declination (Dec, δ) serve as celestial coordinates analogous
to longitude and latitude on Earth as illustrated in Figure 2.6. RA measures the east-west
direction on the celestial sphere, akin to longitude on Earth, while Dec measures the
north-south direction, similar to latitude.
Figure 2.6: Comparison of (longitude, latitude) and (RA, Dec) [11].
RA is typically expressed in hours, minutes, and seconds. This convention arises from
the fact that the sky appears to rotate once per day to the west as the Earth rotates to the
east. Consequently, the celestial sphere moves one hour of RA westward for each hour,
covering 24 hours of RA over a complete day. Given that this rotation encompasses 360
degrees, one hour of RA equates to 15 degrees of rotation (360◦/24hr = 15◦/hr). Similar
to lines of longitude, RA lines form great circles that converge at the north and south
celestial poles. Just as the Greenwich meridian serves as the zero line for longitude on
Earth, the zero meridian in RA is designated as 00hr 00m 00s. It intersects the celestial
equator at a point known as the vernal equinox.
Measurements of positions north and south on the celestial sphere are referred to as
declination, commonly abbreviated as Dec, DEC, or dec. Similar to latitude on Earth,
declination is quantified in degrees, minutes, and seconds, with positive values indicating
positions north and negative values indicating positions south. Each degree of declination
is further divided into 60 minutes, and each minute is divided into 60 seconds, mirroring
the subdivision of angular measurements in latitude [11].
Latitude and longitude are coordinates used to specify positions on the Earth’s surface.
Latitude measures the angle north or south of the Equator, ranging from -90° at the South
Pole to +90° at the North Pole. Longitude measures the angle east or west of the Prime
Meridian, ranging from -180° to +180°. Just as latitude and longitude can be divided into
angles and represented as shown in Figure 2.7, RA and Dec can also be expressed in
angular measurements, which can be understood similarly.

2.4
Black and white vs. color images
The telescope’s camera operates in monochrome, capturing only black-and-white images.
While color cameras (called RGB, for red, green, and blue color mixing) offer aesthetic
appeal and potentially richer information, they are also more expensive. However, utilizing
a monochrome camera, filters, and a software program enables the creation of simulated
color images. These are generated by sequentially capturing images through red, green,
and blue filters. Consequently, this method is typically unsuitable for swiftly moving objects
due to the need for multiple exposures and intricate image-merging processes.
Despite the complexity of producing color images from monochrome captures, the monochrome
camera has several advantages. Notably, it lacks a fixed set of filters, resulting in greater
Quantum Efficiency and enhanced precision in position and brightness measurements.
Furthermore, a significant benefit lies in the ability to combine three color images with one
luminance image, a technique known as LRGB. The luminance image, captured without
a filter at maximum chip sensitivity, yields a brighter depiction. However, this approach
necessitates a corresponding flat field and dark image for each unique image setting,
entailing considerable effort.
Variations in light transmission through different filters can impact image fidelity. For in-
stance, imaging an emission nebula rich in deep-red hydrogen alpha lines might yield an
inaccurate portrayal if adjusted to a color-neutral standard. Consequently, most astro-
nomical images require manual balancing. Fortunately, tools like SIPS (see description
in section 3.5) offer control over color stretching, optimizing each color channel for the
best overall image quality, as demonstrated in Figure 2.13 [4][16].

2.5
Calibration
Calibration is performed to enhance the signal-to-noise ratio in the desired image, referred
to as the real image. Images captured in low-light conditions typically exhibit a low signal-
to-noise ratio, necessitating calibration, particularly for star images. The noise present in
these images is non-uniform, making it imperative to compare and subtract master bias,
dark, and flat field frames to effectively minimize noise. This calibration process ensures
almost uniform behavior of all pixels in the camera for each photon captured [4, page
53-54][16, page 32-37][17].
2.5.1
Bias frames
Bias frames are generated by capturing an image with the shortest possible exposure
duration. Typically, this image is taken in a dark environment, achieved by either closing
14
Detection, tracking and identification of man-made space objects
==PAGE:24==
the camera shutter or covering the telescope or camera. By utilizing a short exposure
time, bias frames effectively capture electronic read noise (read-out noise) and identify
damaged pixels in the camera sensor.
It is advisable to acquire a substantial set of bias frames and subsequently create a master
bias frame of them. This master bias frame is then subtracted from the real image and
utilized to calibrate individual dark and flat frames. Ideally, all calibration frames should be
captured at the same temperature as the real frames used for calibration. If the telescope’s
temperature remains relatively stable between observations, the master bias frame from
the previous sessions can be reused.
While it is possible to generate bias frames using software like SIPS (described in Section
3.5), pre-calibrated bias frames are provided by this camera.
2.5.2
Dark frames
The dark frame serves to eliminate random and thermal noise from dead or hot pixels,
ensuring uniformity among all pixels when not exposed to light. To create dark frames, an
image is captured under the same temperature, exposure time, gain, and binning settings
as the real image, but in a dark environment. Consequently, each new setting necessi-
tates a unique set of dark frames. Similarly, a new set of dark images is required for each
setting used for the flat field frame.
It is advisable to capture at least five images and then compute the average or median
to create a master dark frame, following the same procedure as for bias frames. If the
settings remain almost identical, the dark frame can be reused like the bias frames. Dark
frames are typically achieved by keeping the shutter closed. Notably, the absence of a
shutter in the camera being used necessitates covering the camera’s view to simulate the
shutter closure, akin to the process for bias frames.
2.5.3
Flat field frames
Flat field frames are essential for correcting variations in field illumination, ensuring uni-
form pixel values when exposed to identical light levels. This process effectively elimi-
nates spots, dust, and discrepancies in illumination across the image, often observed as
brighter centers and dimmer edges.
To create flat field frames, images are captured of a uniformly illuminated surface, this can
be a calibration LED panel or the sky near sunset. The exposure time is carefully selected
to utilize the camera’s dynamic range. A flat field is important to generate for each filter
and binning configuration used.
While approximately 25 flat field frames are considered optimal, the improvement gained
from capturing additional frames is minimal.

2.6
Noise
To comprehensively evaluate the quality of data for orbit determination, is it essential to
understand the noise present in the image. Data obtained to do AIOD with around three
images are assumed to have zero measurement noise. However, when conducting orbit
determination, is it imperative to identify the sources of noise.
Smaller telescopes are susceptible to a range of noise sources stemming from physi-
cal, electrical, and digital factors. In contrast, larger telescopes, are endowed with larger
budgets, and better facilities, resulting in reduced noise and fewer sources.
This section delves into the most common noise sources encountered in smaller tele-
scopes, their characteristics, and their impact on data quality [4, page 40-44].
2.6.1
Physical noise
Change in location
When the telescope is not positioned at the same spot, comparing images taken on dif-
ferent days becomes challenging. The telescope planned for use in this study had two
primary locations: one at DTU for testing and one at Weibel to produce coherent data
with their radar. The DTU location may experience minor fluctuations over time. The fi-
nal image data used comes from a stationary telescope and, therefore, did not have this
problem.
Light pollution
Light pollution originates from various sources in the surroundings, such as buildings or
other artificial light sources, and significantly affects the quality of images captured. In ar-
eas with higher levels of light pollution, longer exposure times are required to achieve com-
parable results. Specifically, for every magnitude increase in sky brightness gained from
light pollution, approximately 2.5 times longer exposure time is necessary. Figure 2.18
illustrates various degrees of light pollution [18]. As depicted in the figure, light pollution
diminishes the signal-to-noise ratio and imparts a hazy appearance to the images.

2.6.2
Atmospheric effect
From observing through the Earth’s atmosphere as described in Section 2.3, several neg-
ative effects arise compared to space observation.
2.6.3
Optical noise
Optical defects in mirrors or lenses can result in unfocused images, while dust and dirt on
optical elements may lead to imperfections, manifesting as spots in the image. Achieving
perfect focus for all wavelengths within the visible light spectrum may be challenging due
to variations in the optical properties of the glass used. The quality of focus is contingent
upon the gearing of the focuser on the telescope.
2.6.4
Thermal noise
Thermal signals that emanates from the camera or other mechanical components can
introduce thermal noise into the images. The camera may inadvertently register some
of this heat as false light, thereby affecting the image quality. Managing the temperature
within the camera is crucial, as it directly impacts the level of noise present in the images.
To mitigate this type of noise, the camera is typically cooled to ensure it remains sufficiently
cold.
However, an additional challenge arises if both the telescope and camera become too
cold, as may be the case in Greenland, where the telescope is intended to be deployed.
This necessitates ensuring that all cables and mechanical functions remain operational
even in extremely cold environments.
2.6.5
Electronic noise
The noise happens during the process of capturing, reading, and saving images. Two
primary forms of electrical noise contribute to this: read noise and dark current. Read
noise occurs during the conversion of the camera’s signal into pixel values, while dark
noise stems from potential temperature discrepancies during pixel reading. Both forms
of noise originate within the sensor and are typically accounted for during the camera’s
calibration by the manufacturer.
2.6.6
Digital noise
The final type of noise discussed in this report occurs in the process of saving images.
When images are stored, the computer allocates limited space for each pixel, imposing
a precision limit. Additionally, during noise reduction processes, round-off errors may
occur. These errors are also present during image analysis, as the software used typically
truncates values to a certain number of decimals. While these errors may seem minor,
even small deviations of a few pixels can introduce inaccuracies in the determination of
the locations of RSO and ultimately impact the accuracy of their orbital predictions.
2.7
Observation modes
The methodology for capturing images of celestial objects, the one called RSOs, encom-
passes various approaches. In this section, are the four primary types crucial for execut-
ing the AIOD process described. These approaches differ in the elements they track as
RSOs traverse the image, often referred to as different modes of observation [4].
2.7.1
Sidereal rate observation
When a telescope gazes at the sky for even a brief moment without adjustment, all ce-
lestial objects appear to be in motion. This phenomenon arises from the Earth’s rotation,
necessitating the telescope to track the objects it observes for clear imaging. Fortunately,
most contemporary telescopes are equipped with the capability to track celestial bodies
across the sky, encompassing stars, satellites, and asteroids.
Detection, tracking and identification of man-made space objects
19
==PAGE:29==
One prevalent technique for observing RSOs is through sidereal rate observations. In
this mode, the telescope synchronizes with the Earth’s rotation, tracking the movement
of stars or at least one star in the background. Consequently, stars appear stationary
in the image, while objects moving at different velocities, such as satellites and space
debris, manifest as streaks across the sky. This mode offers a significant advantage,
that the background stars can serve as reference points for plate-solving techniques (see
section 2.8), facilitating more precise coordinate estimations.
Illustrated in Figure 2.20a, this mode shows the stationary stars against the streak formed
by the RSO, providing a clear visual distinction. In this mode, it is not guaranteed that
the RSO will pass through the image center, it depends on the star or coordinates the
telescope is set to follow [19].
2.7.2
Satellite rate observation
Another common method for capturing AIOD images involves tracking the desired RSO
at its rate of motion across the sky. This process entails star alignment, rapid tracking,
and appropriate software. RSO tracking mode can be implemented in both open and
closed-loop configurations, each necessitating knowledge of the RSO’s location.
In open-loop mode, up-to-date orbital data of the RSO is utilized. Conversely, closed-
loop mode utilizes an initial satellite position to slew close to the RSO before locking onto
it. This approach offers the advantage of allowing the camera to accumulate more light
from the object, while also maintaining the RSO’s static position within the image frame.
However, a drawback is the requirement for precise telescope positioning since plate
solving is not feasible.
In Figure 2.20b, an example of this mode is depicted. Here, only one point is discernible,
the RSO, while the remaining lines represent other objects or stars in the background.
(a) An example of a sidereal mode.
(b) An example of a satellite mode.

2.7.3
Earth rate observation
A third method of observation is Earth rate observation. This technique exclusively applies
to RSOs in a Geostationary Equatorial Orbit (GEO). In this mode, the telescope tracks
the sky at the same rate as the Earth’s motion. As a result, objects in GEO orbit will
appear almost stationary, while the background stars may exhibit slight streaking across
the image.

2.7.4
Point source observation
If all objects in the image are wanted to be captured as point sources, this mode is the ideal
choice. Achieving this involves employing a sufficiently short exposure time to prevent
objects from exhibiting motion blur. Nevertheless, a notable disadvantage is the challenge
of distinguishing RSOs from stars, necessitating the acquisition of more images for clarity
to see which object is moving. Another challenge arises from the need for objects in the
images to be sufficiently bright, given the short exposure time.
2.8
Plate solving
The process of matching stars in a space image to known star positions in a catalog is
known as plate solving. This technique enhances the accuracy of the telescope’s angular
measurements. Star Trackers employ a similar process, typically utilizing a limited catalog
to expedite processing. The matching process involves comparing the detected stars in
the image to known star orientations.
Astrometry.net is a software program designed to perform plate solving for any astronom-
ical image. For optimal results, multiple stars should be visible in the background, and the
image should be captured in sidereal rate mode or point source mode (See section 2.7).
This ensures that the stars are accurately represented, facilitating effective plate solving
[3, page 77].
2.9
Segmentation
After noise reduction, the next step is to identify all potential RSOs in the image. This
involves determining the center of all objects within the image. The process of distin-
guishing objects from the background sky is often referred to as ”segmenting” the image.
A straightforward and effective method is to use a threshold, categorizing regions based
on their dimensions as either points or lines. Another approach is to utilize the Hough
function, which identifies straight lines within the image. The Hough function is part of
the OpenCV library, providing robust tools for detecting lines in images. For more infor-
mation and detailed usage, see the OpenCV documentation and resources on this topic
[20]. However, exhaustively detecting all points may be computationally inefficient if the
image contains numerous stars in the background. To mitigate this, a minimum area for
each point can be specified to filter out smaller and weaker stars.
Once all objects in the image have been identified, they must be classified as either stars
or RSOs, with the determination based on the observation mode used to capture the
image (see section 2.7). In sidereal rate observation, stars appear as points while RSOs
appear as lines, whereas in satellite rate observation, the opposite holds. By leveraging
the know mode, or by recognizing that stars are more prevalent than RSOs in the image,
the center of the RSO can be identified. If multiple RSOs are present in the image, it is
assumed that the desired one is closest to the center.
2.10
Radar
A radar is an electromagnetic sensor used for detecting, locating, tracking, and recog-
nizing various objects at considerable distances. It operates by transmitting electromag-
netic energy toward targets and observing the echoes returned from them, as seen in
Figure 2.21. These objects can include aircraft, ships, spacecraft, automotive vehicles,
astronomical bodies, and even birds, insects, and rain. Beyond determining the pres-
ence, location, and velocity of such objects, radar can sometimes ascertain their size and
shape.

==PAGE:31==
Figure 2.21: The reflection of the transmitted pulse on the target.
What sets radar apart from optical and infrared sensing devices is its capability to detect
distant objects under adverse weather conditions and precisely determine their range,
or distance. As an “active” sensing device, radar possesses its own source of illumina-
tion (a transmitter) for locating targets, this means that it can see objects in the Earth’s
shadow. It typically functions in the microwave region of the electromagnetic spectrum,
with frequencies ranging from about 400 MHz to 40 GHz [21].
Weibel’s long-range Doppler radars are instrumental for Space Situational Awareness
(SSA) analysis, offering real-time data on the positions and movements of space ob-
jects. These radars are capable of measuring space traffic, aiding in collision avoidance,
fragmentation analysis, and re-entry analysis services. Weibel’s multi-frequency, long-
range tracking radars excel in detecting and monitoring space debris, threats, and satellite
anomalies.
In addition to tracking, these systems collect observation vectors, which are essential for
computing orbital parameters and radar signatures. This capability reveals the intrinsic
motion of objects, including their rotation and tumbling rates. The SST program’s inte-
gration of radar detection with optical surveillance techniques ensures the most accurate
data for orbit determination, enhancing the reliability and precision of SSA efforts [22].

indicated its efficacy. However, despite numerous attempts to understand and implement
it, the complexity led to exploring alternative methods. Gooding’s method employed Lam-
bert’s problem in some cases, which led to the investigation of Lambert’s method. Al-
though Lambert’s method initially appeared straightforward, it required two position vec-
tors as input rather than angles, which made it unsuitable for this project.
Gauss’s method emerged as a favorable alternative. It was well-documented in multiple
sources and could be reproduced to yield nearly correct results. Consequently, the Gauss
method was included twice in this project. By comparing the two implementations of
Gauss’s method, their reliability and differences can be more easily seen and investigated.
The investigation of Gauss’s method led to discovering additional methods in the papers.
By comparing these methods across different sources, Laplace’s and Doubler R methods
were identified, implemented, and tested with at least two examples from the sources.
This comprehensive approach ensured a robust selection of methods for the project, al-
lowing for a thorough analysis and application in orbit determination.

3.2
Difference between the methods
The main differences between various sources of orbital determination methods, such as
Gauss, Laplace, and the Double R method, lie primarily in their approaches, accuracy,
and dependence on initial guesses:
1. Methodology:
• Gauss: Utilizes three observations to calculate the Line of Sight (LOS) vectors,
positions, and (possibly with help from the Gibbs method) velocities by solving
a system of linear equations derived from orbital mechanics. It assumes the
observations are relatively close in time (taken on the same night and < 60°
apart), making it suitable for short observational arcs.
• Laplace: Compared to Gauss, this method is a bit more complex because it
takes the differential of the LOS and R vector and works with determinants.
Besides this, it is the method with the fewest steps to find the position and
velocity vector for the second position.
• Double R: Similar to Gauss’s method but involves an iterative process requir-
ing initial guesses for the magnitudes of the position vectors r1 and r2. It up-
dates these guesses until convergence, making it more robust for longer ob-
servational arcs but sensitive to the initial guesses.
2. Sensitivity to Initial Guesses:
• Gauss and Laplace: Do not require initial guesses for position magnitudes,
leading to a straightforward solution process.
• Double R: Requires initial guesses for r1 and r2, and the convergence of the
solution heavily depends on the accuracy of these initial guesses. Poor initial
guesses can lead to slower convergence or incorrect solutions.
3. Iterative Process:
• Gauss: The first version has an iterative approach when improving the position
and velocity. The second version solves the equations without iteration, making
it faster for initial orbit determination.
• Laplace: Like the second Gauss method, it has no iterations.
• Double R: Uses an iterative approach to refine the position vectors until con-
vergence, which can be more accurate but computationally intensive.
4. Applicability:
• Gauss: Best suited for cases with three closely spaced observations, providing
quick and reasonably accurate initial orbit estimates.
• Laplace: This method was not intended for RSOs but should still give some
estimates. Other methods should be considered more reliable than this.
• Double R: More flexible and can handle observations spread over longer pe-
riods, but at the cost of requiring good initial guesses and more computation.
26
Detection, tracking and identification of man-made space objects
==PAGE:36==
5. Accuracy:
• Gauss: Provides accurate results for short observation arcs (less than 60°
apart) but may be less reliable for long-duration observations over multiple
days.
• Laplace: Since it is not intended for Earth-orbiting objects, the results cannot
be trusted on their own.
• Double R: Made for observations of RSOs and other Earth-orbiting objects. It
can achieve higher accuracy for long-duration observations due to its iterative
refinement process, but its accuracy is highly dependent on the quality of the
initial guesses.
These differences highlight the trade-offs between computational simplicity and robust-
ness to initial conditions, guiding the choice of method based on the specific requirements
of the orbital determination task.

Once the telescope’s orientation is established, it can be used to observe specific targets.
This can be done in several ways:
• Selecting a stationary and known point in the sky from the sky image provided in

• Searching for a specific object using the program’s search feature.
• Inputting the TLE into the program and setting it to track the object across the sky.
These features enable precise control and tracking, ensuring accurate observations and
data collection.

Image transformation
The images captured by the telescope are originally in FITS format, which contains all
the necessary information within the image header. Specialized libraries are required to
read FITS images in Python. To simplify the process, these images are transformed into
PNG format. The code for this transformation was provided by an expert from Aarhus
University who also assisted in capturing the images with their FUT telescope. During
this transformation, a Gaussian filter is applied to eliminate small errors. However, various
issues can arise during the transformation process, leading to the need to discard some
images.
In some cases, the RSO in the image may not be bright or large enough to exceed the
minimum area threshold. This can be addressed by reducing the minimum area threshold,
which increases the likelihood of detecting errors. Additionally, if the RSO is too small,
the Gaussian filter may remove it from the image entirely, resulting in a black image.
This occurred in one of the observations intended for use, leading to the selection of an
alternative data set.
Another potential issue is related to the exposure time. If the exposure time is long enough
to capture stars, the RSO might overlap with a star, making the two objects appear as
one. This means that the program will only detect the line part of the image. If the image
is taken with a satellite rate observation where the RSO should be a point, it might get
obscured by the star. However, the likelihood of this happening is low due to typically

short exposure times and the limited area of stars and RSOs. It is essential to review all
images and discard any with such issues. This quick review process also helps ensure
the overall quality of the images.
Some images from the second observation with the FUT telescope exhibited significant

they were inverted, with stars appearing black and the sky white. The conversion code

4.3
Errors
When working with observations, errors will inevitably occur, which need to be accounted
for when calculating the orbit. Some values are assumed to be error-free for all observa-
tions taken at the same place, regardless of the time and day. These include the minimum
area, FOV, the change in UT time, and coordinates for the position. While minor errors
may exist in the three position inputs (latitude, longitude, and altitude), these errors would
be consistent across all cases.
TLEs are known to contain errors often, especially if they are outdated. The webpage
from which the TLE was taken did not specify the last update time, rendering the TLE’s
error unknown.
Below, the other inputs and their assumed errors are described. A summary of the errors
is shown in Table 4.3.
4.3.1
Time error
Time is a crucial input in both seconds and Julian Date (JD). For simplicity, it is assumed
that the error is the same for both. The image used in the report has an exposure time
of 0.1 seconds, with a new image taken immediately after. Ideally, the computer would
save 100 images each second, but due to data-saving constraints, a new image is saved
approximately every 22 seconds. This results in a potential error of 11 seconds from this
estimation alone. Examination of the FITS file data reveals a maximum difference of 3
seconds between the time the computer created the file and the time in the file name,
assumed to be when the image was taken. Therefore, the time error is assumed to be 3
seconds, equivalent to 3.4 · 10−5 JD.
4.3.2
Image precision error
An image consists of a finite number of pixels, which limits the precision. The camera
used has a FOV of 0.5 degrees and a width of 1025 pixels, resulting in a pixel precision
of 4.88 · 10−4 deg/pixel.
4.3.3
Telescope position angles error (RA and Dec)
The RA and Dec given by the telescope will also have errors. Estimating these errors
is challenging due to the lack of quality information. The FITS file lists angles with 3-7
significant decimals, leading to an assumed error of 10−3 deg.
4.3.4
Derotator angle error
The quality of the derotator angle is also unknown. It is typically given with 2-3 significant
decimals. Due to its significant impact on the RSO’s final position, the error is assumed
to be 10−1 deg instead of the smallest significant decimal like for RA and Dec.

Doubler-R guess
Because the Doubler R method requires initial guesses for two magnitudes of positions,
the robustness and sensitivity of the measurement error were tested. If the optical tele-
scope were working with radar, the guess could be based on the radar data. However,
since it does not work with radar, the guess is based on the result from the Orbital Gauss
method, which is considered the most reliable method.
In some cases, the magnitude of the first position vector increases to the extent that
the eccentricity becomes greater than 1. This is impossible because all the objects the
program deals with are in orbit around the Earth. When this happens, the program will
send an error message and stop the calculation. This was what happened in the two
Meteor Rocket cases in the test of intervals. The guesses are adjusted within the interval
so that the program does not result in an eccentricity higher than 1 in the first iteration.

a basis for the guess and assumed to be the real value. The guess is then adjusted in
seven steps: ±2.5%, ±5%, ±10%, and the value from Gauss method. The FUSE satellite,

4.9
Discussion
4.9.1
Assumptions
To achieve accurate results, several assumptions about the data have been made. Firstly,
the information in the FITS header is presumed to be correct. While there may be minor
errors due to noise or reading time, it is generally assumed that the provided information
is accurate.
Secondly, it is assumed that the images have been calibrated using all three types of
calibration described in section 2.5. Although the master frames used for calibration may
not always be recent, they are assumed to remain effective. Unless exposed to dust, rain,
or other conditions that could introduce errors into the images.
Finally, the functionality and orientation of the derotator system in the FUT telescope are
assumed based on the expert from Aarhus’s input. It is believed that the derotator has its
zero point aligned towards the North and the degrees are in a clockwise rotation direction
when set free.
These assumptions provide a foundation for the processing and analysis of the observa-
tional data.
4.9.2
Radar data
The original plan involved combining optical observations from the DENASSI telescope
with radar data from Weibel’s long-range Doppler radars. By synchronizing observations
of the same object, the combination of optical and radar data would yield improved orbital
determinations. The telescope and radar were to be situated approximately 1 km apart on
the property. Despite this distance, accurate position tracking would mitigate any potential
issues.
However, synchronizing these instruments posed significant challenges. Timing compli-
cations and delays were common when coordinating instruments and people. The radar
was not always operational and was sometimes repurposed for other tasks. Addition-
ally, aligning radar observations with optical observations required clear weather and the
availability of personnel, further complicating coordination.
Using the Herrick-Gibbs method for RSO determination with purely optical data at high
altitudes proved unfeasible. The system could not capture and save optical images quickly
enough to maintain an angular separation under 3 degrees (see figures in section 4.5).
In contrast, radar can capture images at a faster rate, making it suitable for the Herrick-
Gibbs method. While this method is not crucial for enhancing accuracy, it could provide
alternative results.
Pros and cons
Radar offers several significant advantages and some drawbacks in the context of space
situational awareness (SSA) and orbital determination. One of the primary benefits of
radar is its ability to directly measure the distance to an object, in addition to the two
angles of orientation. This range information allows for faster, simpler, and more accurate
data analysis [3, page 426]. If radar data were available for this project, it would improve
the initial range estimate for the Doubler R method, making the initial values much closer
to the precise result, thereby enhancing the overall accuracy.
Moreover, radar data could optimize the Gauss-Gibbs method by substituting the initial
position vector obtained from the Gauss method with radar-determined positions. This in-
tegration would simplify the process and potentially yield better results, making the meth-
ods more robust and efficient.
Detection, tracking and identification of man-made space objects
63
==PAGE:73==
Radar’s flexibility in observation conditions is another significant advantage. Radar can
operate under various conditions [3, page 911]:
• In the ”Visible” scenario, the object is in sunlight while the radar is in darkness, which
aligns with the ideal conditions for optical observations.
• During ”Radar Night,” the object is not sunlit, the radar site can be either sunlit or
dark.
• In the ”Radar Sun” condition, both the object and the radar site are in sunlight.
This flexibility means radar can observe objects at all times, unlike optical systems that
are restricted to nighttime observations with sunlit objects [3, page 911].
Both radar and optical observations can be employed to estimate the size of an object.
In addition, optical observations, with the aid of filters, can provide an estimation of the
object’s color, as detailed in section 2.3 and 2.4. This capability is particularly valuable
because the color information obtained through optical methods can offer insights into
the material composition and surface properties of the object, complementing the size
estimation provided by both radar and optical data.

Program architecture and user manual
In this section, the final versions of the programs developed throughout this project are
described. The description is divided into multiple steps and includes both text and math-
ematical explanations to illustrate what is happening and what to do in each step. At the
beginning of each program description, the required inputs are provided. If any step of
the program can potentially cause problems, this is noted to help users avoid common
pitfalls. By following these steps, users should find it easier to reproduce the programs
developed in this project, compared to the initial process of creating them. In Appendix A
are examples of most of the program shown, with results for each step.
5.1
Centroiding
Centroiding is the process of finding the centroids of regions of contiguous pixels in an
image to identify RSOs. This can be accomplished using various methods. Here is the
threshold followed by regionprops, and the Hough Transform used. These techniques
can identify points and lines that may represent stars, RSOs, or other objects in the sky.
The program requires three images and a minimum object size. The input images should
be calibrated with all three calibration images as described in section 2.5. The program
processes one image at a time in a loop. The implementation is inspired by multiple sec-
tions from ”Space Image Processing and Orbit Estimation Using Small Aperture Optical
Systems” [4].
Step 1: Read image
The image is read using an OpenCV image reader. Although other libraries can be used,
OpenCV provides essential functions for this task.
Step 2: Convert til gray
Even if the image is initially in grayscale, it is converted to ensure optimal performance of
the thresholding step.
Step 3: Gaussian smoothing filter
A Gaussian smoothing filter (also known as Gaussian blur) is applied to reduce pixel
errors, or random image noise, which may arise from various sources described in sec-
tion 2.6. This filter works by averaging neighboring pixels, with more weight given to cen-
tral pixels, effectively acting as a low-pass filter to remove high-frequency content from
the image. With very small or dim RSOs, this filter might also remove them. This occurs
because the Gaussian filter can eliminate faint or small objects and noise. Adjusting the
filter parameters or exploring alternative noise reduction methods can help preserve these
RSOs.
Step 4: Threshold
A threshold filter is applied to eliminate the possible remaining background noise. The
threshold can be set manually, but OpenCV’s OTSU function can automatically determine
the optimal threshold for each image. If another object is significantly brighter than the
RSO, manual thresholding may be necessary.
Step 5: Hough Transform
The Hough Transform is used to detect line segments in the image as an additional feature
check. The threshold for this transform is set to 100. While this value could be manually
specified as an input for each image, setting it in the code simplifies the process.
Detection, tracking and identification of man-made space objects
65
==PAGE:75==
Step 6: Regionprops
The regionprops function identifies all points and lines in the thresholded image, consid-
ering all white regions as objects and the rest as background. To avoid computational
inefficiency and false positives from noise, a minimum area criterion is set for the contigu-
ous pixel regions.
Step 7: Division of lines and points
Objects near the image edges are excluded to avoid frame errors. The other objects
identified in step 6 are classified as either lines or points. An object is labeled as a line if
its area is less than 60% of the bounding box area or if its length significantly exceeds its
width. Otherwise, it is classified as a point.
Step 8: Print information
This step provides feedback on whether the RSO was identified as a line or point, as-
suming the RSO has a unique shape in the image. Based on the observation mode (see
section 2.7), the RSO can be a line or point. If only one object type it is classified as the
RSO. If there is the same amount of lines and points found, the RSO is assumed to be
a point. If both types are present, the RSO is assumed to be the category with fewer
objects, as multiple similar objects are likely stars.
Step 9: RSO closest to center
In cases where multiple objects qualify as RSOs, the one closest to the image center is
assumed to be the target observed by the telescope.
Step 10: Plot on image
An optional feature allows the user to plot the identified RSO and other objects on the
image. This visual aid helps verify the program’s accuracy, especially when working with
new data

5.3
R-Matrix
The R-matrix describes the telescope’s position in Earth-centered inertial (ECI) coordi-
nates, which are fixed to the stars and originate at the Earth’s Center of Mass (CoM).
Because these coordinates are inertial, they depend on the precise time of the obser-
vation. Calculating the R-matrix requires inputs such as position in the form of latitude
and longitude in radians and altitude in kilometers. The time should be in Julian Date
(JD) and the time zone in hours. These inputs convert the geographic coordinates of the
telescope’s location into the ECI frame.
This involves translating the telescope’s position on the Earth’s surface to a position vector
in a coordinate system fixed to the Earth, then rotating these coordinates into the ECI
frame using the Greenwich Sidereal Time (GST) or another appropriate time measure.

coordinate system. The methodology for this calculation is inspired by ”Orbital Mechanics
for Engineering Students” algorithms 5.3 and 5.4 [10, page 217 and 232].

5.6.5
Lambert’s problem
Lambert’s problem is not an angles-only method, but it is essential in many orbit determi-
nation processes. Finding the orbit between two positions given the time of flight between
the two positions is known as Lambert’s Problem. Multiple solutions exist to use Lambert’s
problem as a method for determining an object’s orbit. Lambert’s original approach to the
problem focuses on finding the minimum energy orbit using only the geometry of ellipses.
However, Lambert’s method requires two position vectors as input, not angles, which is
the focus of this project. Although it could be used instead of Gibbs in the second Gauss
method, the differences in the sources made it too complex to easily understand. Due to
time constraints and the investigation of multiple other methods, implementing Lambert’s
method was deemed not worthwhile [4, page 26].
5.6.6
Gooding’s method
The difference between Gooding’s method and Lambert’s problem is that Gooding’s method
uses a minimum of three points with two parameters (angles), while Lambert’s problem
only uses two position vectors. Gooding himself claims that his method can achieve a
precision of 13 decimal places after three iterations [32, page 11].
Gooding’s method takes the same input as the three methods used in this project but
requires two additional guesses and an assumption. Specifically, a guess for the edge
range ρj for j = 1, 3 must be provided, along with a guess for the spin direction (see sub-
section 2.2.3). The only assumption made is that the object is moving in an unperturbed
Keplerian motion, meaning only the gravitational force from one other body (the Earth) is
considered, and the object moves in a straight line or bow.
Although Gooding’s method is highly regarded and widely used for both space and ground-
based observations of RSOs in all orbit regimes, due to its computational ease and ro-
bustness for most orbit geometries, it is challenging to find a source with a straightforward
guide for replicating the method [4].

Conclusions
Numerous methods have been developed for Angles-Only Initial Orbit Determination (AIOD).
Various methods were investigated in this project, and those with sufficiently detailed de-
scriptions were implemented and tested. For most methods, it was necessary to reference
at least two sources to fully understand and rewrite a functional code.
Testing the implemented programs required examples to verify the calculated values. Un-
fortunately, only two sources provided such examples, which were available only for two
of the methods. These examples included step-by-step solutions that were invaluable
for identifying and correcting errors during code development. The lack of examples for
other methods significantly extended the debugging process, as it required step-by-step
verification to easily find and fix the inevitable small errors in the code.
After validating the programs with the initial examples, additional data was needed for
further testing. However, the DENASSI telescope was not fully operational, resulting in
data of sub-optimal quality. Consequently, the programs could only be tested to the extent
allowed by the available data. Higher-quality data for comprehensive testing was obtained
only late in the project, limiting the ability to thoroughly validate the programs in earlier
stages.
The differences between the methods were expected, yet some steps were comparable
across them. Unexpectedly, many sources describing the same method had steps that
were not comparable, exhibiting differences similar to those between entirely different
methods. For instance, Laplace shared some similarities with the Fundamental Gauss
method, to the same or higher degree as the similarities between the two Gauss methods.
Finding functions to calculate the telescope position in ECI coordinates and handling the
rotation and movement of coordinates for the RSO proved challenging. Many sources
described these processes but did not provide detailed explanations of how they were
implemented.
The results indicate that the Laplace method performs poorly for LEO objects, suggesting
either a fundamental issue with the method for this application or a hidden error in the
code. Consequently, the Laplace method is not considered trustworthy in this project.
Both the position and velocity calculated using this method significantly deviate from those
obtained with other methods.
The other methods yielded similar values for the parameters, with the Fundamental Gauss
method showing slight variations compared to the Orbital Gauss and Doubler R methods.
However, by examining the parameters, it becomes evident that the values from all meth-
ods are still not entirely accurate compared to the TLE value.
When examining the time and angle differences in section 4.5 , it is evident that the three
trustworthy methods stabilize once the angle and time increase beyond a certain thresh-
old. Within this range, the specific values of angle and time are less critical as long as
they surpass this threshold. For the FUSE satellite, this point was found at an angle of
36 degrees and 90 seconds, whereas for the Cosmos 1833 Rocket, it was 23 degrees
and 75 seconds. This insight is valuable for the DENASSI project, as it suggests that for
objects in similar orbital heights, results will not be reliable if the angle or time is much
smaller than these thresholds. Most parameters calculated for the FUSE and Cosmos

datasets did not closely match the values derived from TLE data. Generally, the Orbital

even those below the assumed error margin. Two sets of inputs were examined: JD with
three to nine significant decimals, and the angles RA and Dec with zero to five significant
decimals. For JD, it became apparent that beyond four significant decimals, further pre-
cision had a negligible impact on all parameters. This suggests that the significance of
JD precision is lower than the assumed time error of 10−5 JD, indicating it has minimal
influence when used in JD form. For RA and Dec angles, a maximum of five significant
decimals was tested. Having at least two significant decimals is advantageous. Given
that the assumed error for these inputs is 10−3 degrees, using two or three significant
decimals is optimal.
The Doubler-R method relies heavily on initial guesses, and the significance of these
guesses was examined in section 4.7. It was observed that larger intervals between inputs
increased the likelihood that the results would converge toward the real values. The initial
iterations of position estimates were closely aligned with the initial guesses. It was found
that higher initial guesses generally had a better chance of converging, whereas lower
guesses were more stable but less likely to reach the real value. Outliers, particularly
in the semi-major axis parameter, could occur with both small and large changes in the
guess, and both positive and negative directions relative to the true value. This was seen
across different parameters and intervals, suggesting that neither large nor small intervals
are inherently more reliable. The argument of perigee also varied across all intervals,
further indicating that higher intervals are not always more trustworthy.
In section 4.8, the results from this project were compared to those in the AC source. The
AC study showed better performance for the Laplace method, raising questions about
the implementation of this method in the current project. This discrepancy suggests that
the code for the Laplace method might contain errors or differences in implementation,
and further investigation would be needed to understand these differences fully. The AC
study favored the Doubler R method, despite its higher likelihood of failed runs. This
method also showed smaller errors in the AC study compared to the current project. Both
studies indicated that errors and failed runs could occur with various changes in guesses,
emphasizing the importance of accurate initial guesses.
The results from this project and comparisons to AC do not provide a clearer understand-
ing of which methods are more reliable, due to the opposite conclusion of the best method.
The optimal input precision has become more evident, showing that more precise data
does not always lead to better results. When data precision exceeds its error margin,
using excessive decimal places is unnecessary. It is more sensible to match the number
of significant decimals to the error margin.

6.0.1
Future work
The code in general can be optimized, the focus for the project has been to achieve func-
tionality and demonstrate how it aligns with the data from the TLE. Running the program
with a new dataset takes around 15 minutes to process all the methods and generate
the plots. However, if the same dataset is run again, it only takes a few seconds, as the
program can remember the results from the previous run. The code contains numerous
loops, some of which could be made more efficient to reduce the initial runtime. If more
time were available, it would be preferable to implement these changes to optimize the
program.
The information about the time in Julian Date (JD) from the FUT telescope is provided in
UT rather than the local time for the observers or the telescope’s location. This simplifies
the usage of the program since the user does not have to determine the time zone of the
telescope and convert it to UT time. If the time is not given in UT, the program for writing
the FITS data should be designed so that the ∆UT is included and does not need to be
manually input by the user. This approach ensures accuracy and ease of use, reducing
potential errors in time conversion.
If more time had been provided for this project and additional observations had been
made, the use of filters would have been implemented. Utilizing filters might have made it
possible, in some cases, to determine if the observed object was indeed the one targeted.
This could be achieved by examining the object through multiple filters to observe if it has
a specific color signature.
Error propagation could have provided this project with a better understanding of the qual-
ity of the results. It would have indicated whether the input errors had a significant effect
on the outcomes, potentially validating some values that currently appear incorrect. How-
ever, this was not undertaken due to time constraints and the complexity of implementing
error propagation throughout all steps of the code. Given the intricate nature of this pro-
cess, it was determined that there was insufficient time to test and correct the inevitable
errors that would arise from such a comprehensive implementation.
