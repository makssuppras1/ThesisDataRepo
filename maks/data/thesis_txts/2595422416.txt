ii 
Abstract 
 
Global shipping emissions have increased over time with the expansion of the maritime sector. Ship-
ping activities have an impact on local air quality and contribute to air pollution in urban cities. Port-
related emissions severely affect human health in the local environment due to the proximity of ships 
to densely populated areas. Abatement solutions are therefore needed to reduce air pollution caused 
by the maritime sector. This study aims to evaluate the effectiveness of shore power facilities as a 
mitigation measure in ports to reduce air pollution, taking the ports of Copenhagen and Oslo as case 
ports. This study consists of a theoretical and a practical investigation covering emission estimations, 
through the bottom-up methodology and on-site measurements of several air pollutants from ferries 
in ports. The findings of this study indicate that shore power facilities can provide an effective meas-
ure to reduce port-related emissions. The connection of one operating ferry to shore power in 2019 
while at berth in Oslo port, resulted in emission reductions of 92%, 77%, 91%, 77%, 94%, and 89% 
annually covering the air pollutants NOX, CO2, CO, SO2, PM, and NMVOCs, respectively. In this 
regard, external costs related to the emissions showed socio-economic benefits of approximately 
DKK 2.03 million in terms of NOX, SO2, and PM2.5 reductions from one ferry in Oslo in 2019. The 
socio-economic benefits are expected to balance the expenses of a shore power facility in each port 
in three years and nine months taken into account the two operating ferries between Copenhagen and 
Oslo. Local air quality measurements obtained by monitoring devices, showed considerably higher 
pollution in the presence of ferries in the port of Copenhagen, compared to background pollution in 
terms of PM0.1 levels. Shore power facilities and other alternative abatement solutions can be a step 
forward for the improvement of local air quality.

Sammenfatning 
 
Globale emissioner fra skibe er steget over tid med udvidelsen af den maritime sektor. Skibsaktivite-
ter har en effekt på den lokale luftkvalitet og bidrager til luftforurening i byer. Havne relateret emis-
sioner påvirker menneskers helbred i det lokale miljø grundet skibe og havnes beliggenhed til tæt 
befolkede områder. Derfor er det nødvendigt at udarbejde løsninger, der kan være med til at reducere 
luftforurening relateret til skibsindustrien. Denne rapport beskæftiger sig med at undersøge effektivi-
teten af landstrøm faciliteter i havne, som en løsning der kan bidrage til reducering af luftforurening 
i havneområder, med fokus på havnene i København og Oslo. Denne undersøgelse består af en teo-
retisk samt en praktisk del, der dækker over emissionsestimeringer gennem ’bottom-up’ metoden og 
felt relateret målinger af flere luftforureningsstoffer fra færger i havne. Resultaterne af denne under-
søgelse indikerer, at landstrøm faciliteter kan være en effektiv metode til at reducere havnerelaterede 
emissioner. Forbindelsen af en færge til landstrøm i Oslo havn i 2019, resulterede i årlige emissions-
reduceringer på 92% af NOX, 77% af CO2, 91% af CO, 77% af SO2, 94% af PM, og 89% af NMVOCs. 
I denne forbindelse viste eksterne omkostninger relateret til emissionsreduceringer af NOX, SO2, og 
PM2.5 en samlede samfundsøkonomisk fordel på omkring 2,03 mio. Kr. relateret til en færge i Oslo i 
2019. De samfundsøkonomiske fordele kan afbalancere udgifterne til et landstrømsanlæg i hver havn 
på tre år og ni måneder taget de to færger mellem København og Oslo i betragtning. Lokale luftkva-
litets målinger opnået gennem feltmålinger i havneområder i København, viste betydeligt højere for-
ureningsniveauer ved tilstedeværelsen af færger i form af PM0.1 sammenlignet med baggrundskon-
centrationen. Landstrøm faciliteter samt andre alternative løsninger indenfor luftforureningsreduk-
tion, kan bidrage med forbedring af den lokale luftkvalitet, og derved reducere effekter på miljøet og 
menneskers helbred.

1 Introduction 
 
Global emissions from shipping have increased over time with the expansion of the maritime sector 
(Nunes et al., 2017a; Fridell, 2019). Transportation through this sector is the major cargo transporta-
tion mode corresponding to approximately 80% of global trade (Chu Van et al., 2019; Nunes et al., 
2017a). Shipping activities and electricity-generating ship engines have an impact on local air quality 
and contribute to air pollution in urban cities. Main air pollutants related to shipping are Carbon 
Dioxide (CO2), Carbon Monoxide (CO), Sulfur Dioxide (SO2), Nitrogen Oxides (NOX), Volatile Or-
ganic Compounds (VOCs), and Particulate Matter (PM). Emissions of PM are associated with res-
piratory and cardiovascular diseases, lung cancer, asthma, and premature deaths. Annually, it has 
been reported that shipping-related emissions of PM are responsible for around 60,000 deaths due to 
cardiopulmonary and lung cancer, mostly along East Asian, South Asian, and European coastal areas 
(Alver et al., 2018; Winkel et al., 2015). Emissions related to marine engines at berth contribute to a 
minor fraction of total ship emissions. However, berth related emissions severely affect human health 
in the local environment due to the proximity of ships to densely populated areas (Chu Van et al., 
2019). 
 
According to the European Commission (EC) air pollution in cities is a key concern since it results 
in negative effects on the environment and human health (Winkel et al., 2015).  Mitigation measures 
are therefore needed to reduce air pollution caused by the shipping industry (López-Aparicio, 2017). 
The maritime sector has significant abatement potential and environmental benefits can be reached 
through appropriate measures. For the improvement of air quality in coastal and port areas, proposed 
solutions include Emissions Control Areas (ECAs) and the implementation of shore power facilities 
(Winkel et al., 2015). According to the International Maritime Organization (IMO) (2012), shore 
power is defined as “a measure to improve air quality in ports and port cities, to reduce emissions of 
air pollutants and noise and, to a lesser extent, to reduce carbon dioxide through ships at berth re-
placing onboard generated power from diesel auxiliary engines with electricity supplied by the 
shore” (IMO, 2012).  
 
DFDS, an international Danish company dealing with international shipping and logistics, is an ex-
ample of a shipping company that has retrofitted its passenger ferries to receive shore power in ports. 
Currently, two DFDS ferries operating on the Copenhagen-Oslo route receive shore power while at 
berth in the port of Oslo. While at berth in the port of Copenhagen, the ferries operate on fuel-powered 
auxiliary engines onboard (DFDS, 2020b).

1.1 Aim and Objective 
 
This study aims to investigate the effectiveness of shore power facilities as a mitigation measure in 
ports to reduce air pollution, taking the ports of Copenhagen and Oslo as case ports. The study con-
sists of a theoretical and a practical investigation covering emission estimations and on-site measure-
ments of several air pollutants related to ferries in ports. By the use of the bottom-up approach, also 
referred to as the activity-based method according to the European Monitoring and Evaluation Pro-
gramme/ European Environment Agency (EMEP/EEA), it is possible to estimate emissions from the 
DFDS passenger ferries based on ship information and data. The practical investigation of this study 
focuses on local air quality evaluated by the conductance of on-site measurements in the port areas 
in the two cities. Through a collaboration with DFDS, it has been possible to collect data and infor-
mation regarding fuel consumption, technical information, and access to port areas and engine rooms 
onboard, including a trip to Oslo. 
 
The structure of this study is as follows: Chapter 2 provides a general overview of port-related ship-
ping emissions and air pollutants. Regulation on the maritime sector from an international as well as 
European perspective is outlined in Chapter 3. Chapter 4 provides a literature review of shore power 
installations in research. Chapter 5 consists of a general presentation of port infrastructure and char-
acteristics of the ports of Copenhagen and Oslo, whereas Chapter 6 provides an overview of the 
DFDS Group and the specific ferries considered in this study. The methodology on emission estima-
tions, their externalities, and on-site measurements are explained in Chapter 7. The results are pre-
sented and discussed in Chapter 8. Uncertainties and limitations of this study are outlined in Chapter 
9. Finally, concluding remarks and recommendations are included in Chapter 10.

2 Air Pollution in Ports 
 
The importance of the maritime sector and the demand for the transportation of goods and passengers 
overseas are increasing worldwide, which has resulted in increasing use of fossil fuels (Fridell, 2019; 
Toscano and Murena, 2019). One of the main sources of air pollutants affecting human health and 
contributing to air pollution problems are port activities, mainly due to combustion processes in ship 
engines (Bailey and Solomon, 2004; Styhre and Winnes, 2019). The growth of maritime traffic rises 
the attention and concern towards the environmental and health effects caused by the shipping indus-
try. Emissions from ships impact air quality both considering local and global aspects. Local effects 
are mainly dependent on emissions in ports, where emissions of SO2, PM, NOX, and VOCs impact 
human health in ports and nearby residential areas. Global effects are mainly depending on emissions 
of CO2 from ships that contribute to global warming (Toscano and Murena, 2019). Port-related pol-
lution is mainly caused by ships, corresponding to approximately 10 times greater pollution compared 
to other port-related operations. The majority of ship emissions occur at sea due to their long operat-
ing time at sea. However, port-related emissions contribute to a much higher degree of human expo-
sure to air pollution since the emissions occur in the urban environment and close to densely popu-
lated areas (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). The demand for residential areas is increasing as population 
in urban cities grows over time. Therefore, it is currently observed that areas near harbors are recon-
structed to residential areas to accommodate this demand (Ariza et al., 2019; Copenhagen Munici-
pality, 2012). 
 
In general, passenger traffic has been observed to increase over time. Moreover, the cruise industry 
is characterized by continuous growth for over three decades. Around the world, the number of cruise 
ship passengers was at 24 million in 2017, this number is expected to increase to 30 million by 2024 
(Toscano and Murena, 2019). In urban cities, cruises tend to arrive in ports close to city centers to 
satisfy passengers (Rodrigue and Notteboom, 2012). However, this results in higher exposure levels 
of air pollution to a larger share of the population located in close distance to port areas (Styhre and 
Winnes, 2019). 
 
In this chapter, emissions from ships and the different types of fuels used in the shipping sector 
worldwide will be elaborated. Furthermore, major air pollutants caused by vessels in ports and their 
related human health and environmental effects will be presented.

2.1 Shipping Emissions and Fuel Types 
 
The following section on shipping emissions and fuel type theory is mainly extracted from the sources 
Styhre and Winnes (2019) and Fridell (2019).  
 
In ports, pollutants from ships are emitted during different activities such as maneuvering and at berth 
operations. It is expected that the main fraction of emissions from ships occurs during berth mode. 
However, it should be noted that days of anchorage and long fairway channels, which can be the case 
for tankers and dry bulk ships, also have high contributions to port-related emissions (Styhre and 
Winnes, 2019). Total emissions vary depending on ship type, type of engines, fuel used, the size of 
the ship, operation profile, and if any technologies for emission reduction are used (Fridell, 2019).   
 
The composition of exhaust gas emissions from ship engines is influenced by several factors. One 
factor is the engine type since different engine types are related to different combustion temperatures. 
In general, a slow-speed two-stroke engine has higher combustion temperatures compared to a high-
speed or medium-speed engine of four-stroke. On many ships four-stroke engines are often used as 
main engines. Four-stroke engines are often installed in cruise and passenger ships, whereas two-
stroke engines are the preferred option for larger ships. Engines of two-stroke require significant 
height in the engine room and are more fuel-efficient, while the design of four-stroke engines is more 
compact. Auxiliary engines are mainly small marine four-stroke diesel engines that are used for elec-
tricity generation onboard ships. Furthermore, fuel-powered boilers on ships are used for steam, heat, 
and hot water production. When main engines are used on high loads, heat from the excess exhaust 
gases can be used for heat production. However, at berth and while the main engines are operating 
on low loads, the demand for heat and steam cannot be met by the low exhaust gas heat, therefore 
boilers are used. The power and fuel consumption for auxiliary engines, boilers, and main engines 
are, therefore, diverse at different operational modes (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). 
 
The fuel type is as well considered as an important factor since the fuel choice can influence the 
emissions of, especially PM and SO2 (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). Heavy Fuel Oil (HFO) originating 
from residual oils is a low-cost fuel, which is the main fuel type used by the shipping industry. This 
fuel is a highly viscous liquid with high sulfur content and contains potentially carcinogenic sub-
stances. Furthermore, distilled fuel and natural gas are used as fuel type for ship engines as well 
(Fridell, 2019), and is mainly used in port areas due to stricter regulated requirements (IMO, 2020a). 
Marine Gas Oil (MGO) is the lightest fuel oil used and is a clean distillate oil that has a maximum 
sulfur content of 0.1%, a density of 860 kg/m3, and a viscosity of 1 - 10 cSt. This fuel type is mainly 
used in auxiliary engines and smaller ships. Another fuel type is Marine Diesel Oil (MDO), which 
contains heavier distillates and smaller amounts of heavier oil. The viscosity and density of MDO are 
higher compared to MGO and can have higher sulfur content (Fridell, 2019). In comparison, the 
sulfur content for land-based transport fuel is between 0.001% - 0.005% (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). 
Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) is a fuel used in the shipping industry on LNG tankers. The fuel is 
produced by cooling natural gas to 127 Kelvin (K) and, thereafter, compressing it to liquid in order 
to reduce the volume. To meet restrictions on sulfur emissions, LNG has been introduced to the 
==PAGE:17==

market as an alternative fuel instead of MGO. The sulfur content in LNG is negligible and the main 
component of this fuel is methane (CH4). Currently, it is used as fuel for several larger ships. Metha-

produced by natural gas and has the advantage of being a liquid at room temperature, which makes it 
more manageable on ships compared to LNG (Fridell, 2019).  
 
2.2 Major Air Pollutants and Effects 
 
Emissions of air pollutants from combustion processes have consequences for the environment and 
human health. Major air pollutants related to international shipping are NOX, SO2, PM, CO2, CO, and 
VOCs (Winkel et al., 2015). It was reported that shipping-related emissions of PM are responsible 
for around 60,000 deaths annually due to cardiopulmonary and lung cancer (Alver et al., 2018; Win-
kel et al., 2015). The emissions of sulfur containing air pollutants result in acidification of freshwater 
and soil that occur by deposition processes of acidic substances. The emissions of NOX cause eu-
trophication due to the enrichment of nutrients. NOX and SO2 emissions contribute to severe impacts 
on ecosystems. Issues occurring due to climate change are related to flooding, drought, and in general, 
increased risk and impacts on ecosystems and human health (Fridell, 2019; Styhre and Winnes, 2019).

2.2.2 Carbon Monoxide (CO) 
  
CO is a widely distributed and common air pollutant, which is an odorless and colorless gas that has 
a poor water solubility, a slightly lower density than air, and a lifetime of 2 - 4 months in the atmos-
phere (WHO, 2000a; Miller, 2015). It has been reported in 2011 that the annual global emission of 
CO into the atmosphere, was estimated at approximately 888 million tons, where 68% originated 
from anthropogenic sources, and 32% occurred from natural processes (Zhong et al., 2016). Anthro-
pogenic emissions of CO mainly originate from the incomplete combustion of fossil fuels (Fenger 
and Tjell, 2009; WHO, 2000a).  
 
In the atmosphere, CO is a stable gas, and the main route of human exposure is the lungs (WHO, 
2000a). CO can enter the bloodstream through the lungs and reduce the delivery of oxygen to the 
organs in the body. Exposure to high levels of CO can be poisonous and can even affect healthy 
individuals by reduced work capacity, poor learning ability, visual impairment, and reduced manual 
dexterity. Low levels of CO are most severe for individuals with cardiovascular diseases (Miller, 
2015). CO contributes to climate change by interacting with tropospheric ozone (O3), CO2, and CH4 
through reactions with other atmospheric chemicals such as hydroxyl radicals (OH), and thereby CO 
can be classified as an indirect GHG (Satein, 2009). By the use of catalytic converters, the emissions 
of CO can be reduced (Fenger and Tjell, 2009). 
 
2.2.3 Particulate Matter (PM) 
 
Airborne PM is represented by a complex mixture of both organic and inorganic substances. The 
mixture can contain liquid, solid, or liquid and solid particles that are suspended in the air. Particles 
can be the results of many sources and have a large variety of shapes, compositions, and sizes (Fenger 
and Tjell, 2009; WHO, 2000b).  Furthermore, particles can differ in physical and chemical properties. 
In the atmosphere, the particle size varies over four orders of magnitude and ranges between few 
nanometers to more than 100 µm (WHO, 2000b). The largest particles are the coarse particles (PM10), 
which are produced by the break-up of larger solid particles. These solid particles can originate from 
mining operations, unpaved roads, wind-blown dust from agriculture processes, uncovered soil, or 
road dust (WHO, 2000b; Combes and Franchineau, 2019). As the size of particles decreases, the 
amount of energy required in order to break the particles to smaller sizes increases (WHO, 2000b). 
Fine particles (PM2.5) that are smaller in size originate from the combustion of biomass and fossil 
fuels (Combes and Franchineau, 2019), as well as secondary particles formed from gases such as 
NOX and SO2 in the atmosphere (Anderson et al., 2016). Ultrafine particles (PM0.1) which are parti-
cles smaller than 0.1 μm are related to several emission sources (Oliveira et al., 2019). The typical 
size distributions of PM in urban air can be seen in Figure 1.

shipping activities depends on fuel type and combustion characteristics. High levels of Polycyclic

to particle formation. Furthermore, the sulfur content in fuels has an impact on particle emissions due 
to oxidation of sulfur and the formation of sulfate particles. In general, refined marine fuel cause 
fewer pollution emissions with particles compared to heavier fuels such as HFO (Styhre and Winnes,

association between pollution of PM and lung cancer has been observed (Bailey and Solomon, 2004).

In general, it is observed that children are more vulnerable to the adverse health effects of air pollution 
when exposed compared to adults. Children are more physically active and thereby, inhale greater 
amounts of air volume per body weight than adults, they have immature defense mechanisms for 
detoxification, and a different metabolism. Therefore, children can be exposed to higher levels of 
pollution compared to adults within the same environment. During spring and summer, higher expo-
sure levels of children and adults to air pollutants are expected since people spend more time outdoors 
(Oliveira et al., 2019).  
 
Coarse particles (PM10) 
 
PM10 has an aerodynamic diameter of less than 10 µm. Short-term exposure to PM10 can cause effects 
on respiratory health. However, exposure to PM2.5 is a stronger risk factor in terms of mortality com-
pared to PM10 (WHO, 2013). PM10 is often formed by resuspension and mechanical grinding of solid 
material. The primary composition is crustal elements of organic debris and resuspended road dust. 
The half-life of PM10 is minutes to hours and can travel from <1 to 10s of km in air (Kim et al., 2014).    
 
Fine particles (PM2.5) 
 
PM2.5 can be formed during combustion processes. The half-life of PM2.5 is days to weeks and can 
travel from 100s to 1000s of km in air (Kim et al., 2014). According to the World Health Association, 
it was estimated that 1.2 million deaths worldwide in 2015 were due to PM2.5 emissions (Combes and 
Franchineau, 2019). On the other hand, human exposure to ambient PM2.5 concentration has been 
ranked fifth as a mortality risk factor and related to 4.2 million deaths in 2015 (Gidhagen et al., 2018; 
Cohen et al., 2017). In 2010, ambient PM2.5 exposure was ranked 11th out of 79 risk factors contrib-
uting to the global burden of disease (Clifford et al., 2018). Air quality standards in terms of limit

values of several ambient air pollutants, including PM, have been implemented by the European Un-
ion (EU) (see Section 3.2.1 Air Quality Standards).  
 
Exposure to PM2.5 has been associated with an increase in cardiovascular risk and associated mortal-
ity. Impacts of these particles on the cardiovascular system include activation of prothrombotic path-
ways, inflammation, oxidative stress, neurological and vascular dysfunction (Combes and Franchi-
neau, 2019).  
 
Ultrafine Particles (PM0.1) 
 
PM0.1 are known to have potential adverse health effects due to their small size of less than 0.1 μm 
that allows them to penetrate deep into the lungs and through different systems of the body, such as 
cardiovascular and respiratory systems (Clifford et al., 2018). PM0.1 has several sources, which are 
mainly from combustion processes, including transportation, cooking, and home heating. According 
to research the exposure of children to elevated levels of PM0.1 can result in a high risk of reduced 
lung function and development of asthma (Oliveira et al., 2019).  
 
2.2.4 Non-Methane Volatile Organic Compounds (NMVOCs) 
 
NMVOCs are a collection of organic compounds excluding CH4, which differ in chemical composi-
tions. Emissions of NMVOCs into the atmosphere originate from several sources, including combus-
tion and production processes (EEA, 2015). Anthropogenic emissions that are mainly related to fossil 
fuels are the main contributor to VOCs sources in urban areas (Vullo et al., 2015). The residence time 
for NMVOCs in the atmosphere varies from few hours to years and depends on the chemical activity 
of the compounds. Furthermore, these compounds are reactive and influence atmospheric chemistry. 
Through oxidation processes, the organic compounds can result in the formation of secondary pollu-
tants as well as altered chemical properties of the compounds themselves (Salo et al., 2016).  Second-
ary pollutants that can be formed from these compounds include secondary particles and tropospheric 
O3 (EEA, 2015; Salo et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2017). Some of the NMVOCs are toxic compounds 
that can cause direct impact on human health and are considered carcinogenic (Salo et al., 2016; 
Huang et al., 2017).  
 
2.2.5 Nitrogen Oxides (NOX) 
  
The sum of Nitric Oxide (NO) and Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2) is indicated as NOX in emission invento-
ries (Fenger and Tjell, 2009). The formation of NOX is depended on combustion temperatures and 
constant supply of oxygen that result in NOX from nitrogen content in fuel (Styhre and Winnes, 2019; 
Salo et al., 2016). Generally, the main fraction of the nitrogen will be emitted as NO, which afterward 
will be oxidized by O3 in the atmosphere to NO2 (Fenger and Tjell, 2009). Furthermore, atmospheric 
reactions of NOX with other gases on the surface of existing particles can form secondary fine parti-
cles (Salo et al., 2016). Additionally, secondary fine particles can as well be formed by atmospheric 
reactions of NOX with ammonia (NH3) (Hodan and Barnard, 2004). According to numerous studies, 
it has been observed that NOX, as the health damaging NO2, can result in toxic effects on the airways 
in the human body, which can lead to asthmatic reactions and inflammation (Baily and Solomon, 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
10 
2004; EPA, 2011; Salo et al., 2016). People with asthma or allergies react stronger to common aller-
gens such as pollen in case of NOX exposure. When ambient levels of NOX are high in the air, children 
who already have asthma are more likely to wheeze, cough, and suffer from decreased pulmonary 
function (Bailey and Solomon, 2004). Furthermore, NOX can impact nature and ecosystems by eu-
trophication due to nutrient enrichment (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). Additionally, this air pollutant 
can contribute to acid rain and thus impacting ecosystems by atmospheric transformation of NOX into 
nitric acid (HNO3) (Press-Kristensen, 2018; Salo et al., 2016). 
 
The change in combustion conditions and the use of catalytic converters can reduce the emissions of 
NOX (Fenger and Tjell, 2009). In addition, Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR) is found to be an effec-
tive emission control technology for NOX reductions. EGR ensures that a part of the exhaust gas is 
cooled and recirculated to the combustion chamber, resulting in lower combustion temperatures and 
NO formation through changes in the levels of oxygen and heat capacity (Parsmo et al., 2017). The 
use of Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) has been found to be an effective technology for NOX 
and noise reduction. By adding a specific amount of urea to the exhaust gas, NH3 released from urea 
at high temperatures will react with NOX in the exhaust gas and thereby be converted to harmless 
nitrogen and water vapor (Press-Kristensen, 2018; Kristensen and Psaraftis, 2015). 
 
2.2.6 Sulfur Dioxide (SO2) and Sulfur Oxides (SOX) 
  
SO2 is formed during fuel combustion, where sulfur bounds to hydrocarbons, which react with oxy-
gen. The amount of SO2 formed is higher compared to the amount of SO3, and generally, 3 - 5% of 
SO2 will be oxidized to SO3 in diesel exhaust. Sulfate (SO42-) particles are rapidly formed in the 
atmosphere by SO2 (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). In addition, SO2 can react with other pollutants and 
in particular NH3 in the atmosphere and thereby health damaging secondary fine particles can be 
formed (Hodan and Barnard, 2004). Furthermore, SO2 cause environmental impacts while deposited 
such as acidification (Styhre and Winnes, 2019). Sulfur Oxides (SOX) can be formed by burning 
sulfur-containing fuels. Acidic aerosols can be created when SOX reacts with water vapor in the air. 
These aerosols can cause irritation in airways, coughing, and discomfort in healthy people, whereas, 
in asthmatics, severe respiratory symptoms can occur (Bailey and Solomon, 2004).  
 
SOX can be removed from the exhaust gas by the use of scrubbers, where seawater is used to wash 
out the SOX, and thereby forming SO42- (Zis et al., 2016). SO42- is thereafter discharged at sea with 
the scrubber water that can contain other toxic compounds. Scrubber water can cause effects in case 
of discharge near coastal areas, therefore, it is recirculated with the addition of sodium hydroxide 
(Press-Kristensen, 2018). Scrubbers are found to reduce SOX with more than 98%, and PM can be 
reduced with 40 - 75%. Scrubber technologies can as well be alternatives to the low-sulfur fuels, and 
thereby shipping companies can meet implemented sulfur regulations (Kristensen and Psaraftis, 
2015) (Details on fuel sulfur content regulation can be viewed in Section 3.1 IMO).  
 
In general, the heavier the fuel, the higher is the sulfur content. An important factor for fuel quality 
is the sulfur content as it controls the emissions of pollutants such SO2 and PM (Hulskotte and Van 
der Gon, 2009).  The lower the sulfur content in marine fuels, the lower is the fuel specific emission

3 Regulation 
 
In this chapter, regulation on shipping in terms of atmospheric pollution will be outlined concerning 
IMO and the EU. 
 
3.1 International Maritime Organization (IMO) 
 
IMO is the regulating actor for international shipping, which is the specialized agency of the United 
Nations (UN) that is responsible for the security and safety of shipping as well as the prevention of 
atmospheric and marine pollution by ships (IMO, 2020b). Marine Pollution (MARPOL), which is the 
International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships deals with ship pollution from 
operational as well as accidental reasons. MARPOL is divided into six annexes regarding oil, harmful 
substances carried by sea in packaged form, noxious liquid substances carried in bulk, sewage, gar-
bage, and air pollution from ships. Emissions to air and fuel efficiency of ships are regulated through 
the international framework of Annex VI, which entered into force in 2005 (IMO, 2020c).   
 
IMO has established limits for NOX and SOX emissions after the revision of Annex VI in 2008 and 
since July 2010 (Nunes et al., 2017a; IMO, 2020a). The main changes were the implementation of 
ECAs, and thereby reduction of NOX, SOX, and indirectly PM (Nunes et al., 2017a; Tichavska et al., 
2015; IMO, 2020a). Currently, four ECAs are implemented, including the North Sea, Baltic Sea, 
Caribbean Sea, and North America (Nunes et al., 2017a). 
 
From January 2020, a new global limit of sulfur content in marine fuels has been established and is 
currently on 0.5% (IMO, 2020a). With the new limit of sulfur content, it is expected that an overall 
reduction of 77% in SOX emissions from ships corresponding to an annual reduction of 8.5 million 
tons of SOX will be achieved. Additionally, it is expected that PM emissions from shipping will be 
reduced as well with this new limit, and thereby reductions of lung cancer, asthma, stroke, cardiovas-
cular and pulmonary diseases are expected (IMO, 2020d). From an environmental perspective, the 
reduction of sulfur emissions will contribute to the prevention of ocean acidification, acid rain, and 
benefit forest, crops, and aquatic species. Vessels that use exhaust gas cleaning systems for limiting 
air pollutants, such as scrubbers, can be approved as an alternative to meet the requirement of the new 
sulfur limit (IMO, 2020d). Under the regulation framework MARPOL Annex VI, a maximum sulfur 
content of 0.1% in marine fuel is set for all ships in ECAs including EU-territories such as the English 
Channel, the Baltic Sea and the North Sea, which represent the SOX Emission Control Areas (SECAs) 
(Tichavska et al., 2017; Nunes et al., 2017a).  
 
Annex VI contains as well a three tier approach (Tier I-III) regarding NOX emissions, which identify 
the allowable emissions of total NOX (López-Aparicio et al., 2017). Tier III applies to new ships that 
are built after January 2016 operating in the United States (U.S.) Caribbean and North American NOX 
Emission Control Areas (NECAs) (IMO, 2020a). The Baltic Sea and the North Sea will become 
NECAs in 2021. Therefore, Tier III will become mandatory for ships built after January 2021 oper-
ating in these areas as well (IMO, 2017; Nunes et al., 2017b). Table B provides an overview of the

3.2 European Union (EU) 
 
At European level, shipping emissions and the prevention of air pollution from ships are regulated 
according to the provisions of Directive 2012/33/EU (Dragović et al., 2015; EU, 2012). The EU has 
on the basis of the Strategy for Sustainable Development expressed concerns regarding the impact of 
maritime transport on air quality (Tichavska et al., 2017). This resulted in the establishment of the 
Directive 2012/33/EU (2005/33/EC and 1999/32/EC) covering sulfur regulation for marine fuels. The 
EU directives in force require that all vessels calling in an EU port should, either use low sulfur fuels 
with a sulfur content of less than 0.1% or use shore power facilities in ports if their hoteling time 
exceeds two hours (Tichavska et al., 2017; EU, 2012).  
 
Policy action regarding shore power installations in European ports has been published in the binding 
Directive 2014/94/EU on the deployment of alternative fuel infrastructure. According to the EU, 
Member States shall assess the need for shore power facilities for seagoing and inland waterway 
vessels in ports in their national policy frameworks. As a priority shore power shall be installed in 
ports of the Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) Core Network1, and in other ports by 31st 
December 2025, unless the costs are disproportionate to the benefits including environmental benefits 
or in case of no demand (EU, 2014). Additionally, the EU recommends through Directive 
2012/33/EU that “Member States shall, as an alternative solution for reducing emissions, encourage 
the use of onshore power supply systems by docked vessels.” (EU, 2012). However, there are cur-
rently no obligatory international requirements that would facilitate or mandate the use of shore power 
(Winkel et al., 2015).

positive feedback of shore power, and in particular, environmental and social benefits has been re-

4.1 Literature Review of Shore Power in Ports 
 
In the research area of shore power, this aspect is referred to as ‘shore-side electricity’, ‘cold ironing’, 
’alternative maritime power’, ‘alternative power supply’, ‘onshore power supply’, ’shore-to-ship 
power supply’, and ‘shore-side power’ (Tseng and Pilcher, 2015; Radwan et al., 2019). Cold ironing 
is defined as a practice for cooling down iron coal-fired engines when ships are connected to power 
while in port. Ships can be connected to cold ironing from ship to ship, which is more related to 
military applications or from shore in ports (Zis, 2018). Generally, a shore power installation implies 
that a ship while at berth can be connected to a port electricity network instead of using diesel-pow-
ered auxiliary engine onboard for electricity production (Winkel et al., 2015; Tseng and Pilcher, 
2015). According to IMO (2012), shore power is defined as “a measure to improve air quality in 
ports and port cities, to reduce emissions of air pollutants and noise and, to a lesser extent, to reduce 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
18 
carbon dioxide through ships at berth replacing onboard generated power from diesel auxiliary en-
gines with electricity supplied by the shore” (IMO, 2012). Shore power costs and benefits are de-
pendent on several parameters such as port size, electricity price, proximity to urban areas, grid factor 
and conditions (Winkel et al., 2015). Three components are entailed in shore power: 1) shore-side 
electrical system and infrastructure, 2) cable management system, and 3) ship-side electrical system 
(Tseng and Pilcher, 2015).

4.1.1 Benefits of Shore Power Facilities  
 
According to Zis (2018), the use of shore power in ports can be beneficial regarding local air quality 
and the reduction of ship emissions. However, ships at berth that use fuel-powered boilers, as opposed 
to electric boilers or other alternatives, to provide heating supply will still cause emissions in ports 
(Zis, 2018; Oslo Municipality, 2018). The emission reduction of ships in ports by the use of shore 
power depends on the fuel type burned from the ships and the energy mix that provides the electricity 
to the shore power facility (Winkel et al., 2015). Winkel et al. (2015) studied the potential and envi-
ronmental benefits of shore power use in Europe. In the study, it was found that shore power is an 
option for the reduction of unwanted environmental impacts caused by ships in ports. Furthermore, 
it was concluded that the expected total benefit in terms of health in Europe by the use of shore power 
was estimated at € 2.94 billion in 2020. In general, shore power can be beneficial for ships that overall 
have a high electricity demand, and for ships that frequently visit a specific port (Winkel et al., 2015). 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
19 
Therefore, Winkel et al. (2015) has recommended that mitigation action should first be aimed at 
cruises and ferries. Furthermore, implementation of shore power in ports close to residential areas 
should be considered, where the impacts of shipping emissions are more intense (Winkel et al., 2015).  
 
A study conducted by Sun et al. (2018) estimated the total emissions of air pollutants in the port of 
Qingdao in China caused by shipping vessels. Based on the findings of the study, it was concluded 
that effective control measures should be established together with the adoption of shore power. 
These solutions were found to be feasible strategies to reduce emissions as well as protecting the 
environment from air pollutants. According to Kotrikla et al. (2016), the implementation of shore 
power is an effective method to reduce air pollution and noise by the auxiliary engines at berth. In 
the study, the implementation of shore power in the port of Aegean islands was investigated. The 
findings showed that the introduction of shore power could be explored in a context of sustainable 
development in terms of protection of natural environment, biodiversity, and human health. Further-
more, it is mentioned that the implementation of shore power will support the economic activity by 
the reduction of adverse effects of human health and the environment (Kotrikla et al., 2016).   
 
The shipping emissions in Nordic ports were as well estimated, and possible mitigation strategies 
were assessed by López-Aparicio et al. (2017). In the study, the port of Oslo was taken as a case port 
investigating the possible reductions of emissions that can be obtained by implementing several strat-
egies and technologies. It was found that the main contributors to shipping emissions are international 
ferries, cruises, and container vessels among the oceangoing vessels in the port of Oslo. Based on the 
study, it could be concluded that emissions of NOX and CO2 can be reduced by 15% when combing 
shore power and a speed reduction zone by 2020, compared to 2013 as a baseline. For international 
ferries, it was reported that in 2020, the increase of NOX emissions due to higher maritime traffic will 
be balanced compared to the baseline year 2013 by the use of shore power. In general, it was found 
that shore power will be an effective method to reduce emissions in the port (López-Aparicio et al., 
2017). Vaishnav et al. (2015) investigated the costs and benefits of implementing shore power for 
vessels at ports in the U.S. The findings showed that an annual air quality benefit of $45 - 150 million 
could be obtained by retrofitting ports and vessels to receive shore power. According to the study, 
environmental and health benefits2 would balance the cost of implementing shore power and vessel 
retrofit. In 2014, the California Air Resources Board (CARB) required that 50% as a minimum of a 
fleet’s visits to major ports in California either use shore power for most of the time or reduce the 
power generation from auxiliary engines by at least 50%. The regulation implemented by CARB is 
applied to passenger vessels, refrigerated cargo, and container vessels (Vaishnav et al., 2015).

4.1.2 Challenges of Shore Power Facilities 
 
The installation of shore power in ports can be challenging since many factors influence the imple-
mentation of this technology. The challenges can occur from different perspectives, such as eco-
nomic, technical, regulatory, environmental, or managerial aspects (Radwan et al., 2019). Financial 
challenges include all costs related to this facility in terms of investment, operational, maintenance, 
and electricity costs. The investment costs cover the total costs of retrofitting shipside, berth as well 
as port infrastructure (Radwan et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2019). According to Power Con (2020), which 
is a supplier of shore power installations, the implementation cost of shore power in the port of Co-
penhagen, was estimated at € 4.5 million covering one shore power supply with one connection in 
Ocean-quay for cruises. Social and environmental benefits from the use of shore power are difficult 
to distribute or quantify. Therefore, a key challenge in the EU is that potential investors do not nec-
essarily obtain an economic benefit from shore power (Winkel et al., 2015).  
 
From a technical perspective, the power demand needed for ships at berth differs, therefore, the re-
quirement of power creates challenges to the port and demands sufficient power stability and availa-
bility. Major steps that are constituted are beforehand assessment of total power capacity of the entire 
port network, the risk of possible overload to the power grid, and the risk of destabilizing the elec-
tricity network (Radwan et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2018). In 2012, the major technical problems were 
solved after the release of the High Voltage Shore Connection (HVSC) standard. Onboard and shore-
side characteristics should match in terms of frequency and voltage. The majority of vessels are 
equipped with 60 Hz, therefore, technical solutions to solve the 50/60 Hz variation is the use of con-
verters, whereas the use of transformers will ensure voltage conversion (Winkel et al., 2015; Radwan 
et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2018). Other technical aspects of shore power are delays in connection and 
disconnection time and the risk of high voltage cable handling. The management of shore power can 
bring burdens to the port authorities. The installation and use of shore power involve several actors 
such as port authorities, shipping companies, and electricity suppliers. Therefore, the collaboration 
between port authorities and ship operators is an important aspect regarding shore power installations 
in ports (Radwan et al., 2019).  
 
As presented in Section 3.2 EU, the EU recommends through Directive 2012/33/EU and 
2006/339/EC, the promotion and use of shore power as a method to reduce air pollution (Radwan et 
al., 2019; EC, 2006; EU, 2012). Additionally, the EU Directive 2014/94/EU requires that Member 
States by December 2025 shall install shore power facilities as a priority in ports of the TENT-T Core 
Network and in other ports, unless the costs are disproportionate to the benefits and if there is no 
demand (EU, 2014). However, the implementation of such facilities in ports is not internationally 
mandatory yet. Thus, on a global level, the lack of regulatory pressure is an aspect that impairs the 
motivation of port authorities and ship operators to implement shore power facilities (Radwan et al., 
2019; Winkel et al., 2015). The environmental challenges of shore power are related to the energy 
sources that are used for the onshore electricity generation (Radwan et al., 2019; Winkel et al., 2015). 
High share of renewable energy sources can result in higher benefits of shore power installations in 
ports from environmental and economic perspectives. However, the installation of shore power, in 
general, has the advantage of moving air pollutants from shipping in terms of emission reductions 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
21 
from populated areas, to more remote areas where power plants usually are placed (Winkel et al., 
2015).  
 
A number of research studies investigated the prevention of shore power and the feasibility of this 
system in different ports worldwide. A study conducted by Radwan et al. (2019), investigated the 
critical barriers to the installation of shore power facilities for green port development with a focus 
on the port of Djibouti in Africa. The key barriers that have influenced the shore power installation 
in Djibouti port are power requirement, electricity cost, and investment cost. Tseng and Pilcher (2015) 
investigated the feasibility and potential of shore power for the port of Kaohsiung in Taiwan. The 
findings of the study were that shore power will provide environmental and socio-economic benefits 
in the long-term such as reduction of noise, improving air quality, and reduction of health impacts 
from shipping-related activities. In addition, it was found that by 2020, NOX and CO2 can be reduced 
by 428 tons and 25,391 tons, respectively. Regarding the environmental benefits, it is found that in 
2020 the benefit that could be achieved is US$ 660,166 and US$ 2,136,148 for CO2 and NOX. How-
ever, it is mentioned that several issues, such as electricity prices, the responsibility of shore power 
costs, coordinating different departments, and balancing the different stakeholder needs, are to be 
considered when planning the introduction of shore power. Therefore, the implementation of the fa-
cility is currently not considered in the port of Kaohsiung, mainly due to economic challenges. In the 
port, the implementation costs of shore power will need to be carried by the government to avoid 
trade discourage (Tseng and Pilcher, 2015).  
 
Other alternatives have been found to be more appropriate solutions to reduce air pollution in ports. 
Wang et al. (2015) investigated the costs and benefits of shore power in the port of Shenzhen in 
China. The findings showed that the cost benefit of reducing one ton of CO2, NOX, SOX, and PM are 
$2,300, $56,000, $290,000, and $1.4 million, respectively. In the study, the advantage of switching 
the fuel type from 0.5% (sulfur content) MDO to 0.1% (sulfur content) MGO as an alternative to 
reduce air pollutants in ports were investigated as well. It was found that switching the fuel type will 
reduce the emissions of SOX and PM with much lower costs. However, lower reductions will be 
obtained compared to shore power. In general, it was concluded by the study that fuel switching as a 
method to reduce air pollution is prioritized over shore power since it is a cheaper and less challenging 
solution (Wang et al., 2015).  
 
Based on current research, it is observed that shore power can result in environmental and health 
benefits and is a technology that can improve the local air quality in ports and nearby populated areas. 
However, the challenges of installing this facility remain in several ports worldwide. Installation costs 
are observed to be one of the main challenges of shore power facilities. However, according to stud-
ies, installation costs can be solved by electricity sales for visiting ships, which can represent a po-
tential business case for port operators to invest in shore power facilities (Winkel et al., 2015; COWI, 
2019).

7 Methodology 
 
This study covers a theoretical and a practical investigation of shore power effectiveness on air pol-
lution in ports. The theoretical part consists of emission and external cost estimations, whereas the 
practical part covers on-site measurements of air pollutants in the local urban environment near and 
in port areas. In this chapter, the methodologies and materials used for both investigations will be 
presented. Furthermore, details regarding the on-site measurements and specific measuring locations 
will be provided.  
 
7.1 Methodology for Emission and External Cost Estimations 
 
The methodology used to estimate air polluting emissions from the DFDS ferries is based on the Tier 
3 Ship movement methodology according to the EMEP/EEA Air Pollutant Emission Inventory Guide-
book 2019 (Trozzi et al., 2019). This method is also known as the ‘Bottom-up Approach’ or the 
‘Activity-based Approach’ and is based on information regarding ship movement for individual ships 
(Trozzi et al., 2019; Nunes et al., 2017b). Furthermore, Emission Factors (EFs) specific to the ship-
ping activities are applied in this method. During a certain activity, the EFs are used to relate the 
emitted quantity of a specific pollutant with the energy used by the engines of a ship (Nunes et al., 
2017b). According to EMEP/EEA, it is recommended to use this method if detailed technical infor-
mation and data are available, such as power installed or fuel consumption, as well as hours used 
during different modes of ship activity. Additionally, this method can be used for emission calcula-
tions following the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE)/EMEP definition of 
national and international shipping (Trozzi et al., 2019).   
 
In this study, two ship activities are considered related to port emissions: a) Maneuvering, which is 
based on the average time used for maneuver operation inside port area and ship specific information, 
and b) Hoteling, also known as ship berth mode, where ship information in terms of fuel consumption 
is considered. The quantity of air pollutant emissions produced during maneuvering and hoteling in 
the two case ports were estimated for the two DFDS ferries Pearl and Crown Seaways operating on 
the Copenhagen-Oslo route. The air pollutants considered in the theorical part of the study are NOX, 
SO2, CO2, CO, PM (PM2.5, and PM10), and NMVOCs. Data in terms of fuel consumption and tech-
nical information of the ferries were provided by DFDS. Data in terms of load factors (LFs) and EFs 
were gathered from literature, environmental agencies, and, in particular, the EMEP/EEA Air pollu-
tant Emission Inventory Guidebook 2019. It should be noted that related EFs for the pollutants esti-
mated in this study were not all available under the Tier 3 approach. According to EMEP/EEA, EFs 
given under the Tier 1 Default Approach in the Emission Inventory Guidebook 2019 could be used 
as an alternative. Additionally, EFs provided by Entec (2002)3, ICF (2009)4, and Moldanová (2010)

7.1.2 External Cost 
 
The damage produced by air polluting emissions can be evaluated through several methods. Damage 
costs or external costs of estimated emissions in this study are derived from a study conducted by the 
Danish Centre for Environment and Energy (DCE), which applies the method Economic Valuation 
of Air pollution (EVA). EVA is a model system developed by Aarhus University in Denmark that 
can be used to estimate population-level exposure to various air pollutants based on an impact path-
way chain. Thereby, it is possible to conduct an integrated assessment of the external health cost 
related to specific pollution sources in the air (Ballini and Bozzo, 2015; Andersen et al., 2019).   
 
The external costs are based on data from 2016 related to shipping in the Baltic and North Sea. These 
costs are related to several diseases, admissions, sick days, and acute deaths and the emissions of air 
pollutants including NOX, SO2, and PM2.5. According to findings of DCE, the external cost of SOX 
(SO2/SO4), NOX, and PM2.5 are equal to DKK 104, 241, and 589 per emitted kg, respectively. In 
addition, the proportion of air pollution that has effects on Danish area is provided as well from the 
DCE study, indicating the percentage of the total costs of each air pollutant that is paid by the Danish 
Government. These percentages correspond to 4% of SOX, 3% of NOX, and 3% of PM2.5 emissions 
==PAGE:46==

(Andersen et al., 2019). In this study, it is as well assumed that 30% of the emitted pollution affects 
the city locally. Therefore, local exposure surcharge has been included in the external costs in terms 
of small non-industrial domestic incinerators, since external costs related to shipping specific engines 
and boilers are not available. The local exposure surcharges are found to be DKK 1143 per emitted 
kg of PM2.5 and DKK 653 per emitted kg of NOX (Andersen et al., 2019) (More details can be viewed

7.2.1 Instrumentation 
 
PM0.1 was measured by the use of a P-Trak (TSI -Model 8525 Ultrafine Particle Counter). In the port 
of Oslo, two P-Trak devices were used for the measurements. The P-Trak recorded one measurement 
each second, and thereby one minute consisted of 60 measurements. Pollution was measured at se-
lected locations near or in the ports with respect to the wind direction. To ensure accurate measure-
ments, isopropyl alcohol characterized by a 99.5% purity was inserted into the instrument cartridge 
cavity by using a cartridge. This cartridge was only in use during the conductance of the measure-
ments and was stored in an alcohol fill capsule containing the alcohol when the instrument was not 
in use. The purpose of isopropyl alcohol is to enlarge microscopic particles in the air, and thereby 
particles can be detected and counted easily (TSI, 2013). The used instruments were recently cali-
brated by the manufacturer and zero checks were carried out during the study period. Therefore, the 
instruments are expected to function properly. Measured data were transferred to the TrakPro Soft-
ware and Excel for further data treatment.

7.2.3 Air Quality Index  
 
Local air quality measurements can be evaluated and communicated to the public by the use of the 
Air Quality Index (AQI). AQI is a tool used for reporting the air quality on a daily basis, and is 
calculated for five key pollutants that harm the environment and human health including PM (PM10, 
PM2.5), SO2, NO2, O3, and CO (EU, 2020; EPA, 2014). The AQI covers six levels of health concerns 
representing different air quality conditions from good to hazardous ranging from 0 - 500. The ‘Good’ 
condition (0 - 50) indicates that air quality is satisfactory and poses no or little health risk. Moderate 
air quality condition (51 - 100) indicates an acceptable quality level; however, a moderate health 
concern in terms of respiratory symptoms for individuals that are sensitive to particle pollution may 
occur. The air quality condition ‘unhealthy to sensitive groups’ (101 - 150) indicates that the local air 
may cause health effects for sensitive groups including children, elderly people, and people with lung 
and heart diseases. However, health effects are unlikely for the general public. Health effects may 
occur for everyone when the air quality condition is classified as ‘Unhealthy’ (151 - 200) and more 
serious health effects can occur for sensitive groups. The conditions of ‘Very Unhealthy’ (201 - 300) 
and ‘Hazardous’ (301 - 500) indicate that air quality can cause more serious health effects for every-
one (EPA, 2014). An overview of the AQI value ranges with corresponding concentrations of the air 
pollutants are provided in Table I. PAM uses the AQI to provide an overview of readings while 
measuring the local air quality. PAM relate every single measurement with a color code correspond-
ing to the AQI scale (2B Technologies, 2019).

were obtained by connecting Pearl Seaways to shore power in February 2019 in contrast to onboard 
power generation by the use of auxiliary engines. It is expected that approximately the same reduc-
tions will be obtained in the port of Copenhagen for both ferries in case of an equal shore power 
installation, as the annual fuel consumption, berthing duration, ship construction, and operation are 
mainly the same in the two ports.  
 
In 2019, a report investigating alternative energy sources to be used for cruises and other types of 
vessels while at berth in the port of Copenhagen was conducted by COWI a Danish consultancy 
company. According to COWI (2019), it was reported that the estimated total emissions of CO2, PM, 
and NOX from the two DFDS ferries could be reduced by 912, 0.4, and 18 tons/year by the use of 
shore power, respectively. It should be noted that COWI (2019) estimated the reduction based on a 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
45 
standard berth duration of seven hours related to 75 minutes connect/disconnect time for a shore 
power supply considering pollution from the auxiliary engines. The total connection/disconnection 
time for shore power is overestimated in the COWI (2019) report, as it is observed that in total the 
time used for the connection activity is around 10 - 20 minutes (DFDS, 2020c; PowerCon, 2020). 
The results of this study are based on the exact berthing duration and the inclusion of both boilers and 
auxiliary engines. The emission reductions by shore power use are based on a comparative assessment 
of emissions without shore power and emissions after shore power use in 2019 in the port of Oslo for 
the same operating ferries in the two ports.  
 
The estimated reductions obtained for NOX and PM in this study are comparable to the reductions 
reported by Ballini and Bozzo (2015) that investigated air pollution from cruise ships in Copenhagen 
and the benefit of shore power. They found that reductions at 98% and 90% could be obtained for 
NOX and PM, respectively. The study takes into account that 100% of the cruises visiting the port 
used shore power in 2012. Additionally, Entec (2005) reported emission reduction efficiencies of 
shore power at 97%, 89%, and 94% for NOX, PM, and VOCs, respectively. Other studies reported 
lower reductions of shore power use despite the CO2 reduction. For instance, Zis et al. (2014) reported 
emission reductions at 48 - 70% for CO2, 3 - 60% for SO2, and 40 - 60% for NOX. The study consid-
ered containership emissions in ports in the U.S. and United Kingdom (U.K.) (Zis et al., 2014; Yu et 
al., 2019), where the energy demand is expected to be lower compared to the used energy onboard 
passenger ships.   
 
Based on the findings of this study, the use of shore power while at berth will reduce emissions from 
ferries. However, port-related emissions will still occur if boilers are fuel-powered. Alternative solu-
tions in terms of electric boilers or district heating to avoid pollution from fuel-powered boilers should 
be taken into account. In regard to electric boilers, the Oslo Port Authority has reported that the con-
struction of the shore power facility in Vippetangen, can cover the need for power to boilers as well 
in case they are electrified (Oslo Municipality, 2018). Another alternative is connecting the ferries to 
district heating network by the same approach as the shore power connection. In Gothenburg, Swe-
den, the ferry Stena Danica operated by Stena Line is currently connected to the district heating net-
work of the city to ensure heating onboard. By the implementation of this technology, it is expected 
to reduce the emissions of CO2, SOX, and NOX by 60%, 90%, and 90%, respectively. The total in-
vestment cost is approximately € 330,000; however, Stena Line reported lower costs for the project 
at € 77,000 in addition to a funded contribution of € 52,000 from the EU (Celsiuscity, 2019; Celsi-
uscity, 2015).

The total costs related to maneuvering operations have been estimated (see Table L). These costs are

arrival and departure, the ferries use both main and auxiliary engines, which pollute more than the 
use of boilers and auxiliary engines while at berth. Additionally, upon arrival, the ferries use three 
auxiliary engines due to the narrow maneuver. Therefore, the estimated external costs are high com-
pared to hoteling in ports when considering the total time of 55 minutes maneuvering and six hours 
and 45 minutes berthing duration. In 2019, the total costs of hoteling related emissions were 74% 
lower compared to ship maneuvering costs considering emissions of NOX, SO2, and PM2.5 in the port 
of Oslo. Shore power use can benefit port-related emissions; however, emissions during maneuvering 
operation can remain a problem as reflected by the exceeded levels of emission-related costs. Addi-
tionally, engine switch-off while at berth results in higher emissions during departure due to engine 
cold-start resulting in a higher amount of fuel consumed and thereby in an increased level of engine 
emissions. Cold-start is defined as the time from the first start of the engine until the coolant temper-
ature of the engine reaches 343 K, corresponding to 70 °C (Chu Van et al., 2019). Alternative solu-
tions should, therefore, be considered and investigated to reduce emissions from maneuvering oper-
ations. As an alternative battery hybrid solution, onboard ships could support maneuvering opera-
tions, and thereby reduce emissions (Oslo Municipality, 2018). As an example, the ferry Stena Jut-
landica operated by Stena Line uses installed batteries among others during maneuvering operations 
(Offshore Energy, 2018; Stena Line, 2018). Additionally, the ForSea ferries operating between Elsi-
nore (Denmark) - Helsingborg (Sweden) are battery-powered passenger ferries operating on a 20-
minute route (State of Green, 2018). Thereby battery use during maneuvering operation of ferries 
could be included in further investigations as a potential alternative solution to maneuvering related 
emissions in and near port areas. In addition, cold-start related engine emissions could be included in 
further investigations as well to provide a general understanding of the emission contributions during 
these conditions.

8.2.3 Health Effect Associations with Particle Pollution  
 
The obtained results of particle pollution in this study considering all particle sizes represent short-
term concentrations covered by one-two hour measurements, and, therefore, it is not possible to con-
nect the observed concentration levels directly to health effects based entirely on the collected data. 
However, the association of air particle pollutants in the urban environment to health effects has been 
investigated in several research studies. Scientific studies have connected human exposure to PM 
pollution to various health issues including premature death of population with lung or heart diseases, 
decreased lung function, aggravated asthma, increased respiratory symptoms and non-fatal heart at-
tacks, as well as an increase of cardiovascular diseases associated both to long-term and short-term 
exposure (Kim et al., 2014; Heinzerling et al., 2016). According to Gold et al. (1998), it was observed 
that children exposed to an excess level of PM2.5 e.g. 65 µg/m3 for a period of 24 hours are under high 
risk of reduced lung function, respiratory symptoms, and asthma medication use during an investiga-
tion of school children in Mexico City (Gold et al., 1998; Kim et al., 2014). In the study, a mean 
concentration of 30 µg/m3 and a range from 9 - 69 µg/m3 was observed during the study period (Gold 
et al., 1998). Additionally, a short-term investigation of PM2.5 on respiratory mortality in Madrid 
showed that the increase of PM2.5 concentrations resulted in respiratory caused mortality considering 
traffic-related pollution. The daily mean PM2.5 concentrations during the study period (2003 - 2005) 
ranged from 5 - 71 µg/m3. Furthermore, PM2.5 levels above 15 µg/m3 are found to cause a reduction 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
57 
of life expectancy on 0.22 years considering the city of Madrid (Guaita et al., 2011). A study con-
ducted by Schwartz et al. (2002) showed an increase of 1.5% in daily death in case of a 10 µg/m3 
concentration increase of PM2.5 considering an exposure range from 0 - 35 µg/m3. The study investi-
gated the concentration-response relation between daily death and PM2.5 concentration covering six 
U.S. cities.  
 
Compared to PM2.5, evidence on epidemiological PM0.1 health effect is limited due to the highly dy-
namic and variable nature in time and space of these particles, which makes the exposure assessment 
complicated (Habre et al., 2018). However, research studies investigating the association between 
PM0.1 and health effects have been conducted. A study conducted by Franck et al. (2011) investigated 
the particle size effect on cardiovascular disorders. The findings showed that PM0.1 exposure has 
significant adverse effects on cardiovascular diseases, in particular, hypertensive crises compared to 
PM2.5 and PM10 considering exposure to an annual average PM0.1 concentration of 12,094.5 parti-
cles/cm3 (Franck et al., 2011).  
 
Based on the findings of these research studies, PM can affect human health at different concentration 
levels. Additionally, research shows that increased levels of exposure concentrations result in a higher 
risk of human health, and thereby greater health effects. According to several studies, no evidence of 
safe levels of exposure has been supported, and thus it has not been possible to identify threshold 
levels for no observable health effects (Pascal et al., 2014; Kim et al., 2014; Martinelli et al., 2013; 
Schwartz et al., 2002). Therefore, the observed concentration levels in this study might have potential 
health effects on the local residential areas and particularly in case of elevated levels of particle pol-
lution.  
 
The AQI scale presented in Section 7.2.3 Air Quality Index is a good tool to assess the daily air quality 
and short-term health implications (Khwaja et al., 2012). The obtained results in this study in terms 
of PM10 and PM2.5 concentrations represent short-term air quality data covering one-two hours of 
measurements of the local environment near port areas in the presence and absence of ferries. There-
fore, the AQI scale cannot be directly applied to the obtained results, due to limited data. However, 
it has been observed that on ferry arrival the concentrations of both PM10 and PM2.5 reach peak levels 
at concentrations on 20 - 30 µg/m3 and 27 - 34 µg/m3 for PM2.5 and PM10, respectively (see Figure 
20b and 20c). The peak concentration levels for PM2.5 would correspond to the AQI category of 
moderate air quality in case of daily exposure to this concentration range, indicating a health concern 
in terms of respiratory symptoms for individuals that are sensitive to particle pollution. In compari-
son, a study conducted by Khawaja et al. (2012) investigated the effect of air pollution on daily mor-
bidity in Karachi, Pakistan. In the study, the AQI scale was applied on the findings, showing that 19% 
of the investigated days corresponded to the category unhealthy for sensitive groups, whereas 67% 
and 12% of the days corresponded to the unhealthy and very unhealthy categories during the study 
period (2008 - 2009), indicating the high level of pollution in the city. It should be noted that the 
study considered the pollution from road-traffic and industry in a heavily polluted city with severe 
health effects (Khawaja et al., 2012). This reflects the rather low concentration levels of PM2.5 and 
PM10 according to the AQI scale observed in the ports of Copenhagen and Oslo. In general, the 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
58 
concentration levels observed in the two ports would correspond to a good-moderate air quality index, 
in case the same level of the measured concentrations would be observed on daily basis (24-hour 
period).  
 
The daily presence of the DFDS ferries at berth in both ports corresponds to six - seven hours; there-
fore, the PM pollution exposure due to ferry pollution considered in this study is limited compared to 
other sources of pollution in the cities. Most research studies that investigate the association of health 
effects and particle pollution consider sources such as road-traffic and industry on a daily (24-hour 
period) or annual basis. Therefore, research concerning health impact association to port and ship-
related pollution is limited. However, the occurrence of potential health effects in case of ferry pol-
lution exposure on a daily basis considering the six - seven hours per day cannot be excluded. This is 
due to high pollution levels observed considering local PM0.1 and general peak levels of PM in Co-
penhagen, which is reflected by the results of this investigation. It should be noted that the presented 
studies in this section, represent a minor part of the research concerning the association of adverse 
health effects and ambient particle pollution.

8.3 The Ports of Copenhagen and Oslo: An Overall Assessment 
 
According to the findings of this study, shore power installations in ports can reduce air pollution and 
improve local air quality, in relation to ferries in Copenhagen and Oslo. Based on the emission esti-
mations of this study, high reductions of several port-related air pollutants were obtained by the use 
of shore power in Oslo in 2019. These reductions are also expected in Copenhagen due to the similar 
situation when a shore power facility is implemented in the port. In Copenhagen, the commencement 
of a shore power construction is planned in 2020 (CMP, 2019). The demand and potential for shore 
power installations, in general, are increasing over time (PowerCon, 2020). Additionally, environ-
mental and health benefits have been reported by several research studies worldwide (presented in 
Section 4.1 Literature Review of Shore Power in Ports). The findings of this study showed that the 
cost of a shore power facility suitable for the DFDS ferries will be repaid in three years and nine 
months from a socio-economic perspective. However, it should be noted that repayments in terms of 
electricity sales can benefit owners of the shore power facilities as indicated by reports and studies 
(COWI, 2019; Winkel et al., 2015).  
 
Pollution from ferries in ports contributes to a minor fraction of the total air pollution from other 
sources, such as road-traffic and industry, in the two cities. According to Oslo Municipality, port-
related GHG emissions in 2018 accounted for 4% of the total emissions in Oslo, whereas road-traffic 
contributed to 62% of total emissions in the city (Oslo Municipality, 2018). In 2018, the energy and 
transportation sectors represented 38% and 34% of total CO2 emissions in Copenhagen, while mari-
time traffic contributed to 13% of the total traffic emissions (Copenhagen Municipality, 2019). How-
ever, the development of environmental solutions and controls when considering road-traffic or the 
energy sector is increasing over time, and, in particular, for Copenhagen, which aims towards carbon 
neutrality by 2025 (Copenhagen municipality, 2012; COWI, 2019). Therefore, the introduction of 
abatement technologies for the reduction of port-related pollution will benefit the local environment 
around ports as well as urban cities in general. Shore power use will, in addition to benefiting human 
health, contribute to the improvement of climate-related conditions. 
 
Local air quality measurements performed in this study considering both ports were interrupted due 
to the COVID-19 situation. However, measurements collected in Copenhagen showed increased lev-
els of air pollution in the presence of ferries, compared to the observed background levels. Therefore, 
it will be necessary to install shore power in the port of Copenhagen to reduce ferry pollution in ports 
as reflected by the emission reductions in Oslo. Thereby, the local air quality can be improved in the 
residential as well as office areas near ports that are increasing and extending over time.  
 
Overall, Copenhagen and Oslo are cities that are committed to sustainability and sustainable devel-
opment. In this regard, port-related abatement technologies and alternative solutions to ensure air 
quality improvements on a local scale should be considered and implemented. The city of Oslo and 
Oslo Port Authority have set ambitious goals towards the creation of zero-emission and sustainable 
ports, including the goal of shore power use on all visiting cruises by 2025 (Oslo Municipality, 2018; 
Oslo Port Authority, 2020). In Copenhagen, the pollution levels from cruises are expected to be higher 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
60 
due to more port calls compared to Oslo. The high pollution levels from cruises compared to ferries 
indicate the importance of shore power facilities. Therefore, the port of Copenhagen should consider 
possible installations of shore power in cruise terminals as well, which is a considered case in the city 
investigated by studies and reports (COWI, 2019; Ballini and Bozzo, 2015). Shore power installations 
for cruises have not been the focus of this study, and therefore, further research might be needed.

9 Uncertainties and Limitations  
 
A number of uncertainties and limitations have arisen on the basis of emission estimations and on-
site measurements of air pollutants. In this section, the importance and magnitude of these uncertain-
ties and the limitations of this study will be outlined.  
 
 
Uncertainties 
 
The accuracy of port-related emission estimations in this study is dependent on the assumptions made, 
and input data applied. In this study, technical ship information, the number of port calls, maneuver-
ing, arrival, and departure information related to the two ferries were provided by DFDS. For boiler 
use onboard, measured data on fuel consumption was provided. However, the fuel consumed in ports 
considering the auxiliary engines was based on the exact berthing duration and an estimated hourly 
usage provided by DFDS. This might induce uncertainties related to the exact fuel consumed in ports. 
However, an overview of the approximate emission levels could be provided by this approach. 
 
Uncertainties related to the emissions arise as well from the applied EFs and LFs in the estimations. 
Exact data on LFs could not be provided by technical engineers onboard since it is known that these 
factors change and varies during different activities and weather conditions. Therefore, data on LFs 
were gathered from literature. In addition, uncertainties related to EFs can result in uncertainties as 
well, since they were based on several environmental agencies and studies. However, the selection 
of EFs was based on fuel type and ship-related information that are most representative of the two 
DFDS ferries.  
 
Based on ship reports provided by DFDS, the sulfur content in the used marine fuel is found to be 
0.09%, which has been applied in the estimations when possible. However, a standard sulfur content 
of 0.1% has been mainly used, due to more standard values in literature and agencies. This is not 
expected to change the overall conclusions due to minor difference. In addition, it will mainly affect 
SO2 and PM emissions that in fact contribute less to the total emissions estimated in this study. Scrub-
ber technology is used onboard Crown Seaways on main engines to ensure that the sulfur content is 
below 0.1%. During maneuvering operations, it is assumed that scrubber ensures a fuel type with a 
sulfur content of 0.1% with no further reductions considering the main engines. Therefore, the emis-
sions of SO2 and PM can be lower in case of possible further reductions. Moreover, some information 
regarding maneuvering of Pearl Seaways was as well applied for Crown Seaways due to the COVID-
19 situation, which limited contact with technical engineers onboard. Available information related 
to the maneuvering operation of Crown Seaways might affect the results. 
 
The estimated costs related to Oslo are based on externalities from Copenhagen, due to lack of data 
and information related to Oslo. This induces a certain degree of uncertainty since the costs related 
to Oslo, in reality, could be potentially higher or lower. Additionally, external costs were based on 
data from 2016 for limited air pollutants, which can develop and change over time. Therefore, the 
==PAGE:74==
 
 
62 
inclusion of other air pollutants such as PM0.1 and the availability of more recent data can affect the 
estimated costs presented in this study.  
 
Local air quality data were based on measurements conducted on-site in both ports and nearby areas. 
Measurements conducted with PAM caused some challenges, and therefore not all data could be 
extracted from the device resulting in uncertainties related to the representativeness of data.  Meteor-
ological conditions and the COVID-19 situation limited the investigation of the local air quality in 
both ports. In particular, data representing the port of Oslo were not comprehensive compared to 
measurements in Copenhagen. Uncertainties are therefore introduced, as neither the measuring con-
ditions matched in the two ports nor enough data were collected to perform a comparative assessment 
based entirely on local measurements. However, this assessment has been supported by emission 
estimations and external costs presented in this study, limiting uncertainties on the overall assessment. 
 
Limitations  
 
Several challenges during the project period resulted in limitations, which will be briefly presented. 
Due to weather conditions in Oslo and the situation of COVID-19, on-site measurements in both Oslo 
and Copenhagen were limited due to the suspension of the DFDS ferries. Thereby, the conductance 
of a comparative evaluation of the local air quality in the two ports was limited. Furthermore, it was 
not possible to investigate the relationship between pollution levels and seasonality change due to 
measurement interrupt, which limited the scope of this study as well.  
 
The possibility of measuring air pollution levels from offices or recreational areas such as roof terrace 
near the port of Copenhagen were investigated, to provide an overview of human exposure to pollu-
tion near ports. Two companies were contacted regarding this specific purpose. However, access was 
denied by both companies due to limited resources and safety reasons (see Appendix E Personal 
Communication (Mails)).

10 Conclusion and Recommendations  
 
10.1 Conclusion 
 
In this study, the effectiveness of shore power facilities as a mitigation measure of ferry-related air 
pollution in ports was investigated. This has been conducted by a theoretical and a practical investi-
gation covering emission estimations and on-site measurements. The theoretical part of this study 
covered the main port-related air pollutants during 2018 and 2019 of the two operating DFDS ferries 
between Oslo and Copenhagen. The bottom-up approach recommended by EMEP/EEA has been 
applied for the emission estimations. The practical part consisted of local measurements of several 
air pollutants from ferries in both ports by the use of monitoring devices. In conclusion, the findings 
of this study showed that shore power facilities can provide an effective measure to reduce port-
related emissions. Considering the port of Oslo, the connection of one operating ferry to shore power 
in 2019, resulted in emission reductions of 92%, 77%, 91%, 77%, 94%, and 89% annually covering 
the air pollutants NOX, CO2, CO, SO2, PM, and NMVOCs, respectively. In this regard, external costs 
related to the emissions showed a total socio-economic benefit of approximately DKK 2.03 million 
in 2019. This benefit was based on NOX, SO2, and PM2.5 reductions from one operating ferry in Oslo 
during 2018 - 2019. Thus, it is expected that socio-economic benefits would balance the expenses of 
a shore power facility in three years and nine months, considering a total installation cost of DKK 15 
million. The practical investigation of this study showed considerably higher pollution in the presence 
of ferries in Copenhagen in terms of PM0.1 levels, compared to background pollution. Additionally, 
peak concentrations were observed for local PM2.5 and PM10 concentrations. According to research 
studies, no evidence of safe or threshold level of PM exposure has been supported. Therefore, the 
observed concentration levels of PM, in general, might contribute to potential health effects on local 
areas. This indicates the importance of shore power installations, as reflected by the theoretical in-
vestigation as well.  
 
Port-related emissions, in general, correspond to a minor contribution to urban air pollution in Co-
penhagen and Oslo, when considering the share of other pollution sources. However, residential and 
office areas near ports are increasing as population of urban cities is expanding. Therefore, shore 
power facilities, in addition to other alternative abatement solutions, can be a step forward for the 
improvement of local air quality and creation of sustainable ports. These solutions can provide ben-
efits for social, economic, and environmental sectors in urban cities both on short and long-term 
perspectives.

10.2 Recommendations  
 
Based on the findings of this study following recommendations are provided: 
 
• 
Implementation of a shore power facility for the DFDS ferries in the port Copenhagen 
 
According to the results of this study, shore power is found to be a beneficial technology on air pol-
lution reduction in ports. Therefore, it is recommended to implement this technology in ports of urban 
cities, including the port of Copenhagen. It should be noted that a shore power facility is planned to 
be implemented in the port in 2020.  
 
• 
Consider more sustainable solutions for boiler use onboard and during maneuvering opera-
tions 
 
In the port of Oslo, the only source of pollution from the DFDS ferries is the use of fuel-powered 
boilers. Alternative solutions to avoid pollution from these boilers should be considered. This could 
be in terms of electric boilers or connecting to the district heating network of a city. The use of shore 
power in Oslo has shown that emissions from maneuvering operations can contribute to a high share 
of port-related emissions. Therefore, it is recommended to consider and investigate potential alterna-
tive solutions such as batteries for maneuvering operations.  
 
Future Investigations 
 
Further research is needed to improve the findings of this study and to minimize uncertainties. In 
order to conduct a more comprehensive assessment of the local air quality in the two case ports and 
shore power effectiveness, more on-site measurements should be collected over a longer time-scale. 
The main focus of this study has been on shore power facilities. Therefore, further research on other 
abatement technologies in terms of feasibility and effectiveness should be conducted, to provide a 
comprehensive overview of mitigation solutions on air pollution in ports.
