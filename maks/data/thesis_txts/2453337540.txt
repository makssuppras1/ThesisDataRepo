Abstract
MAN (PrimeServ) is in the business of selling engine spareparts to shipowners. The product portofolio
of the parts MAN provides is rather large and as of July 2019 there are around 6500 standard parts
that MAN sells to customers. To accommodate such a complex product portofolio MAN’s factory has
a Flexible Manufacturing System (FMS) with a Job-shop conﬁguration. This means that workcenters
that performs unique operations are combined with other workcenters in order to produce parts, while
all workcenters are operated individually.
MAN has over a period of time had problems with their factory being overloaded, resulting in delayed
customer orders. This is partly because of how expected load is distributed over a period of time and
partly because of non anticipated load being introduced to the factory. The decision to anticipate how
many SKUs the factory can provide over any period of time is called capacity allocation.
”Capacity” should be understood as how many hours a workcenter is available to produce parts and
”Load” is the time consumed from this capacity to manufacture parts. Utilization is the diﬀerence
between the two. the workcenter capacity should not be confused with the capacity referred to in the
capacity allocation. The workcenter capacity is understood in hours while the capacity from the capac-
ity allocation is in SKUs
The Capacity allocation output should be provided in the form of a product mix that utilizes as much
workcenter capacity as possible while fulﬁlling the forecasted demand. This makes the capacity alloca-
tion decision making much more complex than it already is. Fortunately, a mathematical model can
be formulated that can provide a product mix that considers all these things and can even consider
decisions that can increase workcenter capacities.
This model can also be tweaked to easily accommodate alternative decisions and can be used to get
analytical insights of the factory. This model will be the focus of this paper.
To make sure this model is relevant and its results are easy to understand a full integrated system
has been implemented that MAN’s planners can use to support their decision making. This tool pulls
up-to-date data from MAN’s internal ERP system and through some programming the mathematical
model is executed and the results displayed in a visual dashboard that is available in MAN’s internal
report server.

Introduction
In a market with high demand of products with high product variety and complexity it is necessary to
have an eﬀective planning of operations, planning of resources and foresight to meet demand on time.
Additionally, when a plant is experiencing a heavy overload it should be carefully considered which
products should be produced in house and which products should be outsourced to external suppliers/
subcontracted in order to minimize cost and fullﬁlling demand. The Master planning function has the
responsibility for these types of decisions.
1.1
The Problem
The Master production schedule gives an overview of how much can be produced every week and is a well
known approach to the high level production planning. In MAN there are weekly meetings between
operations planning and masterplanning to balance demand and supply coming from the factory of
MAN. The load for the monthly timeslots are much higher than the capacity for the current week
as shown in ﬁgure 1. The reason is that the capacity allocated to diﬀerent product groups does not
match the actual capacity of the full factory. This eventually leads to orders being delayed and eﬀecting
customer satisfaction and violated deals made with customers.
The fact that the factory is being
perpetually overloaded means that there are some products that has to be subcontracted/ outsourced.
As of current there is no process in place to determine which product groups and products should be
taken out of the factory to realize the promised load and have it be ﬁnished on time. The lead time for
receiving goods from subcontractors is fairly long . An issue with subcontracting is that it often leads
to much longer delivery times to customers since the parts ﬁrst have to be manufactured and sent to
MAN and then out to the customer unless the part is already on stock. It can take up to 4 months for
parts to arrive at MAN from subcontractors.
The planning process should allow for purchase orders to be issued in a timely manner so that they are
available when needed.

1.2
Key Performance Indicators
The Key Perfomance Indicator for the Master Planning department in MAN is the so-called On-Time
Delivery - Ready-on-Shelf (OTD-ROS). This KPI is the percentage of orders that are ready on time to
be shipped out in order for the customer to get the parts on the promised date. the shelf that is being
reﬀered to in ”Ready-on-Shelf” are MAN’s shelves in their warehouses from where the parts are usually
being shipped out.
The issuing of subcontract orders has to be done in a timely manner such that the it is reﬂected on
OTD-ROS. This means that a longer time horizon has to be considered. The Aggregate Production
Plan is the mid to long term planning. The decision to make-or-buy is currently not part of the aggre-
gate production planning hence making the buying decision untimely as a subcontract order is triggered
when it is already too late. By adding the make-or-buy decision to the APP the timeliness factor comes
into play. When knowing which parts are to be procured from subcontractors the orders can be issued
early to intercept large loads.
There is however a problem with with ﬁguring out which exact parts to be subcontracted since the APP
considers products from the lens of aggregated product groups.
This project will introduce a framework to do the Aggregate Production Planning and will also provide
a framework to support the Purchasing of individual parts.
1.3
Responsiblity of Masterplanning
The Master planning function is responsible for the balancing of supply and demand. Masterplanners
are often in contact with many stakeholders from the supply end and communicates to internal demand
stakeholders. In other words: Master Planning is the link between the internal and external supply to
the internal demand. This means that the Masterplanning at all times needs to have an overview of
what the plant is able to supply and what has to be supplied from external suppliers.
Figure 4: Flowchart depicting the Master planning’s function in terms of supply and demand
The Master planning function often times has diﬃculty in seeing the impact of decisions that are made
in terms of increasing or decreasing capacity for one planning group (in pieces) , naturally to increase
capacity for one group means to decrease for another or adding more slack to another workstation.
To meet demand some decisions has to be made from master plannings side. These decisions are often
linked to whether some planning groups should be outsourced or whether there should be allocated
capacity for manufacturing parts inhouse which means to issue production orders. From a master plan-
ning point of view, issuing a production order either means that there is being produced to stock or

Litteratur Review & Method
The general structure of the planning processes are being viewed through the traditional framework
of production planning. (Halevi, 2014) [1] in his book chapter 5 shows the structure and hierarchy of
planning in terms operational, tactical and Strategic paradigms. The planning stages that are being
highlighted for production planning are:
• Master Production Schedule
• Material Requirements Planning
• Capacity planning
• Shop Floor Control
• Inventory Management and Control
For this project the Master Production Scheduling and Capacity Planning stages will be dealt with in
particular as they are the most outstanding planning activities in terms of diﬃculty in MAN.
Halevi tackles the so-called Production Planning Dilemma which is the rationalization of the pro-
duction planning being a complex task because of the production environment being dynamic and there-
for vulnerable to disruptions. He claims that this complexity is a results of the traditional approach
and not a built-in necessity. The main argument for his claim is that the traditional approach invites
disruptions because of the stiﬀness of systems in which decisions are being made untimely. Through a
diﬀerent approach, one that will introduce ﬂexibility into the planning process, the disruptions can be
dealt with by elimination. In MAN the same problems exist because of sudden loads being introduced
and disrupting the long term planning decisions primarily in terms of output rates of parts. By having
a long term planning system that adapts to changes in load corrective actions can be taken as a result
of the planning system and not an improvised decision making
Both the traditional method and the ﬂexible method performs the production planning tasks through
the afore mentioned ﬁve stages were both employs diﬀerent technologies. Production planning should
ensure the products being delivered on time in the most economical way. For the ﬂexible approach a
range of optimization problems with a range of criteria of optimization are proposed to planning alter-
natives or to support decision making under disruption in the production. Halevi suggest a philosophy
he refers to as the Theory of Constraints that aims to understand processes through the mindset of
limiting factors that hinders a system of from achieving higher performance. This frame of mind is
most commonly found within Operations Research practitioners and will also be used in this project.
A thorough understanding precedes the modelling of the problem to ensure all relevant constraints are
part of the model.

Problem Description
The Problem dealt with in this project is to provide MAN with a framework for their Aggregate Pro-
duction Planning . The APP Gives a product mix that utilizes the most machine/man hours in the
production facility while also giving the products that should be manufactured by subcontractors at a
cost in order to realize supply. The candidates for subcontracted parts has to be disaggregated from
the APP to speciﬁc parts before any purchase orders can be placed. This process will also be dealt with
in this project.
Additionally there should be an option to consider the non-standard load that may be introduced to
the plant.
3.1
Business Problem Description
The aim of this project from a bussiness point of view is to ensure delivery to customers without delay.
Through recent years an increasing focus on service has been on the rise. One particular metric for good
service is on-time delivery (OTD).In order to have a rigid supplychain the planning process should aim
to fulﬁll demand AND deliver orders on-time to satisfy customer needs.
When shifting the paradigm to an operations planning point of view the main measure for performance
is the utilization of the factory which means A high utilization is desired. However, Throughout 2018
the demand has been increasingly growing to the point where the factory is heavily overloaded which
in return results in delays, meaning bad OTD because of lack of capacity while some workcenters are
without work. This is indicative of suboptimal highlevel production planning
Because of the long lead times between placing a purchase order and having goods in hand some pre-
emptive actions has to be taken to place the purchase orders early such that products are available
when they are needed and congestion in the factory is averted.
The aim of this project is to revise the aggregate planning process to account for all above mentioned
problems which in short are: ensuring on time delivery, maximizing factory utilization and placing pur-
chase orders preemptively to avoid delays. The planning process should in short decide what the product
mix should look like and which relief purchases should be placed preemptively to avoid congestion and
ensuring realization of Supply.

nonstandard load at all and sometimes there is a lot.

warehouse which makes it diﬃcult to set up a standard tool that gives the optimal solution while
accounting for the nonstandard load. The way it will be implemented in the model will be through

7.5
Demand based on Forecast
The demand for the aggregate planning is based on forecast. this forecast is made on the product group
level (see appendix A.1). The forecast is provided as is, and is a crucial part of the aggregate planning
model. in the aggregated model the demand in constraint 25 is based on this forecast. The way it is
set up is so that the produced and purchased parts for a product group must be greater than or equal
to the demand for the product for a halfyear period were the demand is based on forecast. This means
that the forecast has to be reliable in order for the optimal solution from the aggregate model to be
reliable.
The Forecast comes from a dedicated team, namely demand planning who are in charge of providing
the forecast with tight communication with all stakeholder in the organization that requires internal
supply.
These stakeholders are namely
1. Spareparts - volatile demand over the course of the year
2. Components Sales - Steady demand with occasional bulk orders
3. Retroﬁt & Upgrade - Bulky demand only
Close communication with these stakeholders is what eventually ends up as forecast that is used in the
aggregate model.

Removing time horizon - T set from the MPS model
The MPS model puts loads into weekly time bucket based on avaialble capacity to minimize purchasing
cost and maximize utilization. There is however a problem with how the Purchasing is suggested. The

of this project is. The aim of this project is to come up with a frame work that gives an overview of

placed to counter long lead times. purchase orders are also usually something that is negotiable which
sourcing is in charge of. By providing an aggregated view of what is needed in the following six months
Sourcing has much more negotiation power which is better than placing small orders. Another beneﬁt

within seconds. This is optimal since this is only the base model and when adding more decision variable

From the Aggregate planning model the output that is given is twofold:
1. decisions to regulate capacities
2. decisions to make-or-buy
The decision to regulate capacities are in of them selves tangible in the sense that actions can be taken
directly from the output of the aggregate model.
for the make-or-buy decisions there are however
something that are not so tangible speciﬁcally for the purchasing part. The ”Make” part of the make-
or-buy decisions are eﬀectively giving the product mix of the factory for the next halfyear. This is then
disaggregated into what is known as the Master Production Schedule (MPS). The Master Production
Schedule is much closer to the operational planning in that it shows the capacity in pieces rather than
hours.
This transformation of hours into pieces brdiges the gap between Operations planning and
Master Planning and eﬀectively the rest of the organization. The product mix is then distributed into
weekly time slots where each slot represents what the factory can deliver each week.
Figure 34: Example of a Product mix disaggregated into a Master Production Schedule for spindle
guides. A total of 20.000 SG are distributed into weekly timeslots
In an operational sense the way this works is that whenever an order comes in a production order is
placed and as a result the slots are being ﬁlled. The limit of how much can be produced every week is
reﬂected on the capacity allocated for a week. This is presented as the blue bar in ﬁgure 34) and the
load of course is the orange bar.
The loads that are being applied here are of either the type make-to-order or make-to-stock. The make-
to-stock parts are the ones that are being frequent sellers and are being kept in stock. Whenever a
part that is being made to stock reaches a so-called re-order point a production order is triggered to
replenish the stock. The Make-to-stock parts has some ﬂexibility in terms of when the order can be
issued and which time slot the load should be applied to. This is due to having safety stock and/or
being able to trigger replenish order earlier than what the re-order point allows for. There is no real
target for how much the stock should be at and overstocking is also allowed if the parts has ﬁxed order
lot-sizes. The load should be ﬁlled out such that there is balance between load and capacity.
The operational aspect of this balancing between load and capacity comes in to play in a weekly handover
meeting between master planning and operations planning where a range of factors are discussed. issues

Further work and implications
The APP planning is considered the rough capacity planning. To get more accurate planning a disag-
gregation of the APP has to be made. This was the case for purhchasing but a more relevant planning
process is the manufacturing. For manufacturing the level of complexity needs layer of disaggregations
to make it manageble. The planning levels that has not been addressed in this project in terms of
planning is Master Production scheduling an Operations scheduling. The Master production scheduling
would consider available capacity in weekly timeslots and allocate parts to these slots such that capacity
is represented in pieces and not hours. This disaggregation is similiar to that of the purchasing in the
sense more detailed planning decision are made.
The shop ﬂoor-level production control is the lowest level of production planning. This is also referred
to as the operations scheduling. The output of this planning activity are operations to be carried out
in individual workcenters. this planning activity is an disaggregation of the out put from the MPS as
shown in ﬁgure 56

not covered in this project
Scheduling problems is a well researched topic in operations research because of the generic model for
the problem being NP-hard as described by (Ehab, Fahd ;2010) [17]. The same paper gives a range
of metahueristic based frameworks to solve job shop scheduling problems within in a reasonable time
frame and even having results that are close 0.5% of the optimal solution for their case studies.
Currently a tabu search algorithm is being used to make the runsheets (køresedler) for individual
workcenters which is performing quite well and an increase of throughput was experienced when the

19
Conclusion
A range of results has been presented in this project, the results will be summarized in this section.
An optimization model was formulated to do the APP where primarily capacity and demand was con-
sidered. The main emphasis of the APP model is to provide a product mix that utilizes the capacity
of the factory in the most optimal way while fulﬁlling demand. The parts that are not part of the
product mix are instead added to the purchase mix which are the parts that will be supplied from sub-
contractors. Before the purchase mix can be realized it has to be disaggregated from product groups to
speciﬁc parts such that purchase orders can be issued for the parts. This disaggregation is done through
another optimization model that suggest exact parts to be subcontracted. This way a full process has
been established for procurement of external supply while a process for internal supply is already in
place and the product mix serves as an input to the these planning activites
A tool has also been provided to MAN that can run the models and allow manual input for nonstandard
load. This tool is integrated into MANs internal report server that presents the results in a visual format
that makes the results easy to understand and makes it easier to see eﬀects of nonstandard loads and
other capacity policies. A 10% capacity buﬀer policy was implemented for the APP for the 1st halfyear
of 2020 which had a product mix that is going to be implemented for this period.
The parts that was taken out of the factory for this period was primarily Spindle Guides and MultiWay
Valves, these parts was also the parts that were anticipated to be taken out of the factory by operations
planning.
Besides determining product mix and purchase mix the APP also supports workforce level decisions
which is done by making an extension to the APP model. The extension allows for hiring as well as
moving multiple shifts between workcenters. By doing so not only is an optimal workforce level achieved
but also bottlenecks related to machine capacity can be identiﬁed.
Identifying bottlenecks on an aggregate level also makes for a good way to communicate capacity issues
to higher level management that can promptly take corrective actions. Another analysis to dwell deeper
into bottlenecks and what causes them has also been provided. This analysis provides an overview of
what the product mix would look like with varying degree of machine and man capacity available.
A third analysis is provided to see which exact purchases that can be placed to relief loads that are
not the parts being suggested from the APP. This in turn gives insight to which parts that are worth
purchasing relative to consumption of capacity.
From the point of view of MAN the real results would be to see if this planning system had any eﬀects
on their key performance indicator: On-Time-Delivery Ready-On-Shelf (OTD-ROS). The change in
OTD-ROS can unfortunately only be seen at the end of the 1st halfyear of 2020 which would be july
2020 which is after the hand in deadline for this project. Another measure for validating the results
would be to compare actual factory throughput with the capacity allocation. This unfortunatly can
also only be done at the end of the period.
All in all, a framework has been provided to do capacity allocation that accounts for factory capacity
rather than only considering load. The APP optimization model considers a six month aggregated
period of demand and capacity and provides the make-buy decision that can be translated to product
mix and parts for subcontracting while also supporting workforce and operations related decisions.
Since there is no formal process for subcontracting, another model was provided to support the decision
making of parts the should be purchased in the beginning of the period to have the parts on stock when
needed and prevent stockout.
