ABSTRACT
This project investigates the development of novel concept of dual-utility furniture. The speciﬁc case is a changing table
which is thought to have a lifetime which extends beyond a traditional changing table by serving an additional function in
the home after the child grows beyond needing it. The concept further seeks to satisfy a speciﬁc niche, by focusing on
families who live in small homes. To show the potential of the product co-creation methods are used to ﬁnd the right focus
of the solution. Furthermore both marketability and sustainability is assessed and, lastly, the project in its entirety in done in
collaboration with a Danish furniture company who provides feedback on the production-aspects of the project. Ultimately
a working prototype is developed and the collaborating company is likely to include the product in its future product-line.

INTRODUCTION
This report describes the process undertaken in order to understand important issues when being a family with small chil-
dren, who in addition lives small, and develop a useful and novel concept for the families in question. The report is divided
into ﬁve chapters, describing the process:
1. Introduction: Containing a reading guide, aim of the project, and learning goals.
2. Data collection and analysis: By researching existing solutions, taking a theoretical approach towards child develop-
ment, and interviewing families, an analysis is carried out.
3. Exploration of the solution space: A broad idea generation is carried out based on the analysis. After an analysis of
chosen ideas, two ideas are chosen for further development. At last one idea is chosen for the ﬁnal solution.
4. Conceptualizing: Based on a market analysis and an analysis of use of similar products, four concepts are developed.
5. Detailing: Describes the process of detailing the concept in order to reach the ﬁnal solution, as well as the ﬁnal
solution.
6. Recapitulation: The process and ﬁndings are evaluated.
The project is carried out during the fall of 2015 - winter 2016, with Per Boelskifte from the department of Mechanics at DTU
as supervisor. The project is carried out in collaboration with Griffenshop, a small design company who has a small product
base and develops customized solutions for private users.
The project allocates to 30 ECTS points, equivalent to a workload of 810 hours.
References for literature is shown in parentheses (Author year) and references for appendixes are shown in parentheses

Aim
The aim for this project is to create a novel and useful piece of furniture for families with small children, who also live small.
The concept must help families consume less, making them think about how furniture is used.
2.2.1
Reason
In Denmark the house prices have risen over the last couple of years, making it hard to ﬁnd a suitable housing for young
people who wants to live in the city, unless we begin to ﬁgure out how to live small. The housing problems are not the only
reason for living small, as we have a smaller impact on the environment the less we use. During the last decade we have
become increasingly aware of our consumption of resources and the impact it has on our planet. The understanding of our

The aim of this chapter is to provide you with an overall understanding of the different stages a child goes through from

of the families interviewed had infants (and should be included), and that the approach towards the solution space should
be as broad as possible.
The chapter is divided into two parts: motor skills, and cognitive skills, as these are fundamentally different areas of devel-
opment.

Physical changes in the child is only taken in to consideration in terms of height, weight and reach of the child as changes
in looks, such as maturing of the face is not considered important for the development of furniture.
The information sought out in this chapter is relevant as one goal of the project is to develop a concept for the user and it
is still unknown who the ﬁnal concept in this project is for, a coherent understanding of the family members are sought out.
The knowledge gained from the investigation will be used in the development of the concepts later on.
3.1.1
Children’s motor skills
Motor skills can be divided into two subcategories: Rough motor skills and ﬁne motor skills, which develops in different
pastes and are accommodated in different ways. The development of rough motor skills happens both due to stimulation,
but also because different muscles and areas of the brain which controls our movements matures. The rough motor skills
include actions such as crawling, grabbing, and standing. Fine motor skills is basically coordination of small muscle move-
ments. Examples of ﬁne motor skills include eating with a spoon (or fork), drawing, or building with bricks. Fine motor skill
actions often involve coordination between hands and eyes (“Deﬁnition Fine Motor Skill” 2015).
When babies are born, they are capable of actions necessary for keeping them alive, such as sucking (for food) and breathing.
A newborn can even grab your ﬁnger with their ﬁst, holding it tight. As time goes by a baby is capable of more and more
things, for example holding their own head.
Because a baby has to develop many different motor skills, there is no time to describe them in depth, instead a table is
made to show the development. The steps described in the following are cross checked in different books, online and in
magazines in order to make sure, that these motor skills are really aquired at the aged staged. The table shows the key
milestones in the motor skill development (“Website on Child Development” 2016),(Daniel F. Cuffaro 2013), (Cooper 2006):

3.1.2
The cognitive development
When investigating the cognitive development of children, it is clear that some controversy on the ﬁeld exists. The theories on
the subject are diverse, and reflects how different philosophies as a basis for observation manifest itself in the output. Some
believe children understand if not words, then intend from birth, whereas other believes the child understands nothing, or at
least very little from birth (Cooper 2006), (Dyhrberg 2011). There is, however agreement on the fact that children perceive

3.1.2.1
Different stages
About 100 years ago, drawings made by children were for the ﬁrst time acknowledged as some-
thing unique; today drawings made by children have become a way of evaluating their level of perception as well as their
motor skills. According to the theory stated by Hanne Bistrup (Bistrup 2014) drawings help children create an understanding
of them self as an individual because when you draw, you create your own mark on the world.
Today drawings made by children can help determining how far along in their cognitive development they are. While they
might have difﬁculties addressing this with words, a drawing reveals different stages related both to ﬁne motor skills and the
cognitive development, as well as issues and trauma the child might have experienced. According to Piaget [Bistrup2014],
A child goes from birth until the age of 12 years through four stages, this is both valid in drawings, as well as other aspects.
1. Sensor motoric period (0-2 years): Knowledge of the world is limited to sensory perceptions and motor activities.
Behavior is limited to simple motor responses caused by sensory stimuli. Uses skills and abilities they were born
with to learn about their surroundings.
2. Preoperational period (2-7 years) At this stage a child is ego-centred The child begins to develop logical reasoning
(however, it is stil not fully developed)
3. Concrete operations period (7-12 years) Logical thinking improves, but children still have difﬁculties grasping hypo-
thetical or abstract concept
4. Formal operations period (12 - adulthood) The logical thinking improves even more and the child can comprehend
abstract concepts
A more detailed list of development in children can be found in appendix cognitive development, [B].
3.1.2.2
Play
The research on cognitive development related to social skills focuses on how child play develops. In the
Western world we normally have an understanding of children’s play as a way of developing different skills - a way of learning
and as with everything else, the way we look at it shapes the information we extract and the results we get. However, Maria
Øksnes, who is a Swedish PhD in child play, claims that there are many different theories on why children play, and that a
lot of play is absolutely without reason, but purely for entertainment. It can be compared to how adults enjoy carnivals and
entertainment for the sake of entertainment (Øksnes 2012). She also points that research on children’s so called “role play”,
however research on children’s jumping games only exists in a very small amount (Øksnes 2012), which provides us with
little knowledge on how these games are carried out an at what age. However, she reveals that understanding child play
only as a means to develop different skills, may be too narrow-minded, and thus lead to a wrongful way of handling children
as well as a lack of understanding on their part.
Normally children play by them self until they are about two years old. However, children younger than is are capable of
playing with others in games such as playing with a ball, running from point to point or slide on a slide, especially if the play
is organised by an adult.

The theory of living small
Different cultures have different views on what a home is and what it must contain, in Denmark we consider our home
extremely important as it is both a status symbol and it is where we spend most of our free time (“DR Danskernes Bolig”
2015). In order to understand the underlying dependency paths of how Danes use their homes, a study of the use of the
home and the furniture was carried out as a base for this project (Lütken 2015). An understanding of the development paths
is important in order for me to be able to make a product which has a possibility of being domesticated.
The way we view our homes creates certain demands as we expect to be able use our homes for most of the activities
outside of work. The homes is no longer only a base for the family, a place to be together and relax, it is also a place for us
to work, to workout - the home has to embrace - and function for everything. Families living in small homes do not have the
opportunity to use their homes this way, which might create a clash between expectations and reality.
The theory of living small takes offset in the idea that we all need less stuff than we actually keep in our homes. In order
to live small, you often have to downsize. Downsizing refers to the act of sorting your stuff and getting rid of items you no
longer need (“Bemorewithless” 2016).
3.2.1
Trends
As an opponent to the materialism, a movement with focus on core values and few possessions has risen. The trend goes
by many names and is present in many different forms, but the name covering the movement best, is Simple Living.
Simple living describes the idea, that as your life gets simpler you will feel better and more content with yourself and your
life. The philosophy is, that it becomes easier to keep the overview of your life when you have less stuff, this is meant both
as physical and mental. To say it short: your life is simpler, resulting in you feeling better. Simple living is also a way to
eliminate - or at least tone down the materialism that is such a big part of our society, however it is not necessarily correlated
to living small. Simple living is a way of creating a healthy balance between your physical surroundings and your mental
well-being (“Website on Small Living” 2014).
The process of getting rid of unnecessary things (both physical and psychological) is not a one-time action, as you have
to change your mindset from a more materialistic view to this simpler view. The more accustomed you get to living with a
smaller amount of stuff, the more you will be able to throw out.

can defend it. This group is experienced as a relatively small group based on the interviews [D], [E], [H], [I], which also ﬁts
well into the Danish traditions.
Families who are in transition poses a large sample on the market and they are an interesting group because many young
people are in a similar situation for many years, for example while studying. The “here and now mindset”, only focusing on
short term solutions combined with expectations of low prices makes it hard to come up with sustainable, quality driven
solutions, which is why the idea generation does not show a very large number of solutions for this group.
The group with families experiencing change refers to families where the parents are divorced or where one of the parents
are divorced. The fact that their home has to meet different needs from week to week (or any other amount of time), and
families who belong in this group can belong in either of the other two, makes it hard to ﬁnd the exact needs and demands
deﬁning this group.

and the solutions seem rather similar. In order to get a greater diversity in the ideas, the three categories are temporarily
dropped and instead the approach to idea generation takes the form of seeking inspiration on the internet, in shops, nature
and other places in order to discover as many ideas as possible [X].
The initiating phase of the idea generation is carried out over short intervals of time, as it is hard to come up with new ideas
for longer periods of time. The phase is carried out in a iterative fashion, as the process of coming up with ideas, ﬁnding

Because the idea generation is only carried out over shorter periods of time, the work done in this phase sporadic rather
than systematized, and time spend on generating ideas is rather hard to set because new ideas constantly are sought out.
As the number of ideas becomes higher and the solution space is slowly investigated, the approach to the work becomes

The market analysis for the two different functions (desk and changing table) are carried out individually in order to simplify

6.1.1
Porter’s ﬁve forces
The analysis of Porter’s ﬁve forces takes offset in the current status that Griffenshop has on the market. To elaborate;
this means a small design company that is somewhat known on the market for tables and storage solutions, as well as
customized solutions for children’s rooms, however they are still small and cannot compare to chains.
In the area of furniture for nurturing purposes, they are completely unknown. This is an obstacle for Griffenshop as products
for children are often made by well established companies, who provides security for the user. As opposed to the changing
table market, the level of trust needed to be established is a lot lower when discussing desks, which is an advantage. The
variety on the market when discussing the tables makes it harder to stand out in almost any aspects, expect size and
flexibility (interior and placement), this is where the main opportunities for reaching the blue ocean lies.
6.1.1.1

The market for changing tables is already big, and even though the similarity in designs of the
tables are many, the size of the brands on the market makes it difﬁcult to enter. For a small, unknown design company,
the market is difﬁcult to enter, making the threat of entry high. A way to lower the barriers for entering the market is to
make the table in collaboration with a larger, well-known design company who can help marketing the table. The design
of the table makes it possible to sell it as a environmentally friendly, novel idea on the market and this can pave the way
for publicity through different medias, such as “Bo Bedre” and through articles establish the changing table as an exclusive
design object.
If we take a look at the market for wall attached desk, it seems easier for the concept to be domesticated, as the barriers for
reaching this market is not considered as high as the barriers for a changing table. However, the market is small and directs
itself at a very speciﬁc group of users. A desk is normally something that does not require installation and it is easy to move
it if necessary. This table requires installation and is not easily moved. However, if you live in a small space, the advantages
that comes with the function closing your table, is considered to outweigh the disadvantages of the installation process.
In order to evaluate the concept and the threat of entry, all attributes must be considered in a holistic perspective. As the

Shelves
For the storage solution, shelves are chosen, as they provide excellent storage opportunities and makes it easy to substitute
them with other storage solutions, such as boxes, later on. Drawers are quickly ruled out as a useful storage option, because
it will be hard to reach items placed inside them based on the height of the table. Boxes inside the cabinet are considered,
however, they do not allow for the same flexibility in use as shelves. For these reasons, shelves are chosen as the interior.
The table must accommodate two different storage needs: one for diapers and other materials for changing a baby, and one
for storing ofﬁce supplies. In order to meet those needs, the interior should somehow be flexible while keeping the number
of different parts down.
Initially measurements of the table (the depth) is based on diaper size, since this is the main function of the storage opportu-
nity. Diapers are also the basis for the number of shelves needed in the table. As a diaper is 22-24 cm in height, the distance
between (at least some of) the shelves should be 25 cm. This allows room for two to three shelves above each other inside
the cabinet (this is also the number of shelves in existing changing tables).
Figure 47: Measurement of diapers
For the majority of the idea generation for shelves, it considered necessary to have a edge holding onto whatever is stored
because the shelves are relatively narrow. In existing cabinets, the shelves are often like small trays similar to those you ﬁnd
in a refrigerator door, and while they keep stuff from falling down, the downside is the high edge sometimes “hides” stuff
that is needed, such as small containers of ointment. Based on this knowledge, the edge on the shelves is a stick placed a
few centimeters above the shelves [BE].
Initially the idea is to create a stand for items such as computers, but the resources that will go into designing, shaping, as
well of the extended use of materials has let the design down another road. Instead of adding a complete stand, it is decided
that larger objects does not require further support than the support provided by the edge. Solutions similar to this already

the design, such having a strong box with the edges on the inside and then covering this up with another box on the outside

8.4.2
Safety edges
Detachment of the safety edges when the table is no longer in use as a changing table is considered necessary both due to
the size of the tabletop, but also to alter the look of the table.
The development of the safety edges is extended in two main directions: one being reflections on how to attach the safety
edges to the hinge and these are hold in the right direction. The other main direction is, to create a hard mat with ﬁxed safety
edges, allowing a simpler attachment [BH]. The latter is developed based on questions such as: Can you clean the table
well enough after use to use it as something else, and even more important than the facts, how is the hygiene perceived, do
people want to use it as something else after using it as a changing table for years?
8.4.2.1
Attachment on the hinges
Let us begin at describing the safety edges which attach directly onto the tabletop.
These do not need to come of easily since you are unlikely to use the table as both a desk and a changing table at the same
time in this scenario. each edge is attached to the hinge by inserting two connecting bolts (which are attached to the edge)
into two holes in the hinge and afterwards tightening an extender around the bolt. The safety edge has to be held into place
somewhere else (preferably on the top of the tabletop) in order to make sure it is tight and controlled. One solution is to add
a list to the safety edge, grabbing around the edge of the tabletop, this can be both on the top and along the side. Another
solution is to drill a small hole the edge can ﬁt into or a small edge grabbing onto the saftety edge. The trick is to make a
solution without making holes in the tabletop or any other change to this that has the potential of “ruining” the tabletop for
further use.

Calculations
Calculations on critical points of the table are carried out to ensure it is strong enough for needed purposes. The calculations
made are based on an extreme case, where a force of 500 N presses down on the edge of the tabletop. This highly unlikely
to happen, however it is considered that if the table is strong enough to carry this kind of force, the table is strong enough
when used as a changing table. The basis for the calculations also allows me to simplify the problem, as the force from a
baby will be distributed over the tabletop and not be present in only one point.
Wood is a complex material to do calculations on, and is in this case considered as a polymer with long ﬁbers, as it is a
good substitution. The calculations are only carried out for ash since the prototype will be created in it. Because plywood
is stronger than ash, calculations for this is considered unnecessary. Oak is a little weaker, and based on the calculation for
ash, it is estimated that the same construction is strong enough for oak.

table results in a great force on the hinge, and as a result the dimensions in the bottom of the table is changed. The rotating
part of the hinge should be as far away from the backstop on the table as possible. The new dimensions of the hinge are
found by drawing the bottom part of the table in scale 1:1 and trying different options while simultaneously making new
calculations on the table and can be found in appendix Calculations [8.6].

8.7.1
Mechanical aspects
The table is attached to the wall through the backing of the table and a crossbar placed behind the backing. It is placed
along the top of the cabinet, between the sides in order to minimize the moment. The cross bar also helps to distribute
the pressure from attachment of the screws. Three screws reaching 6 mm into the wall is enough to hold the table. As
different walls requires different types of plugs, it can be necessary to have a selection of different plugs (“Article on Plugs
and Screws” 2015).
The metal part of the hinge that turns, being the metal components, are custom made because I have been unable to ﬁnd an
existing solution ready for purchase. It consists of three components. The ﬁrst one is screwed into the side of the cabinet,
the second one inserted into the hole in the plywood hinge from the side turning against the side of the cabinet. Finally, the
components are aligned along a common axis and the “pin” is guided though the hole in the plywood hinge and inserted
into the ﬁrst part. It functions by a click-mechanism. The pin is now screwed together with the component placed in the
hinge, securing that it will not move.

Wood is a material that changes over time, it extends in the spring and in winter it will withdraw (“Website on Wood” 2015).
Because the tabletop is made in wood and is placed within the frame, the changes in width over time must be taken into
consideration resulting in a solution with a couple of millimeters from the edge of the tabletop to the sides. Wood elongates
most across the ﬁber direction, making it most likely to be in this direction problems can occur. The hinges are made long
enough to allow room for this expansion.
The ﬁbers laying parallel to the outer edge on the top, bottom, and sides in the cabinet, and are not expected to provide any
problems as they will elongate in in the same directions.

8.9
Life Cycle Calculations
For the life Cycle calculations, the program CES (Cambridge Engineering Selection) is used. The calculations are based on
the LCC analysis made earlier in the project. Every part and the materials used for this part is entered into a table describing
how they are are processed. The data is then used to calculate impact on different areas, such as cost, CO2 footprint, and
End of Life (EoL) potential. The calculations are made for a table with ash as the material for the exterior and the interior of
plywood, as described above.
From calculations it is clear that the biggest impact from the table is the use of materials, as the table does not consume
any energy during use. It is interesting though, that the potential energy gain from combustion is small, almost insigniﬁcant
compared to the CO2 emission created in this process, making this one of the less proﬁtable ways of the discarding the
table. The biggest potential for changing the size of the impact is by reusing the materials as this provides the biggest
EoL potential, which is not surprising [BP]. Based on the results gained in the analysis, the users are urged to either reuse,
re-manufacture or down-cycle the materials.
The results are made upon highly simpliﬁed numbers and scenarios, and the conclusion driven by these estimates can
therefore be questioned. It is, for example, unlikely that a 100 % of the materials will be reused when the table is discarded.

RECAPITULATION
The following contains an evaluation of the process and suggestions for further work is described
9.1
Further Work
This chapter is meant to provide you with an overview of the further work that can or have to follow in order to make the
design successful on the market. Because the prototype is not yet ﬁnished, the list of potential alterations and possibilities
for future work, is long, some of changes needed for the table might only show while building it. A functioning prototype in
scale 1:1 of the table is carried out as an extension of the hand in of this report.
9.1.1
Further development of the concept
• As the design ﬁts well into many different rooms, a series of other sizes of cabinets in the same style can be made in
order to strengthen the dependency path relating it to the living room. Additional cabinets should ﬁt into the platform
developed for the table in order to keep cost down.
• The idea of a garbage bin was discarded due to lack of space in the cabinet, but as some people have difﬁculties
ﬁnding a ﬁtting garbage bin, it is possible to develop one ﬁtting the style of the current changing table.
• From the beginning, many ideas on accessories, such as a mirror in the front of the table was in play, and the potential
for those should be investigated further.
• Further development of the interior with focus on colors can be carried out in order to provide a product with a holistic
color scheme. The interior is designed in order to allow new solutions for storage to be created, such solutions could
be boxes, pencil holders, or other items ﬁtted special needs.
• Since the design can be used both for tasks you normally do while seated as well as tasks you do standing, it is likely
to be proﬁtable to develop the desk in a height-adjustable variant to target a market for home ofﬁces.
9.1.2
Tests and calculations
• In order to follow the standard for changing tables and get a CE certiﬁcation, the table has to pass several tests. This
is an important aspect of the marketing, as it will have stronger foothold on the market if it is certiﬁed. If the table is
not strong enough, or other issues arises when testing it, alterations have to be made in order to follow the standard.
• Tests and calculations of the safety edges should also be carried out to make sure they are stiff enough and to make
sure that fastening them only by pressure from the sides and the small room is enough to hold them.
• Test of the durability of the table should also be carried out in order to verify the expected lifetime is realistic. The
main concern is the hinge as this will experience wear when used.
• Further investigation of the tolerances needs to be carried out in order for the table to ﬁt into a mass production.
• A more in-depth analysis of the environmental impact can be carried out
9.1.3
Branding
An in-depth market analysis of users and the placement on the market should be carried out to ﬁnd the best way of reaching
customers. The analysis should take Griffenshop’s business strategy into consideration, as this will help imĺuminate aspects
such as time to customer from time of purchase. A new market analysis, reaching a larger group of potential users will also
help to determine how a product that combines changing table with a desk is perceived.
If more branding is wanted corporation with a larger design company should be sought out as this will provide better expo-
sure on the market. By reaching larger shops with furniture, a bigger market is reached. Shops or design brands should ﬁt
into the style of Griffenshop, both in pricing and design.

Reflections
The initial scope of project has formed the process of the project, providing both beneﬁts and challenges. The offset of
investigating problems and challenges for families living small, has provided me with a unique insight to the needs of these
families, and is considered a strong basis for developing the concept Simplicity. On the other hand, the starting point for
the project has resulted in a project going in many direction and the ﬁnal concept is not developed as far as it would have
been with a more focused direction.
One of the goals was to ﬁnd families who wanted to follow the design process in order for me to get other points of views on
the process, the three families who followed the project from the beginning to the end has provided me with a lot of insight
and new ideas. The ideas carried out in the project are based on how interviews with the families are carried out - if it had
been possible to watch the families, or the families had been asked to ﬁll out different questionnaires after my visits, the
results of the ﬁeldwork might differ from the results gained.
Communication between me and my supervisor is vital, and unfortunately I was struck by illness in the middle of the process,
which put a strain on the communication for a duration of the process. The lack of communication has attributed to some
choices in the process of developing the four concepts, creating obstacles for the further development.
The collaboration with Griffenshop functioned well during the project, and the size of the company proved to be beneﬁcial
for the design process since I was able to discuss solutions and ideas with the owner and because an overview of the
assortment was quickly gained. Furthermore, the limitations in choices of materials and manufacturing methods provided
a sense of direction to the project from the beginning. The limitation in materials and manufacturing methods have resulted
in a wast number of iterations for each of the parts in the table because the best and simplest solutions was constantly
sought, and were at times hard to ﬁnd.
9.3
Conclusion
The end result of this project is a ready-for-market product which is developed in collaboration both with potential users as
well as a supplier of similar products. Towards this end goal several aspects and impacts of the ﬁnal solution has been
investigated.
Ultimately this piece of furniture will beneﬁt both small children and their parents alike by both accommodating the traditional
use-cases of a changing table, while favoring the needs of small homes.
Based on existing literature I have gained a preliminary but sufﬁcient understanding of development of cognitive and motor
skills within children, and have designed the product such that it supports crucial parent-child communication.
Based on a signiﬁcant amount of user interaction through different co-creation methods i have developed an understanding
of the needs and challenged that meets those who live with limited space, and have designed the product such that it solves
the oft-repeated need for additional storage space while functioning as a desk and using minimal amount of space. This
is done while maintaining a focus on aesthetic properties, which allows the developed product to be placed alongside the
users regular living room furniture, furthermore this is done with a focus on the production capabilities my collaborating
company, Griffenshop. To further support the marketing potential of the product a analysis showcasing the blue ocean
potential of the product.
Aside from user and market suitability, a speciﬁc goal of this project is to create a product which is environmentally friendly
and lives up to relevant standards. In terms of standard compliance, the product fulﬁlls to the possible extent the require-
ments of DS-12221-1. In terms of environmental friendliness a life-cycle check has been carried out, showing that the
sustainability of the product in the traditional sense of materials and manufacturing process is very close to that of similar
furniture, however, the features of the product is likely to give a long-lasting position in peoples home, and can be seen as a
highly sustainable alternative to furniture that will be discarded sooner, however this is not realistically measurable at this
stage, and remains speculation.
