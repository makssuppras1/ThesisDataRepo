Abstract
This study aimed to investigate the potential of inkjet printing for fabricating microelec-
trodes for electrochemical biosensing. Key parameters that impact electrode size and
performance were explored, such as splat diameter (SD) and the number of printed lay-
ers. Findings revealed that smaller SD and a higher number of layers improved electrode
performance by minimizing defects caused by printing errors.
However, maintaining consistency in day-to-day printing is challenging, mainly due
to the influence of environmental factors like temperature and humidity. These condi-
tions can lead to nozzle clogging, emphasizing the need for controlling the environment
during printing sessions. Further, the study discovered that electrode size and perfor-
mance is influenced by the printing direction, and the optimal approach involves printing
alternating layers in multiple directions.
Based on these results, electrodes were printed with a 20 µm SD and two lay-
ers on FR4 and PET substrates, followed by a thermal sintering process in a tunnel
oven.
A comparison between inkjet-printed electrodes and those fabricated through
gold standard photolithography (CR) revealed that cleaning inkjet-printed electrodes
enhanced performance.
However, CR electrodes demonstrated better reproducibility
without cleaning.
The electrodes were functionalized with an anti-CA125 antibody and tested for detec-
tion of CA125, a cancer biomarker. The study, however, did not find a statistically sig-
nificant difference between analyte and control measurements, which may be attributed
to issues in the functionalization chemistry, low analyte concentration, or electrode han-
dling. Lastly, scanning electron microscopy was used for determining the thickness of
the electrodes, and atomic force microscopy was used to find that the surface rough-
ness of inkjet-printed electrodes is three orders of magnitude larger than CR electrodes
which is a relevant feature for electrochemical biosensing. Future work should focus
on optimizing the printing process, managing environmental factors, and improving the
reproducibility of the electrodes.

Resumé
Dette projekt sigtede mod at undersøge potentialet ved inkjet printning til fremstill-
ing af mikroelektroder til elektrokemisk biosensing. Nøgleparametre, der påvirker elek-
trodestørrelsen og -ydelsen, blev undersøgt, såsom ’splat diameter’ (SD) og antallet af
printede lag. Fundene viste, at en mindre SD og et højere antal lag forbedrede elek-
trodeydelsen ved at minimere defekter forårsaget af udskrivningsfejl.
Imidlertid er det udfordrende at opretholde reproducibilitet i daglig printning, hoved-
sageligt på grund af miljøfaktorernes indflydelse, såsom temperatur og fugtighed. Disse
forhold kan føre til tilstopning af print dyserne, hvilket understreger behovet for at
kontrollere miljøet under print sessioner. Yderligere opdagede undersøgelsen, at elek-
trodestørrelse og -ydelse påvirkes af printretningen, og den optimale tilgang involverer
printning af vekslende lag i flere retninger.
Baseret på disse resultater blev elektroder fabrikeret med en SD på 20 µm og to lag på
FR4 og PET-underlag, efterfulgt af en termisk sintringsproces i en tunnelovn. En sam-
menligning mellem inkjet printede elektroder og dem fremstillet gennem guldstandard
fotolitografi (CR) afslørede, at rengøring af inkjet printede elektroder forbedrede ydelsen.
Imidlertid demonstrerede CR-elektroder bedre reproducerbarhed uden rengøring.
Elektroderne blev funktionaliseret med et anti-CA125-antistof og testet for detektion
af CA125, en kræftbiomarkør. Undersøgelsen fandt dog ikke en statistisk signifikant
forskel mellem analyt- og kontrolmålinger, hvilket kan tilskrives problemer med funk-
tionaliseringskemien, lav analytkoncentration, eller håndtering af elektroden. Til sidst
blev scanningselektronmikroskopi brugt til at bestemme tykkelsen af elektroderne, og
atomkraftmikroskopi blev brugt til at finde ud af, at overfladeruheden på inkjetprintede
elektroder er tre størrelsesordener større end CR-elektroder, hvilket er en relevant egen-
skab for elektrokemisk biosensing. Fremtidigt arbejde bør fokusere på at optimere print
processen, håndtere miljøfaktorer og forbedre reproducerbarheden af elektroderne.

Preface
This Master’s thesis was written at the Department of Bioengineering in combination
with Danish Technological Institute and focuses on the investigation of inkjet printing
in microelectrode fabrication for electrochemical biosensing.
This work constitutes the final requirement for the completion of my M.Sc.Eng. in
Physics and Nanotechnology at the Technical University of Denmark, DTU, and was
conducted from the 31st of January to the 23nd of July 2023.
This thesis consists of my report on this work and the related experimental results.

Drop-on-demand piezoelectric inkjet printer. The piezoelectric inkjet printer
applies a piezo-ceramic plate to create ink droplets.
A thin diaphragm is
bonded to the piezo-ceramic plate to prevent unintended interactions between
the inks and the plate. The piezoceramic plate deforms in response to an
electric impulse to generate a pressure wave to eject the ink.
The ink is
replenished on the removal of the electric pulse. At the same time, the piezo-
ceramic plate returns to its normal shape. A: Squeeze mode; B: bend mode;
C: push mode; D: shear mode. From [61] . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Biosensors are analytical devices that combine a biological component with a physico-
chemical detector to detect biomolecules [1, 2, 3]. The biological recognition element,
which can be an enzyme, an antibody, or a strand of DNA, interacts with the target
molecule, while the detector converts this interaction into a measurable signal [1, 3].
This unique combination allows biosensors to provide rapid and accurate measurements
of a wide range of biological substances, making them invaluable tools in numerous fields
[2, 3].
In the medical field, for instance, biosensors are widely used for disease diagnosis
and monitoring. One of the most well-known examples is the glucose sensors that al-
low diabetics to monitor their blood sugar levels easily and accurately[[4]]. Similarly,
pregnancy tests, which detect the presence of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) in
urine, also rely on biosensor technology [2, 5]. More recently, biosensors have been de-
veloped for the detection of specific biomarkers associated with various cancers, offering
the potential for early diagnosis and personalized treatment [[4], [6]].
Biosensors also have important applications outside of healthcare. In the environ-
mental sector, they are used to monitor pollution levels, detect harmful substances in
water and soil, and track changes in biodiversity [[6]]. In the food industry, biosensors
are used to detect pathogens and toxins, ensuring food safety and quality [[2]]. More-
over, in defense and security, biosensors are deployed to detect biological and chemical
threats, such as toxins, viruses, and other biohazards [[3], [2]].
Commercially, the biosensor market is dominated by a few key players, which primarily
produce sensors for healthcare applications [[4]]. However, the market is also witnessing
the entry of numerous startups, driven by advancements in technology and growing de-
mand from various sectors [[3], [2]]. With an estimated global biosensor market size of
$25.5 billion in 2021, the sector’s forecasted growth is expected to reach $36.7 billion by
2027, attesting to its increasing significance [[4]].
This expansion in the biosensor market is driven by several factors. The escalation
in the prevalence of chronic and infectious diseases has led to a surge in the demand
for point-of-care (POC) testing [[4]]. POC devices provide prompt results that allow for
immediate interpretation and disease management, thereby helping to minimize hospi-
tal stays or unnecessary visits [[4]]. Moreover, technological advancements, the rise of
nanotechnology, and a growing geriatric population are fueling market growth [[3, 4]].
Yet, a major roadblock remains. The high cost of development and reluctance among
end users to adopt new technology due to lack of awareness remain significant challenges
[[4]].

1 Introduction
The commercialization of biosensors faces several substantial challenges that often
stem from the transition of the sensor from a controlled lab environment to real-world
conditions.
One primary challenge lies in the reproducibility of biosensor responses,
which can be affected by a multitude of factors such as variations in the fabrication
process, changing conditions during usage, and the stability of biological components
[57][[6]].
Fabrication process variations can impact the consistency of biosensor performance
significantly. Even minor changes in the manufacture of a biosensor can result in different
responses to the same input, thus affecting the reproducibility of the biosensor’s readings.
This is particularly evident in the production of biosensors based on nanomaterials,
where variations in synthesis parameters can lead to significant differences in the final
product [[4]][[6]].
Biosensor performance can also be influenced by changing conditions during usage.
Real-world samples can present complex mixtures of substances that can interfere with
the biosensor signal, affecting its accuracy and reproducibility [57].
For instance, a
biosensor designed for use in environmental monitoring could be affected by varying
temperature, pH, and other environmental factors that were not accounted for during
the development phase [[6]].
Furthermore, the stability of the biological components used in biosensors remains a
significant issue. Enzymes, antibodies, or other biological elements utilized in biosensors
are susceptible to changes in their environment and can degrade over time. This insta-
bility can lead to a reduction in the sensor’s performance over time and compromise the
reproducibility of the results [57][[6]].
Commercializing biosensors also requires careful consideration of economic factors.
The cost of producing a biosensor needs to be balanced against its sensitivity, selectivity,
and complexity of its design and fabrication process [[3]]. Often, improvements in one
area can lead to increases in cost, making it diﬀicult to find the optimum balance for
commercial success [[2]].
Finally, meeting the regulatory standards is another critical hurdle in commercial-
izing biosensors. They must pass rigorous validation tests to demonstrate their safety,
eﬀicacy, and reliability, which often requires considerable investment in time and re-
sources [[6]].
In summary, while the potential for biosensors in numerous applications is vast, the
path to their commercialization is fraught with challenges such as achieving reproducibil-
ity, managing the stability of biological components, balancing cost with performance,
and meeting regulatory standards [57][[4]][[3]][[6]][[6]][[2]].

2.1.1
Photolithography
Photolithography is recognized as one of the most utilized microfabrication techniques in
the semiconductor industry [2, 27, 42], It leverages ultraviolet (UV) light, ranging from
10 to 400 nm [43], to expose light-sensitive polymers and develop intricate patterns [44].
It allows for the fabrication of both 2D and 3D microelectrodes, marking its versatility
[44, 27]. Conventional photolithography is capable of manufacturing structures down to
100 nm [45], but with the introduction of deep UV, structures as small as 50 nm can be
realized [46].
The photolithographic process involves four key stages [44]. Initially, a photoresist
layer is applied on the substrate, the coating parameters of which are dependent on
the specific photoresist selected [47]. Following this, the photoresist layer is exposed to
UV light and subsequently developed using an appropriate solvent depending on the
photoresist used. Ultimately, the pattern is transferred through either etching or lift-off
processes [44, 27]. Initially, the fabrication technique was generally split into contact
and proximity photolithography. The first method required the photomask containing
the pattern to be in contact with the substrate while the latter allowed a non-contact
exposure [44, 48]. Advancements in lithographic technology now allow maskless expo-
sure of patterns much like inkjet printing, as maskless exposure utilizes a digital image
to only expose the areas of the pattern on the substrate [48]. An example of a lift-off
process using maskless photolithography is shown in Figure 2.1. The applications of
photolithography are prevalent in recent studies. Carriere et al., in 2021 [49], demon-
strated the fabrication of platinum microelectrode arrays (MEA) using a ceramic wafer
as a substrate, on which the photoresist was applied and subsequent development and

2.2
Inkjet printing
Inkjet printing is an additive manufacturing technique that has emerged as one of the
leading technologies for fabricating precise, high-quality patterns on both small and large
surfaces ([2], [58]). The central components of an inkjet printer include an ink chamber
for ink storage and a channel connecting the chamber to the printer’s nozzles ([2]). The
printer operates digitally, controlling the ejection of fluid droplets onto specific locations.
Inkjet printers, particularly those utilizing drop-on-demand (DOD) technology like
thermal and piezoelectric printers, play a pivotal role in producing microelectrodes and
enhancing a wide range of applications such as electronics, opto-electronics, displays,
biosensors, and chemiresistive sensors ([58], [59]). This category of printers includes
electrostatic, acousto, electrohydrodynamic, and valve inkjet printers ([2]). The DOD
technology assures that ink is ejected only when needed, preventing wastage of valuable
materials and promoting the creation of smaller droplets with higher placement accuracy
([2]). The waste reduction is particularly relevant for critical raw materials such as gold
and copper.
Inkjet printing is highly versatile, making it suitable for depositing functional fluids,
such as metal inks, conductive and semiconducting polymers, surface coatings, proteins,
and nanoparticles onto various substrates ([58]). The digital nature of inkjet printing
allows for on-the-fly corrections and easy pattern changes, making it adaptable and cost-
effective ([58]). The technology aids in the deposition of nanomaterials for fabricating
microelectrodes, vital in applications like flexible electronics and point-of-care sensors
([59]).
Its solution processing and maskless printing capabilities allow for a myriad
of applications ([2]).
Despite the high cost associated with inkjet printing research

In the field of electrochemical applications, inkjet printing is gaining popularity due to its

2.2.1
Continuous inkjet printing
Continuous inkjet (CIJ) printing is a deposition technique that enables the construction
of images and structures in a droplet-by-droplet fashion. The produced droplets are gen-
erally consistent in size, unless changes are made to the jetting parameters or the ink,
offering a high degree of reproducibility [60]. In CIJ printing, a continuous stream of
droplets is generated using a high-pressure pump that vibrates the nozzle with a piezo-
electric crystal [61]. These droplets are then charged via printer signals and directed
towards the substrate by an electric field. Unused droplets are caught in a gutter for
recirculation back into the reservoir [60, 61]. The working principle of CIJ is shown
in Figure 2.3 from [61]. A: In a binary deflection system, the uncharged droplets are
printed onto the matrix and a single nozzle can only print at one dot position; B: in a
multiple deflection system, the charged droplets are deflected onto the matrix. Multiple
dots are deposited per nozzle [61].
CIJ printing stands out for its high speed and the velocity of the ink droplets, allowing
them to travel longer distances to the substrate [61]. This method is also particularly
eﬀicient as it forms continuous lines through the continuous stream of ink instead of a
sequence of dots, eliminating the need for overlapping ink droplets to produce a contin-
uous line [61]. Its application is therefore quite suitable for low-resolution patterning of
colloidal solutions [60]. Moreover, the continuous generation of ink droplets and the use
of volatile inks minimize the risk of nozzle clogging and ensure rapid drying, respectively
[61].

Despite its disadvantages, this technique can achieve high printing frequencies (up to
80 kHz) and jet velocities (20 m/s), proving particularly useful in certain applications,
such as high-speed textile printing or product expiration date marking [60, 61].
2.2.2
Drop-on-demand inkjet printing
The Drop-on-Demand (DOD) inkjet printing method operates differently from continu-
ous inkjet printing, offering several unique advantages. Rather than continuously eject-
ing ink, DOD printers only release ink when necessary, eliminating the need for a complex
==PAGE:30==
2.2 Inkjet printing
13
droplet charging, deflection, and recycling system [60, 61]. This results in smaller drop
size generation and higher placement accuracy, with ejected drop sizes often approxi-
mating the diameter of the orifice. Droplets can be as small as 20 microns [60, 61]. The
primary subclasses of DOD printers are defined by the methods they use to generate the
pressure pulse that forms ink droplets. These include thermal, piezoelectric, acoustic,
electrostatic, electrohydrodynamic (EHD), and valve methods [60, 2, 61]. Of these, ther-
mal and piezoelectric methods are the most prevalent in modern inkjet printing, while
EHD is gaining prominence. The other methods remain in developmental stages [60, 2].
DOD printers are prevalent in both industrial and research settings, with piezoelectric

Drop-on-demand technology presents fewer contamination risks due to the lack of

It is commonly used in most printers due to the ability to

2.2.2.1

the vapor bubble collapses, generating a force that refills the ink chamber [60, 61]. This

to piezoelectric technology [60, 61, 2].

nozzle, concentrates, and jets out by overcoming the surface tension under electric field
modulation [37]. This dual-controlled mechanism and special jetting mechanism enable

2.2.4
Ink formulation
Ink formulation is a complex process, involving the combination of different constituents
that each bring unique properties and effects to the ink. There are multiple components
in an ink formulation, including functional particles, binders, solvents, and additives [66,
67].
The base in an ink formulation is the liquid medium carrying the functional particles
for binding to the matrix post-printing. It falls into four groups: aqueous-based, non-
aqueous-based, phase-change, and reactive, each with unique properties and applications
[61].
• Aqueous-Based: Primarily uses water as the main solvent, it’s environmentally
friendly due to the lack of organic solvents. However, image quality may degrade
due to potential ink diffusion ([61].).
• Solvent-Based: Composed of organic solvents, such as various alcohols, ketones,
or other organic compounds. It shares the same drying and diffusion characteristics
as the aqueous-based base ([61].).
• Phase-Change Base: Solid at room temperature, this base melts to liquid dur-
ing jetting, then solidifies on reaching the matrix, preventing ink diffusion and
==PAGE:38==
2.2 Inkjet printing
21
improving image quality. However, they may struggle on non-porous surfaces due
to poor adhesion ([61].).
• Reactive Base: Remains stable until exposure to ultraviolet (UV) irradiation
triggers polymerization for compact matrix adhesion. While it improves image
quality by reducing ink dispersion, it also leads to higher ink consumption. Never-
theless, the absence of volatile organic solvents makes it environmentally friendly
([61].).
Choosing the base depends on various factors such as the matrix type, desired image
quality, environmental considerations, and specific printing process requirements.
2.2.4.1
Additives
Additives are crucial components of ink formulation. They serve to stabilize and adjust
the properties of the ink, ensuring optimal performance during printing and storage. Ma-
jor types of additives include surfactants, viscosity modifiers, dispersants, humectants,
biocides, and chelating agents.
Among these, polymers, surfactants, and stabilizing
agents merit further discussion due to their significant impact on ink behavior and func-
tion[61, 66].
• Polymers in inks can serve a multitude of functions, including acting as binders,
viscosity modifiers, and dispersants.
As binders, they ensure that the metal
nanoparticle stays attached to the printed material after drying. When acting
as viscosity modifiers, they regulate the thickness of the ink, helping to control the
rate at which it spreads on the print medium. An example of a polymer used for
this purpose is poly(vinyl alcohol)[61, 66]. As dispersants, they help maintain the
distribution of metal nanoparticles within the ink, preventing the metal nanopar-
ticles from clumping together, which can lead to issues such as clogging the print
head.
• Surfactants, short for surface-active agents, reduce the surface tension between
two substances, such as a liquid and a gas, or a liquid and a solid. In the context of
inks, surfactants can modulate the surface tension of the ink, preventing clogging
of the print head and controlling the release of the ink from the nozzle[61, 66].
They are classified based on the charge of the hydrophilic head: anionic (negative
charge, such as sodium dodecyl sulfate), cationic (positive charge, such as cetyl
trimethyl ammonium bromide), non-ionic (no charge, such as Triton X-100), and
zwitterionic (both positive and negative charges, such as betaine)[61, 66].
• Stabilizing agents are additives used to maintain the homogeneity and stability
of the ink. These agents prevent the aggregation of metal nanoparticles within the
ink, which can lead to undesirable effects such as inconsistent color and clogging
of the print head. Stabilization is typically achieved through electrostatic and/or
steric mechanisms. For instance, the anionic surfactant sodium dodecyl sulfate can

In summary, each constituent of ink plays a crucial role in determining the overall

2.2.5
Functional inks
Metal inks play a crucial role in the production of printed electronics (PE) and are char-
acterized by their metal content, metal type, precursor compounds, and the physical
structure of the metallic elements within the ink. The key differences among various
types of metal inks hinge on these attributes, and they significantly affect the perfor-
mance, cost, application compatibility, and processing requirements of the inks.
2.2.5.1
Conductive inks
The type of metal used in ink formulation greatly influences the performance and cost
of the inks. Silver and gold are noble metals that are the most commonly used due to
their high conductivity and resistance to oxidation. Silver, though it oxidizes, forms a
conductive oxide layer that doesn’t significantly affect the ink’s conductivity over time
[67]. Gold (Au) is frequently employed due to its excellent conductivity and resistance
to oxidation, enabling durable and high-performance electronic devices, particularly use-
ful in bio-applications [61, 66]. However, gold inks can be costly due to the high cost
of the metal itself.
For gold nanoparticle inks, a common stabilizer used is a thiol-
containing compound that binds to the gold surface, forming a robust layer that protects
the nanoparticles from aggregation and oxidation. On the other hand, silver nanoparti-
cle inks often employ short-chain carboxylic acids or polymers as stabilizing agents to
achieve the same effects[61, 66].
Furthermore, copper, aluminum and nickel are less expensive alternatives to silver. How-
ever, they are not very stable against oxidation and can form insulating oxides that seri-
ously affect the inks’ conductivity. As a result, post-processing methods often need to be
carried out in a vacuum or under a protective or reducing atmosphere to avoid oxidation
[67]. Copper, while slihgtly less conductive than silver, is considerably cheaper, which
can make it an attractive alternative despite the need for a controlled environment to
prevent oxidation [67]. Nickel inks are often used for applications requiring magnetic
properties[61, 66].

Metal Structures:
• Nanoparticle inks are commercially available and stable, offering good shelf life

nozzle clogging and the lower volume fraction of nanowires in an ink, multiple
passes are often needed to produce an electrically conducting part [61, 68].
• Unlike nanoparticle inks, Metal Organic Decomposition (MOD) inks are
particle-free solutions, eliminating the risk of nozzle clogging. Gold MOD inks are

suitable for use on heat-sensitive substrates [61, 68].

2.2.5.2
Bio inks (May reduce for relevance)
Bio-active inks, also known as biomolecule inks, have been gaining increased attention
due to their possible use across various sectors, including biomedical research, pharma-
ceutical manufacturing, and bioengineering [61, 69]. These inks, which contain at least
one type of biomolecule such as proteins or nucleic acids, need to meet specific criteria
for compatibility with inkjet printing technologies [61, 69].
Key parameters like viscosity and surface tension greatly impact the effectiveness
of bio-inks. Typically, these inks are water-based, showcasing low viscosity and high
surface tension [61, 69]. While the low viscosity can be handled using current Drop-

surfactants, which can unfortunately cause some biomolecules to irreversibly unfold,
thus leading to a loss of their bioactivity [61, 69]. The challenge in using bio-active inks,
therefore, lies in maintaining the stability of these biomolecules under these conditions
and their capacity to retain or regain their native conformation[61, 69].
Nevertheless, despite these advancements, challenges still persist. Mass production of
bio-active inks in industrial processes remains a significant challenge, particularly due
to issues related to biomolecule storage [61, 69]. However, innovative solutions like the
UV-photoprintable ionogel-based microarray developed by Tijero et al. show promise
for eﬀicient storage of avidin biomolecule for over a month at room temperature[61, 69].

2.2.6
Ink physicochemical properties
Physicochemical properties, including viscosity, surface tension, and evaporation rate,
are also crucial to achieving high printing accuracy and resolution [66, 69]. For instance,
during the inkjet printing process, surface tension can significantly affect the formation
of droplets. Higher surface tension values may result in earlier droplet breakup and
faster drop velocity [66, 69].
2.2.6.1
Viscosity
Central to the inkjet printing process is the ink formulation, which is characterized by
several crucial parameters, among them being viscosity. The role and impact of viscosity
in inkjet printing cannot be overstated, as it is instrumental in governing the flow of ink
and ultimately the print quality [66, 69].
Viscosity can be broadly described as a fluid’s resistance to flow. It influences how
ink moves within the inkjet printing system, particularly inside the printer’s nozzle [66,
69]. As the ink travels through the narrow passages within the printer and finally gets
ejected from the nozzle as droplets, the viscosity of the ink can influence the size, velocity,
and formation of these droplets [66, 69]. Thus, the viscosity has a significant impact on
the formation and behavior of ink droplets, affecting the dot placement accuracy and
overall print resolution [66, 69].
The optimum viscosity range varies depending on the specifics of the printing system.
For example, piezoelectric consumer device printheads typically require a viscosity value
of the inks in a range of 7 to 30 mPa*s at room temperature to achieve good printing
performance. On the other hand, research and development inkjet printers can handle
a broader range of viscosities due to built-in nozzle heaters [66, 69, 63]. For instance,
high-viscosity fluids (>30 mPa*s) can be printed by increasing the nozzle temperature,

while the printing of fluids with lower viscosity (<7 mPa*s) can be adjusted by altering
the waveform pattern applied to the dispensers or by adding low-volatile humectants
[66, 69, 64].
Balancing the viscosity is critical to ensure the smooth operation of the inkjet printing
process. A too high viscosity can lead to issues such as nozzle clogging, while a too low
viscosity may result in an uncontrolled ink flow, leading to poor print quality [66, 69].
Additionally, the viscosity of the ink affects the drying time of the printed pattern and
the interaction of the ink with the substrate, impacting the print morphology [64, 63]. A
high viscosity ink might result in the formation of taller, narrower printed lines, while a
low viscosity ink could cause excessive spreading or merging of the printed droplets [66,
69]. Therefore, understanding and controlling ink viscosity is vital for successful inkjet
printing.
2.2.6.2
Surface tension and wetting
In addition to viscosity, two of the most important parameters are surface tension and the
wetting behavior of the ink on the substrate. The significance of these two parameters
in inkjet printing becomes apparent when considering the roles they play in ink droplet
formation, deposition, and spread on the substrate, and ultimately the print quality
[wetting2, 66, 69].
Surface tension is a critical property of a fluid, it describes the force that makes
the surface of that fluid behave like a stretched elastic sheet. In inkjet printing, surface
tension controls the formation of droplets at the nozzle and also influences their flight and
landing onto the substrate [66, 69]. Moreover, surface tension is a key factor determining
the shape and size of the deposited droplets [wetting2, 66, 69]. Typically, the ideal
surface tension value for most of the inks is in a range between 20 and 70 mN m−1
[wetting2, 66, 69, 2]. By controlling surface tension, one can prevent problems such as
the splitting of the ejected drop into smaller droplets before they reach the substrate, or
excessive tailing of droplets, both of which can compromise print quality [wetting2, 66,
69].
Wetting behavior, on the other hand, describes how an ink droplet spreads across the
substrate after it is deposited. It is largely dictated by the interplay between the surface
energies of the ink and the substrate [wetting2, 66, 69]. The phenomenon of wetting
is critically important as it influences the distribution of the functional material carried
by the ink across the substrate, which can greatly affect the function and performance
of printed devices [66, 69].

The selection of a substrate for inkjet printing is critical to meet a variety of require-
ments and depends on the specific application. The ideal substrate should present a
balance of properties, including physical and thermal stability, flexibility, impermeabil-
ity, adhesiveness, cost-effectiveness and transparency. Furthermore, it should also be
economically viable ([60, 38]).

along with their transparency and cost-effectiveness, make them a favored choice in labo-

above the sintering temperatures of most functional inkjet inks [60, 38].
On the other hand, paper substrates are favored for being inexpensive, flexible, and
recyclable. The mechanical, physical, and chemical properties of paper can be tailored
through additives or alterations in the manufacturing process, making them adaptable
to various inks. Despite their porosity and rough surface, the addition of coatings can
significantly improve the printing resolution and the functioning behavior of the ink [60,

at 233◦C. Hence, for applications that require high thermal stability, substrates such
as Polyimide (PI), Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET), and Polyethylene Naphthalate
(PEN) are commonly used. Even though they don’t match the thermal properties of PI,
PET, and PEN have chemical resistance to solvents and intrinsic clarity that make them
suitable for applications that require the transmission of light and electrical conduction
([60, 38]).

or topology of the substrate to match the ink.

The use of sustainable and biocompatible substrates in the realm of inkjet printing for

2.2.9
Curing and Sintering
The drying process, which removes moisture and/or solvents from the ink after it is
transferred to the substrate, also plays a role in ink formulation. The drying process
ensures the proper adhesion of the ink to the substrate, and it involves mechanisms
such as evaporation of water and/or solvent, penetration into the printing substrate,
and air oxidation [38, 67]. Sintering is a vital process in the development of effective
conductive paths and is responsible for welding the solid metal particles below their
melting point, especially in metal-based nanoparticle inks [38, 67]. After inkjet printing,
a post-treatment, like curing, is necessary to transform the as-deposited ink into highly
conductive films [38, 67].
In the traditional thermal sintering method, the temperature is typically between 100
and 400◦C and is performed on a hotplate, box oven, or tunnel oven on a conveyor belt
[38, 67]. The required temperature varies from ink to ink, depending on factors such
as the boiling points of the organic solvents used, the dissociation temperatures of the
metal precursor salts, and the degree of conductive network formation [38, 67].
Typically, heating at 90–200◦C for 5–60 minutes is required to evaporate the organic
solvents, induce the decomposition or reduction of the metal precursor, and to obtain
sintered metal coatings with resistivity comparable to that of the bulk metal [38, 67].
However, it should be noted that the sintering temperature should be below the soften-
ing temperature of the substrate and often requires anti-oxidation protective conditions
[38, 67].
Electrical Sintering involves applying a voltage over the printed structure, causing cur-
rent to flow and generate local heating within the film [38, 67]. The resistivity of the
patterned film is determined by the sintering voltage used. Advantages of this technique
include a short sintering time (often less than 1 min), reduced damage to the substrate
due to area-specific heating, and real-time monitoring of the sintering process through
current measurements. However, electrical sintering requires contact with the printed
film, which may not be possible for some applications [38, 67].
Chemical sintering operates on the desorption or decomposition of the protective layer
on the surface of the metal nanoparticles by chemical agents [38, 67]. The removal of
this organic layer is necessary to produce conductive patterns, and the selection of an ap-
propriate sintering temperature in nanoparticle-based inks is extremely important. The
sintering temperature is primarily defined by the removal of the stabilizing molecules
from the metal surface rather than the melting point depression due to a smaller parti-
cle diameter[38, 67]. Various strategies like decomposition, desorption, or evaporation
of the material are employed to enable close contacts between the particles in the dried
printed patterns [38, 67].
Lastly, it’s worth noting that the long-term stability of sintered films, especially those
using oxidation-sensitive metals like copper, is not only influenced by changes in chem-
ical composition but also by the film’s microstructure evolution [38, 67]. Thus, recent
research has been directed towards developing low-temperature sintering methods that

oxidation effects and achieving similar or better electrical conductivity values compared
to conventional thermal sintering [38, 67].
One such method is photonic sintering that employs a broad spectrum of electromag-
netic radiation from ultraviolet (UV) to infrared (IR) to sinter printed metal patterns
[38, 67]. This method is particularly eﬀicacious when used with metal nanoparticles on
conventional substrates like polymer foils or glass, which possess robust plasmonic prop-

regulate various parameters, such as the pulse length, flashing frequency, sintering time,
and lamp intensity to prevent substrate damage due to overheating [38, 67].
Infrared (IR) sintering, another variant of photonic sintering, uses the Near-Infrared to
Mid-Infrared region (700 to 15,000 nm) radiation to expedite the drying and sintering
of printed inks [38, 67]. In an impressive application of this technique, Sowade et al.

adjusting the process parameters correctly is crucial to prevent potential damage to the
flexible substrate and printed patterns [38, 67].
Photonic flash sintering, also known as intense pulsed light (IPL) sintering, uses a Xenon
flash lamp with a broad wavelength range to stimulate heat generation in the target ma-
terials, facilitating a quick temperature increase [38, 67]. Despite its advantages, this
method can cause pattern defects such as delamination, cavities, and cracks due to the
high-intensity light [38, 67]. To mitigate these issues, a back-irradiation method was
proposed that irradiates the back surface of the patterns through a transparent plastic
substrate [38, 67].
Aside from photonic sintering, plasma sintering is a practical method for printed electron-
ics [38]. The technique exposes printed patterns to low-pressure argon plasma, producing
excited species such as ions, radicals, and UV irradiation that contribute to the decom-
position of the stabilizing agent and facilitate direct contact between metal particles [38,
67]. It’s worth noting that plasma sintering occurs from the top to the bottom of the
structure, and sintering is not achieved beyond the plasma species’ penetration depth
[38, 67].

silver by combining low-pressure argon plasma sintering and microwave flash sintering
at temperatures that are compatible with the glass transition temperature of common
polymer foils. Moreover, Wünscher et al. [38] combined atmospheric pressure plasma
sintering with a mild thermal treatment to decrease the sintering time and enhance the

2.2.10
Challenges and Limitations
Inkjet printing technology offers numerous benefits in the field of microelectrode fab-
rication and biosensing, such as cost-effectiveness, waste reduction, and the ability to
process a variety of organic and inorganic materials. However, the technology is not
without its limitations and challenges[60, 2, 36].
One of the major challenges associated with the use of inkjet printing in microelec-
trode fabrication is the issue of resolution and accuracy. Inkjet printing, despite its high
precision compared to other printing technologies, can still encounter problems with
resolution, especially when fabricating photodetector arrays where a higher resolution is
desirable [2]. This problem is often exacerbated by the speed of printing, which can often
be slow and thus limit throughput [2]. This is further exacerbated by potential nozzle
clogging, which can disrupt the printing process and affect the quality and reliability of
the printed microelectrodes [2]. Issues related to nozzle clogging and slow printing speed
have also been mentioned in other studies [2].
Additionally, the formulation of inks for inkjet printing presents its own set of challenges.
Inks need to be carefully synthesized to meet the demands of the rapidly advancing field
of nanotechnology. They need to possess different properties and functionalities to fa-
cilitate the manufacturing of multifunctional devices [2]. Issues such as poor stability,
surface control, and thickness size have been identified as challenges associated with ink
formulation.
The materials used for inkjet printing also pose challenges. While inkjet printing
can be executed on almost all substrates, the performance and quality of the printed
electrodes may vary depending on the substrate used. High porosity substrates may
require surface modification to reduce ink penetration and increase the adhesion between
ink and substrate [60, 2, 36]. The compatibility between the ink and the substrate,
therefore, is a significant factor that affects the effectiveness of the inkjet printing process
[60, 2, 36].
Inkjet printing also faces challenges in the fabrication of flexible and wearable elec-
tronics. Human epidermal devices demand an elaborate design for higher stretching
strain to match the modulus of the human skin. The development of intrinsically flexi-
ble and stretchable inks is required to prevent the pattern’s cracking and delamination
on polymer or paper-based substrates [60, 2, 36].
Lastly, the environmental impact and longevity of the printed electrode materials pose
==PAGE:52==

another set of challenges. The fate of these materials after long-term use and their en-
vironmental impact need to be assessed before they can be fabricated on an industrial
scale [60, 36]. To date, there is still a need to test these electrodes in real environmental
samples, composed of complex matrices, prior to commercialization [60, 36].
In conclusion, despite the potential benefits of inkjet printing in microelectrode fabri-
cation and biosensing, it is not without its limitations and challenges. These range from
technical issues such as resolution, speed, and nozzle clogging to more complex issues re-
lated to ink formulation, substrate compatibility, flexibility, environmental impact, and
longevity. Despite these challenges, research continues to advance the field, bringing
inkjet printing closer to realizing its full potential in the fabrication of microelectrodes
and biosensing devices [60, 2, 36].

2.3.1
Types of electrochemical sensors and detection
Electrochemical sensors function by measuring changes in an electrical parameter, such
as current, potential or conductance, as a response to a specific analyte in a sample [70,
71, 72]. These changes can be monitored using various types of electrochemical sensors
and detection techniques, each with its own advantages and limitations.
• Amperometric sensors, for example, operate by applying a constant potential
to the working electrode and then measuring the resulting current, which is pro-
portional to the concentration of the target analyte. The primary advantage of
amperometric sensors is their high sensitivity and fast response time, making them
ideal for applications that require real-time monitoring [70, 71, 72]. However, they
are more susceptible to interference from other electroactive substances in the
sample, which could potentially lead to false readings [70, 71, 72].
==PAGE:54==
2.3 Electrochemical sensor basics
37
• Potentiometric sensors, on the other hand, measure the potential difference be-
tween the working and reference electrodes in the absence of current flow. These
sensors are especially useful when the target analyte is an ion, as the Nernst equa-
tion can be used to directly relate the measured potential to the ion concentration
[70, 71, 72]. Despite their high selectivity, the main limitation of potentiometric
sensors is their relatively lower sensitivity compared to amperometric sensors[70,
71, 72].
• Conductometric sensors operate by measuring changes in the electrical conduc-
tivity of the sample, which can be affected by the presence and concentration of
certain analytes [70, 71, 72]. They are particularly effective when used with ionic
analytes, and have the advantage of being insensitive to oxygen. However, they
lack the high selectivity of potentiometric sensors, making it diﬀicult to distinguish
between different types of analytes in a complex sample [70, 71, 72].
As for detection methods, electrical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is one of the
most utilized due to its sensitivity to the surface of the electrode[70, 71, 72].
EIS
measures the impedance, or opposition to current flow, of a system over a range of
frequencies. EIS is highly versatile and can provide information about various aspects
of the system, such as charge transfer resistance and double-layer capacitance. However,
EIS measurements can be complex and require careful interpretation [70, 71, 72].
Cyclic voltammetry (CV) is another technique where the potential at the working
electrode is cycled between two values, and the resulting current is measured[70, 71, 72].
This allows for the study of redox reactions and the determination of reaction rates, but
the technique requires the analyte to be electroactive [70, 71, 72].
Square wave voltammetry (SWV) involves applying a square wave potential to
the working electrode and measuring the current at specific points[70, 71, 72]. SWV is
highly sensitive and can resolve overlapping redox peaks, making it particularly useful
for complex samples. However, like CV, it requires the analyte to be electroactive[70,
71, 72]. The detection methods are plentiful and vary depending on the application.
In conclusion, the choice of sensor type and detection method depends on the specific
requirements of the application.
Amperometric sensors and CV or SWV techniques
might be preferred for their high sensitivity, while potentiometric or conductometric
sensors and EIS might be chosen for their selectivity or versatility, respectively [70, 71,
72]. In this thesis, both CV and EIS are chosen to characterize the electrochemical
performance of the electrodes. Thus these are explained in more detail.

during the redox process exhibits the ∆Ep of about 59 mV. Thus, if the redox system
transfers 1 electron during the reaction, the closer the ∆Ep is to value of 59 mV, the better
the reversibility of the electrodes. Furthermore, ∆Ep is also a measure of electrochemical
==PAGE:56==
2.3 Electrochemical sensor basics
39
cleanliness of the electrode surface where a smaller value indicates a cleaner surface [70,
71, 72]. Any increase in this value is credited to surface imperfections or contaminations.
This can be used to determine the quality of the electrode. The ideal current peak
ratio is Ipa/Ipc = 1 for a reversible reaction. However, most experimental cases are not
completely reversible but exhibit near-reversible behavior referred to as quasi-reversible
reactions[70, 71, 72]. These satisfy Ipa/Ipc ≈1. The reproducibility of the ∆Ep and
Ipa/Ipc for different electrodes indicate high reproducibility of the electrode, which is
an important factor in electrochemical sensing[70, 71, 72].
Additonally, CVs are used in the cleaning of the electrode surface. In the cleaning
process of gold electrodes using 0.1 M sulfuric acid, H2SO4, an electrochemical polish-
ing of the electrode surface occurs[72]. The sulfuric acid acts as an electrolyte, and
the potential is swept across a range where gold can be oxidized (gold going from its
metallic state to a gold ion) and then reduced (gold ions being returned to their metallic
state). This process helps to remove any organic contaminants or oxides that might
have formed on the surface of the gold electrode, giving you a clean, active electrode
surface[72]. It is important to note that other oxidation or reduction processes can oc-
cur on the gold electrode surface during cleaning, especially if there are contaminants
present. These processes can sometimes result in additional peaks in the CV data. That
is why it’s crucial to perform this cleaning process as it ensures that the measurements
are due to the intended electrochemical reactions, and not due to unwanted reactions
with contaminants[72].
CV, however, also has limitations. The interpretation of CV data can be challenging
due to the possible influence of several factors like the electrode material, surface condi-
tion, and the nature of the electrolyte [40]. Moreover, the speed of the electrochemical
reaction needs to be suitable relative to the scan rate to obtain accurate CV data, which
may not always be the case in complex biological systems [70, 71, 72].
Despite these limitations, CV remains a fundamental technique in electrochemistry,
particularly in the characterization of electrodes and biosensing systems, due to its
straightforwardness, high sensitivity, and cost-effectiveness [70, 71, 72].
2.3.1.2
Electrical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS)
Electrical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) is an extremely useful non-invasive technique
for the evaluation of the electrical properties of a system over a range of frequencies[70,
71, 72]. Impedance, which is the comprehensive resistance offered by an electric circuit
to the flow of alternating current (AC) of a single frequency, is a complex quantity char-
acterized by a magnitude and a phase angle, reflecting the fact that there is a phase
difference between the applied voltage and resulting current. EIS involves applying an
alternating current (AC) of varying frequency to a system and measuring the resulting
voltage. This gives information about the system’s impedance at each frequency, which
can provide valuable insights into its electrochemical behavior [70, 71, 72].
The Nyquist plot is a graphical representation that plots the imaginary component of
impedance (-Z”) against its real component (Z’)[70, 71, 72]. A Nyquist plot typically
==PAGE:57==
40
2 Theoretical Background
consists of one or several semicircular regions, each corresponding to a different electro-
chemical process. This can be seen in Figure 2.14[74]. A common way to model the
Figure 2.14: Schematic illustration of a nyquist plot. Rct indicates the charge transfer
resistance, Re indicates the solution resistance and the warburg region is
seen as the straight line at a 45◦angle. From [74].
impedance of electrochemical systems is by using equivalent electrical circuits, such as
the Randles circuit. A Randles circuit is an equivalent circuit model that is used in the
analysis of EIS data, especially in corrosion studies [70, 71, 72]. The Randles circuit con-
sists of four key components: solution resistance (Rs), charge transfer resistance (Rct),
double layer capacitance (Cdl), and the Warburg impedance (Zw) [70, 71, 72].
• Solution resistance (Rs): This resistance arises due to the movement of ions
through the electrolyte solution. It represents the resistance between the reference
and working electrodes, appearing in the high-frequency region of the Nyquist plot
as the intercept on the real axis [70, 71, 72].
• Charge transfer resistance (Rct): It is the resistance related to the redox
reactions happening at the electrode interface. It can be influenced by the reaction
kinetics and the nature of the electrode surface. Since Rct is a measure of the
resistance encountered by the electron as it travels between the redox species and
electrode surface, a lower Rct value indicates a cleaner and more electrochemically
active surface. In a Nyquist plot, Rct manifests as a semicircle, with its diameter
equivalent to the magnitude of Rct [70, 71, 72].
==PAGE:58==
2.3 Electrochemical sensor basics
41
• Double layer capacitance (Cdl): When a potential is applied to an electrode
immersed in an electrolyte, it creates a charge separation layer, or a double layer,
at the electrode-electrolyte interface. This double layer behaves like a capacitor.
In the Nyquist plot, Cdl influences the curvature of the semicircle. However, its
specific value cannot be directly read off from the Nyquist plot [70, 71, 72].
• Warburg impedance (Zw): This impedance is associated with the diffusion of
electroactive species from the bulk solution to the electrode surface. It appears as
a linear part in the low-frequency region of the Nyquist plot, typically inclined at
a 45◦angle [70, 71, 72].
Additionally, the Constant Phase Element (CPE) is an element introduced in EIS
modeling to account for deviations from ideal capacitive behavior in electrochemical
systems [70, 71, 72]. This non-ideal behavior can arise due to a variety of factors such as
surface roughness, heterogeneities in the electrode-electrolyte interface, or the presence of
different reaction pathways [70, 71, 72]. Ideal capacitive behavious assumes homogenous
and uniform surfaces which often does not match the experimental conditions.
The
representation of a system with a CPE in a Nyquist plot is often not a perfect semicircle
as seen in a simple Randles circuit model with a pure capacitor, but rather a ”depressed
semicircle”, reflecting the non-ideal capacitive behavior captured by the CPE [70, 71, 72].
Utilizing the CPE in place of the double-layer capacitance (Cdl) in the Randles circuit
can result in improved fitting of experimental EIS data, allowing for more accurate
system characterization. It’s especially relevant when the system under study exhibits a
certain level of heterogeneity or complexity such as surface roughness [70, 71, 72]. Each
of these elements in the Randles circuit reflects a different aspect of the electrochemical
process. Therefore, changes in these parameters can lead to shifts in the Nyquist plot,
allowing for the analysis and interpretation of the underlying electrochemical processes
[70, 71, 72].
While EIS can offer rich information about the electrochemical processes occurring in
the system, it does come with challenges. One common pitfall involves the interpretation
of the EIS data. For instance, not all processes may be accurately represented by the
Randles circuit, especially in complex systems where more than one process may be
occurring simultaneously. Another challenge comes from the noise, particularly at high
frequencies, which can distort the Nyquist plot and yield misleading results [70, 71, 72].
In the context of biosensing, EIS is a powerful tool for characterizing and optimizing
sensor performance. It can be used to study the interfacial properties of the sensor,
evaluate the kinetics of the target analyte binding, and even detect the presence and
concentration of the analyte in a label-free manner, making it a versatile technique in
biosensor development[70, 71, 72].
Despite its challenges, EIS has shown great promise in a variety of sensing applica-
tions. The ability to non-invasively probe the electrical properties of a system over a
range of frequencies makes it a powerful tool for studying and monitoring electrochemical
processes, from corrosion to biological interactions [70, 71, 72].

3.5.1
Resistance measurements
Resistivity is a key determinant of the performance of inkjet prints, especially in the case
of functional conductive inks. By definition, resistivity is an intrinsic physical property
that indicates a material’s resistance to electric current flow and remains independent
of the sample’s shape or size [60]. The formula for resistivity, is given as ρ = A/L ∗R,
where A denotes the cross-sectional area in m2, L is the length in meter, and R is the
resistance in ohms Ω. The unit of resistivity is ohm-meters Ω∗m [60]. The resistance
of material measures the extent to which it opposes electric current flow and can vary
depending on the length, width, and thickness of the sample [60].
Materials with low resistivity, such as copper wires, allow for easy flow of electricity, while
==PAGE:69==
52
3 Methods
materials with high resistivity like glass, impede the flow of electricity [60]. Electrical
conductivity, σ is the inverse of resistivity [60] and given by σ = 1/ρ with the unit of
S/m. For gold the bulk conductivity is 4.11 ∗107 S/m at 20◦C and for silver the bulk
conductivity is 6.30 ∗107 S/m at 20◦C [60].
Measurement of the conductivity of a sample is often carried out using a two-point
probe (ohmmeter) or a four-point probe (Kelvin probe). The ohmmeter measures the
sheet resistance for which the geometrical shape and thickness are needed to calculate
the resistivity and conductivity. However, the ohmmeter’s accuracy comes into question
when the measurement value is close to the resolution of the meter [60]. For instance, an
ohmmeter with a resolution of 0.1 ohms would not be suitable for measuring a sample
of 0.5 ohms [60]. Additional resistances such as lead resistance in the wires, contact
resistance between the probes and the sample, and the equipment itself can lead to an
overestimate of the sample’s resistance, thus introducing systematic error [60]. In this
thesis, a two-point probe using an ohmmeter was used. However, the probes are quite
sharp and thus damage the surface of the prints. The measurement of the resistance also
does not stabilize as the damage done by the probes changes the resistance constantly.
Furthermore, the distance between the probes has to be constant if different prints have
to be compared. Thus a probe station was needed. As this was not available, a self-
made probe station was designed using a cardboard box, probes, an ohmmeter, and
an optical microscope. Figure 3.7 shows the self-made probe station setup. The idea
is that the probes are taped onto the cardboard box to maintain the same distance
between them during measurements. Furthermore, the optical microscope is only used
to move the probes up and down the prints. This allows measurements with minimal
damage to the prints as the resistance can be measured by moving the probes down
slowly until a stable value is obtained on the ohmmeter. This procedure was maintained
for all the conductivity measurements. The only parameters changed between different
investigations are the distance between the probes as some prints had smaller dimensions
and thus needed smaller distances to be able to measure.
To determine a conductivity value, the thickness of the prints is needed. However, a
profilometer was not available and thus it was not possible to measure the thickness of
individual prints. Therefore, each design is only comparable to one another. To obtain
the conductivity or resistivity further measurements of the thickness are needed for each
individual print, as this may differ from print to print. Therefore, to evaluate the optimal
parameters for the printing of the final electrodes, resistance is the primary parameter
chosen to compare. Furthermore, as the reproducibility of the printing is important, the
relative standard deviation (RSD), given by the RSD = STD
Mean ∗100%, is also determined.
The lowest RSD along with the lowest resistance values indicates the optimal parameter
for the electrodes.

3.5.4
Environmental effects
As mentioned in section 2.2, humidity and temperature play a vital role during the
printing process. The temperature directly affects the ink properties such as viscosity
and surface tension which in turn changes the dynamics of jetting. Often only 1 or
2 nozzles were jettable and this greatly affects the printing time and print fidelity as
potential clogging of any of these nozzles would inhibit the entire printing. Therefore,
the number of jettable nozzles changed from day to day. Furthermore, the humidity
also changed along with the temperature which is directly related to the drying of the
nozzles. Dry humidities below 40% are unfavorable for inkjet printing as this increases
the drying of the ink at the nozzles which ultimately induces nozzle clogging[69, 60].
Too high humidities above 60% also impact the substrate by potentially warping and
affecting the ink drying and solvent evaporation process. Therefore, a humidity range of
40-60% is recommended for inkjet printing [69, 60]. This was also seen in this thesis, as
few to no nozzles were jettable when working at temperatures above 25◦C and humidities
below 35%. For the most part of the thesis, ranging from February 2023 to mid-June
2023, the humidity was below 40%. This greatly impacted the quality of the printing and
inhibited the investigations greatly. However, when the air conditioning and ventilation
were fixed in the printing room, the humidity levels increased to 50-60%, depending on
the day. This vastly improved the jetting of the nozzles where some days a total of 7
nozzles were jettable. It should be mentioned that this environmental factor naturally
inhibited the progress of the investigations until this point.

drop overlap and drop spacing. Increasing the SD results in larger distances between
the drops of the printed line and reducing it brings them closer together.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The aim of this chapter is to reflect on the results presented in section 4 and discuss
possible sources of error and confounders along with reflections on future work.
As mentioned in chapter 1, the goal of this study is to investigate the fabrication and char-
acterization of inkjet-printed gold electrodes for the purpose of electrochemical biosens-
ing.
The first step of this study was the investigation of the printing parameters that
influenced the electrode size and performance. One of the more significant parameters
was found to be splat diameter (SD), which is dependent on the drop spacing and drop
diameter. The investigation yielded that the designed sizes and printed sizes may differ.
The best print fidelity depends on the SD and designated size. The best SD also changes
depending on the substrate as the best SD for a designed size of 1000 µm was SD20 and
SD15 for FR4 and PET respectively. For a designed size of 250 µm the best SD was
found to be SD13 and SD25 for FR4 and PET respectively. However, for the designed
linewidth of 25 µm, the printed lines are at least 63% higher, some being almost double
the size of the designed widths. Even for larger linewidths, the print fidelity is not good.
This may be credited to satellite drops or slow ejecting drops that are ejected from the
nozzles misdirected or later and thus do not land on the substrate where it is supposed
to. This leaves holes and defects in the printed lines. Thereby, reducing the print fi-
delity and percentage of error for the prints. Figure 4.5b shows an example of this. A
way to mitigate this is to optimize the pulse waveform in the dropanalyzer software.
The pulse should be optimized on a day-to-day basis due to the environmental effects
such as humidity and temperature that greatly influences the printing procedure, as
seen in this thesis. An optimized pulse may remove the formation of satellite drops and
non-matched velocity drops. Furthermore, the impact of the number of layers printed
was investigated. This investigation showed that printing multiple layers reduces the
holes and defects in the printed lines. Both the ErrP and resistance of the printed lines
were reduced by printing multiple layers. Potentially clogged nozzles, satellites, and
mismatched drops all influence the formation of a consistent line. Printing multiple
layers reduces the influences of these as the random nature of these sources of error is
mitigated to some degree by increasing the amount of printing over the same line. This
proved to be a way of mitigating the sources of error seen in the previous investigation.
The repetition of these investigations showed that day-to-day printing is not necessarily
reproducible. Environmental conditions significantly affect the printing process. Param-
==PAGE:116==

eters such as humidity and temperature should be well controlled in order to obtain
the reproducibility of prints in a day-to-day production setting. Furthermore, this also
requires day-to-day optimization. It is, therefore, advised that the environment is con-
trolled to be as constant as possible during a printing session. This could be achieved
using a humidifier that can set the humidity to a specific value and maintain it there to
some degree. Furthermore, a constant temperature would be advised as well. However,
this may be diﬀicult as using the tunnel oven for sintering increases the temperature
and reduces the humidity in the room. Therefore, it should be considered how to re-
duce the overlap of using the tunnel oven while printing as this may change the printing
conditions throughout a print.
One should also consider the stability of the ink and printhead in a production set-
ting. As the printhead is only recommended for one printing session without too much
idle time, it is important to keep the printing going in order not to dry the ink and
clog the nozzles. Furthermore, inks are not stable forever, and printing does increase
the temperature of the ink. This may induce particle agglomeration faster and reduce
the stability of the ink over multiple days. As the ink often lasts longer than a print-
head, due to the 1.5 mL ink capacity being more than enough for one printing session,
it is recommended that the printhead is removed after the printing session and the ink
cartridge placed in the refrigerator. This proved to improve the stability of the ink for
longer periods without frequent nozzle clogging.
In the next investigation of the space investigation, it was seen that the direction of
printing may impact the print fidelity. This was found to be dependent on the print
design as well. The separation between the boxes was printed better when the printing
direction matched the direction of the border. Printing in the opposite direction yielded
less print fidelity as the separation between the boxes was reduced. This is likely still
attributed to the clogging of the nozzles, satellite- and mismatched velocity drops men-
tioned earlier. It should be noted that when printing a line on the same axis as the
designed line, the line can be printed in fewer steps than printing a line on the oppo-
site axis as the designed line. This requires a printing action multiple times back and
forth across the entire line. Therefore, the same axis printing reduces the printing time
and printing actions across that line and thus reduces the effects of potential clogging,
satellite drops, and mismatched velocity drops. This may explain why the same axis
printing improves the print fidelity. However, this investigation must be repeated as the
clogging during the printing and single repetition of the structures is not representative
of the investigation.
The fabrication of the electrodes greatly depended on the findings of these investigations.
The investigation of the directional printing for the electrodes yielded that printing in
the x-direction first and then in the y-direction reduces the impact of potentially clogged
nozzles. This was an important finding to produce electrodes that are somewhat consis-
tent throughout the design. Naturally, any clogging would reduce the reproducibility of
the electrodes which is not preferred. Furthermore, the sintering investigation was lim-
ited to the tunnel oven due to the lack of more printed electrodes. Only duplicates were

seem like an angled scratch which is not in either printing direction. It seems something
in the inkjet printer may have scratched the print after the printing of the electrode.
However, it is diﬀicult to determine the cause exactly. Therefore, it is advised to repeat
the printing of the electrodes with larger printing heights, indicated by the substrate
thickness in the ceraslice software, to avoid possible scratching. This defect may be
the cause of the larger RSD value of 11.4 % for cleaned FR4 electrodes. Therefore, the
fabrication of these electrodes should be repeated in a more controlled setting in order
to investigate the superior substrate for electrochemical sensing applications.
Additionally, the electrochemical experiments should be repeated for the newly fabri-
cated electrodes as these variations during fabrication may be the cause of the large vari-
ations at the different functionalization steps. The functionalization chemistry should
also be optimized for the inkjet printed electrodes as the ink formulation and compo-
sition greatly affects the functionalization chemistry. From the FIB-SEM investigation
using EDX, it was seen that even after sintering the organic compounds remain in the
printed structures. Therefore, it is important to adjust the functionalization parameters
to improve the performance of the electrochemical sensing. Furthermore, it is advised
to increase the analyte concentration initially to prove the detection capabilities of the
electrodes and functionalization chemistry, before reducing the concentration to the ap-
propriate limit of 35 U/mL. Comparing the performance of the inkjet printed electrodes
to the cleanroom fabricated electrodes, it is seen that there is still some room for im-
provement in terms of reproducibility. While the EIS characterization showed that the
cleaned PET electrodes obtain similar RSD of the Rct, 4.4%, to the CR electrodes,
4.41%, the electrochemical functionalization, and detection showed that the variation
of the PET electrodes is much larger than the variation in the CR electrodes. Based
on these findings, the CR electrodes are, therefore, still more reproducible. This was
expected due to photolithography being the gold standard technique for fabricating mi-
croelectrodes.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This research aimed to explore the application of inkjet printing in microelectrode fabri-
cation for electrochemical biosensing, focusing on printing parameters like splat diameter
(SD) and layer quantity. Smaller SD was found to enhance ink volume per area and thus,
improve electrode size and conductivity. Additionally, printing more layers reduced the
occurrence of defects due to errors such as clogging and misdirected droplets.
The study also highlighted the diﬀiculty of achieving reproducibility due to envi-
ronmental factors, like humidity and temperature, affecting the printing process and
nozzle clogging. It also found that electrode performance was impacted by the printing
direction, with the best results achieved by alternating the direction across multiple
layers.
Based on these findings, electrodes were printed with a 20￿m SD, two alternating
layers on FR4 and PET substrates.
Each electrode was printed with a volume of
0.21±0.073µL with 16 minutes and 43 seconds printing time.
Further investigation
revealed that thermal sintering at higher temperatures improved conductivity and per-
formance, though PET electrodes deformed after four cycles of 174 s sintering at 180°C,
while FR4 substrates remained unaffected even after ten cycles. 7 cycles of sintering for
FR4 and 2 cycles for PET electrodes was performed prior to electrochemical characteri-
zation.
Electrochemical characterization of both FR4 and PET electrodes was performed,
comparing the results with gold standard photolithography, cleanroom (CR), fabricated
electrodes. The study found that cleaning the inkjet-printed electrodes improved per-
formance. Charge transfer resistances of cleaned FR4 electrodes, R82.21 ± 9.37Ωwith
RSD=11.4%, and cleaned PET electrode, 57.39 ± 2.55Ωwith RSD=4.44%, were lower
than CR electrodes, 168.6 ± 7.44Ωwith RSD=4.41%, while the reproducibility of the
CR electrodes was better.
The electrodes were functionalized with an anti-CA125 antibody and tested for detec-
tion of CA125, a cancer biomarker. The study, however, did not find a statistically sig-
nificant difference between analyte and control measurements, which may be attributed
to issues in the functionalization chemistry, low analyte concentration, or electrode han-
dling. Lastly, scanning electron microscopy was used for determining the thickness of
the electrodes to be 948.5 ± 111.5nm. using atomic force microscopy, it was found that
the surface roughness of inkjet-printed electrodes is three orders of magnitude larger
than CR electrodes which is a relevant feature for electrochemical biosensing. Future
work should focus on optimizing the printing process, managing environmental factors,
and improving the reproducibility of the electrodes.
