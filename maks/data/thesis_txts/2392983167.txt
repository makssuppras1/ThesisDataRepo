Abstract 
Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells are commonly-used cells for recombinant protein 
production in pharmaceutical industry; it has the ability of post-translational modification on 
secretory proteins. microRNA (miRNA) is a type of non-coding RNA which has the function 
of regulating translation of multiple mRNA targets. It has become the target of CHO cell 
engineering. Erythropoietin (EPO) is an obligatory growth factor for the proliferation and 
differentiation of committed erythroid progenitor cells and it is highly glycosylated. Medically, 
EPO is used to treat certain forms of anemia. CRISPR/Cas9 is a revolutionary genome editing 
technique which allows researchers to cleave specific targets of genome. In the present study, 
CRISPR/Cas9 was successfully used for knocking out microRNA targets from the genome of 
a CHO-DG44 EPO-producer cell line. Four targeted miRNA-deficient CHO-DG44 EPO-
producer cell lines were established, including mir-A1 complete knockout, mir-A1 partial 
knockout, mir-D2 complete knockout, and mir-H partial knockout. RT-qPCR result showed 
that compared to the reference cell lines, expression level of mir-A1 decreased by 76% – 90% 
for mir-A1 complete knockout, and decreased by 20% – 76% for mir-A1 partial knockout; 
expression level of mir-H decreased by 9% – 54% for mir-H partial knockout; for mir-D2 
complete knockout, no obvious decline was observed regarding the mir-D2 expression level. 
Mutant cell lines were subjected to 7-day flask-scale batch culture. According to batch culture 
result, the specific growth rate of mir-A1 complete knockout, mir-A1 partial knockout, mir-D2 
complete knockout and mir-H partial knockout were 0.021 h-1, 0.024 h-1, 0.015 h-1, 0.028 h-1, 
respectively; and the specific EPO titer of those cell lines were 4.97 pcd, 2.72 pcd, 3.65 pcd 
and 2.91 pcd, respectively. The result indicated that a) knockout of mir-A1 may facilitate the 
cell metabolism and EPO production; b) knockout of mir-H facilitate cell growth and had 
positive effect on EPO production; c) mir-D2 may be involved in CHO cell growth and 
metabolism. The cell line development process used in the present study seems to have negative 
influence on cell growth. In order to improve cell survivability and cell growth, an optimized 
single-cell cloning medium formula was used and has been exhibited improved performance 
than commonly-used culture medium; this optimized medium contained recombinant human 
albumin and transferrin. Taken together, it was demonstrated that the present CRISPR/Cas9-
driven cell line development process can be successfully applied on non-coding RNA knockout 
in CHO genome, and the miRNA-deficient cell lines generated in this study can be used for 
further research and industrial application. 
Key words: Chinese hamster ovary cells; CRISPR/Cas9; erythropoietin; microRNA; cell line 
development; batch culture; mir-A1; mir-D2; mir-H

1 Introduction 
1.1 An Overview of the Study 
This study was aimed at knocking out some specific microRNA (miRNA) targets on the 
genome of a Chinese hamster ovary (CHO)-DG44 erythropoietin-producer cell line. The 
potential influence of selected microRNAs on CHO cells has not been fully understood; 
however, according to the bioinformatic analysis result as well as literature reviews of miRNAs 
studies in other mammalian cells prior to this study, they are possibly related to cell growth, 
protein production as well as protein N-glycosylation process. Therefore, knockout of selected 
microRNAs may result in phenotypic changes of cell growth, protein production and protein 
glycosylation. Knockout of microRNA targets was performed by using CRISPR/Cas9 
technique. Single-cell cloning medium formulation was optimized, in order to increase clone 
survival rate in single-cell cloning process. To my knowledge, there is a similar method that 
uses CRISPR/Cas9 method to delete targeted genes in mammalian cell genome (1). However, 
there is only very limited number of study that uses CRISPR/Cas9 technology to delete the non-
coding RNA target on Chinese hamster ovary cell genome. 
The structure of the thesis is listed as followed: 
Chapter 1 is the background knowledge summarized from the relevant literature, covering the 
fundamental knowledge as well as some up-to-date aspects of Chinese hamster ovary cells, 
microRNA, CRISPR/Cas9, and Erythropoietin. 
Chapter 2 provides an outline of the whole study, as well as a list microRNA targets addressed 
in this study. 
Chapter 3 describes the materials and methods used in this study. 
Chapter 4 describes the experimental results obtained from the study. 
Chapter 5 is the discussion regarding the results. 
Chapter 6 summarizes the whole study, and gives an outlook about potential improvement and 
further development based on this study.    
1.2 An Overview of Chinese Hamster Ovary Cells 
Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells are commonly-used mammalian cells in pharmaceutical 
industry. During the recent years, although microbial expression platforms still predominate in 
pharmaceutical industry, the proportion of pharmaceutical product expressed by mammalian 
cells has been increasing steadily. It is estimated that the total amount of biopharmaceutical 
manufacturing activity in 2010 is equivalent to 26.4 metric tons of pure protein (active

pharmaceutical ingredients), of which about 68% was produced by microbial expression 
systems, while the remaining 32% was produced by mammalian systems (2). This is especially 
the case for newly approved pharmaceutical products in recent decades, since biosimilar 
monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) and some other types of recombinant proteins have attracted the 
interest of pharmaceutical industry (2). Among several kind of mammalian expression system, 
CHO cell lines remain the most commonly-used cell lines for production. During the period 
from 2010 to 2014, about 33% of the newly approved products are based on CHO cell 
expression systems (see Figure 1-1) (2). 
The predominance of CHO cells as the host cell can be attributed to the following reasons. 
Firstly, CHO cells have been used as host cells for more than 30 years by pharmaceutical 
industry since tissue plasminogen activator, the first recombinant therapeutic protein produced 
using CHO cells by Genetech, was approved in 1987 (3, 4). Therefore, it may be easier for 
CHO cell-expressed products to get approval from regulatory authority such as FDA, since the 
application of CHO cells in pharmaceutical production is more mature compared to other 
mammalian cell lines, and the potential risk has been minimized. Secondly, the availability of 
gene amplification system on CHO cells make them overcome the low specific productivity q, 
which is one of the common disadvantages of mammalian cells (4). Thirdly, CHO cells possess 
the function of conducting post-translational modification on secreted protein (4). Many 
recombinant protein products need to be glycosylated, in order to be compatible with and 
bioactive in humans. prokaryotic host cells, such as E. coli, lack the requisite machinery to 
synthesize human-like glycoforms, whereas mammalian cell lines, such as CHO cells, have the 
desired machinery to produce those glycosylated proteins (3). Fourthly, CHO cells can be easily 
adapted to suspension growth condition, which is the preferred growth condition for large-scale 
cell culture in industry (4). Lastly, CHO cell lines were originated from inbred animal, and has 
been developed for several decades, thus CHO cells are functionally hemizygous for many 
genes, primarily due to gene inactivation, although they are diploid (3, 5). A big challenge of 
using mammalian model organisms for genetic research is the complexity of genotyping 
analysis led from their diploidy (“the Mendelian inheritance”), compared to the microbial 
counterparts (3).  
Chinese hamster (Cricetulus griseus) is a hamster species which is originally from the desert 
area of northern China and Mongolia (3). They were firstly used as laboratory specimen in 1919 
for typing pneumococci (3). The Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells were first isolated from 
the ovary of a female inbred Chinese hamster by Dr. Theodore T. Puck in 1957 (6). He initially 
used those cells for chromosome studies (6). It was soon found out that the CHO cells possess 
some advantages, such as low chromosome number (2n = 22), more varied sizes of

The isolated CHO cells were then maintained in vitro and developed as the original CHO cell 
line. After the original CHO cell line was established by Dr. Puck, it was distributed to and 
maintained in many laboratories around the world, which formed the basis CHO cell line 
variation today (7). These original CHO cells were favored by researchers because they grew 
well and fast in adherent culture and had a high cloning efficiency (7).  Due to the various 
culture conditions in different laboratories and further manipulations made on the original CHO 
cell line, many subtypes of CHO cell lines were established. One of the most popular CHO cell 
line is called CHO-K1. It was derived from the original CHO cell line, and established in the 
late 1960s from Puck's lab (7). Some well-known contract manufacturing companies in 
pharmaceutical industry have used CHO-K1 as the host cells for production, combined with the 
use of glutamine synthetase (GS)/MSX amplification system, making it become popular in 
research work and pharmaceutical industry. It is also the first CHO cell line that the genome 
was sequenced and analyzed (8). The original CHO cell line was also maintained in another lab 
in Toronto, in which the CHO-S cell line was established (7). The CHO-S cell line was also 
commercialized in 2002 (7). Compared to CHO-K1 cell line, CHO-S cell line was adapted to 
suspension growth rather than adhesion growth. 
The first CHO cell line that used to produce recombinant protein is CHO-DXB11 (also called 
DUK-XB11). This cell line was derived from CHO-K1 cell line and was established by Drs. 
Urlaub and Chasin at Columbia University (7, 9). The most important characteristics of this

During early decade of CHO cell development, it was essential to include fetal bovine serum 
(FBS) in the culture medium, usually in a percentage of 10% – 20% (7). However, researchers

had attempted to reduce the use of serum in medium since the early decades, and CHO cells 
were firstly adapted to serum-free medium in 1965 (13, 14). Today, the use of animal-derived

used as carbon source, therefore, metabolism shift is commonly seen in batch culture. 
1.3 microRNA 
microRNA (miRNA) is a type of non-coding RNA with the length of around 22 nt. It is 
expressed in a wide range of higher eukaryotes, but highly conserved across species. The 
function of miRNA is to regulate the translation of its targeted mRNAs. One miRNA is able to 
regulate multiple mRNAs. It is estimated that one third of all human genes is regulated by about 
1900 of miRNAs. The mechanism of this regulation is binding between a miRNA and its 
targeted mRNAs. A miRNA can base-pair with its targeted mRNAs at 3’ untranslated region 
(3’ UTR), repressing the translation of the mRNA. The binding between the miRNA and 
mRNAs is imperfect, i.e. not all the nucleotides are needed to base-pair with its targeted 
sequence. Actually, it is shown that the base-pairing between bases 2 – 7 of the 5’ end of a 
miRNA (“the seed sequence”) and 3’ untranslated region (3’ UTR) is most critical for the 
targeted mRNA selection (see Figure 1-3) (19)

miRNAs are transcribed by RNA polymerase II. The product of this transcription is primary 
miRNA transcript (pri-miRNA). A pri-miRNA can contain sequence of one or more miRNAs. 
Apart from pri-miRNA, an excised intron or 3’ UTR of a pre-mRNA can also be the source of 
miRNA. A pri-miRNA is usually several hundred nt long. Within the pri-miRNA, there are 
some 70-nt sequences which can form the hairpin structure. These hairpin-structure sequences 
are cleaved by the enzyme Dorsha and DGCR8, and transports from nucleus to cytoplasm. In 
cytoplasm, they are furtherly cleaved by Dicer and and TRBP complex. The product of the 
cleavage is a 21 – 23-bp double-strand RNA. One or both of the strands will be selected and 
become mature miRNA, by integrating into RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC) (see 
Figure 1-4).

A set of naming rules for microRNA has been established. A typical miRNA name contains 
several letter and numerals. A miRNA name starts with a three- or four-letter prefix, indicating 
the species of genome where this miRNA located. The pre-miRNA sequence is annotated as 
“mir-“, while the mature sequence is annotated as “miR-“, with the suffix”-5p” or “-3p” 
indicating its orientation. Related precursor loci expressing related mature microRNA 
sequences have lettered suffixes. For plant miRNAs and viral miRNAs, some special rules are 
applied. Some naming examples of miRNA are shown in . (20) 
As mentioned above, a miRNA is able to regulate the translation of multiple targeted mRNAs. 
Therefore, it can be efficient to carry out CHO cell line development by altering miRNA 
expression. Due to the lack of genomic information of CHO cell lines, it was not able to carry 
out such a research until the time when CHO-K1 genome was sequenced and well-annotated.

expression. Recently it has become the latest trend of CHO cell line engineering. 
1.4 CRISPR/Cas9 Technique 
Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) is a family of DNA 
repeats which is found in most archaeal (around 90%) and bacteria (around 40%) (22). CRISPR 
is used by many prokaryotic microorganisms as an adaptive immune system against 
bacteriophage or some other foreign genetic elements (22). A typical CRISPR sequence 
consists of several noncontiguous direct repeats separated by some variable sequences called 
spacers, and cas genes (CRISPR-associated genes) which is adjacent to repeat-spacer array (22). 
The cas gene is expressed as Cas protein, which is a family of enzyme that has the functional 
domains of nucleases, helicases, polymerases and polynucleotide-binding proteins (22). 
CRISPR sequence, together with Cas proteins, forms the CRISPR/Cas system (22). 
According to the latest classification system, CRISPR/Cas systems are divided into two classes; 
Class 1 CRISPR/Cas systems possess multi-subunit crRNA-effector complexes, whereas Class 
II systems possess a single protein which carries out multiple functions, such as Cas9 (23). 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
Class 1 CRISPR/Cas systems consist of three types: Type I, Type III and Type IV; these three 
types are further divided into twelve subtypes (23). Class 2 CRISPR/Cas systems consist of two 
types: Type II and Type V; And Type II systems have three subtypes (23). Due to its simplicity 
of the structure, CRISPR/Cas9 (Type II) system has been developed as a tool for genome 
editing. It has been found that Cpf1, another CRISPR-associated protein, can be categorized 
into Type V system. Therefore, Cpf1 may be another potential enzyme that can be used for 
genome editing. 
Cas9 endonuclease contains two domains, one is homologous to HNH endonuclease, which is 
called HNH domain; the other is homologous to RuvC endonuclease, which is called RuvC-
like domain (24). Each domain cleaves one DNA strand: the HNH domain cleaves the strand 
which is complementary to the crRNA, whereas the RuvC-like domain cleaves the non-
complementary strand (24). Defect in one of the two domains results in incompletely nicked-
open cleavage of targeted DNA double strand, in which only one strand is cleaved (24). Based 
on the functional difference of the Cas9 domains, a mutated Cas9 nickase has been developed. 
This Cas9 nickase can generate a DNA single-strand break (“nick”) at a specific site of target 
sequence. Using two nickase at the same time can generate a sticky end of double strand end, 
rather than a blunt end generated by wild-type Cas9. By using Cas9 nickase, the off-target issue 
can be greatly minimized (25). 
In the wild-type CRISPR system, when the foreign genetic material (e.g. bacteriophages) 
invades the host cell, their DNA sequence will be cleaved into short segments by Cas protein. 
Those segments will be integrated into CRISPR array as “spacer”. The spacer is 20 nt long (26). 
There are also some identical short sequences in the array, which is named “repeat”. The 
spacer/repeat sequence is transcribed and become precursor CRISPR RNA (pre-crRNA). The 
pre-crRNA is further modified by enzyme and become CRISPR RNA (crRNA). The spacer 
sequence on crRNA is complementary to the original sequence from invading DNA, hence 
crRNA can bind to the target site. The binding of spacer sequence to target sequence is not 
necessary to be strict, the minimum requirement is binding of 17 bp. Another RNA element in 
this system is called trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA). This RNA has the sequence that 
complementary to part of the crRNA, and it has the function of guiding the Cas protein to the 
specific site (24). Therefore, when the crRNA:tracrRNA complex is formed, Cas protein will 
be guided to the target site and cleave the target sequence (24). It has been revealed that at some 
positions of spacer sequence, specific types of nucleotides are preferred; for example, Guanine 
is preferred to be the first position from 5’ end of spacer (27). Therefore, sometimes an extra 
guanine is added to the 5’ end of spacer sequence, rendering a 21-nt long spacer sequence. 
To simplify the procedure of CRISPR application for genome editing purpose, the feature 
sequences of crRNA and tracrRNA are fused together into a single chimeric RNA (24). This 
==PAGE:20==
9 
 
artificially synthesized RNA was proved to have the equivalent function to the 
crRNA:tracrRNA complex (24). Therefore, when CRISPR/Cas9 system is applied to genome 
editing use, only gRNA sequence needs to be modified to adapt to target sequence, the rest part 
acts as a scaffold (26). 
Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM) is a short DNA segment which is adjacent to target 
sequence of Cas9 (28). The length of PAM varies from 2 to 6 bp (24). Cas9 protein cleaves the 
target site by recognizing the PAM. Without PAM, Cas9 will not cleave the target site, even 
though the target sequence presents (24, 26, 29). Therefore, it is a mechanism used by 
CRISPR/Cas9 system to distinguish self- and non-self-sequence, since the target sequence 
presents both on the target genetic materials and CRISPR locus (29). The precise sequence and 
positon of PAM varies between different CRISPR systems (24). For S. pyogenes type II 
CRISPR system, the PAM is NGG, and these three base pairs are within the target DNA (24). 
Due to the ease of operation, CRSPR/Cas9 technique has been widely used for mammalian cell 
line engineering (1). Some online tools have been developed, which is specifically for aiding 
CHO cell line engineering using CRISPR/Cas system (30).

1.5 Erythropoietin 
Erythropoietin (EPO) is an obligatory growth factor for the proliferation and differentiation of 
committed erythroid progenitor cells. Naturally, it is primarily produced from the kidney by 
endothelial, peritubular and epithelial tubular cells; it can also be produced the liver (32). In the 
human body, EPO is produced in response to hypoxia, and suppressed by hyperoxia and 
polycythemia. The presence of EPO increases the number of developing erythroid precursors 
and accelerates release of reticulocytes from the bone marrow, as illustrated in Figure 1-6 (32). 
There is also evidence showing that EPO increases the pool of cells for erythroid differentiation 
(33). Medically, EPO is used for treatment of the anemia of chronic renal failure, since it was 
approved by US Food and Drug Administration in 1989 (34). 
 
 
Figure 1-6 A simple illustration of EPO regulation in hypoxia and normoxia condition. HIF, hypoxia inducible 
factor. Source: (32) 
 
EPO is a glycoprotein which contains 166 amino acids and has a molecular weight of 39 kDa 
(32). It is estimated that 40% of the molecule composed of sialic acid (34). This sialic acid 
composition contributes to the biological activity and stability of EPO, while desialation 
reduces the half-life of EPO (35). EPO molecule contains four carbohydrate chains, among 
which three contain N-linkages to asparagine at position 24, 38 and 83, while one contain an 
O-linkage to serine at position 126 (36). Those glycosylation sites play an important role in 
biological activity of EPO, as removal of them (especially N-linkages) leads to drastic decrease 
of its biological activity (36). EPO is a hydrophobic molecule; thus, intact disulfide bonds are 
required to maintain its activity. Due to the glycosylation property, mammalian cell lines were 
==PAGE:22==
11 
 
preferred for EPO production. It has been reported that EPO can facilitate cell proliferation (37). 
CHO cell line is the primary expression platform for EPO production, and has been investigated 
intensively. One of the changes regarding EPO production was the tremendous structural 
heterogeneity of N-linked glycosylation; this has been attempted be solved by glycan 
remodeling (38).

2 Project Overview 
This study was aimed at knocking out several glycosylation pathway-related microRNAs on 
the genome of anan EPO-producer CHO-DG44 cell line, by using CRISPR/Cas9 technique. It

5) Transfection of CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids into CHO-DG44 genome; 
6) Transfectant cell sorting; 
7) Cell line generation by single-cell cloning and clone expansion;

Among these five formulations, Formulation 1, 2 and 3 were PowerCHO-2-based media, and

for each type of formulation, each well was filled with 125 µL of cell culture. Therefore, the 
seeding density was 125 µL of medium per cell per well. The cell growth of each plate was 
examined 10 days after seeding. The formulation with best performance will be chosen as 
single-cell cloning medium for later use. 
3.5 Transfection 
CHO-DG44 cells with the viability higher than 90% were used for transfection. 24 h before 
transfection, the cell culture was centrifuged at 200× g-force for 5 min, and then cells were 
resuspended in PowerCHO-2 medium with 4 mM of L-glutamine but without anti-clumping 
agent at the density of 5 × 105 cells/mL, since anti-clumping agent will interfere the process of 
==PAGE:32==
21 
 
transfection. 4 h before transfection, cell culture was centrifuged again and the cells were 
resuspended in FreeStyle CHO expression medium at the density of 1.0 × 106 cells/mL. 
During the transfection, the following steps were performed to carry out transfection: 
1) Dilute 3.75 µg of plasmids in OptiPro SFM (serum-free medium) to a total volume of 
60 µL, mix gently; 
2) Dilute 3.75 µL of FreeStyle MAX reagent in 56 µL of OptiPro SFM to a total volume 
of 60 µL, mix gently and incubate for 3 min at room temperature. 
3) Gently mix the solution of Step 2 with the solution of Step 3, and incubate at room 
temperature for 15 min; 
4) Transfer 3 mL of cell culture to each well of a 6-well plate; add the solution of Step 3 
to each well, and incubate at 37 °C, 5% of CO2 and 120 rpm. 
24 h after transfection, the post-transfection cell pool was centrifuged, and the cells were 
resuspended in PowerCHO-2 medium with 4 mM of L-glutamine. 48 h after transfection, the 
post-transfection cell pool was subjected to Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) to sort 
out the cells with plasmids transfected, and the transfection efficiency was estimated by using 
Celigo. Then, the successfully-transfected cells were concentrated and subjected to further 
incubation.

3.8 Batch Culture Test 
The vessel for batch culture was 125-mL Erlenmeyer flask. The start volume of batch culture 
was 100 mL (40% of flask volume, suggested by the flask manufacturer). The culture medium 
was PowerCHO-2 with 4 mM of L-glutamine and 0.2% of anti-clumping agent. The seeding 
density was set as 5×105 cells/mL. Incubator temperature was set as 37 °C, and shaker speed 
was set as 150 rpm. Batch culture was planned to last for maximum 8 days; for each culture, 
the harvest criterion was when viability was ≤60%, i.e. monitoring would be stopped when the 
viability was below 60%. For each cell line, the culture was performed in duplicate. Cell 
counting was conducted daily by using NucleoCounter NC-200 counter (ChemoMetec A/S, 
Allerod, Denmark); 2 mL of culture supernatant was removed daily for metabolite 
measurement. On Day 4 of culture, a dose of sample containing 5×106 viable cells/mL was 
removed for total RNA extraction. A dose of 200 µL culture supernatant was preserved for 
EPO titer measurement.

4.1 Identification of Unannotated microRNAs in CHO-K1 Genome 
and Design of CRISPR/Cas9 Spacer Sequences 
As mentioned previously, sixteen miRNAs had been chosen as knockout targets by using 
CRISPR/Cas9 technique. In order to conduct CRISPR/Cas9 knockout, the locus of each 
miRNA target was determined, and the spacer sequences for CRISPR/Cas9 knockout were 
designed. 
A workflow of CRISPR/Cas9 spacer sequence design for deleting specific miRNA targets has

4.3 Optimization of Single-cell Cloning Medium 
Single-cell cloning is a time-consuming and risky process. In order to improve the cell 
survivability during single-cell cloning, we had improved the single-cell cloning medium 
formulation. Five medium formulations, denoted as V, W, X, Y and Z, were tested in the 
experiment. Medium V, W an X were PowerCHO-2-based medium. Medium Y and Z were 
Ex-cell-based medium. CHO-DG44_EPO cells (names as REF cell line in this study) with 
viability higher of 95.5% was used in the medium test. the culture volume is 125 µL per well 
in 96-well plate; each well contained no more than 1 cell, as cell culture was diluted to 8 
cells/mL. The growth of the cell was examined on Day 9 after limiting dilution. 
It was observed that there was no cell growth in all the PowerCHO-2-based media (formulation 
V, W and X). Cell growth was only observed in Ex-cell-based media. For Medium Y, 30.2% 
==PAGE:40==
29 
 
of the total well numbers were observed to have partially confluent growing cell clones; the 
confluence varied between 3% and 20%. Another 10.4% of the wells contained cells but had 
growing in isolated state. For medium Z, 28.13% of the total well numbers were observed to 
have partially confluent growing cell clones; the confluence varied between 1% and 30%, but 
for most of the wells, confluence was below 3%. Another 4.13% of the wells contained cells 
but had growing in isolated state. apparently, medium Y had much better effect in facilitating 
cell growth in 96-well plate. Therefore, in the rest of the study, formulation Y was chosen for 
the medium of limiting dilution, and the dilution density was set as no more than 8 cells/mL 
(counted in viable cell density), which is equivalent to 1 cell with 125 µL of medium per well.

4.4.4 Clone Expansion and Stabilization 
Once the subclones with knockout of targeted miRNA were identified by PCR, they were 
immediately transferred from 96-well plates to the 24-well plates, as nutrient depleted very 
soon in 96-well plates. Two fully-confluent wells were subjected to cell counting. The result 
showed that when these two wells just achieved full confluence, their viability were 98.1% and 
98.4%, respectively; and their viable cell density were 3.34 × 105 cells/mL and 3.67 × 105 
cells/mL, respectively. The subclones were incubated statically in 24-well plate, with 1 mL of 
fresh culture medium (PowerCHO-2 with 4 mM of L-glutamine and 0.2% of anti-clumping 
agent, referred to as “culture medium” in the following part) in each well. The cells in each 
well were observed to be gathered in the center of the well, even without any external force. 
Subsequently, the subclones were transferred to 12-well plates and finally 6-well plates when 
more cell clone mass was observed by eyes. culture volume was always maintained at the 
maximum working volume of each type of plates (for 24-well plate, 1 mL; for 12-well plate, 2 
mL; for 6-well plate, 3 mL). When the subclones in 6-well plates achieved the viable cell 
density of 1 × 106 cells/mL, they were passaged to 125-mL Erlenmeyer flasks, with 10 mL of

4.5.1 Differential between Reference Cell Line (REF) and Mock Cell Lines 
The two mock cell lines were developed from mir-J_KO cell pool, but it was verified that the 
targeted miRNAs mir-J1 and mir-J2 were not deleted in their genomes. REF cell line was from 
CHO-DG44_EPO cell pool transfected with PX458 plasmid without spacer insert, and was not 
subjected to single-cell cloning process. Therefore, regarding the microRNA targets, mock cell 
lines presented the same genotype as REF cell line. The differential between REF and mock 
cell lines reflects the influence of cell line development process on cell growth and metabolism. 
==PAGE:51==
40 
 
In Figure 4-15, it was shown that REF cell line presented higher viability than the two mock 
cell lines during the 7-day cultivation period. Viability of REF peaked at 96.25% on Day 3, 
Mock-1 peaked at 93.8% on Day 2, and Mock-2 peaked at 89.7% on Day 2. The two mock cell 
lines did not have apparent log phase; viable cell density of these two cell lines increased at 
slow growth rate over the whole cultivation period. In contrast, REF cell line maintained a high 
growth rate from Day 0 to Day 6; there was a short log phase during the first day, and then an 
exponential phase from Day 1 to Day 4; from Day 4 to Day 6, viability cell density increased 
at a decreased rate; after Day 6, VCD decreased, and the turning point correlated to the nutrition 
depletion (see Figure 4-16). Subsequently, the integral of viable cell density of the two mock 
cell lines was much lower than REF cell lines. Generally, mock cell lines had poorer growth 
profile than REF cell line, indicating that single-cell cloning process may have negative 
influence on cell growth. During Day 0 – 4 (non-stationary growth phase), the average specific 
growth rate of Mock-1, Mock-2 and REF were 0.017 h-1, 0.012 h-1, 0.025 h-1, respectively. 
Figure 4-16 shows the metabolites concentration change of REF and mock cell lines during the 
cultivation period, including the profile of glutamine concentration, glutamate concentration, 
glucose concentration, lactate concentration, and ammonium concentration. 
The start concentration of glutamine was around 4 mM, as L-glutamine concentration in culture 
medium was diluted to 4 mM at the very beginning. Glutamine concentration of Mock-1 and 
Mock-2 gradually declined over the cultivation; however, it was not depleted at the end for both 
mock cell lines. This indicates that the original glutamine in medium was not fully utilized. 
Glutamine concentration of REF declined from Day 0 to Day 4, and became fluctuated from 
Day 4 to the end. The result indicates that glutamine in REF culture might be fully consumed 
on Day 4 and shift from pure consumption to combination of production and consumption from 
Day 4 to the end. The shift might take a very short time; therefore, glutamine depletion was not 
exhibited in the graph. During Day 0 – 4, the average specific glutamine consumption rate of 
Mock-1, Mock-2 and REF were 2.01 × 10-8 µmol/(cell∙h), 3.13 × 10-8 µmol/(cell∙h) and 1.37 × 
10-8 µmol/(cell∙h), respectively. The specific glutamine consumption rate of REF was lower 
than the rate of mock cell lines. 
Correlating to glutamine profile, as the product of glutamine metabolism, the production rate 
of glutamate in REF was much higher than the two mock cell lines. The final glutamate 
concentration of REF, Mock-1 and Mock-2 on Day 7 were 2.50 mM, 2.59 mM and 3.58 mM, 
respectively. Therefore, glutamate concentration of REF was 43.2% higher than Mock-1 and 
38.23% higher than Mock-2, respectively. During Day 0 – 4, the average specific glutamate 
production rate of Mock-1, Mock-2 and REF were 5.66 × 10-9 µmol/(cell∙h), 1.36 × 10-8 
µmol/(cell∙h) and 6.03 × 10-9 µmol/(cell∙h), respectively.

Glucose is the main carbon source for CHO cell metabolism. In REF culture, glucose was 
depleted on Day 6, while in mock cultures, glucose was not depleted at the end of the cultivation 
period. It can be deduced that a) the decline of viable cell density of REF on Day 6 may due to 
the depletion of glucose, b) The poor growth profile of mock cell lines may not be due to the 
nutrition limitation. For Mock-1, the final glucose concentration was 7.55 mM, which was 
22.78% of the start concentration. For Mock-2, the final glucose concentration was 29.83% of 
the start concentration. Obviously, glucose in the medium was not fully utilized by mock cell 
lines. During Day 0 – 4, the average specific glucose consumption rate of Mock-1, Mock-2 and 
REF were 8.19 × 10-8 µmol/(cell∙h), 1.90 × 10-7 µmol/(cell∙h) and 8.37 × 10-8 µmol/(cell∙h), 
respectively. 
Lactate and ammonium are the byproduct of the metabolism. They may also be consumed 
during the cultivation, due to the metabolism shift. The lactate production did not show much 
correlation with the glucose and glutamine profiles. Lactate concentration of REF reached the

5 Discussion 
5.1 Knockout of mir-A1 may Facilitate the Cell Metabolism and 
EPO Production. 
In the present study, mir-A1 was successfully knocked out from the genome of CHO-DG44 
EPO-producer cell line by using CRISPR/Cas9 technique, resulting in two mutant cell line: 
mir-A1_F-KO, in which mir-A1 was completely knocked out; and mir-A1_P-KO, in which 
mir-A1 was partially knocked out. For both mutant cell lines, reversion was not seen in the 
study. The mir-A1 expression level in mir-A1_F-KO decreased by 76% – 93% compared to the 
expression level of reference cell lines (two mock cell lines and the original CHO-DG44 EPO-
producer cell line). The mir-A1 expression level in mir-A1_P-KO decreased by 20% – 76% 
compared to the reference cell lines. And the mir-A1 expression level of mir-A1_F-KO was 
around 1/3 of the expression level of mir-A1_P-KO. The result indicated that, in terms of mir-
A1 knockout profile, these two mutant cell lines were stable cell lines mir-A1 knockout profile 
will be maintained in the cell genome. 
Generally, mir-A1_P-KO presented better growth profile than mir-A1_F-KO, as partial 
knockout cell line had higher viable cell density and specific growth rate (0.024 h-1) than 
complete knockout cell line (0.021 h-1). On the other hand, both of the knockout cell lines 
presented better growth profile than mock cell lines but poorer profile than the original CHO-
DG44 EPO-producer cell line (REF). Therefore, the opposite conclusion will be deduced when 
making comparison between partial knockout versus complete knockout and knockout versus 
non-knockout. Although the two mir-A1 knockout cell lines presented poorer growth profile 
than the REF, a strict conclusion cannot be deduced since the original cell line was not subjected 
to single-cell cloning process. A previous study using bioinformatic miRNA target prediction 
indicated that genes related to cell proliferation and apoptosis may be the target of mir-A1, 
although firm evidence was not given (42). Therefore, it is necessary to carry out further 
research to investigate the relationship between mir-A1 and cell growth. 
Generally, during the early stage of batch culture (Day 0 – 4), mir-A1_F-KO had higher 
glutamine and glucose consumption rate than mir-A1_P-KO, but glutamine and glucose were 
depleted earlier in mir-A1_P-KO than in mir-A1_F-KO, since mir-A1_P-KO had larger cell 
population at the late stage. The metabolism rate of non-knockout cell lines (REF and mock 
cell lines) was between the two mutant cell lines. The specific consumption rate of glucose and 
glutamine and the specific production rate of lactate and ammonium of mir-A1_F-KO were 
higher than the rates of mir-A1_P-KO; besides, the specific glutamate production rate of mir-
A1_F-KO was lower than the rate of mir-A1_P-KO. Therefore, it can be roughly concluded 
==PAGE:72==
61 
 
that mir-A1 may inhibit the cell metabolism, and knockout of mir-A1 may facilitate cell 
metabolism, although more evidence was needed to firmly confirm this point. 
Regarding EPO production performance, it is obvious that during the whole batch culture 
period, mir-A1_F-KO had higher EPO productivity than mir-A1_P-KO, mir-A1 knockout cell 
lines generally produced more EPO than non-knockout cell lines. The specific EPO titer of mir-
A1_F-KO (4.97 pcd) was 1.83 times of the specific EPO titer of mir-A1_P-KO (2.72 pcd). both 
of them had higher specific EPO titer than REF (2.35 pcd) but not exceeded mock cell line 
(3.95 pcd for Mock-1 and 6.35 pcd for Mock-2). Therefore it seems that knockout of mir-A1 
may have positive effect regarding EPO production.  
It has been reported that mir-A1 targets 3’ UTR of the gene named Enhancer of Zeste homolo 
2 (Ezh2), a histone methyltransferase subunit that methylates lysine 27 of histone H3 (H3K27) 
to silence gene transcription (43, 44). It also showed that overexpression of mir-A1 in murine 
myogenic C2C12 cells induced creatine kinase activity (44). A recent study showed that mir-
A1 may inhibit NF-κb signaling pathway (45). Another study showed that mir-A1 directly 
targets microphthalmia-associated transcription factor (MITF) and therefore reduced cell 
viability and induce apoptosis in human malignant melanoma cell line (46). However, there is 
still limited information about mir-A1 regulation towards cell proliferation and cell metabolism. 
As almost all the research was based on human cancer cells, the research about mir-A1 
regulation in pharmaceutical production is totally blank. therefore, further research may be 
necessary to obtain more firm evidence. 
5.2 Knockout of mir-H Facilitated Cell Growth and Had Positive 
Effect on EPO Production. 
In the present study, it was observed that mir-H was partially knocked out from the of a CHO-
DG4 EPO-producer cell line by using CRISPR/Cas9 technique successfully; the knockout 
profile was stabilized and reversion was not seen in the study. Therefore, the mir-H partial 
knockout cell line was established and name mir-H_P-KO. 
The mir-H expression level of mir-H_P-KO was reduced compared to the reference cell lines 
(2 mock cell lines and 1 original CHO-DG44 EPO-producer cell line named REF); the 
reduction percentage varied between 9% to 54%, depending on the reference cell line used for 
comparison. 
It was observed that the growth profile of mir-H_P-KO was similar to the original CHO-DG44 
EPO producer cell line and better than the growth profile than the mock cell lines, indicating 
that by knocking out mir-H, cell growth was facilitated, which making the knockout cell line 
overcome the negative effect of single-cell cloning process. The specific growth rate of mir-
==PAGE:73==
62 
 
H_P-KO was 0.028 h-1, which was 1.65 times of Mock-1 value and 2.33 times of Mock-2 value, 
respectively; it was the highest among the tested cell lines. The result indicated that the cell 
growth can be facilitated by knocking out mir-H. Previous studies showed that mir-H  promotes 
apoptosis by regulating apoptosis-associated proteins, such as Bax and p53; and the direct 
targets of mir-H include NIMA-related kinase 2 (NEK2) and SIRT1 (47–49). The pro-apoptosis 
effect of mir-H has been demonstrated in several types of human cells by overexpressing mir-
H in targeted cells (50). Some other studies demonstrated that mir-H inhibits cell proliferation 
in human cells, and downregulation of mir-H was observed in tumor cells (51, 52). The result 
regarding cell growth of mir-H_P-KO was in accordance with the previous studies. 
In accordance with its growth rate profile, the glutamine and glucose consumption profile of 
mir-H_P-KO was similar to original CHO-DG44 EPO-producer cell line (REF). However, the 
specific glucose consumption rate (8.56 × 10-8 µmol/(cell·h)) was higher than REF value and 
Mock-1 value, but lower than Mock-2 value. The specific lactate production rate (7.22 × 10-8 
µmol/(cell·h)) was higher than REF value but lower than Mock-1 value and Mock-2 value. 
Considering that both mock cell lines and REF cell line represent the genotype that mir-H was 
not knocked out, a correlation between mir-H knockout profile and glucose and lactate 
metabolism profile was not found. A previous study indicated that mir-H directly targets 
phosphofructokinase liver type (PFKL), a key rate-limiting enzyme in glycolytic pathway, and 
inhibits AKT phosphorylation in human lung cancer cells; overexpression of mir-H leads to 
reduced glucose consumption and lactate production (53). In the present study, although 
specific glucose consumption rate and specific lactate production rate of mir-H_P-KO exceeded 
the rates of REF, they were not exceeded the rates of mock cell lines. Therefore, it needs to be 
further investigate whether mir-H have influence on glycolytic pathway of CHO cells. The 
specific glutamine consumption rate and specific ammonium production rate of mir-H_P-KO 
was the lowest among all the tested cell lines, indicating that knockout of mir-H may influence 
glutaminolysis. The relation between mir-H and glutaminolysis needs to be further investigated. 
It was seen that mir-H_P-KO presented desired EPO titer profile, as overall EPO titer along the 
7-day cultivation period was higher than both mock cell lines and REF cell lines, and specific 
EPO titer was higher than REF value but lower than mock cell lines’ values. Therefore, EPO 
production may be facilitated by mir-H knockout, and the detailed mechanism needs to be 
further investigated.  
5.3 mir-D2 may be Involved in CHO Cell Growth and Metabolism 
Regulation. 
In the present study, the PCR result showed that mir-D2 was knocked out from the genome of 
CHO-DG44 EPO-producer cell line, however, the mir-D2 expression level did not decrease. 
==PAGE:74==
63 
 
The contradictory result may either result from imprecision of knockout verification method by 
PCR, or result from deviation of RT-qPCR. Therefore, it needs to be further investigated. 
The specific growth rate of mir-D2_F-KO was 0.015 h-1, which was comparatively low among 
all the tested cell lines. Considering that mir-D2 expression level was not reduced, the growth 
may be negatively influenced by the cell line development process rather than the regulation of 
mir-D2. It was noticed that mir-D2_F-KO cell line had high specific glutamine and glucose 
consumption rates and high ammonium production rate among all the tested cell lines. The 
reason that resulted in the change needs to be clarified by obtaining more experimental data. 
Some previous studies indicated that mir-D2 may regulate cell growth by downregulating 
BCL9 or modulating ubiquitin pathway (54–56). Therefore, it is worthy to consider mir-D2 as 
a target for CHO cell line development research. 
5.4 An Optimized Single-cell Cloning Medium Formula is Essential 
for Efficient Cell Line Development. 
In the present study, an optimized single-cell cloning medium formula was used for single-cell 
cloning process. This medium was based on EX-CELL CHO cloning medium, and 4 mM of L-
glutamine, 0.2% of anti-clumping agent, 1× of antioxidant supplement, 100 units/L of 
potassium penicillin, 100 µg/L of streptomycin, 2 g/L of recombinant human serum albumin, 5 
mg/L of recombinant human transferrin, and 1× of HEPES (4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-
piperazineethanesulfonic acid). 
The experiment result demonstrated that this optimized medium efficiently facilitated the 
single-cell cloning process for post-transfection knockout cells, as those cells failed to survive 
in the PowerCHO-2-based culture that used for routine culture in this study. In cell culture 
research, conditioned medium was usually used for single-cell cloning, since it contained some 
signal peptides which may facilitate cell survivability and growth (57, 58). However, 
conditioned medium has less nutrient, and may not be sufficient to support cell growth. It has 
been reported that recombinant human albumin can facilitate single-cell cloning of CHO cells 
in chemically-defined media (59). Transferrin is the iron transport protein in serum, when using 
in cell culture, transferrin has the effect of facilitating extracellular iron storage and transport, 
and it also acts as an important extracellular antioxidant (60). In the present study, both 
recombinant human albumin and transferrin were added to the single-cell cloning medium, and 
it exhibited the desired facilitation performance for cell survivability and growth. It is worthy 
to further optimize the addition percentage based on the current formula.

6 Conclusion and Future Perspective 
In this study, in order to improve the erythropoietin production of CHO-DG44 cell line, eleven 
miRNAs, which may influence cell growth, metabolism or some other effect, were knocked out 
from the genome of a CHO-DG44 EPO-producer cell line, by using CRISPR/Cas9 technique. 
The post-transfection cell pools were subjected to single-cell cloning and clone expansion. four 
mutant cell lines were finally established, including one with mir-A1 complete knockout, one 
with mir-A1 partial knockout, one with mir-D2 complete knockout and one with mir-H partial 
knockout. 
According to the batch culture result, knockout of mir-A1 may facilitate the cell metabolism, 
and dramatically increase specific EPO titer. Knockout of mir-H facilitate cell growth and 
seems to have positive effect on increasing EPO production. mir-D2 may have effect on CHO 
cell growth and metabolism, but it needs to be confirm by carrying further research. 
An optimized single-cell cloning medium was used and proved to be effective for improving 
efficiency of single-cell cloning process. 
This study paves the way for further research regarding utilizing miRNA to regulate CHO cell 
growth and improving protein productivity. The further research can be carried out in two 
direction: a) Applying miRNA knockout technique on CHO-DG44 cell line without EPO 
transgene, in order to characterize the regulation mechanism of targeted miRNA without 
interference of EPO. Another direction is scale-up the currently available mutant cell line to 
bioreactor scale, either for batch culture or fed-batch culture; as some of the cell lines have 
presented improved EPO titer, it is worthy to conduct the scale-up experiment.
