Summary
The goal of the thesis is to investigate if prognostic factors can be found in
blood sample results, which have been ordered by medical practitioners prior to
diagnosis. We investigate an event free survival for 545 patients diagnosed with
Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia, in a timeframe of two years. An event was
dened as one of the following: Patient has received treatment, is dead or both
in a timeframe of two years.
We applied four dierent supervised learning models, trained on engineered
features from historic blood samples. Four dierent datasets were constructed
and benchmarked against prognostic factors used in the prognostic model CLL-
IPI. As an attempt to improve performance of models we combine prognostic
factors from CLL-IPI and the created features.
Promising indication of prognostic values were found, and the results suggest
that further research should be conducted.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Machine learning (ML) is as popular as it has ever been. Because of the rapid
improvement of computers and development of open source technology, almost
everyone has the opportunity to do complex calculations from their laptops.
This has lead to an ever-growing interest in this eld. In fact according to the
Skill Up Survey 2016[24] ML is the most popular research topic in computer
science at the moment.
ML algorithms are applied in almost all industries. Take for instance Google
search results, Tesla self driving cars, Facebook facial recognition or even the
fully automatic store Amazon Go, all of them are utilising powerful ML algo-
rithms. Furthermore, it is commonly used in clinical terms in the pursuit of
discovering new prognostic factors.
The clinical course of patients diagnosed with Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia
(CLL) is highly challenging to predict. Some patients will never require treat-
ment whereas others are progressing quickly after diagnosis.
This thesis in-
vestigates if pre-diagnostics data can be used for predicting clinical outcomes of
patients diagnosed with CLL. We will use supervised learning to predict whether
a patient will need attention within two years after diagnosis or not.

1.1
Related Work
1.1.1
Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL)
Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL) is a type of cancer of the white blood
cells and is most commonly found in adults. The patients diagnosed with CLL
have a median age at approximately 70 years. In addition, CLL is not found in
children and it is rarely found before the age of 40. It is also known that males
are diagnosed more frequently than females. In Denmark, about 400 people are
diagnosed with CLL every year, i.e 6-7 / 100,000 people / year.[6]
Because of the heterogeneity, there has been an immense research in which pa-
tients will require treatment and when. This has led to new prognostic markers
in the last three decades. These prognostic factors have been combined as a
single score called the International Prognostic Index (CLL-IPI).
1.1.2
Machine Learning using prediagnostics
Recent studies by Enrique J. deAndrés-Galiana et al. [11] has shown promising
results in the prediction of chemotherapy treatments and the development of
autoimmune diseases. This was done by using blood-sample data along with
CLL-specic markers.
The leading prognostic model is however the recently developed CLL-IPI.
1.1.3
CLL - International Prognostic index (CLL-IPI)
The CLL-IPI is a prognostic model[1] which sums up a patient's condition using
a simple scoring system. The model also provides treatment recommendations.
CLL-IPI was developed based on an analysis of 26 prognostic factors and 3,472
CLL patients in both early and advanced CLL stages. The six most important
prognostic factors are used to determine an overall score.

details.

score of the individual patient.

1.2
Project statement
The clinical course of CLL patients is highly heterogeneous and it is dicult
to predict.
By analysing blood samples collected prior to diagnosis date, it
is possible to discover prognostic factors, thus enabling prediction of clinical
outcome.
As the disease evolves over several years, it is believed that pre-
diagnostic blood samples can reect early disease events.
The project focuses on applying various Machine Learning models on blood sam-
ple data analysed prior to diagnosis as well as diagnostics data. Since the data
is, to some degree, unstructured, a fundamental part will consist of structuring
datasets, i.e. selecting relevant data to pull from CopLab database as well as
==PAGE:16==

preparing, cleaning and ltering data. Moreover, it is suspected that the data
contains a great deal of missing values and methods for assessing these will be
crucial for model outcomes.
The expected outcome of the project is to prepare data, construct features and
apply it to various machine learning algorithms in order to determine if pre-
diagnostic bloodsample data holds prognostic value. A visualisation tool for
presenting the dataset will be developed. Furthermore, a conference publication,
in collaboration with partners at Rigshospitalet is expected.
1.3
Scope and Limitation
The purpose of the study was to investigate if results from blood tests, taken
prior to diagnosis, hold any predictive power. It was carried out using supervised
learning for predicting a binary class.
Multiple ML models were tested and
the best few models were optimised and ultimately combined using a voting
classier.
As a basis for comparison, models were also trained on diagnostics data. Diag-
nostics data holds information which are used in CLL-IPI and should therefore
give an estimate of what can be expected.
A barrier to this project is the fact that the data is considered to be sensitive i.e.
not to be exported from an encrypted drive. Meaning that high performance
cloud computing was not an option when performing model tuning. This also
meant that the visualisation tool had to be developed using, to some extent,
dummy data.
In order to encourage discovery of unknown prognostic factors, minimal biolog-
ical knowledge was included in the feature engineering process.
For simplication reasons, it was decided to address event free survival limited
by two years where an event was dened as one of the following:
• Is dead before two years
• Has received treatment before two years.
• Has received treatment and is dead before two years.

Chapter 2
Materials and Methods
Bloodsample data was provided by Copenhagen Primary Care Database (CopLab)
[2]. CopLab is an institution that has gathered the majority of laboratory tests,
which were ordered by medical practitioners in Copenhagen from the year 2000
and onwards.
CLL markers and patient details were provided by Danish National CLL registry
[9]. The registry aims to work as a quality assurance of diagnosis and care of
patients diagnosed with CLL. The registry includes all patients diagnosed with
CLL in Denmark from 2008 and onwards.
This chapter describes the data used and how it was processed. Explanation is
given for which and how features were created. We also briey go over methods
for the supervised learning.
2.1
Data Description
The data contained a combination of pre-diagnostics and diagnostics data for a
total of 944 patients. Diagnostics data is information which has been registered
at diagnosis, as well as biological factors which were used for creating target
==PAGE:18==

classes. Pre-diagnostics data is results from blood tests registered over time
prior to diagnosis.
Pre diagnostics data had been gathered from medical practitioners. Hence, the
number of analysed blood tests, types of tests and time between tests varies for
each individual patient, i.e. the data was sparse.
Apart from that, CopLab also provided an overview of their database, showing
total number of blood tests for patients diagnosed with CLL and information
about methodology changes.
2.1.1
Blood Test Results
Types of blood tests were selected based on the total number of available results,
and not whether it had a known relation to the disease. A complete list of the
types of blood tests initially provided for this project can be seen in Appendix
E. This process left us with some auxiliary tests and some types were found to
have none or a very low number of valid results - these were omitted.
2.1.1.1
Validating Blood Test Results
Validating the blood tests is an important part of the process, as extracted
features can otherwise become inaccurate. In this section we go through the
steps in the validation process.
Examples are used in order to ease up the
explanations.
Each timespan for every type of blood test was carefully investigated before
combined. The validation procedure was meant to detect sudden value changes
and other abnormalities. Additionally, it detected how many unknowns had
been registered. By unknowns is meant one of the following indicators: "!B",
"Bemrk", "Mislyk", "!M", "M", "!A", "Afbes", "!U", ">", "<". "!B" and "Be-
mrk" indicators for "notice", these were used for creating counting attributes.
Results marked with a less than or greater than sign were deleted if the amount
was not considerably large, otherwise the sign was removed. So for instance <0.2
would become 0.2. Results marked with "Mislyk", "!M", "M", "!A", "Afbes",
"!U" were discarded. A detailed description of each indicator for the unknowns
is given in Appendix B

Feature engineering is in many cases crucial for machine learning outcomes and
is often considered to be the most important part. [13] It refers to the process

a prerequisite) about the data, in order to create new features for the ML
models. The process is both time consuming and cumbersome as it requires
brainstorming, implementation and testing. Feature engineering is a repetitive
process and was carried out using the following procedure:
1. Brainstorming/idea generation.
2. Determine which features to create.

4. Check feature performance.

Slope Binning
Slope binning feature was supposed to capture development through time in as
single descriptive values. All slopes for a patient were binned, and ultimately
compared with the total population, giving a proportional factor for each bin.
First, all possible combinations between two points (measurements/results) across
the full dataset and were stored as sets (X1, Y1, X2,Y2).
There are two main requirements for two points to be stored as a combination.
One is that, measurements taken on the same day can not be combined, as
this would yield an innite slope. Second, is that combination are emitted in
lexicographic sort order (left to right), meaning that a slope does not have a
correlating opposite slope. Apart from that, all results which are non numerical
have to be removed.
For all combinations, we calculate the slope between each point. A single slope
was calculated using number of days before diagnosis time as X, giving:

Area proportion represents the proportion of area under the curve for a blood

machine which is performing the analysis is indeed able to measure the high
value thus (ymax) will be used as reference point.

2.3.1
Missing Values
Scikit has three methods for imputing missing values inside a pipeline: mean,
median and Most Frequent. Most frequent performed poorly in every pretest
and was discarded from the imputing strategies. It was eventually decided to
stick with mean imputation, instead of making it a tuning parameter. This was
done for consistency.
2.3.2
Scaling
A MinMax scaler was used for standardizing the data. MinMaxScaler scales
each feature individually so all observations are in a given range. The range was
set to be between 0 and 1.
2.3.3
Feature Extraction
Principal Component analysis (PCA) was applied for reducing dimensions. PCA
is a dimensionality reduction procedure which transform the features, making it
possible to describe the variance of the dataset in fewer features.[4] As reference
for this choice, it has been shown that minimum 10 events per variable is viable
way to calculate number of features for linear regression.[26]. This project has
192 events (Class 1) meaning that minium 19 features. As PCA transforms
features, it was decided that 10 components were adequate.

2.3.4
Classier Selection
According to Scikit [23], the recommended classier for a dataset of this size
is support vector classier (SVC) or K-nearest neighbor (KNN). If the SVC or
KNN are not performing, it is recommended to use ensemble methods. Classi-
ers used for nal testing were: SVC, Random Forest (RF), AdaBoost as well
as a voting classier.
This section gives a concise explanation of each of these classier.
Support vector machine: A support vector machine (SVM) creates a sepa-
rating hyperplane of which is considered to be best split of labelled data. The
SVM was originally characterized using linear hyperplanes. The kernel exten-
sion allows non-linear hyperplanes. [8] A SVC with a radial basis function (rbf)
kernel was applied. Hyper parameter γ (gamma) was set to search in the do-
main: [0.0001,0.001,0.01,0.1], and C in range from 1 to 1000 in steps of 50.
Random Forest: Random Forest is an ensemble method, which uses many
decision trees to vote for a label. The output label is the mode of all votes.
[5] The number of trees in forest can be tuned, a higher number of trees can
typically give a better performance. For hyperparameters, we tuned number of
trees using a search range from 10 to 100 in steps of 5. Min samples per leaf
was also tuned using a search range from 20 to 50 in steps of 5. If the leafs are
too small, the model is prone to capture noise.
AdaBoost AdaBoost is an ensemble method which is a combination of many
weak predictors. The weight for each misclassied point is increased for each it-
eration. Similarly the weights are decreased for correctly classied observations.
Thus, the next predictor focuses more on getting the misclassied observations
right.
Voting A voting classiers combines the output of multiple classiers and makes
a majority vote. This helps balancing out the weaknesses of each classier. We
use the previous three classier, and make a majority vote on output.
The
voting classier tunes all model parameters combined, which is very time con-
suming, as the search grid quickly becomes wide. Therefore it was decided to
use a small search grid for each classier. RF: Min leaf sample = 25 and Num-
ber of estimators = [10,50,100] SVC: C=[1,10,100] and gamma = [0.01,0.001].
AdaBoost: Number of estimators = [10,20]

2.3.5
Cross Validation
Stratied K-fold cross validation was used. K-fold CV partitions data into K
folds and rotates over the folds so every fold works as test set once, and is in the
training set for the rest of the rotations. Stratied, means that all folds includes
roughly the same amount of classes. [14]
2.3.6
Model Tuning
Model hyper-parameters were tuned using gridsearch. Gridsearch uses brute
force to nd the best combinations of hyperparameters from a given range.
First a wide search range with relatively big steps was set in order to get a
rough estimation of an acceptable range. The scope was then narrowed down
and fed into the model. [3]
2.3.7
Target Classes
A binary target class was generated using 2-bits each representing an event,
yielding four classes 00,01,10,11. For simplicity reasons and considering the class
imbalance, a one vs all target class was used for prediction. (00 vs 01+10+11).
• Bit-1: Is alive after 730 Kaplan Meier days (KM-days)
• Bit-2: Has received treatment before 730 KM 730.
The four classes are listed here.
• 00: Only diagnosed with CLL (353 patients)
• 01: Is dead before 2 years without receiving treatment. (31)
• 10: Has received treatment and is still alive after 2 years. (139)
• 11: Has received treatment and is dead before 2 years. (22)

Chapter 4
Discussion
The results indicate that pre-diagnostics data hold prognostic value. Consider-
ing results shown in Prediction of EFS (Section 3.1), we see that all ML models
are performing signicantly better than random guessing. The margin is not as
big for all models, but it proves the point.
Preprocessing: Removing less than and greater than sign might be an error
as they can be considered to be outliers. But in the end these were not included
in the nal testing. Imputing missing values by using mean could have been
done dierently, by clustering patients and imputing based on patients in the
clusters
PCA is capturing a good portion of variance in few components (Figure 3.2).
But as seen, classes are scattered and mixed, which is making linear decision
boundary dicult. This might also explain why AdaBoost is generally perform-
ing worse than the rest. Figure 3.3 shows that using 10 principal components
was an acceptable choice. Also, taking the size of dataset into consideration, it
seems to be sucient as too many features can easily overt.
Feature engineering: The results showed a low number of samples per patient.
As seen in table 3.4 Support rate for samples per patient is greatly decreasing
if minimum samples is slightly increased. This makes it challenging to engineer
robust features. Without generating many missing values. A prerequisite to this
==PAGE:46==
34
Discussion
project was to include minimal biological knowledge when creating features. To
some extent it is believed that better features could be engineered if for instance
reference/interval for the blood test were taken in to consideration. To overcome
potential outliers in the data, all features could be turned in to binary values
representing higher or lower than the total mean. An other approach could be
creating categorical variables.
Targets: As for target classes, there are some biases which can occur. It is
dicult to determine if a patient's death is caused by CLL as it is not regis-
tered. For example, a patient was randomly found to have a wrongly registered
diagnosis date. This indicates that errors were present in the diagnostics data
and this has to be validated, but that was out of the scope of this project.
Furthermore, it can be useful to use other targets. It is not denitive that the
data is best at targeting EFS as described in this project. A two year limit
was decided based on how much information would be lost. Setting the time
limit to 2 years, means that minimum of 2 years of follow up data was required.
This rules out some patients and many blood test results. But more and more
patients will naturally be added to the database.
Much time was spent on manual tuning of model hyper-parameters as an at-
tempt of raising performance. Unfortunately without success of any signicant
dierence. The voting classier which was a combination of the other three
classiers seemed to be the best bet. However, the voting classier tunes hyper-
parameters of all models individually, which is extremely time consuming, there-
fore we had to settle on manually choosing a few hyperparameters to search in.
The code for processing data, was developed with the goal to make it easy to
adjust for errors which occurred due to methodology. But more importantly it
is now easy to engineer new features and construct new datasets. This will help
in future studies.
==PAGE:47==
Chapter 5
Conclusion
We have processed data, engineered features, constructed datasets and ulti-
mately tested the data for predictive value using four dierent supervised learn-
ing methods. The results look promising as pre-diagnostics showed performance
which was signicantly better than random guessing. But performed worse than
already known prognostic factors. This indicates that there is hidden knowledge
which is yet to be discovered.
Combining pre-diagnostics with diagnostics data did not improve performance.
Thus the performance is not good enough to draw any concrete conclusion about
the value of such data, at least for investigated prediction problem.
This study was one of the rst project using these exact pre-diagnostics data
from CopLab for Machine Learning purposes and the results suggest that more
research has to be done in order to quantify the results. It is suggested use do-
main knowledge for creating new features as it is believed to enhance prediction
power. Also, applying unsupervised learning for discovering clusters of patients
is recommended, as it was dicult to resolve features to use for prediction tar-
gets. The clusters can then be labelled and predicted upon.
People are diagnosed with cancer every day and discovering patterns in blood
samples can eventually help to distinguish fast progressing CLLs from the rest
and thus being able to treat patients in time with the right treatment.

Appendix A
CLL-IPI Markers
A patients' clinical stage is determined by the previous traditional staging
systems "Rai staging system" or "Binet Staging System". Binet is for the most
part used in Europe, whereas Rai is often used in the United States. These
systems were developed about 30 years ago and have been used as information
for how far a cancer has spread in a patient.
The ve stages in the Rai system are described as following: [22]
Rai stage 0: Lymphocytosis and no enlargement of the lymph nodes, spleen,
or liver, and with near normal red blood cell and platelet counts.
Rai stage I: Lymphocytosis plus enlarged lymph nodes. The spleen and liver
are not enlarged and the red blood cell and platelet counts are near normal.
Rai stage II: Lymphocytosis plus an enlarged spleen (and possibly an enlarged
liver), with or without enlarged lymph nodes. The red blood cell and platelet
counts are near normal.
Rai stage III: Lymphocytosis plus anemia (too few red blood cells), with or
without enlarged lymph nodes, spleen, or liver. Platelet counts are near normal.
Rai stage IV: Lymphocytosis plus thrombocytopenia (too few blood platelets),
==PAGE:50==

with or without anemia, enlarged lymph nodes, spleen, or liver.
The Binet staging system has three stages, and are described as following:[22]
Binet stage A: Fewer than 3 areas of lymphoid tissue are enlarged, with no
anemia or thrombocytopenia.
Binet stage B: 3 or more areas of lymphoid tissue are enlarged, with no anemia
or thrombocytopenia.
Binet stage C: Anemia and/or thrombocytopenia are present.
Del(17)p: Chromosome 17 is formed as an X with a smaller upper part referred
to as p or the p-arm. Del(17)p means that there is a deletion in the upper part
of chromosome 17.
TP53: is a gene that is responsible for making protein p53 which acts as a
tumour suppressor. The protein regulates cell division and keeps them from
growing in an uncontrolled way. TP53 is located on p-arm of chromosome 17.
IGHV mutation status: Refers to the mutation of the immunoglobulin heavy
chain genes. CLLs with mutated or unmutated IGHV are known to display a
dierence in prognosis. [10]
B2M level, mg/L: Elevated levels of serum β2 −Microglobulin (B2M) are

has been shown multiple times to be a good prognostic marker for the progres-
sion free survival as well as overall survival for CLL. [17] [15]
