Abstract 
This thesis studies the contemporary case of Project University Hospital Køge (UHK). The project aims 
at expanding a current hospital to a larger and modern hospital that can perform all needed types of 
treatments in Region Zealand, Denmark. The project is one of 16 in Denmark, funded by the government. 
Unlike other large infrastructure projects, the hospital projects operate within a fixed cost ceiling and 
scope requirements, and any violations of these requirements are not tolerated. These requirements 
have proven to be difficult to satisfy, and interesting events have happened as a result. As of UHK, they 
had to fire their full-service consultant late into the planning phase, which is rare for these kind of 
projects. Further they are currently 3 years delayed, the most delayed of all 16 government funded 
hospital project. Some of the effects of the government requirements is explored further in this thesis. 
Specifically, a focus has been placed on the development of the project cost and scope, and on developing 
quantitative methods for measuring the developing of the project uncertainty. As the project is 
contemporary and data is limited, focused is placed on developing, testing and applying new and 
pragmatic methods. 
A long with the methods applied, it was through the case study found that while the government 
requirements had a large impact on the project course, they also ensured that the project economy did 
not go of track. They provided opportunities for innovative solutions, and for the project to perform 
design iterations in order to ensure the fulfillment of both scope and cost.

1. INTRODUCTION 
In this section the thesis case and research question will be introduced. The scope of the thesis and 
assumptions made will then be explained to set the delimitation. In the next section the approach to the 
thesis will be expanded upon further. 
1.1 Introduction 
Project University Hospital Køge (UHK) aims at expanding the current Køge Sygehus into a larger 
hospital that that can cater to all treatment options in Region Zealand, Denmark. The project owner team 
hired a full-service consultant through a tender competition. The UHK team and the full-service 
consultant collaborated on developing the design, plan and project requirements to detail in the design 
phase. However, unexpectedly the full-service consultant found that it was not possible to fulfill the 
project scope within the budget and required UHK to either expand the budget by about 350 mil. kr. or 
reduce the scope by about 15%. UHK refused, as both budget and scope requirements are dictated by 
the government and are not negotiable. As a result, the contract between them was cancelled, and UHK 
decided to finalize the project requirements without the full-service consultant thus putting the project 
out to tender as a turnkey contract instead. They were able to receive satisfying offers within the scope 
and budget and choose a winner, and hence proved that the assessment of the full-service consultant 
was flawed. 
UHK is only one large hospital project in Denmark out of many. In 2007 the Danish government made a 
new hospital plan for the country, that aimed at centralizing all hospital activities into larger hospitals 
by 2025 (SUM, 2017). Therefore, they created a large hospital fund (DKK 25 bn PL07), where the 
countries five regions could apply for partial funding (60%) for either creating new large hospitals or 
enlarge existing ones. This resulted in 16 hospital projects, where UHK is one of them (DKK 4 bn PL09). 
While most of the hospitals experienced time delays, none of them other than UHK had to cancel a major 
consultancy contract due to large budget overruns. This makes the example of UHK specifically 
interesting. A major reason as to why the hospitals were experiencing project delays, was the 
governments strict requirement that no cost overruns were allowed, and the hospitals had to offer all 
treatments options for the region. These requirements leave little flexibility in the projects both to cost 
and scope, making time the only variable. It is for the very same reason that UHK had to refuse their full-
service consultant’s revised offer of either enlarging the projects budget or reducing its scope (which 
would reduce the number and type of treatments). While the contract cancellation became a significant 
uncertainty factor for the projects future, the primary impact was further delayed in the project 
schedule. This was not considered a critical problem, as focus is placed on fulfilling the cost and scope 
requirements. However, delays do have a significant economic impact on Region Zealand’s budget, so 
the experience of UHK was far from unproblematic. 
In this thesis the experiences of UHK is examined further. Specifically, how the cost and scope 
requirements impact the project. This is interesting because little or no experience with large public 
infrastructure projects working under such requirements exist in Denmark and elsewhere. The case of 
the 16 hospital projects therefore becomes the first experiences of working under such conditions in 
the country. It is interesting to explorer whether large projects could still flourish under these 
conditions, and what benefits and disadvantages they could have. This thesis will look at specific 
impacts, mainly on the development of UHK’s cost and scope throughout the design process, and the 
consequences of the strict cost scope requirements. This will help us understand some of the dynamics 
that emerge from these conditions and how a project might benefit from them.

1.2 Research Question 
The master thesis research question is as follows: 
“How did UHK’s cost and scope evolve through the design process of the project? Which consequences 
emerged in the process as a result of the outlined cost ceiling and fixed requirements?” 
The first question enables the development of specific methods within this thesis that will allow us to 
quantify some of the data collected from the project. These methods aim at mapping the development 
of key economic indicators of the projects, as well as how the detailing process of the project evolved 
through time. 
The second sentence then focuses on a quantitative analysis of the data, and how the results links back 
to the cost and scope requirements of the project. 
1.2.1 Scope 
To delimit the thesis further, this section details the scope of the thesis in relation to the research 
question. 
The thesis will be based on a single case study of Project University Hospital Køge. This limits the 
application of its findings onto other projects and cases. This demarcation will therefore also exclude 
the other concurrent Danish hospital projects; each of which had their own experience with working 
under the government-set conditions. 
As the hospital project is ongoing and is many years from closure, the thesis can only study a limited 
period of the project, namely, from its beginning in 2013 until June 2018. This period includes the 
planning of the project itself where after main construction will begin. This thesis therefore, is not able 
to track the project to its end, and beyond. 
The thesis is based on quantitative analysis of the project data, focused on economic and risk reports, as 
well as the measurement of the project work outcome in the form of drawings, calculations, and 
documents that contributes to the progress of the project planning phase. Extensive qualitative analyses 
of the project details are therefore not possible, and instead the thesis will rely on exploiting the full 
data pool available. 
The thesis develops new specific methods for the data analysis of the projects progress. Its objectives 
are therefore centered on developing and testing the methods, rather than offering the most 
comprehensive or exact data analysis. Instead it is forwarded to the research community for further 
testing and development. 
1.2.2 Learning objectives 
As an addition to the research question a number of learning objectives are defined to support the 
learning process of the thesis. This thesis will define 3 – 5 objectives that are neither to generic nor to 
specific. The defined learning objectives are the following. 
•

•

2. Methodology 
The methodology of this thesis is bound to some key elements. It is considered to be of utmost 
importance to conduct the thesis while the project is ongoing, relevant and highly accessible. Further it 
has been relatively easy to reach, as it is a public project subject to publicity requirements. The fact that 
it is ongoing, also made it more accessible as the project stakeholders are currently dedicated to it. 
It is important to stress that the methods developed in this thesis therefore are specifically suitable for 
pragmatic and applicable analysis of ongoing projects, rather than being restricted to projects with its 
full span and data pool available. 
With these points in mind, the general approach of the thesis is described first, followed by the 
description of the specific methods applied. 
2.1 Approach 
In this sub-section the general approach of the thesis is outlined. Further the motivation behind it is 
explained. 
The thesis is inspired by a Ph.D. research project on project robustness and resilience, and have been 
managed as an independent sub-branch of the research project. It is based on data provided by 
University Hospital Køge, and historical documentation that are publicly available through Region 
Zealand. Several explorative and confirmative interviews were conducted to support the analysis and 
interpretation of the data collected and case study developed. The process has been centered around 
the development of the case study and the new methods used for the quantitative analysis. Supportive 
academic literature has simultaneously been chosen and applied to support the case and analysis 
development process. 
The development process of the thesis consists of multiple steps described in the following. 
First the subject of interest was chosen, this was based on the thesis background in the Ph.D. research 
project. Then a collaboration agreement with UHK was made and the development of the case study 
began. It was mainly based on the publicly available historical documentation of the project and on 
explorative interviews with a UHK contact person. 
The case study lead to the formulation of the research question, which was motivated by the initial 
project subject and by the Ph.D. study. Then, the main hypotheses of this thesis were fully developed 
and consulted through further interviews with UHK.  
The hypotheses lead to the development of the data analysis methods, which were both motivated and 
limited by the case data available. The data was provided by UHK for testing and further development 
of the methods. Data analysis was conducted in consistency with the hypotheses and underlying 
theories. This was an iterative process were data deficiencies was addressed when possible. 
Finally, the analysis is discussed including its key strengths, weaknesses, and potentials. 
2.2 Method 
Case study 
The case study is conducted as a single case study; as the research question is a how-question, the study 
does not require control of behavioral events, and it focuses on contemporary events (Yin, 2014). The 
methodology allows us to dig deeper into the case within limited time and resources, at the expense of 
the ability to generalize learnings. However, the single case study allows us to conduct analytical 
generalizations, which is highly relevant for the purpose of developing case specific analytical methods, 
which can then be developed and verified by others and thereby result in statistical generalizations 
based on multiple case studies (Yin, 2014).

Flyvbjerg argues strongly for the use of case studies, as opposed to only benefitting from purely 
quantitative research. In that he also rejects that no generalizations can be made from case studies, that 
they cannot be used to verify hypotheses, and that they are inherently biased (Flyvbjerg, 2006). This is 
further justification for the choice of method.

The literature search was centered around the methodology development and conducted

subject was available, no additional studies relevant to include was found. This also confirmed the

The data processing is described in detail in chapter 5. 
Interviews 
The interviews were conducted as explorative interviews (Brinkmann & Kvale, 2015). This means that

3. Theory 
Systems Engineering 
According to De Weck et al., an engineering system is characterized by being large, advanced, complex, 
and within a larger system. It is not developed in a top-down manner, rather it evolves without the 
possibility of mapping out the process beforehand. Instead it evolves through iterations until it reaches 
its final form (De Weck et al., 2011). 
An important difference between system design and engineering design is that previously, focus was on 
designing single components. These, then, had to be fitted into a larger system. On the other hand 
systems design undertakes a holistic approach and the single components are designed according to the 
systems that they are to operate within (De Weck et al., 2011). 
Today infrastructure projects, such as the construction of new hospitals, can be considered engineering 
systems. They are systems operating within larger systems, e.g. in a network of hospitals as part of 
regional and national health care plans. They are composed of many systems, including IT systems, 
infrastructure, logistics, medical treatment specializations, existing hospital facilities, etc. 
Complexity 
Because every component is part of a larger system, problems cannot be isolated. Instead they will affect 
multiple other components that they are linked with and so on. This is the complexity factor, that allows 
problems to develop in unforeseen ways. In complex systems it is therefore almost impossible to predict 
how impacts on single components will affect the larger system (Williams, 2017). This could lead to 
small problems escalating to critical stages, a so-called snowball effect. 
In relation to the case at hand, this has important implications. Risk and uncertainty cannot simply be 
measured and mitigated linearly. They can activate other risks and escalate exponentially in unforeseen 
ways. Therefore, one must be careful in the project risk work, which is highly needed for projects of this 
stature. 
Megaprojects 
Flyvbjerg et al. has done extensive research into megaprojects e.g.: public infrastructure projects, the 
Olympic Games, and has documented constant cost overruns and time delays. He argues that a main 
factor for cost overruns is optimism bias from project owners, shareholders, and stakeholders. This is 
largely because of inexpedient incitement structures, that motivates people to lie about their projects. 
Instead of being a question of putting forward the most accurate and realistic project budgets and 
schedules, it becomes a question of playing a game to secure funding. This search for funding could be 
incentivized by political prestige projects, high status stakeholder projects etc.; all of whom want their 
brand or name on a particular end project/product (Flyvbjerg et al., 2003). 
Another reason for continuous project problems is that projects keep getting more complex. They 
include new types of IT and technology that are often on the cutting edge. Projects therefore increasingly 
carry the characteristics of R&D projects. Whereas previously, they would be based on best practices 
and well tested methods. This tendency adds to the unpredictability of projects (Flyvbjerg et al., 2003). 
Flyvbjerg et al. points towards solutions such as better planning and project ownership. In other words; 
place the responsibility of planning, executing, and running the hospitals on the end users (Flyvbjerg et 
al., 2003). 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
Relating this to the case at hand, several points can be made. One is that there is a main difference 
between the hospital projects and the megaprojects that Flyvbjerg studied, which is the fixed budget 
ceiling. This changes the incitement structure greatly towards a more realistic approach, where no gains 
can be obtained by over optimistic budgets. 
Secondly, the hospital projects also have significant R&D like components. They need to include cutting 
edge equipment and technology to stay future proof, this includes IT, logistics, treatment equipment, 
technical solutions, etc. Further they are all executed simultaneously which confines the possibility to 
share experiences across the projects. 
Regarding better planning and project ownership as possible solutions, this have been seen greatly 
implemented in the Danish hospital plan. Better planning has been a key effect of the cost ceiling, to 
ensure that no unpleasant surprises turn up late into the project execution. 
Rather than executing the projects themselves, the government has placed the responsibility on the 
regions which will eventually operate the hospitals. This increases incitement to deliver the best 
projects possible in order to prevent disasters, economic crises, and losses of reputation. In the case at 
hand high end user ownership can be seen as the project director is also a vice president for the hospital 
and will be directly responsible for its functioning after handover. 
The project iron triangle 
In the project management literature, the project iron triangle is found, which depicts the three main 
variables that any project must balance, and which is often in conflict. Cost, time, and scope. The success 
of this will directly affect the project quality. It is generally held that only one of the three variables can 
be retained, while the other two will have be compromised when the project is under pressure (Larson 
& Gray, 2015). For example, if project cost is to be retained, and the expected cost goes over budget, one 
must mitigate the cost overrun by either reducing the scope or if possible and effective, delay the 
completion date. While in most cases delays can be prevented by increasing work efforts and thereby 
expenditure, it is not always possible to save on expenses by extending the schedule. It could be that the 
project is already running at slowest possible pace, or simply that slowing down the project would not 
result in any cost savings. If scope is considered simply a to-do list of project deliveries, one would 
simply cancel deliveries to reduce cost. On the other hand, if scope was retained, a conflict would usually 
lead to increased expenditure to maintain the scope. 
In relation to University Hospital Køge and the Danish hospital plan, both cost and scope is to be secured. 
This leaves little flexibility as only the third variable time can be altered. Given sufficient pressure and 
the secured variables will bust, it therefore becomes a key priority to maintain the balance of the project 
iron triangle. This have lead to special focus on project optimization whenever setbacks have been 
encountered. It could however be argued that to much optimization and work around solutions would 
decrease the project quality significantly. 
Exactly how this conflict is handled and whether the hospital projects succeeds in maintaining their cost 
and scope, could lead to cutting edge experiences with large infrastructure projects experiences. An 
important addition to Flyvbjerg et al. megaproject studies. 
Uncertainty development 
There is a strong relationship between the development of project knowledge, project uncertainty, and 
project commitment. The relationship is that for the project to progress commitment must be made, as 
the project is progressing, knowledge is gained and as such uncertainty is reduced. This introduces a 
paradox as commitment must be made when uncertainty is high for the project to progress and 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
uncertainty to decrease, while it is undesirable to commit oneself to a project with high uncertainty 
(Wright, 1936). This is a paradox experienced by every large and complex project. 
Commitment can be understood as irreversible decisions and expenditure, which can therefore be 
estimated by project cost and budgeting. Uncertainty can be understood as risk level; perceived 
uncertainty can therefore be estimated by project risk assessments and budget buffering. True 
uncertainty however, cannot be calculated, as it is by nature unpredictable, especially for complex 
projects. 
This relationship creates a framework for understanding and analyzing the case at hand, and specifically 
how its cost ceiling affects the paradox and its impact on the development of the project. 
A model can be setup depicting the relationship in the context of UHK.

Deep uncertainty 
Lempert categorizes uncertainty as either mechanical or novel (Lempert, 2003). Mechanical uncertainty 
can be calculated and exists within closed and controlled systems (Lempert, 2003). An example is an oil 
refinery, where the transportation and transformation of the oil is conducted in a closed mechanical 
system. The uncertainty is then directly related to the quality and possible failure of each mechanical 
process. It can therefore be minimized greatly by controlling and developing each component. It can also 
be estimated to a precise degree which allows facility owners to optimize the system to a high degree, 
thereby taking fully advantage of it without compromising safety. 
Novel uncertainty is then uncertainty which cannot be estimated and exists in open systems that cannot 
be controlled. It relates to initiatives with no or little experiences and therefore no historical background

to forecast uncertainty from (Lempert, 2003). In for example large and complex projects, this can 
become deep uncertainty, that is high uncertainty in a system where problems can easily inflate 
exponentially (Lempert, 2003). One cannot foresee the risks and therefore high uncertainty levels must 
be accounted for. 
Deep uncertainty can be categorized as unknown unknowns, in a framework of a) known knowns, b) 
known unknowns, c) unknown knows, and d) unknown unknowns (Knight, 1921). 
a) known knowns, is then the planned project events and costs, b) is known and accounted for risks, c) 
is events and costs that should have been known but have not been accounted for, and d) is risks that

4.1 Context 
In 2007 the Danish government changed the local governmental structure in Denmark. Amongst other, 
5 regional areas where established, one of them being Region Zealand. These were created mainly to 
manage all public health care including all public hospitals and was based on local democratic 
governance similar to municipalities and the previous counties. In fact, this was a key move to reach the 
underlying goal, centralizing the Danish health care system. At the same time a new health care plan was 
voted for, and with it came Kvalitetsfonden, a foundation of approximately 41.7 bn kr. created to fund 
new large and centralized hospitals across the regions. About 60% of the funds is covered by the national 
budget, and 40% by the regions themselves (SUM, 2017). From the outset, the five regions had to 
compete for the funds, and now also had to come up with local plans that completely transformed the 
existing health care system of each individual region. A sufficiently centralized health care plan was a 
precondition for applying for the funds. It was a highly complex task as the regions where made up of 
several previously-existing counties that had been amalgamated; each with their own previous health 
care systems and needs. 
In total 16 hospital projects across the country ended up being funded by Kvalitetsfonden (SUM, 2016). 
For the first and smallest of them it went well, a renovation of Slagelse Sygehus for about 300 mil. kr. 
The hospital is in Region Zealand which therefore had a good start regarding keeping up with the 
schedule. Two smaller projects have been completed since then, one also in Slagelse and one in Kolding, 
both however were delayed by one year. Delays are indeed, a repeating part of the story. So far 5 projects 
in total are on schedule, one of them exceptionally being 2 years in front of it. However as of the 11 
others, they are all behind schedule by at least 1 year. Only two of the delayed projects are delayed by 3 
years: a smaller one in the Capital Region and the other being the case at hand. So far University Hospital 
Køge is the last project to be completed which will seal the countrywide healthcare plan shut (Jyllands-
Posten, 2017). Below is a table with an overview of all the Kvalitetsfonden funded projects.

The most important part of the story is not how delayed any of the projects are, but that they are all on 
budget. The government had clear conditions for all the hospital projects. They must all be completed 
within budget and scope. These conditions are the first of their kind in such large infrastructure projects, 
at least the first to be upheld. It is therefore not surprising, looking back at the project iron triangle, that 
the last variable, time, is the one being sacrificed. It has been the only type of change accepted by the 
government, instead projects with continuous problems is placed under increased inspection to 
minimize further problems. A few cases with cost overruns have been experienced, overall, they can be 
considered minor issues, and the regions themselves had to cover the extra costs. However, because of 
the general strict course of the program, the overruns have firstly been greatly reduced, before finally 
being approved, as a last resort. 
Two years forward from the new national health care plan, in 2009, Region Zealand voted for a new 
2020 health care plan. Right after, the first application was send to Kvalitetsfonden. This was the first 
step towards University Hospital Køge. Very soon after an investigation of where the new hospital 
should be located was initiated. It was found that Køge was the best location followed by Ringsted. Not 
everyone was happy with the report, some had wished that the new hospital would be located nearer 
to their homes, specifically interest groups from Roskilde pressured for the relocation of as many 
hospital functions as possible in their municipality (I1UHK). A compromise between the many 
municipalities was found, whereby Køge would be the central location. An application was sent to the 
Health authorities. The application was turned down, the health care plan submitted was too 
decentralized and had to be revised. At this point it was clear that if the region wanted to compete for 
the funds, it had to centralize its activities much more, perhaps to the displeasure of some of its 
municipalities. 
In NOV 2010 the region achieved a preliminary approval of their fund application. However, they 
applied for approximately 6.4 bn kr., they only received approval for 5.35 bn kr.; approx. one billion kr 
was lost (Region Sjælland, 2010). Certainly, they were unsatisfied. The region posited that they were 
entitled to exactly 6.35 bn kr. if the funds were to be allocated fairly throughout the five regions. None 
the less, the Danish Health Authority was not to be negotiated with. 
Soon after Project University Hospital Køge was initiated. At this point the project owner organization 
had not been established. The main aim was to develop the tender documents and initiate a contest 
based on a full-service consultant contract. The tender contest was initiated on AUG 2012 with a budget 
of 4 bn kr. PL09 for UHK, the remaining funds were assigned to the rest of the health care plan. 
Mid 2013 the winners were chosen: a syndicate led by C.F. Møller architects teamed up with Alectia and 
Rambøll consulting engineers. Around the same time the Project owner team was established, and the 
collaboration between the two had begun.

4.3 Hypotheses 
Based on the theory and case study several hypotheses have been developed, which is the basis for the 
data analysis. The hypotheses are motivated by the research question and is meant as a framework for 
the subsequent data analysis. It also depicts key differences between “normal” large public 
infrastructure projects and the case of UHK. It is presented in three parts and sub-sections. The first part 
is an illustration of how normal large public infrastructure projects often is affected by multiple cost 
overruns. This is inspired by the work of Flyvbjerg et al., who documents the constant cost overruns 
experienced in large infrastructure projects in the public sector. It also takes output in theoretical 
models on the learning curve, the project cost cone, and theory on project risk. The second part is a 
similar model but modified for the UHK case and key differences can be detected. The third and last part 
is then an alternative depiction of project uncertainty and is based on the development of the detail level 
of the project. It is developed according to the case of UHK and is also based on theory related to project 
indicators. 
4.4.1 General conceptual model for large public infrastructure projects 
In this section a number of figures is presented as a coherent story line that depicts the cost development 
of certain projects that experiences cost overruns. Each figure is commented to complete the story line. 
 
Figure 3. Learning curve related to cost in project design phase 
Figure 3 is simply a S-shaped learning curve, where learning accelerates at the middle of the depicted 
timeline. The learning curve is depicted as the development of the max cost estimate in a project design 
phase, which decreases as learning - and thereby certainty - increases. This is because as certainty 
increases, possible variation between the expected cost estimate and the maximum cost estimate 
decreases. 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
 
Figure 4. Cost buffer in project design phase 
Figure 4 adds a cost buffer. The buffer is the area between the red line and the target cost. This is usually 
used to mitigate possible cost overruns in projects. The need for a buffer decreases as certainty 
increases, just as the case of the max. expected cost. The expected cost now becomes a target cost, as the 
buffer is used to achieve this target. 
The last addition is the contracting point. It is inserted at a certain point where, possible cost variation 
is so low that the project can continue to its next phase, which here is depicted as a move from the design 
phase to the contracting phase. A contract can only be signed when variation is low enough to ensure 
the robustness of the contract, which otherwise could go over budget because of the uncertainty. 
 
Figure 5. Cost overrun 
In figure 5 a cost overrun is detected, that is the revised expected cost exceed the max. expected cost. In 
this case three main options are available. A) Abort project. B) Reduce project scope. C) Increase budget. 
In many cases, as Flyvbjerg et al. documents, option C is chosen for various reasons (Flyvbjerg et al., 
2003). In some cases, it is also possible to mitigate the cost overrun by extending the deadline, this is 
however not always the case, and is therefore not a part of the generic main options available.

however inaccurate as it is not a max expected cost, but rather an average expected cost, the project has 
therefore no economic flexibility nor any guarantee that this cost can be reduced and remains 
vulnerable for further cost overruns. A target cost and buffer adjustment are also depicted in the figure.

Figure 7 depicts the common case where projects experiences multiple cost overruns as documented by

Figure 10 is similar to figure 1 in the generic case. However, for UHK a cost ceiling of 4 bn. kr. has been

Similar to Figure 4, figure 11 includes a cost buffer. In the case of UHK the buffer began at 20% and was

5. Data 
In this section, the data collection and treatment process are described, and data results are presented 
in various tables and graphs. The data processing is based on the hypotheses developed in the previous 
sub-section. 
5.1 Project detail level development 
One of the aims of this thesis is to map the development of the project detail level through time. To do 
this, one or more approximately representative indicators must be identified. Further the indicators 
must be available for processing, or else they will not be applicable. In the case of UHK, there were 
several possible options to consider and the selection process is described below. Key is that an 
emphasis has been placed on quantitative metadata that can be processed in large amounts. The project 
detail level mapping will therefore not include a qualitative assessment of neither the detail level of 
documents nor of the quality of the content. Instead the aim is to process large amounts of data to depict 
the overall development through many years of progress. This approach is deemed more efficient, more 
practicable, and in line with the thesis research questions. 
5.1.1 Project detail level indicators selection 
The project documented its progress in different ways at different times. The following is a list of the 
type of documents and files available along with a short description. The file formats are listed as well 
for data processing purposes. 
• 
Tender documents. These are comprehensive descriptions of functional project requirements 
and some technical requirements as well. Some project requirements are then updated 
afterwards in separate documents until they are locked by design decisions. In the turnkey 
contract tender documents, an emphasis has also been placed on technical project requirements, 
as this tender competition is based on a higher detail level. 
This type of documents is usually in PDF format. 
• 
Tender proposals. These are drawings, calculations and documents submitted by the winning 
consultants and contractors. Subsequently they are further developed and updated in separate 
documents. 
This type of documents is usually in PDF format. 
• 
Summarized reports submitted to the regional and national governments and parliaments. 
These focus on the overall project, the specific interests of the governmental bodies, and the 
information needed for voting and decision making. 
The file format is either PDF or Word. 
• 
Drawings. Various concept and construction drawings that are either comprehensive or detail 
oriented in different depictions. Some are then exported to separate PDF documents for 
presentation purposes. 
Drawings and supporting files have many types of formats. 
• 
Calculations. These are either based on the drawings or project requirements and have various 
purposes. Some are included in the drawings supporting files. 
This type of files has various formats. 
• 
Project descriptions. These documents specify project requirements and solutions, either 
functional or technical, with various recipients in mind. Some are oriented towards decision and 
control bodies and some are oriented towards contractors. 
• 
Process descriptions. These documents outline the many project processes required to 
undergo the project. They describe and support the work and decision-making processes and 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
set the rules of the project as well. They are oriented towards all involved project partners, and 
acts as documentation towards the governmental bodies as well. 
The file format is either PDF or Word. 
Financial and risk reports are excluded from the list above. This is because they are stored in different 
folders in the project document archiving system. These reports are instead used for mapping the 
project finances and risk development in the next section. 
It is important to note that some documents fit into more than one category in the list above and can 
have several purposes and intended recipients. This is especially important to consider when selecting 
files and indicators for the detail level progress mapping. 
The pool of documents can be classified and processed in many ways, here are some key considerations. 
Original files and summarized files. Here we differentiate between to main type of files. The original 
files consist of original documents that are mainly either technical or documentation of work and events. 
Summarized files are mainly deliveries to third parties and depicts and collects information and 
drawings from the original files. If it were possible to clearly divide files in each of the two categories, it 
could be assumed that the summarized files are only a depiction of the original files and therefore should 
not be included in the project detail level mapping, in order not to distort the charting. This however 
requires that the summarized files do not include any new information. In practice this is hard to 
determine, as summarized files exist on many levels, and original files can often rely on information 
from other original files. Further when information is aggregated it often discloses new information. 
While it is clear, that this categorization holds a lot of ambiguity it leads to the following suggestion. 
PDF files and raw files. This categorization is a modification of the one above. The main idea is that 
PDF files are somehow depictions of raw files, which is almost all other file types. Here PDF files are 
considered summations of other file types that aims at aggregating the information and making it easily 
available for both project managers and third parties. However, this distinction holds a lot of the 
ambiguity elaborated on above. The key difference is that this distinction is more rigid and do not 
require qualitative assessment of documents to determine which file belongs to which category. It is 
therefore possible to compare a chart of all files with a chart of PDF files only to see which differences it 
generates and assess whether it could be a reasonable distinction for the specific data set. 
Work files and publication files. In the case of UHK, documents are stored in two main folders, a work 
folder and a publication folder. These two folders exist within each milestone folder. All files are 
therefore stored in a specific milestone folder that they each belongs to, but some duplicate files across 
milestones do exist. The main difference between work files and publication files is, that all files are first 
stored in the work folder and when the milestone is reached, all necessary and accepted files are also 
stored in the publication folder. Publication files is therefore mainly duplicates of work files and 
including both folders would therefore be pointless. Further as of the specific data set at hand, some 
milestones are yet to be reached and therefore lack data in their publication folders. It has therefore 
been determined that only files from the work folders are included, which has further benefits. Work 
folders includes more original files and more accurate completion dates, whereas files in publication 
folders are mainly uploaded on the same date which is the milestone date. 
Drawings, calculations, tables and charts, and descriptions. This differentiation focus on the content 
of the files. The main consideration here is how to weight different types of data. If for example data is 
calculated in megabytes, should a megabyte of drawings weigh differently than a megabyte of 
description text? While this is an important consideration it is hard to set up accurate generic rules that 
reflects the content of the files. Alternatively, one must assess each file qualitatively which is outside of 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
the scope of this thesis and deemed impractical for large amounts of data. To differentiate content based 
on metadata is not deemed possible either, as random sample tests executed on the data at hand has 
shown large varieties in content information and quality levels across the data set. How much 
information for example a page or megabyte contains varies largely within each of the outlined 
categories. 
However some methods to quantitatively assess information levels of summarized reports has been 
considered, and the considerations is described below. 
Content count and LIX test. These are methods to assess the information level of each summarized 
report and have partly been tested. The methods assume that the summarized reports of the project 
reflect all project work and information available at the time of publication. On this basis the content of 
the reports is assessed by quantitative indicators to differentiate between the various types of 
information, for example plain text, section and subsection counts, tables, graphs, illustrations, 
drawings, appendixes, etc. Each type of information is counted and weighted. How to weight different 
type of information quantitively is, as discussed above, highly ambiguous. The amount of information 
contained in a table cell, drawing, illustration, etc. varies a lot, and in general must be assessed 
individually. Alternatively, one could grant equal scores to all types of information, for example a 
subsection equals a table, which equals a drawing, etc. However, this also assumes a weight relation 
between different content types, though being equalized. 
For plain text, a solution could be a LIX test. The lix test grants a specific piece of text a score which 
reflects the readability of the text. High scores mean low readability and high amounts of advanced 
words and sentences. The assumption here is that the LIX score is proportional with the complexity 
level of the text and therefore also detail level. However, this specific method is highly simplified and 
holds a lot of ambiguity in relation to the data at hand as well. For example, the project reports highly 
rely on information in non-text formats, such as tables, drawings, etc., and is written by multiple authors 
and in multiple styles. The LIX score is therefore deemed non-applicable to the specific case and this 
project genre in general. 
Besides all ambiguities described and considerations made, further problems arise when attempting to 
use this method. In the case of UHK the reports publicized do not represent the aggregated information 
available at the time of publication. They are developed towards specific purposes and recipients and 
only contains information in that regard. They do not build upon another and the development of detail 
level can therefore not be detected through this method. Issues in this regard was experienced when 
attempting to use this method. 
File count, megabytes or pages. These are three ways to determine the content level of a file category. 
For example, if files are categorized by file types, each file type has a file count, a collective number of 
megabytes, and in many cases a total number of pages. Because some of the key files of UHK do not 
include pages, e.g. drawings and data programs, this option is not further considered. As of file counts 
and megabytes both options have been depicted and will be presented in the following section. A key 
finding has been that in general using megabytes is more accurate than a simple file count. This is 
because the file sizes vary a lot, both across file types and within each type. For example, PDF files varies 
from a few kilobytes to hundreds of megabytes, which a file count do not consider. A key problem in this 
regard is internal differences between file type sizes. Two file types that contains exactly the same 
information, for example a drawing file and a PDF file, can have very different sizes because of their 
nature and compression. A sensitivity test has therefore been conducted on the specific data set to 
compare the overall average sizes of different file types. The sensitivity test is presented in the following 
section, and do not show significant differences between the main drawings file format, DWG, and the 
PDF file format. It does however show that less represented file formats accounts for almost half of the 
data collected. However further examination shows that these files also contains relatively large 
==PAGE:32==
31 
 
amounts of information, for example master drawing files of the overall construction project. It can 
therefore be assumed that no significant differences exist across file formats and that normalization is 
therefore not needed. But to separate the most common file format, PDF, from the others, separate 
graphs depicting only PDF files have been developed in line with the previously discussed 
differentiation between PDF files and raw files.

5.2.3.3 Risk assessments 
The risk assessment is documented in several steps, where the last step is depicted here. First all 
significant and relevant risks are recorded in a risk register. Then the likelihood and economic impact 
of each risk is estimated. Depending on the likelihood and impact the risk is categorized as low (green), 
medium (yellow), or high (red). At last a mitigation plan or activity along with a person responsible is 
assigned to each risk. 
In the monthly risk assessment yellow and red risks with a weighted impact of at least 2 M. kr. is 
included. The impact is weighted by multiplying it with likelihood, e.g. if likelihood is 50% the impact is 
multiplied by 0.5 / cut in half. The total weighted impact is reported along with its allocation on the 
construction and equipment tracks. The part of the total risk which can be absorbed by the existing 
budgets without the use of buffers is reported as well. In general, it is found that most of the reported 
risk can be absorbed by the existing budgets, and the remaining amount is not highly significant when 
compared to the buffer, in general it amounts to less than 10% of the buffer.

buffers is presented below.

assessed and are usually highly significant. Examples of highly significant ‘unknown knowns’ that where

5.3.2 Constructing a comprehensive overview of the project budget development 
For the purpose of creating an integrated model where both the detail level mapping and budgeting 
development is included, a combined budgeting dataset is produced. 
The dataset is combined by the budget information presented in the quarterly reports and the cost 
estimates presented in the monthly reports. From the quarterly reports total budget with and without 
reserves is gathered for the period JUL 2014 – APR 2018, as these were found to be the most precise in 
the previous data analysis sections. 
From the monthly reports in the period APR 2015 – JAN 2017, cost overruns or surplus is deduced from 
the difference between cost estimate and phase budget in percentage and added to the total budget. The 
reason why the overruns is added as percentages, is that these are only recorded for the phase budget. 
The actual cost overrun that led to the contract cancellation however, was stated as an overrun of 300-
350 mil. kr. of the entire budget. This was calculated by reflecting the phase overrun to the overall 
budget, as the full-service consultant estimated that similar overruns would be experienced for the rest 
of the project. When using this method, a cost overrun of 7.31% / 299 mil. kr., is calculated in JAN 2017, 
which is close to the actual estimate announced by the consultants. It is noted that the full-service 
consultants informed that this would be the minimum cost estimated, and the cost overrun could very 
well end up at higher levels. 
The data set had an error in timepoints 33 to 35, because the estimated cost for the equipment track 
was artificially low as seen in figure 24. This has been corrected by adjusting it to the level before the 
error, which is the same as the level after and therefore confirms that it was an error.

An estimated cost excluding reserves is also calculated, subtracting the buffer noted in the quarterly 
reports from the calculated cost estimate. For the rest of the period where no cost estimate is noted, the 
periods between JUL 2014 – APR 2015 and FEB 2017 – APR 2018, the budget has been used as the cost 
estimate, as this is the desired position of UHK and therefore no other estimate has been calculated. 
While no cost estimates were presented after the overrun, it can be estimated when UHK regained

5.4 Analysis 
Comparing the constructed data model in section 5.3.3 with the hypotheses presented in section 4.4, 
some key differences have to be addressed. Section 4.4.2 concludes with a model comparable with the 
budget-based figure 24. The differences being that no max expected cost can be calculated, and the 
learning curve instead becomes the expected cost. Also, it does not appear as a smooth learning curve, 
but rather a bumpy one with an initial period of cost overruns in between APR 2015 and NOV 2015. This 
is then brought back to budget ceiling level until the final cost overrun appears which marks the end of 
the full-service contract. 
Further, the budget buffer does not reduce in a linear smooth manner, rather it reaches certain 
milestones where big portions are released to the main budget at once. This is seen at JAN 2016 (37) 
and again at APR 2017 (52). The original buffer plan, table 3, is set up in a similar manner, with drops at 
certain milestones. 
The idle period is quite clear from the estimated cost remaining in place while negations took place, and 
then dropping very similar to the hypothesis model. 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
The cost ceiling is also set in place, with a single exception because of the new loan offered by the 
government for upgrading the hospitals to energy standard 2020, a minor change. 
Now comparing the constructed project detail level model in section 5.3.1, taking output in scenario 2, 
it is flipped on its head compared to the hypothesis model. This is because having a ‘burn down’ model 
would require a max detail level, which cannot be determined without introducing assumptions. Instead 
uncertainty levels can be set to highest at project start, and low at turnkey contracting point, and 
anything beyond that is even lower. 
Instead it has been layered with the budgeting model to create a combined model, were uncertainty is 
understood through the relationship between cost estimate and project detail level. 
If so, the varying buffer size is proportional with the perceived uncertainty, and the estimated cost is 
proportional with the real uncertainty. 
Looking into the detail level development, at the first major milestone of data change (DEC 2015), the 
estimated cost decreases to cost ceiling level. An interpretation is then, that the detail level increase 
reduced the uncertainty. 
At the second major milestone however (JUL 2016), simultaneous with the data change, the estimated 
cost rises again. An interpretation is, that the project development introduces new uncertainties. 
At the third major data change (JAN 2017), which is the drawback, the estimated cost is readjusted to 
cost ceiling level. An interpretation is, that the previously introduced uncertainty was cancelled out, due 
to rolling back changes that were made. 
At the fourth major milestone (SEP 2017), data changes are introduced as the estimated cost reaches 
ceiling level, therefore uncertainty is kept low, and the project can enter a new phase by signing a 
turnkey contractor (JAN 2018). 
Noticing that to reach the cost ceiling level and the fourth milestone, a major reduction of the budget 
buffer was introduced. This has been justified by the changes in contracting conditions, but also appears 
as a mitigation strategy to decrease the estimated cost. The decrease of the buffer in April 2017 (approx. 
268 mil. kr), and decrease in cost estimate between JAN 2017 and JUL 2017 (approx. 308 mil. kr.), only 
differ by 40 mil. kr. A key deduction of the analysis is therefore, that not only did the project 
optimizations produced by the project owner team play a major roll in reducing the cost estimate, the 
change in contracting strategy had a major role to play as well. 
This simultaneous movement of detail level and cost changes showcases their relationship. At the same 
time, perceived uncertainty and real uncertainty differ greatly and a quantitative rise in project detail 
level does not guarantee an uncertainty drop. On the other hand, it is shown that in some cases, project 
development can introduce new uncertainty, which can be mitigated by rolling back changes.

6. Discussion 
6.1 Data analysis 
Looking back at the data analysis several assumptions had to be made for the analysis to proceed. The 
quality of these can be discussed, and in general estimations such as the project detailing drawback 
could have been developed in closer relationship with the project owners, given more time and 
resources. 
Further, differences in filetypes and their effect on the data results could have been scrutinized more to 
develop more exact models. A sensitivity test was performed, and alternative results with only PDF files 
considered was presented, but much more can be done in this regard. 
In regard to errors found in the data sets collected, these were mostly corrected through an independent 
assessment. Given more time and resources, it would be preferred to discuss any errors found with UHK 
or the partners responsible. 
The main concern of this thesis is however not to offer the most exact data analysis, but rather to 
maintain focus on developing new methods for quantitative project uncertainty development 
assessment. This was achieved through not only data analysis, but by generic modelling based on the 
underlying theory and case study performed. 
Clear differences between the models developed through the case analysis and the actual data models 
was found. Some of these were discussed in the data analysis. A specific difference which should be 
discussed further here, is the fact that budgeting buffers do not reduce in a continuous linear manner. 
UHK had a four-step plan for buffer reduction, which could be used instead in the modelling. However, 
their actual buffering strategy ended up very different and perhaps more pragmatic. From the data 
models it is seen how the budget buffer is reduced in steps just as the plans suggest. But the estimated 
buffer varies according to the estimated cost and risk assessments. Further studies into the modelling 
of the project buffer would therefore be recommended. 
The data collected was concentrated around milestones, and therefore appears as a lot of work is 
accomplished in a few days followed by long idle times. This is of course not the case and in reality, the 
work is a continuous process through the whole period. If data was collected in collaboration with the 
former full-service consultants, perhaps it would be possible to depict the actual flow of work more 
precise,

6.2 Cost overrun and improvised solution 
Perhaps the most important discussion is, whether UHK should have predicted or at least been prepared 
for the risk of a cost overrun. In interview 3, this discussion was brought up with UHK, and several points 
were made. 
Though the cost overrun came as a surprise and was not included in the project risk register as a realistic 
possibility, the resulting outcome is not considered a disadvantage. Rather they see it as an opportunity 
to roll back some of the bad decisions made by the full-service consultants and optimize the project 
themselves. The full-service consultant project is essentially considered a first attempt, which had a lot 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
of potential for improvement and which was made possible by the cost overrun and contract 
cancellation. If they had been able to find another solution with the consultants, they would perhaps not 
have had this opportunity and could end up accepting some of the less optimal solutions. 
Of course there is a strong bias in this point of view, that heavily rely on the fact that they were able to 
steer the project on the right track. Had that not been possible they would have been much worse off. 
In interview 3, when UHK was asked about whether they had considered the possibility of not being 
able to complete their project on their own and receive a satisfactory turnkey contract offer, they were 
keen to answer that this possibility could not be integrated in the project risk mitigation work. The 
explanation given is that though the risk was not unrealistic, it would require the whole project to be 
transformed. It was therefore out of the scope of the project, as the main goal at the time was to maintain 
the project without the need for major transformations. 
Therefore, looking back at the project course, one could argue that the project developed with the full-
service consultants was a first iteration and ended up becoming an experiment rather than a solution. 
Possibilities were explored along the way and it was made clear exactly what was possible within the 
project restriction, and what was not. In the next phase after contract cancellation, the second shorter 
iteration began based on the first, where the good parts were sustained and the suboptimal parts where 
improved, resulting in a better project within cost and scope. A third iteration is then introduced, which 
is also an improvised solution to mitigate with the negative consequences of the contract cancellation 
(I3UHK). The iteration is introduced by integrating an exit clause in the turnkey contract, allowing UHK 
to pull out of the contract if the project plan is not completed to satisfaction before execution. A stage 
gate is thereby setup before the actual construction begins, at which point the project can be cancelled 
without further complication. 
If the exit clause is actually taken into effect, it could be a major setback for the project. The success of 
the solution of UHK can therefore first be confirmed when the stage gate has been passed. This is an 
important reservation that must be made in the assessment of the project. 
The possibility of optimizing the project with the full-service consultant, in the same manner as the 
project ended up without them, was also discussed. In fact, the full-service consultant was required to 
complete the project within its cost and scope without further payment, regardless of setbacks as it was 
a fixed royalty contract. They maintained however, that it could not be done, and therefore the 
requirements had to be changed. An independent auditor was brought in to assess the project and 
concluded that it was possible to optimize it sufficiently. But even then, the full-service consultant 
maintained their position, and UHK had to complete it on their own. 
Why the full-service consultants did not agree to optimize the project is not clear but can have multiple 
explanations. At the time of the cost overrun, it is told that they had most likely spend all their royalty 
from the project, and therefore could not afford further efforts. This is not a legally acceptable reason, 
and UHK could have pursued a court ruling, but did not choose to. Instead they attempted to move on 
fast and paid the consultants a relatively small settlement to get out of the contract without further 
difficulties and delays. This was perhaps not the cheapest solutions if they could of uphold their 
demands in a court case, but this solution was most likely the fastest solution. 
6.3 Project constraints and incitement structures 
In the project management literature, the Iron Triangle is viewed as an unescapable condition for all 
projects (Larson & Gray, 2015). Every project has three main variables, cost, time, and scope, which 
when balanced will result in the project quality or output. Only one variable can be maintained at the 
sacrifice of the other two when difficulties are encountered (Larson & Gray, 2015). However, the Danish 
hospital projects shows that within certain limits, it is possible to both withhold the cost and scope of a 
project, with the sacrifice of time. At a certain point however, setbacks experienced in cost and scope, 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
cannot be mitigated by time delays. The project therefore must be planned very carefully and realistic. 
As a result, much more realistic planning from the beginning have been experienced in the hospital 
projects when compared to some of the cases presented by Flyvbjerg et al. A key criticism by Flyvbjerg 
et al. is optimism bias by project stakeholders. They underestimate costs, oversell scope, and present 
overoptimistic schedules because of unbalanced incentive structures (Flyvbjerg et al., 2003). In the 
hospital cases the incentive structures seem to be well balanced regarding cost and scope, but time 
delays are experienced to a high degree. The incentive structures related to project scheduling is 
discussed further below. First, it should be noted that in our case, the incentive of the full-service 
consultant for delivering a satisfactory budget could be obscured. While they would benefit from 
succeeding, one could question the lack of penalty for not doing so. It does not seem like they would lose 
anything even if they won the initial tender contest based on an overoptimistic project. The contractual 
requirements set in place did not prevent them from leaving the project without further losses. This was 
then learned from by UHK and adjusted for in the turnkey tender contest, but the learning is also of 
general interest. In order to ensure well balanced incentive structures in these types of projects, 
contracting and legal requirements become important tools, and the experiences of the hospital projects 
should be brought forward. 
Lastly it is discussed what consequences the large project delays endured in UHK has on the project and 
region. In general project delays are tolerated by the government across the hospital projects. Projects 
could be placed under intensified supervision but will not be rejected. While it is agreed upon that delays 
can be necessary to comply with the cost and scope requirements, they do not come without a cost. As 
documented in the case study, a key motivation for the project is the operational efficiency 
improvements that they expect from it. It is estimated that about 160 mil. kr. can be saved each year. 
These savings can then be subtracted from the regions budget to cover expenditures, such as the project 
itself. When the savings is not realized in time, it is considered a loss. However, the loss will only affect 
the region’s economy, not the governmental fund, neither the isolated project within the region. It is 
therefore not a main concern for the project, but at the same time it is operating within the region which 
continuously must approve it for it to proceed. In relation to the unbalanced incitement structures 
discussed by Flyvbjerg et al., this is a case of lack of ownership (Flyvbjerg et al., 2003). The isolated 
project does not own the consequences of the delays, and therefore are missing incitement to prevent 
them. This does not mean that they do not care at all, but the incitement would be much stronger if the 
loss in potential savings each year, was fully or partly covered by the project economy, thereby 
penalizing it with each year delayed. This of course introduces another problem as locks are being 
placed on all three corners of the iron triangle. It could be derived that any possible savings after project 
completion should not be incorporated in the region’s economy before they are realized. 
6.4 Project uncertainty and risk mitigation 
Project uncertainty and risk is a major concern of this thesis and of UHK. UHK has partly developed a 
budget buffer release plan, and partly a risk register and monthly risk report. These tools help them 
ensure mitigation of unplanned risks. In general, it was found that the actual buffers set in place, were 
lower than in the buffer plan. The reports studied shows that UHK sometimes justifies the buffer levels, 
e.g. if a certain part of the project is ahead of schedule. At the same time the buffer plan is questionable, 
as the levels set seems quite artificial, rather than carefully estimated. On the other hand, UHK have 
justified their buffer levels by detailed estimation which seems reasonable. They did not of course 
mitigate with the cost overrun as previously discussed, but they have not been exceeded either. 
In the risk register they quantify the risks in cost and allocate the cost on either the main budget or 
buffer. Most of the risk calculated is allocated to the main budget, and therefore only a small portion of 
the risk must be mitigated by the buffer. This begs the question, why is there so much buffer 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
unaccounted for? This could be explained by the presence of deep uncertainty in the project, so called 
unknown unknowns. This type of uncertainty that cannot be predicted nor estimated, is a characteristic 
for highly complex projects. Further the level of novelty increases it as well. As of UHK, it holds both high 
complexity and high levels of novelty. Novelty as many of the solutions in it and in the other hospital 
projects is sought at the cutting edge of technology. Regarding IT, logistics, medical equipment, and even 
the project requirements themselves are novel as extensively discussed throughout the report. 
Complexity is increased by novel solutions and by the many sub-systems that it incorporates, and at the 
same time the systems that it exists within. It is therefore not unreasonable that the project risk register 
is not exhaustive as that would prove impossible. 
6.5 Thesis biases and strengths 
In this last sub-section, a short note must be made on this thesis own biases and how they affect the 
report. A big portion of the thesis has been centered around the conflict between UHK and its former 
full-service consultant. But only UHK have been consulted. This introduced a lot of bias in their favor. If 
the thesis has balanced inputs from both parties, it would most likely be more nuanced. While it has 
been of great interest to include both parties point of views, it has not been possible. The reason being 
that the conflict is still considered ongoing and sensitive, and therefore access to dive into it is very 
limited. At the beginning of the thesis work in FEB 2017, the settlement had only just been reached, and 
turnkey tender offers had yet to be received. This is both a key strength and weakness of the report. 
While it cannot access both parties, it can document an ongoing case that are highly relevant at the 
current moment and an important contributor of experiences. 
The thesis then offers new methods to pragmatically analyze project progress while it is in progress. It 
is not the goal to deliver the most precise calculation methods, nor the most exhaustive. Rather it is 
aimed at delivering fast and applicable methods that can effectively be used on ongoing projects to 
reveal their progression and uncertainty development. As earlier mentioned it is considered a first of its 
kind and development project. Any defects encountered in this first attempt must therefore be 
considered for further development and testing of the methodology on other projects.

7. Conclusion 
To answer the research question, hypotheses on how UHK’s cost and scope along with its uncertainty 
evolved throughout the project. The hypotheses were tested through quantitative analytical methods 
developed specifically for this purpose. A case study along with a data analysis was conducted to 
determine the impact of the cost ceiling and scope requirements on the project. 
It was found that the cost ceiling and scope requirements set by the government, had an extensive 
impact on the project, beyond what is seen in other comparable projects. At the same time these 
restrictions also provided opportunities for the project and supported its effort to keep the project 
budget on track. A discussion of the impacts and their strengths and weaknesses was therefore 
provided. 
The methods developed for the data analysis is considered novel and under development. They are 
therefore a key product of the study. Their applicability on other cases will have to be tested, as they are 
further developed. This and more will be discussed below in a sub-section.
