Abstract
We present a novel water­drinking reminder device designed specifically for the elderly.
The distinctive feature of this device is the integration of a physical plant with a visual dash­
board screen. The base of the plant houses a circuit board, a motor and a mini­computer.
A screen is positioned on the surface of the plant base, powered by the mini­computer
along with the motor. The hydration reminder device, equipped with a display screen, pro­
vides a visual representation of the user’s daily water intake, sending hourly reminders
through both the plant and the screen. Moreover, the design incorporates principles of
behavioral change for the elderly, allowing them to engage in the process by watering the
plant themselves, adding an element of fun while ensuring proper hydration.
To enhance the effectiveness of the hydration reminder system, elderly individuals wear
Empatica’s smartwatch, transmitting Electrodermal Activity (EDA) and activity intensity
data to a server. This data is then fetched and imported into a mini­computer. A pre­
trained neural network model assesses their hydration levels and provides reminders for
the required water intake. This algorithm, as compared to simply encouraging seniors
to drink enough water each day, offers increased credibility and improves the elderly’s
hydration balance throughout the day. This system serves as a valuable life assistant for
residents in care homes who require timely reminders to stay hydrated or elderly individ­
uals living independently who are often at risk of dehydration. Its promising applications
make it a beneficial addition to the realm of elderly care.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Dehydration is commonly considered a reason for emergency room visits and secondary
diagnoses during hospitalization for elderly individuals. However, some argue that dehy­
dration might be misdiagnosed in long­term care patients [1][2][3]. Uncontrolled dehydra­
tion can lead to changes in body chemistry, kidney failure, kidney stones, urinary tract
infections, colorectal cancer, and even death [4][5][6].
As people age, the body can produce changes in water metabolism and sodium balance,
resulting in changes in plasma osmotic pressure and body fluid volume distribution. As a
result, the frequency and severity of hyperosmolaremia in the elderly will increase. Clini­
cally, it is important A hypervolemic state manifested by hypernatremia. Based on records
from over ten million healthcare providers in the United States, it is shown that 17% of el­
derly individuals diagnosed with dehydration die within 30 days [7]. However, this data
encompasses different age groups and levels of disease severity. Even so, among el­
derly individuals residing in the community, over 60% report dehydration.
The elderly are at increased risk of developing water and sodium balance disorders due
to intrinsic diseases or iatrogenic reasons. Therefore, medical workers who serve the
elderly should understand the deficiencies in the elderly’s ability to compensate and regu­
late water and sodium balance, and should Pay attention to the diagnosis and prevention
of water and sodium balance disorders.
The emergence of hypernatremia in the elderly reinforces the importance of hydration
testing. Older adults are more susceptible to symptoms of dehydration due to physiologi­
cal changes. These physiological changes may also be exacerbated by various illnesses
and heavy medication use, both of which are common in older adults.
Personalized hydration and horizontal monitoring play a crucial role in people’s move­
ment, health, well­being, and safety during specific activities. In hospitals, clinical staff
must pay attention to numerous physiological symptoms to determine the optimal envi­
ronment and water intake for individuals’ best hydration.

Dehydration poses significant health risks for the elderly in nursing homes and hospitals,
encompassing urinary tract infections, kidney problems, cognitive impairment, increased
fall risks, exacerbated existing health conditions, impaired wound healing, electrolyte im­
balances, and adverse effects on mood and emotional well­being. Proper hydration is es­

1.2.2
Manual monitoring
Effective hydration management for the elderly in nursing homes and hospitals is hindered
by the reliance on manual monitoring methods, leading to the potential for human errors
and a lack of real­time insights. This traditional approach often falls short in providing
timely and accurate information about individuals’ hydration statuses, underscoring the

Timely intervention
Timely intervention is paramount in addressing dehydration risks among the elderly. Cur­
rent healthcare practices, reliant on periodic assessments, may result in delayed re­
sponses to evolving hydration needs. Implementing a hydration level detection system
enables real­time monitoring, facilitating proactive measures to maintain optimal hydration
levels. This not only prevents health complications but also ensures a more responsive
and personalized approach to care for each elderly individual in nursing homes and hos­

1.2.4
Patient­Centric care
Achieving patient­centric care for the elderly entails recognizing and responding to individ­
ual hydration requirements. A hydration level detection system allows for a more person­
alized approach, generating alerts and interventions tailored to each person’s real­time
needs. By prioritizing patient­centric care, healthcare providers can significantly enhance
the overall well­being of elderly individuals in nursing homes and hospitals, addressing hy­
dration needs in a manner that respects their autonomy and unique health circumstances.
1.2.5
Technology integration
Integrating technology into healthcare practices is essential for advancing hydration mon­
itoring for the elderly. Wearable devices equipped with sensors offer a non­intrusive and
continuous solution for tracking hydration levels. However, seamless integration into the
healthcare environment remains a challenge, requiring interdisciplinary collaboration to
ensure compatibility with existing systems and adherence to healthcare standards. Suc­
cessful technology integration promises to revolutionize the efficiency and effectiveness
of hydration management in nursing homes and hospitals.
1.2.6
User acceptance
User acceptance is a critical consideration in implementing hydration monitoring technol­
ogy. The design of user­friendly interfaces for both healthcare professionals and elderly
individuals is crucial. Ensuring comfort, privacy, and autonomy in the use of such systems
fosters positive engagement. Addressing concerns related to data security and privacy
further enhances user acceptance. By prioritizing usability and ethical considerations,
healthcare providers can encourage widespread adoption of hydration level detection sys­
tems, ultimately improving the overall quality of care for the elderly in nursing homes and
hospitals.

Significance and relevance of the study
Monitoring the hydration status in real­time and providing reminders for adequate water
intake has been a persistent challenge in nursing homes and hospitals. Typically, whole­
body water assessment and plasma osmolality are considered reliable measurement indi­
cators. However, the commonly used methods for both approaches are invasive, requiring
procedures like isotope dilution for plasma osmolality. Although non­invasive hydration

whole­body water assessment, they face limitations in continuous estimation due to their

Determining when and how much water to drink to prevent dehydration is the motiva­
tion behind this study. The research explores a dynamic monitoring approach: utilizing
portable devices to collect biometric data (Electrodermal Activity ­ EDA and activity in­

1.6
Scope and target users
1.6.1
Scope
This project focuses on developing a dynamic and non­invasive system for real­time mon­
itoring of hydration status, specifically targeting elderly individuals. The scope encom­
passes the integration of portable devices, such as those measuring Electrodermal Ac­
tivity (EDA) and activity intensity, to continuously collect biometric data. The server­side
processing involves applying predefined algorithms to estimate the hydration status based
on the acquired data. The project further extends to the implementation of a user­friendly
device that encourages water intake by providing personalized reminders to the elderly
individuals. The user interface allows for input and feedback on water consumption, creat­
ing a closed­loop system that adjusts recommendations based on real­time and historical
data. This holistic approach aims to improve hydration management, promote healthy
water intake habits, and enhance overall well­being among the elderly population in care
facilities.
1.6.2
Target users
Our target users are elderly individuals who wish to improve their hydration habits or
caregivers responsible for the well­being of the elderly. As individuals age, there is an
increased risk of dehydration due to the loss or delay in the sensation of thirst. Our design
aims to provide an intuitively simple, real­time monitoring device that encourages proper
hydration, helping the elderly become more aware of their hydration levels.
1.7
Limitations
Despite its innovative approach, the project has certain limitations. Firstly, the accuracy
of the hydration status estimation relies on the reliability of the biometric data collected,
and external factors such as ambient conditions and individual variations may impact the
precision of the results.
Additionally,a significant challenge lies in the acquisition of comprehensive training data
to develop accurate hydration level prediction models. The ideal dataset would include
detailed information such as urine frequency, color, drinking times, and amounts. How­
ever, obtaining such data from elderly individuals, particularly those residing in nursing
homes, presents practical difficulties. The elderly may experience fatigue or a lack of
motivation to consistently document these details. This limitation in the training dataset
poses a potential barrier to the system’s accuracy, as the predictive models heavily rely
on the quality and quantity of the input data.

Literature Review
2.1
Fluid balance on the elderly & Hypernatremia
Due to physiological changes that occur with aging, older individuals are more prone to
dehydration. Overall body fat increases by 5%­10% in the elderly, while body fluids de­
crease by a similar amount [8]. Compared to younger individuals, the plasma volume in
older people is reduced by approximately 21% relative to body weight and surface area.
Such changes are akin to experiencing an equivalent amount of acute fluid gain or loss.
As a result, mild dehydration or excess volume can both cause significant changes in
blood sodium concentration in older individuals. Among these issues, high sodium lev­
els, known as hypernatremia, are a typical representation, with dehydration being the
most common cause.
Hypernatremia usually reflects increased plasma osmolality. A survey of hospitalized el­
derly patients and those in long­term care showed that the incidence of hypernatremia
ranged from 0.3% to 8.9%. However, 60% to 80% of hypernatremia in the elderly oc­
curs during hospitalization. More than 30% of the elderly in home care develop hyper­
natremia after hospitalization. A study tracked the outcomes of 162 elderly patients with
hypernatremia (age ≥60 years old, serum sodium ≥148 mmol/L) and found that 43% had
hypernatremia upon admission, and the remaining 57% developed hypernatremia after
admission. Natremia; 42% of the patients died due to various reasons, and the mortality
rate was 7 times that of patients of the same age with normal serum sodium; and 38% of
the surviving patients had significantly reduced self­care ability after discharge.[9]
2.2
Dehydration
Dehydration occurs if the amount of fluids consumed by the body is less than the flu­
ids expended, results in an insufficient water supply within the body. This phenomenon
is categorized into three conditions based on the osmotic pressure of bodily fluids: hy­
pertonic, isotonic, and hypotonic. Illustrates the distinctions among these three states
are shown in Figure 2.1. Among the elderly, hypertonic dehydration(HD) is particularly
prevalent, characterized by a greater loss of water than sodium. In such instances, swift
rehydration becomes imperative. Isotonic dehydration is commonly observed in surgical
patients, who are susceptible to proportional losses of sodium and water. Conversely,
hypotonic dehydration is often associated with conditions such as diarrhea and vomiting,
and is more frequently encountered in younger demographics like infants and children.
Each type of dehydration manifests distinct symptoms. HD prompts feelings of thirst,
reduced urine output, and dark­colored urine, all of which can be alleviated through in­
creased fluid intake. Isotonic dehydration, owing to the unchanged osmotic pressure, can­
not be discerned through urine color but is characterized by symptoms such as anorexia,
nausea, weakness, and dry lips and tongue. Hypotonic dehydration is identified by light­
colored urine, and patients may not experience thirst, potentially leading to a deceptive
appearance of adequate hydration, known as chronic dehydration. In such cases, supple­
mentation with saline solutions is deemed necessary. This comprehensive understanding
of the various forms of dehydration is crucial for effective management and targeted in­

2.3
Hydration Assessment Techniques
To commence, a set of foundational definitions will be presented. Total Body Water (TBW)
is the sum of Extracellular Fluid (ECV) and Intracellular Fluid (ICV), accounting for ap­
proximately 63.3% of body weight. The volume of TBW is associated with the balance
of water and sodium and is regulated by fluid­regulating hormones. Plasma osmolality is
the concentration of dissolved particles in the blood. It is maintained around a euhydra­
tion set­point of 285 mOsm/kg. Monitoring hydration status can typically be determined
through a combination of blood osmotic pressure and Total Body Water (TBW), which is
considered the gold standard for assessment.[10]

2.3.1
Invasive
Blood Biochemical Indicators(BBI)
BBI involves analyzing blood samples to assess hydration status. It can measure elec­
trolyte concentrations, especially sodium concentration, providing a detailed understand­
ing of fluid balance. This method is widely used in clinical settings and offers precise
biochemical information, making it a high­precision measurement technique. However, it
requires blood sample collection, which may cause discomfort and cannot allow for con­
tinuous monitoring.
2.3.2
Non­invasive
Urine Measurement
In the case of normal kidney function, when the body is dehydrated and conserving wa­
ter, urine becomes concentrated and less abundant. Conversely, when the body has
temporarily excess water, urine becomes diluted and more abundant. The relationship
between urine measurements and hydration status can be explained through three di­
mensions: urine osmolality, urine specific gravity, and urine color.
Urine osmolality is a method for measuring the total solute content in urine, influenced by
all dissolved particles in a known volume (i.e., mass) of fluid. Similar to the principle of
urine specific gravity, which refers to the density of the sample compared to pure water
(mass per unit volume). The normal specific gravity of a healthy adult’s urine sample
typically ranges from 1.013 to 1.029, but during dehydration, it may exceed 1.030.[11][12]
Urine color is commonly considered a quick method for observing dehydration. However,
research indicates that when consuming a large amount of water within a short period
(1.5L/h), urine can be diluted despite being in a dehydrated state. This is because urine
color more reflects the intake of water rather than the body’s water content, and intake
actively influences hydration status [13].
Compared to urine specific gravity or osmolality, urine color may not provide the same
precision and accuracy, but it may be effective in occasion which do not require high
precision.
Dilution Techniques
Before and after fluid sampling, oral or intravenous administration of a tracer substance
allows for the measurement of the size of the body fluid compartments. [14]If the quantity
of the substance is known and baseline and equilibrium concentrations are measured, the
volume to which the tracer has been diluted can be calculated. Total Body Water (TBW)
can be assessed using stable (non­radioactive) isotopes of hydrogen or oxygen, which
distribute throughout almost all body fluid compartments within 3 to 4 hours [15]. Dilution
techniques are often more cost­effective, making them applicable to a broader range of
research and clinical applications. However, their sensitivity may be lower, limiting their
ability to detect subtle changes in hydration levels.
Bio­electrical Impedance(BIA)
BIA assumes that the human body is a uniformly composed conductor with a fixed cross­
sectional area and a uniform current density. The electrical current flowing through the
body (from hand to foot) encounters resistance from body tissues and water. Measure­
ment of bioelectrical impedance utilizes this characteristic to provide estimates of body
composition, including an estimation of total body water. However, it is a complex method
of indirect measurement and cannot be used for continuous monitoring of hydration lev­
els.[16]

Electrodermal activity (EDA)­based approach
Skin conductance, also known as Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) or Electrodermal Ac­
tivity (EDA), is currently referred to as skin conductance. It is based on the primitive ”fight
or flight” response, where the body prepares for the effort needed to cope with perceived
threats by increasing sweat activity to cool itself. Liaqat[17], utilizing wearable devices,
monitored changes in EDA under different exercise states and hydration levels. The study
found a positive correlation between EDA and hydration levels, providing a non­invasive
detection method.
2.4
Existing Products for Encouraging Hydration in the
Elderly
Apart from manual reminding from healthcare professionals, there have also been prod­
ucts in the market designed to remind and encourage the elderly. They can be broadly
categorized into three types:
Smart Water Bottles and Cups
Some smart water bottles and cups are equipped with reminder features to prompt the
elderly to drink water regularly. These products typically utilize sound, vibration, or LED
lights to alert individuals. They can record water intake and provide customized hydration
plans by connecting to smartphone applications. The advantage of such products is the
upgrade from traditional water cups, integrating automatic water intake tracking through
applications. However, in the drinking habits of the elderly, a significant portion of wa­
ter intake comes from beverages like coffee, and they tend to use multiple cups. This
highlights the limitations of a single smart water bottle.
Hydration Reminders
Traditional hydration reminders are simple yet effective products that use timed sound
or vibration cues to remind the elderly to drink water. These reminders are easy to use
but lack some smart functionalities. They usually can only preset reminder times and
cannot track the real­time intake of the recommended water amount, potentially leading
to overhydration scenarios.
App­Assisted Products
Some app­assisted products monitor the water intake of the elderly by connecting to
smartphone or tablet applications. These apps offer personalized reminders, statistics,
and health advice. Their advantage lies in the ability to track the individual’s hydration
levels and provide real­time reminders. However, the user experience heavily depends
on the simplicity of data collection and the motivating nature of the reminder methods.
2.5
Thirst Regulation
The kidney’s function related to fluid balance is regulated by the secretion of antidiuretic
hormone (ADH) from the posterior pituitary. The release of ADH is controlled by osmore­
ceptors and pressure receptors. An increase of 1% in plasma osmolarity leads to a 1
ng/L increase in plasma ADH concentration, resulting in a rapid decrease in urine vol­
ume. Thirst stimulation occurs when signals generated by osmoreceptors are transmitted
to the hypothalamus, causing a sensation of thirst and prompting water intake.[8]
In elderly individuals, the sensation of thirst may gradually diminish with age. Research
has observed that after a 24­hour water restriction in elderly individuals, despite a signifi­
cant increase in blood sodium and plasma osmolarity, there is a lack of subjective thirst.

Moreover, when allowed to drink water, older individuals tend to consume less water than
their younger counterparts, making it challenging for their blood sodium levels to return
to pre­restriction levels. This suggests that the thirst response to changes in osmolarity
in the elderly is delayed, indicating that a higher stimulus of plasma osmolarity may be
required for them to experience a thirst response.
The reduced sensation of thirst in the elderly results in decreased water intake, making
them more susceptible to dehydration. Studies have shown a link between dehydration
and various diseases, including cardiovascular and renal conditions.
2.6
Drinking habits of elderly people
2.6.1
In the community
According to the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey conducted by Claire
A. Zizza and colleagues, elderly individuals predominantly consume fluids in the morning,
with water and beverages being the two main contributors, contributing almost equally
to their overall intake [18]. Among beverages, coffee constitutes the largest share (ap­
proximately 40%), followed by milk and soft drinks (each around 20%). In comparison to
younger and middle­aged individuals, those aged 65 and above have the lowest propor­

2.6.2
In the nursing home
Encouraging fluid intake to prevent dehydration is one of the numerous daily tasks vying
for the limited time of caregivers. Despite having a more structured water­drinking sched­
ule in nursing homes compared to elderly individuals living at home, residents may still
exhibit reluctance to drink water. In a survey, staff members identified depression and
loneliness as the primary factors contributing to inadequate fluid intake among nursing
home residents[20]. These residents require attention akin to that given to a small child,
and there is a palpable need for someone to engage in conversation with them.
Staff members at nursing homes (NH) acknowledge that their hectic schedules and the
inability to spend substantial time with residents may result in residents not consuming
sufficient fluids. They also draw a connection between their lack of time and the residents’
feelings of loneliness or depression, further suppressing the residents’ desire to drink
water.

well­established health behavior theories encompass both internal and external factors.

Habit Formation
According to Leslie Kernisan, a geriatrician and former health services researcher, ”Do not
expect older adults to drink a large amount of water at once; it’s better to offer beverages
to older adults regularly and according to a plan” [22]. Providing smaller, more frequent
amounts of water is an essential strategy to encourage water intake among older individ­
uals. Research findings indicate that habits play a crucial role in water consumption and
may be a key determinant of whether participants are getting enough water [23]. Accord­
ing to the Dual Process Model [24], drinking water is an effortless activity when it becomes
a habit (i.e., an unconscious process), but if it’s not a habitual behavior (i.e., a conscious
process), it becomes effortful and less frequent.
Operant Conditioning
Operant conditioning is, ” a method of learning that uses rewards and punishment to
modify behavior”[25]. It states that behavior is less likely to be repeated if the behavior is
punished, whilst behavior is more likely to be repeated if the behavior is rewarded. By ap­
plying consistent positive or negative reinforcement to behaviors, a person will associate
certain behaviors with its related consequence. Consistently applying positive stimulus
for desired behaviors can result in these behaviors to be repeated more often, potentially
leading to habit formation [26].
Positive Reinforcement
Nursing home caregivers suggest that rewarding residents for drinking fluids can be ef­
fective, as positive reinforcement helps boost the motivation of older individuals to drink
water [20]. Drawing from a study on exercise improvement in the elderly, it is evident that
when positive reinforcement transitions from reinforcement to non­reinforcement, there

is a decline in weekly exercise levels among participants [27]]. This insight underscores
the potential impact of positive reinforcement on sustaining desired behaviors among the
elderly, including the promotion of adequate fluid intake.
Cognitive Factors
In the preceding discussion, it has been established that older individuals often strug­
gle to meet recommended water intake levels, facing an increased risk of dehydration
due to reduced sensitivity to thirst signals and lower body water reserves. A proposition
from Science [28] introduces the concept of ’Seeing That You Are Thirsty’ as a method
to enhance fluid intake. The author suggests that thirst and visual perception are mutu­
ally modifiable, proposing that exposure to items or words associated with drinking can
stimulate the brain’s desire for fluid intake, even when an individual may not feel thirsty.
Social Interaction
Integrating solutions into public spaces and social activities can be a powerful strategy.
Even simple measures like installing a large mirror in front of a beverage station may
encourage people to drink more water. Hearing sounds related to others drinking or hy­
drating may trigger mirror system activity [29].
Taking the promotion of exercise as an example, Hamari investigated how social influ­
ences aid in fostering beneficial behaviors that encourage people to continue and sustain
exercise [30]. Specifically, they examined how social factors concurrently operate to in­
crease the willingness to continue exercising. Drawing on theories of group formation
and kinship relationships, the conceptualization of social influence includes three main
factors: subjective norms, cognition, and reciprocal benefits. Positive acknowledgment
and reciprocity positively influence the extent to which people are willing to change and
their attitudes and willingness to use the proposed service solution. They found that the
more friends users have in the service, the greater the effect.
Health Literacy
In Scence’s study on water­drinking motivation [28], few participants regarded the act of
drinking water itself as a goal. Many participants were also unaware of the importance
of maintaining adequate hydration levels, the benefits of drinking water, and the reasons
behind both, limiting their water intake. Additionally, as individuals age, their processing
abilities for health literacy decline, necessitating a simplified understanding of knowledge
acquisition to reduce reliance on processing capabilities [31]. Assessing the health liter­
acy of older individuals, clear and simple information can empower them to make choices
beneficial to their well­being.
Intrinsic Motivation
Intrinsic motivation stands out as a primary technique for behavior change. Researchers
in the journal ”Nature Medicine” argue that hobbies, as a natural form of intrinsic motiva­
tion, can support the life satisfaction levels of older individuals by enhancing their sense
of purpose and providing a sense of control over their thoughts and bodies [32]. Tai­
loring interventions based on specific interests can transform external behavior change
techniques into internal ones, making participants more receptive to the interventions.

Device Design
5.1
Introduction to Device Design
When designing the prototype, the overarching goal was to create a device that would
ensure that individuals would be encouraged to drink enough water to have a healthier
hydration level. It was important for the device to make frequent reminders to the user to
drink water, as continuous water consumption throughout the day is a key factor in ensur­
ing a healthy hydration level. For the device to work as intended, it was crucial that the
users were willing to engage with it for a long enough duration to start developing habits
around drinking more water. To help keep engagement with the device, operant condi­
tioning and gamification were used to reinforce the behavior of drinking to stay hydrated
in the hopes of creating long lasting habits.
5.2
Methodology
5.2.1
Agile Development
Due to the nature of working with individuals and interactions throughout the project, an
agile approach was deemed to be the main driver of development. A more agile approach
was meant to make the project able to quickly come up with prototypes that could be tested
both on a technical level, but also with users. This would allow for potential changes to
the requirements and the planned features as to better accommodate the end users re­
quirements and experience with the device. During development, it would also be less
challenging to make changes if technologies or features were to be deemed unfeasible
than if a more static methodology such as the waterfall approach were to be used. Catch­
ing these potential feasibility issues early would also be paramount to the final prototype
as necessary shifts can be made earlier in the process. Iterations were planned around
being one month long, whilst the sprints were planned to be two weeks and consist of
tasks with similar or symbiotic functionality.
5.2.2
Incremental Modular Design
Since changes throughout the development of the device were expected, separation of
concerns was a priority from the beginning. This would allow for swift module changes,
both on a hardware and software level, to adapt to the alterations to the requirements.
This approach would also provide for easy ’plug and play’ interfaces between modules as
the code would develop through each iteration. The nature of incrementalist design can
be paired very well with agile development as they are both iterative and the decoupling
of different modules results in more flexibility as requirements change.
5.2.3
Event­Driven Architecture
The approach for how the communication between the server and the prototype should
be decoupled to meet the requirements of a modular design. Within an event­driven
system, there are producers of events and consumers of events. These events, when
produced by the producer, are sent to an event broker, which is used to exchange events
between two or more systems. A consumer listens on the event broker and when the
event broker receives an event from a producer, the consumer is notified and can then
consume the event. This approach seemed ideal, considering the anticipation that most
of the communication between the server and the prototype would be caused by events
such as the user recording their intake or the server completing its prediction of the user’s
hydration level. It also furthered the idea of decoupling and separation of concern.

When creating the initial concept for the device, it was paramount to design it in a way that

which enables users to better leverage their existing knowledge of watering real plants,
thus minimizing the learning curve. If the plant leans to the side, it illustrates to the user

6.2
Hydration level feedback scheme based on data
interaction
According to the work in Chapter 4, we discussed combining EDA and activity intensity to
predict the hydration levels 1­6 of the elderly using a neural network model. This section
will present a data interaction solution where the hydration level changes and feedback
effects are displayed on the panel when the elderly input their water intake data.
6.2.1
Prediction frequency
The optimal frequency for providing feedback became a focal point of our attention. Based
on the water intake records of two participants, with an intake frequency ranging from 5 to
10 times per day and daily activity duration between 15­17 hours (awake hours).In Chapter
2,we learned that small, frequent water intake is a crucial aspect of maintaining hydration
balance. Therefore, we have ultimately set the frequency for automatic calculation and
prediction to once every hour.
6.2.2
method of prediction
At the start of each hour, we aim to derive a hydration level based on the data from the
past hour. This level should represent the required water intake, such as level 1 requiring
more water compared to level 2. Due to the 30­minute delay in Empatica watch data,
even if participants upload data in real­time, the latest data available from the cloud is 30
minutes before. Therefore, we divided the hydration level prediction algorithm into two
UX Design of Visual Health Data Dashboard for the Elderly Based on Fluid Balance Monitoring
31
==PAGE:37==
parts:
Part I (first 30 minutes) During this time, we can obtain corresponding EDA and activity
intensity, predicting 30 hydration level data points with trained model. The mean of them
will be the result of the first part.
Part II (next 30 minutes) Since the biological data is missing, to get the recommended
water intake (i.e., predicted hydration level), we refer to the daily recommended intake for
the elderly, i.e., 1700ml for males, 1500ml for females. Combining this with their daily ac­
tivity time, we calculate the average water intake required in thirty minutes as a reference
value. Then, based on the comparison table (Table 6.1), we calculate the correspond­
ing hydration level value for this water intake requirement. The sum of these two parts,

Similarly, referencing Table 6.1, each level is has a distinct water intake requirement.

Duration

Objectives
• Record Water Intake The user should be able to, using the hardware on the plant,
be able to record the amount of water that they drink. This will be done using physical
buttons, a scale and a display. The user will put their empty water glass on the

the glass again. When the full glass of water is on the scale, the user will press

When the prototype was tested by the users, the main feedback was that weighing the

The workload throughout the first iteration was well­balanced, and the time estimation for

was pushed closer to the deadline as to make room for an unplanned third iteration. This
was deemed necessary based on the feedback and the current state of the project.

6.4.3
Iteration 3: Refinement
For the last iteration, it was important to focus on making the Aqua Bloom a standalone
item that did not require any manual processing of the estimated hydration level and it
should be completely autonomous once set up. The vision for the Aqua Bloom was for
the user to wear the watch, record their water intake and for their predicted hydration
level to be calculated and displayed to the user. Since the watch does not gather real­
time data but only gathers data up to 30 minutes ago, the last 30 minutes would have to
be accounted for by relying on the water intake reported by the user.
Duration
The time allotted for the third iteration on the project timeline was from the 1st of January
to the 28th of January, which equated to 4 weeks of work. This would consist of two
sprints, two weeks each. Overview of workflow can be seen in Appendix E.3.
Objectives

the last 30 minutes of water intake and output a predicted hydration level value. This
value should be sent to the Aqua Bloom to update the health of the plant, as well
as update the digital representation on the website. The predicted hydration level
should also be calculated every 30 minutes and update the plant health.

Challenges and Obstacles
Running tensorflow on the raspberry pi server deemed a difficult task. The raspberry pi
was running a 32 bit OS which was not supported by tensorflow. Due to this issue, the
raspberry pi had to be upgraded to run 64 bit instead and the server functionality such
as redis and the database had to be replicated again. Due to memory limitations, the
raspberry pi had to run tensorflow­lite, resulting in having to convert parts of the original
code. A side effect of having upgraded OS was more frequent freezes, so a watchman
had to be used to ensure that the server would restart in case of a crash.
Very rarely, but sometimes when the raspberry pi would attempt to move the motor into
the proper position to represent the plant health value, the servo motor would seemingly
act irrational. It would move to the wrong position even though it would indicate that it
moved to the correct value. After researching this issue, similar issues were encountered
with the same motor and the issue was generally blamed on the cheap components within
the motor. This issue could not be solved.

6.4.4
Validation Methods
To help validate the prototype at this stage, the users in the experiment were tasked with
using the Aqua Bloom over a two day period each. During this period, they were asked to
keep the Empatica watch on as much as possible and to log their water intake throughout
the day. They were instructed on how to use the Aqua Bloom and the web application for
recording water intake and understanding their hydration level. It was not specified that
they had to use the physical Aqua Bloom solution or the web application, rather that they
should use the solution that they most preferred.
After the two day period, they were asked questions from a survey regarding their expe­
rience with the Aqua Bloom in a Think Aloud approach. This was done to ensure a more
well­rounded understanding of the users experience, and that the questions on the sur­
vey were understood and answered properly. This setting allowed for the user to answer
questions more in depth or raise issues or points that were not highlighted by the survey.
The survey can be seen in Appendix C.1.
Candidate 1
Candidate 1 only interacted with the Aqua Bloom from the web application. They were
on the move for most of the duration of the two days and were therefore not able to use
the physical prototype. It was very intuitive for them to record their water consumption
with the one click interface and stated that doing this did not hinder them during their daily
activities. When they were first introduced to the concept, they expected to have to write
in water consumption amount manually but was relieved and impressed by the simple
interface. Having the Empatica watch on their wrist was a reminder to drink more often
and if the digital hydration indicator was especially low, they would drink more water at
one time. They reported that they consumed more water throughout the day and at a
continuous rate.
Candidate 2
Candidate 2 ended up only using the physical Aqua Bloom, opting not to use the web
application. Their first impressions of the Aqua Bloom was that it was easy to understand
and intuitive to operate. The device was placed close to the kitchen sink as to not forget
to record their water intake. They reported drinking more water throughout the day and
their water habits changed from a lot of water over a short period to smaller amounts at
a continuous rate. Whilst they had both the physical plant and the digital display with the
green circle to indicate their hydration level, they found themselves mostly relying on the
digital indicator as they preferred the more precise representation. The hydration indicator
was a main driver for them to drink more water. Every time they had a break, they would
check the display and if it displayed low hydration level, they would drink water when they
might not have otherwise without this indicator. After having used the device, they have
been more aware of their water consumption habits and have put more of a focus on
getting enough water throughout the day.

The candidates were then asked to repeat this behavior, but instead rely on the Aqua
Bloom as the means of recording the water intake and to read their hydration level. To

7
Conclusion
7.1
Summary of Findings
Although the results were inconclusive, by participating in the study, participants were
made more aware of their hydration level. The participants stated that they were reminded
of drinking water more frequently and the gamification of their hydration kept them more
engaged. They also preferred monitoring the digital display more than the physical plant
due to it feeling like a more precise indicator of their hydration.
7.2
Contributions to the Field
In the project, we initially proposed a non­intrusive method for detecting hydration levels,
involving the combined use of data collected from wearable devices and a pre­trained
neural network model. The accuracy of the model depends on the size of the training set
and the quality of the data. During data preprocessing, we observed a potential correla­
tion between hydration levels, electrodermal activity (EDA), and activity intensity, which
became a crucial foundation for our subsequent solutions.
Simultaneously, we introduced a concept to encourage the elderly to drink water—providing
power to mechanical plants through water consumption, restoring their ’health.’ The in­
troduction of this concept is based on principles of behavior change in the elderly and
motivations to encourage people to drink water.
Combining these two aspects of our work, we have developed a solution aimed at raising
awareness among the elderly about the need for hydration and promoting water consump­
tion through behavioral incentives within a system.
7.3
Limitations and Recommendations for Future Research
To get more conclusive results on applying interventions to encourage higher and more
consistent water consumption, a larger sample size should be utilized. Moreover, testing
over a longer duration with each individual would remove inconsistencies within their daily
routine which could lead to more consistent results.
The participants within this study would have liked more information on their hydration
level and their drinking habits. An interesting approach could be to monitor which infor­
mation to display for best water consumption behavior.
