Abstract 
The scope of this project was to assess biodegradation at a site contaminated with chlorinated ethenes. This was 
done by applying an integrated approach including traditional methods as hydrological, geochemical and 
chemical measurements as well as the new promising techniques - microarrays and compound specific isotope 
analysis. This integrated approach was composed so it should be able to document the existence, extent and 
rate of the biodegradation. 
The traditional methods indicated optimal conditions for anaerobic reductive dechlorination just downgradient 
the source zone with strongly reduced redox conditions and the presence of specific degraders and functional 
genes known for complete anaerobic reductive dechlorination. The biodegradation potential was found to 
decrease with distance from the area of optimal conditions. All chlorinated ethenes as well as their non-
chlorinated end-products were present in the aquifer providing documentation for the occurrence of anaerobic 
reductive dechlorination. The highest concentration found was 130 µg/L for TCE, 700 µg/L for cis-DCE and 120 
µg/L for VC. These maximum concentrations were found in and just downgradient the source.   
Compound specific carbon isotope analysis verified the observed trend by the traditional assessment methods, 
namely that the biodegradation was highest just downgradient the source zone and decreased with distance, as 
the δ13C values of both cis-DCE and VC decreased (i.e. became more negative). The contaminant plume of this 
site is thus non-trivial in the sense that it is opposite the commonly observed plume pattern where degradation 
occurs along the plume, and not as here were the main degradation happens in the source zone. Given these 
conditions, it was however still possible to document the extent and rate of the biodegradation in the zone of 
optimal conditions by the application of compound specific isotope analysis. This revealed that the degraded 
extent of chlorinated ethenes to the harmless non-chlorinated end-products was limited, < 5 %, in this area of 
highest biodegradation and concentrations. Contamination that has surpassed this area of optimal conditions 
will therefore most likely not be completely degraded. Consequently, even though anaerobic reductive 
dechlorinated occurs on the site, only a limited amount of the chlorinated ethenes are completely dechlorinated, 
which is important knowledge in the assessment of the risk the contamination pose.

1. Introduction 
Chlorinated solvents, e.g. tetrachloroethylene (PCE) and trichloroethylene (TCE), are among the most prevalent 
groundwater contaminants (Tiehm and Schmidt 2011). Several processes can lead to lower concentrations of 
these compounds in the groundwater, as dispersion, sorption and degradation, however only the latter leads to 
contaminant destruction (Schmidt et al. 2004; Braeckevelt et al. 2012). The most common biodegradation 
pathway of the chlorinated solvents is anaerobic reductive dechlorination via the degradation products cis-1,2-
dichloroethylene (cis-DCE) and vinyl chloride (VC) (Bradley and Chapelle 2010). Because natural degradation can 
reduce contaminant mass in the aquifer, interest has grown in using it as a cost-effective alternative to traditional 
remediation methods (Wiegert et al. 2012). However, the degradation products are also hazardous, and for 
successful remediation of the chlorinated ethenes complete dechlorination to the non-toxic end-product ethene 
is required (Kuder et al. 2013). Documentation of the existence, extent and rate of the degradation processes is 
therefore of absolute importance when using monitored natural degradation as a remediation method (Carreón-
Diazconti et al. 2009) and to conduct accurate risk assessments (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). Monitored natural 
attenuation is a clean-up and risk management remedy just as any other remedies, however this remedy is not 
designed, as other remedies would be, but is described and documented only. Site characterisation is therefore 
necessary, which typically includes an assessment of monitored contaminant concentrations and a 
hydrogeological and geochemical description. The introduction of new techniques over the last two decades, as 
polymerase chain reaction (PCR) techniques and compound specific isotopes analysis, has increased the 
knowledge on natural attenuation of chlorinated ethenes in groundwater (Wilson 2010). 
PCR is a technique to produce multiple copies of a target gene with a known sequence, thereby facilitating the 
detection of genes with an initially low abundance. The basic PCR technique gives information on the presence 
or absence of the specific gene in the sample (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). The only known bacteria capable of 
complete reductive dechlorination all the way from chlorinated solvents to ethene is Dehalococcoides (Lee et al. 
2008), and Löffler et al. (2000) were the first to publish a PCR assay for this strain (Wilson 2010). Thereby 
introducing a method to determine the presence of this important bacteria species. Later it has been argued that 
functional genes responsible for the process of interest should be used as biomarkers and not only the bacteria 
itself (Lee et al. 2008). More recent advances of the PCR technique are the introduction of quantitative PCR that 
can be used to quantify the abundance of a specific gene, and reverse transcription PCR that can be used to 
assess microbial activity. Further the introduction of microarrays makes it possible to target multiple genes 
simultaneously (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). These rapid developments in molecular techniques open new 
==PAGE:8==
 
 
7 
possibilities for description of microbial communities in the subsurface (Badin et al. 2016), and thereby enhance 
our understanding of the degradation processes occurring. 
Another promising tool for assessing in situ biodegradation of chlorinated ethenes is compound specific isotope 
analysis (CSIA) (Braeckevelt et al. 2012). Contaminants containing heavy isotopes degrade slower than those 
containing light isotopes (as the chemical bond containing the light isotope breaks easier), and an enrichment in 
heavy isotopes compared to the initial situation is therefore a strong indication that degradation occurs (Elsner 
2010). A huge benefit of this method is that physical processes such as dilution and volatilisation cause an 
insignificant shift in isotopes, which makes it possible to differentiate a decrease in contaminant concentrations 
caused by physical processes from a decrease caused solely by degradation (Morill et al. 2005). The extent of 
degradation can further be determined by applying a laboratory determined enrichment factor that relates the 
extent of isotope fractionation to degradation (Elsner et al. 2012). By comparing chlorine and carbon isotope 
ratios, i.e. dual isotope approach, it may even be possible to distinguish different degradation pathways (Badin 
et al. 2016). However, studies applying the dual isotope approach for chlorinated ethenes are scarce, as 
availability of the analytical method has been limited, and further investigations are necessary to determine the 
strength of the dual isotope method for site assessment (Wiegert et al. 2012). 
Even though these new techniques are very promising tools for biodegradation assessment, no universally 
reliable method exists at the current knowledge level, and methods providing various lines of evidence should 
therefore be combined (Bombach et al. 2010). In recent studies integrated approaches combining CSIA, 
hydrogeochemical characterisation and PCR techniques to investigate biodegradation of chlorinated ethenes 
have been applied in field scale studies (Imfeld et al. 2008; Courbet et al. 2011; Hunkeler et al. 2011; Damgaard 
et al. 2013; Badin et al. 2016). However, few studies apply integrated approaches where the dual isotope 
approach and PCR techniques are combined (e.g. Hunkeler et al. 2011; Badin et al. 2016). Further investigations 
are thus needed to assess the benefits and limitations of such an integrated approach under different 
hydrogeological settings. 
The overall scope of this project is to assess biodegradation in groundwater contaminated with chlorinated 
ethenes by applying multiple lines of evidence. Both traditional methods as chemical, hydrogeological and 
geochemical measurements and new techniques as CSIA and qPCR will be applied at field scale for this 
assessment. This project will thus investigate if an integrated approach applying CSIA and PCR techniques can be 
used to fully assess the degradation of chlorinated ethenes in a typical Danish setting with regards to 
contaminant composition, availability of boreholes and number of samples.

2. Assessment of contamination with chlorinated ethenes 
2.1 Chlorinated ethenes 
This chapter includes a description of the investigated contaminants - chlorinated ethenes. It includes a brief 
introduction to their use, properties, environmental behaviour and risk. However, the main focus is on the 
possible degradation pathways and methods to document and quantify degradation.  
2.1.1 Use and pollution 
The chlorinated solvents, PCE and TCE, have been applied for primarily dry-cleaning and degreasing, respectively 
(McCarty 2010).  Improper handling and disposal of these chemicals leads to spills causing soil and groundwater 
contamination (Wiegert et al. 2012). Chlorinated ethenes are among the most ubiquitous and serious 
contaminants of both surface and groundwater resources (McKnight et al. 2012; Palau et al. 2014), and cause a 
severe risk as PCE is a suspected and TCE a known carcinogen (Kemibrug 2017). Degradation of PCE and TCE can 
occur naturally in the environment leading to contamination with degradation products, cis-DCE and VC, that are 
also of health concern (McCarty 2010), as cis-DCE can cause damage to the nervous system and VC is a carcinogen 
(Kemibrug 2017). The fate of chlorinated ethenes in the environment is influenced by their physical and chemical 
properties (Table A 1 in Appendix A). The chlorinated solvents are slightly soluble, and as can be seen from their 
densities, the chlorinated ethenes (except vinyl chloride) are denser than water. When spilled to the soil this 
dense non-aqueous phase liquid (DNAPL) will migrate downwards until it hits an impermeable layer (McCarty 
2010). In a low permeable clayey till media the chlorinated solvents migrate through preferential flow paths such 
as fractures or lenses with higher permeability. The heterogeneity of the soil thus determines the spreading of 
the source. Low permeability layers also serve as secondary sources, as a concentration gradient between the 
preferential pathways and the matrix will cause diffusion into the matrix, that will be reversed when the 
concentration gradient turns. Long-term chlorinated solvent sources can thus exist in these low permeable media 
(Lu et al. 2011). In the saturated zone the dissolved chlorinated solvents will form a groundwater plume. As the 
Log(Kow) values for all chlorinated ethenes are less than 3 they do not sorb strongly to aquifer solids (McCarty 
2010). If the chlorinated solvent concentration in groundwater is >1% of the pure-phase solubility or >10,000 
mg/kg in the soil this indicates DNAPL presence (Newell and Ross 1992). Chlorinated ethenes are relatively 
volatile compounds (Cwiertny and Scherer 2010a), and spreading of vapor phase in the unsaturated zone is also 
a transport mechanisms for the contaminants.

2.1.2 Degradation pathways and required conditions 
Chlorinated ethenes can be degraded both biotically and abiotically in the environment (McCarty 2010). There 
are three potential degradation pathways carried out by microorganisms: Anaerobe reductive dechlorination, 
direct oxidation and cometabolic degradation (Bradley and Chapelle 2010). PCE can only be degraded 
anaerobically in natural environments (Meckenstock et al. 2004), and aerobic degradation of TCE is limited 
(Bloom et al. 2000).  
Anaerobic reductive dechlorination (Figure 1) is the primary biodegradation pathway of PCE and TCE in the 
environment (Bradley and Chapelle 2010). In this process, bacteria use chlorinated ethenes as terminal electron 
acceptors in anaerobic respiration (dehalorespiration) and hydrogen as the electron donor (Futagami et al. 2008; 
Bradley 2000). It is a sequential degradation pathway where one chlorine substituent is removed at a time and 
the non-chlorinated end-products are ethene and ethane (Hunkeler et al. 2011). TCE is almost exclusively 
dechlorinated via cis-DCE by this process (Alvarez and Illman 2006). The higher the number of chloride 
substituents on the chlorinated ethene, the more likely it is to undergo reductive dechlorination. The reduction 
of the chlorinated ethenes is thus dependent on the redox conditions (Bradley 2000).  
Several dehalorespiring bacteria can degrade PCE to TCE to cis-DCE, e.g. Dehalobacter (DHBt), Desulfitobacterium 
(DSB) and Desulfuromonas (DSM). Dehalococcoides (DHC) are the only group capable of carrying out complete 
degradation to ethene (Tiehm and Schmidt 2011). However, recent results reveal the DHC is not the only group 
capable of dechlorination the less chlorinated ethenes, as some members of Dehalogenimonas (DHG) genus also 
use VC as respiratory electron acceptor for growth (Yang et al. 2015). DHG can also be responsible for trans-DCE 
dechlorination to VC, when they contain the gene tdrA (Molenda et al. 2016). Nevertheless, the far most studied 
group is DHC, because of this strains ability to degrade all chlorinated ethenes. Which contaminants the 
Dehalococcoides degrade depends on their reductive dehalogenase genes (tceA, vcrA and bvcA), which are the 
genes coding for the proteins for respiration. The tceA gene degrade TCE and cis-DCE metabolically and VC co-
metabolically, bvcA degrade TCE co-metabolically and cis-DCE and VC metabolically, and vcrA degrade cis-DCE 
and VC metabolically (Lee et al. 2008). Dehalococcoides with these different genes (tceA and vcrA) even compete 
under conditions where the electron donor availability is limited, to the favour of the tceA comprising 
Dehalococcoides, however Dehalococcoides containing vcrA can survive under long-term (more than 2000 days) 
electron donor limitation, and again reach high levels with donor increase (Mayer-Blackwell et al. 2017). The pH 
and temperature need to be in an optimal range for the microorganisms to grow and perform (Alvarez and Illman 
2006). For Dehalococcoides dechlorination has, in an enrichment culture, been observed to be complete at 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
11 
temperatures between 10 and 30°C, to stall at cis-DCE at 4 and 40°C and to be inactivated at or above 50°C (Friis 
et al. 2007). Dechlorination has been observed to occur at a pH between 6.5 and 8.0 (Löffler et al. 2013). To 
conclude, for complete degradation by anaerobic reductive dechlorination in situ; electron donor availability, 
reduced redox conditions and a mixture of active microorganisms with the required functional genes for 
dechlorination of all chlorinated ethenes are thus necessary. It is common to see accumulation of cis-DCE and 
VC due to incomplete dechlorination (Bradley 2000) because of inadequate conditions.

In contrast to the anaerobic reductive dechlorination, the tendency to undergo oxidation increases with 
decreasing number of chlorine substituents (Bradley and Chapelle 2010). Aerobic oxidation has been observed 
for cis-DCE and VC (Broholm et al. 2005; Mattes et al. 2010). However, aerobic oxidation is not considered a 
significant degradation pathway by microorganisms in a groundwater aquifer, as the presence of degradation 
products indicates that the conditions are reduced, i.e. anaerobic, in the plume. Aerobic oxidation is therefore 
probably limited to the edge of the contaminant plume where oxygen could be present (Bradley 2000). A 
degradation pathway that could be more relevant to the anaerobic conditions present in the contaminant plume 
is anaerobic oxidation, which has been observed for DCE and VC in microcosms studies, where the degradation 
rate decreased with increasingly reduced conditions (Bradley 2000). However, it has later been discussed that 
oxidation under apparent anaerobic conditions at very low oxygen concentrations could actually be through 
aerobic pathways (Gossett 2010). The chlorinated ethenes can also be fortuitously degraded, as the degrading 
enzymes are produced for other substrates, at cometabolic oxidation (Mattes et al. 2010). Cometabolic 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
12 
degradation has been observed for all chlorinated ethenes (Tiehm and Schmidt 2011), but is not considered a 
primary pathway for microbial degradation of chlorinated ethenes in groundwater systems (Bradley 2000). 
Anaerobic reductive dechlorination is thus the only biodegradation pathway that can degrade all chlorinated 
ethenes if the conditions are favourable (Bradley and Chapelle 2010), and is the most commonly occurring 
degradation pathway for chlorinated ethenes by microorganisms (Badin et al. 2016). Often the natural redox 
conditions are not reduced enough and an accumulation of the toxic degradation products are observed. 
Therefore anaerobic/aerobic biodegradation in combination can be beneficial at less reduced conditions (Tiehm 
and Schmidt 2011).  
Abiotic transformation processes can either change the average oxidation state of carbon (e.g. reduction and 
oxidation reactions) or change the compounds without changing the average oxidation state (e.g. substitution 
and elimination processes). An example where the carbon change average oxidation state is when 1,1-DCE 
undergoes hydrogenolysis to become VC, here the carbon attached to two chlorine atoms change oxidation state 
(from two to zero) as one chlorine atom is replaced by a hydrogen atom. An example of reaction where no change 
in the oxidation state occurs is the elimination process from 1,1,2,2-Tetrachloroethane to TCE, where the average 
oxidation state of carbon remains 1. Common abiotic reductants in the natural environment are reduced iron 
species, reduced sulphur species and natural organic matter (Cwiertny and Scherer 2010b). Abiotic reductive 
dechlorination of PCE, TCE, cis-DCE and VC by pyrite and magnetite have been observed in a batch reactor system 
where the main transformation product was acetylene (Lee and Batchelor 2002). Iron oxides including green rust 
have also been shown to degrade cis-DCE via acetylene (Jeong et al. 2011). Biotic and abiotic processes can be 
hard to decouple, as microbes alternate the geochemistry by respiration, and thereby change the potential for 
abiotic processes (Cwiertny and Scherer 2010b). 
2.1.3 Methods to determine occurrence and extent of degradation  
Application of risk assessment in decision making, where the goal is to identify the sites that pose the greatest 
risk to the environment and the human health, has increased markedly over the past decades (Cushman et al. 
2001). Interest in monitored natural attenuation (MNA) as a remediation method has additionally increased, as 
the method is cost-effective compared to traditional clean-up methods (Wiegert et al. 2012). Quantification of 
complete degradation is important, as incomplete degradation of the chlorinated solvents will lead to an 
accumulation of the hazardous degradation products (Kuder et al. 2013). For MNA to be a beneficial remediation 
method it should therefore address both mother compounds and degradation products (McCarty 2010). 
Additionally, the occurrence, extent and rate of the degradation needs to be identified for natural degradation 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
13 
to be included in risk assessments (Carreón-Diazconti et al. 2009; Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). Several methods 
exist to assess the degradation processes occurring in situ, and they can be divided into ‘chemical’ and ‘microbial 
and molecular’ methods. Chemical methods include (Bombach et al. 2010):  
I. 
Geochemical approaches  
II. 
Metabolite analysis  
III. 
Tracer tests  
IV. 
Compound specific isotope analysis  
Measurements of geochemical parameters describes if the conditions are optimal for degradation, e.g. if they 
are reduced enough for anaerobic reductive dechlorination to occur. However, application of geochemical 
approaches does not provide direct indication that degradation occurs.  Metabolite measurements give, for 
chlorinated solvents, a direct indication of degradation since the metabolites are not present naturally in the 
environment (Bombach et al. 2010). Both methods are routinely applied on sites contaminated with chlorinated 
ethenes (Tsitonaki and Clausen 2016). Tracer test, where isotopically labelled analogues of the contaminant are 
introduced into the aquifer system to quantify degradation, are less frequently applied as they require extensive 
monitoring and permission by regulatory authorities (Bombach et al. 2010). Another method that can be used 
to quantify degradation and potentially determine degradation pathways is compound specific isotope analysis 
(Elsner 2010). The principles of this method will be elaborated further in Chapter 2.2. 
Microbial and molecular methods include (Bombach et al. 2010):  
I. 
Cultivation-based methods 
II. 
In situ microcosms approach 
III. 
Specific degraders and functional genes analysis  
Cultivation-based methods are laboratory microcosms with material from the contaminated field site that can 
be used to determine degradation and degradation rates. However, the microcosms only represent a small size 
of the entire aquifer and one should be careful to transfer the results directly to a large scale. In situ microcosm 
studies can also be used to determine degradation and degradation rates, and are beneficial as the degradation 
can be quantified directly in the field so that difficulties associated with laboratory microcosms (e.g. cultivation) 
are minimized. This method nevertheless has the same limitation as the laboratory microcosm, as it only 
represents a small part of the aquifer. Another cultivation-independent method is analysis for the specific 
degraders and functional genes capable of biodegradation, which provides knowledge on the biodegradation 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
14 
potential of the aquifer (Bombach et al. 2010). The analysis for specific degraders and functional genes is the 
microbial method of focus in this project. The most basic molecular target is the gene coding for the small subunit 
of prokaryotic rRNA called 16S rRNA that can easily be sequenced and which all living organisms contain. The 
presence or absence of this gene can be determined by the polymerase chain reaction (PCR) method, that can 
also target functional genes (Alvarez and Illman 2006). The PCR is a replication method applied to produce 
multiple copies of a DNA sequence and thereby enabling detection. The PCR mixture contains DNA extracted 
from the environmental sample, primers, nucleotides and polymerase. First heat is applied to separate the two 
DNA strands, and the reaction is then cooled so that primers can bind to the strands. The temperature is then 
increased again to the optimal temperature for the function of polymerase, which then starts to catalyse the 
production of the two new strands, starting where the primers are situated. By repeating this process numerous 
copies of the DNA sequence can be produced (Figure 2) (Sadava et al. 2011). The detection of the 16S rRNA gene 
alone does not provide adequate information on metabolic function, and for that reason functional genes 
responsible for respiration are additionally useful biomarkers (Lee et al. 2008). 
 
Figure 2: Schematic overview of the polymerase chain reaction (Sadava et al. 2011). 
A quantitative PCR (qPCR) method makes it possible to determine not only the presence of a specific gene but 
also the abundance. Here a fluorophore is added to the PCR reaction mixture, that is designed so that it emits 
light each time it binds to a double-stranded DNA. The amount of fluorescence is then proportional to the 
amount of DNA produced (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015).  
==PAGE:16==
 
 
15 
A new technique where it is possible to detect multiple targeted genes at the same time is microarrays, where 
numerous DNA single-strands (i.e. probes), complementary to the targeted strands, are fixed to a solid surface 
so that only targeted genes will bind/hybridize to these probes. To determine the abundance of the target genes 
by this method a florescent label is also applied (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). Microarrays implementing a qPCR 
technique can thus be applied to provide information on the abundance of specific degraders and functional 
genes, but mRNA analysis is required to reveal information on the activity (Bombach et al. 2010). The technique 
that detect and quantify mRNA is called reverse-transcriptase qPCR (RT-qPCR). Here a process called reverse 
transcription use RNA extracted from an environmental sample to create a complementary strand of DNA called 
cDNA. The cDNA is then exposed to the same qPCR procedure as explained for the DNA extracts. RNA degrades, 
in contrast to DNA, rapidly after the microorganisms dies, thereby making it possible to distinguish between 
living and dead cells (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). The detection of mRNA of a specific gene indicates that the 
microorganisms are expressing this gene, thereby saying if the specific gene is utilized (Alvarez and Illman 2006). 
By quantifying the mRNA concentration of the functional genes responsible for respiration Lee et al. (2008) 
showed that the activity of Dehalococcoides could be traced. A limitation with the PCR-based techniques is that 
only known sequences of genes can be targeted as the correct primers need to be designed. Further, the RNA-
based method may cause challenges because RNA degrades considerable more rapidly than DNA, therefore 
stringent sampling and storage is needed e.g. immediate storage in liquid nitrogen (Yargicoglu and Reddy 2015). 
To conclude, several chemical and microbial methods exist to assess the in situ degradation of contaminants, 
each with benefits and limitations, but no universally reliable method exist. Therefore, to create a more reliable 
and robust assessment various methods should be applied in integrated approaches (Bombach et al. 2010). 
Numerous studies have applied integrated approaches to assess the biodegradation of chlorinated ethenes at 
field scale (e.g. Imfeld et al. 2008; Carreón-Diazconti et al. 2009; Coubert et al. 2011; Damgaard et al. 2013) 
however only few have combined new techniques as dual-isotope analysis (see chapter 2.2.4 for explanation of 
principles) and PCR techniques (e.g. Hunkeler et al. 2011; Badin et al. 2016). An integrated approach including 
these new techniques is very promising as it can potentially determine the occurrence, extent and rate of 
degradation, which is needed in risk assessments, and further it can potentially determine the degradation 
pathway and relate it to the present specific degraders. There is thus a need to investigate the application of an 
integrated approach including these new techniques, in different hydrogeological settings, to assess its potential 
for application in risk assessment.

2.2 Isotope fractionation 
This chapter includes an introduction to the principles of isotopic fractionation and an overview of studies carried 
out using isotope fractionation for biodegradation quantification purposes. Lastly the principles of the dual 
isotope approach are introduced. 
2.2.1 Introduction to principles 
Atoms with the same atomic number (i.e. the number of protons) but different nucleon number are called 
isotopes (Atkins and Paula 2011). Stable isotopes are isotopes that do not undergo nuclear transformations, 
whereas radioactive isotopes do (Appelo and Postma 2013). The stable isotopes will be the focus of this chapter 
and relevant stable isotopes are given in Table 1, where the two first are of relevance for this project. Usually, 
the isotopic ratio (R) of stable isotopes is given as the least abundant isotope over the most abundant isotope 
expressed relative to a standard (Appelo and Postma 2013):

Isotopologues are molecules that differ in their isotopic composition alone (Braeckevelt et al. 2012). Chemical 
bonds containing the lighter isotope are weaker than those with the heavier isotope, and for that reason bonds 
with lighter isotopes preferably breaks in biological processes (Meckenstock et al. 2004). When degradation 
occurs the undegraded fraction of a compound will therefore be enriched in heavy isotopes. This shift in the ratio 
of heavy and light isotopes is called isotope fractionation (Morrill et al. 2006). An enrichment in heavy isotopes 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
17 
will lead to an increase in the isotope ratio, i.e. it becomes more positive (Elsner 2010). Physical processes as 
sorption, dissolution, dispersion and volatilization do not, in contrast to the degradation processes, induce 
significant isotopic fractionation (Morrill et al. 2005; Abe and Hunkeler 2006). Influence by sorption is only 
observed at the front of the plume where it is non-stationary (Breukelen 2007). Investigation of the isotopic 
fractionation of a contaminant can thus help differentiate between degradative and non-degradative processes 
(Morrill et al. 2005), which is important as degradation is the only process that leads to complete destruction of 
the contaminant (Schmidt et al. 2004). However, if DNAPL is present or if contaminant sorption to the sediment 
is strong, interpretations should be conducted carefully as a continuous dissolution of non-fractionated 
contaminants will mask the occurrence of degradation (Braeckevelt et al. 2012). Another process that causes 
concealing is sequential processes where compounds are produced and degraded at the same time (Morrill et 
al. 2005). The Rayleigh equation can be used to link the isotope enrichment to the extent of degradation causing

2.2.2 Studies applying isotopes to assess contamination 
In the late 1990s the first CSIA studies were published focusing on degradation of groundwater contaminants 
(Elsner et al. 2012). The first study to report documentation for complete dechlorination of chlorinated ethenes 
by stable carbon isotope fractionation analysis was Hunkeler et al. (1999), and since then it has been reported 
by others (E.g. Aeppli et al. 2010; Courbet et al. 2011; Damgaard et al. 2013). The first study to examine the 
effects of two different degradation mechanisms of chlorinated ethenes, metabolic and cometabolic, on isotope 
fractionation was Chu et al. (2004). The isotope approach has additionally been used to monitor 
bioaugmentation (Morrill et al. 2005) and to investigate effects of thermal source remediation on natural 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
20 
degradation in a downgradient plume (Badin et al. 2016). The method has also been used to differentiate 
between plumes of chlorinated ethenes of different origin (Palau et al. 2014; Kaown et al. 2014).  
To assess if dechlorination is complete, carbon isotopic mass balances have been applied in several studies. If 
ethene is the final product in the dechlorination then the δ13C value of ethene must correspond with the δ13C 
value of the mother compound when all of the mother compounds has been degraded to ethene (Figure 3) 
(Hunkeler et al. 1999). Mundle et al. (2012) showed that further degradation of ethene under both anaerobic 
and aerobic conditions is an isotope fractionation process, leading to a higher δ13C value of ethene than the 
original mother compound, and the δ13C value of ethene can thus be used to evaluate if a lack of mass balance 
at a site is due to further degradation.

2.2.3 Dual isotope approach 
To differentiate between the possible degradation pathways, a dual isotope approach has increasingly been 
applied (Badin et al. 2014). As the name indicates, the isotope ratios of two elements of a compound are 
measured simultaneously. The principle of this method is that the observed correlation between the isotope 
fractionation of the two elements is characteristic for the reaction mechanism (Abe et al 2009; Kuder et al. 2013). 
Wiegert et al. (2013) provided the first laboratory data set of combined carbon and chlorine isotope fractionation 
for biotic reductive dechlorination of PCE and TCE, while Abe et al. (2009) investigated dual isotope fractionation 
for aerobic oxidation and reductive dechlorinated of cis-DCE and VC. The first study to apply combined stable 
carbon and chlorine isotope analysis at field scale for cis-DCE degradation was Hunkeler et al. (2011), and Wiegert 
et al. (2012) was the first to apply the dual-isotope approach for PCE and TCE biodegradation on field scale. A list 
of dual isotope slopes is given in Table 3. Different degradation pathways influence the isotope ratios differently 
and thereby also the slope. E.g. at reductive dechlorination where the C-Cl bond is broken a significant Cl isotope 
effect is observed whereas oxidation is expected to cause a smaller Cl isotope effect as the C-C bond is broken 
(Hunkeler et al. 2011).  
A benefit with the dual isotope slope approach is that it is insensitive toward masking. Masking appears when 
other steps than the kinetic isotope effects becomes rate-determining (e.g. mass transfer into the bacteria cell 
and substrate binding). The slope remains constant because the kinetic isotope effect decreases the same 
proportion for both elements. Masking can have noteworthy effect on the kinetic isotope effects observed for 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
22 
one element alone, which is also why the enrichment factors are given in ranges rather than one unit, and why 
the most conservative enrichment factor should be used in risk assessment as mentioned earlier (Cretnik et al. 
2013; Elsner 2010). Despite this benefit of the dual isotope approach there are still some knowledge gaps for the 
application of the method as additional laboratory experiments are necessary to produce well-constrained dual 
isotope slopes to gain insight into reaction mechanisms and to establish reference data sets (Badin et al. 2014).

3. Site description – Vassingerødvej 105 
This chapter presents the history, hydrological and geochemical conditions and contamination for the site 
selected for field investigations. First, to select the most optimal site for investigations, four potential sites, where 
degradation had been observed, were considered. The sites were: Blokken 25 in Birkerød, Baldersbækvej 5 in 
Ishøj, Vassingerødvej 105 in Lynge and Skovlundebyvej 96A in Skovlunde. Flow, geology and contaminant 
distribution were investigated for each site before selection. The main criterion was that sampling from the 
source and along the flow line should be possible, being the minimum criterion to obtain information on 
degradation rates based on stable isotope analysis. Therefore, Skovlundebyvej 96A was excluded as the 
remediation pump installed at the site limits the plume distribution (COWI 2011; Danish Soil Partnership); 
Baldersbækvej 5 was excluded as the source area has been excavated (Orbicon 2013); and Blokken 25 was 
excluded as there are several sources with overlapping plumes, making it hard to differentiate between the two 
(COWI 2014). Therefore, Vassingerødvej 105 (Figure 4) was selected for field investigations, where boreholes 
exist in the source area and downgradient in the plume, and where further sampling and drilling were planned 
in the timespan of this project. A study of the existing data from the site was then completed more thoroughly 
for field planning purposes, and the results will be presented is this chapter as well as new knowledge gained on 
flow and geology during the project. Data presented (groundwater and soil concentrations) was kindly provided 
by Orbicon, who has conducted investigations for the Capital Region of Denmark on the site prior and parallel to 
this project.

3.1 Hydrogeology and hydrochemistry 
The regional geology on the site has been interpreted by Holm (2017). Deposits from the last ice age, on top of 
a limestone surface, characterize the regional geology. The depth to the limestone surface varies but it is 
approximately at -5 to 0 m DVR90. Above the limestone, a 30-35 m layer of glacial sand and gravel is deposited 
and above this a clayey-till layer of 2-6 m thickness. Then a second sand layer and clay layer is present, however 
in some places no sand layer exists between the two clay layers (Holm 2017).  
The local geology on the site was investigated before the field sampling, for planning purposes with regards to 
placement of new boreholes and sampling. To accomplish this a cross section from B414 to B307 was made 
(Figure A 2 in Appendix B). A geological profile was then created based on the borehole logs for the existing 
boreholes (Figure 6). This revealed a geology consisting of a fill layer with a thickness up to 5.5 m, below this a 
clay layer with gravel and sand lenses imbedded (shallow groundwater is present here) and below this a sand 
layer of unknown depth. This interpretation correlates well with the regional geology interpretation.

4. Investigative approach - methods and analysis 
This chapter introduce the investigative approach for the field study. Further, it describes the sampling and 
analysis methods for the separate groundwater samples. Field planning and sampling have been made in 
collaboration with Orbicon, and an overview of those responsible for sampling and analysis of the different 
samples is presented in Table A 3 in appendix B. 
Drilling of six out of the ten new boreholes was carried out in week 12 (20th-24th of March) and the remaining 
four were drilled in week 13 (27th-31st of March). Sampling of groundwater was conducted in week 13, starting 
at boreholes furthest away from the drilling. Selection of which of the boreholes should be sampled was based 
on the measured groundwater concentrations of the chlorinated ethenes from the previous field measurements 
conducted by Orbicon. Samples were taken from boreholes were the concentration of at least one contaminant 
was high enough for carbon isotope analysis: > 10 µg/l for TCE and cis-DCE and > 5 µg/L for VC (Hunkeler et al. 
2011). Some screens were excluded due to their position. The boreholes sampled were thus: B402, B403, B404, 
B406, B407, B408, B409, B411, B412, B413-2 and B415-2 and all new boreholes. The boreholes were clean 
pumped before sampling, using whale pumps, until the flow-cell parameters (oxygen, conductivity, pH and 
temperature) were stable. The whale pumps were also used to collect the groundwater samples. Levelling and 
water level measurements were conducted by Orbicon in week 13 and 20, respectively. Slug test were 
additionally carried out on the site by Orbicon and by a DTU student for another ongoing Masters project (Jeza 
2017).  
The goal of the sampling was; to characterise the degradation potential by determining redox conditions and the 
abundance and activity of specific degraders and functional genes; to document and quantify the biodegradation 
by chemical and stable isotope measurements; and to potentially verify the degradation pathway by the dual 
isotope approach.

For investigation of the redox conditions at the site groundwater samples were analysed at DTU for cations

This chapter has been written based on the supervision from the laboratory technicians. The cations were 
sampled in 20 ml PE (polyethylene) vials with screw cap, they were filtered with a 0.20 µm filter and conserved 
with 0.3 ml of 4 M HNO3. The acid prevents the dissolved metals from precipitating. The anions were sampled in 
6 ml PE vials, were filtered with a 0.45 µm filter, and conserved by freezing in the laboratory. The gasses were 
sampled in 6 ml evacuated glass vials where 3 drops of H2SO4 was added in the laboratory before field sampling 
and 3 ml groundwater was added to the vials in the field. The acid was added with a syringe through the septum 
in the lid. The acid is for preventing microbial activity when adding the groundwater. Groundwater for all samples 
was taken with a syringe directly from the pumping tube, and the syringe was changed/cleaned with 
groundwater from the respective borehole each time. All samples were stored in a cooling box in the field and 
brought back to the university where the gas samples were stored upside down (to avoid possible leaking from 
the lid) in a 4°C room, the cation samples in a 5°C refrigerator and the anion samples in a -18°C freezer. 
Description of anion analysis: The anion samples were taken out of the freezer 4 hours before sampling. All 
samples were filtered again with a 0.2 µm filter, as some had sedimentation still, and transferred to 1.5 ml PP 
(poly propylene) vials with screw lids with a silicone/PTFE septum. Thereafter they were transferred to an auto 
sampler with the controls and standards. The anions were analysed by an ion chromatograph (IC) with an AS22 
IC column. Sodium carbonate was applied as the eluent (mobile phase) to introduce the samples into the column. 
The anions were eluted from the column, by a change in conditions. The desorption relates to the number of 
charged groups on their surface, the more negative the anion the more retarded usually. After the column, the 
anions were detected on a conductivity detector (at suppressor mode) that measured the changes in the 
conductivity of the fluid passing through, and thereby creating the chromatogram. Two standards were run, a 
high range from 1 mg/L to 100 mg/L and low range from 0.05 mg/L to 10 mg/L. Run time pr. each sample was 15 
min. The detection limit was 0.05 mg/L for both sulphate and nitrate.

Description of cation analysis: Before analysis 10 ml of the cation samples were transferred to auto-sample glass 
vials. The samples, controls, and standard row were set in an auto-sampler. A pre-screening was made on a ICP-
MS (inductively coupled plasma with mass spectrometer) for both iron and manganese with a standard range of 
0.1 µg/L to 50 µg/L. This revealed that most concentrations were above this range, and therefore iron were 
analysed on a ICP-OES (inductively coupled plasma with optical emission spectrometer) being able to analyse 
higher concentration ranges. The standard range on the ICP-OES was between 0.25 mg/L to 30 mg/l. The samples 
for manganese analysis were diluted a factor 10 and run on the ICP-MS again, with a standard range between 5 
µg/L to 200 µg/L. The ICP part of the analysis procedures were the same for the two methods. By adding argon 
gas, thereby increasing the pressure, the sample was introduced into the system as aerosol droplets. In the argon 
plasma, the elements absorbed energy and an electron was released, thereby creating a single charged ion. In 
the ICP-OES the detectors were placed after the plasma, measuring the compound specific wavelength emitted 
when the electron dropped back to ground level. In the ICP-MS the ions were introduced into a collision cell after 
the plasma. This step is used to remove interference by other ions created in the plasma, e.g. ArO+ which has the 
same mass as iron. In the collision cell the bigger ArO+ will collide more frequently than iron thereby lose more 
kinetic energy, only the high-energy iron will thereby travel all the way through the cell. After the collision cell 
the ions were introduced to a quadrupole mass filter, which filters ions in a certain mass/charge ratio through 
only. The ions were then introduced into the detector where they created a cascade of electron releases by 
hitting dynodes, and the created pulses was counted and can be transferred to concentrations. The detection 
limit was 1 µg/L for both elements. 
Description of gas analysis: The gasses were analysed by a gas chromatograph with a flame ionisation detector 
(GC-FID). The standards, controls, blanks and samples (headspace) were injected manually (0.2 ml) with a 1.0 ml 
syringe. The manual injection was done by the same person to decrease variation in injection. The injection time 
was four minutes. The standard row was injected twice to obtain a better fit and in two ranges: low range 
methane (0.05 – 0.76 µg/L) and ethene, acetylene and ethane (0.2 – 0.4 µg/L) and high range methane (0.05 – 
0.9 mg/l) and ethene, acetylene and ethane (0.3 – 4.5 mg/L). The gasses were separated on an 8 m x 0.32 mm 
I.D x 20.0 µm film thickness HP-plot Q capillary column (Agilent Technologies). Then the samples were combusted 
in the FID thereby creating ions and the electrical signal was measured creating the chromatogram. The detection 
limits were 0.08 µg/L for methane, 0.83 µg/L for ethene, 0.29 µg/L for ethane and 0.19 µg/L for acetylene.

4.3 Stable isotopes 
The groundwater samples to be analysed for chlorine and carbon isotopes were sampled in 40 ml screw neck 
vials of clear glass with polypropylene screw cap and silicone/PTFE septum (duplicates for each isotope). 
Additionally, two 1 L glass flasks with silicone/PTFE septum in the lid were filled if previously measured 
concentrations indicated that up-concentration for the carbon analysis might be needed, as described by 
Hunkeler et al. (2011). All vials and flask was filled from the bottom allowing overflow to avoid air bubbles. The 
samples were preserved with 1 ml HCL (37 %) for the 1 L flasks and 1 drop for the 40 ml vials to obtain a pH 
around 2-3, thereby retaining microbial activity. The volume of needed acid was tested in the laboratory with 
tap water before field analysis. The samples were stored in a cooling box in the field and in a 4°C room after 
sampling until they were send to analysis. 
Analysis of the stable isotopes were performed at the University of Neuchâtel (Switzerland). The carbon isotope 
ratios were analysed for the compounds TCE, cis-DCE and VC by a gas chromatograph coupled to an isotope ratio 
mass spectrometer via a combustion interface (GC-C-IRMS). The principle of this analysis is that the compounds 
are separated on the chromatography, introduced into a combustion oven where they are converted to a 
measurement gas (CO2), and then the ratio is detected on the IRMS (Elsner et al. 2012). The limits for 
quantification of carbon isotopes for the setup with 40 ml vials were 30, 25 and 7 µg/L for TCE, cis-DCE and VC, 
respectively. Samples were measured in duplicates and reported as averages values. The carbon isotope ratios 
are reported relative to the VPDB international standard. The samples in 1 L flasks for carbon analysis and the 40 
ml vials for chlorine isotopes were not analysed within the time frame of this project.

5. Results and discussion 
This chapter presents and interpret the results from the groundwater sample analysis including; redox 
parameters, concentrations and composition of contaminants, presence of specific degraders and functional 
genes and stable isotope fractionation. Further, the results from the different methods are compared to assess 
the biodegradation at the site. 
5.1 Redox conditions 
The results from the analysis for redox sensitive parameters is presented in Table A 4 in appendix C. The redox 
parameters in the boreholes screened in the aquifer are illustrated in Figure 11, where an interpretation of the 
redox conditions is also given. The oxygen level was below 1 mg/L in all boreholes screened in the aquifer, and 
the conditions are thus considered anaerobic. The results indicate that the conditions are strongly reduced 
compared to the background levels, even though the nitrate concentrations are relatively high in some boreholes 
(0.13 to 4.0 mg/L), and the conditions are generally sulphate reducing to methane producing. The sulphate 
concentration varies from 0.10 to 30 mg/L, and the methane concentration from 0.05 to 10 mg/L. The strongly 
reduced conditions relates well with the fact that the NVOC level is relatively high in the aquifer, measured from 
5.1 to 14 mg/L (Table A 4), that could be a source for the methane production. The NVOC could originate from 
the upstream basins at Vassingerødvej 103 that once received wastewater (pers. comm. Tsitonaki 2017). Another 
less prominent source of electron donors could be the aromatic hydrocarbons, which are also present at the site 
in concentrations measured from 3 to 25 µg/L. The distribution of NVOC and hydrocarbon concentrations are 
illustrated in Figure A 13 in appendix D. Borehole B403 (nitrate reducing) and borehole B420 (iron reducing) 
deviated from the pattern indicated by the other boreholes. The non-reduced parameters measured in B403 are 
close to the assumed background concentrations thereby validating them.  
The measured redox parameters in the shallow groundwater, which the geological profiles by Jeza (2017) suggest 
is consistent between the sampled boreholes (Figure A 34), also indicate reduced conditions. As oxygen was 
measured in borehole B421-2 the other redox parameters measured in this specific borehole could suggest the 
background level in this sand lens, which leads to the conclusion that the conditions here are also sulphate 
reducing to methane producing. Due to the geological heterogeneity, one should nevertheless be careful to 
assume that these conditions are consistent. To conclude, the reduced redox conditions in the aquifer and 
shallow groundwater indicate a potential for anaerobic reductive dechlorination of the chlorinated ethenes.

observed range by van der Zaan (2010), and below the concentration where the vcrA genes were found. To assess 
why the abundance of DHC at Vassingerødvej is low when all chlorinated ethenes and non-chlorinated end-
products are observed, the following observations have been made. 
The primarily reason could of course be lower contaminant concentrations and/or limitation in electron donors 
on Vassingerødvej compared to the studies of Van der Zaan (2010). However, by comparing the samples from 
B402 it is clearly seen that the sample filtered in the laboratory (B402) have lower concentrations compared to 
the sample filtered directly at the site (B402-X). The low concentration of DHC could therefore be due to the 
application of different sampling methods. As there is only one borehole for comparison of methods it is difficult 
to determine if this is a general trend and exactly how large this difference then is, but the concentrations of 
halo-respiring bacteria and functional genes might be higher than indicated for the samples filtered in the 
laboratory (B402, B404, B420, B408, B416, B418, B412 and B411). It is assumed that the influence will be the 
same for each specific gene and that the values collected with this method therefore are comparable to each 
other, but not directly comparable to the samples collected by other methods. Borehole B404, situated just 
downgradient the source zone, thus remain the borehole with the largest magnitude of detected DHC bacteria.

Yet another possible explanation that DHC concentrations does not fit with the observed degradation pattern 
could be that the reductive dechlorination of VC is carried out by Dehalogenimonas and not Dehalococcoides. 
The DHG concentrations are higher than the DHC concentrations in most samples, but the DHG concentration 
also seems to be less influenced by the application of different methods, so the magnitudes might not be directly 
comparable. Nevertheless, the capability of DHG to carry out the reductive dechlorination of VC at the site cannot 
not be evaluated, because the analysis does not include the functional gene(s) of DHG.  
To summarise, the results specify that bacteria capable of all steps of anaerobic reductive dechlorination are 
present naturally on the site, and additionally that the functional genes in DHC needed for complete 
dechlorination of the chlorinated ethenes are present in most of the sampled boreholes. The results from the 
QuantArray-Chlor analysis do not reveal if the biomarkers are active, and the results from the samples collected 
for mRNA analysis (activity analysis) were not available within the timeframe of this project. It can therefore not 
be assessed if detected biomarkers are active. The results from the QuantArray-Chlor analysis do however 
provide an indication of biodegradation and reveal a decrease in the abundance of the biomarkers with distance 
from the source zone, the relevance of this will be elaborated further in chapter 5.5.

5.4 Isotope fractionation 
The results from the stable isotope analysis are presented in Table A 11 in appendix C and illustrated in Figure 
17. The results from the chlorine isotope analysis were not available within the timeframe of this project, and 
this chapter will address the carbon isotopes. An initial δ13C value of TCE was calculated (appendix C) by equation 
(6), as done by Hunkeler et al. (2011), because the measured value in the source area represents degraded TCE. 
It is assessed that this equation can be applied, as the fraction of the other DCE isomers and the non-chlorinated 
end-products was below one percent for each individual compound, validating a calculation of concentration-
weighted average value based on the three measured contaminants only. The calculation gives an initial δ13C 
value of -25.50 ‰. This δ13C value is bigger (i.e. less negative) than the range of pure phase TCE provided by 
Warmerdam et al. (1995) of -31.9 ‰ to -27.8 ‰, but is reasonable when compared to values presented by other 
field studies; ≈ -25 ‰ (Chartrand et al. 2005b) and -25.0 to -26.0 ‰ (Lollar et al. 2001). With this as an initial 
value, it can be observed that the δ13C value of cis-DCE in the source zone has almost reached the estimated 
initial value, indicating that most of TCE in the source has been degraded to cis-DCE. In all measured boreholes, 
the δ13C value of cis-DCE is below the initial δ13C value indicating that cis-DCE is produced in these boreholes. 
Nevertheless, degradation of cis-DCE is also occurring, documented by the presence of VC. The δ13C value of VC 
is above the initial δ13C in borehole B404 and B408 documenting that VC is further degraded to ethene in these 
(supported by the presence of ethene in this borehole). The δ13C value of VC is greater (less negative) than the 
value for cis-DCE in two boreholes (B404 and B407), which might indicate that degradation of VC is faster than 
degradation of cis-DCE in this area.

The δ13C values of cis-DCE and VC become less enriched in heavy isotopes (values more negative) further away 
from the source area, indicating that the extent of degradation is highest near the source. This plume is thus 
non-trivial in the sense that it is opposite of the common pattern observed for plumes where anaerobic reductive 
dechlorination occurs and where degradation happens along the flow line (δ13C values become less negative with 
distance) (e.g. Courbet et al. 2011; Aeppli et al. 2010). For that reason, the extent of biodegradation cannot be 
calculated in most boreholes, as the biodegradation extent in a specific borehole should be related to the value 
in the source area, and has to be less negative value than that, see equation (4). Additionally, the calculations 
are limited, because the extent of biodegradation can only be calculated for compounds were no precursors exist 
in the specific borehole. Thus, the extent of biodegradation can not be calculated for TCE or cis-DCE. It can, 
however, be calculated for VC in three boreholes (B407, B408 and B404), but can only be trusted in B408 were 
no precursors are present. In the other two boreholes the biodegradation will be underestimated, but can 
nevertheless provide a conservative estimate as discussed by Chartrand et al. (2005). Applying the lowest and 
highest enrichment factors found for anaerobic reductive dechlorination, given in Table 2, the extent of 
degradation of VC in B408 is 17-24%, in B404 it is 14-19% and in B407 it is 1-2%. Implying that even though 
degradation of VC occurs, certainly not all of it has been degraded. For the same reasons that the extent of 
biodegradation of the individual compounds cannot be calculated, the degradation rates cannot be calculated 
either. Additionally, the boreholes of comparison further needs to be on a streamline. If degradation mainly 
occurs in the source at a site, and if the source composition has not been measured earlier in the process, the 
CSIA method can therefore only to a limited extent be used to document and quantify biodegradation of the 
individual compounds, but can still be used to assess areas of highest degradation.  
In order to evaluate the state of the contaminant related carbon in downgradient points, an isotopic mass 
balance can be applied, see equation (6). In other studies (Aeppli et al. 2010; Hunkeler et al. 2011; Badin et al. 
2016) these values either correspond to the source area or are more enriched, which indicates degradation to 
non-chlorinated end-products. The δ13Csum values were also calculated for selected boreholes at Vassingerødvej, 
where the main components in the specific borehole were available by the isotope analysis. The δ13Csum value in 
B404 (-24.45‰) was more enriched than the source zone as could be expected, but the values in boreholes in 
the outskirt of the plume were less enriched; B407 (-27.09‰) and B412 (-28.37‰). As this is an isotope balance, 
this indicates that the initial δ13C value of TCE was more negative than the one observed in the source now, as it 
would otherwise imply that there has been an increase in contaminant related carbon in these boreholes. In this 
scenario comparison of the individual δ13C values for each compound to assess areas with highest degradation

Based on the isotope results from this site, the following observations were made on the application of 
compound specific carbon isotope analysis on a site with a non-stationary plume: 
I. 
The initial δ13C value of the mother compound in the source zone influence the interpretation in all the 
remaining boreholes in the aquifer and is thus of great importance. In a case like this were the main 
degradation occur in the source zone, the validity of the initial δ13C value should be considered, even 
when calculated as a concentration weighted average. When there is uncertainty about the initial δ13C 
value of the mother compound, the δ13C value for the specific compounds can still be evaluated 
individually to assess areas of highest degradation, as the starting point is the same for all contamination 
when there is only one source. 
II. 
When the δ13C values are more negative than the initial δ13C value of the mother compound, degradation 
of the specific compound cannot be documented, nevertheless that does not document that degradation 
of the compound does not occur. Recall from Figure 3 that there are indeed some grey zones, where 
production and degradation can occur at the same time, but where the δ13C value is lower than the initial 
value. The presence of degradation products can in such case help document degradation even when 
the δ13C values cannot. 
III. 
For non-stationary plumes where the main degradation occur in the source area, compounds that have 
been transported away from the source zone will retain approximately the same δ13C values. This 
knowledge can potentially be used to identify areas where degradation increase again, or to evaluate 
the effect remediation of a source zone have on the downgradient points. An example of this is the 
plume investigated by Badin et al. (2016) where a thermal remediation by stream injection in the source 
provided better conditions (by the release or organic carbon) for degradation downgradient. An increase 
in δ13C values for cis-DCE was observed in certain downgradient points, but the values remained similar 
in the front of the plume. 
Based on these observations it is recommended that in a similar case, where the same conditions are anticipated, 
that the following approach should be conducted. The goal of the isotope approach should be considered before 
field planning, as to whether it is to quantify, document or assess the degradation. Few points of measurements 
in the source zone and along the flowline could be collected initially to assess the potential of applying the CSIA 
approach at the given site. If the potential appear promising for the specific goal additional measurements in the 
aquifer could be collected.

5.5 Assessment of the biodegradation at Vassingerødvej 105 
The individual applied methods each give information that can be used to assess the natural degradation at the 
site, but when combined, they give an even more robust understanding. This chapter will evaluate the 
contamination observed in the aquifer caused by the hotspot near B402 by relating the results from the different 
applied methods. Each chapter relates back to the objectives in the scope presented in the beginning of the 
report. 
5.5.1 Biodegradation potential 
The methods to investigate biodegradation potential at the site revealed that the redox conditions are generally 
highly reduced and that specific degraders and functional genes related to anaerobic reductive dechlorination 
are present. This is promising for the natural degradation of the chlorinated ethenes by the anaerobic reductive 
dechlorination pathway. In the furthest downgradient point (B420) there is however a shift to a less reduced 
redox zone, where the conditions are iron reducing. This indicate that the redox conditions go towards less 
optimal conditions for the natural degradation in the flow direction. The same pattern was observed for the 
presence of the biomarkers in the flow direction, which also decreased in abundance with distance from the 
source zone. The boreholes where DHC constitutes the highest proportion of the total eubacteria population and 
where the VC reductase genes are dominating, are the boreholes directly downgradient the source zone (B404 
and B412). Hence, the biomarkers are prominent in boreholes situated in the main body of the groundwater 
plume, whereas their presence in boreholes situated in the outskirt of the plume (B408, B411 and B418) is 
comparably lower. The microbial screening thus revealed that degradation of contaminants in the outskirt of the 
plume will likely be limited at the current conditions. The limited number of biomarkers in this part of the plume 
could be due to donor competition with other bacteria present on the site and/or because the bacterial 
community have not had time to adapt to the contamination reaching them. A long lag-time (time before 
bacteria act to the contamination they are exposed to and until they start actively using the contaminants as 
growth-supporting electron acceptors) can be of concern in relation to contaminant spreading (Alvarez and 
Illman 2006). At this specific site, it is therefore beneficial that the groundwater flow velocity is relatively slow in 
the aquifer, giving the microbial community more time to adapt, and it is further beneficial that the redox 
conditions favours adaption in most parts of the contaminated aquifer.  
To summarize, when comparing the redox conditions with the microbial population it is apparent that there is a 
decrease in natural degradation potential with distance away from the source zone. It is further apparent that 
concerning the degradation potential the abundance of biomarkers is more critical than the redox conditions. 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
53 
This stress the importance of screening for biomarkers when the redox conditions are similar in the contaminated 
area to achieve a complete understanding of the biodegradation potential. The efficiency of the biodegradation 
downgradient near the source zone, where the degradation potential and the contaminant concentrations are 
highest, is thus of great importance with regards to reducing the risk associated with contaminants surpassing 
the source zone.  
5.5.2 Documentation of degradation 
Investigating the degradation potential at a contaminated site thus provides an indication of what could 
potentially occur, and helps define areas of importance. However, other methods are necessary to document 
the contaminant degradation. The presence of the degradation products cis-DCE and VC is a direct 
documentation for anaerobic reductive dechlorination at the site, which is further supported by the increased 
level of non-chlorinated end-products in the aquifer downgradient the source zone. The extent of the 
contaminant plume is limited to a relatively small area of the aquifer, where a shift in contaminant composition 
to less chlorinated compounds is observed. However, the promising pattern indicated by the degree of 
dechlorination and the mole fractions along the flow line (Figure 14), might be exaggerated due to difference in 
the mobility of the compounds. When comparing the contaminant spreading with the degradation potential it 
can be observed that the dominance of less chlorinated degradation products in the outskirt of the plume 
appears to be due to their higher mobility, and therefore not a representation of the chlorinated solvents being 
completely dechlorinated. The plume composition of mainly degradation products of TCE indicate that 
degradation of the mother compound is not the limiting step in the complete dechlorination, which is also 
reflected by the high abundance of TCE respiring bacteria in the aquifer. As mentioned, the presence of the bvcA 
and vcrA genes correlates with the downgradient area, where the highest concentrations in the plume are. In 
contrast, the highest concentrations of VC are observed outside this area, thereby indicating areas where 
complete degradation is less prominent. This observation is supported by the presence of non-chlorinated end-
products dominantly in the centre of the plume.  
In addition to the documentation of degradation by the presence of the chlorinated solvent’s degradation 
products, another method to document the degradation is the compound specific isotope analysis approach. 
The results from this report stress the importance of determining the correct initial δ13C value of the mother 
compound, as data from all other boreholes is related to that value. A carbon isotope mass balance revealed that 
the δ13C value of the mother compound must have been lower initially than the one observed in the source, 
which can be counterbalanced by calculating a concentration weighted average value for the source. However, 
==PAGE:55==
 
 
54 
the observed degradation occurring in the vertical direction towards the aquifer, might even complicate the 
calculation of such a value. This uncertainty means that some points, were documentation of degradation might 
have been possible given the correct initial value, could potentially now be overseen. The range of the measured 
δ13C value for cis-DCE is close to the initial δ13C value and a small change in the initial value can therefore change 
the conclusion of the approach. However, the fact that the calculated initial value might be larger (i.e. less 
negative) than the true value does not change the conclusion for the individual compounds in boreholes were 
degradation could be documented. The determination of the initial value is thus of great importance for the 
application of CSIA, and it is recommended in case CSIA is considered applied at a site, to measure the isotopic 
composition in the source as early as possible.  
What the isotope results can do, are supporting the statement that degradation is restricted to near the source 
zone, whereas the biodegradation potential could only imply it (Figure 18). The isotope results indeed reveal that 
the δ13C values decrease (i.e. become more negative) with distance from the source zone for both cis-DCE and 
VC, following the expected trend based on the microbial screening. However, because most values are below 
the assumed initial value in most boreholes, it cannot be assessed exactly which compound (precursor or 
metabolite) is degraded here. The reason being, referring back to Figure 3, that when the values are below the 
initial value, it cannot be separated whether the increase in the δ13C values is because the degradation products 
are being produced or because they are degraded themselves, as both processes would lead to an increase in 
weight (i.e. the δ13C value becomes less negative). This observation could explain the almost constant δ13C value 
of cis-DCE between borehole B402 and B404, where in contrast a decrease in the concentration is observed, 
simply by the fact that the numbers represent two different processes (i.e. production of cis-DCE in the source 
and degradation of cis-DCE in the downgradient point). This is possible given the fact that cis-DCE is produced 
before it reaches the aquifer, and the earliest produced cis-DCE reaching the aquifer first would therefore have 
been the lightest. The degradation in the vertical direction thus complicates the interpretation of the results. 
To summarise, an increase (i.e. becoming less negative) in δ13C values indicate degradation, and can be used to 
evaluate the potential for biodegradation, even when it cannot document it. This gives a stronger indication of 
degradation potential than the microbial screening, in the sense that a change in isotope composition must be 
induced by degradation, whereas the presence of biomarkers does not, in contrast, guarantee that degradation 
occur. The isotope results show the same pattern as the biodegradation potential methods, namely that the 
biodegradation near the source zone is of great importance with regards to limiting the contaminant spreading 
further downstream. It was documented by carbon isotope mass balances that a limited amount of the 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
55 
chlorinated ethenes introduced into the aquifer was degraded to their non-chlorinated end-products within the 
area of highest biodegradation potential (B402B404), and the degradation of cis-DCE appears to be the limiting 
step in the dechlorination process.

By comparing the results from the individual methods applied on the site, the importance of applying an 
integrated approach for a more robust documentation of biodegradation becomes apparent. A schematic 
overview of the knowledge gained by the addition of each method, also the planned methods that was not

==PAGE:58==

Measurements of contaminant concentrations can provide a partial insight into the degradation extent and 
pathway. The presence of degradation products provides documentation for degradation, however, it cannot be 
used to assess the extent, as transport processes will influence the observed pattern. Additionally, only 
degradation products that are not rapidly degraded can be used as indicators. The limitations when looking solely 
on the contaminant distribution becomes apparent at this site, as one could get the erroneous impression that 
almost complete reductive dechlorination appears downgradient the source zone. However, when comparing 
the contaminant concentrations with the results from methods used to determine the degradation potential 
(QuantArray-Chlor and redox conditions) it is indicated that this is not the case, as the degradation potential 
decrease with distance from the source zone, and the observed pattern is therefore likely transport induced. The 
microbial screening give a more robust indication of biodegradation than the redox measurements, and the latter 
should therefore be applied to support the conclusion of the screening instead of being a stand-alone method. 
The redox measurements can assist in defining areas and microbes of importance by the assessment of 
aerobic/anaerobic conditions. Nevertheless, without analysis of activity, documentation of degradation cannot 
be done based on the DNA based Quantarray-Chlor results.  
The assessment method in this integrated approach capable of documenting degradation is the compound 
specific isotope analysis. The carbon isotope fractionation revealed the same decrease in degradation potential 
as was indicated by the methods used to determine degradation potential. Both the microbial screening and the 
stable isotope approach can thus be applied to assess areas of highest degradation, however the first give an 
indication the latter a documentation. At a complex site like this, where the main degradation occurs at the 
source zone (vertically and horizontally), the possibility of quantifying biodegradation by the stable isotope 
analysis is limited, and application of both the QuantArray-Chlor analysis and the CSIA approach give a more 
robust understanding of the biodegradation. The microbial screening provide information on the potential 
degradation pathway(s) of relevance and the isotope fractionation documents that the biodegradation is indeed 
occurring. Another assessment method that could potentially have provided information about the degradation 
pathway(s) is the dual isotope approach by the analysis of chlorine isotopes. This method (not available for the 
assessment of this project) could have helped defining if other though less prominent degradation pathways 
occurred at the site, which could have helped assessing the site-specific contaminant elimination. The remaining 
carbon samples yet to be analysed in B416 and B417 (1 L flasks) could potentially also provide further information 
with regards to defining the initial value of TCE, that could also provide a more robust documentation of 
degradation. Nevertheless, it is not assessed that critical information was lost due to the lack of these data. 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
58 
Because of the complex settings at Vassingerødvej 105, where degradation both occur vertically and horizontally, 
the complete yield of the integrated approach cannot be obtained, as degradation could only to a limited extent 
be quantified. The results imply that the hazard the contamination pose at the given moment is limited to a small 
area, which is beneficial for remediation purposes. Nevertheless, it appears that some of the contamination has 
surpassed the area of highest potential for biodegradation, and might not be sufficiently degraded, which would 
with time expand the area that pose a risk. It was possible to calculate one degradation rate from the 
transformation of the total chlorinated ethenes to the non-chlorinate end-products within the area of highest 
degradation potential. The degradation rate revealed that only limited complete dechlorination occurred in the 
zone. This result provide enriching knowledge with regards to risk assessment, as it implies that even though 
dechlorination occurs, only a limited amount is transformed to the harmless end-products. The results from the 
integrated approach therefore provide the needed information for risk assessment even though the 
quantification of biodegradation is limited. In order to evaluate the risk for downgradient waterworks, the data 
provided by the integrated approach, should be combined with modelling approaches, which is outside the scope 
of this project. It is worth mentioning that if this site is considered to pose a risk and is prioritized to be cleaned 
up, it would be beneficial to do it as soon as possible, due to the relatively limited spreading of the current plume. 
The results from the integrated approach even indicate that biostimulation (addition of electron donors) might 
be a remediation strategy of choice, as the necessary biomarkers are present naturally on the site.  
5.5.4 Ideas for future research 
This chapter proceeds what could be learned from the incorporation of the integrated approach on the actual 
site to evaluate future needs of research to improve the general application of the integrated approach. 
In order to fully understand the potential of biodegradation based on the microbial assays, the influence of the 
individual bacteria groups in the microbial community should be comprehended. Bacteria that have no direct 
influence on the biodegradation of the contaminants of interest might have a competitive influence on e.g. donor 
availability. This is an area where research is ongoing and as knowledge is gained it should be implemented in 
designing appropriate of micro arrays. An example of this is the discovery by Yang et al. (2015) that the bacteria 
group Dehalogenimonas can use VC as a respiratory electron acceptor for growth, which makes it relevant to 
identify the functional gene(s) capable of this, and further to incorporate it in the micro arrays for screening. To 
summarize as more knowledge is gained on the microbial populations it should be assessed which bacteria 
should be included in the screening. 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
59 
Concerning the application of compound specific isotope analysis on a site where both vertical and horizontal 
degradation occur in the source zone, it should be evaluated exactly what knowledge the isotope fractionation 
results can provide, in order to assess the complete benefit of applying it. To obtain this knowledge more 
investigations are required on sites with similar settings. The approach might, for an example, be strengthened 
by sampling from the shallow groundwater in the source area, to assess the reliability of the initial δ13C value of 
the mother compound, and to incorporate the influence of degradation in both directions into the assessment.  
What is needed to strengthen the application of integrated approaches to assess biodegradation is thus 
investigations from sites with different and similar settings to develop a sufficient background knowledge level 
for application of these methods. This is necessary in order to improve the certainty in the interpretation of the 
results.

6. Conclusion 
The integrated approached applying qPCR and CSIA techniques revealed that natural biodegradation of 
chlorinated ethenes, through the anaerobic reductive dechlorination pathway, occur at the site. The 
biodegradation potential was found to decrease with distance from the source zone. The redox conditions were 
assessed to be mainly sulphate reducing to methanogenic, but reached iron reducing conditions in the most 
downgradient point from the source zone. The highest abundance of biomarkers was documented in the centre 
of the plume just downgradient the source area, however, it too decreased further downgradient. The chemical 
analysis revealed that all chlorinated ethenes, TCE, DCE (mainly cis-DCE) and VC, as well as their non-chlorinated 
end-products ethene and ethane, were present in the aquifer. The highest observed concentrations was 130 
µg/L for TCE and 700 µg/L for cis-DCE in the hotspot, whereas the highest concentration of VC at 120 µg/L was 
found downgradient the source zone. The presence of the degradation products, for the originally spilled TCE, is 
a direct indication that anaerobic reductive dechlorination occurs.  
Compound specific stable carbon isotope analysis supported the observed pattern from the biodegradation 
potential methods – that the main degradation occur near the source zone and decrease with distance, as the 
δ13C values for cis-DCE and VC became more negative. This site is thus non-trivial in the sense that the plume 
trend is opposite the commonly observed pattern of degradation increase along a flow line. The documented 
fraction of degraded VC was approx. 20% near the source zone, documentation of cis-DCE degradation could not 
be provided but this does not imply that it is not occurring. The presence of VC in the aquifer is a direction 
indication that cis-DCE is degraded further. A quantification of degradation in the area of highest biodegradation 
potential, just downgradient the hotspot, revealed that limited degradation of the total chlorinated ethenes to 
the harmless non-chlorinated end-products occurred. Consequently, even though anaerobic reductive 
dechlorination clearly occurs at the site, only a limited amount of it is completely dechlorinated. The integrated 
approach could thus successfully be used to assess the current biodegradation at the site.  
The investigation, however, did reveal areas where the approach could be improved. Further research should 
focus on improving the microbial assays, e.g. by the inclusion of functional gene(s) responsible for VC degradation 
in Dehalogenimonas. Additionally, on creating a theoretical background for the application of the CSIA approach 
when the main degradation occur at the source (vertically and horizontally). The results further emphasised that 
no stand-alone method exist to assess biodegradation in a non-trivial contaminant setting like this. Nevertheless, 
the application of integrated approaches incorporating qPCR techniques and stable isotope analysis appears 
promising for the use of evaluating natural biodegradation of chlorinated ethenes.

Evaluation of plume length: 
Darcy’s law, equation (9), was applied to calculate the flow velocity between borehole B409 and B406 to 
represent the flow velocity in the aquifer. These boreholes were selected as they are the only comparable 
boreholes situated on the assumed flow line with measured hydraulic conductivities. The applied hydraulic 
conductivity is an average between that measured in each borehole by the slug tests carried out by Jeza (2017).

Applying the site-specific retardation factor for TCE of 1.54 by Jeza (2017), and to begin with ignoring the vertical 
transport time to the aquifer, the plume of TCE could have reached a length of approx. 50-80 m (covering the 
span of production from 1992-2001). That is if retention would not have occurred in the vertical direction, 
however by assuming that the documentable plume length of approx. 20 m is representable, a back calculation 
reveal that the vertical travel time through the overlaying layers would have had to be approx. 10-19 year 
(breakthrough to aquifer in 2011). Which is somewhat reasonable taking into consideration the assumed lower 
permeability of the supra-glacial facies matrix and the subglacial till. A breakthrough to the aquifer in 2011 would 
cause a maximum cis-DCE plume length of 26 m (applying the retardation factor of 1.11 by Jeza (2017)) and a 
maximum VC plume with a length of 29 m (not taking the limited retardation into account) if they were produced 
immediately. It is thus assessed that the documentable plume length based on the groundwater measurements
