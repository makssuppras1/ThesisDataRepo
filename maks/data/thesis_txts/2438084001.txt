Abstract 
 
  
Since the 1990s, Denmark’s construction industry has witnessed significant changes to improve 
production and innovation. In recent years, with increasing globalization and constantly changing 
customer demands, the focus has been on partnering and strategic partnerships as pioneered by the 
American and British construction industries. However, the Danish construction sector is faced with a 
predicament. Should they embrace the global trend to achieve economies of scale by developing 
innovative solutions? Alternatively, should they remain independent flexible organizations with the 
freedom to respond to changing demands and opportunities in the market?   
This Master Thesis aims to identify the “hidden” barriers that may further burden the industry with 
this dilemma, thus inhibiting strategic partnerships. An empirical survey research methodology has 
been adopted to identity the barriers to process innovation under three proposed domains – Interest, 
Understand and Practice.   
The qualitative analysis revealed the predominant interest and noteworthy understanding of strategic 
partnerships by the industry practitioners. Conversely, it also revealed the incompetency of the 
industry to implement efficiently the desired practices of procurement. Substantial differences in 
price-based appraisal of contracts were found between the initial and final stages of procurement, 
indicating the dominance of price in procurement decisions. Furthermore, this suggested the absence 
of barriers in the Interest and Understand domains, but the presence of barriers in the Practice 
domain.  
From a Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) perspective where procurement decisions establish 
governance forms, the Danish construction procurement practices need to be governed by trust 
instead of price to realize the spectrum of benefits associated with strategic partnerships. Therefore, 
inter-organizational relationships need to be characterized by the constructs of trust, i.e. Closeness, 
Openness, Honesty and Dependability, as the absence these are the “hidden” barriers to the successful 
implementation of strategic partnerships leading to the synergistic growth of business and society.

1. Introduction 
 
This chapter introduces the background and the problem statement along with the formulation of 
the research objective and questions, which define the scope of this Master Thesis. 
 
1.1 Background 
 
The Danish building and construction industry is one of the most significant business communities of 
the country and accounts for approximately 80% of the national wealth (Dansk Byggeri, 2016b). The 
economy has grown by 78% since the 1980s, with an energy consumption that has more or less 
remained the same (National & Fund, 2017). In 2010, the Danish Commission on Climate Change Policy 
established the idea of transforming Denmark into a fossil fuel independent society, thereby initiating 
Energy Strategy 2050 (The Danish Government, 2011). Buildings currently consume 30% to 40% of the 
total energy consumed by the country (Grøn vækst og bæredygtighed Dansk Byggeris klima- og 
energipolitik, n.d.). 
The repair and maintenance sector is a significant part of the Danish construction industry (40%) 
(Conference, Con, & Trends, 2015), which has been partly driven by large investments in social housing 
renovation (Dansk Byggeri, 2016a). This has created a drive for the efficient use of the large 
investments through a community partnership called REBUS (Renovating Buildings Sustainably). 
REBUS has been set up in Denmark with the aim of developing and implementing innovative solutions 
to improve building renovation for social housing. Three work packages have been set up to achieve 
this goal, which have been aligned with the 2050 energy goal, through 50% reduction in energy 
consumption, 30% reduction of resource consumption and 20% increase in productivity (for more 
details see section 1.5). This Master Thesis aims to contribute to the work package concerning ‘New 
Strategic Partnership and Business Models’.  
Strategic partnership or strategic collaboration is a process innovation emerging from a diverse 
cultural setting (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007). It is not exclusive to any country, continent or 
culture. Partnering has been applied for some time now in the US, UK and South-East Asia, and has 
reached a mature state. However, it is still in its infancy in many Nordic countries (Bygballe, Jahre, & 
Swärd, 2010). Only a limited number of pilot partnered projects were implemented in Holland and 
Scandinavia by the beginning of the 21st century (Skeggs, 2003). 
Since the 1990s, the Danish construction industry has been under significant change due to the 
implementation of a wide array of legislations and public policies that have been initiated to improve 
production and innovation. Lately, with frequent changes in consumer demands and increasing 
globalization, focus has been on the concept of partnering and strategic partnerships. This has been 
primarily driven by the cost minimization agenda put forward by the National Agency of Enterprise 
and Construction and the Ministry of Social Affairs (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006). To further advocate 
partnering in Denmark, an official partnering guideline, Erhvervs- og Boligstyrelsen was produced in 
2004 and a performance measurement unit, Byggeriets Evaluerings Center was launched (Kadefors, 
2013). However, the financial crises of 2008 led to a decline in the construction activity (Nistorescu & 
Ploscaru, 2010). The Danish construction industry has been on a revival path with a 17.2% increase in 
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
 
productivity, between 2010 and 2016. The growth is further predicted at 3.5% in 2017, 3.2% in 2018, 
3.5% in 2019 and 2.4% in 2020. However, the fragmented value chain and the highly stringent 
regulatory environment for construction service providers still affects the productivity and the 
competitiveness (Eurostat, 2017).  
 
1.2 Problem statement 
 
In Denmark, the construction industry is facing a paradox. On one hand, the sector is influenced to 
embrace strategic partnerships to develop innovative solutions due to consumer demands, 
globalization and the desire to achieve economies of scale. On the other hand, construction service 
providers demand flexibility to adapt to rapidly changing customer demands and the ability to respond 
to new opportunities as they arise (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006). Construction companies may not 
recognize key “hidden” barriers to partnerships that may further deepen the dilemma that they face 
today, thus inhibiting strategic partnerships.   
 
1.3 Research questions 
 
The research objective of this Master Thesis is: 
Identifying the hidden barriers to strategic partnership in the Danish 
construction industry 
To approach this research objective, the following research questions have been formulated: 
• 
Is the industry interested in strategic partnerships? 
• 
Does the industry understand the implications of strategic partnerships on business and 
society? 
• 
Does the industry implement the existing procurement strategies efficiently?

1.5.1 About the project 
 
REBUS or Renovating Buildings Sustainably is a community partnership representing the entire value 
and knowledge chain of the building sector. The partnership brings together manufacturers, 
contractors, engineers, architects, universities, RTO and social housing building owners to develop the 
knowledge, method and tools for a radical change in renovation.  The project aims to transform 
sustainable building renovation into a productive business with export potential by developing energy 
reducing, cost effective, resource efficient and reliable renovation solutions to benefit the 
environment, employment, indoor climate and innovation in the construction industry. The project 
targets reduction in energy consumption by 50%, reduction in resource consumption by 30% and

following work packages (WP) (“REBUS - Renovating Buildings Sustainably,” n.d.):

Development of forms of strategic partnerships/ collaborations with framework agreements 
that ensure the development of long-term, flexible and optimal, planning and management 
strategies in renovation projects. 
• 
WP2: New Holistic Renovation Methods from Planning to Operation 
Development of innovative technical solutions in the industry for façades and installations. 
• 
WP3: The Façade as the Construction Site 
Development of digital performance models to document and evaluate efficiency of 
renovations with emphasis on good indoor climate and functionality.

2. Methodology 
 
This chapter describes the design and methodology that has been adopted to address the research 
objective along with an overview of the steps taken to answer the research questions. This chapter 
concludes with the limitations to this research. 
 
2.1 Research design 
 
To approach the research objective, three important domains of barriers have been proposed which 
may inhibit process innovation in the construction sector. These are Interest, Understand and Practice. 
The reasons behind the selection of these three domains are as follows: 
• 
Interest 
Interest of the industry drives the shift to process innovation. 
• 
Understand 
Lack of a proper and complete knowledge base leads to misunderstanding and unrealistic 
expectations. 
• 
Practice 
In accordance with the interest and information base, business practices need to change for 
successful implementation of process innovation. 
These domains draw similarities to the domains of Know, Understand and Use, which have been used 
by Pont and Oliveira in their study. The difference being that their study focused on the barriers to 
market innovation (García Pont & Rocha e Oliveira, 2012).   
The research questions were formulated such, as to explore these three domains through an empirical 
survey. It is important to note that the first research question is simple and straightforward, and 
aligned with the Interest domain, which is addressed in the survey with a single question. The second 
research question regarding the understanding of the implications has been addressed in the survey 
with multiple questions. These questions address the Understand domain by evaluating the 
perceptions of industry practitioners on the various effects, benefits and barriers for strategic 
partnerships. However, as strategic partnerships have neither been incorporated into the entire value 
chain of the industry nor have been practiced for any significant time period, it is difficult to evaluate 
the practice of this. As a primary change advocated with strategic partnership is the shift to 
collaborative procurement processes, the third research question attempts to explore Practice by 
evaluating the practice of the existing procurement strategies in the survey.

Survey design 
 
The empirical survey design has been partly based on the findings in the literature that has been 
reviewed as part of this Master Thesis and partly based on a workshop, REBUS debatmøde. The 
workshop was conducted on 19th September 2017 at the Dome of Visions in Aarhus with over 50

The survey has been divided into the following three sections: 
1. Baggrundsoplysninger (Background information) 
This section of the survey deals with the basic information of the survey respondent and the

2. Udbud og kontrakter (Procurement and contracts)

This section attempts to gather information on the viewpoints of industry practitioners on 
strategic partnership or strategic collaboration. It focuses on the effects, benefits and barriers

As REBUS focuses on Denmark’s construction industry alone, the survey has been formulated in 
Danish. The complete survey can be found in the Appendix (Appendix A). An English translation of the

2.3 Limitations 
 
The major limitations of this study are in relation to the empirical survey responses. The sample size 
of 49 responses to the survey is petite compared to, the more than 70,000 companies which are 
present in the construction sector in Denmark as of 2016 (Eurostat, 2017). Of these, multiple 
responses have been recorded from a single company, which results in a smaller count of the number 
of organizations truly participating in the survey. The responses were also found to be 
disproportionate in terms of organizational category, i.e. contractors dominated with about 57% 
responses, while the responses from other categories varied between 2% to 15%. Due to the small 
and askew sample, perspectives recorded in this survey may not be representative of all the 
companies in the industry. This has also restricted the use of more complex statistical methods to 
understand possible interrelationships that may exist between factors.

In addition, the validity of responses strongly influences the results that have been achieved. To begin

Furthermore, the findings of this study could not triangulated with qualitative research interviews, as

3. Literature Review 
 
This chapter constitutes an extensive review of the literature associated to the main research area – 
Strategic Partnership in the construction industry. The review examines the studies performed in 
context to relational contracting, strategic partnerships and its requirements, benefits and barriers. 
Lastly, the chapter concludes with strategic partnerships in Denmark based on the limited literature 
available. 
 
3.1 Origin of construction partnership 
 
Over the last few decades, interest in collaborative arrangements in the construction industry has 
increased substantially all over the world. The concept of partnering was introduced in the 1980s by 
the United States Army Corps of Engineers as part of the Construction Industry Institute task force 
(Kadefors, 2013). In the early 1990s due to recession, the United Kingdom saw a decline of 39% in the 
construction industry output by 1993, as compared to the 1990 peak (Latham, 1994). Concerned with 
the productivity, the UK government initiated a change process beginning with the Latham report in 
1994 which was followed by the Egan report in 1998 (Kadefors, 2013). According to Egan, client 
dissatisfaction and the fragmented nature of the construction industry are the primary reasons for the 
need for improvement. The experiences and success of other industries, such as car manufacturing, 
steel making, grocery retailing, offshore engineering etc., with partnering strongly inspired the 
achievement of continuous improvement. This was possible through performance measurement and 
close long term relationships (Egan, 1998).  In 2003, inspired by the development of the UK 
construction industry partnering was agreed upon as the default model for government funded 
projects in Denmark. However, the 2008 financial crises led to the decline in commitment to 
partnering as well as the number of partnered projects (Kadefors, 2013). 
The following sections delve deeper into the types of collaborations that exist in the market and, 
where relational contracting and strategic partnerships lie with respect to the continuum of 
contracting relations.  
 
3.2 Types of collaboration 
 
According to Williamson, Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) is a theory of governance of how complex 
business transactions are structured in challenging decision environments. Market, Hybrid and 
Hierarchy are different forms of governance structures with varying magnitude of the key attributes: 
incentive intensity, administrative controls, adaptation and contractual law (Williamson, 1999). In 
other words, whether a company makes (Hierarchy or full vertical integration) or buys (Market or one 
time transaction) its basic inputs defines the strategy of collaboration between different 
organizations. The Market offers a great extent of flexibility in terms of choosing and changing 
partners as per the rewards offered in a short-term perspective. Whereas, Hierarchy – full vertical 
integration – offers the most beneficial way to coordinate activities. Hybrid governance forms lie

somewhere in between the continuum with combined attributes of Market and Hierarchy  
(Thomassen & Jørgensen, 2013).

Studies have identified and characterized contractual relation in the construction industry. Primarily 
four types of owner-contractor relationships have been identified, namely adversarial, guarded 
adversarial, informal partnership and project partnership. These reflect the level of cooperation and 
collaboration between them. Adversarial relations are dominated by mistrust and self-interest. 
Whereas, the guarded adversarial relationships involve treating other organizations as necessary but 
suspicious allies. In informal partnering, organizations work together cooperatively. Formal 
partnerships is featured with committed cooperation, common objectives and collaborative problem 
solving (Drexler & Larson, 2000; Larson, 1995).  
 
3.3 Relational contracting 
 
The construction industry has been fragmented, competitive and adversarial with deep rooted 
problems leading to low productivity (Egan, 1998; Latham, 1994). These problems include antagonistic 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
 
relations between organizations, poor information exchange and contracting styles (Kwawu & Hughes, 
2005). Over the years, the UK construction industry has repeatedly criticised the traditional arm’s 
length relationship between organizations and advocated a change from traditional procurement 
methods to more collaborative and integrated procurement methods (Egan, 1998; Latham, 1994). 
Relational contracting is a contracting system/ tool that gives explicit recognition to the commercial 
relationship between organizations. They are essentially different forms of collaborative approaches 
to procurement that are based on trust, cooperation and commitment to foster long term working 
relationships with less importance given to the legal agreement (Colledge, 2005). In recent years, 
these commercial relations have emerged in the form of project partnerships, strategic partnerships, 
supply chain management, joint ventures, lean construction etc. (Kwawu & Hughes, 2005).  
The concept and values of relational contracting align with the economic concept of Hierarchy. It 
creates value for those involved in a project by forming commercial relationships that foster mutual 
trust and, facilitate knowledge sharing and innovation. The creative application of this knowledge 
gives a competitive advantage to those involved. This leads to the achievement of project success in 
terms of time, cost and quality objectives, despite the complexities and the challenges. Significant 
sharing of all project risks and benefits is an important feature of relational contracting. Additionally, 
the connection and alignment of commercial and social corporate agendas is vital to the creation of 
sustainable communities. Therefore, relational contracting creates value for the involved stakeholders 
beyond the project to the broader industry and community (Colledge, 2005).   
 
3.4 Strategic partnership 
 
The construction industry differs from most of the other production industries. The unique, 
unpredictable and changing production site conditions along with the fragmented nature of the 
traditional construction processes, creates problems with regards to construction schedule, cost and 
quality (Bayramoglu, 2001). The UK construction industry identified exploitation, rigid specifications, 
decisions made with limited knowledge and short-term focus as primary reasons for the failure of 
traditional adversarial relationships (Black, Akintoye, & Fitzgerald, 2000). This has strongly advocated 
the shift towards strategic partnerships from traditional adversarial relations by many as the new 
game plan for the improved operation and working of the construction industry with an increase in 
productivity (Construction Industry Institute, 1991; Egan, 1998; Larson, 1997; Latham, 1994). 
Partnering coordinates the efforts of various parties from the design stage which reduces redundant 
costly designs and offers technologically feasible and innovative solutions thereby, reducing 
unforeseen risks during implementation. It also encourages the selection of contractual partners 
based on qualitative criteria rather than lowest price for products and services (Bayramoglu, 2001). 
 
The Construction Industry Institute task force describes ‘partnering’ as, 
“A long-term commitment between two or more organizations for the purpose of 
achieving specific business objectives by maximizing the effectiveness of each 
participant’s resources. This requires changing traditional relationships to a 
shared culture without regards to organizational boundaries. The relationship is 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
 
based on trust, dedication to common goals, and an understanding of each 
other’s individual expectations and values. Expected benefits include improved 
efficiency and cost effectiveness, increased opportunity for innovation, and the 
continuous improvement of quality products and services.”  (Construction 
Industry Institute, 1991) 
 
The UK construction task force defines ‘partnering’ as,  
“Partnering involves two or more organizations working together to improve 
performance through agreeing mutual objectives, devising a way for solving any 
disputes and committing themselves to continuous improvement, measuring 
progress and sharing the gains.“ (Egan, 1998)  
 
As per the Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) theory, strategic partnerships is a form of collaboration 
(relational contracting) - Hybrid - that lies somewhere in between the continuum of contracting 
relations of Market and Hierarchy. It combines the advantages of flexibility in the market with the 
advantages of continuation in the hierarchy  (Thomassen & Jørgensen, 2013). Furthermore, in the 
construction industry, procurement decisions establish governance forms. From a TCE perspective, 
procurements that are governed by Price, Trust and Authority, are equivalent to the Market, Hybrid 
and Hierarchy governance forms respectively (Eriksson, 2006). Thereby, making Trust based 
procurement characteristic to strategic partnerhips.  
In the construction industry, strategic partnerships are a form of collaboration based on a binding 
commitment set up between two or more independent organizations with the purpose of obtaining 
mutual and high priority goals with a long-term perspective. This is established over either a single 
long-term project or repetition of numerous activities/ projects. These conditions involve sharing of 
responsibilities, risks and benefits amongst the stakeholders (Thomassen & Jørgensen, 2013). 
Gadde and Dubois concluded from their study that the expectation of rapid shift towards strategic 
partnerships is unrealistic. They suggest that changes in the industry should include decentralized 
projects and reduction of competitive tendering by the gradual introduction of partnering at the local, 
intermediate and central levels (Gadde & Dubois, 2010). 
Furthermore, it has been contended that the application of tools and techniques along with 
commitment to partnering is not enough for the materialization of a successful partnership. Long-
term collaborations cannot be standardized but rather need to be customized as per sensitivity to 
salient local and national conditions. Therefore, partnering is a multi-faceted practice rather than a 
simple strategy, which is encouraged or inhibited by various economic, institutional, technical and 
organizational conditions (Mike Bresnen & Marshall, 2000).   
The following sections describe in detail the requirements, benefits and barriers to successful strategic 
partnership.

Integrated teams and processes 
 
The construction industry classically deals with a project as a sequence of largely disjointed activities 
undertaken by architects, contractors and suppliers. Changing this culture to integrate the process 
and the team around the product is crucial in improving quality and efficiency in construction. 
Integrated project processes should focus on the key elements of continuous product development, 
proper project implementation, innovation partnering with the supply chain and component 
production (Egan, 1998). The development of a dedicated team is one of the crucial factors required 
for successful long-term strategic partnership (Beach, Webster, & Campbell, 2005), especially in the 
opinion of those that have been involved in  partnering before (Black et al., 2000). To build the 
integrated and dedicated teams, emphasis has been paid on the need of additional and sufficient, 
training and development workshops, especially for managers and supervisors (Egan, 1998). These 
workshops provide the opportunity for workgroups to interact in a cooperative environment (Anvuur 
& Kumaraswamy, 2007). Team building is essential as it helps groups through the formative stages by 
promoting group identity and harmony. It also encourages the feeling of ownership in the project and 
avoids steep learning curves for the project participants. Moreover, sustaining this integration is 
important through ongoing interactions such as away days and social events to prevent stagnant 
relationships. With regards to team building, it has often been criticized that the workshops are no 
substitute for the actual experience and that the lower hierarchical levels are rarely involved (M. 
Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). In addition, it is important that team building exercises should be rooted 
to technical and task related issues rather than superficial exercises that generate a temporary sense 
of camaraderie (Larson, 1997). The identification of common goals and potential problem areas, and 
establishment of problem solving methods as part of the team building workshops has been suggested 
as useful for project success. Workshops must also include establishment of joint project charter that 
define shared objectives and member responsibilities (Beach et al., 2005; Larson, 1997).  
 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
 
Early involvement of parties 
 
The involvement of clients, architects, consultants, contractors and key subcontractors in the decision 
making process from an early stage of the project is an important prerequisite for successful strategic 
partnerships as it encourages value engineering solutions (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007; 
Bayramoglu, 2001; Beach et al., 2005). Each of these organizational categories hold valuable 
knowledge in their respective fields and by bringing this to the project at an early/ pre construction 
stage can save valuable time and resources. In the case of a contractor firm, it is involved in the actual 
production process but since it usually enters the team after the bid, it has no control over design 
decisions. This can lead to redundant and costly design solutions and failure to recognize technological 
problems/ barriers that may arise in the construction process (Bayramoglu, 2001). A contractor’s 
direct input can contribute to more accurate project costing. It also assists greatly in value and risk 
management (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). For instance, in the Amphion consortium the early 
involvement of contractors especially in the acquisition of key site-based information was essential 
for effective initial feasibility studies, which helped in the realistic estimation of cost and performance 
targets (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007).  Contrary to most studies, where early involvement of 
contractors is solely based on the judgement of decision makers, one study showed that clients were 
interested in integrated design and construction unless, it was restricted by national policies (Eriksson 
& Pesämaa, 2007).  
 
Government commitment and national policies 
 
A crucial prerequisite for successful strategic partnership is setting up of cooperative national or 
regional frameworks and policies with support and commitment from the government. The 1994 
Latham report proposed that the client should be at the core of a construction project for partnering 
to be successful. As the UK government is the single largest client of the construction industry, one of 
the recommendation was that they should set an example in the industry by committing themselves 
to becoming a best practice client by promoting excellence in design. Examples of suggestions of 
project strategies and briefs were, the Construction Industry Council (CIC) should issue a guide to brief 
clients, Department of the Environment (DOE) should publish a simply worded Construction Strategy 
Code of Practice to deal with project management and tendering issues. Furthermore, a contract 
strategy that one-third of the government funded projects starting in the following four years should 
use the New Engineering Contract (NEC) was suggested (Latham, 1994). The Egan construction task 
force recommended the Deputy Prime Minister to transform his Department’s Best Practice 
Programme into a knowledge centre for construction, giving the entire industry and its client’s access 
to information and knowledge from the demonstration projects suggested with a £500 million 
investment (Egan, 1998). However, industrial barriers such as standard contracts, and national laws 
and regulations are not considered critical by some (Erik Eriksson, Nilsson, & Atkin, 2008).  
 
 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
 
Mutual trust 
 
The 1998 Egan report recognized the need for a no-blame culture based on mutual interdependence 
and trust amongst organizations for partnering to be implemented (Egan, 1998). In a survey study 
conducted by Black, Akintoye and Fitzgerald, mutual trust has been recognized as the most crucial 
factor for success in partnering relationships by consultants, contractors and clients alike. This is 
reassuring as the contractor and client relationships have been typically mistrustful in the past (Black 
et al., 2000). However, many have also argued that trust is a consequence of cooperation rather than 
the means to achieve it (Albanese, 1994; Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007). The development of trust 
between organizations is not something that can be engineered with formal tools and techniques. 
Essentially the development of mutual trust between organizations is a function of the relationship 
length and the alignment mechanisms used, such as repetition, routine, understanding etc. (Mike 
Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). Furthermore, the quality of products and services that are delivered within 
a stipulated time frame influence the trust and confidence level amongst major players in a 
construction project (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007). The establishment of trust in procurement 
methods has been associated to other factors such as closeness, openness, commitment, security, 
honesty, competence and dependability (Dahwa, Al-Hakim, & Ng, 2013) 
 
Committed leadership 
 
The agenda for improvement in the construction industry through partnerships and collaborations 
needs to be driven forward by the commitment of the top management. Apart from display of 
commitment to improve quality and efficiency, the managers also need to successfully communicate 
the required cultural and operational changes throughout the organization (Egan, 1998). This support 
is vital in making the collaboration both credible and legitimate with the alignment of goals and 
relationships (Beach et al., 2005; M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000; Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007; Larson, 
1997). However, this may not be an equally important factor from the viewpoint of different types of 
organizations. For example, in the UK most consultants are partnership based and they are involved 
in the decision making process from the early stages. They are likely to be less influenced by a board 
of directors not involved in the day-to-day problems of construction. Furthermore, differences in 
opinion lie based on whether a company has prior experience with partnering or not (Black et al., 
2000).  
 
Effective communication 
 
Communication is the key to proper and smooth functioning of a team, and business success. In the 
construction industry, effective communication between organizations is imperative to avoid 
misunderstanding, errors and conflicts, which may lead up to litigation. Contractors associate effective 
communication as the most important requirement for partnering relations as they can deeply relate 
to the problems associated with poor communication on site between the various involved 
organizations. Furthermore, the parties that have been previously involved in partnering recognize 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
 
this as a vital factor over those that have not been involved (Black et al., 2000). Effective and open 
communication is also key to project success in terms of improved construction schedule, cost and 
quality. This requires the establishment of proper channels of communication between the site and 
various parties (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007). 
 
Financial investment 
 
A company size is determined in terms of the annual turnover, balance sheet and/ or the number of 
employees.  Financial investment is a key element of commitment to the partnering process (Beach 
et al., 2005). However, a 2005 UK study viewed a company’s financial security as one of the least 
significant factors for successful partnering relations (Black et al., 2000). In addition, a 2006 Danish 
study shows that respondents (42%) report ‘no specific expenditure’ (less than 25,000 DKK) is required 
in the form of direct financial investment (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006). Even though aligning all 
organizations with the partnering agreement and frameworks requires a considerable upfront 
financial investment (Larson & Drexler, 1997), companies do not monetize the investment made in 
terms of time, contractual agreements, development activities, external consultants and, education 
and training (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006).  
 
Conflict resolution strategy 
 
Anvuur and Kumaraswamy, argue that the problem in the construction industry is the lack of 
recognition of task interdependence rather than the absence (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007). With 
the involvement of multiple organizations in a construction project, conflict is bound to rise from a 
range of organizational differences and divergent goals, to power imbalances. Poorly managed 
conflicts can lead to breakdown in trust and lost productivity, which is why the establishment and 
communication of a conflict resolution strategy is crucial (Beach et al., 2005). It includes the 
development of a control and resolution mechanism, and establishment of an effective conflict 
resolution strategy. This can be achieved by improving communication and enhancing mutual goals 
with top management support (Chan et al., 2004).  
  
Clear definition of responsibilities 
 
The formation of new integrated teams in partnering projects results in new and redefined, roles and 
responsibilities for everyone involved from the top management to the frontline staff at the project 
site.  Clear definition of the responsibilities need to be determined and understood to avoid conflicts 
amongst the different organizations such as, the design responsibilities of building service engineers, 
duties of project managers etc. (Latham, 1994). This should include the areas of authority and extent 
of decision-making power. Furthermore, individuals and parties need to be able to explain the vision 
of the project and its relation to their respective roles and duties (Chan et al., 2004).  
 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
 
Common goals 
 
Establishment and existence of common goals has been identified as one of the factors essentials for 
optimal relations between project participants. Learning and understanding each other’s interests and 
concerns helps to mutually generate, modify and identify with a common set of goals for the project 
(Beach et al., 2005). An organization’s technical and functional competence is key to this process as it 
strongly predicts the ability to achieve these goals (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007). 
 
Equal empowerment of organizations 
 
The respondents of a UK study in the year 2000 have held the equal empowerment of parties as one 
of the least important requirements (Black et al., 2000). Conversely in another 2005 study, more than 
80% of the respondents felt that contractual relations could be fair, but fewer (less then 60%) consider 
that the relationships could be equal. However, a fair or equal contractual relationship is not reflected 
in the industry as financial control is perceived as crucial (Wood & Ellis, 2005). 
 
Commitment to quality 
 
Quality projects can only be delivered when clients award contracts to architects and contractors 
based on overall value for money rather than lowest price. The procurement criteria needs to change 
from price-based to quality-based. It is also important to understand that quality just does not mean 
zero defects but also, delivery within the stipulated budget and schedule, innovative solutions, 
reduced waste and, lower operational and maintenance costs (Egan, 1998). 
 
Resource sharing 
 
Every construction company owns resources in terms of knowledge, technology, information, specific 
skill and capital that they can share amongst project participants.  To guarantee success in partnering, 
organizations should be willing to share power and resources that would benefit the overall project 
goals (Chan et al., 2004).

Improved budget 
 
Sir Michael Latham claimed that an implementation of the partnering process would lead to a 30% 
cost reduction by the year 2000 (Latham, 1994). Similarly, the Egan report set its measurement as 
reduction in capital costs (excluding land and finance) by 10% per annum (Egan, 1998). Improved 
budget has been perceived by industry practitioners as one of the primary benefits to partnering 
(Black et al., 2000; M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000; Wood & Ellis, 2005). With strategic partnerships, the 
tedious procurement processes are eliminated (Bayramoglu, 2001) in addition to a reduction in 
supplier base in favour of ‘partnered’ relationships (Beach et al., 2005). This leads to lower 
administrative costs (Beach et al., 2005; Black et al., 2000; Chan, Chan, & Ho, 2003a). Improved budget 
can be attributed to multiple other reasons such as reducing construction schedule, increasing 
member involvement, reducing rework and change orders through open communication and, 
improving problem solving and eliminating blame shifting with understanding and trust (Albanese, 
1994).  
 
Improved quality 
 
In the traditional, competitive tendering procurement procedure it is known that contractors often 
compromise on quality to submit a lower priced bid amongst their competition (Bayramoglu, 2001). 
Due to this decline in quality construction projects, a shift towards new procurement procedures 
through partnering has been advocated by many (Construction Industry Institute, 1991; Egan, 1998; 
Latham, 1994). As per the Egan report, numerous clients have reported an increase in predictability – 
number of projects completed on time and within budget – of 20% every year and regularly achieve 
predictability rates of 95% or more, post-partnering process. Furthermore, the reduction in the 
number defects has been set at 20% annually with many construction firms already achieving zero 
defects at handover (Egan, 1998).  
 
Closer relations 
 
The UK construction industry has been suffering due to the arsing conflicts between the organizations 
involved in a project. These organizations believe that the main benefit of long-term partnerships 
would be less adversarial relationships (Wood & Ellis, 2005). Apart from this, a better understanding 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
 
of the other organizations goals and requirements can also be achieved, which enables the parties to 
work well together and achieve quality projects in time and within budget (Black et al., 2000; Chan et 
al., 2003a). Eliminating blame shifting through open communication and trust also results in 
decreasing adversarial relations (Albanese, 1994; Chan et al., 2003a). All-in-all partnering contributes 
to more productive and collaborative working relations between contractual partners (Larson, 1997). 
 
Increased turnover and profits 
 
Some of the leading construction firms in the UK increased their turnover and profits by 10% to 20% 
a year after being involved in partnering with other firms. They raised their profit margins 
proportionally with the annual turnover well above the industry average (Egan, 1998). Even though 
an increase in profits and market share is an obvious project-based benefit of partnering (Wood & 
Ellis, 2005), it is often not emphasized as much as the relational benefits, as the betterment of 
relationships between parties results in project-based benefits (Black et al., 2000). In Denmark, 
strategic partnerships have had a positive influence on a company’s turnover where one-third of the 
experienced organizations attributed at least 10% of the turnover to strategic partnerships (Gottlieb 
& Storgaard, 2006).  
 
Improved construction time 
 
Based on the performance of leading clients and construction companies in the UK, partnering can 
reduce the time from client approval to practical completion by 10% per year (Egan, 1998). Reduction 
of construction schedules has been identified as one of the evident advantages of strategic 
partnership (Black et al., 2000; M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000; Wood & Ellis, 2005). Additionally, these 
long-term collaborations eliminate the tedious and time consuming procurement process, thereby 
leading to considerable reduction in time (Bayramoglu, 2001). However, organizations without prior 
experience in partnering do not strongly believe that long-term collaborations could lead to improved 
time-scales (Black et al., 2000). 
 
Customer satisfaction 
 
In the experience of the Egan construction task force, the building sector tends to focus less on the 
needs of the current customer and more on the next employer. They suggest that by the 
implementation of the partnering process, the focus shifts to the needs of the present end customers 
at prices that reflect the product value, thereby resulting in increased customer/ client satisfaction 
(Egan, 1998). Even though increased customer satisfaction is one of the most vital benefits 
attributable to partnering, organizations without any partnering experience do not seem to think so 
(Black et al., 2000).  
 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
 
Equity 
 
The working arrangement of partnering is based on equity where the sharing of both risks and rewards 
is encouraged (Bayramoglu, 2001; Beach et al., 2005). As all involved parties are decision-makers in 
the project, they all take responsibility when unforeseen risks emerge instead of the contractor 
bearing majority of the risk. Similarly, if a project is completed within budget there is sharing of the 
additional profits (Bayramoglu, 2001). However, it is important to establish upfront an agreed upon 
framework which entails risk sharing and equitable profit for each party (Drexler & Larson, 2000).  
 
Efficient problem solving 
 
Decentralization of projects can often lead to development of self-governing teams with efficient 
problem solving mechanisms (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). Partnering encourages disputes to be 
resolved at the lowest organizational level possible. The establishment of mutual trust, effective 
communication and informal working relations are instrumental in achieving rapid and fair solutions 
to problems and disputes (Bayramoglu, 2001). It also results in the development of better quality 
solutions for the clients (Wood & Ellis, 2005).  
 
Reduced litigation 
 
Partnering discourages lawsuits and litigations which are a major problem in construction projects 
(Bayramoglu, 2001). With partnering the problems of disputes, claims and litigations can be greatly 
reduced through effective communication and open working relationships (Construction Industry 
Institute, 1991; The Construction Industry Institute Partnering II Research Team, 1996). Avoiding 
lawsuits also helps in realizing potential saving (Chan et al., 2003a).  
 
Future work 
 
Organizations perceive strategic collaboration as an indirect marketing tool as a proof of their record 
of accomplishment. They clearly recognise the benefit of prospective future work, which comes with 
successful strategic partnerships (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). In addition, through strategic 
partnerships organizations are somewhat safeguarded from the harsh and constantly changing market 
conditions (Bayramoglu, 2001). 
 
Cooperation  
 
Cooperation between organizations is based on common goals, shared information, operation 
methods and interpersonal teambuilding. All of these are strongly influenced by the task-related 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
 
attributes of a partner organization (Eriksson & Pesämaa, 2007). A successful strategic partnership 
entails all the aforementioned characteristics leading to synergistic alliance and cooperation.   
 
Better safety performance 
 
With an improved commitment to people through better site conditions and care for the health and 
safety of the workforce, a target could be set to reduce the number of reportable accidents by 20% 
per annum, which would lead to substantial reductions in project costs (Egan, 1998). 
  
Enhanced communication 
 
In a study conducted by Bresnen and Marshall on nine different collaborating projects in the UK, the 
involved clients, contractors and designers expressed high levels of satisfaction with information flows 
and inter-organizational communication as a result of partnering (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000).  
 
Potential for innovation 
 
Innovation potentials are sources of inspiration to trigger innovation. Long-term strategic partnering 
has in itself been identified as an innovative procurement method (Chan et al., 2003a). Nevertheless, 
strategic partnerships are considered to have led to further innovation in companies (Gottlieb & 
Storgaard, 2006) 
 
Continuous improvement 
 
Continuous improvement is an ongoing effort to improve products, services or processes. Partnering 
provides an opportunity for all parties to strive for continuous improvement with a long term joint 
effort focused on eliminating wasteful barriers to improvement (Black et al., 2000; Chan et al., 2003a).  
 
Improved productivity 
 
Productivity refers to how well an organization converts its input into products and services. The early 
implementation of the partnering process already saw productivity gains of 5% a year in the UK. 
Following this, it was aimed to increase the value added per head by 10% every year (Egan, 1998).

Reduced risk 
 
Strategic partnerships foster an environment, which reduces the inherent risks of construction 
projects.  With increased information sharing and shared culture, organizations are exposed to less 
risk and there is a culture of fair risk sharing (Black et al., 2000; Wood & Ellis, 2005).

Misunderstanding and inexperience 
 
Understanding and knowledge of the partnering concept are essential to create successful partnering 
(Chan, Chan, & Ho, 2003b). Limited experience of this approach can often effect the extent of 
understanding and knowledge of the project participants (Larson & Drexler, 1997). A 2008 study of 
the Swedish construction industry showed that two-thirds of the construction clients perceived 
increased cooperation to be more important than competition for project success. However, this 
desire did not reflect in their project management and procurement procedures, which still focused 
on competition. The study also concluded the existence of a lack of understanding of the correlations 
between partnering, perceived barriers to partnering and procurement procedures. This 
misunderstanding and unawareness of the partnering concept was due to the lack of practical 
experience and theoretical training regarding long-term strategic partnerships. In addition, the lack of 
strong industry norms advocating partnering and limited regional/ national exposure could have led 
to misinterpretation of the concept (Erik Eriksson et al., 2008). Similarly in the UK, as the Amphion 
consortium was the pioneer partnering experience, the partnering agreement was detrimental to 
project advancement as staff members were unclear about the aim and objective of the initiative. 
They also found it difficult to adapt to a changed mind-set required for successful partnering 
(Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007). Furthermore, a 2005 study of contractor experiences in the UK showed 
that deep-rooted cost-driven agendas persisted in partnering transactions, which is contrary to the 
ethos of partnering (Wood & Ellis, 2005). Similarly, a 2006 Danish study showed that the most cited 
aim of partnerships was increasing market share, clearly showcasing the short-term financial aim 
(Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006).

Cultural and organizational barriers 
 
The differences in organizational culture and internal structure are sensitive factors where 
complications arise when change is implemented (Mike Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). Cultural barriers 
for instance are conservative culture, adversarial attitude, short-term perspective, lack of sub-supplier 
involvement in specifications and labour unions. Whereas, organizational barriers can be in the form 
of traditional organization of the construction process, new competence requirements and traditional 
procurement procedures (Erik Eriksson et al., 2008). It is often difficult to introduce and sustain these 
new changes, as they require a shift from the traditional ways of working for both management and 
site workforce. For example, tensions could emerge between the parent organization’s desire to 
control and the project team’s autonomy, leading to conflicts between project team identity and 
organizational culture (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). Another complicating element is the presence 
of subcultures within organizations that can be correlated to horizontal and vertical differentiation. 
Horizontal differentiation or departmental specialization arises when collaborating groups across the 
organization are driven by their own interest. Whereas vertical differentiation or hierarchical 
stratification arises when there is improper diffusion of new working ways through the wider project 
organization (Mike Bresnen & Marshall, 2000).  
 
Relationship problems 
 
Traditionally, inter-organizational relations in the construction industry are adversarial in nature. Even 
with the implementation of project partnering the relations remain at an ‘arm’s-length distance’. For 
collaborative efforts such as project partnering to shift to strategic partnering, construction companies 
need to change the inter-organizational relationships from an ‘arm’s-length distance’ to a ‘high-
involvement relationship’. For this, they need to introduce and develop new relationship dimensions 
such as longevity, adaptation, dependence, interaction, atmosphere and mutual orientation over 
multiple projects in a substantial span of time (Gadde & Dubois, 2010). The individual style and 
behaviour of key personnel can also greatly influence the decline or improvement of inter-
organization relationships (Drexler & Larson, 2000). Relationship problems also arise from the failure 
of fair risk sharing and over dependency on others (Chan et al., 2003b). Intense dependency on others 
can make it difficult to isolate and measure accurately each parties contribution to any outcome, 
especially liability for faults (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007).   
 
Inefficient efforts 
 
Partnering is a considerable upfront investment of time, energy and resources which requires active 
top management support and continuous commitment to teamwork (Larson, 1997). Efforts need to 
be made in terms of adequate training of project participants, involvement of key organizations and 
commitment by the top management. The success of the partnering venture is greatly influenced by 
the contributions of the individual participants, which makes the inadequate training of individuals 
and lack of an integrated team, a vital reason for failure of long-term partnerships. It is common to 
==PAGE:35==
 
35 
 
assume that partnering just involves clients and contractors but the reality is that a successful 
partnership involves clients, contractors, architects, consultants, key subcontractors and suppliers. 
Unless all the key parties are involved, the entire range of benefits associated with partnering will not 
be realized. Moreover, the commitment to partnering does not filter down to the frontline staff easily 
unless the top level management pursues it with complete dedication and commitment (Chan et al., 
2003b).  
 
Continuity of work 
 
Based on the portfolio of the building client, finding continuous work may or may not inhibit 
collaborative relations between contractual partners. Clients recognize the dangers of continuous 
relations turning into ‘cosy’ long-term relationships. To avoid these problems, the workload could be 
evenly distributed over time and quasi-market mechanisms such as financial incentives, continuous 
improvement and benchmarking can be used (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). In addition, the demand 
for construction should always justify the investment to ensure continuity of quality work. When the 
actual demand fails to meet the predicted demand, it restricts the cash flow, which jeopardizes the 
work of the contractor and their ability to sustain the investment leading to poor quality production 
(Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007).   
  
Undefined authority and duties 
 
The lack of clear demarcation of roles and responsibilities of individuals in integrated teams can often 
create confusion and misunderstanding. This problem is more evident amongst the frontline staff at 
the site as an improper team integration and inefficient communication of redefined duties may lead 
to a misunderstanding of individual obligations and of reporting authority (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 
2000). The lack of legally binding contracts can also cause problems in the decision making process if 
undecidedness exists amongst the executive board and core group members. Certain group members 
may find themselves making decisions outside their authority, thereby alienating other members and 
creating an unspoken hierarchy (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007).  
 
Lack of top management support 
 
Lack of top management commitment may stem from a reason as simple as the lack of will to 
implement change with regard to partnering. This may deter other individual decision makers from 
trying new and innovative approaches even though they are potentially more suited than traditional 
approaches (Erik Eriksson et al., 2008). Occasionally, top management support and commitment may 
wane as the project progresses due to complexities in the partnering agreement such as 
implementation of the agreement, dwindling confidence in the project, inability to manage rapid 
changes in procurement methods and initiatives, and unrealized benefits that are initially regarded 
attainable (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007).   
==PAGE:36==
 
36 
 
Continuity of relations 
 
At the heart of any collaboration lies people and relationships, both at organizational and individual 
levels. The lack of continuity of relationships hinders the realization of the full benefits of strategic 
collaboration and the transfer of experience across projects. For instance, in the long-term reliance 
upon a key individual and their unstated knowledge, leaves the organization vulnerable if the 
individual were unavailable. Therefore, relying excessively on individual employees, restricts 
knowledge sharing and organizational learning (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000)   
 
Communication problems 
 
The lack of an information sharing platform creates barriers for project success. Particularly, sharing 
problems encountered by one contractual partner, so that the other partners may learn and benefit 
from this (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007). Even though information technology is important in 
supporting open communication and information sharing, the use of sophisticated technology can 
inhibit personal forms of communication, which is important for the progress of partnerships (M. 
Bresnen & Marshall, 2000).  
 
Uneven commitment 
 
Partnering requires equal commitment from all project participants rather than just lip service. 
Differing levels of commitment can arise from the differing objectives of the involved parties which is 
problematic for successful completion of the project (Chan et al., 2003b). 
 
3.5 Strategic partnership in Denmark 
 
As per a 2006 survey study in Denmark, strategic partnership is a well-known phenomenon in the 
Danish construction industry with 51% of the survey respondents previously engaged in a long-term 
cooperative relationship within the last three years. However, the survey shows that the average time 
span of these partnerships is approximately three years. The reason for this has been attributed to 
the current Danish market structure, where companies need to adapt to rapid changes in demand. In 
addition, respondents state that the problem is the realization of tangible benefits of partnerships. 
Without this, organizations hesitate to invest in new solutions and concepts, and resort to adopting 
existing products (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006).  
The primary reasons for engaging in strategic partnerships for these organizations have been increase 
in market share, concept development, rationalisation, increase in product quality and product 
development. This clearly indicates that organizations have an external outlook focused on future 
market rather than the internal company process (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006).  
==PAGE:37==
 
37 
 
When it comes to the contractual agreements of these strategic partnerships, 72% of these are still of 
the traditional written format with only 13% verbal and 7% a combination of written and verbal 
contracts. This contradicts the theoretical assumption that partnering leads to reduction in 
contractual regulations, and to the development of goodwill and trust amongst partnering 
organizations (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006). 
Finally, an interesting revelation is the reluctance of companies to integrate vertically as the benefit 
of remaining an independent and distinct party is seen as a competitive advantage in a market that 
demands flexibility with rapidly changing customer demands (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006).

5. Discussion 
 
This chapter highlights the Danish construction industry’s attitude and expectations from strategic 
partnerships or strategic collaborations. This has been analysed and discussed in depth as per the 
proposed research model and based on the responses gathered from the empirical survey.  
 
5.1 Interest 
 
Collaborative arrangements have been successful in multiple industries, such as car manufacturing, 
offshore engineering, grocery retailing, steel manufacturing etc. This has piqued the interest of the 
construction industry around the world to enter into strategic partnerships (Egan, 1998). The practice 
of this form of long-term collaboration has not been matured in Denmark, unlike the US, UK and 
South-East Asia (Bygballe et al., 2010). With the increasing global trend of strategic partnerships, the 
question arises if the Danish construction industry has a legitimate interest in this to drive it forward.  
In the survey, majority of the respondents (almost 70%) disclose that their company is interested in 
or plans to enter into long-term collaborations in the future. This predominance in the interest of the 
industry towards strategic partnerships helps to address the question of Interest and deduce the 
absence of any barrier in this domain. 
 
5.2 Understand 
  
Strategic partnership was introduced in the construction business as a viable solution of improving the 
productivity of the entire industry whilst reducing competition and the adversarial nature of inter-
organizational relations (Egan, 1998; Latham, 1994). Project partnerships are viewed by many as a 
steppingstone towards the successful implementation of long-term collaborations where, the success 
of the project and related experiences shape the possibilities of future collaborations.  
Project success is often evaluated in terms of the project management triangle (also known as the iron 
triangle) where the performance is defined in terms of time, cost and quality which are interrelated 
parameters (figure 15).  
 
==PAGE:54==
 
54 
 
 
Figure 15: The iron triangle. 
 
Strategic collaboration aims to achieve this success with improved budget, construction time and 
quality along with the development of long-term personal relationship. 
An improved budget suggests the betterment of the life cycle costs of construction and not the capital 
construction cost alone. The life cycle cost of a project entails multiple costs such as procurement, 
capital, administration, operation etc. Theoretically, long-term partnerships help in reducing the 
expenditure associated with the various stages of a project, even though they may require an initial 
investment to set up a collaboration (Larson & Drexler, 1997). The overall budget may not be scaled 
down in equal proportion for all the expenditure areas and for all the involved organizations equally. 
In fact, budget improvement may never be realized if the attributing factors such as construction 
schedule, member involvement, communication, problem solving etc. are not efficiently managed 
(Albanese, 1994).   
In the survey, all the cost related effects and benefits have been agreed upon within the range of 40% 
to 70%, depending on the kind of cost. To elaborate, almost 70% of the respondents agreed to the 
reduction of costs associated with tendering. This supports the primary advantage that has been 
advocated with the implementation of the new collaborative procurement process. By cutting down 
on the tedious procurement procedures and, the administration and management of a large supplier 
base, procurement and tendering costs can be cut down substantially (Bayramoglu, 2001; Beach et 
al., 2005). On the other hand, reduction in overhead costs and start-up costs, found only about 45% 
in agreement for both and more than 15% in disagreement for the former. This is probably because 
the industry recognizes that setting up a collaboration between organizations requires an increase in 
certain direct and indirect forms of investment such as capital, time, administration, development 
activities, education, training etc. (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006; Larson & Drexler, 1997). Furthermore, 
for the capital expenditure on construction, half of the survey respondents acknowledged this, with 
more than 30% having a neutral viewpoint. It could be deduced that the industry practitioners do 
recognize budget improvement as a primary benefit. 
In a world where “Time is money”, improved budget cannot be achieved without a reduction in 
construction schedule. A considerable amount of time is believed to be saved simply by the 
elimination of the tedious and time consuming traditional procurement process (Bayramoglu, 2001).

Further, a reduction in rework, change orders and conflicts by improving member involvement, inter-
organizational communication and problem solving techniques, minimizes the time spent on non-
productive works. In the survey, more than 60% of the survey respondents acknowledge partnerships 
leading to faster execution of a project. However, about 10% disagree and another 10% do not know 
of this basic advantage. Just as in Black, Akintoye and Fitzgerald’s study, the opinion of these 
respondents may be because of prior inexperience with partnering (Black et al., 2000). 
As per the iron triangle, cost, time and quality are interrelated where a change in any one of the 
parameters affects the other two. Thereby, it only makes sense that an improvement in budget and 
construction time leads to an improvement in the quality of the project delivered. Multiple studies 
have advocated an improvement of performance, which is judged by cost, time and quality (Egan, 
1998; Latham, 1994). It may be inferred that the survey respondents hold a similar opinion as 80% of 
them agree to an increased quality in projects, which makes it one of the higher ranked benefits.  
It is important to understand that an enhancement in the three primary interrelated factors leads to 
successful strategic partnerships. To accomplish this, a variety of other factors (which maybe 
interrelated as well) need to be satisfied or implemented accurately. Supposing that it is done 
properly, it leads to benefits and if not, they become barriers for successful strategic collaborations.  
When two or more independent organizations come together to form long-term collaborations, the 
proper understanding, alignment and commitment to the concept and objective is required by all the 
involved companies and members (Chan et al., 2003b). Partnerships can often have deep-rooted cost-
drive agendas which is contrary to its ethos and therefore, inhibits organizations from realizing the 
spectrum of positive effects and benefits that long-term working relations has to offer for business 
and society. Prior studies in Denmark, Sweden and UK have shown that the intent of strategic 
partnerships can often be misunderstood due to the lack of practical experience and theoretical 
training (Erik Eriksson et al., 2008; Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006; Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007).  
Sometimes even though organizations desire cooperation more than competition as their way of 
operating, challenges arise to change the mind-set suddenly. This may directly reflect on the 
unchanged working methods and practices, which continue to focus on price and competition. 
Industry norms and national policies need to be supportive of the shift towards collaborative working. 
For instance, the Latham and Egan reports in the UK suggested the government to publish codes and 
practices for better understanding of the partnering concept by the construction industry. It was also 
suggested that the government support the transition to collaborative practices by sharing 
information and experiences of the government invested demonstration projects (Egan, 1998; 
Latham, 1994). However, industrial barriers such as lack of political will and unclear legislations may 
not always be viewed as critical. This study gives a similar result as a 2008 study in Sweden (Erik 
Eriksson et al., 2008), where these industrial barriers are not considered critical. As per the survey, 
only about 35% of respondents view these as barriers. 
The alignment of the organizations and thereby project members is crucial for the optimal success of 
strategic partnerships (Beach et al., 2005). Without the establishment of common goals and 
objectives, it is difficult to obtain the desired project results with minimal conflicts. Common goals 
have often been viewed as a prerequisite rather than a benefit of collaborations. However, one can 
argue that goal alignments can change through the duration of a project with changing focus or 
relations with involved organizations and people. As of result of this, organizations may experience an 
==PAGE:56==
 
56 
 
improvement or decline in alignment. This improvement would be viewed as an effect or a benefit of 
strategic collaborations, which 80% of the survey respondents acknowledge.  
Mutually generating a set of common goals through understanding each other is not enough. 
Technical and functional competency of organizations need to be complimentary for achieving these 
objectives (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007). Latham advocates that construction projects should be 
client centric as client dissatisfaction with the project quality has been one of the primary reasons for 
the shift towards strategic partnerships (Latham, 1994). However, the incompetency of a client in a 
construction project can be a big barrier for the other organisations involved in the project. For 
instance, if the client is incapable of defining his needs and expectations from a project, or constantly 
changes them, it leads to increase in conflicts and reworks. Industry practitioners have ranked missing 
client competency as the third biggest barrier with 55% in agreement, which proves that the client has 
an important role to play for smooth and efficient project completion.  
In this regard, proficiency and knowledge base also grows with experiences, which make organizations 
and individuals more competent. In the survey, respondents view strategic partnerships to have the 
biggest effect on competency from a business perspective (80% in agreement).  
Commitment within and between organizations, to the partnership and its goals are vital for a 
successful collaboration (Beach et al., 2005; M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000; Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007; 
Larson, 1997). Absence of this is a challenge to the proper and successful implementation of strategic 
partnerships (Erik Eriksson et al., 2008). A strategic collaboration demands cultural, operational and 
organizational changes, which are driven forward by the leaders and the top management. A smooth 
transition is possible with the display of commitment and efficient communication from the top 
management to establish these changes through the wider project organization (Egan, 1998). Lack of 
this commitment and support from top management inhibits the development of organizational 
relations.  
Commitment between organizations is also as important as commitment within a company. The 
involved organizations need to exhibit an equal commitment not just to the project goals but also to 
the quality. Quality projects do not simply refer to improved budget and schedules, but also entails 
innovation and, reduction of waste and defects (Egan, 1998). It may be deduced that the industry has 
a similar opinion based on the responses of the survey. Waste reduction has been ranked third in 
terms of benefits that partnerships create for business with more than 80% in agreement. Similarly, 
almost 70% agree that collaborations create a basis for innovation and 50% agree to the development 
of innovation products in construction. This further shows that industry practitioners recognize the 
need of sustainable and innovative practices to deliver higher quality projects.  
As mentioned earlier, long-term inter-organizational collaborations are not possible without a change 
in mindset, culture, structure and working methods. Industry practitioners recognize the formation of 
new relations, organizational restructuring and cultural change as some of the most prominent effects 
on their business (55% to 70% in agreement). However, changing multiple intrinsic qualities in a short 
span of time may not always result in better operation. This is at least in the view of the respondents 
of this survey where only about 50% respondents agree to better operating methods, with more than 
10% in disagreement. It is one of the lowest ranked benefits and may be attributed to these essential 
but sensitive changes, which if not executed properly may become the biggest complications to 
strategic partnerships. 
==PAGE:57==
 
57 
 
It is important to remember that, people and relationships are what lie at the heart of any 
collaboration. A positive relationship development would lead to closer relations based on mutual 
understanding, trust and dependence, thus resulting in decreasing adversarial relations (Chan et al., 
2003a). However, it is vital to understand that closer inter-organization relationships based on trust 
cannot be engineered but are developed with repeated interactions through time (Mike Bresnen & 
Marshall, 2000). Problems arise when these closer relationships turn into over-dependent relations. 
One of the many forms of this is the long-term reliance on a key individual and their unstated 
knowledge, which leaves the organization vulnerable if the individual were unavailable (M. Bresnen & 
Marshall, 2000).  In this survey, respondents have recognized this vulnerability with over-dependence 
on individuals as the biggest barrier for their business with almost 70% in agreement and no one in 
disagreement. 
Speaking of barriers, the centralized decision making structure in the building sector has been viewed 
as a barrier for many years now. In the survey, the respondents have recognized it as the biggest 
barrier from a societal perspective. Decentralization needs organizational and inter-organizational 
restructuring which is important for the formation of integrated teams and processes, with equal 
empowerment of all partners.  
The formation of an integrated project team serves multiple purposes and is crucial for successful 
long-term strategic partnerships (Beach et al., 2005). One of the most popular methods to do this is 
through team building workshops with top management support (Egan, 1998). These provide project 
participants the opportunity to interact in a cooperative environment and develop strong working 
relations (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). However, it is important that team-building exercises are 
rooted to technical and task related issues such as the identification of common goals and problem 
areas, and establishment of conflict resolution strategy and member responsibilities (Larson, 1997). 
Inefficiency in this gives rise to complexities in the future especially with respect to the redefined roles 
and responsibilities.  
The problems associated with undefined authority and duties is more evident amongst the frontline 
staff due to poor establishment and inefficient communication of the same. This can give rise to 
confusion and misunderstanding (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). This is why the clear definition of 
responsibilities has been identified as a prerequisite of successful partnerships (Latham, 1994). 
However, as per the empirical results only about 30% of the survey respondents view a shift in 
authority and undefined responsibilities as a barrier with an almost equal percentage in disagreement. 
This contrary opinion may arise due to the lack of substantial experience of the Danish industry with 
long-term partnerships.   
Effective communication is key to the smooth and successful operation of an integrated team.  
Through the establishment of proper channels of communication between organizations, the 
opportunity for knowledge sharing increases manifold whilst avoiding misunderstanding, error and 
conflicts (Black et al., 2000; Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007). In the survey, more than 80% industry 
practitioners acknowledge the opportunity for knowledge sharing as a benefit of strategic 
partnerships along with 70% agreeing to it leading to fewer conflicts. Furthermore, this has been 
recognized to lead to reduction of litigation (Construction Industry Institute, 1991) and efficient 
problem solving at the lowest organizational level possible (Bayramoglu, 2001). Better quality 
solutions are known to be developed with the establishment of effective communication and informal 
working relations (Wood & Ellis, 2005). With improved knowledge sharing and development of quality 
==PAGE:58==
 
58 
 
solutions, repetition of the solutions is seen as a viable benefit to the business by almost 80% of the 
survey respondents. Strategic partnerships also observe an enhancement of inter-organizational 
communication with the development of relations.      
The efforts involved in the formation of integrated teams and processes is pointless without the 
involvement of all key organizations - architects, clients, contractor, consultants, key subcontractors 
and suppliers. The early involvement and empowerment of parties encourages value engineering 
while substantially saving time and money for all the involved parties (Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007; 
Bayramoglu, 2001; Beach et al., 2005). A fair empowerment is not restricted to design decisions alone, 
but delves deeper into equity. The entire arrangement of partnering is based on sharing risks as well 
as rewards amongst the key decision makers (Bayramoglu, 2001; Beach et al., 2005). In the survey, 
more than 65% respondents agree that strategic partnerships are great in case of project failure. This 
can be associated with the recognized need of fair risk sharing amongst project participants, instead 
of blaming a single organization.  
As mentioned earlier, all of these interrelating factors and their successful implementation lead to 
improvement in budget, construction time and quality, which ultimately define project success and 
successful strategic partnerships.  
Accomplished strategic partnerships have their own advantages associated to them such as improved 
productivity, customer satisfaction, better cooperation and, increased turnover and profits. Majority 
of the survey respondents acknowledge these benefits for their business and the society, which might 
be the motivation behind their interest in strategic collaborations. 70% to 75% agree to achieving, 
higher productivity and added value for their business through long-term partnerships.  Synergistic 
cooperation between organizations stems from common goals, information sharing, operating 
methods and interpersonal relations, which forms the essence of partnerships (Eriksson & Pesämaa, 
2007). This is the highest agreed upon benefit as per the survey respondents with more than 90% in 
agreement. The commercial benefits of partnerships have previously been realized in the Danish 
construction industry (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006). As per this study as well, survey respondents 
recognize most of the commercial benefits for business and society. Almost 80% acknowledge long-
term collaborations creating better business for their companies. Another, 55% to 60% acknowledge 
the stability of earnings and expenses, and ability to afford more building activity. In addition, from a 
societal perspective, with an increase in profit margin for companies the tax payment on this profit 
also increases in the opinion of about 55% respondents. However, less than 40% think that these 
commercial benefits can be related to segment specialization, international competition, increased 
employment or new job types, with 10% to 20% in disagreement. These benefits have not been 
recognized as substantial benefits in mature markets as well.  
The traditional procurement method has always been characterized with competition. On the other 
hand, strategic partnerships have been advocated to assure reduced competition in the market for 
companies as well as prospect for future work from successful collaborations (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 
2000). At the same time concerns have been expressed whether a single client can offer continuous 
work or compatible projects with the new working methods (M. Bresnen & Marshall, 2000). In the 
survey, even though about 50% respondents agree that the portfolio of buildings may be small with a 
single client, 20% disagree. When further questioned about the risk of creating a smaller market with 
only a few suitable projects, respondents agreeing are found in the range of 10% to 30% while those 
in disagreement are found in the range of 20% to 40%. Based on the responses these concerns can be 
==PAGE:59==
 
59 
 
put to rest for the Danish construction industry. However, reduction of competition is one of the 
lowest ranked benefits with a high percentage of disagreement (25%), which shows that they are yet 
to realize this benefit of strategic collaborations.  
From the extensive review of the literature and the analysis of the results from the empirical survey, 
it may be deduced that the comprehensive results are in line with the increasing global trend of 
strategic partnerships in the construction business. Overall, the survey respondents form a higher 
percentage of those in agreement of the positive effects and benefits than the barriers, both from a 
business and societal perspective. In fact, some of the highest percentages of disagreement have been 
associated with the barriers. Only a handful of disparities exist between the empirical results and the 
literature, which may be due to different national and industrial environment or limited experience 
with strategic partnerships. Based on the overall results, it may be suggested that indirectly the 
advantages have been ranked higher than the challenges. This goes on to prove that industry 
practitioners perceive strategic partnership as a positive and beneficial investment for the synergistic 
growth and development of business and society. This comprehensive understanding of strategic 
partnerships helps to address the question of Understand and deduce the absence of any critical 
barriers in this domain. 
For an easier overview of the effects, benefits and barriers of strategic partnership from a business 
and societal perspective, the survey results and the literature citing the various factors have been 
tabulated in the Appendix (Appendix E). In view of the tabulated comparisons in this chapter, it is 
important to understand that the various factors that have been studied as the requirements, effects, 
benefits and barriers of strategic partnerships, are neither independent nor bound to the respective 
categories. Rather it depends on the perceptions and experiences, in addition to the word building of 
these factors. For instance, mutual trust has been recognized by most as an important requirement 
for strategic collaboration, except a few who argue that trust is a consequence of collaborating 
practices. Similarly, committed leadership has been viewed as a precondition to successful long-term 
collaboration while the lack of top management support is a barrier. The cited literature in the tables 
emphasizes on the respective factors, but not necessarily in agreement or disagreement, in view of 
the survey.      
  
5.3 Practice 
 
Many in the UK since the 1990s have advocated the shift to long-term strategic partnerships from 
traditional adversarial relations in the construction industry (Egan, 1998; Latham, 1994). The most 
sought after method to do this is by changing the traditional procurement methods that are centred 
on price and competition, to new innovation collaborative procurement where the focus in on quality 
and cooperative relations (Egan, 1998).  
As mentioned in section 2.2, in the Danish construction industry strategic partnerships have neither 
been incorporated into the entire value chain of the industry nor have been practiced for any 
significant time period. Therefore, an attempt has been made to understand the possible barriers to 
the future procurement methods by evaluating the efficiency of the current procurement process. 
The operating methods of both, suppliers and buyers have been evaluated. 
==PAGE:60==
 
60 
 
As previously described in section 4.3, substantial differences exist in the weightage of price in the 
final execution from the initial strategy in the procurement process. In the survey from an overall view 
of suppliers and buyers, a considerable difference of 30% between the initial policy and final 
implementation is found. Similar results are found for architects, clients and consultants where a 
substantial difference of 25% to 55% exists between the influence of price during evaluation and 
during final decision. However, a disparity can be found in the case of the procurement methods used 
by contractors. For them, the difference is only 15% to 25%. This may be due to the traditional 
competitive tendering procurement procedure that contractors face more often and their tendency 
to compromise on quality to submit a lower bid amongst their competition (Bayramoglu, 2001). This 
may be the reason they have a price based procurement strategy from the initial evaluation stage.    
The entire construction industry is still rooted in price based procurement. This may reflect the deep-
rooted cost-driven agendas that run through the Danish industry. A 2005 UK study had similar results 
where partnering transactions were driven by price (Wood & Ellis, 2005). In addition, a 2006 Danish 
study showed that short-term financial aims were the most cited objective for organizations to enter 
into long-term collaborations (Gottlieb & Storgaard, 2006). Unless the price based mindset changes, 
a shift cannot be made from competition to collaboration, irrespective of their interest and 
understanding of strategic collaborations.  
This inefficiency in the implementation of the current procurement strategies helps to address the 
question of the industry’s ability to implement future Practices and thereby deduce the presence of 
barriers in this domain. 
 
5.4 Hidden barriers 
 
As per a 2006 Swedish study, from a Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) perspective, procurement 
decisions establish governance forms. In the study, Price, Trust and Authority are the governance 
forms equivalent or characteristic to Market, Hybrid and Hierarchy respectively (Eriksson, 2006).  
As per this study, the current procurement decisions in the Danish construction industry have been 
Price dominated. For the successful implementation of strategic partnerships, which is a form of 
Hybrid governance, procurement decisions need to be governed by Trust (Eriksson, 2006). Therefore, 
a shift needs to be made to trust based procurement to lower the weightage of price in the 
procurement process and bridge the existing gap between the evaluation and award criteria. This 
would foster an environment of trust and understanding which is vital for successful strategic 
partnerships.   
Existing studies have established that mutual trust is the most crucial prerequisite for success in 
partnering relations (Black et al., 2000). However, many have argued that trust is a consequence of 
cooperation rather than the way to achieve it (Albanese, 1994; Anvuur & Kumaraswamy, 2007). Some 
have further questioned the existence of genuine trust and whether trust could be a consequence of 
changing procurement methods (Wood & Ellis, 2005). 
==PAGE:61==
 
61 
 
Whether mutual trust is a prerequisite or a consequence, it definitely cannot be engineered with 
formal tools and techniques. The development of trust between organizations is a function of 
relationship length and alignment mechanisms (Mike Bresnen & Marshall, 2000).  
To make this shift from Price to Trust in the procurement decisions, it is crucial to understand what 
factors lead to the establishment of trust. In a 2013 study regarding the importance of trust in 
procurement practices, the following were established as the constructs of trust (Dahwa et al., 2013): 
Closeness 
Openness 
Commitment 
Security 
Honesty 
Competence 
Dependability 
The Price focused procurement process in the Danish industry may arise from a multitude of reasons. 
It may be due to an organization’s own reluctance or from their unawareness of the proficiency and 
ethics of the associating company.  
Based on the limited knowledge of the procurement practices, it is difficult to point out exactly which 
of the abovementioned constructs inhibit trust development. The substantial differences between the 
percentage distribution of the evaluation criteria and the award criteria in procurement may be 
attributed to any of the constructs of Closeness, Openness, Commitment, Security, Honesty, 
Competence and Dependability, which are essential to Trust based procurements.  
However, throughout the literature of collaborative practices in the construction industry, few of 
these constructs have been emphasized on, in relation to the development of mutual trust. Openness 
has been cited the most in terms of relationship and trust development (Black et al., 2000; Mike 
Bresnen & Marshall, 2000; Latham, 1994). Openness and honesty are synonymous terms and actions 
(Constructing Excellence, 2015; Wood & Ellis, 2005), which is in conjunction with closeness. 
Furthermore, the delivery of quality projects/ products within the stipulated budget and time frame 
aids the development of trust by proving the suppliers dependability (Kaluarachchi & Jones, 2007). 
Therefore the hidden barriers could be identified as the absence of Closeness, Openness, Honesty and 
Dependability. 
Mutual trust is the crux of long-term collaborative relations between organizations. Unless the inter-
organizational relationships are characterized by Closeness, Openness, Honesty and Dependability, the 
spectrum of benefits associated with strategic partnerships will not be realized. Furthermore, unless 
the construction industry realizes this and puts in into practice, the absence of the same constructs 
become the “hidden” barriers to the implementation of successful strategic partnerships in Denmark. 
Trust and its constructs can be established in partnering relations by the alignment of organizational 
objectives and interests of collaborating parties. Parties should also identify challenges, issues and 
risks that may arise through the duration of the partnership. This would help organizations to 
understand better each other’s concerns and perspectives in a harmonious environment. 
Furthermore, it aids the establishment of mutually agreed upon working principles especially in the 
case of conflict resolution and important decision-making. Moreover, contracting parties need to 
review regularly their working relationship to recalibrate objectives and resources, while ensuring that 
trust is continuously and consciously strengthened and sustained.  
==PAGE:62==
 
62 
 
Although this Master Thesis aimed to contribute to the work package under REBUS, the entire value 
chain of the Danish construction industry and not just the repair and maintenance sector can use the 
findings of this study. This study is especially important to the organizations who have an interest in 
strategic partnerships. It could provide a better understanding of the notion of partnering, particularly 
its effects and benefits. It could also make companies aware of the possible barriers so that they can 
come up with contingency plans. Additionally, this study would help organizations to map out the 
areas of self-improvement and implement the necessary changes throughout the organization before 
entering into long-term collaborations. Moreover, it would help the entire construction sector to 
establish realistic expectations from strategic partnerships building on the current mind-set.  
 
5.5 Further research 
 
The findings of this Master Thesis has been based on an extensive review of the literature and 
responses to an empirical survey.  
Qualitative research interviews with industry practitioners should be conducted to further validate 
the results of this study with respect to the business and societal perspectives towards strategic 
partnerships. In addition, these studies could have different focus areas such as commercial factors, 
innovation, organizational barriers etc. 
Future research could be carried out on the importance of trust in strategic partnerships and 
collaborative procurements. The study could entail the different constructs of trust, such as Closeness, 
Openness, Commitment, Security, Honesty, Competence and Dependability to pinpoint the absence of 
which of these creates barriers to trust based procurement practices in Denmark. The interrelations 
between the constructs of trust (along with their underlying dimensions) and, business focus or 
business performance may also yield interesting results. Furthermore, the impact of individual 
behavioural characteristics on trust can be studied as well.

6. Conclusion 
 
 
In 2008, Denmark’s construction industry witnessed a decline in activity due to the financial crises. 
The industry has been on a revival path since 2010. However, with the fragmented nature of the value 
chain still affecting productivity and competitiveness, the focus is lately shifting towards partnering 
and strategic partnerships.   
Construction companies have not fully embraced strategic partnerships, as they face the dilemma to 
either adopt this global trend and achieve economies of scale, or remain independent flexible 
organizations with the ability to respond to changing demands and opportunities. This Master Thesis 
aspired to identify the “hidden” barriers to strategic partnerships, which may further burden the 
industry with this predicament.    
In the study, three important domains of barriers were proposed which may inhibit process innovation 
in the construction sector. These domains – Interest, Understand and Practice – were addressed with 
the empirical analysis of the survey questionnaire, Spørgeskema om Byggeri og Samarbejde 
(Questionnaire on construction and cooperation). 
The analysis revealed that majority of the players in the construction industry were interested or 
planned to enter into long-term collaborations in the future. This suggested the absence of barriers in 
the Practice domain. 
Additionally, the industry practitioners had a good understanding of the concept of strategic 
partnerships and its implications on business and society. The highest rated benefits from a business 
perspective were better cooperation, repetition of processes, less waste in process and opportunity for 
knowledge sharing. Competency was identified as the biggest effect on their business while 
vulnerability with unavailable key individuals was associated as the biggest barrier. Reduced 
competition on individual projects was however, the lowest rated benefit with quarter of the 
respondents dismissing the benefit. Shift in authority and lack of artistic freedom were disregarded as 
a barrier by a substantial percentage of respondents. From a societal perspective, better business for 
companies was the highest ranked benefit while poor architecture and few suitable projects were the 
least important barriers to strategic partnerships. Only a few disparities existed in the empirical 
results, which might be because of different national and industrial environments, and/ or prior 
inexperience. This comprehensive understanding of the partnering notion, suggested the absence of 
critical barriers in the Understand domain.        
On the contrary, the analysis revealed the industry’s incompetency to implement the desired practices 
of procurement. The substantial differences between the evaluation and awarding of contracts based 
on price, discloses the deeply rooted price based procurement methods adopted by the Danish 
industry. This is contrary to the ethos of partnering, where procurement should be quality based 
instead of price based. This suggested the presence of “hidden” barriers in the Practice domain. 
Approaching this from the perspective of the Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) theory revealed that 
procurement decisions need to be governed by Trust to successfully establish Hybrid governance 
forms in transactions – strategic partnerships – in Denmark. Furthermore, based on the findings in the 
==PAGE:64==
 
64 
 
literature, the lack of trust was associated with the constructs of trust – Closeness, Openness, Honesty 
and Dependability.  
Therefore, the Danish construction industry identifies strategic partnerships as a positive and 
beneficial investment for the synergistic growth and development of business and society. 
Nevertheless, inter-organizational relationships need to be characterized by Closeness, Openness, 
Honesty and Dependability, as the absence of these constructs of Trust are the “hidden” barriers to 
the successful implementation of strategic partnerships in Denmark.
