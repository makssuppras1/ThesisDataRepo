Abstract
Sea levels are rising globally at an unprecedented rate as a result of climate change. At
the same time, the occurrence and severity of storm surges are increasing. A country
like Denmark with a large coastline is very much exposed to coastal flood risk and munic-
ipalities have developed storm surge strategies outlining a range of planned adaptation
measures for these future events. However, so far no estimates of flood damages or
required protection levels further into the future than year 2100 have been made. Fur-
thermore, the storm surge strategies are typically centered on preserving the existing city
by traditional protection in the form of dikes, sluices, flood walls, and nature-based so-
lutions. More novel adaptation approaches such as managed retreat have barely been
discussed in Denmark till date. This thesis addressed this research gap by (1) investigat-
ing how coastal flooding in Denmark will change further into the future than the current
design year for coastal protection measures, and (2) evaluating what levels of protection
measures are viable from a socioeconomic perspective. The investigated case area is
the city of Vejle, Jutland. Storm surge sea levels were modeled based on data from IPCC
as well as data from the Danish Coastal Authority and damage categories as well as cri-
teria for unacceptable flood risk were defined. Three different damage frameworks were
compared in a flood risk assessment where one was chosen for the subsequent damage
calculations in a cost-benefit analysis of four different flood risk adaptation scenarios. In
this analysis, the cost-effectiveness of protection until year 2100 as well as protection
and managed retreat until year 2300 were evaluated. It was found that storm surge sea
levels in Vejle will increase considerably towards year 2300 and that extreme events will
become more common. Furthermore, it was found that the damage categories will be
exposed to unacceptable flood risk already in 2050 and that this risk cannot be eliminated
for all categories if only implementing a protection level of 3 m DVR90. Of the damage
frameworks, the framework based on DTU SkadesØkonomi was chosen for the damage
cost assessment. In the cost-benefit analysis, it was found that both short-term protec-
tion until year 2100 and long-term protection until year 2300 are socioeconomically viable
whereas managed retreat may be cost-effective depending on the retreat costs and re-
treat strategy. The total cost of protection was found to be dominated by the maintenance
cost whereas the total cost of retreat was found to be dominated by the acquisition cost.
The total protection cost for Vejle until year 2300 can be expected to be approximately
3 billion DKK whereas if managed retreat is initiated, the total investment cost would lie
between 1-33 billion DKK. Lastly, it was concluded that it is necessary to look further into
the future than 2100 which is the current practice when planning flood risk adaptation, as
today’s adaptation measures are typically not resilient in the long term.

Introduction
Global mean sea level has experienced an unprecedented increase since 1900 than over
any preceding century in at least the last 3,000 years. The current increase in mean sea
levels that we are observing is caused by thermal expansion from ocean warming, ice loss
from glaciers and ice sheets, and changes in land-water storage, all but the latter being
a direct consequence of climate change (IPCC, 2021). Sea level rise will bring cascad-
ing and compounding impacts resulting in losses of coastal ecosystems and ecosystem
services, groundwater salinization, flooding and damages to coastal infrastructure. The
damages from flooding in turn jeopardize livelihoods, settlements, health, well-being, food
and water security, and cultural values in the near to long-term (IPCC, 2022). In addition
to the rise in mean sea level rise, climate change is expected to increase the number and
severity of storms, thus increasing the occurrence of extreme water levels and the risk of
storm surge (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2019). Only in 2015, coastal flood losses in Europe
amounted to almost e1.4 billion annually, with about 100,000 European citizens being
affected from the flooding (Vousdoukas et al., 2020). By 2100, sea level rise threatens
to displace 72-187 million people globally (Hino et al., 2017a). Due to climate change,
growing populations and assets, and subsidence, flood exposure in coastal cities is only
expected to increase in the future (Hallegatte et al., 2013a).
The increased risk of coastal flooding creates a strong need for resilient and adaptive
protection measures that can accommodate expected future sea levels and storm surge
events as well as the high uncertainty in future water level estimates. The available adap-
tation measures to increase the resilience of coastal settlements can be summarized as
protect, accommodate, retreat, avoid (Wiberg et al., 2023), and do nothing (Vousdoukas et
al., 2020). Hard protection such as flood walls or dikes is arguably the most implemented
adaptation measure (e.g. Wenger, 2015). In Europe only, at least 83% of flood damages
could be avoided by elevating dikes in an economically efficient way along 23.7-32.1%
of the coastline (Vousdoukas et al., 2020). Walls or dikes can also be made in order to
buy time while other adaptation measures are being implemented. There is however an
increased recognition of the limitations of structural measures such as dikes, resulting in
countries turning to new climate change and flood risk adaptation approaches (Wenger,
2015). One such approach is managed retreat which is increasingly being viewed as an
effective tool to manage flood risk and adapt to climate change (Raikes et al., 2023).
In Denmark, the total damage cost the next 100 years for storm surges in 48 coastal cities
was in 2017 estimated to be almost 100 billion Danish kroner. These cities have a total
population of 2.4 million people (COWI, 2017). The ten biggest cities in Denmark are all

Theory
This chapter describes the relevant theory for the present study, namely future climate
scenarios, elements of coastal flooding, different flood risk adaptation measures including
design considerations and costs for dikes and managed retreat, Danish regulations and
current design practice, and lastly flood risk assessment and cost-benefit analysis.
2.1
Climate change and coastal flooding
Below the essential concepts to understanding the elements of coastal flooding and the
impacts of climate change on the same are explained. These include climate scenarios
as defined by IPCC and impacts of climate change on sea level rise and storm surges.
2.1.1
Climate scenarios
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) define five different climate sce-
narios based on Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs): SSP1-1.9, SSP1-2.6, SSP2-
4.5, SSP3-7.0 and SSP5-8.5. The SSPs describe a range of plausible trends in society’s
evolution over the 21st century, with SSP1 representing ”sustainability”, SSP2 represent-
ing a ”middle-of-the-road” path, SSP3 representing ”regional rivalry”, SSP4 representing
”inequality”, and lastly SSP5 representing ”fossil fuel-intensive development”. The num-
ber following the hyphen denominates the expected radiative forcing levels of each sce-
nario by year 2100 in W m−2 (Chen et al., 2021, p.230). Effective radiative forcing (ERF)
is defined as the energy gained or lost by the Earth system following an imposed pertur-
bation (e.g. in GHGs, aerosols or solar irradiance) (Forster et al., 2021, p.941). Thus,
SSP5-8.5 is the worst-case scenario, with a development governed by fossil fuels and a
radiative forcing of 8.5 W m−2 by year 2100, causing a global surface temperature rise
of 3.3-5.7°C by year 2100 relative to the period 1850-1900 (IPCC, 2021, p.14). This tem-
perature increase will result in a multitude of impacts, one of which is mean sea level rise,
see Section 2.1.2. The ranges of the scenarios with respect to expected global surface
temperature change and mean sea level rise are seen in Figures 2.1a, 2.1d, and 2.1e.
2.1.2
Impacts of climate change on mean sea level and storm surges
The current increase in mean sea levels that we are observing is caused by thermal ex-
pansion from ocean warming, ice loss from glaciers and ice sheets (Figure 2.1b), and
changes in land-water storage, all but the latter being a direct consequence of climate
change. During 1971-2018, thermal expansion explained 50% of sea level rise whereas
ice loss from glaciers and ice sheets explained 22% and 20%, respectively. During 2006-
2018, the dominant contributors to the increase in global mean sea level rise were ice
sheet and glacier mass loss (IPCC, 2021, p.11).
The global mean sea level is expected to continue to rise over the 21st century and be-
yond: relative to the years 1995–2014, the likely global mean sea level rise by 2100 is
0.28–0.55 m under the very low GHG emissions scenario (SSP1-1.9), 0.32–0.62 m un-
der the low GHG emissions scenario (SSP1-2.6), 0.44–0.76 m under the intermediate
GHG emissions scenario (SSP2-4.5), and finally 0.63–1.01 m under the very high GHG
emissions scenario (SSP5-8.5). Due to deep uncertainty in ice-sheet processes, global
mean sea level rise above the likely range - almost 2 m by 2100 and 5 m by 2150 in the
SSP5-8.5 scenario - cannot be ruled out (Figures 2.1d, 2.1e). If we today were to achieve
a global net negative CO2 emissions, resulting in a gradual reversal of the increase in

In addition to the rise in mean sea level, climate change is expected to increase the num-
ber and severity of storms, thus increasing the occurrence of extreme water levels and
the risk of storm surge (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2019). Storm surges are caused by the
rise of the seawater level, which in turn is caused by tidal surges and waves created by
strong winds. The sea water level is also governed by air pressure, with decreasing air
pressure resulting in increasing water level (Kystdirektoratet, n.d.-b). The number and
strength of storms are however not expected to be the greatest contributors to the impact
of storm surges in Denmark - rather, it is the rise in the mean sea level that will have the
greatest impact. This holds true both for Denmark and internationally (Arnbjerg-Nielsen
et al., 2019). The impacts from storm surges include not only flooding, but also acute
erosion of structures and the coastline. Thus, coastal protection includes protection from
both flooding and erosion (Kystdirektoratet, 2023).
With increased global warming, compound extreme events - e.g. storm surge and extreme
rainfall/river flow happening simultaneously - will also become more frequent (IPCC, 2021,
p.9, 27). Maximum river flow and high groundwater tables due to increased precipitation
and groundwater recharge usually occur during winter and over a longer time period,
which is when storm surges also occur. Thus, the river flow is expected to be greater
than average and in low-lying areas it is expected to be a high risk from flooding from
groundwater. Currently winters in Denmark are too cold to generate heavy cloud bursts
while summers do not have the low pressure needed to generate powerful storm surges.
With climate change, however, this is expected to change in the future. Compound ex-
treme events will most often result in damage much greater than for separate events, and
protection measures such as dikes are particularly vulnerable as they assume there is
only a surplus of water on one side (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2019).
2.2
Adaptation measures for coastal flooding
In this section, different adaptation measures for coastal flooding are described. There is
a focus on design considerations and costs for sea dikes and managed retreat, as these
are relevant for the cost-benefit analysis to be conducted in the present study.
The available climate change adaptation measures to increase the resilience of coastal
settlements can be summarized as protect, accommodate, retreat, avoid (Wiberg et al.,
2023), and do nothing (Vousdoukas et al., 2020). It seems unrealistic however to do
nothing when considering the large presence of critical infrastructure along coastlines
as well as the predicted economic loss from coastal flooding. Protect involves prevent-
ing flooding altogether through natural or artificial structures, beach nourishment, and
nature-based solutions. Accommodate includes reducing damages by implementing ef-
fective forecasting/warning systems and emergency response as well as flood-proofing
structures. Retreat is a term to describe the relocation of households and infrastructure
in order to protect them against coastal flood risk (Vousdoukas et al., 2020). Lastly, avoid
involves not building in risk areas so that there is no need of the above measures (Wiberg
et al., 2023). An overview of the different adaptation measures is seen in Table 2.1.

A way to plan flood risk adaptation in a strategic and structured way is to use so called
dynamic adaptation pathways (DAP) where different adaptation paths are developed over
time as new choices are made. In essence, it is about identifying the decisions that must
be taken now and the ones that can be taken in the future as well as prioritizing invest-
ments. Most importantly, it involves assessing how long a certain solution is sufficient
and when it is necessary to revise the plan. This way it is possible to keep options open
for as long as possible and change the plan based on new data or other developments,
resulting in a plan that is flexible yet robust. Furthermore, a dynamic pathway approach
may help determine whether a given solution may eventually lead to a ”lock-in” where you
can no longer adapt or change the solution (Wiberg et al., 2023).
2.2.1
Hard and soft structures
Physical solutions are usually divided into hard and soft structures. Examples of hard
structures are dikes, dams, and flood walls, whereas soft structures are usually synony-
mous with nature-based solutions (NBS). These include e.g. dune landscapes, wetlands
and beach meadows, mangrove, and barrier islands. Usually NBS require time and space
to establish, but on the other hand they have other positive effects in the form of increased
biodiversity, recreation, and possibilities of CO2 uptake. Hard structures can be dynamic
(e.g. sluices) or static (e.g. dikes or flood walls), or a combination of both. Static structures
will usually require installation of pumps or sluices, as river and rain water needs to be
able to exit the catchment. Furthermore, ecosystems may be cut off and hard static struc-
tures may have a higher CO2 footprint. Dynamic structures generally only protect against
storm surges and not a general increase in sea level (Wiberg et al., 2023). Amongst the
adaptation options, hard protection such as flood walls has been the strategy that delivers
the most predictable level of safety and has been implemented widely across Europe, de-
spite the possible negative effects on the landscape including increased erosion, reduced

amenity value, and more severe events should the measure fail (Vousdoukas et al., 2020).
They are well-documented and may intuitively feel safer than they actually are (leading
to the so-called ”levee effect”). However, a hard structure designed for a 100-year event
could be worthless in a 110-year event (Wiberg et al., 2023). Nature-based solutions will
also be insufficient to deal with events above the design magnitude but unlike levees they
will not fail completely but rather delay and reduce flood magnitude regardless (Wenger,
2015).
In densely populated urban areas, the most appropriate solution may in some cases con-
sist of a hybrid solution which combines several of the measures in Table 2.1. For exam-

reef) before they hit the wall. Walls or dikes can also be made in order to buy time while
city development on higher terrain is planned or while nature-based solutions are con-
structed. Another example is creating a nature-based solution with a ”hard core”, e.g. the
Katwijk dike in the Netherlands which has a concrete core with an underground parking lot
clad with sand and dune vegetation. Many of the solutions are however most appropriate
for a single event in the form of a storm surge rather than a long-term continuous rise in
sea level (Wiberg et al., 2023).
2.2.2
Sea dikes
A dike is an elevated construction on land which protects the built-up area and the land
behind the dike against flooding. Its protection capacity is decided by its height and its
geometry (Kystdirektoratet, 2018c). Challenges and a range of design considerations and
costs that are associated with the construction and maintenance of a sea dike are further
explained below.
Challenges
Case studies have revealed that dikes, levees, and associated infrastructure are becom-
ing increasingly unreliable with the escalating flood threats caused by climate change to
the point that they may even be maladaptive: they reduce the vulnerability of the popu-
lations they protect, but only for more frequent, low-level flooding, whereas vulnerability
to rare, major floods increases (Wenger, 2015). Furthermore, dikes may have other neg-
ative side-effects including adverse impacts on biodiversity, an increased CO2 footprint
and resource use, poor aesthetic value, large maintenance costs or need to increase the
height when mean sea level increases, and risk for levee effect (Wiberg et al., 2023).
Design and costs
Sea dikes are commonly composed of a core of sand, a watertight outer protective layer,
toe protection, and a drainage channel (RISC-KIT, n.d.).
The core may also consist
(partly) of clay and the dike may be covered with grass, asphalt, or concrete (Kystdirek-
toratet, 2018c). The dike has four distinct parts: a submerged base, the middle area that
has a constant loading from waves, the upward middle zone that may experience wave
pressure during storms, and lastly the dike crest that mainly absolves wave run-up (RISC-
KIT, n.d.) (Figure 2.2). A sea dike should be placed away from the coastline with foreland
(e.g. in the form of a tidal flat) to minimise the risk of erosion from the direct impact of
waves during normal and high sea levels. A high foreland reduces the wave energy and
consequently allows for the dike to be lower and still ensure protection. If the sea dike is
instead placed as a protruding dike and without foreland, slope protection or revetment
should be established, as there will be a significant impact and resulting wear from waves
(Kystdirektoratet, 2018c). Sea dikes should generally be constructed with slightly slanting
foreslopes, as the sloped dike forces the wave to break when the water becomes shallow
and since the wave energy is evenly distributed over a larger area, thereby reducing the
impact from the waves. The dissipation of wave energy reduces the risk of failure as well

risk of failure will otherwise increase. The costs of maintenance include small operations

2.2.3
Managed retreat
Managed retreat is defined by Hino et al., 2017a as ”the strategic relocation of structures
or abandonment of land to manage natural hazard risk”. It requires at least two parties:
an implementing or enabling party and the residents affected by the intervention (Hino et
Cost-effectiveness of flood risk adaptation measures for storm surges in the far future
9
==PAGE:23==
al., 2017a). It is considered a proactive strategy in areas prone to e.g. erosion, flooding,
and sea level rise (Wiberg et al., 2023). By 2100, sea level rise threatens to displace
72-187 million people. Over the past three decades, only about 1.3 million people have
relocated through managed retreat (Hino et al., 2017a). Managed retreat is increasingly
regarded as an effective tool to manage flood risk and adapt to climate change (Raikes
et al., 2023) and may sometimes be a more cost-effective approach to natural hazard
mitigation than traditional flood protection such as levees, dikes, weirs, and dams (Pinter
and Rees, 2021).
Challenges
Values, goals, and worldviews are all factors that determine whether or not residents
initiate retreat, as well as the implementing party’s motivation to support retreat. Often
managed retreat is considered controversial because of the social, economic, and psy-
chological difficulties in displacing people from their homes. Residents may furthermore
oppose retreat despite severe natural hazard risk due to cultural heritage or access to
coastal resources (Hino et al., 2017a). Furthermore, the measure highly influences pri-
vate property rights and it discriminates between landowners as some gain and others
lose, raising the issue of social justice. As for the implementing party, public administra-
tion and communities have largely ignored managed retreat as an adaptation measure
due to its high impact on individual livelihoods - and thus high risk of low acceptance and
public outcry among at-risk residents - as well as the high relocation costs (Thaler, 2021).
The prospect of high intangible costs such as loss of cultural heritage is also something
that decision-makers must address (Hino et al., 2017a). Relocation can also be tricky due
to practical limitations, e.g. moving a port or a power plant (Vousdoukas et al., 2020).
Design and costs
The retreat requires that there are areas to retreat to, and therefore managed retreat must
also include planning of attractive areas, or alternatively securing of land, which can later
be used once people start retreating (Wiberg et al., 2023). If the destination can preserve
valued features, the retreat may reach a higher level of acceptance within the community
(Hino et al., 2017a). With an adaptive approach, managed retreat can be performed in
many different ways and with different time horizons (Wiberg et al., 2023). Examples
include:
• A coastal residential area is slowly transformed to a different land use, e.g. coastal
nature, within a period of 10, 100, or 200 years
• Retreat will occur at certain sea level rise thresholds (e.g. SLR = 1 m)
• Retreat will happen when buildings need substantial renovation and repair
• Incentive-based retreat where retreat only occurs when the land owner wants to
move
A private residence in Denmark gets a new owner every 10-20 years on average. As
such, there could be several economic incentives for retreat if the retreat takes place over
80-100 years. Usually there is a need for financial support or an acquisition strategy to
achieve a coordinated and well-planned retreat. Furthermore, for the managed retreat
to be a success, it is crucial to achieve a strong community engagement and have the
citizens involved in the process every step of the way (Wiberg et al., 2023).
Typically managed retreat is achieved through publicly funded property buyouts, i.e. gov-
ernment acquisition and removal of properties damaged by flooding or at risk of future
flooding. Although property buyout programs involve high initial costs for the government,
in some studies the benefit-to-cost ratio of property buyouts was estimated to be as high
10
Cost-effectiveness of flood risk adaptation measures for storm surges in the far future
==PAGE:24==
as five to one. A well-designed property buyout program can effectively eliminate flood
risk as well restore natural flood protection along shorelines. It may also release land for
recreational purposes. On the other hand, a poorly designed program lacks transparency,
increases social inequities, incites opposition from property owners, and erodes trust in
the process. Some important design considerations for the property buyout program are
whether participation should be mandatory or voluntary and the level of compensation
offered to property owners. Voluntary participation is usually more socially acceptable,
however the acceptance rate is typically low or moderate. Mandatory buyouts on the
other hand are more efficient but less socially acceptable. Higher levels of compensation
(e.g. a buyout offer based on the pre-flood market price) increase the cost of the program
but has a higher social acceptability (Raikes et al., 2023).
Thus, the total costs of retreat can be described as an acquisition cost as well as an
opportunity cost arising from the fact that the land areas to which relocation takes place
cannot be used for other purposes such as agriculture. There exist some studies on the
total cost of the retreat (e.g. Pinter and Rees, 2021) as well as unit costs (e.g. Han et al.,
2023). There is generally a lack of data on retreat unit costs, however. The unit costs
are very difficult to generalize: in the case of a property buyout program, for example, the
cost is largely location-dependent and may also be influenced by socioeconomic factors.
2.3
Institutional practice in Denmark
In this section, the governing institution on coastal flood risk in Denmark is described as
well as the regulations on assessment and management of flood risk. The identified risk
areas in Denmark to whose municipalities the regulations apply are presented. Lastly, the
current design practice is described.
2.3.1
Governing agencies & regulations
In Denmark, the official coastal government agency is Kystdirektoratet (The Danish Coastal
Authority). They perform regulatory tasks in collaboration with stakeholders, including the
production of data on storm surges and coastal protection that the municipalities later
use in their storm surge strategies as well as implementation of different coastal protec-
tion measures (Andersen, 2023; Kystdirektoratet, n.d.-a). They base their calculations
on climate data from the portal Klimaatlas developed by DMI (Andersen, 2023). Every
few years Kystdirektoratet produces high tide statistics for their water meters, the latest
being produced in 2017. These statistics are comprised of the sea level rise relative to the
mean sea level for different return periods and are computed by use of distribution func-
tions using observed extreme values (Kystdirektoratet, 2018a). The regulation governing
assessment and management of flood risk from the ocean in Denmark is the Floods Di-
rective (BEK nr 894 af 21/06/2016, 2016) which is the Danish implementation of EU’s
directive (Directive 2007/60/EC, 2007).
Kystdirektoratet has determined 14 areas that are particularly prone to flooding, see Fig-
ure 2.4. According to Danish law, the municipalities in the risk areas must develop risk
management plans including a flood risk assessment and adaptation measures. The risk
assessment must include at least three present time scenarios (Kystdirektoratet, 2020a):
• Events with small probability of occurrence or extreme events (the 95th percentile
of a 1,000-year event or the largest historical storm surge)
• Events with moderate probability of occurrence (T=100 yrs)
• Events with large probability of occurrence, where it is relevant (T=20 yrs)

2.3.2
Current design practice
The current practice in the municipalities in Denmark is to design the protection measures
so that the city is protected against a storm surge occurring in 2100 at a return period of
100 years and using the climate scenario RCP-8.5 as described in the methodology devel-
oped by Kystdirektoratet (Kystdirektoratet, 2020a). Some cities want to secure protection
at a higher return period, such as Copenhagen at T=1000 years by 2100 with the metro
being protected at T=10,000 years (Københavns Kommune, 2017). Many of the protec-
tion technologies currently used and investigated in Denmark assume that storm surges
occur independently from other extreme events such as increased river flow (Arnbjerg-
Nielsen et al., 2019).
The required protection level PL for a particular return period and year is estimated using
the following equation:
PL = RL + HTS + MSLR −PGRB
(2.1)
where RL is the reference level, HTS the high tide statistics, MSLR the mean sea level
rise change, and PGRB is the post-glacial rebound. To this is added the additional impact
from waves (Klagenberg, 2023b).
In a study by Faragò et al., 2018, it was found that contrasting with historical protection
interventions, coastal protection measures implemented in recent years tend to enable
multi-functionality, e.g. by combining protection, recreation, and improved connections
from the coasts/harbour to the city, thus improving urban landscape, recreation, and aes-
thetic values. Hard solutions such as dikes, sea walls, and sluices still seem to be the
default protection technology. The same pattern is seen at an international level, notably
in the Netherlands. When it comes to designing hard solutions, Yang et al., 2015 propose
to use a conditional risk probability-based seawall height design method to determine
the optimal seawall height, this to incorporate the dependence between water levels and
wind speeds. There is no universal design standard for seawall heights - instead, the de-
sign depends on the socio-economic conditions and the natural environment (Yang et al.,
2015), and in Denmark’s case, on regulations.

2.4.1
Flood hazard assessment
Flood hazard is the quantification of the flood characteristics such as extent and loca-
tion given a certain return period. Commonly the flood hazard is described as the spatial
distribution of the calculated inundation depth as a function of the return period (Olesen
et al., 2017). The assessment is comprised of three components: the extreme external
loading of the system, the hydrology in the urban context, and the urban drainage rout-
ing including the physical layout of the city, the sewer system, and other water drainage
sources (Zhou et al., 2012). In a storm surge context, the extreme external loading is
comprised of tidal surges and waves. Climate change will impact the return period of a
given extreme external loading. Most commonly, climate change leads to a decrease in
the return period, resulting in a higher probability of a given loading to occur in the future
(Zhou et al., 2012).
2.4.2
Vulnerability assessment
The flood vulnerability analysis describes what losses are incurred when an area is ex-
posed to a given hazard (Zhou et al., 2012). It is commonly described through a damage
cost assessment in which the flood damage costs are divided into direct and indirect
losses and whether the loss is tangible or not. Direct losses are losses that occur as a
result of direct contact with the water, whereas indirect losses occur merely as a con-
sequence of the flooding. As for the difference between tangible and intangible losses,
tangible losses are losses that can be objectively quantified in monetary value. Direct
losses are furthermore correlated with the flood duration while indirect losses can have
long-term effects (months or years) (Olesen et al., 2017). An overview of the different

Methods of cost estimation
There are a range of different methods used for quantification of the different classes.
The best understood damage class is direct tangible damages which is always partly or
fully included in the damage assessment. The three leading methods for quantification of
direct tangible damages are damage assessments through insurance data, the unit cost
method, and stage-depth damage curves. Using the first method, the cost represents the
replacement costs and is found using the insurance pay-out. In the unit cost method, an
average loss value is applied to each individual damage type. The average loss value can
be found based on e.g. insurance pay-outs, expert knowledge, or previous flood experi-
ences. The stage-depth damage curve method accounts for both the area being flooded
and the inundation depth (Olesen et al., 2017). For intangible losses, the quantification
can be difficult and have thus been neglected in the majority of damage cost assess-
ments. Some of the methods include the concepts of ”willingness to pay”, the travel cost
method, and using indices (Kystdirektoratet, 2018b; Olesen et al., 2017). An overview of

Methods
This chapter covers the applied methods in this thesis. As seen in Figure 4.1, the thesis is
divided into four parts: determination of future storm surge scenarios, definition of damage
categories and unacceptable flood risk, flood risk assessment, and finally a cost-benefit
analysis of different flood risk adaptation measures. The aims of each analysis are as
follows:
1. Determination of future storm surge scenarios
• Obtain an equation for determining the sea level in a storm surge and thus the re-
quired protection level in any year x at return period T under the SSP5-8.5 climate
scenario
2. Definition of damage categories and unacceptable flood risk
• Define four damage categories to be evaluated later in the flood risk assessment and
cost-benefit analysis and define when flood risk is unacceptable for each damage
category
3. Flood risk assessment
• Evaluate three flood damage frameworks and based on this decide on one frame-
work to use for the subsequent cost-benefit analysis
4. Cost-benefit analysis of different flood risk adaptation measures
• Evaluate four different flood risk adaptation scenarios in terms of their cost-effectiveness
In this thesis, we are approaching the topic from two different angles: one is changes
in unacceptable flood risk over time whereas the other is cost-benefit of protection. The
two have some overlaps, but they have different assumptions and may lead to different
results.

Definition of damage categories and unacceptable flood
risk
In this section, the damage categories and the criteria for unacceptable flood risk are
defined. Furthermore, the mapping of unacceptable flood risk in GIS is explained.
4.2.1
Damage categories and unacceptable flood risk
It was decided to include four damage categories in the analysis: buildings, city develop-
ment, critical city infrastructure, and critical transport infrastructure. Each damage cate-
gory has a number of subcategories. In order to define the unacceptable level of flood
risk for each category, a lifetime and design return period were found for each category,
based on available data and assumptions from discussions with supervisors where the
following questions were asked:
• Lifetime: How long is the building expected to last before it is replaced or undergoes
extensive renovations?
• Design return period: What kind of event does the building need to be able to han-
dle?
In essence, if the storm surge level for return period T in lifetime years from now on
is greater than the acceptable level, then we do not permit buildings there. The overall
considerations for the lifetime and design return period include:
• Lifetime: Attraction of other city development, investment cost, expected renova-
tions/replacement, ease of moving
• Design return period: Magnitude of damage/indispensability, existing damage frame-
works, existing natural disaster scheme

The categories with their corresponding subcategories and the underlying assumptions for
their lifetimes and design return periods are seen in Table 4.1 and are further elaborated
below.
A regular building is usually assumed to have a lifetime of about 80 years. However,
the maximum time period during which you can take a loan for real estate in Denmark
is 30 years. A property owner should be able to leave their property without a debt, as
they would otherwise lose their entire savings in the house. Based on this, it could be
argued that buildings should have a lifetime of 30 years. As for the design return period,
currently businesses and private owners may apply for compensation from the Danish
Natural Hazards Council if the storm surge classifies as a 20-year event or greater (LBK
nr 1184 af 15/08/2022, 2022). Combining the lifetime and this return period, it is assumed
that nobody wants to buy a building that will be flooded at a 20-year event in 30 years.
City development infrastructure is defined as businesses inside the city core and cultural
and educational institutions. The city core, culture, and education are assumed to attract
further development and make the city overall attractive to live in. This also makes the
structures hard to move. Thus, they could be argued to have a long lifetime. Compared
to cultural and educational institutions, it could be argued that businesses may have a
relatively short lifetime (80 or 60 years). As for the required protection level, the design
return period for a school is normally set to 100 years (Klagenberg, 2023a). On the other
hand, some cultural institutions may be invaluable and should be protected at all costs
(i.e. T = ∞), arguing for greater protection.
Critical city infrastructure is defined as utilities (water, energy, waste) and health facilities
(hospitals, medical centers) and can be assumed to have a relatively long lifetime. Water
and energy supply and waste management are integral to a functioning city, and hospitals
are needed for treatment if there are any casualties from an extreme event. As such, the
design return period is commonly set to 10,000 years (Klagenberg, 2023a).
The critical transport infrastructure category only contains one subcategory: the main
railway station. It could be argued that its lifetime depends on the surrounding structures
which function as feedback elements to the railway station, giving it a longer lifetime. This
also works the other way around: as in the category city development, a railway station is
assumed to attract further development and is therefore difficult to move. Furthermore, if
moved, then it is expected that many surrounding structures must also be moved. Den-
mark’s oldest railway station dates back to 1847 (Dansk Jernbanehistorisk Arkiv, n.d.),
likewise arguing for a long lifetime. When it comes to the design return period, the railway
station can be considered indispensable to the city and and its design return period is thus
set to 10,000 years (Klagenberg, 2023a).
Some simplifications have been made to limit the number of scenarios and thus analyses
to be made. One of them is a uniform lifetime and design return period across each
damage category. Another is limiting the number of categories to four and not including
e.g. agriculture or green areas. A third simplification is the exclusion of highways as
critical transport infrastructure which would have required a lifetime of 25-30 years and
a design return period of 25 years (Vejdirektoratet, 2017), which is too different from the
expected lifetime and design return period of the railway station.
The unacceptable levels of flood risk are defined by combining the lifetime and the design
return period, arguing that one should not invest in a category in a particular year if the
category will be flooded within its lifetime. For buildings, for example, if assuming a lifetime
of 30 years and a design return period of 20 years, this means that if a building is flooded

4.3.3
Risk model
The EAD is calculated by integrating flood damage over all probabilities using numerical
integration and the trapezoidal rule as described by Olsen et al., 2015 and using Eq.
2.3. The selected return periods for the EAD calculation for the comparison of damage
frameworks are T={1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100} years and it is calculated for current climate
(year 2023). In the calculation of the EAD for the scenarios in the cost-benefit analysis,
13 return periods are used (T={1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000,
10,000}) and it is calculated for the years 2023, 2050, 2100, 2150, 2200, 2250, 2300, and
2350. If damage occurs at T=1 - at which level it is assumed to be zero damage - then
the damage at T=1 is set to zero. The EAD is interpolated between the years for which
the calculation has been conducted.
4.4
Cost-benefit analysis
In the cost-benefit analysis, four different adaptation scenarios are assessed in terms of
their cost-effectiveness. In each scenario, it is assessed whether a protection measure
is socioeconomically viable when considering a reference alternative. This is done by
computing the NPV for each scenario. In each scenario, protection is economically viable
as compared to the reference alternative if the NPV is positive. The overall assumptions
and considerations taken in the calculation of the NPV are as follows:
• The EAD is linear over time and can thus be interpolated between 2023-2050, 2050-
2100, 2100-2150, 2150-2200, 2200-2250, and 2250-2350
• The sea level beyond year 2300 is assumed to be constant due to lack of data
beyond year 2300. Thus, the EAD in 2350 equals the EAD in 2300
• For the reference alternative managed retreat undertaken during a protection level
of 3 m, the EAD is interpolated between the years 2050-2150, 2150-2250, and 2250-
2350. In year 2350 the EAD is assumed to be 0 due to all exposed buildings having
been moved
• No changes apart from the climate adaptation scenario will occur in the catchment
for the entire time horizon considered
• The only benefit in all scenarios is the reduction in flood risk
• The protection measure used in all scenarios is a sea dike with an assumed lifetime
of 100 years
• There is no protection once the sea level is 1 cm or more above the dike crest. Thus,
there is no difference between the failed defense and no defense for events above

In SCALGO, it is possible to construct a sea dike and generate flood maps taking the dike
into account as well as extract the total volume of the dike. Thus, this would allow for
computing a unit cost based on the volume and the cost of materials. This construction
would require details on both dike width, volume, and height as well as the construction
of a side slope. Furthermore, in order to ensure full protection against a certain sea level,
the critical points for flooding at that sea level would need to be identified so that the
dike can be placed in the right location to prevent intrusion and thus flooding in low-lying
areas behind the critical point. Moreover, every time the dike would be raised, a new
dike with a new height would have to be constructed. For every of these new changes in
SCALGO, the map has to be recomputed. It was deemed that this entire process would be
too cumbersome and that it would need too many technical assumptions on dike design.
Consequently, the flood maps in SCALGO are generated without a dike and it was chosen
to go for a unit cost that only requires the length and height of the dike as inputs.
Managed retreat
As described in Section 2.2.3, finding generalized unit costs for managed retreat is tricky.
As we do not know what the retreat will cost, we make simplified assumptions and divide
the cost of retreat into (1) acquisition of buildings and (2) opportunity cost arising from
the fact that the land areas to which we relocate the buildings cannot be used for other
purposes.
The maximum acquisition or buyout cost is estimated to be 17,655 DKK/m2 which is the
average property value across all residence types in Vejle (Boliga, n.d.). Property buyouts

is arguably the most expensive acquisition option as a managed retreat strategy due to
high property prices. It is not very likely that all residents will accept 100% buyout, nor is it
likely that the municipality will offer buyouts at 100% of the market price. Even when con-
sidering the other costs of retreat - new infrastructure (water, electricity, roads), buildings,
city core, and recreation - this high estimate should cover all costs and likely a bit more.
An acquisition cost of zero is not realistic as people will want to receive compensation for
giving up their home and assets. Thus, by investigating both an extremely low (0 DKK/m2)
and an extremely high (17,655 DKK/m2) acquisition cost scenario, we hope to estimate a
cost interval for retreat. As for the opportunity cost, it is set to 5,000 DKK/ha/yr. This is an
estimate of how much you can profit from 1 ha of agricultural land in Denmark based on
revenue data from forestry, crop production, and livestock production (Danmarks Statis-
tik, 2021; Thomsen, 2015). Almost 60% of Denmark’s total area consists of agriculture
(Danmarks Statistik, 2020) and as such the value of agricultural land is probably the most
relevant for Denmark when determining this cost.
The municipality will have to take measures outside of the city with new attractive city
development as part of the retreat strategy in order to avoid a ”dead” city caused by the
expected lack of investments in the affected area as well as to make the strategy socially
acceptable amongst the residents (also see Section 2.2.3). We do not know when these
measures will be taken, and as such we distribute the retreat costs on all years. The
retreat will take place gradually with a new investment decision in years 2050, 2150, and
2250 based on the level of unacceptable flood risk in years 2150, 2250, and 2350. As
such, the retreat area and hence cost of retreat will be interpolated between years 2050-
2150, 2150-2250, and 2250-2350. As described above, we will investigate two scenarios:
a low retreat cost scenario with an acquisition cost of 0 DKK/m2 and a high retreat cost
scenario with an acquisition cost of 17,655 DKK/m2.

Discussion
This chapter contains a discussion of the obtained results. It starts with the key findings
followed by limitations of the present study and lastly an outlook with next steps.
6.1
Key findings
Below the key findings from the thesis are presented. These are compared with findings
from other studies whenever relevant.
6.1.1
Future sea levels and unacceptable flood risk
The projected future storm surge levels and thus required protection levels are increasing
rapidly towards the end of the century and year 2300. What used to be a 10,000-year
event in 2050 will be equivalent to a 20-year event in 2100 and a 20-year event in 2300 will
far exceed a 10,000-year event in 2100 (Figure 5.2). This increase is largely dominated
by the increase in mean sea level which rises exponentially towards year 2300. By year
2050, the mean sea level will have risen to 29 cm DVR90 whereas by 2300 it will be 4.28
m (Figure 5.1). The protection levels for a T=100 year event in the same years are 1.93 m
and 5.72 m (Table 5.2). The impact of the mean sea level on the required protection level
especially in the future is thus evident, with the mean sea level going from contributing by
only 15% to the protection level in year 2050 to contributing by 75% in year 2300. The
post-glacial rebound - our natural countermeasure against sea level rise - increases with
time, but its impact still amounts to only 23 cm in year 2300 (Table 5.3). Thus, we will
need to adapt quickly and smartly in order to accommodate these expected higher sea
levels - both the everyday mean sea level and the increased number and severity of storm
surges.
All damage categories are exposed to unacceptable flood risk already in 2050 without any
protection measures implemented (Figures 5.4, D.1, D.2, D.3). By 2300, more than 50%
of the buildings in each category are expected to be exposed to unacceptable flood risk
(Figure 5.5). If we implement a protection level of 3 m, then buildings and city development
are protected only temporarily (Figure 5.6). The damage category with the most damages
is expected to be the category buildings followed by the category city development. It is
clear that Vejle is already exposed to unacceptable flood risk across all categories and
that they will need to implement appropriate protection measures in order to alleviate this
risk both on a short- and long-term basis.
6.1.2
Flood risk assessment
In the comparison of the three flood damage frameworks, the framework from Kystdirek-
toratet led to the most damages followed by DTU SkadesØkonomi. The urban pluvial
flood risk unit cost model yielded very little damage in comparison (Figure 5.7, Table 5.5).
It was decided to go with the framework DTU SkadesØkonomi as it had damage functions
available for all subcategories and because of the logarithmic nature of the same, as well
as since its damage cost estimates were the most realistic when compared to the study
by COWI, 2017.
6.1.3
Cost-effectiveness of protection
In the cost-benefit analysis, it was found that both short-term protection until year 2100
and long-term protection until year 2300 can be economically viable, with scenarios 1-3 all
having high NPVs (Table 5.7). There is a considerable difference in total costs between
long-term and short-term protection, however: in scenarios 1 and 2, the total costs in

present value amount to 698 M DKK and 697 M DKK, respectively, whereas the total
costs for scenario 3 amount to more than 3 billion DKK (Table 5.6). Of these costs, the
maintenance cost is substantial, constituting 50-75% of the total costs. This is consistent
with the study by Nicholls et al., 2019 where maintenance costs were found to be more
than half of the total annual costs under a RCP8.5 scenario. The considerable immediate
costs associated with traditional flood risk protection - which will only increase in the future
as we build higher and higher dikes - as well as the concept of protecting our current life
and lifestyle raise the question of how we can rethink our cities and our lifestyles so that
we minimize or avoid risk altogether in a future where high mean sea levels and frequent
storm surges will undeniably become the norm. As described by Wiberg et al., 2023,
dynamic adaptation pathways could be used in the flood risk adaptation planning, so as
to not over-invest and so as to easily adapt the solution to new data and avoid so called
lock-ins. If we just continue raising the protection level of the city, then we easily become
locked in on this one solution that is not robust in the long run and which, as Wenger,
2015 pointed out, may even be maladaptive.
Managed retreat was shown to be cost-effective only in a low cost scenario where we have
an acquisition cost of zero. In the high cost scenario where we have an acquisition cost
equivalent to the property value, managed retreat was not cost-effective (Table 5.7). It is
evidently the acquisition cost that drives the cost of retreat, with the opportunity cost being
negligible in comparison (Figures 5.14, 5.15). A realistic retreat cost may lie between the
two acquisition cost estimates, resulting in a total investment cost of 1.06-33.4 billion DKK
(Table 5.6). If we were to have compared managed retreat with the alternative of doing
nothing, it is possible that managed retreat would have proven to be cost-effective even if
the acquisition cost is high: there are some documented cases of managed retreat with a
positive CBR (e.g. Hino et al., 2017b) and in some studies the BCR of property buyouts
has been estimated to be as high as five to one (Raikes et al., 2023).
When looking at a time horizon to year 2100 only, it is probably pretty easy and straightfor-
ward to just construct a dike or make other smaller interventions rather than a life-altering
intervention such as retreat. In Vejle’s case, a 100-year event in 2100 will ”only” result in
a storm surge sea level of about 2.3 m (Figure 5.2). However, once we look at year 2300
where the storm surge level will be almost 6 m, we realize that just continuing to raise the
dike indefinitely may not be the best solution. Faragò et al., 2018 argue that hard protec-
tion technologies traditionally treat the symptoms only and do not address the root of the
problem - rather, they claim that climate change adaptation is seen as an event and tends
to be a one-shot operation that aims to fix the problem in one go. It could be argued that
the short-term thinking where we only look until year 2100 may reinforce this approach
to coastal protection, whereas if we look further ahead, suddenly retreat becomes an at-
tractive - and possibly even the only - option. Many municipalities pride themselves on
their ”adaptive” storm surge strategy - but can it really be called adaptive if it is not fully
embracing the prospect of flood risk beyond 2100 and that climate change is here to stay?
As described by Thaler, 2021, the adopted retreat strategy leads to the question of so-
cial justice. Questions that are commonly asked here include who is living in the hazard
prone areas, who bears the costs of the structural and non-structural measures, and who
gets involved in the decision-making process? These are all questions that need to be
addressed in any flood risk management process (Thaler, 2021). For example, from a util-
itarian perspective, the decision on managed retreat should be based on a cost-benefit
analysis only and the distribution of costs and benefits within the community is ignored. In
an egalitarian setting, managed retreat should instead ensure the same possibilities and
changes to each member of the community and vulnerable groups should be supported

by public administration. In a libertarian managed retreat policy, private property owners
need to organize the relocation process by themselves alone, without any financial or
other support as they are solely responsible to knowing and deciding to live in a hazard-
prone area. The contribution of public administration is here negligible (Thaler, 2021).
Regardless of the adopted strategy, it is important that it is accepted by the community.
Furthermore, extensive citizen engagement where local residents and decision-makers
are empowered and put in the center of these processes is crucial in managed retreat
as demonstrated across case studies in the US where effective community engagement
enhanced adaptation planning and built community support for managed retreat strate-
gies (Spidalieri et al., 2020). One thing is for certain: we need to rethink how we protect
or design our cities in the future where high sea levels will be the norm rather than an
exceptional case. Retreat may be an essential part of this transformation: as argued
by Siders et al., 2021, ”the concept of managed retreat has potential to promote social
transformation by changing perceptions, narratives, and norms about climate adaptation”.
They argue that retreat may be able to turn climate risks from abstract events into imme-
diate and personal experiences. As the word ”retreat” is politically difficult and often seen
as a negative word, it is important to consider the framing of the retreat strategy. A fa-
mous example taken from fluvial flood risk adaptation is the Dutch program ”Room for the
River” that suggests a conceptualization of the river as an equal worthy of equal space
within the developed landscape. Although the program has not resulted in a cessation
of levee-building in the Netherlands, it has spread internationally and to other hazards.
Siders et al., 2021 argue that the idea of ”making room for the river” suggests the need
for a co-existence of nature and humans in ways that are protective of nature as opposed
to human interest alone.
6.2
Limitations
In this section, the different limitations of the present study are discussed. These include
the uncertainties in future sea levels, the limitations in the flood risk assessment, and the
limitations in the cost-benefit analysis.
6.2.1
Future sea levels and unacceptable flood risk
As seen in Figure 2.1, there is a considerable uncertainty associated with the projection of
mean sea level, especially beyond year 2100. There is no projections from IPCC beyond
2300, furthermore, making the estimated sea levels for unacceptable flood risk (Tables D.2
and H.2) very uncertain. The rate of mean sea level rise furthermore changes across the
globe (NOAA, 2021), with the rate having shown to be greater in the northern hemisphere
(Wöppelmann et al., 2014). There is also a high uncertainty associated with the high tide
statistics used to estimate the required protection levels, as they are based on very little
data (see Section 3.2.2).
6.2.2
Flood risk assessment
In the damage cost assessment, three different damage frameworks were compared so
as to choose one framework for the subsequent damage cost calculations in the cost-
benefit analysis. For both Kystdirektoratet and DTU SkadesØkonomi, the damage func-
tions used in the present study are largely a simplification of the actual frameworks on
which they are based. One of the biggest simplifications in the damage cost assessment
is that only tangible direct damages are considered in the damage framework whereas
intangible direct damages and indirect damages are not considered at all. As an exam-
ple, the damage cost of a flooded hospital is expected to be much greater than the costs
resulting from damages to the building itself and its inventory, as the lack of a functioning
hospital will greatly increase the stress on other health centers and negatively impact the
58
Cost-effectiveness of flood risk adaptation measures for storm surges in the far future
==PAGE:72==
lives of countless people. Furthermore, we only consider four damage categories, and
some of these damage categories do not have their own damage function in the dam-
age framework from which they were taken and must instead assume a damage function
from the most similar damage function class considered in the framework. This leads to
the damage functions across categories to be very similar or the same, thus resulting in
a lack of variation in damages between different damage categories. The damages to
basements or other floors than the ground floor are also not accounted for in these sim-
plified damage frameworks. On the other hand, the flood maps extracted from SCALGO
Live are likely overestimating the water depths compared to the observed water depth in
a real storm surge due to infiltration not being considered.
In the damage framework Kystdirektoratet, the use of a property value in the damage
cost calculations leads to a high discrepancy in total damage between locations, with
private houses in municipalities with the lowest property values having much less damage
than private houses in municipalities with high property values. Thus, it could be argued
whether it is really adequate to use property values in the calculations which vary greatly
across locations (K. Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2022).
6.2.3
Cost-effectiveness of protection
The cost-benefit analysis in the present study has several limitations. One of these is
the uncertainty in the unit costs for dike construction. The costs of dikes vary widely
as seen in Table 2.2. Furthermore, using a dike unit cost that only has the height and
length as inputs is a very simplified way to estimate the dike cost, as the cost will increase
greatly depending on the material used as well as other design considerations such as
the construction of a foreland. Higher protection levels are furthermore likely to require
longer and possibly wider dikes, increasing the cost of protection. The simplified unit
cost is however useful when you do not know the detailed design as in the present study.
Realistically, coastal defense in Vejle Fjord would likely need to be a combination of static
and dynamic structures, e.g. by installation of a sluice or pumps as part of the protection
measure, to allow for the continued use of the harbour and rain and river water to exit
the catchment. These additional costs would also need to be accounted for. Lastly, the
additional impact of waves leading to higher required protection levels and thus higher
dikes would also lead to increased costs.
As for the estimated annual damages from flooding, there are many simplifications in the
calculations. One of the largest simplifying assumptions is that defenses breach when the
design level is exceeded, meaning that there is no difference between the failed defense
and no defense for events above the design level. This is what Hallegatte et al., 2013b call
the most pessimistic model. In reality, the coastal defense may still continue to provide
some residual protection even when the sea level exceeds the protection level. Here
it could be assumed that in a storm surge with a sea level of e.g. 4 m and a protection
level of e.g. 3 m, the water level inside the protection will be reduced to 1 m. That residual
protection would continue indefinitely is however a very optimistic model. An intermediate
assumption could be that defenses progressively collapse as the sea level increases until
it reaches a certain threshold where full collapse occurs (Hallegatte et al., 2013b).
Other simplifications in the damage cost assessment include the interpolation of EAD
between years, that damage from erosion is not included, and that the additional impact
of waves is not considered in the estimation of the sea level in a storm surge event.
We also do not consider compound extreme events, which is not a realistic assumption
for future storm surges where compound extreme events are expected to occur more
frequently (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2019; IPCC, 2021). In Kystdirektoratet’s modelled

scenarios for the risk areas, only the combined effect of increased river flow and storm

risk area like Vejle, however, threats also include a high groundwater table and possible
extreme rain (Kystdirektoratet, 2020b).
The major uncertainty when estimating the costs of managed retreat is the great lack of
data on retreat costs as well as the choice of retreat strategy which strongly affects the
total costs. In the present study, the retreat area was based on the exposure to unac-
ceptable flood risk which in turn was defined based on assumptions regarding the design
return period and lifetime of a certain building type. The maximum retreat cost was based
on a 100% buyout strategy so as to make sure to also account for the costs of new infras-
tructure such as roads and sewerage. Lastly, the investment decision on the new area to
retreat was taken every 100 years. Thus, both the decision and financing of the managed
retreat are taken by the municipality. In a real-life scenario, the municipality may choose
to retreat a much smaller area the first 100 years or take a new retreat decision more often
based on the newest data for storm surge levels, thus adapting the retreat strategy when-
ever needed. Or, the municipality may choose to only support low-income households
or decrease the support for high-income households, or partner up with other private or
public institutions. Furthermore, the criteria for unacceptable flood risk determined in this
study may not be accepted as a design standard by the municipality or its partners or the
citizens. Instead, they would need to develop their own criteria for when to initiate retreat,
together with all stakeholders.
In the present study it was seen that the total opportunity cost was negligible compared
to the maintenance costs for the dike and the acquisition cost for retreat. This result was
unexpected considering the large area used in the calculation. This opens the question
whether the opportunity unit cost was set too low. In the present study it was set to equal
the approximate profit from agricultural land. However, one could imagine that if buildings
were moved to areas close to the city or to otherwise attractive areas or if industry or
business replaced agriculture, the opportunity cost would be much greater.
6.3
Outlook
In future research on flood risk adaptation for storm surges, there are a few aspects that
would be interesting or even required to investigate further. These topics are based on
the key findings from this thesis (Section 6.1) and its identified limitations (Section 6.2).
One major aspect is the large need of data on the costs of retreat which must be addressed
in future research. Another is the managed retreat strategy, in particular the incorporation
of social justice, leading to new cost assumptions and possibly new recommendations
for flood risk adaptation. Thus, in future studies, it would be helpful to consider different
alternatives of retreat as well as involve the municipality and citizens in this process to get
more realistic alternatives.
It is recommended to have a dynamic adaptive pathways approach so as to minimize
the uncertainty of the storm surge level projections as new data come in, adapt to new
technological developments, avoid lock-ins, and minimize the costs of flood risk adapta-
tion. As for the question whether we really need to look further into the future than 2100,
the answer is yes: current flood risk adaptation measures do not prepare the city nor the
people for the whole new reality that we can expect beyond year 2100 where we may
experience flooding from mean sea level rise alone. Today’s storm surge strategies will
arguably lead to lock-ins and solutions that are not resilient in the long term. Thus, future

Conclusion
The objectives of this thesis were to (1) investigate how coastal flooding in Denmark will
change further into the future than the current design year for coastal protection measures,
and (2) evaluate what levels of protection measures are viable from a socioeconomic
perspective, using the city of Vejle, Jutland as a case area. The first research question
was addressed by investigating the future mean sea levels and storm surge levels for
Vejle as well as developing criteria for unacceptable levels of flood risk and assessing
which areas in Vejle would be exposed to this risk. The second research question was
addressed by a cost-benefit analysis where four different flood risk adaptation scenarios
were assessed. In three of the scenarios, adaptation consisted of the construction and
incremental raise of a sea dike, whereas in the fourth scenario, protection by incrementally
raising the dike was compared against managed retreat as an alternative solution. The
conclusions of this thesis are as follows:
• Mean sea levels and storm surge levels in Vejle are projected to rise fast. Thus,
we will need to adapt quickly and smartly in order to accommodate these expected
higher sea levels - both the everyday mean sea level and the increased number and
severity of storm surges
• Almost all of Vejle inner city is already exposed to unacceptable flood risk by 2050, in
particular difficult-to-move structures such as the railway station. Thus, appropriate
adaptation measures need to be implemented in order to alleviate this risk both on
a short- and long-term basis
• Short-term flood risk adaptation until year 2100 where we protect to 2 m as com-
pared to doing nothing as well as protecting to 3 m as compared to maintaining a
protection level of 2 m are both cost-effective. Long-term adaptation where we pro-
tect against a 100-year event until year 2300 is also cost-effective when compared
to maintaining a protection level of 3 m. When considering managed retreat as an
alternative option to this protection, high retreat costs result in a continued raise of
the dike being the most cost-effective option
• Managed retreat may be cost-effective depending on the retreat costs, the retreat
strategy, and the alternative it is compared against
• The total cost of protection is dominated by the maintenance cost whereas the total
cost of retreat is dominated by the acquisition cost
• The total protection cost for Vejle until year 2300 can be expected to be around 3
billion DKK. If one were to initiate retreat instead, the total investment cost would lie
between 1-33 billion DKK
• It is necessary to look further into the future than 2100 when planning flood risk
adaptation, as today’s adaptation measures are typically not resilient in the long
term
• It is recommended to use a truly adaptive approach that prevents lock-ins, incor-
porates the newest research and technologies, prevents overinvesting, and treats
the root of the problem rather than its symptoms - i.e. treating climate change as a
phenomenon that is here to stay
• We need more data on the costs of different managed retreat strategies

C
GIS workflow
Below the workflow in SCALGO and QGIS for creation of flood hazard maps is explained
in detail.
C.0.1
Extracting flood rasters from SCALGO Live
Firstly a workspace encompassing the risk area for Vejle (3.2 was created in SCALGO
Live, with the analysis Sea-Level Rise enabled. Subsequently, the buildings data and
flood maps were downloaded. All hydrological corrections (one-way or two-way subterra-
neous passages/sluices/sea walls) as reported by the municipality to GeoDanmark were
enabled when creating the flood maps. The flood maps were created based on the value
for the estimated sea level rise change as computed by Eq. 2.1. Whenever the available
value in SCALGO Live did not correspond with the calculated value, the value closest to
the calculated value was chosen. After the update in July 2023, all values were available
and as such the calculated value was chosen.
C.0.2
QGIS
Creating the buildings vector layer
Firstly, the buildings layer was downloaded as a table where subsequently each building
was assigned a subcategory and category according to their BBR code (Tables B.1-B.3)
using the function VLOOKUP in Microsoft Excel. The table was then joined with the build-
ings layer in QGIS. A city core area was then defined in QGIS based on the pedestrian
way, with all businesses within this area being defined as businesses within city core
and thus pertaining to the city development category (Figure C.1). To filter out smaller
negligible/misclassified structures, a 25m2 area filter was applied. At this point, 12.8% of
the buildings do not have an assigned category due to missing a BBR code. For these
buildings, the procedure was as following:
1. A 50 m2 area filter was applied to filter out structures that were not buildings (e.g.
tanks in the water treatment plant)
2. A 3x3 neighbourhood filter was applied on the remaining buildings without a BBR
code using the tool ”Join attributes by nearest”, assigning buildings within a distance
of 5 m to other buildings to these buildings’ category (n=3)
3. Any remaining buildings without an assigned category were assigned to the subcat-
egory households in the category buildings

Extracting water depth for generation of unacceptable flood risk maps by use of
tools in QGIS
For the flood rasters for unacceptable flood risk in 2050, 2100, 2200, and 2300, a 3x3

package so as to expand them to intersect with the buildings vector layer. This was fol-

statistics”.

E
Python damage tool
The damage tool requires four inputs: a vector layer with the buildings containing the
subcategories, a raster layer containing the inundation depth, the damage functions for
each subcategory, and a vector layer with the extent of the calculation. The damage func-
tion can be of the type stage-depth or unit cost and are defined so that damage occurs
at 10 cm intervals. If the water depth is in between an interval then it is rounded down.
The script then calculates flood damage by intersecting the buildings vector with the flood
raster inside the specified extent, returning the total damage for each specified subcate-
gory. The outputs of the tool are the buildings vector layer now with a unique ID for each
building as a new feature and text files for each subcategory containing the damage and
water depth for each building ID. The different inputs and outputs of the script is seen in
