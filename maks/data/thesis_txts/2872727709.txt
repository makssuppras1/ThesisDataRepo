Abstract
Neutron Stars (NSs) are among the most extreme astrophysical objects, characterized by
immense mass density, strong gravitational fields, and powerful magnetic fields. Rotating
Neutron Stars (RNSs) exhibit a complex spacetime geometry with a frame-dragging effect
induced by their rotation as for Rotating Black Holes (RBHs). However, unlike RBHs which
are well described by the Kerr metric, RNSs metrics do not correspond to the Kerr metric
because their rotation induces equatorial flattening which makes them non-spherical objects:
additional relativistic multipole moments are needed to improve the description of RNS
spacetime geometry. Having a correct description of this environment is important to study
the motion of particles around RNSs which is crucial for gaining insights into astrophysical
processes such as accretion dynamics, relativistic jets, and high-energy radiation emission
like X-Ray bursts. We here aim to study the motion of particles in the curved spacetime
surrounding RNSs by numerically solving the geodesic equations. We start by reviewing dif-
ferent models used to describe the spacetime geometry of RNSs and we select an appropriate
metric that will be used to realize simulations of the motion of particles around RNSs. In
order to solve the equations of motion, we employ a geodesic solver implemented in Python,
using an energy-preserving Hamiltonian scheme. By analysing the influence of the RNS met-
ric parameters on the surrounding spacetime and the orbital properties, we aim to contribute
to a deeper understanding of relativistic astrophysics and provide insights into observational
signatures relevant to high-energy astrophysical missions.

Introduction
When they die, massive stars expel their outer layers in a supernova explosion, and their
core has two possible fates following gravitational collapse: either the collapse halts at some
point, leaving behind a compact remnant known as a Neutron Star (NS), or, if the degeneracy
pressure is insufficient to counteract gravity and maintain equilibrium, the remnant becomes
a Black Hole (BH). This latter outcome occurs if the mass-to-radius ratio of the progeni-
tor star exceeds a certain critical threshold value. Regarding NS, they are among the most
extreme objects in the universe because of their immense density, their strong gravitational
field and their powerful magnetic fields. These rotating relativistic stars exhibit complex
spacetime dynamics that require the framework of Albert Einstein’s theory of General Rel-
ativity (GR) for accurate understanding. Published in 1915, GR has completely changed
how gravity is considered, moving from an instantaneous gravitational force between massive
objects to a geometric interpretation as the curvature of the unified spacetime around mas-
sive objects. Given their extreme mass and compactness, NS require GR to get relevant and
correct descriptions of astrophysical processes such as accretion dynamics, relativistic jets,
and high-energy radiation emission like X-Ray bursts.
They represent a valuable opportunity to study the behaviour of matter in their extreme
environment, providing insights into the physics of matter at supranuclear densities, where
their Equation of State (EoS) remains uncertain, as well as into General Relativity, by testing
some of its predictions through the influence of the frame-dragging effect induced by their
rotation on particle motion, which affects the accretion dynamics, while light is deflected due
to the spacetime curvature of these compact objects. Studying the properties of a single NS is
also useful for better understanding systems containing a NS. In particular, Low-Mass X-ray
Binaries (LMXBs), where astrophysical processes such as accretion disks and X-ray bursts
result from the interaction between the NS and its low-mass companion star. One interesting
phenomenon is the Quasi-Periodic Oscillations (QPOs) of the observed X-ray flux from these
systems. QPOs are related to the accretion process in LMXBs through the characteristic
frequencies of orbital and precessional motion of particles around NSs. They could be used
to improve our understanding of the structure of accretion disks and provide an additional
method to estimate various properties of the NS, such as its mass, radius, and rotational
frequency. NSs are also of interest in the study of gravitational wave sources, such as binary
systems and NS mergers.
To study NS dynamics, it is necessary to have an accurate description of the spacetime geom-
etry, which is given by the metric tensor in GR. It plays a central role in both the Einstein
field equations (describing how energy-mass curves spacetime) and the geodesic equations
(governing the motion of objects in this spacetime). The metrics of compact objects have
been studied since shortly after the publication of General Relativity, beginning in 1916 with
Karl Schwarzschild’s solution to the Einstein field equations. This solution describes the
vacuum spacetime around an uncharged, non-rotating, spherically symmetric body and laid

the foundation for analytical studies of BHs within the framework of General Relativity.
In 1963, Roy Kerr discovered the exact solution for an uncharged but rotating BH (RBH),
which has a relatively simple form, depending only on the mass and angular momentum of
the BH. However, the Kerr metric is not accurate enough to provide a correct description of
the spacetime geometry around a rotating NS (RNS). Indeed, RNSs have a more complex
spacetime geometry than RBHs, which depends not only on their spin but also on their in-
ternal structure, which remains poorly known today. Being “physical objects” in the sense
that they have a material surface, RNSs possess a shape that can be distorted by their rota-
tion and consequently have a different multipolar structure than RBHs. As of today, there
exists no analytical solution for RNS metrics, contrary to RBHs, which have the unique Kerr
metric to describe their curvature of spacetime. For RNSs, different approaches have been
studied to derive their metrics in terms of multipole moments (mass, angular momentum,
mass quadrupole, etc.). One of the first is the Hartle-Thorne metric, derived by James B.
Hartle and Kip S. Thorne in 1968 for slowly rotating NSs. Other models of analytic, sta-
tionary, and axisymmetric external RNS metrics (vacuum solutions of GR) have since been
derived, without the slow-rotation approximation, though many of them have very complex
structures and expressions. Various codes have been developed to obtain numerical solutions
for RNSs in both slow and fast rotation regimes, such as the RNS code (Stergioulas &
Friedman 1995), with different options regarding their rotation (uniform or differential), the
considered EoS, etc.
In this project, we aim to study the motion of particles in the curved spacetime surround-
ing RNSs by numerically solving the geodesic equations. To achieve this, we first conduct
a literature review on relevant NS physics, relativistic dynamics, and RNS metric models.
This will allow us to select a suitable and convenient metric model that provides an accurate
description of the RNS spacetime geometry outside the NS body (exterior metric, in vacuum)
with a relatively simple form. We will use the Pappas metric (2017), which satisfies these
conditions and also has the advantage of not being restricted to the slow-rotation assumption,
unlike the Hartle-Thorne metric. It is an approximate vacuum solution constructed using
the Ernst formalism and expressed as an expansion in Weyl-Papapetrou coordinates (ρ, z),
considering the first five multipole moments of the RNS: the mass M, angular momentum J,
mass quadrupole M2, spin octupole S3, and mass hexadecapole M4. However, we will use its
expression in Schwarzschild-like spherical coordinates (r, θ), as given by Frutos-Alfaro (2019).
This metric will be implemented in a numerical Python code that uses an energy-preserving
Hamiltonian scheme to solve the geodesic equations, using the ADM formalism which uses a
“3+1” decomposition of the usual spacetime. We will then carry out simulations in different
scenarios to explore various topics, such as the frame-dragging effect of the RNS by compari-
son with the Schwarzschild and Kerr metrics, the equatorial plane motion by studying Stable
Circular Orbits (SCOs) and the Innermost Stable Circular Orbit (ISCO) property, and pro-
vide insights into perturbed circular orbits with their associated characteristic frequencies.
We also discuss, through the project report, recently investigated universal EoS-independent
parametrisations that reduce the parameter space for the RNS metric. This should serve as

Theory
2.1
General Relativity
2.1.1
General Relativity and Einstein’s field equations
To correctly describe NSs and their associated astrophysical processes, General Relativity
(GR) is required due to their extreme gravity, which arises from their immense matter den-
sity. Indeed, Newtonian physics is unable to provide relevant explanations for phenomena
such as spacetime frame-dragging, gravitational redshift, or gravitational waves from NS
mergers, as these effects are not modelled within a Newtonian framework. Moreover, it fails
to accurately describe the structure and stability properties of NSs, such as the maximum
mass. Fortunately, Albert Einstein’s theory of 1915 is capable of accurately representing
these concepts while making observable predictions that continue to be confirmed by current
observational data.
In GR, gravity is no longer an attractive force between massive objects with infinite range
and instantaneous action, as in Newton’s theory. Instead, it has a geometric interpretation,
associated with the curvature of a unified spacetime (three spatial and one temporal dimen-
sion) caused by the presence of massive objects. Gravity is represented by the metric tensor
gµν, where each component plays a role analogous to the Newtonian gravitational poten-
tial: the shape of spacetime is determined by gravity, and gravitation becomes the geometry
of spacetime. GR considers a tensorial gravitational potential, whereas Newton’s theory of
gravitation involves a scalar potential; GR therefore encompasses Newton’s theory, which
remains “valid” in the limit of weak gravitational fields. The metric tensor gµν is symmetric:
it has 16 components, but only 10 of them are distinct, and it is used in the definition of
the general expression of the Riemannian spacetime interval (corresponding to the spacetime
distance element), given by the following formula [1]:
ds2 = gµνdxµdxν
(1)

constant) [1]:

2.1.3
Particle motion in General Relativity (GR)
In GR, the motion of free-falling particles and light rays is governed by the geodesic equations.

2.2
Neutron Stars metrics
2.2.1
Key differences with Black Holes (BH) metrics
Contrary to Rotating Black Holes (RBHs), which can be fully described using very few param-
eters—namely their mass and angular momentum (for uncharged rotating BH)—Rotating
Neutron Stars (RNSs) have a much more complex structure that influences the spacetime
geometry around them. In particular, their gravitational multipoles differ from those of the
Kerr metric, which are expressed solely in terms of M and a = J/M [2]. Indeed, the RNS
has a “material” surface, and its shape is distorted by its rotation: its multipole structure
differs from that of a RBH and depends on its internal structure, which remains poorly known
today; the EoS is currently uncertain and affects the multipole coefficients [5]. Consequently,
the RNS metric differs from the Kerr metric close to the compact object, but approaches
the Kerr metric asymptotically at sufficiently large r. While the Kerr metric is the unique
metric describing uncharged RBH [2], the situation is much more complicated for RNSs.
First, a reliable NS structure model is required—which remains uncertain—before solving
the EFEs (Equations 2). However, it is almost impossible to solve them in practice because
of their highly non-linear nature. Different approaches have been attempted over the years
to construct analytical stationary and axisymmetric RNS metrics, which are a key element
for developing accurate models of NS properties and associated astrophysical processes [6].

In the remaining part of this section about RNS metrics, the Hartle-Thorne metric is pre-
sented as a slow rotation approximation, followed by the metric that will be used in this
project for the simulations: the Pappas metric. Other models of analytical, stationary, and
axisymmetric vacuum solutions outside RNS have been derived since, without assuming the
slow rotation approximation. However, many of these models have very complex structures
and expressions. The Manko et al. three-parameter solution (2000) [7] is particularly inter-
esting because it can provide an accurate description for rapidly rotating NSs. However, it
does not match well with slowly rotating NSs (compared with numerical models) [8], because
the solution has a non-vanishing quadrupole moment when the angular momentum tends
towards zero, which is not physically consistent with the fact that a non-rotating spherical
object is not deformed by rotation. This metric is a special case of the two-soliton solution
previously constructed by Manko et al. (1995) [9]. It uses four parameters and does not
have this slow rotation issue: it can be continuously reduced to the Kerr and Schwarzschild
metrics. However, it is difficult to express because of its very complex form [8].
In general, two main approaches are used to derive a spacetime metric for RNSs. Either
a slow rotation approximation is assumed, employing models like the Hartle-Thorne metric
or perturbed versions of the Kerr metric that introduce higher-order multipole moments
[10], or the full EFEs are solved numerically for stationary and axisymmetric spacetimes
surrounding a rotating perfect fluid compact object [6].
A review of RNSs in relativity
is provided by Paschalidis & Stergioulas [11].
Additionally, RNSs can be considered as
uniformly rotating objects but also as differentially rotating, which introduces another layer
of complexity beyond the choice of the Equation of State (EoS) modelling the behaviour
of their matter. Numerical solutions can be obtained for all rotation regimes, though they
are particularly challenging for rapidly rotating NSs [12]. A widely used numerical tool is
the RNS code developed by Nikolaos Stergioulas and John L. Friedman [13] for computing
rapidly rotating, axisymmetric equilibrium configurations of relativistic stars in GR. It solves
the coupled Einstein and hydrostatic equilibrium equations for rotating stars with arbitrary
rotation rates and realistic EoS. Unlike perturbative methods such as the Hartle-Thorne
approximation, the RNS code can model stars near the mass-shedding limit, making it
particularly valuable for studying rapidly rotating NSs. The code outputs the full spacetime
metric and physical properties of the star, such as mass, radius, moment of inertia, and
quadrupole moment. It has been extensively validated and serves as a benchmark for testing
approximate models [11].

2.2.4
Multipole moments
Contrary to RBHs, RNSs acquire a deformed shape due to their rotation, introducing de-
viations from a purely spherical form that significantly influence the surrounding spacetime
metric [11]. Consequently, the mass M and angular momentum J alone are insufficient to
determine the corresponding exterior vacuum metric, unlike in the Kerr RBH case. Higher-
order multipole moments are required, contributing through an expansion in powers of 1/r.
In the Pappas-FA metric, the first five multipole moments (M, J, M2, S3, M4) are used to con-
struct an approximate exterior vacuum metric around a RNS (neglecting any electromagnetic
effects). A priori, this yields a five-parameter metric. However, investigations considering
various “realistic” equations of state (EoS) [11] suggest that in practice, the metric may ef-
fectively depend on only three parameters due to empirical relations between the multipole
moments.

Parameter space
To obtain the Pappas metric, only the first three multipole moments are necessary: M, J,
and M2. However, it is also possible to use the introduced dimensionless parameters j and α
instead of J and M2, which are easier to manipulate in simulations—particularly for the mass
quadrupole, as its quadratic dependence on j varies with the assumed EoS for the given RNS
[5]: α ≡α(M, EoS). The mass M plays a specific role as a scaling parameter; this is why it
is often set to 1 (corresponding to 1 km in geometrised units with G = c = 1) in numerical
simulations, with the actual scaling for the real NS mass applied afterwards to interpret the
results. Thus, in practice, the parameter space can be restricted to (j, α).
Spin parameter j
The spin parameter j ranges from 0 (small values corresponding to a slowly rotating NS)
up to the Keplerian limit (j ≈0.7 −0.8). Indeed, a RNS has a maximum rotation rate it
can sustain before matter at its equator becomes unbound due to centrifugal forces. In other

2.2.5
EoS-independent parametrisations
Recent work has revealed “universal” (EoS-independent) relations between various physical
properties of NS, such as the I-Love-Q relations [11], which involve quantities like the mo-
ment of inertia, spin, dimensionless multipole moments, or the compactness ratio C = M/R
(in geometrised units); see also [17]. In 2015, Pappas investigated how astrophysical prop-
erties of RNSs relate to their spacetime geometry [18]. According to Pappas, it is possible
to accurately describe RNSs properties—such as orbital and accretion characteristics—in an
EoS-independent manner using only two dimensionless parameters: the spin parameter j and
the reduced quadrupole (quadrupolar deformability) α, given that the mass M acts solely
as a scaling parameter. One can then produce contour plots over the parameter space (j, α)
for different observables, which can be used to test assumptions about NS properties, as well
as to determine (j, α) for a given NS by analysing the intersection of curves in this param-
eter space from at least two observables, once its mass is known through an independent
measurement. The spin parameter j and the reduced quadrupole α can subsequently be
used to evaluate the angular momentum J and the mass quadrupole M2 of the observed NS,
meaning the first three multipole moments of the NS are thus determined [18]. As Luk &
Lin (2018) explain [19], directly obtaining J and M2 from observations is challenging, and it
is often much easier to measure other physical quantities, such as the rotational frequency
f of the NS. Finally, knowing the first three multipole moments for different RNSs allows,

2.3
Notion of Ergoregion
2.3.1
Ergoregion for rotating Kerr Black Holes
When studying black holes, a new concept arises when moving from the Schwarzschild metric
(non-rotating BH) to the Kerr metric (rotating BH): the ergoregion (or ergosphere). It is a
region surrounding the rotating compact object (outside the event horizon) where spacetime
is dragged along with the object’s rotation due to frame dragging. In this region in particular,
observers are forced to co-rotate with the object. More precisely, no observer on a time-like
worldline can remain “at rest” relative to an asymptotic observer. This notion of being “at
rest” only has meaning in stationary spacetimes [20], in which each spatial hypersurface is
moved invariantly along the time direction by a Killing vector field ∂t. This Killing vector field
is related to the time-time component of the metric gµν in such a way that it is time-like at
infinity, whereas it becomes space-like inside the ergoregion; thus, being “at rest” (following
a worldline tangent to ∂t) is impossible. Consequently, the border of the ergoregion is found
where ∂t · ∂t = gtt = 0: gtt < 0 outside the ergoregion (time-like) and gtt > 0 inside the

The point to be addressed is whether RNSs could sustain an ergoregion like RBHs, and

2.3.2
Energy extraction processes
The ergoregion is associated with two important astrophysical mechanisms, both related to
the extraction of energy from a RBH: the Penrose process and the Blandford-Znajek process.
Indeed, a particle with four-momentum pµ in the ergoregion can have a negative conserved
energy ˜E = −pµ · ∂µ = −pt. In the Penrose process, a particle enters the ergoregion and
then splits into two: one particle crosses the event horizon with negative energy and falls into
the BH, while the other particle leaves the ergoregion asymptotically with more energy than
the original particle. Energy conservation implies that the BH loses some of its rotational
energy [2]. The Penrose process is not powerful or efficient enough to significantly decrease
the rotational energy of the BH at an astrophysical scale.
Figure 3: Schematic representation of the Penrose process for a RBH: a particle enters in the
BH’s ergoregion (grey), splits in two (red point) particles with one falling beyond the event
horizon while the other goes out at infinity.
The Blandford-Znajek process is a mechanism for powering relativistic jets from RBHs that
extracts some of the BH’s rotational energy through its magnetic field [22]. When a RBH is
surrounded by magnetized plasma—typically from an accretion disk—the twisting of mag-
netic field lines by the BH’s spin can generate powerful electromagnetic outflows. This process
channels rotational energy into highly collimated jets of plasma that can travel vast distances,
making it a leading explanation for the energetic jets observed in quasars and AGNs.

2.3.3
Ergoregion for Rotating Neutron Stars (RNSs)
According to Schutz & Comins (1978), nothing indicates that compact objects other than
RBHs cannot sustain an ergoregion according to its definition and properties [20]. Conse-
quently, RNSs could in principle also exhibit an ergoregion. However, a key difference with
RBHs is that RNSs have a physical body that interacts with the spacetime geometry: it
seems that such an ergoregion would rapidly disappear after the NS have spun down, while
their rotational energy is dissipated because of the Friedman instability [23]. This is related
to the fact that, in the ergoregion, a freely moving particle can have negative energy with
respect to an asymptotic observer because a non-axisymmetric perturbation can radiate pos-
itive energy at infinity while increasing the negative energy in the ergoregion due to energy
conservation [24]. This runaway effect makes the system dynamically unstable, as RNSs do
not have an event horizon, contrary to RBHs. Schutz & Comins (1978) conclude, after their
calculations, that no realistic RNS can sustain an ergoregion.
Recently, Tsokaros & al (2019) developed new models of RNSs that exhibit an ergoregion
and remain dynamically stable over multiple timescales (more than a hundred dynamical
timescales) [23]. These “ergostars” models were constructed using a compressible and causal
EoS and correspond to hypermassive stars with differential rotation and a compactness ratio
around C = M/R = 0.3 (in geometrised units). This raises questions about how such equi-
librium behaves along with internal dissipation mechanisms, such as viscosity and magnetic
fields powering jets, and about how ergostars could be formed (for example, as the remnant
of a merger of two NSs).
As this is still theoretical and numerical modelling, it is important to be relatively cautious
with the conclusions from such speculative studies. Anyway, those questions remain largely
beyond the scope of this project, where we focus on the study of the motion of particles around
RNSs. One point that is certain is that, even if some RNSs could have an ergoregion under
specific circumstances—which observed RNSs are far from satisfying [24]—their ergoregion
would be contained within their physical body and not be present outside of them. Thus, the
exterior spacetime of a RNS never presents an ergoregion which could influence the motion
of particles around the RNS. However, this does not mean that the frame-dragging effect is
removed: particle motion is still influenced by the rotation of the compact star, which drags
spacetime around itself.

Overview of the numerical method
In this section, we briefly introduce the solver used to solve the geodesic equations 10 in
order to derive the motion of a particle around a RNS. We begin by presenting the numerical
method implemented in the solver and how the setup is configured. A description of the
execution process is also provided in Appendix B to clarify the various components and files
involved in computing the simulations, along with the necessary inputs and expected outputs.
The principle of the solver was originally investigated by Bacchini et al. in 2018 [4]. In
this paper, they propose a new numerical scheme designed to enhance the performance of
solving the geodesic equations using standard metrics such as Schwarzschild and Kerr, as
well as more advanced and custom metrics. Indeed, conventional explicit schemes such as
Runge-Kutta, and even symplectic implicit schemes, tend to fail in critical regions near the
horizon. These methods often suffer from insufficient accuracy, excessive error accumulation,
and poor efficiency in terms of both computational cost and runtime [4]. To address these
issues, Bacchini et al. introduce a novel energy-conserving implicit integration scheme based
on a Hamiltonian formulation. They compare its performance with traditional schemes and
observe significant improvements in both accuracy and efficiency [4]. The algorithm also
has the advantage of supporting generic spacetime metrics and allows for extensions beyond
purely geodesic motion, such as incorporating conservative external forces like electromag-
netic forces through the Lorentz force.
As the intricate details of this scheme are not essential to this particular project, they will
not be presented in depth. Interested readers are referred directly to the original article
by Bacchini et al. (2018) [4]. However, as is commonly done in Numerical Relativity, the
algorithm relies on a specific formulation of the metric tensor—and consequently of the
geodesic equations—using a decomposition of the four-dimensional spacetime into a “3+1”
formulation: the ADM formalism. This mathematical framework was originally developed
by Arnowitt, Deser, and Misner in 1959 to improve numerical computations, particularly
for solving the EFEs and geodesic equations. This “3+1” form explicitly separates the time
coordinate t from the three spatial coordinates [27]: three-dimensional spatial slices denoted
by Σt (space-like manifolds at constant t) evolve over time as they are embedded within the
four-dimensional spacetime manifold of General Relativity. New variables are introduced to
perform this ADM decomposition, which allows the metric to be rewritten as follows [4]:

Considering Equation 42 and Stable Circular Orbits (SCO), the potential V (r) has to satisfy,
at the radius r = r0, certain conditions to ensure the particle describes a SCO around the
RNS: V (r0) ≡0 ≡V ′(r0) and V ′′(r0) ≥0. However, it may not be possible to maintain
a SCO at any radius r for some RNS: there may exist an Innermost Stable Circular Orbit
(ISCO) which corresponds to V ′′(rISCO) = 0. We say it “may exist” because if rISCO ≤rNS,
then all radii r ≥rNS admit a SCO and it does not really make sense to talk about orbits
for particles that would be inside the compact object. The ISCO in the prograde case is
generally not the same as the ISCO in the retrograde case (Equations 44, 45, 46). As the
general approximate analytical relations presented in Section 2.4.2 are given for prograde
orbits, we will exclusively discuss the prograde ISCO for now in this section.

4.6
Discussion
In this project, we investigated particle motion around RNSs, and in particular equatorial
plane motion, considering a massive particle. Most of the simulations were computed for a
“typical” NS with M = 1.4M⊙, R = 12 km and f = 700 Hz. Before looking at the specific
studied configurations, general observations can be made regarding the validity of the results
under the different assumptions. Assuming that the particle orbits in the equatorial plane
means that θ = π/2 and uθ = 0 should always remain constant throughout the different
simulations. We found that their numerical error (variation around these theoretical values)

We first studied the Stable Circular Orbits (SCOs) for both prograde and retrograde orbits
around this “typical” RNS. The conditions to obtain an SCO with the Pappas-FA met-
ric were derived using general analytical expressions from the theory, valid for a general
spacetime metric with diagonal coefficients and the off-diagonal coefficient gtϕ related to the
frame-dragging effect. This allows us to correctly set the initial conditions, with in particular
LSCO(r0), to obtain the SCO at this radius r0 in either the prograde or the retrograde case
(their respective LSCO(r) functions are not the same, as shown in Figure 6). The results
are extremely accurate compared with the theoretical expectations for both prograde and
retrograde orbits. The frame-dragging induced by the rotation of the NS clearly affects the
particle motion, with a difference in the required LSCO between the prograde and the retro-
grade cases. This was further studied by comparing the behaviour of the particle motion with
the standard Kerr metric for RBHs, which can be a relatively good metric approximation for
RNSs not too close to the compact object. Indeed, both Kerr and Pappas-FA metrics give
similar particle trajectories relatively far away from the RNS, while the differences are more
pronounced closer to it. This is expected from their corresponding analytical expressions, as
the Pappas-FA metric includes higher order multipole moments that are supposed to better
capture the deformation of the NS shape due to its rotation, which influences the spacetime
geometry around it. This highlights the importance of having a “good” model for the RNS
spacetime metric in order to carry out relevant simulations and ultimately improve the mod-
elling of associated astrophysical processes.
To better evaluate the influence of the frame-dragging, we created an artificial “Kerr mod-
ified” metric that considers the Kerr metric for the diagonal components but the gtϕ com-
ponent of the Pappas-FA metric. We observed that the particle bound eccentric orbit with
the “Kerr modified” metric is slightly closer to the SCO with the Pappas-FA metric in the
prograde case, but slightly further away in the retrograde case compared to the particle orbit
with the Kerr metric. Consequently, even if both the Kerr and the Pappas-FA metrics differ
the most in their gtϕ component, it is not the true main factor that explains the differences
between both metrics, and even very small deviations in the other diagonal components also
affect the particle motion. This underlines the need to use accurate RNS metric models, be-
cause even slight deviations in the metric components can have a relatively strong influence
on particle motion. As a first approximation, the Kerr metric is a simple and efficient way
to approximate the metric around a RNS, but only relatively far from the compact object:
coming closer requires the use of more advanced models such as the Hartle-Thorne metric,

improve our understanding of RNS metrics, also taking into account observational data.

5
Conclusion and Outlook
In the current work, we have performed an investigation into the different elements needed
to simulate the motion of particles around RNSs. This project began with a literature re-
view of the theoretical aspects involved in the simulations: RNSs require the use of General
Relativity to accurately describe them and their surrounding spacetime, where a particle
follows a trajectory determined by the geodesic equations. Contrary to BHs, which have
simple and exact analytical metrics to describe the spacetime around them, RNSs have a
much more complex structure that influences the spacetime in their vicinity. In particular,
their shape is deformed by rotation and, consequently, higher-order multipole moments need
to be used to take into account this oblateness and how it modifies the spacetime geometry.
However, their physical description is still incomplete, with large uncertainties remaining —
for example, regarding their EoS, which remains poorly known. The NS structure makes the
EFEs even harder to solve, and no relatively simple and exact metric model exists today to
describe their spacetime geometry. Many different approximations have been developed by
various researchers. One of the oldest and most well-known is the Hartle-Thorne metric [14]
[12], which uses a slow rotation approximation. We selected a promising model developed
by Pappas (2017) [6] that does not rely on this slow rotation approximation, and we used
in particular its expression in spherical-like coordinates given by Frutos-Alfaro (2019) [10]
(named the “Pappas-FA” metric throughout this project) to simplify the comparison with
the usual BH metrics: the Schwarzschild and the Kerr metrics. Indeed, the Kerr metric
is a good approximation of the RNS metric, but only relatively far from the RNS; larger
deviations occur closer to the compact object due to the action of the multipole moments
related to the deformation of the NS shape.
After implementing the Pappas-FA metric into the code, we explored different configurations
and computed the corresponding relevant simulations. We focused on the equatorial plane
motion of a massive particle around a “typical” NS with M = 1.4M⊙, R = 12 km and f = 700
Hz. We began by examining stable circular orbits (SCOs) and studied the influence of frame-
dragging by analysing both prograde (co-rotating) and retrograde (counter-rotating) orbits,
as well as comparing the results with those obtained using the Kerr metric. We observed that
the code is highly accurate with respect to the theoretical predictions for particle motion,
while also being computationally efficient. We also noted the impact of frame-dragging, which
is more pronounced closer to the RNS, along with deviations in the trajectories between using
the Pappas-FA and Kerr metrics under identical initial conditions — these deviations also
become more significant near the RNS. We then turned to general equatorial plane motion
and investigated how the potential V (r) can be used to predict orbital properties such as
the perigee and apogee. The results obtained with the code were in agreement with the
predictions derived from the V (r) curve. Finally, we studied the ISCO radius parameter by
conducting a parametric study over the parameter space (j, α) in the prograde case (with
the mass M acting as a scaling parameter). Comparing the corrected results (Appendix D)
with the results of Pappas (2015) [18], who used a different RNS metric, we observed that

The simulations and studies performed in this project could be extended to further investi-
gations into particle motion around RNSs — for example, by examining perturbed circular
motion in order to expand the possibilities for predicting orbital properties using the po-
tential U(r, θ) (Equation 50), such as the characteristic orbital frequencies. This could be
relevant for studying Quasi-Periodic Oscillations (QPOs), in which some of these frequen-

accretion processes in LMXBs, for example. Similar work is performed by Urbancov´a et al.

spacetime around RNSs. In addition, general particle motion around a RNS could be studied
further to explore related astrophysical processes and to continue testing the code, both with
massive particles and with photons — for example, to study light deflection induced by the
strong gravitational field of the compact object. Such ray-tracing applications are investi-
gated by Oliva & Frutos-Alfaro (2021) [30] for rapidly rotating NSs. All these applications
could also contribute to improving our current understanding of NS structure by offering
insights into the relationships between the various physical parameters. As discussed in this
project, it appears that, for example, the spin octupole S3 and mass hexadecapole M4 of an
NS can be expressed in terms of its mass M, angular momentum J, and mass quadrupole M2
using universal relations (EoS-independent: Equations 28, 29, 30, 31, 32) [6]. Other universal
relations also appear to exist between various combinations of NS parameters, such as the
universal parametrisations proposed by Pappas (2015) [18], presented in Equations 33 and
35. Further related parametrisations are presented and explored by Papigkiotis & Pappas
(2023) [17].
Regarding the code used to solve the geodesic equations and perform the simulations, an
improved version has been developed by Aleksandar Topic using the AMReX environment,
with a future paper in preparation [29]. As the initial Python version used in this project
was shown to be extremely accurate with respect to theoretical predictions, this improved
version should prove even more valuable for studying particle motion around RNSs. It will
allow for the simulation of multiple particles in the same run (useful for accretion disks),
and also the inclusion of additional fields (such as the NS magnetic field) and other particle
properties.
Additional simulation tools associated with the AMReX environment should
make it possible to simulate more complex configurations, such as accretion processes and
relativistic jets, while improving computational efficiency through more advanced processing
strategies such as Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR). Moreover, it should be possible to
use discretised spacetime metrics, avoiding the restriction to analytical metrics with closed-
form expressions. This promising code would allow for further exploration of astrophysical

Appendix B: How to run the code
Below, the general structure of the code is presented with the key files and folders to consider,
along with the required inputs and the generated outputs of the simulations with a short
explanation of how to start a simulation.
8.1
Code Structure
The code is contained in a folder gathering the different elements called RNS geodesic motion.
In this folder, the main elements to consider to make simulations are:
• model data: this is the folder that regroup the different simulation files (.txt) storing
the results.
• lib: this folder contains a plotting script. The original one from Topic is plotting.py,
and a second one inspired from this to plot results of multiple simulations to compare
them and adapted for RNS is plotting RNS MultiSim.py
• init.sh: this script is used to set the virtual environment and load the libraries from
requirements.txt
• simulation analytical.py and simulation fdm.py: original simulation scripts from
Topic.
• Simu RNS Names.py: simulation script with the Pappas-FA metric implemented as the
RNS metric.
• Launcher RNS.py: script used to setup a simulation using Simu RNS Names.py.
It
presents what parameters need to be provided and what do they correspond to.
• MultiLauncher RNS.sh: script used to start multiple simulations using
Simu RNS Names.py at the same time, using screen commands. It is a simple way to do
this, and the user should be careful if the working environment is shared among other
users like with an HPC cluster and use appropriate job submissions.
For this project, the simulations were realised from a Linux terminal using DTU HPC cluster.
8.2
Inputs

provided to the script: the metric that will be used (for now, only the Pappas-FA metric and
the standard BH Schwarzschild and Kerr metrics are implemented), its parameters, the type
of particle considered (photon or massive particle) and its initial conditions. For example:

8.3
Outputs
Once a simulation has started, a text file is created and associated with this simulation to
store the different variables describing the particle motion such as its position, velocity along
with the corresponding time index. The first line stores the metric used and its parameters
along with the type of particle.
For now, the name of this output file is automatically
generated by the script using the parameters of the metric and the initial conditions, to help
keep track of them. This behaviour can be modified in the future if needed. The output text
file is generated in the folder model data.
8.4
Starting a simulation
As the DTU HPC cluster was used in this project, some intermediate steps must be performed
before properly starting to simulate particle motion. First, a terminal session must be opened,
and the user must navigate to the folder containing the code (here RNS geodesic motion).
Then, the linuxsh command is used to log into a CPU node and execute the script init.sh,

Plotting
To analyse the results from one or multiple simulations, the user can create their own analysis

This notebook (located in the folder tools of the code folder RNS geodesic motion) contains
different complementary tools used for this project:
• A first section computes the Universal parametrisations from Pappas (2015) using
