Abstract
Out-of-plane wrinkles are the most common and severe defects that greatly aﬀect the structural
integrity of wind turbine blades. They are a leading cause of blade failures which are dangerous
and costly and must then be prevented. Accurate modeling of the compression strength of wrinkled
composite laminate is needed by manufacturer. In this way this thesis is the continuity of a previous
study made for through-thickness wrinkles and now deals with partial wrinkles.
Four diﬀerent models of partial wrinkles are created, depending on the diﬀerent combinations pos-
sible between a wrinkled laminate and undisturbed laminates. A 2D FE model of alternative layers
of elastic orthotropic ﬁber laminae and isotropic elastic-plastic matrix is used. A series of geometric
parameter studies are conducted to determine the kinking strength given by the limit load. A frac-
ture mechanics based approach is used to determine if the global wrinkle model would have failed
by delamination before the kinking instability.
The main conclusion is that the addition of undisturbed laminate next to the wrinkle along the length
reduces the kinking strength, which afterwards increases when undisturbed laminate are on top of
that added below or/and above the ﬁber waviness. The delamination study demonstrates that the
global wrinkle model actually fails by delamination for an initial misorientation equal to or larger
than 4°.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Wind turbine blades and wrinkles
In a wind turbine, the blades are responsible for extracting the energy from the wind. They are
critical components regarding their design because they play the key role in the process of collecting
energy. But they are also critical regarding the design of the rest of the turbine, because their size
and weight will inﬂuence the other components. Nowadays, blades are made longer and longer in
order to retrieve even more energy. But that creates a new challenge for their structures that have to
withstand greater load but without too much increase in weight. They have to be designed so that
there is a good balance between their structural integrity and their cost. But as Mandell et al. (2003,
p.1) states, "since blades must be produced at a ﬁnished cost in the range of $10/kg, only relatively
inexpensive resins, reinforcements and processes are competitive." Thus, defects are inevitable when
producing blades in lengths up to 88.4 meters, as a consequence of the scale and the manufacturing
process. And those ﬂaws can soon become the origin of a catastrophic failure. Indeed, according
to Griﬃn and Malkin (2011, p.2), the leading cause of failure in terms of "frequency and severity"
are manufacturing defects, such as wrinkles in laminates, missing or incomplete bond lines, and dry
ﬁbers.
Therefore, blades do not always live up to their designed lifetime. Failures do occur, which has both
ﬁnancial and safety-related consequences. But replacing or repairing blades is extremely expensive
and that is why it is important for wind turbine manufacturer to prevent their failures. Even though
failure investigations are often complicated, many researches have been performed to better un-
derstand what is needed to improve blade reliability. Indeed, "the low cost materials and processes
combined with strength-driven design produce a signiﬁcant concern with the eﬀects of ﬂaws on the
various strength-based properties" (Mandell et al., 2003, p.1).
According to Griﬃn and Malkin (2011, p.3), "wrinkles and/or waviness of ﬁbers in laminate are one
of the predominant sources of problems for wind turbine blade structural integrity". They are deﬁned
by "the presence of a group of ﬁbers whose orientation is inclined with respect to the nominal ﬁber
direction of the laminate" (Kiran Manikarnika, 2016 p.2). Wrinkles can be divided in two groups:
out-of-plane waviness, where the waviness is present between the layers, and the ﬁber misalign-
ment is deﬁned with respect to the plane of the laminate ; or in-plane waviness, where the waviness
is deﬁned between the ﬁbers inside one ﬁber lamina, and the ﬁber misalignment is deﬁned with
respect to a straight ﬁber’s direction.
==PAGE:22==
2
Introduction
Wind turbine rotor blades are loaded both in tension and compression via aerodynamic forces. How-
ever, as Mandell et al. state, "the reinforcements usually contain signiﬁcant ﬁber waviness and mis-
orientation. [...] Severe ﬁber misorientation reduces properties like tensile strength, but even mild
waviness can cause signiﬁcant reductions in compressive strength" (2003, p.1). Indeed, uniaxial
composites loaded in compression are sensitive to ﬁbre misalignment where the ﬁbre misalignment
governs the compression strength. Even a slight oﬀ-axis ﬁbre misalignment gives a notable reduc-
tion in the compression strength and the laminate stiﬀness. Those two values are yet important
parameters that will deﬁne blades’ design and structure. A mistake in their value due to the pres-
ence of the wrinkles can lead to premature failure compared to straight undisturbed laminates. As
Nelson et al. (2011, p.2) explains, further research of the criticality of these ﬂaws is still required.
That’s why this thesis shows a particular interest in ﬁber waviness and the inﬂuence of wrinkled
laminate on the compression strength.
1.2
From global to partial wrinkles
Wrinkles can occur in diﬀerent ratios of the laminate’s thickness. Those which occur in only a
fraction of the thickness of a laminate are known as partial wrinkles. At the contrary, those which
occupy the full thickness of the laminate are known as global wrinkles, or through-thickness wrin-
kles.
Kiran Manikarnika (2016) previously investigated the inﬂuence of out-of-plane global wrinkles on
their compression strength, since this case of through-thickness waviness is the most severe ﬂaw that
can occur in an unidirectional ﬁber laminate, like those found in wind turbine. The ﬁnite element
model was done in two-dimensions and the studied laminate is made of an alternation of elastic-
plastic matrix layers and elastic orthotropic ﬁber laminae. Several geometric and material parameter
studies have been performed in order to determine the compression strength of diﬀerent kinds of
wrinkled composite laminates loaded in compression. Two failure modes have been studied in order
to determine the criticality of global wrinkles: plastic microbuckling (also known as kinking) and
delamination.
This thesis is now the continuity of Kiran’s work, and explores the criticality of out-of-plane partial
wrinkles. Instead of having a through-thickness ﬁber misalignment, this work goes a bit further in
the modelling of wrinkle defects in composite laminates. Indeed, even though global wrinkles are
the most severe case of defect, they are not realistic. When ﬁber misalignment occurs in blades, it
only aﬀects a fraction of the thickness and the length in the unidirectional laminate. The wrinkle is
then only a local defect surrounded by straight undisturbed layers, as it is shown in Figure 1.1.
Depending on where the defect is within the laminate, many diﬀerent kinds of partial wrinkles can
be deﬁned. For instance, the ﬁber misalignment can aﬀect layers until the surface and then be visible
from outside, or the wrinkle can die out by the surface and just be within the laminate’s thickness
without being visible from outside. In the present work, the inﬂuence of several partially wrinkled
laminate on the compression strength is then investigated.

2.1.2
Strength prediction with an instability approach
The instability approach is used for strength determination. It is based on the fact that stability is lost
when the load reaches a critical point, deﬁned as being the point at which the stiﬀness is no longer
positive deﬁnite. If an instability occurs, the assumption is that the failure point is given by this
critical point. There are two kinds of instabilities: the global instability and the localized instability,
as previously mentioned in the failure causes of microbuckling (Table 2.1. The global instability
leads to a global deformation, whereas the localized instability leads to localized deformation. After
a localization, the strain ﬁeld is no longer uniform.
A common global instability in compression is the buckling of a Euler-Bernoulli column. Usually
structures are design so that the buckling load is not reached, for them not to buckle. A common
localized instability in compression is the existence of a limit load, lower than the buckling load, and
beyond which the structure can not support any additional load. The post-instability behavior can
be a snap-back behavior, but failure can occur. The compression strength is then considered to be
the critical point. Those two types of instability are found in the models discussed just below.
Rosen (1965) developed a model that showed a global instability through global buckling. For that,
his model was a perfectly aligned structure, with a completely elastic material. His important con-
clusion is that the matrix shear behavior has a great importance in the failure of the composite. In-
deed, he obtained an analytical expression of the compressive strength as equal to the elastic shear
modulus of the full laminate:

2.1.3
Strength prediction with a failure criteria approach
The failure criteria approach is another way to predict the failure strength. This consists in compar-
ing the model’s strength with material strength properties to state whether the specimen would have
failed or not. In this way this approach requires a good knowledge of material properties, which is
not always easy to get accurately. When using a failure criteria approach, the failure mechanism
takes the form of delamination between layers (as it can be seen in Figure 2.3). The delamination is
then followed by global buckling of the separated layers, which implies ﬁber buckling and fracture.
Several models have exhibited this delamination failure mechanism.
Indeed, two purely linear analysis have been conducted. The ﬁrst one is from Hsiao and Daniel (1996)
and represents a sinusoidal imperfection, while using the Tsai-Wu failure criterion and a progressive
damage model. The main conclusion is that there is a gradual failure between the ﬁrst ply and the
last ply. Besides, the distribution of the wrinkle within the laminate and the defect size appeared to
be important. In this way, Garnich and Karami (2005) focused on the eﬀect of localization on strength
reduction. Their conclusion is that the addition of undisturbed laminate around the structure would
greatly aﬀect the strength reduction, which is relevant for the study of partial wrinkle.
However those models do not take the material and geometric non-linearity into account whereas it
was shown to be important in other work. That is why non-linear models are also studied. For exam-
ple, Takahashi and Chou (1987) used a maximum stress failure criterion and their work emphasizes
the importance of the in-plane shear behavior of the laminate and the non linearity of this response.
Then, Leong et al. (2012) used a NU failure criterion to predict the interlaminar failure that actually
represents the initiation of a layer-wise delamination. More recently, Mukhopadhyay et al. (2015)
used cohesive zone elements and a progressive damage model. The study showed that the failure
mechanism changes for a certain value of ﬁber misalignment: failure mode is kinking for low mis-
alignment angles and smoothly transitions to delamination for greater misalignment angles. This
conclusion is a very important result and is actually similar to what Kyriakides et al. (1995) found out
when studying the post-instability behavior that becomes softer with greater misalignment angles.

On the other hand, fracture mechanics can also be a way to model the delamination failure. Indeed,
as Krueger (2006) states, "for laminated composite materials, interlaminar fracture mechanics has
proven useful for characterizing the onset and growth of delaminations". A method can then be to
simulate a small crack that will be the onset of the delamination and compute the energy release
rate by means of the J-integral. The J-integral is indeed a technique to compute the energy release
rate G. This value is locally computed at the crack front. A crack growth criterion such as J > GIIc
can be used to determine if the crack will grow at a certain load, which would lead to delamination
of the layers and so failure.
It should be noted that the mode II is considered for the compression of wrinkled composites, as
the component of the strain energy release rate which is of interest is the one due to interlaminar
sliding shear. Actually, Hutchinson and Suo (1991) studied mixed mode cracking in layered materials.
When studying a subsurface crack laying in an isotropic and homogeneous material, they deﬁned
the critical stress by being the Euler buckling load. One conclusion was that the crack tip suﬀers
mixed-mode loading (mode I and II) at the buckling load. However the mode II component increases
under remote load until the delamination cracks suﬀers pure mode II loading.
All these analysis are relevant for modeling wind turbine composite laminates with partial wrinkle
because they highlight the importance of delamination as a failure mode, as well as the impact of
the localization of the defect. However, one diﬃculty with modeling delamination is the required
material information that is sometimes diﬃcult to get and that can be an obstacle to such analysis
using a failure criteria approach. It should also be noted that modeling the propagation of delamina-
tion is not always relevant because several experimental studies showed that failure occurs quickly
and without any warning.
2.1.4
Conclusion on the key failure mechanisms
Now that diﬀerent models have been quickly described, some important conclusions can be made
regarding the strength of wrinkled composites under compression. Two key failure mechanisms
have been highlighted (kinking and delamination) and several models have shown the importance
of the matrix plasticity, the initial defect size, and the shear behavior.
Kinking
Plastic microbuckling, also known as kinking, leads to a kink-band formation. For this failure mode,
the compressive strength is driven by the ﬁber misalignment and the plastic shear deformation in
the matrix. Indeed, the high shear stress due to the out-of-plane wrinkle leads to plastic deformation
of the matrix which causes the localized instability to occur.
Delamination
For bigger initial ﬁber misalignment angles, layer-wise delamination occurs progressively and can
lead to Euler buckling of the separated lamina layers. Indeed, interlaminar shear leads to a crack
growth within the resin layer between individual lamina, until the individual laminae are fully sep-
arated from one another. Once they are separated they can undergo elastic buckling, which leads to
the ultimate failure.

2.2
Previous study of global wrinkles
From the previous conclusions, a composite material with a global wrinkle was modeled by Kiran and

Then, a description of the parametric model made on Abaqus is shown, as well as the parametric

2.2.1
Chosen approaches
Two failure mechanisms are of interest: kinking and delamination. As mentioned previously when
discussing the diﬀerent compressive failure modes (section 2.1.1), only one failure mode will be at
the origin of the failure. In order to determine which one will prevail in the considered situation,
each of the failure mechanisms needs to be modeled to get the strength of the composite for this
particular failure mode. Afterwards the diﬀerent strengths are compared and the smallest is then the
failure strength. Indeed, the failure will occur when the load reaches one of the diﬀerent compressive
strengths (from the diﬀerent failure modes), and the smallest one will be reached the ﬁrst one.
In this way, for a given model, a kinking strength and a delamination strength were determined.
The failure strength was the smallest of those two, and the failure mode was then determined.
To model kinking, a non-linear analysis is required, as well as the inclusion of the matrix plasticity.
The kinking strength was given by the limit load, which corresponds to the critical point where the
stability is lost and where a localization appears.
To model delamination, a stress-based failure criteria was used. The maximum interlaminar shear
stress, that was pointed out as important by many authors, was then compared to a threshold value
of 45 MPa. The delamination strength was given by the load at which the maximum shear stress
reached this value.
Numerous parametric studies were conducted, in order to study the impact of diﬀerent parame-
ters on the compressive strength. For each parametric model, both kinking and delamination were
modeled and the failure mechanism was then given by comparison of the strengths.
2.2.2
Parametric model for a global wrinkle
The global wrinkle was modeled on Abaqus CAE with 2D continuum elements. Python scripting
was used to make the parametric studies easily feasible, and oﬀered the possibility to improve the
model and make it move on to partial wrinkles, by modifying and adding routines. So this model
will be a real basis for the partial wrinkle considered in this thesis. That is why a particular interest
is given to this description. Most of the assumptions will be indeed kept for the partial wrinkle,
especially when talking about the layup and the material properties.

Wrinkle geometry
The global wrinkle was modeled as a sinusoidal wave. Thanks to its symmetry, only its left-half was
modeled in order to reduce computational cost. The diﬀerent partitions gave the layered structure
which is made of alternative layers of unidirectional (UD) ﬁber laminae and resin matrix.
Indeed, when making a laminate by vacuum infusion, ﬁber bundles are ﬁrstly placed next to each
other and on top of each other to preform the diﬀerent layers. Then the resin is pulled by vacuuming
and ﬁlls the gaps between the lamina layers. Those gaps ﬁlled with resin will become the matrix
layers. But the resin also ﬁlls the gaps between the various ﬁber bundles and between the individual
ﬁbers within the ﬁber bundles. Therefore, the lamina layers are actually made of the ﬁber bundles
and the resin that impregnated the gaps. Figure 2.4 shows a micro-graph of a typical cross-section
of UD ﬁber laminate, where the ﬁber bundles and the resin are visible.
The ﬁber lamina volume fraction is the fraction of ﬁber lamina in the whole specimen. This value
was given to be 0.78, whereas the ﬁber volume fraction (in one ﬁber lamina) was set to be 0.60.

Material properties
About the material properties, the model had to include the eﬀects of matrix plasticity (as discussed
previously). In this way, the matrix material which is a polymer resin, was considered to be elastic-
plastic, with J2 ﬂow theory. To get the plasticity data, isotropic hardening was used, with a Ramberg-
Osgood power law. Also the matrix material was considered to be homogeneous and isotropic.
Unlike the matrix material, the laminae were modeled as orthotropic linear elastic. The linear elas-
ticity implies that the resin present between the ﬁbers and the ﬁber bundles is neglected. This
assumption is accurate since the study concerns macroscopic wrinkles. The goal is to model the
kinking of the lamina and not the microscopic kinking of the ﬁbers in the lamina. However, when
using carbon ﬁbers, that implies that the non-linearity of the ﬁbers is neglected. Also, the ﬁber lam-
inae were modeled as orthotropic, or more exactly, as transversely isotropic, meaning that the plane
normal to the ﬁber direction can be considered as isotropic. The ﬁber laminae were then modeled
as a single material, with an average set of properties between the resin and the ﬁbers.
Thus, as the plasticity was only modeled with the resin in the matrix layers, the plasticity behavior
can only occur in the thin resin layers between the ﬁber laminae.
2.2.3
Several parametric studies and conclusions
Many parametric studies have been conducted to study the impact of diﬀerent parameters on the
compression strength. At ﬁrst the ﬁber misalignment angle was varied. The same conclusion as Kyr-
iakides et al. (1995) was found about the post-insatbility behavior that becomes softer with greater
misalignment angle. Also the same conclusion as Mukhopadhyay et al. (2015) was found about the
failure mode transition. For little misalignment angle, the failure mode is kinking, but for greater
misalignment angle the failure mode smoothly transitions to delamination, when considering a ﬁxed
geometry and material system. Moreover, both the kinking strength and delamination strengths de-
crease with a bigger misalignment angle. This is because a bigger misalignment angle implies bigger
shear stress. This bigger shear stress leads to the matrix yielding at a lower load, causing the mi-
crobuckling. But also this bigger shear stress reached the threshold value at a lower load, indicating

delamination. So the strength reduction is bigger for a bigger misalignment angle.
Then, the inﬂuence of the slenderness ratio (height to length) was studied. The conclusion was that
the compressive strength is reduced as h/L decreases. Other geometric parameters have been studied,
such as the number of layers. The conclusion for this study was that 13 layers were considered
suﬃcient so that the model is not very sensitive to this number of layers. This number will be kept
for the study of the partial wrinkle, assuming that it is also suﬃcient when adding straight and
undisturbed layers around the wrinkle.
A sensitivity analysis have also been conducted and showed the importance of the volume fraction
of ﬁber laminae and of the lamina shear modulus. Moreover, the matrix plasticity has been varied
while changing the hardening power law parameters. One of the conclusion was that resins with
higher yield strengths are better when considering wrinkles because a higher yield strength delays
the onset of plastic deformation, which consequently delays microbuckling, and because the shear
stress is also aﬀected and reaches the threshold value at higher loads, which delays delamination.
Thus, several parametric studies were done and conﬁrmed the important eﬀect of the ﬁber misalign-
ment angle and the matrix plasticity, as well as the wrinkle’s shear response.
2.3
Approach for the partial wrinkles
Through-thickness wrinkles were a good starting point for modeling such defects in wind turbine
laminate. Now the next step is to model partial wrinkles, still by considering unidirectional laminae
since the main load carrying structure of wind turbine blades is made of unidirectional laminate.
The new ﬁnite element models of partial wrinkles will also be made of 2D continuum elements, as
3D eﬀects are still beyond the scope of this project.
The layered structure made of alternative layers of linear elastic orthotropic ﬁber laminae and
isotropic elastic-plastic polymer matrix will be retained. The geometry of the wrinkle will still have
an idealized sinusoidal shape. However, some straight and undisturbed layers will be added on each
side of the wrinkle, as well as above and below the wrinkle.
Figure 2.6 shows the geometry of a localized waviness, the black lines being the ﬁber direction of
diﬀerent lamina layers. It is then clear that the ﬁber misalignment only aﬀect a localized part of the
specimen, since some straight ﬁbers keep their orientation without being aﬀected by the wrinkle.
In this way, diﬀerent partial wrinkle models will be created, each model being diﬀerent by the po-
sition of the undisturbed ﬁber laminae with respect to the wrinkle (next to, above or below the
wrinkle). Also, the waviness will be modeled to be able to decrease regarding its amplitude, so that
the wrinkle can die out at the surface of the specimen.
Unlike the study made for the global wrinkle, the inﬂuence of the ﬁber misalignment angle will not
be studied. This thesis will be dedicated to the impact of the addition of undisturbed lamina layers
on the kinking strength. Indeed, a particular analysis will be conducted considering only the plastic
microbuckling as the failure mode, for only two diﬀerent angles but various partial wrinkle models.

This J value is taken at the time corresponding to the limit load for the kinking instability. In this
way, the J value can be compared to a maximum J value in order to ﬁnd out whether the specimen
would have already failed because of delamination, or if the maximum J value is still not reached
and then the specimen fails because of kinking. Indeed, when the computed J contour plot reaches
the threshold value, that means that the crack will propagate and lead to delamination failure.

Various Models of the Wrinkle
In this chapter, the diﬀerent models that are created to describe a partial wrinkle are presented.
Indeed, as explained previously, a partial wrinkle can diﬀer from another one with respect to the
position of the straight and undisturbed layers around the imperfection.
A partial wrinkle is a local defect that only aﬀects a fraction of the thickness and the length in the
unidirectional laminate. This ﬁber misalignment can reduce through the thickness to eventually die
out. Three components of a partial wrinkle can then be introduced:
• the ﬁber misalignment itself, modeled as a sinusoidal imperfection
• the attenuation of the ﬁber misalignment through the thickness
• the undisturbed and straight layers that can surround the wrinkle
When combining those three components in diﬀerent ways, various models can be created. At ﬁrst,
the three components will be presented, and then the resulting geometries of interest for this analysis
will be described.

Chapter 4
Material Properties
The partial wrinkle is modeled as a layered structure of alternative layers of matrix and ﬁber lamina.
The model is then composed of two material phases. In this chapter, the diﬀerent material proper-
ties of each phase will be presented. The Finite Element Model described in chapter 5 needs some
material inputs and some material assumptions to best represent the material model behavior.
The ﬁrst section will present the data and properties for the matrix, whereas the second section
will present the data and properties for the ﬁber laminae. Then, the last section will discuss the
evolution of those properties with the amplitude attenuation of the wrinkle that increases the ﬁber
volume fraction.
4.1
Isotropic elastic-plastic polymer matrix
The matrix is considered to be a homogeneous and isotropic material, which means that its proper-
ties are the same in all directions. Four diﬀerent polymer resins are studied: three polyester resins
with diﬀerent strain rates and one epoxy resin.
As explained previously, the kinking instability which is modeled in this thesis can only occur when
the material can deform plastically. Indeed, the matrix plastic behavior is indispensable to let the
ﬁbers rotate. As the load is mainly carried by the ﬁbers, when they begin to rotate, they can no longer
carry as much load and the composite’s strength decreases. So the plasticity plays an important role
in the kinking failure because the matrix’ yielding is the origin of kink-band formation. In order to
study this kinking instability, the matrix is then modeled to be elastic-plastic.
4.1.1
Tensile tests to get the material inputs for shear loading
The model inputs for an elastic-plastic isotropic material are the Young’s modulus Em, the Poisson’s
ratio νm, and the plasticity data. The theory of plasticity used for this matrix model is the J2 ﬂow
theory, which assumes that the material can be characterized by isotropic hardening. This assump-
tion of isotropic hardening suits the model especially because there is no interest in cyclic loading
and unloading. Moreover, several studies like Budiansky and Fleck (1993), Kyriakides et al. (1995),
or Pane and Jensen (2004) have shown that the J2 ﬂow theory assumption gives good results for
predicting kinking strength. The used plastic ﬂow theory also assumes that the yield surfaces are

4.1.2
Analysis of the experimental data to obtain the matrix properties
Experimental data from tensile tests are available for the three polyester resins and the epoxy resin.
Only the data until the maximum load are of interest because of the localization (like necking or
kinking) that appears when reaching this point. Because of the localization the strain ﬁeld is no
longer uniform. The data after the maximum load are then not taking into account in the next
discussions.
The Young’s modulus Em, the Poisson’s ratio νm, and the plasticity data as a table of true yield
stresses vs. logarithmic plastic strains need to be retrieved for each polymer. Following, the method
is described for one polymer, and can then be applied to each of the considered materials. All the
data were analysed in MATLAB.
Young’s Modulus
From the data, the nominal stress vs. engineering strain curve can be plotted. The Young’s modulus
can usually be obtained by the slope of the linear elastic behavior of the material. However, for
composites and polymers the curve is not really linear, so the Young’s modulus has to be calculated
in a well-deﬁned strange range given by ISO standards: ε ∈[0.05, 0.25]%. In this way, the polymer
resin behavior is supposed to be linear up to 2.5%.

4.2
Orthotropic linear elastic ﬁber lamina
Only one ﬁber material is studied in this thesis: the E-glass ﬁber material which is the glass ﬁber the
most common type of glass ﬁber used in ﬁberglass. Indeed, the lamina is then a GFRP (Glass Fiber
Reinforced Polymer), where the glass ﬁbers are the reinforcement ﬁbers in the polymer matrix.
4.2.1
Model used for the ﬁber lamina
Unlike the matrix, the ﬁber lamina is considered to be linear elastic, even though there is some resin
that should be plastic between the glass ﬁbers. The consequence is that the matrix yielding can only
occur in the thin resin layers between the ﬁber laminae to allow the rotation of the ﬁbers for kinking.
Moreover, it is considered to be homogeneous, whereas the lamina is actually composed of ﬁber
bundles embedded in some resin. But as this study is about macroscopic wrinkles, modeling the
ﬁber lamina as a single material is actually a good assumption since it is not of particular interest
how the individual ﬁbers behave. Its properties are then an average set of properties between the
resin and the ﬁbers.
The ﬁber lamina is then considered to be a transversely isotropic material, with the isotropic plane
being the one normal to the ﬁber direction (which is the direction 1 in the local coordinate system).
So the properties are the same in the transverse isotropic plane (which is the plane 2-3). This means
that only 5 engineering constants are needed to fully characterize the material (instead of 9 for a
non-particular orthotropic material): E1,lam, E2,lam, ν12,lam, ν23,lam and G12,lam.
4.2.2

4.4
Evolution of the lamina properties for attenuated wrinkles
A particular attention must be paid to wrinkles with amplitude attenuation, which means when the
minimum attenuation factor Bmin is not equal to 1. In such cases, the ﬁber volume fraction in the
ﬁber lamina will change, and as discussed in section 4.2.2 this will then change the lamina properties
that are a function of Vf.
Indeed, as the layers are compressed together because of the wrinkle amplitude attenuation, the
thickness of the diﬀerent layers (resin layers and laminae) will decrease from the beginning of the
ﬁber misalignment until the middle of the wrinkle. And then the layers’ thickness will symmetrically
increase from the middle of the wrinkle to the end of the wrinkle where the ﬁber are straight again.
Figure 4.5 shows the left-half of a global wrinkle with total attenuation at the surface (Bmin = 0).
The reduction of the layers’ thickness is clearly visible and leads to a increase of the ﬁber volume
fraction in the lamina. Because the layers are compressed together, the layers’ thickness is smaller
and the gaps between the individual ﬁbers and the ﬁber bundles are also smaller. But as those gaps
are actually ﬁlled with resin during the infusion, the volume of resin in the composite and especially
in the laminae is smaller, which is why the ﬁber volume fraction is bigger.

Finite Element Model and Analysis
In this chapter, the Finite Element Model will be described. The creation of the geometry, the materi-

is built on the previous model made by Kiran Manikarnika (2016) for a global wrinkle (described in
section 2.2.2). Then, the analysis and the post-processing will be described. The insertion of a crack
will in a global wrinkle will also be explained.
The models are created using Abaqus CAE by running a python script programming the diﬀerent
steps of the FE analysis. This is done to allow parametric studies, and so that the model is easily
modiﬁable with a view to being improved. Appendix A contains the full code for one parametric
study, in order to show how the models are built.
5.1

The partial wrinkle is a single part in Abaqus, modeled as a two-dimensional continuum, and the
diﬀerent layers are made thanks to the diﬀerent partitions. The layup is [M,GFRP,M] for each layer

between thin layers of pure resin. So one phase of resin will actually be composed of two plies of

The partial wrinkle is modeled to be clamped at both ends, and with a compressive load applied at

At ﬁrst, the symmetry is modeled by applying a boundary condition that constrains the right edge
(that corresponds to x = Lw/2) in the horizontal (⃗x) direction. This is shown in blue in Figure

plane must be constrained. The right edge is then free to translate in the vertical (⃗y) direction. The
symmetry condition implies that no shear load can be supported at x = Lw/2, which is expected as
the misalignment angle is back to zero in the middle of the wrinkle.

diﬀerent stresses for each constituent). The value of this displacement δ is set to 5% of the length of
the wrinkle in most cases (because it can actually set to be higher in order to study higher strains).

5.5
Mesh
Once the part and the partitions have been created, the entire part is meshed as structured. The
elements are chosen to be continuum plane-strain quadratic elements with 8 nodes (CPE8 elements),
so that there is no shear locking introduced by bending. Indeed, the element edges can assume a
curved shape, so if bending occurs, the angles between the deformed isoparametric lines remains
equal to 90°. Also, full integration is chosen to calculate stresses and strains which are then calculated
at the integration points (whereas the forces and displacements are calculated on the nodes).
When seeding the part, the size of an element is speciﬁed to be 0.4 mm. As the layers are not the
same height, this will give two elements in the height direction for the lamina layers (for a wrinkle
height hw of 12.5 mm), whereas only one element will be in the height of the thin resin layers (so
one resin phase will have two elements along its height).

At the limit load, the composite cannot support any further loading and past this point, if the load
continues to increase, it is expected that the composite would undergo signiﬁcant deformation in a
very short amount of time and that failure can occur. Instead of modeling this behavior dynamically,
the limit load is considered to give the compressive strength (when kinking is the failure mode) and
the post-instability behavior is modeled with the Riks method in order to study the post-instability
behavior and the form of the localization (which is actually a kink-band).
Thus, the limit load corresponds to the initiation of the instability where the structure becomes
unstable for the ﬁrst time. The localization of deformation starts immediately after the limit load:
the ﬁbers signiﬁcantly rotate in the middle area of the half-wrinkle (at Lw/4) and the localization of
deformation appears with a localized kink-band. This is a distinct band of higher deformation, whose
width and inclination will afterwards increase as the process of localization progresses (with the
post-limit load response, between points (2) and (4)). The instability actually spreads and propagates
into the intact domain of the structure, with the kinked region growing and becoming a larger part
of the specimen. Figure 6.2 shows the localized deformation at the last increment. The kink-band

6.2
Comparison between a global and a partial wrinkle
Now that the strength and the stiﬀness reductions have been illustrated with the model 4, some ﬁrst
comparisons are done in this section, between the model 4 and the global wrinkle. The model 4 is
actually the model that is the closest to reality because of the fact that the defect is surrounded by
straight and undisturbed layers, whereas the global wrinkle is just the defect in itself and does not
take the straight parts of the laminate into account. Those two models are the opposite regarding
their level of partial wrinkle, and comparisons are made in order to see the inﬂuence of some param-
eters that will not be varied afterwards in the parametric studies (chapter 7). At ﬁrst, the inﬂuence of
the maximum misalignment angle θmax will be studied and then the inﬂuence of the resin properties
will be discussed.
6.2.1
Varying the misalignment angle
Figure 6.6 shows the end-shortening response of a global wrinkle and a model 4 for varying mis-
alignment angles. As expected, the limit load decreases for increasing misalignment angles, and for
both the global wrinkle and the model 4. However, the behavior is not the same for those two mod-
els. Indeed, for the model 4, the snap-back behavior is seen for all angles (from 0.5° to 7.5°) whereas
for the global wrinkle, this snap-back behavior is only seen for the small angles (from 0.5° to 2°).
For the model 4, the response is linear almost until the limit load, because the non-linearity is mainly
due to material non-linearity (plasticity) since the wrinkle is only a local defect. After the limit load,
the post-instability behavior is really similar and the equilibrium paths are almost on top of each
other. The sharp cusp of the limit load is clearly visible and there is a big snap-back, for all the
diﬀerent angles. Indeed, the kink-band is only a small fraction of the overall length of the structure
for any angles in the model 4 (thanks to the surrounding undisturbed layers that make the specimen

long enough to have a high recovered deformation). Therefore, the elastic unloading of the non-
rotated ﬁbers will elongate the structure enough to have this snap-back.
On the contrary, the end-shortening response for the global wrinkle is quite diﬀerent. For small mis-
alignment angles, the pre-limit load behavior is nearly linear but there is much more non-linearity
for big misalignment angles and this is mostly due to geometric non-linearity. Then the limit load
is not always a sharp cusp and the snap-back is not visible for all the angles of the global wrinkle.
Indeed, the kink-band will aﬀect a larger fraction of the overall length of the structure for bigger
misalignment angles and there is not straight layers before the wrinkle. So the elastic unloading of

In the previous chapter, diﬀerent parametric studies have been conducted in order to discuss each

Actually as many studies already predicted, interlaminar delamination is an important failure mech-
anism for macroscopic wrinkles, and especially for large ﬁber misalignment angles. The failure
mechanism is then expected to smoothly transition from kinking failure to delamination failure,
as the initial misorientation increases. Kiran (2016) studied this transition between the two failure
mechanisms by using a maximum shear stress criterion. In this chapter, the improvement is then
that a fracture mechanics based approach is implemented. The delamination study is only conducted
on the global wrinkle model, and the transition between kinking failure and delamination failure for
the diﬀerent models is not discussed since the goal of this chapter is mostly to implement the new
approach. In the future, the pre-existing crack can easily be added to the other models and more
parametric studies can be conducted in order to discuss the transition between the failure modes.
At ﬁrst, the approach of the study will then be explained. Then the evolution of the energy release
rate during the compression will be studied. Finally two parametric studies will be conducted in
order to see the inﬂuence of the maximum misalignment angle as well as the inﬂuence of the initial
crack length on the energy release rate.

9.1
A fracture mechanics based approach
In the delamination failure mode, interlaminar cracks begin to grow where the shear stresses are
the highest, until the individual laminae are fully separated from one another. The ultimate failure
is then due to the elastic buckling of the individual and separated lamina which experiences a global
instability.
As introduced previously, a small crack is then simulated in the matrix resin layer. This debond
crack will be the initiation of the interlaminar crack at the origin of the layers’ separation. For
each simulation, a J contour integral is computed in order to determine the energy release rate,
which is the energy dissipated during fracture per unit fracture area. The strain energy release rate
corresponds to how much energy is released around the crack tip when the crack is growing and
the J-integral is then a technique to calculate this value. It should be noted that the J-integral is valid
for a non-linear elastic material and therefore is also valid for an elastic-plastic material such as the
resin. Nevertheless, this is true as long as there is not any elastic unloading. As explained in the
previous chapters, if the kinking instability is reached (before delamination failure occurs), there
will be elastic unloading but only for the non-rotated ﬁbers and not for the kink-band which keeps
contracting even when the load is decreasing. As the crack is inserted where the shear stresses are
the highest, the crack will then also be in the potential kink-band. Furthermore, the J-integral is
also only valid inside an homogeneous material or at the interface between two material. The resin
being homogeneous, the J-integral is a valid approach.
The chosen approach will then be to determine if the composite has already failed by delamination
when reaching the limit load of the kinking instability. In this way, the J-integral computed at the
limit load will be compared to a threshold value to determine if at this point of the loading, the crack
would have propagated. This would lead to delamination before reaching the kinking instability. If
this is the case, the conclusion would then be that the failure mode is not kinking but delamination.
The threshold value is given by the experiments made by Sørensen and Jacobsen (2009) which al-
lowed to determine a crack growth initiation value J0 for varying mode mixity. J0 is then the critical
value of the J-integral which has been evaluated around the crack tip, and corresponds to the value
for which the propagation of the crack would start. In this thesis, the crack tip suﬀers mixed-mode
loading, meaning that the mode I and II are combined. To calculate the mode mixity, the values of
the stress intensity factors KI and KII are needed, but those values can only be calculated for an
elastic case. That is why the assumption of pure mode II is made. Indeed, mode II corresponds to
in-plane shear, which is exactly the dominant loading mode in the compression of wrinkled com-
posites. The real mode mixity must be very close to pure mode II, that is why this assumption is
quite accurate. The experimental results from Sørensen and Jacobsen (2009) then give for this pure
mode II the following range of crack growth initiation values:
J0 ∈[1000, 1200]

In this way, for each simulation, the value of the J-integral at the limit load will be compared to the
threshold value J0 which actually belongs to a range of 200 J/m2 wide.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
As a consequence of the scale and the manufacturing process, defects are inevitable when produc-
ing blades in lengths up to 88.4 meters. They can lead to drastic failure, which is then extremely
dangerous and costly. In particular, out-of-plane wrinkles are the most common and severe of all
defects since they greatly aﬀect the structural integrity of wind turbine blades. This thesis makes its
contribution to better understand the inﬂuence of out-of-plane ﬁber waviness on the compression
strength and the longitudinal stiﬀness of composite materials typically found in the main laminates
of wind turbine blades.
Actually, this thesis is the continuity of the study made by Kiran Manikarnika (2016) about composite
materials with a global wrinkle. A ﬁrst model has been implemented, to which the present work
brought several improvements in order to get even closer to the real defects present in wind turbine
blades. Indeed, the ﬁber waviness have been made partial instead of global, by adding undisturbed
laminate around the global wrinkle.
10.1
Work completed
First of all, a preliminary study has been conducted in order to present an overview of the existing
models used to predict the compression strength in composite materials in literature. It has been
determined that the two important failure modes for wrinkled composites are plastic microbuckling
(kinking) and delamination, and that the important aspects to consider are the initial ﬁber misalign-
ment, and the plasticity of the matrix and its behavior in in-plane shear.
Then, the previous work about global wrinkles has been described. The main conclusion is that the
ﬁber misalignment drives the failure mode which smoothly transitions from kinking (at low values
of misorientation) to delamination (at higher values of misorientation). Moreover, the compression
strength has been shown to be very sensitive to ﬁber misalignment which governs its reduction.
Other parameters such as the slenderness ratio have been shown to have a great inﬂuence on the
mode of failure and the compressive strength of wrinkled laminates.
In this thesis, the main interest being to implement diﬀerent geometric parametric studies for lami-
nates with a partial wrinkle, the kinking strength has been assumed to give the compression strength
for all the diﬀerent cases. The driven motivation was indeed to study the inﬂuence of the geometry
of the partial wrinkle, and its level of localization. The delamination has only been studied in the
==PAGE:122==
102
Conclusion
last chapter in order to improve the maximum shear stress criterion used by Kiran (2016) and to
implement a fracture mechanics based approach on the global wrinkle.
Diﬀerent models have been created. Also some analyses of the plasticity data for the matrix have
been conducted. Then diﬀerent parametric studies have been done in order to discuss the inﬂuence
of the geometry parameter on the kinking strength and the stiﬀness. Some parametric studies have
also been conducted for the delamination cases.
10.2
Creation of the models
Diﬀerent partial wrinkle models have been created: the model 1 one has undisturbed laminate next
to the wrinkle (along the length); the model 2 has on top of that some undisturbed laminate below
whereas the model 3 has some undisturbed laminate above; then, the model 4 has actually additional
undisturbed laminate all around the wrinkle. Besides, the python script in which the FE models are
implemented oﬀers the possibility to attenuate the wrinkle amplitude.
In this way, the FE partial wrinkle models are all made of a layered structure with 2D-continuum
elements. The layers are alternatively linear elastic orthotropic ﬁber laminae and isotropic elastic-
plastic polymer matrix. The models take in as input geometry and material parameters. The diﬀerent
simulations allow to plot the end-shortening response of the considered model and to determine the
kinking strength with the limit load.
About the delamination study, a small crack supposed to be the interlaminar crack initiation is in-
serted in the middle of the resin layer which undergoes the highest shear (right is the center of the
modeled half-wrinkle), for the global wrinkle case. The results of the simulations allow the compar-
ison of the J-integral value at the limit load to a threshold value to determine if the specimen would
have delaminated before losing its stability.
10.3
Main results
The diﬀerent geometric parameter studies predicted the kinking strength reduction and the stiﬀness
reduction due to the initial imperfection.
At ﬁrst, the global wrinkle was studied for varying wrinkle amplitude attenuation. The conclusion is
that there is a higher strength reduction and a higher stiﬀness reduction for non-attenuated wrinkles.
However, it should be noted that the wrinkles with 0.5° misalignment angle haven’t shown any
variation of stiﬀness since this defect size is too small for the stiﬀness to be sensitive. And this has
been observed for all the models.
Then, the model 1 was studied for varying length-wise wrinkle ratio. The addition of undisturbed
laminate next to the wrinkle decreases the strength in comparison to the strength of the global
wrinkle. However it has no inﬂuence on the stiﬀness. Also, there is more strength reduction for
lower values of the length-wise wrinkle ratio, since the kinking strength becomes closer to the Euler
buckling stress. The composite actually fails by interaction between kinking and buckling.
==PAGE:123==
10.4 Further work
103
Afterwards, the models 2 and 3 have been studied for varying thickness-wise wrinkle ratio. The
addition of undisturbed laminates below or above the model 1 results in a higher strength and a
higher stiﬀness compared to the model 1. Indeed, the slenderness ratio is then increased and the
specimen is more resistant to Euler buckling. Also, there is more strength reduction and more stiﬀ-
ness reduction for higher values of the thickness-wise wrinkle ratio. The added straight layers along
the thickness help to hold the ﬁbers steady and delay the instability. However, it should be noted
that there is a bigger stabilization when the same number of layers are added closer to the wrinkle,
like in the model 2.
Finally, the model 4 is a combination of the model 2 and 3, which means that the straight layers of
the model 2 and the straight layers of the model 3 are both added to the model 1. This results in a
lower thickness-wise wrinkle ratio, and therefore the stabilization is even more important compared
to the models 2 and 3. The strength and the stiﬀness are then even higher compared to the model 1.
For small misalignment angle (like 0.5°), the augmentation of the strength provided by the model 2
and 3 in comparison to the model 1 can be not enough to recover the high strength of the global
wrinkle. Indeed, the global wrinkle with 0.5° misorientation has a really high strength since it does
not undergo large shear stress and can easily be much more resistant to Euler buckling. Its slender-
ness ratio is often much higher than the other models which are longer (due to the addition of undis-
turbed laminate next to the wrinkle) since the numbers of straight layers added in the thickness-wise
direction has to be relatively low to be realistic.
Thereafter, the study of delamination shows that for a global wrinkle with a misalignment angle
between 2° and 3°, the failure mode transitions to delamination. The conclusion is then that delam-
ination is for sure the failure mode for global wrinkle with θmax ≥4°. However, the length of the
initial cracks has been shown to have an inﬂuence on the J-integral value and can then have an
inﬂuence on the failure mode.
10.4
Further work
Those models are an improvement of the previous global wrinkle model. The eﬀects of the undis-
turbed laminates surrounding the wrinkles in the real wind turbine blade laminates are taken into
account for the prediction of the compression strength.
On the one hand, some more studies can be done with those models of partial wrinkle. For example,
the transition between kinking and delamination can be studied. Parametric studies can be done for
varying misalignment angle for each model in order to determine for which angle the delamination
starts before kinking. To do so, the fracture mechanics based approach developed for the global
wrinkle can be implemented on the other models. The pre-existing crack can also be inserted in
one lamina layer or at the interface between a matrix and a lamina layers in order to discuss the
inﬂuence of the position of this crack initiation. Also, the delamination failure can be studied for
varying geometry parameters. The same study as the one done for the kinking strength can be
conducted for the delamination strength which can then be determined by the load at which the
J-integral value reaches the threshold value of crack growth initiation. Otherwise, cohesive zone
elements can also be implemented to model the matrix-lamina interface. Also, the inﬂuence of the
plasticity data for the matrix material can be studied with another power-law hardening such as
Ramberg-Osgood power law. Finally, the mesh sensitivity can also be studied in order to validate
==PAGE:124==
104
Conclusion
the results found in this thesis.
On the other hand, the partial models in themselves can be improved. For example, some misorien-
tation of 0.5° or 1° can be added within the straight and undisturbed laminate added around a larger
amplitude wrinkle, since in reality the laminate can not be perfectly aligned. Then, some experi-
ments can be done to validate the models. But for an accurate comparison between the models and
the experiments, the creation of the model should include the possibility to give some point coordi-
nates as an input in order to better represent the real geometry of the tested sample. Those points
can for example be retrieved by observing the sample at the microscope and taking the coordinates
of the points which will allow to sketch the real waviness. It may also be relevant to change the
model to 3D continuum elements, in case this eﬀect is shown to be important in the experiments.
Finally, some hybrid composite materials can be tested on those partial wrinkles, since for example
the compression strength is expected to be higher when including carbon ﬁbers in the lamina.
10.5
Final remarks
Thus, this thesis has made its contribution to better understand the inﬂuence of out-of-plane ﬁber
waviness on the compression strength and the longitudinal stiﬀness of composite materials. Im-
provements can still be made, and the python script allows to easily add and modify the diﬀerent
functions.
This research can be used by blade manufacturers in order to better determine when blades need to
be repaired, but also by designers in order to consider the strength and stiﬀness reduction during
the blade structure design.

def plasticity (Eresin,resinPlasticity):
if resinPlasticity[0] == ’file’:
with open(resinPlasticity[1], ’r’) as csvfile:

elif resinPlasticity[0] == ’hardening’:
Sy = resinPlasticity[1][0]
N = resinPlasticity[1][1]
plasticTable = [(1.0,0.0),]
# Zero plastic strain for 1st yield
for i in range(2,200):
Strue = float(i)
ep = (3.0/7.0)*(Sy/Eresin)*(Strue/Sy)**N
plasticTable.append((Strue, ep))
plasticTable = tuple(plasticTable)

def rom (Efiber,Eresin,nufiber,nuresin,Gfiber,Gresin,nu23,Vf):
E1 = Efiber*Vf + Eresin*(1-Vf)
# Rule of mixture
nu12 = nufiber*Vf + nuresin*(1-Vf)
# Rule of mixture
E2 = Efiber*Eresin/(Efiber*(1-Vf)+Eresin*Vf)
# Inverse rule of mixture
G12 = Gfiber*Gresin/(Gfiber*(1-Vf)+Gresin*Vf)
# Inverse rule of mixture
G23 = E2/(2*(1+nu23))
# Plane 2-3 isotropic
return (E1,E2,E2,nu12,nu12,nu23,G12,G12,G23,Vf)
