Abstract
In association with preparations for the Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge
(MBZIRC) in Abu Dhabi it was desirable to explore techniques for solving the challenges
involved. One such challenge was the Challenge 1 which involved catching a ball carried by
an ’intruder’ drone by use of an ’intercepting’ drone.
In this project it was investigated if it is possible to solve the problem of object tracking for
feedback control of a drone using convolutional neural networks (CNN).
It was investigated what network architectures are best suited for feedback control of a drone,
how performance can be guaranteed across diﬀerent environmental conditions and how a
neural network can be utilized for tracking purposes. The suitability of diﬀerent approaches,
to solve the problems, were analyzed.
It was found that the most suitable CNN architecture was the state-of-the-art neural network
YOLOv3 as well as its smaller and faster version, tiny YOLOv3. Training on a dataset of
910 labelled images enabled the network to achieve a mean average precision of 92.61% and
86.59% for the full YOLOv3 and the tiny YOLOv3 networks, respectively.
To solve the problem of robustness towards a change in environmental circumstances, a
method for synthetic data generation was formulated and implemented with inspiration from
related research. The implemented solution was found to improve the performance of the
YOLOv3 network in an environment from which no data had been actively collected, reduc-
ing the number of false positives by 76%.
In order to utilize the trained networks for tracking purposes a context driven methodology
was applied. The implemented tracking algorithm utilizes hysteresis conditioning based on the
distance to the ball in order to switch (1) between the YOLOv3 and tiny YOLOv3 networks
and (2) between a highest conﬁdence and a context tracking strategy. Through empirical
testing it was demonstrated that the tracking algorithm was able to track a target ball.
For a ﬁnal test, the tracking algorithm was implemented in a feedback control loop on a drone.
It was found that this feedback control system was capable of controlling the position of the
drone in relation to a target ball. Hence, it was evident that CNNs are suitable for visual
object tracking of objects for feedback control of a drone.
In future work it could be investigated if a faster and less accurate network architecture
could be used instead of the YOLOv3 architecture to increase the overall speed of the system.
Furthermore, work in the ﬁeld of automation through neural networks might expand the
usefulness of machine learning tools.

Resume
I forbindelse med forberedelserne til The Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Chal-
lenge (MBZIRC) i Abu Dhabi, var det hensigtsmæssigt at udforske teknikker til at løse de
involverede udfordringer. Én af udfordringerne var "Challenge 1", hvor opgaven involverede
at fange en bold, som hænger under en ﬂyvende drone, ved brug af en anden drone.
I dette projekt blev det undersøgt, hvorvidt det er muligt at benytte Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNN) i forbindelse med objektsporing til brug i tilbagekoblingsregulering af en
drone.
Det blev undersøgt, hvilke netværksarkitekturer, der er bedst egnet til tilbagekoblingsregule-
ring af en drone, hvordan ydeevne kan garanteres på tværs af forskellige miljøforhold samt,
hvordan et neuralt netværk kan benyttes til sporingsformål. Anvendeligheden af forskellige
tilgange til at løse problemerne blev analyseret.
Det blev fundet, at den mest passende CNN arkitektur var det moderne neurale netværk
"YOLOv3"samt dets mindre og hurtigere version "tiny YOLOv3". Træning på et datasæt med
910 markerede billeder gjorde det muligt for netværket at opnå en mean Average Precision
(mAP) på 92, 61% og 86, 59% for henholdsvis YOLOv3 og tiny YOLOv3 netværkene.
For at løse problemet med robusthed over for en ændring i miljøforhold, blev en metode til
syntetisk datagenerering formuleret og implementeret med inspiration fra relateret forskning.
Den implementerede løsning viste sig at forbedre YOLOv3-netværkets ydeevne i et miljø, hvor
der ikke aktivt er indsamlet nogen data, hvilket reducerede antallet af falske positiver med
76%.
For at udnytte de trænede netværk til sporing, blev der anvendt en kontekst-drevet metode.
Den implementerede sporingsalgoritme anvender hysterese-betingelser baseret på afstanden
til bolden for at skifte (1) mellem YOLOv3 og tiny YOLOv3 netværkene og (2) mellem
’kontekstsporings-strategien’ og en ’højeste konﬁdens-strategi’. Det blev gennem empirisk test
bevist at sporingsalgoritmen var i stand til at spore en bold.
I en test af det samlede system blev sporingsalgoritmen implementeret i tilbagekoblingsløkken
på en drone. Det blev fundet at dette tilbagekoblingsregulerings-system var i stand til at
kontrollere positionen af dronen i forhold til en bold. Derved blev det påvist, at CNN’er er
egnede til visuel objektsporing af objekter til tilbagekoblingsstyring af en drone.
I fremtidigt arbejde er det relevant at undersøge om en hurtigere og mindre nøjagtig netværks-
arkitektur kunne bruges i stedet for YOLOv3-arkitekturen, for at øge systemets samlede ha-
stighed. Desuden kan forskning inden for automatisering gennem brugen af neurale netværk
udvide brugbarheden af machine learning metoder.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge (MBZIRC) is a robotics competi-
tion taking place in Abu Dhabi, that challenges teams from universities all around the world
to solve problems that are relevant to real world scenarios. The focus of the challenges is on
autonomous operation and includes ﬁreﬁghting, law enforcement and construction of build-
ings. The development of tools to perform such tasks may contribute to save lives as well as
help improve the living conditions in both ﬁrst and third world countries.
DTU aims to be recognized as an elite university with an international reputation of being
on the forefront of technical and natural sciences. This comes with a desire to show the world
that DTU is capable of competing with other universities within these ﬁelds. Participation in
the MBZIRC is a great chance to showcase the competences and dedication of the students
and staﬀ.
In association with preparations for the MBZIRC in Abu Dhabi it is desirable to explore
techniques for solving the challenges involved. One such challenge is the Challenge 1 which
has been described on the MBZIRC website as follows:
”
In Challenge 1 a team of UAVs will autonomously track and interact with a set
of objects (for example intruder UAVs) following 3D trajectories inside the arena.
Challenge 1 is motivated by UAV safety, where the team UAVs will attempt to
capture and neutralize intruder UAVs inside the arena.
MBZIRC website[16]
”
The challenge comes two fold; ﬁrstly a drone should be ﬂying around in an "8"-shaped path
with varying height, carrying underneath it a yellow ball (see ﬁgure 1.1). The drone travels
along its path at a speed of 8m/s for the ﬁrst half of the time after which it travels at a speed
of 5m/s. The tasks to be carried out is to intercept the ball using a drone, catching it and
landing it inside a speciﬁed landing area. Secondly, ﬁve green balloons are placed on poles at
random positions on the ground of the arena. The task is to ’pop’ these balloons. The scope
of this project aims to develop means to detect and track the drone and yellow ball, with the

The task at hand involves a complex image analysis problem; to be able to localize a drone
and ball and track it as it moves in 3D space. A ball has a relatively simple shape compared
to a drone that has a much more complex and angle dependent shape. Traditional image
analysis may be able to solve the problem, however, recent advances in neural network image
analysis makes machine learning an obvious candidate tool to solve the problem.
Using image analysis for tracking purposes has its caveats: The detections made by the de-
tection algorithm, needs to be sorted and tracked. Another caveat stems from the problem
of data. Implementing robust image analysis algorithms for detection requires an extensively
varied dataset on which to design said algorithm. For machine learning this is the data on
which to train. If it is possible to narrow the scope of the usage of the algorithm to environ-
ments from which a dataset is constructed, this would most likely not cause further challenge.
However, in this project, the proposed system is designed and tested in Denmark with the aim
of being able to operate in Abu Dhabi under diﬀerent environmental circumstances. Since
it is very diﬃcult to obtain such varied data, it is necessary to examine how else to ensure

1.2
Problem formulation
The aim of this project is to answer the following research questions:
• Is it possible to solve the problem of object tracking for feedback control of a drone
using convolutional neural networks?
a. What network architectures are best suited for feedback control of a drone?
b. How can performance be guaranteed across diﬀerent environmental conditions?
c. How can a neural network be utilized for tracking purposes?
1.3
Reading guide
This thesis consists of four main chapters. The ﬁrst of which, chapter 2, deals with the physical
setup for the Challenge 1 and thereby deﬁnes the physical scope of the project. Chapters 3, 4
and 5 tackles research questions a, b and c, respectively. Each chapter consists of an analysis,
implementation as well as a test and results section. Chapter 6 presents a ﬁnal test of the
overall system and the results of this. Finally the results of the project are discussed and
concluded upon in chapters 7 and 8.

Chapter 2
Deﬁnition of Physical Setup
2.1
The Challenge
In this section some of the technical details of the challenge is further explored. Information
has been extracted from the MBZIRC website[16] as well as informational e-mails from the
MBZIRC organisers. The arena in which the challenge took place was covered in a netting to
allow unrestricted ﬂight outdoors. The size of the arena is approximately 100m × 60m × 20m
and the landing area makes up a square area of approximately 10m × 10m in size. A drone,
with a ball attached with a metal wire underneath, was ﬂying as described on the website:
"Figure of 8 in 3D space with random orientation"[16] in a height of 5m −25m. The ball was
originally determined to be of a red color. However, in later information its color was changed
to be yellow. For this reason a few sections in this project features a yellow ball. The ball
was attached to the drone by a magnetic couple through a ﬂexible wire approximately 1.5m
in length. The size of the ball was reported to be 15cm in diameter and of a soft material. A
red and a yellow ball, that matches these parameters, were acquired in order to develop and
test the necessary systems. The arena in which the challenge takes place had a neighbouring
arena in which other teams were competing to complete other challenges. This means other
drones operate within sight of the challenge 1 arena.
2.2
Drone Platform
The drone employed in this project was a quadcopter of the model DJI M100. It is a developer
focused drone platform with which it is possible to interface with the main drone controller by
the use of, for example, a Raspberry Pi. A Raspberry Pi model 3b (henceforth called ’RPi’)
has been used to interface with the drone controller and the computation hardware. The built
in controller of the drone performs drone stabilization and GPS interfacing and the RPi works
as a mission controller and sensor interface. The DJI M100 has a takeoﬀweight capacity of
3600g[9]. Testing showed that this value is conservative, however, ﬂight performance decreased
rapidly when exceeding 3600g. The drone itself (with propeller guards) with battery and
Raspberry Pi weighs approximately 2790g leaving 810g for system implementation.
The heaviest of the mounted objects is the net that was designed to catch the ball. The
net weighs approximately 630g and is mounted on top of the drone close to the centre of lift

3.1
Analysis
In recent years, Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN) has gone through a substantial growth
in both usefulness as well as use[18].
Neural networks solve a variety of task through a
data oriented approach. The main diﬃculty in achieving an accurate network is to obtain
a dataset of a satisfactory size and variety. However, given a consistent dataset including
varied circumstances and environmental conditions ANN’s can result in powerful tools that are
robust to variations in conditions and noise. The task which the machine learning algorithm
in this case must perform is to classify and localize a drone and a ball in an image. The
combined task of classifying and localizing will henceforth be called detecting or predicting.
Multiple diﬀerent state-of-the-art Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architectures that
show prominent results exist. It is outside the scope of this project to dive into each of these
and therefore only the chosen architecture is presented here along with the reasoning behind
the selection.
3.1.1
Comparison of networks
The hardware on which the system was implemented on was limited to a small computation
device, in contrast to a full size system with multiple graphics cards.

3.1.2
YOLOv3 and tiny YOLOv3
YOLOv3, You Only Look Once version 3, is a state-of-the-art CNN architecture for object
detection built on darknet - an open source neural network framework in C[20]. It features
a total of 106 layers and it is a fully convolutional network (FCN). This means there are
no fully connected layers. YOLOv3 is a so-called One-shot detector that performs detection
on the whole image in one pass through the network and it features scale-space detection.
Furthermore, its sibling, tiny YOLOv3, is a much smaller network (it has fewer layers) that
features all the same state-of-the-art techniques but in a smaller package. Tiny YOLOv3 is
smaller and thus faster, however, less accurate. Tiny YOLOv3 does still feature scale-space
prediction at two scales and the architecture thus still retain some of the scale invariance that
the full YOLOv3 architecture features. [21]

One of the most salient features of the most recent version of YOLO is prediction at three
diﬀerent scales. Figure 3.2 shows a concatenated illustration of the full YOLOv3 architecture.
The prediction is performed in a Scale-space manner.
First, the CNN feature extraction
architecture performs residual network feature extraction on the input image. The resulting

Chapter 4
Application of Synthetic Data
Generation
4.1
Analysis
The motivation for the utilization of synthetic data is clear; even a well functioning neural
network may not work as well as intended in environments for which the network has not been
trained. In this case it involves relatively high costs to collect data in the intended location
and therefore it is necessary to explore another way of achieving a level of robustness towards
a change of environment thereby improving the overall performance of the networks. In this
chapter a strategy to increase performance through synthetic data generation is investigated.
State-of-the-art synthetic data generators incorporates the use of multiple neural networks for
the data synthesizing alone. An example of this is Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)
developed by Ian J. Goodfellow et. al. in 2014 [12]. In simple terms the system involves
training two models on real data. One network is then tasked with ’fooling’ the other and
thereby learns to generate the most realistic looking images possible. A later development
of this method involves training a third network that help fooling the second network by
helping the ﬁrst network become better at generating realistic images [23]. These methods
are great at improving a network by embedding context into the synthetic data. This context
could include obvious ones such as: "a whale most likely never will appear on an image of a
dining room", hence a successful outcome would not include such an image. The task in this
project only involves two classes (drone and ball) with one simple context: "a ball is hanging
underneath a drone". The drone and ball objects could appear in any region of the images
given that only relevant background images are used. The only relevant background images
are images of the surroundings which the network should learn. Therefore, due to the simple
nature of the task in this project a simpler solution is investigated.
A more simple solution has here been hypothesised with basis on a paper by Dwibedi et al.
in 2017[10]. The paper shows that improvements to the performance of a network can be
made by ensuring patch-level realism in the synthetic data: "Our key insight is that ensuring
only patch-level realism provides enough training signal for current object detector models. We
automatically ‘cut’ object instances and ‘paste’ them on random backgrounds. A naive way
to do this results in pixel artifacts which result in poor performance for trained models." -
16
==PAGE:26==
s144015 Joachim Axelsen
Automation and Control
17
Dwibedi et al., (2017), pp.1 [10]. In simple terms: if data is generated by simply cutting
and pasting the objects from each class unto a given background image, artefacts in the
form of pixel discrepancies appear along these pasted objects. A network trained upon such a
dataset might learn to ﬁnd these pixel artefacts instead of learning to detect the actual objects
themselves. The most salient features of the generation procedure proposed by Dwibedi et al.
[10] are distractors and blending functions.
The four main components of the solution hypothesised in this project include: Backgrounds,
Objects, Distractors and Blending functions.
Backgrounds are images captured of the environment(s) in which it is desired to increase
performance. Synthetic data play two roles in terms of improving the performance of a neural
network; (1) It decreases the labour involved in acquiring a lot of data by manually capturing
and labelling data and (2) it eliminates the need for ’real’ data from the environment in which
the network needs to be able to operate in. However, a prerequisite for this solution is images
from the environment for use as background images.
Objects are instances of objects which are to be pasted and labelled onto the background
image. The objects are cut-outs of the objects that has been extracted from images from real
data.
Distractors are similar to objects in the sense that they are also objects that has been cut-
out from an image and pasted onto the background images. However, distractors are not
labelled and should not be predicted by the network. These are present to ensure that the
network does not learn how to ﬁnd objects simply by ﬁnding pixel artefacts from the pasting
process.
Blending functions are a variety of ﬁlter-functions that are applied in the pasting process
or to the image as a whole. These are intended to change how the pasted objects appear
and thereby also prevent the network from learning how a certain pasting procedure looks in
terms of pixel discrepancies.
The aforementioned components and conditions represent the key framework of the selected
and rather simple approach assumed suitable for solving the challenge of robustness towards a
change in environment. The process of implementing the approach is outlined in the following
section.

Chapter 5
Development of Tracking Algorithm
5.1
Analysis
The primary challenge with tracking is to ensure that the object being tracked is the intended
object. One of the problems with image analysis methods is their tendency to return false
positives. Neural networks are no exception to this. For this reason it is necessary to weed
out the false positives (or in this case potentially also the irrelevant true positives) to ﬁnd the
intended object.
At this stage of development a neural network that is capable of detecting balls and drones
with an accuracy in the range of 80∼90% has been trained.
5.1.1
Context Tracking
The most simple approach to visual tracking is to search for the object with the highest con-
ﬁdence [1] [4]. Such methods are highly sensitive to false positives and the object leaving the
ﬁeld of view (FOV). Another popular, simple approach to tracking is to perform region based
analysis based on previous detections [1] [4] [13]. Such methods are sensitive to sudden and
abrupt movements of both object and camera and they are more cumbersome to implement
with neural networks since multiple neural networks need to be trained in order to predict
on the changing resolutions of the regions of interest. Advanced methods for tracking negate
such problems. One such method was outlined by Chen et al. in 2018[6] and involves the use
of unscented Kalman ﬁltering to enable non-linear tracking of multiple objects while being
robust to occlusion. Such advanced methods are in fact unnecessary for this project for one
simple reason; one important fact about the challenge at hand was that only one speciﬁc ball
and drone, which it was desired to track, was present at any given time. Therefore, it was
possible, based solely on context, to distinguish between detections; any ball can only be the
intended object if it has a drone object a predeﬁned distance above it. It was hypothesised
that it was possible to develop a tracking algorithm that exploit this context. This methodol-
ogy is in line with the one applied by Dinh et al. in 2011[8]. The algorithm should only mark
a ball as the target if a "support object" (in this case a drone) appears above it.

5.1.2
Tracking speed
The full YOLOv3 network runs at a relatively low framerate of 3 FPS on the Jetson TX2
hardware (see table 2.1), and therefore it was necessary to switch to the faster network (tiny
YOLOv3) when the target ball moved closer than a predeﬁned distance. This was based on
the assumption that as the target ball moves closer, the control of the position relative to
it should be more ﬁne-grained in order to allow the drone to catch the ball. The distance
at which the faster network is utilized must be calibrated through trial and error. Utilizing
the full YOLOv3 network when the ball is further away and the tiny YOLOv3 network when
the ball is close results in a trade-oﬀbetween tracking accuracy and tracking speed - higher
accuracy when the ball is further away and higher speed when the ball is closer.
It was
hypothesised that it would be possible to reliably track the correct target ball by exploiting
context. However, as the drone and ball approach the camera, the drone may move out of
the ﬁeld of view. When this happens the contextual information is lost. For this reason
another tracking strategy was needed for when the ball is close. Quantitative inspection of
the performance of the tiny YOLOv3 network when the ball was close (1m∼10m distance)
showed that it was suﬃcient to use a simple ’highest conﬁdence’ strategy once the tracking
algorithm switches to the tiny YOLOv3 network, simply because at this approximate distance
the ball is detected with a conﬁdence near 100%.
In this section, the ﬁeld of object tracking has been explored. It was found that a simple
solution utilizing the context of the challenge was suﬃcient. Furthermore, a speciﬁc strategy
for tracking has been outlined. In the following sections, the implementation and testing of
this strategy is outlined.

Network switching
A simple decision tree was implemented to govern the neural network switching. The switch
was implemented in a simple hysteresis controlled by three settings from the settings ﬁle
(’setting.py’): TINYTAKEOVER, FULLTAKEOVER and PATIENCE. The switching is un-
dertaken in the following manner:
• If the full YOLOv3 network is predicting and the ball gets closer than the deﬁned
TINYTAKEOVER, the tracking algorithm switches to tiny YOLOv3.
• If the tiny YOLOv3 network is predicting and the ball is gone (not detected) for PA-
TIENCE number of frames, the tracking algorithm switches to full YOLOv3.
• If the tiny YOLOv3 network is predicting and the ball moves further away than FULL-
TAKEOVER, the tracking algorithm switches to full YOLOv3.
This completes the development of the tracking algorithm. The next step in the process is to
test that the behaviour of the system matches what is expected.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In this project it was shown that it is possible to utilize CNNs for object tracking for feedback
control of a drone. Through comparative analysis as well as physical testing it was found
that the YOLOv3 network architectures was suited for the task. Evaluating the performance
of the tiny YOLOv3 network, it could be theorized that using this network alone may be
suﬃcient for relatively simple object detection tasks such as this one. One may argue that
the balance between inference speed and prediction accuracy could have been leaned more
towards speed than it was in this project. This theory leads to the consideration that the
tiny YOLOv2 architecture also might be a relevant candidate. The YOLOv2 architecture is
faster but less accurate than YOLOv3[22].
The implemented networks achieved a mAP of 92.61% and 86.59% for the full YOLOv3 and
the tiny YOLOv3, respectively, while predicting a minimal number of false positives on the
validation data. Examination of the false positive predictions of the networks show that some
predictions were labelled false for the reason that the IOU was lower than 50%. In the actual
use case of the network it is much better to have an approximate prediction rather than
no prediction. It is better to move relative to an estimate than to not know anything at
all. Therefore, the predictions of the networks may actually be more valuable than the mAP
calculations express. One has to realize that the line must be drawn somewhere, and the limit
of 50% IOU is one commonly used in other work. Furthermore, as shown in ﬁgure 3.7.c in
chapter 3 the false positive predictions are sometimes not actually false but instead caused
by an error in labelling. In the ﬁgure the prediction is rated a false positive even though
the network has in fact detected the drone solely based on a small stick in the edge of the
image. However, since the person labelling the data had deemed it unnecessary to predict
the case at all, the network was being penalized for it. This is an example of how machine
learning algorithms sometimes are imposed inaccuracies due to the inﬂuence of the ground
truth labelling, that in terms is limited by the accuracy of the person labelling the data.
The application of synthetic data generation increased the performance of a network, when
exposed to new environments. Thereby a level of assurance, that the network can perform
satisfactory across diﬀerent environmental conditions, even when data is unavailable for that
environment, was added. Many aspects of the implemented solution included arbitrary quan-
tities; an arbitrary number of object instances and an arbitrary number of distractors etc. It
would be beneﬁcial to investigate if a larger set of libraries and background images, might

Another promising ﬁnding was that context can be utilized to increase the usefulness of neural
networks for tracking purposes. Context provides a way to extract useful information from
prediction results. But how relevant is that context in a real world scenario? The motivation
for this project was to participate in the MBZIRC competition but the aim of the competition
is to inspire technological development to tackle real world problems. A real world scenario
relevant to the Challenge 1 could be to task a drone with intercepting drones trespassing in
no-ﬂy zones - for example an airport. However, for a drone to be carrying a brightly colored
ball is equivalent to a deer wearing a target - not an ideal situation for the deer and not very
realistic. Perhaps the challenges should be more loosely deﬁned and at the same time more
realistic; "Catch a drone autonomously using any means necessary". The deﬁnition of more
realistic challenges with more loosely deﬁned rules could motivate creative thinking and more
eﬀective solutions.
The implemented system was tested and demonstrated to be eﬀective in providing localization
feedback in a control loop. The intercepting drone was able to control its position relative to
a target ball. However, it was not possible to test the systems capability to actually catch
the ball and the test was performed without the net mounted.
In a situation where the
net is mounted poorer performance could be expected from the controller, however it may
be presumed that substantial performance improvements could be achieved through control
parameter tweaking.
In future work it would be interesting to investigate if the machine learning algorithm has been
fully exploited. Machine Learning has a huge potential reaching beyond performing object
classiﬁcation and localization. One could imagine that it is possible for a neural network to
predict movement before it even happens. An example on how this might be possible could
be derived from the fact that the ball is hanging under the drone by a ﬂexible wire; if the
drone starts moving in a direction the ball will move in that same direction with a slight delay.
Thereby it might be possible to predict the movement of the ball based on the behaviour of
the drone. This is possible to hard-code into the tracking program but a neural network may
be able to learn more abstract chains of events to utilize in the prediction of future movements.
It is thus worth exploring in future work, if machine learning can enable automation systems
to act on predicted events rather that on information of what has already occurred.
The next step in the development of the proposed system is to perform a substantial amount
of testing. Testing to tweak control parameters and testing to identify potential pitfalls in
the tracking strategy. The new ASTA (Autonomous Systems Test Arena) at DTU is a great
addition to the Institute of Automation and Control for the testing of all forms of autonomous
systems in future projects.
These facilities will surely prove valuable in preparations for
MBZIRC 2022.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Through comparative analysis and testing it was found that the YOLOv3 architecture was
suitable for feedback control of a drone. Two versions of the YOLOv3 architecture was imple-
mented: YOLOv3 and tiny YOLOv3. The networks achieved a mAP of 92.61% and 86.59%,
respectively, while predicting a relatively low number of false positives on the validation data.
It was shown that the manual labelling of the data represents a source of error that potentially
reduce the performance of the network. Future research should also consider the potential
eﬀects of higher inference speed more carefully, for example the tiny YOLOv3 architecture
may provide suﬃcient accuracy for the task.
The application of synthetic data generation increased the performance of a network when
exposed to a new environment. Testing showed an 1.11% increase in mAP and a 76% decrease
in false positive drone predictions. Thus it was shown that synthetic data generation can
be applied to improve performance across diﬀerent environmental conditions. It would be
beneﬁcial to investigate if larger sets of libraries can improve the eﬀectiveness of the synthetic
data generation method, thereby further increasing performance.
Another promising ﬁnding was that the neural network architecture, YOLOv3, can be utilized
for tracking purposes by application of a context driven tracking algorithm. Through empirical
tests it was shown that the implemented program was capable of tracking the path of a target
ball, while utilizing the context of the task. The context utilized in this solution was derived
from the relatively narrow scope of the MBZIRC Challenge 1.
In real world scenarios it
may presumably be more challenging to identify relevant context. This is an issue for future
research to explore.
The trained networks and the tracking algorithm were implemented in the feedback control
loop of a drone. This system was empirically tested. The resulting predicted positions showed
an approximate normal distribution, demonstrating the capability of the system to provide
localization feedback in a control loop.
This allows for the conclusion that convolutional
neural networks are capable of solving the task of visual object tracking.

Appendix C
Detailed Steps in Synthetic Data
Generation
The following is a more detailed list of steps that the synthetic data generation, ’dataGen’,
program undertakes to generate each image.
1. Get settings from the settings-ﬁle
2. Get random background image from the background image folder
3. Get random cut-out of the background image
a) Get a random scaling between 640 × 480 and the size of the background image -
while keeping the aspect ratio locked
b) Get a random placement within the bounds of the image
4. Resize background image to 640 × 480
5. Get a random number of random distractors from the distractor library
• MIN_NO_OF_DISTRACTOR_OBJECTS and MAX_NO_OF_DISTRACTOR_OBJECTS
deﬁned in the settings ﬁle deﬁne the minimum and maximum number of distractor
objects
6. Paste the distractors at random locations with random scale with a random blending
method
• MIN_SCALE_DIS and MAX_SCALE_DIS deﬁned in the settings ﬁle deﬁne the
maximum and minimum scaling of the distractors
7. Create label (annotation) ’.txt’ ﬁle
8. Get a random number of objects
• MAX_NO_OF_OBJECTS deﬁned in settings deﬁnes the maximum number of
objects. The minimum is hard coded to zero
42

9. Paste the object at random location at random size
• MIN_SCALE_OBJ and MAX_SCALE_OBJ deﬁned in the settings ﬁle deﬁne
the maximum and minimum scaling of the objects
10. Check the overlap of the objects. If the overlap is too big (more than MAX_ALLOWED_IOU
deﬁned in settings) go back to step 9 and try again
• MAX_ATTEMPTS_TO_SYNTHESIZE deﬁned in settings sets the maximum
number of times to reattempt pasting due to overlaps
11. Write the bounding boxes and labels in the annotation ﬁle
12. If applicable - apply motion blurring
13. Save image and annotation ﬁle
14. Repeat steps 2 to 13 for ’n’ number of times

capable of capturing images of higher resolution than this, however this size was chosen since
