1
Abstract
The viral component of microbiomes is increasingly associated with an important
role in its community, naturally spurring an interest in high quality assembly of
viral contigs from metagenomic samples. This is no easy task, and current ap-
proaches struggle with viral assembly because of the lack of reference genomes,
high mutation rates, lateral gene transfer, and several other issues. In order to
circumvent these issues, this project tries to identify viral reads from metage-
nomic sequencing data, assemble into contigs and ﬁnally extend the contigs using
a custom database, metaSPAdes and a Paired-Read Iterative Contig Extension
assembler - PRICE. The custom assembly was succesfully performed, albeit the
resulting contigs did not contain more viral contigs according to VirSorter predic-
tions. Apart from testing the custom workﬂow, chimeric contigs from assemblies
of several gut microbiome were investigated, presenting actual cases of the issues
mentioned above.

Introduction
Viruses and phages are hugely abundant in virtually any ecosystem that includes
suitable viral hosts. This includes open oceanic waters, terrestrial soil, freshwater,
oceanic sediments, the human gut and numerous others [1, 2]. The presence of
viruses play an important role in the homeostasis and evolution of bacteria [3].
In certain coastal environments, viral populations turn over up to twice a day,
giving rise to a large transfer of carbon from biomass to organic matter through
bacterial lysis [4]. More directly linked to human health is the gut microbiome.
The microbiome has been shown to be very closely linked with health and disease
via physiological functions such as digestion, immune response, and neurodevelop-
ment [5]. The viral component of the microbiome is predominantly comprised of
bacteriophages, which co-exist and -evolve with their hosts, the bacteria [6]. Apart
from the ability to lyse host bacteria, phages have the ability to integrate into host
DNA as prophages and provide new genes. Here, the genes might confer new func-
tions such as new metabolic functions or the ability to produce virulence factors
to the bacteria [7]. Despite the importance of the virome in gut microbiomes, it
remains the least understood component [8]. This makes assembling high-quality
and trustworthy viral contigs a high priority task. The current approach often in-
volves a mechanical separation of viruses and virus-like particles from the sample,
however, this will include many non-viral elements and thus does not represent
a clean virome. When using next-generation sequencing without separation of
viruses, assembly tools are being used to assemble reads stemming from all types
of nucleotide sequences in the gut. Because viruses share genes with their hosts,
there is a high risk of assemblers merging reads from e.g. a phage and it’s bacte-
rial host, resulting in chimeric contigs with sequence from two diﬀerent organisms.
The hypothesis of this study is that the issues can be avoided by ﬁrst selecting
reads of viral origin and creating trustworthy contigs, and then extend the contigs
with more reads from the sample, thereby making sure viral reads are not lost
within faulty contigs.

3
Methods and materials
3.1
Data
The data used for this project are 5 metagenomes sequenced from faecal samples
on Illumina NovaSeq. Adapters were removed with BBDuk and quality trimming
was done using Sickle [9, 10]. Subsequently human contamination was removed
using a masked version of the human genome and bbmap. The masked version
of the human genome ensures that shared sequences between human and bacteria
are kept in the metagenomes. All said preprocessing was done before use in the
project at hand. To verify the data quality for this investigation, fastQC was used
and revealed no issues [11].
3.2
Database creation
The database consists of amino acid sequences from Bacteria, Archaea, Virus and
plasmids. Bacterial and archaeal sequences are from the NCBI RefSeq database.
The plasmid sequences in the databases are selected by downloading genbank ﬁles
for all RefSeq plasmid entries. All entries from the RefSeq database were then
compared to the PLSDB database of plasmid nucleotide sequences [12]. If the
accession number of a genbank entry was in the curated nucleotide database as
well, all open reading frames from the given genbank entry was included in the
plasmid amino acid database. Finally, the viral section of the database is custom
data containing sequences from the MillardLab phage database and approximately
8.000 novel phage sequences identiﬁed by Shiraz Shah from the EarlyVir project
(unpublished data). The EarlyVir phage sequences were carefully identiﬁed from
COPSAC viromes, and in this study combined with ~6.000 phage sequences from
MillardLab (~14.000 sequences clustered at 90%) [13]. After establishing the 4
individual parts of the database, each individual part had duplicates removed.
Following this, sequences that appeared in more than a single part of the database

3.3
Assembly overview
The overall workﬂow of the assembly starting from sequencing reads begins with
BLASTing all reads with the goal of selecting viral reads. This is followed by three
assembly steps. The ﬁrst step is assembling the selected reads using a standard
metaSPAdes assembly.
Second step is extending the contigs from step 2 with
unclassiﬁed reads from step 1 using the assembler PRICE. The third and ﬁnal
step is extending the contigs from step 2 with all reads from the given sample,
again using PRICE. All steps are further described below and illustrated in ﬁgure1.
Figure 1 – Schematic presentation of the three assembly steps in the custom work-
ﬂow.
3.4
Read sorting
A key component of the proposed iterative assembly is selecting reads that are
found only in phages. These will be the starting point of the iterative assembly. To
4
==PAGE:9==
select these ﬁrst input reads from a metagenomic sample, all sequenced reads will
be categorized utilizing DIAMOND BLASTX with an e-value cut-oﬀat 0.001.
All reads (both forward and reverse) are BLASTed against the four databases
separately, giving a total of 8 runs of BLASTX. A single read can potentially ﬁnd
hits in all four databases, but only reads that exclusively got hits in the phage
databases were extracted. If only a single read of a pair was classiﬁed as a unique
phage read, the pair was excluded from the inital pool of phage reads. The read
sorting is visualized in ﬁgure 2.
Figure 2 – The concept of read sorting. All reads are BLASTed against the 4 parts
of the database seperately. Only reads that exclusively get hits in the viral section
of the database are used in the initial assembly.

3.5
metaSPAdes assembly
All assemblies using metaSPAdes (initial phage read assembly and assembly of the
full metagenome for comparison/benchmarking) where performed with SPAdes
v3.13.1. The command used was "spades.py –meta -t <number of threads> -o
<output directory> -1 <forward.fastq> -2 <reverse.fastq>" for both the phage
assembly and the full metagenome assembly [14].
3.6
PRICE assembly
Both ﬁrst and second round of contig extension was performed with the PRICE As-
sembler v1.2. using the command "PriceTI -fp <forward.fastq> <reverse.fastq>
870 -a <number of threads> -nc 80 -icf <initial contig ﬁle> 1 1 5 -o <output di-
rectory> -logf <verbose log>". The number 870 corresponds to the insert length
of the read pairs plus the standard deviation multiplied by two, as per the rec-
ommendations from the PRICE documentation. The ﬂag -nc sets the number of
cycles in the assembly. -icf takes the contigs that are to be extended as well as 3
other parameters. The initial 1’s sets number of addition steps and the number of
cycles per step. Here, all initial contigs are added at the beginning of the assem-
bly, rendering the second 1 to be ignored by PRICE. The ﬁnal "5" is a constant
multiplier for the quality score used during assembly. For contigs as initial input
sequences, using a constant over 1 is recommended by the creators of PRICE [15].
3.7
Global alignments
GGSEARCH from the FASTA v36.3.8 package was used to search global align-
ments between the initial assembled contigs from exclusively phage reads and all
reads from assembly of the full metagenome using the command "ggsearch36 -m 8
<query ﬁle> <library ﬁle> > <output ﬁle>". The ﬁle with initial phage contigs
were used as query ﬁle and the contigs from the full metagenome as library.

3.8
Local alignments
For all local alignment tasks, BLAST v2.9.0 was used with standard settings
except from getting output as BLAST tabular format 6. After BLASTing initial
viral contigs against FMG contigs the top hit for each viral contig was extracted
from the results.
For all samples, there were several cases where many phage
contigs had their best alignment to the same FMG contig. The FMG contig with
most phage contig hits were then chosen for further inspection and were BLASTed
against the NCBI nucleotide database.
3.9
VirSorter
VirSorter v1.0.6 was used for identifying viral contigs in both custom and metaS-
PAdes assemblies. It was run with the standard command "wrapper_phage_contigs_sorter
_iPlant.pl -f assembly.fasta –db 1 –wdir output_directory –ncpu 4 –data-dir
/path/to/virsorter-data". –db 1 sets VirSorter to use the default database, RefSeq
viruses infecting bacteria and archaea. The sequences from the database are clus-
tered using MCL clustering and protein clusters with more than 3 members are
used to create a database of proﬁles searchable with HMMER3. Protein sequences
not included in a cluster were included in a BLAST database [3].
3.10
Gene prediction
Predicting genes was done by utilizing Prodigal v2.6.3.
The software was run
with the -p meta ﬂag for analyzing metagenomic data and with output including
the predicted open reading frames as a fasta ﬁle. Notably, Prodigal is developed
for predicting protein-encoding genes in prokaryotic genomes, and thus not the
ideal tool for predicting viral genes. Even though this investigation focused on
assembling viral contigs, the use-case for Prodigal was actually trying to ﬁnd
bacterial genes in a given set of contigs [16].

3.11
Analysis of ﬁnal contigs
VirSorter was used to identify viral contigs. The identiﬁed contigs from VirSorter
where examined for bacterial content using an approach based on ViromeQC.
ViromeQC is one of the best contemporary tools for analyzing bacterial contami-
nation in virome sequencing data. ViromeQC only works on sequencing read level,
but to mimic the function on contig level, a similar approach was implemented in
this study. It consists of a BLASTn search for ribosomal subunits and searching
for 31 bacterial single copy marker genes using HMMER3 hmmsearch and hidden
markov model proﬁles of the 31 genes from AMPHORA2 [17, 18]. In this inves-
tigation, HMMER 3.2.1 and BLAST 2.9.0 was used with default settings. The
marker genes code for proteins involved in transcripton, translation, replication,
or central metabolism for bacteria. The method was tested on selected contigs
from assemblies of the full metagenome to conﬁrm the functionality with positive
results for both BLASTn and hmmsearch.

4
Results
4.1
Database
The ﬁnal database contains 23,993,008 amino acid sequences from archaea, bac-
teria, phages and plasmids. Of these, 648,302 are archaeal, 22,015,436 bacterial,
719,593 plasmid, and 609,677 are phage sequences.
4.2
Read classiﬁcation
The ﬁrst step of the custom assembly was to identify reads that with high con-
ﬁdence originate from virus.
For all samples, a high degree of all reads were
successfully classiﬁed as seen in table 1. As an example, of the total 20,888,479
read pairs of sample #16, 13,758,216 could align to bacteria, 1,762,573 to archaea,
4,387,906 to plasmids and 1,968,131 to phages in the database. Many reads aligned
to more than a single part of the database. As the input for the initial assembly
round should be phage reads exclusively, pairs also aligning to other categories
than phages were excluded, resulting in 279,453 viral read pairs. For the remain-
ing samples, the classiﬁcation was similar; there were a high degree of overlapping
classiﬁcations, resulting in many potential phage reads not getting classiﬁed as ﬁt
for use in the initial assembly.
4.3
Initial virus assembly
The identiﬁed subset of viral reads from each sample were assembled using metaS-
PAdes generating a number of contigs. Short contigs where weeded out by remov-
ing sequences below 500 bp. With sample #16 as the example again, 5079 contigs
where the result of the assembly, but after trimming for length, 972 contigs were
left for contig extension. Assemblies from the remaining 4 samples were sorted for
length as well, also with removal of a large amount of short contigs as result. The
number of contigs from the ﬁrst assembly for all samples can be seen in table 2.

4.4
Comparing initial contigs to full metagenome assembly
To ensure that the approach is sensible and that careful selection of reads might
prevent chimeric contigs, the initial contigs of sample #16 were compared to a
metaSPAdes assembly of the full metagenome. Comparison was performed us-
ing GGSEARCH, a global aligning tool from the FASTA suite of programs. Of
the 972 virus contigs, only 181 could align to contigs from the metagenome con-
tigs, conﬁrming that the majority of initial virus contigs where diﬀerent from the
metagenome contigs, and that it might be an advantage to do sequential assembly.
Besides a global alignment to the metagenome contigs, a local alignment was
carried out using BLASTn.The results showed that numerous virus contigs in many
cases would align to the same contig from the full metagenome assembly. The best
example being 31 virus contigs aligning to contig #6 (259,002 bp) from the full
assembly. All virus contigs aligned to contig #6 somewhere between bp 233 and
162,614. BLASTing contig #6 against the NCBI nt database revealed alignment
with Anaerostipes hadrus, a Gram positive bacterium isolated from human faeces.
The alignment stretched from bp 210805-254888 - i.e. not overlapping with the
alignments of the virus contigs.

assembly of the full metagenome. The same contig #6 also align with A. hadrus
from the NCBI nucleotide database at a completely separate area of the contig.
This is a clear case of a chimeric contig.

4.5
PRICE assemblies
The next step of the targeted virus assembly is extending initial contigs from
the metaSPAdes assemblies of viral reads with unclassiﬁed reads. This was done
using PRICE and resulted in a signiﬁcant increase in both numbers and lengths
of contigs for all 5 samples as can be seen in tables 2 and 3.
Finalizing the assemblies was done by another round of PRICE assembly, this time
with contigs from the PRICE assemblies above and all reads from the respective
metagenomes. Again, the number of contigs and the N50 increased for all samples,
with the exception of sample #579. The number of contigs decreased, but on the
other hand, the N50 increased drastically.

5
Discussion
As for many types of bioinformatical work, the database is the starting point for
good results. Selecting as many true positive sequencing reads originating from
phages is desirable for a good result on the initial assembly of the contigs that are
to be extended. Even though the database used in this study contains 8000 novel
phages, a large number of viruses will not be in the database, and therefore not
be included in the initial assembly.
Another point to discuss about this project is the crude method for selecting
the reads based on hits in the database. Completely excluding reads that hits
more than the phage database from the initial assembly in a binary fashion likely
prevents many true phage reads from being included, as there are many sequences
that can be found e.g. both in virus and prokaryots.
Evaluating the results from both assembly approaches (stepwise custom assembly
versus metaSPAdes) with regards to viral contigs is not a straightforward task.
Most tools are based on some kind of recognition of sequences or patterns from
known viruses in databases, meaning unknown viral sequences will not be rec-
ognized, as they do not exist in current databases. This is also the case with
VirSorter, which uses information from the RefSeq database of viruses infecting
bacteria and archaea. As previously mentioned, a huge part of viral diversity is
not represented in the current databases, including RefSeq. As a consequence, it
is likely that many true viral contigs are not classiﬁed as viral by VirSorter - it
predicts with a high degree of false negatives [19].
Another evaluation approach taken by researchers is to evaluate the performance
of diﬀerent assemblers on datasets with known composition. This could be in the
form of synthetic datasets consisting of simulated reads or metagenomes spiked
with known viruses, or by comparing to a known virome from a fecal sample [20,
21]. Comparing the hybrid assembly from this current study to assembled contigs
of sequenced viromes from the same samples would be interesting and should be
16
==PAGE:21==
done, but was unfortunately not possible within the time frame of this study.
An important result from this investigation was the fact that in several cases, there
were clear indications of erroneous contigs assembled with a standard metaSPAdes
assembly. The contigs were assembled with reads originating from diﬀerent or-
ganisms because of shared genomic sequence stemming from lateral gene transer.
Shared enes is a widespread phenomenon, meaning that the issue could appear to
a high degree in assemblies. This implies a huge problem with assemblers current
approaches, especially regarding assembly of viral contigs. The project at hand is
not a suﬃcient investigation of the extent of the issue, but it showcases the need
for addressing the problem and conduct additional research on the area.
6
Conclusion
The approach to viral contig assembly in this project was succesfull in identifying
exclusive virus reads, assembling them and extend them.
The results diﬀered
from the standard metaSPAdes assemblies, but did not produce more viral contigs
according to VirSorter. The project also identiﬁed cases of the suspected issues
with standard assemblies, where reads of diﬀerent organisms are assembled into
chimeric contigs, possibly due to shared genes.
