ABSTRACT
Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) play an important role in today’s in modern technology, due
to their capabilities in fields such as robotics, unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV), autonomous
driving, etc. These models are widely used in computer vision tasks like object detection,
which is crucial for identifying and locating objects in images or videos, used in applications
like autonomous vehicles and smart surveillance. However, deploying such sophisticated
models on edge devices presents significant challenges due to computational and resource
constraints. This project focuses on deploying YOLOv5, a state-of-the-art object detection
model known for its speed and accuracy, on an Edge TPU accelerator. This process involves
converting the YOLOv5 model from its original PyTorch framework to TensorFlow, and
then to an 8-bit quantized TFLite version compatible with the Edge TPU. Finally, Edge
TPU compiler is used to compile the TFLite model into a format that can be efficiently
executed on the Edge TPU hardware. In this study four models are examined: single-class
YOLOv5s, single-class YOLOv5n, multi-class YOLOv5s, and multi-class YOLOv5n. Our
study reveals that model conversion and quantization decreases model size by 50%. As a
result, it significantly impacts their performance, reducing it by up to 35%. Additionally, it
becomes evident that effective training of multi-class object detection models need a more
diverse dataset for better performance. Compilation results indicate that approximately
95.5% of operations can be executed on the TPU, with the remainder running on the CPU.

INTRODUCTION
In the rapidly growing field of artificial intelligence, computer vision has become a crucial
technology, enabling machines to interpret and make decisions based on visual data. These
improvements are mainly powered by progress in deep learning, particularly through the
use of deep neural networks (DNNs). DNNs have shown remarkable abilities in tasks
such as image classification, segmentation, and notably, object detection. At the core of
computer vision are Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), a specialized type of DNN
designed to process grid-like data structures such as images. Leading DNN-based object
detection models include YOLO (You Only Look Once) [1], SSD (Single Shot MultiBox
Detector) [2], and Faster R-CNN [3].
This study focuses on YOLOv5 [4], a state-of-the-art object detection model known for its
balance of performance and efficiency. YOLOv5’s architecture utilizes CNNs to detect and
classify objects in real-time efficiently, making it excellent for scenarios requiring instant
processing. We aim to deploy YOLOv5 on the Google Coral TPU, a hardware accelerator
designed for edge devices to run machine learning models efficiently. The Google Coral
TPU offers significant advantages in power efficiency and processing speed, making it
suitable for deploying complex models like YOLOv5 in real-time applications.
The integration of DNNs into autonomous systems, such as unmanned aerial vehicles
(UAVs), autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), and unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs),
has been growing rapidly. These systems perform tasks such as surveillance, environmental
monitoring, search and rescue, autonomous navigation, and so on. Achieving full autonomy
in these systems depend on the integration of artificial intelligence software with hardware,
that enables real-time decision-making based on visual input.
Deploying sophisticated models like YOLOv5 on edge devices presents challenges due to the
resource constraints of these devices. A significant challenge is the need for quantization,
a process that reduces model size and computational load by converting weights and
activations from floating-point to integer representations. This quantization is crucial for
making the model deployable on the Edge TPU.
In the next sections, we’ll take a closer look at the YOLO architecture, the specifics of
the Edge TPU, and the quantization process needed for efficient deployment. We’ll see
how these tools and solutions can work together to tackle the challenges of deploying
deep learning models in power-constrained, real-time settings. By doing so, we can boost
the performance and capabilities of autonomous systems and explore the possibilities of
computer vision applications.

1.1.1
Deep Neural Networks (DNNs)
Deep learning is a subset of machine learning and artificial intelligence that mimics the
workings of the human brain in processing data and creating patterns for use in decision
making. This advanced form of machine learning employs neural networks with many
layers, hence the term "deep." These layers consist of interconnected nodes, each simulating
a neuron in the human brain, which processes and transmits information.
Deep neural networks (DNNs) are the backbone of deep learning, characterized by their
multiple hidden layers between the input and output layers. Each layer in a DNN transforms
the input data into increasingly abstract and complex representations. This hierarchical
structure enables DNNs to learn intricate patterns and features from large datasets, which
makes them highly effective in tasks such as image and speech recognition, natural language
processing, and object detection in autonomous driving.
Among the various types of neural networks, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are
a type that excel at processing grid-like data structures, such as images. These networks
typically include a variety of layers, such as convolutional layers, batch normalization
layers, and fully connected layers, although the specific layers and their arrangements can
vary between different CNN models. The convolutional layers use filters to scan the input
data and extract important features like edges, textures, and patterns. Each filter moves
over the image and performs a convolution operation, creating a feature map.
After each convolution operation, an activation function is applied to introduce non-
linearity into the model. Common activation functions include ReLU (Rectified Linear
Unit), Sigmoid, and Tanh. ReLU and its Variants such as Leaky ReLU and Parametric
ReLU are particularly popular as they help the model learn faster and more effectively by
addressing the vanishing gradient problem, which occurs when gradients become too small
during training, causing the model to stop learning properly.
Batch normalization is another important part of the process. It normalizes the output of
the previous activation layer using the batch’s mean and standard deviation. This process
helps make training more stable and faster by reducing changes in the data distribution
during training.
Finally, after several rounds of convolutional layers, activation layers and batch

normalization, CNNs usually include one or more fully connected layers. These layers
take the features extracted by the convolutional layers and use them to make the final
classification or regression decisions. In essence, CNNs work through a combination of
these specialized layers to effectively analyze and interpret image data.
Training a DNN involves two main phases: training and inference. During the training
phase, the network learns from a vast amount of labeled data through a process called
backpropagation. This involves adjusting the weights of the connections between nodes to
minimize the difference between the predicted outputs and the actual labels, a measure
known as the loss function. This iterative process continues until the model achieves
an acceptable level of accuracy. Training typically requires substantial computational
resources and time, often leveraging powerful GPUs or specialized hardware accelerators.
Inference is the phase where the trained DNN applies the learned weights and biases to
new, unseen data to make predictions or classifications. While inference is generally less
computationally intensive than training, it still demands efficiency, especially in real-time
applications. Deploying trained models on edge devices, such as smartphones, drones, or IoT
sensors, is crucial for enabling AI-driven functionalities in everyday applications. Techniques
like model quantization and pruning are often employed to optimize DNNs for inference
on resource-constrained devices. Quantization reduces the precision of the model’s weights
and activations, which lowers the computational and memory requirements. Pruning,
on the other hand, involves removing less important neurons and connections, thereby
reducing the model’s size and complexity without significantly affecting its performance.
The depth of a neural network, indicated by the number of layers, significantly impacts
its ability to learn complex patterns. Non-deep networks, or shallow networks, typically
consist of one or two hidden layers and are suitable for simpler tasks. In contrast, deep
networks can have dozens or even hundreds of layers, enabling them to model highly
intricate relationships in data. However, deeper networks also pose challenges, such as
increased training time, greater computational resource requirements, and a higher risk of
overfitting.
Training deep neural networks is resource-intensive, requiring large datasets and substantial
computational power. The process can take hours, days, or even weeks, depending on the
network’s complexity and the hardware used. One significant challenge is the vanishing
gradient problem, where gradients become too small to effect meaningful updates to
the weights during backpropagation. Techniques like ReLU activation functions, batch
normalization, and advanced optimization algorithms help mitigate this issue.
Evaluating the performance of deep neural networks (DNNs) involves several key
metrics, which are derived from the confusion matrix. The confusion matrix provides a
comprehensive view of the model’s performance by displaying the counts of true positive
(TP - where the model correctly predicts the positive class), true negative (TN - where
the model correctly predicts the negative class), false positive (FP - where the model
incorrectly predicts the positive class, also known as Type I error), and false negative (FN
- where the model incorrectly predicts the negative class, also known as Type II error)
predictions.
The confusion matrix summarizes these counts, and provides a detailed view of the model’s

Precision indicates how many of the predicted positive instances are actually positive,
thus focusing on the correctness of the positive predictions.
• Recall: Recall, or sensitivity, measures the model’s ability to identify all relevant
instances in the dataset. It is the ratio of true positive predictions to the total actual
positives:
Recall =
True Positives (TP)

Recall indicates how many of the actual positive instances were correctly identified

in the context of object detection tasks. It summarizes the precision-recall trade-off
across different thresholds. For each object class, a precision-recall curve is plotted,
and the area under this curve (Average Precision, or AP) is calculated. The AP values
for all object classes are then averaged to obtain the mAP. This metric provides a
detailed understanding of the model’s ability to detect and classify objects correctly
across different classes.
Understanding these is crucial since they provide a robust framework for evaluating the
performance of DNNs and Computer Vision tasks, gaining a comprehensive understanding
of a model’s strengths and weaknesses in classifying data correctly and detecting objects
accurately. This detailed evaluation is important for fine-tuning models and improving
their accuracy and reliability in real-world applications.
1.1.2
YOLO Series
Object detection is a crucial task in computer vision. It involves identifying and localizing
objects within an image, with applications ranging from autonomous driving to surveillance
systems. Accurate and efficient object detection models can significantly enhance these
applications by providing real-time insights and decisions. Over the years, several object
detection models have gained prominence due to their accuracy and efficiency. Notable
among these is the You Only Look Once (YOLO) series, CNN-based object detection
model, that is known for its speed and accuracy and has set a benchmark in this field.
4

1. Introduction
YOLO is an object detection model, first introduced in 2015, known for its fast speed and
accuracy. Since then, there has been several versions released (figure 1.1). Each version has
brought improvements in terms of performance, accuracy, and the ability to detect smaller
objects in various conditions. In 2017, YOLOv2 (also known as YOLO9000) was published
with a slightly deeper CNN, a modified loss function, and multi-scale training, allowing
the model to extract more features [5]. Following that, the third version of YOLO, called
YOLOv3, was introduced with further updates on the model structure and loss function,
enhancing the model’s speed and accuracy [6]. YOLOv4, published by different authors
than the previous versions, incorporated techniques such as Spatial Pyramid Pooling (SPP),
Weighted-Residual-Connections (WRC), and Path Aggregation Network (PAN), which
significantly enhanced performance and accuracy [7]. YOLOv5, introduced in 2020, stands
out due to its implementation in PyTorch, which made customization and integration into
various projects easier. It offers different model sizes, from nano to extra large, making it
suitable for deployment on edge devices with limited computational resources. In 2022,
YOLOv6 and shortly after, YOLOv7 were introduced, each enhancing the architecture
and training strategies to further improve detection capabilities. YOLOv6 focused on
an optimized and lightweight model, allowing it to run faster with fewer computational
resources, suitable for industrial applications [8]. YOLOv7 set new benchmarks in real-time
object detection performance by using a new loss function called "focal loss," which enabled
the model to detect a wider range of object shapes and sizes, and reduced false positives [9].
The most recent version, YOLOv8, released in 2023, has several alterations to the model
architecture and modules, including using C2f instead of C3 layer and a revised prediction
format. Although the latest three models (YOLOv6/7/8) offer more features, this study
focuses on YOLOv5 due to its comprehensive documentation, well-structured studies, and
strong community support, which facilitate quicker development and experimentation [10].
Figure 1.1. YOLO series timeline.
The primary difference between YOLO and other detection models lies in how they handle
the input image. In traditional detection systems, the model uses a sliding window approach,
moving over the image and applying the detection algorithm to different sections. Only the
parts of the image where the model scores highly are considered detections. In contrast,
YOLO processes the entire image in a single pass, dividing it into a grid and simultaneously
predicting bounding boxes and class probabilities for each grid cell. This allows YOLO to
be significantly faster and more efficient. This complex process is time-consuming since
the computations are applied in a loop for each windows of the image. However, YOLO’s
approach is straightforward; one single convolutional network is applied once on the full
input image that is devided into regions or (grid cells). Each grid cell predicts bounding
boxes and class probabilities for the objects within it. In other words, object detection task
is re-framed as a regression problem, resulting in obtaining the bounding box coordinates

As detailed in Table 1.2, although the number of parameters differs, the structure of the two
models is identical. Both models include 9 Conv layers, 8 C3 layers (each containing 3 Conv
layers), and a final custom detection layer. The Conv (convolutional) layers apply filters to

by a batch normalization layer to stabilize and speed up the training process. Immediately
after batch normalization, an activation layer, is applied to introduce non-linearity, which
helps the model learn complex patterns in the data. The C3 layers, which are composed
of three Conv layers, are designed to capture more complex patterns and features by
combining the outputs of multiple Conv layers. The SPPF (Spatial Pyramid Pooling-Fast)
layer enhances the model’s ability to recognize objects at different scales by pooling features

which is useful for detecting smaller objects. Concat (concatenation) layers combine feature

the network. Finally, the Detect layer is crucial as it handles the bounding box predictions,
objectness scores, and class probabilities, ensuring that the model can accurately identify
and localize objects within an image.

1.1.3
Edge TPU
Real-time inference in object detection is a critical requirement for many applications,
such as autonomous vehicles, surveillance systems, and mobile robotics, where decisions
need to be made instantaneously based on visual data. Edge devices, which are computing
resources located close to the source of data generation, are increasingly used for these
applications to reduce latency and improve responsiveness. However, deploying complex
object detection models like YOLOv5 on these devices poses significant challenges due to
their limited computational power and memory resources.
Tensor Processing Units (TPUs) and Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) are specialized
hardware accelerators designed to handle the intensive computations required by deep
learning models. While GPUs have been the go-to solution for training and deploying
neural networks due to their high parallel processing capabilities, TPUs offer a compelling
alternative specifically optimized for accelerating machine learning workloads. TPUs
provide significant performance improvements, particularly for models involving large
matrix multiplications and convolutions, which are common in deep learning.
The Google Coral TPU, known as EdgeTPU, is a specialized hardware accelerator designed
to handle the intensive computations required by deep learning models on edge devices. It
is available in two types: USB Accelerator which is in our interest in this study, and Coral
Dev Board (see figure 1.4). The Coral Dev Board is a single-board computer that includes
an Edge TPU coprocessor. This is suitable for prototyping and deploying AI applications
directly on the device without needing a separate computer. On the other hand, the USB
Accelerator is a compact, plug-and-play device that connects to existing systems via USB,
providing an easy and cost-effective way to add powerful AI capabilities to a wide range
of devices, though it requires a host device, such as a computer or a Raspberry Pi, to
function. Edge TPU is designed to deliver high throughput and low latency, making it a
suitable choice for real-time applications. Its power efficiency and processing speed are
particularly advantageous for deploying sophisticated models like YOLOv5 in scenarios
where rapid decision-making is crucial.
Figure 1.4. Google Coral TPU devices: (a) USB Accelerator, (b) Dev Board.
Key specifications of the Google Coral TPU include its ability to perform 4 trillion

embedded systems. The TPU does not support floating-point numbers but only signed-

This incompatibility of the coral TPU with framewords such as PyTorch is one of the key

the model to a format that it can execute. TensorFlow, which has native support for this
accelerator, is commonly used for this purpose. Converting a PyTorch model to TensorFlow
Lite can introduce challenges, including potential differences in the model’s behavior and
performance due to differences in how the two frameworks handle operations. Furthermore,
some operations in one framework may not be supported by the other one. For instance
custom layers like certain normalization techniques or specialized activation functions in
PyTorch might not have TFLite counterparts. These incompatibilities lead to changes in
the model’s performance in inference mode.
Model conversion typically involves exporting the PyTorch model to an intermediate
format such as ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange), which can then be imported
into TensorFlow, and finally the TenforFlow model can be converted into its quantized
light-weigh model, TensorFlow Light. This conversion process requires careful validation
to ensure that the model’s accuracy and performance are preserved.
1.1.4
Model Quantization
Object detection models, such as the YOLO series, are known for their high accuracy and
real-time performance capabilities. However, these models are inherently heavy, involving
millions of parameters and requiring significant computational resources for both training
and inference. This complexity poses a substantial challenge when deploying these models
in resource-constrained environments like mobile devices, IoT systems, and edge computing
platforms, including devices like the Edge TPU. To address these issues and ensure efficient
deployment on devices like the Edge TPU, model quantization helps reducing the size
and computational requirements of deep learning models. Quantization is essential for
making sophisticated models like YOLOv5 run efficiently on the Edge TPU, maintaining
the necessary balance between speed and accuracy for real-time applications.
Quantization is the process of converting a model’s parameters and activations from a
high-precision format (such as 32-bit floating-point) to a lower-precision format (such
as 8-bit integers). This reduction in precision results in smaller model sizes and faster
computation times. For instance, consider a YOLO model that initially uses 32-bit floating-
point representations for its weights and activations. By converting these to 8-bit integers,
the model’s size is reduced by a factor of four, and the inference speed can significantly
increase due to the simpler arithmetic operations required for lower-bit representations.
There are several methods to implement quantization, each with varying levels of complexity
and effectiveness. The two primary methods are Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) and
Quantization-Aware Training (QAT), and both are supported by popular machine learning
frameworks such as PyTorch and TensorFlow.
9
==PAGE:18==
1. Introduction
Post-Training Quantization (PTQ): This method involves quantizing a pre-trained
model without additional training.
PTQ is relatively straightforward and involves
converting the weights and activations of a fully trained model to lower precision. While
PTQ is simple and fast, it might not achieve the best accuracy, especially for complex
models like YOLO, due to the abrupt reduction in precision.
Quantization-Aware Training (QAT): QAT integrates quantization into the training
process itself. During training, the model simulates the effects of quantization, allowing it
to adapt and minimize the loss of precision. This approach typically yields better accuracy
compared to PTQ because the model learns to handle the reduced precision during training.
However, QAT requires more computational resources and time due to the additional
training steps.
In TensorFlow, the TensorFlow Lite (TFLite) framework provides tools for both PTQ and
QAT. TFLite is designed specifically for deploying models on mobile and embedded devices,
making it an excellent choice for deploying quantized models in real-world applications.
While quantization offers substantial benefits, applying it to complex object detection
models like YOLO presents several challenges. One significant challenge is the potential loss
of accuracy. YOLO models rely on precise bounding box predictions and class probabilities,
and reducing the precision of weights and activations can lead to less accurate detections.
This is particularly problematic for small objects or objects in cluttered scenes where high
precision is crucial.
Another challenge is the increased complexity in the quantization process itself.
Implementing QAT requires modifications to the training pipeline, which can be highly
complicated and time-consuming. Furthermore, quantizing models for specific hardware
targets (e.g., different mobile processors) requires fine-tuning and extensive testing to
ensure optimal performance across various devices.
1.2
Formulation of the problem
In short, deploying a YOLOv5 model on the Edge TPUs presents three main challenges:
• Model Conversions: The Edge TPU is only compatible with TensorFlow models,
which necessitates the conversion of models from other frameworks. This conversion
process can lead to a drop in the model’s performance accuracy. For example, models
initially designed and trained in PyTorch need to be converted to ONNX and then
to TensorFlow, during which subtle differences in the frameworks’ implementations
can introduce errors or inefficiencies.
• Model Quantization: YOLOv5 has a large number of parameters compared to
simpler models and quantizing such a model with millions of parameters involves
more potential for accumulated quantization errors, which can lead to a loss of detail
and accuracy and eventually degrade in performance.
• Inference on Edge Devices: While the goal of quantization is to make models
more efficient for edge devices, complex models like YOLOv5 still require substantial
computational and memory resources even after quantization. In order to ensure that

1.3
Methodology, scope and conditions
In this project, YOLOv5n and YOLOv5s models pre-trained on the Microsoft Common
Objects in Context (COCO dataset) [11] are fine-tuned on a custom dataset to detect "boat"
and "person" in maritime scenes. The COCO dataset is a large-scale object detection,
segmentation, and captioning dataset containing over 300,000 labeled images with 80
different object categories. Its diverse range of objects and contexts provides a robust
foundation for transfer learning, making it ideal for fine-tuning models. Fine-tuning is
especially suitable when the custom dataset is relatively small, as it provides a balance
between model complexity and data availability.
Two model types are selected for this study: YOLOv5n and YOLOv5s. The letters "n"
and "s" at the end of the model names stand for "nano" and "small," respectively. These
are the two smallest versions of YOLOv5 among the five variants (nano, small, medium,
large, and extra-large), making them more suitable for edge device deployments where
computational resources are limited.
Two types of object detection tasks are applied to each model: single-class and multi-
class object detection. The key difference between these two tasks is that single-class
object detection does not involve a classification and labeling step, making the model less
computationally intensive.
After each model is fine-tuned, it is a converted to ONNX (Open Neural Network
Exchange) format. ONNX is specifically designed for deep learning models and serves
as an intermediate step, facilitating the model conversion from PyTorch to TensorFlow.
Following this, the ONNX model is converted to TensorFlow. At this stage, quantization
is applied by converting the TensorFlow model into a TensorFlow Lite int-8 model, where
the weights and activations are quantized into 8-bit integers, optimizing it for Edge TPU
deployment. The final stage involves compiling the quantized model with the Edge TPU
compiler, which converts a TensorFlow Lite model into a file that’s compatible with the
Edge TPU, and eventually deploying the model on the TPU accelerator.
Quantization could also be applied before model conversion at the PyTorch level. However,
PyTorch’s quantization methods are often better optimized and tested for simpler
models and are not fully compatible with a complex model like YOLOv5 that are with
various types of layers including custom layers. Thus, applying these methods requires

4. Width: The width of the bounding box, normalized by the image width.

2.1.2
About Our Dataset
In this study, we use transfer learning techniques for training, specifically the fine-tuning
method. This approach is especially beneficial when a large-scale training dataset is not
available. Fine-tuning a deep learning object detection model requires high-quality images
that are relevant to the target objects. Our object detection model aims to assist drones
in search and rescue operations at sea, where it needs to detect boats, persons, and other
objects.
The dataset used in this study was provided by Monopulse, the collaborating company
on this project. It contains a total of 4,306 images, which were split into two subsets:
70% for training and 30% for evaluation. Specifically, the training dataset consists of
3,444 images, while the evaluation (test) dataset contains 862 images. This ratio ensures
that a substantial portion of the data is used for training the model, while a sufficient
number of images are reserved for evaluating its performance. Each image is accompanied
by a corresponding text file with information about the objects present. The dataset
includes images depicting scenes from seas, beaches, and similar environments, with three
classes (labels): boat, person, and other. Although the dataset was mostly clean and
annotated, some additional cleansing steps were necessary. Duplicate images across folders
were identified and removed to avoid redundancy. Additionally, some image labels differed
from our classification standards, so these images were separated.
The above were mainly done in Python. After these steps, the data was restructured
to ensure it was accessible and readable by the model. The data was organized into a
directory containing separate folders for training and testing images, along with necessary
configuration files such as obj.data which holds the dataset’s metadata, and obj.names
which lists the class names. This structured format ensures that the model can efficiently
access and process the data during training and testing phases.

Data Preprocessing
Data preprocessing is an important step to transform the raw data into a clean and
structured format. It improves the quality of the data and ensures consistency. Doing so
the model is able to work more efficiently with the data, as it prepares the data for the
training process and further analysis. It usually involves several steps which can differ
from task to task depending on the objectives. In this work, the techniques below are
applied on the dataset before feeding the images to the model during fine-tuning:
1. Image resizing: The images in our dataset were relatively large and there was a
variety in sizes, Thus all the images were resized to the 960 as the first step so all the
feeding inputs of the model have the same dimensions which is necessary for batch
processing.
2. Normalization: It’s used to transform features in a dataset to standard range from 0
to 1, which helps with improving the performance and accuracy of the model.
21
==PAGE:30==

3. Data Augmentation: This step creates images based on existing images in the
dataset by applying transformation techniques like flipping, cropping, and rotating
to the images. Doing so, the dataset diversity is enhanced which will lead to better
generalization and robustness of the model.
4. Anchor Box Calculation: This step involves determining a set of predefined bounding
boxes, called anchor boxes, which the model uses as reference points to predict the
location and size of objects in an image. The locations, widths, and lengths of these
boxes are extracted from the annotation files provided in the dataset.
The preprocessing steps above are built-in functions in YOLOv5 source codes. By creating
a correct data structure, using the right formats in the dataset, and adjusting the function
inputs making them more suitable for our data, the process runs based on the defined
hyper-parameters. Given that these built-in preprocessing functions cover all the necessary
steps we intended to implement, we did not add any additional preprocessing steps. This
comprehensive preprocessing framework ensures that the data is well prepared for the
best possible model performance, aligning with our training objectives and simplifying the
overall process.

Since our dataset is not large enough for training a deep learning model, we need to use a

of this study is called fine-tuning. In fine-tuning the pre-trained weights of a model which
are normally trained on large, general-purpose datasets are further trained on a smaller,
targeted data set. This allows the model to adapt to the specific domain and nuances of
our target dataset and improves the performance of object detecting for our specific task.
YOLOv5 is provided us with a pre-trained model that is trained on the COCO (Common
Objects in Context) dataset, which is one of the most commonly used datasets for object
detection. This dataset contains 80 object categories, with over three hundred thousand of
images and annotations that include objects in different contexts and lighting conditions.
The COCO dataset’s diversity helps the pre-trained model learn a wide range of features
applicable to various object detection tasks. Fine-tuning this pre-trained model allows the
model to adjust its weights to better detect the specific characteristics and objects in our
dataset, which are boats, persons, and other objects.
Object detection is the process of identifying and locating objects within an image. YOLOv5,

2.3
Converting and Compiling Models: TFLite and Edge
TPU
After training the YOLOv5 models in PyTorch, we needed to convert them to formats
suitable for deployment on edge devices. This involves converting the PyTorch model
weights to ONNX, then converting the ONNX model to TensorFlow, and finally quantizing
the TensorFlow model to TensorFlow Lite (TFLite) 8-bit format since the Coral TPU
requires a quantized TFLite model to perform efficiently.
The conversion process from PyTorch to TensorFlow involves several critical steps. We start
by loading the trained PyTorch model and converting it to ONNX using the "onnx.export"
function which is a tool designed in the PyTorch framework. This function exports the
model in the ONNX format. ONNX serves as an intermediate representation allowing for
interoperability between different frameworks.
Next, the ONNX model is imported into TensorFlow. This step involves translating the
model’s architecture and weights from the ONNX format to a TensorFlow-compatible
format. Tools such as the ONNX-TensorFlow converter (onnx-tf) facilitate this process by
mapping ONNX operations to their TensorFlow equivalents. During this conversion, it is
crucial to ensure that all layers and operations are correctly translated to maintain the
model’s performance and accuracy.
Once we have the model in TensorFlow, the next step is to optimize it for edge deployment.
This is achieved by quantizing the model to the 8-bit TFLite format. Quantization reduces
the model size and improves inference speed, making it suitable for resource-constrained
25
==PAGE:34==
2. Methodology and Data
devices like drones equipped with Coral TPUs. TensorFlow provides a comprehensive
toolkit for this process, including the TensorFlow Model Optimization Toolkit, which offers
post-training quantization techniques.
The challenges in this conversion process are several. During the conversion from PyTorch to
ONNX, there might be lack of compatibility due to unsupported operations or differences
in layer implementations between frameworks.
For instance, even though the ReLU
activation function is defined and commanly used in both of the frameworks (PyTorch and
TensorFlow), and it’s also supported by ONNX, the conversion of the YOLOv5 PyTorch
model to TensorFlow failed when this activation function was used, and therefore the
models were trained once more using SiLU activation function instead, which turned out
to be compatible and convertible. Furthermore, the ONNX-TensorFlow converter may not
perfectly map all of the operations, especially custom or less common layers used in deep
neural networks like YOLOv5. This challenges especially occur when dealing with complex
models like YOLOv5.
On the other hand, quantizing the TensorFlow model to TFLite format poses another
challenge which is loss of model precision, since the 8-bit representation is less precise than
the 32-bit floating-point format. This especially happens when the quantization is done
after the model is trained with 32-bit floating-point images, but receives 8-bit images in
the prediction (inference) stage.
To ensure the effectiveness of the conversion process, we evaluate the TFLite models
using the same metrics that were used for evaluation after the models were trained. This
includes assessing metrics such as precision and recall to compare the performance of the
quantized models against their original counterparts. By conducting these evaluations,
we aim to understand how the conversion steps have affected the models’ performance.
This comparison provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between model accuracy and
efficiency, ensuring that the deployment on edge devices maintains an acceptable level of
performance.
The final step before model deployment on Edge TPU is to compile the models using using
Coral Edge TPU compiler. The input model of the compiler is a TensorFlow Lite (TFLite)
model. In our case, the models are in 8-bit integer format which ensures that the models
are in the quantized format with a smaller size. The compiler compiles the TensorFlow Lite
file into a format compatible with Edge TPU and ensures that the model runs efficiently
on the TPU’s architecture. This includes the model’s compatibility as well. The Edge
TPU has specific requirements such as the model size and the existing operations for the
models it can execute. If the model is too heavy, it wouldn’t be compiled. Also, not every
operation is supported by the Edge TPU. In case of any unsupported operation in the
model structure, the specific operation would run on CPU instead. In this study, having
operations in the model’s structure that are unsupported by Edge TPU is likely to happen
since YOLOv5 is a DNN model with several custom layers.

The compiler maps the high-level operations in the model to the TPU’s hardware operations

• Number of operations that will run on Edge TPU,
• Number of operations that will run on CPU,
• And a table that provides a detailed status report on how various operations in the
model are handled by the Edge TPU. The possible rankings are:
– Mapped to Edge TPU: The operation is supported and will be executed on the
Edge TPU.
– Operation is otherwise supported, but not mapped due to some unspecified
limitation: The operation could theoretically be run on the TPU, but a specific
limitation (not detailed in the output) prevents it from being mapped.
– More than one subgraph is not supported: The operation involves multiple
subgraphs, which the TPU does not support.
– Operation not supported: This status indicates the operation is not supported
by the Edge TPU at all.
The basic requirements for the Edge TPU Compiler are as follows: Firstly, the input must
be quantized to 8-bit fixed-point numbers. This ensures efficient processing on the Edge

TPU. Secondly, tensor sizes must be static, meaning they cannot change dynamically in
size during runtime. Additionally, parameters such as bias tensors must remain constant at
compile time, ensuring consistent and predictable behavior. The tensors should be three-
dimensional or less; if they have more dimensions, those additional dimensions must have
a size of one. Lastly, the model operations must be supported by the Edge TPU, as only
compatible operations can be offloaded and accelerated by the TPU. These requirements
are essential to ensure that the model can be successfully compiled and executed on the
Edge TPU.
By converting the YOLOv5 models to TFLite, we have ensured that the input data is
quantized to 8-bit fixed-point numbers and that all tensor sizes are static, meeting the
Edge TPU Compiler’s requirements. We have also ensured that parameters such as bias
tensors remain constant at compile time and that tensor dimensions are adjusted to be
three-dimensional or less, with additional dimensions constrained to a size of one, thereby
optimizing the model for Edge TPU compatibility and performance.

• Installing PyCoral library: PyCoral is a Python-based library designed to speed up
the process and provide additional functionality for the Edge TPU. While this step is
not mandatory, in this study, we found it beneficial to use this library for simplicity

4. Discussion
The observed performance degradation after quantization was expected due to the inherent
impacts of the conversion and quantization processes. The already lower accuracy of
Models 3 and 4 before these processes further declined their performance issues, likely due
to the lack of variety in the training dataset. To address this, a more diverse dataset could
be employed to improve model performance. Additionally, data pre-processing techniques,
such as the Flip-Mosaic algorithm used by Wu et al. in traffic scenario object detection [25],
could potentially improve the dataset variety and consequently boost multi-class detection
accuracy. This remains an area open for further exploration.
Moreover, as shown in Figure , quantization significantly affects the pixel values in the
images, converting them from 32-bit floating-point to 8-bit integer representations. This
reduction in precision makes the object detection process more challenging for the model.
The primary reason for this difficulty is that 8-bit integers offer a much lower dynamic
range compared to 32-bit floats, leading to a loss of subtle details and nuances in the image
data. Consequently, the model struggles to accurately detect and classify objects, resulting
in a noticeable drop in performance.
This performance degradation highlights the inherent trade-offs in the quantization process.
While it enables models to run efficiently on hardware like the Edge TPU, it also
compromises the precision of the computations, affecting the accuracy of object detection.
However, alternative quantization methods, such as Quantization-Aware Training (QAT),
are expected to perform better with less drop in accuracy, since in QAT, the model learns
to detect objects with quantized images during training, allowing it to adapt to the lower
precision and maintain higher accuracy.
In summary, while conversion and quantization processes do affect model performance,
the results highlight the resilience of well-trained single-class models and underscore the
importance of dataset variety in multi-class detection tasks.
It’s worth highlighting how effective the quantization process has been in drastically
reducing the size of all four models. As shown in Table 3.6, quantization cuts the model
sizes to about half of their original size. This significant reduction is consistently seen
across all models, showcasing how efficient quantization is in making models better suited
for deployment on resource-limited hardware like the Edge TPU accelerator. This size
reduction not only helps in saving memory but also enhances the speed and efficiency of
the models, making them more practical for real-world applications on edge devices.
4.1.3
Compiling Outputs
Based on the detailed breakdown in Table 3.5, all four YOLOv5 models were successfully
compiled to run on the Edge TPU, with only 12 out of 266 operations being executed
on the CPU which is around 4.5% of the whole existing operations in the models. These
CPU-bound operations were identical across all models and included: one instance of
CONV-2D, one MUL, two QUANTIZE, three STRIDED-SLICE, one PAD, three CONCATENATION,
and one RESHAPE.
These operations run on the CPU primarily due to the Edge TPU’s limitations, such
as its inability to support multiple subgraphs within a single model or other unspecified
42
==PAGE:51==
4. Discussion
constraints that prevent certain operations from being mapped. A subgraph is a smaller,
self-contained section of a larger computational graph that can be executed independently.
Because the Edge TPU cannot handle multiple subgraphs within a single model, certain
operations are be offloaded to the CPU. Running these operations on the CPU, rather than
the specialized Edge TPU, can impact real-time performance in terms of speed and power
usage. The CPU, designed for general-purpose tasks, may not handle specific operations
as efficiently as the TPU, leading to potential bottlenecks and slower processing times.
While there has been substantial research into deploying deep learning models on
accelerators like the Edge TPU ([13], [15], [12]), there is a gap in understanding the
performance implications of a few operations running on the CPU. This aspect could be
crucial for optimizing model deployments and enhancing overall performance, making it
a promising area for future research. Exploring how these CPU-bound operations affect
the model’s efficiency and developing strategies to mitigate their impact could provide
valuable insights into improving model deployment on edge devices.
Compiling models for the Edge TPU involves adding specific components that ensure they
run efficiently on TPU hardware. As shown in Table 3.6, the Edge TPU compiler almost
doubles the size of quantized models. This size increase can be attributed to several factors:
• Edge TPU-specific instructions and metadata:
These elements are crucial for
optimizing operations and ensuring compatibility with the TPU architecture.
• Padding and alignment requirements:
To meet the hardware’s specifications,
additional padding and alignment may be necessary, which increases the overall
model size.
• Runtime dependencies: Extra code or libraries needed for runtime execution on the
TPU are often bundled into the compiled model.
Both the single-class models (Model 1 and Model 2) and the multi-class models (Model 3
and Model 4) exhibit consistent size changes due to quantization and TPU compilation.
This indicates that these processes are systematically applied across different model
architectures. Although the size increases after compilation, the models are optimized to
take full advantage of the Edge TPU’s specialized hardware, likely resulting in faster and
more efficient inference when deployed.
The table highlights how effective quantization is at reducing model sizes, making them
more efficient for deployment on edge devices. The moderate size increase after TPU
compilation is necessary to fully optimize the models for the Edge TPU’s architecture. This
shows why it’s important to perform quantization before compiling the model with the TPU
compiler. Since the compilation process increases the model size, starting with the smallest
possible model through quantization is crucial for successful Edge TPU deployment.
This dual-phase process underscores the importance of both size efficiency and operational
optimization when deploying deep learning models, like those in the YOLO series, on the
Edge TPU. Future research could explore ways to refine these steps, aiming to minimize size
increases while maximizing performance after deployment. This could involve developing
more advanced quantization techniques or optimizing the compilation process to better
handle model size constraints.

CONCLUSION
This final chapter sums up the key findings, practical uses, limitations, and future research
directions related to using YOLOv5 models on TPU accelerators. The study focused on
how well these models work after fine-tuning, conversion, and quantization, especially in
terms of their suitability for Edge TPU deployment. Through this detailed examination
and analysis, we got significant insights into how single-class detection models compare
to multi-class ones, how quantization affects performance, and how these models can be
used in real-life scenarios on edge hardware. This chapter gives an overview of what we
discovered, the progress we made, and the future steps in this area of research.
5.1
Summary of Findings
This study aimed to evaluate the performance of YOLOv5 models after fine-tuning,
conversion, and quantization, specifically focusing on their deployability on TPU
accelerators. The results indicate that using our training dataset to fine-tune YOLOv5
models, the single-class detection models significantly outperform those designed for
multi-class detection across all metrics, even after undergoing the quantization process.
Single-class models are advantageous for applications requiring high accuracy and speed
for specific object detection tasks, such as security monitoring, industrial automation, and
specialized medical imaging.
This quantization, while reducing model sizes by about half and making them more suitable
for resource-limited hardware like the Edge TPU, resulted in an expected accuracy drop
of up to 35%. Despite this reduction, the single-class models maintained relatively high
performance levels, whereas multi-class models experienced a more pronounced decline in
accuracy.
The findings also highlight the importance of dataset variety. The superior performance
of single-class models is attributed to the more focused and simpler task of single-class
detection, in contrast to the multi-class models which struggle due to the lack of diversity
in the training dataset.
Furthermore, after compiling these models for the Edge TPU, which involves adding
specific components that nearly double their size post-quantization, the output shows that
approximately 4.5% of operations still run on the CPU due to TPU limitations.
5.2
Contributions to Real Life
Deploying YOLOv5 models on edge devices equipped with TPU accelerators marks a
significant advancement in computer vision and object detection. These models are often
large and complex, requiring significant computational power to run effectively. To bring

these powerful AI capabilities to scenarios such as autonomous driving, surveillance drones,
and industrial automation, where real-time decision-making based on visual data is crucial,
edge devices are essential.
Edge devices are small, often portable, computing devices that operate at the edge of a
network, close to where the data is generated. Unlike cloud-based systems, which rely on
remote servers to process data, edge devices process data locally. This local processing
reduces latency and enhances the speed and efficiency of data handling.
The deployment of YOLOv5 models on edge devices involves several critical steps:
converting, quantizing, compiling, and finally deploying the models to ensure efficient
operation on hardware with limited resources. This deployment is vital for bringing
advanced AI capabilities to the edge, where immediate and efficient data processing is
crucial.
Quantizing sophisticated object detection models like YOLOv5 and making them compact
is essential for edge devices, which typically have limited computational and memory
resources compared to cloud-based systems. Smaller, quantized models consume less power,
offer faster inference times, and can operate more effectively within the constraints of
edge hardware. This reduction in model size helps balance performance and resource
usage, ensuring that edge devices can run these models continuously without overheating
or rapidly draining battery life.
The practical applications of this technology span various industries, improving operations
and outcomes. For instance, edge-deployed object detection models like YOLOv5 can
be used on drones for search and rescue or surveillance operations, enabling real-time
identification of people or objects in need of attention, even in remote or hazardous areas.
In smart cities, these models can monitor traffic in real-time, reducing congestion and
enhancing public safety. In healthcare, portable diagnostic devices can use these models to
analyze medical images on-site, providing instant feedback and improving patient care.
By enabling powerful AI processing at the edge, we achieve faster response times, increased
privacy (as data doesn’t need to be sent to the cloud), and more robust, scalable AI
solutions that can operate independently in diverse and dynamic environments. Integrating
advanced AI into edge devices is a key step towardss realizing the full potential of the
Internet of Things (IoT) and intelligent edge computing. This approach not only enhances
the capabilities of individual devices but also contributes to a more connected and efficient
technological ecosystem.
5.3
Limitations and Future Work
This study, while providing valuable insights into the deployment of YOLOv5 models on
TPU accelerators, is not without its limitations. A primary constraint is the lack of variety
in the training dataset, which consisted mainly of frames from recorded drone videos
with minimal differences in object positions. This uniformity limited the effectiveness of
multi-class detection models, leading to significantly lower accuracy compared to single-
class models.
Additionally, time constraints prevented the application of alternative
Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) or Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) methods on

accuracy retention for the models post-quantization.
Future work could address these limitations and further enhance the deployment of YOLOv5
models on TPU accelerators. Incorporating a larger and more diverse training dataset with
a broader range of scenarios would likely enhance the performance of multi-class models,
improving their ability to classify different objects accurately. Additionally, exploring
advanced quantization techniques could yield better optimization results, helping retain
more accuracy while still benefiting from reduced model sizes. Optimizing the operations
that remain CPU-bound during TPU deployment is also crucial; this could involve refining
the compilation process or creating new methods to handle these operations more efficiently
on the TPU. Furthermore, evaluating the real-time inference performance using TPU
accelerators would provide valuable insights into the practical deployment capabilities of
these models. Future research could assess the models’ performance in terms of speed,
latency, and power efficiency during real-time operation on edge devices, uncovering
potential bottlenecks and areas for optimization to ensure the models meet the demands
of real-world applications.
5.4
Final Thoughts
In conclusion, this study has demonstrated the potential of YOLOv5 models for deployment
on TPU accelerators, highlighting both their strengths and areas for improvement. The
evaluation showed that single-class detection models consistently outperformed multi-class
models, especially after quantization, due to the simplicity and focus of their task. However,
the lack of dataset variety and the limited application of advanced quantization techniques
were identified as significant constraints. Future research aimed at diversifying the training
dataset and exploring more sophisticated quantization methods could further enhance
model performance. Additionally, investigating real-time inference performance on TPU
accelerators would provide deeper insights into optimizing these models for practical,
real-world applications, ensuring they are both efficient and effective in various edge
computing scenarios. This study lays the groundwork for continued advancements in
deploying deep learning models on resource-constrained hardware, offering a roadmap for
future enhancements.
