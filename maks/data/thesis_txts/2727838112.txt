Abstract
In a world striving towards a green transition, the rapid growth of renewable energy is
causing electricity markets to become increasingly volatile. As a result, utility┬¡scale en┬¡
ergy storage systems have been found to be an attractive technology to balance the
production and demand of energy across time. With a focus on optimizing social wel┬¡
fare for the integration of storage systems in the energy market, non┬¡merchant storage
has in recent years been proposed. However, in existing research, the challenges as┬¡
sociated with maximizing market efficiency while simultaneously ensuring cost┬¡recovery
for the non┬¡merchant storage in such markets have been highlighted. Consequently, the
FAP method is proposed to restore both of these market properties in a perfect foresight
setting. This work analyzes the market properties of the FAP method in an imperfect fore┬¡
sight setting using a robust optimization approach to steer the storage levels at the end
of a market horizon. By using this method we show, that while the FAP method ensures
cost┬¡recovery for the non┬¡merchant storage, it is no longer guaranteed for other market
participants. In response to the identified flaws of the FAP method, we propose the novel
VOS method in which the charging price of stored energy is reflected by an artificial bid┬¡
ding price in the market clearing process. By testing the VOS method on the example in
which the FAP method failed, we demonstrate that cost┬¡recovery is ensured in both the
perfect, and imperfect foresight setting. Additionally, we conduct a comparative analysis
in an imperfect foresight setting, in which the market efficiency of the VOS method with
storage levels steered by the robust optimization approach is compared to that of a myopic
approach in which the stored energy at the end of a market horizon is fixed to zero. The
results of the analysis suggest that the VOS method outperforms the myopic approach in
terms of social welfare.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivations
Goals for higher shares of renewable electricity are getting continually more ambitious and
in May 2022, a new benchmark of 69% renewable power generation in Europe by 2030
was estimated as a requirement to meet the REpowerEU plans made by the European
Union[1]. However, as the majority of the planned installation to meet this goal will be from
intermittent renewables such as wind or solar power, other technologies are needed to aid
in aligning production and consumption of electricity across time. Currently, this is largely
balanced using dispatchable power from non┬¡renewable sources such as coal and natural
gas, but also in part by renewable power from hydro and nuclear sources. However,
as the share of intermittent renewable power increases, while dispatchable conventional
power decreases, additional balancing mechanisms are needed for the stability of the
power grid. A possible solution to the aforementioned issue is the integration of utility┬¡
scale energy storage which was predicted in the IEA Energy Outlook to increase from
approximately 20 GW in 2020 to over 3000 GW in 2050 for utility┬¡scale battery storage[2].
This increase leads to significant opportunities in energy markets, as the integration of
energy storage systems (ESS) in energy markets allows for higher shares of renewable
electricity using temporal arbitrage of energy. In other words, the generated energy can
be stored in times when generation is abundant and prices are low and discharged at a
later time where there is a lack of generation and thus higher prices. However, there are
also significant challenges to the integration of ESS as most current electricity markets
are not explicitly designed to enable their participation. This is due to mechanisms not
being in place for handling the time┬¡linking constraints which are an inherent property
of ESS. Nevertheless, Order No. 841 was released by the Federal Energy Regulatory
Commission in 2018 to regulate current electricity markets to facilitate the participation of
ESS, considering physical and operational challenges[3].
At present time ESS are generally included in the market clearing model in the same way
as any other market participant where the storage operators submit bid prices and quan┬¡
tities as either a load if the ESS is planning on charging, or as a generator if planning to
discharge energy. We refer to this as merchant storage, since the storage is owned by
individual entities all seeking to maximize their profit. However, research has shown that
strategic merchant storage bidders may have incentives to underuse the ESS to maximize
their profit, leading to sub┬¡optimal operation in terms of the social welfare[4], and in some
cases, the inclusion of ESS may even reduce the social welfare depending on its market
power[5]. As a result, alternate market structures are being investigated for the inclusion
of ESS. The concept known as non┬¡merchant storage, or passive storage, is one such
structure that has been investigated in recent years. In a non┬¡merchant storage setting
the ESS do not submit bids, but are co┬¡optimized with generators and loads in the market
clearing process. Furthermore, the non┬¡merchant storage will often be an asset owned by
the market operator, but can also be owned by private entities. As previously mentioned,
the principles of non┬¡merchant storage mean that for the system to optimize the use of the
ESS, the constraints of the storage need to be included in the market clearing process.
This creates some challenges, as the storage is no longer viewed as an individual market
participant trying to maximize its profit, but as a ÔÇØtoolÔÇØ within the market clearing process
that aids in increasing social welfare. At the same time, in an ideal market, market prop┬¡
erties such as cost┬¡recovery and dispatch support should still be ensured for the storage

system, generators, and loads. Consequently, the temporal arbitrage performed by the
storage system should be lucrative to ensure revenue adequacy for the market operator
and potential storage owners.
A main difference in the inclusion of non┬¡merchant versus merchant storage in the energy
market is that with merchant storage time┬¡linking constraints are considered within its
individual bidding problem while for non┬¡merchant storage the time┬¡linking constraints
become the responsibility of the market operator. As energy markets are usually cleared
for independent market horizons, however, there is no straightforward mechanism in place
for handling the time┬¡coupling of the market. An analogy to time┬¡coupling is the more well┬¡
known geographical market coupling, which refers to the coordinated transfer and trading
of electricity between different locations and markets that create a unified and efficient
market with harmonized prices. Time┬¡coupling the markets aims to achieve the same
results, as electricity can now be traded not only across different geographical zones but
also across market horizons.
In that regard, weÔÇÖre left with the question of how to best handle the time┬¡linking constraints
and time┬¡coupling of the markets, in a way that ensures market efficiency and other desir┬¡
able market properties across not only the current but also subsequent market horizons.
In a setting where perfect foresight is assumed addressing this question is a challenge
in itself, but when an uncertain future market is considered it becomes increasingly more
difficult. In reality, there are many inherent sources of uncertainty in energy markets, and
with the share of intermittent renewables seen in todayÔÇÖs power markets, the assump┬¡
tion of perfect forecasts between market horizons is far from an accurate representation.
When this assumption is disregarded, a solution that might seem optimal based on the
forecasts will often prove to not be optimal at the time that the uncertainty is realized. As
such a market model and pricing mechanism must be formalized that ensures the desired
properties even in the case of uncertainty.
1.2
Contributions
This thesis makes several contributions to the field of market clearing and non┬¡merchant
storage modeling under imperfect foresight. The key contributions are outlined below.
In recent work, a method hereby referred to as the future┬¡aware┬¡plus (FAP) method has
been proposed that preserves market efficiency and cost recovery in a perfect┬¡foresight
setting[6]. As an initial contribution made in this thesis, we discover an edge┬¡case of the
method, in which market efficiency is no longer guaranteed due to price multiplicity. Due
to this, a modification is made in collaboration with the authors of [6] which reinstates the
market efficiency.
As another significant contribution, we analyze the FAP market clearing method under im┬¡
perfect foresight. By using a robust solution of the uncertain parameters for a full┬¡horizon
model, we get an input for the end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage level that optimizes the worst┬¡case
solution of social welfare. Through examples using possible realizations of the uncertain
parameters, we show that cost┬¡recovery is not guaranteed for the market participants
when this solution is used as input for the method.
The primary contribution of this thesis is the development of an enhanced market clearing
method specifically designed to restore the cost┬¡recovery of market participants for an
energy market with non┬¡merchant storage under imperfect foresight. The method which
we have named the Value of Storage (VOS) method, utilizes the charging cost of stored
energy to reflect an artificial bidding price of the storage in later market horizons, thereby
allowing the stored energy to affect the market price. By testing the method with the same

Prior Work
This chapter provides an in┬¡depth review of the prior works and literature that have been
found relevant for the research done on the topic of non┬¡merchant storage under imperfect
foresight in this thesis. Non┬¡merchant storage as a concept has been investigated in
recent years as a way of optimizing the social welfare increase that energy storage can
offer to the energy market[8, 9, 10, 11]. However, paper [12] which focuses on the current
state┬¡of┬¡the┬¡art on energy modeling, draws attention to a research gap as most of the
prior work done on non┬¡merchant storage either completely ignores or uses simplifying
assumptions when considering the connection between market horizons.
In the prior research, two main simplifying assumptions are often used for the end┬¡of┬¡
horizon storage levels. The first assumption is that there are no constraints on the storage
levels, which is the method that was used in [8, 9, 10, 11]. The second assumption
allows a slightly larger degree of coordination between market horizons by setting the
final and initial levels of the storage to be equal, as is the case for the work done in [13,
14, 15]. However, in both of these myopic approaches to the market clearing process,
optimal end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage levels are neglected and the value that could be offered
from the storage systems between market horizons is not optimized, which causes market
inefficiencies. A visualization of this is seen in Figure 1, where the myopic state┬¡of┬¡energy
profile fails to closely simulate the profile of the state┬¡of┬¡energy profile that is selected with
perfect knowledge of future market horizons.
Figure 1: The effect of simplifying assumptions on the state of energy over several market
clearing horizons. Reprinted from [6].
To address the issue of myopic decision┬¡making, a better alternative is to use a future┬¡
aware method of determining the optimal end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage levels by using predic┬¡
tions on the parameters of future market horizons. By using a future┬¡aware method as
is visualized by the red line in Figure 1, the storage profiles can indeed better approach
the profiles of the perfect benchmark. Several previous studies have researched differ┬¡
ent ways of setting future┬¡aware end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage levels. The standard method
of determining the levels in previous work has been by using so┬¡called ÔÇØrolling┬¡horizonÔÇØ,
or ÔÇØrolling┬¡windowÔÇØ dispatch implementations, where each market clearing considers the
forecasts for several future market horizons when the optimal dispatch is determined, but
where only the dispatch decisions of the current market horizon are binding[16, 17, 18].
However, the authors in [6] show that even when the end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage levels are set
optimally for a multi┬¡interval market┬¡clearing process cost┬¡recovery is not always ensured
for a non┬¡merchant storage system due to market price multiplicity. This issue arises due

that can be perceived as less discriminating. Another proposal is that of a new energy

using virtual links that capture the shift of power across time[23]. The authors show that
the use of virtual links for load shifting ensures cost┬¡recovery for the ESS in a merchant

3
Energy Market Theory
To comprehend the dynamics of an energy market, it is important to define the properties
and mechanisms that concretize the stability and performance of that specific market.
This section aims at providing some background knowledge on the theory behind energy
markets, with a specific emphasis on the key topics and definitions relevant to this thesis.
3.1
Market type and pricing mechanism
For the market type in this thesis, we assume that weÔÇÖre dealing with a market under per┬¡
fect competition. In this kind of market, there are usually lots of smaller participants, none
of which have any significant market power. As such, all participants in the market are
price┬¡takers who can not individually affect the price. Normally, perfect information will be
available for participants regarding costs and prices, but as we start to consider the mar┬¡
ket under uncertainty this will be disregarded. What is especially important for this work
is that generators and loads bid their true utility and cost in perfect competition, meaning
that to focus on the effect of the storage system on the market price and properties, we
will disregard strategic bidding from other market participants.
For perfectly competitive markets a short┬¡term equilibrium is reached at the point where
price is equal to marginal cost. In the case of a merit┬¡order curve, this point will be reached
when the next unit of the most expensive generator will no longer provide a positive profit,
and the cost of the last unit is thus often used to set the price of the market. In the market
models in the later chapters, this price is reflected by the dual value ╬╗t of the balancing
constraint, unless otherwise specified. This dual variable reflects the marginal cost or
price of the last generator or demand, or in cases of price┬¡multiplicity any value between
the two, meaning that if all costs are captured in the market clearing model every market
participant will recover their costs [31]. Furthermore, we assume a uniform price where
every load and generator either pays or receives the same market price per quantity of
energy.
Furthermore, we define a market┬¡clearing process with linear cost and utility functions
in a convex market. This means that the bidding price from each generator or load is
linearly proportional to the quantity that they are scheduled to use, and in the examples
used in this thesis they are even treated as a constant. We use this simplification, as non┬¡
linear cost┬¡ and bidding functions can lead to a much more complicated model without
significantly impacting the main conclusions.
3.2
Market Properties
For electricity markets, it is paramount that proper pricing mechanisms are in place, as it
incentivizes market participants to follow expectations when bidding into the market. Fur┬¡
thermore, the pricing mechanism is defined in an attempt to satisfy a range of market
properties such as non┬¡discrimination, dispatch support, cost┬¡recovery, and revenue ade┬¡
quacy, while simultaneously trying to maximize social welfare. The properties are defined
below[21][6].
dispatch support:
A market offers dispatch support if the determined market price is such that no
participant is to gain from deviating unilaterally from the market outcome. In other
words, there is no lost opportunity cost for any participant.
On the Efficiency of Energy Markets with Non┬¡Merchant Storage Under Imperfect Foresight
7
==PAGE:17==
cost-recovery:
The revenue for any market participant should cover its costs, and guarantee a non┬¡
negative profit. This property is especially important for the work done in this thesis,
as the difference in price between two market clearings may cause a negative profit
for a storage unit if this is not carefully considered in the pricing mechanism.
non-discrimination:
In a market with non┬¡discrimination, all market participants are paid the same and
pay the same per commodity. This is generally expected for a market with uniform
pricing, with an exception being out┬¡of┬¡the┬¡market uplifts where the market operator
remunerates participants with costs not captured by the pricing mechanism.
revenue adequacy:
Revenue adequacy is essential for any market, as it means that the market operator
is guaranteed a non┬¡negative profit. For this to be guaranteed the payment of the
consumers should be larger or equal to the price of the generators.
Market e´¼Çiciency:
A market is efficient if the social welfare of the market is maximized, and the market

3.3
Decision┬¡making under uncertainty
In current energy markets, there are always sources of uncertainty, for which decision┬¡
making methods need to be considered. This is becoming even more important with
a growing renewable energy sector, as the generation from such market participants is
subject to large uncertainties due to the inherent unpredictability and errors that come with
wind, solar and other forecasts. In this section, we present some of the fundamentals
of decision┬¡making under uncertainty, specifically focusing on its application in energy
markets.
When it comes to decision┬¡making under uncertainty in operations research, there are a
few popular methods that have different suitable application areas, strengths, and weak┬¡
nesses. Two of the most widely used ones are stochastic programming and robust opti┬¡
mization. One of the main differences between these methods lies in the way in which the
methods characterize the uncertainty of the parameters. While Stochastic Programming
creates a set of scenarios with different probabilities, robust optimization relies on contin┬¡
uous uncertainty sets which bounds the possible realizations of the uncertainty without
considering the probability distribution.
3.3.1
Robust optimization
Robust optimization is one of the methodologies that can be employed to address uncer┬¡
tain parameters in optimization problems. Robust optimization has two main objectives.
Firstly, it ensures feasibility for the full range of the defined uncertainty set in contrast
to deterministic problems where a slight deviation in parameters can render a solution
infeasible. Secondly, robust optimization is commonly utilized in situations where a risk┬¡
averse approach is desired, as it aims to minimize the worst┬¡case regret by optimizing the
worst┬¡case solution. The worst┬¡case regret refers to the difference between the optimal
8
On the Efficiency of Energy Markets with Non┬¡Merchant Storage Under Imperfect Foresight
==PAGE:18==
objective value and the objective value obtained by making decisions based on a realized
scenario. To achieve this, robust optimization solves a model by considering the most
pessimistic estimate for the parameters. Since the realized outcome will always be less
restrictive than initially anticipated, any solution deemed feasible by the model will also
be feasible in practice.
One of the challenges with robust optimization is that it can sometimes be difficult to
choose the bounds of the uncertainty sets for the uncertain parameters. For instance, if
the uncertainty set is overly pessimistic it may lead to very conservative solutions that sac┬¡
rifice optimality. However, if the uncertainty set is too optimistic it might not adequately
account for every possible scenario, which could lead to infeasible scenarios. Further┬¡
more, depending on the type of distribution of a parameter, it simply may not be possible
to bind every possible outcome of the parameter. As an example, this would be the case
for normal distributions where the probability gradually diminishes but never reaches zero.
In summary, robust optimization typically characterizes the uncertainty as continuous un┬¡
certainty sets rather than discrete scenarios and optimizes the worst┬¡case realization of
uncertainty which gives a very conservative risk┬¡averse solution that ensures feasibility
for all cases of uncertainty. [32] [33]

3.3.2
Stochastic programming
Another way of addressing uncertain parameters in optimization is with stochastic pro┬¡
gramming.
In stochastic programming, the uncertain parameters are incorporated by
considering a set of possible scenarios each associated with a probability of being re┬¡
alized. By simultaneously considering all of these scenarios when the optimal solution is
found for the problem, a solution can be found that performs well across multiple scenar┬¡
ios taking into account their probabilities, rather than optimizing for a single deterministic
outcome. Furthermore, when a stochastic programming method is employed, the solution
that is found returns a better objective value across the scenarios on average than if the
expected value of each parameter is used to find a deterministic solution.
In two┬¡stage stochastic programming, which we will be focusing on henceforth, there are
two decision stages. The first┬¡stage decisions are the decisions that have to be made
before the uncertainty is realized, while the second┬¡stage decisions, also known as wait┬¡
and┬¡see or recourse decisions, are the ones that can be made after the uncertainty has
been realized. We consider two market horizons of an energy market with non┬¡merchant
storage as it is defined in this thesis as an example. While the first market horizon is ongo┬¡
ing, the first┬¡stage decisions in this example are the scheduling of market participants and
energy storage decisions for the current market horizon. These have to be made before
the uncertainty of the second market horizon is realized. In the second market horizon,
the first┬¡stage decision can no longer be changed, and thus the second┬¡stage decisions
must be made based on what is optimal for the specific scenario with the predetermined
level of the energy storage system. Hence, the decisions for the two┬¡stage stochastic
problem are chosen in a manner that optimizes the weighted expected value across the
scenarios. This optimization takes into account the range of possibilities available for the
second┬¡stage decisions, given each potential solution for the first┬¡stage decisions.
While two┬¡stage stochastic programming is a useful approach for decision┬¡making under
uncertainty, it does have some limitations and challenges. Firstly, as the method uses a
scenario┬¡based approach, feasibility is not guaranteed if realizations outside of the con┬¡
sidered scenarios are a possibility, for example in the case of an uncertain continuous pa┬¡
rameter. Furthermore, the quality of the acquired solution is very dependent on whether
the representative scenarios are generated accurately. Secondly, two┬¡stage stochastic
programming typically focuses on the expected value of the objective function, without
considering preferences concerning risk┬¡aversion. Thus, the selected solution which opti┬¡
mizes the expected value might in specific scenarios lead to an extremely high opportunity
loss[33][34][35].

assumptions that have been commonly made in prior literature on non┬¡merchant stor┬¡

welfare. This results in the stored energy being at its minimum by the end of a market

While cost┬¡recovery is ensured for both generators and loads within a market horizon for

when the previously mentioned assumptions are used as conditions for the operation of
the storage, cost┬¡recovery is indeed ensured for the storage system within each market
horizon. Although having cost┬¡recovery ensured for every individual market horizon is a
safe and easy approach, it is not a necessity as cost┬¡recovery can be ensured in subse┬¡
quent market horizons. Because of this, it could indeed be preferable to instead consider
the cost┬¡recovery over a multi┬¡interval market clearing of as many market horizons as pos┬¡

other there is no way to ensure cost┬¡recovery. Setting equal starting and finishing levels
of the storage overcomes this issue, but there are downsides to using the assumptions
as this can have an impact on the market efficiency.

While the previous section explains how the market clearing model works for a single
market horizon, this section focuses on the dynamics of a multi┬¡interval market clearing
procedure, and the impact that splitting the market clearing into intervals has on the mar┬¡

market horizon, and use the two interchangeably. The splitting of the market is necessary
as the future demand and production of energy can only be known with relative certainty,
and thus in terms of the day┬¡ahead market, a sequential clearing process allows the par┬¡
ticipants to make informed decisions on a day┬¡to┬¡day basis. To this effect, two problems
are defined, namely the full┬¡horizon problem, and the split┬¡horizon problem.
Let us imagine a finite time┬¡period T consisting of multiple market horizons (days) (T d1, T d2),
in which there are multiple time┬¡steps (hours) T = (T d1, T d2) = ([t1, t2] , [t3, t4]). For such
a market the full┬¡horizon problem solves the entire time┬¡period simultaneously in a single
market clearing given by F(T ). Although it assumes perfect knowledge of more than the
current market horizon, the full┬¡horizon problem is useful as it can be used to determine
the optimal solution across multiple market horizons, and will provide the optimal level of
storage at the end of each horizon, which we denote as eÔêù
H. Thus, a model that splits the
market clearing into multiple intervals should ideally yield the same solution as in the full┬¡

all except the last T d1 market horizons using the constrained market clearing formulation
C(T d1), and subsequently solves the free formulation of the market clearing F(T d2) for
T d2. The free formulation is only used for the last market horizon in the time┬¡period, such
that the split┬¡horizon problem solutions can be compared to that of the full┬¡horizon.

4.4
The FAP method with uncertain parameters
In the previous section, it was shown that the FAP method is a feasible way of guiding
the market prices leading to cost┬¡recovery for the storage system for a perfect foresight
setting. However, in a setting with imperfect foresight, there is no guarantee that the future
market price is correctly predicted, and the storage level might not be set optimally as a
result of this. In this section, the performance of the FAP method is investigated in the
case where uncertainty is introduced to some of the participants in the market. In this
thesis, we define the considered uncertainty in such a way that we know the value of any
parameter within the current market horizon with certainty, while the parameters in any
subsequent market horizons are given under uncertainty.

4.4.2
Decision┬¡making under uncertainty method
To investigate the impact of uncertainty on the FAP method solution, we formalize a
method of selecting which solution of the full┬¡horizon model to use as input for the FAP
model, when the realization of uncertainty is not known at the time when the decisions on
the end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage levels have to be found. Two different methods from the field
of decision┬¡making under uncertainty are considered for this purpose, namely, stochastic
programming and robust optimization which have been described in detail in chapter 3.
For the application at hand, there are pros and cons to using both methods.
On the Efficiency of Energy Markets with Non┬¡Merchant Storage Under Imperfect Foresight

==PAGE:31==
If we consider the problem using the two┬¡stage stochastic programming method, we need
to choose a subset of the possible outcomes for the uncertain parameters as represen┬¡
tative scenarios with individual probabilities. Using these scenarios it is possible to find
the solution of the first market horizon as a first┬¡stage decision which maximizes the so┬¡
cial welfare in expectation. In other words, the scheduled consumption and generation
becomes a second┬¡stage decision that is optimized for each scenario, and thus the first┬¡
stage decision which has the highest weighted average of the social welfare across the
scenarios can be selected.
Alternatively, the first┬¡stage decisions can be determined using a robust optimization ap┬¡
proach. With this approach, we consider the scenario in which the uncertain parameters
are the most restrictive as the worst┬¡case scenario. By using this scenario for the solution
to the problem it is ensured that every other outcome of the uncertainty is feasible. Further┬¡
more, by using robust optimization we get a solution that is more robust and conservative,
and which aims to improve the outcomes in which the social welfare is the lowest.
In the remainder of this thesis, we have proceeded with using robust optimization as the
selected method for investigating models under uncertainty. This decision is made as we
wish to focus on optimizing the worst┬¡case regret of social welfare. By using this approach
we know that we have optimized the social welfare in the solution that would lead it to
taking the lowest value. If the realized outcome differs from the worst┬¡case, it can only
lead to higher social welfare than expected, as the production and demand constraints
are soft constraints that will simply offer more freedom in these scenarios. As a result, the
approach acquires a lower bound on social welfare. However, using the robust solution of
the full┬¡horizon model does not necessarily give the solution in which the most storage is
stored, which one might mistakenly assume as this could be considered the most ÔÇØcarefulÔÇØ
solution for future market horizons. In this regard, one should also consider that the stored
energy is not guaranteed to be needed within the considered time┬¡period as the realized
outcome could include more cheap generation than anticipated. Thus, having charged an
excess amount into the storage in the first market horizon could lead to negative profits
of the storage and an overall reduction in social welfare. In the following sections of this
chapter, we explain how we use the robust solution of the full┬¡horizon model as an input
for the FAP method.

market horizons and the price at which it was stored, such that this energy can be used in
subsequent market horizons while simultaneously ensuring that the stored energy will not
be discharged at a lower market┬¡price than the one it was charged at. This also means
that if a load has a utility that is higher than the price of stored energy, the storage system
will enter the merit┬¡order┬¡curve and the market price will settle on the ÔÇ£value of storageÔÇØ if
this has the highest ÔÇ£generation costÔÇØ.
5.1
Market Modelling
In the market model where the Value of Storage method is implemented, which we will
henceforth refer to as the VOS method, the split┬¡horizon problem is modified by adding
a few new variables, parameters, and constraints. First and foremost, two additional
variables stv, and qtv related to the storage level, and operation of the storage unit are
added to the problem. stv is the remaining amount, at time t, of storage charged in a
previous market horizon at value index v, which keeps track of the available amount of
energy in the storage from one market horizon to the next. qtv is the amount of energy of
value index v discharged at time t. It is important to note with this variable that the sign
convention is opposite compared to bt, as a positive value means energy being discharged.
While the s and q variables have been added to track the state of storage from previous
market horizons, the charging and discharging of the energy originating from within the
current market horizon still need to be accounted for. These variables however can not
be associated with a price until the market has been cleared, and therefore the variables
et and bt are kept in the model as a way of tracking these operations.
Additionally, two new parameters are added to the model. ╬╗s
v contains the market prices
of the previous market horizons at which the energy remaining in s was stored, while Sall
v
is the initial storage level of the different value indexes at the beginning of a new market
horizon. When the energy that was stored at a specific value index v has been completely
drained, these indexes can be removed from the parameters to simplify computations.

One consideration with the implementation of the VOS method is that due to there being

allows these two operations to occur simultaneously. An example of such a solution is that
storage is discharged from the variable qtv into bt and discharged from bt again later in
the same market horizon. However, when this happens it does not lead to an increase in
the objective function or changes to social welfare and market prices. This scenario only
happens when qtv in reality should have been used at the later time┬¡step instead of bt, as

5.1.1
Storage value management (SVM) algorithm
As previously mentioned, after a market clearing is finished for a market horizon there
is a possibility that there is remaining storage left in the variable et. In this case, this
energy needs to be associated with the price at which it was charged, such that it can
be merged and collected in the parameter Sall
v
for subsequent market horizons. For this
purpose, an algorithm is implemented that gets the market price for each timestep in the
horizon and determines the remaining amount of energy stored at each given market
price. Although this thesis does not explicitly incorporate factors such as self┬¡discharging
over time, charging efficiency, and discharging efficiency, it is essential to recognize the
significance of these parameters. In a real┬¡world scenario, correcting the value of storage
for these specific parameters becomes crucial for ensuring cost┬¡recovery,
The algorithm essentially works by subtracting any discharged energy in bt by order of
the cheapest previously┬¡stored amounts as an iterative process until the remaining stored
energy┬¡price pairs are determined. The remaining energy of the market horizon is cap┬¡
tured by the variable sadd and the associated price at which it was stored is captured by the
variable ╬╗add

horizons in sall

and ╬╗s

5.2.4
Limitations and challenges
While the VOS method exhibits promising results in terms of cost┬¡recovery and social
welfare under both perfect and imperfect foresight, it is important to recognize and address
some of the key limitations and challenges that could impact the applicability of the model.
These limitations stem from various aspects, such as assumptions and side effects of the
modeling procedure.
Firstly, one of the most notable limitations of the model is caused because the discharging
from the variable qtv is penalized in the objective function, while the variable bt is not. This
modeling choice is made to correct for the value of storage from previous market horizons
when determining the market price. As a side┬¡effect of this implementation, from the
modelÔÇÖs point┬¡of┬¡view any storage contained in the variable et will always be used before
the storage contained in the variable stv despite having been charged at a higher price.
Because of this, the storage kept in variable stv will not get used as often as it should, and
thus will also not be exchanged with more recently stored energy as often. However, as
the energy in the storage will not as often be replaced with more expensive energy, this
simultaneously reduces the frequency of very expensive energy getting transferred across
multiple market horizons due to repeatedly being valued higher than the market price.
The limitation still needs to be addressed in future work, however, as it can be considered
discriminating in a case where multiple individual storage units are considered.
Secondly as was explained in the presentation of the model, due to the limitations of
linear programming the VOS method does not prohibit solutions that have simultaneous
charging and discharging. These simultaneous opposite flows of power are theoretically

impossible for ESS, and thus these solutions should be avoided. With the implementation
of charging and discharging efficiencies, however, any unnecessary charging/discharging
operation will reduce the objective value and the solutions would no longer be optimal.
This implementation requires some modification to the model, however, as the charging
and discharging within a single horizon can no longer be represented by a single variable
bt when efficiencies are considered.

Discussion and Conclusion
In this thesis, we have investigated the topic of non┬¡merchant storage for energy markets
focusing on how we can strive to uphold certain market properties, namely market effi┬¡
ciency, dispatch support, and cost┬¡recovery for market participants with an emphasis on
the ESS. Our investigation particularly highlights addressing the challenges and implica┬¡
tions of imperfect foresight in this context.
In previous work, the authors of [6] showed that, while some dual solutions to the split┬¡
horizon market problem may fail to provide dispatch┬¡following incentives for non┬¡merchant
storage, there always exists a dual solution to the split┬¡horizon problem which preserves
properties in a setting with perfect foresight. Subsequently, the challenge to this issue
is that of selecting the correct dual solution. To this end, the authors propose a method
that we refer to as the FAP method, which restores market properties in the split┬¡horizon
problem in the perfect┬¡foresight case. The initial part of the investigation in this thesis is
done specifically by evaluating the effect of imperfect foresight on the performance of the
FAP market clearing method in terms of the previously mentioned properties and overall
feasibility. Since the model was originally designed under a perfect foresight assump┬¡
tion, where the end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage level could be set optimally for any given example,
investigating the imperfect foresight setting is a natural continuation as the selected end┬¡
of┬¡horizon storage level can prove to be sub┬¡optimal in this case.
By using a robust solution of uncertainty sets as an input for the FAP method, we show
that a constant market price is acquired for the uncertain market horizon regardless of the
realized outcome of the uncertain parameters. Furthermore, we show that cost┬¡recovery
is not guaranteed for the FAP method under imperfect foresight, as one of the generators
is scheduled to produce energy at a higher cost than the market price depending on the
realized outcome. As a further continuation of attempting to improve the FAP method,
we have investigated whether it is possible to alter the application of the dual variable ╬Â
used for pricing in the FAP method, to restore cost┬¡recovery. Although cost┬¡recovery is
ensured if the uncertainty sets for the robust solution are perfect, we argue that this is
a weak assumption as the uncertainty sets would have to be extremely conservative to
achieve this with absolute certainty. As a result of this, we show that in the case where
the uncertainty is realized outside of these uncertainty sets, cost┬¡recovery is not ensured
for the loads as ╬Â can turn out positive and correct the price to be higher than that of a
scheduled loads bidding price. Furthermore, it is essential to emphasize that the reason
for cost recovery not being ensured is not attributed to the use of robust optimization
as a method. On the contrary, it is exactly due to the robust optimization approach that
cost┬¡recovery can be ensured within the uncertainty set.
Recognizing the limitations of the FAP method, we introduce a novel approach called the
Value of Storage (VOS) method which better supports an imperfect foresight setting. In
the VOS method, the future does not need to be accurately anticipated to set a property
restoring market price, as the value of stored energy is considered as an artificial bidding
price. In other words, at any time the storage is being charged, the charged amount
is connected to the market price of that time┬¡step. In examples in which the previously
considered methods failed to do so, we have shown that cost┬¡recovery has indeed been
restored for the non┬¡merchant storage with the VOS method even in the specific case of

==PAGE:49==
price multiplicity where there is no marginal generator or load. This was determined to be
the case in the setting of both perfect and imperfect foresight.
In addition to cost┬¡recovery we have also evaluated the results of the VOS method in terms
of market efficiency. While we show that the VOS method is not able to ensure complete
market efficiency in a perfect foresight setting, we show that in the case of uncertainty, a
future┬¡aware version of the VOS method in which the end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage level was set
using the optimal robust full┬¡horizon solution was able to outperform a myopic approach
in every tested example where a large enough storage system was used. This was tested
both in the case where the realization of the uncertain parameters took their worst┬¡case
outcome and the average outcome of the uncertainty set.
In conclusion, we have shown that in an energy market with uncertain generation and
demand the VOS method presents a promising avenue for enhancing market efficiency in
comparison to myopic alternatives. Furthermore, while we have not proven that the VOS
method ensures cost┬¡recovery, we have through examples given strong evidence for it.
While the examples tested on the VOS method have yielded promising results, the method
has some key challenges that have been highlighted. One of the main challenges that we
identify happens in the case where the ESS gets charged at a very high price compared
to the average market price, as the ESS might be stuck with the stored energy across
multiple market horizons until the next time its value is surpassed by the market price.
However, the magnitude of this issue decreases in direct proportion to the method and
forecast quality employed in determining the end┬¡of┬¡horizon storage levels, as they reduce
the likelihood of energy being charged without subsequent discharge within a reasonable
time frame.
The previously mentioned conclusions that have been drawn give us insight into some of
the challenges and possible methods that can be used when investigating the efficiency
of energy markets with non┬¡merchant storage in an imperfect foresight setting, and some
key assumptions and simplifications are instrumental in arriving at these insights. One
such simplification is that the examples presented in this thesis assume a market with
constant linear costs and bid prices. Although this type of market clearing is common in
practice, other electricity market types such as non┬¡linear convex markets and non┬¡convex
markets can also lead to the same problems for cost┬¡recovery and market efficiency when
non┬¡merchant storage is considered. However, these markets are more challenging to
model by nature, and we leave it to future works to analyze the proposed VOS method for
this case. In addition, this thesis relies on the assumption of perfect competition, wherein
participants bid based on their true utility and cost, and strategic bidding is disregarded.
While electricity markets often deviate from perfect competition, this assumption serves
to mitigate the influence of strategic bidding on the results. Investigating the implications
of markets with non┬¡perfect competition thus stands as another potential extension to this
research.
Another area of the thesis in which we make some simplifications is in the implementation
of the ESS. The first simplification that is used here is that we only consider a few pa┬¡
rameters of a single ESS in the thesis, whereas in a real┬¡life scenario, there would likely
be multiple storage systems of different types with different parameters in terms of e.g.
charging and discharging efficiencies, degradation, power rating, and self┬¡discharge. All
of these parameters need to be considered if the methods in this thesis are to be used
in a real┬¡world application, as many of them have an impact on costs which should be
included when valuing the storage in the VOS method, or because additional constraints
and other modifications of the methods and algorithms might be necessary to incorporate

In this thesis, we use a robust optimization approach to determine the end┬¡of┬¡horizon stor┬¡

not change as they get closer in time. In a real┬¡life setting with data from realistic forecasts,
