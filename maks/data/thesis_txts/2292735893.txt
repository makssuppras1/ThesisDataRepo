Summary 
Climate change is expected to cause an increase in rain volume in Copenhagen with an over-
proportional increase in extreme events. The Copenhagen Municipality is addressing this problem by 
developing cloudburst management plans (CMP) for the whole city. In this thesis, the CMP for the 
Nørrebro catchment is analysed regarding its environmental impacts and compared to a traditional 
“grey” scenario. The impacts are quantified using a life cycle assessment (LCA) approach, which 
accounts for all life cycle stages from material generation to disposal. 
The selection of methods and the system definition is based on a literature review of previous 
work in LCA in water management systems and technologies, revealing a lack of research in holistic 
assessment of stormwater management (SWM) systems. Also, different functions provided by the 
system are often not accounted for. To avoid these limitations, a territorial LCA approach is chosen: 
The catchment is defined as the reference flow in which different functions are provided by the SWM 
system.  
The scenarios follow different design approaches and elements: The “green” scenario utilizes 
green areas and streets to delay runoff, and discharges water above the surface during extreme 
events. The water is discharged to Sortedams Sø after treatment in an underground drainage layer. 
The “grey” scenario handles all runoff in underground pipes and basins and discharges it to the 
harbour after purification in a wastewater treatment plant. The runoff takes different flow paths through 
the system using various elements depending on the intensity of the rain event, which directly 
correlates to the return period. The total runoff volume is therefore divided into different fractions 
based on return periods using the Three Points Approach. 
The results are calculated with the “ILCD recommended LCIA method” using EASETECH. 
They are normalised at midpoint level to represent the physical changes in different environmental 
categories and analysed for sensitivity of significant parameters. The “green” scenario has 20% to 
more than 2000% lower impacts than the “grey” scenario in 8 out of 11 impact categories. In both 
cases, the most significant life cycle stages are material generation, transport and disposal. The 
impacts of both scenarios are mainly influenced by a few key parameters: disposal of organic mass, 
disposal of organic mass, and production of cobblestones and gravel for the “green” scenario, and 
production of reinforced concrete, bitumen, and asphalt for the “grey” scenario. This highlights the 
possibility to influence the environmental performance of both systems by design choices.  
Allocating the impacts to the different functional units mirrors the difference in design 
approach: While most impacts of the “green” scenario arise in connection with handling everyday 
rainfall, the impacts of the “grey” scenario are dominated by management of extreme events.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Climate change as a global challenge 
In its latest synthesis report, the UN’s Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) 
states that global warming is unequivocal: “The atmosphere and ocean have warmed, the amounts of 
snow and ice have diminished, and sea level has risen” (IPCC 2014a).  Other associated effects 
include an increase in precipitation, acidification of oceans and regionally increased risk of flooding, 
caused by anthropogenic emission of greenhouse gases. Further emissions will lead to even higher 
temperatures, possibly causing irreversible impacts on ecosystems (IPCC 2014a). The World Health 
Organisation predicts severe effects for human health, expecting an additional 65,000 additional 
deaths in elderly people due to heat alone (WHO 2014). Economic losses are growing steadily: The 
Munich Climate Insurance Initiative states that weather related events relevant for losses have been 
continually growing since the 1980s. While losses are still highest in developed countries, the average 
annual economic damages from 2007 to 2011 in low and lower middle income countries now add up 
to US$ 1.3 bn and US$ 6.8 bn respectively (MCII 2012). 
The health-related, economic and environmental consequences are often most visible during 
extreme events, which have and will further become more frequent and intense (Figure 1). The global 
warming causes changes in the water cycle, and the consequences vary strongly regionally. Droughts 
as well as extremely high precipitation events are expected to become more frequent in different parts 
of the world (IPCC 2008). For mid-latitude regions, an increase of frequency and intensity of extreme 
events is predicted, which adds to the risk of regional flooding. The effects are most severe in urban 
areas, where humans, assets and ecosystems alike are at risk (IPCC 2014b; IPCC 2014a; IPCC 
2008).

1.2 Climate change adaptation in Copenhagen 
While Denmark is not amongst the countries hit worst by a changing climate, there is still a 
need for adaptation to prevent severe damages from happening. 2 July 2011 marks a defining 
moment for Copenhagen citizens: The strongest rain event, both in intensity and amount, since the 
==PAGE:14==
 
14
beginning of systematic measurements in 1955 was recorded. In a period of approximately two hours 
only, 100mm to 150mm of accumulated precipitation fell over central Copenhagen (Korsholm et al. 
2014), which is more intense than a 1000-year event. The average total amount of rain for the month 
of July is 67mm, which is not even half of the amount of precipitation of this single even (Institut for 
Beredskabsevaluering 2012; Cappelen 2013). Large parts of the metropolitan area were affected by 
severe flooding: Roads were blocked, public transport was interrupted, and parts of Rigshospitalet, 
one of the city’s biggest hospitals, had to be closed down. Basements were flooded and supply lines 
damaged, which led to power outages in almost 10,000 households. In the inner city, many citizens 
were without heat or hot water for nearly a week (Institut for Beredskabsevaluering 2012; HOFOR & 
Københavns Kommune 2013a).  
Observations like these indicate that the effects of global climate change have already 
reached Copenhagen. Also, the severity is expected to increase further, resulting in a higher risk of 
flooding (Arnbjerg-Nielsen 2012). The IPCC defines different scenarios to predict the future 
development of the climate, of which the Danish Ministry of Climate and Energy recommends the use 
of the Special Report on Emissions Scenario (SRES) A1B. This recommendation came after the 
development of the Climate Change Adaptation Plan, so for that SRES A2 was used. Also, since the 
plan was written in 2011, it is based on the IPCC report of 2007 (Københavns Kommune 2011a), 
predicting a 3.4°C increase in global average surface temperature, and up to 51cm of sea level rise 
until the year 2100 (IPCC 2007). In the newest version of the IPCC report, even more severe changes 
are anticipated: For the Representative Pathway Concentration (RPC) 8.5 scenario, which is broadly 
comparable to SRES A2, a global mean surface temperature change of 3.7°C and a global mean sea 
level rise of 63cm is predicted until 2100 (IPCC 2014a). 
Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2012) forecasts a 30% increase in intensity of 10-year events and a 40% 
increase in intensity of 100-year events until 2100 in Denmark. Adaptive measures have to be taken to 
prevent severe damages. In this context, the Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan serves as a 
framework for implementation. The different hazardous aspects of climate change and their possible 
effects on the city are prioritised based on risk estimates, defined as the product of probability and 
costs of damages. The risk of flooding is judged to be unacceptably high even today, with 595ha 
flooded and an estimated damage of DKK 4.5 mn during a 100-year event. To decrease this risk, 
different measures are defined and prioritised (Københavns Kommune 2011a):  
1. Reduce the likelihood of events, e.g. through dikes, raised building elevation and local 
adaptation and stormwater management; 
2. Reduce the scale of events, e.g. through warning systems, watertight basements and 
increased retention volumes in public spaces; 
3. Reduce the vulnerability, e.g. through extensive basement utilisation and emergency 
preparedness measures. 
Based on the Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan in combination with detailed flood 
mapping, the Cloudburst Management Plan (CMP) further specifies methods, priorities and measures. 
The overall goal is for sewer surcharge to reach ground level only once every ten years and to have a 
==PAGE:15==
 
15
maximum of 10cm of water on the ground not more than every 100 years. The present sewer system 
is not capable to meet these objectives. Thus, the Copenhagen municipality suggests disconnecting 
storm water and daily rainwater from the sewer system and to discharge water to the sea via new flow 
routes during extreme events. The realization of this CMP is technically possible in 20 years, and is 
expected to cost DKK 3.8 bn at present-day prices (Københavns Kommune 2012). 
Besides meeting the flood safety targets, the plan also aim to increase the quality of urban life 
through green and blue elements in the city. The measures should be “representing an asset in 
themselves, regardless of the extent of the expected climate change” (Københavns Kommune 2011a). 
Underground pipes are only used when drainage at ground level is not sufficient to meet the targeted 
flood protection level (Københavns Kommune 2012). Various elements like green roads, central 
retention elements in green spaces and other local measures like green roofs are combined in a 
master plan. The costs and benefits, synergetic effects with other plans, flexibility and other 
parameters are evaluated (HOFOR & Københavns Kommune 2013a). This specification of the general 
CMP has been developed for all 26 sub-catchments in Copenhagen, which can be grouped into 7 
main catchments (Figure 2). In this thesis, two different stormwater management (SWM) solutions for 
the Nørrebro catchment are evaluated. This area was chosen because it is relatively small and there 
are many options for local drainage of rainwater and green infrastructure in the catchment. The 
catchment and CMP specifications are described in more detail in Section 2.

1.3 Assessment of environmental impacts of climate change adaptation plans 
There are numerous methods to support environmental management, ranging from risk 
assessments to environmental performance evaluation or auditing (ISO 2006b). Another approach is 
environmental impact assessment, for which (Morris & Therivel 2009) state that it allows to evaluate 
alternatives and their effects on individual aspects of the environment, e.g. climate or population, to 
support decision-making. For determining these effects, diverse methods, from checklists to 
mathematical models, exist. 
For all catchments in Copenhagen, an environmental assessment (Danish: “miljøvurdering”) 
has been carried out to evaluate effects of the proposed CMPs. These assessments are very general 
and only partly quantify consequences. For the Nørrebro catchment, the status and expected changes 
are described for three different categories: nature and surface water, groundwater and soil, and 
population and health. The findings are only partly quantitative, e.g. for Sortedams Sø, a rise in water 
level of up to 30cm and stormwater nutrient concentrations of 2 mg/L for nitrogen and 0.5 mg/L for 
phosphorus are expected when discharging stormwater to the lake during cloudbursts. However, 
==PAGE:17==
 
17
possible consequences are only described qualitatively: The increased nutrient loads could have 
adverse impacts on fishes and plants in the lake, the light conditions at the bottom of the lake could 
change and negatively impact the development of ground covering plants. It also states that the 
changing hydraulic conditions generally pose a risk of loss of biodiversity (Københavns Kommune 
2013). However, no holistic quantitative assessment of environmental impacts of the different 
proposed solutions has been carried out so far. This thesis aims to close this gap. 
1.4 Problem statement 
The existing SWM plans for the Nørrebro catchment put risk reduction and direct economic 
effects first and do not quantify the positive and negative environmental consequences. Depending on 
the combination and type of elements of the SWM solution, different environmental impacts arise. To 
get a holistic understanding of the effects of implementing the CMP in the Nørrebro catchment, the 
following questions are addressed in this thesis: 
1. How do existing studies use life cycle assessments (LCA) to quantify the environmental 
impacts of water technologies and SWM?  
2. What are the environmental impacts of a “green” and a “grey” SWM scenario for the 
Nørrebro catchment, using a LCA according to international standards (ISO 2006b; ISO 
2006a) to quantify these?  
3. How can a functional unit and system boundaries be defined to account for all 
environmental impacts? What methods can be chosen to quantify these impacts? 
4. What is the importance of wastewater treatment (WWT) processes regarding the impact of 
the “grey” SWM scenario? 
5. What are the most influential parameters in the system, and how does their variation 
influence the environmental impacts? 
6. How can the system design be adapted to decrease negative environmental impacts? 
The scope of the study excludes the following aspects:  
1. Changes in traffic due to building activities, changes in infrastructure and parking space 
availability, and subsequent environmental impact; 
2. Additional benefits of vegetation not considered in the standard LCA, i.e., carbon 
sequestration and shading effects; 
3. Recreational benefits and effects on human health and liveability; 
4. Interaction with neighbouring catchments; 
5. Impacts on freshwater depletion, as it is not planned to use runoff to replace drinking 
water. 
The LCA is not intended to support decision making, but to understand the impact of an 
existing plan in more depth, which could, but not necessarily will, have implications for the 
development of future plans.

2. Background 
2.1 “Green” vs. “grey” solutions 
There are numerous different options to manage stormwater in urban areas. Traditionally, 
stormwater is mixed with other wastewater in pipes that lead to a wastewater treatment (WWT) plant. 
The water is cleaned there before discharge to surface waters or groundwater. Traditional, or “grey” 
solutions aim to discharge the water in underground pipes most efficiently. In contrary to that, “green” 
solutions are designed to decrease the impervious area in urban environments and increase the 
retention volume, where rainwater percolates and is stored. Also, discharge of water, e.g. towards 
retention spaces, is mainly done above the surface, e.g. in channels. Roughly speaking, “green” 
solutions are volume capacity based: They can handle water up to a certain volume after which they 
“fail” and flooding occurs. “Grey” solutions, on the other hand, can constantly handle water up to a 
certain quantity and the excess water will cause flooding, i.e. they are flow capacity based. This 
means that both solutions “compete” partly for the same fraction of runoff volume. The efficiency of 
both options depends on many parameters, e.g. previous weather conditions and placement in the 
catchment, and influences the flood risk level. However, the relationship between environmental 
impacts and flood risk of different scenarios is not included in the field of study. 
This thesis evaluates the environmental impacts of a “green” and a “grey” SWM solution for 
the Nørrebro catchment to make the CMP comparable to a “status quo” solution. The CMP mainly 
uses “green” elements in combination with underground pipes to reach the desired flood security level. 
In the “grey” scenario, the existing sewer system is extended and additional basins are built to store 
stormwater runoff. This scenario is analysed by HOFOR & Rambøll (2015) mainly to economically 
benchmark the “green” solution. It is not planned to be implemented, and its specifications are partly 
not realistic (see Section 2.2.3). 
In both scenarios, the existing “grey” sewer system is maintained (Appendix 1.1). The 
operation and maintenance demands are not part of the analysis, as they are considered equal for 
both scenarios. As a response to the already unacceptably high flooding risk today (Section 1.2), both 
systems decouple 30% of the runoff that is currently being treated in the existing sewer system.  
2.2 Nørrebro catchment 
2.2.1 
Catchment characteristics 
The Nørrebro catchment has an area of 2.6km2 and consists of parts of the quarters Indre 
Nørrebro, Indre Østerbro and Indre By. It is characterised by very diverse urban structures, 
concentrated on a relatively small area. Substantial parts of the catchments are residential areas with 
different densities of development. Fælledparken, Fredens Park, and Amor Park provide large open 
green areas. The catchment is also home to part of the University Campus, and to one of the largest 
hospitals in the city, Rigshospitalet. The Southern part of the area is dominated by Sortedams Sø, 
==PAGE:20==
 
20
which was built as a drinking water reservoir, and is now one of the city’s main recreational 
destinations.

2.2.2 
 “Green” scenario: Cloudburst Management Plan for Nørrebro 
The governing principle of the CMP is to retain as much rainwater as possible in upstream 
areas, and to create robust flow paths from downstream areas to recipients. The target is to ensure a 
maximum of 10cm of water on the surface during a 100-year event as projected for the year 2100

implementing flood-defence measures to protect their own property, e.g., by installing backflow valves 
(Københavns Kommune 2012). The solutions should be multifunctional, and provide a value during 
dry periods by increasing green areas in the city, too. This is achieved by utilizing five different 
elements (HOFOR & Københavns Kommune 2013a): 
 
Cloudburst roads, which discharge rainwater in a controlled way; 
 
Central retention elements, which delay large volumes of rainwater; 
 
Retention roads, which delay road and roof runoff; 
 
Green roads, which integrate green strips, islands or parks into the road; 
 
Local drainage of rainwater, which includes green roofs and areas where rainwater can be 
collected. 
The elements are described in detail in Appendix 2. 
For the Nørrebro catchment, different master plans (MP) have been developed (HOFOR & 
Københavns Kommune 2013a). The newest version is MP 4 (Figure 4), which will be assessed as the 
“green” scenario in this thesis. It focuses on using the large existing park areas for retention of 
rainwater. Main roads are transformed to cloudburst roads, and adjacent roads are being remodelled 
as green roads for retention of runoff from rain events up to a return period of 10 years. During heavier 
rain events, the water is discharged to the cloudburst roads or, if available, to green areas locally. The 
single elements of the MP are described in Appendix 3. 
The runoff is discharged to Sortedams Sø. There is a theoretical buffer volume of 36,000 m3 
available for cloudburst events, but the lake receives water also from other areas with a total 
catchment size of 12ha. The planning of measures in the single basins should therefore also be 
conducted in coordination with the surrounding catchments. However, this analysis goes beyond the 
scope of this thesis – thus, interdependencies are not included in the analysis. Furthermore, there are 
water quality standards for the lakes that have to be met. The CMP states that water should be 
cleaned using sustainable technologies. Also, only water from streets carrying less than 5,000 
cars/day should be discharged, while other runoff should be handled in the conventional sewer 
system. However, the CMP does not clearly specify how these targets could be achieved, but 
assumes that 95-99% of the annual mean rainfall is treated before discharge.  The purification 
happens by percolation through a gravel drainage layer. A report by Rambøll (2013) concludes that 
the concentrations of chlorophyll and phosphorous will not reach the thresholds (0.021mg/l for 
chlorophyll and 0.96mg/l for nitrogen) even during summer time and after cloudburst events. The 
concentration of phosphorous does exceed the threshold (0.07mg/l) temporarily after extreme events, 
which is not considered to have long-term negative effects.

3. Previous work 
3.1 Life Cycle Assessment in general 
LCA “is a structured, comprehensive and internationally standardised method. It quantifies all 
relevant emissions and resources consumed and the related environmental and health impacts and 
resource depletion issues that are associated with any goods or services (“products”)” (JRC 2010b). 
All processes of the life cycle from cradle to grave are considered, including everything from raw 
material excavation through construction and use to disposal. “Shifting of burdens”, i.e. creating new 
environmental problems while solving another, is avoided (JRC 2010b). The assessment follows the 
ISO 14040 and 14044 standards and the International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) 
handbook of the European Commission.  
A LCA is divided in four phases: 1. goal and scope definition; 2. inventory analysis; 3. impact 
assessment; 4. interpretation (ISO 2006a; ISO 2006b). LCA is an iterative approach and results of 
individual phases are reconsidered in the process of the assessment.  
The goal definition forms the basis of the assessment by defining the object and purpose of 
the assessment. From that, the scope of the LCA is derived, specifying the requirements, boundaries 
and limitations. The width and depth of the LCA can vary, and comparison to other systems is 
optional. The functional unit and reference flow, which determines the considered processes and 
system boundaries, are defined in this initial stage. 
In the second phase, a life cycle inventory (LCI) modelling principle is selected. This choice 
determines the method approach, e.g., for handling of multifunctional process and definition of cut-off 
criteria. LCI data are collected to quantify the relevant inputs and outputs, e.g. material demands, 
emissions and energy consumption (ISO 2006a). The data quality demands are derived from the 
chosen LCI modelling approach, and the definition of foreground and background system (JRC 
2010b). Different databases, tools and software exist to facilitate modelling of the LCI. 
Thirdly, the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) phase provides additional information and 
clarifies the environmental importance of the LCI outcomes. Depending on the LCIA method, the 
results are aggregated in different impact categories, which can then be normalised and weighted to 
enable comparability (JRC 2010b; ISO 2006b).  
Finally, the results can be used for product improvement, product comparison, 
communication, decision support and accounting (JRC 2010b).  
3.2 LCA in the context of stormwater management 
The range of products and services for which LCA has been applied is very wide and growing 
steadily. Water technology and management are relatively new topics in connection with LCA. Urban 
water systems are very complex and consist of numerous different elements fulfilling different 
functions: water sources (e.g. well fields), water distribution and collection (e.g. pipes), treatment 
plants and flood protection elements (e.g. reservoirs).

Most SWM studies compare different scenarios or technologies, with the number of scenarios 
varying between two (Spatari et al. 2011) and up to seven alternatives (Taylor & Barrett 2008). The 
assessment is often limited to single installations or technologies (Kirk 2006; Kosareo & Ries 2007; 
Taylor & Barrett 2008). More rarely, a whole SWM system is assessed (De Sousa et al. 2012; Spatari 
et al. 2011). Loubet et al. (2014) determine a similar trend for LCA of DWS and WWS and state that 
while only 24 papers take into account the technological urban water system, more than 100 studies 
assess the impacts of single plants. They even conclude that no studies so far have considered urban 
water systems as a combination of technology, users and resources. However, most recent studies 
show more comprehensive approaches, e.g., Rygaard et al. (2014) assess different options for water 
supply in a newly developed neighbourhood, taking into account user preferences, health risks, 
impacts on freshwater resources, and technological and economic feasibility.  
Loubet et al. (2014) identify three different types of functional units used in LCA of urban water 
systems, which can be expressed in different units: treatment and provision of a volume of water (in 
m3), treatment and provision of water per capita (in capita/year), and treatment and provision of water 
for a geographical area (in city/year). They also promote a new approach, and propose the application 
of a territorial approach introduced for land use scenarios by Loiseau et al. (2013). Instead of focusing 
on a technology or process, a territory is defined as the reference flow of the LCA. Based on that, 
multiple functional units are defined, which allows taking into consideration land use functions 
==PAGE:28==
 
28
provided to different stakeholders. This definition has partly been applied in LCA in SWM, e.g., by 
defining a drainage area as the functional unit (De Sousa et al. 2012; Flynn 2011; Spatari et al. 2011). 
Both attributional and consequential modelling can be found in LCA in SWM, though often not 
explicitly stated in the papers. In (ISO 2006a), the two different approaches are described as follows: 
 
Attributional modelling: The actual, forecasted or average system is assessed including all 
relevant processes and background processes. 
 
Consequential modelling: The effects of changes in the foreground system on the 
background and other system are taken into consideration and therefor a hypothetic, 
instead of an actual system is used. 
Since the water systems are mainly unlikely to affect the market in a large scale, an 
attributional approach is usually chosen (Taylor & Barrett 2008; Andrew & Vesely 2008). Spatari et al. 
(2011) name changes in hydrological processes as the reason for a consequential LCA.  
Most researchers utilize a combination of data from general databases (e.g., ecoinvent, PE 
International database, US Input Output), specific databases (e.g. standards and guidelines by 
departments), literature, and site-specific data.  
The number of impact categories taken into account varies significantly between the different 
studies. Two different classes of impact categories can be distinguished (JRC 2010b; JRC 2010a): 
 
Midpoint impact categories: Typically, around ten categories representing precisely the 
results from the LCI ; 
 
Endpoint impact categories: Usually three areas of protection (human health, natural 
environment, natural resources), in which the midpoint results are aggregated. 
(Loubet et al. 2014) find that some studies only use a single midpoint impact category to 
express life cycle impacts. Other studies make use of different midpoint impact categories provided by 
the LCI method chosen, e.g. (Kirk 2006; Wang et al. 2013). Only few studies aggregate results in 
endpoint categories (Kosareo & Ries 2007; Godskesen et al. 2013). 
The LCA method allows assessing environmental impacts over a wide range of categories, 
however, there are certain limitations in connection with LCA of water systems. Lerer et al. (2014), 
who analyse different tools and methods to evaluate water sensitive urban design, state that while 
LCA addresses different biophysical and human aspects, it can only be used to quantify non-flow 
related impacts. However, there are approaches to extend LCA to include these impacts: Loubet et al. 
(2014) name water use impact as a factor which is growingly assessed in LCA studies, e.g., by 
Godskesen et al. (2013), who assess the changes in freshwater withdrawal based on a water stress 
index and then apply a characterization, normalization and weighting factor to facilitate integration into 
the LCA. LCAs of SWM scenarios can also include changes in run-off quantity and quality and the 
consequences on waste water treatment requirements (Spatari et al. 2011; Flynn & Traver 2013; 
Kosareo & Ries 2007). Other tools can be combined with LCA to assess flow-related impacts, and 
recreational and urban forestry benefits. For example, Flynn & Traver (2013) include carbon storage 
and sequestration by plants with the U.S. Department of Agriculture Forest Service i-Tree tool and 
==PAGE:29==
 
29
then account for the avoided global warming potential using the EPA’s Greenhouse Gas 
Equivalencies Calculator. Social impacts are not included in any of the reviewed papers.  
Depending on the functional unit and the assessed impact categories, different hydrological 
models are chosen ranging from hydrologic and hydraulic models for the whole catchment (De Sousa 
et al. 2012) to more simplified models only using one or multiple runoff coefficients as system 
parameters (Spatari et al. 2011; Kosareo & Ries 2007). Runoff quality alterations are often assessed 
using averaged factors (Taylor & Barrett 2008; Wang et al. 2013).

4. Methods 
4.1 Life Cycle Assessment 
The environmental impacts of two different SWM scenarios are assessed with a LCA 
according to international standards (ISO 2006b; ISO 2006a). The scenarios use a combination of 
different elements distributed over the Nørrebro catchment area (Sections 2.2.2 and 2.2.3) 
The full life cycle is considered and divided into the following stages: 
 
Materials: material generation and manufacturing of basic elements, e.g. pipes; 
 
Transport: transport from the material generation and manufacturing sites to the project 
area; 
 
Construction: implementation of the sub-projects, where only excavation demands and 
consecutive disposal of soil are considered; 
 
Operation: cleaning and maintenance of the single elements, e.g. mowing of grass areas; 
 
Decommissioning: dismantling of the single elements, where only excavation is 
considered; 
 
Disposal: end-of-life of the different material fractions, e.g. concrete. 
The foreground and background elements of the system are defined using a specificity 
perspective, as described in Table 4. The foreground system is defined as the urban water system in 
the area, including flood protection elements and the sewer system, and also the WWT plant outside 
of the catchment. The background system includes among others energy supply, and transport of 
material in- and outside of the area. Large-scale changes in the background system are assumed to 
not affect the results of the LCA significantly. Shifts in the energy market are expected, as the Danish 
Energy Agency projects a step-by-step substitution of non-renewable by renewable energy sources 
(Danish Energy Agency 2014). This change is however only partially relevant for the LCA, as some 
materials are not produced in Denmark and electricity and energy consumption are not main 
processes of the system. Consequently, an attributional approach is chosen. This approach makes 
use of “historical, fact-based, measureable data of known (or at least knowable) uncertainty, and 
includes all the processes that are identified to relevantly contribute to the system being studied” (JRC 
2010b). According to the ILCD handbook, technology-, and site-specific data is used for modelling the 
foreground system, while background processes can be represented by market-averaged data (JRC 
2010b).

4.2 Three Points Approach 
The Three Points Approach (3PA) is used for quantifying the amounts of rainwater the system 
has to handle and consequently defining the functions of the system. (Fratini et al. 2012) introduced 
the three points approach to facilitate structured decision-making in urban flood risk planning, and 
defined three domains of SWM:  
1. Domain of technical optimization of water systems, where rain events with medium return 
periods are considered and managed by implementing technical solutions; 
2. Domain of urban resilience and spatial planning, where events with larger return periods 
that cause greater damage are handled; 
3. Domain of day-to-day events, where everyday wastewater and stormwater is transported 
and treated. 
==PAGE:33==
 
33
Sørup et al. (2012) enhance this approach to quantify the efficiency of different SWM options. 
As efficiencies based on total rainwater volumes can be misleading, they state it is necessary to 
evaluate the performance of SWM scenarios in case of extreme events as well. They propose the 
definition of three domains, following the approach of Fratini et al. (2012), based on rainfall return 
periods (Figure 7): 
A. Everyday domain or rainwater resource utilisation, where events with a low return period 
are handled by the urban drainage system 
B. Design domain or urban stormwater drainage, where events with larger return periods are 
managed by the stormwater infrastructure; 
C. Extreme domain or pluvial flood mitigation, where extreme events with very large return 
periods exceed the design capacity of the sewer system and floods occur.

5.2 System boundaries 
The different functional units described above use different quantities of water, which pass 
through the SWM system in different ways. The boundaries and “flow paths” are described in the 
following paragraphs, and are visualized in Figure 8 and Figure 9. 
Only the amount of rain falling in the catchment area is considered, i.e. inflows from 
surrounding catchments are neglected. A yearly average of 700mm in present conditions is assumed, 
which is expected to increase by 30% until 2110 (based on Arnbjerg-Nielsen 2012). A uniform runoff 
factor of 0.57 is estimated for the whole catchment area and for both scenarios, which does not take 
into account the changes in runoff coefficient due to the changes in surface type after implementing 
the CMP. This leads to an annual runoff of 400mm in present conditions, which will respectively 
increase by 30% until 2110. The rest of the water either evaporates or infiltrates to the ground water. 
The interdependencies between this process and the flow path of the runoff are not considered. Only 
the processes “after” runoff formation are analysed in this thesis and are marked by a box in Figure 7 
and Figure 9. This is a simplification that does not take into account holistic changes in the water 
balance, which have implications on ecosystems and humans. The “green” solution establishes a 
more natural water balance and these benefits are not taken into account here.  
==PAGE:36==
 
36
The “green” scenario is designed to reduce the inflow to the sewer by 30% (Københavns 
Kommune 2011a). This means that the “green” elements have to handle 30% of the current runoff 
besides the additional runoff volume caused by climate change, which adds up to a total volume of 
46,800,000m3 over 100 years. The “grey” scenario is designed accordingly to ensure comparability 
and therefore handles the same amount of water. 
The total volume of water can be divided into three domains, following the 3PA  (Section 4.2). 
Depending on this allocation, the water is managed by different parts of the system: While some 
elements are designed to handle everyday rain (domain A), others are only used during extreme 
events (domain B and C). The uncertainties resulting from these assumptions are discussed in 
Section Error! Reference source not found.. 
In the “green” system, water from domain A events is assumed to be completely managed by 
planted and permeably paved areas before partially being discharged sub-surface to the drainage 
areas and partially evaporating or percolating to the groundwater body. It is assumed that 80% of the 
water from vegetated areas and 100% of the water from permeable paved areas is discharged to the 
drainage areas. Water from domain B and C events is considered to be collected in channels and 
pipes, where it is led to lowerings and then to drainage areas. There, the runoff is treated by 
percolating through a gravel layer before it is discharged to Sortedams Sø, from where it flows to the 
harbour.  
All runoff in the “grey” system is considered to flow through the pipe system. The water from 
domain A events is directly led to the WWTP, where it is treated before discharge to the harbour. The 
runoff from domain B events is stored in retention basins before being led to the WWTP, where 25% is 
treated while 75% is discharged to the harbour without treatment. During a domain C event, the water 
is discharged from the pipes to the harbour without passing through the basins or the WWTP, as 
described in Section 2.2.3.

5.3 Temporal scope 
The definition of the temporal scope is crucial for assessing maintenance and renewal 
demands. The CMP is designed for the year 2110, therefore the time scope of the LCA covers the 
years 2010 to 2110. The different elements of the CMP will be established step-by-step according to a 
priority ranking and a total implementation time of 20 years is expected (Københavns Kommune 
2012). Parts of the measures will be in operation phase, while others are still under construction. 
There are continuous maintenance demands in the “green” system, e.g. replanting of grass after 
heavy rainfalls, or renewal of road areas. For both systems, discontinuous demands exist, e.g. 
renewal of roads, which is only carried out every 30 years. These renewal demands are not allocated 
to the “operation” phase, but are included in the corresponding life cycle stages. For example, the 
asphalt layer, which has to be renewed, leads to increased material, transport, construction and 
decommissioning (excavation) and disposal demands, which are accumulated and modelled at the 
beginning and end of the time period respectively. It does not influence the operation phase, which 
includes only processes independent of lifetime assumptions, e.g. cleaning of streets and mowing of 
vegetated areas (Section 6.1.1). Operation and maintenance is assumed to go on over the whole 
period (Figure 10). Inter-annual variation in maintenance demands is taken into account.

6. Results and discussion 
6.1 Life Cycle Inventory 
6.1.1 
System processes 
In the assessment of SWM scenarios of the Nørrebro catchment, all LC stages from raw 
material extraction to decommissioning with the corresponding processes are considered (Figure 14). 
Most processes are modelled using the ecoinvent database (Weidema, Bauer, Hirschier, et al. 2013). 
As there are no datasets available specifically for Denmark, global data or, whenever possible, data 
for Europe is selected. For some disposal and maintenance procedures, processes provided in 
EASETECH were adapted. 
For the construction phase, only the excavation process and the consequential disposal of soil 
are considered. No other use of machinery is included in the model as the resulting impacts of the 
other associated processes are assumed to be marginal. The same assumption applies to the 
decommissioning stage. For planted areas, sowing and planting demands are considered. 
Only transport from the material manufacturing site to the project site is considered. Transport 
from the construction site to disposal is neglected. The distances are estimated to be only 10-20% of 
the distance from manufacturing to construction. 
The disposal of most materials is modelled using ecoinvent waste treatment processes. Soil 
and stones are modelled using only the construction and operation steps of landfills. No leachate or 
carbon storage processes are included in the model. This is based on the assumption that in fact the 
material would be used for other construction projects, e.g., noise barriers next to motorways, and not 
being landfilled. Therefore, it is irrelevant for the impacts if the material is stored inside or outside the 
project area.

7. Methodological challenges and uncertainties for future research 
LCA is a relative and iterative approach, and the single steps can always be revisited and 
refined to improve comprehensiveness and consistency (ISO 2006a). This simultaneously results in 
uncertainties and the necessity for assumptions at every point of the process.  
The choice between “green” and “grey” scenario does not only change material, maintenance 
and disposal demands, but also the water balance in the system. The shift towards a more natural 
water balance in the “green” scenario has benefits for both the ecosystem and humans, which are not 
accounted for in this thesis. As they are partly subjective, they are difficult to assess. The research in 
this field is still limited and therefore it is difficult to estimate the significance of these impacts in the 
considered systems. Also, other impacts not assessed in a standard LCA are not included, e.g. 
sequestration and storage of carbon. Other researchers found these impacts to be significant, e.g. 
Flynn & Traver (2013) state that due to urban forest benefits offset the environmental impacts from 
construction of a rain garden after only four years. Further work and the integration of additional tools 
would be necessary to quantify these impacts for the considered systems. 
The system design defines the type, number and dimensions of the single elements. This 
design is not yet implemented and is only preliminary in parts, which leads to necessary 
simplifications. The configuration assessed is based on the planning documents of HOFOR & Rambøll 
(2015), which differs to some extend from the design description in the CMP. In LCA of already 
implemented systems, for which measured, site-specific data is available, the uncertainties of results 
are reduced significantly. Additionally, the “grey” system is not designed to be implemented, but only 
to function as a baseline for the CMP. The elements are dimensioned to handle the same amount of 
water as the “green” system in the same areas, which leads to an unrealistically high number of 
basins. By optimization of the system, the demand for reinforced concrete and the subsequent 
environmental impacts are reduced significantly, as shown in Section 6.3.4.  
Simplifications are made regarding the lifetime of the green elements: No complete renewal of 
the vegetated and drainage area is included in the model. This is necessary due to contamination of 
soil. No projections for discharge of toxic compounds to soil are available for the area and the lifetime 
is therefore associated with a high degree of uncertainty. More research regarding the concentrations 
of the different runoff fractions and the removal efficiencies in the soil is needed to define a lifetime for 
these elements. The impacts of complete renewal are not expected to be major, as production of 
seeds and tree seedlings, transport and planting together only account for less than 6% in all impact 
categories. However, the impacts on disposed organic mass have to be assessed in more detail, as 
this LC stage has a high relative significance on climate change, terrestrial acidification and terrestrial 
eutrophication impacts. The disposal processes in general are associated with a high degree of 
uncertainty, as it is difficult to predict development of technologies and treatment processes for the 
year 2110. The model therefore uses present technologies and more research regarding future 
options is needed.

To take into account the multiple functions the SWM systems provide, three different 
functional units are defined in this thesis based on the reference flow, which is the catchment area. 
Only one functional unit would not take into account the different flood safety levels for different rain 
domains. Also, this approach allows dividing the flow of water through the system into different 
compartments: the runoff of daily and extreme events goes through the system in different ways and is 
handled by different elements (Section 5.2). Different assumptions are made when defining the flow 
paths: 
 
The same runoff coefficient is considered for both scenarios, which is not realistic as in the 
“green” scenario the permeable area is increased significantly.  
 
No inflows from other catchments are considered. This is both relevant for the flow in the 
catchment and for the impacts on Sortedams Sø, which receives water from a total 
catchment area of 12ha. 
 
It is assumed that all runoff from everyday events goes through the drainage area of the 
“green” system, where it is treated before discharge to Sortedams Sø. A more rational 
approach would be that the runoff is only delayed in the vegetated and permeable paved 
areas. It then flows to the sewer system, where it is discharged to the harbour after WWT. 
 
25% of the runoff from domain B is considered to be treated at the WWTP in the default 
“grey” scenario. This fraction could in fact vary and be even reduced to 0%. 
More comprehensive analysis of the complete water balance, including the capacities of the 
existing sewer system, is needed to assess the impacts of these assumptions. This would also allow 
quantifying discharge of harmful substances to receiving waters. This parameter is not included in the 
LCA, even though the impacts are expected to be significant. While there are reference values 
available for different “grey” treatment options, which are used to exemplarily quantify discharge of 
copper (Section 6.3.2), no data for the “green” scenario is available. The emission of heavy metals 
and other toxic compounds to surface water and soil in the “green” system is significant and the 
resulting effects on impacts, e.g. due to soil treatment processes, are expected to be important. For 
the “grey” system, it is anticipated that including this parameter would increase the differences in 
impacts when comparing the different WWT options, as they have different treatment efficiencies.

8. Conclusions 
In this thesis, the environmental impacts of two different SWM scnearios are assessed, for 
which LCA proofs to be a useful tool. To enhance the different functions a SWM system provides, the 
catchment area of 2.6km2 is defined as the reference flow and three different functional units are 
derived based on the Three Points Approach, which divides the rainfall in an everyday, a design and 
an extreme domain. This is an approach not found in previous work. The use of EASETECH to model 
the system shows to be advantageous when using a reference flow, as it is based on a material flow 
through the system, to which the impacts in the different LC stages are related.  
The “ILCD recommended LCIA method” is used to calculate normalized impacts at midpoint 
level. The “green” scenario has lower impacts in 8 out of 11 impact categories, ranging from 
47PE/2.6km2 to 37,250 PE/2.6km2. The impacts of the “grey” scenario range from 127PE/2.6km2 to 
28,060PE/2.6km2. Toxicity categories, depletion of abiotic resources (fossil) and particulate matter are 
not analysed further as some are assumed to overestimate the actual impacts.  
In both scenarios, construction, operation and decommissioning account for less than 3% of 
the impacts. Material generation, transport and disposal dominate the results. The defining parameters 
of the “green” scenario are disposal of organic mass from vegetated areas, production of concrete for 
channels, and production of gravel and cobblestones for paved areas. The latter has the highest 
relative impact. High variations are possible for the disposal mass of the vegetated area due to 
different potential end-of-life scenarios, which makes it an important parameter even though the 
relative sensitivity is low. The same applies to the mass of concrete for channels, which can be directly 
influenced by the system design and is a relevant driver when optimizing the system regarding 
environmental impacts. 
The results of the “grey” scenario are mainly influenced by production of reinforced concrete 
for basins, where steel only accounts for 3% of the mass, but contributes two thirds of the impacts. 
Even though the relative change is low, the impacts of the scenario can be influenced to a large 
degree by optimizing this parameter: If fewer, bigger basins instead are built, the impacts can be 
reduced by up to 70% for some impact categories. The second biggest contributor in the “grey” 
system is production of bitumen and asphalt for roads. Also here, the system design can be modified 
to minimize the negative impacts even though the total road area is only 0.5% of the catchment area, 
as the relative changes are high for this parameter. The choice of wastewater treatment option does 
not significantly influence the results, which is expected to change when impacts of discharging 
harmful substances to receiving water is included. 
The analysis of impacts in relation to the functional units shows significant differences for the 
“green” and the “grey” scenario: The majority of impacts in the “green” scenario arises from handling 
everyday runoff. Opposed to that, management of runoff from 10-year events contributes most to the 
“grey” impacts. The prevention of more than 10cm of water on the surface during 100-year events, 
only contributes insignificantly in both scenarios (up to 5% of the “green” and up to 1% of the “grey” 
impacts). This reflects the different design approach of the scenarios: While “green” SWM systems are 
==PAGE:64==
 
64
mainly designed to handle everyday rain and reduce runoff, “grey” systems are optimized for flood 
protection during extreme events. Both systems are not designed to handle all runoff during 100-year 
events, but only to limit flooding, which is done in the same elements that are also utilized for 10-year 
events. 
As the CMP is still in the planning phase, significant data gaps were found. Emission of 
harmful substances to soil and surface water is not considered. Still, the calculated results, which are 
tested for sensitivity, are considered to give a good indication of the impacts of the different SWM 
scenarios. Some impacts, e.g. recreational impacts or changes in the water balance, cannot be 
accounted for in the standard LCA framework. Research is still limited in this area and further work is 
needed to quantify the influence of aspects that are not included in the standard LCA.

A1.2 Traffic conditions 
Six main roads run through the catchment: Nørre Allé, Tagensvej, Øster Søgade, 
Blegdamsvej, Jagtvej, and Nørrebrogade. The latter has been transformed in 2008 by limiting the 
capacity for cars, while the others are characterized by heavy traffic with frequent traffic jams during 
rush hours. Bike paths or lanes have been installed at all main roads, and Nørrebrogade is one of the 
most frequented bike roads with approximately 36,000 cyclists per day in 2012 (HOFOR & 
Københavns Kommune 2013b). The sidewalks in the area are generally designed to accommodate 
pedestrian traffic, but cannot handle larger customer turnouts, e.g. in front of restaurants or shops. 
A1.3 Existing urban planning 
There are several other plans in place for the area, with which the CMP aims to create 
synergies. The most important ones are: 
 
The development of a new Metro line including three new stations in the catchment 
(Trianglen, Vibenhus and Nørrebros Runddel); 
 
The expansion of Rigshospitalet by a new building, where wastewater and rainwater will 
be separated whenever possible; 
 
The expansion of Nørre Campus by a new park area connecting the buildings of the 
Panum Institute, and new buildings for the Niels Bohr Science Park, which will all utilize 
local drainage measures and green roofs; 
 
The transformation of the Sølund Plejecenter, which will create a new green area adjacent 
to Sortedams Sø, and green roofs and roof gardens will be installed. 
A1.4 Hydrological conditions  
Figure A 2 shows the flood depths during a 100-year event in present conditions based on a 
hydraulic model coupled with a model of the existing sewer system. Additional hydraulic calculations 
and terrain analysis have been carried out to understand the hydraulic conditions in the catchment. 
Also, surveys have been conducted to assess the extend of damage observed by residents in the 
area, and the findings are in line with the modelled results (HOFOR & Københavns Kommune 2013a).  
The model predicts severe flooding in Fredens Park (1) and the surrounding area, which is a 
natural low point of the catchment. Especially Ryesgade (2), up to Helgesensvej, is affected during 
extreme events, when water flows towards the area via Nørre Alle (3), Fælledparken (4) and the area 
around Ravnsborggade (5). This increases the risk of flooding at Rigshospitalet (6). The catchment 
has a natural decline towards Sortedams Sø (7). From Jagtvej (8), which is the highest area of the 
catchment, water flows towards Vibenhus Runddel (9) and Mimersgade (10). There is another local 
peak at Panum Institute (11) (Figure A 2). In present conditions, the water is discharged to the main 
underground pipe at Blegdamsvej, which leads to the pumping stations in Præstøgade and 
Kløvermarken.

Appendix 2: Elements of the Cloudburst Management Plan 
Cloudburst roads (Danish: “skybrudsveje”) utilize the natural slope to discharge rainwater in a 
V profile or on the street, restricted by elevated curbs. Alternatively, trenches or troughs that act as 
urban creeks or green ribbons can be established at the side of the road. Another variation is the 
installation of hollow sidewalks, where the water flows under the surface. These measures sometimes 
have to be complemented by underground pipes. 
In the CMP for the Nørrebro catchment, cloudburst roads are often implemented in 
combination with retention elements (Danish: “forsinkelsesveje”). Retention roads typically lie 
upstream from a vulnerable point and can hold back a certain volume of water before flooding occurs. 
When cloudburst roads are combined with retention elements, different flow conditions for rain events 
with different intensities are expected. While for every-day events, all water can be retained 
underground, during cloudbursts water flows in lowered areas, which then work as channels (Figure A 
3).  
 
 
Figure A 3: Flow conditions at a combined cloudburst and retention road at Jagtvej for a (a) daily precipitation and (b) 
cloudburst event (source: HOFOR & Københavns Kommune (2013a)) 
Cloudburst roads and retention roads both are implemented as green roads (Danish: “grønne 
veje”). Green roads can also be established independently, but decline towards cloudburst roads then. 
They are designed to retain the total water volume of events up to a return period of 10 years, which 
reduces the risk of flooding. Also, daily rainwater is decoupled from the existing sewer system, which 
makes extensions in adaptation to climate change unnecessary. Green roads can be implemented in 
numerous different versions, as can be seen by the example of Kartoffelrækkerne (Figure A 4).  
 
 
Figure A 4: Three different implementation versions of a green road at Kartoffelrækkerne (source: HOFOR & 
Københavns Kommune (2013a))

existing green areas, where water can be stored above or below the surface. They are usually

Appendix 4: Literature review of LCA in SWM 
A4.1 Introduction  
Loubet et al. (2014), who review 26 papers on LCA in water technologies, state that the first 
LCA studies for water systems were carried out in the late 1990s, focusing mainly on wastewater 
treatment and drinking water production. Since 2005, there has been a increase in research, now also 
considering drinking water distribution and wastewater collection. The majority of papers focus on 
assessment of single plants instead of comprehensive urban water systems. While research on 
stormwater management (SWM) and urban systems for flood prevention is also expanding, it is still 
not as developed as work focusing on water supply and treatment. The existing literature is very 
diverse regarding everything from system definition to selection of methods. In the following sections, 
a range of papers is being reviewed and compared with the aim to show possible interpretations and 
applications of LCA in connection with water systems. The review approach follows Loubet et al. 
(2014) and covers the four phases of LCA defined in the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 standards. The 
focus lies on studies on LCA for SWM, which have been found through Web of Science using a 
combination of different keywords (Figure A 7). The research on LCA in SWM is still very limited, and 
only 8 papers (Table A 2) were found to be relevant for this thesis. The discussion is complemented 
by including research on drinking water systems (DWS) and wastewater systems (WWS) and the 
review of Loubet et al. (2014).

A4.2 Goal and scope definition 
The first phase of the LCA, the goal and scope definition, is decisive for the next steps and 
results of the study (JRC 2010b). Very different approaches have been chosen already at this first 
stage by different researchers. However, most SWM studies compare different scenarios or 
technologies. Rarely, only “green” solutions are considered (Flynn 2011; Kirk 2006), but usually a 
comparison with “grey” solutions is drawn (De Sousa et al. 2012; Kosareo & Ries 2007; Spatari et al. 
2011). The number of scenarios varies greatly, starting with only 2 alternatives, e.g. the comparison 
between conventional street design and a scenario with a combination of permeable pavement and 
trees (Spatari et al. 2011). Other researchers compare more different scenarios, as done e.g. by 
Taylor & Barrett (2008), who compare seven single different installations with the same boundary 
conditions. 
The assessment is often limited to single installations or technologies, e.g. a green roof on a 
building (Kirk 2006; Kosareo & Ries 2007; Taylor & Barrett 2008). More seldom, a whole system is 
assessed: De Sousa et al. (2012) analyse strategies to reduce combined sewer overflows (CSOs) to 
the Bronx River from a catchment of 784 ha; and Spatari et al. (2011) assess the impacts of different 
road designs in a neighbourhood block. Loubet et al. (2014) determine a similar trend for LCA of DWS 
and WWS and state that while only 24 papers take into account the technological urban water system, 
more than 100 studies assess the impacts of single plants. They even concluded that no studies so far 
have considered urban water systems as a combination of technology, users and resources. However, 
==PAGE:81==
 
81
most recent studies show more comprehensive approaches: For example, Rygaard et al. (2014) 
assess different options for water supply in a newly developed neighbourhood, taking into account 
user preferences, health risks, impacts on freshwater resources, and technological and economical 
feasibility.  
The functional unit, which is defined at the first stage of LCA, is crucial for the quality of the 
results as all inputs and outputs are related to that unit (ISO 2006a). Loubet et al. (2014) identify three 
different types of functional units used in LCA of urban water systems, which can be expressed in 
different units: treatment and provision of a volume of water (in m3), treatment and provision of water 
per capita (in capita/year), and treatment and provision of water for a geographical area (in city/year). 
Additionally, researchers have defined more specific functional units, e.g. the replacement of 1m3 of 
potable drinking-water using other sources (Godskesen et al. 2013; Godskesen et al. 2011). In SWM, 
even more diverse approaches have been chosen. One option is to define a drainage area (De Sousa 
et al. 2012; Flynn 2011; Spatari et al. 2011), or more specifically the treatment of storm water runoff 
from a drainage area (Kirk 2006). The functional unit is sometimes not defined explicitly, but is implied 
by the definition of the considered systems, e.g. infrastructure that can handle a specific amount of 
storm water runoff (Wang et al. 2013) or a specific roof area (Kosareo & Ries 2007).  
Loubet et al. (2014) promote a new approach, and propose the application of a territorial 
approach introduced for land use scenarios by Loiseau et al. (2013). Instead of focusing on a 
technology or process, a territory and its interactions with other territories are defined as the reference 
flow of the LCA. Based on that, multiple functional units are defined, which allows to take into 
consideration land use functions provided to different stakeholders, e.g. recreation or labour. The 
ILCD Handbook supports this approach by stating that if a single function can’t clearly be defined, a 
reference flow, also called “declared unit” can be chosen instead (JRC 2010b).  
The full life cycle consists of “raw material extraction and acquisition, through energy and 
material production and manufacturing, to use and end of life treatment and final disposal” (ISO 
2006a). While most researchers consider all life cycle stages, some papers don’t include 
decommissioning and disposal (De Sousa et al. 2012; Taylor & Barrett 2008; Wang et al. 2013). This 
stage is very problematic regarding the long lifetime of most technologies and the associated 
uncertainties regarding available technologies, disposal and recycling and other parameters at the end 
of life. The ILCD Handbook states that for lifetimes over five years, it is possible to integrate data on 
best available technology or most recent data, if fully time-representative data is not available. 
However, this is often not done in the reviewed papers. Loubet et al. (2014) also observe that the 
construction stage is often not included in the LCA.  
A4.3 Life cycle inventory modelling approach 
Both attributional and consequential modelling can be found in LCA in SWM, though often not 
explicitly stated in the papers. In (ISO 2006a), the two different approaches are described as follows: 
==PAGE:82==
 
82
 
Attributional modelling: The actual, forecasted or average system is assessed including all 
relevant processes and background processes; 
 
Consequential modelling: The effects of changes in the foreground system on the 
background and other system are taken into consideration and therefor a hypothetic, 
instead of an actual system is used. 
The definition of foreground and background system is crucial for determining the model 
approach. This is not addressed specifically in the ISO standards. The ILCD handbook (JRC 2010b) 
distinguishes between a specificity and a management perspective for defining the two different 
systems (Table A 3). The definition of the boundaries of the foreground system is therefore flexible, 
and so is the choice of modelling approach.

Since the systems are mainly unlikely to affect the market at a large scale, an attributional 
approach is usually chosen (Taylor & Barrett 2008; Andrew & Vesely 2008). De Sousa et al. (2012) 
analyse changes in the operation of wastewater treatment plants, caused by variations in run-off 
quality and quantity. This approach can also be seen at Kosareo & Ries (2007) and Flynn (2011), but 
still should be assigned to attributional modelling, as the WWT is part of the foreground system. Wang 
et al. (2013) and are explicitly stating a consequential approach, but also do not consider large-scale 
changes in background systems. Spatari et al. (2011) name changes in hydrological processes as the 
reason for a consequential LCA.  
The modelling approach defines the data requirements regarding representativeness, 
completeness and accuracy and to document these parameters. Attributional modelling should be 
based on “historical, fact-based, measureable data of known (or at least knowable) uncertainty” (JRC 
2010b). Most researchers utilize a combination of data from general databases (e.g. ecoinvent, PE 
International database, US Input Output), specific databases (e.g. standards and guidelines by 
departments) and literature. Site-specific data is either conducted from measurements, or, if a future 
scenario is assessed, from plans. Especially in SWM, many studies focus on the assessment of 
planned systems (De Sousa et al. 2012), or the comparison of an existing and a possible scenario 
(Andrew & Vesely 2008). In this case, measured data is usually very limited and sometimes not even 
available for comparable systems since many new technologies for SWM are being tested and 
implemented. Andrew & Vesely (2008) face this problem by proportionally reducing measured values 
and using measured data that is not site-specific, Flynn & Traver (2013) conduct interviews with 
professionals and Spatari et al. (2011) make wide use of averaged data, e.g. for transportation. The 
documentation of data sources and quality varies greatly in different studies, also for DWS and WWS, 
where measurements are more widely available usually and can also be used for the prognosis of 
future scenarios through extrapolation (Lundie et al. 2004).

A4.4 Life cycle impact assessment method 
Different methods for life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) exist, which affect the result 
categories of the assessment. Loubet et al. (2014) identify four different databases (CML-IA, Eco-
indicator 99, ReCiPe, EDIP) in 16 reviewed papers on DWS and WWS. Kosareo & Ries (2007) use 
Impact 2002+ in their assessment of green roofs and Niero et al. (2014) analyse wastewater treatment 
plants using ReCiPe and USEtox. While some studies make use of several methods (Renou et al. 
2008), some do not use any LCIA methodology. This is often the case if only one impact category is 
considered, e.g. energy consumption or global warming potential, and the results are only based on a 
single mass or energy flow. Other studies do not explicitly state which LCIA methodology is used (Del 
Borghi et al. 2013).  
The number of impact categories, in which the results of the LCIA are expressed, varies 
significantly in the different studies. Two different classes of impact categories can be distinguished 
(JRC 2010b; JRC 2010a): 
1. Midpoint impact categories: Typically around ten categories, which represent precisely the 
results from the LCI. 
2. Endpoint impact categories: Usually three areas of protection (human health, natural 
environment, natural resources), in which the midpoint results are aggregated. 
Loubet et al. (2014) find that some studies only use a single midpoint impact category to 
express life cycle impacts, which is not sufficient and should be avoided. Other studies make use of 
different midpoint impact categories defined by the LCI method chosen (Kirk 2006; Wang et al. 2013). 
Only few studies aggregate results in endpoint categories (Kosareo & Ries 2007; Godskesen et al. 
2013). 
The results can optionally be normalized, which is defined as “calculating the magnitude of 
category indicator results relative to reference information” (ISO 2006b). The basis is usually the total 
value for a specific area (e.g. national or global), or for an average person, each per year. 
Communication is facilitated by relating to a reference value, but does not enable comparison between 
midpoint results. Only after choosing a set of weighting factors reflecting the relative importance of the 
single categories can an evaluation like that be conducted (JRC 2010b). While several researchers 
choose to apply normalisation based on per capita contributions to impact categories (Kirk 2006; 
Godskesen et al. 2011; Rygaard et al. 2014); some follow different approaches, e.g., Kosareo & Ries 
(2007), who normalise the impacts of a green roof based on the impacts of a conventional roof. By far 
not all researchers apply normalisation in the LCIA and Loubet et al. (2014) conclude that only half of 
the papers reviewed include that step, and even fewer conduct weighting. Choosing a set of weighting 
factors is difficult and always subjective, as assumptions have to be made (JRC 2010b). Godskesen et 
al. (2013) choose weighting factors in accordance to the distance from current impact levels to 
politically set impact levels and Rygaard et al. (2014) use the PE LCIA Survey 2012 method, which is 
based on LCA expert estimates.

A4.5 Other impacts 
The LCA framework allows assessing environmental impacts over a wide range of categories, 
but certain limitations have been identified in connection with LCA of water systems. Lerer et al. 
(2014), who analyse different tools and methods to evaluate water sensitive urban design, state that 
while LCA addresses different bio-physical and human aspects, it can only be used to quantify non-
flow related impacts.  
However, various researchers include additional impact categories, often to include flow 
characteristics and water consumption (Table A 4). Loubet et al. (2014) name water use impacts as a 
factor which is growingly assessed in LCA studies, e.g. by Godskesen et al. (2013), who assess the 
changes in freshwater withdrawal based on a water stress index and then apply a characterization, 
normalization and weighting factor to facilitate integration into the LCA. Rygaard et al. (2014) follow 
this approach and complement it with the assessment of health risk for water users through 
microorganisms and public acceptance of different water supply solutions. Many LCAs of SWM 
scenarios also include changes in runoff quantity and quality and the consequences on waste water 
treatment requirements (Spatari et al. 2011; Flynn & Traver 2013; Kosareo & Ries 2007). The 
underlying models and limitations are discussed in more detail in Section A4.6. Kosareo & Ries (2007) 
integrate the effects of green roofs on annual building energy consumption. Using the i-Tree Eco tool, 
carbon storage and sequestration by green infrastructure is calculated and normalized by Flynn & 
Traver (2013). The avoided global warming potential is then accounted for in the LCA using the EPA’s 
Greenhouse Gas Equivalencies Calculator.

Social or economic impacts are usually not included in a LCA, but the life cycle approach can 
also be applied to these categories. Often, an economical evaluation over the whole life cycle of 
scenarios is conducted parallel to the LCA. Flynn & Traver (2013) integrate the results in the LCA by 
introducing a new impact category “cost” (in 2001 USD). They also analyse changes in labour and 
demanded qualifications in connection with implementation of new SWM technologies.  
Numerous studies have evaluated the benefits of green infrastructure for SWM without using 
LCA, and alternative methods have been developed and used to assess different benefit categories. 
Stratus Consulting Inc. (2010) compare low-impact to conventional solutions using the US 
Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) Environmental Benefits Mapping and Analysis Program 
(BenMAP). Amongst others, recreational opportunities, property values and wetland services as well 
as disruption costs due to construction and maintenance are evaluated. CNT & American Rivers 
(2010) develop a method to systematically quantify and value benefits by green infrastructure.  Other 
researchers focus on single impact categories, e.g. Voigt et al. (2014), who assess the recreational 
value of urban parks, and Swanwick (2009), who determines social and health benefits. New tools 
allow a more detailed analysis of mass flows and impacts of green infrastructure, e.g. the U.S. 
Department of Agriculture Forest Service i-Tree tools. Based on tree population data, different benefit 
categories like carbon dioxide reduction, air quality improvement, energy conservation and aesthetic 
benefits can be calculated (USDA Forest Service 2013). 
A4.6 Underlying hydrologic and hydraulic models 
Depending on the functional unit and the assessed impact categories, different models are 
utilized in the reviewed papers on LCA in SWM. De Sousa et al. (2012) apply a hydrologic and 
hydraulic model developed by the NYC Department of Environmental Protection to the whole 
catchment contributing to two CSO discharge points, as defined in the functional unit. Spatari et al. 
(2011) use the open-source Low Impact Development Rapid Assessment (LIDRA) tool to model 
stormwater runoff reductions. Wang et al. (2013) calculate flow changes using the EPA’s Storm Water 
Management Model. Some researchers calculate changes in runoff quantities solely by runoff 
reduction coefficients, e.g. Kosareo & Ries (2007), who use and extrapolate values for different roof 
types found in literature. Also runoff quality alterations are often assessed using factors (Taylor & 
Barrett 2008; Wang et al. 2013), which are sometimes specified for average, dry and wet conditions 
(Spatari et al. 2011; Wang et al. 2013).
