Abstract
The quality of bread is aﬀected by various variables during the manufacturing. Fac-
tors aﬀecting the bread quality are both present in the choice of ingredients, the
processing and the conditions during the processing of the bread.
This master thesis has investigating the eﬀect of relative humidity, temperature and
type of ﬂour on the rheological properties and expansion of wheat dough during proof-
ing and baking.
The eﬀect of these factors on the rheological properties were examined by dynamic
rheological oscillation test; time sweep, frequency sweep and amplitude sweep and
transient creep test measuring the eﬀect of relative humidity at 50, 60, 70 and 80
% and temperature at 30, 50, 60, 70, and 80 ◦C respectively. The expansion of the
dough was examined by 3D-scanning of the dough measuring volume, surface area
and height during prooﬁng and baking at 35 ◦C and 80 % relative humidity.
The eﬀect of rheological properties and expansion were measured on two commercial
ﬂours with diﬀerent protein contents (10.8% and 13%).
Results showed that relative humidity has an eﬀect on the time where major in-
creases in the structure development are observed in the dough. It was found that
the time for change in the structure development was reached earlier if the relative
humidity was lowered. Further more a lower relative humidity was observed to facil-
itate higher rates of structure development in dough. This caused a more developed
end structure of the dough. It is expected that the more developed end structure and
rate of structure development for low relative humidity (50%) mainly was caused by
drying. Whereas the structure development at higher relative humidity are expected
to be caused by gelatinization of starch.
Temperature was shown to have an eﬀect on the structure of dough at the beginning
of experiment where temperature > 60◦C was shown to have a high developed and
more elastic dominant structure that dough treated at temperatures <60 ◦C. This is
expected to be caused by gelatinization.
The expansion experiments showed that the ﬂour type with the highest protein con-
tent had the most suﬃcient expansion during prooﬁng and baking.
The rheological properties of the high protein ﬂour showed a less developed structure
at temperatures below the point of gelatinization. Based on the diﬀerence between
the structure of the two ﬂours it is expected a higher protein content in the ﬂour
causes weaker structure of the dough that allows for more expansion.

Introduction
Bread is an essential part of the diet for a majority of people around the world and
has been consumed and produced for thousands of years. It is traditionally based
on wheat ﬂour derived from wheat cereal but several variations of bread in terms
of composition and production techniques has been invented throughout the years
which have an impact of the texture, taste, shape and other organoleptic features of
bread (Cauvain, 2014; Van Bockstaele, 2011).
In tratitional bread making with wheat ﬂour; ﬂour, water and yeast are mixed and
the energy from the mixing results in formation of dough which possesses unique
viscoelastic properties. These viscoelastic properties are related to the protein com-
position in the wheat ﬂour which makes the dough capable of retaining gas that is
produced from fermentation facilitated by the yeast. The encapsulation of the gas
makes the dough areated which is a main contributor to the quality of the end prod-
uct. These proteins are know as gluten and the level of gluten can vary noticeably
between diﬀerent wheat ﬂours. (Van Bockstaele, 2011).
It is commonly known that the rheological properties of dough is a fundamental
part of assessing the end quality of bread. A dough with a too strong structure are
shown not to be able to retain gas from fermentation suﬃciently which causes the end
product to become small and compact. On the other hand, a dough with too weak
rheological properties result in a poor ability to maintain a structure when retaining
gas which will cause the dough to collapse (Belton, 2003). Thereof, rheology can act
as a useful tool in the baking industry to characterise and predict the performance of
dough during deformation.
Many factor both in the composition and processing of dough can aﬀect the rheologi-
cal properties e.g. mixing, prooﬁng, environmental conditions, water content, protein
content etc. Hence, it is important to investigate how these factors inﬂuences the
rheological properties of dough to increase the understanding of the impact of these
factors to ensure optimal quality of the end product (Dobraszczyk, 2015).
Several studies have investigated the interactions between the factors altering the
rheological properties of dough e.g.
Stear (1990); G´elinas and McKinnon (2004);
Phan-Thien and Safari-Ardi (1998); Migliori and Gabriele (2010) to name a few.
However, to the knowledge of this project no studies have investigated the eﬀect of
relative humidity in the surrounding environment on the rheological properties of
wheat dough.

1.1
Scope of the project
The scope of this project is to investigate how the rheological properties of wheat
dough are aﬀected by changes in the environmental conditions in regards to rela-
tive humidity and temperature. Additionally, the project will examine the impact
of changing the ﬂour type in the product composition. Lastly, the expansion of the
wheat dough formulated with the two types of ﬂour will be examined and correlated
to their rheological properties.
Thereof, the objectives of this project is to:

2
Background
This section will give an overview of the relevant theory behind wheat bread dough
rheology and bread quality and cover pertinent scientiﬁc work that has been done
within this area.
In order to cover what deﬁnes the quality of wheat bread several things have to
be covered; the functions of ingredients, the dough making process and the baking
process.
Before looking into the processes of making dough and bread the common ingredients
in wheat bread dough will be described.
2.1
Ingredients - Functions and quality
The ﬁnal quality of wheat bread relies over 90 % on the raw materials and the recipe
formulation. Thus, the choice of ingredients is a critcal part of the ﬁnal quality (Cau-
vain and Young, 2007). Thereof, the selection of raw materials and recipe formulation
is crucial for the outcome of the bread quality.
2.1.1
Wheat ﬂour and gluten network formation
Wheat ﬂour is the main ingredient in wheat bread and is an important component
to produce a good quality bread product. This is mainly due to the storage protein
found in wheat ﬂour which is gluten. Gluten has the ability when hydrated to form
a protein structure that can retain CO2 (carbon dioxide) and thereby facilitate ex-
pansion of the bread roll (Cauvain, 2015a). It is therefore considered that a ﬂour’s
gluten content deﬁnes the end quality of the end product.
Gluten is a mixture of various proteins that are capable of forming a elastic protein-
network after hydrating and mixing the ﬂour. The two most prominent proteins in
ﬂour when investigating formation of the gluten matrix in dough and bread is the
two protein groups glutenins (polymeric proteins) and gliadins (monomeric proteins).
The two protein groups forms a gluten network that acts elastic and plastic making
the dough able to stretch without tearing and spring back when the network is de-
veloped (Stauﬀer, 2007; Wang and Fowler, 1993; Batey and Huang, 2015).
Glutenins, the polymeric proteins in the gluten network, provides the elastic proper-
ties of the dough which makes it able to spring back after stretching and gliadins, the
monomeric proteins, provides the viscous properties to the dough (Xu et al., 2007).
Hydration of the ﬂour will cause the two groups of protein to form a homogeneous
viscoelastic mixture known as dough which will be capable of retaining CO2 during
yeast fermentation. Kneading or mixing is an important part of the formation of the

amino acids e.g. other glutamines, serine and tyrosine and proline ’bends’ the gluten
protein chains to enhance the elasticity of the network (Damodaran, 2017).

dant on the formation of the gluten network. It is stated that in order to initiate the
development of this network the addition of water is necessary.
The amount of water that should be added to wheat ﬂour in order to develop a dough

2.1.3
Yeast and fermentation
Baker’s yeast (Saccharomyces cerecisiae) is a fungus and is the ingredients that pro-
motes fermentation in the dough and thereby makes the dough expand. There are
several diﬀerent types of yeast e.g. dry yeast, compressed yeast, pumpable yeast etc.
Depending on the type of yeast and the time of fermentation the yeast also have an
impact on the taste of the ﬁnal product.
In the process of fermentation the starch in the dough are broken down by enzymes
during resting and prooﬁng which causes reducing sugars to be available in the dough.
The yeast will then use the reducing sugars to produce ethanol and CO2 (carbon diox-
ide) in the dough by an anaerobic metabolism which is shown in ﬁgure 2.2 (Wiggins

2.1.4
Salt
Salt is added as a part of bread dough for a number of reasons and the amount added
is aﬀected by the user preferences. This is due to the salts impact on the ﬂavor of the
end product and in the western part of the world the salt concentration is 1-2% of
dough based on the ﬂour weight (Gould, 2007). Furthermore, the addition of salt can
be used to maintain a better control of the fermentation rate in the dough. Salt has
an retarding eﬀect on the yeast resulting in a slower fermentation if the salt content
is increased (Cauvain, 2015b; Luchian and Cristina, 2010).
2.2
Main processes of dough and bread making
The processes involved in the manufacturing of bread have the aim to convert the
wheat ﬂour into an edible food. The main processes in traditional bread making are
shown below (Cauvain, 2015a).
• Mixing of wheat ﬂour with raw materials most commonly water, yeast and
salt. The mixing will start to develop the gluten structure by hydrating the
proteins in the ﬂour.
• Resting, also referred to as ripening or maturing, makes the dough ’relax’ and
provide further development of the dough and the protein structure.
• Division and shaping of the rested dough is done to obtain the wanted shape,
size and weight.
• Prooﬁng of the dough pieces will cause expansion of the bread dough during
yeast fermentation.
• Baking will provide the ﬁnal expansion and ﬁxation of the bread by providing
high temperatures to the dough. Thus, turning the dough into bread
In the following subsections the above mentioned bread making processes will be
described with the exception of division and shaping.

2.2.1
Mixing
Mixing is an essential process step in the development of dough.
The mixing of dough on industrial scale and in bakeries are mainly performed by mix-
ing machines. There are several diﬀerent types of mixers for mixing dough depending
on the type of dough there should be mixed. Common for all of the diﬀerent types
of dough mixers is that they aim to meet the basic requirement of suﬃcient dough
mixing (Marsh and Cauvain, 2007);
• Ingredients should be uniformly dispersed to create a homogeneous dough mix-
ture.
• Facilitate hydration of the ingredients to develop the dough structure.
• Provide energy to develop the gluten network from the hydrated ﬂour proteins.
• integrate air into the dough preparing the dough to retain gas developed by the
yeast fermentation
If all these requirements are met the mixing is considered successful and the dough
should be ready for further processing.
Industrial mixers are designed to both carry out the mixing of the ingredients and
the kneading of the bread dough. Mixing and kneading are therefore considered one
process step (Marsh and Cauvain, 2007).
2.2.2
Resting
After the mixing the dough is moulded and sometimes divided in smaller pieces and
set to relax. The resting process can be used for diﬀerent purposes depending on
which type of dough there should be produced. This process is often referred to as
the intermediate prooﬁng stage before ﬁnal moulding and bulk prooﬁng/fermentation
in a controlled environment (Gould, 2007).
The time of the resting stage is crucial in terms of the quality and characteristics of
the end product and can vary from 0 minutes up to 2 hours. With longer resting
times the yeast activity is allowed to ferment the dough and produce CO2. This will
cause formation gas bubbles in the dough before the moulding and ﬁnal fermentation.
Thus, it will result in a bread with larger air bubbles prior to baking. Shorter resting
times does not aim for an intermediate fermentation but allows the dough to relax
in order to make the dough soft, relaxed and extensible before the ﬁnal mould of
the bread rolls to provide optimal performance of the dough during prooﬁng.(Gould,
2007)

2.2.3
Prooﬁng
This process step determines many diﬀerent organoleptic features of the bread and
the end quality of bread. Prooﬁng is the process step between the ﬁnal mould of the
dough and the baking. Hence, the prooﬁng facilitates the bulk fermentation and it is
carried out in a controlled atmosphere to provide a controlled expansion of the bread
(Wiggins and Cauvain, 2007).
The expansion of the dough during prooﬁng will increase the product size by a factor
of three to four depending of the dough and former processing (Wiggins and Cauvain,
2007).
During the prooﬁng enzymes in the dough will breakdown the starch molecules into
fermentable sugar which the yeast can convert into carbon dioxide and ethanol. The
produced carbon dioxide will aerate the dough causing the dough to expand. A de-
veloped gluten network is crucial for the dough to retain the developed gas from
fermentation (Wiggins and Cauvain, 2007). An example of the prooﬁng steps impact
on the dough cell structure is shown in ﬁgure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Example of cross-section in a bread roll before and after prooﬁng. Before
prooﬁng (left) and after prooﬁng (right) (Wiggins and Cauvain, 2007).
In the example the dough is proofed in a tin shape forcing the dough only to expand
in height. In cases where the dough are placed on an open baking sheet the expan-
sion will both be vertical and horizontal making the dimension of the whole bread
roll increase (Wiggins and Cauvain, 2007).
Depending on the strechability of the gluten network the gas bubbles within the

2.2.4
Baking
The ﬁrst crucial part of maintaining the quality of the proofed bread dough is the
transportation from the prooﬁng chamber to the oven where the product is still a
soft areated dough. Here, just small damages or disturbances can cause the dough to
collapse and thereby disrupt the structure of the dough. Thus, the transport to the
oven should be done as gentle as possible (Wiggins and Cauvain, 2007).
When the proofed dough is placed in the oven the temperature is around 200 ◦C,
but this can vary from product to product.
The high temperature on the oven causes immediate heating of the dough’s surface
and the crust begins to form. However, the dough underneath the crust (the crumb)

also applies to sensory properties of the end product (Capuano et al., 2008).
2.3
Wheat starch gelatinization
The gelatinization of wheat starch is a thermal induced physicochemical change oc-
curring in the bread dough during baking that changes the wheat starch structure
and causes the conversion of the foam-like dough into a sponge like bread structure
(Schirmer et al., 2015; Wiggins and Cauvain, 2007).
This conversion from dough to bread is an irreversible structural transformation and
is caused by a combined eﬀect of heating and hydration of the native starch granules
present in the wheat ﬂour. The starch polymer consist of amylose (a linear polymer:
4000 glucose units) and amylopectin (multi-branched polysaccaride: 100000 glucose
units) in a ratio of 3/1 (Schirmer et al., 2015).

2.4
Wheat dough rheology
Rheology is the study of ﬂow and deformation based on an applied stress or strain of
a matter, in this case wheat dough. Through rheology a foodstuﬀcan be classiﬁed
as either newtonian or non-newtonian and the majority of foodstuﬀs have a non-
newtonian viscoelastic behaviour (Steﬀe, 1996; Hadnadev et al., 2011).
Wheat dough has a unique rheological system due to the the complex structure caused
by the starch and protein network; in rheological terms wheat dough is classiﬁed as a
viscoelastic system with shear-thinning and thixotropic behaviour (Hadnadev et al.,
2011).

2.4.2
Rheological test methods
In rheology there are several methods to describe the rheological properties and be-
haviours of foodstuﬀs. The diﬀerent methods can be divided into two main categories;
Empirical test and Dynamic tests.
Empirical test are widely used in the industry a to measure the ﬂow behaviour of
foods. Nonetheless, these methods can not be used to determine the fundamental
rheological properties of foods.
Empirical methods can provide data to validate the quality of the product. This
project does not look into any empirical test of wheat dough thus, the empirical test
methods will not be further described (Hadnadev et al., 2011).
Dynamic rheological tests can be divided into two groups; the transient tests in this
report creep test and the oscillation test in this report amplitude sweep, frequenzy
sweep and time sweep (Wrolstad et al., 2005).

3.2.1
Rheometer setup
In order to make sure that the dough samples were ready for rheological analysis it
is important to set everything up correctly to assure that the dough undergo as little
fermentation as possible before it is loaded to the rheometer. Given that this project
focuses on relative humidity and temperature as factors that will aﬀect the rheological
properties a humidity chamber is required in order to control these parameters (shwon
in ﬁgure 3.2. These parameters takes some time to setup correctly thus, this section
will illustrate how this should be setup while preparing and resting the dough. In
ﬁgure 3.4 below, a ﬂowchart shows how the setup of the rheometer should be done.

From ﬁgure 3.4 the ﬁrst thing that should be done is that the rheometer should be
turned on along with the computer. This should be done so it is possible for the
user to use the TA Instruments TRIOS software. With this software the rheometer
can be controlled and the upper part of the rheometer (the rotational part) can be
moved up and down. This part should be moved up in order to attach the humidity
chamber to the rheometer. In order to attach the humidity chamber the lower part of
the rheometer should be removed, allowing the user to ﬁxed assemble the humidity
chamber with three screws using an allen key. In order to avoid any complications

with the attachment of the humidity chamber the three screws should not be tight-
ened completely before every screw is threaded properly. Furthermore, the humidity
chamber consist of two part where one of them is movable. The movable part should
be removed before the chamber is attached, this is done with an allen key.
When the humidity chamber is in place the geometry can be installed. The geometry
used throughout all experiments performed in this project is a 25 millimetre parallel
plate and a humidity chamber steel surface (see ﬁgure 3.2). The humidity chamber
steel surface is attached to the equipment by removing the plastic protection from
the magnet on the bottom of the steel surface and placing it on the magnet on the
rheometer. Afterwards the 25 mm parallel plate is tightened to the upper part of the
rheometer.
The the movable part of the humidity chamber can be reattached and tightened to
the humidity chamber. In the TRIOS software the geometry should then be selected
in the geometry drop-down menu ’25mm parallel plate, Humidity chamber Steel’.
When this is done it is possible to set the temperature and relative humidity in the
chamber in the condition section. The heating rate is maximum ±1 ◦C/min in the
entire temperature range and the relative humidity rate ±2% RH/min thus, the setup
of the environment takes some time depending on the wanted conditions. Thereof,
the preparation of the dough can start during the environment setup.
When the dough samples was prepared and rested for 15 minutes the dough was
wrapped in transparent ﬁlm and placed in a refrigerator at 5 ◦C to decrease the yeast
activity. When the humidity chamber reached the temperature and relative humidity
the geometry can be zero gaped to set the correct gap for the sample. The loading
gap size is set to 25000 µm and the gap size is set to 1000 µm for all rheological
experiments in this project (Zheng et al., 2000).
After the zero gap the geometry can be set back to the loading gap position and
the sample can afterwards be placed in the rheometer. The dough was taken out of
the refrigerator and the ﬁlm wrap was removed from the dough. A spatula was then
used to cut out a piece of the bread dough which should be approximately the size
of a kidney bean. The movable part of the humidity chamber was then removed and
the sample was placed on the center of the steel surface. The movable part of the
rheometer was then placed back on the chamber without tightening any screws to
avoid a large temperature decrease in the chamber while setting the geometry the
gap size. When the gap size was reached the movable chamber side was then removed
and the leftover dough was removed by scraping it of. Lastly, the chamber side was
tightened back on to the humidity chamber and the rheological analysis was started
when the temperature has reached the wanted level again.

the movable side of the humidity chamber was removed. The the upper part of the

humidity in the chamber will reset to 0.00% RH.

Limitations
As stated previously in the result and discussion section at great limitation of this
project is the low number of replication done for each sample. Duplicates have been
made for each sample at rheological measurement which gives clearer evidence of how
a sample behaves than making one replication. However, if more replications for each
sample were made standard deviations could have been diminished and the statisti-
cal arguments would have been stronger and more precise. e.g. four replications was
done for each sample in the expansion experiments which made the results more clear
by lowering standard deviations and giving a clear picture of the trend.
In the rheological experiments the dough was stored at 5 ◦C to inhibit fermentation.
However, fermentation still occurred during the storage time between experiment
which might have an aﬀect on the end results.
Furthermore, the removable side of the humidity chamber was removed in order to
load a sample in the rheometer which caused the temperature in the chamber to drop.
This resulted in some time (∼2 minutes) to regain the wanted temperature. For this
period of time the rheological behaviour of the sample could not be measured making
it not possible to examine the changes occurring in the sample during this time period.
The expansion experiments required time for the 3D-scannings of every bread roll
approximately 2 minutes per scan. Given that fermentation in occurring at room
temperature (∼20 ◦C) expansion already happened during measuring. This could
be expected to have an lowering inﬂuence of the results of the expansion experiments
given that the bread rolls would expand during the measurements at time 0 minutes.

5
Conclusion
Rheological time sweeps of wheat dough showed that the relative humidity had a
signiﬁcant eﬀect on the time were a prominent increase of the complex modulus (G*)
was observed (deﬁned as the critical time (ct)). It was found that the critical time
for prominent structure development (at temperature 30, 50, 60, 70 and 80 ◦C) oc-
curred at the earliest for dough treated at 50 % relative humidity and latest for dough
treated at 80 % relative humidity.
Furthermore, it was found that changes in the relative humidity had a signiﬁcant
eﬀect in the rate of structure development of dough during heat treatment in terms
of G*. Dough treated at 50 % relative humidity was found to have a signiﬁcantly
higher rate for structure development compared to dough treated at 60, 70 and 80
% relative humidity. The second highest structure development rate was observed in
dough treated at 60% relative humidity.
The higher rate of structure development observed in dough treated at low relative
humidity was found to result in signiﬁcantly higher developed end structure of the
doughs at low relative humidity. A prominent increase in the end structure between
30 and 50 ◦C was observed in dough treated at 50% relative humidity. Since gela-
tinization of wheat starch ﬁrst occurs and will alter the structure of dough at 53-64
◦C the observed increase is expected to be caused by drying. Increase of the end
structure for dough treated at 60, 70 and 80 % relative humidity was ﬁrst observed
beyond the point of gelatinization.
It was found that temperature had signiﬁcant eﬀect on the dough structure at the
critical time. Dough treated at 80 ◦C showed the highest developed structure at the
critical time and dough treated at 30 ◦showed the lowest. Moreover, dough threaded
at 80 ◦C showed the most elastic dominant behaviour whereas dough treated at 30
◦C showed a least prominent elastic behaviour.
When changing the ﬂour type in the product composition Flour type 2 (high protein
content) showed signiﬁcantly greater expansion than ﬂour type 1, both in surface area
and volume during prooﬁng at 35 ◦C and 80 % relative humidity over a time period
of 60 minutes.
During baking for 10 minutes at 200 ◦C both ﬂour types showed an increase in surface
area and volume within the ﬁrst 2.5 minutes of baking. Flour type one continued to
expend until 5 minutes of baking afterwards both ﬂour types decreased in volume
and surface area.
No signiﬁcant diﬀerences were observed between the two ﬂour types rheological prop-
erties. However, ﬂour type 2 showed general lower G’ and G” at temperatures below

of ﬂour type 2 is expected to cause the dough formulated with ﬂour type 2 to expand

6
Perspectives
For future work it would be relevant to collect more rheological data for dough for-
mulated with diﬀerent ﬂour with varying nutritional composition e.g. fat content,
ﬁbre content etc. in controlled environments in terms of relative humidity and tem-
perature. With more knowledge about the interactions between diﬀerent types of
ﬂour, relative humidity and temperature mathematical models could be developed
to predict the impact of changing the ﬂour type and altering the process conditions
during prooﬁng and baking.
Such models could help industrial bakeries to understand how a change of these fac-
tors would aﬀect a product. This could be a useful tool in product development and
innovation within the baking industry.
Furthermore, more levels of both relative humidity and temperature should be ex-
amined to get a more nuanced knowledge about how these conditions aﬀect wheat
dough both in prooﬁng and baking.
Together with this, sensory studies should be considered to investigate how these
process conditions aﬀect the sensory quality of the ﬁnal product.
