Abstract
Energy-based acoustical models are widely used to predict the acoustic parameters in rooms. One
such model, specializing in rooms where most of the absorption is concentrated on the ceiling, was
developed by Nilsson. The present project aims to ’¼ünd optimal values for a number of the modelŌĆÖs
parameters so that, given a roomŌĆÖs data, its prediction of the sound decay matches the measured
sound decay of the existing room. A method to extract octave-band decay curves from measured
impulse responses is developed. An optimization method is developed by designing a cost function
and implementing a combination of an optimization algorithm and brute-force approach. Di’¼Ćerent
acoustical modelling options are examined. The optimal set of model parameters is obtained while
using the better-performing models.
Keywords
Optimization, room acoustics, SEA model, sound decay.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation of study
Energy-based models, like the Sabine model, have been widely used for many decades to predict the
sound decay in rooms and to estimate acoustic parameters like reverberation time. Most of those
models assume a di’¼Ćuse sound ’¼üeld, are simple to use and can be quite e’¼ācient in many situations.
However, for rooms where the sound ’¼üeld is far from di’¼Ćuse those modelsŌĆÖ results are unrealistic
and misleading. More advanced models based on more realistic assumptions have been developed,
one of which is the Nilsson model, used in the present project.
Rooms with highly absorbent ceilings and re’¼éective walls are among the situations where the tra-
ditional models are failing. Such rooms include classrooms, o’¼āces, health care facilities, dining
establishments, etc. The design of many of those rooms is strictly regulated with regard to the
acoustical conditions, thus there is the need to accurately predict their acoustics during the design
phase. Traditional room acoustics models tend to overestimate the e’¼Ćect of absorption placed at
the ceiling, to underestimate the e’¼Ćect of placing additional absorption on the walls and disregard
the sound scattering e’¼Ćect of furniture and other items in the room.
The Nilsson model aims to predict the acoustic parameters of such rooms, by considering the sound
’¼üeld as an superposition of two sound ’¼üelds: one in a grazing (to the ceiling) direction and one in
a non-grazing direction. During the development of the model some assumptions have been made.
Some of those assumptions lead to non-de’¼ünitive values for a few model parameters. The modelŌĆÖs
performance can be further improved by ’¼üne-tuning those parameters so that its predictions match
real-case measurements as closely as possible. Also, some physical quantities used by the Nilsson
model can be obtained using various approaches (e.g. the ceiling acoustic impedance) and this is
another factor that can in’¼éuence the modelŌĆÖs performance.
1.2
Scope of study
The present project regards the optimization of the Nilsson model, as it is implemented by a Matlab
script, by means of obtaining optimal values for a number of model parameters and by means of
testing various ways of modelling speci’¼üc physical quantities used by it. The aim is to adjust the
model so that it predicts the sound decay in a number of rooms as close as possible to the measured
decay in said rooms.

2.2
Acoustic room parameters
2.2.1
Reverberation time
Reverberation time (T) is used as an objective measure of reverberance. It is related to speech
intelligibility as in high reverberation conditions consonants tend to be masked by the preceding
vowels, making speech harder to comprehend [3]. Reverberation time also a’¼Ćects the perception
of music. In dry (non-reverberant) rooms the temporal details of a music piece stand out and the
separation between di’¼Ćerent instruments is more prominent [3]. Other room acoustic parameters,
as mentioned in more detail in the following paragraphs, can be directly calculated from T. For
those reasons reverberation time is the single most signi’¼ücant parameter in room acoustics.
Reverberation time is de’¼üned as the time it takes for the sound pressure level to drop by 60 dB after
a continuous sound source is turned o’¼Ć[4]. Assuming a linear decay (di’¼Ćuse sound ’¼üeld conditions)
reverberation time can be estimated by considering smaller level drops and multiplying accordingly
to relate the measure to the 60 dB drop. Thus the following reverberation times are de’¼üned:

2.4
The Nilsson model
2.4.1
Overview
The theoretical model that is being implemented in the context of this report is an energy-based
(statistical) model that has been developed by Nilsson [12] [13] [14] [15] [16]. It regards rooms
with non-uniform absorption in which the absorption is mainly concentrated on the ceiling, also
accounting for smaller absorption surfaces located on side walls. It takes into account the scattering
and absorption caused by objects in the room. Its central concept is that the sound ’¼üeld can be
considered as the superposition of two sound ’¼üelds (subsystems):
ŌĆó the grazing sound ’¼üeld (waves travelling parallel to the ceiling),
ŌĆó the non-grazing sound ’¼üeld (waves travelling perpendicular to the ceiling).
The presence of sound scattering objects in the room results in the transfer of energy between the
two subsystems, coupling the subsystems.
The Nilsson model can be used to predict the reverberation time, clarity and sound strength in
rooms such as closed o’¼āces, classrooms, health care institutions, and other rooms of similar size
and at which the absorption is mainly concentrated at the ceiling.

Optimization
A typical optimization problem is [24] to minimize a function F(X) : R ŌåÆRn, called the cost
function, over a set H ŌŖéRn, called the feasible set. X = (P1, P2, ...Pn), X ŌłłRn is a vector of the
optimization parameters (OPs), a point in the n-dimensional space.
The existence of constraints on the values of X, as de’¼üned for each speci’¼üc problem, lead to the
feasible set H. The points X ŌłłH are called feasible points or feasible solutions.
A local optimal solution, or local minimum, is a point X ŌłłH for which, for some neighborhood u
of X, one has F(X) ŌēżF(X) for all other feasible solutions X [24]. A local minimum is a global
minimum when the above inequality is valid in H instead of only in the u neighborhood. The
value of F at those points is called optimal value, locally or globally respectively. Depending on the
problem there may be zero, one, or more local minima. The aim is to ’¼ünd the global minimum.

4.2.1
Decay-based optimization
Optimization is achieved by comparing measured decay curves to decay curves calculated with RAC
using di’¼Ćerent sets of OPs. If the acoustical parameters are used to calculate the cost (i.e. measured
T20 minus calculated T20) there is the risk of tuning the model to those speci’¼üc numbers but the
backbone of the model not being robust to new cases. By optimizing using the decays it is made
certain that the model accurately predicts the sound decay in the room, thus achieving a faithful
representation of reality.
The cost function is the normalized, summed, squared di’¼Ćerence of the measured decay curves from
those calculated by the model, both curve data in dB. If the pressure squared domain (p2 or h2) was
used the optimization would be heavily based on the ’¼ürst few milliseconds where h2 takes extremely
high values compared to later on.
Normalizing by the total number of time-points used in the calculation is vital as the decays are
not of the same length. Normalizing by the number of bands and the number of cases allows for
the comparison of costs obtained by considering one or more cases and/or one or more bands.

4.3
Calculating model decay
The decay is calculated using a normalized version of equation 18. Since the decays are normalized
to 0 dB at their start point, the time-independent part of equation 18 is omitted. The function
Decay.m (see appendix section 12.4) is called by RAC using as inputs ŌłåNg, ŌłåNng, Tg and Tng.
One decay curve per octave band per case is calculated, with a time length of 2 seconds and a
temporal resolution of 1 ms.
4.4
Using fmincon function
For the optimization the Matlab function fmincon is used. It is a gradient-based method designed to
work on problems where the objective function is continuous. It accepts as inputs the cost function,
the OP starting point X0 and any constraints. It then starts from X0 and stops at a point X in the
OP space where the cost function is non-decreasing further to any feasible direction, while satisfying
the constraints. There is no guarantee that this point is the global minimum of the problem.
In the present project the only constraints are upper and lower limits for the OPs.
fmincon can use a number of di’¼Ćerent algorithms to search of the minimum point. The ŌĆÖinterior-
pointŌĆÖ algorithm, which is the default one, is used, according to the recommendation on the Math-
works website [26]. An in-depth explanation of how the algorithm works can be found at [27].
fmincon is run with the following command:
[x, fval] = fmincon(@cost,x0,A,b,Aeq,beq,lb,ub);
Since there are no linear constraints, the following are set to 0:
A = []; b = []; Aeq = []; beq = [];
x0 is the starting point, X0,
lb and ub contain the upper and lower bounds of X,
x returns the coordinates of the minimum, X ,
fval returns the value of the cost function at X.

4.6
A note on terminology
The cost can be calculated by considering only a subset of the available cases. For example, when
"optimizing for cases with wall panels", the cost is calculated by comparing the RAC-calculated
decays for the wall panel cases, to the measured decays of the same cases. In the context of this
report, the term "optimize for <descriptor of set of cases>" means that the optimization is run
considering only the cases described by the descriptor, that is, trying to match the calculated-to-
measured decays. So "optimizing for case 5", means that the algorithm ’¼ünds a point X that best
tunes the model to the measured decays of case 5, and this is achieved by considering only the
case 5 calculated-to-measured decays in the cost. "Optimizing for all cases" means that the cost is
calculated by comparing all the RAC-calculated decays to (all) respective measured ones.

5
Code description
5.1
Best practices for time-e’¼ācient coding
The process of optimization requires the scripts to be run many times. Thus there is the need for
the code to be as time-e’¼ācient as possible. For example, optimizing for P3 requires tens of hours
of running time. To this end the following are implemented:
1.
Calculations spread in more than one commands, usually using intermediate variables, are
rewritten in one command. This makes the code harder to read and understand but greatly improves
its running time. The more intuitive code is kept but commented out. For example the following
part in RAC:
As1 = S1*aback;
As2 = S2*aback;
As3 = As1;
As4 = As2;
Afloor = Sfloor*aback;
Asurf = As1+As2+As3+As4+Afloor;
Aceilg = Sceil*alphagrazceil;
Atotg = Aceilg + Ascatt + Asurf + Awall;
is reduced to the more time-e’¼ācient:
Atotg = Sceil*alphagrazceil + Ascatt + aback*(2*(S1+S2) ...
+ Sfloor) + Awall;
Atotng = Sceil*alpha_ng + Afurn + aback*(2*(S1+S2) + Sfloor) + Awall;
2. Some formulas with additions and multiplications are rewritten using common factors, reducing
the amount of calculations.
3. For-loops are used only when absolutely necessary. Instead, matrix calculations are introduced
where possible. For instance the normalization of calculated decays for all cases and all octave
bands in the cost function is done as:
cal_decays = cal_decays - repmat(cal_decays(:,:,1),1,1,2000);
4. When the length or size of a variable was used often in the code as, for instance, length(f), it
is now replaced by a new variable, length_f = length(f). Thus Matlab doesnŌĆÖt need to calculate
itŌĆÖs value every time, it simply refers to the value of the new variable.
5. RAC is implemented with input control variables that allow skipping of calculations of decay-
unrelated parameters when optimizing.
6. Although the use of nested functions is not recommended for time-e’¼ācient code, the cost function
is a nested function so that it doesnŌĆÖt need to load the measured decays every time, saving time.
Also, using variables with broader than local scope is one of the few ways of passing extra parameters
to the cost function of fmincon.

5.2
Optimization ’¼éowchart
A ’¼éowchart providing an overview of the optimization process can be seen in ’¼ügure 18. In each box
the performed action and the Matlab function that performs it (in italic) are shown.
Two major loops are used to perform the optimization. In the inner loop fmincon runs the cost
function with various values of the vector x (containing P1 (P1.1 to P1.6) and P2) until it locates
the minimum. The values of x in each run of the loop are chosen by the algorithm of fmincon. The
outer loop runs the inner loop going over a grid of P3 values (P3.1 and P3.2). P3 being an integer
number, it cannot be used as an OP in fmincon.
The P3 grid point with the lowest cost provides the optimal P3.1 and P3.2 values. The optimal P1
and P2 for the optimal P3 along with the optimal P3, form the optimal parameter set.

7
Conclusions
The RAC model was optimized considering both the Komatsu and the Miki surface impedance
models. They were found to perform almost equally well, with the Komatsu model having a slightly
better performance, with lower cost and higher consistency among di’¼Ćerent cases.
Optimization was also run considering both the Morse low frequency model and the standard
absorption model for the grazing sector. The Morse model gave a lower cost and is thus considered
a signi’¼ücant improvement over the standard one.
Overall the matching of the decay curves improved greatly after the optimization, as shown by
DEI and as seen in the decay plots. The modelŌĆÖs prediction of the acoustical room parameters did
improve overall, but not as much as would be expected, considering the good decay matching. This
observation could lead to a reconsideration of BarronŌĆÖs equations used by the model to calculate
C50 and G. Optimization based on the acoustic parameters was attempted but the method used was
not found to be suitable. Having obtained optimal RAC model parameters by decay matching, the
optimization of BarronŌĆÖs equations could be possible, by means of matching the equationsŌĆÖ output
to the measured values, given the calculated decays.
Two sets of optimal parameters (one for cases without panels and one for cases with panels) that
improve the performance of the RAC model are ’¼ünally chosen. But those sets contain a few extreme
values. The low-frequency, non-grazing sector that is suggested by the optimal OPS is much broader
than initially assumed. The high-frequency non-grazing sector suggested by the OPs is much smaller
than initially assumed. And the optimal low-frequency grazing sector was found to be as small as
possible. Those observations can provide motivation to review some of the modelŌĆÖs assumptions.
