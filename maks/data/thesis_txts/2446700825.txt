Abstract 
 
Ready-to-eat food, although gaining popularity, is a major safety concern for food producers. 
Long shelf-life in refrigerated temperatures and no need of further preparation before consumption 
makes such products perfect for supporting the growth of L. monocytogenes.  
The objective of this thesis was to investigate the inhibitory properties of mayonnaise towards 
the growth of Listeria monocytogenes in ready-to-eat meat-mayonnaise salad mixes. As 
representatives of mayonnaise-based salads, mixes containing 20 and 40% mayonnaise and cooked 
pork meat have been prepared and kept at refrigeration temperature of 4.4ºC for 14 days. Two 
independent experiments were conducted. 
This research showed that the addition of mayonnaise to the meat product lowered the growth 
potential of L. monocytogenes. The decrease in both total growth in 14 days and growth rates of L. 
monocytogenes increased with rising concentrations of mayonnaise in the mix. Enumeration on the 
selective Palcam agar showed that relative to the final bacterial concentration in the meat product 
with no mayonnaise at the end of the 14-day storage time, growth of L. monocytogenes decreased 
by 1.4-2.2 log CFU/g in the 20% mayonnaise mix, and by 2.5-3.4-log CFU/g in 40% mayonnaise 
mix. Although the growth in the experiment was evaluated using both selective and non-selective 
agars, statistical analyses showed that there was no significant difference (p > 0.05) in concentration 
of L. monocytogenes estimated from counts on the two media. 
While the results from the first experiment were similar in the second experiment, the growth 
observed in the latter experiment was higher in all types of tested products. Although statistical 
analyses suggested that the differences between the results of these two experiments were not 
significant (p > 0.05), it should be noted that the differences amounted to 1-log higher numbers, 
which may be important from a food safety perspective and establishing a safe shelf-life for the 
product. In the second experiment, a presence of non-Listeria microorganisms, probably 
Corynebacterium, was observed.  
L. monocytogenes growth rates in the various ham-mayonnaise products were obtained by 
fitting the non-linear models in the DMFit software to the growth curves and compared to growth 
rates predicted by the ComBase Predictor and FSSP programs, using two scenarios: with or without 
an estimated acetic acid concentration. The comparisons showed that the ComBase Predictor and 
FSSP predictive models, especially when considering the estimated specific acetic acid content, 
cannot be used for meat-mayonnaise salads, as most of the time they under-predicted the growth 
rates.

1 Literature review 
1.1 Introduction and thesis objectives 
 
Today’s world is developing quickly. Entrepreneurs and scientists are racing in coming up 
with more and more ideas of how to make people’s lives easier and less challenging. Living in 
today’s world means living fast and being busy all the time (Gupta & Dudeja, 2017). This fast 
paced lifestyle is a huge part of pop culture, portrayed in the movies and music videos as exciting 
and glamorous, and promoted in the media by influencers of all kind (Kaufman, 2010). To be able 
to keep up with the fast pace of life, different solutions for saving time are available to customers, 
one of them being a major change in the food industry. The switch from “traditional” cooking meals 
at home with fast-food, canned and frozen food and ready-to-eat (RTE) meals is a common 
phenomenon in present days, as these options fit well with the new, fast-paced and easy lifestyle 
most people live nowadays (Gupta & Dudeja, 2017). 
Ready-to-eat meals are prepared in a way which requires no to minimal further processing 
from the consumer, before it is ready to be eaten. It can be pre-cooked, frozen, partially-frozen or 
preserved (Gupta & Dudeja, 2017). Although such solution may be convenient for every-day use, 
the concerns regarding its safety are rising. RTE products can be at risk of being the source of 
various foodborne illnesses (Paudyal & Karatzas, 2016) and mishandling during any step of 
production or distribution can lead to food poisoning. Different pathogens are associated with 
foodborne illnesses or outbreaks but Listeria monocytogenes is the most dangerous one, as it causes 
the most food-related deaths (Paudyal & Karatzas, 2016). This bacterium will be the focus of this 
thesis.  
As Paudyal & Karatzas (2016) write, RTE foods can be divided into two categories: high-
risk and low-risk. High-risk foods are described as foods which have the following properties: have 
the potential of being contaminated with L. monocytogenes and support its growth to high numbers; 
are ready to eat and stored in refrigeration temperatures for a longer period of time (ILSI Research 
Foundation/Risk Science Institute, 2005). Among others, cooked meat and meat products belong to 
this category,  (Paudyal & Karatzas, 2016). 
For the purpose of this thesis, two ingredients have been chosen for the experimental part of 
this research: cooked pork meat and mayonnaise. They are a representation of mayonnaise-based 
ham salads, which are widely available in shops and a popular choice for a lunch meal. Moreover, 
==PAGE:6==
 
6 
some of the employees from a catering company, which is collaborating with the university, 
expressed their concerns about the safety of a ham-mayonnaise salad they are preparing and 
delivering to customers. For the experimental part of the thesis, pure, raw pork meat instead of 
regular ham was chosen, to exclude different additives, which are used for preservation of ham and 
may have an inhibiting effect on growth of Listeria monocytogenes. 
The objective of this thesis is to examine and evaluate the correlation between the growth of 
L. monocytogenes and the amount of mayonnaise in ham-mayonnaise mix, with the focus on 
whether the mayonnaise added to the contaminated ham inhibits the growth of Listeria 
monocytogenes and if it does, then at what concentration. 
 
1.2 About Listeria 
1.2.1 Characteristics of the genus and L. monocytogenes 
 
Listeria are small, Gram positive, facultatively anaerobic and non-sporulating bacteria with 
rounded ends. Cells appear either as single units, or they can be also found in short chains (see: 
Figure 1.) (Rocourt & Buchrieser, 2007; Mraheil et al., 2013). There are currently 17 species 
recognized within the Listeria genus, out of which fifteen are non-pathogenic, one is a pathogen for 
animals (L. ivanovii) and one is a human pathogen (L. monocytogenes) (Orsi & Wiedmann, 2016). 
 
 
Figure 1. Listeria monocytogenes taken from experimental plates, viewed under the light 
microscope (x 1000 magnification). 
 
Mraheil et al. (2013) described L. monocytogenes as an ubiquitous bacterium found 
naturally in the environment like soil, water, sewage, wild animal feces, vegetation, farms and other 
==PAGE:7==
 
7 
food-processing facilities. This bacterium is very dangerous due to its virulence and high tolerance 
to different conditions. It has an ability to grow in a wide range of temperatures, pH values and salt 
concentrations (Rocourt & Buchrieser, 2007). As this thesis will be focusing on Listeria 
monocytogenes, more exact conditions will be discussed for this species. 
As mentioned before, L. monocytogenes is very tolerant to various environmental conditions 
and that makes it a very dangerous pathogen to control. Its optimum growth temperature is 
estimated to be between 30 and 37°C but it grows at temperatures from around 0 to 45°C and the 
lower growth limit depends on other factors such as the pH. Although it does not continue to grow 
at temperatures below -1.5°C, it survives much lower temperatures characteristic of the freezing 
process. Temperatures above 50°C are considered lethal (Lado & Yousef, 2007). 
The optimal water activity (aw) for growth of L. monocytogenes is equal or higher than 0.97 
but contrary to most other foodborne pathogens, it can also multiply at lower aw reaching 0.90. 
(Lado & Yousef, 2007). This bacterium can also grow in high salt concentrations (up to 10% NaCl) 
and survive in even higher (Rocourt & Buchrieser, 2007). 
L. monocytogenes is also tolerant to a wide range of pH but this topic will be discussed in 
section 4.1 of the thesis, together with different acids which influence bacteria’s growth. 
 
1.2.2 Listeriosis in humans 
Listeria monocytogenes causes a disease in humans called listeriosis. Most cases of 
listeriosis (95%) are caused by serotypes 4b, 1/2a and 1/2b (Martins & Germano, 2011). Most 
frequent cases of occurrence of this illness are observed in pregnant women, immunocompromised 
and the elderly, and these people are the main risk group for contracting listeriosis. The illness 
usually manifests itself as meningoencephalitis and/or septicemia, and has a high mortality rate of 
approximately 20 to 30 %, unlike other foodborne diseases (Allerberger, 2007; Painter & Slutsker, 
2007).  Listeriosis is especially dangerous to pregnant women, as it affects the fetus. Infection can 
result in fetal loss/stillbirth (20% of cases), premature delivery or neonatal infection (63% of cases). 
While the mother usually experiences flu-like or mild illness, the infection for neonatal is serious 
and can be fatal (Painter & Slutsker, 2007). Early-onset (illness at birth or shortly after) usually 
presents as sepsis meanwhile late-onset (illness from one to several weeks after birth) is typically 
meningitis. In the case of healthy adults, being exposed to contaminated food usually results in 
some gastrointestinal problems without any illness; however, it is not uncommon to experience 
==PAGE:8==
 
8 
gastroenteritis, especially in the case of immunocompromised people (Painter & Slutsker, 2007; 
Allerberger, 2007). 
1.2.3 Past recalls of Listeria in the EU/EEA 
According to EFSA & ECDC (2018), there has been a significant increase in confirmed 
listeriosis cases in the past five to ten years in the EU/EEA territory. In 2017, listeriosis was the 
most severe zoonosis in the European Union. A total of 2,480 cases were reported, which compared 
to other common foodborne diseases like campylobacteriosis (total of 264,158 cases) or 
salmonellosis (total of 91,662 cases) is not as much in absolute numbers. However, listeriosis was 
found to be the one with highest hospitalization (98.6%) and mortality rate (13.8%).  
In general, in EU the number of reported cases was similar in the years 2017 and 2016, and 
both of them were higher than the numbers from year 2015 and 2014. These, in turn, are higher than 
the number of reported cases in 2013 (EFSA & ECDC, 2018). This confirms the previously 
mentioned increasing trend in the number of listeriosis cases. The exact data are presented in the

1.3 Presence of L. monocytogenes in different food groups 
1.3.1 Meat products 
 
As meat is considered one of the main high-risk foods (ILSI Research Foundation/Risk 
Science Institute, 2005), a lot of research has been carried out regarding L. monocytogenes’ growth 
rate and survival in meat products. 
An important factor influencing growth of L. monocytogenes in meat products is the 
temperature of incubation (storage). According to Skalina & Nikolajeva (2010), the growth of 
Listeria monocytogenes in smoked ham was higher in the one stored in 7°C than in 3°C.  Similar 
results are presented by Szczawiński et al. (2017), where Listeria’s growth rate in cooked ham in 
different temperatures (3, 6, 9, 12, and 15°C) was monitored for 16 days. The results showed that 
throughout the time the growth of Listeria in ham kept in 3°C was insignificant, but it was 
significant in other temperatures and increased the most at 15°C. Growth rate was increasing 
simultaneously with rising storage temperatures of the product. Another research report shows 
similar results, as an increase in Listeria’s population was observed in proportion to temperature 
rise (4, 15, 25 and 35°C) (Luo et al., 2015). 
Besides the temperature of incubation of food samples, time is another important factor 
influencing the growth of bacteria in meat. In all mentioned research, passing hours were working 
in favor of increasing growth: the longer incubation period, the higher population growth. 
RTE meat in general is a good medium for supporting growth of this bacterium but there are 
differences in the growth rates among different types of meat. In their research, Martins & Germano 
(2011) found out that it is more likely to get listeriosis from salami than from cooked ham. Vorst et 
al. (2006), quoted by Martins & Germano (2011), suspected that the reason was probably connected 
to salami’s higher fat content and its accumulation on slicing machines, which may create 
conditions favoring growth and spreading of Listeria. Luo et al. (2015) observed slightly higher 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
amounts of L. monocytogenes in ham, compared to sausage, whereas Uyttendaele et al. (1999) 
described a higher prevalence of Listeria in raw cured ham than in cooked ham. 
 
1.3.2 Mayonnaise 
Commercial mayonnaise is a processed food product, which is microbiologically safe and 
shelf stable. It is not likely to be contaminated with Listeria monocytogenes or to support its 
growth, due to its low pH (usually below 4.0) (Hwang, 2005; Erickson & Jenkins, 1991). Because 
of that, there is not much research about survival of Listeria spp. in mayonnaise itself. Skalina & 
Nikolajeva (2010) state that Listeria inoculated into mayonnaise dies, which confirms what Smittle 
(2000) writes in his literature review about mayonnaise and other salad dressings.  
Erickson & Jenkins (1991) evaluated inactivation of L. monocytogenes in different kinds of 
commercial mayonnaise dressings and real mayonnaise. The study showed that initial high 
concentrations (6 log10 CFU/g) of Listeria were inactivated within 72 h. This proves mayonnaise to 
be a very safe and stable commercial product. 
 
1.3.3 Meat-mayonnaise mixes 
 
Ready-to-eat refrigerated salads are usually consumed without further preparation and 
therefore are a concern as regards microbial safety. Listeria monocytogenes was found in 21% of 
mayonnaise-based salads tested (Hwang, 2005). As mentioned in the two preceding sections, meat 
is an ingredient promoting the bacterium’s growth, while mayonnaise is rather inhibiting it. 
Research and experiments have been done in the past in order to determine what would be the result 
of combining a growth-promoting and growth-inhibiting ingredient in terms of the microbial safety 
of the product.  
Erickson et al. (1993) conducted an experiment on a chicken-mayonnaise salad with 
mayonnaise concentration of around 9%, stored at two different temperatures (4°C, pH 5.65 and 
12.8°C, pH 5.23). The results showed that although the growth in the salad was suppressed at the 
low temperature (4°C) or increased at the higher temperature (12.8°C), it was still much lower than 
the growth observed in the chicken salad without the addition of mayonnaise. That indicates that 
mayonnaise added to the salad in even low concentrations (below 10% w/w)  have inhibitory 
properties against Listeria monocytogenes. 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
Similar results were obtained by Hwang (2005), examining the difference between growth 
rate of L. monocytogenes in ham and ham-mayonnaise salad. It was concluded that Listeria grows 
slower in a salad mix with a ham to mayonnaise ratio of 3:1 than the ham alone, and that these 
findings suggest that mayonnaise may be used as a factor for decreasing, but not completely 
preventing, growth of L. monocytogenes in high-risk food products. 
Other similar studies showed that pH of the mayonnaise has a big effect on the duration of lag 
phase of Listeria’s growth (Skalina & Nikolajeva, 2010) and that bacterial growth was slower in 
salads with lower pH values, compared to higher pH values (Hwang & Huang, 2014). 
 
1.4 Factors inhibiting growth of Listeria relevant for ready-to-eat 
salads 
1.4.1 Acidic conditions 
1.4.1.1 pH 
 
Listeria monocytogenes, like many other bacteria, grows optimally in the environment with 
pH close to neutral. However, it is very tolerant to a wide range of pH values and its growth has 
been reported in products with pH between 4.0 and 9.6. Although the growth of L. monocytogenes 
has not been reported in environments with pH values below 4.0, it is able to survive in more acidic 
conditions (Lado & Yousef, 2007).  
Smittle (2000) mentions in his review that Listeria can develop an acid-adaptive response 
when exposed to mildly acidic conditions. This results in developing cells being capable of 
surviving for a longer period of time in pH as low as 3.5.  
The lowest values of pH the bacteria can grow in, though, depend on other storage 
conditions. According to Lado & Yousef (2007), stoppage of growth can already be seen at pH of 
5.5 if some other conditions (for example temperature) are not optimal for L. monocytogenes’s 
survival. It is also stated that refrigeration temperatures, although being inhibitive for growth, favor 
Listeria’s survival in acidic conditions.  
Hwang & Marmer (2007) found out that the inhibiting effect of mayonnaise’s pH is 
apparent when the combination of other influencing factors like storage temperature and food 
composition are less optimal for Listeria. They reported that this effect decreases when one of the 
growth conditions is improved (for example, the temperature of incubation is higher than the one 
typical for refrigerators). They also reported that pH value [of mayonnaise] may not have a 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
significant effect on the bacteria, depending on the food product - in their research, it was the case 
for an egg-mayonnaise salad. It was discovered that the temperature of storage has the most 
important inhibiting effect on L. monocytogenes and its ability to develop.  
The authors of the research also stress that the moment of when Listeria contaminates the 
product may be crucial for evaluating the efficiency of the inhibiting effect the mayonnaise has on 
the product. If the contamination would happen to the ready product, it is possible that Listeria 
would not multiply in it, as it would adhere mostly on the surface of mayonnaise, which does not 
support the growth. However, if the bacteria would contaminate one of the ingredients belonging to 
high-risk foods, like meat or eggs, before it gets mixed with mayonnaise, then the growth could 
occur and the influence of low pH of mayonnaise would be much lesser (Hwang & Marmer, 2007). 
 
1.4.1.2 Organic acids and their salts 
 
Organic acids, such as: acetic acid, propionic acid, citric acid, lactic acid, benzoic acid and 
sorbic acid are commonly used in the food industry to ensure product safety. Their addition to 
various food items extends shelf life of products and prevents the growth of pathogens. Usage of 
organic acids is generally safe, if applied according to Good Manufacturing Practice; however, it is 
important to control the amounts added to the products, as it may affect organoleptic properties of 
food (Hwang & Huang, 2013; Smittle, 2000; Vasilopoulos et al., 2013). 
According to Smittle (2000), Listeria monocytogenes, at equal pH values, is affected by 
different acids in the following order: acetic > lactic > citric, where acetic was mentioned as the 
most detrimental one.  
Vermeulen et al. (2007) reported that acetic acid is the most effective inhibitor of pathogenic 
bacteria in the acidic environment and pH values common for mayonnaise-based salads. According 
to their research, most L. monocytogenes strains have similar acetic acid resistance (0.4%) and even 
a small concentration of this acid (0.2%) in the product reduces the probability of growth. Another 
source stated that the addition of 0.1% of acetic acid has inhibitory properties, and the effect is 
rising proportionally with the temperature of storage (Smittle, 2000).  
Salts of lactic acids are used in meat, and their addition to food items does not change the 
pH of the product. The study of Hwang et al. (2012) found out that immersion of meat in lactic acid 
in concentrations above 1.5% may be implemented to increase microbiological safety of foods 
supporting growth of Listeria monocytogenes. Sodium and potassium lactate addition in 
concentrations of 4% have been found to have listeriostatic effect at refrigeration temperatures, and 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
low temperatures have been reported to enhance this effect in meat products (Lado & Yousef, 
2007).  
 
1.4.2 Salt 
 
Salt has been used for a long time as a preservative. Its addition to a product reduces the 
growth of L. monocytogenes by decreasing water activity of food (Lado & Yousef, 2007; Harper & 
Getty, 2012). As mentioned earlier, Listeria is very tolerant to low aw and high salt concentrations 
(growth in up to 10%). Since salt has been found to have negative effects on human health, the 
attempts of food producers to lower the amount of salt in their products have become a common 
practice (Harper & Getty, 2012).  
Hristo et al. (2013) presented their research evaluating the effect of salt on growth and 
survival of L. monocytogenes in raw dried cured sausages and it was discovered that salt 
concentration in the product (between 2.46 to 6.28%) was not affecting Listeria’s growth in the 
product. Therefore, the addition of salt may be a slightly inhibiting factor for Listeria’s growth but 
in most of the standard products, where the concentration of salt is low, it will not be enough to 
prevent the bacterium’s growth (Liu et al., 2005). Therefore, addition of sodium chloride should be 
used as an additional preventive method but not the only one. 
 
1.4.3 Sodium Nitrite & Potassium Nitrite 
 
Sodium nitrate is a preservative used in food industry for products like meat and fish. It 
shows slight inhibitory properties towards Listeria monocytogenes mainly by prolonging lag phase 
and increasing generation time, especially in combination with low temperature or pH value (Lado 
& Yousef, 2007).  
Christieans et al. (2018) tested the effect of potassium nitrite and potassium nitrate on L. 
monocytogenes in dry fermented sausages. While nitrate did not show inhibiting properties towards 
L. monocytogenes, it was discovered that the addition of nitrite to the product prevented L. 
monocytogenes from growing. This suggested that nitrite had inhibitory effects towards the bacteria 
and may be useful in controlling the growth of Listeria monocytogenes in the product. 
 
1.4.4 Competitive microflora 
 
Presence of Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB) in the product can inhibit the growth of other 
pathogenic and food spoilage microorganisms in food products. This phenomenon is called 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
microbial antagonism and its concept is based on inhibitory effect of other microorganisms by 
competing for nutrients or producing microbial metabolites. Generally, growth of LAB in ready-to-
eat meat products is not wanted as it can cause food spoilage. Yet, at refrigeration temperatures, 
most of the LAB strains do not grow but they still produce various metabolites. Therefore, adding 
these bacteria to RTE products intended to be kept in the fridge may have positive inhibitory effects 
(Amézquita & Brashears, 2002). 
Amézquita and Brashears (2002), conducted an experiment which results’ showed that in all 
evaluated RTE meat products there was a significant difference in growth of Listeria 
monocytogenes between the samples with or without added LAB (mix of three strains). Nilsson et 
al. (1999) obtained similar results, with 5 tested LAB strains being able to control Listeria’s growth. 
However, Harris et al. (1989) reported that not all strains of lactic acid bacteria used in their 
research had an inhibitory effect on L. monocytogenes. These differences in previous studies 
indicate that it is important to properly choose the LAB strains to ensure a desired inhibitory effect 
on Listeria monocytogenes and no spoilage of the product (Amézquita & Brashears, 2002).

All experiments were conducted in DTU laboratories in building 204 on Lyngby campus,

2.1 Food products 
 
Experiments were carried out using two types of food items, which are considered the main 
ingredients of the salad: ham and mayonnaise. All products were bought in one of the main chain 
grocery stores in Denmark (Fakta). 
Ham bought in stores usually contains various additives and preservatives like organic acids 
or salt, which may have an impact on growth of Listeria. It was decided that the experiment would 
be conducted as a worse-case-scenario for the bacterium’s growth in RTE mayonnaise-based salad 
and therefore 100% pure pork meat was used instead of ham. The labels taken from products are 
shown on figures 2 and 3.

2.2 Preparation and heat treatment of meat 
 
For both the pre-experiment and main experiments, the meat was prepared in the same way. 
It was washed under the running water, cut into small pieces (cubes of approximately 2 by 2 cm) 
and put into a few sterile vacuum package bags (PA/PE, PM PACK A/S, Denmark) - approximately 
100 g of product in each. The product was then vacuum packed and placed in the water bath for 30 
minutes in 65°C. After the heat treatment, the meat was taken out of the water bath, cooled in the 
bucket with ice and placed in the refrigerator at 5°C until the next day. That was done 
simultaneously with incubation of the preculture, to have the product prepared when the 
standardized cell suspensions were ready to be used. 
 
2.3 Preparation of Listeria monocytogenes culture 
 
According to the European Union Reference Laboratory (EURL) for L. monocytogenes (2014), 
experiments conducted to assess growth potential of L. monocytogenes should be performed with a 
mixture of at least two strains, to account for possible variations and differences among the strains. 
Therefore, for the purpose of the experiment, three strains of Listeria monocytogenes were used as 
the inoculum: 
==PAGE:18==
 
18 
§ Nr 46: DTU strain collection MS22246 is serotype 1/2a, isolated from process environment; 
§ Nr 58: DTU strain collection MS22258 is serotype 1/2a, isolated from trout; 
§ Nr 54: DTU strain collection MS22254 is serotype 4b, isolated from smoked pork breast. 
 
Preparation of L. monocytogenes cultures was the same in the case of pre-experiment and both 
main experiments. The precultures of these three strains were taken out of the freezer. One ml of 
each strain was added to 40 ml of Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) (Thermo Fischer, USA) three tubes 
with three separate strain precultures in total) and placed into the incubator in 37°C for 24 hours. 
After that time, tubes with precultures were moved into a fridge (5°C) and left there for further 
incubation for around 67 hours. 
After the incubation process, the precultures were taken out of the fridge, diluted tenfold in 
BHI and the A450 nm was measured in the spectrophotometer (Thermo Spectronic) for each of the 
strains, using BHI as a blanking liquid. 
Further, 10 ml of every preculture was put into separate centrifuge tubes. They were 
centrifuged for 5 minutes at 9000 rpm at room temperature (20°C). After the supernatant was 
discarded from all of the precultures, the pellet was resuspended by adding the appropriate amount 
of 0.9% saline to each tube. The amounts needed to be added (x ml) to each tube were calculated 
based on the initial measurements of A450 nm for every preculture separately, using the following 
equation:

2.4 Pre-experiment  
 
2.4.1 Purpose and research question 
 
Two inoculation methods were considered for the main experiment. First option was to 
inoculate a large sample of meat with Listeria monocytogenes and then divide it into smaller 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
samples for all sampling days; the other was to first divide the meat into smaller samples and then 
inoculate each of them separately. While the former was quicker to perform, it was not sure whether 
it would be as precise and reliable as the latter one. Therefore, a research question was posed:  
Can Listeria monocytogenes be distributed well in a large (around 400 g) sample of meat?; 
and a pre-experiment was conducted in order to answer that question. 
 
2.4.2 Description 
 
Preparation of L. monocytogenes culture used for inoculation of meat, as well as preparation 
and heat treatment of meat were done in the same way as described previously (see: preparation of 
L. monocytogenes culture; preparation and heat treatment of meat).  
After these steps, the cooled meat was inoculated with L. monocytogenes, mixed thoroughly 
with a spoon for 1 minute and left for 30 minutes for the bacteria to adjust. Afterwards, smaller 
samples were taken out of the big sample of meat in a way where different samples were taken from 
different parts of the meat container. 10 different samples of 10 g each were measured, numbered 
from 1 to 10 and put into stomacher bags. To each stomacher bag, 90 ml of FKP was added, the bag 
was placed in a stomacher machine and stomached for 60 s on high speed. From such mixture, from 
each sample a volume of 0.1 ml was taken and plated onto two Listeria selective Palcam (Statens 
Serum Institute (SSI), Denmark) and two Tryptic Soy Agar (TSA) plates (Thermo Fischer, USA) 
(total volume: 0.2 ml on two Palcam plates and 0.2 ml on two TSA plates). 
The plates were plated with 0.1 ml of material each, as it was not sure what growth should 
be expected, therefore the volume was divided onto two plates. Nevertheless, the colonies amount 
were counted together from both plates and treated as if it was one (total plated sample volume of 
0.2 ml).  
To check the obtained concentration of inoculum, pure cultures of Listeria monocytogenes 
were diluted and plated onto Palcam plates. The results of pre-experiment are presented in section 
3.2.

2.5 Main experiment 
 
2.5.1 Description 
 
Taking into consideration what Hwang & Marmer (2007) wrote about the importance of at 
what point of production the mayonnaise-based salad gets contaminated, it was decided to perform 
the experiment assuming the worst-case scenario. That means that this research will be done 
assuming that L. monocytogenes contaminated the meat before it was mixed with mayonnaise, and 
therefore preparation of mayonnaise-meat samples will be done by mixing parts of inoculated meat 
with parts of uninoculated mayonnaise. 
Preparation of L. monocytogenes culture and heat treatment of meat was performed in the 
same way as previously described. 
It was decided that all samples will be measured on inoculation day and stored in identical 
stomacher bags (80 ml, LDPE, Seward, UK) in the refrigerator (5ºC). Each sample consisted of 10 
(± 0.5) g of product in total. Four types of main samples were prepared for the main experiment: 
§ Meat sample: 10 g of heat-treated pork meat 
§ 20% mix sample: a mix consisting of 20% (2 g) of uninoculated mayonnaise and 80% (8 g) 
of inoculated pork meat; 
§ 40% mix sample: a mix consisting of 40% (4 g) of uninoculated mayonnaise and 60% (6 g) 
of inoculated pork meat; 
§ Mayonnaise sample: 10 g of inoculated mayonnaise. 
In addition to these, four samples consisting of 10 g of uninoculated mayonnaise each and four 
samples of 10 g of uninoculated ham each were made (to be tested on first and last day of storage). 
 
First, samples containing uninoculated products were made. That included samples of meat and 
mayonnaise (four of each), to be tested on day 0 and day 14, as well as samples of ham, 
mayonnaise, 20% mix and 40% mix (one of each), for measuring the pH values. 
Since 20- and 40% mix samples in the experiment were decided to consist of inoculated ham and 
uninoculated mayonnaise, the second step was to measure the “clean” mayonnaise into stomacher 
bags (2 g and 4 g of product into each bag, depending on which type of sample was it). 
After heat treatment, 400 g of meat was measured into the large bowl, inoculated with L. 
monocytogenes culture, mixed thoroughly with a spoon for one minute and left for 30 minutes to

in appendix 2.  
Afterwards, meat was measured into stomacher bags as standalone sample, as well as a part

2.7 Additional tests 
 
In the second experiment, a major growth of another kind of bacteria was observed on TSA 
plates. Due to the fact that meat used at the on-set experiment was at the end of its expiry date, it 
was suspected that there may be some kind of endogenous LAB in the samples. To find out which 
kinds of bacteria were found in the products, a few colonies from different sampling days and 
different dilutions were taken from TSA plates, plated on Blood Agar plates (Thermo Fischer, 
USA) with a streak method, and left in the incubator for 48 hours (same time as they were 
incubated during the experiment). Then, some additional tests were performed on the bacteria to 
pinpoint their more exact kind. 
 
2.7.1 Aerobic and anaerobic growth 
 
To check the ability of these bacteria to grow in conditions with or without oxygen, two sets 
of plates were put into the incubator: one in a standard way allowing the oxygen for the growth; the 
other set of plates was put in the special system - AnaeroGen™ 2.5L - which is generating 
anaerobic conditions, and then put in the incubator. 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
2.7.2 Shape and motility 
 
This is a basic test which was conducted by visual observation under the digital microscope 
(phase contact, 100 x magnification, VisiScope, VWR), in order to determine bacteria’s shape and 
motility. Colonies were put onto separate microscope slides and examined under the microscope.  
 
2.7.3 Determining the Gram type 
 
Instead of using the commonly known Gram stain method, a KOH test was used to decide 
whether bacteria belonged to Gram (+) or Gram (-) type. To perform the test, 3% KOH was put on 
microscope slides and then colonies were put into the drops and thoroughly mixed. If 
microorganisms belonged to G (-) type, the mixture was gelling. If stickiness was not observed, 
then bacteria were G (+) (Buck, 1982).   
 
2.7.4 Catalase test 
 
This test detects catalase enzyme in microorganisms’ cells. If bacteria have this enzyme, 
they are able to break down hydrogen peroxide (Bisen et al., 2012). If the test result is positive for 
presence of catalase, bubbles can be observed. To conduct the test, colonies were placed onto 
microscope slides and 3% hydrogen peroxide was added on top of them. 
 
2.7.5 Oxidase test 
 
This test is detecting presence of the enzyme in bacteria cells – cytochrome oxidase – and if it is 
present, the oxidase reagent undergoes the process of oxidization and changes its color from 
colorless to purple (Bisen et al., 2012). In this test, colonies were placed onto a special oxidase test 
stripes (Bactident Oxidase, Merck KGaA, Germany), which indicated recoloration or its absence. 
 
2.7.6 Oxidation – Fermentation (OF) test 
 
In this test, the bacteria are differentiated by checking if they metabolize carbohydrates – 
and if so, then whether it is done through the process of fermentation or oxidation. If the bacteria 
are able to reduce sugars, the medium changes its color from green to yellow (Leboffe & Pierce, 
2019). Each bacteria type was put into two tubes filled with Hugh and Leifson’s OF medium and 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
then a layer of mineral oil was put on the top of this medium in one of the tubes, in order to create 
anaerobic conditions for fermentation. Tubes were placed into the incubator in 37ºC for 48 hours 
and then the results were checked. 
 
All results of these tests are presented in table 8 in section 3.7. 
 
2.8 Comparison of experimental growth data with exisiting predictive 
models 
 
2.8.1 Assumptions for microbiological predictions of bacteria 
 
In order to compare the microbial results obtained through the experiments described in this 
thesis with existing predictive models and achieve the most realistic and reliable fittings, the 
following assumptions have been made: 
§ In commercial mayonnaise, there is 1 teaspoon of vinegar per 200 g of mayonnaise 
(https://www.epicurious.com/recipes/food/views/mayonnaise-241083); 
§ 1 teaspoon of vinegar is 5 ml (5 g); 
§ Acetic acid accounts for 5% of vinegar (https://da.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddike); 
§ pKa of acetic acid equals 4.76 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid), and since the pH 
of all samples apart from mayonnaise are higher than this pKa value, at least 50% of all 
acetic acid will be present in the water phase; 
§ Sodium and water content in meat used for experiment was the same as found in database 
table for food items (https://frida.fooddata.dk); 
§ Temperature of storage of samples was 4.4ºC – it is the average from thermolog values 
measured during 14-day period of sample incubation. 
 
2.8.2 Data analysis 
 
For each type of sample, bacterial colonies on both types of agar plates were counted and 
converted to log (CFU/g) values, which were later calculated and presented in log (CFU/g) increase 
values. In case there were no colonies found on the plate, a concentration of 1 CFU/g was used for 
calculations. This concentration was also set as the detection limit, 1.44 log CFU/g, apart from 
mayonnaise samples which had a lower detection limit of 1.14 log CFU/g after day 3 in the first 
experiment and after day 0 in the second experiment.  
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
Since each type of samples was analyzed twice every sampling day (sample A and B), the 
final value used in the data analyses was an average from these two results.  
To decide whether the data were statistically significant, average log CFU/g values and 
standard deviations were calculated in Microsoft Excel program. Also, a one-sided analysis of 
variance (ANOVA) test was performed, also in Microsoft Excel program, where it was necessary to 
compare the data. A significance level of 5% (p < 0.05) was used for evaluation of the differences.  
 
2.8.3 Data fitting 
 
Logarithmic increase or decrease values in tested samples were applied to DMFit 
(https://browser.combase.cc/DMFit.aspx), a curve fitting software. This was done to evaluate the 
experimental results and get a maximum growth rate for different sample types, in order to compare 
them with existing predictive models and literature data. Fitting the data from the growth curves 
also provided additional information on bacteria, such as the duration of lag phase and the amount 
of time which would take to reach some critical log levels of pathogens in food samples.  
Predictions were applied by using log increases measured on Palcam agar both in the first 
and second experiment. The results of fitting obtained data into curve showed big differences in 
growth rate between the samples from first and second experiment. Depending on sample type, 
different models were applied to fit the curve. Wherever possible, Baranyi and Roberts model 
(Baranyi & Roberts, 1994) without asymptote was used, as there was not enough data from the 
experiment to assume that the growth in tested samples reached its maximum and would stabilize 
after day 14. Some samples were chosen to be fitted according to Baranyi and Roberts model 
(Baranyi & Roberts, 1994) without lag phase or using linear model, when the data suggested that 
the growth was so quick that there was not enough information to predict the duration of the lag 
phase.  
 
2.8.4 Predictive models: ComBase and FSSP 
 
ComBase Predictor and FSSP (Food Spoilage and Safety Predictor) are both tools which take 
into consideration various parameters of products, e.g., pH value, temperature, salt and acetic acid 
concentration, to ensure as precise predictions of pathogenic growth as possible. The pH value and 
temperature of incubation of samples were measured during the experiments. Estimates for the salt 
in the water phase (WPS) and acetic acid concentrations were calculated using Frida food database 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
(https://frida.fooddata.dk). It is important to remember that the calculations are based on some 
assumptions previously made (see: section 2.9.1), therefore they may not be 100% accurate.

increased during the storage time by 0.01 log, 0.25 log and 0.4 log in meat, 20- and 40% mix

During the second experiment, microflora in the mayonnaise sample was more stable than in 
the one from the first experiment. L. monocytogenes concentration measured on Palcam agar was 
reducing over time and became undetectable after the third day of incubation of samples; therefore, 
all bacteria counted after 96 hours belonged to other species. Most of the sampling days, the count 
of microflora was oscillating between 0.12 log reduction and 0.03 log increase, which gives 0.15 
log difference in bacterial concentration throughout the duration of experiment. The only 
measurement, oddly low in comparison to other ones, was observed after 7 days of storage.

4 Discussion 
With the rising consumption and production of RTE foods, the risk of falling ill with listeriosis 
is higher as well. A significantly increasing trend of listeriosis on the EU/EEA territory has been 
observed in the last 10 years (EFSA & ECDC, 2018). RTE products, especially cooked meat, are a 
serious safety concern, as they support the growth of L. monocytogenes, have long shelf-life at 
refrigerated temperature and do not require further heat treatment before consuming (Zhu et al., 
2005). This study was done in order to evaluate the influence of mayonnaise on the growth of L. 
monocytogenes in meat products and the microbiological safety of such mix, representing a 
mayonnaise-based, ready-to-eat salad. The results of this experiment showed that mayonnaise did 
slow down the growth of L. monocytogenes, but the effect and the level of inhibition was not 
always the same or comparable between the two trials. It was discovered that there may be various 
other conditions which affect bacterial growth and certain conditions must be fulfilled in order to 
expect a significant inhibitory effect from mayonnaise.  
 
Growth of L. monocytogenes in meat 
In this study, it was found that L. monocytogenes was able to grow in all tested samples 
which included meat, kept at the refrigerated temperature of 4.4ºC. The increase in the number of 
bacteria in heat-treated pork meat varied from 4.66 to 5.36 log CFU/g, depending on the experiment 
number and type of agar (selective or non-selective) used for counting bacterial numbers. The 
difference in the growth in the samples of same kind of meat, kept under the same temperature 
conditions and time was 0.7 log, which was quite big and unexpected, and it did not depend on 
counting medium since growth on selective and non-selective agar was found to be almost the 
same. This result suggested that there may be other factors, for example freshness of the pork or its 
pH, which had a great influence on the development of bacteria in meat and, as a result, in samples 
containing meat. 
For example, growth to higher numbers was observed in the second experiment, which may 
be connected to worse meat quality than in the first experiment. Although the type of pork meat was 
exactly the same (a portion of neck fillet of around 800-900 g each), the pH of the meat and, as a 
result of that of all mixed samples, was higher in the second experiment. The difference in the pH 
value of meat itself was 0.34 units but as the optimum growth for L. monocytogenes is close to 7.0 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
(Lado & Yousef, 2007), even such little change brings the conditions closer to the optimum and 
may increase the growth of this pathogen. 
Higher pH of meat could have been connected to poorer conditions in which the animal was 
kept. Hartung et al. (2009) reported that animal welfare has a big influence on quality of the meat, 
and that the conditions of housing system, transport, stunning and slaughter have an impact on pH 
of meat. For example, meat coming from animals which experience a lot of stress due to being 
transported, stunned or slaughtered, tend to have higher pH in comparison to the meat from animals 
which were treated in a less stressful way. Therefore, since meat was not bought at the same time, it 
was probably coming from another batch of production and there are many things that may have 
happened to the animal that made it anxious and resulted in a worse microbiological quality of 
meat.  
Another thing which may have influenced the pH value of the pork was its freshness. Meat 
bought for the second experiment was at the end of its shelf-life, as the day of heat treatment was 
the day of its expiration. Kandeepan & Biswas (2007) reported that buffalo meat kept at refrigerated 
temperature (4ºC) showed gradual increase of its pH with the increasing storage time. According to 
Borch et al. (1996), vacuum-packed pork meat’s shelf-life is shorter than e.g. beef’s, as glycogen 
and glucose decrease at a faster rate, which leads to quicker amino acids degradation and, in 
consequences, to faster rise of pH value. Therefore, it is also possible that higher pH value of 
product in the second experiment was connected to its upcoming expiry date. 
The obtained values of log increase in meat (between 4.66 and 5.36) after two weeks of 
storage time were quite close to what Luo et al. (2015) found in their research. They reported the 
increase in L. monocytogenes concentration in cooked ham stored at 4ºC to be 5.96 ± 0.5 log 
increase after 22 days of storage, and from the graphs illustrating the growth of L. monocytogenes, 
it can be assumed that the increase after 14 days of incubation of samples reached around 4 ± 0.5 
log. Also, the findings of this thesis are similar to Hwang’s research (2005), where a similar 
experiment was conducted to the one presented in this thesis, to evaluate the influence of 
mayonnaise on growth of Listeria monocytogenes in ham. It was discovered that ham stored in 4ºC 
reached a 4-log increase in the number of L. monocytogenes after 12 days. However, experimental 
values of bacterial increase obtained during the experiments differed drastically from results of 
experiment performed by Szczawiński et al. (2017) who found that Listeria monocytogenes counts 
in cooked ham did not increase almost at all during storage in 3ºC for 16 days. Yet, it should be 
noted that in all three mentioned research, the main product supporting L. monocytogenes’ growth 
==PAGE:41==
 
41 
was ham. Compared to the product used in this thesis, which was pure pork, ham usually contains 
various additives, like nitrite, salt, or lactate, which have inhibitory influence on growth of L. 
monocytogenes. Hams used in mentioned research included at least one of the mentioned inhibitors, 
and sometimes a mix of more, therefore it is not surprising that the growth of Listeria was found to 
be lesser than what was observed in the experimental part of this thesis. 
 
Growth of L. monocytogenes in meat-mayonnaise mixes 
In the conducted study, L. monocytogenes showed growth increase between 2.39 and 4.00 
log in a mix of 80 % pork meat and 20 % commercial mayonnaise, and between 1.02 and 3.08 log 
in a mix of 60 % pork meat and 40 % mayonnaise. Since these increases are lower than in the 
cooked meat samples, it shows that addition of mayonnaise indeed lowered the increase in the 
number of L. monocytogenes, but the extent to which it worked varied and other factors need to be 
taken into consideration when evaluating the safety of such mixtures.  
The effect of pH and differences between the samples were discussed already for meat and it 
was the factor which increased the pH values for mixed samples. Therefore, higher pH of samples 
was probably the reason of higher increase in concentration of L. monocytogenes in those samples.  
Experimental results on growth increase turned out to be a bit higher than data provided by 
other researchers. Erickson et al. (1993) achieved increase in counts in the level of 2-log increase in 
a chicken-mayonnaise salad with around 9% concentration of mayonnaise. This was a lower 
increase than the one observed in this thesis in 20% mayonnaise mix, which was between 2.39 and 
4.00-log increase. This was contradictory to the observations from this thesis, which suggested that 
more addition of mayonnaise should inhibit L. monocytogenes’ growth in higher degree. However, 
the salad components in Erickson et al. (1993) were a bit more complex, as it contained other 
ingredients which may have an inhibitory effect on bacteria’s growth, like lemon juice and salt, so 
low increase may be a result of mixing only a few inhibiting factors.  
Another study reported a 3-log increase after 10 days and a 5-log increase after 21 days of 
storage at 4ºC in a salad containing 25% mayonnaise and 75% eggs with the pH of mix being 6.3 at 
the beginning of the experiment (no data about changes in the pH value during storage) (Hwang & 
Marmer, 2007). The pH of the mix in that study was slightly higher than the one of 20% mix 
investigated in this thesis in the first experiment (pH 5.9), and probably similar to the 20% mix 
sample in the second experiment (pH 6.24, according to the assumption), but the bacterial increase 
after 14 days reaching around 3.5 log is in between the results obtained in this thesis, which was 
==PAGE:42==
 
42 
2.43- and 4.0-log increase on Palcam, and 2.39- and 3.98-log increase on TSA, in first and second 
experiment respectively. It should be also noted that Hwang and Marmer’s experiment was 
conducted with a different product (egg not pork) in the mix, therefore it may have an influence on 
L. monocytogenes growth.  
Hwang (2005), in previously mentioned experiment regarding the effect of mayonnaise on 
pathogenic growth in ham, found out that depending on the pH of mayonnaise, it is possible to slow 
down, but not prevent the growth of L. monocytogenes, which seems to be consistent with the 
overall conclusions of this thesis. Using mayonnaise with pH of 3.8, 4.2 and 4.6, the 4-log increase 
was slowed down from 12 days in ham alone to 26, 21 and 17 days, respectively, in samples stored 
at 4ºC. 
 
Growth of L. monocytogenes in mayonnaise 
Contrary to meat containing products, mayonnaise was not found to be supportive to the 
growth of Listeria monocytogenes, which was not a surprise. The pH of the mayonnaise was 4.0 or 
4.3, depending on the experiment, which are the values on the lower end of what L. monocytogenes 
can multiply in. Additionally, mayonnaise naturally contains acidic acid (in vinegar, which is used 
to make the product), and it had the addition of salt and citric acid, which all have been reported to 
be inhibitory towards L. monocytogenes (Smittle, 2000; EURL Lm, 2014). 
This study found that L. monocytogenes injected into the mayonnaise was, in most cases, 
below detection limit between day 3 (after 72 hours) and day 5 (after 120 hours). This agrees with 
findings described by Erickson & Jenkins (1991) who observed a 6 log (full) decrease of L. 
monocytogenes concentration injected in mayonnaise after 72 hours of storage. Since the inoculum 
concentration in the thesis experimental part was not as high as the one described in the study, there 
is no possibility to compare decrease rates but the outcome of the experiment, meaning a decrease 
in number below detection limit, became the same. 
However, in the first experiment conducted for the purpose of this thesis, the number of L. 
monocytogenes on TSA plates was irregular and colonies were found throughout the whole 
experiment. The reason may be that even if some colonies were found, the concentration was right 
at the borderline of detection limit. Therefore, it was not possible to make any assumptions on 
growth of L. monocytogenes based on the information. Also, the irregular growth pattern was not 
confirmed by three other concentration measurements (the other one from the first experiment, and 
both from the second experiment), it is possible that there has been a mistake in counting the 
==PAGE:43==
 
43 
number of Listeria colonies on the TSA plates. This idea of a possible mistake was upheld by one 
more factor, which is that during the first experiment the growth of L. monocytogenes was almost 
identical in the case of all other samples, regardless of the type of agar used for enumeration. Since 
this was the first experiment, it may have not always been clear whether the colonies which 
appeared on non-selective agar were for sure Listeria or not. Perhaps some of the uncertain types of 
colonies were immediately classified as L. monocytogenes, due to lack of experience in 
differentiating bacterial species on TSA agar, but it seems like working with the pathogen during 
laboratory experiments provided some skills in recognizing species, as it seems like such mistake 
did not take place during conducting the second experiment. 
 
Safety of salads 
When evaluating growth rates for different types of samples, it was decided to take into 
consideration the safety of potential meat-mayonnaise salads. Fitting the data in the DMFit software 
provided information on time to reach certain chosen log increase values: 0.5, 2.0 and 3.4 by 
different meat-mayonnaise mixes. A 0.5 log increase is a value which decides, whether food is 
categorized as supporting or not L. monocytogenes growth (EURL Lm, 2014). European law on 
RTE foods require that L. monocytogenes is absent in 25g sample of food before it leaves the 
immediate control of the producer, and that the maximum concentration of L. monocytogenes in the 
food will not be higher than 100 CFU/g at the end of product’s shelf-life (EC, 2005). This applies 
for all food products which are not intended for infants or for special medical purposes. The 
criterium of final concentration of 100 CFU/g for L. monocytogenes applies if it is possible for the 
manufacturer to prove that the product will not reach it. Other else, the criterium of absence in 25 g 
applies. Absence in 25 g of product matches the concentration of -1.4 log CFU/g, and 100 CFU/g 
during shelf life is a value of 2 log CFU/g (Lokerse et al., 2016).  
As known from literature and mentioned before, many meats and meat products belong to 
the kinds of food which support the growth of L. monocytogenes, and the data obtained in the 
experiments confirm it. The results show that for cooked pork meat it takes a maximum of 2.8 days 
to reach the log increase of 0.5 and the shelf life of meat is much longer. From fitting the 
experimental data, the decision on whether the prepared mixed salads would be safe for the 
consumers, depends on the shelf life picked for such salads. According to Söderqvist (2017), 
usually such premade salad mixes have the shelf life shorter than 5 days, and because of that they 
are classified as foods unable to support the growth of L. monocytogenes and need to fulfil the 
condition of not reaching the value of 2.0 log CFU/g before the end of shelf life. However, the 
==PAGE:44==
 
44 
experiment was held for this thesis for 14 days, as that was the desired shelf life suggested by one 
of the catering companies working with DTU researchers. If a salad mix would be considered as a 
food which does not support the growth of L. monocytogenes with shelf life shorter than 5 days, 
then all of the tested samples would be safe for the consumers, as all of them reached the 2-log 
increase value later than that (the shortest time would be 7 days, for the 20% mix sample in 2nd 
experiment). However, if the shelf life would be prolonged to 14 days, then the 20% mixed salad 
samples may not be considered as safe anymore. Taking into consideration 3.4 log increase in the 
case of salads, only one 20% salad (experiment no. 1) would be safe to consume at the end of the 
shelf life. As the other one reaches that increase value in 11.48 days and, therefore, cannot be 
considered not harmful, it cannot be said that 20% salad would fulfil the needed conditions for 
storage up to 14 days, because it depends on other factors, like for example freshness or pH of meat 
used for preparation of mix. However, both 40% salads turned out to not reach the 3.4 log increase 
value within two weeks.  
 
Growth rates and predictive models 
Growth rates have been obtained by fitting the experimental results data to models in the 
curve fitting software, DMFit. For one sample, a 40% mix from the first experiment, the growth 
rate seemed to be false, as it was calculated to be higher than the one for meat, although reaching a 
0.5 log increase was calculated to take 12.6 days. Therefore, one day was removed from the 
calculations (day 12) and then the result appeared to be more reasonable. This changed value was 
taken into consideration when finding the compatibility of other models with obtained data. 
The growth rates predicted in DMFit software were, of course, conclusive with the 
observations from the experiments: that with the increasing concentration of mayonnaise in the mix, 
the growth rate of Listeria monocytogenes decreased. The comparison with growth rates obtained 
from predictive models, however, did not match very well with the experimental results. 
Both the ComBase Predictor and FSSP software were used to predict the growth rates of L. 
monocytogenes in the food with two different scenarios. One, that acetic acid concentration in the 
product containing mayonnaise was not taken into consideration and therefore the value was set to 0 
ppm. This predictions were done for all three types of product: meat, 20% mix and 40% mix. The 
other scenario was, that acetic acid concentration in the water phase was equal to 50% of total 
amount of acetic acid in the product. Therefore, acetic acid was set to concentration of 1300 ppm 
and 1600 ppm for 20% and 40% mix, respectively, and this predictions was done for only these two 
==PAGE:45==
 
45 
types of products (since no acetic acid was expected to be in the meat). The calculations for 
estimated acetic acid concentrations can be seen in appendix x and they were based on assumptions, 
as the exact amount of acetic acid in mayonnaise was not known.  
In the case of predictions made with the specified acetic acid content, both predictive 
software showed a tendency to under predict the growth rate of Listeria in the products. Predictions 
being the furthest from obtained results were obtained with the use of FSSP (including acetic acid). 
In this case, all of the values for mixed samples were estimated to be lower than the values obtained 
in the experiments. The difference was especially visible in the case of 40% mix, where the 
software predicted L. monocytogenes’ growth to be 0 log/day or very close to that (exactly 0.000 
log/day for the first, and 0.006 log/day for the second experiment). The obtained values for these 
samples were, however, 0.223 and 0.254 log/day, respectively. The difference for the 20% mix 
were not as far away as for the 40% mix, however, they were still underestimated by around 0.1 
log/day. 
Similar problem appeared with the use of ComBase predictor, also in the case of including 
acetic acid content. And again, the estimated growth rate were not exactly matching values obtained 
in the experiment. Predicted values for 40% mixes were a bit closer to obtained growth rates than in 
the case of FSSP model, but they were still way too low – 0.024 and 0.048 for experiment one and 
two respectively. The growth rates predicted for the 20% mix were either underestimated (0.216 
log/day for experiment 1) or overestimated (0.336 log/day for experiment 2), compared to the 
obtained experimental value of 0.262 and 0.309 log/day for experiment one and two, respectively. 
Both ComBase Predictor and FSSP described the predicted values better when acetic acid 
content was not taken into consideration and estimated for 0 ppm. Although closer to matching 
obtained data, the FSSP model still gave underpredicted results for all types of products, including 
meat. The ComBase Predictor, on the other hand, provided three out of six values very similar to 
the obtained data - 0.408 log/day predicted to 0.416 log/day obtained for meat in the second 
experiment, and 0.216/0.264 log/day predicted to 0.223/0.254 log/day obtained for 40% mix in 
experiment one and two, respectively. The other three growth rates were overpredicted – 0.384 
log/day predicted to 0.329 log/day obtained in meat in the first experiment, and 0.336/0.432 log/day 
predicted to 0.262/0.309 log/day obtained in 20% mix in experiment 1 and 2, respectively.  
It was surprising that predicted growth rates with estimated acetic acid content were so far 
away from obtained values. It seemed like both predictive models were overestimating the 
inhibiting effect of acetic acid, since the predicted growth rates were so low, and sometimes even 
==PAGE:46==
 
46 
close to zero, which was completely untrue to what was observed during the experiments – where 
Listeria monocytogenes grew in all samples containing meat. It is of course possible that the 
assumptions about the content of acetic acid in the mayonnaise were wrong and the estimated 
values were too high, and that was the reason the predictions were not describing obtained values 
well. However, the FSSP model underpredicted growth rates in all products, with zero acetic acid 
content as well. Actually, the description in FSSP predictor states that for mayonnaise-based 
products, the model is not fitted for products with pH value lower than x, so it was suspected that 
this model would not be found very matching for the obtained results. However, ComBase did not 
have any statement, and the predicted growth rates for products with estimated acetic acid content 
were not that far better matched than the FSSP model.  
It should also be mentioned that obtaining the results with a realistic salt and acetic acid 
content would be a long time process and would need a lot of measurements and research. The 
process would include a deeper understanding of mayonnaise contents, like fat and water, and also 
the mechanism of acetic acid available in the water phase of mayonnaise, which would be affecting 
L. monocytogenes’ growth. Also, to get realistic values, acetic acid and pH value would need to be 
measured in all of the products throughout the duration of the experiment, to be able to trace the 
changes meticulously. 
All in all, it seems like none of these predictive tools can be used for mayonnaise-based 
meat salads, as none of them described obtained data well. If one model would have to be chosen, it 
would be ComBase Predictor with 0 acetic acid content. Out of four possibilities tested, that was the 
only one which gave half of the values close to obtained ones, ale the other half was overestimated. 
In that way, the predicted growth rates would not be perfectly matched, but they would probably be 
estimated on the safe side, as the predicted growth of Listeria monocytogenes would be faster.  
 
Growth of additional microflora 
Contrary to the first experiment, where the occurrence of microflora other than Listeria was 
rare and very low counts were observed mostly in mayonnaise sample, the second experiment 
revealed that some additional bacteria were developing in the product. Because the count of the 
unknown other species of bacteria was definitely quite high some of the sampling days, it was 
decided to make additional tests on these microorganisms, in order to get a better knowledge on 
which type of bacteria was found in the product.  
==PAGE:47==
 
47 
At first, the idea, and suspicion, was that these would be lactic acid bacteria, taking into 
consideration that meat was vacuum-packaged and not too fresh at the beginning of the experiment 
and it was possible that some spoilage reactions started in the product. However, further tests 
revealed that the high count of bacteria most probably belong to either Kurthia or Corynebacterium 
species. 
Kurthia can be naturally found in meat and meat products, as well as feces of certain animals 
like chickens and pigs, taken from the soil. As regards the meat, this bacterium was frequently 
isolated from the product, particularly after a few day storage in abused temperature (16ºC) 
(Stackerbrandt et al., 2011). It has been reported that Kurthia may take part in the process of 
spoilage of meat, either fresh or processed, but there is no evidence of Kurthia spp. being 
pathogenic to humans (Voss et al., 2011; Stackerbrandt et al., 2006). However, both Voss et al. 
(2011) and Stackerbrandt et al. (2006) unanimously reported that Kurthia is strictly aerobic, which 
would indicate that it could not have been the bacteria observed in the products, as it grew in 
anaerobic conditions. 
Corynebacterium occurs naturally in meat, dairy products and soil. These bacteria have been 
isolated from refrigerated food and have an ability to grow at the temperature of 5ºC. Within the 
genus of Corynebacterium, there is a human pathogenic species Corynebacterium diphtheriae, 
which produces toxins and causes a sickness called diphtheria. Apart from this strain, some of the 
other strains have also been found to be pathogenic for plants and animals (Alibi et al., 2016; Kraft 
et al., 1966). Some of the species of Corynebacterium have been reported to have survived a heat 
treatment of 63ºC for 30 minutes (Alford et al., 1955), therefore, if this is the bacteria which was 
contaminating the meat, the executed heat treatment might not have killed all the cells. Some strains 
of Corynebacterium have been reported to be used in food industry for production of e.g. lysine or 
L-glutamic acid (Alibi et al., 2016), which would be a possible explanation of the increased pH 
value of meat. 
Although the source with the table of first-stage tests (Barrow & Feltham, 1993) suggested that 
the bacteria found may be either Kurthia or Corynebacterium, further research showed that Kurthia 
should not be taken into consideration as a possibility, as it cannot grow anaerobically (and tests 
showed that bacteria found in the meat was able to do so). Also, it has been found that 
Corynebacterium is heat resistant enough to survive through the process of heat treatment done to 
raw meat, and could have been responsible for increased pH value of the product. The high growth 
of bacteria within the two weeks of storage was the easiest to observe in the uninoculated meat. 
==PAGE:48==
 
48 
Unfortunately, there is no information on exact final concentration of bacteria in uninoculated meat 
sample from the second experiment, as the counts were done in only one dilution. Pictures of the 
plates with the growth of bacteria in uninoculated meat sample on day 0 and day 14 from 
experiment no. 2 can be seen in appendix 3.  
Regarding mayonnaise and its microflora, some colonies, most probably Bacillus, have been 
found in the samples throughout the storage period of mayonnaise. The counts have been rather low 
and very irregular as regards the growth, therefore, it is suspected that that was just the presence of 
naturally occurring microflora in mayonnaise. There were no signs of spoilage or increasing growth 
in the samples throughout storage time, and the final counts of bacteria in mayonnaise were similar 
for uninoculated mayonnaise sample (log increase of 0.29 and decrease of 0.24) and the samples 
inoculated with L. monocytogenes (log increase of 0.03 or decrease of 0.32).

5 Conclusion 
In this thesis, the influence of mayonnaise on the growth of L. monocytogenes in meat-
mayonnaise RTE salads has been investigated. The results showed that the higher the concentration 
of mayonnaise in the product, the more inhibitory effect it had on the growth of L. monocytogenes. 
Concentrations of L. monocytogenes in tested products increased after 14-days at 4.4°C by 4.66-
5.42 log CFU/g in meat, 2.39-4.00 log CFU/g in 20% mix and 1.02-3.08 log CFU/g in 40% mix. 
The level of inhibition was different in the two experiments, and the difference in increase between 
these two experiments was from around 0.8-log to 2-log, depending on type of the product. These 
variances in between the same products were important especially when evaluating microbiological 
safety, according to EU laws on limits of L. monocytogenes presence in RTE foods. These results 
indicated also that the level of inhibition did not depend only on the concentration of mayonnaise, 
but also on other factors like for example pH of the product, which was  approximately 0.3 units 
higher in the pork meat used for the second experiment, and led to higher increase of L. 
monocytogenes at the end of the experiment (after 2 weeks). 
Growth rates obtained in this experiment by fitting the growth curves to the DMFit models 
were compared with predictions from ComBase Predictor and FSSP. Both software were used in 
two ways – one was without including an estimated acetic acid content in the mixes (therefore, it 
was set to 0) and the other was including the acetic acid, according to the assumptions that 50% of 
total acetic acid concentration in the mayonnaise was transferred into the water phase of the 
product, which gave the concentration of 1300 ppm in 20% mix and 1600 ppm in 40% mix.  In 
general, none of the predictive models described obtained data well. Most of the time they were 
under-predicting the growth rates, and sometimes over-predicting. What was surprising, was that 
both models more accurately predicted growth rates when acetic acid content was not taken into 
consideration. Both ComBase and FSSP estimated very low growth when acetic acid content was 
specified and taken into consideration in predictions. There are many explanations to that, one of 
them being that the assumptions were not correct and the estimated acetic acid content in the 
aqueous phase of mayonnaise was incorrect, and as a result of that, the predictions were incorrect. 
For more accurate values and knowledge, more research about mechanisms of acetic acid transfer 
into the water phase should be done to have a better understanding of the topic and possibly make 
assumptions which would be matching the actual values in a better degree.  
==PAGE:50==
 
50 
This thesis provided also some information about predicting the shelf life of the product. If the 
shelf life of a mayonnaise-based salad would be maximum 5 days, and this is what is usually set for 
chilled pre-made salads, it should be checked if the increase of Listeria monocytogenes crosses the 
limit of 2-log before 5 days. If not, then the salad should be safe for consumption within this limit. 
However, a few trials should be done to estimate the safety correctly, because as the experiments 
showed, the results may not necessary be the same and then the result with the shortest time to 
reach the increase should be taken into consideration, as then all other samples with slower growth 
will still be safe for the consumer in the given time.  
It should be also pointed out that this experiment, as mentioned previously, was done as a 
worst-case-scenario. Numerous literature positions suggest that changing the moment of inoculation 
of the sample or using different main ingredient like ham, which has additives, could lower growth 
of Listeria and then the results could be very different from the ones which were obtained in this 
thesis. 
 
Future perspectives 
Although the results of this thesis confirmed that mayonnaise showed inhibitory properties 
towards L. monocytogenes, it was not effective enough to prevent the growth of bacteria in the 
mixed products and ensure their microbiological safety for up to 14 days at chilled storage. In the 
future, it could be relevant to do more research on mayonnaise-based salads, focusing for example 
on: 
§ Conducting the same experiment with further analysis of chemical composition of the 
product and the mechanisms of transfer of acetic acid into the water phase, in order to get 
more accurate results of the content of the acid; also, with monitoring the pH change in the 
product during storage time 
§ Making more specific measurements of not only acetic acid content, but also other 
inhibitory substances like salt, citric acid etc. to be able to apply their exact amounts into 
predictive models for better comparisons 
§ Conducting similar experiments with addition of different mayonnaise concentrations, like 
10% and 30%, to check whether the smaller differences in added mayonnaise amount have a 
big impact on growth of bacteria; 
§ Conducting similar experiments using different type of meat or other main ingredient (eggs, 
pasta, potatoes) ; 
==PAGE:51==
 
51 
§ Testing the growth increase in meat-mayonnaise salad which would be inoculated after the 
mayonnaise is added, to check if the moment of contamination changes a lot in the final 
concentration levels of bacteria; 
§ Conducting similar experiments with more “real” mayonnaise-based salad, which would 
have more than two ingredients (vegetables, spices); 
§ Testing growth increase of L. monocytogenes in products stored at abused temperatures, to 
simulate the conditions in extreme cases which could happen in real lide if certain rules 
were not followed;  
§ Studying the effect of packaging type of product on growth of L. monocytogenes
