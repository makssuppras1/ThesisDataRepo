Abstract 
Plant uptake of chemicals is an important pathway of human exposure. Therefore, a plant uptake 
model is developed to assess chemical hazards. However, owing to the progress of the model, the 
more input variables have been introduced, which makes it more difficult to apply the model. 
Knowledge Distillation (KD) is the process of transferring the knowledge from the original model to 
a new model. Commonly the new model is more compact with comparable performance, and one 
hopes to benefit from the model’s compactness. However, a new perspective emerged in this 
study: rather than compressing the model to be more compact, a back propagation neural network 
(BPNN) was applied to simulate the plant uptake dynamic processes utilizing the generated 
datasets from a selected mechanistic model (PPCP), then compressed the model based on the 
importance of input variables to meet the demand of fewer input variables for applying the model. 
Thanks to the nature of the black box model, the neural network is adaptive to the various given 
input variables without re-considering the architecture of the model. The model compression was 
conducted by discarding the less essential variables and retaining the effective variables in the 
model. And 2-factor limit was applied as the threshold of the increasing error, indicating that the 
error of prediction should not exceed twice the initial error (the cases with no input variables 
discarded). The Morris method was found to be more suited for identifying the importance of input 
variables than the rank correlation method, resulting in fewer input variables retained in the model 
within the tolerance limit. The prediction is required optimized before the simplification of input 
variables as a poor prediction case would fail the model compression by cannot reach the limit. The 
acids and bases in roots and neutral compounds in corns and leaves were optimized as the poor 
prediction cases with an unsatisfactory error. The dataset containing both highly and weakly 
dissociated compounds was the major cause for acids and bases in roots, leading to poor 
prediction, whereas the inadequate extreme data was the main reason for neutral compounds in 
corns and leaves. Those cases were optimized by separating the highly and weakly dissociated 
compounds and adding more extreme values, respectively. Furthermore, the prediction of strong 
acids and bases in roots was still not ideal due to the unreasonable Kaw value and air concentration 
as ions do not have a vapor pressure. Therefore, both variables were lowered. After the 
optimization, the relative errors (RMAE) of all the predictions were below 20 percent , which is 
acceptable and hence were compressed. As a result, the number of retained variables was all below 
15, which achieved an optimal simplicity of the model. Furthermore, the pKa and the log KOW were 
the most important input variables in all the cases (except for neutral compounds as the pKa was 
set as 0 and therefore was not an input variable) and were always kept in the model. 
Considering that PPCPs in the air are uncommon in daily life, the scenarios without air pollution are 
more realistic. Therefore, the air concentration was adjusted to zero. In the absence of chemical 
deposition from the air onto leaves and corns, the concentrations of chemicals in plant tissues 
reduced dramatically compared to the original cases. Moreover, in the majority of cases, the 
predictions were not accurate, and the main cause was the inadequate quantity of extreme values 
since the model underpredicted the extreme data, while the prediction of normal data was 
satisfactory. After replenishing extreme data, the case of acids and bases in leaves, as well as 
neutral chemicals in corns and leaves, still showed unacceptable inaccuracy. Particularly for neutral 
==PAGE:3==

substances in corns and leaves, the improvement of predictions was constrained due to the 
difficulty of generating extreme data, resulting in inadequate extreme data to train the model. The 
bases in leaves and neutral compounds in corns and leaves failed the model compression due to

of 5 to 12 input variables. The success of model compression for acids in leaves is surprising 
since the initial prediction was also unsatisfactory. However, the RMAE after model compression 
reached 60%, which restricted the applicability of the model.

1. Introduction  
Machine learning (ML) is a method of data analysis that does not require much prior knowledge 
and has shown strong adaptability in solving complex data patterns or formats (Zhong, Zhang et al. 
2021). Compared to classical statistical models, the ML model compensates for the insufficient 
ability to capture complex relationships between enormous amount of input variables, and classical 
statistical inferences become less accurate (Ij 2018). In recent years, machine learning techniques 
have received increasing attention from the environmental modeling community (Masmoudi, 
Elghazel et al. 2020) and has been applied to diverse environmental modeling targets. E.g., plant 
uptake of cadmium by an artificial neural network model (ANN) (Rossi, Bagheri et al. 2019); The 
prediction of pollutant concentration by Ensemble of Regressor Chains-guided Feature Ranking 
(ERCFR) (Masmoudi, Elghazel et al. 2020); The forecast of the concentration of effluent in a 
wastewater treatment plant by artificial network and support vector machine (SVM) (Guo, Jeong et 
al. 2015).  
Chemical compounds are ubiquitous in our daily life (Roy 2016). Some of them may severely affect 
the living being through different processes (Schug, Janesick et al. 2011). E.g., transporting in the 
water cycle (Richardson and Bowron 1985), absorbing into soil particles (Selim 2014), and 
volatilizing into the air (Howard, Saxena et al. 1978), may eventually lead to exposure to humans. 
Plant uptake of chemicals is also one of the crucial ways of human exposure (Paterson, Mackay et 
al. 1990, Brunetti, Kodešová et al. 2021). Chemicals are taken up by plants through the 
translocation of the water in the soil via roots, then transferred upper, and eventually 
bioaccumulating in the plant tissues (Paterson, Mackay et al. 1990, Trapp 2004, Collins, Fryer et al. 
2006, Chuang, Liu et al. 2019). Soil is one of the major contamination source for plants (Rodríguez-
Eugenio, McLaughlin et al. 2018), and it has already become a global problem of human health 
(Barsova, Yakimenko et al. 2019, Huang, Wang et al. 2019, Khan, Naushad et al. 2021). In China, 
there are 19.4% of farmland that classified as contaminated (Qu, Shi et al. 2016). In addition, there 
are 3.4 million sites in Europe that were identified as polluted (Centre, Environment et al. 2014). As 
a result, a great interest in developing a plant uptake model to predict chemical risk has been 
growing for decades. In the early days, a plant uptake model for neutral and ionic chemicals was 
published, which for the first time considered the ion trap process (Trapp 2004). Fantke et al. took 
into account the wheat-environment system specifically in the dynamic plant uptake model 
(Fantke, Charles et al. 2011). Brunetti et al. coupled a hydrological model (HYDRUS) with a dynamic 
uptake model to enrich multiple physiochemical processes in soil-plant systems (Brunetti, Kodešová 
et al. 2019). Undoubtedly, these model advances have witnessed a more reliable prediction of 
chemicals in dynamic systems. However, the more complex models also bring a trouble, i.e., 
requiring a large amount of input data to accurately predict specific cases, including chemical 
properties, soil data, plant data, etc. This requirement is a new challenge for the users who find 
that it is difficult to obtain all the required data from the site, so they have to compromise between 
looking up one from the previous cases or using the default settings, which would potentially 
increase the uncertainty of the prediction comparing to reality. E.g., Trapp demonstrates that 
chemicals in a typical plant uptake model, the data for plant species and site-specific conditions 
could essentially impact the plant uptake (Trapp 2015). Conducting sensitivity analysis of the input 
parameters is a promising way to simplify the model. Discarding some of the non-sensitive

parameters, in principle, would not have a significant impact on the prediction accuracy. However, 
it is still arduous to re-consider the formulas that connect compartments from the remaining input 
variables 
To this end, an ANN was developed to simulate the plant uptake of chemicals. Being of “black box” 
nature, ANN is hard to interpret (Zhong, Zhang et al. 2021). Even if there are already some simple 
methods to uncover the interpretability of machine learning models (Molnar 2020). The 
interpretation to the model is still commonly overlooked (Zhong, Zhang et al. 2021). Unlike the 
traditional mechanistic model, the internal calculation processes in an ANN model are less 
practically meaningful. It is just a designed function approximation machine to achieve statistical 
generalization (Goodfellow, Bengio et al. 2016). However, this feature also led us to a new insight 
into the simplification of the mechanistic model without re-considering the layout and the internal 
formulas. Breiman demonstrated that an ensemble of diverse models with similar performances 
would achieve a superior prediction for each of the single constituent model. On the contrary, 
there is always a simpler model among those single constituent models that could approximate the 
ensemble’s performance (Breiman 2001). Therefore, it is possible to compress an ensemble, or a 
more complex model, to a smaller model with fewer parameters and a simpler structure but can 
still achieve a comparable performance. This process is well known as Knowledge Distillation (KD) 
(Buciluǎ, Caruana et al. 2006). The idea is typically to transfer the knowledge from a teacher model 
to a compact student model (Ba and Caruana 2014, Urban, Geras et al. 2016). The student model 
may not have the similar performance to the teacher model if directly trained on the original 
dataset but learning from the teacher model would make student model much closer to the 
accuracy of the teacher model’s predictions (Furlanello, Lipton et al. 2018).Initially, the original 
model labels a large unlabeled dataset, and transfers it to an ANN model for training. As a result, 
the ANN model would display predictions similar to that of the original model with a less complex 
structure and fewer parameters.  
The teacher model in this research is a selected mechanistic plant uptake model, while the student 
model is the ANN model. However, doing KD to simplify parameterization is not the final target. 
Following the transfer of information from the teacher model, the model compression targeting 
input variables was conducted. The ANN model was constantly compressed by simplifying input 
variables based on correlation or sensitivity analysis until a tolerance limit was reached. This 
continuous model compression technique trades prediction uncertainty for model compactness. It 
is feasible owing to the nature of the black box model, which removes the constraint of discarding 
input variables, while in the mechanistic model, discarding input variables always damages the 
internal computation and the structure of the model. The final model simplification is determined 
by the correlation or sensitivity analysis of the input variables, as well as the tolerance criteria used. 
The objectives of the study are: (1) to test the feasibility of the ANN model to display a comparable 
performance to the chosen mechanistic model of plant uptake of pharmaceuticals and personal 
care products (PPCP); (2) to do the sensitivity and correlation analysis to the PPCP model and the 
ANN model; (3) to simplify the input variables based on the sensitivity or correlation analysis within 
an applied tolerance limit of error; (4) to discover the inapplicability of the ANN model for some 
specific cases. The developed ANN model would be expected to present a similar prediction

demand of fewer given input variables.

2. Background 
2.1 What is PPCPs 
PPCPs are a class of emerging contaminants that have raised global concern for decades. It is the 
abbreviation of Pharmaceuticals and Personal Care Products. The United States Environmental 
Protection Agency (EPA) defines PPCPs as “any product used by individuals for personal health or 
cosmetic reasons or used by agribusiness to enhance growth or health of livestock.” The most 
common products in our daily life include pharmaceuticals for treating human and animal diseases, 
moisturizers, lipsticks, shampoos, hair colors, deodorants, and toothpaste (Boxall, Rudd et al. 2012). 
PPCPs contain numerous groups of organic chemicals, e.g., antibiotics, hormones, anti-
inflammatory drugs, antiepileptic drugs, blood lipid regulators, β-blockers, contrast media, and 
cytostatic drugs for pharmaceuticals, and antimicrobial agents, synthetic musks, insect repellants, 
preservatives, and sunscreen UV filters for personal care products (Daughton and Ternes 1999, Liu 
and Wong 2013). Those chemicals are primarily derived from agriculture, urban, and industrial area 
and could accumulate in plants (Bartrons and Peñuelas 2017). Therefore, the dietary intake of those 
plants could also lead to human exposure to them.  
In addition, many PPCPs are highly bioactive, polar, optically active, and all usually occur at no more 
than trace concentrations (Barceló and Petrovic 2007). The PPCPs and its bioactive metabolites may 
lead to long-term accumulation and promote continuous but unnoticed adverse effects on 
organisms (Barceló and Petrovic 2007). Many PPCPs are discharged into the sewage system 
(Daughton and Ternes 1999). It can then enter the surface water or terrestrial system by, e.g., using 
sewage effluent for irrigation or sewage sludge for fertilization (Ternes, Joss et al. 2004, Kinney, 
Furlong et al. 2006). Veterinary pharmaceuticals can enter the environment from the use of 
treating pasture animals or the land application of manure from livestock facilities (Boxall, Kolpin et 
al. 2003).  
2.2 The source and fate of PPCPs 
There are more than 4,000 pharmaceuticals are currently in use, and many types of chemicals are 
used in personal care product (Boxall, Rudd et al. 2012). Therefore, it is extremely hard to identify 
the hazards and risks by experiments for all of them since those compounds have diverse 
physicochemical properties and applications, resulting in different behaviours in the environment. 
Compared to the experimental assessment, focusing on predicting the fate of PPCPs is more 
promising, which would compensate for the lack of hazard information on PPCPs, e.g., the 
environmental modelling in plant uptake of PPCPs is emerging due to the importance of plant 
uptake for human exposure. However, these approaches should be further developed for different 
situations covering different regions, climates, demographics, and cultural backgrounds and should 
be developed in such a way that they account for the use practices, complex fate processes, and 
the specific modes of action associated with many PPCPs (Boxall, Rudd et al. 2012). 
PPCPs can enter the environment through the use of sewage water and sludge on agricultural land, 
leaching from disposed solid waste, applications of pesticides, and leaks of domestic or industrial 
wastewater (Bartrons and Peñuelas 2017). Furthermore, it may diffuse from the agricultural runoff, 
urban runoff, and leakage from the urban sewage system. However, the management and uses 
may vary in different regions. An important exposure pathway in an area may be less important in 
==PAGE:11==
 
5 
 
the other regions. E.g., wastewater treatment technologies may be limited in an undeveloped 
country, resulting in a higher amount of discharge of PPCPs in the sewage effluent, while it is less 
relevant for a developed country where the treatment of the wastewater is not an issue. The 
application of pesticides is strictly restricted in many European countries, while the regulatory of 
using pesticides is still inadequate for some countries. So, the model for predicting exposures 
maybe not always relevant in different regions.  
The pathways of transport for PPCPs through air, water, and soil are difficult to identify due to the 
scarcity of information on the fate and behaviour in the environment of most of these compounds 
(Bartrons and Peñuelas 2017). However, it will depend on their physicochemical properties. E.g., 
the KOW is the partition coefficient between octanol and water. If high, the chemical will accumulate 
in the high lipid content medium. The pKa is the acid dissociation constant. It and the pH in the 
domain determine the degree of dissociation of chemicals. If the pKa is lower than the pH, the 
chemical will tend to release proton, resulting in dominated dissociated form for acids (strong 
acids) but a non-dissociated form dominated for bases (weak bases). If the pKa is equal to the pH, 
the dissociation rate would be 50%, meaning that the half of the chemical will exist as ions and the 
other half as neutral molecular. The Kaw is the partition coefficient between air and water. It 
determines the tendency of the chemicals to volatilize to the air or dissolve in the water. However, 
the ion does not have vapor pressure. The highly dissociated compound generally has a low Kaw and 
therefore mainly stays in the water. From those characterizations of chemicals, the transport 
pathway for PPCPs can be described in the model to predict its behaviours. Furthermore, the 
collection of surrounding matrices data is also important since the chemical would also lead to 
varying degree of risks in different environment. 
2.3 The transport of PPCPs to plants 
Plant uptake of PPCPs is also one of the crucial ways of human exposure (Paterson, Mackay et al. 
1990, Brunetti, Kodešová et al. 2021). Chemicals can reach plants through the application of 
sewage sludge for fertilization and wastewater irrigation, and it becomes more and more common. 
There is more than 85%, 71%, and 46% of treated wastewater used for agricultural irrigation in 
Israel, Spain, and California, respectively (Sato, Qadir et al. 2013). Moreover, these numbers will 
potentially increase in the future, resulting in the presence and accumulation of PPCPs in plants. 
The treated wastewater and sewage sludge is rich in minerals, nutrients, and organic compounds, 
which can improve the soil fertility and restore organic matter. Therefore, the application of them 
on agricultural land become crucial to improve the growth of plants. Knowing the amount of PPCPs 
in wastewater and sewage sludge before the application is the key to avoid PPCPs presence in 
plants. But, PPCPs in the wastewater and sludge are not regularly monitored and therefore are not 
considered when deciding the irrigation (Bartrons and Peñuelas 2017). 
PPCPs predominately present in soils at first after irrigation. Plants uptake PPCPs through roots via 
diffusion or mass flow. For the neutral compound, it diffuses across the cell membrane. However, a 
significant proportion of PPCPs are ionizable substances (Boxall, Rudd et al. 2012). Therefore, it is 
more common for the ionizable compound to cross the cell membrane via the combination of the 
diffusion of the neutral fraction and the electronic interaction with the membrane potential of the 
ionic fraction. For hydrophobic compounds, they may accumulate in the lipid in roots, while most 
==PAGE:12==
 
6 
 
hydrophilic compounds will continue to move to the xylem, where PPCPs are transported to leaves 
and corns, and accumulate in those tissues or volatile to the air. If PPCPs are also present in the air, 
they may also enter plants via deposition on leaves and fruits, resulting in a bi-direction exchange 
of substances between plants and air. Because most PPCPs are ionizable compounds that do not 
have a vapor pressure. It is not common that PPCPs to exist in the air, and therefore the uptake of 
compounds via roots is the predominated pathway. Furthermore, most compounds will be retained 
in the plant in the long term until the plant is in dietary intake, which potentially exposes the hazard 
to humans. 
2.4 The hazard of PPCPs 
The exposure of plants to PPCPs may inhibit the plant's growth and reduce crop production. E.g., 
antimicrobial pharmaceuticals can inhibit the activity of microorganisms in plants or soils (Peñuelas, 
Farré-Armengol et al. 2014, Peñuelas and Terradas 2014), which affects plant–microorganism 
symbiosis and nutrient cycling (Carvalho, Basto et al. 2014). The effects of PPCPs on plants depend 
on the concentration and the properties of substances and the plant species. However, most 
research that only tested the hazard of an individual substance which ignored the synergistic effect 
with other chemicals in the environment. Furthermore, those test were based on a concentration 
higher than that in the environment (Carvalho, Basto et al. 2014). The exposure level of PPCPs to 
plants is generally low. But the advert effect from long-term exposure is still of emerging concern. 
According to the study (García-Santiago, Franco-Uría et al. 2016), the risk of advert effects of PPCPs 
on human health could be low. However, the potential hazard of PPCPs on organisms are of an 
increasing concern because of (Bartrons and Peñuelas 2017): (1) the long-term exposure to the 
substances; (2) the scarcity of comprehensive information about the fate and effect of PPCPs in the 
environment, and the lack of pharmacovigilance programs testing the environmental effects 
(Boxall, Rudd et al. 2012); (3) the potential synergistic effect of PPCPs with other pollutants; (4) the 
potential hazard of the PPCPs metabolites that higher than its parent compounds; (5) the multiple 
pathways of exposure of PPCPs; (6) the increasing selection of antibiotic-resistant microorganisms 
in the environment. 
PPCPs can have a severely advert effect on wildlife, including birds, fishes, and mammals. E.g., the 
inappropriate use of diclofenac for the disposal of animal carcasses led to a decreasing population 
of three vultures species in Asia (Oaks, Gilbert et al. 2004). The application of anti-parasitic 
veterinary medicines can dramatically reduce the quantity of insects, resulting in a shortage of food 
for bats, birds, and other insects-eating animals, which will potentially cause an indirect effect on 
those predators (McCracken 1993).  
Humans are also exposed to PPCPs in daily life, including the daily use of toothpaste, hair care 
products, body washes, soaps, and makeup, but at a low level (around 1.02 mg/g for synthetic 
musks) (Liu and Wong 2013). PPCPs also existed in drinking water as the dietary source for human 
exposure. Four kinds of PPCPs were found in the drinking water from distribution networks in south 
China (Qiao, Yu et al. 2011). Fluoroquinolone antibiotics existed in the tap water in Guangzhou and 
Macau (Wang, Mo et al. 2010). Although the exposure of PPCPs for humans widely exists, the acute 
toxic effect of those substances on human health is not likely, based on the toxicological data and 
environmental concentration levels (Brausch and Rand 2011). However, the chronic effects on 
==PAGE:13==
 
7 
 
human health cannot be excluded since continuous exposure to PPCPs could potentially 
accumulate and cause irreversible harm to human beings (Fent, Weston et al. 2006, Brausch and 
Rand 2011). E.g., exposure to antibiotics would increase the persistence of antibiotic resistance 
genes (ARGs) (Fent, Weston et al. 2006, Brausch and Rand 2011). Hormones can have advert effects 
of endocrine disruption in terms of reproduction and development, including reduced fertility, 
feminization of males, and induction of vitellogenesis in males, and intersex phenomena (Lai, 
Scrimshaw et al. 2002). The investigations about PPCPs are incomplete since most studies are 
mainly about synthetic musks, antimicrobial agents, and parabens (Liu and Wong 2013). But the 
investigation of the other groups is less developed. Furthermore, a comprehensive understanding 
of PPCPs exposure in different routes is also needed.

The ANN model was developed based on a plant uptake of pharmaceuticals and personal care 
products mechanistic model (PPCP). Figure 1 shows the structure of the PPCP model. It is expanded 
from a standard model that simulating the plant uptake of neutral compounds to that of ionizable 
compounds with relevant processes.

3.1 Uptake into roots 
The chemical enters roots with water and flows out to the xylem. Living roots will grow to dilute the 
concentration of chemicals in roots. Assuming that the growth of roots is exponential and the ratio 
transpiration to plant mass is a constant, then growth dilution is considered as a first-order process, 
described by a growth rate. The flux in phloem occurs from leaves to both fruits and roots (Trapp 
2009), which increases the concentration in roots. A first-order metabolism may also occur, and it is 
added to the growth rate of roots to be the total loss rate. For very polar chemicals, the uptake into 
roots is slower than that of water. Therefore, it is corrected with a correction factor. The mass 
balance differential equation for the change of chemicals in roots is

There are also other processes occur in roots. The chemicals also adsorb to membrane lipids and 
proteins. The adsorption to lipids is described by the partition coefficient between octanol and 
water, and that to proteins is estimated from the partition coefficient to human serum albumin. 
However, for ionic compounds, the adsorption to lipids is small. 
Ion trap would occur when the pH inside and outside the root cell membrane are different. For 
acids, if the pH inside of the membrane is higher than that in soils, and the pKa is also higher than 
the pH in soils but is up to 3 log-unit below the pH inside the membrane. Then, the acid is present 
as neutral molecular and rapidly diffuses into the cell in roots (Trapp 2009). Due to the higher inside 
pH than the pKa, the acid dissociates. Due to the polarity of ions, the dissociated compound diffuses 
out of the cell membrane slowly and therefore is trapped inside the cell. Similar to bases, when the 
pH inside is lower than that in soils, and the pKa is lower than the pH in soils but is 3 log-unit above 
the pH inside, the base is trapped inside the cell.  
As a result of ion charge and membrane potential, the electrical repulsion and electrical attraction 
can occur. E.g., if the cell membrane potential is -120 mV (at cytosol to outside), anions are repelled 
from the membrane, resulting in very low uptake of strong acids (Trapp 2009). On the other hand, 
the cations are attracted to the cell membrane and accumulate inside the cell.

4. Data and methods 
4.1 Principle of the method 
According to the universal approximation theorems (Cybenko 1989, Hornik, Stinchcombe et al. 
1989), ANN can approximate any continuous function for the input variables with a limited range. 
The way to conduct it is to utilize an activation function, or non-linear function, to transform the 
feature of the dataset several times and output from the model. ANN model shows a strong 
application in general modeling with a relatively compact structure. Moreover, there are no strict 
prerequisites for datasets or the use of ANN. However, the ANN model has its own limitations. First, 
it is not a suitable method if the objective is to provide an explanation of the simulation processes. 
As a black-box model, ANN is only for predicting the results. The most additional information that 
can be obtained from ANN model is the importance of input variables, namely which variable has a 
significant effect on the output. It can be done through sensitivity analysis or analyzing the 
connection weights (this term will be explained in the coming section) in the model (Yang, Wang et 
al. 2005, Yi-Fan, Chang-Zhu et al. 2017). Secondly, training ANN model requires large datasets to 
ensure the quality of regression. There are no strict requirements for the size of a dataset. 
However, an insufficient number of data will impact the prediction performance. Furthermore, 
collecting a large dataset can often be time-consuming for the environmental chemistry field since 
most real datasets are from experiments. Another disadvantage is that designing the architecture 
of an ANN model has no general rules, i.e., it can take a lot of attempts to design the structure 
before it can show a satisfying prediction. Finally, training ANN is inefficient due to thousands of 
epochs for updating the parameters in the model. The training process is an algorithm continuously 
attempting to reduce the training error rather than analytically solving the optimal solution. 
Therefore, training an ANN model on a powerful computation machine is also necessary to save 
time. 
So far, the latest research using the ANN model to simulate the plant uptake of organic compounds 
is predicting the transpiration stream concentration factor (TSCF), root concentration factor (RCF), 
and fruit concentration factor (FCF) at the steady-state (Bagheri, Al-Jabery et al. 2019, Bagheri, Al-
Jabery et al. 2020, Bagheri, He et al. 2021). To the best we know, this article is the first study to 
simulate the dynamic plant uptake system by an ANN model combining model compression based 
on the correlation and sensitivity analysis.

4.2.4 Batch and epoch 
The batch size is a hyperparameter given to decide the amount of data from the training dataset to 
feed the model for one update of the model’s parameters. One batch of data is fed forward to the 
output, and one loss is then calculated by averaging the error of each data in the batch. It is then 
propagated back to the model for updating the parameters by gradient descent. The batch size 
should be equal to or greater than 1, equal to or smaller than the size of the whole training dataset. 
The default batch size in the model is 32, and it is popular to set to an integer multiple of 2, e.g., 64, 
128, 256. However, it may occur that the size of the training dataset cannot be evenly divided by 
the batch size. So, the last batch of data is smaller than the others. 
Epoch is the number times of working through the entire training dataset. One epoch contains one 
or multiple batches that update the parameters. Generally, the number of epochs for training a 
model is large, and it could be up to thousands or even higher, depending on the complexity of the 
model. 
The larger the batch size, the faster the model is trained, and the more epochs are needed. 
However, an over large batch size will severely impact the prediction performance. In the study, 
batch size was given as 256. 
4.2.5 Early stopping function 
Early stopping function is applied to terminate the training process when the training error does 
not decrease significantly. Before the training starts, it is unknown how many epochs it will take to 
train the model. Therefore, a large maximum training epoch up to 10000 or even higher would be 
given. However, if the training does not stop in time, it may occur overfitting and would waste the 
time.  
In the function, min delta is the threshold that the decrease of error lower than which will be 
regarded as no improvement. The min delta was set as 0.00001 times the minimum value of the 
output dataset. Patience is the number of epochs with no improvement, after which training will 
stop. It was set as 40 in the function. Those values are given from multiple attempts. Overall, the 
model training process works well with the setting.

However, in non-linear models, the OAT method as a local sensitivity analysis ignores the

Figure 3 shows an example of Morris’ method for a 3 input variables dataset. In the Morris’ 
method, several points are initialized within the given range space. These points are then moved to 
a new coordinate along the x1 axis by varying the first input variable x1. Starting from the new 
position, the points again move along the x2 axis by varying x2, and then repeat for the following 
input variable until the last one. This continuous variation process generates trajectories, as shown 
in Figure 3. And it is the successive OAT calculation for each input variable. Furthermore, the 
sensitivity factor of Morris’ method is calculated as the average of the multiple OAT sensitivity 
factors.  
In this study, 2000 trajectories of the entire input variables were generated by using the Latin 
Hypercube Sampling method to fully explore the sensitivity in the range space. Firstly, the given 
ranges for input variables were divided into 2000 sub-intervals, and the initial points were set at the 
center of each sub-interval. Each of these points was moved along the x1 axis by increasing the first 
input variable by a factor of 1/2000 and was repeated until the last variable variated. And the 
sensitivity factor for each input variable was calculated. The Morris method for the global 
sensitivity analysis was implemented by using the Python code from the course 12104 Modelling of 
Environmental Process and Technologies.

5.3.1 Training initialization 
Every time to start the model training, the parameters in each hidden layer are initialized by 
randomly assigning the default value from a uniform distribution if the initializer is not set in the 
program, resulting in a difference in predictions each time after the model is trained. Therefore, the 
ANN model training was initialized ten times for each case and predicted on the test dataset to 
analyse the uncertainty caused by the random initialization. 
Figure 7 shows the prediction RMAE of ten times initializations for model training in different cases. 
The RMAEs are converged in the confidential interval in most cases, whereas, in the case of acids 
and neutral compounds in corns, an outlier appears above the upper edge, which is 1.6 and 3.3 
percentage units higher than each of the median. In addition, an outlier appears above the upper 
edge and below the lower edge for bases in roots, being 10 percentage units higher and 7.4 
percentage units lower than the median.

6. Discussion 
6.1 Prediction performance 
Figure 4 shows that the model produced a satisfying prediction in most cases, excluding the acids 
and bases in roots, with a relative error of 47.27% and 52.98%, respectively. All cases demonstrate 
the difficulty in predicting the extreme concentrations at various levels. In addition, these extreme 
concentrations are sporadic in the data set, which is considered to be the cause of the poor 
prediction for acids and bases in roots, as well as neutral chemicals in corns and leaves. However, 
there is no indication that they were improperly calculated or that the values of input variables 
were inapplicable. Another factor potentially contributing to the low accuracy of the prediction is 
the fact that chemicals with considerably different properties provide contrasting outputs during 
training, which may have confused the model. 
6.2 Poor prediction cases 
Figure 9 and Figure 10 show the distribution of log KOW and the pKa in different cases. Within a 
certain range, it was observed that these two variables had a bigger portion of inaccurately 
predicted data, as indicated by an RMAE greater than the RMAE of the whole dataset. Therefore, 
the investigation of the two variables was conducted to explore the reason of the poor predictions. 
The frequency of the big RMAE (larger than the RMAE of the entire dataset) data decreases in corn 
and leaves but increases in roots (this feature was less evident in the bases in roots) with the 
growing log KOW. Thus, the range of log KOW was divided into two groups equally, with the value 
lower and higher than 3.5 (polar and non-polar), to observe if the ANN model would have a better 
prediction in these two separated cases. A higher pKa for acids and lower pKa for bases results in 
more frequent big RMAE data showing up. This observation implies that the prediction of the 
weakly-dissociated chemicals is more frequently inaccurate. So, the datasets for acids and bases 
were also divided into two groups, namely the highly dissociated (pKa < pH for acids, pKa > pH for 
bases) and the weakly dissociated group (pKa > pH for acids, pKa < pH for bases). In addition, the 
output data were restricted by setting a threshold of 1000, i.e., the output values higher than the 
threshold were discarded to see if it improves the prediction without those large output values. 
Table 2 shows the change of RMAE by the adjustment to log KOW, pKa, and output concentrations. 
The prediction of the acids and bases in roots was improved by pKa, especially for the weak acids 
and bases, and the simulation of the neutral compounds in corns and leaves was improved at 
different levels by log KOW and discarding big output values (higher than 1000).  
The strong acids and bases generally taken up into roots are a factor 10 higher than weak acids and 
bases, according to the simulation outputs. However, the ionizable compounds have more complex 
processes that potentially increase the difficulty of machine learning, e.g., the ions are not 
accumulative to the lipid in the plant due to a lower log KOW value (Trapp and Horobin 2005), so 
they may also flow reversal to roots and corns from leaves simultaneously through the phloem flux. 
In addition, ion trap, electrical attraction or repulsion may also occur for ions to affect the 
concentration in roots (Briggs, Rigitano et al. 1987, Trapp 2009).  
Simulation of the small output values (smaller than 1000) of neutral compounds in corns and leaves 
is satisfying but was underpredicted for the big output values (higher than 1000). For those larger 
values, log KOW above 8 m3/m3, air concentration above 0.7 mg/m3, and plant lipid content above 
==PAGE:35==
 
29 
 
0.3 kg/kg are dominant and relatively high in their respective given ranges. The large air 
concentration of compound hinders the loss to the air and the compounds in the air may also 
absorb into leaves and corns. Furthermore, it starts to accumulate in the lipid of the plant due to 
the high lipid content and the large log KOW in these cases. Combining those consequences 
produces an extremely high concentration of neutral compounds in corns and leaves. These large 
values are rare and therefore extreme in the datasets, making the ANN model hard to reproduce 
them. 
Figure 11 shows the predictions when setting air concentration to 0. For the neutral compounds in 
corns and leaves, the concentrations are below 1 mg/kg because of the absence of deposition from 
the air and, therefore, less accumulation in the plant, which proves the inference. However, the 
lower concentrations did not improve the prediction as most show varying degrees of increase in 
errors. The ANN model failed to predict the extreme values. This observation indicates that the 
non-symmetrical datasets lead to poor prediction results rather than large magnitude output 
values. 
A higher log KOW (non-polar) to the neutral compounds also decreases the RMAE. The reason for it 
could be the high accumulation in plants, similar to the weak acids and bases, while the polar 
neutral compounds are poorly absorbed into the lipid content in plants. The MAE of combining 
these two adjustments for the neutral compounds results in even lower values (15.89% for corns 
and 14.02% for leaves).

6.3 Optimization of predictions for highly dissociated compounds 
6.3.1 Observing the distribution of big RMAE values 
Table 2 suggests that the ANN model tends to predict the non-polar substances accurately (log KOW 
value above 3.5). For the highly dissociated compounds (strong acids and bases), the log KOW of the 
ion is 3.5 logarithm units lower than neutral compounds (Trapp and Horobin 2005), indicating a 
generally low log KOW value. The optimization for strong acids and bases in roots was conducted by 
observing the distribution of the data with big RMAE (higher than the RMAE of the entire dataset) 
and adjusting the ranges of input variables step by step. 
==PAGE:39==
 
33 
 
Initially, the range of log KOW was raised to between 3.5 and 9 for the strong acids in roots to 
increase the non-polarity of substances. However, the RMAE of the prediction was 31.93%, which 
shows no significant improvement. Figure 12 (a) shows the pKa distribution of the data with big 
RMAE in this case, suggesting that data with pKa above 3 led to more large RMAEs. The reason for it 
could be that the pKa value was close to the pH which results in an insufficient dissociation of 
compound. Therefore, the pKa was lowered to between -1 and 3 to ensure the strong dissociation.  
After adjusting the pKa, the high log Kow (above 6) values were also found to result in more large 
errors (Figure 12 (b)), indicating that the higher log KOW does not improve predictions. Therefore, 
the log KOW was then set to between -2 and 3.5.  
Figure 13 (a) shows that the concentrations in air below 0.5 mg/m3 led to more large RMAEs after 
lowering the log KOW. If the concentration in the air is low, the neutral substances would potentially 
volatilize to the air. On the contrary, if the air concentration is high, it may hinder the volatilization 
of substances from leaves, while the substances in the air would deposit on leaves to significantly 
increase the concentration in leaves and therefore the phloem flow from leaves to roots. However, 
it is unknown how the low air concentration (below 0.5 mg/m3) affected the predictions that led to 
large RMAEs. 
Figure 13 (b) shows the membrane potential in cytosol below -0.04 V and above 0.04 V led to more 
larege RMAEs showing up after adjusting the concentration in the air up to between 0.5 and 1 
mg/m3. This can be explained by the electrical attraction and repulsion processes. In soil, the strong 
acid is presented as ionizable compound with a  negative charge. If the membrane potential in the 
cytosol is large in negative,  the anion would be repulsed from the membrane and hard to be taken 
up by the plant cells. On the contrary, if the membrane potential is large in positive, the anion 
would be attracted to the membrane and accumulate in the cytosol. Therefore, these two 
additional effect may lead to an over- or underestimation of the accumulation of ionizable 
compounds in the cells (Trapp 2009). The membrane potential in cytosol was then adjusted to 
between -0.04 and 0.04 V.  
Combining those adjustments, the RMAE of strong acids in roots was 20.44%, which is significantly 
decreased compared to the initial error of strong acids in Table 2 (37.61%).  
Same adjustments were also conducted for the strong bases. The pH of soils was set to between 4 
and 7, and the range of pKa was set to between 11 and 15 to ensure the high dissociation of strong 
bases. other variables were kept consistent to the strong acids. The final RMAE of the prediction 
was 15.23%, which has achieved the same level of performance as the other cases. 
Overall, the poor prediction of the strong acids and bases was due to the additional complex 
processes. The electrical attraction and repulsion process increases and decreases the accumulation 
of the ionizable compound in the plant cells, respectively. However, the adjustments also bring 
limitations to the model. The range of pKa is not fully covered, resulting in the model is not 
applicable in all the cases. In addition, if the membrane potential is low, suggesting that the cell in 
the plant is not active, which is not normal for a living plant.

6.4 Optimization of predictions for neutral compounds in corns and leaves 
The predictions of neutral compounds in corns and leaves can be improved by discarding the 
extreme values. However, this adjustment makes the model less practical since those extreme 
values are of genuine concern and should not be abandoned in the dataset. Therefore, a new 
recombined dataset was generated by manually balancing the output data from reproducing more 
extreme values to improve the prediction without eliminating the extreme output values.

First, two datasets with the size of 500 and 900000 for neutral compounds were generated. In the 
larger dataset (size = 900000), the outputs higher than five times the mean value were regarded as 
extreme values, which were extracted from the dataset and added to the small dataset (size = 500) 
to compensate for the scarcity of extreme output values. Furthermore, before training the model, 
the standardization of output data was replaced by the logarithm to increase the uniformity of 
dataset. 
Figure 15 shows the predicted values of the recombined dataset of neutral compounds in corns and

6.5 Compressed models 
The input variables were gradually discarded by order of correlation (rank correlation) or sensitivity 
(global sensitivity). The global sensitivity curve is always superior to the other two since it mostly 
shows a lower MAE through the model compression process. For the neutral compounds in corns 
and leaves, the MAE curve of rank correlation in ANN is evidently inferior to the others. However, 
three curves do not differ in the case of neutral compounds in roots. 
For the neutral compounds in corns and leaves, the MAE is relatively stable with the reducing 
number of input variables, showing a robust regression capability, excluding the MAE based on the 
rank correlation in the ANN model, which has a surge in the simplification process. Furthermore. 
the underpredicted extreme output values are the main factor that caused a large relative error. 
But the small values (lower than 1000) prediction is still satisfying.  
Due to the initially poor prediction, the final RMAE in the bases in roots cannot reach the 2-factor 
tolerance limit even though only feeds 1 input variable to the model, resulting in a failed 
compression case. Simulating the strong and weak bases separately in the ANN model could solve 
this problem, and significantly decrease the RMAE.  
==PAGE:43==
 
37 
 
Overall, the model achieved an ideal simplicity of parameterization no matter based on which 
analysis, excluding the prediction of bases in leaves and roots. According to the rank correlation 
analysis in the PPCP model, the log KOW and pKa are always the crucial variables to be retained after 
simplification because both are most decisive in the translocation of chemicals into roots (Trapp 
2009).  Fruits and leaves exchange chemicals with the air, making the concentration in air 
(Chemicals_C_air), Kaw, the lipid content in plants (Plant_lipid content), and the density of leaves 
(Leaves_shoot density) important to be kept in the model. However, they are less related to roots 
due to the neglect of roots exchanging with the soil air. Therefore, the translocation from soils as 
the most significant process to the uptake of chemicals into roots, is more sensitive to the variables 
related to soils and roots, including the organic carbon in soil (Soil_OC bulk), root growth rate 
(Root_growth rate), and the concentration in soils (Chemicals_C_soil).   
The rank correlation analysis and global sensitivity analysis in the ANN model mostly agrees with 
the rank correlation analysis in the PPCP model within the top ten correlated variables, suggesting 
that the ANN model did well learn the feature of datasets in most cases. However, the rank 
correlation shows a large difference in the case of neural compounds in leaves, where only three 
input variables within the top ten are consistent with the PPCP model. This result potentially 
explains the surge in the curve since the true correlated variables were discarded through the 
simplification. 
Based on the result in Table 1, it can be concluded that the global sensitivity analysis in the ANN 
model is more appropriate than others since it compresses the model with fewer input variables 
retained in the model. 
6.6 Compression of the model for the optimized cases

This problem suggests that the compression should be based on a well-trained model with a

result since the prediction error will be unacceptable although it requires fewer input variables. 
Furthermore, the 2-factor tolerance limit would also be invalid since it is over lenient for a bad-
predicted model, and the model possibly could not reach the limit which would fail the 
compression. 
According to the analysis above, the poor prediction cases that were optimized were acids, bases in 
roots, and neutral chemicals in corns and leaves. Therefore, the compression was repeated on

6.8 Other findings  
Bagheri et al. predicted the TSCF of 7 compounds (bisphenol A, estriol, 2,4-dinitrotoluene, DEET, 
carbamazepine, acetaminophen, and lincomycin) in 3 plant species (potato, corn, wheat) using 
neural network model (NN), suggesting the importance of including more parameters related to 
chemicals properties, plant species, and biodegradability in the NN model for a more accurate 
prediction compared with the experimental data (Bagheri, He et al. 2021). Most of those 
mentioned variables are sufficiently considered here, excluding biodegradability, which may 
significantly decrease the concentration in plants. The importance of taking biodegradability or 
stability was supported by comparing the predicted values with experimental values, observing that 
some chemicals concentrations decreased in the exposure media over time, while the other 
chemicals concentrations increased in the exposure media due to exclusion at the root membrane. 
Hu et al. predicted the dynamic effluent concentration of an expanded granular sludge bed (EGSB) 
reactor by an optimized ANN model and did sensitivity analysis for the input variables by listing the 
connection weights (Yi-Fan, Chang-Zhu et al. 2017). After training the model, each input variable is 
fed with a value to pass forward through the model from the input layer to the output layer. 
Eventually, each input value is transformed by the model and is output from the output layer. If the 
individual absolute output value of the input variable is high, meaning that this variable contributes 
much to the final output. Therefore, the rank of input variables is based on the individual output 
values to identify the most sensitive input variable. This method for sensitivity analysis is 
consistently identified as correct in ranking the importance of the variables in the ANN model 
(Olden, Joy et al. 2004). However, this method ignores the interaction between input variables and 
the sensitivity at varying ranges. The author also did not illustrate the selection of input values for 
identifying the contributions. To that respect, the Morris method shows its superiority for 
considering the sensitivity of input variables comprehensively.  
Yang et al. developed a 3-layer (1 input layer, 1 hidden layer, and 1 output layer) neural network 
model to predict the lethal concentration 50 (LC50) and assess the relative importance of variables 
to the outputs (Yang, Wang et al. 2005). The activation function in the ANN model was also the 
sigmoid function, and the test result was R2 ≥ 0.974 and RMSE = 0.1812, which is satisfying. The 
authors applied two methods to assess the relative importance of input variables, the partial 
derivative method, and the weight method.  
The partial derivative method is to calculate the partial derivative of the output variable with 
respect to each input variable at different input values. Then, a set of graphs of partial derivative 
versus the corresponding input variable are plotted. It is accessible that each variable at different 
input values will have what kind of influence on the output. If the partial derivate of an input 
variable is positive, the increase of this variable tends to increase the output variable. Conversely, if 
the partial derivate is negative, then the increase of the input variable will decrease the output 
variable. Furthermore, the higher the absolute partial derivate is, the more sensitive the output 
variable is to the input variable. The influence on the output variable may also vary at different 
input values. Finally, the relative importance of input variables can be calculated as:

make the dataset more uniform. However, the dataset in our study is exceptionally imbalanced, 
resulting in this method being insufficient to improve the prediction of extreme values. Whereas 
combining with the regrouped dataset improved the prediction more effectively. 
Abdollahi et al. developed an artificial neural network model to predict the p-cresol 
photodegradation rate from several input variables including irradiation time, pH, photocatalyst 
amount, and concentration of p-cresol. The dataset was obtained from the experiment. 
Furthermore, the authors optimized the model by comparing using different numbers of hidden 
neurons and learning algorithms (Abdollahi, Zakaria et al. 2013). The ANN model in this study was a 
3-layer network, and the activation functions of the hidden layer and output layer were both 
sigmoid. The numbers of neurons varying from 1 to 20 were tested under the use of four learning 
algorithms, namely Quick Propagation (QP), Incremental Backpropagation (IBP), Batch 
Backpropagation (BBP), and Levenberg-Marquardt (LM), respectively. The result shows that QP was 
the most effective algorithm with the highest training R2 (R2 = 0.9997), and the optimum number of 
hidden neurons was 10. Furthermore, the validation R2 was 0.98, indicating the excellent prediction 
accuracy of the model. In our study, the learning algorithm was BBP, which is the most commonly 
used algorithm for the fully connected neural network, and no other algorithm was tested. It is 
unknown if QP replacing BBP for model training would improve prediction quality. The authors also 
examined the importance of input variables. However, the paper did not explain the method of the 
importance index. 
Ebrahimi et al. compared the prediction of aluminum corrosion inhibitor efficiency by using the 
ANN model with that using Multiple linear regression (MLR) (Ebrahimi, Kalhor et al. 2016), showing 
that the ANN model presents more satisfactory results, while the multiple linear regression 
between input and output variables was poor. The ANN model’s structure was optimized before 
the training, resulting in a 1 hidden layer with 20 hidden neurons and sigmoid as activation 
function. The structure was simple whereas the number of hidden neurons was high compared to 
that only 5 input variables were chosen to predict the output. The learning algorithm for model 
training was Levenberg-Marquardt, and the learning rate was set as 0.32, which is 1000 times 
higher than that in our study. This difference may be due to a different learning algorithm being 
used.

Bucilua et al. first proposed the idea of model compression (Buciluǎ, Caruana et al. 2006). The best 
performing supervised learning model may demand a large amount of space to store the parameter 
and a significant amount of time to execute the model, limiting its applicability. As a result, 
developing a more compact model with comparable performance to replace the original model is 
required. According to the study, compressing the large model into a smaller one by training the 
dataset from the large model outperforms directly training on the original dataset. Training the 
compact model on the dataset from the original model is called Knowledge Distillation, and the 
original model and the compact model is called teacher model and student model, respectively. 
This model compression approach focuses on parameterization simplification, however in our 
study, model compression was taken a step further by simplifying input variables. 
Frosst and Hinton (Frosst and Hinton 2017) compressed a neural network model to a soft decision 
tree model to realize the interpretation of the model. In addition to the regression, the ANN model 
can also be very effective to perform the classification task. However, due to the black box model's 
feature, the ANN model cannot be interpreted, restricting the analysis of the complicated 
relationships between input and output variables. To overcome this limitation, the decision tree 
model was chosen as a white box model to receive knowledge from the ANN model and using the 
decision tree model would be easier to explain how the model came up with results. Additionally, 
the Knowledge Distillation retains the original model's performance, outperforming training on the 
original dataset. Same to the regression task, compressing the ANN model to a white box model, 
e.g., the multiple linear regression model can address the issues of non-interpretability. 
Nonetheless, if the relationship between input and output variables is complicated, the white box 
model cannot predict output values as accurately as the black box model. 
Furlanello et al. found that the ensemble of Knowledge Distillation model for multiple generations 
would outperform the original model significantly (Furlanello, Lipton et al. 2018). Instead of 
compressing the teacher model to achieve compactness, the Knowledge Distillation develops the 
student model with identical parameters to the teacher model. The information contained in the 
output distribution of a teacher model may provide a rich source of training signal, resulting in a 
second solution with enhanced generalization ability. The neural network reborn from the teacher 
model is called next generation model (BAN). The identical procedure was repeated until the 
performance enhancement hit a plateau. The final output value was determined from the 
ensemble of BANs by averaging the predictions of successive generations of BANs, and its 
performance was remarkably superior to that of the teacher model. In addition to the compactness 
of parameterization, interpretability, and the compactness of input variables, this is another 
breakthrough of Knowledge Distillation. The training BAN approach may be coupled with our 
research to enhance predictions before simplifying input variables. The author, however, only 
tested BAN on DenseNets, ResNets, and LSTM-based sequence models. Uncertainty exists as to 
whether the performance enhancement would still be valid in our research. 
 
6.9 Limitations 
The ANN model has several limitations that might be problematic when it comes to simplifying the 
input variables. To begin, the ANN model is difficult to be interpreted on its whole. As a result, 
==PAGE:57==
 
51 
 
when training is complete, the model can only be used for prediction and cannot be used to 
investigate the relationship between the variables. In addition, the ANN model is unable to predict 
the output of a dataset that contains compounds that have contrasting properties; as a result, the 
model's prediction is inaccurate, and the error rate is large. For example, it was found that the ANN 
model had great difficulty predicting the case of acids and bases in roots; however, the situation 
was addressed by dividing the chemicals into strongly and weakly dissociated compounds, which 
resulted in a much lower RMAE. As a result of the uneven data distribution, the model showed poor 
performance in predicting the extreme values throughout the study. To be more explicit, the lack of 
extreme data led to a loss in prediction accuracy since the model did not get enough training from 
the extreme dataset, resulting in a failed model compression. Because the ANN model requires an 
extensive dataset in order to be trained, just correcting for the uniformity of the dataset is not likely 
to necessarily enhance the prediction, while adding more extreme data will do so. On the other 
hand, it is difficult to reproduce the extreme values. As a result, the improvement that may be 
achieved by supplementing extreme values is restricted. The ANN model's architecture and 
parameters were developed via experiences and multiple attempts. Since the model was not tested 
with a variety of settings, it is not possible to determine whether the setting is optimum. However, 
the performance is still acceptable in the vast majority of cases. The tolerance limit was not always 
applicable. For the prediction of a case with a relatively high RMAE, it may not fail the model 
compression but will present unsatisfactory performance with the determined retained variables, 
suggesting that the 2-factor tolerance limit is over lax. Besides, both the chosen mechanistic model 
(PPCP model) and the ANN model in the study were not validated by the experimental dataset. 
Therefore, it is unknown how the compressed model performs when it comes to the real dataset. 
6.10 Future work and outlook 
The study needs extra effort to be enhanced. First, a more appropriate tolerance limit may be 
required, given that the 2-factor limit is inapplicable to the moderately large error case. 
Alternatively, the threshold for assessing if the 2-factor limit applies to the prediction should be 
determined. Then, a new tolerance limit would be established only for the prediction outside the 
threshold, while the 2-factor limit would continue to be used for the remaining instances. 
In the study, model compression was accomplished using the Morris method in the ANN model, 
whereas additional methods for evaluating the importance of input variables may be tested, and a 
more appropriate indicator may result in a more compressed model. In addition, the final 
determination of importance may be based on the combination of numerous indicators by adding a 
contribution rate to each of them. 
More extreme data are necessary to be obtained from the mechanistic model. Since it is difficult to 
reproduce, an analysis might be undertaken for the ranges of variables that contribute to an 
extreme output, making it simpler to generate extreme values within the specified variable ranges. 
Alternatively, the ANN model may be developed to identify extreme cases rather than directly 
predicting the extreme values. Due to the fact that the predicted extreme values are often lower 
than the actual values, an additional linear model might be trained for the regression between the 
predicted and actual values for the identified extreme cases, hence modifying the prediction of 
them.

As a black box model, the ANN model cannot be interpreted. Thus, a new perspective on combining 
the ANN model with the mechanical model of the environment emerges. Instead of completely 
replacing the mechanistic model with the ANN model, the ANN model may be used to replace a 
section of the compartments in the mechanistic model, therefore simplifying the internal 
computation while maintaining the interpretability of the model. In addition to realizing the 
advantages of both, the combined model may also simplify the input variables. However, 
combining the two models needs a large experimental dataset to support.

7. Conclusion 
The ANN was well trained from the generated dataset, but the prediction for the acids and bases in 
roots and neutral compounds in corns and leaves was unsatisfactory. For the acids and bases in 
roots, it was due to the dataset containing both the highly and weakly dissociated compounds, 
which have contrasting behaviors that confused the model. Therefore, separating two types of 
chemicals improved the prediction. However, the strongly dissociated compounds in roots were 
also not well-simulated with a relatively high RMAE due to the unreasonable Kaw values and air 
concentration since the ions do not have a vapor pressure, thus the ionizable compounds do not 
tend to volatilize to the air. It was fixed by lowering the range of two variables. The main factor that 
led to the poor prediction for neutral compounds in corns and leaves was the lack of extreme 
values. It was identified by discarding the extreme values and found that the RMAE was significantly 
reduced. However, the extreme values in the study are the real concern that may cause a potential 
hazard to the environment and human beings and, therefore, should not be abandoned. Thus, 
more extreme values were reproduced from a far larger dataset (size = 900000) to replenish the 
two cases, resulting in a significantly improved prediction of the extreme data and a much lower 
RMAE. Finally, the RMAE of each case was around 20% or even lower. 
The global sensitivity analysis using the Morris method in the ANN model was found to be superior 
to the others (rank correlation analysis in the mechanistic model and the ANN model) for 
simplifying input variables. The poor prediction cases failed the model compression since the RMAE 
became less sensitive to the decrease of input variables and did not vary significantly, and 
therefore, cannot reach the 2-factor tolerance limit. It is very necessary to achieve a satisfactory 
performance before the model compression. So, the optimization was conducted before the 
simplification of input variables. As a result, the compressed models achieved an ideal simplicity of 
input variables, and 37 input variables were simplified to only 15 to 5 left in the model, where the 
pKa and the log KOW were always the important variables to be retained. 
For the no air pollution cases, the magnitude of output values was significantly decreased when 
setting the air concentration to 0. The reason is the deposition of chemicals onto plant tissues does 
not occur, making the uptake of chemicals via roots become the main pathway. The scarcity of 
extreme data was the main factor in the poor prediction of no air pollution cases. Compensating for 
the extreme values improved the prediction significantly for most cases with RMAEs below 20%, 
while the acids and bases in leaves and neutral compounds in corns and leaves were still not ideal. 
Especially to the neutral compounds in corns and leaves, the RMAE did not decrease significantly, 
resulting in an RMAE of 80%. More extreme data were generated from a maximum size dataset to 
supplement those two cases, but the prediction was still not acceptable, with an RMAE decreased 
to 60%. It can be concluded that the extreme data in the case of neutral compounds in corns and 
leaves are scarce and hard to reproduce. Finally, the case of bases in leaves and neutral compounds 
in corns and leaves failed the model compression while the acids in leaves surprisingly reached the 
tolerance limit with only 5 input variables kept in the model. However, the RMAE of prediction after 
compression was 60% which was relatively high and made the model less applicable. The amount of 
retained variables in other cases were reduced to between 9 and 13.
