Abstract
In recent years, diﬀerent types of crowdsourced logistics services have emerged in the
Danish market. Understanding how people are stimulated to engage in crowdsourced lo-
gistics can clarify what measures should be implemented to support its further growth in
Denmark.
This study aims to examine the concept of integrating crowdsourced logistics for parcel
deliveries in a Danish context in order to explore its possibilities. Speciﬁcally, it investi-
gates how a proof-of-concept might be designed for a crowdsourced parcel delivery service
integrated with existing transport channels. In addition, it explores which business rules
should be deﬁned and what value proposition can be made for a minimum testable crowd-
sourced parcel service application. The study is primarily based upon the design thinking
approach where a protocol of diﬀerent methods was set as the guideline. By conducting a
workshop about existing logistics services, we learned that problems such as misdeliveries
and delivery time should be addressed. Also, an online survey was distributed to verify the
interest in participating in a crowdsourced logistics service as either a non-professional
courier or parcel sender. The results showed a high interest because of, among other
things, its contribution to reducing environmental harm, but some concerns related to
credibility of the service have to be addressed. These results led to creating some in-
teractive interfaces showing how an ideal version of the solution could work in real-life
and three iterations of models simulating parcel deliveries based on crowdsourcing. The
simulations were modeled with a simulation software called Arena, which allowed us to
imitate parcel delivery scenarios in every iteration. The ﬁnal iteration’s model was put
to the test with an expert within logistics services and supply chain. The goal was to
verify the model and the business rules we deﬁned from a realistic viewpoint. However,
the expert did not clearly verify the concept with its deﬁned business rules. These results
suggest that there is a potential for a crowdsourced approach but that further research
has to be made. This study’s simulation model did not have suﬃcient data about people’s
availability and usual trips to verify from a realistic perspective. Moreover, a veriﬁcation
of the simulation model with the deﬁned business rules has to be obtained.

On this basis, the concept of a crowdsourced parcel delivery service can be realized if the
above-mentioned temporal elements can be further investigated.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background & Motivation
Nowadays, customers’ eagerness for faster and ﬂexible freight delivery is snowballing,
which is putting much pressure on traditional delivery procedures. Customers’ demand
for same-day delivery obliges retailers to devise creative alternatives to reduce resources
such as transport costs. Moreover, they encounter competition from other big companies
such as Amazon, which have a large share of the retail market [1]. Also, they would have
to consider sustainability, as crowdsourced logistics does have some beneﬁcial implica-
tions. One aspect of logistics, which has had a signiﬁcant impact on logistics services, is
the information and communication technology (ICT) (e.g., matching algorithms, track-
and-trace, and routing software). It has had a signiﬁcant impact on the last mile delivery
[2]. The establishment of new business models and revision of existing ones, in the ﬁelds
of freight and personal transport, and even e-commerce, leads to new strategies, such as
“crowd logistics”. In brief, crowd logistics is a concept where ordinary people undertake
the delivery task [3]. Based upon the idea of sharing, crowd logistics constitutes an es-
sential factor in improving the sustainability of urban logistics [4, 5]. It has turned out
to be a focal area of research for handling the problem of last-mile delivery in cities [5].
One of the beneﬁts of crowdsourcing logistics is its impact on emissions. The emissions
of pollutants, such as nitrogen oxides from vehicles (e.g., cargo trucks), can potentially
be reduced by approximately 55% [5]. It is also worth noting that the "crowds" can be
granted a monetary reward while having a joyful experience in the process of participation
[6, 7].
Business models applying crowdsourced logistics are valuable and impactful as proven by
personal transport (e.g., Uber, Mytaxi, and GetTaxi), freight transport (e.g., Bringbee
1
==PAGE:12==
and MyWays), and by purchase and delivery services (e.g., Shopwings, Algel, and Yipbee)
[8]. The last-mile delivery services inspired the development of concepts, business models,
and research on expanding to the broader scope of crowd logistics [9].
1.1.1
Setting the scene
E-commerce businesses and local courier companies in Bangladesh are struggling with
maintaining last-mile delivery services, which is the problem the startup company "Deli-
man" based in Bangladesh addresses. According to the company’s owner, they have inter-
viewed several merchants and local courier companies that do not utilize any technological-
based solution. 95% of the merchants stated that due to delays in deliveries, their business
progress is on hold. Moreover, Deliman also learned that local courier companies do not
have a good ﬁnancial standing to invest in expensive technology, providing a better cus-
tomer experience. Last-mile delivery outside the capital, Dhaka, such as in rural areas
or semi-urban, is even worse.
It is taking considerable time, and hence, a signiﬁcant
number of potential customers are avoiding e-commerce business. Therefore, Deliman
has established a crowdsourced logistics service in Bangladesh [10], in which merchants,
couriers, and customers are connected in a single platform, where merchants update their
desired product to ship using Deliman Merchant tools. The Deliman backend helps ﬁnd
the nearest courier/deliveryman and sends the order to the end-customer with real-time
tracking. A stepping stone towards revolution for them is exploring the possibilities of en-
tering the Danish market within the ﬁeld of crowdsourced logistics. A challenge Deliman
is encountering is due to their lack of knowledge about how a logistics service, including
crowdsourcing, is run in Denmark.
1.1.2
Scope of the thesis
In this thesis, a proof-of-concept prototype will be explored to integrate a door-to-door
parcel delivery service in a Danish context. A door-to-door delivery is deﬁned as a service,
where the courier will pick-up the parcel from a pick-up location, and deliver it to the de-
livery address [11]. In this particular project, it is going to be one or more non-professional
couriers depending on the route. Due to the context-sensitivity of the number of partners
involved and lack of publication, particularly on the diﬀerent interfaces and roles in the
multi-agent service delivery scenarios, the project’s novelty is the design activities’ out-
comes and business rules. Based on some sample orders we fabricate in a Danish context,
a simulation will be built to simulate a parcel delivery process deﬁned for the anticipated
business process.

1.2
Aim, research questions & assumptions
The concept of crowdsourced logistics, these days, is not incorporated into the transporta-
tion of parcels, but rather the common procedure is that each enterprise has its vehicles,
which are used to transport the parcels. The idea of having a crowdsourced logistics
service has been a subject of broad interest for some time in Denmark, but there is no
clear answer as to whether it can also be integrated with public transport as the trans-
port channel to deliver parcels in Denmark - It is integrated with other existing transport
channels, which will be described later. However, we have seen it being successfully used
with personal transport (e.g., GoMore), and food transport (e.g., Wolt). For instance,
Wolt oﬀers deliveries based upon crowdsourcing within short distances and designated
areas in Denmark (i.e., intracity). The solution of this project will strive to explore an
intercity crowdsourced logistics approach upon existing transport channels. It is note-
worthy that current crowdsourced solutions generally seem restricted to shorter distances
meaning they only operate within a city or nearby cities. With that in mind, this thesis
will investigate the following questions:
• How might we design a proof-of-concept prototype for a crowdsourced parcel delivery
service, integrated with existing transport channels in a Danish context?
• Which business rules should be deﬁned, and what value proposition can be made for
a minimum testable crowdsourced parcel service application?
We intend to answer the ﬁrst research question by diving into the modeling of a sim-
ulation, which should simulate how such a concept could work in real life and give an
impression of what it would mean to incorporate a crowdsourced approach. As for the
second question, based on sample data set and simulation of business rules we deﬁne along
the way, we will explore a minimum viable product.
During our meetings with the owner of Deliman, we learned that they are interested in
entering the Danish market for delivery of parcels. They would like to devise a new way of
doing it, which deviates from the existing ways of doing it by incorporating crowds, thereby
providing crowdsourced logistics integrated with existing transport channels yielding ad-
ditional value. These days, there is no service considering public transport as a transport
channel for parcel deliveries in Denmark, and therefore, they would like to be among the
ﬁrst to suggest the approach where all kinds of transport channels are supported.

Delimitation
The proof-of-concept will be restricted to the business rules deﬁned. Moreover, due to the
time-constraint, we will not be focusing on the sender when creating artifacts but rather
the carrier and admin. However, the sender will be included in the simulation as senders
trigger the initial step (i.e., ordering a parcel delivery). They will also be included in some
of the methods we will utilize to obtain some insights about existing logistics services.
In addition to the aspect of the time-constraint, we have also noticed many similarities
among logistics services (e.g., PostNord, GLS, UPS, etc.)
on how a sender orders a
delivery, which is why we have decided upon not focusing on the senders. Even though
our ﬁrst research question involves existing transport channels, we will not be focusing on
route optimization, but rather providing deliveries regardless of the transport channels.
Furthermore, normally we would develop the software used for the simulations ourselves
due to our background in software development. However, we opt for using a simulation
software instead due to the time-constraint. We do not believe we can develop the software
and compose the report within time.

2.1
Prior researches on crowdsourced logistics
This section will investigate previously done research on crowdsourced logistics and what
is related to it with some examples of success.
The term “Crowd Logistics” has its roots from “Crowdsourcing”, which is a neologism
initially popularized by JeﬀHowe [12] and a combination of the words “crowd” and “out-
sourcing”. The word "crowd" represents the people aspect (i.e., a mass of people) while
"outsourcing" deﬁnes the shift of certain processes and activities to third parties [9]. In-
tegrating the concept of crowdsourcing in logistics is recognized as crowd logistics. The
concept is based on a network of connected people helping other people in their spare
time for a certain fee. The whole crowd working aspect is coordinated through an infor-
mation and communication platform controlled by a company or an agent [13], and can
be accessed externally through the internet or internally through the intranet [14].
The literature review conducted by Mehmann et al. [15] examine the topic of optimiz-
ing algorithms and shows that almost 50% of the papers centered around the technique.
Usually, these algorithms utilize data that is generated based upon crowds for real-time
traﬃc information, and routing [16, 17, 18] or the optimization of cities or networks [19].
6
==PAGE:17==
Upon investigating crowd logistics models, Mehmann et al. [8] were able to propose a
new concept for a sustainable implementation of crowd logistics services. They learned
that two aspects of the concept were mandatory:
• The network consisting of the customer and carrier crowd signiﬁes the vital resource
and must be maintained.
• The return on investment is usually not expected to be positive in the short term.
However, it may be positive in the long-term.
Moreover, upon analyzing, they identiﬁed four implications for practice.
• Having an in-depth strategy, comprehensive vision of the competitive situation, and
an innovative concept before implementing a service is essential.
• Financial planning should allow service rearrangement.
• Since region-speciﬁc characteristics might impact crowd logistics, these should also
be considered and analyzed before the service is implemented.
• The network should be established before implementing the service since it comprises
the essential resource and sets the requirement for having a functioning service.
Fessler et al. [20] have investigated the possibilities of integrating crowdsourced delivery
of parcels in Copenhagen in order to assess the potential and opportunities of utilizing
existing capacity in passenger transport for parcel deliveries. More speciﬁcally, they in-
vestigated a concept compromised of parcel locker terminals and individual use of public
transport. The idea was that passengers would carry one (or more) parcel(s) from one
train/bus station to another, allowing van drivers to drop smaller parcels at stations on
the outskirts of a city rather than driving into and across a city. Such an approach has
not been practically tested out, but from an academic point of view, researches [21, 22]
have been conducted estimating the willingness of engaging in such an approach in Rome.
The research’s ﬁndings were that it would contribute to reducing pollution. However, the
remarkable ﬁnding was that people’s willingness to engage in such an approach was based
upon the location of the parcel locker terminals. The investigation, which was based on a
survey they issued through Epinion involving 500 participants, showed that “26,7% of the
respondents answer "Often" or "Always" to an introductory question about how often they
would carry a parcel on their most-used trip, when only brieﬂy introduced to the concept.”
[20, p. 1]. In addition, they were able to identify the characteristics of potential non-
professional couriers. The characteristics were younger people, students, and especially
7
==PAGE:18==
people who consider themselves environmentally conscious. Besides, they introduced an
economic compensation for the carriers consisting of credits for the transport platform,
allowing them to have a cheaper upcoming trip. A general insight they acquired was, for
instance, that “it would be advantageous to impart the concept as a community-oriented
movement, where environmental, social and economic sustainability are the baseline” [20,
p. 10]. When it comes to the monetary reward, previous researches have shown that for
crowdsourced logistics, compensations serve as vital factors for carriers continued partic-
ipation [6, 23]. The carriers will probably use their leisure participating in crowdsourced
logistics to get an additional income [23]. Although the monetary aspect is not the sole
reason for carriers to engage in crowdsourcing, ﬁnancial rewards are still essential [24].
Because of this, some challenges may arise, such as reasonable pricing and compensation
plans [25].
2.2
Existing solutions
The potential of crowd logistics can also be assessed based on various successful compa-
nies, such as the taxi service Uber, the last mile delivery service Trunkrs, or the service
Wolt [9]. Some other notable mentions are Amazon’s Flex program and mover. What
they all have in common is the goal in mind. They all strive to crowdsource their deliv-
ery tasks meaning that they are outsourcing the delivery tasks to an indeﬁnite group of
individuals through an online platform [26]. For instance, Amazon Flex program [27] is
a crowdsourced delivery service meaning that regular people can spend a shift delivering
packages, groceries, or dinner. Amazon grants the carriers a monetary reward for deliv-
ering Amazon’s packages. Being a Flex delivery driver resembles the position of an Uber
driver. However, instead of transporting people, drivers deliver packages.
A Danish success is the transport company "mover" [28], which provides an on-demand
transport service enabling drivers to help people and businesses with both international
and domestic deliveries. mover exploits the possibilities provided by sharing economy and
digitization, and based on that, they provide a platform where private, professional, and
truck drivers can register themselves informing they have got some available capacity in
their vehicle, and can do some delivery tasks. This is considered to be a direct competitor
of the solution this thesis is going to dive into. What distinguishes the concept mover
from the solution this thesis will dive into is that the drivers from mover are only vehicle
drivers - Smaller packages in Copenhagen can be delivered by bicycle riders. They do not
consider public transport as a transport channel. By requesting a quote for a delivery
on their webapp [29] from Greve to Århus C, we discovered the hefty price tag. Though
they oﬀer same-day delivery, the cheapest quote for a shipment weighing maximum 100kg
8
==PAGE:19==
and has the dimensions 80 x 80 x 80 cm was estimated to around 2.800 DKK. Based on
this, mover might be an optimal choice for larger packages. In addition, the solution we
intend to investigate does not single out the transport channel, but rather function as an
intermediary between the carriers and senders.
2.3
Takeaways
A thorough review of the literature revealed the roots of the term "Crowd logistics",
its meaning, and the concept behind it. Also, we learned about aspects that should be
considered and what implications follow. One key aspect of the concept that we will be
focusing on in our project is the network consisting of the customer and carrier crowd.
Since this project does not involve the monetary aspect, we will not be considering the
return on investment as a crucial part of our investigation. Moreover, since this will be a

parcels that is on their way to a particular destination for a good cause (i.e., reducing
pollution) while receiving a monetary reward. Moreover, their ﬁndings also conﬁrm our

are interested in participating in a crowdsourced approach. Consequently, this review
highlights some key points in the ﬁeld of crowdsourced logistics and contributes to an-
swering our research questions.
We have now acquired an overview of the person characteristics that would likely engage
in such a concept and key parameters (i.e., an environmentally, socially, and economi-

the existing solutions, we can conﬁrm that no solution in Denmark yielding the concept
we will deal with is established. Furthermore, we have learned about some user roles in
a logistics service. Typically, some of the stakeholders are the senders sending parcels,
couriers delivering the parcels, and the support/admin, who sometimes manages parts of
the interaction between the carriers and senders. Otherwise, it is handled by an IT-based

Design thinking
In order to understand the users, challenge assumptions, redeﬁne problems, and create a
solution to prototype and test, we undertook the design thinking process, as it extricates
the bloat and uncertainty of product design. In addition, it pushes toward an objectively

grounded result to bring an appropriate solution to light [40]. In other words, it is a
process for creative problem solving, which helped us in developing an innovative solution
based on the identiﬁed needs. Moreover, it is an iterative process allowing us to adapt
our solution as we learned throughout the process. According to d.school [33], the design
thinking process consists of ﬁve modes, which we underwent throughout the journey of
writing this thesis. In the following, we will be explaining how we undertook the process.
Empathize: This mode helped us to ascertain the feasibility of a crowd-

Empathy Workshop
Furthermore, we conducted an empathy workshop with ﬁve participants who would
like to take part in a crowdsourced parcel logistics service as either a carrier, sender
or both. According to Polaine et al. [44], when preparing for a user workshop,
one should encourage pairs of friends to attend the workshop since they are more
likely to be comfortable and honest in their answers when undertaking a workshop.
Moreover, groups of 4 to 16 people are ideal.
We did indeed adopt the idea of

==PAGE:25==
asking friends to participate in our workshop, but we only conducted it with ﬁve
participants, as it was a relatively small workshop. All participants were users of
diﬀerent parcel delivery services in the age group 20-30 studying diﬀerent engineer-
ing branches at DTU.
We gave the participants some tasks enabling them to pour out their take on the
existing parcel logistics services and create the outcome. This enabled us to see po-
tential senders’ behaviors in the scenarios and learn what people usually like about
logistics services and what they dislike. The empathy workshop was conducted as
follows:
– Breaking down the ice: Since the goal was to understand people’s problem
areas and needs regarding existing parcel logistics services, the activities were
intended to let the participants remember and reﬂect on past experiences.
While it might have been alluring to begin the problem discovery directly, this
activity constituted an essential purpose. The motive behind performing this
activity was sharing experiences, thoughts, and feelings. We wanted to create
an environment where participants felt comfortable sharing by breaking the ice
with something sweet, such as chocolate.
– Reaction words: Next, they had to reﬂect on the most recent experience and
write down the words that came to mind. We set 60 seconds for this. After-
ward, we discussed the story behind the words, which helped with refreshing
the memories and putting them in the discussion context. It enabled us to get
a sense of how participants feel and think about prior experiences.
– Problem identiﬁcation: Next, they had to write down up to three problems
related to the topic and ask themselves what might have caused the problems.
We set 5 min for this. Afterward, we discussed the problems allowing us to
identify problems and categorize them based upon whether they were related
or unique.
The workshop’s preparation was inspired by Sara Chizari et al. [32], who currently
work within the ﬁeld of user experience at Red Hat. They have collectively worked
on deﬁning an empathy workshop enabling one to undertake a design thinking ap-
proach in order to discover problems. In particular, we conducted activities derived
from their deﬁnitions of warming up and empathizing. Although their work inspired
us, we did have our twist on it. First of all, we did not use any of the activities
suggested for the warm-up. We broke down the ice with something sweet and had a
quick talk with the participants about diverse matters. Moreover, we did add a time

identiﬁcation").
Deﬁne: After having understood the users’ needs and problems, we were
able to deﬁne problem statements.
3.1.4
Interpretation of results
We used the Point of View (POV) Madlib method described by d.school [33] to
shape users’ problems based upon what we identiﬁed in the ﬁrst mode (i.e., Em-
pathize). They were shaped into practical problem statements launching us into
kick-starting ideation in the right direction, namely, in a goal-oriented manner. The
beneﬁts of POV are summarizing the perspectives on users’ needs, which can be used
throughout the design thinking process. The problem statement in POV consists
of three key components: User + User’s need + Surprising insight. These
three components comprise a complete problem statement by using the POV madlib
described by d.school as “[USER] needs to [USER’S NEED] because [SURPRISING
INSIGHT]” [33, p. 21]. In our context, the "user" was a participant of the empathy
workshop conducted (i.e., a person sending/ordering a parcel), while the "user’s
need" goes without saying; it was the needs we identiﬁed in the workshop. Lastly,
we have got "surprising insight", which was the insights we uncovered during the
workshop. It is worth mentioning that not all POV statements advanced to the
next mode. We excluded some statements as the potential solutions were common
among the existing logistics services (i.e., the solutions would not provide any value
setting them apart from existing solutions) or out of this thesis’ scope.
Ideate: The ﬁrst two modes helped us understand the users and their
problems concerning existing logistics services and deﬁne problem statements related
to their problems. This mode represented the transition from identifying problems
to exploring solutions. Here, we prioritized breadth over depth as we looked for a
diverse range of ideas to prototype and test with real people.

Prototype: In this mode, we created a prototype of the solution that

Going forward, we will be omitting some aspects:
• Tracking: Learning about the parcel’s whereabouts and the status of the delivery
is a common procedure among the logistics services such as GLS and Postnord.
Therefore, we will not be considering this aspect moving forward. However, we will
be assuming that the tracking service is already integrated as per the standard.
• Misdelivery: Human errors are likely to happen. Though we do not believe we
can preclude them, we are ﬁrm believers in the possibility of minimizing the number
of misdeliveries.
• Damaged goods: Damaged goods are an unfortunate but inevitable expense and
might leave a bad impression on the senders. However, with proper measures, it
might be possible to minimize the likelihood of damaged goods.
• Queue waiting time: Finding a solution to avoiding a queue at the post oﬃce/a-
gency when sending a parcel can indeed be achieved. With proper measures, the
senders would be able to avoid standing in a queue at the post oﬃce altogether.
• Delivery time: Minimizing the delivery time or guaranteeing that a parcel will be
delivered within the estimated delivery time might lead to a complicated aspect in
a crowdsourced approach since the crowds are not employees. Therefore, they have,
to some extent, the freedom to deliver whenever it suits them during their travel.
In addition, since the approach will rely on crowds, estimating the delivery time can
be a hassle, as it depends on a carrier’s availability.
• Postage: Determining the cost of parcel deliveries can become a hassle since it
depends on several factors such as competitiveness and monetary reward for the
carrier. Hence, we decided not to dive into this aspect since it is out of our scope.

• Solution 1: An application-based platform that will facilitate cooperation between
authorized (e.g., NemID validated) carriers and an admin, which will oil the wheels
of parcel deliveries. This includes an application for both the admin and carrier.
They should be provided with the necessary features in order to function per their
roles. For instance, since it will be deliveries carried out by crowds, the carriers
would need a feature allowing them to register when they are available to carry a
parcel and from/to where. Another example of a feature is directing the carriers to
the destination, where they need to drop-oﬀthe parcels. This could either be to the
recipient or an intermediate parcel locker terminal, which will store the parcel until
the next carrier picks it up and delivers it to the recipient or another intermediate
terminal. Concerning the admin, a feature that would make sense is enabling the
admin to manage the parcel orders, which are the senders’ requests for someone to
deliver a parcel from location A to B. This solution covers:
– 1., 2., and 3. HMW questions of Queue waiting time
– 1. and 3. HMW questions of Delivery time
– 3. HMW questions of Misdelivery
– 2. and 3. HMW questions of Damaged goods
• Solution 2: A portal in which senders have requested a quote for someone to deliver
a parcel for them. Within this portal, the carriers, who are authorized, should be
able to give a quote and await the senders’ response. The idea is that a carrier gives
a quote if he has got a trip near the destination and can pick up the parcel from
the sender. When the sender selects an oﬀer, they will be provided with the option
of exchanging necessary information (i.e., addresses, date, time, etc.). This solution
covers:
– 1., 2., and 3. HMW questions of Queue waiting time
– 1. HMW questions of Delivery time
– 2. and 3. HMW questions of Damaged goods
• Solution 3: A mobile application preventing misdeliveries. Every time the carrier
picks up a parcel for delivery, the application will be tracking the carrier’s location.

5.1
Summary of the problem
Crowdsourcing is a well-known sourcing model used by individuals or organizations to
obtain various goods and services. Recently, crowdsourcing has been used in multiple
tasks such as transporting goods and people. However, crowdsourcing has not been fully
incorporated with parcel deliveries in Denmark. Moreover, only one established company
in Denmark called mover crowdsources parcel deliveries. Nonetheless, the company does
not consider public transport for parcel transportation. Instead, the company only relies
on vehicles such as vans and cars to deliver parcels. This study investigates crowdsourcing
in parcel delivery by not singling out the transport channel but is open for all types. A
study conducted by Fessler et al. (2020) in Denmark has investigated crowdsourcing for
parcel deliveries using public transportation. The study examines the characteristics of
60
==PAGE:71==
people who would engage in a crowdsourced approach to deliver parcels. A conducted sur-
vey showed that 26,7% of the 500 respondents would likely engage when only being asked
an introductory question about how often they would carry a parcel on their most-used
trip. In addition, characteristics of potential non-professional couriers were identiﬁed.
The characteristics were younger people, students, and especially people who consider
themselves environmentally conscious. Their study’s key ﬁnding was that it would be
advantageous to emphasize the concept as a community-oriented movement, where en-
vironmental, social, and economic sustainability are the cornerstones. Nevertheless, the
study did not report any prototype to measure the diﬀerence with and without public
transportation for parcel deliveries in Denmark due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Instead,
the study only concerned the last-mile delivery to a parcel locker terminal. According
to the best of our knowledge, no previous studies in Denmark have reported a design
thinking approach to monitor the usefulness of crowdsourcing in providing parcel deliv-
ery services using public and other means of transportation. Consequently, this study
investigates the feasibility by investigating demand, simulating load using the simulation
software Arena while deﬁning underlying business rules, and developing and testing a
click-through prototype.
5.2
Why is this study important?
For this study, we did a SWOT analysis to conduct a feasibility study to assess whether it
would be feasible for Deliman to enter the Danish market of parcel logistics. The SWOT
analysis helped clarify that Deliman has several opportunities to enter the market with
something new. For example, the startup can become the ﬁrst to provide a crowdsourced
service for parcel deliveries in Denmark, where the transport channel is irrelevant. Ad-
ditionally, the SWOT analysis made it easy to check the project’s feasibility. Some risks
include a lack of participation of carriers and competitors having the resources to put
the idea into eﬀect before Deliman. Furthermore, examining users’ viewpoints toward
existing logistics services was vital. Therefore, we conducted a survey and a workshop to
learn how people would eventually feel about a crowdsourced approach. After conducting
the survey and workshop, we observed that a crowdsourced parcel delivery service could
have a potential in a Danish context. This is based upon the fact that 79,63% of the 54
participants of the survey would be interested in utilizing the service as either a sender
or carrier. Moreover, we learned about several problems related to the current logistics
services, such as the queue waiting time at the post agency and tracking. Overall, the
methods utilized so far veriﬁed the potential of a crowdsourced approach in a Danish con-
text. Narrowing down the research problem is important for research. We used the Point
61
==PAGE:72==
of View method to deﬁne problem statements for the diﬀerent problem types we learned
by conducting the workshop. This allowed us to narrow down the problems we would
be diving into. The problems we decided upon diving into were related to misdeliveries,
receiving damaged goods, queue waiting time at the post agency, and the delivery time.
On the contrary, the problems we decided upon omitting were related to traceability and
postage. As we experienced a common procedure of how parcels are tracked among Dan-
ish logistics services, we decided upon these. In addition, the postage aspect was out of
the scope of this thesis.
5.3
Proposed solution
We began by asking "How Might We" questions to generate various ideas to come up
with solutions for the identiﬁed problem statements. As a result, we opted for the solu-
tion that covered most problems. This study’s solution is based on an application-based
platform that facilitates the cooperation between authorized carriers and admin. Using
this application-based platform, the carriers would be able to register their upcoming
travels. In case the carrier’s travel plans ﬁt with a destination route, the carrier could
carry the parcel and deliver it to a certain destination (i.e., either the recipient or a parcel
locker terminal). In addition to this, initially, we deﬁned assumptions based upon our
experience with software development about what people would consider intuitive in an
application. For instance, we assumed that people would consider an application intu-
itive if the clickable object’s outcomes were either represented by instructions or conveyed
by general symbols. To verify these assumptions, we created interactive wireframes and
conducted Wizard of Oz tests to observe how people would interpret them. The goal was
to ensure that the test users would understand the task ﬂow and its context. However,
some assumptions were not fulﬁlled. For example, we assumed that the interactive ﬂow
experience would be smooth if there were a few functions. Nonetheless, this assumption
was refuted as more features were suggested. Furthermore, we concertized the problem-
solution by building a prototype simulating parcel deliveries based on crowdsourcing. This
helped to understand which aspects of the idea proved diﬃcult or impossible to imple-
ment. We were inexperienced in the supply chain aspect besides working as a courier for
PostNord. Therefore, we coped with the situation by creating iterations of simulations.
We deﬁned a scenario and assumptions for each iteration and ended up with three it-
erations. First, we wanted to learn about the simulation software and how it works in
general. This aimed to establish a starting point where we could iterate until we felt we
had a realistic simulation model according to our limited understanding and supply chain
experience. We asked for the assistance of an individual who had prior experience with
62
==PAGE:73==
logistics services and supply chain to verify the model. Nevertheless, he did not verify
the model but proposed how matters such as misdeliveries and damaged goods should
be dealt with. He also guided us about traceability, which needs to be addressed for a
successful solution.
5.4
Outcome of the study
This study’s outcome is based on a proof-of-concept derived from the design thinking
process to devise a solution. To propose a solution, we modeled a simulation that shows
how a crowdsourced parcel delivery service could work in a Danish context. In addition
to the simulation, we also designed wireframes showing how the end product could work
for an admin and carrier. The proof-of-concept is comprised of the wireframes and sim-
ulation. When it comes to our second research question, we believe that the scenario
and assumptions regarding the last iteration and the wireframes’ features constitute the
business rules for minimum testable service. Concerning the value proposition, one of the
beneﬁts of a crowdsourced approach is the reduction of CO2 emission. A crowdsourced
approach could be a service that minimizes harm to the environment and thereby provides
sustainable transportation of parcels.
The key ﬁndings of this study are constituted by:
• SWOT analysis: Analyzing the internal and external factors of Deliman clariﬁed
that the company does not have any competitive advantages at the moment, but
that it does not mean it will jeopardize the success since they have several opportu-
nities to capitalize on. A big opening is the ﬁrst-mover advantage since there is no
crowdsourced parcel delivery service in Denmark, including all transport channels.
The analysis helped conﬁrm the feasibility, but that some threats can jeopardize the
success. For instance, competitors having the resources to enter the Danish market
with the same idea. However, considering Deliman’s prior experience on how to run
a crowdsourced logistics service, we assessed that it could provide Deliman with a
good starting point.
• Results of the survey: As mentioned earlier, the survey showed that a crowd-
sourced approach has a potential in a Danish context since 79,63% of the 54 par-
ticipants would be interested in utilizing the service either as a sender or carrier. It
showed how the participants’ habitual used type of transport impacts their choices.
Participants using their vehicle are more likely to prefer a delivery option that
will reduce the expense or delivery time regardless of the emissions of CO2, while
63
==PAGE:74==
participants riding a bicycle or using public transport are moving toward a more
eco-friendly option.
• Results of the workshop: Conducting the workshop provided us with insights
about problems related to existing logistics services that should be coped with. The
workshop participants had to reﬂect upon their last experience with a parcel logis-
tics service and describe it with the words that ﬁrst come to mind. Hereafter, the
participants had to point out up to three problems related to parcel logistics ser-
vices. This enabled us to categorize the problems into related problems and unique
problems. The related problems comprised postage, delivery time, and misdeliv-
ery, while the unique problems comprised queue waiting time at the post agency,
receiving damaged goods, and parcel tracking.
• Wizard of Oz test: Conducting the tests on the wireframes showed that people
felt they were missing some features. When asking them about what they would
change if they had a magic wand, a suggestion for the admin perspective was to
add more space between the direct and indirect sections to make the view more
clear. Besides, additional features such as a chat between the carrier and admin,
so the carrier can prove with a photo that a parcel is delivered. From the carrier’s
perspective, it was suggested that an additional feature detailing the parcels (e.g.,
size and weight) should be added. Overall, the participants understood the ﬂow and
the perspective, which is a good sign for future work. It worth noting that we did
not address the sender’s perspective, since it was set as a delimitation of this study.
• Simulations modeled in Arena: As mentioned, we iterated over three rounds
of simulation models. We started by learning about the simulation software, which
helped us in modeling a more sound simulation. The second iteration taught us
about how to incorporate realistic routes with their travel times. In the last iteration,
we utilized the acquired experience to model a simulation that seemed reliable to
the extent of our knowledge of supply chain. The model was reliable enough to the
extent that we only considered the processing time of parcel deliveries.
5.5
Limitations of the study
The ﬁndings of this study have to be seen in light of some limitations. Though the ﬁndings
were reasonable, we did not have suﬃcient data on people’s availability, which is why we
assumed the availability constraints. The primary limitation to the generalization of these
results is the data on people people’s availability, usual trips, and sample orders from a
64
==PAGE:75==
real life context. Those are needed to make the simulation more reliable. The simulation
modeling is complete, but is reliable to the extent that we only consider the total delivery
time.
The second limitation concerns the business rules we deﬁned. We were unable to get the
simulation model with the business rules veriﬁed by someone experienced within the ﬁeld
of logistics services and supply chain. We did not get a clear answer as to whether the
model can be applied in a Danish context and whether the deﬁned business rules were
realistic. Therefore, the simulation is limited to providing a structure of the data and
their relationships within the deﬁned business rules but lacks reliability due to lack of
contextual data. To sum up, we do believe the concept of a crowdsourced parcel delivery
service can be realized if the above-mentioned limitations are further investigated.
5.6
Future work
For future work, this research shows some directions to understand the proposed mecha-
nism deeply and implementing diﬀerent new methods in a Danish context to reduce the
system’s curiosity. This section also presents some ﬁndings and interesting research topics
for further topic understanding.
• Considering the Wizard of Oz test results, the next step would be to develop the
application of the wireframes. Since the most popular mobile operating systems
are Android and iOS [57], it would be advantageous to develop the application
supporting those operating systems.
Therefore, we would suggest developing a
web application by ﬁrst utilizing the MoSCoW method to identify and prioritize
functionalities of the application. A web application can cover the perspectives of
all actors, since it works on desktops and mobile devices. The carrier would likely
use the application on a mobile device since he/she is on the go when delivering
the parcel(s). On the contrary, the parcel sender and admin might use the desktop
instead.
• An important aspect to look into is verifying the amount of parcels that are realis-
tically sent. This would help improve the simulation model so that it gives a more
realistic view of the processing of the parcels (i.e. how well it goes). This would
also aﬀect the capacity of the parcel locker terminals. The simulation model of this
study has assumed the capacity since Deliman informed that the terminal could be
provided regardless of the capacity.
• It is also important to learn what the outcome would be if the simulation model
with its business rules gets veriﬁed, as it will give a better idea of the concept’s
65
==PAGE:76==
potential. Since we were unable to get the business rules veriﬁed, we cannot deem
them reliable.
• Another important aspect is to learn what the outcome would be if data concerning
people’s availability is available to improve the simulation model with more realistic
time-intervals of when a carrier is available to carry a parcel. The time-intervals of
carriers’ availabilities are assumed in this study. So, it would give a more realistic
simulation in case the data is contextual.
• It would be interesting to consider a European startup compared to Deliman’s ex-
isting one to know the use of ICT from a European perspective concerning native
behavior and approaches. It has been mentioned in the scope of the thesis that this
prototype is not fully aligned with the extracted system of that startup because of
the limitation and sensitivity. That is why collaboration with the native system is
a must to know the complex regional problems.
• The model that has been constructed in this research can also be changed as it
only touches environmental aspects but does not provide a healthy study regarding
sustainable parcel delivery (SPD). One notable aspect that could be considered
is integrating electric and fossil fuel vehicles, which are attracting at a remarkable
pace in Europe. This could reduce the environmental concern of cautious people and
increase the model’s eﬀectiveness compared to conventional transportation means.
• Another aspect that could be interesting is the reliability of communication in all
the processes. In future work, standardizing the communication between the sender
and recipient could be standardized to check our prototype’s eﬀectiveness and the
development or modiﬁcation of existing protocols. In addition, apart from limited
activities about current prototype research, the involvement of existing delivery
structures, especially in Europe, utilized in the future because this resource manip-
ulation would check out the mainstream processes of the proposed system. With
this, it is expected to test willing-to-deliver citizens’ responses and delivery man-
agement.
• While our proposal can help reduce traﬃc jams, noise, and pollutant emissions,
which is a growing concern among nations, its value can be increased if environmentally-
friendly vehicles (e.g., bicycle, electronic vehicles, etc.) are used. Developing sepa-
rate delivery routes for parcels would not be necessary, leading to cost eﬃciency and
reduction. The key factor in this would lie in the computation of sustainable distri-
bution routes for parcel deliveries, which will console the people who are conscious
about sustainable management.
