Abstract
The aim of the project is to investigate time-varying dynamic properties by the use of opera-
tional modal analysis (OMA). To investigate the time-varying dynamic properties an experi-
mental setup is used. The setup consists of a five-storey shear frame, which is very flexible in
one and only one direction. The structure is mounted on a shaking table which can apply any
form of displacement loads. The displacements of the structure are measured at each floor with
a laser. The dynamic properties can be changed throughout the experiment by changing the
damping. The damping of the structure is implemented in the form of eddy current damping,
using two pairs of electromagnets. Each pair can be turned on and off independently of each
other and thereby change the dynamic properties of the structure.
A digital twin is produced along with the experimental setup. The digital twin allows to es-
timate the physical output a given input would create. The digital twin is tuned so that the
numerical frequencies and the numerical response, follows the measured frequencies and re-
sponse as closely as possible.
Two different OMA methods are used to find the dynamic properties. The two different methods
are the Ibrahim Time Domain method (ITD) and the Time Domain Poly Reference method
(TDPR). Both of the methods are well documented, and used to calculate the frequencies,
damping ratios and modal participation factors from a random load. In this project, the two
methods are used to analyze the time-varying dynamic properties by dividing the original time
series into many smaller subseries, each with overlap. A 90% overlap has been found satisfying.
This gives the dynamic properties for each of the subseries which can be analyzed as a function
of time.
Two different algorithms are developed and tested to estimate changes in the dynamic proper-
ties and estimate the dynamic properties before and after the change. Both of the algorithms
are optimized to find instantaneous changes in the dynamic properties. The first algorithm
is based on the absolute change in damping ratio between two points in the time series. The
distance between the two points are constant throughout the entire time series. The changes
in the dynamic properties are estimated by the peak values of the absolute change. The second
algorithm are based on the CUSUM method which are used to detect a change in the mean
value of a series of data, by calculating the cumulative sum of the individual deviations from
the mean damping ratio.
Both of the two algorithms make it possible to estimate changes in the dynamic properties.
However, with the limitation that changes must be greater than the noise that occurs by
analyzing a long data series with constant damping. The amount of noise can be reduced by
increasing the length of the subseries, but that requires more data and longer calculation time.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Mankind has since the old days constructed buildings and bridges. The technology of the time
has always set the limits to heights and slenderness of the buildings and the length of the spans
of the bridges. That is also the reason why old bridges always have shorter spans. Additionally
they are also made typically made out of stone, and are therefore very heavy and massive. The
dynamic properties of these kind of structures were not part of defining the limits. Today, on
the other hand, the opposite is true, and the dynamic properties are more important than ever.
Sky scrapers in New York are getting higher and higher, but keeping the same limited ground
floor area, making the structures more slender. Suspension bridges in Turkey and Japan with
record braking long spans. Increasing interests in sustainable energy, including streamlining of
the wind turbine structures.
Dynamics in general is becoming more and more important in civil engineering. Civil engineers
are all over the world taking structures closer to the limits, in terms constructing structures
which are higher, longer or slender, and the engineers are always trying to save materials and
creating smarter solutions. The most famous example on where the limits has been crossed
is in the construction of Tacoma Narrow Bridge, which opened July 1, 1940, and collapsed
November 7, 1940 due to wind of normal speed activating the first torsional mode shape, caus-
ing the bridge to collapse. Since then has the structural dynamics and the interactions with
winds become a subject getting more attention.
The estimation of the damping in structures are linked to these kind of problems. The Tacoma
Narrow Bridge is an example of what can go wrong when the damping ratio becomes negative,
and energy keeps accumulating in the system. The estimation of the damping can be useful in
different situations. In the design process it is used to predict the dynamic properties of the
structure during different scenarios. The dynamic properties are products of the mass, stiffness
and damping of the structure. The inertia and stiffness of the system are often pretty accu-
rately determined from formulas and knowledge about the materials, but the damping requires
dynamic testing to be estimated. The estimation of damping in the operational stage, is also
useful. A change in the dynamic properties are in most cases caused by a change in the struc-
tural system, which often indicate a damage to the structure. This is the reason for monitoring
the dynamic properties in a structure, namely to perform damage detection, which is highly
relevant for structures that are difficult to access, for example an offshore wind turbine. The
phenomena flutter is also relevant for a dynamic structure like a wind turbine. Monitoring the
damping ratios when flutter is being initiated would be noticed by the damping ratios dropping
to negative values. In general is it a great advantage in such structures to have great knowledge
about the dynamic properties. Also from an economical point of view, because the knowledge
could be exchanged to a more efficient structure.

In this project is the detection of change in the dynamic properties being studied. This is
done experimentally with use of a five-storey steel frame structure mounted on a shaking table.
The structure is shown in figure 1.1. The column are made of steel rulers, and the floors are
made of aluminium plates. The structure is much stiffer in one direction compared to the
other, and the movement is assumed only to be in the flexible direction. Pairs of electromag-
nets are installed on some of the floors. One electromagnet above the floor and one beneath
it. The magnets has opposite poles pointing towards each other, which creates a magnetic
field through the aluminium floor. The magnetic field creates eddy currents in the floor plate,
that when it moves, results in a viscous damping force. The size of damping force depends
on the velocity of the floor plate, but also the magnetic field, which is controlled by a given
voltage of the electromagnets. Hereby the damping is changeable during tests, either by turn-
ing on or off the electromagnet on the switch, or by changing the voltage from the power supply.
Figure 1.1: Picture of MDOF model.
The reader is aimed to be at a master student level, with basic knowledge about structural
dynamics. The reader will therefore be lead through the project by quick explanations of the
used theory, and formulas, and why some decisions have been made. During the mathematical
derivations, the amount of intermediate results is kept to a level where the reader clearly un-
derstands the founding principles and understand how they are used, but also leaves room to
check out the results by pen and paper themselves.
The report follows a rather chronological order, such that the information needed to understand
the text, in most cases is found in the previous pages. This is of course not maintained all the
time, because as for many subjects in this report, the learning process has been an iterative
journey. So some choices in the report might appears to be made by a fortune teller, but this
is more a result of a trial and error process. When such process enters a reference to the future
knowledge is attached.
The report starts with a section about the theoretical background, explaining the mathematics,
structural dynamics, operational modal analysis (OMA), and analyzing methodology in gen-

in the end to be a uniform distributed upsampled signal created in matlab. Another example
is the choice of electromagnet variant. The first choice of magnets gave nearly undetectable
changes in damping. At this stage the magnets were installed using classical physics stands
with arms. The initial magnets were instead used to investigate how the best magnetic field
were created on a little single degree of freedom structure, which is seen in figure 1.2. These
initial tests included non-aligned magnets, change in number of magnet pairs, different levels
of voltage and placement of the magnetic poles. The results of these tests were used to decide
the variant of the next set of electromagnets to be used.

3.1.3
Input signal to actuator
The load in OMA is as principle ignored or considered unknown. More precisely it is considered
to be a combination of different kinds of loads. For example is a bridge subjected to both traffic,
wind and rain, which all have different kinds of probability distributions. Using the central limit
theorem, it is in OMA assumed that the load tends to be Gaussian distributed.
A random signal should be able to excite any natural frequency of a structure, but having a
signal that is able to activate very high frequency modes comes with some problems. The high
frequency random input causes the amplitudes of the actuator to become small. Therefore the
signal is created such that it ignores frequencies above the highest natural frequency of the
structure. The signal should also mimic a continuous signal. Both problems are solved by
up-sampling a random discrete signal.
Initially a random signal of length N is created using the rand function in matlab. This signal
is set to be uniformly distributed on the interval [1.5;3.5] V. This interval is chosen a little
more narrow than the goal interval [0;5] V, because the up-sampled signal probably exceeds
the initial interval, while still inside the goal interval.
From earlier performed tests, it is known that the natural frequencies of the structure is all
below 12 Hz. To be on the safe side i terms of exciting all the modes, the Nyquist frequency
fν is set to 17 Hz. This then gives the initial input frequency fi = 2fν = 34 Hz. When the
random signal now has an frequency attached to it, then it can be Fourier transformed. The
transformed signal is then zero padded by the factor r. This means that the transformed signal
is extended by zeros on both sides such that total number of elements is rN. All the created
input signals have used r = 15, because a frequency around 510 Hz was aimed at. In figure
3.3 the Fourier transformed input signal is shown in the top, and the zero padded transformed
signal is shown in the bottom.

Damping on the Experimental setup
3.2.1
Eddy current damping
In this section the basic principle of eddy current damping is described. The description is
based on the two articles [1] and [10].
If a non-magnetic but conductive material experience a change in a magnetic field, eddy currents
are generated in the conductive material. The eddy currents can be induced by either changing
the strength or the position of the magnetic field or by moving the conductive material through
the magnetic field. The varying magnetic field produces an electric field in the conductive
material which causes the eddy currents in the conductive material. The eddy currents dissipate
energy as they flow though the conductor and a resulting drag force is created. The resulting
force is proportional to the velocity relative to the field. In a dynamic system the conductive
material is continuously moving in the magnetic field and thereby experience a continuously
change in the magnetic flux. This allows the induced current to regenerate and produce a
force which is proportional to the velocity of the conductive material. The induced currents
are dissipated into heat and thereby energy are removed from the system, thus allowing the
magnet and the conductive material to function like a viscous damper. The principle of eddy
current damping is sketched in figure 3.5.
Figure 3.5: The damping force due to the eddy current in the conductive
material. The damping force is proportional to the the velocity.
Two of the most useful properties of eddy current damping is: the ability to remove energy
from a structure without ever coming in contact with the structure, and the ability to vary the
amount of damping by varying the strength of the magnetic field. The amount of damping is
highly dependent on the generation of the eddy currents. The eddy current J induced in the
moving conductive material can be described by
J = σ(v × B)
(3.1)
In which σ is the electrical conductivity of the material, v is the velocity of the moving con-
ductive material and B is the magnetic field. From the eddy currents is it possible to estimate
the damping force F for the specific case. The damping force can be calculated by
F =

The force can be estimated for a specific case, by solving the equations above. This has been
done in series of articles including [1] and [11], but deemed outside the scope of this project.
The tree most important factors which affects the damping force are listed below.
The conductivity of the material The electrical conductivity σ represents a material’s abil-
ity to conduct electric current. The conductivity is proportional with the generated eddy
currents and thereby the damping force. In the experimental setup aluminium is used as
the conductive material, which has a high conductivity given as σ = 3.77 · 107 S/m.
The magnetic field The strength of the magnetic field is a direct effect of the selected mag-
nets and their placement. A stronger magnet will give a stronger magnetic field which
cause a larger damping force. The strength of the magnetic field, at a given point, decrease
as the the point gets further away from the magnet.
The velocity in relation to the magnetic field The velocity directly affects the genera-
tion of the eddy currents as it can be seen in equation 3.1. To maximise the amount of
damping, the magnets must be placed around the structure where the highest velocity
are achieved.

The eddy current damping is implemented in the experimental setup by the use of electromag-
nets. The electromagnets used in the experiment are of the type Intertec 400 N 24 V/DC 11 W.
The full data sheet and the technical information are shown in appendix D. The electromagnets
are placed in pairs with opposite magnetic poles. The conductive metal (in this case, the floors

distance is too large, the magnets will apply less damping, since the distance between the elec-

is too small there is a chance that the structure will physically hit the electromagnets and
thereby apply friction damping, which is not the purpose of them. This is especially a problem
for large amplitudes, where the floors are forced down a little due to the large deflections in
the structure. The placement and the magnetic field is sketched in figure 3.6.

3.3.1
Impact test
The main idea of the impact test is to find the dynamic properties of the structure by using
the logarithmic decrement method. The approach for the impact test is given below
1. Fix the snaking table to the strong table below.
2. Examine and adjust the dampers if needed and set the wanted damping scenario.
3. Measure the structure in rest for approximate 30 seconds.
4. Apply an impact force at the 5th floor.
5. Measure the response until the response has died out. The response lasts approximately
1-2 minutes.

move freely in x-direction. When the impact test is performed, the ground floor is not allowed

the table below, the stories of the structure are forced down by a few mm, and the dampers
must then be re-adjusted to ensure that they do not mechanically touches the floors in the

The main idea of the SIMO test is to generate the data which are used to find the dynamic

2. Examine and adjust the dampers if needed. The first damping scenario is set.
3. Measure the structure in rest for approximate 30 seconds.
4. Initiate movement of the shaking table and measure the response.
5. Change the damping scenario during the experiment as planned above.
6. Measure the response until the end of the test.
The input signal to the actuator are generated as described in section 3.1.3. The length of

dampers do not mechanically touches the floors in the structure or that the distance between
the dampers and the structure are too big, and thereby reduce damping. The structure are
measured at rest to find the initial position which is used as the point of origin for the further
analysis. The shaking table is turned on and the response is measured. When time comes the
damping scenario are changed by turning the dampers on or off. The timestamp of the change

experiment. The analysis of the data are described in section 4.3 and 4.4 and the result of the
experiments are shown in section 5.2 and 5.3.

The value of N is chosen to fit the correlation functions, such that the noise tail is disregarded.

noise tail begins. The beginning point of the noise is dependent of the damping ratio, and
hereby the damping configuration, but because the configuration might change over time, so
should the value of N in order to get the best results. This is unfortunately not a good solution
as the damping configuration is considered to be unknown. Therefore this is solved by using
50% of the correlation function for the damping configuration which has the earliest beginning
point of the noise tail. Using the same value of N in the correlation functions for the damping
configuration which has the latest beginning point of the noise tail, is roughly corresponding
to using 30% of the best part.

OMA tests with varying damping
To identify the time-varying dynamic properties, the two different OMA methods are both used.
The time series are divided into a number of sub series with a given length and a given overlap.
These two parameters is defined by a length parameter Tsub and a time shift tshift between
the two subsequent sub series. For each sub series ITD and TDPR are used to calculate the
dynamic properties. The natural frequency and the damping ratio for the first mode are saved,
along with the time stamp of the sub series number n. The time stamp for the sub series has
been chosen to be the mid point in the time interval of the sub series. A sketch of the first two
sub series are sketched in figure 4.2. The blue area indicate the part of the time series which
only appears in the first sub series, the yellow part indicate the part which appears in both sub
series and the orange part which only appears in the second sub series. This process is repeated
until the end of the time series.

Since the dynamic properties are found for each sub series, the dynamic properties can now
be analysed as a function of time, in which it is possible to see the change in the dynamic
properties and particularly changes in the damping ratio.
To estimate when the change in the dynamic properties occurs a number of different methods
are examined and tested. The methods have in common that they are searching for a sufficiently
large change in the damping ratio. A detailed description of the different methods is given in
section 4.5.
4.4.1
Sub Series
As introduced in section 4.4, the two parameters used to define the sub series are given by a
length parameter Tsub and a time shift parameter tshift. The length parameter must be chosen
such that the time series contains enough data to give an accurate result. The sub series can
also be too long which increases the calculation time without giving a more accurate result.
The time shift is defined as the time between to subsequent sub series. By setting the time shift
to a fixed value, then the time shift determines how large the overlap between two subsequent
sub series is. The dynamic properties calculated for two subsequent sub series, will not be
independent, because the subsequent sub series shares a part of the same data. This means
that an instantaneous change in the dynamic properties will be seen as a continuous change in
calculated dynamic properties. This process is outlined in Figure 4.3. The vertical blue line
in the figure illustrate an instantaneous change in the dynamic properties which happens at a
given time in the experiment. Before the change the natural angular frequency is given by ωI
and the damping ratio ζI. By taking a sub series which in its entirety lies before the change
in the dynamic properties, the OMA methods would give the natural frequency ωI and the
damping ratio ζI. This is shown with the yellow sub series in figure 4.3. If the sub series in
its entirety lies after the change in the dynamic properties, the OMA methods would give the
natural frequency ωII and the damping ratio ζII. This is shown with the blue sub series in
figure 4.3. For the dynamic properties is expected to vary linearly for sub series between these
two sub series. In other words, sub series where the change happens inside the sub series. This
is illustrated by a straight line in figure 4.3. This implies that, to find an instantaneous changes
in the dynamic properties, should there be searched for a change in the dynamic properties
with the same length as the sub series.

4.5
Algorithms to find changes in dynamic properties
To find the changes in the dynamic properties, two different algorithms have been developed.
Both of the algorithms search after a sufficiently large change in the damping ratio ζ.
4.5.1
Absolute value based algorithm
The absolute values based algorithm is the simplest of the two developed algorithms. The
algorithm uses the calculated damping ratio ζ for each sub series and the associated timestamp.
The absolute value algorithm is searching for the jump or drop in damping ratio which is
sketched in figure 4.3. As the sloped part of the curve should have the same length as time
window, a difference in damping ratios with this time difference are being investigated. The
algorithm uses the damping ratio ζ at a given time t and the damping ratio one length sub
series later t + Tsub. The absolute value between these two points is calculated and can be used
to estimate if a change in the dynamic properties has happened. This process is repeated for
the entire length of the time series.

The timestamp of the peaks in the changes in damping ∆ζ indicate in which sub series the
largest change in dynamic properties occur. This can be used to detect time the instantaneous
changes in the dynamic properties were made. To minimize changes due to noise only peaks
above a given boundary are used. The boundary is set such that the change must be greater
than 0.0020.
It must be noted that the precision of the algorithm is highly dependent on the amount of noise
in the calculated result. The noise can be reduced by choosing a sufficiently long sub series,
but can never be eliminated due to the randomness of the applied load on the structure. The
precision of the algorithm can also be increased by using a running mean over the dynamic
properties. This reduces the noise but at the expense of precision at the start and end section
of the sloped line shown in figure 4.3. This means that the sharp corner between the horizontal
line and the straight line in figure 4.3 becomes more rounded. In this project the running mean
is based on five data points.
The algorithm is optimised to find one instantaneous change in the dynamic properties. Thereby
problems arise if two or more changes happens within a given sub series as the algorithm will
only estimate one change. Similarly if the change in the dynamic properties occurs slowly over
time, the algorithm will not detect any change.
4.5.2
CUSUM based Algorithm
The CUSUM algorithm for detecting the changes in damping takes two inputs as described in
(2.147), the control limit c and the mean shift n. How to set these values depends much on
35
==PAGE:45==
which type of data the method is applied on. In this project where the method is applied on
varying damping ratios, knowledge about the calculations is important, because the data often
is not independent. The dependence is based in the fact that the time windows overlaps by
90%. Therefore will a damping ratio at a given time stamp often be close to the damping ratio
close to that time stamp. This gives a great effect on the cumulative sum, i.e. the overlaps
results in the cumulative sum to run off really quickly. This is solved by setting the control
limit at a high level, but keeping the mean shift quite low. Then it is able to detect small
changes, without detecting false changes. The best result were reached using
c = 16
n = 1
(4.5)
The setback of using these values is that an actual change in damping is detected quite late,
because the cumulative sum needs to reach the high level of 16 standard deviations.
The amount of input can also be expanded to include the mean value and standard deviation.
As default in matlab, the mean and standard deviation is calculated using the first 25 samples.
These settings are often too inaccurate because of the earlier mentioned overlap of the time
windows. The mean value and standard deviation is instead calculated for the samples in the
first period of 2000 seconds. This period of time is made to fit the test plan, where most tests
had more than an hour between the changes.
From the reference tests described in section 5.2, the mean damping ratio and standard devi-
ation are calculated for all used damping configurations and all three types of time windows.
This would be the expected behavior of the damping ratios between the changing points. The
reference tests then gives some expected levels of mean value and standard deviation. The
length of the time window is known, but the damping configuration changes during the test.
It is then an opportunity to force the calculated mean value to be changed to the closest ex-
pected value. The calculated standard deviation could also be changed to the corresponding
expected value. This opportunity has been partly discarded as the best results were obtained
by using the calculated mean value directly, but force the standard deviation to be change to
the corresponding value for the closest expected mean value.
The script created for the CUSUM algorithm has the option to select how the mean value and
standard deviation should be used as input. It contains the five options:
1. Use the calculated mean value and standard deviation directly.
2. Change the mean value to closest expected value, and use calculated standard deviation.
3. Use calculated mean value, and change the standard deviation to expected value for the
closest expected mean value.
4. Change the mean value to closest expected value, and change the standard deviation to
the corresponding value.
5. Use calculated mean value, and use a fixed value as standard deviation
Option 3 was preferred for tests including sudden changes in damping. The fixed value in option
5 is taken as the mean standard deviation for used window length. Option 5 was preferred for
tests including slow changes in damping.
The problem with the late detection, is tried to be corrected by the inclusion of a fixed delay.
This means that the detection point is subtracted by a fixed number of data points. As the
results shows, a fixed delay is mostly not solving the problem as the delay varies depending
of the standard deviation. Therefore the used delay is also set to take values which are fixed
for the closest mean damping ratio. The values used for the changing values tool using a time
window of 600 s is written in table 4.1. The values for the 900 s and 1200 s are written in

Chapter 6
Digital twin
This section is about the creation of the digital twin. A digital twin is a virtual representation of
the physical structure. A digital twin makes it possible to predict the physical outputs a given
input would create. Digital twins are useful during the research and development of a product,
because a lot of data can be created instead of performing a lot of physical tests. Especially in
the fine-tuning of the product. The digital twin of the five-storey steel frame structure will take
its birth in the basic laws of physics. Later on, the experimental data will be used to tune the
model. The digital twin will then try to predict a response given the movement of the actuator,
and the numerical response is both compared to the measured response, and analyzed like a
measured response would be. Hereby is it also possible the compare the damping ratio over
time.

External damper
Adding a external damper to the system is done with the connectivity vector w and the damper

M¨x(t) + ¯C ˙x(t) + Kx(t) + fd ˙x = fe(t)
(6.48)

proportional to the velocity, and can then be included in the damping matrix.
fd ˙x = cwwT
(6.49)
where c is a constant called the viscous damping coefficient, which depends on the magnetic
field. The vector w is a connectivity vector. The connectivity vector takes binary values, and
controls if the dampers are active or not. For a damper on the second floor the connectivity
vector is written as w = [0, 1, 0, 0, 0]T. The damping matrix is now calculated as

Modeling of the external force
In the equation of motion 6.1, x is the displacement measured from the ground plate, which is
able to move. x represents the relative displacements on terms of subtracting the displacements
of the moving ground plate. The lasers are on the other hand fixed and measures displacements
y as the sum of both the ground displacements and the local displacement of the floor
y = x + 1xg
(6.54)
where xg is the displacement of the ground plate, and 1 is a 5x1 vector where all coordinates
takes the value 1.
The shaking table is used to create ground accelerations, and hereby creating relative displace-
ments of the floors. To estimate the equivalent external forces the equation of motion with
ground acceleration ¨xg is used
M(¨x + ¨xg) + C ˙x + Kx = 0
(6.55)
which can be rewritten as
M¨x + C ˙x + Kx = −M¨xg
(6.56)
In this expression the equation of motion is recognized for local coordinate system x, where the
force can be estimated as
f1(t) = −M¨xg
(6.57)
Another way to do it, would be to insert the expression for the lasers 6.54 in the equation of
motion with ground acceleration.
M¨y + C( ˙y −˙xg) + K(y −xg) = 0
(6.58)
Which can be rewritten as
M¨y + C ˙y + Ky = C ˙xg + Kxg
(6.59)
In this expression the equation of motion is recognized for global coordinate system y, where
the force can be estimated as
f2(t) = C ˙xg + Kxg
(6.60)
If numerical differentiation is considered inaccurate, it could be assumed that the damping term
is not contributing
f3(t) = Kxg
(6.61)

performance in section 6.8. The numerical differentiation is performed by taking the Fourier
transform, then multiply by iω, and hereafter taking the inverse Fourier transform, as described
in equation (2.7). This is because of the use of lasers which measures displacements. If ac-
celerometers were used instead, then the velocities and displacements would be calculated using
numerical integration. This could be done using equation (2.8).

6.7
Estimation of response history
The method used to predict the response history is Newmark time integration. The average
accelerations scheme is used, i.e. γ = 0.5 and β = 0.25. In terms of system matrices, the mass
matrix is unchanged, the tuned stiffness matrix is used, and one of the four estimations of the
damping matrix is picked.
For a start no external damper is applied, and the actuator movement from the undamped
reference test is used to estimate the external force, in one of the three ways. In Newmarks
method it is necessary to set the initial conditions, i.e. initial displacement vector x0 and initial
velocity vector v0. The initial displacements are taken as the first displacements measured in
the data set. The initial velocity is taken as the slope of the secant of the first and second point
in the data set.
x0 = x(0)
(6.62)
v0 = x(∆t) −x(0)
∆t
(6.63)
where ∆t is the time step, which matches the sampling rate in the data set of the undamped
reference test. In some cases it might be more accurate to include more data points in the
estimation of the initial velocity. This is especially the case if tests have been performed at
higher frequencies, because the slope then is more a result of noise of the signal than actual
change in displacement. This has not been a problem in the presented data sets.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This master’s thesis is containing the study of varying damping in a five-storey steel frame
structure. This is done experimentally where three types of tests has been performed. Impact
tests, random vibration tests with constant damping and with varying damping. During the
project has the input signal to the actuator, creating random vibrations, been improved. In
the end, the input signal was created to be equally likely to active modes in the interval 0 −17
Hz. which were fitted to include all five modes of the model. A digital twin was created based
on the experimental work and basic structural mechanics.
The purpose of the hammer tests were to investigate the frequency and damping ratio of the
first mode, for all four damping scenarios. The results are summarized in table 5.2. These values
are comparable to the results found from the random vibration tests with constant damping.
The frequency were close to each other as the difference were around 0.01 Hz. The damping
ratio in the undamped impact test was around 0.12%, but 0.20% in the corresponding random
vibration test, and this is the greatest disagreement between the methods as the found damping
ratios for the damped scenarios were nearly identical. The damping ratios were found to be
about 0.41%, 0.49% and 0.78%, for active electromagnetic dampers on 4th floor, 5th floor, and
both 4th and 5th floor respectively.
These were also the levels of damping ratio that were switched between in the random vibra-
tion tests with varying damping. In general were the changes from undamped to both 4th and
5th floor damped and vice versa, the easiest change to detect, as this would be the greatest
change in damping ratio. The change in damping ratio was also a result of the length of the
window, as shorter time windows would give steeper slopes around the change in damping, but
the shorter windows would also cause more deviation in the damping ratio. In the data set
OMA_SIMO_timed_14, the absolute value algorithm using the 1200 s window, were able to
detect the first change in damping to happen 300 s after the actual change actually happened.
For live measurements, the detection would be delayed by 900 s because of the relatively long
time window. The CUSUM method using the 600 s window were able to detect the same change
in damping to happen 420 s after the actual change has happened. For live measurements, the
detection would be delayed by 660 s, because of the delay correction which do not make sense
for live measurements.
The two methods works quite well in different ways. The absolute value algorithm were able
to detect changes really fast. The delay in detection were nearly alone a result of the length
of window and size of change in damping ratio. The downside of the algorithm were that it
lacks robustness, and were vulnerable to a single outlier in damping ratio. The CUSUM al-
gorithm were more robust for this kind of problem, but on the other side were the detection
further delayed. The quickness of the method are specific for this five-storey steel structure. For
a structure with higher natural frequencies and higher damping, the window length could be
67
==PAGE:77==
reduced, or the deviation in damping ratios could be reduced, both making the detection faster.
The digital twin gave the best result using the proportional damping, where regression over the
three first assumed damping ratios were used. The predicted response did not make an exact
match with the measured response. The first part of the response followed the measurements
quite well, but over time it clearly lost its track.
It leaves room for improvements, which
could happen in estimation of the stiffness matrix or the damping matrix.
Other ways of
estimating the lumped masses, in terms of methods to lumping the distributed mass, might
also be beneficial.
A predicted response for the data set with varying damping were also made, and even though
the predicted response did not match the measured response exactly, it clearly showed that
similar changes in damping were able to be predicted. The values of the damping ratios were
estimated too large, but the progress were nearly identical to the results from the measurements.

Chapter 8
Future work
All analysis in this report are based on the dynamic properties of the first mode and external
damping has been implemented to give greatest changes in the first mode. This is because the
dampers are placed where the first mode shape has the largest relative displacement, which is
not quite the case for mode shape 2-5. This causes the changes in the higher modes to be less
significant. If the damping occurs where the second mode has the largest relative displacement,
the change in the damping ratio for the second mode are expected to be larger and thereby
easier to detect.
In continuation of this, it could also be interesting to use the other dynamic properties to esti-
mate the changes. In particular, the relative modal participation could be useful together with
the already used damping ratio. The changes in the frequencies are considered to be too small
to assess changes in the dynamic properties. The two different algorithms for estimating the
changes in the dynamic properties could most likely be optimized by using both the damping
ratios and the relative modal participations and maybe even the higher modes.
A retro-fit of the structure could be beneficial. If the structure for example has lower distances
between the floors, then the natural frequencies would be higher. The columns of structure
could also be changed to a column type of higher stiffness. This could have the same effect.
The higher frequencies would lower the recommended test duration, which would give more
accurate results, or quicker detection of damping.
Last but not least, there is the possibility of a large number of smaller optimizations, which
overall could increase accuracy and performance of the experiment. Some examples could be, a
redesign of the armrests that hold the magnets in place, so that they sit more precisely opposite
each other and make the arms more stiff to counteract their deflection. Optimize the sampling
rate, to allow for shorter calculation time. Improve the digital twin to give a more accurate
representation of the response history and the damping ratios.

structure always starts in the middle of range of the LTDS.
