Abstract
Ideological polarization on social media reflects a crucial issue of contemporary so-
cieties.
Several studies within social sciences and psychology tried to investigate
the root causes and formulated theories to describe this phenomenon. Information
retrieval through social media platforms like Twitter has the means to hinder a demo-
cratic approach towards political debates. This aspect fosters the creation of echo
chambers that enhance the spreading and reinforcement of polarized opinions among
ideologically similar individuals. The aim of this study was to find a scientific way to
measure the impact of controversial topics on Twitter users’ behavior from a tempo-
ral perspective. By relying on third-party software and the Twitter Developer API, I
collected a set of raw tweets from Twitter related to political controversies that took
place between September 2013 and December 2016. Each controversy was related
to a set of hashtags conventionally adopted by the users to express their political
affiliation. A large network of Twitter users, mutually interacting in the same time
window, along with the collection of tweets designated the subject of the study. I
revealed evidence of a positive bonding effect on Twitter users mutually communi-
cating as part of the global conversations represented by such controversial hashtags.
This reflected into an increase of the mutual interaction consistency in both the short
and long term, fostered by the online controversies. I further discovered that the
mutual interactions network obeyed the laws of a precise topological structure which
highlighted the presence of community clusters growing around the most influential
users. The obtained results hinted the existence of the echo chamber phenomenon.
The role of the community hubs and the high in-community communication suggests
a clear resemblance of a realistic political debate between opposing groups. Further
investigation within NLP suggested that predicting tweets’ political leaning is a viable
task.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the department of Applied Mathematics and Computer
Science at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the requirements for
acquiring a Master of Science in Engineering degree in Digital Media Engineering.
The reasons why I decided to undertake the work hereby presented come from my
passion for technology, big data and its strong relationship with the real world and how
we, as ordinary users of hundreds of online platforms, are affected in terms of social
behavior. What are commonly known as social networks represent today something
way bigger than what they were originally meant to be, therefore there’s much to
discover and infer from their users’ utilization patterns that might tell more about how
people actually connect and what really connects them. The strong social psychology
context, with lots of hypothesis about users’ behavior in social networks, played a
significant role in convincing myself to commit to such work. Besides this, of course,
I was constantly driven by the crave to learn more and experiment new technologies,
and this thesis gave me the perfect opportunity to accomplish this academic and
personal goal.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter, I state the general topic of my research by first contextualizing it
and providing the required background notions. I show why there is a need to pursue
further research by outlining and evaluating the current situation. Finally, I clearly
define the scope and terms of my proposed research, I identify its importance and the
research aims and objectives.
1.1
Background
In this section, I describe the background context, the main terminology and core con-
cepts that represent strict requirements to understand the research goals of my work.
Starting with a general description of social networks and Twitter, I then emphasize
inner characteristics of Twitter and what makes it outstanding compared to similar
social networks, all followed by the description of the problem that my research pro-
cess takes inspiration from.
In the modern world, getting connected to somebody who is at the other side of
the world takes just a few clicks. We are much closer to each other than we would
expect and this is mainly due to Internet and the Web 2.0. The advent of social net-
works gave users all around the world the possibility to interact and collaborate in a
social media dialogue as creators of user-generated content. The term social networks
has actually a longer life than one would expect, but only in the early 2000s it coined
what turned out to be a global phenomenon. Nowadays there is plenty of choice and
it would be even hard to come up with a full list of all the alternatives, but all of
them feature a set of common key points. If I were to properly define what a social
network is, according to the original purpose of its concept, Boyd and Ellison, 2007
provides an exhaustive definition: a social network is a web-based service that allows
its users to:
• construct a profile within a bounded system, usually with visibility settings
enforced by the user;
• articulate a list of other users with whom they share a connection;
• view and traverse their list of connections and those made by others within the
system.

2
1 Introduction
By chronological order, MySpace, Facebook and Twitter are ranked among the
top social networks of all times, especially because of their innovative aspects, their
massive, worldwide outreach and popularity compared to their predecessors. Among
these, special attention is given to Twitter, the famous micro-blogging platform that,
starting from 2006, gathers every day millions of users that tweet a tiny message of
140 characters1.
Technically speaking, Kwak, Lee, Park, and Moon, 2010 defines
Tweets as simple text messages (eventually including media content such as pictures
or hyperlinks) that a user sends out to all its followers. Being a follower on Twitter
means that the user receives all the tweets from those the user follows.
The 140
characters maximum length for tweets is what makes Twitter peculiar for brevity,
along with its own set of mark-up vocabulary. It’s worth mentioning that Twitter is
the first social network where the concept of hashtags started to catch on2. Hashtags
are nowadays adopted as a built-in feature by most social networks and represent a
powerful grouping framework to connect people around the same topic. Let it be for
political activism, visibility gain or social movements, hashtags are widely used and
most Tweets feature at least one hashtag that identifies the central topic or category
its content relates to. Unlike most online social networking sites, such as Facebook or
MySpace, adds Kwak et al., 2010, Twitter stands out because of the different nature
of the concept of relationships, since the action of following or being followed requires
no reciprocation. A user can follow any other user, and the user being followed need
not follow back. Last but not least, the retweet mechanism empowers users to spread
information of their choice beyond the reach of the original tweeter’s followers. It’s
easy to see, at this point, how much potential Twitter gained, and it makes sense that
today it represents the top platform for fast-spreading information around any topic,
i.e. the most well-known micro-blogging social networking site.
1.1.1
Twitter: a social network or a news media platform?
Twitter is only one example where network analysis researchers have access to ex-
plore large-scale patterns of friendships, usage and other visible indicators (Boyd and
Ellison, 2007).
This is mainly because all these platforms provide rich sources of
naturalistic behavioral data.
Twitter has been under the spotlight for long, it has grown curiosity among re-
searchers who wanted to study its followers’ network topology and how information

The social problem of ideological polarization
If we were to believe that Twitter is the perfect platform that combines typical social
networking features with a built-in news media style, we would be totally wrong. Un-
fortunately, Twitter, as well as the rest of the leading social networking sites where
news spread daily and are constantly shared by users (how not to mention Facebook),
suffers from what is commonly known as ideological polarization phenomenon.
This has become more apparent in the latest years, mainly because social media web-
sites have played an increasing role in news and information consumption, and what
has additionally contributed to make it more evident is the emerging phenomenon of
the fake news 3. Ideological polarization is a global phenomenon, it doesn’t apply
exclusively to social media: in general, it occurs when societal groups are ideologically
opposed to each other, when nations feel very much politically divided. This leads
to often clustered, semi-isolated and like-minded groups. Ideological polarization has
gained increased attention in the recent years because of the 2016 U.S. Presidential
Elections and the 2016 EU Referendum in UK, which since then have often been rais-
ing popular debates (Spohr, 2017). Currently there are opposing arguments about
the origins of ideological polarization and I hereby briefly present them. By one side,

It’s quite intuitive, at this point, the reason why filter bubbles and echo chambers
have the means to be considered the main responsible for fostering ideological polar-

a well spread phenomenon that frequently occurs in several contexts such as web

control), as Stewart, Arif, and Starbird, 2018 suggests. Filter bubbles might also be
thought as pre-selected personalization, meaning that websites/advertisers transpar-
ently personalize the contents the user is exposed to.
However, the reasons for increased ideological polarization in social media do not
exclusively lie in the combination of the two phenomena of filter bubbles and echo
chambers. The second cause lies in psychology and behavioural economy: selective
exposure behaviour and availability bias make us more likely to interact with con-
tent which confirms our pre-existing views. Spohr, 2017 defines selective exposure
what is conventionally known as psychological biases of the user within social me-
dia. Specifically, the phenomena that individuals have a tendency to consume media
which aligns with their views and beliefs and avoid such content that is different in
perspective or even challenging to their position. Although most social media web-
sites learn users’ behavior to rank content and present first what they are more likely
to enjoy (algorithmic ranking), there is a strong belief that individuals’ choices play
a stronger role in limiting exposure to cross-cutting content, which candidates selec-
tive exposure for the strongest trigger of ideological polarization. As opposed to the
pre-selected personalization previously mentioned, selective exposure better relates
to self-selected personalization. A straight forward example of selective exposure on
Twitter is the act of following people.
At this point, one might wonder: what are the reasons to think that ideological
polarization in social media actually represents an issue, if it relies on the principle
according to which users are naturally inclined to prefer content they feel more aligned
with? Spohr, 2017 points out that the main reason is that it threatens the healthy
development of democracies, leading to the loss of diversity of opinions and arguments,
therefore it has a negative impact on society and democracy as a whole. There is
evidence that filter bubbles and fake news affected the 2016 U.S. Presidential Election.
For instance, DiFranzo and Gloria-Garcia, 2017 reports that in the months before
the election the amount of fake stories on Facebook actually outperformed real news

3.1
The Mutual Mention Relationships (MMR) network
The MMR dataset was originally stored in the form of a large set of files with a very
simple structure. As it was previously hinted in the report, a mention on Twitter
stands for the act of directly replying to or explicitly mentioning another user in one’s
tweet. The dataset keeps track of mutual mentions in the sense that, in a certain
period of time, a pair of users mutually mentioned, replied to or retweeted each
other. The dataset holds a huge list of mutual Twitter mentions occurred between
pairs of users over a time frame of 3 years, starting from September 1st, 2013, until
December 31st, 2016. The MMR data is therefore organized as a set of folders each
one representing a 3 months period, spanning from the period pointed by the folder
name and the one pointed by the immediately subsequent folder, in chronological
order. The time interval has to be considered right-open, with the right endpoint
not included, meant already as the left (inclusive) endpoint of the following interval.
This results in a total of 13 three months periods (T = 13). Each folder includes a
set of files with consistent structure: each line in a single file is a username-username
pair, standing for a mutual Twitter interaction occurred in the period represented by
the parent folder. The verse of the interaction direction is unknown. The structure

4.1
Network Science and basic terminology
Network science is the discipline that provides a common language for researchers
from different fields to study the structure of the wiring diagram of their underlying
systems.
In the previous chapters I have frequently mentioned some network specific ter-
minology, such as nodes and links. Scientifically speaking, a network is a diagram
used to highlight how a given system’s components interact with each other. A social
networks is the type of network where people are nodes and the connections they
establish between each other are links. This applies either online or offline, but in
both cases the type of the interactions depends on the context.
In reality, a network is nothing but a graph where vertices are called nodes and
edges are called links. There is therefore a common and similar terminology yet the
two terms network and graph are slightly different. Barabási et al., 2016 explains
that networks and related terminology usually refer to real systems, whereas graph
theory exposes the mathematical representation of such systems. From now on, I
refer to the MMR data as a network compliant with this definition, hence I further
identify relevant network properties such as the number of nodes N (the number
of unique Twitter usernames) and the number of links L (the number of mutual
interactions between the users). It is worth pointing out that the considered network
has undirected links, meaning that the verse of the link arrow is not specified or
irrelevant.
Describing a network requires to keep track of its links. Mathematically speak-
ing, the most common representation is the adjacency matrix: assuming that an
undirected network has N links, the adjacency matrix A has shape (N, N), and the
individual cells of the matrix are described as:

The last data pre-processing step involved what usually requires patience, time and

that I was dealing with a data generated from a random sample of Twitter data, I
potentially had a significant amount of irrelevant data that would have biased or
spoiled my results.
Further data exploration indeed revealed that a large number of nodes had an
unexpectedly high degree, in the order of 104. Recalling what Barabási et al., 2016
says, the node degree is a key property representing the number of links it has to
other nodes. I denote with ki the degree of the i-th node in the network. I then easily
inferred that most of the top-degree nodes were represented by famous companies
and organizations whose presence in Twitter is led by the need to get in touch with
customers and provide technical support by replying to users’ inquiries. This did
not only apply to ad-hoc customer support accounts but also to accounts personified
by TV/radio programs, internet providers, restaurants and in general any corporate
account whose outstanding activity would have negatively interfered with my results.

Visualizing communities in networks
Graphically visualizing networks with large sizes is always a hard challenge and re-
quires the right set of tools. Visualizing the analyzed portions of the MMR network,
with sizes N ∼104 and L ∼105, was definitely an unattainable task with the tools
I was working with. Neither SNAP.Py or NetworkX could handle visualizations for
such large networks, yet I was still very much driven by the curiosity of deeply under-
standing their structure. I knew NetworkX offers intuitive graph plotting features,
thus I exploited them to show at least the most recurrent mutual interactions over
the whole MMR time window.
From the raw tweets data, I extracted detailed information about the frequency
of each mutual interaction. I obtained the frequency of a mutual mention by simply
counting its occurrences (i.e. how many different tweets featured such interaction),
regardless of lack of constant reciprocity, as long as the mention was returned at least
once.
I set a lower threshold on the interaction, thus filtered out the occasional and
unsteady mentions and kept only the most recurrent ones. I varied this threshold
depending on the size of H, yet I guaranteed that the resulting sub-network out of the
top interactions had size ranging between approximately 3000-8000 links (hence way
smaller than the original sub-network but considerably descriptive). As a meaningful
landmark, I exclusively kept those considered interactions that occurred at least 5-15
times over the whole MMR time window. I additionally collected the exact degree of
each username by aggregating the number of mutual mentions it was part of, which
==PAGE:62==
46
4 Methods
provided me with a criteria to identify network hubs and visually emphasize them. I
indeed mapped the frequency of the interactions to the corresponding edges’ weight
(visually translated into edge width) and the nodes’ degree to their size.
I took advantage of the Louvain Method for community detection (Blondel, Guil-
laume, Lambiotte, and Lefebvre, 2008) to extract communities, visualize them and
shed light on the cross-communities high-intensity beams of interaction. The algo-
rithm has time complexity equal to O(n log n), hence pretty much computationally
efficient, and was inspired by the need of optimizing the calculation of the modularity.
The modularity is a useful metric that quantifies how much a given network natu-
rally splits into communities; its value ranges in [−1, 1] and its interpretation says
that high modularity values depict networks with highly clustered groups, with dense
links within the same communities and sparse link across different communities. In
short, the modularity quantifies what is the fraction of links that concentrate within
the communities compared to a random distribution of the links regardless of the
communities. The Louvain Method computes modularity with a greedy algorithm,
by first creating small communities and optimizing its value locally on all nodes, then
communities are merged when they contribute positively to the global modularity of
the partition; in order to evaluate the improvement in the modularity, this method
calculates the global modularity by starting with a partition where each node stays
in its own community, then by moving each node to the community of each of its
neighbors. The best partition is therefore obtained when the increase in the global
modularity is maximized. The modularity Q of a partition is more specifically de-
fined as the fraction of the number of links within each community out of the number
of links between each community, minus the same ratio obtained from a completely
random partition.
The Louvain Method for community detection is inspired by the Clauset-Newman-
Moore algorithm (Clauset, Newman, and Moore, 2004). SNAP.Py comes with a built-
in implementation of the latter, along with the Girvan-Newman algorithm (Girvan
and Newman, 2002). The GN algorithm is significantly slower compared to the other
two, since it relies on a repeated calculation of the edge betweenness centrality and
the assumption according to which the edges with highest centrality (where most of
the shortest paths run pass through) mostly connect communities.
For each of the sub-networks of top interactions, I produced two visualizations,
showing (1) the structure of the whole network of the top interactions and (2) the
flow of information across communities in the giant component. In the second case, I
also labelled the biggest hub of each community to reveal evidence of any correlation
with ideologically homogeneous groups.
I calculated the modularity with the three different algorithms previously outlined
and I used the values to further support the obtained visualizations.

4.9
Natural Language Processing of political tweets
Last step of my analysis has seen me committed to a new venture sourcing from a
different context than the one seen so far, mainly related to network analysis. Senti-
ment analysis and text classification are common tasks within NLP, however Twitter
exposes NLP-ers to a higher degree of difficulty due to the exceedingly ambiguous
nature of the tweets’ text content. Internet slang and abbreviations are widely used
on social networks, nevertheless Twitter deserves a remarkable note since it enforces a
short, fixed maximum message length. The sense of a tweet text is also highly misled
by irony, potentially hindering a correct sentiment polarity classification.
I undertook a simple yet effective tweets’ text analysis to:
1. Compute the average sentiment score for the two political hashtags, symbols of
the left/right leaning political groups during the 2016 U.S. Presidential Election
campaigns: #ImWithHer for democrats/liberals and #MakeAmericaGreatA-
gain for republicans/conservatives;
2. Carry out a political text classification for the above mentioned hashtags to
predict the political leaning of the tweet based on the linguistic style and lexicon
adopted by the authors.
I mainly worked with the TextBlob library for Python, which naively supported
most features from the widely adopted Natural Language Toolkit library among NLP-
ers. I started by filtering out tweets whose auto-detected language was not English
(language code en) and clearly discerned between the two sets of tweets by mark-
ing each tweet with the main political hashtag it featured (either #ImWithHer or
#MakeAmericaGreatAgain). If both hashtags were used simultaneously, I marked
the tweet as not associated to any specific political leaning.
I pre-processed all the texts by passing them to a cleaning pipeline that featured
the following macro-steps: (i) conversion to lower-case characters, (ii) removal of
non-ASCII characters, symbols and numbers, user handles, English stop-words, short
words (less than four characters), (iii) stemming and lemmatisation, Part-Of-Speech
(POS) tagging to select only the most meaningful and descriptive tokens (e.g. nouns,
verbs, adjectives).
The TextBlob library natively supports the calculation of the sentiment score.
The task is performed as a supervised classification problem: I used the default,
built-in and pre-trained PatternAnalyzer classifier, based on the pattern library
(Smedt and Daelemans, 2012). This classifier is trained on a lexicon of adjectives
that appear frequently on product reviews and annotated with scores for sentiment
polarity (negative ↔positive). I fed the classifier with the text of the tweets and let
it predict the sentiment score s for each of them. Values of s range in [−1, 1], such
that values in [−1, 0) associate to a negative polarity and values in (0, 1] to a positive
polarity. A sentiment score of 0 means neutral polarity (e.g. sentiment undefined).
Subsequently, I queried for the top tweets with the highest/lowest sentiment score and
calculated the ratios of the different sentiment polarities for both political hashtags.

Besides sentiment analysis, I performed further NLP on the tweets to extract what
are the most popular supporting hashtags people use in tweets when they feature
either political hashtag. I investigated whether there is a similarity with regards to
the number of hashtags in a tweet between sentiment polarities and the main political
hashtag.
Finally, I laid the foundations for a simple text classification problem, which is
a very common task within supervised machine learning.
Assigning categories to
documents, which may be web pages, library books, media articles, galleries etc. has
many practical applications like e.g. spam filtering and email routing. My purpose
was to investigate whether the lexicon of the tweets could tell something about its
political leaning. I therefore tried to provide an answer to the question: is there
a common vocabulary that ideologically similar people tend to adopt when writing
their tweets?
I first addressed the problem of unbalanced classes, since #ImWithHer tweets
were 66,8% of the total and #MakeAmericaGreatAgain tweets only 33,2%. Having
unbalanced classes represents a weakness in typical machine learning tasks, often
leading to misclassification and overfitting. The reason is that the classifiers always
try to predict the targets by wondering what is the best possible prediction in order
to maximize the accuracy. In case of unbalanced classes, the most clever choice is
always predicting for the largest class. I therefore under-sampled the largest class to
balance the dataset.
Next, in order to analyze the pre-processed data coming from the cleaning steps, I
had to convert it into features. For this purpose, I considered two similar approaches
that rely on assorted techniques: the bag-of-words and the Term Frequency - Inverse
Document Frequency (TF-IDF) model. I used the Python library scikit-learn to carry
out the vectorization of the features and build the classification model. I combined
each vectorization technique with three different classifiers: Naive Bayes, Logistic
Regression and Support Vector Machines (SVM), accounting for a total of six clas-
sification models. I performed a simple Hold-Out Cross Validation, by splitting the
observations in 80% of the data for training and %20 for testing and compared the
classifier performance indicators (precision, recall, F1-score) across all the models.
The choice behind which type of classifiers to use was mainly led by intuitiveness
of use and simplicity. Naive Bayes always represents a good baseline for most classi-
fication problems: it relies on the Bayes’ theorem with the assumption of conditional
independence between the class variable and all the single pairs of features. It per-
forms well and does not rely on expensive memory requirements. Support Vector
Machines instead work by identifying the hyper plane that optimally separates the
classes to predict.

6.1
Key findings and interpretation
In this section, I summarize the key findings of my research and explain how they
relate to the original research aims, hence I attempt to provide a clear answer to the
research questions listed in Section 1.3.
The first part of the research has seen me closely involved in tackling network
analysis problems. I explored the large networks of the mutual interactions related to
specific controversial Twitter conversations. Upon the evaluation of a broad set of rel-
evant network metrics and the outcome of a comparison framework used to measure
the remarkable aspects, I achieved enough results to assess the structure of such net-
works. Despite the difference in size of the analyzed mutual mentions networks, they
all revealed similar structure and properties when it comes to the individual network
metrics. The typical properties described by the the BA model and the “small-world”
model complement each other when it comes to the social network structure acquired
by the networks. This result was overall expected: the huge disparity between low
and high degree nodes, a relatively high clustering coefficient, short path lengths and
diameter meet the expectations of a realistic Twitter network of interactions, as the
role of the hubs is crucial to create paths between users otherwise isolated. My results
tell that in all the analyzed networks, two random users are separated by only 5-6
hops from one another, which is extremely low if we think that these paths poten-
tially connect people from all over the world. A preferential attachment mechanism,
according to which users are more likely to privilege connections with other popular
users, leads to the exponentially decreasing likelihood to observe network hubs. Such
==PAGE:102==
86
6 Discussion
behavior is expected in social networks like Twitter, which as already discussed in the
first chapters, is mostly used as an information medium platform, hence it makes sense
that it fosters the formation of hubs, often prominent figures or organizations, that
are highly mentioned and globally connect people to the topics they are interested
in.
The most significant and peculiar outcomes of the work I undertook lie in the
context of the time line analysis of the mutual interactions on Twitter. My aim was
to unveil to what extent controversial topics on Twitter affect the intensity of the
mutual interactions overtime. I demonstrated that large-scale controversies on Twit-
ter play a significant role in strengthening ties between users, especially in the long
term, even several months after they first got in touch within a conversation related
to the concerned controversies. I demonstrated this phenomenon holds despite the
fluctuations of the overall joint interest in the controversial topics (i.e. the concerned
hashtags generated more or less traffic depending on the period over the years) and
I did not find any concrete evidence of factors that could significantly drive this ef-
fect and bias the achieved results. However, this does not mean that controversies
exclusively strengthened ties overtime: a non negligible number of interactions still
experienced the opposite effect, in a way that the involved users did not preserve the
same communication consistency overtime. Formulating my assessment with a better
rigor, I feel confident in saying that there is stronger evidence of strengthened mu-
tual interactions than weakened ones. A wider interpretation of this result leads to
claim that controversial topics create fertile ground for either more frequent disputes
between ideologically opposing users or the establishment of an ideological “friend-
ship” between them who, aware of the political affiliation they share, may then start
supporting each other in the same discourse or future similar ones. Indeed, I cannot
state which between these two scenarios prevails.
These statements are further supported by the outcome of the network visualiza-
tion and community detection tasks. The key finding I deem important to highlight
within this context is the demonstrated evidence of multiple, medium-sized centers of
activity, growing around hubs that represent politically active users, with their own
audiences and well defined political affiliation.
I provided further evidence about
the formation of a “community clusters” structure in the mutual mentions networks,
given by the modularity of the networks. These networks, compared to the situation
where no reciprocity of the user mentions is enforced, tend to more naturally split into
well defined communities, which take the form of typical centers of activity around a
topic, each one with its own influencer(s) and sources of information. This somehow
contradicts what one would expect to see in a bipolar political discourse that mainly
involves two factions lined up against each other. Indeed, I demonstrated that the
role of some influencers, less prominent than the globally known debating political
figures, is essential to overcome what the main leaders cannot do. It is not rare to see
thousands of Twitter users constantly mentioning these characters, reason why they
represent huge hubs in a one-way Twitter mentions network. However, in a mutual
mentions network, their role turn out to be diminished in favour of the emergence
of new influencers, who may or may not represent relevant characters in the political

debate scenario, but in either case they take charge of disseminating the information,
and due to their significantly reduced prominence, they are more likely to frequently
interact with ordinary users and ease the spreading of polarized information. This
phenomenon, along with the demonstrated evidence of a higher intensity of consistent
mutual interactions between users within the same community, aligns with the idea
behind ideological polarization, according to which users decide to engage more with
like-minded individuals.
As of these results, there is no quantifiable evidence to affirm how cross-community
communication relates to the political leaning of the involved communities. In other
words, I cannot confirm whether the majority of the communication beams across
communities involves groups from opposing factions, yet the nature of the visualiza-
tions I produced shows evidence for both scenarios, and often even highly recurrent
interactions occur across communities. Overall, this approach helped me to visualize
the structure of the networks from an outstanding perspective and further infer key
properties that the preliminary network analysis could not reveal.
The last step in my analysis diverged from the network context and dealt with
NLP. Although the work I carried out within NLP took only a little space in the
whole research, I feel confident in saying that it holds potential which, upon future
improvements, may be even more significant. I investigated only two sets sourcing
from the political hashtags, #ImWithHer and #MakeAmericaGreatAgain, adopted
as campaign slogans by the opposing factions in the 2016 U.S. Presidential Election.
I tried to infer outstanding patterns to correlate users’ political affiliation and the
tweet text properties.
The most relevant result came from the text classification
model I built to predict the main political hashtag of the tweet based on the lexicon
and linguistic style adopted by the tweeters.
Unexpectedly, the model revealed clear signs of extremely accurate predictions,
with no apparent weaknesses that could lead to a typical overfitting issue. Further
investigation made me realize that the prediction problem was just too simple due
to a intrinsic mistake related to feature selection and a missing step in the cleaning
pipeline. Indeed, I did not filter out the hashtags from the texts and just treated them
as ordinary words. During the feature selection step, performed straight after the
bag-of-words/TF-IDF vectorization for feature extraction, I just chose 1000 features
among the whole corpus of words, which represented the 1000 most frequent tokens in
the corpus. This included most of the hashtags that are used to increase expressiveness
of the tweet and supported the main one I wanted to predict. At this point, it is clear
that if most of the #ImWithHer tweets featured similar hashtags and the same applied
to #MakeAmericaGreatAgain tweets, the prediction problem is just trivial.
A first approach that could have mitigated this issue would have required stripping
out all the hashtags from the tweet text. Furthermore, in order to better evaluate
the model performance and estimate the error, I should have applied K-Fold Cross
Validation to estimate the error variance between models trained and tested on dif-
ferent portions of the data. Using a simple Hold-Out Cross Validation method, hence
splitting the dataset in 80% train and 20% test sets, does not tell whether the model
is just over-performing due to the favorable combination of train and test set that
==PAGE:104==
88
6 Discussion
eased the classifier prediction. For instance, if a different combination of train/test
sets yields a much lower prediction accuracy, the error variance would tell that some-
thing is wrong with the choice of the model. K-Fold Cross Validation overcomes this
problem by considering two crucial metrics: the mean and the standard deviation of
the accuracy values obtained for different combinations of train/test splits on each
of the CV folds. In other words, a high mean accuracy and a low variance would
provide enough reasons to claim that the model is independent from the choice of
which observations populate the train/test sets and its performance is therefore not
substantially affected. Advanced model performance evaluation would require fea-
ture selection techniques and classifier hyper parameter tuning, using for instance a
Nested Cross Validation.
Summarizing, due to time constraints I could not further experiment with new
approaches and deeply investigate the nature of the problem. However, I may confirm
that tweets political leaning prediction using NLP techniques is a viable (but not
trivial) task and I laid the foundations for an efficient classification model.

My research originally aimed at providing new, interesting and uncommon results
that could line up with what already widely discussed through previous works within
the context of ideological polarization on social networks. Wrapping up how I ap-
proached the case I dealt with and the subsequent results, the variety of the analyzed
scenarios made me look at the problem from different perspectives. There was no
straightforward approach anyhow, and all the achieved results are the direct conse-
quence of an intense iterative process, often requiring me to try multiple approaches
and evaluate the improvements. Given the original research aims and the final status
of my work, I trust that it managed to yield concrete results and for this reason it over-
all positively contributes to the current research. If compared to the previous work
inherent to the same topic, my work stands out especially for the time line analysis of
the mutual interactions and the peculiar perspective it sheds light on with regards to
the concept of ideological polarization on social media. Most of what the literature
already offers within this topic is mainly focused on studying specific case scenarios
around relevant political events occurred in the past, users’ behavioral patterns and
the identification of the root causes of such phenomenon.
I instead extended the
problem simultaneously to a broader set of controversial topics and studied the pecu-
liar aspects of the respective networks. The topics were often inspired by unrelated
political events and were spanning over very long time windows, yet I demonstrated
that they all somehow relate to each other as of the powerful impact they have on
affecting users’ online behavior.
Objectively speaking, my research has many remarkable aspects: it defined a
thorough methodology that allows to efficiently deal with a large dataset of tweets, it
highlights the non trivial drawbacks and it explains how to overcome potential issues
that could lead to undesired results. The validity of the results it carries is mostly

6.3
Limitations
As already often pointed out throughout this report, my research was affected by some
more or less evident limitations that should be highlighted for the sake of completeness.
This would additionally allow future contributions to be aware of them and potentially
ease them in finding the needed workarounds.
First of all, a stronger assessment that confirms the validity of the results with
regards to the mutual interaction consistency should reconsider the effect played by
the fluctuations of the global communication in the MMR data. A scientific approach
would require a comparison between the MIC variations affecting the links in the
H sub-networks and a set of randomly chosen links.
As for the latter, evidence
of a completely different pattern for the MIC variations, or at least a less steep
increase overtime would be enough to further validate the results and state that the
controversial topics really tend to strengthen ties overtime.
Shifting my attention towards the nature of the collected data, I deem important
to emphasize the weaknesses imposed by having a time window of three years split
in 3 month periods. Throughout the whole time line analysis of the interactions, I
have adopted the length of a period as a unit of measure and expressed the achieved
results assuming they reflected a realistic scenario. However, one single period still
spans over only 3 months, which may be considered a reasonable landmark when
working with very long time lines (in the order of tens of years) or for different
contexts. I have already pointed out that users’ activity on Twitter is highly dynamic
and fast-changing, hence there might be even more relevant patterns in the very short
term that, due to the nature of my unit of measure, are simply not captured. Results
accuracy is therefore very much dependent on this aspect.
Further limitations derive from the exclusive adoption of the SNAP.Py library
to deal with network science problems.
A deeper understanding of the networks
structure would require working closely with different tools in order to take advantage
of the benefits from each of them. For instance, some features exposed by SNAP.Py
do not offer the same flexibility of the NetworkX library, yet either way there are
non negligible drawbacks.
Working with a wider range of tools, by choosing the
appropriate one depending on the task, would allow a higher degree of versatility and
much more potential to infer deeper results by focusing on different aspects of the
networks.
The data cleanup process was still very simplistic and only took into account
a very limited set of unwanted data.
However, the random sample from Twitter
contained much more unneeded data than expected. A more extensive cleanup would

6.4
Future work
As there is always room for improvements, I hereby outline which direction may be
followed to undertake future work based on the results my research provided.
The datasets sourcing from the network data and the tweets should be more
closely related to each other. They should be combined in a way that allows to clearly
correlate well defined political groups with specific network communities. More in-
formation could then be inferred with regards to the intensity of the interactions
across communities and their intrinsic nature (e.g. what are the cross-community
links representing? Do they reflect interactions between ideologically similar individ-
uals, supporting each other, or disputes between dissimilar, not aligned people? Does
the Mutual Interaction Consistency tell anything more about them?).
A better metric that takes an additional temporal dimension into consideration
would also be needed to label highly recurrent interactions: these might reach a high
frequency simply because of repeated mentions heavily concentrated in a very short
time frame, which instead hides actual recurrent interactions that may occur at lower
rates but are more consistent in the longer term and are therefore more relevant
than the former ones. For instance, normalizing the frequency of an interaction by
considering the number of interactions per month would overcome this issue.
Further studies are needed to compare the effects of controversial hashtags with
ordinary hashtags, to highlight the differences and demonstrate that the MIC does
not reflect the same patterns for the latter.
Concerning the text classification task, I have already briefly outlined what could
be done to improve and tweak the model so that it better adapts to the nature of
the classification problem I tried to tackle. Furthermore, a stronger text classification
algorithm should also experiment with n-grams (e.g.
bigrams) besides individual
words. An n-gram is nothing but a subsequence of length n, in our case a sequence
of n adjacent words in a sentence. Using n-grams in text classification problems may
provide further information to assess whether the combination of two or more words
are more distinctive of the type of texts one aims at classifying than a single word.
Additionally, sentiment score may be also considered among the set of predictors
for the text classification problem, to figure out whether it significantly enhances the
model’s accuracy.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The current report describes my approach towards the problem I tackled by diving
into the deep world of ideological polarization on social media and studying its means
and effects on the online users. I started from two large datasets: a network of mutual
mentions on Twitter and a collection of raw tweets related to a set of selected contro-
versial topics; both datasets were spanning over a time window of more than three
years, from September 2013 until December 2016. I dealt with common data science
challenges, with remarkable focus on data pre-processing and cleaning, I carried out
typical network science tasks to analyze the structure of the network, I combined the
two datasets to infer users’ behavioral patterns and better comprehend the impact
of controversies and whether ties between users consolidate or get compromised over-
time. I found a convenient way to visually represent the mutual interactions across
the various political groups and study the information flow between them. A final
outlying task saw me involved in a NLP problem to further study eventual aspects
that deeply characterize users’ writing style on Twitter. Hence, I aimed at unveiling
more behavioral patterns that could firmly state whether there is a clear distinction
between ideologically opposing individuals.
According to the final accomplishments of my work, I revealed strong evidence
to claim that politically active users are more inclined towards strengthening ties
when it comes to controversial discourses. I demonstrated how such users tend to
segregate into well defined communities, growing around prominent users acting as
influencers and sources of information, often revealing a solid support towards a
specific political affiliation.
This phenomenon strongly recalls the effects of echo
chambers and ideological polarization, according to which users are more likely to
engage with other individuals they feel ideologically aligned with. Still, in certain
cases, this finding did not exclude the presence of a robust and consistent cross-
ideological communication. Finally, the oversimplified classification model I built to
predict the political leaning of a tweet yielded successful and promising results, yet I
identified its flaws and provided suggestions to fine tune it and make it more robust.
Altogether, my original expectations and hypothesis have been met, but more research
needs to be undertaken in order to safely claim absolute statements.
My study and the respective achievements have the means to take a relevant
place in the research inherent to the analyzed topic: I have brought new results and
inspired future work to be carried out on top of my findings. I further contributed
to explicate the importance of ideological polarization and its effects on social media
