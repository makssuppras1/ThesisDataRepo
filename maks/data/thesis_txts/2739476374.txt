Abstract
The development of a non-invasive continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) device is an
important research topic, as it can greatly improve the quality of life for diabetes pa-
tients. Terahertz (THz) time-domain spectroscopy presents a promising approach for
achieving non-invasive glucose detection due to its sensitivity to biological molecules.
However, the complex and subtle nature of the signals generated by THz spectroscopy,
makes it diﬀicult to analyze using traditional methods. This is where machine learning
(ML) comes into play, with algorithms for multivariate qualitative and quantitative
data analyses that are used for high-precision recognition of samples. This thesis
explores the feasibility of using THz time-domain spectroscopy in combination with
ML techniques by investigating the ability to distinguish pig skin samples with and
without added glucose. A systematic experimental approach was used to conduct
over 100 experiments, each testing different preprocessing, feature extraction, and
data augmentation techniques on Logistic Regression (LR), Random Forest (RF),
k-nearest Neighbors (KNN), and Support Vector Machines (SVM) classifiers.
The results demonstrated that it is possible to achieve a classification accuracy of
0.608 for distinguishing between samples with added glucose from samples with no
added glucose.
While better accuracy is needed for practical use, this result still
indicates that the models were able to capture some patterns in the data. A key
insight is the importance of how feature extraction should be conducted in relation
to the target class. For example, in the case of Principal Component Analysis (PCA),
selecting components based on their correlation with the target class rather than the
amount of variance captured resulted in better performance. The results also revealed
that larger datasets are essential for improving model performance and that simple
data augmentation methods, like averaging measurements from the same sample, are
likely to be detrimental to the performance.
In conclusion, while the results are preliminary, they provide valuable insights and a
strong foundation for further exploration. Testing other feature extraction techniques,
like feature importance analysis, could result in new knowledge about what part
of the data is important for predicting the classes.
Furthermore, advanced data
augmentation techniques based on neural networks should be tried to create more
realistic artificial data points.

Resumé
Udviklingen af en ikke-invasiv kontinuerlig glukosemonitoreringsenhed (CGM) er et
vigtigt forskningsområde, da det kan forbedre livskvaliteten betydeligt for diabetes-
patienter. Terahertz (THz) tids-domæne spektroskopi præsenterer en lovende tilgang
til at opnå ikke-invasiv måling af glukose på grund af dens følsomhed over for biolo-
giske molekyler. Dog gør THz-signalernes komplekse natur det vanskeligt at analysere
dem ved hjælp af traditionelle metoder. Det er her, maskinlæring (ML) kommer ind
i billedet, med algoritmer til multivariat kvalitativ og kvantitativ dataanalyse, der
bruges til præcis genkendelse af prøver. Denne afhandling udforsker muligheden for
at bruge THz tids-domæne spektroskopi i kombination med ML-teknikker ved at un-
dersøge evnen til at skelne mellem hudprøver fra grise med og uden tilsat glukose.
En systematisk eksperimentel tilgang blev brugt til at udføre over 100 eksperimenter,
hvor hver testede forskellige teknikker inden for præbehandling, feature extraction og
dataforøgelse på Logistic Regression (LR), Random Forest (RF), k-Nearest Neighbors
(KNN) og Support Vector Machines (SVM) modellerne.
Resultaterne viste, at det er muligt at opnå en nøjagtighed på 0,608 for at skelne
mellem prøver med tilsat glukose og prøver uden tilsat glukose. Selvom der er behov
for bedre nøjagtighed til praktisk brug, indikerer dette resultat stadig, at modellerne
var i stand til at opfange nogle mønstre i dataene. Resultaterne viste også, hvor vigtigt
det er, at feature extraction udføres i relation til klasserne. For eksempel i tilfælde af
Principal Component Analysis (PCA), gav det bedre resultater at vælge komponenter
baseret på deres korrelation med klasserne snarere end mængden af varians, de fanger.
Resultaterne afslørede også, at større datasæt er afgørende for at forbedre modellernes
præstation, og at simple dataforøgningsmetoder, såsom at generere nye måliger ud
fra gennemsnittet af to målinger fra samme prøve, sandsynligvis vil være skadelige
for præstationen.
Afslutningsvis, selvom resultaterne er foreløbige, giver de værdifulde indsigter og
et stærkt fundament for videre udforskning.
Testning af andre feature extraction
teknikekr, såsom feature importance-analyse, kunne resultere i ny viden om, hvilken
del af dataene der er vigtig for at forudsige klasserne.
Desuden bør avancerede
dataforøgningsmetoder baseret på neurale netværk afprøves for at skabe mere re-
alistiske kunstige datapunkter.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Diabetes is a significant issue within global health and is expected to double by
2050 [1]. Currently, blood glucose monitoring is typically done using electrochemical
sensors, which require finger-pricking to obtain blood samples, or through minimally
invasive devices that use implanted needles connected to external sensors.
Both
methods involve breaking the skin, leading to pain and discomfort, which can hinder
effective disease management and diminish the quality of life for those with diabetes.
As a result, there is a need for research into non-invasive blood glucose measurement
techniques.
Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) includes a variety of techniques under inves-
tigation, many of which focus on detecting glucose in the interstitial fluid (ISF) of the
skin rather than directly in the blood. ISF glucose levels have been shown to strongly
correlate with blood glucose levels and can be detected just a few hundred microns
beneath the skin’s surface. Some of these methods are considered non-invasive as
they do not require breaking the skin to access the glucose, with many relying on
optical techniques.
One of these novel CGM approaches is based on Terahertz (THz) spectroscopy. This
approach aims to non-invasively measure glucose in the skin’s ISF by analyzing the
interaction between THz light and glucose in a liquid environment. Although the
use of THz technology outside of research has been limited due to the bulkiness of
the systems and their sensitivity to environmental factors, recent advancements at
DTU Electro have led to the development of a compact and robust THz system that
potentially can be scaled down for use in wearable sensors. This is one of the key
aspects that motivates this research.
The reason that THz radiation is effective for the use case of CGM, is its exceptional
non-invasive and non-ionizing sensing capabilities.
The capacity of THz waves to
penetrate deeply into dielectric materials, like biological tissue, combined with their
high spatial resolution makes them very suitable for biomedical applications, includ-
ing both in-vivo and ex-vivo experiments [2]. The resulting spectroscopy data will
therefore hold much useful information, which can be processed and used for diag-
nostic biomedical applications like the aforementioned CGM. One of the increasingly
popular methods to process this data, and a method that has shown great promise,

is the use of machine learning (ML) algorithms. The advantage of using ML is that
it provides a solution for many of the limitations related to THz data. Denoising
algorithms address the issue of noise, which is very prevalent in THz data, and al-
gorithms for multivariate qualitative and quantitative data analyses are used for the
high-precision recognition of samples [3]. Furthermore, some ML-based algorithms
can be used to address the issue of the high cost of collecting THz data by generating
new artificial samples [4]. The state-of-the-art ML algorithms have shown to result in
better performance for THz applications, than methods from before the ML era. [5]
State of the art
The combination of ML and THz spectroscopy has already been proven effective in
many biomedical studies like the work of Liu et al. [6] They investigated a classifi-
cation method for the identification of breast invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC) using
a transmission THz spectroscopy system in combination with ML. They collected
samples from 63 patients who were diagnosed with breast IDC. Both cancerous and
normal tissues were retrieved from the samples and were categorized as IDC, normal
fibrous, and normal adipose samples. This resulted in 97 IDC samples, 100 fibrous,
and 99 adipose samples. Liu et al. used a transmission THz-TDS system for gener-
ating the THz time-domain signals, of the samples, which resulted in 1024 features
per signal. Principle Component Analysis (PCA) was applied for dimensionality re-
duction and different combinations of principal components (PCs) were selected and
evaluated. Multiple classifiers were trained with different hyperparameters: Support
Vector Machines (SVM), K Nearest Neighbor (KNN), and ensemble methods for deci-
sion trees (DTs) and subspace KNN. They achieved the highest classification accuracy
of 88.9% which was obtained using the ensemble method with subspace kNN (k=10)
and 30 principal components.
Terahertz time-domain spectroscopy and machine learning are also studied within
other domains. In a study by Jiang et al. [4] they want to address the lack of THz
spectroscopy data, by artificially generating new samples using a generative adversar-
ial network (GAN). Specifically, they focus on addressing the challenge of insuﬀicient
training data for detecting hazardous materials. They use a specific type of GAN
that includes a fully connected layer and an auxiliary classifier, that uses class labels
to guide the GAN. They mention that these modifications help make the generated
data closer to the true data distribution. They performed this data augmentation
on a THz time-domain spectral data of seven flammable liquids and then used the
augmented dataset to train different types of neural networks. They found that their
method of augmenting data solved the issue of overfitting of models because of in-
suﬀicient data. Compared with direct training on the original data set, the accuracy
of models using an augmented dataset improved by 5.1325% on average, and they
achieved a top accuracy of 99.42% on average, which is 1.09% higher than just using
the original dataset.

2.1
Terahertz spectroscopy
The field of medical scanning and diagnosis systems depends on technology that uti-
lizes the Electromagnetic (EM) spectrum. This ranges from MRI scanning using radio
waves to PET scanning using gamma rays. Different regions of the EM spectrum in-
teract with matter in different ways.
As the frequency increases, the interaction
of electromagnetic waves with the human body becomes increasingly focused, shift-
ing from the scale of the entire body down to the resolution of individual DNA [2].
Higher frequencies carry more energy, which can interact with molecules and poten-
tially damage DNA and proteins, while lower frequencies carry less energy and are
generally harmless. Another key difference is that lower frequencies penetrate deeper
into biological tissue but sacrifice spatial resolution and sensitivity, while higher fre-
quencies offer greater sensitivity and resolution but have limited penetration depth.
Terahertz (THz) is a spectrum spanning roughly 0.1THz to 10THz and lies between
the microwave and the infrared spectrum. The THz spectrum is therefore not within
visible light but a part of the lower frequencies which do not harm molecules [2].
Until recently, it has been diﬀicult to utilize the THz part of the EM spectrum, but
great progress has been made in the last two decades, resulting in the THz spectrum
being used in many different domains. THz waves are non-ionizing, have good spatial
resolution, and have good absorption in polar molecules like water.
THz Spectroscopy is about using THz waves to study the properties of materials,
including their molecular composition, dynamics, and interactions. The advantage
of specifically using THz waves is that their spectra enable investigations into the
rotation and interactions of molecules which is of great interest in many applications
such as diabetes management [9]. THz pulsed spectroscopy (TPS) is a technique that
records the complete electric field of the pulse as a function of time. This means
it captures the exact shape of the pulse, including both its amplitude and phase
information. In other words, it records the pulse in time-domain representation, while
still allowing analysis in the frequency-domain [8]. A typical setup of time-domain
TPS can be seen in figure 2.1 and an example THz pulse, taken by a TPS system can
be seen in figure 2.2.

2.1.1
THz TD-ATR
THz spectroscopy systems typically employ one of three methods for a THz wave to
interact with a sample [10]. In the transmission method, the wave passes directly
through the sample, making it suitable for materials with moderate absorption, such
as gases and some solids. However, this method is less effective for polar liquids,
particularly water, due to their high absorption in this frequency range [11]. The
reflection method involves the THz wave reflecting off the sample, which is more
appropriate for thicker or highly absorbing materials like liquids, especially water.
Finally, Attenuated Total Reflection (ATR) is a specialized reflection technique where
the THz wave first passes through a prism, interacts with the sample at the prism-
sample interface, and then reflects to the THz receiver.
THz ATR time-domain
spectroscopy (THz ATR-TDS) is well-suited for polar liquids, powders, or thin films,
and offers greater sensitivity compared to other methods [11].
In the THz ATR-TDS setup, illustrated in Figure 2.3, the sample is placed on top
of a prism, typically made of high-resistivity silicon, which is transparent and non-
dispersive across the THz frequency range [11]. The term ”non-dispersive” indicates
that the prism does not significantly distort or alter the THz wave. As the THz wave
travels through the prism and reaches the interface between the prism and sample at
a specific angle, it is reflected into the prism—a phenomenon known as total internal
reflection (TIR). TIR occurs based on the angle of the THz wave relative to the
interface, as well as the refractive indices (or densities) of the prism and sample.
Although the THz wave is fully reflected during TIR, an electromagnetic field, known
as the evanescent wave, is generated at the interface between the prism and sample.
Unlike a normal wave, an evanescent wave does not propagate in a typical manner
but instead decays exponentially into the less dense medium, which in this case is the
sample. The distance this wave travels is called the penetration depth, and the sample
must have a thickness greater than this distance. The evanescent wave interacts with
the sample, causing changes to the characteristics of the THz wave. It is these changes
that the ATR-TDS system detects [11].
2.1.2
Relevance to continuous glucose monitoring
The skin performs many vital functions in the body, and various diseases and health
conditions can be detected by examining it. It is made up of three layers of tissues
with the outermost being the epidermis, the middle one the dermis, and the innermost
one the hypodermis. The thickness of these layers varies depending on the location
of the body. For instance, the epidermis is thinnest in the eyelids and thickest in
the palms and soles [8].
Skin is one of the places of the body that is most well
suited for THz spectroscopy as its superficial location is not affected by the limited
penetration depth of the THz waves [8]. This, combined with the fact that THz waves
are non-ionizing, makes pulsed setups like ATR-TDS, well-suited for examining the
skin.

2.1.3
Noise in THz spectroscopy
There can be many sources of noise in a THz spectroscopy system which can affect
how reliable a signal is. The laser itself is one of them [16]. A consistent performance
of the laser is crucial as even small fluctuations can significantly impact the noise
levels. Maintaining stable laser power throughout a measurement, whether it lasts a
few minutes or several hours, is crucial. Temporal uncertainty can also be introduced
to the system by factors like mechanical jitter in the delay line, air fluctuations, or
vibrations of the laser table. Using stable optical mounts can reduce this type of
noise. Environmental factors, like humidity, can cause the THz wave to be absorbed
by water molecules in the air. To address this, the THz emitter, receiver, and sample
are enclosed in a chamber that is purged with Nitrogen or dry air [16]. A source of
error, that is specific to ATR-TDS systems, is when the position or angle of the prism
is slightly changed when adding a sample. The prism needs to be firmly fixed onto the
platform, as even small changes can lead to very different results [11]. A particularly
complex source of noise arises from the variability of human skin. Factors such as
skin elasticity, moisture, thickness, and collagen density can all influence the THz
==PAGE:22==
2.2 Machine Learning
9
signal. While these variations are often considered noise, they also contain valuable
information about the patient.
Thus, rather than being purely detrimental, this
’noise’ can be leveraged to extract meaningful data.
Noise can be minimized but not eliminated, and therefore important to consider in
the analysis of the THz signal. A common technique in the domain of THz spec-
troscopy is to take a reference measurement before measuring the sample [11]. This
reference captures the system and environmental noise present at that specific mo-
ment. The assumption is that the noise remains fairly consistent if the sample is
measured shortly after, allowing the subtraction of the reference measurement to iso-
late the true signal. In summary, while many sources of noise in a THz spectroscopy
system can be mitigated, others are unavoidable and must be sampled or interpreted
correctly. Not all noise is inherently negative and, in some cases, it carries valuable
information that can enhance the understanding of the sample.

2.2.3
Overfitting and Generalization
It is usually assumed that the sampled training dataset represents the overall proper-
ties of all possible instances for a task. Therefore a generalized model can be learned
using only the training set. However, this is rarely the case, and the learned model
tends to learn properties that are only observed in the sampled training data. [5]
This is called overfitting. Another way that a model can overfit, is when it learns the
noise properties as well as the signal properties in the training data. Overfitting is
a common issue in ML but can be mitigated using techniques like ensemble learning
and data augmentation.
Figure 2.6 shows a binary classification problem solved by two different models. On
the left side, we see a model that has perfectly classified every single training point,
and on the right side, we see a model that has some misclassifications. However, if
the same boundaries remain, but a different set of points is sampled from the true
distribution, then the right model will probably perform better. This is because the
right model better follows the true distribution, while the left model has learned the
noise present in that particular sampled training set. We, therefore, say that the right
model generalizes better and the left model has overfitted to training data.
There are no straightforward methods of making a model generalize better. Instead,
there are some strategies to use. [19] Tuning the model complexity is one of these
strategies. This usually means increasing or decreasing the number of trainable pa-
rameters as this reflects how much information can be stored in the model. Too many
make the model store noise and too few would make the model unable to learn the
true distribution. Other strategies include using early stopping criteria, using the
average output from multiple simple models (also called ensemble training), penaliz-
ing extreme weights, or simply adding more training data. The last strategy can be
diﬀicult in practice but can be simulated using data augmentation techniques. Some
of these will be discussed in 2.2.9.

2.2.4
Types of ML
There are three fundamental types of machine learning:
Supervised Learning
In Supervised Learning the model is trained on a labeled dataset. This means that
for every input (feature), the corresponding output (label) is provided. The model
then learns to map inputs to outputs by analyzing the training data. in other words,
the model outputs a specific prediction within the set of provided labels. A task is
said to be a classification task if the label is discrete. Likewise, it is said to be
a regression task if the label is continuous. Random Forest, Logistic Regression,
K-Nearest Neighbors and Support Vector Machines are all supervised algorithms.
Unsupervised Learning
In Unsupervised Learning, the model learns patterns that are embedded in the data
itself without any labels.
The outputs of these types of models tend to be more
abstract and do not have any specific prediction. These types of algorithms are often
used for clustering or dimensionality reduction. Principal Component Analysis is a
well-known unsupervised algorithm used for finding a lower-dimension representation
of the data.
Reinforcement Learning
In Reinforcement Learning there is no data to learn from. Instead, there is an agent
that interacts with an environment given a set of rules to reach a certain goal. The
agent learns by trial and error. The agent learns by being rewarded for good decisions
and penalized for bad decisions. This type of ML is often used in robotics. Currently,
there is no literature about the application of reinforcement learning within the do-
main of terahertz spectroscopy, but it has found success in other areas within life
sciences. For instance, a reinforcement learning model that has shown to be adept at
playing chess has also shown great results within protein design. [20]
The task of this thesis is a supervised classification problem as the dataset includes
binary and discrete labels. However, unsupervised learning algorithms are also used
in the feature extraction step of the pipeline.

2.2.6.4
Fast Fourier Transform
The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is an algorithm that eﬀiciently calculates the
discrete Fourier transform of a signal and is a way to convert a time-domain signal
into the frequency domain. The Fourier Transform expresses a signal as a sum of
sinusoids, where each is defined by a frequency, amplitude, and phase. This gives
access to important information that is otherwise diﬀicult to see in the time domain,
and thus the FFT is a very important algorithm in signal processing. An example of
an FFT can be seen in figure 2.8.
The Fourier transform operates under the following two assumptions [25]:
1. The finite sampled signal is a part of a periodical signal.
2. There are a whole number of periods in the sampled signal.
In practice, the above assumptions rarely hold.
Often the signal is truncated or
measured in a way so that it is cut off in the middle of a period. And sometimes the
signal is not periodic. This creates a phenomenon called spectral leakage where the
frequencies of the FFT are smeared across the spectrum [26]. The main cause is that,
if the signal is to be repeated, then the endpoints of a single period do not meet at
the same value, but instead cause a sharp transition. An example of spectral leakage
can be seen in 2.9. Spectral leakage can be minimized by using a technique called
windowing, which in essence, ensures that there are no sharp transitions between the
periods.

the tapering should start. The shape of the Tukey window can be seen in figure 2.10
and the effect is demonstrated in figure 2.11. The reason that the Tukey window
is preferred over e.g. a pure Hanning window, is that the rectangular or flat part
preserves as much resolution as possible and the signal is minimally distorted.

2.2.7
Feature Extraction
Feature extraction is a critical step in the ML process of transforming raw data into
meaningful inputs for machine learning algorithms. Often it is best not to use the
raw features directly, as they are too high dimensional, as they are redundant, or
as they contain too much noise. A term called, the curse of dimensionality, is often
used in relation to high-dimensional datasets. It refers to the phenomenon where
the eﬀiciency and effectiveness of algorithms deteriorate as the dimensionality of the
data increases exponentially [29]. In these cases, data becomes sparse in the high-
dimensional spaces, making it diﬀicult to find meaningful patterns.
The curse of
dimensionality leads to a higher risk of overfitting, longer training times, and higher
resource requirements. Feature extraction addresses the curse of dimensionality by
extracting key features of the data that better represent the core information in a
lower-dimensional space.
2.2.7.1
Principal Component Analysis
The principal component analysis (PCA) is a technique for transforming high dimen-
sional data to lower-dimensional data with minimal information loss, by using the
dependencies between the variables [5]. PCA finds the directions (principle compo-
nents) of most variance in the feature space and sorts them based on how much
variance each direction captures. Figure 2.12 shows a 2-dimensional dataset with the
mean centered at zero. On the left side, we see that the data has similar variance along
the v and u axis which represent the two features of the dataset. However, two new
axes v′ and u′ can be constructed, such that u′ contains the majority of the variance.
This can be seen on the right side of the figure. The two new axes represent two new
features that the original data points can be projected on. Dimensionality reduction
can be achieved by discarding v′ such that the dataset becomes 1-dimensional. By
visually inspecting the plot, it can be seen that minimal information will be lost by
removing v′. u′ is said to be the first principal component, and v′ is said to be the
2nd principle component. A PCA includes the following steps [30]:

2.2.8
Model Training and Evaluation
After the data has been preprocessed and key features have been extracted, the model
training can begin. There are many different algorithms for learning patterns in the
data, and each has its pros and cons. However, all try to achieve the same goal which
is to minimize some cost function that quantifies how well a model can predict a target
label. Different algorithms use different cost functions. Some of the algorithms that
have found success in the domain of THz spectroscopy will briefly be explained in the
following.
2.2.8.1
Algorithms
Logistic Regression
Logistic Regression is a fundamental algorithm in machine learning, primarily used for
binary classification tasks where the goal is to classify data into one of two categories.
It is one of the simplest models for binary classification and often serves as a baseline.
An example can be seen in figure 2.13. It works by fitting a sigmoid function that
maps any value to a probability between 0 and 1. The sigmoid function is defined as:

iteratively updates the weights in the direction that reduces the cost function [32].
The pros of using logistic regression include its easy implementation and fast eﬀicient

that a linear decision boundary can be drawn to separate the classes. For non-linearly
separable data, more complex models are needed.

Support Vector Machine is an algorithm that finds a hyperplane that best separates

increase the number of features exponentially. Instead, pairwise distances between the
data points in the higher dimensions can be calculated in the original lower dimensions
using kernel functions [37].

2.2.8.2
Cross Validation
After preprocessing and feature extraction, a dataset is typically divided into a train
set and a test set.
The train set is used to train the model, allowing it to learn
patterns and relationships in the data. The test set is kept separate and used to
evaluate the model’s performance on unseen data, ensuring it generalizes well and
avoids overfitting. However, when the dataset is limited, it is not possible to evaluate
the performance reliably because of insuﬀicient training and testing data after the
partition. To address this problem, a technique called cross-validation is used. This
involves running multiple independent train and test rounds on different partitions
of the dataset. K-fold cross-validation runs k number of train and test rounds where
it splits the dataset into k-partitions, trains on k-1 partitions, and tests on the last
partition. This is repeated k-times where the test partition is changed each round
resulting in k different models. The overall performance is then computed as the
==PAGE:38==
2.2 Machine Learning
25
Figure 2.17. Decision boundary from KNN at different values of k. Taken from [38]
average of the performance of each model. In extreme cases with very limited data, a
version called leave-one-out cross-validation is used, where k is equal to the number of
data points. Another version, called GroupKFold, is used for grouped data which is
commonly found in medical studies that take multiple samples from each patient [39].
It is important to ensure that all data points from the same sample are only found in
either the training or test set. If the data is split in a way, such that the same group
can be found in both the training and test set, then the evaluation would be overly
optimistic due to the high correlation of data points of the same group. It will not be
possible to get an accurate estimate of how the model would perform on completely
unseen data, due to information leakage between the test and train set.
Nested Cross Validation and hyperparameter tuning
In model training, there is a difference between a parameter and a hyperparameter. A
parameter is a variable that is learned from the data during the training process. This
could be the support vectors from SVM or the coeﬀicients from the logistic regression.
A hyperparameter, on the other hand, is a variable that is set before the training
process begins. These are parameters external to the training and can therefore not
==PAGE:39==
2.2 Machine Learning
26
be learned from the data. Some examples include k in KNN, the number of principal
components, the max depth of a tree in RF, or the kernel type in SVM. The optimal
combination of hyperparameters can be set manually by using domain knowledge or a
rule of thumb, but it is usually found by a process called hyperparameter optimization.
Hyperparameter optimization is the act of searching the hyperparameter space cre-
ated by all the possible combinations of hyperparameters, to find the combination
that results in the best performance of the model. A simple technique is to brute-
force search all the possible combinations within a predefined interval of values and
train the model on the selected hyperparameters. This is usually time-consuming
and rarely results in an optimal combination. Instead, more intelligent search tech-
niques can be used like the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) which is based
on Bayesian optimization [40]. The TPE searches the hyperparameter space more
eﬀiciently in a tree-like manner and discards any paths that do not show promising
results.
Hyperparameter optimization is not recommended for use with normal cross-validation,
as it will result in too optimistic evaluation of the model. This is because the hyper-
parameters are optimized for the model’s performance on the test data over multiple
rounds. Thus information from the test set might leak into the training, which means
that it is no longer possible to have an accurate estimate of the performance on un-
seen data. Nested cross-validation addresses this problem by creating an additional
partition and thus creating an inner and outer loop [41]. Part of the train set from
normal CV is partitioned into a validation set which is used for the hyperparameter
tuning. There is no information leakage as the test set remains in the outer loop. An
overview of nested cross-validation can be seen in figure 2.18.

considering the area under the curve (AUC). An AUC of 1 will represent a perfect
classifier, while an AUC of 0.5 will represent random guessing.

3.2.2.4
Model Training and Evaluation
The model training and evaluation process uses a nested group-k-fold cross-validation
approach. Figure 3.11 is a flow diagram that shows this process where the outer
loop is utilized to estimate the generalization performance of the model, while the
inner loop focuses on optimizing the hyperparameters. The loops are defined by two
parameters, a K-outer, and a K-inner, and this ensures that there is no information
leakage between a test and training set, which could lead to overly optimistic results.
The final metrics, used to estimate the generalization performance, are the result of
the aggregation from the metrics of each outer fold. A final model is created for
deployment, by using the entire dataset, leaving no data points for its evaluation.
However, the aggregated metrics from the outer folds serve as an estimate of how
it would perform on completely unseen data. For some runs, flow diagram 3.11 is
slightly simplified, as some feature extraction and data augmentation is performed
after the outer and inner splits of the dataset.
The following models were selected for training: LR, RF, KNN, and SVM, based
on their diverse underlying principles and ability to handle various data characteris-
tics. For instance, LR is well-suited for linear relationships, RF provides robustness
through ensemble learning, SVM is effective in high-dimensional spaces, and KNN
is advantageous for non-parametric data classification.
Thus each model analyses
the data in a different way, which can lead to interesting insights. Each model was

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The following will be a discussion of the results from the previous chapter in the
context of distinguishing pig skin samples, and the broader context of creating a
non-invasive CGM device. Subsequently, a discussion on the generalizability of the
conclusions will be made, followed by a subsection about ethical considerations.
After running and testing 100+ different configurations, including different preprocess-
ing, feature extraction, data augmentation, and algorithms, the highest test accuracy
is still only 0.608, as can be seen in table 4.1. This is not far from the accuracy of
a classifier that outputs completely random binary guesses, and therefore not a good
accuracy for practical uses. There are multiple reasons for why this is the case, some
of which, were hinted at in the previous chapter. However, the main reason is likely to
be the limited quantity of 44 data points, as seen in figure 4.3. The models performed
better on unseen data, regardless of algorithm, the more original data points were
used for training. Also, the training accuracy decreased indicating that the model
becomes better at handling noise, which is another big challenge of the dataset.
Most of the feature extraction techniques used did not result in top-performing models,
meaning that they likely oversimplified the patterns in the data, losing important
information in the process.
For many runs, PCA was used in a way where the
PCs were chosen based on how much variance they had captured.
However, the
best-performing PCA run showed that it is better to select the PCs based on how
much correlation they have with the positive class. Understanding which PCs are
correlated with the target label can give important insights into which directions in
the data space are most relevant for distinguishing between classes. This can help
in understanding the underlying structure of the data in relation to the classification
task. In other words, it might have information about which exact features contribute
to each class, which is very valuable information to understand the effect of glucose
on the THz signal. However, PCA is notoriously diﬀicult to interpret as the PCs are
linear combinations of all features. Therefore, it might be worthwhile to look into
other feature extraction methods, that are easier to interpret the result of.
While a test accuracy of 0.608 is not very high, it still indicates that the model has
learned some pattern in the data, that helps it differentiate between classes in the
sample. The ROC-curve in figure 4.1 shows that the model is better than just random
guessing due to the higher AUC. Also, a different threshold, than the default at 0.5,

The main objective of this project was defined as investigate the possibility of distin-

can be questioned, as it is not sure that the same results would be achieved with a
different dataset. In the method chapter, it was described how nested cross-validation
was used to estimate the generalization performance of a model on completely unseen
data. The best estimate is calculated by measuring the performance across different
subsets of the dataset, which is based on the folds. However, keep in mind that even
though using nested-cross-validation gives the best possible generalization estimate,
it is still based on the bias in this particular dataset. Meaning, that this dataset
might not be representative of all the variations of the real-world data. For instance,
another dataset might be based on pigs that grew up under different conditions than
the pigs in this dataset. It is then possible that models might not generalize well to
these pigs, as their data was not included. Another reason why the certainty can be
questioned is that the small amount of data can mean that the results were based
on chance. Another dataset would result in completely different results. The only
certain conclusion that can be made, is: If you apply the same methods, to the same
dataset, then you will get the same results. However, the findings from this project
can serve as a hint of what ML methods may work well with THz data, and as di-
rection on what future studies should focus on. These results are one step closer to
creating a non-invasive CGM device using THz spectroscopy and ML.
Ethical Implications
In this project, the use of ex-vivo pig skin samples inherently involves fewer ethical
complexities compared to studies involving living subjects, whether animal or human.
However, it is crucial to consider the broader ethical implications, especially given
that the ultimate goal of this project is to develop findings that could assist the
development of non-invasive CGM devices for diabetes patients. There are no papers
that specifically look at the ethical implications related to the combination of machine
learning and THz spectroscopy, however, some exist about the medical field in general.

This is exactly what is discussed in a paper by Basu et. al, where five key ethical
concepts are outlined [48]:
• Privacy is about data ownership and the use of personal health records for
training machine learning models. This is data that can be highly sensitive
and private to a patient, and it is therefore important to ensure the consent
of using it for training models. ML developers need to understand the specific
requirements of current regulations about privacy in ML. For instance, the
General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) exists within the European Union,
which gives individuals control over their data more broadly and is not just
limited to health data.
• Fairness is about avoiding selective and discriminatory bias against certain
groups of individuals. This can happen in the data collection process if more
data is collected from certain geographical regions, or if the measurements them-
selves work better on certain groups. This is important to keep in mind, to
ensure equal treatment for all individuals, regardless of gender, nationality, cul-
ture etc.
• Accountability is about holding someone responsible for the decisions and
consequences of that individual’s actions. Machine learning algorithms create a
new situation, where the developer of a model, cannot predict its future behavior
and thus cannot be held morally responsible or liable for it.
• Transparency is about openness, clarity, and honesty in actions, decisions,
and communication. For machine learning, it means that developers accurately
report the performance of the models and the techniques used. Metrics can be
very misleading and used in malicious ways. For instance, a model’s accuracy
can be reported as very high for an imbalanced dataset.
In these cases, it
is important to report the imbalance or use another metric, such as balanced
accuracy. It is also important to acknowledge that the performance and results
are highly dependent on the specific dataset. Retraining the model on a different
dataset, or for a slightly different use case, can result in much different results.
• Conflict of Interest occurs when an individual or organization has competing
interests or loyalties that could potentially influence their decisions, actions, or
judgment in a way that compromises their integrity, impartiality, or professional
responsibilities. For instance, a company might be interested in creating the
best possible model, and thus as much data as possible, even if it means that
some of it has been used without consent.
Basu et al. also outlines several common rules and best practices that should be
followed to address the ethical concerns previously mentioned.
These include the
need to improve regulations around data privacy, and potentially establishing global
guidelines to protect patient privacy more effectively. Additionally, it is important
to standardize data collection and storage on a global scale, which would enhance

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This study aimed to explore the feasibility of distinguishing between pig skin samples
with and without added glucose using terahertz spectroscopy combined with machine
learning techniques. The findings suggest that while it is possible to achieve some
level of classification accuracy, there are significant challenges that must be addressed
to enhance the reliability of these models in practical settings. It was shown that it is
possible to distinguish pig skin samples with added glucose from those without with
a maximum accuracy of 0.608. Although this performance is not far from a random
classifier, which would have gotten an accuracy of 0.5 on this balanced dataset, it still
shows that the models were able to learn some patterns in the data that differentiate
between the two classes. Also, the models performed better, and with less overfitting,
more original data was used, indicating that the size of the original dataset is a key
factor for improving performance.
One of the most important insights was that feature extraction can lead to the loss
of important information if the classes are not taken into account when performing
it. For instance, for PCA, it was shown that the PCs that captured the most amount
of variance were not necessarily the ones that were mostly correlated with the pos-
itive class. Thus, better performance could be achieved by selecting PCs based on
correlation, rather than variance. This highlights the importance of considering the
relationship between features and the target variable when selecting and engineering
features, particularly in complex datasets like THz spectroscopy, where the impor-
tance lies in the smaller differences. Other results from the study include that the
data augmentation methods used in the study did not improve model performance
and, in some cases, worsened it. This suggests that the generated synthetic data did
not adequately represent the true data distribution and may have introduced noise.
Many different topics should be expanded upon, based on the findings of this thesis.
First and foremost, it is worthwhile to explore further the relationship between the
features and the positive class, by trying to interpret the PCs. Furthermore, other
techniques for feature extraction should be employed, where it is easier to interpret
the results. For instance, feature importance analysis is a way to score how important
each feature is for predicting a particular class [49]. This could be applied to a signal
in the time domain to understand which parts of the pulse are most important. Or
applied to a signal in the frequency domain to understand which frequencies are most
important for predicting the class. Also, other than trying to sample more original
==PAGE:65==

data, it is worthwhile to try more advanced data augmentation methods, like the
FC-AGAN [4], that can generate more realistic artificial signals.
In conclusion, this study provides a foundational exploration of the use of THz spec-
troscopy combined with ML for non-invasive glucose detection. The results show some
key areas of interest that should be explored further, and may potentially become an
important step towards the realization of non-invasive CGM devices.

The following is the content of the README.txt file which was provided with the
dataset. It is important to note that this is not my own original work. The content

Goal
The overall goal of the experiments on both days is to investigate whether we
can distinguish samples with added glucose from samples with no added glucose.
Samples
The samples used in these experiments are fresh pig skin samples obtained
from a local slaughterhouse. The pig skin samples are kept in sealed containers in
the fridge. To prepare the samples for the experiment, the subcutaneous fat layer is
removed until the thickness of the skin samples is around 2-3 millimeters.
Treatment
The treatment involves:
1. Injecting the samples with a liquid glucose solution of known concentration.
2. Doing so in a non-localized manner that resembles the diffusion of interstitial
fluid (ISF) flow in the skin (i.e., ISF traveling from the innermost to the outer-
most layers of the skin).
To aid glucose solution absorption, the subcutaneous fat layer is removed. A patch
with needle heads is used to pierce micro-holes into the dermis side of the skin sample.
Next, a cotton pad is soaked in either of two solutions: pure PBS (saline solution)
or PBS spiked with 2.5 mg/mL glucose for a few seconds. The cotton pad is then
placed on the dermis side of the skin sample, allowing the solution to diffuse into the
skin sample, which lies in a petri dish with the stratum corneum (outermost layer of
the skin) facing down. The skin sample absorbs the solution for 40 minutes.
Measurements
Each sample is first measured in its ”bare” state (i.e., before treat-
ment). Then, it is treated (as explained above) and measured again.
When measured, the samples lie with the stratum corneum interfacing the surface of
the sensor, and a weight of 250g is placed on top of the sample to:
==PAGE:68==
Appendix A The Dataset
55
1. Ensure good contact between the sample and the surface of the sensor.
2. Simulate the contact pressure corresponding to an average human arm lying on
the sensor.
For each sample, the following measurements are taken:
• 10 consecutive air pulses (reference measurement for bare skin).
• 10 consecutive pulses on the bare skin.
• After treatment, 10 consecutive air pulses (reference measurement for treated
skin).
• 10 consecutive pulses on the treated skin.
Each pulse is averaged 1000 times for a better signal-to-noise ratio.
Data
The data was acquired on two different days:
• On 20230629, 18 samples were measured: 8 with glucose in PBS, 8 with only
PBS, and 2 negative controls (the negative controls were not left to absorb any
solution but were left in a petri dish for 40 minutes).
• On 20230630, 28 samples were analyzed: 14 with glucose in PBS and 14 without.
The data is saved in pickle files. Each pickle file consists of a Python native dictionary
with the following keys:
• scan: A list of length 10 containing the THz pulses data from a skin measure-
ment. Each element in the list is a dictionary with the following keys:
– forward_scan and backward_scan, each of which is a dictionary with the
following keys:
∗time: Time axis of the THz pulse in units of seconds.
∗signal: THz pulse amplitude in units of nA.
∗signal_err: Standard deviation of the pulse amplitude.
• ref: Same exact structure as scan, but for the air reference pulse taken right
before the skin pulses.
• settings: Dictionary containing settings of the Toptica TeraFlash Smart system
for this data collection.
• samplematrix: A string describing the skin sample (e.g., ’sample X {state}’),
where X is the sample number ID (a number) and {state} is either ’bare’, ’PBS’,
or ’g/PBS’. For each sample ID (X), there will be one file where the samplema-
trix is ’bare’ and one where it is treated.

• conc: Concentration of glucose (in mg/mL), which is always 0 for the bare
samples.
• time: DateTime object referring to the time at which the skin measurement
