Abstract
HCV (hepatitis C virus) infection is a severe liver disease which may lead to permanent liver
damage if left untreated. Standard of care treatment for HCV infection include pegylated in-
terferon (IFN) α with or without ribavirin. Half of the population fail to clear the virus with
peg-IFN α and ribavirin. In recent years DAA (direct acting antivirals) are used to treat HCV
infection by blocking the activity of the HCV proteins.Even though DAA’s are eﬀective, drug-
drug interactions and cost of these drugs limit the usage of DAA’s. Gene therapy’s for HCV
infection is a powerful way to target the disease since these gene therapeutics targets the disease
at a genomic level. Due to high replication rate of HCV, it is complex to get a speciﬁc antibody
that targets the HCV infection. Claudin 1 (CLDN1) protein is over expressed in HCV positive
cell lines and chronically inﬂamed liver tissue cells. CLDN1 antibody can be used to target the
HCV cell lines. Combination of therapeutic eﬃciency of gene therapeutics and exceptional tar-
geting ability of antibody is an ideal therapy for HCV infection. RNA interference (RNAi) and
antisense therapy are promising gene therapy’s used to treat diseases. A strategy to synthesize
Antibody-oligonucleotide conjugates (AOCs) with a enzymatically cleavable linker is developed
in this report.
Fluorescence biomolecules are always useful to detect and quantify the biological processes in
a minute scale. Fluorescence labelling of oligonucleotides are powerful tools to detect genomic
or disease associated nucleic acids and also in forensic applications like quantiﬁcation of STRs
(short tandem repeats). Design is made to synthesize a ﬂuorescence labelled phosphoramidites
which can be incorporated manually in the synthesis of nucleic acids. perylene (optical yield =
0.94) is used as a ﬂuorophore to synthesize ﬂuorescence phosphoramidites.

Hepatitis C infection:
Hepatitis C virus (HCV) is a hepatotropic member of the Flaviviridae causes acute and chronic
hepatitis with more than 3 million infections per year. HCV is a major health issue with 170
million chronically infected people in world and 350,000 deaths every year from HCV related
liver diseases [1]. 55-75 % of the patients fail to eradicate the virus and a long term HCV in-
fection leads to permanent liver damage (liver cirrhosis), hepato-cellular carcinoma (25% of the
HCV cases leads to HCC) and may lead to death [2]. Even though eﬀorts are made to fabricate
and develop the vaccines and neutralizing antibodies against HCV infection, there is no perfect
vaccine or antibody available for HCV prevention due to high degree of strain variation in HCV
[3]. HCV has seven genotypes which responds diﬀerently with the same treatment, therefore
regular screening is of great importance for the purpose of providing the best treatment. The
current treatment includes pegylated interferon alpha in combination with ribavirin, which is
costly and also has signiﬁcant side eﬀects and on top of that fails to cure half of all infections
[4]. More recently a new standard-of care treatment has been approved based on a triple combi-
nation with HCV protease inhibitor telaprevir or boceprevir. Gene therapy represents a feasible
alternative treatment as well. Small RNA technology, including RNA interference (RNAi) tech-
niques and antisense approaches are some of the most potentially promising ways to investigate
viral and host cell factors that are involved in HCV infection and replication.Among these RNA
interference is a promising lead to develop antiviral medicines since it acts with regards to the
sequence speciﬁc inhibition of gene expression at post-transcriptional level.
1.1.1
Virology of Hepatitis C virus:
HCV is a small enveloped virus with a positive-sense, single-stranded RNA genome (approx 9600
RNA nucleotides) of the Flaviviridae family that encodes a large polyprotein of 3010 amino acids.
HCV RNA genome consists of a 5’ Non-translated region (NTR) of 341 nucleotides (nt) and is
an open reading frame (ORF) that encodes a polyprotein precursor of 3010 amino acids and a
3’NTR of about 27 nt. The 5’NTR consists of internal ribosome entry site (IRES) that mediates
cap independent translational initiation of the viral polyprotein [5]. The positive strand of HCV
RNA genome provides a template for the synthesis of negative stranded RNA intermediate by
the viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase, (NS5B). The newly formed negative RNA strand
acts as a template for the positive stranded HCV RNA genome that is packed in the newly
formed HCV virions. High degree of strain variation of RNA is due to high replication rate
of HCV RNA genome and lack of proof reading activity of RNA-dependent RNA polymerases.
The rate of mis-incorporation is approximately 10−3 base substitutions per genome site per year.
HCV infection is a highly dynamic process with the production of 1012 virions per day. Because
of this there are 7 genotypes (Which have 60-70% nucleotide similarity) along with multiple

HCV Proteins:
The viral polyprotein is co-transitionally and post-translationally processed by cellular and
virally encoded proteases to produce the 10 mature proteins: C,E1,E2, p7, NS1, NS2, NS3,
NS4A, NS4B, NS5A and NS5B. Out of which 4 are structural (C, E1, E2 and p7) and 6 are
non-structural (NS) proteins (NS1, NS2, NS3, NS4A, NS4B, NS5A and NS5B). Core protein
(C), Envelop proteins (E1 and E2) and small integral membrane protein (p7) in the structural
region are cleaved into individual proteins by host signal peptidases. The nonstructural proteins
are processed by NS2 and NS3/4A viral proteases which are involved in intracellular action of
the viral life cycle. Schematic representation of proteins encoded by HCV is shown in ﬁgure 1.

HCV core is a highly conserved basic protein which makes up the viral nucleocapsid. It consists

Non-Structural proteins, the NS3 acts as a serine-like protease (responsible for RNA helicase)
and also processes other domains of the non-structural proteins in combination with co-factor
NS4A. Studies suggest that NS4B plays an important role in development of hepatocellular
carcinoma. The RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (NS5B) are essential for viral maturation
and replication, Whereas NS5A is useful to evade the IFN-alpha treatment [Book]. It is also
reported that NS5A contributes to insulin resistance by interfering with the functional state of
AKT/PKB (Protein Kinase B) of insulin signaling pathway by signiﬁcantly upregulating the
critical phosphorylation at Ser473 of AKT. NS5A can be a therapeutically important target for
the prevention of chronic liver diseases [8].
1.1.3
Small molecule drugs for HCV:
Pegylated interferon alpha along with ribavirin is the standard-of care treatment for HCV in-
fection, wherein half of the people fail to show a sustained viral response which also depends
on the genotype, viral load and environmental factors. IFN free therapy such as direct acting
anti-virals (DAA) treatment which targets the HCV non-structural proteins have been used
to treat HCV infection. Where these DAA have proved to be eﬀective and well tolerated even
among patients with severe liver disease [9]. Boceprevir, telaprevir, simeprevir, asunaprevir, gra-
zoprevir and paritaprevir are NS3/4A inhibitors. Ombitasvir, ledipasvir, daclatasvir, elbasvir
and velpatasvir are NS5A inhibitors. Sofosbuvir and dasabuvir are NS5B inhibitors. NS3 is a
bifunctional molecule that exhibits serine protease activity that is essential and in combination
with cofactor NS4a processes other domains of the respective NS proteins. A combination of two
or more DAA is potentially the optimum treatment for HCV infection, however clinical concerns
like drug-drug interaction, resistant variance, lack of clinical pharmacological data, side eﬀects
like nausea and high health budget to the patients are the challenges to DAA therapy. The
combination of sofosbuvir with the NS5A inhibitor velpatasvir in a 12-week once a day all-oral
regimen achieved a 99% sustained virologic response (SVR) in patients with HCV genotype 1,
2, 4, 5, or 6 infection, including those who were previously treated or had compensated cirrhosis.
The SVR of patients infected with the diﬃcult-to-treat HCV genotype 3 was only slightly lower

1.1.4
Gene therapy for Hepatitis C:
Even though IFN-alpha along with ribavirin is used as a standard-of care treatment, only 40-
80% of the people able to clean the virus. Due to the gain in information on HCV molecular
biology and being able to get high sustained virological response gene therapy which treats the
disease in RNA level appears to be promising treatment for HCV infection. Antisense RNA
therapy and RNA interference gene therapy’s are considered in this research.
Antisense Oligonucleotide Therapy:
Antisense therapy uses antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) to target messenger RNA (mRNA).
ASOs are capable of altering mRNA expression through a variety of mechanisms, including
RNAse H mediated decay of the pre-mRNA, direct steric blockade against ribosome which in-
hibits translation of mRNA into proteins. miRNA-122 is the most abundant miRNA in liver
and plays an important role in cholesterol and fatty acid metabolism. During HCV infection
miRNA-122 binds to the 5’UTR of the HCV genome and regulates both HCV translation and
replication by unknown mechanisms. Targeting miRNA-122 with an antisense oligonucleotide
helps to regulate the HCV replication by disintegrating the miRNA-122 and antisense oligonu-
cleotide complex to nucleoside monophosphates with the help of RNAse H as shown in ﬁgure 2
[12]. The sequence used to silence the miRNA-122 is Miravirsen (SPC3649) as shown in table
3, which binds to the 5’ end of miRNA-122. As of 2017 miravirsen is in phase II clinical trials
against HCV infection [13].

RNA interference therapy (RNAi):
RNA interference is sequence speciﬁc, post translational gene slicing in both plants and animals
triggered by dsRNA. Andrew Fire and Craig C. Mello shared the 2006 Nobel Prize in Physiology
or Medicine for their work on RNA interference in the nematode worm Caenorhabditis elegans,
which they published in 1998 [14]. The RNAi pathway is found in many eukaryotes, including an-
imals and is initiated by the enzyme Dicer. Which cleaves long double-stranded RNA (dsRNA)
molecules into short double-stranded fragments of approximately 21 nucleotide siRNAs. Each
siRNA is unwound into two single-stranded RNAs (ssRNAs), the passenger strand and the
guide strand. The passenger strand is degraded and the guide strand is incorporated into the
RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC). The most well-studied outcome is post-transcriptional
gene silencing, which occurs when the guide strand pairs with a complementary sequence in a
messenger RNA molecule and induces cleavage by Argonaute 2 (Ago2), the catalytic component
of the RISC. Schematic representation of RNAi mechanism is shown in ﬁgure 3.
shRNA’s targeting CD81, NS5B and HCV IRES region are synthesized for RNAi therapy against
HCV infection, sequences are shown in table 3 [15] [16]. The NS5b shRNA reduced HCV repli-
cation by 97% and IRES shRNA by 82%. The Con (10%) and CD81 (20%) shRNAs showed a
slight reduction in HCV replication but it was not signiﬁcant compared to control [17].

1.1.5
Antibodies targeting HCV:
Due to High degree of strain variation and lack of proof reading activity of RNA dependent
RNA polymerase there is no single monoclonal antibody to cure HCV infection completely.
Even though there isn’t any antibody that can completely cure the infection, there are a few
antibodies reported against E1, E2 envelop proteins and also against CD81 receptor. In 43%
of the patients that had antibodies against hyper variable region (HVR) of E2 envelope protein
within the ﬁrst six months of infection, out of which 13% failed to clear the HCV infection.[18]
More recently, transfusion of human monoclonal antibody (MBL-HCV1) mapping to E2 amino
acids 412–423 protected a naive chimpanzee from HCV challenge and reduced viral RNA lev-
els in an acutely infected animal and also delayed the HCV RNA kinetics in patients infected
with HCV genotype 1a.[19][20] Since antibody against E2 glycoprotein looks promising, there
are reports that shows a role of anti-HVR1 antibodies in selecting viral variants to escape the
humoral response. [21] Neutralizing Antibodies targeting epitopes within the envelope glycopro-
tein E1 have been identiﬁed in some patients, but are generally rare.[22] Most of the neutralizing
antibodies block CD81 receptor interaction and cause the steric hindrance for the envelope pro-
tein (E2), physically shielding the viral interaction sites which prevents the replication of virus.
There are also reports for neutralizing antibodies against E2 glycoprotein.[23] AP33 is a murine
mAb binds to CD81 binding cite that recognises linear epitope of E2 412-423.[24]
Moreover claudin 1 (CLDN1), a tight junction component that is highly expressed in the liver,
acts late in the entry process, after virus binding and interaction with the co-receptor CD81.
However, several cell lines remain resistant to HCVpp infection, even upon the abnormal expres-
sion of CLDN1, which led to the assumption that CLDN1 functions as a co-receptor rather than
a primary receptor [25]. In addition to this research, CLDN1 is over expressed in HCV positive
cell lines and chronically inﬂamed liver tissue compared with normal liver cells. In contrast
CDLN4 hepatocellular staining was comparable in normal and diseased liver tissue. With this
above information CDLN1 is a suitable target for targeted drug delivery systems [26]. Occuludin
(OCLN), which is also a tight junction protein is a key factor for the late entry of HCV but few
studies shows that overexpressed miRNA-122 can downregulate OCLN [27].

Synthetic bioconjugates:
Bioconjugates are more powerful when compared to small molecule drugs because of their low
toxicity, high speciﬁcity and eﬃcacy towards the target. The potency of the toxins and small
molecules can be increased by conjugating to the biomolecules. Several bioorthogonal strate-
gies have been reported to synthesize bioconjugates. Oligonucleotides, peptides, carbohydrates,
lipids and antibodies are few biomolecules which can be conjugated and can be used as a thera-
peutics. Recent advances in the synthesis of oligonucleotides and peptides made relevantly easy
to synthesize these biomolecules in the lab. Solid base oligonucleotide synthesis and solid base
peptide synthesis are widely used strategies to synthesize these oligonucleotides and peptides.
Several bioconjugate drugs such as antibody drug conjugates (ADC), oligonucleotide conjugates,
peptide conjugates have been approved and some are in clinical trials [28] [29]. The main draw-
back for this bioconjugate synthesis is the poor yields of the ﬁnal conjugate, lack of solubility
and potential folding of the conjugate and puriﬁcation of the bioconjugate.
1.2.1
Automated solid base oligonucleotide synthesis:
Due to various applications of oligonucleotides, production of nucleic acids is required and has
high impact on the research. It’s synthesis has become less time consuming with the invention
of solid base synthesis for the large scale. Oligonucleotides are assembled step wise from 3’ end
to 5’ end in a automated machine using phosphoramidites (derivatives of natural or synthetic
nucleosides relatively short fragments of nucleic acid and their analogues) as monomers. 100%
coupling eﬃciency is assumed for each coupling because of the rapid kinetics of the phospho-
ramidites (1-2 min). 5’ DMT protected phosphoramidites are used and the solid base used for
this synthesis is typically controlled pore glass. The schematic representation of the automatic
oligonucleotide synthesis is shown in ﬁgure 5. Controlled pore glass (CPG) with ﬁrst base is
commercially available with no modiﬁers on CPG. General structure of CPG is shown in ﬁgure
4. Functional modiﬁers at 3’ end are also commercially available like alkyne modiﬁers, amine
modiﬁers, maleimide modiﬁers. In general the resin is a form of borosilicate glasses with a pore
size between 50 and 300nm.

Solid base peptide synthesis:
Peptide therapeutics have been in medical practice since the advent of insulin therapy since 1920s
[32]. Over 60 peptide drugs are available in the market and are approved by the FDA. Over
150 peptides have entered the clinical studies and are developing with a rapid rate. Peptides
are intrinsic signalling molecules for many physiological processes. Previously peptides were
puriﬁed from natural resources. Due to increase in demand for peptide therapeutics, peptide
synthesis is needed to be developed. In 1963 merriﬁeld invented the solid base peptide synthesis
which revolutionized the research in development of peptides [33]. Fmoc protected amino-acids
are frequently used in the solid base peptide synthesis because they are orthogonal protecting
groups and the working conditions are relatively safe compared to the peptide synthesis of boc
protected amino acids strategy. Uronium reagents like HATU are used as a coupling reagents
which have minimal recimisation, high yield and rapid coupling. Several linkers have been in
use, such as 2chlorotrityl chloride linker and rink amide linker which are highly used as shown
in ﬁgure 6. The solid base resin is based on polystyrene, polystyrene-PEG(polyethylene alcohol)
composites with a pore size of 75 µm - 149 µm (100-200 mesh).
Figure 6: Linkers for solid base peptide synthesis.
The ﬁrst step of peptide synthesis is the activation of solid support (in case of rink amide linker),
that is removal of Fmoc group. Fmoc group is highly base labile and can be easily removed
by the addition of piperidine. Second step is to pre-activate the ﬁrst amino acid with coupling
agents like HATU before adding to the solid support. After ﬁrst coupling is completed, Fmoc
group on the ﬁrst amino acid is removed and the pre-activated second amino acid is added.
The synthesis is continued until the sequence is completed. About 20-60 amino acids can be
synthesized on the solid support. Deprotection of peptide from solid support is done by 95%
TFA along with scavengers in water. Complete mechanism of peptide synthesis is shown in

Fluorescence chemical labelling:
Detecting biomolecules such as proteins, nucleic acids, antibodies often requires labelling with
a reporter. At present there are several labelling methods such as isotope markers, radioactive
markers, photoswitchable biomaterials, photochromic compounds, electrochemical sensors and
ﬂuorescent markers. Among these methods labelling with a ﬂuorophore has advantage due to its
non-destructive nature and high sensitivity which we can measure even in low concentrations.
Chemical labelling is covalent attachment of ﬂuorescent molecule to a biomolecule which can be
performed over a wide range of functionalities. The advantage of chemical labelling is their irre-
versibility compared to the non-covalent binding and are very potent, easy to handle and provide
maximum eﬃciency. A wide range of functionalities include free amine labelling, thiol labelling,
alcohol labelling, azide labelling, alkyne labelling and even more. Schematic representation of
few of the labelling reactions is shown in the following ﬁgure 12 [42].
Figure 12: Chemical labelling of functionalities. (A) NHS coupling to free
amines [43], (B) Maleimide coupling to free thiols [44], (C) Isothiocyanate
coupling with free alcohols [45], (D) CuAAC (Cu(I) catalyzed azide alkyne
click) click with Azide [46], (E) Sonogashira coupling to alkynes [47].
Each of these couplings can also be done vice versa by interchanging the functional groups.
Free amines can also be coupled with isothiocyanate, isocyanate, sulfonyl chloride and car-
bonate. Thiol groups can be conjugated with alkyl halides, aziridines and disulﬁde derivatives.
Fluorophores based on Oxygen heterocycles, sulfur heterocycles, Nitrogen heterocycles, Naptha-
lene ﬂuorophores, ﬂuoresceins, Rhodamines, BoDIPY ﬂuorophores, squaraines and cyanines are
widely used ﬂuorophores in labelling of respective target molecules [48] out of these ﬂuoresceins,
rhodamines, BODIPY, squaraines and cyanines has emission more than 500 nm [48].

Fluorescent nucleic acids can also be used to ﬁnd short tandem repeats (STR’s) in the genomic
DNA. STRs are also called as microsatellites, which consist of 2-7 bp distributed richly through-

repeats. The analysis of multiple STR loci provides suﬃcient data to guarantee speciﬁc identiﬁ-
cation on an individual level [51]. These STR’s are also used to determine the pathogenic short

1.4
Nano particle delivery of Nucleic acids:
Even though therapeutic eﬃciency of biomolecular conjugates are far potent compared to small
molecule drugs formulating these conjugates is a major issue because of the large size and high
polarity of these conjugates. Formulating these bioconjugates in nano particles is an eﬃcient
way to increase the bio-availability of bioconjugates. Nanoparticles formulated with biodegrad-
able polymers such as poly (lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) are extensively researched because of
its controlled release characteristics, low toxicity and bio-compatibility [53]. PLGA nanoparti-
cles have already been approved as pharmaceutical safe materials for clinical and cosmetic use
[54]. Nucleic acids are negatively charged particles, one can load these nucleic acids in PLGA
nano particles by surface modifying the PLGA nanoparticles with chitosan which makes the
total charge on chitosan modiﬁed nanoparticles positive. Chitosan is biodegradable and bio-
compatible and had also been approved for therapeutic purposes [55]. These chitosan modiﬁed
PLGA nanoparticles were synthesized by emulsion-diﬀusion-evaporation technique as shown in
ﬁgure 14 [56].
Figure 14: Chitosan modiﬁed PLGA preparation process [56]
CLDN1 antibody is conjugated to chitosan modiﬁed PLGA nanoparticle with the help of
Maleimide-PEG(2)-NHS ester linker. NHS ester in Maleimide-PEG(2)-NHS ester is conjugated
to amine groups in chitosan and Maleimide in the conjugate is used to conjugate against anti-
body using michael addition of thiol in antibody to the maleimide from the conjugate. Schematic
representation of the ﬁnal Conjugate is shown in ﬁgure 15. The number of antibodies on one
nanoparticle depends on the size of the nanoparticle and also the size of the antibody that con-

doing dense packing around the nanoparticle.

INTRODUCTION
1.5
Problem, Objectives and rationale:
Pegylated Interferon α with ribavirin was used as a standard of care treatment for HCV infection
for all genotypes. But only half of the patients treated with the above drugs showed good SVR
response. Direct acting antivirals (DAA) has shown to be a promising drug for the treatment of
HCV infection. Current treatment for HCV infection approved by FDA is combination of Sofos-
buvir (NS5B inhibitor) and Velpatasvir (NS5A inhibitor) in all genotypes except genotype 3. In
genotype 3 in addition to Sofosbuvir and velpatasvir, ribavirin is suggested for the therapeutic
purpose. Even though DAA able to clear virus, long term eﬀects of these DAA and drug-drug
interactions of DAA are still unknown.
Few nucleic acid therapeutics have been synthesized by automated synthesis. The primary ob-
jective of this work is to develop the antibody conjugated oligonucleotides with an enzymatically
cleavable linker and to synthesize the PLGA nanoparticles for the delivery of the nucleic acids
with and without the help of chitosan. Oligonucleotide is synthesized on the alkyne support with
2’ methyl protecting groups on both ends one on 5’ end and two on 3’ end to ensure the stability
of the RNA. Enzymatically cleavable peptide linker is designed with azide on the c-terminal
and maleimide on the N-terminal of the peptide to conjugate with both oligonucleotides using
CuAAC and with antibody using maleimide coupling. Antibody used in this work is claudin
1 (CLDN1) which is reported to be highly expressed in HCV infected cells and chronically in-
ﬂamed liver cells. CLDN1 is a key factor in the late entry of HCV.
Fluorescence chemical labelling plays an important role to quantify the biomolecules. Fluo-
rescent oligonucleotides are used in various applications such as detection of genomic nucleic
acids, disease associated nucleic acids and also in forensic purposes like quantiﬁcation of short
tandem repeats(STR’s). Here the ﬂuorophore is attached directly to the nucleic bases. Olio-
gonucleotides synthesized with more than one of the ﬂuorophore conjugated phosphoramidites
have high ﬂuorescence properties for a long time compared to ﬂuorescent probes which have just
one ﬂuorophore on it.

more susceptible to RNA hydrolysis compared to 5’ end, hence methoxy groups were introduced

3
CONCLUSION AND FUTURE PROSPECTS
3
Conclusion and Future prospects
Protease inhibiting DAA (direct acting antivirals) are now utilized for the treatment of HCV
infection. Drug-drug interactions, resisting variants, cost and long term toxicity eﬀects are ma-
jor concerns for DAA therapy.
Therapeutic oligonucleotides are known for their functional,
structural and signaling roles, since they treat the disease at genomic level where there is a
less chance for recurrence of disease. Antibodies known for their exceptional targeting abilities
conjugated to ON (oligonucleotide) drugs are ideal drugs for targeting diseases. A design to
synthesize CLDN1 Ab (claudin 1 antibody) conjugated to therapeutic shRNA’s using a enzy-
matically cleavable peptide linker to treat HCV infection was proposed. Therapeutic shRNAs’s
and cleavable peptide linker were synthesized and conjugated successfully with a full conversion
using CuAAC.
Fluorescence labelling of nucleic acids plays a critical role to detect genomic or disease associ-
ated nucleic acids. Plan to design ﬂuorescence phosphoramidites was done and synthesized upto
the ﬂuorophore deoxy-nucleosides using sonogashira coupling. Perylene is used as a ﬂuorophore
(quantum yield = 0.94) to attach directly on the nucleobases. They can also be used to design
oligo sequences with more than one ﬂuorophores in a sequence that targets a particular STR
(short tandem repeats) leads to detect the corresponding STR’s in low concentration and can
be used for better quantiﬁcation.
Antibody conjugated PLGA nanoparticles were discussed to deliver the therapeutic shRNA’s
to the target cells. shRNA delivery can be done by modifying the surface with chitosan or
introducing PEI (polyethyleneimine) or PLL (poly-L-Lysine) along with shRNA to make the
charge neutral. shRNA-peptide linker can be conjugated to either antibody (as discussed) or to
any targeting peptide since it has a free amine functional group present on it.

Peptide synthesis:
133mg of Rink amide resin was weighted in a standard SPE ﬁltration columns with an assumed
loading of 0.75mmol/g. First the resin was allowed to swell in 1mL of DMF for 2 min and to
remove the Fmoc group on the rink linker 1mL of 20% pipperidine in DMF was added and
allowed to stay in SPE column for 30 min and then ﬁltered. Another 1mL of 20% pipperidine
in DMF was added and allowed to deprotect for another 30 min. After each step washing stage
is necessary. Washing is done usually by adding 2mL DMF, 2mL DCM and 2mL DMF (three
times each for 2 min). At the same time fmoc protected amino acid (4 eq) was preactivated using
HATU (3.9 eq) and DIPEA (6 eq) in 1mL DMF. After washing preactivated ﬁrst amino acid
was added to the resin and allowed to react for 2h with a manual stirring for every 15 min. First
amino acid was coupled twice just to make sure the complete reaction with the linker. Washing
with DMF, DCM and DMF after each reaction was done. After coupling of ﬁrst amino acid,
washing was done. Fmoc group is cleaved from ﬁrst amino acid using 1mL 20% pipperidine
in DMF twice (2min + 18 min) and washing after deprotection. Preactivated second amino
acid is added to the resin and allowed to react for 2h with a manual stirring for every 15 min.
Cleavage of peptide from the resin is performed by adding 1mL of 95% TFA (triﬂuoroacetic
acid) in milli-Q water three times (60 min each) to obtain deprotected peptides. The combined
extracts were reduced under nitrogen ﬂow overnight and freeze dried to yield white powder. The
schematic representation of peptide synthesis is shown in ﬁgure 37.
