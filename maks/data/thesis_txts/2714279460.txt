Abstract
Each year, enteric pathogens cause considerable death and illness around the globe.
These pathogens include, but are far from limited to the Gram-negative enteropathogenic
and enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli and Yersinia species. Common for many of these
pathogens is the use of adhesins like intimin (E. coli) and invasin (Y. eneterocolitica) to
adhere to and infect their host. These adhesins belong to the family of inverse auto-
transporters. Recent studies have suggested that the extracellular passenger of inverse
autotransporters contain a conserved juncture domain so far only characterised in in-
timin (IntD00) which rigidifies the base of the passenger. This study looks to investigate
the putative IntD00-like passenger juncture domains from the Y. enterocolitica adhesin
invasin and from the E. coli adhesins YeeJ and FdeC by characterising their double do-
main structures, InvD0D1, YeeJD0D1 and FdeCD0D1 respectively. These proteins were
expressed and purified using immobilised metal affinity chromatograpy (IMAC) and size
exclusion chromatography (SEC). Through x-ray crystallography and small angle x-ray
scattering (SAXS), protein structure models were acquired for InvD0D1 and YeeJD0D1.
These models showed elongated structures rigidified by different mechanisms, where a
potentially conserved stabilising motif was identified. This work contributes to recent stud-
ies into potentially conserved passenger juncture domains in inverse autotransporters.
Resumé
Hvert år forårsager enteriske patogener betydelig død og sygdom rundt om i verden.

Introduction
1.1
Enteric infections
Enteropathogenic Escherichia coli (EPEC) are pathogenic Gram-negative bacteria coloni-
sing the gastrointestinal tract and are known to cause severe diarrhoea in infants, some-
times leading to death. It is especially prevalent in low-income countries and is considered
one of the main causes of persistent diarrhoea world wide [1]. The food borne enterohe-
morrhagic Escherichia coli (EHEC) is another pathogen causing enteric infections. While
this type does not cause as many yearly deaths, it is more prevalent in developed coun-
tries with more than 63,000 cases leading to over 2100 hospitalisations and deaths in the
United States alone [2]. Latest, in 2011, an outbreak of EHEC appeared in Germany with
almost 4000 cases whereof over 800 lead to hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS), causing
severe kidney damage and concluding with over 50 deaths [3, 4]. It is therefore adamant
that enteric pathogenic bacteria are a significant health issue globally.
Common for enteric pathogens, is the necessity of adhesins to attach to the epithelial
cells of the intestines. In EPEC and EHEC, the most well known adhesin is intimin and
is a key factor in creating attaching and effacement (A/E) lesions by EHEC and EPEC
[5]. Intimin is encoded by the eaeA gene located on the Locus of Enterocyte Effacement
(LEE) pathogenecity island [6, 7]. Some species harbouring this virulence factor includes
aforementioned E. coli pathovars, Citrobacter rodentium and Hafnia alvei [5].
Other bacterial pathogens causing enteric infections include the Gram-negative Yersinia
genus, where 3 out of 17 species are known to be pathogenic to humans and animals.
These are Y. pestis, Y. enterocolitica and Y. pseudotubercolosis. The two latter harbour
the intimin-like adhesin invasin, which is responsible for the first attachment of the bac-
terium to its host, leading to the initiation of internalisation finally causing yersiniolosis
(Y. enterocolitica) or the more rare Far East scarlet-like fever (Y. pseudotubercolosis).
Invasin attaches to the surface receptor β1 integrin on M cells. This triggers clustering
of integrins which further initiates actin remodelling in the host cell leading to engulfing
and internalisation by the ”zipper” invasion mechanism, as illustrated simply in figure 1.1
[8–10]. While Yersinia as EPEC and EHEC uses adhesins to attach to its host in the
gastrointestinal tract, they are, unlike EPEC and EHEC, internalised into epithelial cells
where they can disseminate and cause local and systemic infection [11].

1.2
The inverse autotransporter family
Invasin, intimin and other E. coli adhesins like YeeJ and FdeC belong to the type V secre-
tion system from Gram-negative bacteria, also called autotransporters. These adhesins
have a similar topology consisting of a β-barrel integrated in the outer membrane forming
a hydrophilic pore. Extending hereof comes multiple bacterial immunoglobulin-like (Big)
domains existing as an extracellular chain anchored in the β-barrel. As an adhesin, the
passenger of intimin is capped by a lectin-like domain, facilitating binding to its target Tir
[5, 12]. In the same way, invasin has an integrin-binding domain which facilitates binding
to the β1-integrin on the cell surface of M cells [13]. The extracellular part is also called
the passenger and will be referred to as such henceforth. This combined structure is rem-
iniscent of the classic autotransporters (AT), also termed the type Va secretion system.
Contrary to type Va ATs, however, intimin, invasin, YeeJ and FdeC have a reversed topol-
ogy, where the passenger is C-terminal instead of N-terminal. Due to this fact, Leo et al.
[5] proposed in 2015 a new type of ATs termed inverse autotransporters (IAT) or type Ve
secretion system. Intimin is thus the prototype of this type of autotransporters.

Autotransporters are Gram-negative specific trans-membrane proteins anchored in the
outer membrane. Characteristic of ATs is the secretion process of the passenger domain
being automatic and does not require any obvious external energy source. Several con-
tributing factors for secretion are known, but the source of energy is still undetermined
[12]. Secretion of autotransporters generally follow three steps. First, the whole protein
crosses the inner membrane through the sec secretion system. Following, the β-barrel in-
serts itself into the outer membrane. Finally, through mechanisms still under discussion,
the β-barrel acts as a translocation unit and exports the passenger through the outer
membrane [14]. It has been suggested that it happens through a hairpin-like interme-
diate, where the passenger is exported in an N to C direction (in IATs) using sequential
folding of the Big domains as the driving force [15].
1.3
Rigidity of the passenger juncture domain
Even though ATs are seperated into several distinct groups, these are based more on
topology than function. Therefore, ATs in different groups can exert similar functions while
ATs within the same group can have different functions and one specific AT can even have
several functions [12]. This means that the cap of the passenger, which in adhesins func-
tion as attachment to the host, in other ATs might work in different manners. Nevertheless,
what they all have in common is a potential benefit of the passenger having a large reach
radius to easier find its specific target.

In 2020, Weikum et al. [16] described and characterised the passenger juncture domain
of intimin, connecting the passenger to the β-barrel and thus the membrane. Relating to
the multiple Big domains of the passenger, the juncture domain in intimin is called D00,
while the rest are called D0, D1 and so forth with D0 linking to D00. From their research,
they found that the D00-D0 coupling as well as the D2-D3 exhibits high rigidity while D0-
D1-D2 are more flexible. A passenger that exhibits both rigidity and flexibility allows for a
structure which is rigid enough to achieve a larger reach radius while having the flexibility
to get the adhesin domain in the right orientation for substrate binding. The result is a
potential accelerated binding process. A simulation of a rigid versus a flexible passenger
juncture domain, indeed showed increased reach radius with a rigid juncture domain, as
seen in figure 1.3 from Weikum et al. The rigidity between the D00 and D0 domains of
intimin were found to come from the connector region between the domains, which is
stabilised by a type 1 turn aided by a hydrogen bond network [16].
1.4
Juncture domain in IAT homologues
Until recently, little was known about the structure of the approximately 100 first amino
acids of the passenger of predicted IATs. But homology modelling using HHPred [17] has
predicted that several IATs contain a Big domain at the N-terminus of the passenger [15].
On this background, Weikum et al. (2020) performed an alignment of D00 to these puta-
tive D00-like domains and found that many of them contained several conserved residues
which in D00 are important factors in the rigidity seen between the D00-D0 double domain.
It was then postulated that these residues exhibit similar structural interactions in puta-
tive D00-like domains. Additionally, from these findings they speculated that D00-like

2
Project aim
In this thesis, the goal is to investigate the putative intimin D00-D0 homologues in different
inverse autotransporters, as they have been thought to exhibit the same passenger junc-
ture rigidity as intimin D00-D0. Specifically the double domains of Yersinia enterocolitica
invasin D0-D1, E. coli YeeJ D0-D1 and E. coli FdeC D0-D1. First step is to express and
purify the proteins through protein purification methods. Next is to crystallise the proteins
and obtain protein structure models that can showcase whether they exhibit the same
rigidity. These models will be investigated in regard to whether that rigidity stems from
the same amino acid residue interactions as intimin D00-D0, or if there are some other
motifs carrying out this function. Finally, SAXS data from the purified proteins is to be
analysed in an effort to acquire supportive evidence of this rigidity.

Theoretical background
3.1
Chromatography
One of the most important tools in protein purification, is chromatography. It is the tech-
nique of separating, purifying, analysing and/or identifying molecules depending on dif-
ferent physical characteristics like size, shape, charge, binding sites or presence of hy-
drophobic surface groups. For liquid systems, the most used chromatography technique
is High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC). Other forms of chromatography in-
clude Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) and Gas Chromatography (GC).
In HPLC a pump is used to transport a liquid mixture of compounds (the mobile phase)
through a column (stationary phase) where separation takes place. See figure 3.1 for
a general overview of HPLC. The separation happens when the mobile phase passes
through the stationary phase which separates the compounds of the mobile phase de-
pending on the characteristics of the stationary phase. For some cases the mobile phase
is kept constant through the separation. However, it is also possible to adjust the con-
tent of the mobile phase and have the mixed compounds bound to the stationary phase.
For example, having a hydrophobic stationary phase bound by compounds with vary-
ing hydrophobicity, then increasing the hydrophobicity of the mobile phase, will allow the
compounds to elute with more hydrophilic compounds eluting first [21].
Chromatography will often be linked with an analysis part. After separation, the liquid will
pass through a UV detector measuring at 280 nm. Tyrosine and tryptophan absorb light
highly at this frequency, and since these amino acids are frequently present in proteins,
280 nm is usually used to measure proteins in a sample. For converting the specific
absorption to protein concentration, see methods section 4.2.2. The following will focus
on relevant chromatography methods used in this work.

3.1.1
Affinity chromatography
Affinity chromatography (AC) utilises the binding capacity of the compounds for separa-
tion. In the simple version, the stationary phase will contain an immobilised molecule
which can bind the compound of interest from the liquid mixture. Examples of pairs are
enzymes and their substrate, receptor and ligand and antibody and antigen. After the
mobile phase has run through the stationary phase, the compound of interest should now
be bound to your column without much contaminants. To release the compound again, a
new mobile phase should be run through which either has a higher affinity to the stationary
phase, or reduces the binding capacity of the column. If a higher affinity mobile phase is
run through, it will then compete for the binding sites. Either way, the compound of interest
releases in a relative pure solution. AC is a very powerful and diverse chromatography
method which can be adjusted to specific needs, allowing the scientist to tailor separa-
tion to very specific conditions producing quick, easy and high quality results. As a first
step purification, AC can easily dispose of the majority of unwanted proteins readying the
sample for final polishing steps [21, 22].

3.1.2
Size exclusion chromatography
Size exclusion chromatography (SEC), sometimes referred to as gel filtration chromatog-
raphy, is a separation technique used to separate different sized molecules. More ac-
curately, it separates based on the hydrodynamic radius values of the molecules, also
known as Stokes radius (Rs) [22]. This will be further elaborated on below.
The general concept of SEC revolves around a matrix with porous beads that makes
up the stationary phase and is illustrated in figure 3.3. When the liquid mobile phase
is run through the matrix, molecules small enough to fit in the pores will have a longer
path through the column resulting in a longer retention time. The smaller the molecule,
the longer the path. Exactly how much slower small molecules will be, will depend on
the properties of the matrix. All molecules bigger than any of the pores, will run straight
through and elute at the same time as the front of the mobile phase, i.e. after the void
volume has gone through the column. Pore size is then extremely important to consider
when choosing a SEC column for purification. At a glance, separation will be proportional
to the molecular weight (MW) of a protein since this is closely related to the size, which
we now know is the main contributor to retention time in SEC. Proteins of similar MW,
however, can have very different shapes which contributes to their size. Globular protein
of the same MW will generally have similar retention times, but more fibrous proteins
will appear larger through a lower retention time. This is related to the Rs of the protein
mentioned earlier. Rs is the radius of a sphere with the same hydrodynamic properties as
the molecule of interest. Fibrous proteins will have a greater Rs than a globular protein
with similar MW due to the extended conformation, making it harder for the protein to enter
the pores of the column matrix [22, 24].
Ionic charge of the protein can also affect the retention time. While a neutral protein will
be unaffected flowing through the column in water, a protein with a slight charge could
have non-specific protein-matrix or protein-protein interactions through electrostatic or
Wan der Waals interactions. Therefore, it is common to add some salt to negate these
interactions [25]. In some instances, this weak ionic effect can even be used to make ion
exchange chromatography (IEC)-SEC hybrids [26]. Another thing to take into account,
is sample concentration. You can only run a limited volume of sample through to get
proper separation, therefore concentration of the sample should be high. Nevertheless,
sample concentration is still not recommended to be much higher than 20 mg/mL, or the
viscosity gets too high and separation resolution suffers due to the sample not being able
to flow freely. Additionally, high viscosity will cause high pressure in the column and can

3.2
X-ray crystallography
The first and most well known method of molecule structure determination is x-ray crys-
tallography. It has been in use for over 100 years and is accredited for being used in
solving structures from NaCl to various antibiotics as well as DNA [27]. The Protein Data
Bank (PDB) has served as a repository system to deposit and view protein structures
since 1971 where it was founded [28] and is now a worldwide system containing almost
200,000 molecular structures as of this writing. Of these, almost 170,000 are from x-ray
crystallography, around 14,000 from nuclear magnetic ressonance (NMR) and 12,000
from electron microscopy (EM). In the 90’s NMR became increasingly popular and in the
CHAPTER 3. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
13
==PAGE:22==
last 20 years EM have gained popularity as well. Still, x-ray crystallography remains the
most common method used when characterising protein structures [29].
X-ray crystallography is in short terms shooting a beam of x-rays on a crystalline structure
of a molecule and measuring the diffracted rays. Since the crystal is structured in ordered
patterns, the pattern of diffracted rays can through Fourier transformation, indexing and
repeated data refinement be converted into an electron density map on which a molecular
model can be build. This will be further elaborated on.
3.2.1
Crystallisation
Protein crystals are formed when protein in an aqueous solution start precipitating in a
slow and ordered fashion. If it happens too fast, the protein will just aggregate in an
amorphous precipitate. Controlling precipitation can be aided by adding a precipitant,
such as polyethylene glycol (PEG) or a simple salt such as sodium chloride. Depending
on the protein and precipitate concentration, the liquid can be in one of several states as
presented in the phase diagram in figure 3.4. If the liquid reaches supersaturation, pre-
cipitation might not happen right away, as the nucleation event requires a certain amount
of energy, similar in a sense to that of enzyme activation energy. As the liquid moves
diagonally upwards towards supersaturation (Fig 3.4), the nucleation event becomes in-
creasingly likely to happen. As soon as a crystal nucleus has been formed, dissolved
protein can start to precipitate around the crystal, causing it to grow. Then, any crystals
formed will grow until the dissolved protein concentration passes the solubility limit again.
The principle of increasing precipitate concentration to cause protein precipitation is called
salting out [30, 31].
One way to achieve this is through vapour diffusion. In this technique, a small droplet
of protein solution is mixed with a reservoir solution to create the mother liquor. The
reservoir should have a precipitant concentration around the target concentration wanted
for the protein solution to achieve supersaturation. When mixing the two liquids, the ini-
tial precipitant concentration in the mother liquor is then lower than that of the reservoir.
Mother liquor together with the reservoir is then placed in a sealed well. Due to the higher
concentration of precipitate in the reservoir than the mother liquor, water vapour will slowly
diffuse from the mother liquor to the reservoir to balance the concentration, until the two
concentrations are equal. The reservoir is large compared to the mother liquor, so that it
can be assumed the precipitate concentration of the reservoir will not change significantly.
As the water leaves the mother liquor with protein solution, concentrations of both protein
and precipitate increase slowly. This will cause the solution to move towards the satu-
rated and supersaturated phases (Fig 3.4). When reaching supersaturation, nucleation
can occur. As soon as this happens and crystal grow commences, dissolved protein con-
centration will start to decrease, moving the solution towards undersaturation once again.
If the solution is too long in the nucleation zone, however, many crystals can be formed
which in turn depletes the dissolved protein supply faster, not allowing the crystal to grow

3.2.2
X-ray diffraction
To understand how a protein crystal can give reflections that can help interpret the molec-
ular structure, we first need to briefly go over some of the crystal features. Crystals consist
of stacked unit cells which each contain one or several proteins. Unit cells are the smallest
part of the crystal which if stacked will make up the entire crystal. Unit cells further consist
of asymmetric units, which is the smallest motif that, if rotated through symmetry opera-
tions, can make up the entire unit cells. Asymmetric units contain one or more copies of
the same protein. The way asymmetric units are placed in the unit cell is described by its
space group. This describes which kind of symmetry operations (i.e. translation, rotation,
mirroring) can be performed on the unit cell without changing its appearance and is thus
related to the placement and orientation of the asymmetric unit. Finally, the repeating
pattern of the unit cell is the crystal lattice. Repeating patterns throughout the lattice exist
in planes and are thus called lattice planes. These planes are the source of diffraction
[30, 34].

electron density in specific areas of the structure. These reflections can be Fourier trans-
formed into sinusoidal waves and combined to achieve an electron density map (Figure

3.2.3
Model building
Acquiring electron density from diffraction data requires three things. Wave function am-
plitude, frequency and phase. The first two things are easy to get from the intensity and
location of the reflection (Figure 3.6). But the phase is not measureable and needs to be
acquired in another way. The most efficient way to acquire phase information is to use
an already existing protein model for a protein you believe looks similar to the one under
investigation, also called molecular replacement. By calculating the theoretical phases
produced from the existing model, those phases can thus be used to create an initial
rough electron density estimate. Having an estimation of the electron density allows the
crystallographer to insert amino acid chains to fit the density while complying with the
rules of protein structure (i.e. side chain rotamers, backbone angles, bond length etc.).
In the beginning, the model might not be completely accurate, but from the new model
build, new phases can be calculated and a new electron density map can be made. As-

3.3
Small angle x-ray scattering
Same as with x-ray crystallography, SAXS uses the reflection of x-rays to determine pro-
tein structure characteristics. SAXS, however, can be used on samples in solution instead
of needing crystals. X-rays are shot at the sample and the scattering of x-rays at very
small angles are measured. Depending on the size and shape of the protein, the scat-
tering pattern will differ. Larger structures cause a larger angle of scattering. Together
CHAPTER 3. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
17
==PAGE:26==
with the reflection intensities, the scattering pattern can be used to extrapolate low res-
olution information like folding state, molecular weight and shape of proteins and other
macromolecular structures [37].
A benefit of SAXS is that the experiment is easy to perform, requiring only little prepara-
tion beforehand. The protein only needs to be in solution, where different concentrations
usually will be used to determine whether the results are concentration dependant. Hav-
ing the protein in solution instead of a crystal, as compared to x-ray crystallography, also
allows the protein to adopt a conformation which potentially better resembles the natural
occurring state. Purity must be very high, however, so to not have any other contribu-
tors to x-ray scattering. Furthermore, the measured intensities must be zeroed based on
pure buffer scattering. Results from SAXS can, as mentioned above, be used to acquire a
wide variety of information regarding molecular structure. Structure models from acquired
through SAXS will only be very low resolution, however, and needs other experimental
evidence to support it. For this reason, SAXS is a great tool to use in combination with
x-ray crystallography as they complement each other.

Spectrophotometry

ments could then be used to calculate protein concentration using Lambert-Beers law.
This states that A = ϵlc, where A is absorbance at specified frequency, ϵ is the extinction
coefficient, l is the length the light travels through the sample and c is the protein concen-
tration. The extinction coefficient can be calculated from the amino acid sequences, l in
our case is 1 and when A is measured, there is only c left. Therefore the concentration,
c, can be calculated by rearranging the formula to: c = A/ϵ. This does assume that the
only contribution to absorbance comes from the protein of interest. As the samples rarely
are 100% pure, this is only an estimate.

Discussion
6.1
Purification
Transformation and cell growth went successfully with plenty of cell mass to be harvested.
Cell lysis and purification later revealed enough protein had been expressed to obtain
highly concentrated protein samples. Even though the growth of FdeCD0D1 transfor-
mants yielded vastly lower cell mass than other transformants, it was deemed sufficient
to purify enough protein without doing another round of cell growth and protein expression.
The first IMAC run, with InvD0D1, was done with a linear elution buffer gradient. While this
gave one large peak for the protein, smaller contaminants were included in this peak. The
results were deemed good enough to continue with, as SEC should be able to exclude
those impurities. It did, however, prompt the next IMAC, for YeeJD0D1, to be done as
a step gradient, so as to control the elution better. Fractions collected here did appear
more pure, but the step gradient caused the protein to elute in two peaks. Thus the eluate
was contained in a larger volume requiring more time spent on upconcentration and a
potential of protein loss. For the second YeeJD0D1 IMAC, the step gradient was adjusted
to attempt getting everything out at once. This, however, resulted in more impurities in the
final peak. All was collected nevertheless, as these impurities were deemed separable by
SEC later. Finally, FdeCD0D1 was also done in a small step gradient giving approximately
the same outcome with two peaks and only few impurities. As the affinity of the His-
tagged proteins to the nickel column might vary depending on e.g. availability of the
nickel, and other proteins might have some intrinsic affinity to the nickel due to surface
exposed histidines, completely clean separation might not be possible. It seems a balance
between a proper separation and not having too large an eluate volume. In the future,
repeating IMAC experiments can help determine optimal elution buffer concentrations.
Findings here deem step gradient elution potentially superior to a linear gradient.
Pools from IMAC were cleaved with a TEV protease during dialysis with stirring in contrary
to a stationary falcon tube as recommended by Clausen (2021) [46]. First round was done
overnight, but as it was hard to be sure the TEV protease had cleaved, it was later done
over weekend with one buffer change throughout.
The following rIMAC did, as mentioned, seem to still bind all different protein double do-
mains, as the majority did not elute before a high imidazole buffer was run through the
column. This might be due to insufficient TEV cleavage, however, increasing the cleav-
age and dialysis inoculation time threefold (one day to three) did not seem to improve the
results. It was also only done once for each double domain, and thus no proper compari-
son can be made. Another possibility is that the TEV protease had lost some or all activity
during storage. As the size difference of the protein double domains before and after TEV
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
37
==PAGE:46==
cleavage only was a couple of thousand kDa, it is hard to judge from SDS-PAGE whether
cleavage had taken place. Therefore, it can also be that 10 mM imidazole is not enough to
block the intrinsic binding capabilities of the double domains. One test for a future study
would be to include a TEV protease activity assay to confirm its functionality. Another
would be to run the supposedly cleaved protein sample through the column with differ-
ent imidazole concentrations and compare the binding affinity to tagged proteins. Either
way, as no activity was needed from the double domain and there is no reason to think
the His-tag should prevent crystallisation, we moved forward with the acquired sample.
Later crystallisation and modelling also showed that successful crystallography could be
achieved with the rIMAC results.
Size exclusion chromatography went very well, with high levels of protein eluting around
the expected elution volume. According to the manual for the Superdex 75 Increase
10/300 GL size exclusion column, proteins of approximately 22 kDa should typically elute
closer to 12 mL compared to the 11 mL we observed. This can be explained by the
double domain having a rigid extended conformation increasing its hydrodynamic radius
and making it elute faster than expected for its size. A proper standard curve was not

A stabilising triad
X-ray crystallography gave rise to protein structure models with high enough resolution
allowing us to investigate the rigidification mechanism. The models are only early ver-
sions still requiring additional refining to give completely reliable information about spe-
cific residue interactions. However, the model quality is at this point good enough to give
a rough estimate. As shown in figure 5.6 and presented in results section 5.4.1, InvD0D1
and YeeJD0D1 adopt connector stabilisation strategies utilising π- and hydrophobic inter-
actions.

These stabilisation methods have slightly different approaches, where one relies heavily

could have another unidentified stabilisation mechanism.

Findings here indicate that the invasin and YeeJ passenger juncture domains indeed ex-
hibit rigidity, however using alternative methods to intimin. Both proteins seems to utilise a
stabilising triad of aromatic amino acids, interacting through π-π T-stacking and π-cation

ive stabilising mechanism. Conclusively, this stabilising triad could be a possible con-
served motif found in other IATs. It would be further interesting with a structural analysis
of FdeCD0D1 and other IATs to determine whether they exhibit a stabilising mechanism
like intimin, invasin or YeeJ or if they have a yet undescribed mechanism.

6.4
SAXS supports elongated juncture domain models
Data analysis of SAXS agrees with the conclusions from x-ray crystallography regard-
ing the general protein conformation. Two factors tells us this. The PDDFs calculated
(Figure 5.7a, 5.7b) show an extended right-sided tail typically associated with elongated
structures. Where the graphs peak is the distance which normally would suggest the ra-
dius of a globular protein, as this would be the most common distance. The fact the peak
is not symmetrical but have higher distance probabilities for longer distances means that
the structure have many longer distances that of the ”globular radius”, thus suggesting an
elongated structure.
Furthermore, the PDDFs were used to calculate electron density maps which further con-
firms this more visually clear. Superimposing this map on crystal structure models (Figure
5.7e, 5.7f) allows us to see how the models from both x-ray crystallography and SAXS
tells the same story of an elongated structure. Here, it also becomes more visible where
the two peaks on the InvD0D1 PDDF come from. Near the connector region, a loop ex-
tends out from the structure making an additional ball of electron density adding to the
pairwise electron distances from each single domain to this ball.
Electron density maps were calculated from the 3.75 mg/mL measurements as a middle
way between 2.5 and 5 mg/mL. All functions were normalised to concentration however,
and the overlaps from the graphs indicate that there should not be any difference in which
concentration was used. SAXS thus gives strong supportive evidence that the double
domains do in affect exhibit elongated conformation in solution.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, the structures of two passenger juncture domains were investigated. Pre-
viously, only the structure of the juncture domain from intimin had been reported. Thus
the aim of this work was to continue the research on passenger juncture domains of IATs.
Through transformation, expression and chromatography work, it was shown that the pas-
senger juncture double domains from the Y. enterocolitica adhesin invasin and from the
E. coli adhesins YeeJ and FdeC could be purified and upconcentrated. Protein crystals
were successfully created from InvD0D1 and YeeJD0D1 from where x-ray diffraction gave
rise to high resolution crystal structure models. These models confirmed the hypothesis of
passenger juncture double domains being elongated, potentially to aid the radius of reach
of the active passenger domain. Furthermore, from the crystal structures, new potential
amino acid motifs aiding in the double domain rigidity were identified as stabilising triads
of aromatic amino acids, including a proline from the connector region. Finally, SAXS
experiments gave supportive evidence of this elongated structure in solution.
In conclusion, this work has shown that the rigidity of the passenger juncture domain of
bacterial IATs is not unique to intimin and might very well be a common structural Big
domain. Additionally it has been suggested that the mechanism of rigidity varies between
IATs and many different motifs might be found. Future work should continue the work on
FdeCD0D1 and other IATs to confirm the elongation as well as look for new, or known,
rigidifying motifs.
