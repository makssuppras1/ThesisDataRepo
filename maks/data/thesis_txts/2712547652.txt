Preface 
The aim of this thesis was to discover if PAA exposure at concentrations relevant for 
aquaculture disinfection could induce acute physiological and behavioral stress 
responses in Atlantic salmon. This was explored through trials on oxygen consumption, 
plasma cortisol and behavior.  
Experiments were conducted at DTU Aqua in Hirtshals, Denmark. 
The thesis consists of two parts: 
I. Introduction to Disinfectants in Aquaculture: Relevant background information 
II. Manuscript: Behavioral and physiological responses of Atlantic salmon  
to a commercial disinfectant in aquaculture

PART I  
Introductory review 
1. Introduction to Disinfectants in Aquaculture 
1.1 Recirculating Aquaculture Systems and Pathogens 
Inland aquaculture produced 51.3 million tons of aquatic animals in 2018 and makes up 
an increasing proportion of the world’s farmed food fish production (FAO, 2020). 
Aquaculture production of Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) has played a major role in this 
development and is projected to continue to grow (FAO, 2020). However, the salmon 
aquaculture industry is increasing the use of alternative production systems due to lack 
of suitable areas available to expand sea-cage production and the biological constraints 
of pathogens (Mota et al., 2022).  
Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) offer the possibility of maintaining stable 
environmental conditions and securing animal welfare while achieving a high production 
and causing minimal ecological impact (Martins et al, 2010). Oxygen is added to the 
water to ensure optimal environmental conditions for the farmed animal, and a 
neutralizing or buffering agent can be used to maintain a stable pH (Holan et al., 2020). 
However, the re-use of water in RAS can lead to the accumulation of undesirable waste 
products, requiring mechanical and biological treatment compartments to maintain 
optimal conditions. In a RAS with low water exchange and high degree of water re-use 
mechanical filters remove larger suspended solids, trickling filters degasses carbon 
dioxide (CO2) degassing, and toxic ammonia is converted into harmless nitrate by 
nitrifying bacteria in biofilters (Lekang, 2008).  
Bacteria, viruses, and fine suspended solids are not effectively removed by screen 
filtration. Instead, foam fractionation (“protein skimming”) or ozone (O3) may be used 
(Holan et al., 2020; Gesto et al, 2022). However, there is a risk associated with the use 
of ozone as it can leave residuals and compounds that may be highly toxic to fish. 
Ultraviolet (UV) irradiation in RAS is common to reduce the bacterial load in a system 
(de Jesus Gregersen et al, 2020). However, including UV disinfection in the system is 
not enough to remove all potential harmful bacteria, as particles can shield 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
microorganisms and particulates scatter the light (Liltved et al., 2008). Even though 
technology has improved and more experience has been gained in RAS, disease 
outbreaks are still a challenge (Holan et al., 2020).  
2.1 Disinfectants in Aquaculture 
Disinfecting products, also known as disinfectants or therapeutants, are products that 
may limit prevalence of disease and mortality of fish by improving water quality (Lieke et 
al., 2020) and eliminating bacteria and other pathogens. Traditional disinfectants, such 
as malachite green and formalin have been banned and restricted from use in 
aquaculture industry in several countries due to their toxicity, increasing development of 
resistance and the growing public awareness of both animal welfare and environmental 
protection (Lieke et al., 2020). One approach to find alternatives to these chemicals 
focuses on possible treatments of fish that will make them less susceptible to infections, 
by increasing their resistance to stress or activating their defense systems. Another 
approach is to find ways to treat culture water and remove pathogens without being 
harmful to the natural environment or to the cultured animals (Lieke et al., 2020).  
Commercial disinfectants containing peracetic acid (PAA) and hydrogen peroxide (HP) 
are commonly used in Atlantic salmon aquaculture today. These are considered 
environmentally friendly compared to inert or slowly degradable compounds (Lieke et 
al., 2020), as they rapidly break down into harmless compounds. PAA, also known as 
peroxyacetic acid, acetic peroxide, peroxyethanoic acid, ethaneperoxoic acid and acetyl 
hydroperoxide, is commercially available in an equilibrium mixture of acetic acid, 
hydrogen peroxide (HP, H2O2) and water. PAA has many qualities of an ideal 
disinfectant, including; effectivity at ambient temperatures, stability and long shelf life, 
deodorizing ability, low corrosive properties, widespread availability, reasonable cost 
and toxicity to microorganisms (Tchobanoglous et al., 2004).  
In the US, products containing PAA have been used as disinfectants in agriculture, food 
processing and medical and veterinary facilities (Malchesky, 1993; USEPA, 1993; 
USDA, 2016), and in recent years it’s uses has been expanded to include waste water 
treatment and aquaculture in several countries (Kitis, 2004; Straus et al., 2017). PAA is 
one of the compounds approved for use in cleaning, disinfection and for management of 
aquaculture ecosystems in Europe (Luukkonen and Pehkonen, 2017; EGTOP, 2016).  
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
PAA is far more potent as an antimicrobial agent than HP due to its fat solubility (Block, 
1991), however, HP is an important contributor to the oxidizing power of PAA (Liu et al., 
2015). Water disinfection and oxidation of aqueous (micro-)pollutants by PAA is based 
on the formation of highly oxidative radical species (Shi and Li, 2007), which together 
are referred to as reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Luukkonen and Pehkonen, 2017).  
A wide spectrum of microorganisms are affected by PAA in low concentrations, through 
oxidative disruption of cell membranes via hydroxyl radicals (Kitis, 2004). PAA causes 
damage to bacteria, which they are unable to recover from, so no re-growth takes place 
(Antonelli et al., 2006), while virus are inactivated, possibly through damaging parts of 
the surface functions needed for host infection (Koivunen et al., 2005) 
Many disinfectants face a problem of resistance developing in fish pathogens, where 
the product’s effect becomes weakened, or ultimately negligible, as a result. PAA 
prevents development of resistance in microorganisms due to its non-specific mode of 
action (Lieke et al., 2020). Oftentimes, resistance can be caused by misuse or 
exaggerated use of treatment, however, it may also be caused by low-application 
doses, which only kills the weak pathogens (Lieke et al., 2020). In contrast to 
conventional disinfectants (Lieke et al., 2020), PAA produces little to no toxic 
byproducts after reaction with organic material (Monarca et al. 2002; Luukkonen and 
Pehkonen, 2017), resulting in very low environmental impact concerns.  
An additional aspect of disinfection that must be considered in RAS is the presence of 
bacteria that are vital to the function of the system. Biofilters consist of bacteria attached 
to biomedia as relatively stable and effective biofilm (Lekang, 2008; Holan et al, 2020) 
and these beneficial nitrifying bacteria may be unintendedly affected by water treatment 
with chemical disinfectants targeted against harmful bacteria. If the biofilter would be 
impaired, accumulation of nitrogen compounds could become an additional challenge to 
fish health. Using disinfectants in RAS therefore pose some application challenges 
compared to other systems. If applied in correct dosage, PAA has great potential to be 
applied at low concentrations in RAS without negatively impacting fish or biofilter (Liu et 
al., 2017a), as 1 mg/L PAA has been found to have no effect on ammonia removal 
(Pedersen et al., 2009). A possible solution could also be to temporarily bypass the 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
biofilter while any residual PAA degrades without affecting the nitrifying microorganisms 
(Pedersen et al., 2009). Finding the balance where water quality can be improved but 
avoiding harmful impact on biofilter and fish is a challenge, as increased fish mortalities 
has been observed at the application frequency necessary to maintain improved water 
quality (Suurnäkki et al., 2020).  
3.1 Use of PAA in aquaculture systems with the presence of fish 
Using PAA as a disinfectant in aquaculture systems is permitted both with and without 
the presence of fish. Even though PAA is considered environmentally friendly because it 
degrades quickly without leaving any harmful residues in the environment (Wagner et 
al., 2002), fish welfare should also be considered in application of PAA (Zubiaurre, 
2016; Liu et al., 2017b). Health benefits such as more intact gill morphology have been 
found in mirror carp (Cyprinus carpio) after three months of periodic PAA-exposure 
compared to unexposed fish (Liu et al., 2018). Potentially, bacterial infections were 
hindered by reduction of bacterial load when water was treated with PAA, although 
there were both increases and reductions in bacterial load (Liu et al., 2018).   
Although these positive effects on fish health have been found, there is also a 
considerable risk of toxicity when applying disinfecting products. Toxicology has been 
assessed in a number of fish species by finding the 24-h no observed effect 
concentration (NOEC). Channel catfish (Ictalurus punctatus) fry was found to have a 
NOEC of 1.3 mg/L PAA (Straus et al., 2012), while juveniles of channel catfish, black 
fathead minnow (Pimephales promelas), blue gill (Lepomis macrochirus), blue tilapia 
(Oreochromis aureus), black-nose crappie (Pomoxis nigromaculatus), goldfish 
(Carassius auratus), grass carp (Ctenopharyngodon idella), golden shiner 
(Notemigonus crysoleucas), large-mouth bass (Micropterus salmoides), sunshine bass 
(Morone chrysops × M. saxatilis), rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) and walleye 
(Sander vitreus) all had had NOEC’s ranging from 1.9 to 5.8 mg/L PAA (Straus et al., 
2017).  
Semi-continuous application of PAA of 0.05-0.30 mg/L have been found to not have any 
impact on growth, survival, and feed conversion ratio in rainbow trout, however, there 
were no water quality improvements in most of the parameters tested (Davidson et al., 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
2019). Higher mortalities were observed in rainbow trout with 1.1 mg/L applied twice per 
day in the highest frequency level (four times per week), but was not found to 
significantly affect fish growth at any frequency of application (Suurnäkki et al., 2020). 
Periodic and continuous application of PAA have both been observed to trigger mild 
antioxidant defenses in rainbow trout (Liu et al., 2020). Periodic low dose applications at 
appropriate intervals are believed to minimize the risk of oxidative damage and would 
therefore be the preferred mode of application (Liu et al., 2020). Periodic exposure to 
PAA has been found to be acutely stressful to rainbow trout but, presumably, they 
habituate progressively after repeated exposures (Gesto et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2018). 
Rainbow trout have been able to mount a normal physiological stress response to a 
secondary stressor despite previous exposure to PAA, which suggests there is a true 
habituation to PAA exposure (Gesto et al., 2018).  
PAA shows potential to be used as a bath chemotherapeutant for salmon, as it has 
been observed that fish were able to mount mucosal and systemic responses following 
a PAA exposure of 5 minutes and re-exposure of 30 minutes after two weeks (0.6 –2.4 
mg/L), and following a single 30 minute exposure of 4.8 mg/L PAA including a prior 
crowding stressor (Soleng et al., 2019). Plasma cortisol levels increased in the early 
hours after exposure and returned to basal levels afterwards (Soleng et al., 2019). A 
similar application regime, in which PAA impact on gill mucosal health of salmon was 
investigated, discovered that the fish were not substantially impacted by PAA bathing 
exposure (0.6-2.4 mg/L), and would therefore be considered a safe treatment 
(Haddeland et al., 2021). Salmon skin changes were assessed in the same exposure 
protocol of PAA (0.6-2.4 mg/L) and only marginal alterations were discovered, 
supporting that PAA does not present a significant challenge to mucosal health of 
salmon in these concentrations (Lazado et al., 2020).  
Periodic exposure over a prolonged period is a relevant and practical protocol for PAA 
application in salmon RAS, where efficient disinfection can be achieved (Osório et al., 
2022). A periodic exposure of 1 mg/L every three days for six weeks was found to be a 
mild environmental stressor to Atlantic salmon in a brackish water RAS, however, the 
fish showed no significant changes in plasma cortisol levels, feeding pattern or 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
mortality, and had strong adaptive responses at both systemic and mucosal levels 
(Osório et al., 2022). A handling-confinement stress test revealed that the PAA 
application protocol did not change the typical stress response, which further 
substantiates that periodic exposure does not impair stress responses of salmon to a 
secondary stressor (Osório et al., 2022). Atlantic salmon parr NOEC on survival, 
swimming behavior and mucosal health, was explored through application of PAA in a 
one hour exposure and one hour re-exposure after 52 hours (0–6.4 mg/L), and was 
found to be below 1.6 mg/L (Mota et al., 2022). However, at high PAA doses, acute 
mortality, skin damage and gill necrosis were observed (Mota et al., 2022). Many factors 
influence the decomposition of PAA, such as temperature, pH, salinity, water hardness, 
presence of solids or transition metal ions, application mode and the amount of organic 
material (Falsanasi et al., 2006; Lazarova et al., 1998; Howarth, 2003; Sanchez-Ruiz, 
1995; Kunigk et al., 2001; Liu et al., 2014; Pedersen et al., 2013). These all have to be 
considered when using PAA in aquaculture water with fish, as they can affect the 
toxicity.  
1.4 Conclusions 
Periodic PAA applications of various concentrations have been found to cause minor 
stress in salmonids, but have advantages over continuous application in improving 
water quality. Habituation to PAA exposure has been indicated in both rainbow trout and 
salmon, as they show reduced stress response after repeated exposures, while they are 
able to mount a normal stress response to a secondary stressor. Potentially toxic 
substances should always be used with caution when possibly affecting animals, and 
guidelines should be provided for species specific concentration ranges.  
The full knowledge about physiological impacts of PAA-based disinfection on Atlantic 
salmon is yet to be attained, however, it has been strengthened in the last years. To 
establish guidelines for use of disinfectants, the health advantages that can be achieved 
in the long term has to be considered together with the potential for acute and chronic 
toxicity and stress.

Behavioral and physiological responses of Atlantic salmon  
to a commercial disinfectant in aquaculture 
 
 
==PAGE:20==

Abstract 
Peracetic acid (PAA)-based disinfectants are considered environmentally friendly and a 
suitable alternative in recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) because of the low risk 
of bioaccumulation, fast degradation without toxic residuals and minimal impact on 
biofilter performance. The main objective was to provide new knowledge about the 
welfare of Atlantic salmon upon use of PAA, a disinfectant of increasing use in the 
aquaculture sector. Acute physiological effects of PAA first exposure on Atlantic salmon 
(Salmo salar) in freshwater were explored through trials on oxygen consumption, 
plasma cortisol levels and behavior. Elevations in oxygen consumption rate and plasma 
cortisol suggest that fifteen minutes exposure to higher concentrations (4.8 mg/L) of 
Aqua Oxides PAA can cause an acute physiological response in salmon, however, the 
effect seemed to be transient.  
 
 
Key words: peracetic acid (PAA), disinfection, Atlantic salmon, physiology, RAS, 
aquaculture 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
2. Introduction 
Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) is an advanced land based rearing concept, 
which allows high fish densities, long water retention time and high feeding rates. 
Accumulation of organic matter and micro-particles can occur in these production 
systems creating favorable conditions for bacterial growth (Blancheton et al., 
2013; Pedersen et al., 2017). Microorganisms may be introduced into RAS through 
feed, make-up water, equipment, fish microbiota, staff and visitors of the system 
(Sharrer et al., 2005). Under ideal conditions, the fish immune system would be able to 
counteract pathogenic bacteria, however, environmental related diseases associated 
with increased microbial abundance or stress in fish have been found under realistic 
RAS conditions (Noble and Summerfelt., 1996). Disinfection of water could reduce the 
load of pathogenic bacteria in the system and thereby reduce the risk of infections. 
However, chemical disinfection in aquaculture is challenging considering safe and 
sufficient removal of pathogens without affecting the fish, the nitrifying bacteria, the staff 
or the recipient negatively (Pedersen et al., 2009).  
Commercial disinfectants containing peracetic acid (PAA) and hydrogen peroxide (HP, 
H2O2) are commonly used in Atlantic salmon aquaculture today and are considered 
environmentally friendly compared to traditional compounds, such as formalin and 
copper sulphate (Lieke et al., 2020). HP and PAA have both been found effective as 
disinfectants, however, PAA was found to be a hundred times more potent than HP as 
an antifungal compound (Straus et al., 2012; Mitchell et al., 2009). PAA is commercially 
available in an equilibrium mixture of acetic acid, HP and water. Disinfection treatments 
of PAA has proven effective against various aquatic pathogens, including fungi and 
bacteria (Jussila et al., 2011; Marchand et al, 2012).  
Safe and effective disinfection in freshwater aquaculture require knowledge about 
unintended side effects or possible toxicity to the cultured species (Straus et al., 2017). 
Acute toxicity of PAA has been explored in several commercially important species of 
fingerling fish, including rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss), and the 24-hours no-
observed-effect concentration (NOEC)was found to range from 1.9 to 5.8 mg/L PAA, 
while the mean 24-hour median lethal concentration (LC50) values ranged from 2.8 to 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
9.3 mg/L PAA (Straus et al., 2017). Mortality is not an ideal biomarker ultimately 
demonstrating that welfare has been comprised, however (Ashley, 2007). The aim of 
this study was to investigate sub-lethal markers of stress when salmon smolt were 
exposed to different, realistic PAA treatment scenarios. Acute physiological and 
behavioral stress responses in Atlantic salmon to PAA exposure of two commercial 
trade products have been compared and evaluated.  
3. Materials and Methods 
3.1 Experimental fish and RAS 
All experimental work complied with current Danish and EU legislation (Directive 
2010/63/EU) regarding animal experimentation. 
Atlantic salmon of approximately 100-150 grams were used in these experiments. The 
fish used in these experiments were purchased from Danish Salmon and brought to the 
experimental facility in two batches, approximately a month prior to the respective trials. 
The fish were kept in a RAS comprised of a reservoir tank, a filter complex and twelve 
fish culture tanks (630 L), of which three were stocked with fish and two were used for 
isolation. Throughout the experimental period, there were approximately 50-100 fish per 
tank. The fish were fed daily with pellets (EFICO E 940, 3 mm, BioMar) on automatic 
feeders for approximately 12 hours from 9:30 and photoperiod was set at 24 h light. 
Water quality was monitored approximately weekly throughout the trial period, 
measured with a HQ40d multi Hach Portable. Water temperature in the system was 
12.9 ± 0.1 °C, oxygen saturation levels 103.1 ± 1.3 % in the fish tanks, and a pH of 8.57 
± 0.1 in the sump. Trials were carried out at DTU Aqua facilities in Hirtshals in the 
period of March to May.  
3.2 Experimental design and procedures 
The PAA products Aqua Oxides and Divosan Forte were obtained from S. Sørensen 
(Thisted, Denmark) and Lilleborg AS (Oslo, Norway), respectively, and lab technicians 
at the laboratory in DTU Aqua, Hirtshals, verified the contents. Two PAA concentrations 
(2.4 mg/L and 4.8 mg/L) were tested in this study with an exposure time of 15 minutes. 
Aqua Oxides was used in all trials, while Divosan Forte was used in oxygen 
consumption trial only.

3.2.1 Oxygen consumption 
A trial of individual oxygen consumption (MO2) in respirometer chambers was 
conducted to explore the response of PAA exposure, and to discover any correlation 
between the PAA dose and magnitude of metabolic response. Twelve fish were used 
for each concentration of the two products. Additionally, a control of eight fish was 
included, where water was injected in place of PAA. The experimental system included 
a black rectangular tank with inlet and outlet for water flow (approximately 3 L per 
minute), a filter complex and a sump with heating (Figure 1). Temperature was kept at 
12.9 ± 0.1 °C throughout the trial period. Four respirometric chambers, each equipped 
with a recirculating pump and a flush pump, were used. The recirculating pump moved 
water past an optic oxygen sensor, while the flush pump pulled water from the 
experimental tank, flushed water out from the chambers and returned it to the 
experimental tank (Figure 1). Aeration was provided in the experiment tank to maintain 
oxygen levels near saturation.

3.2.2 Cortisol 
Stress response and recovery time of the fish from exposure of PAA-concentrations of

on each end. The system was the same as in the oxygen consumption trial, except no 
plastic cover was used (Figure 2). Temperature was kept at 13.3 ± 0.3 °C throughout 
the trial period, and the system water was emptied out and replaced after every trial run.  
First measurement was done at time 0, which was right before exposure, while time 1, 
2, 4 and 8 represent hours after exposure. The same PAA concentrations as in the 
oxygen consumption dose-response trial were used, 2.4 mg/L and 4.8 mg/L. There

3.2.3 Behavior 
Three groups of ten fish were recorded for each of the two selected concentrations of 
PAA, 2.4 mg/L and 4.8 mg/L, for a total of 48 minutes, including 16 minutes before 
exposure, 16 minutes during exposure and the following 16 minutes after exposure. The 
first minute of each recording was excluded from the analysis because it was used to 
make the necessary adjustments regarding the exposure procedure. The software 
EthoVision XT Version 11 (Noldus, Wageningen, The Netherlands) was used to analyze 
the videos to assess the behavioral response of the fish to the product. Activity level of 
individual fish was analyzed by EthoVision in the parameters “Distance moved” and 
“Velocity”. Distance moved was defined as the distance traveled by the center point 
from the previous sample to the current one, while Velocity was defined as distance 
traveled by the center point per unit time.

3.3 Statistical analysis 
Individual oxygen consumption data from each sampling day was averaged, to make 
three samples per time group per concentration, for each of the two products. Individual 
behavior data from each tank was averaged, to make three samples per time group per 
concentration. Cortisol data was logarithmically transformed to satisfy homogeneity 
assumptions. 
Levene’s test was performed to assess the homogeneity of the samples. Two-way 
ANOVA was used to discover significances between concentrations. One-way ANOVA 
was used to discover differences in time. Tukey’s post-hoc was used to explore 
significant differences in time. Statistical tests were performed with IBM® SPSS® 
Statistics V27 (IBM Corp., USA). A significance level of p ≤ 0.05 was used for all 
analyses. All data are presented as mean ± standard error (SE).

5. Discussion 
5.1 Oxygen consumption 
A variety of physiological mechanisms are activated in fish to maintain homeostasis 
when they are exposed to a chemical stressor (Beyers et al., 1999) such as a 
disinfectant. Oxygen consumption is an indirect measure of metabolic rate and can 
therefore give an indication of how much energy is being expended in compensating for 
physiological effects of chemical exposure (Beyers et al., 1999). The peak in oxygen 
consumption one hour after exposure (time 60) which was found in 4.8 mg/L Aqua 
Oxides and in both concentration groups in Divosan Forte, suggests that there was an 
increase in energy use as a response to the products. However, only 4.8 mg/L Aqua 
Oxides was found to be significantly different from the control (0.0 mg/L), where mean 
energy difference was found to be 389.8 J from the peak and the following hour (time 
60-120). The observed initial spike in oxygen consumption at the first measurement 
after exposure (time 20) may indicate a slight reaction to the injection protocol, as there 
is a visible increase in all groups, including the control.  
The findings indicate a recovery time of three hours from 4.8 mg/L Aqua Oxides 
exposure, as there was a significant decrease in oxygen consumption from the peak 
(time 60) to that point (time 180). Taking the control into account, these results suggest 
that fifteen minutes exposure to higher concentrations (4.8 mg/L) of Aqua Oxides PAA 
can cause a transient physiological response in salmon. No significant effect of the 
lower concentration (2.4 mg/L) of Aqua Oxides was discovered, which lends support to 
the safe use of this PAA product as a disinfectant in salmon aquaculture at low 
concentrations. Effects from Aqua Oxides and Divosan Forte on salmon seem to differ, 
which might be due to their specific product composition. Aqua Oxides has been found 
to have a 15.5 % PAA content and 10.2 % HP (Zubiaurre and Pedersen, 2016), while 
Divosan Forte is said to have 15 % PAA (JohnsonDiversey, 2004), and the full 
composition of active components has been stated more vaguely as 10-20 % PAA, 10-
20 % acetic acid and 20-30 % HP (Diversey, 2018). Although their composition of active 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
compounds seems quite similar, perhaps the differences could be enough to cause 
different responses in fish. Another possibility is that the other product components, 
such as stabilizing agents, have different properties and therefore might alter the effect 
of the product to fish. 
5.2 Cortisol 
Considering the control (0.0 mg/L, time 1), the results of cortisol elevation indicate that 
15 minute exposure of 4.8 mg/L Aqua Oxides PAA can cause a transient physiological 
response in salmon. A pattern of significant increase in the first hours after exposure, 
followed by a decrease and return to the baseline values have been found in plasma 
cortisol levels (Soleng et al., 2019). Even though this pattern was visible in these data 
as well, post-exposure cortisol levels were generally higher than those observed by 
Soleng et al. (2019) for 2.4 mg/L Divosan Forte, which may indicate a higher magnitude 
of stress from Aqua Oxides. Characteristic peak post-stress elevations in plasma 
cortisol in salmonids have been found to be in the range of 40-200 ng/mL (Barton and 
Iwama, 1991), which shows there is a lot of variation in salmonid cortisol response. 
Elevated cortisol levels throughout the sampling time range suggests that either the fish 
did not recover completely from the PAA-exposure within eight hours after exposure, or 
they were disturbed by sampling procedures. Although disturbance was largely avoided 
through visual isolation of each individual as well as quick euthanizing and blood 
sampling minimized sampling-induced cortisol response (Gamperl et al., 1994). Cortisol 
levels in rainbow trout have been found to stay slightly elevated compared to controls 
up to thirty hours after two pulse applications of 1 mg/L PAA in the first week of 
exposure (Liu et al., 2017), which may also explain the continued elevation. Baseline 
salmon (not exposed to PAA) had cortisol levels lower than 10 ng/mL, which conforms 
to reported basal cortisol values in stress trials considering good husbandry and careful 
experimental design (Pickering et al., 1982; Bry, 1985; Sumpter et al., 1986). In high 
doses of PAA (6.4 mg/L), pH is suspected to have been a confounding factor to toxicity 
(Mota et al., 2022), and this might also be the case of Aqua Oxides at 4.8 mg/L. 
Changes in pH during this trial were not recorded, however, so it is unknown whether it 
went below the pH value suggested to have no impact on salmon (5.6) (Kroglund et al., 
2008).

5.3 Behavior 
No significant effects in any of the behavior parameters tested were discovered as a 
consequence of 15 minute Aqua Oxides PAA-exposure at either concentration. Based 
on visual observation from the analysis process of EthoVision, the fish were not 
recognized by the software throughout all of the video recordings. However, no data on 
proportions of error were provided. Consequently, the results from this video analysis 
have to be interpreted with this in mind. Longer PAA exposure times of one hour have 
revealed changes in swimming behavior in salmon, however, including erratic 
movement and air gasping at concentrations above 1.6 mg/L (Mota et al., 2022), and 
therefore a NOEC for salmon parr below 1.6 mg/L has been suggested. Perhaps a 
larger dataset with more replications would have been able to discover significant 
activity differences in these experimental conditions, as there was a trend (p = 0.098) in 
distance moved of different levels of activity in time when both concentrations were 
considered.  
5.4 Using PAA as a Disinfectant in Salmon Aquaculture 
Higher Aqua Oxides dose (4.8 mg/L) increased oxygen consumption as well as cortisol 
levels, while there were no detectable changes in oxygen consumption or cortisol in the 
lower dose (2.4 mg/L) when considering the controls. Lowered pH as a consequence of 
PAA application could possibly have an effect on salmon (Mota et al., 2022), and might 
be part of the explanation for the different responses to the two dosage levels. 
Combined, the discovered elevations in oxygen consumption rate and plasma cortisol 
suggest that 15 minute exposure to PAA can cause an acute physiological stress 
response in salmon. However, the results also indicated that it was a transient effect. 
PAA exposure has also been found to be acutely stressful to rainbow trout but 
nonetheless, application of PAA in aquaculture can have a beneficial effect on fish 
health (Gesto et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018). Additionally, after repeated 
exposures to PAA (1 mg/L), responses in behavior and cortisol in rainbow trout were 
reduced, and growth and immunity were unaffected (Liu et al., 2017).  
In the field of stress neurobiology, habituation refers to the reduction in physiological 
responses after a number of repetitions of a stressor, compared to the larger response 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
of acute exposure to the stressor (Grissom and Bhatnagar, 2009). There are several 
possible reasons for a reduction in cortisol response to stress stimuli, however, such as 
desensitization or exhaustion after repeated or chronic stress exposure (Cyr and 
Romero, 2009; McKenzie et al., 2012; Barton et al., 2005; Madaro et al., 2015; 
Moltesen et al., 2016) and disruption of neuroendocrine pathway functions in the 
vertebrate stress response (Gesto et al., 2008; Ings et al., 2012; Hontela et al., 1997). 
PAA could potentially have an effect on these pathways in salmonids (Gesto et al., 
2018) but seeing as rainbow trout were able to mount a normal physiological stress 
response to a secondary stressor independent of exposure, there seems to be a true 
habituation to exposure of PAA (Gesto et al., 2018). Mirror carp have also been found to 
adapt quickly to stress from PAA (Liu et al., 2018). Even though findings have indicated 
that there would be a persistent minor cortisol response, periodic application of PAA in 
RAS could still be beneficial to mirror carp health in the long term because of its positive 
effects on improving water quality (Liu et al., 2018). 
A 5 minutes single exposure and 30-minutes re-exposure to PAA (0.6 – 2.4 mg/L) 
increased plasma cortisol levels (Soleng et al., 2019), while post-smolt salmon exposed 
to PAA (1 mg/L) intermittently, every 3 days for 6  weeks, had no significant changes in 
plasma cortisol levels over time (Osório, 2022). Systemic and mucosal changes 
suggested that physiological defenses were used to counteract risks from an oxidant-
rich environment; yet, production performance was not affected. Handling-confinement 
stress tests before exposures and at day 45 revealed that salmon were still able to 
mount a stress response to a secondary stressor (Osório, 2022), while crowding stress 
followed by 4.8 mg/L PAA indicated that the exposure restricted the ability of fish to 
mount systemic antioxidative responses. Stress- and olfactory sensitivity is highly 
variable among teleosts (Bonga, 1997), so it is possible that Atlantic salmon have 
different stress and habituation responses to PAA to than shown in rainbow trout and 
carp. The mechanisms by which the fish are stressed by PAA in the water is unknown 
but might be related to contact with exposed fish tissue, such as skin, gills and olfactory 
epithelium, resulting in discomfort, tissue irritation or increased alertness. In this regard, 
changes in skin, gills and olfactory rosette have been found in salmon after PAA 
exposure (Mota et al., 2022; Soleng et al.,2019; Osorio, 2022). PAA also has a 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
characteristic pungent odor (Luukkonen and Pehkonen, 2017), which might be detected 
by fish in water and induce a stress response.  
Challenges in RAS regarding disease and infections may become pronounced when 
stocking densities and feeding rate are high, as this potentially leads to higher organic 
load in the water. Insufficient solids removal would favor bacterial growth, which 
increases the risk of bacterial infection and subsequent impaired health or suppressed 
immunity in fish (Liu et al., 2018). PAA can be used to control bacterial growth in fish 
tanks and thereby potentially benefit fish health, even if it can be considered a mild 
stressor. Baseline toxicity data for PAA needs to be developed (Straus et al., 2017) for 
relevant species, so that a safe and reliable aquaculture guideline for PAA water 
treatment can be established (Pedersen et al., 2009). As lower concentrations (2.4 
mg/L) of PAA did not cause significant changes in either behavior, oxygen consumption 
rate or cortisol levels, they support the range of 1–2 mg/L that have been suggested for 
prophylactic use (Liu et al., 2021). Results in this study also suggest that lower doses of 
PAA might induce a stress response in salmon but, as the experimental setup induced 
some background noise, there were no conclusive findings. 
The different strengths of available commercial PAA compounds, as well as the 
diversity of aquaculture systems, rearing conditions and pathogen infections, 
complicates the interpretation and review of various studies (Pedersen et al., 2009). 
Divosan Forte was only tested for oxygen consumption in this trial, but the findings 
show that the salmon responded differently to this product than to Aqua Oxides. As both 
of these products supposedly have the same active components and similar PAA 
proportion, it might have been expected that their effects on fish would also be similar. 
This suggests that it may be necessary to explore the effects of each individual PAA-
product to ensure a safe application.  
This study investigated the acute effects of PAA on salmon at first, relatively short, 
exposure and of course, effects from longer and repeated exposures have to be 
considered in the aquaculture application aspect. Although the habituation of salmon to 
PAA has not yet been determined, systemic and mucosal defenses to counteract the 
effect of PAA has been displayed (Soleng et al., 2019), as well as the continued ability 
==PAGE:37==
37 
 
to mount a stress response to a secondary stressor after intermittent exposure (1 mg/L 
PAA) (Osório et al., 2022). Health and welfare consequences of long-term exposure 
should be explored (Mota et al., 2022), including different PAA doses, and application 
frequency should be taken into account (Osório et al., 2022). In contrast to Soleng et al. 
(2019) who included handling procedures prior to exposure, this study investigated the 
sole effects of PAA-exposure by keeping the fish minimally disturbed before and during 
exposure. In a normal aquaculture production cycle there will be times when fish are 
transferred between tanks, however, the fish will not usually be handled prior to 
disinfection, and it was therefore relevant to investigate the isolated effect caused by 
PAA-exposure.  
6. Conclusion  
The presented data displayed some of the acute physiological and behavioral 
responses of salmon to PAA-exposure and demonstrated some stress but a quick 
recovery in higher concentrations. As lower concentrations (2.4 mg/L) of PAA did not 
cause significant changes in either behavior, oxygen consumption rate or cortisol levels, 
these findings support the use of PAA at this exposure concentration and duration as a 
safe disinfectant for use in salmon aquaculture. Even though PAA might cause mild 
environmental stress, it can benefit fish health by reducing the number and severity of 
disease outbreaks.  
 
Since PAA-based disinfectants are available commercially in different strengths, 
composition and stabilizers, the effects documented here should mainly be considered 
for the tested products.
