Summary 
Komagataella phaffii is a non-conventional yeast used as a cell factory to produce many 
valuable products. Throughout time, tools for genetic engineering of K. phaffii have been 
developed, including tools based on the most modern tool for genetic engineering, 
CRISPR/Cas9. The current methods for multi-copy gene expression in K. phaffii rely on 
increasing antibiotic concentrations or auxotrophic strains, which are not desirable as 
cells factories. Methods relying on antibiotics are time-consuming and expensive due to 
the high costs of eukaryotic antibiotics. Moreover, strains with natural antibiotic 
resistance can be obtained for other reasons than the number of integrated copies.  
 
This work aimed to implement CASCADE in K. phaffii. CASCADE is a robust multi-copy 
gene expression platform implemented for the first time in Saccharomyces cerevisiae in 
2017. CASCADE allows integrating multiple copies of a gene of interest within defined, 
stable, and highly expressed genomic sites. Moreover, CASCADE does not rely on 
auxotrophic strains or constant selection with antibiotics to maintain gene copy numbers. 
In addition, CASCADE allows performing marker-free genomic integrations. As a proof 
of concept, an insulin coding sequence was used as the gene of interest to be multi-
integrated during CASCADE implementation in K. phaffii.  
 
For CASCADE construction, K. phaffii general acceptor starter (GAS) strains capable of 
accepting up to four copies of a gene of interest were constructed by simultaneously 
genetic engineering using multiplex CRISPR/Cas9 technology. The constructed GAS 
strains have a common gene acceptor (GA) cassette integrated into up to four pre-
defined genomic sites. CASCADE implementation was then performed by transforming 
GAS strains with a donor amplicon (DA) cassette harboring an insulin coding sequence 
together with a plasmid expressing the I-SceI endonuclease. Once inside the cell, the I-
SceI endonuclease expressed by the plasmid cuts its restriction site found within all the 
GA cassettes. Finally, the DA cassette must be integrated into all cut sites via 
homologous recombination. 
 
 
==PAGE:5==
 
 
Implementation of CASCADE platform in Komagataella phaffii 
As a result, we validated K. phaffii strains that integrated up to four gene copies of insulin 
into their genomes by using CASCADE. In addition, it was demonstrated that four 
simultaneously and precise genomic integrations can be carried out in K. phaffii by either 
using CASCADE or multiplex CRISPR/Cas9 technology; no research work was found in 
the literature reporting this data before in K. phaffii. A fluorescence-based 
characterization of the current integration sites of CASCADE revealed that they have a 
homogeneous gene expression. Moreover, the gene expression increases according to 
the number of integrated gene copies. 
 
In conclusion, CASCADE was successfully implemented in K. phaffii. CASCADE strains 
are most likely to be stable. Further characterization of the growth rates and production 
yields from the constructed CASCADE strains is necessary to evaluate their potential as 
cell factories for insulin production. CASCADE capacity could be increased to accept 
more than four copies of a gene of interest in the future.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Aim of this project 
 
This work aims to implement CASCADE in K. phaffii. CASCADE is a robust multi-copy gene 
expression platform implemented for the first time in S. cerevisiae in 2017. CASCADE allows 
integrating multiple copies of a gene of interest within defined, stable, and highly expressed genomic 
sites. Moreover, CASCADE does not rely on auxotrophic strains or constant selection with antibiotics 
to maintain copy numbers. In addition, CASCADE allows performing marker-free genomic 
integrations. 
 
1.2 Cell factories 
 
Nowadays, chemical production primarily relies on oil-based or plant extractions processes. This way 
of chemical production is depleting our non-renewable natural resources and damaging our 
environment by increasing carbon dioxide emissions. Therefore, it is necessary to change our current 
practices of chemical production into sustainable processes (DTU Biosustain, n.d.).  
 
Industrial biotechnology is an important and well-established economic discipline. It employs 
engineered cells, commonly known as cell factories, to produce their native or heterologous 
metabolites of interest, e.g., pharmaceuticals, industrial enzymes, and bulk chemicals (Davy et al., 
2017). 
 
Up until now, cell factories have demonstrated that it is possible to sustainable produce a wide range 
of valuable chemicals, starting from inexpensive stocks and without depleting our non-renewable 
natural resources (Xu et al., 2020). Even though industrial biotechnology has been widely used to 
produce chemicals for many years, it was until the arrival of genetic engineering that it was possible 
to improve cell factory production yields through their DNA manipulation (Davy et al., 2017). 
 
Since then, it has been possible to provide new functions to cell factories by transferring a few genes 
or entire metabolic pathways from organisms that are not easily genetically engineered into well-
known organisms. The challenges encountered when constructing cell factories include selecting the 
appropriate microbial host, the need for robust genetic control systems, methods to release metabolic 
bottlenecks, and strategies to improve yields and productivities. Despite all these necessities, cell 
factories are considered to potentially replace our ordinary ways of chemical production in the future 
(Keasling, 2010).

S. cerevisiae became one of the most popular cell factories because it offers physiological advantages 
such as fast growth and tolerance to harsh culture conditions, making the fermentation processes 
more economical (Hasunuma et al., 2015; Radecka et al., 2015). S. cerevisiae can perform 
posttranslational modifications and synthesize complex compounds that cannot be produced in 
prokaryotic cells (Ferrer-Miralles et al., 2009; Vogl et al., 2013).  
 
Moreover, S. cerevisiae is generally recognized as a safe organism (GRAS) (Walker & Pretorius, 
2018) and does not contain pyrogens nor produce toxins (Çelik & Çalik, 2012). In addition, its 12Mb 
genome was the first-ever eukaryotic genome to be sequenced and currently is one of the most well-
annotated genomes among eukaryotic microorganisms (Goffeau et al., 1996). One of the most 
important advantages that S. cerevisiae offers is its amenable and well-developed genetic 
engineering tools (Kavšček et al., 2015).  
 
Most of these tools are based on shuttle vectors that can be propagated in both bacteria and yeast. 
Examples of these include the yeast centromeric plasmids (YCps) that can be maintained as an 
additional chromosome, the yeast episomal plasmids (YEps) harboring a yeast origin of replication 
sequence known as 2µm, which allows it to be maintained in high copy numbers extrachromosomally, 
and the yeast integrative plasmids (YIps) designed to be integrated into the genome by homologous 
recombination once they are linearized. The last one takes advantage of the highly efficient 
homologous recombination machinery from S. cerevisiae, which allows for precise and site-specific 
genomic integrations (Gnügge & Rudolf, 2017).  
 
Although S. cerevisiae is a highly characterized microorganism in terms of biology, genetic 
engineering tools, and fermentation processes, its commercial potential as a cell factory has been 
reduced because its production processes are often related to low protein secretion yields. Moreover, 
its use in the production of therapeutic glycoproteins for human use has been limited since the 
glycosylation machinery from S. cerevisiae produces proteins with a shortened half-live in vivo and 
proteins that can trigger immunogenic responses (Wildt & Gerngross, 2005).  
 
In addition, it has also been reported instability on its shuttle vectors, meaning that uneven 
segregations can lose these during cell division or by, e.g., undesirable random integrations into the 
genome (Çelik & Çalik, 2012; Zhang et al., 1968).

1.4 Non-conventional yeasts 
 
The use of yeast cell factories is not limited to S. cerevisiae. There are thousands of yeasts species 
with great potential as cell factories that biotechnologists have classified as non-conventional yeasts, 
and many of them are currently being used to produce valuable products. Non-conventional yeast 
species share a low overflow metabolism, and that their genetic engineering tools are still being 
developed. Many non-conventional yeasts can offer advantages over S. cerevisiae in terms of 
physiology, product requirements, and the used metabolic pathway (Mattanovich et al., 2014; Wagner 
& Alper, 2016).  
 
1.5 Komagataella phaffii as a cell factory

it has gained huge attention among the scientific community. Among its attributes, K. phaffii can grow 
in substrates such as glucose, glycerol, and methanol. K. phaffii grows fast and can form high cell 
densities in minimal media. K. phaffii can also perform posttranslational modifications, including

1.6 K. phaffii genetic engineering 
 
1.6.1 USER cloning 
 
Although numerous molecular cloning techniques exist, the commercial Uracil-Specific Excision 
Reagent (USER®) from New England Biolabs (New England Biolabs, n.d.) is relevant to this thesis 
work. USER cloning allows the combination of multiple PCR fragments with high efficiency. The 
overview of USER cloning can be seen in Figure 1. In preparation for USER cloning, a USER 
compatible plasmid is digested with either PacI and Nt.BbvCI or AsiSI and Nb.BsmI restriction 
enzymes to generate eight nucleotides single-stranded 3´ overhangs. In parallel, the PCR products 
to clone are amplified using primers containing a uracil residue which is introduced during the reaction. 
USER cloning is then carried out by mixing the digested USER compatible plasmid and the PCR 
products into a solution containing the USER™ enzyme mix. The uracil DNA glycosylase and the 
DNA glycosylase-lyase Endo VIII enzymes from the USER™ mix remove the uracil residues from the 
PCR products, leaving single-stranded free ends that hybridize to the digested USER plasmid 
overhangs. After an incubation period, the mixture is ready to be transformed into E. coli (New 
England Biolabs, n.d.; Nour-Eldin et al., 2006).  
 
 
Figure 1. USER cloning The USER compatible plasmid is digested with PacI/Nt.BbvCI enzymes to 
create 8nt overhangs. The digested plasmid is then mixed with the PCR fragment to clone. The 
USER™ enzyme mix removes the DNA strand harboring a uracil nucleotide from the PCR fragment 
to clone, creating 8nt overhangs that complement the digested plasmid’s overhangs. Note: modified 
from (Nour-Eldin et al., 2006).

1.6.2 Transformation by electroporation 
 
Different methods are available to introduce DNA into K. phaffii. Nevertheless, the electroporation-
based transformation method has demonstrated higher transformation efficiencies (Wu & Letchworth, 
2004). In electroporation, K. phaffii cells are pretreated with Lithium acetate (LiAc) and dithiothreitol 
(DTT). After the treatment, the cells are washed and resuspended in sorbitol. Finally, the DNA of 
interest is mixed with the pretreated cells, and an electroporation pulse is applied to the mix.  
 
1.6.3 Promoters and terminators 
 
The most widely used promoters in K. phaffii for recombinant protein production are the constitutive 
promoter glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAP) and the methanol inducible promoter 
alcohol oxidase 1 (AOX1). A constitutive promoter is helpful for standard fermentation processes. 
However, when the product of interest is suspected of harming the cells, an inducible promoter can 
be advantageous because it can be activated once a high cell density has been reached (Felber et 
al., 2014). Terminators are also available for K. phaffii (Ito et al., 2020), and the most common 
terminator used for gene expression in K. phaffii is the AOX1 (Vogl et al., 2016). Shareable promoters 
and terminators coming from other species have also been characterized for their use in K. phaffii 
(Papakonstantinou et al., 2009; Terentiev et al., 2004). The most relevant promoters and terminators 
used in this present work can be visualized in Table 4.

1.6.4 Plasmids 
 
Heterologous gene expression in K. phaffii is usually carried out by the use of episomal and integrative 
plasmids. Episomal plasmids are maintained extrachromosomally and harbor an autonomously 
replicating sequence (ARS) and a resistance or an auxotrophic selectable marker (Gao et al., 2021). 
Unfortunately, episomal plasmids are not desirable for recombinant protein production in K. phaffii 
because their segregation instability results in productivity losses in long-term cultivations (Bernauer 
et al., 2021; Peña et al., 2018).  
 
Therefore, integrative plasmids are preferred over episomal plasmids because they allow performing 
genomic integrations once they are linearized. Genomic integrations are more stable than episomal 
plasmids, but it is usually required to integrate more than one copy of the gene of interest to reach 
the plasmid-based expression levels (Gao et al., 2021). There are currently integrative plasmids for 
K. phaffii that allow integrating single or many copies of a gene of interest. For single copy integration, 
plasmids are selected by auxotrophic or antibiotic markers, and for multiple copy integrations, strains 
are often obtained by increasing antibiotic concentrations (Gao et al., 2021; Yamada et al., 2019). 
However, methods to integrate multiple copies of a gene of interest relying on antibiotics are time-
consuming and expensive due to the high costs of eukaryotic antibiotics. In addition, strains with 
natural antibiotic resistance can be obtained for other reasons than the number of integrated copies 
(Betancur et al., 2017). 
 
1.6.5 Genomic integrations based on homologous recombination 
 
Genomic integrations based on integrative plasmids rely on the capacity of K. phaffii to integrate the 
provided gene of interest by homologous recombination (HR). The linearized gene of interest is 
flanked by DNA sequences homologous to the specific integration site to enhance HR. However, non-
conventional yeasts, including K. phaffii, have a low tendency to perform HR compared to S. 
cerevisiae (Näätsaari et al., 2012). Instead, its dominant non-homologous end-joining repair 
mechanism (NHEJ) results in lower recombination efficiencies and random genomic integrations 
during transformations. Hence, many variabilities are encountered among K. phaffii transformants 
when using integrative plasmids. In addition, it is necessary to carry out time-consuming screenings 
to find the transformants with the desired physiological and productivity characteristics (Löbs et al., 
2017; Schwarzhans et al., 2017).

Methods used to improve HR-based integrations in K. phaffii include incrementing length of the 
homologous sequences flanking the gene of interest to 1000bp each, and using K. phaffii ku70Δ 
strains deficient in their NHEJ repair machinery. When using ku70Δ strains, HR machinery is 
enhanced, and shorter flanking homologous sequences can be used, but other issues such as 
reduced growth rates arise (Peña et al., 2018). 
 
Other methods that allow for more precise HR-based genomic integrations include nucleases that cut 
a specific DNA sequence, e.g., zinc-finger nucleases (ZFN), transcription activator effector nucleases 
(TALENS), or the I-SceI endonuclease. When these nucleases cut a specific genomic DNA sequence, 
the HR-based repair is enhanced, and the DNA construct harboring the gene of interest is integrated 
on the cut site by HR with higher efficiency. Nevertheless, these tools’ construction is time-consuming 
and does not allow for simultaneous gene editing (Cai et al., 2019; Löbs et al., 2017). 
 
1.6.6 Dominant markers 
 
The most relevant dominant markers for this present work can be seen in Table 5. Commonly, when 
doing genomic integrations in K. phaffii, a selectable marker sequence is found adjacent to the gene 
of interest. Once a successful integration took place, it is necessary to screen for the transformants 
that integrated the gene of interest by challenging the used marker. Usually, markers are genes 
providing resistance to antibiotics or auxotrophic complementation (Ahmad et al., 2014).  
 
However, the use of auxotrophic markers requires auxotrophic strains, which are not desirable as cell 
factories. Therefore the number of available markers for K. phaffii is reduced to antibiotic resistance 
markers. Still, if more than one gene of interest is planned to be integrated, it is necessary to use a 
different resistant marker for each gene or recycle the resistance marker to use it many times. To 
overcome this challenge, marker excision tools, e.g., the Cre-loxP system, are available for K. phaffii 
(Li et al., 2017). In the Cre-loxP system, the resistance marker is flanked by loxP sequences from a 
bacteriophage origin. When it is necessary to excise the marker, a vector expressing the Cre 
recombinase is provided, catalyzing the recombination of the loxP sequences and consequently 
removing the marker (Li et al., 2017). However, genetic instabilities are promoted when using marker 
excision systems because these leave DNA scars in the site where the excision occurred (Aguilera 
et al., 2000). Moreover, if more than one gene is desired to be integrated, many rounds of time-
consuming screenings for marker selection and marker excision will be required (Cai et al., 2019).

1.6.7 Counter-selectable markers

1.6.8 CRISPR/Cas9 
 
The CRISPR/Cas9 system from Streptococcus pyogenes has revolutionized the way we do genetic 
engineering. This tool was originally used by S. pyogenes to defend itself against bacteriophages 
attacks (Lander, 2016; Mojica & Montoliu, 2016), but scientists adapted it into an efficient tool for 
genetic engineering. The CRISPR/Cas9 system has been widely used among non-conventional 
yeasts, including K. phaffii, to make, e.g., gene disruptions, gene deletions, or integrations of foreign 
DNA sequences into specific genomic sites (Weninger et al., 2016, 2018).  
 
The system is composed of the Cas9 endonuclease that can make double-stranded DNA brakes 
(DSB) and a single guide RNA (sgRNA) that guides Cas9 to the specifically targeted DNA sequence 
to be cut within the genome (Figure 2). 
 
The targeted DNA sequence within the genome must be a 20 nucleotides sequence, and an NGG 
sequence (called PAM) must be found immediately after the 20 nucleotides (Murugan et al., 2018). 
The 20 targeted nucleotides are often referred to as spacer sequences, and the sgRNA must be 
designed to contain the spacer on its sequence. For the genetic engineering of eukaryotic organisms, 
a nuclear localization signal (NLS) needs to be translationally fused to Cas9 to get into the eukaryotic 
nucleus (Löbs et al., 2017).  
 
When both Cas9 and sgRNA are expressed, they form a complex which performs DSB into the 
targeted DNA sequence (Wang et al., 2015). Once a DSB has been made, the cell must repair its 
DNA to avoid cell death. This process can be carried out either by Non-homologous end joining 
(NHEJ) or homologous recombination (HR) (Figure 2).

1.6.9 Genetic engineering challenges in K. phaffii 
 
The current methods for gene expression in K. phaffii rely on episomal and integrative plasmids. 
Expression by episomal plasmids results in loss of productivities in long-term cultivations due to 
plasmid instabilities. In contrast, a more stable expression driven by integrative plasmids requires 
multiple genomic integrations to increase expression. Moreover, the construction of strains harboring 
numerous copies of a gene of interest is usually based on uncertain, expensive, and time-consuming 
antibiotic-based screenings. In addition, if different genes are desired to be integrated, it is required 
to use a limited repertoire of different antibiotic resistance markers because auxotrophic markers need 
auxotrophic strains, which are not desirable as cell factories. Another option is to carry out many 
rounds of time-consuming marker recycling, but genetic instabilities arise due to DNA scars left in the 
excision sites. Genetic engineering tools such as zinc-finger nucleases (ZFN), transcription activator 
effector nucleases (TALENS), and the I-SceI meganuclease perform marker-free genomic 
integrations. Still, it is time-consuming to construct them, and they do not allow for simultaneous gene 
editing. Fortunately, since the arrival of the most modern genetic engineering tool, CRISPR/Cas9, 
single or simultaneously genomic integrations can be precisely performed.

1.7 CASCADE 
 
1.7.1 CASCADE in S. cerevisiae 
 
CASCADE is a robust expression platform implemented in S. cerevisiae in 2017 (Strucko et al., 2017). 
It allows integrating multiple copies of a gene of interest into defined and highly expressed genomic 
integration sites. CASCADE strongly relies on the highly efficient homologous recombination 
machinery from S. cerevisiae. CASCADE offers advantages such as a controlled number of integrated 
copies, marker-free genomic integrations, the absence of constant selective pressure to maintain 
gene copy numbers, and fast construction of stable strains. The main elements of CASCADE consist 
of a general acceptor starter (GAS) strain, a gene acceptor (GA) cassette, a donor amplicon (DA) 
cassette, and a plasmid expressing the I-SceI nuclease. The overall CASCADE flow can be seen in 
Figure 4. 
 
CASCADE begins with a GAS strain harboring a defined number of GA cassettes integrated into a 
defined number of genomic integration sites. The number of integrated GA cassettes defines the 
number of gene copies allowed to be integrated, e.g., a GAS strain with four GA cassettes can 
integrate four copies of a gene of interest. The GA cassettes harbor a counter-selectable marker 
(URA3), an I-SceI nuclease restriction site, and upstream and downstream synthetic DNA sequences 
for homologous recombination (A and B). 
 
CASCADE is then performed by transforming the GAS strain with a DA cassette harboring a gene of 
interest (GOI), a counter-selectable marker (TRP1) flanked by direct repeats sequences (DR), and A 
and B DNA sequences for homologous recombination. After transformation, GAS strains that have 
integrated the DA cassette are selected in media lacking tryptophan to select the TRP1 marker from 
DA cassettes. After this selection, a counter-selection for the loss of the TRP1 is carried out in 5-FAA 
media. After counter-selection, GAS strains are transformed with a plasmid expressing the I-SceI 
nuclease under the regulation of a galactose-induced promoter. Strains harboring the plasmid are 
selected in media lacking tryptophan to select the TRP1 marker from the plasmid. 
 
A replica plate then induces the expression of the I-SceI nuclease in media containing galactose. 
Once expressed, the I-SceI nuclease cuts all the available I-SceI restriction sites from the GA 
cassettes. Consequently, the DA cassette is integrated into the cut sites via homologous 
recombination. 
 
Finally, CASCADE strains are counter-selected in 5-FOA media to obtain only the strains that lost all 
of their URA3 markers found in the GA cassettes, ensuring that all DA cassettes were integrated were 
the GA cassettes used to be.

1.7.2 CASCADE stability 
 
One of the most important advantages CASCADE offers as a platform for multi-copy gene integrations 
is constructing stable strains. When using CASCADE, up to fourteen copies of a gene of interest can 
be integrated into the genome of S. cerevisiae (Strucko et al., 2017)  
 
Introducing many copies of a DNA sequence can promote genetic instabilities because of their 
homology (Aguilera et al., 2000). Genetic instabilities can lead to chromosome rearrangements such 
as deletions, inversions, translocations, and acentric or dicentric chromosomes. However, genetic 
instabilities are mainly associated with DNA double-stranded brakes derived from cellular metabolism, 
stalled replication forks, or exogenous causes such as gamma-irradiation. In addition, DNA damage 
is unpreventable since it is a naturally occurring event in the genome that has lead to species 
diversification (Gusa & Jinks-Robertson, 2019; Symington et al., 2014). 
 
CASCADE was not designed to prevent genetic instabilities but to minimize genetic drifts resulting 
from direct repeats recombination or ectopic crossovers among copies of a gene of interest. The 
principle of CASCADE stability relies on the genomic sites where the gene of interest will be 
integrated. A seen in Figure 5, the fourteen genomic integration sites are located between S. 
cerevisiae essential genes. Suppose an integrated copy of a gene of interest is lost due to a direct 
repeat crossover or an ectopic crossover event. In that case, cells will most likely die because of 
losing an essential gene or due to a lethal aberration, e.g., a dicentric chromosome (Strucko et al., 
2017). Many of the possible recombination events are described in the following chapters.

1.7.2.1 Homologous recombination  
 
Homologous recombination is an important DNA repair mechanism among eukaryotic organisms to 
repair their DNA. In exponentially growing yeast cells, DNA double-stranded brakes are most 
frequently repaired by the homologous recombination repair pathway. In homologous recombination, 
a DSB is repaired using a homologous sequence located in a sister chromatid or a homologous 
chromosome in diploid cells or a sister chromatid in haploid cells (Aguilera et al., 2000)  
 
1.7.2.2 Gene conversion 
 
Gene conversion is the main mechanism of homologous recombination when a DSB has occurred. It 
mediates the transfer of DNA from an intact homologous DNA sequence (donor) to the DSB site 
(recipient) (J. M. Chen et al., 2007). Once a DSB has occurred, the 5’ ends from the DSB are digested, 
exposing the ssDNA 3’ ends from each strand. After that, one of the ssDNA strands invades a 
homologous double-stranded DNA sequence (dsDNA), forming a displacement loop (D-loop) used to 
prime DNA synthesis using the donor DNA template. After the invasion, there are three models to 
continue the DSB repair: the break-induced replication (BIR), the synthesis-dependent strand 
annealing (SDSA), and the double-strand break repair (DSBR) (Figure 6) (Szostak et al., 1983).

Figure 6. Models for gene conversion in DSB repair. During BIR, one of the recipient DNA strands 
is lost, and the invading ssDNA primes the DNA synthesis in the donor DNA strand until the end of

and then is displaced and anneals again to the complementary ssDNA recipient strand, resulting in a 
non-crossover event. In DSBR, the other ssDNA from the DSB anneals to the displaced strand from

one copy of the gene of interest and excises the region between them as a circular DNA fragment 
(Figure 8) (Gusa & Jinks-Robertson, 2019). In CASCADE, essential genes will be within the circular

1.7.3 CASCADE in K. phaffii 
 
The CASCADE design for K. phaffii presents some variations to the original version. The K. phaffii 
GAS strains have a ku70Δ amd2Δ phenotype to favor DNA repair by homologous recombination and 
to be able to use the AmdS counter-selectable marker. In general, the CASCADE adaptation for 
K.phaffii has the same primary elements from the original version, including the general acceptor 
starter (GAS) strains, the gene acceptor cassettes (GA), the donor amplicon cassettes (DA), and a 
plasmid expressing the I-SceI nuclease. However, there are some differences in their structures, 
which will be explained in the following chapters. The overall flow of CASCADE for K. phaffii can be 
seen in Figure 12. 
 
CASCADE begins with a GAS strain harboring a defined number of GA cassettes integrated into a 
defined number of genomic integration sites. The number of integrated GA cassettes defines the 
number of gene copies allowed to be integrated, e.g., a GAS strain with four GA cassettes can 
integrate four copies of a gene of interest. The GA cassettes harbor a counter-selectable marker 
(AmdS), a visual reporter gene (YFP), an I-SceI nuclease restriction site, and upstream and 
downstream synthetic DNA sequences for homologous direct repair (A and B). 
 
CASCADE is then performed by transforming the GAS strain with a DA cassette harboring a gene of 
interest (GOI) flanked by A and B sequences together with a plasmid expressing the I-SceI nuclease. 
The transformation is plated on selective media containing an antibiotic for plasmid selection. Inside 
the cell, the I-SceI nuclease expressed by the plasmid cuts all the available I-SceI restriction sites. 
Consequently, the DA cassette is integrated into all the cut sites via homologous recombination. The 
resultant phenotype from the colonies growing on the transformation plates must be the absence of 
yellow fluorescence because of the loss of the YFP marker from the GA cassettes. 
 
CASCADE pure single colonies are obtained by streaking the non-fluorescent colonies growing on 
transformation plates in counter-selectable media containing fluoroacetamide (Fac) without 
antibiotics. Only the colonies that lost all the AmdS markers from the GA cassettes will grow on plates 
and most likely lost the plasmid expressing I-SceI because of the absence of selective pressure. 
 
As an optional step, the obtained CASCADE pure single colonies can be restreak in media with 
acetamide as the sole nitrogen source (Ac) to confirm the loss of the AmdS markers. CASCADE 
strains cannot grow on acetamide media unless they harbor an AmdS marker.

Diabetes is a disease characterized by hyperglycemia due to defects in the function and secretion of 
insulin. There are currently about 422 million people living with diabetes, and by 2030 the number of 
deaths attributed to this disease is expected to be more than 300 million. Insulin is a 51 amino acid 
polypeptide hormone that maintains the normal levels of glucose. Therefore it is used as a treatment 
for people with diabetes. Insulin sales are expected to increase from 12 billion USD to 54 billion USD 
in the following decades. The expected increase in demand requires improvements in cell factories' 
production yields in order to be able to produce insulin at affordable prices (Baeshen et al., 2014; 
Gurramkonda et al., 2010). 
 
As part of this thesis work, DA cassettes harboring the coding sequence of insulin will be used to 
evaluate the performance of CASCADE as a platform for multi-copy gene integrations.

3.8 K. phaffii transformation by electroporation 
 
A K. phaffii single colony was inoculated in 4mL YPD media and incubated O/N at 30°C and 200RPM. 
500uL from the culture was transferred to a 50mL falcon tube with 25mL YPD and incubated for 5 
hours at 30°C and 200RPM. Cells were pellet at 3000G for 5 minutes, and the supernatant was 
discarded. Cells were gently resuspended in 8 mL 100 mM LiAc, 10 mM DTT, 0.6 M sorbitol, and 6 
mM Tris HCl pH 8.5, then transferred to new 50mL falcon tubes and incubated at room temperature 
for 30 minutes. Cells were pellet at 3000G for 5 minutes, and the supernatant was discarded. Cells 
were resuspended in 1.5mL 1M ice-cold sorbitol and transferred to sterile 2mL Eppendorf tubes. Cells 
were washed three times by centrifuging them at 13000G for 30 seconds and then resuspended 1M 
ice-cold sorbitol. Cells were distributed in 5 sterile 1.5mL Eppendorf tubes, 1µg of plasmid was added 
to the mix, and 1µg of repair templates were added when required. The mix was allowed to rest on 
ice for 5 minutes and then transferred to a 0.2cm gene pulser/micropulser electroporation cuvette 
previously chilled on ice. Cells were electroporated by one pulse at 2.0kV for 6.10 milliseconds and 
immediately transferred to 2mL falcon tubes containing 1mL of ice-cold YPD 1M Sorbitol. The mix 
was allowed to rest on ice for 5 minutes and then incubated at 3 hours at 30°C without shaking. 
Transformed cells were plated on selective media and incubated for 3-5 days at 30°C.

4. Results and discussion 
4.1 Simultaneous genetic engineering and characterization of the integration sites. 
 
The first step towards CASCADE implementation was to demonstrate that it would be possible to 
carry out simultaneously genomic integrations into the IS using a set of previously constructed 
multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based tools. If the tools were able to work, they would be used to construct 
GAS strains. The main advantage of using multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based tools is optimizing the time 
to construct GAS strains because more than one GA cassette could be integrated into their respective 
IS with a single transformation round.  
 
Moreover, CASCADE requires IS suitable for gene expression. If we managed to integrate a set of 
DNA repair templates harboring YFP into all the IS, we would be able to conduct a fluorescence-
based characterization of the IS to measure their performance as sites for gene expression. 
 
This experimental section consisted of performing simultaneously genomic integrations into the IS by 
transforming K. phaffii with multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids and DNA repair templates 
harboring YFP, measuring transformation efficiencies, validating the correct genomic integration of 
the DNA repair templates into their respective IS, and make a fluorescence-based characterization of 
the IS  
 
4.1.1 K. phaffii transformation with multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based tools 
 
K. phaffii strain sDIV_0147 (ku70Δ::uidA) was transformed with multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based 
plasmids pDIV_519 (targets IS1 and IS3), or pDIV_520 (targets IS1, IS3, and IS7), or pDIV_521 
(targets IS1, IS3, IS7, and IS5). DNA repair templates expressing YFP flanked by 1kb sequences 
homologous to their specific IS were provided during transformations. (Figure 23). After three days 
of incubation, yellow fluorescent colonies appeared on transformation plates, and transformation 
efficiencies were calculated (Figure 24).

4.1.2.3 Fluorescence characterization of the IS 
 
During the simultaneous engineering of the IS, we obtained strains harboring YFP integrated into 
more than one IS (Figure 30). Therefore, we were able to conduct a fluorescence-based 
characterization of the IS to measure how well these sites perform as sites for protein expression

were summited to a fluorescence spectrometry assay. The fluorescence spectrometry results showed 
a homogeneous fluorescence intensity among strains harboring a single copy of YFP. Moreover, the 
fluorescence intensity increased in strains harboring multiple copies of YFP. The fluorescence 
intensity increased approximately one time per copy of YFP integrated (Figure 31).

4.1.3 Chapter discussion 
 
As reported in other K.phaffii simultaneously engineering works, we found that our genetic validation 
efficiencies decreased as the number of intended simultaneous integrations increased (Dalvie et al., 
2020; Liu et al., 2019). Nevertheless, we optimized our genetic validation efficiencies when we realize 
that the brighter colonies from the transformation plates were clones that most likely had integrated 
more than one copy of YFP into their genomes.  
 
Based on this discovery, we could validate K. phaffii strains that simultaneously integrated up to four 
copies of YFP into their genomes. No research work was found in the literature reporting more than 
three simultaneously genomic integrations in K. phaffii using multiplex CRISPR/cas9-based 
technology. However, the visual screening based on fluorescence is not 100% effective since we 
found that approximately 1 out of 5 colonies did not integrate YFP into their respective IS. Therefore, 
colonies with strong visual fluorescence intensities cannot always be related to the number of 
integrated copies of YFP. Unknown genetic mutations could have arisen, causing some colonies to 
overproduce YFP instead. It is well known that variations in physiology and productivities can be 
encountered among K. phaffii transformants (Löbs et al., 2017; Schwarzhans et al., 2017).  
==PAGE:61==
 
 
Implementation of CASCADE platform in Komagataella phaffii 
61 
Nevertheless, since the fluorescence spectrometry results showed that the fluorescence intensity 
increases linearly and according to the number of YFP copies integrated, this is not likely to be 
happening. 
 
The fluorescence characterization of the IS suggests that the expression of a protein of interest could 
have a homogeneous expression and that the amount of protein produced could increase when more 
than one copy of a gene of interest is integrated. Nevertheless, each protein of interest would require 
its expression characterization if it is integrated into the IS. Protein expression does not depend only 
on the number of integrated copies; other factors such as culture conditions, promoters, secretory 
signals, and coexpression of helper proteins also influence the expression (Yamada, 2021). 
 
In conclusion, the IS are suitable for genomic integrations and gene expression. Moreover, multiplex 
CRISPR/Cas9-based tools were successfully used to perform simultaneous genomic integrations into 
the IS. Therefore they were used to construct GAS strains towards CASCADE implementation, as 
seen in the following chapters.

4.2 Construction of GAS strains. 
 
GAS strains are one of the most critical elements in CASCADE. The GAS strains harbor GA cassettes 
integrated into the IS. Currently, there are four IS available to integrate a GA cassette on them. The 
number of integrated GA cassettes defines the number of maximum copies of a gene of interest that 
can be integrated during CASCADE. This experimental section aimed to construct GAS strains 
harboring up to four GA cassettes integrated.  
 
As the first step, it was necessary to delete the AMD2 gene from K. phaffii sDIV_0147 in order to be 
able to use the AmdS counter-selectable marker.  
 
The second step consisted of transforming the obtained strain with multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based 
plasmids and GA cassettes to integrate more than one GA cassette simultaneously.  
 
The third step was to genetically validate the correct integration of the GA cassettes into their 
respective IS. In the final step, the phenotype of the constructed GAS strains was validated by plating 
them on acetamide and fluoroacetamide media to challenge the AmdS marker. The GAS strains 
would grow in acetamide media but could not do in fluoroacetamide media because of the AmdS 
marker found in their GA cassettes.

4.2.1 AMD2 gene deletion in K. phaffii 
 
K. phaffii strains harboring the AmdS counter-selectable marker can grow in media with acetamide 
as the sole nitrogen source. On the other hand, they cannot grow in fluoroacetamide media because 
the AmdS marker converts this compound into a toxic one.

found in K. phaffii wild-type strains. The AMD2 gene from K. phaffii has been cataloged as a putative 
amidase with acetamidase activity, meaning that K. phaffii wild-type strains can sporadically grow in 
media containing acetamide as the sole nitrogen source. Hence, it is necessary to delete the AMD2 
gene from K. phaffii to carry out trustable dominant selections in acetamide (Piva et al., 2018).

counter-selections of GAS strains. Therefore, the first step towards constructing GAS strains was to 
delete the AMD2 gene from K. phaffii strain sDIV_0147 (ku70Δ::uidA) to construct the strain K. phaffii 
sDIV_0210 (ku70Δ::uidA, amd2Δ).

(ssDNA) as a repair template for homologous recombination (Figure 32). Gene deletions in K. phaffii 
using single-strand DNA repair templates have been widely demonstrated at DTU bioengineering

4.2.7 Chapter discussion  
 
The first step in constructing GAS strains was to delete the AMD2 gene from K. phaffii to perform 
dominant selections in acetamide media by using the AmdS marker. We successfully deleted the 
AMD2 gene from K. phaffii by using ssDNA as a repair template. ssDNA has been used to perform 
other gene deletions in K. phaffii (non-published), and its use was inspired in a filamentous fungi 
genetic engineering research work (Nødvig et al., 2018). Genetic engineering in K. phaffii with ssDNA 
could improve GAS strains for specific processes, e.g., deletion of the PEP4  protease has been 
reported to improve production yields in K.phaffii cell factories (Cregg et al., 1993) 
 
Once we constructed the strain sDIV_0210 (ku70Δ::uidA, amd2Δ), we confirmed its phenotype by 
plating it on media with acetamide as the sole nitrogen source. As reported by (Piva et al., 2018), our 
K. phaffii sDIV_0210 stopped forming colonies on acetamide. However, we could detect a thin layer 
of cells with slow growth when plated on acetamide media. A secondary acetamidase activity, the 
acetamide concentration in the media, or impurities in the acetamide supply could explain this growth 
(M. Hamilton et al., 2020).  
 
The possible options to improve the selection in acetamide could be to decrease the acetamide 
concentration in the media or use highly pure acetamide. Another option could be to screen the 
genome of K. phaffii to find other gene/s with acetamidase activity and disrupt them. However, no 
research work was found in the literature reporting genes with acetamidase activity other than the 
AMD2 in K. phaffii. We did not perceive any difference in the growth or fitness of sDIV_0210 
(ku70Δ::uidA, amd2Δ) compared with sDIV_0147 (ku70Δ::uidA), but it is necessary to perform a 
growth rate analysis of sDIV_0210 to confirm it. 
 
During the construction of GAS strains, we could simultaneously integrate up to three GA cassettes 
using multiplex CRISPR/Cas9 technology. We were not able to perform four simultaneous 
integrations of GA cassettes. Only a few colonies grew on the transformation plate expected to have 
colonies with four simultaneous integrations of GA cassettes, and none of them integrated the four 
GA cassettes. Perhaps, a sequence within the GA cassettes could have homology to the upstream 
and downstream sequences where a cut was made, making unprecise integrations of the GA 
cassettes.  
 
 
==PAGE:73==
 
 
Implementation of CASCADE platform in Komagataella phaffii 
73 
An increase in the length of the DNA sequences flanking the GA cassettes could improve homology-
directed repair in K. phaffii (Peña et al., 2018). Another explanation could be wrong processing of the 
multiple sgRNA during their expression, making them unable to guide Cas9 to the cut site. However, 
because we managed to construct a GAS4 strain by retransforming a GAS2 strain with a plasmid 
expressing all four sgRNA, this is not likely to be happening.  
 
In ddition, we realize that the electroporation pulses were affected when more micrograms of GA 
cassettes were added to the transformation mix. Perhaps, improvements in the transformation 
protocol of K. phaffii could be made by finding the best transformation mix ratio for cells and 
micrograms of GA cassettes.  
 
Once we constructed GAS strains, we validated their phenotype by plating them on acetamide and 
fluoroacetamide media. The AmdS marker allowed to perform dominant selections of GAS strains in 
acetamide media either by harboring one or four GA cassettes integrated. On the contrary, when 
plating the GAS strains in fluoroacetamide media, we saw the strongest counter-selection in GAS 4, 
suggesting that the number of copies of the AmdS marker influences the counter-selection. Based on 
this finding, the counter-selection of GAS strains may be improved by constructing GAS strains with 
a higher number of integrated GA cassettes or increasing the fluoroacetamide concentration (M. 
Hamilton et al., 2020). 
 
In conclusion, GAS strains were constructed, and their phenotype was validated. Therefore, we were 
able to continue the process of CASCADE implementation by performing the first test of CASCADE 
in K. phaffii, as seen in the next chapter.

4.3 CASCADE. 
 
Once we constructed GAS strains harboring GA cassettes integrated into up to four IS, we could 
perform CASCADE in K. phaffii. CASCADE aims to integrate multiple copies of a gene of interest by 
transforming a GAS strain with a DA cassette harboring the gene of interest together with a plasmid 
expressing the I-SceI endonuclease. Once inside the cell, the I-SceI endonuclease expressed by the 
plasmid must cut all its restriction sites found within the GA cassettes. Finally, the DA cassettes must 
be integrated into all the cut sites by homologous recombination. This experimental section aimed to 
perform CASCADE in K. phaffii. CASCADE implementation was divided into two experiments: 
 
- 
The first experiment consisted of carrying out a CASCADE proof of concept experiment by 
demonstrating that two “empty” DA cassettes could be integrated. An “empty” DA cassette 
does not harbor any gene of interest but only sequences for homologous-directed repair. By 
doing the CASCADE proof of concept experiment, we would demonstrate that the elements 
of CASCADE are functional on their own and that a minimum of two integrations can be made 
in K. phaffii before trying to integrate a gene of interest.   
 
- 
The second experiment consisted of carrying out CASCADE using DA cassettes harboring an 
insulin coding sequence. GAS strains accepting from one to four copies of a DA cassette will 
be used during this experiment. If more than one copy of insulin were integrated, we would 
have successfully implement CASCADE in K. phaffii for the first time. 
 
4.3.1 CASCADE proof of concept experiment 
 
For this experiment, GAS1 and GAS2 strains were transformed with an empty DA cassette and a 
plasmid pDIV_196 expressing I-SceI (Figure 41). After three days of incubation, colonies appeared 
on transformation plates (Figure 42).

4.3.6 Chapter discussion 
 
In general, CASCADE allowed for markerless, controlled, and precise multi-copy gene integrations in 
K. phaffii. CASCADE relies on K. phaffii strains with a ku70Δ amd2Δ background to enhance HR-
based integrations and enable dominant selections in acetamide (Peña et al., 2018; Piva et al., 2018).  
 
The current methods for multi-copy gene expression in K. phaffii are based on episomal plasmids and 
multiple genomic integrations. Expression by episomal plasmids results in loss of productivities in 
long-term cultivations due to plasmid instabilities. Therefore genomic integrations are preferred, but it 
is necessary to integrate multiple copies of a gene of interest to reach high expression levels. 
However, available methods for multi-copy gene integrations in K. phaffii rely on increasing antibiotic 
concentrations or auxotrophic strains (Gao et al., 2021; Marx et al., 2009; Yamada et al., 2019), which 
are not desirable as cells factories. In addition, methods relying on antibiotics are time-consuming 
and expensive due to the high costs of eukaryotic antibiotics. Moreover, strains with natural antibiotic 
resistance can be obtained for other reasons than the number of integrated copies (Betancur et al., 
2017). 
 
In contrast, CASCADE is an expression platform allowing for multi-copy gene integrations in K. phaffii 
that is not based on auxotrophic strains or constant selection with antibiotics to maintain copy 
numbers (Strucko et al., 2017).  
 
We can speculate about CASCADE stability in K. phaffii. The current integration sites IS1, IS3, IS5, 
and IS7, are located in chromosomes number I, II, III, and IV, respectively. If a crossover occurs 
between sites IS1 and IS3, or IS5 and IS7, a chromosome translocation would be encountered (Gusa 
& Jinks-Robertson, 2019). These crossovers most likely would not affect the CASCADE strains unless 
a gene disruption occurs, resulting in unregulated gene expression (Roukos & Misteli, 2014), 
reduction of growth rates, or cell death. However, the affected CASCADE strains would not dominate 
among the others (Strucko et al., 2017).  
 
 
==PAGE:83==
 
 
Implementation of CASCADE platform in Komagataella phaffii 
83 
On the other hand, a crossover from sites IS1 or IS3 with sites IS5 or IS7 can form a dicentric 
chromosome and an acentric fragment (Gusa & Jinks-Robertson, 2019) leading to cell death (Strucko 
et al., 2017). Therefore, we could say that K. phaffii CASCADE strains are most likely to be stable. 
 
The CASCADE proof of concept experiment demonstrated that the elements of CASCADE worked 
on their own to perform either one or two genomic integrations with an empty DA cassette. Although 
high efficiencies were obtained in this experiment, it will be necessary to perform the same experiment 
for three and four genomic integrations with an empty DA cassette to obtain the complete data about 
CASCADE performance without using a gene interest. This data could be helpful to measure the 
specific transformation efficiencies of CASCADE for any gene of interest. The CASCADE proof of 
concept experiment proved that we could perform visual screenings based on non-fluorescent 
colonies. In addition, we confirmed that the non-fluorescent colonies grew in fluoroacetamide since 
their AmdS marker was lost in all cases. 
 
During CASCADE with a DA cassette harboring an insulin coding sequence, we observed that the 
amount of yellow-fluorescent GAS strains survivors increased on transformation plates as the number 
of insulin copies intended to be integrated increased. The GAS strains survivors had the yellow 
fluorescent phenotype, meaning that a minimum of one GA cassette somehow was not lost. A 
possible explanation could be that the I-SceI could not cut its restriction site within the GA cassettes 
because of random mutations in the recognition sequence. However, unlike restriction 
endonucleases, I-SceI could have tolerated some changes within its recognition sequence (Argast et 
al., 1998; Gimble & Wang, 1996) 
 
Assuming that I-SceI cut its restriction sites within the GA cassettes, the GAS strains could have 
survived by repairing the DSB via a single strand annealing event (SSA) (Bhargava et al., 2016). In 
SSA, the 3’ ends product of the DSB resection could have complemented each other, making the I-
SceI restriction site no longer recognizable. However, it would be required to sequence the GA 
cassettes from the GAS strains survivors to confirm it.  
 
It was observed that the growth of CASCADE colonies harboring more than two copies of the insulin 
coding sequence was affected, which could be explained by metabolic stress (Strucko et al., 2017). 
It could also be possible that insulin somehow caused toxicity among K. phaffii cells since insulin 
structure is very similar to the insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) (Wrigley et al., 2017), which causes 
toxicity to yeast cells (Shuster et al., 1989).

In general, the integration efficiencies of CASCADE were similar to those obtained in simultaneous 
genetic engineering works of K. phaffii (Dalvie et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019). However, higher 
efficiencies were obtained with CASCADE when doing more than two simultaneous integrations. We 
cannot compare CASCADE efficiencies with the currently available systems for multi-copy gene 
integrations in K. phaffii since those are based on auxotrophic strains or a constant selective pressure 
with antibiotics (Betancur et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2021; Yamada et al., 2019). However, CASCADE 
efficiencies could vary depending on the gene of interest to be integrated (Strucko et al., 2017).

5. Conclusion 
CASCADE was successfully implemented in K. phaffii. CASCADE allowed for multi-copy gene 
integrations in K. phaffii without auxotrophic strains or a constant selective pressure with antibiotics. 
Moreover, CASCADE allowed for marker-free, controlled, and precise multi-copy gene integrations. 
CASCADE strains are more likely to be stable. Further characterization of the growth rates and 
production yields from the constructed CASCADE strains is necessary to evaluate their potential as 
cell factories for insulin production. The current fluorescence-based characterization of the CASCADE 
genomic integration sites revealed that they have a homogeneous gene expression. In addition, the 
gene expression increases according to the number of integrated gene copies. Finally, It was 
demonstrated that four simultaneous genomic integrations could be made in K. phaffii by either 
CASCADE or multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based tools.

6. Perspectives 
CASCADE showed promising results in K. phaffii for multi-copy gene integrations. Currently, up to 
four genomic integrations can be done. However, another four new genomic integration sites have 
been proposed to be explored (supplementary material). Therefore, CASCADE capacity could be 
increased to accept more than four copies of a gene of interest in the future. It will be necessary to 
construct the multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based tools and GA cassettes for the new genomic integration 
sites. 
 
Based on the CASCADE transformation efficiencies results, it seems that a fifth integration could be 
challenging. The K. phaffii transformation protocol could be improved to find the best cells/repair 
template ratios to increase the transformation efficiencies when doing CASCADE or when more than 
three GA cassettes are intended to be integrated simultaneously with a single transformation round.  
 
Maybe, two transformations would be required to perform more than four genomic integrations using 
CASCADE. GA cassettes harboring a different visual marker such as RFP could be constructed and 
be integrated into the new integration sites. CASCADE would then be performed by doing the first 
transformation with a CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid targeting the YFP gene from the current four GA 
cassettes and a second transformation with a CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid targeting the RFP gene within 
the new four GA cassettes. 
 
Finally, strains with different genotype backgrounds could be tested as GAS strains, e.g., ku70Δ 
amd2Δ pep4Δ. In addition, the phenotype from the obtained K. phaffii CASCADE strains could be 
reverted to a wild-type phenotype in step by using multiplex CRISPR/Cas9-based tools. However, it 
is necessary to construct these tools.

integration sites. The sites are named IS2, IS4, IS6, IS8, located in chromosomes number I, II, III, IV, 
respectively (Figure 50). Genetic validations shown the correct integration of YFP into the novel IS
