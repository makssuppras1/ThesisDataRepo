Abstract
This study aims to develop the necessary framework to perform absolute environmental sustain-
ability assessments AESAs on educational buildings, based on the newly established principles of
‘Fulfilment of human needs’(FHN) rooted in sufficientarianism developed by Heide et al (2022).
The method is further developed for educational buildings and applied on a case-study at the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU), to assess whether the environmental impacts of the
building exceed the sustainability reference, which in this study is defined based on the planetary
boundaries (PBs) framework developed by Rockström.
The study consists of two parts. The first involves developing a formulation of the share of
the safe operating space (SoSOS) for an educational building using FHN. This is supplemented
by two alternative SoSOS defined for the case study, based on metrics of final consumption
expenditure (FCE), and equal per capita (EPC). The second part consists in performing a life
cycle assessment (LCA) of the university building using SimaPro and Ecoinvent data.
The
analysis consists of a static inventory for construction materials and a dynamic inventory for
the energy consumption of the building, based on forecasts of future energy production and
emissions. The material and energy results are compiled to obtain the best- and worst-case
results for the building. Both the SoSOS definition and LCA are carried out for the categories
of climate change, marine eutrophication, land use, and water resource depletion compatible
with ILCD 2011 Midpoint+, for assessment periods of 50, 80, and 120 years.
Absolute sustainability ratios (ASRs) are used to compare the SoSOS assigned to the building
with its total environmental impacts. For all result scenarios, all SoSOS definitions, and assess-
ment periods defined in the study, the ASR show exceedance of at least one boundary. Thus the
study concludes, that the building is not absolute sustainable according to any of the principles
applied. To achieve a different conclusion for this AESA using the defined SoSOS, fundamental
changes in construction and operation of the building would have been needed.
Although some uncertainty was associated with LCA results, sensitivity studies found that the
governing factor of the AESA was the sharing principles and global boundaries used in the SoSOS
definition. This emphasises the need for standardised guidelines for the practice of AESAs.
Perspectives associated with the defined SoSOS were compared with LCA practice and boundary
definitions in the Danish construction industry. This is to shed a light on how climate change
requirements within the 2023 Danish Building regulations (BR) compare to the PB definitions
used in this study. While the BR requirements are detached from PBs, industry-wide require-
ments are an important element in the transition towards a more sustainable industry. It is
however considered alarming that the current regulations only formulate thresholds in the Cli-
mate Change category. Work is still required to establish an equilibrium between comprehensive
regulations and consideration for PBs.

Introduction
As anthropogenic emissions are causing unprecedented stress on natural resources, humanity
finds itself in a position where the habitability of the Earth is put at risk (Fairbridge, 1989).
The possible implications of climatic changes caused by these stresses have gained political
momentum in recent years. Action on the issues have however been complicated by the respon-
sibilisation needed to mitigate the pressure on the ecosystems of the Holocene state. This is an
important issue to address to prevent unpredictable climatic fluctuations.
The planetary boundary (PB) framework (Rockström et al., 2009) identifies nine earth sys-
tems. Each of these systems has corresponding thresholds, which are oftentimes used as global
references in Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessments (AESAs). This is a novel sus-
tainability practice which allows us to assess impacts of an object on a planetary scale. The
assessment does however require a procedure for sharing the global boundaries and the responsi-
bility to respect them among aspects of society. This has been addressed using different justice
theory practices. It is however relevant to assess the fairness of these existing principles. This is
the focus of the PhD work of Mia Heide who developed the Fulfilment of Human Needs (FHN)
method, based on the objective, that all should be given the opportunity to satisfy basic needs.
Currently, the concept of AESAs is mostly used in the research community, whereas the indus-
try commonly performs Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) on a relative scale that only allows to
compare the impacts of objects against each other or against a reference level. This is the case
in the Danish building industry, which has recently introduced requirements that are based on
empirical data rather than on planetary limits. The Danish building industry accounts for 30%
of Danish greenhouse gas emissions out of which two thirds can be attributed to building opera-
tion (Regeringens klimapartnerskaber, 2020). Therefore, it is relevant to investigate whether the
current requirements within the building regulations are sufficient from a planetary perspective.
The aim of the present study is to conduct an LCA-based AESA of an educational building at
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Emphasis will be put on modelling of the building
operation through forecasts of the future energy mix. The LCA results will then be compared to
sustainability references based on three sharing principles. One of the principles will be adapted
from the ’Fulfilment of Human Needs’ (FHN) methodology developed by Mia Heide.

Figure 1.1: Study Overview.
An overview of this project is shown in Figure 1.1. The AESA in this study consists of two
parts:
a) Developing a formulation of the share of the safe operating space (SoSOS) for an educational

whether the building is absolute sustainable relative to the sustainability references defined
in the study. Those will also be used to assess whether the new Danish Building Regulation
thresholds are sufficient.
The methodology developed for the study is based on the FHN theory defined by Mia Heide.
All sections regarding the approach developed by Heide are described in detail in the theory
section, which we then further develop and adapt in the methodology sections to reflect our
specific case. This study will be a part of Heide’s PhD work.

1.2
Case-Study
The building used for this study is a modern university building located at the DTU campus in
Lyngby. Building 324 was initiated in 2013 as part of the mathematics and computer science
department at DTU Compute. The building has three storeys and a glass facade with a mix
of transparent elements and white blinders. Figure 1.2 shows one of the main entrances and
the exterior facade of the building. Inside it is a large open space separated by eight vertical
towers with lecture rooms and offices. Light-hanging bridges and stairs connect towers and floors
together. Outside the blocks is a large atrium, which extends from floor to ceiling and serves as
a shared study area with furniture islands for study groups. In the atrium, several large trees
stand in each section which creates an outside climate within the building envelope. Figure 1.3
shows a picture of the shared study area with a view of all the floors. The total area of the
building is 4 592 m2. On the ground floor, there are six lecture rooms, with a capacity of 8 to 40
students, 144 in total. The two upper floors hold offices and smaller meeting rooms, with a total
capacity of 62 people on each floor. The basement contains a technical room and tunnels that
connect the two surrounding buildings (Christensen Co, 2023). The construction methods and
materials are traditional for the period it was built in. Heavy structural elements are made of in
situ concrete, concrete elements, and steel. Light structures are made of gypsum, mineral wool,
and steel or wood framework. The facade is primarily made of glass, with aluminium mullions.
The case is provided by Christensen & Co Architects.

2.1
Planetary Boundaries
Technological advances and global population growth are leading to unprecedented stress on
natural resources (Sala et al., 2016), and we are fast approaching ecological tipping points, which
could cause irreversible changes to the environment. New insights about the deterioration of the
Holocene equilibrium due to anthropogenic emissions are continuously gained, and frameworks
are needed to identify the cause of those natural effects.
In 2009 Rockström et al. approached the difficult task of linking cause and plausible effects across
nine areas of protection within the Earth Systems. This led to the definition of a framework of
precautionary boundaries that makes it probable for Earth Systems to stay within the Holocene
equilibrium (Rockström et al., 2009). These are therefore translated to impact thresholds, which
- if exceeded - will present risks of causing significant and irreversible environmental changes
to the Earth. According to Rockström et al., three out of nine PBs were already exceeded in
2009. The work of Rockström et al. was later revised in 2015 and 2022, where the framework
and the current state of emissions were reassessed and the safe operating space was quantified
for additional boundaries (Steffen et al., 2015; Persson et al., 2022). A 2022 illustration of the
nine boundaries, their status, and the safe operating space is shown in Figure 2.1 from the 2022
update (Persson et al., 2022).
2.1.1
Planetary Boundary Allowance
After the development of the PBs definitions, the field of absolute sustainability emerged with
new methods to combine PBs with commonly used midpoint impact categories in LCA via
the use of normalisation references (Bjørn and Hauschild, 2013; Bjørn and Hauschild, 2015).
An example is the European Commission’s Joint Research Centre report from 2016 in which a
safe operating space was determined for categories that match LCA methodology units (Sala
et al., 2016). New ideas also emerged regarding the downscaling of PBs to relevant levels of
interest, such as personal allowances that match LCA impact assessment methods (Doka, 2015)
or downscaling the operating space to national shares (Häyhä et al., 2016). Specific methods for
the derivation of the safe operating space will be investigated and calculated in this study.
Climate Change
Following the establishment of PBs, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)
has published six reports to date (IPCC, 2022) where planetary thresholds defined in the Paris
Agreement are quantified and translated into Shared Socio‐Economic Pathways (SSPs) regarding

2. Theory
Figure 2.1: Illustration of the nine PBs and their current status updated in 2022
by the Stockholm Resilience Centre. (Credit: Designed by Azote for Stockholm Resilience

The SSPs are pathway alternatives for emissions based on population, Gross domestic product
(GDP), economic growth, and climate response projections. They depend on mitigation and
adaptation challenges and range from the best-case scenario, SSP1, which corresponds to effective

2.2
Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment
The quantification of the PBs discussed in the previous section has led to the development
of AESAs, where impacts of an object or product can be deemed absolute sustainable or not
depending on whether its environmental impacts surpass a defined threshold. This is done by
determining the object’s (SoSOS) by scaling the safe operating space (Doka, 2015) according
to a sharing principle that reflects the importance of the object relative to a planetary level
(Ryberg et al., 2016). This proportionalisation of the safe operating space is discussed in the
following sections. The found SoSOS is then compared to the impacts of the object, determined
through an LCA. If the impact is lower than the assigned SoSOS, the object is deemed absolute
sustainable (Fang et al., 2015).
2.2.1
AESA Methods and Sharing Principles
Sharing methods and parameters used for the definition of a share towards a given item or
service should ideally be universally applicable to any aspect of society and based on widespread
consensus.
The distribution of natural resources is, however, a complicated and oftentimes
political issue, as there are many views on how to justly share global resources among nations,
companies, people, etc. The distribution key is commonly based on general social patterns and
currencies. How these are quantified and distributed is a matter of the chosen ethical norm and
sharing principle (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, 2023).
Distributive justice is a result of the underlying sense of fairness in the economic, political, and
societal frameworks of a society. It is a fluid definition based on societal consensus, which can
7
==PAGE:24==
2.2. Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment
2. Theory
change with time depending on the economic or political climate. Egalitarian distributional
justice theory is often applied in the definition of SoSOS (Ryberg et al., 2020). Egalitarianism
is based on the notion that it is unjust for some individuals to be worse off than others. All
resources, welfare, and opportunities should therefore be equally distributed. Another justice
theory frequently used is utilitarianism.
Utilitarian sharing principles maximise the sum of
’welfare’, which necessitates a defined measure for the principle to be operational.
Another
distributional principle is sufficientarianism. This type of justice theory is not based on equality,
but on the importance of everyone having enough. As a result, those below the thresholds of
the defined sufficiency level are prioritised. Libertarianism is also founded on the principle that
everyone should have basic equal rights but otherwise diverts from the before mentioned value-
based distributions, as it rests on the premise that distributions derived from an initially just
system are justified in themselves. An example of this is the attitude that market dynamics are
just, as they are a result of voluntary and just spending by the individual. (Ryberg et al., 2020;
Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, 2023)
An additional method for sharing global resources, not covered by the traditional distributive
justice theories, is the acquired rights (AR) principle. With this approach, the safe operating
space is distributed based on patterns of current or historic social structures such as wealth
or environmental footprint. The approach was used in the definition of reduction targets for
the Appendix I countries of the Kyoto Protocol, as the included industrialised countries and
economies in transition countries committed themselves to reduction targets relative to 1990
levels (Ryberg et al., 2020).
2.2.2
Determining the Share of Safe Operating Space
The anatomy of an SoSOS definition is often to convert the global boundaries to a national
or individual scale using a downscaling measure.
This is followed by appropriate upscaling
parameters to further target the sector, item, or service in question. Elements of justice theory
and sharing principles can be combined in the formulation (Hjalsted et al., 2021).
The application and implementation of the principles depend on quantifiable currencies to be
operational. National populations are monitored and widely accessible, making the parameter
easy to implement in Equal per Capita (EPC) sharing principles, often used in SoSOS definitions.
Economics, a metric that has been used to track how society produces and consumes goods
since ancient times, is also often used as a way of quantifying the value of products or sectors
in the upscaling process. gross value added (GVA) or final consumption expenditure (FCE) are
both economic indicators used as currency in allocation principles. Other intrinsic parameters
for value to individuals, levels of sufficiency etc. are however harder to quantify in globally
applicable measures (Hjalsted et al., 2021; Ryberg et al., 2020).

Life Cycle Assessment
LCA is a practice that can be used to hold actors accountable for the impacts of their emissions
during the lifetime of their products (EC-JRC, 2011). It is most commonly used to compare the
relative sustainability levels of two products that have the same functionality. LCA is also used in
product design to identify potential hotspots of a product and to narrow down which components
could be improved to potentially reduce impacts (Hauschild et al., 2018b). The field is regulated
by European guidelines to a large extent (EC-JRC, 2011; ISO 14044, 2006) in order to ensure
standardisation and transparency. This study will attempt to follow these recommendations
as stated in the International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) guidelines (EC-JRC,
2011).
While the use of LCA for comparisons of products and investigations of hotspots is a good
practice in general, it is important to note that the conclusions of such studies are solely relative.
If a certain product performs better environmentally than another, LCA itself does not provide
any indication of the sustainability of said products in the big picture. This is illustrated in
Figure 2.4 where product A performs better than B, but both exceed the sustainability reference.

which allows concluding whether its impacts are sustainable for the planet. The results of the
LCA of the building are used for a comparison with the SoSOS and further described in Method

2.4
Dynamic Inventory - Energy Modelling
In 2015, 195 nations signed the Paris agreement, in which they agreed to limit the average
global warming to below 2.0 ◦C (United Nations, 2023). In 2020, the Danish parliament agreed
to additional limitations and to decrease greenhouse gas emissions by 70% in 2030 and 100% by
2050 compared to the 1990 levels (Energistyrelsen, 2022c). One of the keystones in reaching this
goal is to transform the energy sector, replacing the current use of fossil fuels with renewable
energy sources in a more efficient energy grid. This energy transformation will have an effect on
the impact of the energy consumed during the use stage. To account for the expected changes
in the electricity sector, a dynamic LCA is established, where the impact of e.g. the energy
mix is modelled for each year and accumulated over the assessment period. This part of the
study focuses on the prediction of future changes in district heating and electricity sources. The
efficiency improvement of photovoltaics PVs and wind power is also taken into consideration.

2.4.1
Energy Mix Projection
The electricity grid in Denmark consists of both a national mix, and of imports and exports to
and from neighbouring countries. A large share of Danish electricity production can be attributed
to wind energy. When the wind speeds are low, electricity is purchased and imported to the
Danish grid from Germany, Sweden, or Norway. On the contrary, when wind speeds are high,
Danish wind turbines produce excess electricity which can be sold to other countries (Energinet,
2022b). Historical energy production until 2023 can be modelled from national data on produced
and imported/exported electricity. Predictions for the future electricity mix are available until
the year 2050 from several sources such as the Danish Energy Agency (Energistyrelsen, 2022b)
and independent agencies such as Ember (Ember, 2022b).
Renewable energy sources such as solar and wind energy are expected to be the main contributors
to the future electricity mix. These sources are often regarded as ”carbon neutral” however this is
true only when the use stage is considered. The production of technologies such as solar panels
and wind turbines still have impacts that need to be accounted for.
The impacts of power
generated by these technologies are expected to become less significant with time, as materials
and production methods improve and as their efficiencies increase. Since predictions regarding
new materials and new manufacturing technologies are limited, in this study a large focus is put
on predicting efficiency improvements.
District heating consumption is a significant contributor to the use stage, and is also forecast
in this study. In Denmark, district heat is delivered to buildings at a regional or oftentimes
local level. The district heating supplied to Building 324 comes from Holte Fjernvarme’s grid.
DTU owns 50% of the company (Holte Fjernvarme, 2022) and has its own heat plant located
at Lyngby campus. The plant is fuelled by natural gas and is connected to the local district
heating grid.

In this section we describe the LCA procedure for the building, starting with the object of
assessment as well as the goal and scope. The definition of the scope also includes an explanation
regarding a separate inventory for materials and energy. These two inventories are then further
described in the two sections that follow. The dynamic LCI is time dependent and complex, this

3.2.1
Object of Assessment
The object of this LCA is Building 324 in Lyngby. As mentioned previously, the building belongs
to DTU and has several functions. The first floor of the building area is dedicated to lecture

researchers. Overall it can be said that the function of the building is to provide a working
environment for students and researchers while complying with all requirements of the Danish
building code. Three periods of assessment will be considered; 50, 80, and 120 year, starting in

The building is connected to the electricity grid and to the local heating station at Holte Fjern-

consumption will remain constant over the years.

Static and Dynamic LCI
Since the study is performed for long assessment periods, it was deemed pertinent to separate
the LCI into two main parts: a static and a dynamic LCI. The static LCI will consist of processes
representing building materials and their replacements during the lifetime of the building. For
the sake of simplicity, we assume that the static LCI processes will remain constant during the
years, even if in reality manufacturing methods and processes in the background system will
become more efficient and less impact intensive. On the other hand, the dynamic LCI which
reflects the use stage of the building, is modelled continuously, with a focus on forecasts of elec-
tricity and district heat mixes used. This is an attempt to improve temporal representativeness
which is crucial in LCAs with such long assessment periods where the use stage represents a
significant share of total impacts. Our procedure is to forecast the resulting impacts for 1 kWh
in our inventory after which they are multiplied by the heat and energy use of the building. An
illustration of the data span of both the static and dynamic LCI is shown in Figure 3.4 below.

biomass and waste incineration is expected to remain constant. The overall heat mix shares

Predicting PV Panel Impacts
Currently, predictions of solar eco-efficiency and impacts are lacking, which is mostly due to the
novelty of PV LCA. However, the efficiencies in the field are increasing steadily and are expected
to entail a decrease in environmental impact. This is why we assume that the reduction in
environmental impacts can in theory be proportionally associated with the increase in efficiencies.
In this section we provide an extensive description of how future impacts of PV are derived. The
resulting impacts [CO2-eq./kWh] are shown in Figure 3.13. The prediction of PV impacts is
done in three parts. First, we forecast the efficiency of PVs in research. This research efficiency
is then used to determine future market efficiencies. Finally, we use those efficiencies to scale
current PV impacts to reflect efficiency improvements.
To determine the efficiency forecast of PV research, we use a combination of historical data
with assumptions and theoretical limits. As a first step we investigate the well documented
historical evolution of PV efficiency in research (NREL, 2022) which is shown as blue dots in
Figure 3.12. The increasing and relatively linear trend of the efficiencies achieved in laboratories
can be combined long-term with a theoretical efficiency threshold, which can be seen as a limit
that PV efficiencies will never surpass. This efficiency maximum was calculated to be set at 68%
for a multi-junction solar cell exposed to unconcentrated sunlight (De Vos, 1980). This value
is based on the mathematical limit of the efficiency function, for an n-layer multi-junction cell
exposed to direct sunlight, where n is assumed infinite. By combining the historical data and
the theoretical limit, we assume that research based time dependent efficiencies will follow an
inverse exponential trend which will stabilise before showing horizontal asymptotic behaviour at
68%. The procedure is shown in Figure 3.12.
The trend we determined for the best PV efficiencies achieved in research is defined by equation
ylab(t) and is shown in blue in Figure 3.12.

Similar efforts were made to assess the impacts of efficiency development within the wind energy

concrete and steel.

5.1
AESA Outcome
It is indisputable that Building 324 is not absolute sustainable according to the SoSOS definitions
of the study. The discrepancy between the established SoSOS and the building’s impacts is
deemed vast regardless of which principle is used and of the assessment period investigated. The
most significant exceedance occurs in the climate change category where the SoSOS assigned to
the building is surpassed up to 26 times.
Reducing the climate change impact of the building to stay within the SoSOS defined by the
EPC+FCE and FHN principles of the study would require considerable impact reductions related
to materials and energy used in the building. As the change in energy sources is part of the
LCA background systems, only reductions within the use of utilities and materials can be altered
at building level. A reduction of 70% to 600% is necessary to satisfy the SoSOSFHN. Such a
reduction is not expected to stem from energy efficiency improvements and is difficult to attain
from improved production of raw materials alone. This means, that the concept of Building 324
might need to be fundamentally rethought to achieve the defined sustainability limit.
Another option to mitigate the challenges would be to elaborate on the FHN sharing principle
further. An idea would be to further differentiate the SC shares according to the specific activities
within each sector. This would allow buildings to take up more of the sector-based SoSOS for
climate change, if other elements in the sector do not require their assigned climate change share.
The uncertainties of this study are present and significant in many of the used components. The
LCA results show a high variation range, however, it is the uncertainties linked to the definitions
of the SoSOS that are the most impactful on the conclusion of this AESA. The same conclusion
was reached by Ryberg in his AESA of an industry case study (Ryberg et al., 2018).
Parameters and uncertainty related to each element of the AESA are addressed in the following
sections.
First with a discussion of the SoSOS definitions, then LCA, followed by industry
perspectives. The chapter is finalised with a short discussion of future work.

5.2.1
Sharing Principles
The SoSOS formulations used in this study are created in the context of the case study of Build-
ing 324 to illustrate the possible outcome space of the AESA. It would, however, be possible
to do other variations of these or define shares depending on other values entirely. The shar-
ing principles could also be adapted to other cases by using corresponding metrics of another
university building.
The three SoSOS are the most lenient for the 50-year assessment period. Still, even for this
shorter assessment period, the SoSOS were exceeded in at least one category, even for the FCE
formulation which presents a more lenient boundary for shorter assessment periods. This is
caused by the fact that SoSOSFCE and SoSOSEPC+FCE are aggregated differently from an
annual accumulation of shares, and the building is thereby gaining high importance due to
the large annual spending in the years following the initiation. Had the FCE-boundaries been
calculated for prolonged assessment periods, they would however, eventually become smaller
than the FHN due to this very same fact. This illustrates the large influence of the length of
the assessment period.
The FCE principle relies on the utilitarian justice theory, using FCE as an indicator of a given
items value to society. The benefit of FCE, is that financial flows are widely monitored and
accessible. Using monetary metrics in sharing principles also secures, that all aspects of the
economy are attributed an SoSOS. A challenge in using this principle is the difference in national
economy that distorts the value of items on a global level. The direct link between money spent
and assigned impact encourages further financial spending to secure a higher SoSOS. Since the
current economy is not absolute sustainable, this incentive seems contradictory.
The justice theory and currency used in the measure used for scaling the PBs from global to
national level has a great impact on the results, as evident from the vast differences in SoSOSFCE
and SoSOSEPC+FCE results, which are otherwise the same. These reflect that the share of the
PBs allocated to Denmark when using present GDP is five to six times larger than the ones
allocated based on population predictions. This is because Denmark is wealthy relative to its
population size.
While the metrics of FCE and EPC are easy to use, the principle does not consider whether
the shares assigned by the principle are fair or sufficient to satisfy and enable satisfactory living
conditions for the global population. The shares are highly dependent on which currency is
chosen to perform the downscaling. In our case, Denmark gets a large share based on national
GDP compared to global GDP. This might not be the case for other countries where their GDP
does not entitle them to shares big enough to support basic living conditions because the national
financial activity is small. On the other hand, Denmark obtains a much smaller share when the
metric used is population numbers. This is because on a planetary scale, the Danish National
financial activity constitutes a larger proportion than the number of inhabitants. While EPC
is widely regarded as a more just metric, for some communities EPC distribution of PBs might
result in communities of some regions not being able to support themselves due to challenges

FHN, being a sufficiantarian sharing principle, meets the before-mentioned challenges by differ-

What Heide (2022) introduced with the FHN methodology is a principle that prioritises sufficient
nutrition and differentiates the safe operating space assigned on a sector level based on the impact
category in question. In her work, Heide bases this differentiation on the consumption of less
affluent countries deemed sustainable. This assumption is based on Engel’s law stating that in
these countries most of the income is spent on necessities, contrarily to wealthy countries that
are not necessarily representative of sustainable spending habits, since money is also spent on
non-essentials. Had these been used as a reference, insufficient resources would be assigned for
essential goods. This ensures that the boundaries are not disproportionately distributed between
industries and that all objects and services -ideally- should have enough to serve the functions
we need from them. This use of countries with sustainable consumption patterns is what makes
FHN different from the other sharing principles presented in this study, and what makes the
result patterns stand out from the alternative SoSOSFCE and SoSOSEPC+FCE definitions in a
Danish context.
When FHN is used within a construction context, the principles are to be developed for a specific
building typology and the SoSOS of a building is defined based on the value of the service it
provides within its respective sector. This results in significant differences in the SoSOS between
typologies and detaches the allocation of shares from the traditional metrics of area or costs.
This differentiation based on value to society is crucial and is apparent when comparing to other
building typologies. One can imagine that the value of one square metre of a hospital is more
valuable to society than one square metre in Building 324.
One challenge with the FHN method is that the shares defined for categories are not specifically
targeted depending on the emission type. In our case, the university building is not exceeding
the boundaries in any of the non-GWP boundaries and it could be argued that these shares
might be more crucial elsewhere, where the needs of the educational sector are not met in the
categories in question.

Defining the Safe Operating Space
There are layers of methodologies involved in the definition of the PB-definitions and the sub-
sequent translation to LCA compatible boundaries that enable the practice of AESA. This
introduces several uncertainties and makes the boundaries susceptible to change, as emission
levels change and models are updated and improved. This is evident from the continuous up-
dates on the status of the Earth systems considered in the PBs, and the recurring publications
on climate change from the IPCC.
An effect of this is that the Steady State boundaries defined by Bjørn and Hauschild, 2015 need
to be readjusted according to the updated IPCC operating space (IPCC, 2022). These newest
IPCC boundaries are taken into account in a yet unpublished article for RR (Petersen et al.,
2022), where the 2015 Steady State methodology is used for the updated boundaries and where
newly updated characterisation factors for methane are also implemented. This new boundary
used in the RR would however have further reduced the Steady State boundary by 64% from
6.88E+12 kg CO2-eq./year to 2.51E+12 kg CO2-eq./year. As none of the ASRs, regardless of
the principle used, result in a sustainable assessment for Building 324, this change would not
alter the main conclusions of the study. It would however dramatically increase the rate at which
the total climate change impacts of the study exceed the assigned SoSOS within the steady state
boundary.
Another crucial issue with current boundaries is that some impact areas should be assessed on
a local or regional level which is generally not possible with the current global characterisation
models such as ILCD Midpoint+. This is relevant for the boundaries of marine eutrophication,
land use, and water resource depletion used in our study.
While the impacts found in LCA do not show indications of alarming emissions in these cate-
gories, these should be further assessed on a regional and local level in order to comprehensively
investigate the case building’s impacts. Although these investigations are pertinent, they are
difficult to carry out, since they differ depending on the geographical and climate conditions in
all places of production involved in the materials used in construction and building operations.
That being said, the results generated by the ILCD 2011 Midpoint+ methodology in this study
provide an initial insight into the issues related to these impact categories.
The tone of the recent research and reporting on the state of the PBs is generally increasing

5.3
LCA
LCA is a highly useful tool that can be used to identify environmental issues associated with
the object of analysis and allows to model a wide range of components on a precise level.
In this case, the specific use of dynamic modelling allows to account for changes in emissions
due to the transition towards renewable energy, which is not commonly done in LCA. The total
results of the two LCA components allow us to identify critical impacts related to the construc-
tion and operation of the case-study building, and to conclude on the absolute sustainability of
with high confidence.
At the same time, conducting an LCA also involves methodological choices and data limitations,
causing uncertainties that must be considered when processing results.
5.3.1
Static Inventory Limitations
The overall uncertainty of the static LCI stems from limited access to data, and increases with
time. This is due to the fact that processes are not updated when materials are replaced at the
end of their lifetimes, which means that their future emissions might be overestimated.
One of the main issues regarding materials in general is that the most impactful parameters are
also the most sensitive. Even moderate uncertainties for those parameters therefore induce a
high variation of the overall impacts. This is a major issue when modelling elements such as
reinforced concrete, where the quantities are relatively precise but where the impact is significant
and vastly dependent on the specific type and resistance of the material. A seemingly apparent
solution is for contractors to provide more documentation regarding materials and consumption
during the construction phase.
Although the documentation practices related to LCA have
improved in the ten years since the construction of Building 324, there are still aspects of the
construction phase, where this issue still present in the industry of 2023.
Another challenge in the modelling of materials stems from the database itself. Because while
Ecoinvent is a database with many product types modelled in detail, some types of construction
materials are not included. While the openness surrounding the process inventory in the database

5.3.2
Dynamic Inventory Limitations
The uncertainties of the dynamic inventory mostly originate from the prevailing unknowns en-
countered when predicting the future.
Both the Local and national scenarios for future district heating are dependent on external
factors. The heat mix that will be supplied in the upcoming years is highly dependent on the
future choices of Holte Fjernvarme management, which will be influenced by the Danish Energy
Agency and the heat fuel market in general. At this point, we rely solely on interpretations of
the 2022 national heat mix predictions by the Danish Energy Agency and the current trends in
the heat market with an additional educated guess regarding the lifetime of a natural gas power
plant for the Local scenario.
The heat market is volatile and depends on international dynamics. It is also possible that, due
to unforeseen circumstances, the transition to lower-impact heat sources will occur faster than
predicted by the Danish Energy Agency. It is important to question, however, how fast local
grids can adapt to such changes. In this case, we estimate that the conservative Local scenario
will have the highest likelihood.
Another important note regarding electricity and heat modelling is that in this study, the pro-
cesses and impacts of energy production were not altered with time with the exception of PVs.
This means that the projections of the use stage impacts are presumably overestimated, as we
expect all technology efficiencies, renewable or not, to improve with time.
The two electricity scenarios are established based on existing predictions. This means that the
uncertainty and assumptions of those predictions propagate onto this study. For the Frozen
Policy electricity scenario, a prediction from the Danish Energy Agency released in 2022 was
used as a base. The forecasts released by the agency only include energy agreements accepted in
parliament prior to the year of publication. The report includes a capacity increase in offshore

updated.

Conclusion
An AESA has been performed for a university building based on the FHN methodology for the
categories of climate change, marine eutrophication, land use, and water resource depletion. The
LCA of the building was carried out in two parts, with a static LCI of materials and a dynamic
LCI for energy consumption throughout the use phase. Results show that the largest impacts for
the categories of climate change were found within the materials used in the building, whereas
the energy use of the building influenced the categories of land use and water resource depletion
the most.
Total results revealed that for the defined assessment periods of 50, 80, and 120 years, the
building is not absolute sustainable according to the FHN approach, nor to the other alternative
sustainability references applied. The greatest exceedances of the case-specific boundaries were
found within the category of climate change. Achieving different conclusions of the AESA would
either require drastic changes in the construction and operation of Building 324, or significant
alterations of the composition of the sharing principles.
The principles used in the study results in very different shares of the global boundaries being
allocated to the building. The most restrictive shares were SoSOSEPC+FCE which led to a share
five times smaller, than its GDP-based counterpart, SoSOSFCE. The main share definition,
SoSOSFHN, differs from the others applied in the study, due to its sustainable consumption PB
allocation. This differentiation between impact categories shows a different pattern in AESA
results and is a benefit of the FHN method. The practice could also advantageously be extended
to further distribute SC and SQ shares between elements of the sector.
Sensitivity studies on the elements of the AESA showed that though uncertainty associated with
LCA could ultimately cause great variations in total results, the decisive factor of the AESA
was the SoSOS definition and its underlying assumptions related to sharing principles and global
boundary definitions.
The continuous updates of the underlying theory of the AESA methodology pose a general issue
when working with PBs. The great methodology sensitivity of the assessment highlights the
need for streamlined guidelines for the practice of AESA. The study concludes, that while the
BR requirements are not enough to reach absolute sustainability as defined in the boundaries
defined for the study, industry-wide requirements are an important element in the transition of
the industry and provide a simple methodology, that will hopefully encourage the widespread
use of LCA and AESA within the sector.
Efforts are however still needed to establish an equilibrium between comprehensive and easily
applicable methodology and considerations of all PBs in the Danish building industry.

We then use the equation for the average efficiency 𝑦𝑎𝑣𝑔(𝑡), to determine the environmental impact of 
future photovoltaic energy. An initial idea had been to modify the 2015 ecoinvent process in SimaPro to 
match the predicted average efficiencies for all years until 2133. However, the lack of transparency for 
calculations made within the ecoinvent database, especially concerning elementary flows, did not allow 
to modify the process components in order to account for the predicted increased efficiencies. Due to 
these encountered challenges, we decided that since the efficiency of a solar cell is defined as a 
percentage output out of incoming sunlight, a good guesstimate would be to assume that the predicted 
efficiency would be inversely proportional to impact. 
The recent ‘Carbon Neutrality in the UNECE Region: Integrated Life-cycle Assessment of Electricity 
Sources’ report (UN,2022), presents LCA impacts (with ILCD methodology) for 1 kWh coming from poly-
Si, ground-mounted, photovoltaic power production, in Europe, 2020. This specific process was chosen 
among the two ground-mounted solar farm options. We conservatively selected this specific panel as it 
was the worst performing alternative among the two ground-mounted panels present in the UN report 
suitable for solar farming. It is important that the presented impacts are for the whole European region 
which is probably an overestimation, however it is not possible to recreate the inventory upon which 
this data is based, therefore we chose to continue with the European values. Another issue with the UN 
report is that while the results are calculated with the ILCD methodology, four of the categories have 
units differing from the ones used in this AESA. This means that for the four categories: particulate 
matter, ionizing radiation, land use and water resource depletion, the 2020 results were taken from the 
SimaPro simulation of  ‘Electricity, low voltage {DK}| electricity production, photovoltaic, 3kWp slanted-
roof installation, multi-Si, panel, mounted | Cut-off, U’ instead of the UN report. This choice is deemed 
conservative, as the SimaPro Ecoinvent process performs worse than the ILCD UN results for all 
categories that are availiable for comparison. The values for the impacts of 1kWh from photovoltaics in 
2020, and the sources of data for each category are shown in Table B.2.3.1.

B.2. Modelling of PV impacts
B.2.5
Limitations of PV forecasting
A multitude of limitations occur within procedure when attempting to forecast photovoltaic
impacts 120 years into the future, especially within such a short time frame allowed for this
section of the project. These limitations are discussed in the two following sections. The first
section regarding the limitations of PV efficiency modelling and the second relating to the overall
limitations faced when estimating the impacts.
When modelling the efficiency of PV cells multiple assumptions have been made to allow a
simplified generalised procedure for all types of photovoltaic cells, this generalisation itself is
linked to uncertainties, especially concerning emerging technologies that might achieve better
efficiencies than the ones that exist today or simply provide other renewable means of creating
energy which will take over from the solar sector.
The practical threshold of 60% we use,
adapted from De Vos’ theoretical 68% (De Vos, 1980) is also a limiting factor and in reality,
there is no way to predict whether 60% is a realistic value for the final practical threshold. In
reality, practical limitations are likely to occur and lower the efficiency threshold or slow down
the efficiency progression which in turn would significantly alter long time predictions for both
laboratory and market efficiencies. On the other hand, it is also possible that the use of solar
concentrators will become a standard procedure and that De Vos’s theoretical threshold for
concentrated sunlight of 86% should be used instead. It is also important to note that while the
predicted curves we found for efficiency forecasting present very good and excellent R2 values for
respective laboratory and market efficiency predictions, it is crucial to note that these curves are
based on the limited availability of historical data which only represents a small concentrated
interval, in the first section of the estimated time span. Another assumption had to be made
further on in the process when forecasting impacts based on the efficiencies combined with
the UN Carbon neutrality report results (UN, 2022a). The LCA presented in the report was
calculated with ILCD categories, however, four of them did not correspond to ILCD categories
used in this AESA and present in the SimaPro ILCD methodology. This means that for the four
categories: particulate matter, ionizing radiation, land use and water resource depletion, the
2020 results were taken from the SimaPro simulation of ‘Electricity, low voltage DK| electricity
production, photovoltaic, 3kWp slanted-roof installation, multi-Si, panel, mounted | Cut-off,
U’ instead of the UN LCA report. This is an additional source of uncertainty, however, it is
deemed conservative, as the SimaPro process performs worse than the ILCD LCAs for all other
categories available for comparison.
The impact scores provided by the UN Carbon neutrality report (UN, 2022a) also carry their
uncertainty in terms of location-dependent solar exposure. The report only states LCA results
for a solar panel located in Spain (where the power output of PV is 2320 kWh/m2/y) and only
mentions the corresponding power output in Denmark (power output of PV is 936 kWh/m2/y).
This ratio of the two power outputs was assumed directly proportional to efficiency and evaluated
in a sensitivity analysis for the two electricity mix scenario impacts, following the sensitivity
coefficient procedure from Chapter 11 in the LCA Handbook (Hauschild et al., 2018b) and is
documented in Appendix B.2.4 Sensitivity of the electricity mix scenarios to PV power output.

E
Project Description
The purpose of this Master thesis is to conduct an LCA-based absolute environmental sustain-
ability assessment (AESA) of a building at the technical university of Denmark (DTU).
In the assessment the main focus is testing a new sharing principle called fulfilment of human
needs (FHN) and compare it with one or two other sharing principles.
The AESA will be conducted consisting of two parts:
1) Assigning a share of the safe operating space (SoSOS) for the building with 3 different
sharing principles. In this part a large focus will be put on developing the formula for the share
determined with the FHN principle.
2) An LCA of the building including a dynamic LCA for the energy use (including several
forecast scenarios for the energy mix consisting of different shares and efficiencies of the renewable
energy sources). The LCA of the building and the energy use will be modelled in SimaPro for
comparison.
The time span of the assessment is 50, 80 and 120 years.
The influence of the chosen sharing principles on the share of safe operating space will be
evaluated and compared to the requirements defined in the volunteering sustainability class
and the Danish regulation (building code) implemented in January 2023, which permit 8 and
12 kg CO2-eq/m2/year respectively, to determine in which ways the current requirements are
beneficial or disadvantageous. This will be followed by a discussion of why it might be necessary
to develop methods to differ the CO2 requirements between building typologies.
Learning Objectives
• Explain how fulfilment of human needs is essential to include and consider in sustainability
assessments.
• Conduct an LCA-based absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA) for a
specific case study (a building with educational purpose) and determine whether the build-
ing remains within the assessed share.
• Utilise and compare different sharing principles to define a specific building’s share of the
PBs (share of safe operating space) and asses the challenges and benefits of the different
principles.
• Conduct a dynamic LCA and estimate long-term forecast for energy production and the
emissions hereof.
