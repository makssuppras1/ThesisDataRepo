Abstract
Since the introduction of the shipping container in the 1950s, the liner shipping industry has proven be an
essential part of the world economy. Currently, the entire shipping industry is totalling more than 80% of the
international trade volumes. However, the environmental cost of conducting this extensive sea-born trans-
portation is estimated to be equivalent to 2.2% of the global CO2 emissions. For this reason, the industry
has been subject to a decarbonization process in the recent years. The most radical and effective proposed
solution is the introduction of a new maritime fuel - thus moving away from the traditionally used bunker
fuel. The introduction of an alternative fuel (AF) presents a great set of unknowns. Amongst these are the
sourcing/production sites of AF and how this might affect the network of shipping routes. This project will
be amongst the first to look into exactly how shipping routes are affected by using and the sourcing of AF.
For this, a traditional Liner Shipping Network Design Problem is considered with an extension focused on
AF sourcing. The overall problem framework is solved using an Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search and
applied to the second largest multi-hub instance (WorldSmall) (WS) known from LINER-LIB.
The final results were quite promising. The overall best found solution was less than 2% worse than the best
known solution for WS. Even though the largest difference in profit between a non-AF and AF solution was
13.2%, the ALNS was capable of finding network structures that led to promising AF production prices. The
found AF production sites were all part of the same group of seven port locations. These seven locations
favoured a proximity to European ports and had wind turbines as the most crucial renewable energy source.
Lastly, it was found that in order to level out the difference between non-AF and AF-based network, a bunker
levy or increase of freight rate seems unavoidable. Here, an increase in freight rate of 3.7% would result in a
network running on ammonia to be equally profitable to a non-AF network. In combination with a bunker
levy, the increase of freight rate might even be lower.
In total, the results seen in this project indicate that introducing AF to liner shipping is a feasible and
possibly even profitable action. Naturally, a great deal of technological developments are essential to achieve
this goal. However, the utopian idea of liner shipping running on AF in the nearby future might not seem
that far-fetched after all.

Introduction
Since the introduction of the shipping container in the 1950s, the liner shipping industry has proven be be
an essential part of the world economy. Currently, the entire shipping industry is totalling more than 80%
of the international trade volumes. However, the environmental cost of conducting this extensive sea-born
transportation is estimated to be equivalent to 2.9% of the global CO2 emissions - with container vessels
accounting for a quarter of those emissions [16]. In the recent years, elements like Energy Efficiency Design
Index (EEDI) and the application of slow steaming have respectively been used to limit the CO2 emissions
for newly built ships and reduce bunker consumption - leading to lower emissions since 2007. However, the
shipping industry’s emissions are highly volatile with respect to the general transport demands and since
these are expected to increase in the future so are the emissions [13].
In order to handle the significant Green House Gas (GHG) emissions, several strategic plans have been pro-
posed. In 2018, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) adopted an Initial Strategy deciding the
industry target to be to decrease the maritime GHG emissions by 50% compared to the 2008 levels by 2050.
It is worth noticing that this goal is a general guideline set by the organization and thus by no mean legally
binding for the shipping companies. For this reason, it is of much greater significance that the largest liner
shipping company A.P. Moller Maersk launched their Towards a Zero-Carbon Future strategy in the same
year of 2018. A strategy that initially targeted a net-zero CO2 emission from their operations in 2050. The
strategy was considered as quite ambitious, which is noticeable when comparing to the Initial Strategy by
IMO. Maersk had a milestone of achieving a 60% CO2 reduction by 2030, which is highlighted to be a greater
reduction and occurring 20 years before the IMO deadline. Maersk has since changed their strategy such
that the current goal is to achieve net-zero CO2 emission already by 2040 - Starting to implement new vessels
operating on alternative fuels (AF) from 2023 [26].
Fundamental for all plans towards decarbonizing the liner shipping is the introduction of AF to replace the
current fossil-based fuels. These new AF all have in common that during their life cycle they have a low-to-
no carbon footprint. Among these AF, ammonia is considered to be a highly promising long-term solution.
Currently, there are a great deal of uncertainties related to ammonia’s operational usage and availability. The
availability of ammonia will include both the actual production and distribution to the liner network - both
of which will impact the price of the using ammonia as a maritime fuel [1]. For the next decade, methanol is
considered to be the gateway into using AF within the liner shipping industry. Methanol as a fuel is a quite
mature technology and uncertainties related to its usage are resolving around large scale availability and a
competitive operational price [26]. Traditionally, the bunker prices have a significant impact on the network
design and the fleet deployment. Therefore, when introducing a new fuel, it is highly relevant to look into how
this will affect (and potentially) change the design of the liner networks. The challenge of decarbonizing the
liner shipping using AF will thus have a two-folded objective seen from the network operational perspective,
namely the sourcing of the fuel and the route network effects [29].
In this project, these objectives will be investigated by solving the Liner Shipping Network Design Problem
(LSNDP) and its associate Cargo Routing Problem (CRP). The overall solution framework will be based on

an Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic. In LSNDP, the essential part is the cre-
ation of rotations such that container demand can be delivered - this delivery is found in the CRP. Here, it is
noted that a rotation is defined as a sequence of port calls where the container vessel to go to load/unload its
containers. A set of rotations then make up an entire liner shipping network. As an addition to the LSNDP,
the Alternative Fuel Sourcing Problem (AFSP) will be defined. The AFSP will resolve around the problem of
sourcing AF, which will be integrated into LSNDP to model fuel supply/demand in the liner shipping network.
In order to solve the LSNDP with AFSP on a global scale, the WorldSmall (WS) instance known from
LINER-LIB is used [5]. WS will not to a full extent represent an entire liner shipping operation such as the
one of Maersk. A single instance truly representative of Maersk’s operation would be too computationally
difficult to find quality solutions or even find a feasible solution. In this context WS fits perfectly, since it
will reflect a global operation while being solvable within reasonable time.
The project will contain the following sections: In Section. 2, a literature review is conducted with focus on
previous work for LSNDP and CRP. Furthermore, the literature review will present a general introduction
to research related to fuel usage in liner shipping. In Section 3, the perspective of introducing AF into liner
shipping is presented along with the AFSP itself. A general presentation to LSNDP is given in Section 4
along with a description of the version considered in this project. In Section 5, the CRP is explained and
the chosen solution approach is presented. In order to generate initial solutions to the LSNDP, a backbone
flow method has been implemented, which is presented in Section 6. In Section 7, the network improvement
metaheuristic is presented along with a parameter tuning. Lastly, the computational results will be shown
in Section 8 with the final conclusion and future work ideas presented in Section 9 and 10, respectively.

Literature Review
The purpose of this project is to investigate the decarbonization of the liner shipping industry using mathe-
matical methods. The work will resolve around the LSNDP-CRP while also considering the construction of
production facilities and operational usage of AF. The literature review of research already done within this
field will be split into three parts. The first part in Section 2.1 will focus on the existing methods for dealing
with the general LSNDP-CRP, whereas a more elaborate review of the CRP is considered in Section 2.2. The
CRP is here investigated separately, since this is essentially a Multi Commodity Flow (MCF) problem, which
is a well-known problem with multiple proposed solution methods. Lastly, in Section 2.3, a more general
review of fuel usage within liner shipping is presented. This will both include the ’traditional’ consideration
of fuel usage as seen from the Operations Research (OR) perspective and also present some of the preliminary
work regarding the transitioning into using AF. Notice especially the fields under review in Section 2.2 and
2.3 both contain a vast of amount of research papers. The work presented in Section 2.2 has been selected
based on their relation to LSNDP. Meanwhile the papers presented in Section 2.3 have been selected because
they ’fit the narrative story’ of implementing AF in liner shipping.
2.1
LSNDP-CRP
The LSNDP is operating at the strategic planning level within the liner shipping industry. The LSNDP aims
to construct a set of services that satisfies a group of origin-destination demands while minimizing operational
expenses with the resources available. The problem is often applied to a large scale operational area and
its two main versions with/ without transit times have both been proven to be NP-hard [2] [19]. For this
reason, a great deal of solution methods have been proposed and here Meng et al. (2014) and Christiansen
et al. (2020) are recommended for providing a general overview of these.
Agarwal and Ergun (2008) were among the first to propose a solution method that could solve the LSNDP-
CRP simultaneously. This is done via a two-phase algorithm, which as the name suggest solves the problem
in two stages: Designing the rotations (LSNDP) and then flow the containers through the found network
(CRP). Here, there occurs a feed-back process such that results from the second phase is used to improve the
rotations in the first phase. Agarwal and Ergun uses a total of three different methods consisting of greedy
heuristics, column generation and Benders decomposition. The paper achieves quite promising results when
applying the column generation and Benders decomposition methods, however, some pitfalls are highlighted.
Firstly, the used test instances are rather small in comparison to actual operational networks. Secondly,
throughout the majority of their work they have to assume a transshipment cost of zero due to the structure
of their methods. Transshipment costs will have a significant influence on the network design and in general
transshipments are avoided. For Maersk only around half of their containers experience transshipment [22].
So ignoring transshipment costs will lead to biased and less valid results with too many transshipments. They
do conduct an analysis of the effect of transshipment costs by using a fixed cost for all ports. However, this
only shows the expected tendency of fewer transshipments at higher transshipment costs and thus miss the
true influence of diverse transshipment costs in the network construction.
These drawbacks regarding transshipment cost and small scale applicability are managed directly in the
3
==PAGE:14==
work by Mulder and Dekker (2014). Like Agarwal and Ergun, they also apply the two-phase methodology
and use a genetic algorithm to solve the problem. Mulder and Dekker use a port-specific transshipment
cost when solving the CRP and thus have these costs as fundamentally embedded in their solution method.
Furthermore, by using a aggregation method of clustering ports, they are capable of solving a large instance
covering 58 ports on the Europe-Asia trade lane. By clustering ports, they can reduce the complexity of the
problem structure, but the result relies heavily on the hub-and-spoke structure with feeder services within
the clusters. Since these clusters are defined by distance, the outcome might reduce the significance of larger
ports closely located to each other. They show how their best obtained result yields a profit improvement of
more than 10% compared to their reference network, which is based on the corresponding Maersk network.
It is, however, worth noticing that the used data is purely based on external and academical sources and
own assumptions. As per the authors themselves, it is difficult to obtain real or standardized data. For
this reason, it can be questioned how valid their results are and likewise how effective their solution method
actually is.
Around the same time as Mulder and Dekker, Brouer et al. (2014) presented their work of a base IP model
for the LSNDP. Like the work presented in Mulder and Dekker, the base IP model has a clear distinction
between transshipment and (un)loading cost, but could model rotations such that they at least have a bi-
weekly frequency, whereas Agarwal and Ergun and Mulder and Dekker only considered weekly port calls.
For this reason Brouer et al. does allow a greater flexibility and utilization of the constructed services with
respect to the vessels, since the base IP model only allowed a butterfly rotation structure. The proposed
solution method is also based on the two-phase approach, where a heuristic column generation algorithm is
used to generate rotations. These rotations are collected in a candidate list and a tabu search algorithm is
then applied to construct the network.
A highly noteworthy element from Brouer et al. is how they made their instance data publicly available and
referred to as LINER-LIB. LINER-LIB, which is elaborated further upon in Section 4.2, consists of seven
test instances spanning from a regional to global domain. Brouer et al. provide a thorough presentation
of the data including distance between ports, demand matrices and vessel information - highlighting the
authenticity/ validity, since it has been developed in collaboration with Maersk. LINER-LIB has then been
used extensively for benchmarking results for LSNDP-CRP solution methods since its publication. Brouer
et al. provided the first LINER-LIB results, where they were able to solve six of the seven instance, but not
to optimality with the largest left unsolved. To highlight the sizes of the test instances (and the problem
itself), it is noted how they solved the smallest instance within five minutes and the largest possible took
four hours.
Taking the above mentioned solutions into account, the work presented by Krogsgaard et al. (2018) had a
significant impact on the LSNDP solution methods. Krogsgaard et al. proposed a new method to counter
the greatly used two-phase approach and showing drastically lower run times. Opposed to the two-phase,
Krogsgaard et al. propose a backbone flow approach, which essentially aims to reverse the solutions process
such that routes are constructed based on container flow. An initial container flow is found via a relaxed
network (denoted backbone network) and based on this the rotations are then found - thus aiding the search
4
==PAGE:15==
for rotations with a greater focus on the initial container demands. Krogsgaard et al. use only heuristics
in their solution approach with a Variable Neighbourhood Search (VNS) as the driving algorithm. A fast
Lagrange heuristic used to solve the CRP leads to low run times - more on this in Section 2.2. By only
allowing 10% of the runtime used by Brouer et al., Krogsgaard et al. is capable of finding a better solution
in four out of six instances. Futhermore, Krogsgaard et al. are the first to report a solution to the largest
instance available from LINER-LIB. In general, the structure of the solutions are complex like those seen
by Mulder and Dekker. However, like all the above mentioned solution methods, Krogsgaard et al. fail to
include transit time constraints - making the solutions less realistic for an actual operator.
Koza et al. (2020) present some of the most recent work done within the field of LSNDP-CRP. In their work
Koza et al. propose a model integrating LSNDP-CRP and scheduling with the purpose of including transit
time constraints. This integrated model framework is solved using a column generation mathheuristic that
utilizes advanced LP techniques. Including transit times makes it necessary to construct a rather complex
time-space network which is depending on the used time discretization. In their work Koza et al. show the
importance of including transit times, since they observe significant differences (for direct vs transshipped
containers) between networks being scheduled or not in the solution process. Koza et al. is able to solve
six of the seven LINER-LIB instances and shows improving solutions in all of these when considering the
classical LSNDP-CRP. To the extend known by this author, it is the best preforming solution considering
transit times for the LINER-LIB instances. However, taking transit times into account also comes with an
extensive run time cost. Koza et al. use a time limit of 129,600 seconds for their largest solved instance with
a time discretization of 12 hours. In comparison, Krogsgaard et al. solved the unscheduled version with a
time limit of 1440 seconds.
In Section 4.1, an overview of the LSNDP work presented is summarized in a table alongside with a description
to the LSNDP considered in this project.
2.2
MCF
As specified earlier, the CRP can be considered to be a MCF problem, since each pair of origin-destination
container demand can be labelled as a unique commodity. Given a set of rotations, this will represent a net-
work structure to flow the commodities through - assuming transshipments between rotations and additional
omissions arcs to handle surplus and thus unmet demand. The MCF is a well-known, large-scale and difficult
problem within network optimization and is often considered to be the cornerstone within network models
[33]. Since its introduction in 1958, a great deal of extensions and solution methods have been proposed.
In general, solution methods to the MCF can be categorized in three overall groups; approximation, exact
methods or heuristics. Here, Salimifard and Bigharaz (2022) is a highly recommended reading in order to
gain an overview of the application of these methods and associate industry fields.
Cariou et al. (2018) introduces a heuristic method using a genetic algorithm-based (GA) approach to solve the
MCF embedded in a LSNDP. Cariou et al. initially models the LSNDP as a Mixed Integer Problem (MIP),
but later characterize the entire problem using a total of four solution chromosomes representing ports call
sequence, speed levels, selected commodities and lastly the commodity flow. In this solution setting, the
5
==PAGE:16==
difficulty of the MCF is, however, reduced drastically. This is due to the scope of the considered LSNDP,
since their Europe-Asia instance only considers 17 ports that can be covered using a single-service system.
Following this choice, the commodity flow is deduced from the other chromosomes, where the selected com-
modities are fully assigned to the route. For those arcs having an overflow, a simple reduction of commodity
flow is executed based on a random selection. As expected when using a heuristic Cariou et al. reports low
run times, but they do also observe convergence for their largest instance, which gives the possibility of an
optimal solution. However, they lack a more elaborate focus on transshipment of commodities, since their
current setup resemble a simple direct service operation. Their single-service system relies greatly on the
input demand data and thus lacks the possibilities (and complexity) of transshipment with multiple routes -
especially if the method was applied to a larger operational area.
Krogsgaard and Thorsen (2016) proposed a heuristic price-directive decomposition approach when solving
the MCF for the LSNDP. This approach can be categorized as an approximation method, since it relies
on a Lagranian Relaxation (LR). Krogsgaard and Thorsen solve some of the largest instances presented in
LINER-LIB - thus considering large networks with up to 9615 commodities. Their primarily focus has there-
for been to minimize the run time while still achieving consistent good results when solving the MCF. For
this reason, Krogsgaard and Thorsen solve their LR MCF model heuristically where their relaxed model can
be decomposed to a series of shortest paths subproblems. Through an iterative process, they manage to both
adjust their Lagrangian multipliers and establish an overall feasible solution by merging the subproblems
via a repair function. For an instance with 47 ports and 1764 commodities, the run time is less than five
seconds, which is a speed up factor of more than 200 compared to an exact MIP solution. It is noted that
the Lagrangian multipliers do not show any significant convergence, however, the method reports consistent
results without major fluctuations. For the previously mentioned instance, their optimality gap is 5.5% and
Krogsgaard and Thorsen conclude that this is within an acceptable range for their LSNDP search procedure.
Since the MCF acts as a guideline in the search procedure, the significant speed up factor and stable solution
quality is essential when evaluating several LSNDP solutions.
After the results shown by Krogsgaard and Thorsen, it is reasonable to consider the effects of obtaining
the optimal solution to a MCF. This can be seen in Karsten et al. (2015) where an exact method based on
column generation (CG) is proposed. Karsten et al. explore the MCF by both considering the problem with or
without the extension of time constraints. Based on the path-formulation of the MCF, a delayed CG process
can be applied in order to achieve an optimal solution. Here, the master problem (MP) is defined by a reduced
set of columns for for each commodity such that a feasible solution can be achieved by selecting amongst
these columns. The dual variables produced by the MP is then used in a sub-problem for each commodity.
These sub-problems aim to find a path having a negative reduced cost, since adding these to the MP have the
potential to improve the solution value. These sub-problems are reduced to (resource constrained) shortest
path problems. Karsten et al. test different transshipment structures and graph contraction techniques and
show highly promising results when they solve six of the LINER-LIB instances. Solving the same instance
as Krogsgaard and Thorsen with 47 ports and 1764 commodities using their star structure (More in Section
4.3), they can achieve a run time of 13 seconds - not considering time constraints. This run time is based
on their graph contraction techniques, excluding this will lead to a run time of 15.7 second - about three
6
==PAGE:17==
times slower than Krogsgaard and Thorsen. Karsten et al. have shown an exact method that seems to be at
most three times slower than a fast approximation method. Depending on the LSNDP scope and solution
approach, the CG method could play a significant relevance, since it seem like a reasonable trade-off between
run time and solution quality.
2.3
Fuels in Liner Shipping
The subject of fuels in liner shipping is arguably one of the most fundamental aspects of operating a sustain-
able business within the industry field. This statement holds for an economical, operational and environmental
perspective. A common factor for these perspectives is also the fact that fuel usage generally can be defined
as a cubic function depending on the vessel speed [5]. Historically, the perspective on fuels has primarily
been focused on the financial costs and operational feasibility. This is simply due to the fact that the overall
objective in LSNDP is equal to profit. However, in recent years a growing concern has resolved around the
emissions related to usage of the bunker fuel in liner shipping. Initially, actions were made to reduce the
more toxic emission by introducing Emission Control Areas (ECA), however the next more radical step is
to reduce the GHG emissions - the so-called decarbonization process of liner shipping. The decarbonization
process is defined by three time-dependent phases; short, medium and long term measures with the end-goal
of introducing new AF.
For short term measures, the concept of green liner shipping within OR methods focus on the current manage-
ment of bunker fuels with respect to operational usage and purchasing. Wang and Zhao (2021) are amongst
the latest to deal with speed optimization for liner shipping, thus representing the classical idea of less fuel
usage is equal to less emissions. Wang and Zhao proposes a probabilistic tabu search algorithm as opposed
to the standard tabu search, which allows them to save computational efforts. The method shows promising
results for especially large-scale problem instances and finds significant operational cost savings. Farina et al.
(2014) look into bunker purchasing with contract, which can be highly useful for handling and evaluating
ECAs. Here, it is noted how a ECA essentially is a zone with a mandated fuel type associated to it. The
purchasing problem itself deals with the fundamental questions about where and when to fuel the vessels.
This problem is modelled as a MIP, however, due to its NP-complexity it is Dantzig-Wolfe decomposed and
solved using a delayed CG process. The solution method is applied to real world instances with up to 500+
vessels and contracts and yield near optimal solutions. Even though, Farina et al. only considers current
bunker fuels, it is worth noticing that the methodology is highly applicable for AF as well and might be
necessary for the operation of AF-based liner shipping networks.
With the long term goal of using AF in mind, Psaraftis (2019) evaluates solutions that are necessary on the
path to achieve significant GHG reductions - based on a medium time horizon. The centrepiece of the critical
review by Psaraftis are the two measures of EEDI and Marked Based Measures (MBMs). Both measures
and their effectiveness are discussed from an economical, technical and political perspective. EEDI induce
change at the technological level that would reduce emission in the vessel fleet of the world. The EEDI is a
GHG emission measure that all current and future marine engines have to obey to. It is considered to be
the most important regulatory measure, since it so far is the only mandatory action taken by IMO. Psaraftis
notes that EEDI suffers from basic deficiencies and argue that EEDI is unlike to lead to significant GHG
7
==PAGE:18==
reductions. MBMs is on the other hand an economical tool aiming to reduce usage of bunker fuels. MBMs
can be considered to be a group of several methods, however, the most frequently discussed is essentially a
bunker levy. This is widely deemed to be a game-changer and highly effective for the adoption of AF and
thus major GHG reductions. The discussions about MBMs were initiated in 2010, but has since experienced
several setbacks, which Psaraftis conclude to be clear political reasons. Due to MBMs radical possibilities
and implementations issues, MBMs are greatly investigated within the research community. In order to gain
an excellent overview of the current MBM research, it is recommended to see the work of Lagouvardou et al.
(2020).
Initial work has likewise started to reflect how the liner shipping industry can operate on AF. In ZeroCarbon-
Shipping (2021), they look into multiple scenarios for ’A path to zero’, meaning greatly limited dependency
of bunker fuels and a net-zero CO2 vessel operation.
The scenarios by ZeroCarbonShipping present the
mixture of marine fuels consisting of bio-fuels and the so-called e-fuels with the latter covering more than
half the fuel demand. The e-fuels are desirable, since they can be produced sorely from electricity from
renewable energy sources and they do not emit CO2 when used on vessels. Among the e-fuels considered,
e-ammonia is by far the most desired fuel, since it covers more than 90% of the e-fuel demand. The increased
interests in e-ammonia is also noticeable in the work by Fahnestock et al. (2021), where they investigate
ammonia-powered vessels to operate in Northern Europe. Fahnestock et al. conclude that the Nordic region
is in a unique position to pioneer ammonia-powered shipping, which is based upon the technical, sourcing,
economical and political environment present in this region. Throughout a case vessel, Fahnestock et al. go
through the carriers and barriers of achieving a feasible ammonia-based vessel operation.
Engberg et al. (2021) uses this perspective of feasible ammonia-based operation and are among the firsts
to introduce the AF concept to liner shipping studies.
Engberg et al. investigate the construction of e-
ammonia production facilities and how this is supplied in a single service setting. For their analysis, they
make use of an existing Maersk Line Europe-Asia service and allow a variable tank size - also affecting the
vessel container capacity.
By adjusting the vessel tank size in their MIP, Engberg et al. investigate the
cost trade-off between e-ammonia availability (facility construction) and operational feasibility (tank size)
at a larger operation area. Their findings show significant capital investment cost and a more costly AF
operation compared to a bunker operation. Thus indicating the major challenges faced by the liner shipping
industry in the process of adopting AF. It is, however, noted that the investigated service by Engberg et
al. has been constructed based on a ’bunker-mentality’, which is known to influence the design of services [29].
The challenges faced by the liner shipping industry in the process of adopting AF has been mapped out in the
work by Campion et al. (2021). Here, a collection of papers from the MarE-Fuel project all have the purpose
of detailing a road-map for sustainable maritime fuels seen from the AF sourcing perspective. The work by
Campion et al. have looked into the best production setups for AF and in this context made predictions for
AF prices. In addition, they have considered external factors that are necessary in order for AF to be a
feasible option. Campion et al. state that an bunker levy seems unavoidable if the liner shipping industry
should be incentivized to adopt AF in their operation.

3
Alternative Fuels in Liner Shipping
A total of 275 million tons of CO2 emissions or 0.75% of the annual global emissions. That was the GHG
emission status for the liner shipping industry in 2018 according to the fourth GHG study conducted by
IMO. The primary source for these ’black’ environmental indicators is the usage of the conventionally used
maritime bunker fuel. Bunker is typically seen in two different forms - either as Heavy Fuel Oil (HFO) or
Maritime Diesel Oil (MDO). Both of these have an emission factor for CO2 at around 3100, which means a
consumption of 1 ton of bunker will emit 3.1 ton of CO2 [16].
In the recent years, a set of initiatives and measures have been proposed in order to reduce the GHG emissions
of liner shipping. A process better known as decarbonizing liner shipping. So far the decarbonization actions
have mainly consisted of the following elements [14]:
• Speed Optimization: Adjustment of sailing speed - often with the purpose of introducing slow
steaming (lower speeds) to reduce the fuel consumption
• (Re)Design Network: Modifications to the current network setup to reduce sailing distances and
thus the necessary fuel demand
• EEDI: Technical requirement for newer marine engines to increase fuel efficiency
• Marked Based Measures (MBMs): The core principle being a bunker levy to motivate slow
steaming and thus lower bunker consumption
Of the above, it is worth mentioning that MBMs have been heavily discussed in political organizations,
but has yet failed to be implemented - unlike the other actions. However, all of the above are generally
considered to be short or medium term solutions, since their actual effect on GHG reductions are limited
[31]. The widely acknowledged long term measure is the introduction of a new maritime fuel - commonly
referred to as AF. Besides being financial and operational feasible, the main feature of a new maritime fuel
is to have a low-to-no environmental footprint. One of the key reasons for why MBMs are discussed to such
a great extend is because they are considered as a crucial driver for introducing AF. MBMs would exactly
incentivize a transformation to using AF, when the currently used bunker becomes less financially preferable.
3.1
Selecting a New Maritime Fuel
MBMs will undoubtedly be a helpful external factor for the introduction of AF, however, the main driver
must be the capabilities of the AF itself. A new maritime fuel must therefore live up to a significant set of
criteria. In overall terms, these fuel criteria can be categorized as follows [14]:
• Fuel Price: The cost of purchasing any new AF will first and foremost be compared to the currently
used bunker fuels
• Reliable Supply: This will both involve the general production capacity (and limits thereof), the
geographical locations of the production facilities and the competition with other sectors
9
==PAGE:20==
• Energy Density: Here, the overall chemical abilities relevant for space requirement and distance
ranges are considered. The technical usages in an internal combustion engine (ICE) and/or fuel cells
(FC) is also taken into account. In all, this reflects both the functionality and maturity of an AF
• Operational and Capital Cost: The cost of any required changes associated with implementing and
actually using a new AF. This will include the production, storage, distribution and the actual usage
with the latter seen from the operator/vessel perspective
• Fuel Infrastructure Cost: Here the construction and availability of the bunkering system is consid-
ered and namely how this is incorporated around the ports
• Climate Impact: Here, the direct emissions when using the fuel is evaluated, however, the general
fuel life-cycle is likewise important to take into considerations
• Safety Issues: Concerns related to bunkering, storage and fuel usage on the vessels. This will also
consider any potential impact on air and water quality. In all, this criteria covers the risk of accidents
and potential health impact.
The above list of criteria is not intended to be seen in an order of priority, however, it does raise a set of
questions that decrease in complexity further down the list. The importance of each criteria is naturally going
to differ depending on which stakeholder is asked, but the Climate Impact is definitely rising in significance.
When selecting a new maritime fuel/ propulsion system, the fuel criteria must be evaluated for a set of
candidate solutions. In general, these can be subdivided into three categories of new chemical compounds,
bio-fuels and new propulsion systems.
Here, the new chemicals considered are ammonia, methanol and
hydrogen. Bio-fuels are here defined as being similar to the currently used bunker fuels, but are made from
biodegradable materials and those considered here are biodiesel and liquefied bio gas (LBG). New propulsion
systems will either aid the current ICE/FC systems or replace them completely with electrical engines - the
options considered are wind power or batteries. All of these candidate solutions have already been rated in
similarity to the above fuel criteria in the work by Hansson et al. (2020), which can be seen in Figure 1

as being the most obvious choice. In fact, biodiesel is already widely used in liner shipping. A company like
Maersk currently run a program called ECO Delivery where the carbon footprint of cargo transported under
this name will be neutralized through use of carbon neutral biodiesel in the Maersk network [26]. However,
despite of being carbon neutral the usage of biodiesel emits GHG in the range of the currently used bunker
fuels. This means a great deal of biomass (carbon source) is required in order to produce biodiesel. Due
to this strict dependency of biomass, one could argue that the production potential of biodiesel to supply
the entire liner shipping industry is relatively low. This is due to biomass being a limited resource that
other industries are interested in as well. For this reason, bio-fuels have been ruled out as the long term
AF. The same goes for both wind and batteries, since these cannot feasibly be implemented as a stand-alone
alternative in the liner shipping operations [14]. Batteries would simply not be able to store the tremendous
amount of energy required to power a container vessel for long-distance sailing. Likewise, wind power would
most likely be used in combination with a electric-battery system, since an ’old-fashioned’ sail-setup could
not propulse the vessels.
The most promising candidates are thus ammonia, methanol and hydrogen. Hydrogen is by many considered
to be a highly promising propulsion fuel (and is already in use for some automotive vehicles in a FC setup),
but it is not the best fit for liner shipping. This is mainly due to the extensive amount of hydrogen on gas
form that would be necessary to operate a larger container vessel. Besides occupying much physical space,
this would also lead to some significant explosive risks. To store hydrogen on liquid form would likewise be
infeasible, since liquid hydrogen requires cryogenic temperatures, which is difficult to maintain on vessels
[14]. For these reason, the AFs considered in this project will be ammonia and methanol. Notice that the
selection process just explained has had a simple narrative setup. The actual procedure is of course much
more extensive and complex, but it still reflects the general reasoning within the shipping industry. The
largest liner shipping company Maersk has likewise selected ammonia and methanol as the maritime fuel of
the future [26].

to be used in an equal manner. As Figure 1 also highlights, methanol is a much more mature fuel technology

’moon-shot’ and most promising fuel of the future. The reason for this mentality and adoption process comes
down to the characteristics of ammonia and methanol as fuels. As mentioned earlier, both fuels are expected
to be feasible within the liner shipping operations and therefore the main driver is the emissions perspective.

Ammonia - In Short
Ammonia (NH3) is a well-known and used compound in the industrial world, where it is mainly used as a
fertilizer in agriculture. It is likewise amongst the top ranking chemicals in term of its used volume - the
current production volume is equal to 30% of the global shipping fuel consumption [14]. However, the usage
as a fuel is very limited. ICEs powered by ammonia is in development and the leading marine engine company
MAN Energy Solutions is hoping to have a functioning system in 2024 [25]. These new propulsion systems
comes with a great deal of uncertainty and concerns for spill, since ammonia is a highly toxic compound. It
is, however, worth noticing that ammonia so far has a perfect safety record, which indicates a well-establish
handling system [1]. Another major barrier for ammonia as a fuel is the current production cost of around
3800$/ton, which by no mean is a sustainable financial option for a marine fuel. The central contributor to
this large production price is the usage of natural gas that is currently necessary to produce ammonia [1].
Lastly, the container vessels themselves would also require larger fuel tanks or be fuelled more frequently due
to the lower energy density. This would either lead to new vessel design and/or greater dependency of the
fuel infrastructure.
The production process of ammonia is known as the Habor-Bosch (HB) process (N2 + 3H2 →2NH3) and
the hydrogen is supplied via steam reforming of natural gas. However, a new technology being developed by
several companies including Haldor Topsoe will use a solid oxide electrolysis cell to produce hydrogen and
nitrogen from water and air which then can be fed into the HB process. The new technology will only require
an electricity source to run and by using renewable energy sources the production of ammonia can be entirely
carbon-free [32] - known as Green Ammonia. Furthermore, it allows for a high production scale, since the
required elements of water, air and (green) power is available in large quantities. Lastly, by having a carbon-
12
==PAGE:23==
free production process and no usage emission as seen in Table 1, ammonia will have no GHG emissions
in its entire life-cycle. When the toxicity concerns along the ammonia propulsion system have been dealt
with, the potential for ammonia is thus quite promising. This is again highly dependent on the propulsion
system, since an ICE setup is noted to leave to NOx emissions. Depending on the specific green ammonia
production setup and scale, it is likewise projected to be financially sustainable. The current estimate is that
green ammonia can have a price in the range of [320, 480] $/ton [36].
Methanol - In Short
Methanol (CH3OH) is an alcohol-based type of fuel, which provide it with a great set of similarities to
conventional bunker fuels with respect to operational usage. Due to these similarities, it will not require
radical propulsion changes to the current ICE bunker setup. In some cases, it might actually be a simple
manner of retrofitting the current ICE to run on methanol instead. Like ammonia, it would, however, still be
necessary to increase the fuel tank sizes or have more frequent fuelling. Methanol is for this reason an obvious
intermediate AF, since it can easily be implemented in operations while having a lower GHG emission factor.
The environmental perspective of methanol can also be improved upon in its production process depending
on which carbon source is used. Green Methanol is the term for methanol produced using renewable energy
sources and utilizing biomass as a carbon source. In this setup, green methanol will have a net-zero GHG
emission like the already used bio-diesels. The difference being a lower amount of biomass required relative
to the energy received compared to traditional bio-diesel [18].
That being said, the dependency of biomass is the Achilles’ heel for green methanol – both with respect to
production amount and the final fuel price. The current global trend of reducing GHG emission will unde-
niably increase the demand for biomass across several industries. Furthermore, the availability of biomass is
to some extent quite limited and the mixture of these conditions will lead to fluctuating biomass prices and
limited production. An alternative to being independent of biomass is by applying the so-called Direct Air
Capture (DAC) technology. With DAC the purpose is to capture carbon (via CO2) directly from the air and
made available for an methanol production. However, DAC is still an emerging technology and it is therefore
unknown how effective and to what scale this technology will work. As of now, DAC is likewise a relatively
expensive carbon source option. Green methanol is still quite promising from an operational perspective and
with the carbon sourcing in mind, it still has a fairly simple production setup. The predicted fuel price for
green methanol is expected to be in the range of [250, 630] $/ ton. Notice the significant gap in the range,
which exactly is the result of the uncertainty related to the carbon source [18].
3.3
Alternative Fuel Sourcing Problem
The matter of implementing AF to the liner shipping operations can in a simplistic way be boiled down to
where to fuel and at what cost. The current HFO/MDO bunkering system is available to some extent in
all container ports (CP), but at various fuel prices. Exactly, the price for fuel is traditionally known to be
the most important cost perspective for liner shipping and thus quite determining for the design of rotations
[29]. The importance of fuel cost will only be more important for AF, since these are expected to be more
13
==PAGE:24==
expensive. Furthermore, the AF might not be as easily available in all CP. In this project, the purpose is to
investigate the locations of AF production and determine where the vessels can fuel. From this an estimate
of the AF prices can be made to evaluate the quality of the vessel network. So given a set of rotations
with fuel demand, the investigation will determine the most optimal production locations and how the fuel
is distributed to the visited CP. This problem will from now on be referred to as Alternative Fuel Sourcing
Problem (AFSP).
3.3.1
AF Production Setup
The AF production setup considered is that of green ammonia and green methanol. The setup will consist
of both a production (with storing) facility and the elements to provide renewable energy. This setup is
also known as an ‘off-grid’ solution where the entire infrastructure is owned by the producer – thus being
independent of the established electricity grid [6]. For green ammonia, this setup assumes a carbon-free
technology and will rely on water, air and electricity from renewable energy sources. It is worth noticing that
during ammonia production there is an expected energy loss of 55%. This means for all (electrical) energy
used in the production process, only 45% will be bound in the ammonia itself [9]. Engberg et al. (2021)
estimates that the fixed costs associated with constructing and maintaining an ammonia production facility
during the life time is 8.5045 M$. Notice this cost is independent of the size of the production facility. The
size of a production facility is defined by the units of renewable energy sources. The marginal cost related
to increasing the production facility size is therefore embedded in the cost for renewable energy sources. A
simple illustration of the green ammonia setup can be seen in Figure 2.
Figure 2: Illustration of ammonia production setup
For green methanol, the setup is quite similar with electricity from renewable energy sources, while the
selected carbon source is the DAC technology. By choosing DAC, the elasticities of prices and quantities for
biomass is avoided. This will naturally provide a more stable fuel price, but the added expenses of using this
technology should be accounted for. These added expenses are already seen in the fixed costs related to a
green methanol production facility. Based on key values provided by Kang et al. (2021), the fixed costs for
14
==PAGE:25==
a production facility with DAC technology are estimated to be 17.8981 M$. For methanol, the energy loss
factor is assumed to be 51% using a DAC setup - meaning 51% of the input energy ends up in the methanol
[18]. The fixed costs are noted to be more than twice as much than for ammonia, but a better energy loss
factor is present, which is favourable on a large-scale production. A simple illustration for the green methanol
production considered is shown in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Illustration of ammonia production setup
As also seen in Figure 2 and 3, the renewable energy sources considered in this project are wind and solar
power. For wind power, the specific unit of measure is going to be a sea-based bottom-fixed 15 MW wind
turbine (WT). The reasoning behind this choice is due to sea-based WT tend to be more wind effective (more
wind exposed) and easier to find locations for compared to land-based turbines. Furthermore, it is mentioned
that it is a simplified approach only to consider bottom-fixed turbines, since it might not be possible to attach
the turbine to the ocean bottom at all potential locations. It is, however, often the case and is therefore
an acceptable assumption. As previously mentioned, the cost of renewable energy sources would include
the marginal cost for its associate AF production facility.
Therefore, the variable cost per WT is going
to differ according to which AF is being produced. Engberg et al. (2021) states that the construction and
maintenance cost during the entire life time for the WT considered is 35.84115 M$ in an ammonia production
setup. Furthermore, they apply a marginal cost for production facility increment equal to 36% of the fixed
costs. In this project, it is assumed that a marginal cost of 36% also apply for the methanol production.
Therefore, the calculation for the variable cost per WT for ammonia and methanol are given by:
Ammonia : 0.36 · 8.5045 + 35.84115 = 38.9531 M$
(1)
Methanol : 0.36 · 17.8981 + 35.84115 = 42.390 M$
(2)
For solar power, the used unit of measure is set to be a 100-block of 500W solar panels (SP). Even though,
500W SP are still quite new in commercial usage, these high efficiency SP are expected to be the dominant
type in the future. According to Heegaard (2022), the expected construction and maintenance cost for a

a 100-block of 500W SP at most can produce 30.24 GJ/week. This scaling of relative difference is applied to
the marginal cost. The variable cost per 100-block 500W SP can be calculated for ammonia and methanol
as seen in (3) and (4) respectively.

3.3.2
AF Production Sites and Distribution
Recall that for the AFSP, a set of liner shipping rotations with fuel demand/usage is given.
The core
elements of AFSP are then the selection of AF production sites and how AF is delivered to the CP visited
in the rotations. In this project, the candidate set of production sites consists of two components. The first
component assumes that an AF production can be established within the proximity ( 30 km) of each CP
– a production near an CP is referred to as CPP. The intension with CPP is that it will have a limited
production setup and can only supply fuel to its associate CP. The second component among candidate sites
contains a list of external production sites, where a port is within reasonable range ( 100 km). These external
production sites will be referred to as fuel ports (FP) and their associate production as FPP. Notice that CPs
and FPs are typically mutually exclusive, but in some cases a few ports might overlap between the sets. The
intension with FPP is to allow a greater production potential and require a distribution by tanker vessels to
the supplied CPs. Furthermore, the following list of assumptions for CPP and FPP holds:
16
==PAGE:27==
• At most 1 type of AF can be produced at both CPP and FPP
• Production facility size (measured in amount of WT or SP) for CPP is 60% less than for FPP. Here,
60% was selected to reflect a medium production size
• CPP can only have 1 type of renewable energy source (WT or SP)
• Only FPP is allowed to ship fuel to CPs. CPP only supply its associate CP
• Road transportation cost from production site to port is neglectable in the overall cost scheme
• Life time of production facilities is 20 years
In addition, a linear shipping cost depending on the distance travelled is used. The linear shipping cost
function has been found by applying a linear regression on (cost/distance)-values presented by Al-Breiki and
Bicer (2020), which can be seen in Figure 4 for ammonia and 5 for methanol.
Figure 4: Shipping cost function for ammonia. R2 = 0.9992
Figure 5: Shipping cost function for methanol. R2 = 0.9986

Ammonia : y = 8 · 10−5x + 0.0914
(5)
Methanol : y = 5 · 10−5x + 0.0587
(6)
Here, x is distance in km and the cost y is measured in $ per GJ of fuel shipped. Notice the generally greater
cost of shipping ammonia, which is due to specific safety and storage conditions [3].
The AFSP is still a relatively new problem to be investigated, but the scope of previous work has mostly
been limited to a single liner shipping rotation as seen in the work by Engberg et al. (2021) and Heegaard
(2022). In this project, the purpose is to investigate a more global covering area. For this reason, the 47 most
significant global ports will be used as CP. These 47 global ports originates from the WS instance known
from LINER-LIB, which is an academically available data instance for liner shipping [5]. A more in-depth
introduction to the WS instance is presented in Section 4.2. In Figure 6, an overview of the locations for the

3.3.3
AF Production Size and Efficiency
The phrase ‘a promising production location’ has been mentioned quite frequently in the previous section,
but what characterize such a location? In highly simple terms, it boils down to a matter of production size
and energy efficiency. Recall that production size is measured by the amount of renewable energy source
units. In order to define an upper limit for the size of the productions, this project will consider the currently
built wind and solar farms. For WT, the upper limit is set to 175, which is similar to the Hornsea Project
One wind farm in the UK. The setup here consists of 174 sea-based 9.5 MW WT that occupy a space of
407km2 [15]. Notice that the Hornsea Project One WT are smaller than the 15 MW WT considered in this
project. This is, however, not an unreasonable assumption, since larger/more energy efficient WT must be
expected in the future due to technological developments. The more restricting condition is the matter of
space requirement, which is why the WT amount is set similar to the facility in the UK. For the FPP, it is
thus assumed that the space for such a wind farm is available. As mentioned, the production size for CPP
is assumed to be 60% less, which result in a WT upper limit of 100.
For solar power, the facility of reference is going to be the Bhadla Solar Park in India. Here, a 57km2 area
is covered by 4.490.000 individual 500W SP. For a unit measurement in 100-blocks, this defines an upper
limited for the considered SP of 44.900 [15]. Notice how, no technological improvements are assumed in
comparison to the setup considered. The reason for this being that the availability of a 57km2 land area in
proximity to all considered FP might be a too strict assumption in itself. Any technological improvements
will hopefully lead to a reduction in space required instead, which is why an upper limit of 44.900 of 500 W
SP is kept. For the reduced production size of CPP, the upper limit for SP will be 25.700 and here, the same
land space concerns are likewise noted.
The last characteristic of a promising production site is the energy efficiency. Energy efficiency is here more
fittingly defined as the utilization of the renewable energy sources. As previously mentioned, the maximum
possible energy production by a WT and 100-block SP is respectively 9070 GJ and 30,24 GJ in a single week.
However, this is assuming a constant flow of wind and solar radiation for the entire time during the week –
known as an energy production capacity (EPC) of 100%. The flow of wind and solar radiation will naturally
both differ throughout the day and most places also during the year. For all production sites (CPP and
FPP), it is thus necessary to assign an EPC value. In this project, the selected EPC is going to be the yearly
average and thus not take any seasonal effects into account. This is an acceptable simplification, since any
surplus AF production can be stored in tanks and used for when the EPC is low. A more exact approach
would naturally be to study the influence of the energy supply fluctuations throughout the year, but that is
left to future work. The EPC values can be found via https://www.renewables.ninja/, which is a simulation
tool that can estimate the average capacities during a year for a given location [30]. The EPC values found
and the resulting energy production for the chosen WT and SP setup can be seen in Table 3. In addition
Table 3 will present the UNLOCODE names of the ports considered, their type (CP/FP) and production
size limits. Notice that UNLOCODE names are explained further in Section 4.2 and a ’dictionary’ of the
considered ports can be seen in the appendix.

3.3.4
AF Vessels and Fuel Scenarios
The central part of AFSP is the production and distribution of AF to the CP network, which has been cov-
ered in the previous sections. However, in a more operational perspective, it is necessary to coordinate this
fuel supply to the individual vessel demand. The container vessels are operationally bounded by their fuel
tanks sizes and the fuel supply must therefore match these range limitations. This perspective is especially
important for the newer AF vessels, since they will have almost twice as much fuel consumption. In case, the
vessel fuel tanks are doubled in size, it would not be much of an issue – this is, however, an unrealistic sce-
nario. Double size fuel tanks would be financially unviable due to increased vessel sizes or significant reduced
container capacity. An increment in fuel tank sizes still seems unavoidable and, in this project, an increase of
33% compared to current tank sizes is assumed. As a matter of reference, the newly bought methanol-based
vessels by Maersk are informed to have a tank size of 16.000m3. These new vessels have a container capacity
of 7500 Forty Feet Equivalent (FFE) and current bunker-based vessels with similar capacity have a tank size
around 12.000m3. The difference being roughly an increment of 33% [10].
In this project, a total of six different container vessel types is considered – all varying in the container
capacities measured in FFE. The considered types have a capacity of {450, 800, 1200, 2400, 4200, 7500} FFE
and via https://www.scheepvaartwest.be/CMS/index.php/containerships a representative operational vessel
has been identified. For each operational vessel their tank size is known and will be used as baseline tank
sizes. An overview of the considered vessel types and their increased tank sizes measured in m3 and equivalent
fuel amounts of bunker, ammonia and methanol in GJ can be seen in Table 4.

Liner Shipping Network Design Problem
The Liner Shipping Network Design Problem (LSNDP) is a well-known and NP-hard problem from the
route-optimization family of OR problems [8]. In simple terms, the problem consists of the following three
steps.
• Given a set of (bi) weekly container demands defined as: (Origin, Destination, Amount, Price, Time
Limit)
• Given a set of vessels - typically varying in capacity size and amounts
• Construct network of rotations to maximize profit = Container Revenue – Operational Cost
The problem thus depends on utilizing vessel resources such that (profitable) containers are delivered while
operational costs are minimized. Notice how demands are given in weekly terms, which is due to the nature
of liner shipping. Liner shipping is often compared to bus services, since any port on a rotation will be visited
every week and the coverage area is thus constantly being served. This type of weekly service will dictate the
amount of vessels required by each rotation. A given rotation might take five weeks to complete for a single
vessel and therefore a total of five vessels are also needed to maintain the weekly service in all ports. Similar
to bus services, the flow of goods through a liner shipping network of rotation has a large degree of mobility.
The containers can either be delivered directly on the rotation, but a highly used service is to transship
the containers between rotations as well. This is possible due to the standardization of the container itself,
large vessel fleets and collaboration agreements. In total, this connectivity makes the entire network of ports
accessible. A simple illustration of a network consisting of three rotations with two transshipment ports can
be seen in Figure 7.
Figure 7: Illustration of a simple network with three rotations and two transshipment ports
In addition, the core principle of maximizing profit is explained a bit further. The matter of estimating con-
tainer revenue is fairly straight forward in cases where the asking price is fixed/known. Here, it is possible to
add up all containers delivered with their prices. In simple terms this is only limited by visited ports and ves-
sels capacities. The perspective of minimizing operational costs is, however, more complex. The operational
cost consists of capital cost of the vessels themselves, fuel cost, port call costs, (un)loading/transshipment
26
==PAGE:37==
costs and fees for traversing through canals. The magnitude of all of these costs are dependent on the design
of rotations with fuel costs typically being the most dominant factor [29].
The complexity of designing rotations is many-folded. Firstly, the structure of the rotation can take several
forms depending on the type of port call sequence. The most basic structure is typically referred to as the
simple structure where all the ports in the rotation are visited (called) exactly once - creating a ’circle’
rotation form. A more complex rotation structure involves butterfly ports. A butterfly port is being called
more than once where a ’loop’ pattern going from and to the butterfly ports is created - these loops gives
the visual effect of a ’butterfly’. In case, a rotation has exactly one butterfly port and the remainder is called
only once, the rotation structure is defined as a butterfly structure. Lastly, if the rotation contains more than
one butterfly port and no other characteristics applies, the rotation structure is known as complex [8]. An
illustrative example of the three structure types can be seen in Figure 8.
Figure 8: Illustration of the three rotation types
(a) Simple rotation structure
(b) Butterfly rotation structure
(c) Complex rotation structure
Alongside the rotation design, the deployment of vessels is occurring. The deployment of vessels takes several
operational perspectives into account. Firstly, the mixture of vessel types used in rotations will manage
the trade-off between container capacity and capital costs. Furthermore, the deployment cannot exceed the
amount of vessels available for each vessel type, since the weekly call requirement needs to be fulfilled. As
mentioned, the amount of vessels for each rotation is depending on the travel duration. Here, the travel
duration is naturally bounded by the travel distance, but more importantly the travel speed. For higher
speeds, fewer vessels are needed, but the fuel consumption is likewise larger. Hopefully, this short description
of the many trade-off occurring during designing will support the ’narrative’ about LSNDP being a complex
and NP-hard problem[5].

4.1
Simplifications and Version Considered
The LSNDP presented in (20)-(27) is difficult to solve, however, it still contains some simplifications com-
pared to actual liner shipping operations. Firstly, for all rotations and vessel classes a fixed port stay time
of 24 hours is used. Although, the time of an actual port stays depends greatly on the amount of containers
being moved and port berth/crane availability. In addition, the time spent of piloting around the port and
for bunkering is neglected or at least embedded in the 24 hours port stay. Therefore, it might seem unfitting
to apply this fixed time and a more exact approach could be to apply a (un)loading activity based function
to determine the port stay time. This can, however, be difficult to estimate such a function and implement it
to the problem. A fixed time might therefore be the best option and Brouer et al. (2014) has likewise found
the 24 hours to be a reasonable average port time.
Another simplification occurs in the perspective of container units, since all of these are considered in a generic
way. Any special consideration to container content requirements such as weight, reefers or dangerous goods
are not taken into account. Alongside this, the perspective of capacity constraints in the ports with respect to
either containers or vessels is neglected. Both of these issues would require some understanding of the exact
arrival/departure of vessels, which is not of the nature of the basic LSNDP. This is due to LSNDP being a
strategical problem. A strategical problem will provide the foundation for problems of tactic and operational
nature. Here, the tactical decision could be to find a sailing schedule for the vessels in the network. Whereas
the concern for berth/quay crane assignment and container loading (stowage) is operational elements relying
on the given tactical schedule. So the simplifications presented are all reasonable given that LSNDP is a
strategic problem.
On top of the simplifications mentioned, the LSNDP can come in different versions, which was also apparent
in the literature review in Section 2.1. Firstly, the rotations will only have a weekly call at ports whereas
some allow for a biweekly call such as in the work by Brouer et al. (2014). Secondly as mentioned, it can
often be more profitable to reject demand and ’pay’ the goodwill cost instead, which is why demand can
be rejected in this project. Furthermore, this project will not consider the transit time constraints for each
commodity. This is done, because of implementation reasons and the transit times might differ in the future.
These transit times could with some certainty be argued to be extended for rotations running on AF. A
customer might allow a slower transit time given that the transport solution is free of GHG. Some studies
ignore the cost of transshipment, but these are highly important for the container flow cost scheme and are
therefore included in this work. The fuel cost is maybe the most important cost and this is depending on the
chosen speed. The speed can be optimized/ set on three different levels:
30
==PAGE:41==
• Network: Here, the same speed is selected for all the vessels in the network
• Service: Here, the same speed is selected for the entire rotation, but can vary between the rotations
• Leg: Here, the speed can be adjusted between all sailing legs for all rotations
In this project, the speed optimization is done on the service level. Lastly, the considered rotations structure
will be complex as known from Figure 8c. In Table 5, an overview of the work presented in Section 2.1 is

4.3
Network Representation
A graph representation is needed in order to measure the cost of flowing containers through the network
(flow cost). In order to create a graph to represent the LSNDP network, a set of ports P and rotations R
are necessary. In addition, for each rotation, the following information must be available:
• Vessel Class: The type of vessel operating the rotations. It is here assumed that only a single vessel
class is going to service a rotation - providing a homogeneous vessel fleet for the rotation. For a graph
representation, the vessel class is important to model the correct capacities
• Port Call List: The visiting sequence of the ports serviced by the rotation. The port call list for a
rotation r is denoted Qr with each port call referred to as q. These port call lists are the backbone of
creating a graph representation, which is crucial for calculating the flow of containers
With the above information available, it is possible to create a graph G = (N, A) to represent the rotations

6
Initial Solution
In order to obtain an initial solution for the modified LSNDP-CFP at hand, it is worth noticing that the
solution structure is three-folded - consisting of initial rotations, flow of cargo and AF production setup. As
mentioned in Section 5 and Section 3, the flow of cargo and AF production setup cannot be solved without
initial rotations and their vessel deployment. For this reason, the fundamental part of initializing the solution
process is the ability to gain initial rotations. However, these initial rotations are often closely related to the
overall proposed solution method for the LSNDP-CFP.
As presented by Christiansen et al. (2020), the solution methods for LSNDP-CFP can generally be segmented
into four groups; MIP-based algorithms, two-stage algorithms, subset of routes and backbone flow. For subset
of routes, the idea is to generate an overall service network using a candidate list of services as input, which is
seen in the work by Balakrishnan and Karsten (2017). Here, it is not necessary to create an initial solution,
since complete sets of routes are available, which are often based on existing knowledge. The solution space
is thus quite limited, but the method can have great practical use due to less radical network modifications
for a shipping company.
For the methods of MIP-based and two-stage algorithms, the creation of initial routes is embedded in the
iterative solution method. Both methods also rely on constructing routes where the demand data is used in
a heuristic/ ’rule-of-thump’ setup to guide the construction. For a MIP-based algorithm as seen in the work
by Brouer et al. (2014), they apply an advanced MIP neighbourhood based on heuristic measures on cargo
composition to generate rotations. The method developed by Brouer et al. can solve instances to optimality,
but only for smaller instances due to a significantly increasing complexity. For a large instance like WS, this
method would struggle to find a useful initial solution within reasonable time. Solution time is less of an
issue for two-stage algorithms, since these rely on heuristics. However, the quality of an initial solution in
the two-stage mentality often leads to poor results. In the work by Mulder and Dekker (2014), the initial set
of rotations is simply obtained by randomness. Mulder and Dekker do check and adjust the routes based on
some feasibility conditions, but otherwise put great faith in their later improvement methods. Agarwal and
Ergun (2008) use an iterative search algorithm in order to generate rotations, however, the guiding feasibility
check is based on operational criteria like amount of ports, cycle length, port locations and a simple demand
check. Agarwal and Ergun thus construct initial rotations based on general routes features, which by no
mean will aid the cargo flow.
The methods so far indicates that in order to gain an initial solution either rotations are given or the
generation process will perform poorly with respect to time or quality. However, the backbone flow method
first presented by Krogsgaard et al. (2018) present a generation method with focus on both computational
time and solution quality. By introducing a complete network between ports, it is possible to create an
initial relaxed (backbone) flow of the cargo. Based on this desirable cargo flow, a set of rotations can be
created to accommodate ’actual’ flow. Creating routes based on cargo flow will intuitively provide a good
solution quality and low run times are achieved since the method can be solved heuristically. Furthermore,
it is noted that the perspective of flowing cargo on AF vessels can easily be implemented in the method.
43
==PAGE:54==
For these reasons, the backbone flow method is used in order to generate an initial solution to the modified
LSNDP-CFP.
6.1
Auxiliary Network and Backbone Flow
The essential part of the backbone flow method is, as the name suggest, the creation of a backbone flow.
This backbone flow will represent the ideal cargo flow where no rotation restrictions are taken into account.
Since this method works in the reverse order of the two-stage method, the flow cannot be found based on any
rotations. However, the operational mentality behind a route can be included in the process of finding the
flow. Two elements are necessary in this process, namely what network structure to use and how to create
the flow.
6.1.1
Initializing Network
As mentioned, the backbone flow will resemble an ideal cargo flow and for this reason no directional re-
strictions are used. This means the introduction of a relaxed network that consists of a complete graph to
represent the connection between the ports. This network will be referred to as the auxiliary network denoted
Gaux. Let P denote the set of ports in the instance, then Gaux = (N, A) where:
• Set of nodes N = {i | i ∈P}
• Set of arcs A = {(i, j) | i, j ∈P, i ̸= j}
Notice how Gaux will resemble a network containing direct services between all ports, which of course would
be far too expensive to operate in reality. Due to the characteristics of Gaux, no capacity restrictions are used
for arcs in A. In order to take the expenses of complete direct services into account when creating a cargo
flow, this behaviour must be controlled via the costs of the arcs. Setting up these arc costs should, however,
mimic the dynamics of sailing a given leg between two ports. In this context, it is possible to estimate
an average cost of such a leg, but one must not forget the perspective of diminishing costs depending of
goods transported. Generally, costs associated to sailing a given leg can be considered fixed when ignoring
a load-dependent fuel consumption. This does not apply to the cost of ports calls, since these costs are
much more varying due to load activities. For this reason, the perceived cost of sailing for a shipper will be

6.1.2
Creating Backbone Flow
Having the relaxed network Gaux available, it is now possible to create the fundamental backbone flow of
containers - later used to create rotations. The principle of doing so is quite straightforward. It is a matter
of flowing the commodities through the network via the cheapest path possible. By considering the cost as a
distance measure, this will be equivalent of solving their shortest paths. However, since the load-dependent
cost function is highly sensitive to load changes, it is unwise to flow the commodity demand in their entirety.

6.2
Route Generator
The process of generating a set of rotations must now have the objective of covering as much of the found
backbone flow. Since the backbone flow represent a preferable structure of the container flow, the coverage
of this will likely lead to a good initial solution. Notice that the backbone flow is still just an accumulation
of the container flow and no clear paths are thus known. The process of covering flow is therefore still a
somewhat ’shot in the dark’, however, the structure and a sense of direction of the flow ensure a more guided
process.
The problem at hand can thus be seen as a simplified version of LSNDP with the sole objective of creating
rotations to cover backbone arc flows. This can be modelled mathematically as a MIP model. The proposed

any rotation k ∈Ω, k is assumed to be a feasible rotation with respect to connectivity and still allow a

For the above reasons, the flow covering problem is solved heuristically. Notice how heuristics in general
can aid the above solution process in two ways. The first option is to apply a pure heuristic approach not
taking the above MIP into consideration. This approach would thus construct rotations covering as much
of the backbone flow as possible. The second option would be to apply a similar construction heuristic, but
with the purpose of adding the found rotations to a candidate set similar to Ω. This candidate set would
therefore be significantly reduced and only contain the most promising rotations. The above MIP model
would then be used to find the best set of rotations. In case the same construction heuristic is applied,

deal with the complex AF perspective and enhance the overall solution quality.
6.2.1
Construction Heuristic
A construction heuristic can have many variations, however, it is often beneficial to introduce some stochas-
ticity in order to generate multiple solutions in the neighbourhood. Furthermore, the parameter for the upper
limit for arc usage (L in MIP model) is likewise an important part of creating multiple solutions. The upper
arc limit will directly determine the structure of the initially found rotations. For this reason, the parameter
is simply too fundamental to make stochastic and will instead be a variable factor when evaluating the quality
of the construction heuristic. In this project, the chosen element of stochasticity is therefore based on the
maximum duration of a rotation. For each vessel class a range for the maximum duration has then been
made, which can be seen in Table 15.

of the rotation created, it is specified that the construction heuristic will only use the design speed of the vessel.
When it comes to the dynamics of the construction heuristic itself, this can take many forms. For problems
with a route-nature a well-known construction method is the so-called insertion procedure. The principle
being an iterative insertion of travel legs to expand a route until some conditions are met - typically the
highest possible utilization of capacities. The insertion procedure can in general be executed in two ways
52
==PAGE:63==
defined by which order the resources are allocated - either in a serial or parallel order.
• Serial Insertion: Here, only a single resource (type) is assigned to a solution in each overall insertion
iteration. This means the single resource will in this case have a complete rotation before the next
resource is used.
The order of which these resources are considered can either be random or pre-
determined.
• Parallel Insertion: Here, multiple resources (types) are assigned to a solution in each overall insertion
iteration. In this case, it would translate to several resources experiencing the insertion process simul-
taneously - thus making more rotations in each iteration. The amount of resources experiencing parallel
insertion can either be the complete set or a selected subset.
In order to determine which insertion version to apply, it is important to notice the objective at hand -
covering as much backbone flow as possible. One possible serial insertion order could be sorting the vessel
classes by size such that the largest vessels are assigned to the network first. In this case, it is insured that
large flow quantities are covered by the biggest vessels. This will increase the likelihood of containers flowing
along their ’preferred’ path, since the biggest backbone flows are typically greater than the capacity of the
largest vessels. Due to this nature of large flow amounts concentrated on few arcs, it will make the serial
insertion more affected by the chosen arc limit. In case, the arc limit is fairly low (e.g. 1), the most promising
arc will only be assigned to large vessels. This would only leave a limited flow available in the final stages of
the insertion process for the smaller vessels. The serial insertion can then easily be screwed in the quality of
solutions across the entire vessel fleet with a favour for the biggest vessels.
This exact effect could be less of an issue in case a parallel insertion was used instead. Here, a structure
could be to run a parallel insertion involving a vessel for each vessel class - ensuring a better coverage across
the classes. However, this might result in too much importance (flow coverage) for the smaller vessels in
comparison to the larger, since they would be considered as equals. A mentality that might not be beneficial
when trying to cover as much flow as possible, which a larger vessel undoubtedly will be better at. An
alternative could be a mixture of the two methods such that a serial insertion was made by the classes, but
within each class a parallel process could be made. This would potentially neglect the drawbacks of the two
individual approaches, since it would ’even out’ the flow a bit better while still emphasizing the importance
of larger vessels. However, the actual increased effect of adopting this hybrid is uncertain and due to a more
simple setup, the regular serial insertion is selected. An overview of this can be seen in Algorithm 3.

Network Improvement
In order to improve the network found in the initial solution, the metaheuristic Adaptive Large Neighbourhood
Search (ALNS) is applied to the problem. The ALNS will operate as a general framework from which heuristic
methods are used to investigate different parts of the solution space. The adaptive part of ALNS will adjust
along the search to the parts of the neighbourhood that are most promising. As per any large neighbourhood
search (LNS), the search consists of destroying and repairing a known solution. In general, an ALNS will
contain the following elements:
• Initial Solution - xini: Since ALNS is an improvement heuristic and not an construction heuristic, it is
necessary to have an initial solution of the problem instance
• Destroy Functions - Ω−: The purpose of destroy functions is to remove some of the structure in the
known solution. The removal of structure/solution parts is typically targeted towards parts with low
solution quality, but can also be completely random. The setup for these functions is thus often simple,
since it consists of identifying a structure part and then removing it
• Repair Functions - Ω+: The purpose of the repair functions is to either restore feasibility for a destroyed
solution or insert new solution parts - both done with an intention to improve the (destroyed) solution.
Like destroy functions, these will often also have a stochastic element. However, in general, repair
functions can be quite complex/ time consuming, since estimating an improving move often requires a
series of delta-evaluations. Notice that delta-evaluations consist of implementing a potential improving
move which has an immediate effect for several parts of the solution. At the end, the move with the
best immediate effect is selected as the repairing move
• Acceptance Criterion - Accept(x′, x∗): During the search procedure of destroying and repairing a solu-
tion, the matter of accepting a new solution can have a significant influence. Naturally, any improving
solutions are accepted, but a great deal of metaheuristics will typically benefit from allowing a ’slightly’
worse solution than the current. The reason for this being the possibility of escaping local optima and
explore a greater area of the solution space. During the search, it is therefore often allowed to accept a
new worse solution x′ with a Y % probability. Alternatively, it is also possible to accept a new solution
that is Y % worse than the best found so far x∗. The first acceptance method is known as Simulated
Annealing Criteria and the latter is referred to as record-to-record travel. Record-to-record travel is
simpler and has been empirically shown to work just as well as the other. The size of Y will decrease
during the search time - ensuring a more scattered search initially and more focused at the end

repair). ALNS will on the other hand change the probability of selecting a specific function based on its
performance. So in case, the newfound solution is either improving or accepted, the used destroy/repair
function will experience an increase in selection weight. In this way, the ALNS can adjust the search
method during the search time. This works particularly well if certain methods are better in the initial
search and vice versa. Likewise, it is beneficial if different problem instances favour a specific method

Rotation Graph
As mentioned, the repair function FeederCall will have to make delta-evaluations based on container flow.
Even though the current CRP approach is solved relatively fast, it is still too time consuming when several

introduced, which will only represent the container flow resolving around a single rotation r. In r, containers
can flow in four different ways:

rotation at transshipment port p2.

Destroyers
A total of three destroy functions have been developed: RandomCall, LowActivityCall and WorstAFCall.
The common trade for these functions is that they will all remove a single call for a rotation - which type
of call is function-dependent. In case, a rotation only contain two calls, the rotation will simply be removed
from the solution in its entirety if one of its calls is selected. After a call has been removed, the cycle is
restored by connecting the previous and next call, which is denoted as the function RestoreCycle. All three
functions make use of a hyper-parameter nRotations, which will determine the amount of rotations being
affected by the destroy procedure.
RandomCall
As the name suggests, RandomCall will provide a completely stochastic destroy element, since it will have
no sense of targeting on the rotations or calls being destroyed. The function will simply select nRotations
rotations at random and then again remove a call by random choice. This is definitely the simplest destroy
function, which might not help directly to removing bad solution structure. However, the stochastic element
is still expected to have a significant impact in the search process. An overview of the RandomCall function
can be seen in Algorithm 6.

7.3
Repairs and Speed Optimization
A total of four repair functions have been developed: FeederCall, OmissionToRoute, OmissionAsRoute and
GoodAFCall. In addition, a speed optimization function has been implemented as well, which will also find
the best suitable canal traversals. The four repairs function will like the destroy function all make use of
the hyper-parameter nRotations to control the amount of rotations being affected by repair changes. The
function RestoreCycle will in the following represent the creation of a complete feasible cycle, when a new
call has been inserted - like in Section 6 this includes adding a return leg and removal of the former return
leg.
FeederCall
This function will make use of the rotation graph to test if container flow on feeder arcs should be part of
the main considered rotation. The reasoning behind this being to save transshipment costs and add capacity
at the feeder port. It might also be preferred to offer a greater share of direct services due to business or
customer considerations. The latter perspective cannot directly be captured in the network improvement,
unless an additional ’penalty fee’ is applied in the feeder arc costs in (58). Alternatively, it would be necessary
to increase the cost of transshipment if the ALNS and CRP should make more direct services.
For the FeederCall, the added capacity when sailing to a feeder port will be the main incentive for increasing
solution quality. Indirectly, a port call to a feeder port will release capacity for the rotation(s) previously used
66
==PAGE:77==
in the transshipment process. However, a more promising opportunity is that the feeder port call might lead
to an increased demand flow from/to the port being implemented in the considered rotation. This increased
demand flow will arrive from demand currently not being delivered (omission) due to limited capacity on
the connecting rotations. This will be the strong force of using the rotation graph, since it will help identify
promising feeder arcs to implement in the main rotation. This is done by finding the CRP solution for the
rotation graph using a reduced demand matrix with omission included. Once the flow of containers through
the rotation graph is found, identifying a ’promising feeder arc’ will have to meet three requirements:
• Feasibility: Adding the feeder arc to the rotation should be operationally feasible with respect to
route duration and port/canal feasibility for the rotation vessel
• 100% Utilization: Recall from (57) that the capacity of a feeder arc was limited by the actual amount
of flowing containers from the original CRP solution and the lowest spare capacity of the connecting
rotation(s). When the demand is re-flown through the rotation graph with omission, the arcs being
100% utilized is of most interest. These arcs will all have a spare capacity of zero and no additional
omission demand can be delivered to the rotation. For arcs not being fully utilized, the CRP solution
tells us that it is not possible to send additional containers along the feeder arc - either because of no
spare capacity on other feeder arcs or all demand is simply delivered
• Flowing Amount: As explained in the LowActicityCall destroy function, it is not beneficial to have
port calls with a low (un)loading activity. Therefore, if a feeder port call is to be implemented in
the rotation, the amount of flow on the feeder arc should meet a minimum limit. In this project, the
minimum flow limit of a feeder arc is set to be 5% of the vessel capacity
Finding the flow in the rotation graph x′ along with the known rotation r, a baseline profit c(x′, r) can be
found. After the feeder arcs fulfilling the above requirements Af have been found, a delta-evaluation can
be made. This delta-evaluation consists of implementing the feeder arc in the rotation making r and a new
rotation graph from which the demand with omission is flown through again xtest. Notice that when a feeder
arc is implemented in the original rotation, it will be done in the natural direction of the arc. This means,
if a feeder arc from port p3 connects to rotation r at port call p1, it will be inserted before p1 - the previous
call will thus go to p3. For a feeder arc connecting from p2 in r to port p3, the list of calls will go from p2 to
p3 and then return to the cycle again. An illustration of this implementation of feeder arcs can be seen in
Figure 18.
A feeder arc with an improved profit c(xtest, r) > c(x′, r) will present a promising repair move. For the
best single move, all arcs in Af should be evaluated. However, Af can grow quite large and the process of
modifying rotation graph and flowing demand is relatively time consuming. Instead of spending significant
time on a single FeederCall iteration with the best possible move, it is rather preferred to make multiple
iterations with less quality assurance. For this reason, only a single feeder arc from Af will be chosen at
random and tested for improvement. In case, the chosen feeder arc indicates improvement, it is implemented

Speed Optimization
As mentioned several times, a crucial factor of a LSNDP solution is the costs for fuel - especially when the
AFSP perspective is added. With fuel consumption having a cubic dependency on sailing speed (see equation
(29)), the adjustment of speed will have a significant effect on the solution quality. The adjustment of speed
will follow the same pattern as shown in Figure 15, which will dictate the amount of vessels/rotation dura-
tion. In addition, the implemented speed optimization function will be able to alter the used canals for each
rotation. Furthermore, the speed/canal optimizer will be applied to all rotations in each ALNS iteration.
Even though some rotations have not been subject to any changes (destroyed/repaired), the optimizer is still
used, since (AF) fuel prices might have changed.
The optimizer will have a slight heuristic approach, but in general enumerate most possible canal/speed
combinations. The heuristic element consists of iterating through rotations with decreasing vessel capacity
and then fixing the canal/speed. For each rotation, the set of all possible canal combinations Cr
combi is first
generated and the maximum possible duration is found T. For each canal combination c ∈Cr
combi, the total
rotation distance dc
r is known. Firstly, the canal combination is checked for feasibility, which consists of
sailing the distance at maximum vessel speed to find the minimum sailing duration T c
min. In case, T c
min < T
the canal combination is deemed feasible. For a feasible canal combination, the speed optimization will try
to lower the sailing speed until rotation time equal T or minimum sailing speed is made. Along the process of
71
==PAGE:82==
lowering the sailing speed, the optimal speed is sought at the ’tipping points’ where the amount of rotation
vessels are changing (see Figure 15).
The optimal speed will typically occur at a whole week duration, but this is not necessarily preferred during
the ALNS search. If the speed is selected such that the rotation duration has a whole week value, it would not
leave any room for inserting calls in the above repair functions. For this reason, a buffer time δ is introduced
during the ALNS search. This means that the optimizer will evaluate candidate speeds such that the rotation
time will be a whole week minus δ. E.g. for a given rotation T = 12 weeks and a given canal combination has
Tmin = 8.6 weeks. In this case, the optimizer will evaluate the speeds, which would result in rotation length
of {9-δ, 10-δ, 11-δ, 12-δ} - assuming minimum speed is not reached. After preliminary tests, it was seen that
δ = 0.05 weeks provided the best looking results. For each speed candidate, the cost will be calculated in
terms of TC expenses and fuel cost. For the fuel cost, (29) is used to measure fuel consumption for the total
distance dc
r for the canal combination. From AFSP, the average fuel price is extracted to calculate the total

7.4
Algorithmic Tuning
In order to optimize the performance of the developed ALNS algorithm, a parameter tuning is conducted.
This tuning will be executed on a subset of the fuel scenarios and only on the WS-instance. The fuel scenarios
chosen as the training set will try to cover the nature of all fuel scenarios. Therefore, FullBunker, Bunker-
Methanol and FullAmmonia are selected as training fuel scenarios. These will represent the two extremes of
only bunker and only ammonia and then an intermediate fuel instance. With a tuning process only involving
the WS instance, it is noted that the proposed ALNS solution method is going to be ’tailor-made’ for an WS
instance size and global span. This mentality will naturally fit well for the overall purpose of this project,
but the method will lack a generic applicability. Once the preferred algorithmic setup has been found, it
will be tested on the self-created instances presented in Table 7. By evaluating the found results for non-WS
instances, a ’good’ objective is not known, but it might give an indication on the generic applicability of the
ALNS.
The tuning process of the ALNS will consist of a parameter tuning and a test of the destroy/repair function
setup. For the parameter tuning, the following three elements are tested:
• Initial Acceptance Probability - Y : The initial acceptance probability is important in the matter of
searching the solution space and escaping local optima. The following set of initial probabilities are
tested: Y ∈{0%, 0.5%, 1%, 3%, 5%, 7%}
• Solution Modification Degree - β: This degree of modification will determine the amount of alterations
(nRotations) being done in each ALNS iteration. The degree will be given in percent and be used
relative to the amount of ports in an instance P. Therefore, nRotations = ⌈β · P⌉. The set of β being
tested are: {2.5%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%}
• Reset Time Fraction - α: Here, the reset feature is tested in a binary setting. Recall that the reset
feature would reset the current solution back to the best found, in case the ALNS had not accepted a
new solution within a certain time α. Here, α will be determined based on the total solve time available
Tsolve. It is here only tested to see, if the feature will have an influence vs not using it. Therefore, only
the following two values are tested: α ∈{Tsolve, 0.1 · Tsolve}. This means either the feature is not used
α = Tsolve or the solution is reset after 10% of the solve time 0.1 · Tsolve
With the parameter values introduced, a total of 6 · 5 · 2 = 60 different parameter combinations exists. In
addition, all of these combinations will be tested on all three training fuel scenarios; FullBunker, Bunker-
Methanol and FullAmmonia. This accumulates to 180 test runs and in order to account for the stochasticity
each test run is repeated 10 times. The solve time limit Tsolve for each test run is set to 60 minutes (= 3600
seconds).
The result of running these test runs clearly showed that the reset feature should be used.
All of the
following tuning results will thus make use of the reset feature and the results without the feature can be
seen in appendix. Recall that the best known solution for the WS instance has a weekly profit of $58.14
M found by Krogsgaard et al. (2018). The most direct comparison to this value will be by considering the

Reaching Equilibrium
To summarize, the overall best found solution in each scenario has an objective value of:
• FullBunker: $ 57.22 M
• BunkerMethanol: $ 55.27 M
• FullMethanol: $ 52.05 M
• MethanolAmmonia: $ 50.52 M
• FullAmmonia: $ 49.68 M
In case, FullBunker is used as a baseline to represent a current liner shipping operation, then the full adoption
to FullAmmonia leads to a loss of profit of 13.2%. A loss that is probably not preferred by any operator
despite their environmental ambitions. However, the presented solutions might actually become equal in case
82

one or both of the following actions happen. Either a bunker levy is introduced (MBM), which will increase
the price of bunker fuel or a operator sailing on AF can ask for a higher freight rate (asking price) from
customers, which will increase profit. Especially, the latter action of asking for a higher price for carbon-free
delivery seems quite reasonable - an action already done in the Maersk ECO delivery program. For this
reason, it is tested how much bunker levy or increase in freight rate would be necessary for the above best
AF scenario solutions to reach an equilibrium with the baseline solution. For the bunker levy, this is a simple
matter of increasing the fuel price in the FullBunker solution until the objective value of the AF scenario
is reached. This procedure is modified slightly when comparing to the BunkerMethanol, since half of the
vessel fleet is also using bunker fuel. To test the increased profit in AF scenarios, the shipped demand will
be identified and its freight rate increased until the same profit as the FullBunker is reached. Notice that
both of these approaches are static approaches only using the found solutions. Both of the above procedures
of increasing bunker price and freight rate will undoubtedly have a more dynamic effect if the ALNS was rerun.

Conclusion
In this project, it has been investigated how the liner shipping can be decarbonized by applying emerging
technologies and route-optimization. This investigation has involved the development of an Adaptive Large
Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic framework from which the Liner Shipping Network Design
Problem (LSNDP) was solved. This project has introduced a new perspective to the LSNDP with the imple-
mentation of Alternative Fuels (AF) to the operations as a decarbonization action. The overall purpose of
the project was then to investigate the network effects of introduction AF to liner shipping in a global scale.
For capturing the global scale of a liner shipping instance, the WorldSmall (WS) known from LINER-LIB
has been the backbone of the project.
In order to model the introduction of AF to liner shipping, the Alternative Fuel Sourcing Problem (AFSP)
has been developed. Initially, a selection process was presented where the findings showed that the two AF
of most interest was methanol and ammonia. Here, methanol will work as an intermediate AF that is easy
to implement in the operation. Assuming an ammonia-based propulsion system can be developed, ammonia
will be the ideal AF with no emissions throughout its entire life-cycle. The main benefit for both AFs is that
they can be produced only relying on water, air and electricity from a renewable energy sources. The AFSP
thus had the objective of locating AF production sites such that it can supply with an sufficient amount of
AF to the liner shipping network. For this, a MIP model was developed that showed optimal solutions could
be found within a second when a set of rotations covering the WS instance was given.
For the LSNDP version considered, it was noted that no time transit constraints were considered for the
cargo. In addition, demand could be rejected, transshipment costs were used, the vessel speed optimization
was done on a service basis and the network structure could be complex. As input data, some updates and
additions were made on the LINER-LIB data regarding canal fees, vessel tank sizes and AF consumption. In
order to model the liner network on a graph form, three crucial types of nodes were introduced. The centroid
nodes would define the source and sink for cargo demand, for each port call two nodes defining the arrival
and departure of a vessel was made and lastly, a transshipment node was used to create a star structure.
This star transshipment structure was particularly useful when solving the Cargo Routing Problem (CRP).
To solve the CRP, a column generation (CG) algorithm was developed on a path-based multi-commodity
flow (MCF) model. This CG could exploit certain data structures, which led to it being able to solve a WS
size MCF within five seconds.
Before the ALNS framework could be applied, it was necessary to construct an initial solution of rotations for
the LSNDP. Here, the backbone flow method was used, which was first proposed by Krogsgaard and Thorsen
(2016). The method consisted of introducing a relaxed network where demand could flow in their preferred
path and then construct rotations based on this flow. In order to construct rotations based on flow, a simple
construction heuristic was developed. Some modifications to the backbone flow method was introduced in
order to take the AF perspective into account. Despite the AF-based rotations showing a significantly worse
weekly profit, the initial solutions showed AF production prices within an expected range. In the algorith-
mic tuning process of the ALNS, it was already seen how the ALNS was capable of levelling out the profit
84
==PAGE:95==
differences between AF and non-AF rotations. The tuning also showed some quite unexpected results. Here,
the ALNS preferred to make minor changes and only accept improving solutions in each iteration. Using
this setup, the ALNS also proved to be able to provide consistent results for WS-derived instances spanning
multiple continents.
Lastly, the final results were quite promising. The overall best found solution was less than 2% worse than
the best known solution for WS. Even though the largest difference in profit between a non-AF and AF
solution was 13.2%, the ALNS was still capable of find network structures that led to significant drops in
AF production prices. In general, the developed ALNS did favour a great deal of minor rotations with a
low sailing speed. The majority of vessels was deployed and they had generally quite high load factors.
The structure of many minor rotations did, however, lead to a large degree of transshipments, but less than
16% of demand was rejected. The found AF production sites were all part of the same group of seven port
locations. These seven locations favoured a proximity to European ports and had wind turbines (WT) as
the most crucial renewable energy source. The last findings showed that in order to level out the difference
between non-AF and AF-based network, a bunker levy or increase of freight rate seems unavoidable. Here,
an increase in freight rate of 3.7% would result in a network running on ammonia to be equally profitable to
a non-AF network. In combination with a bunker levy, the increase of freight rate might even be lower.
In total, the results seen in this project indicate that introducing AF to liner shipping is a feasible and
possibly even profitable action. Naturally, a great deal of technological developments are essential to achieve
this goal. However, the utopian idea of liner shipping running on AF in the nearby future might not seem
that far-fetched after all.

Future Work
As always, there is room for improvements, which leads the way for further work. Firstly, it is mentioned
that the developed solution method is not generally applicable in its current setup. The entire ALNS and
AFSP are ’tailor made’ to the WS instance, which might be the strongest and weakest feature of the solution
method. On the positive side, the WS instance fits perfectly to the strategic nature and purpose of the
problem investigated. The fact that a global area can be covered while having reasonable running time fits
nicely to this preliminary work of mixing LSNDP and AFSP. On the negative side, the solution method will
lack a generic nature for other instances - especially for smaller intra-continental instances, which was clearly
seen for the WS-EU instance.
In principle, it should not be too difficult to modify (and tune) the solution methods to fit smaller instances
as well. However, it may not co-align well with the objective of mixing LSNDP and AFSP. The AFSP will
essentially make high-level strategical decisions regarding the AF production location and would you not
exactly like to have this information based on your entire operation? Does it make sense to sub-optimize the
production locations for each smaller region? That might be unlikely. Instead, it would probably make sense
to have the current setup to choose the global production locations and then have them fixed when applying
a route-optimization model for each region. In this mentality, the true value of this project is the location
selection that can be backed by an improved route-optimization model.
The current route-optimization ALNS will for sure benefit from further work. Firstly, the unexpected result
of having no room for worse solutions will at some point be a drawback in the solution search. Currently,
the ALNS will be stuck if the current best solution cannot be improved at all in a single iteration. Even
with the introduced reset feature, the ALNS will simply loop back to the same solution, since it reset to
best found solution. This means that the current reset feature is completely useless in the found setup. One
possible action to accommodate both the desire to only accept improving solutions and introduce a helpful
reset feature is to allow worse solution when the search is stuck. In this way, the reset feature could be to
introduce an acceptance probability until a new solution is found. In this way, the local optima is escaped
and the search can continue to look for only improving solutions.
In addition, some of the destroy and repair functions will have to be redesigned. In particular, note how none
of the AF specific functions ended up being useful in the ALNS search. These AF specific functions might,
however, lack the perspective of demand flow/fuel production dependency. Especially, the GoodAFCall would
probably benefit from introducing the demand flow when selecting a ’good’ AF port. Alternatively, the def-
inition of a ’good’ AF port could be implemented in the FeederCall function such that an additional feeder
arc requirement could resolve around the AF fitness of the feeder call. Lastly, in order to comply to actual
liner shipping rotation, the OmissionAsRoute function would likely need to have limited role such that an
extensive amount of small rotations are reduced.
For the AFSP, an interesting addition would be to look into the renewable energy supply variation throughout
the year. The seasonal effect will undoubtedly have an influence when selecting a production site. In the
86
==PAGE:97==
current setup, it is not unreasonable to assume that any surplus production can be saved in large tanks for
later deficit production times. This is not explicitly taken into account in this project, since it would likewise
have some financial and space requirement perspectives. In general, the presented work should be seen in the
perspective of it being amongst some of the first projects looking at this strategical problem. A great deal
of the used values are thus either based on current bunker operations or assumptions from the most recent
research. As the AF initiatives and technical solutions emerge, the used values will need to be updated. In
particular, this project would benefit significantly from having AF vessel performance characteristics. Since
methanol vessels are just emerging into liner operations on a larger scale, this data is still fairly uncertain.
Likewise, with the brand-new ammonia production and propulsion technology, the future is yet too unknown.
In total, this means that the project would benefit from either ’newer’ AF data or a greater sensitivity analysis
of the input data.
