Abstract
Asthma is one of the most common non-communicable diseases. In addition to the symptoms caused
by airway inflammation, many patients experience having difficulties paying attention, memorizing and
planning all contributing to the burden of the disease. Our current knowledge of how the brain is affected
is not comprehensive enough to be able to treat the cognitive symptoms. Immune cells in the lungs
associated with asthma have recently been discovered in the healthy meninges, a site long considered
immune privileged. This project aimed to assess the presence and functionality of type 2 innate lymphoid
cells (ILC2s) and T helper 2 (Th2) cells in the meninges. The study also explored the similarity of these
cells with their counterparts in the lung, with the goal of developing treatments to alleviate the cognitive
symptoms associated with asthma. The project was conducted based on the hypothesis that it is possible
to isolate the meninges from the skull of C57BL/6J mice and that ILC2s and Th2 cells are present in
this tissue. Furthermore, we hypothesized that the cells can be identified using flow cytometry applying
same method used to identify ILC2s and Th2 cells in the lung and that they are functionally related
expressing IL-5 and IL-13. ILC2s are characterized by the lack of lineage markers. This study successfully
isolated cells from both the lung and meninges and identified a population of single, live immune cells
positive for CD45. Furthermore, a Lin−and a Lin−/TCRB+ population were identified. Quantification
of the cells revealed that the number of cells identified in the meninges was significantly lower than
in the lung, which was expected. The study also identified a population expressing CD90.2 in both
Lin−and CD4+cells. ST2 and GATA3 were found to be stable markers of ILC2 and Th2 cells. The study
identified GATA3+ and ST2+cells among Lin−/CD90.2+ and CD4+/TCR+/CD90.2+ cell populations
isolated from the meninges. The expression of IL-5 and IL-13 by ILC2s and Th2 cells isolated from the
lung and meninges were investigated. While no expression of IL-5 or IL-13 were observed in Th cells in
either the lung or meninges, IL-5 and IL-13 expressing cells were identified in the Linpopulation isolated
from both lung and meninges, suggesting that they are functionally related. The results obtained in this
project suggest that identification of ILC2s and Th2 cells in lung and meningeal tissue can be done using
flow cytometry with GATA3 and ST2 as positive cell markers. This makes it possible to isolate the cells
for further analysis, which is needed to understand how they affect the brain in an asthma model.

Introduction
Around 300 million people globally have asthma, a disease caused by a type 2 immune response towards
environmental allergens. It is one of the most common non-communicable diseases [Dharmage2019].
Some of the symptoms are dyspnoea, wheezing, cough and phlegm and loss of lung function [Porsb-
jerg2023]. In addition to the obvious symptom such as breathing difficulties caused by airway inflamma-
tion, many patients with asthma are also affected by cognitive dysfunction leading to having difficulties
paying attention, memorizing and planning [Irani2017]. The mechanisms by which asthma affects brain
function create a cognitive burden which, in conjunction with impaired lung function, contribute to the
disease’s impact on both individual and societal levels. And it has been proposed that asthma should
be considered a secondary neurological disease [Alb´eri2013]. However, so far no treatment is aiming at
relieving these symptoms.
Type 2 immune cells such as T helper 2 (Th2) cells and innate lymphoid type 2 cells (ILC2s) are highly
associated with asthma, and it has recently been discovered that Th2 cells and ILC2s are present in the
brain - also in healthy state [Holgate2015][Mamuladze2023]. How these immune cells of type 2 in the
brain are affected by a peripheral response such as observed in asthma patients has not yet been suffi-
ciently studied and there is still a lot of knowledge to be sought about the brain’s own immune system.
Mechanisms in which asthma affects brain function have been studied based on the ”lung-brain-axis”
theory looking at neuro-immune mechanisms such as cytokines being released in lung tissue transmitted
to the central nervous system (CNS) stimulating a brain response affecting asthma symptoms [Wang2023].
How chronic asthma leading to peripheral inflammation increases the level of inflammatory cytokines
in the brain [Xia2014]. The majority of studies investigating the cognitive symptoms of asthma patients
build upon the idea, that the mechanisms leading to cognitive decline observed in asthma patients are
due to a peripheral immune response ”spreading” to the CNS and brain. One of the biggest incentives for
investigating how asthma affects the immune cells in the brain, is the possibility of developing treatment
aiming at alleviating the cognitive symptoms, which current research does not yet enable.

5.1
The structure of the barrier of the brain
The brain has long been considered an immune-privileged site. This perception is built upon studies
showing that brain transplantation can take place without the transplanted brain being rejected as a
consequence of an immune response. The lack of an immune response led to the hypothesis that the
brain does not have an immune system, making it immune-privileged. However, the findings of the
glymphatic system and a diverse immune population including ILC2s and Th2 cells at the borders
of the healthy brain have altered the perception that the CNS is clearly separated from the immune
system [Rustenhoven2022]. What separates the CNS and immune system is an elaborate barrier system
comprised of meninges and specialized impermeable blood vessels. For many years it was thought that
the main function of these barriers was physical protection of the CNS from stress and entry of pathogens
and that no interaction between the CNS and the peripheral immune system was established. However,
the findings of a meningeal lymphatic system set the framework for anatomical, cellular and molecular
mechanisms enabling neuro-immune responses [Smyth2024].

5.1.2
Type 2 immune response in the lung
As already mentioned, we do not know very much about how type 2 immunity works in the brain and
what significance Th2 cells and ILC2s have for brain function. Type 2 immunity is mainly associated with
allergic pathology and characterized by the production of IL-4, IL-5 and IL-13 [Molofsky2023]. However,
the role of ILC2s and Th2 cells in the lung has been studied, and it is known that type 2 immunity
play an important role in the development of asthma. Asthma is a disease characterized by chronic
airway inflammation causing airway remodeling leading to respiratory symptoms such as wheezing,
shortness of breath, cough, and chest tightness [Holgate2015]. Most cases of asthma begin in childhood
as sensitization to common environmental allergens such as allergens from dust mites, fungi, pollens
and pets. Inhaling pollutants leads to disturbance of the airway epithelium. This will initiate danger
signaling and activation of innate signaling receptors. The epithelial cells of the airway will react and
secrete chemokines and cytokines such as IL-25 and IL-33 and activate ILC2s and Th2 cells.
The role of ILC2s in the lung
For many years the focus has mainly been on Th2 cells when discussing
type 2 immunity. However, it has become clear that ILC2s play a very important role, which also explains
why a type 2 immune response can be induced in RAG-mice [Gurram2019]. ILC2s are tissue-resident
cytokine-producing cells, which closely resemble Th2 cells in activation and cytokine-profile. The role of
ILC2s is dependent on developmental time points, inflammatory conditions and types of tissue, making
them a complex type of cells to understand [Messing2020]. However, there are some mechanisms that
are common to all subsets of ILC2s as seen in Figure 2.

ILC2s are activated by IL-33 and IL-25 which are alarmins released from stromal cells (such as epithelial
cells) primarily when dying or damaged. When IL-33 binds its receptor, ST2, expressed by ILC2s, it ac-

transcription factor GATA Binding Protein 3 (GATA3). GATA3 binds to the promoter-region upstream of
type-2 cytokine genes [Messing2020]. GATA3 is also activated in response to thymic stromal lymphopoi-
etin (TSLP) signaling. Activated ILC2s produce IL-4, IL-5, IL-9, IL-13 and Areg, which play an important
role in eosinophil activation and maintenance of M2 macrophages.
ILC2s also play a major role in combating tissue damage and depletion of ILC2s impairs lung function
and tissue repair following respiratory viruses. In a study investigating the role of ILC2s in regulating
airway epithelial integrity and orchestrating tissue homeostasis, mice were infected with influenza virus.
Influenza virus replicates mainly in airway epithelial cells causing damage to the epithelium. The results
of Monticelli et. al showed that mice lacking ILC2s had lower lung function following infection [Mon-
ticelli2011]. The study found that mice lacking ILC2s were not able to generate hyperplastic epithelial

The role of Th2 cells in airway inflammation
Th2 cells play a major role in the pathogenesis of airway
inflammation in which infiltration of eosinophils and Th2 cells is a central feature [Hogan1998]. Hogan et
al. investigated the role of antigen-activated CD4+ Th2 cells in induction of bronchial hyperreactivity by
looking at IL-5−/IL-5−mice. They saw that mice lacking IL-5 did not develop lung damage in response
to sensitization and aeroallergen challenge. They performed an adoptive transfer of CD4+ Th2 cells to the
mice lacking IL-5 and observed an induction of bronchial hyperreactivity and lung damage demonstrat-
ing the role of Th2 cells in the pathogenesis of asthma. Differentiation of Th2 cells happens upon contact
with dendritic cells (DCs) in the lymph node. Epithelial cells produce chemokines that causing DCs to
migrate to the mucosal epithelium. DCs loaded with allergens from pollutants migrate to the lymph node,
where they interact with na¨ıve T cells and drive the differentiation of T cells into Th2 cells [Molofsky2023].
Th2 cells migrate back to the airway mucosa and will upon activation by epithelial-derived cytokines
(IL-25 and IL-33) secrete granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), IL-3, IL-4, IL-5,
IL-9 and IL-13 leading to activation of eosinophils and mast cells and production of mucus. A subset of
allergen-specific Th2 cells will migrate to the B cell follicle in the lymph node and facilitate class switching
to from IgM to IgE. IgE drive mast cell and basophil degranulation leading to the release of inflammatory
mediators, which are associated with airway remodelling [Froidure2016].
5.1.3
Type 2 immune responses in the brain
Studies on how Th2 cells and ILC2s located in meninges are affected by asthma are, as already mentioned,
still lacking. However, a few studies have investigated their role in brain function.
Th2 cells in learning and aging
IL-4 has been shown to play an important role in learning and memory.
Naive CD4+ T cells differentiate into Th2 cells following IL-4 stimulation. The function of Th2 cells is also
linked to their own production of IL-4 creating a positive feedback loop. A mouse study investigating

In contrast to their protective role when it comes to learning, it has been shown that Th2 cells in the brain

The role of ILC2s in the brain
Like our knowledge of the importance of Th2 cells in the brain, what we
know about the role of ILC2 is also limited. It is known that ILC2s are immediate responders to injuries
in peripheral and barrier tissue, and that they are activated by IL-33. IL-33 is also a key CNS alarmin
and is found to be present in the CSF after spinal cord injury [Gadani2017]. A mouse study investigating
ILC2s in the meninges found that ILC2s located in the meninges are activated following spinal cord injury
[Gadani2017]. They used YET-cre 13 mice which have YFP expression regulated by the IL-13 promoter in
order to compare IL-13 induction in injured and uninjured mice. The levels of YFP were measured one
day post-injury showing increased expression in brain ILC2s in injured mice. Using flow cytometry, they
detected an expansion of ILC2s at the site of injury from 3 days post injury to 10 days post-injury showing
that ILC2s migrate to the lesion site. The functional impact of ILC2s on recovery was investigated using
a global ILC depletion strategy subsequently adding ILC2s into CSF before injury. Level of recovery was
measured using Basso mouse scale score (a behavior score) and lesion volumes, both showing improved
recovery of ILC2s-treated mice. However, they used lung-derived ILC2s for reconstitution, which could
underestimate the true impact of ILC2s on spinal cord injury [Gadani2017].
ILC2s are also playing a role in Alzheimer’s disease. In a mouse study investigating ILC2 function
in 3xTg-Alzheimer’s diseased mice, ILC2 function and abundance were impaired compared to control
wild-type mice [Fung2021]. Using flow cytometry to quantify the abundance of ILC2s, whole-brain
tissue was examined, with ILC2s being defined as CD45+Lin−Thy1+T1/ST2+. In accordance with the
results of previous studies, the brain parenchyma of both wildtype and diseased mice were lacking
ILC2s. They observed a greater number of ILC2s in the barrier tissue (choroid plexus, perivascular tissue,
leptomeninges) of the wild-type mice compared to diseased mice. They also compared the abundance
of ILC2s in the lung-tissue but did not see any difference between the two groups. Thus, emphasizing
that the defects of ILC2 in Alzheimer’s diseased mice are specific to brain-associated cells. In order to

5.2
Flow cytometry in neuro-immune research
Flow cytometry is a method for cellular analysis. As the name implies it is a method used to measure cells
suspended in a flowing stream. The technique enables measurements of cellular physiology, cell number,
viability etc. Flow cytometry is a widely used method within the field of immunology and enables
identification and characterization of subsets of cells within a heterogeneous sample [Cossarizza2021].
The cells are identified based on different biomarkers - typically antibodies tagged with fluorophores.
5.2.1
The principle of flow cytometry
The three main components of a flow cytometer are the fluidics system, optical system and the electronics.
The fluidics system is transporting the sample from the sample tube to the flow cell and either transferred
to waste or sorted (if using a fluorescence activated cell sorter (FACS)). The fluidics system organize the
sample into a single stream of cells. The optical system is responsible for creating a signal depending
on light sources, lenses and filters used. Detection of the sample takes place in the interrogation point,
where laser light interact with the cells, causing the light to scatter [Cossarizza2021]. The light scatter is
measured and correlated with relative cell size and cell structures. Forward scatter light (FSC) is correlated
to relative cell size as it is the amount of light that is scattered in the forward direction. Side scatter light
(SSC) correlates to structural complexity of the cell surface or inside the cell, as the cell compartments are
causing the light to scatter at larger angles. Besides light scatter, fluorescence emitted by fluorophores will
also result in a voltage pulse that is measured. The signal is dependent on the number of fluorophores
bound to the cell and the brightness of the fluorophores that are tagged to the antibody. The voltage pulse
data for each parameter measured such as FSC, SSC and the fluorophores are assigned to a bin for that
parameter. The electronics of the flow cytometer convert the data into a file that can be analyzed.

Lasers and filters in flow cytometry
Lasers with different wavelengths are the most common source
of light in flow cytometry. Each fluorophore has an optimal excitation and emission wavelengths that
is excited by a specific laser and detected by a specific filter. However, spectral overlap is a condition of
the method that one will have to deal with. Spectral overlap occurs when a fluorophore’s fluorescence is
detected by a filter other than the primary one. When designing a panel, the combination of fluorophores
must be chosen in order to minimize spectral overlap [Cossarizza2021]. One way to minimize and correct
spectral overlap is through compensation, a mathematical method used to correct the spillover from one
channel to another. This is done by making single color controls as a part of the experiment setup.
Panel design
As already mentioned, spectral overlap is important to consider, when designing a panel.
However, it is also important to consider aspects such as relative expression levels of antibodies, co-
expression of cellular-markers and the brightness of the fluorophores [Mair2019]. In order to minimize
spillover within a population, it is preferable to match fluorophores with a large distance on the spec-
trum with co-expressed antigens. If the density of an antigen is high, it is optimal to pair it with a dim
fluorophore and vice versa.

5.3
The activity of ILC2 and Th2 cells in the brain
It is known that ILC2s and Th2 cells located in the lung play an important role in the development and
pathogonesis of asthma. It has only recently been discovered that ILC2 and Th2 cells are also present in
meninges of the healthy brain, and it has been shown that the immune cells located in the meninges have
an effect on cognition. However, studies assessing their role in brain function, reveal opposites results
when trying to define whether they have a protective or pathogenic function in the brain. However, the
investigation of meningeal ILC2 and Th2 cells is in its infancy. There are still many things that need to
be investigated such as where the cells originate, when and how they are activated, and what is their
function. In order to answer all of these questions and map the role of ILC2 and Th2 cells in the brain of
Page 21 of 62
==PAGE:23==
MSc Thesis

asthmatic patients, it is necessary to develop methods enabling identification of the cells. Meningeal tissue
is delicate and contains very few immune cells compared to lung tissue. Thus, the initial experiments
can advantageously be based on what is already known about the lungs and applied to meningeal tissue
from a healthy brain.
5.4
Aim and hypothesis
The aim of the overall project, which this study supports, is to investigate the role of brain immune cells
of type 2 in an asthma disease model. The aim of the present thesis is to assess ILC2s and Th2 cells in
the meninges, a site previously thought to be immuno-compromised. The project specifically aims to
explore whether ILC2s and Th2 cells in the meninges are functionally related to those in the lung. Type
2 immune cells in the meninges could hold the mechanism for the cognitive burden in airway allergy
and asthma. Thus, assessing presence and function of ILC2 and Th2 cells in the meninges versus lung
will provide valuable insights into future therapeutic applications. This study was conducted based on
the hypothesis that it is possible to isolate the meninges from the skull and that ILC2s and Th2 cells are
present in healthy meninges. Furthermore, we hypothesized that the cells can be identified using flow
cytometry applying same method developed to identify ILC2s and Th2 cells in the lung and that they are
functionally related expressing IL-5 and IL-13.

Materials and methods
Lungs and meninges from untreated C57BL/6 mice were harvested and digested with the purpose of
identifying ILC2s and Th2 cells in the tissue. Titration of antibodies was done in lung-tissue in order to
ensure a high enough cell number.
6.1
Sample preparation
6.1.1
Harvesting of tissue
C57BL/6 mice were euthanize with CO2 and injected with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) into the lower
left part of the heart to perfuse the lungs. To remove excess blood the freshly removed lungs were placed
into PBS then transferred directly into ice cold RPMI-1640 containing 10% fetal calf serum (FCS) in a
24-well plate. After harvesting the lungs, the head was removed using scissors. Fine surgical scissors
were used to cut and remove the skull cap. The scull was placed into the 24-well plate containing ice cold
PBS with 3% FCS while extracting other tissue. The meninges was slowly removed from the skull using
a dissecting scope. Fine forceps were used to remove the meninges from the surface of the skull cap by
gentle pulling off the membranous layer. The meninges was gently scraped from the edges of the skull
downward to nose bone. The free meninges was transferred into Eppendorf tube containing 0.5 mL ice
cold RPMI-1640 containing 10% FCS. .
6.1.2
Digestion and homogenization
Lungs were cut into small pieces and transferred into 40 mL tubes containing pre-heated RPMI-1640
with FCS (10%). Digestion enzymes were added. 75 µL DNase I (3 µg/µL) and 100 µL Liberase TM
(5µg/µL) were added to each sample. The lungs were placed onto a cell shaker and digested at 37 ◦C at
150 rpm for 60 minutes. For the digestion of meninges pre-heated RPMI-1640 and FCS (1%) was added
to the Eppendorf tubes containing the meningeal tissue. Digestion enzymes, 20µL Liberase TM with the
concentration of (5µg/µL) and 15µL DNase I with the final concentration of 3 µg/µL were added. The
meningeal tissue was incubated at 37 ◦C for 30 minutes. The digested tissue, both lung and meninges, was
homogenized by passing it through 100 µm filters. Remaining red blood cells in lung-tissue were lysed
using Red Blood Cell lysis reagent (0.83 w/v% ammonium chloride). 25 µL 123count eBeads Counting
beads were added to each sample (1.000.000 beads/mL).

6.2
Cell count
The cell concentration of the lung-samples was counted using DeNovix CellDrop FL fluorescence cell

channel of the cell counter and the cell concentration was recorded. Cell concentration of each sample

together with the PMA and ionomycin in order to block intracellular transport processes.

Staining
6.5.1
Live/Dead Staining and Fc-Block
Dead cells were stained using 50µL Live/Dead Scarlett in a 1:1000 dilution in PBS. The cells were placed
covered from light and on ice. After 15 minutes PBS was added and the samples were spun down twice
and the supernatant discarded. With the aim of limit unspecific binding of the Fc-region to Fc-receptors
on the cells, 25µL Fc-block solution (eBioscience, clone 93) with the concentration of 0.005 µg/µL was
added to each well for 15 minutes covered on ice.
6.5.2
Staining of extracellular markers
The antibodies are diluted in Fluorescent Activated Cell Sorter (FACS) buffer (PBS containing 3% FCS).
The concentration of antibody used for staining was determined based on results obtained during the
titration experiment with staining time of 30 minutes. Only GATA3 and IL-5 and IL-13 were stained
overnight. 50 µL of antibody-mixture was added to each well and the plate was covered and placed on
ice for 30 minutes. The samples were washed with FACS buffer, spun down and resuspended, twice.
6.5.3
Fixation and permeabilization
Before staining of intracellular markers the cells need to be fixed and permabilized. This was done using
FOXP3/Transcription factor staining Buffer set from eBioscience. 100µL fixation buffer was added to
each sample and left on ice for 1 hour. The samples were washed with 100µL permeabilization buffer
spun down (450g, 4 minute, 4 ◦C) and washed with 200µL permeabilization buffer then spun down again
(450g, 4 minute, 4 ◦C). The supernatant was discarded and the cells were ready intracellular staining.
6.5.4
Intracellular staining
Intracellular antibodies are diluted in permeabilization buffer. The antibody concentration is presented
in results. 50µL of antibody mix was added to each sample and left in the fridge overnight. 150µL perme-
abilization buffer was added to each well and and spun down (450 g, 4 minutes, 4 ◦C). The supernatant
was discarded. One more washing step was done before resupension in FACS buffer.

8
Discussion
The overall purpose of the study was to identify ILC2 and Th2 cells in the lung and in meninges of
mice. With the aim of investigating the abundance and activity of these two types of immune cells in
the meninges, a panel for flow cytometry was designed and tested on lung tissue as reference organ and
applied to meningeal tissue from C57BL/6J mice. Quantification of CD45+ cells, Lin−and Lin−TCRβ+
was done in both lung and meningeal tissue. CD127 and CD90.2 were tested as identification markers of
ILCs. ST2 and GATA3 were tested as positive markers for ILC2 and Th2 cell identification in lung tissue
and further applied onto meningeal tissue. The activity of the cells were tested assessing IL-5 and IL-13
expression in lung an meningeal tissue.
8.1
Titration of antibodies and time of staining
With the purpose of finding the optimal dilution of antibodies in order to maximize the signal when
doing flow cytometri, titration of antibodies was conducted. A very low antibody concentration will
result in low signal and no identification of a positive population. An increase in antibody concentration
will increase the signal of the positive population in a linear manner, however at some point, it will
increase the risk of non-specific binding moving the negative population closer to the positive population
[Burn2024]. This can be seen in the results of the titration of CD4. The extracellular antibodies were
tested with a staining time of 30 minutes and overnight and a separation index was calculated. The
separation index enables identification of the dilution that gives the maximum brightness with minimum
background. When comparing the same antibody dilution between samples stained for 30 minutes and
overnight, the separation index is in general higher calculated for overnight stains. This could be due to
the fact that non-specific binding is caused by low affinity interactions. Overnight staining enables the
use of very low antibody concentrations, which lowers the risk of low affinity binding of the antibodies.
However, it was decided to proceed with a staining time of 30 minutes when doing extracellular staining
in order to do overnight staining of the transcription factor GATA3 and cytokines IL-5 and IL-13. It would
not have been possible to do overnight staining of both extra- and intracellular markers, taking the time
perspective of the experimental work into account. Longer antibody incubation for GATA3, IL-5 and IL-13
was priorotized. The decision was based on the assumption that low antibody concentration would limit
non-specific binding and that the longer staining time would optimize the antibody’s access to the cell
and binding to markers. Furthermore, since the purpose of cytokine staining was to evaluate the function

. In an optimal setting, the titration would have been tested on the samples of interest [Burn2024]. In this

8.2
Quantification of immune cells in lung and meninges
As hoped it was possible to identify CD45+ cells, Lin−and Lin−TCRβ+ cells both in the lungs and
meningeal tissue of three mice using the panel designed for this experiment. Quantification using cell
count in FlowJo showed that the number of cells identified in the lungs were much higher than the cells
identified from meningeal tissue. This was to be expected as the meninges is very small compared to the
lung. In addition, the meninges is fragile and very hard to identify, and the removal of the meninges from
the skull had to be done using a microscope. However, the results show that we did manage to isolate
the meninges from the skull, since it is known that immune cells in the brain are located in meninges
[Alves2020]. The number of cells isolated from the meninges compared between donors does not vary
as much as one might have expected, due to the fact that the process of isolating the meninges is very
challenging.
8.3
Identification of ILCs and Th cells using CD127 and CD90.2 as markers
All CD4+ T cells seem to express CD90.2 and no distinct CD90.2−sub populations can be identified based
on the data. Besides being a marker of ILCs, CD90.2 is also used as a marker of T cells. It was the first T
cell marker to be identified and became a marker of thymus-derived lymphocytes [Haeryfar2004]. This
could explain why no CD90.2- sub population is appearing amongst the Lineage−TCRβ+/CD4+ gated
cells, since all cells in that population are T cells expressing CD90.2. CD127 is the IL-7 receptor, of which
ILCs require for development. Studies have shown that mice deficient in IL-7 have reduced ILC2s in
the lung [Sheikh2022]. IL-7 is also important for CD4+ T cells as it promotes cell survival of both CD8+
and CD4+ T cells [Bradley2005]. Thus, we would expect a sub population within the gated Lin−and
CD4+ T cells expressing CD127. Surprisingly, this is not reflected in the data. In addition, the absence of

a CD127 FMO complicates defining a positive gate. A study done by Cavagnero et al. found ILC2 cells
producing type 2 cytokines not expressing CD127 [Cavagnero2019]. This could explain why we don’t see
any CD127 positive subsets because CD127 could be down regulated. The study also found that CD127
is down regulated or even absent on activated T cells [Cavagnero2019]. Even though we expected to find
CD127+ sub populations within the gated cell populations both within the population defined as Th cells
(Lin−TCRβ+/CD4+) and Lin−cells (Lin−TCRβ-), several studies show that down regulation of CD127
on Th cells and ILC2s in tissue occurs.
8.4
GATA3 and ST2 as positive markers of type 2 immune cells in lung
Staining with antibodies against GATA3 and ST2 was done in lung tissue of mice. This was conducted in
order to investigate whether they can be used as valid positive markers of ILC2 and Th2 cells - with the
purpose of being applied to the meninges. ST2 is the IL-33 receptor and is expected to be expressed on
both ILC2 and Th2 cells, since both types of cells are activated by IL-33. GATA3 is the main transcription
factor responsible for the expression of type 2 cytokines such as IL-5 and IL-13 and is also expected to be
present in ILC2 and Th2 cells. It is already known that ILC2 play a major role in airway inflammation and
are abundant in asthmatic lungs [Badrani2023]. Since the cells are tissue-resident, we hoped to find them
in the lung of healthy mice using ST2 and GATA3 as cellular markers. We were able to identify GATA3+
and ST2+ cells within the Lin−population. This is consistent with work done by Chang. et al, where
they identified around 5000 Natural helper Cells (CD45+/Lin−/ST2+) in lung tissue from BALB/c mice
on day 0 of infection, which corresponds to a healthy lung [Chang2011]. We were also able to identify
ST2+ and GATA3+ cells within the CD4+ T cell population, however to a much lesser extent. Similar
results was obtained in another study, showing that few CD4+ T cells were found in lung tissue of in
naive mice[Kobayashi2023]. Unsurprisingly, further quantification of the ST2 and GATA3 positive cells
revealed that the numbers of Th2 cells identified as CD4+/ST2+ or GATA3+ cells were much lower than
the numbers of ST2+ or GATA3+ Lin−cells. ILC2s are tissue-resident, whereas Th2 cells need activation
via antigen recognition before entering the tissue. The mice used for this experiment had not been exposed
to allergens that would lead to a Th2 response. Which is most likely the reason for the low cell number
of CD4+ identified as ST2+ and GATA3+. The cell number is shown to vary between lung donors. This
could be due to variations in the experimental work. The abundance of these type 2 immune cells are
very low, and only few variations in the experimental work make a big difference. Looking at the number

on ST2 within same donor. The difference between cell count based on the two different markers ST2
and GATA3 is not affected by experimental variation, since they are measured from the same samples.
The small variation could indicate that both proteins are valid as positive markers of ILC2 and Th2 cells.
Looking at the data comparing the unstimulated and stimulated samples, we observe a small difference

results, which emphasizes that both GATA3 and ST2 can be used as valid positive markers of ILC2 and
Th2 cells.
8.5
Identification of ILC2 and Th2 cells in meninges
Analysis of Lin−and CD4+ T cells’ expression of ST2 and GATA3 enabled us to identify Lin−and CD4+
T cells positive for GATA3 and ST2 in the meninges of two mice. Recent studies have shown that both
ILC2s and Th2 cells can be found in the meninges of healthy mice [Gadani2017][Derecki2010]. Since
ILCs are characterized by lacking lineage specific markers, it has been difficult to find consensus in
the use of positive markers used for identification, which is still a topic of discussion [Cavagnero2020].
Furthermore, ILC2s and Th2 cells are only recently been discovered in meningeal tissue and taken into
consideration that ILCs are able to adapt to local environments, makes them a complex population to
identify using techniques such as flow cytometry. However, it has been shown that CD90.2, ST2 and
GATA3 are expressed on meningeal ILC2s [Gadani2017][Barron2023]. Further quantification of cells
identified as ST2+ or GATA3+ revealed that ILC2s were more abundant in the meninges than Th2 cells.
Derecki et al. were able to identify around 800 CD4+ T cells in meninges of a naive mouse, which is much
higher than the result obtained in this study [Derecki2010]. They did not use neither ST2 or GATA3 as Th2
marker, but IL-5 expression. This could explain the difference. The difference in the abundance of Th2
cells could also be explained by the mouse model used. It is not clear whether Derecki et al. use BALB/c
mice or C57BL/6 mice in their experiment. The two mouse strains differ in their immune responses.
C57BL/6 is more primed towards a type 1 response whereas BALB/c mice is more primed towards a
type 2 response. This experiment uses C57BL/6 mice, which could explain why a lower number of Th2
cells is observed in this study. This study found between 200 and 600 ILC2 cells in meninges. The number

ILC2s in meninges of healthy mice using CD90.2 and ST2 as cellular markers [Fung2021]. Together, this
suggests that ILC2 and Th2 cells were successfully identified in the meninges of healthy mice.
8.6
Cytokine expression of ILC2 and Th2 cells in lung and meninges
The activity of ILC2 and Th2 cells was investigated by analysing IL-5 and IL-13 expression. ILC2 and Th2
will upon activation produce IL-5 and IL-13, therefore we used antibodies against those two cytokines.
In this experiment, due to the design of the panel, it was not possible to combine positive markers of
ILC2 and Th2 cells with the analysis of cytokines. And in order to determine the percentage of ILC2 and
Th2 cells that produce IL-5 and IL-13, the cells need to be gated based on positive markers such as ST2 or
GATA3. Instead the percentage of Lin−and CD4+ T cells positive for IL-5 and IL-13 was analysed in lung
and meningeal tissue. The cytokine production was compaired between tissue. No significant differences
in IL-5 and IL-13 expression were seen analyzing the cytokine expression of Lin−cells. However, the
data shows that there could be a tendency to IL-13 being more expressed both in the lung and meninges
compared to IL-5. Another study found that ILC2s in steady state constitutively express IL-5 and that
IL-13 expression is induced by type 2 inflammation [Nussbaum2013]. However, this was only observed
in lung ILC2s. ILC2s in the lamina propria express IL-13 constitutively. We did not observe a significant
difference in neither IL-5 or IL-13 expression comparing lung and meningeal tissue. Work conducted by
Gadani et al. revealed that the basal activation state of ILC2s in lung are increased compared to ILC2s
in the meninges [Gadani2017]. However, this is not reflected in our data. ILC2 are a complex group of
cells and likewise the cytokine profile, which is stimulated by many different types of molecules and
pathways [Messing2020]. Studies have shown that ILC2 cells stimulated via the cAMP/PKA pathway
reduce the expression of IL-5 compared to cells stimulated via IL-33 [Olgu2022]. In this experiment the
cells were stimulated using PMA and ionomycin. PMA stimulates protein kinase C and NFKB. NFKB
is also stimulated via the cAMP/PKA pathway, which might alter the cytokine profile. In addition to
the change in the cytokine level as a result of the stimulation, the cytokine profile itself can also change
according to the stimuli. Studies have shown that upon IL-33 and retinoic acid stimulation, ILC2s are
able to produce IL-10 [Bal2020]. The plasticity of the cells makes it difficult to asses the activity based
upon limited cytokine detection. No detection of IL-13 or IL-5 positive CD4+ T cells was found in neither
lung or meninges, which is in agreement with another study looking at the expression of IL-5 in CD4+ T

8.7
The role of type 2 immune cells in meninges
The plasticity of ILC2s make them difficult to identify using surface-markers since those are regulated
dependent on stimulation, specific tissue environment and so on. Furthermore, the function of these cells
is constantly expanding in line with new discoveries of different sub populations. Recent studies have
identified a population of IL-10 producing ILC2s cells and discovered that intracisternal administration
of IL-33 increased the number of ILC2s in meninges in mice [Emami2023]. This protective role of limiting
inflammation could also be the case for ILC2s in the meninges of asthmatic patients. When discussing the
influence of ILC2 and Th2 cells on brain function, it is natural to ask whether they play a protective role.
It has been shown that mice immunized against ovalbumin avoided ingestion of solutions containing
the egg protein, that otherwise was preferred by mice not allergic to ovalbumin. Florsheim et al. have
investigated which mechanisms of the immune system lead to altering of the CNS [Florsheim2023].
They found that injecting mice with IL-4 enhanced the avoidance response. In the case of avoidance
behavior caused by immune sensing of food allergens, it is somewhat clear that the role of the CNS
alterations by IL-4 are protective by limiting the exposure to the allergen [Florsheim2023]. What about
the cognitive effects seen in relation to asthma? Does cognitive impairment have a protective effect on
asthma pathology? It has been shown that Alzheimer’s affects the severity of asthma in mice [Wu2019].
The mouse study was conducted by Wu et al. in order to investigate how a neuropsychiatric condition
such as Alzheimer’s is able to modulate the severity of asthma. They measured different features of
asthma in mice with and without Alzheimer’s and found that the severity of asthma features is reduced
in the group of diseaded mice. One of the features they measured in order to assess the severity of asthma
is eosinophilic accumulation in the pulmonary airway. The influx of eosinophils measured in the BALF of
diseased mice was significantly lower compared to control mice. The mechanism causing this is unclear,
however an exciting topic for further investigations.

Conclusion
This project aimed to identify ILC2s and Th2 cells in meninges of mice, based on knowledge obtained
by looking in the lung. In order to achieve this aim, a panel for flow cytometry was designed based on
cellular markers used in other studies. It was possible to isolate cells from both lung and meninges and
identify a population of single and live immune cells positive for CD45. Furthermore, the study identified
a Lin- and a Lin−/TCRbeta+ population. Quantification of the two populations revealed a big difference
in number of cells identified in the meninges compared to the lung. This was expected, as the meninges
is much smaller. Next, CD127 and CD90.2 were tested as markers of ILCs and Th cells in the lung. No
CD127+ population could be identified, however, CD127 is shown to be downregulated in different
tissue, which could explain our findings. It was possible to identify a population expressing CD90.2 both
in Lin−and CD4+ cells. Investigating the stability of ST2 and GATA3 upon stimulation showed that the
cell count was not affected. Using the knowledge gained by investigating lung tissue, it was possible to
identify GATA3+ and ST2+ cells among Lin−/CD90.2+ and CD4+/ TCRbeta+/CD90.2+ cell populations
isolated from the meninges. Due to the design of the panel, the activity of cells gated as Th cells and
Lin−were investigated. While no expression of IL-5 or IL-13 were observed in Th cells neither in lung or
meninges, it was possible to identify IL-5 and IL-13 expressing cells in the Lin−population isolated from
both lung and meninges, suggesting that they are functionally related. However, further investigation
is needed in order to understand the role of ILC2s and Th2 cells in the brain and how they are related
to ILC2 and Th2 cells in the lung. Studies investigating the activity of ILC2s have led to the discovery of
many different sub populations expanding the understanding of the role the cells are playing in different
tissue. Even though the plasticity of the cells complicates the understanding of them, single-cell RNA
sequencing would allow a deeper understanding of the specific role ILC2s are playing in meningeal and
lung tissue. The results obtained in this project suggest that identification of ILC2s and Th2 cells in lung
and meningeal tissue can be done using flow cytometri with GATA3 and ST2 as positive cellular markers.
The identification of ILC2s and Th2 makes it possible to isolate the cells for further analysis, which is
needed in order to understand their function and how they affect the brain in a asthma model.

Future perspectives
In order to optimize the panel for investigating the activity of ILC2 and Th2 cells in the meninges, anti-
bodies against ST2 or GATA3 could be conjugated with either PE or APC, leaving the spare fluorophore
free to detect cytokines. That would make it possible to identify ILC2 and Th2 cells based on a valid
positive marker and then assess the activity based on cytokine production. Furthermore FMOs of ST2,
GATA3, IL-5 and IL-13 should be included in both lung and meningeal samples. It would be beneficial to
be able to identify ILCs, thus testing another clone of CD127 conjugated to a brighter fluorophore would
be beneficial for further identification of the three different subsets of ILCs.
It would obviously be very interesting and necessary for the following experiments to set up an asthma
model and compare ILC2 and Th2 cell numbers in the meninges between asthmatic and non-asthmatic
mice. Studies have shown that progesterone increases the number of ILC2 and amplifies allergic inflam-
mation in the lung [Trivedi2024], thus taking the sex into account is necessary. The ILC2 population is, as
already discussed, a very diverse cell population and their function and activity is very much dependent
on the environment. It would be interesting to look at single-cell analysis of ILC2s from the meninges
in order to get a better understanding of their role. Comparison of single-cell analysis between ILC2s
found in the meninges of healthy versus asthmatic mice but also ILC2s from lung versus brain tissue.
There is still a long way to go in terms of clarifying their role - especially how they affect brain-function.
The use of single-cell RNA sequencing would enable us to characterize the gene program underlying the
different mechanisms of development, activation and function determining the sub populations of ILC2s,
which is still very unclear.
The main purpose of clarifying the role of ILC2s and Th2s in the brain of asthmatic patients is the pos-
sibility of developing a treatment that relieves the cognitive symptoms that asthma patients experience,
which are not treated by the medication they are already administered. With the current knowledge, it
cannot be conclude whether the cells are pathogenic or play a protective role in the brain. It would be
fatal to assume that they play the same role in the brain as in the lungs and inhibit their function, if they
actually have a protective effect as observed in studies examining the role of ILC2s in CNS injury or in
mucosal tissue, where they elicit an anti-inflammatory phenotype by expressing IL-10 [Emami2023].
