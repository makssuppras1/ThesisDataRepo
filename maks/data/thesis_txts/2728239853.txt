CO2 Heat pump cycle for full electrification of milk powder spray dryer
Abstract
The industrial sector alone accounts for 22 % of total GHG emissions in the year 2021. There-
fore, the electrification of industrial processes is significant. Particularly, food, chemical, and
pharmaceutical industries that require very high-temperature process heat for drying pro-
cess. In this study, an investigation into the possibility of full electrification of a spray dryer
system for the milk powder process is carried out. In the milk powder process, the spray
dryer is the highest energy-intensive unit as the inlet air is heated from 15 ◦C to ≈240 ◦C.
The existing spray dryer system uses convectional natural gas boilers, steam heaters as the
heat source. For which the possibilities of heat pump integration is examined thereby the
dependency on fossil fuels can be decreased. A detailed study of its performance and eco-
nomic indicators are discussed for different heat pump configurations.
Four heat pump configurations are modelled for heat pump operating temperature 200 ◦C
and 240 ◦C each. From the results of process modeling, performance analysis and techno-
economic analysis the total COPsystem varies between 1.64 to 2.53 and 1.97 to 3.25 for Heat
Pump (HP) operating temperature 200 ◦C and 240 ◦C. The LCOH varies by 63 e/MWh to
88 e/MWh.

Introduction & Literature
The European Union aims to achieve climate neutrality by 2050, with net-zero greenhouse
gas emissions, which is the core objective of the European Green Deal and the Paris Agree-
ment. The Industrial sector accounts for 22 % of total net emissions for the year 2021 [1]. In
order to reach the goal of net-zero emission, the conversion of the industrial energy supply
process is significant by electrification and improving the energy efficiency of the production
process. The escalating fuel costs serve as a driving force for industries to adopt renewable
energy sources into their production process. In particular, industries that supply high-
temperature process heat by using natural gas boilers by combustion of fossil fuels turns out
unattractive in both economic and environmental aspect. Therefore, integrating electricity
from renewable energy sources to electrify the industrial energy supply process emerges as a
novel solution to mitigate the impact [2].
This project aims at electrifying the heat source of the spray dryer unit in a milk powder
plant. The spray dryer is the energy-intensive unit in a milk powder plant as the inlet air
supply has to be heated from 15 ◦C to ≈240 ◦C for drying the liquid milk passing inside the
drying chamber. At present, the required energy is being supplied by natural gas boilers and
electric heaters. After the drying process, the air streams exit the drying chamber and are
released into the atmosphere as waste heat, resulting in the release of a substantial amount
of useful heat as waste. Even though necessary measures have been taken up in recovering
the waste heat, the solutions are not up to the state of the art.
The system releases enormous heat in the form of waste heat or exhaust air. This heat can
be utilized as heat source of the heat pump system thereby absorbing the heat energy from
the source, upgrading it and utilizing it to heat the inlet air streams. As for the heat pump
system that runs completely on electrical power so not only the CO2 emission is mitigated
but also maximizes the utilization of energy from the process streams. In this project, the
electrification potential of the spray drying system by integrating a high-temperature heat
pump as a replacement for the existing heat source is addressed. Prior to this, preliminary
knowledge of the heat pump configuration and identifying the suitable refrigerant for the
required temperature lift is essential. Therefore, several literature findings were helpful in
finding the appropriate configuration and refrigerant.
Furthermore, numerous studies have been conducted on high-temperature heat pumps, pro-
viding valuable insights for the design and integration of heat pump systems. Liang et al.
[3] investigated high-temperature heat pumps that supply process heat up to 236 ◦C by
integrating three heat pump systems with low global warming potential refrigerants, i.e.
ammonia, hydrocarbon, and water/steam. Zühlsdorf et al. [4] analysed high-temperature
heat pump with process heat above 150 ◦C using two different refrigeration cycles 1)multi-
stage cycle using R718 (water) as refrigerant 2)Reversed Brayton cycle using R744 (CO2) as
the refrigerant. Zühlsdorf et al. [5] studied the integration of a heat pump using a zeotropic
mixture as the refrigerant in a milk powder spray dryer, to preheat the drying air up to 120

2.1
Design & Data of Existing Spray Dryer System
Figure 2 depicts the schematic of existing spray dryer system inspired from [3]. The feed line
supplies homogenized and pasteurized liquid milk, which is then passed through an atomizer
and sprayed into the drying chamber of the spray dryer unit, where it dries across varying
temperatures. The spray dryer unit is equipped with three air inlet ports, each supplying air
streams with distinct temperatures, which enter the chamber and evaporate moisture in the
milk droplets. The air streams include Main Air (MA) stream of 240 ◦C, Static Fluid Bed
(SFB) stream of 110 ◦C, and Vibrating Fluidizer (VF) stream of 60 ◦C. After the drying
process the powdered milk is collected from the VF and the air steams used to dry the milk
is released into the environment as exhaust air with a temperature around 67 ◦C from the
bag filter through the cyclone. The fine particles from the exhaust air are collected in the
cyclone & bag filter and supplied back into the drying chamber.

2.3
Heat Pump Modelling
In this section the assumptions, and configuration used in the modelling heat pump are
discussed. The complete heat pump configuration is modelled in Aspen Plus (V11) process
simulation software. The Peng-Robinson property method [12] and Peng-Robinson cubic
equation of state in figure 23 is used to calculate the refrigerant properties of the system.
The thermodynamic properties of the heat pump cycle are based on the steady-state mass
and energy balance equation given by
˙Q = ˙m · cp · ∆Tmin
(9)
The following design assumptions and methods were considered in the heat pump modelling
process:
• All the heat exchanger components (gas cooler, evaporator, HX & IHX) in the model
were assumed to have a minimum temperature difference of 10 K.
• The compressor isentropic and mechanical efficiencies are assumed as 0.75 and 0.95
respectively [3].
• The refrigerant’s mass flow rate is calculated by the sink outlet temperature of the MA
stream for all heat pump models and calculated using the manipulators (Design spec
block).
• The discharge pressure of the compressor is calculated by the Design spec block. The
outlet pressure is limited by the ∆Tmin of 10 K across the gas cooler sink outlet and
temperature at the compressor’s outlet.
• The evaporation pressure is controlled by regulating the expansion valve pressure using
a design spec block by assuming a minimum temperature difference of 20 K on the

2.3.1
Heat Pump Configuration
In this project four heat pump system configuration is modelled in Aspen Plus for operating
temperature 200 ◦C and 240 ◦C. Especially, two models with Internal heat exchanger com-
ponent. Latter models with cascade system configuration. In general the heat source inlet
temperature is at 67 ◦C, and the system has large temperature lift of 133 ◦C and 173 ◦C for
heat pump models 200 ◦C and 240 ◦C respectively. Only with the available source it requires
high electrical power to upgrade the heat to reach the desired temperature. Therefore, it is
expected that using INX component helps increase the degree of superheating and subcooling
of refrigerant so, it is positioned accordingly in the model. Further, in the cascade system,
the bottom cycle is considered similar to a booster system that helps reaching the required
temperature with minimal energy consumption. The following model configurations were
considered:
• Model-1: Single-stage Transcritical heat pump cycle with IHX between the gas coolers.
• Model-2: Single-stage Transcritical heat pump cycle with IHX end of gas coolers.
• Model-3: Cascade heat pump system with R744 at HTC and R600a at LTC.
• Model-4: Cascade heat pump system with R744 at HTC and R600 at LTC.
The natural refrigerant R744 is used in model-1,2 and HTC of model-3,4 (cascade system),
due to its high heat transfer coefficient, large temperature glide and negligible ODP & GWP
and hydrocarbon R600a, R600 is used as a refrigerant for the bottom cycle in a cascade heat
pump system due to its very low ODP & GWP and suitable for low-temperature glide.
To study the behavior, performance, and efficiency of heat pumps under different conditions
the heat pump models are designed with distinct configurations inspired from [6]. For in-
stance, in Model-1 the IHX is positioned between the Gas Coolers, while in Model-2, the

Model-1: Transcritical heat pump cycle with IHX between the gas coolers
Figure 4 depicts a graphical illustration of the heat pump configuration. The Aspen model
with the thermophysical properties is depicted in figure 24. The heating process begins with
preheating the inlet air of 15 ◦C in a heat exchanger by extracting a portion of the source
heat(exhaust air) until its saturation vapour point of 43.9 ◦C, as it has a specific humidity
of ≈50 g/kg of its total mass flow rate and to avoid contamination of inlet air. The pre-
heated air then undergoes sequential heating in a series of gas coolers: GC-VF, GC-SFB,
and GC-MA. Each gas cooler has a specific target temperature of 60 ◦C, 114 ◦C, and 240 ◦C,
respectively. The remaining source heat is directed into the heat pump system’s evaporator
to superheat the refrigerant before entering the IHX. After passing through the IHX, the
superheated refrigerant further increases its temperature by absorbing heat energy from the
refrigerant in a supercritical state, which is being discharged from the GC-SFB. Thereby
minimizing the compression work required by the compressor.

The cascade heat pump system with refrigerant couple R744 at HTC and R600a at LTC is
represented in figure 8 & 32. As hydrocarbon has lower operating pressure than R744 and

cascade system.
The cascade heat pump system uses an Intermediate heat exchanger (IX) component that
serves as a condenser for the bottom cycle and evaporator for the top cycle. The heat ab-
sorbed from the exhaust air by the LTC evaporator is upgraded and transferred to the HTC
through the Intermediate heat exchanger.
The heat sink air inlet is heated across the three gas coolers namely GC-VF, GC-SFB, and
GC-MA to its required temperature in the top cycle. The heat source is cooled in the evap-
orator of the bottom cycle.

Discussion
The initial assessment results for performance indicators, shows the system has higher
COPLorenz than the calculated minimum required value of 2.87 from the energy price ratio,
even for HP operating at lower temperatures. But, the actual system is highly influenced by
the factors such as the efficiency of the compressor, the minimum temperature difference in
the heat exchanger, type of refrigerant used and the type of heat pump system configuration,
therefore, the actual heat pump performance COPHP is expected to be comparatively lower.
Moreover, the study limits to analysing the performance of the HP in the initial assessment
for fixed ηLorenz of 60 %. It would be worth discussing the effect of system performance for
varying ηLorenz.
For LCOH calculation the assumed natural gas price is based on the year 2022 H2 [14].
Which is comparatively higher than the considered electricity price. That leads to higher
profitability for HP system when used as a substitute for NG boilers and results in lower PBP
of around 1 year. Further, the high NG price leads to a higher LCOH of 311 e/MWh, if the
same spray dryer is heated by natural gas boiler as the heat source. Which is comparatively
88 % higher than the calculated LCOH value (35.8 e/MWh) in Liang et al. [3]. So, it will
be interesting to discuss how the LCOH, NPV, and PBP vary for varying natural gas and
electricity prices instead of a fixed value.
From the results of exergy analysis, only the components with major exergy destruction con-
tributors are addressed for each model. In addition to this, performing an exergo-economic
analysis gives a broad perspective on the optimization of a particular component to increase
savings.
For cascade heat pump configuration models, it will be interesting to study the model for
different refrigerant couples eg: R718 and zeotropic refrigerant, R600a and R600. Thereby
analysing the perfect match in the temperature profile between the refrigerant and heat sink
& source. As exergy destruction is highly influenced by the non-ideal heat transfer between
the fluids.
In this study, the refrigerant mass flow rate is manipulated according to the main air stream
heat sink outlet temperature. This could also be a possible reason for mismatching heat
transfer profiles of other heat exchanger components. That corresponds to a high pinch
minimum temperature difference inside the heat exchanger component that has an adverse
effect on the system performance.

Conclusion
The initial assessment paved the way for obtaining general insights about heat pump inte-
gration as a substitute for the present conventional based heat source. The combination of
process modelling, performance and economic analysis provided insights into the trade-off
between profitability and efficiency that helps choose the best heat pump configuration and
operating temperature, based on which the conclusions were drawn :
• The total COPsystem of the fully electrified spray dryer system for operating tempera-
ture 200 ◦C and 240 ◦C falls between 1.64 to 2.53 and 1.97 to 3.25 respectively.
• The Levelized cost of heat ranges between 63 e/MWh to 88 e/MWh for all the heat
pump models with operating temperature 200 ◦C and 240 ◦C.
• From the performance and economic results heat pump system model-1 and model-3
operating at 240 ◦C, turned out to be the best possible configuration among the other
compared models. As it has the right balance between the performance and economic
indicators.
• Particularly, the COPsystem is 3.25 and 3.12 and LCOH of 63 e/MWh and 65 e/MWh
respectively. Although a significant difference is not observed in the NPV and PBP
for all the models.
