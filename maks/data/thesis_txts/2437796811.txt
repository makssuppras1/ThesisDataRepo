Characterization of CZTS
Abstract
Global energy demands are rising, due to increasing population and living standards. To accommo-
date this in a sustainable way, solar powered electrical energy from solar cells is a promising solution.
Today most solar cells are based on crystalline silicon devices, requiring much material for up-scaling.
Lately Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) has gained much interest, due to promising properties for use in solar cells,
namely optimal (direct) band gap and high absorption coefﬁcient. Furthermore, CZTS is composed of
Earth abundant and environmentally friendly elements, why it potentially could meet the terawatt scal-
ing challenge.
Despite having good properties, many issues are related to the use of CZTS for absorber layers. High
efﬁciency solar conversion is yet to be achieved, and many aspects need to be addressed, including fab-
rication processes, stability, and electrical properties. More knowledge is needed on the various techno-
logical areas, in order to further optimize fabrication and device performance.
The aim of the project is to study the properties of CZTS absorber layers grown by vacuum-deposited
techniques, such as pulsed laser deposition or co-sputtering. The fabricated CZTS absorber layers will be
characterized based on SEM, EDX, photoluminescense, Raman spectroscopy and XRD, giving insights to
the surface morphology, elemental composition, defects, phases and crystal structure.
Characterization has been done on thin-ﬁlm kesterite samples, made from three different types of
precursors; oxide based (CZTO), sulfur based (CZTS) and sulfur based containing silver (ACZTS).
Samples fabricated from both the CZTO and CZTS precursors showed improved properties when
thermally treated at elevated temperatures for longer durations. Ensuring an excess S and Sn is found to
be important, for maintaining good conditions for kesterite growth.
For the CZTO precursors, thermal treatment at low pressure was found advantageous, with improved
surface morphology, dense kesterite layers and less secondary phases.
Samples fabricated from ACZTS precursors, showed promising results from photoluminescence. Fur-
thermore, elemental composition of deposited samples, showed similar behaviour as has been observed
for CZTS precursors. This indicates the possibility for controlling cation ratios directly from laser depo-
sition. And thus, thin-ﬁlm absorber layers with partial Ag substitution could potentially become even
more efﬁcient and thereby overcome the current efﬁciency limitation, that CZTS kesterite solar cells face
today.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Increasing world population combined with the desire for higher living standards, especially in develop-
ing countries and Asia, consequently leads to higher global energy demands, see Appendix A. The primary
power consumption exceeded 17.6 TW by 2016 [1] and is expected to rise to approximately 30 TW by 2050
[2, 3].
Today 86% of the primary power is based on burning fossil fuels, i.e. oil, coal and natural gas [1]. The main
consequences of burning fossil fuels are depletion of the reserves and emission of green house gasses (GHG)
[4]. Namely CO2 represents a major problem as a GHG, since increased atmospheric concentrations yield
wider absorption bands around 12-13 µm wavelengths, thus increased radiative forcing [5, 6]. In short, ra-
diative forcing is the measure of how much energy is trapped at the Earth’s surface in the form of heat, as
heat radiation from the surface can be reﬂected back from clouds and GHG. The increase in radiative forcing
from GHG is the main reason for a majority of climate and environmental researchers to emphasize the ne-
cessity for reducing the world’s GHG emission, which is achievable by replacing fossil fuels with renewable
energy (RE) sources.
There are a number of different RE sources, each with different potential and origin. RE are energy sources
which for all intents and purposes are inexhaustible [7] in contrary to fossil fuels, that stem from decompo-
sition of biological matter under subterranean pressure over millions of years. Thus there is a ﬁnite amount
that require much time to rebuild [8].
The most prominent choices of RE sources are solar power (23000 TW), wind power (25-70 TW), hydro
power (3-4 TW), geothermal (0.3-2 TW) and biomass (2-6 TW) [9]. As stated, the potential of each RE source
varies much. Most of the sources are connected to the sun, as sun is heating the Earth, where temperature
ﬂuctuations creates winds, which then can generate waves. Solar radiation is also the driving force for build-
ing biological matter via photosynthesis, which allows the glucose producing Calvin cycle to occur [10, 11].
More RE is being installed every year. From 1997 till 2016, the cumulative installed photovoltaic (PV) power
around the world increased nearly 1300 times, closely resembling an exponential rise. The average yearly
growth rate from 2005-15 was 48.9% and 33.2% in 2016. In the same time period, the cumulative installed
wind power increased by a factor 60, with a growth rate of 21.6% and 12% from 2005-15 and 2016 respectively
[1]. Hydroelectricity is still being installed, but at a much lower rate, since much of the possible sites for
hydroelectric power generation is already being exploited [7].
By far, the greatest potential lies in solar power, with many orders of magnitude more incoming power than
we consume. Despite the immense incoming power and the noticeble increase in installed capacity, there
was only about 300 GW installed in 2016 on global scale [12], corresponding to less than 2% of the power
consumption. To this the capacity factor of solar panels should be taken into consideration, as the solar
panels only produce electricity when the sun is shining. In 2016 this resulted in just over 1% of the electricity
production covered by solar panels [1].
The solar cell market is currently dominated by crystalline silicon (Si) single junction cells, though there is a
general trend towards more polycrystalline technology, due to reasonable efﬁciencies and cost savings [13].
Second generation solar cells are made of thin-ﬁlms, thus considerably less material is used. Thin-ﬁlm solar
cells are usually referred to as second generation solar cells, where the later developed third generation cells,
are composed of multiple absorber layers, such that higher efﬁciencies are potentially obtainable [14].
Though Si solar cells have matured much, and achieved high efﬁciencies beyond 25% [15], it has the big
disadvantage having an indirect band gap [16]. Because of this, the absorption coefﬁcient is rather low, and
bulk thick absorber layers are needed in order to absorb the incoming light. The thin-ﬁlm solar cells on the
other hand, have a high absorption coefﬁcient, why only thin absorber layers and therefore less material is
needed.

MOTIVATION
The typical thin-ﬁlm technologies found today is based on Cadmium Telluride (CdTe), amorphous Si (a-Si),
copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS), gallium arsenide (GaAs) and organic photovoltaics (OPV) [17, 18,
19, 20]. CdTe has the biggest share on the market, but suffers of two main drawbacks - toxicity of Cd and
scarcity of both elements [21]. CIGS have shown high efﬁciencies in laboratories, but have not yet been
possible to scale and is also hindered by scarce elements. GaAs have shown impressive efﬁciencies of 28.8%
[22, 23] but costs are very high due to scarcity of Ga.
To this, Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) thin-ﬁlm absorber layers have received considerable attention. CZTS has many
favourable properties, for using as a PV-device. It has a direct band gap of 1.5 eV, which is ideal for achieving
high efﬁciencies in a solar cell. It has a high absorption coefﬁcient of > 104cm−1, thus only thin layers are
needed to absorb and convert incoming light [24, 25]. These two properties does not differ much from the
expensive GaAs or CIGS materials. The main advantage of CZTS is that all the constituent elements - copper,
zink, tin and sulphur - are earth abundant and non-toxic [21], why CZTS has the potential for being truly
scalable, cheap and with a minimum of regulatory limitations for use [26].
Many challenges are still connected to the use of CZTS for efﬁcient PV applications [25, 27, 28, 29], why more
research is needed. This project is aimed towards providing more insight to some of the fundamentals of
fabricated CZTS thin-ﬁlms, their composition and physical properties, in order to further improve the use
and applicability of this promising material for PV applications.
2
Problem
It is desired to fabricate more efﬁcient CZTS solar cells, based on thin-ﬁlm absorber layers fabricated by
pulsed laser deposition. In order to achieve this goal, the fabrication process has to be optimized. There is
thus a need for more insights and knowledge on the fabrication conditions and the effect hereof on CZTS
grain formation.
3
Project description
CZTS thin-ﬁlm absorber layers will be characterized using various methods, including SEM imaging, EDX
measurements and photoluminescence. This characterization will be carried out, in order to study the ef-
fect of fabrication processes to the absorber layer performance and composition. Additional measurements
provided by the work group, will be used to support the characterization and discussions hereof. From the
sample characterization, suggestions for improvement to the fabrication process will be proposed.
4
Motivation
One of the most eminent problems we are facing today, is our ability to meet the increasing energy demand
in a sustainable way. As restructuring our energy grid and supply chain, is both energy and time demanding,
it is better to engage the issue now rather than later. It is improbable that a single technology will be able to
solve the challenge by it self, why a broad palette of possibilities ought to be considered and looked into.
As an engineering student, I ﬁnd such a pressing challenge important to engage in, and contribute to the
necessary search of applicable and complementary solutions. Exploiting the sun’s rays to our own advan-
tage, I ﬁnd as one of the most promising solutions to our energy challenge. With this project on CZTS, the
challenge is tackled by an environmentally friendly and scalable solution, which hopefully will become reli-
able as well. But for this to become true, more knowledge is required on the material, why characterization
is necessary for improving and optimizing the quality.

6
Theory
In this section I will introduce and discuss the underlying physical background for the use of various mate-
rials in solar cells, and how conversion of sunlight into electrical energy is possible.
Solar cells are electrical devices, converting electromagnetic radiation from the sun into electricity, by the
photovoltaic effect. The term "photovoltaic" is an appropriate use of words, referring to the incoming light,
i.e. photons, from the sun and the output of electrical energy, described by the energy of electrons, i.e.
voltage.
To describe how solar cells work, it is necessary to look into the internal electronic properties of materials
using a quantum mechanical description, namely band theory.
6.1
Energy bands
Back in 1913 Niels Bohr suggested that electrons in atoms exist in well deﬁned energy states with quantized
values of angular momentum [30, 31]. Crystals are composed of many atoms ordered in a periodic lattice.
Thus there are many electrons in the crystal contributing with well deﬁned energy states.
In 1925 de Broglie suggested how electromagnetic radiation exhibit particle like properties, and likewise
particles could be treated as waves. This is often referred to as the particle-wave duality [32]. This inspired
Schrödinger to write his famous equation describing wave mechanics in 1926 [33].

6.2
Band gap and absorption
In real lattices, i.e. three dimensional lattices, we ﬁnd the same kind of behaviour, though the band diagram
becomes more complex. The complexity comes from the presence of multiple interacting electrons and the
more lattice parameters to take into account. With regards to materials for use as solar cell absorber layers,
it is semiconductor materials that can be used. This will become evident in the following.
The electronic states in the valence band is energetically separated from the states in the conduction band.
Thus, electrons in the valence band are limited in momentum k and energy E in which they can exist.
Though they can absorb energy provided from an external source, and thereby be excited from the valence
into the conduction band. For a valence electron to be excited into the conduction band, it has to absorb
an energy great enough to overcome the band gap barrier. As a ﬁrst approximation1, the excitation energy,
Ee, should be equal to or greater than the band gap energy, Ee ≥Eg.

6.3
Band bending
The electron-hole pair can thus be created by absorbing a photon, with a wavelength comparable to or
shorter than the band gap energy. As discussed, the excited electron will quickly relax to the conduction
band minimum. The e/h-pair can recombine again. This process is the reverse of the absorption process,
where the energy, separating the e/h-pair is released as a photon while the electron falls down to the valence
band again. Since the electrons in the conduction band, will be situated close the conduction band mini-
mum, the emitted photon from the radiative recombination will have the wavelength given by the band gap,
λemission = hc/Eg. This process is more efﬁcient for direct band gap materials, of the same reasons as dis-
cussed for the absorption process, and is the foundation of LED lighting. The e/h-pair can also recombine
through non-radiative processes, which is only regarded as loss, since this is non-useful.
Since the e/h-pair is energetically separated, it can be exploited as electrical energy. This is the core princi-
ple behind solar cells, using photons to energetically excite electrons, and extracting this as electricity. But
in order to do this, we need to efﬁciently extract the photogenerated charge carriers, and thereby avoid them
to recombine, neither radiative or non-radiative. I.e. electrons and holes need to be separated and guided
away from each other, and then be collected with contacts.
6.4
Doping
This can be done by so-called doping, which is depicted in Figure 6.4. Doping is intentional introduction
of foreign atoms to the crystal, with a different valence compared to the original material. I.e. not the
same number of electrons in the outer-most electron orbitals. By introducing these impurities, conduction
electrons and holes can effectively be added or subtracted from the lattice, thus changing the electronic
properties [7, 16, 37].
To explain the concept of doping, Si will be used as an example. Si is in group 14 in the periodic table with
valence 4. One Si atom will therefore have 4 unpaired electrons, so when crystallizing, it will covalently bond
to four other Si atoms. Pure, undoped Si is in a diamond structured tetragonal lattice. If one of the Si atoms
is replaced with an atom of valence 5, e.g. P or N, this will contribute with 5 electrons to the lattice. 4 of
these will be used for covalent bonds to four neighbouring Si atoms, leaving behind 1 free electron. Hereby,
an extra electron has been added to the lattice. The impurity atom, is called a donor impurity, as it donates
an electron to the material. It is denoted as n-type, as there now is a majority of negative charges. In the
opposite situation, an atom of valence 3 could be added, e.g. B. With only 3 valence atoms, there will be one
missing electron, when B substitutes a Si atom. Hereby a hole is added instead. This is called an acceptor
impurity, and is denoted as p-type, due to the majority of positive charges.
When n-doping with e.g. P, the impurity atom has a charge of +5. But with only 4 negatively charged elec-
trons bound, there will be a surplus of positive charge. The added electron will be loosely bound by a
Coulomb force, due to this charge imbalance. The Coulomb interaction corresponds to a small binding
energy, which can be estimated as [37]

6.5
Junctions
Having laid the foundation of doping, the problem of efﬁcient charge extraction can be tackled. Depending
on the materials used, one can either make pn- or pin-junctions. The p and n refers to the doping type, and
i refers to intrinsic and non-doped material.
6.5.1
pn-junction
A pn-junction is a p-type and an n-type semiconductor placed together as illustrated in Figure 6.4d. Since
the Fermi-level across the entire material is aligned, band bending of the valence and conduction band i
created.
This can also be described through considering the involved charges, as illustrated in Figure 6.5. When
the two semiconductors come in contact, charges will diffuse from one region to the other. This happens
because of the charge gradient at the interface. The p-type has an excess of free holes, where the n-type
has an excess of free electrons. In the p-type, holes and electrons are the majority and minority carriers
respectively, and vice versa for n-type. I.e. there will be a large concentration of electrons in the n-type
material, but only a very low concentration in the p-type. The opposite is true for holes. While charges
diffuse into the other region, a counteracting mechanism will arise. With the free carriers diffused away, the
charged atom cores in the crystal, will be unbalanced. This will build up an electric ﬁeld between the p- and
n-type region, pulling on the charges making them drift back towards their ﬁrst region. Thus there will be
an equilibrium between the diffusion and drift of charges. This equilibrium of charges will be in the vicinity
of the junction, and is called the depletion region. These concepts are schematically shown in Figure 6.5. It
should be noted that the depletion region only makes up for a very small part of the cell.

When a photon of energy Eph > Eg enters the diode, e/h-pairs can be created. If the e/h-pair is created
near the junction in the depletion region, the electric ﬁeld between the two regions will make the generated
holes and electrons drift to the p- and n-side respectively. If the e/h-pair is created in the p-type region, the
photogenerated hole can be neglected, due to the vast majority of holes already present. But if it happens
within a diffusion length from the depletion region, the electron can diffuse to the depletion region, where
the electric ﬁeld will make it drift across the junction. Likewise for generated holes in the n-type region [39].

6.6
Principles of solar cells and design
The previous sections were focussed on the fundamental theoretical background for how solar energy con-

This process is happening in the absorption layer. The implementation of the absorption layer in a solar cell
device, will now be regarded.
As previously mentioned, the majority of the solar cell market is based on Si absorber layers. The basic

in 1962 [46]. A few years later in 1965 it was used for solar cells, to power a Russian space mission [47]. Thus
GaAs has been widely investigated, and also holds the efﬁciency record for single junction solar cells, as

thickness of absorber layer [50], and potentially much higher efﬁciencies are possible [51]. Unfortunately
CIGS is facing the same scalability challenge as GaAs, with scarcity of both In, Ga and Se [21].
6.8
CZTS crystal and properties
The reoccurring problem with these alternatives to Si solar cells, is the scalability challenge, due to scarce
and toxic materials. An attempt to face this challenge, is done by atom substitution in CuInS2 (CIS). By
replacing half of the In atoms with Zn and the other half of In with Sn, the Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) kesterite
structure is obtained. With this (I2−II−IV−VI4) composition, all the constituent components are both earth
abundant and non-toxic. Furthermore, the band gap of CZTS is 1.5 eV while having a high absorption co-
efﬁcient, giving optimal conditions for a good solar cell. Depending on the exact position of each atom,
CZTS could form in a stannite structure as well, see Figure 6.8. Though there is a small difference in binding
energy of a few meV per atom, whether the kesterite or stannite structure is regarded, it is found that the
kesterite structure is the most stable structure [52].
Figure 6.8: Schematics showing the kesterite and stannite crystal structure of CZTS. As indicated on the
right, atomic representation is given as blue: Cu, yellow: S, orange: Zn and red: Sn. The CuZn-ZnCu
antisite defect is marked in the kesterite structure. Figure adapted from [27].
There are both favourable and unfavourable aspects of working with a quarternary crystal, i.e. crystal com-
posed of four different elements [53]. An advantage is, as seen with CIGS, the possibilities of intentional
(partial) substitution of atoms to adjust physical properties such as band gap and lattice constant. But with
more complex structures, it is more difﬁcult to ensure the crystal to behave as intended. This gives rise to
loss mechanisms, defect states and electron-hole recombinations, which reduces the open circuit voltage,
Voc of the ﬁnal solar cell, and thus the efﬁciency.
A major problem for CZTS is anti site defects, where atoms switch position in the crystal. With multiple
different elements, many such anti sites are possible, each having a speciﬁc energy state associated with

Some anti sites are difﬁcult to form and others are easily created, depending on the energetics in the crystal.
Using the Kröger-Vink notation [54], the most critical anti site defect for CZTS is CuZn [55], where a Cu
atom is located on a Zn site. The associated defect, ZnCu, is less problematic for the optical and electrical
performance. The CuZn defect is easily formed, due to a low formation energy, stemming from the near
identical atomic radii of Zn and Cu of 135 Å [56], causing only a small lattice strain energy for the defect. The
CuZn defect acts as an acceptor defect, whereas the ZnCu defect acts as a donor impurity. The impurity states
are only shallow, i.e. lying close to the conduction and valence band maximum and minimum respectively.
But with the low formation energy, the defect concentration is high and effectively smears out the band
edges causing so called band tails. All this leading to Voc deﬁcit and limiting the CZTS solar cell efﬁciency[57,
53, 58]. A means for circumventing the anti site defect is by fabricating the CZTS crystal off stoichiometry,
having less Cu and more Zn atoms, thereby minimizing the probability of forming CuZn defects [59]. Sn
related crystal defects are also a cause for Voc deﬁcit, as these defect states lie deep within the valence band
of CZTS [55]. They are not as easily formed, but will have a detrimental effect on the efﬁciency, if the material
is used for solar cells, due to increased Schockley-Read-Hall recombination Ito2015.
Intrinsically CZTS is slightly p-type, due to VCu and CuZn defects [60, 61], i.e. having electron-holes as the
majority carrier. As previously discussed, a pn-junction is needed for charge collection. The n-type CdS is
commonly used for CZTS. At the interface between CZTS and CdS, the CuZn defect tend to form, pinning
the Fermi-level in the middle of the band gap preventing efﬁcient band bending. This too causes a large Voc
deﬁcit [53, 55].

6.8.2
Cation substitution
Knowing that one of the major reasons for the CuZn defects is due to similar atomic radius, several groups
have worked with partial substitution of Cu with Ag, (AgxCu1-x)2ZnSnS4 (ACZTS). Both elements are in the
same group in the periodic table. Their electronic conﬁguration are comparable, both having one valence
electron. The important difference is, that Ag is in the ﬁfth period, whereas Cu is in the fourth, thus Ag is a
larger atom. The covalent radius is about 15% larger than Cu, increasing the formation energy of anti site
defects with Zn, AgZn and ZnAg. It has been calculated that the energy barrier is 2-3 times larger compared
to that of Zn, effectively lowering the anti site defect density by a factor of 10 [62]. With the lower probability
of anti site defect formation, near stoichiometry would be favoured [63]. Regarding the impact of Ag substi-
tution to the band gap, it has been shown that the band gap is slightly lowered with increasing content of Ag
[64].
The interest in ﬁnding other substitution alternatives has been investigated by multiple groups. Cation
substitution of Zn with Fe and Mn showed poorer properties regarding solar cell performance, but partial
anion substitution of S with Se improved the efﬁciency signiﬁcantly, compared to pure CZTS [65]. It has
been generally known for a long time, that partial anion substitution with Se, i.e. Cu2ZnSn(S,Se)4 abbre-
viated CZTSSe, shows better performance than pure CZTS [53, 57, 65, 66, 67, 68]. The efﬁciency record for
kesterite solar cells is 12.6% [66] measured on a CZTSSe cell, whereas pure CZTS has reached a cell efﬁciency
of 9.1% [69].
6.9
CZTS solar cell design
A schematics of a typical reported CZTS solar cell design is shown in Figure 6.10, with approximate layer
thickness indicated [70, 71]. The TCO is an abbreviation of transparent conductive oxide, needed for con-
ducting charge carriers while also allowing the incoming light to penetrate. In this proposed design, it is
made of Al doped ZnO, but In doping has also been reported [72].
In order for making a pn-junction, a thin layer of n-type CdS is deposited on top of the CZTS. The charges
can then be collected through the top metal contacts and the Mo back contact. The thickness of the CZTS
layer can be varied. But to have adequate light absorption, it is usually between some hundred nanometers
to micrometer scaled.
The main difference between the design of CZTS cells and Si cells is the absorber layer thickness. This is
also the entire motivation behind thin-ﬁlm solar cells, that the absorber layer thickness can be decreased
by two orders of magnitude.

Experimental setups and characterization methods
7.1
Scanning electron microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is an imaging technique able to reach high magniﬁcation, nanometer
resolution and good depth of focus. This is possible, since high energetic electrons are used for visualizing
the object being analysed [73].
A simple schematic of a SEM is shown in Figure 7.1a. The main principle is guiding electrons down through
a column to the substrate by using magnetic ﬁelds, and then look at the electron signal hereof.
The imaging starts with accelerating electrons from the Field emission Gun (FEG) by setting a potential
between the FEG cathode and an anode. This acceleration voltage is usually in the range from <1 kV to 30
kV. An aperture blocks some of the emitted electrons, to ensure more unidirectional electron trajector.
A series of condenser lenses and objectives are then used to manipulate the electron beam. Due to the
nature of electrons, the electron trajectory is affected by magnetic ﬁelds, described by the Lorentz force
[74]. This controls focusing and beam diameter.
Finally, the beam is focussed on the substrate in the specimen chamber. The imaging is done, by scanning
the electron beam across the surface, thus a deﬂection coil is used for controlling the focus point position.
The beam is rastered across the specimen, making line scans from left to right and top to bottom. When
the electron beam reach the specimen, a variety of signals are created, due to the interaction between beam
electrons and specimen. Two of these signals are backscattered electrons and secondary electrons.
The incoming electrons can be deﬂected by elastic scattering, from the electric ﬁeld of the nucleus of the
specimen atoms. The deﬂection results in the electron being guided backwards as a backscattered electron,
without a signiﬁcant change in energy.
Inelastic events can also occur, where the incoming electron interacts with specimen electrons, through the
electric ﬁeld of the specimen atom. A transfer of energy to the specimen electron, results in expulsion from
the atomic electric ﬁeld, creating a secondary electron.
The electron signals from the surface are detected by a number of electron detectors, used to convert the
signals into an image.
There are many variables when working with a SEM. These need to be adjusted according to the speciﬁc
characterization intended with the SEM. The most signiﬁcant features are the probe current, acceleration
voltage, pressure and working distance together with alignment and astigmatism.

High probe current sends more electrons to the specimen surface, thus also increasing the signal to be
measured. But because of the increased number of electrons, the specimen can potentially be damaged
by this. It is necessary that the specimen is conducting, since the incoming electrons have to be grounded
away from the surface. Otherwise electrons can pile up, and distort the imaging. Increasing the probe, can
therefore also lead to more charge build-up, unless adequate conduction is ensured.
The acceleration voltage affects the SEM image in various ways. Increased voltage decreases lens aberration,
why higher resolutions are obtainable. The probe current and electron penetration depth increase as well.
The latter due to the higher energies involved.
To ensure long mean free paths of the electrons, it is necessary to maintain a vacuum in the column. The
pressure in the specimen chamber, is not necessarily as low. In the case of poorly conducting specimens,
an intentionally increased pressure can be used for discharging the specimen through the air molecules.
The working distance (WD) is the distance between the bottom of the column to the specimen. Changing
the WD affects the focus convergence angle, thus affecting the depth of focus. Long WD increase the depth
of focus, but higher magniﬁcation can be obtained with short WD.
The ﬁnal step for making high resolution SEM images, is to adjust the column alignment and astigmatism.
It is necessary to have the column aligned, so that objectives are centered with respect to the optical axis. If
not, the image will move when the beam is focussed. Astigmatism arise due to imperfect lens symmetry. In
order to obtain a symmetric and well deﬁned spot size, small manual corrections has to be made.

7.3
Photoluminescence
Photoluminescence (PL) is a characterization technique for obtaining information on material band gap,
defects and band tailing. The principle is based on radiative recombination of photoexcited electron-hole
pairs (e/h-pairs). Examining the wavelength of the radiative recombination processes, the energetic sepa-
ration of the e/h-pair can be determined.
A schematic of the involved processes is shown in Figure 7.2. An internal laser is pointed to the semicon-
ductor sample. As discussed in the theoretical section on absorption, Section 6.2, an electron can be excited
into the valence band, hereby creating an e/h-pair. The excitation only occurs, if the excitation laser is of
higher energy than the band gap. In Figure 7.2 the standard green 532 nm laser is illustrated. Other wave-
lengths can also be used. To analyse a high band gap material for instance, a short wavelength laser would
be required.
After some time, usually in the order of some milliseconds [76], the e/h-pair will recombine and emit a
photon with a wavelength corresponding to their energy separation. This phenomenon is called photolu-
minescence.
In a pure semiconductor with no defects, the e/h-pair will be situated at the band edges. The wavelength
of the PL signal would therefore be corresponding to the band gap. There will be defects present in non-
idealistic materials. These defects can cause defect states, with energies lying within the band gap of the
semiconductor. These interband defects can be a part of the PL emission process, causing smaller energetic
separation between the e/h-pair. An emitted photon from such a defect state, would therefore have a longer
wavelength, than a band gap separated e/h-pair.
As shown in Figure 7.2, the defect states can result in a variety of different recombination routes, thus also
a spectrum of emitted photons. As indicated, both radiative and nonradiative recombination can occur. PL

Fabrication methods
The characterized samples in this project, have been prepared and fabricated at DTU Risø via Pulsed Laser
Deposition (PLD) by Mungunshagai Gansukh.
8.1
Pulsed laser deposition
The pulsed laser deposition setup is sketched in Figure 8.1.
Referring to the ﬁgure, a laser is guided via mirrors and focused onto a target. The target and substrate is in
a vacuum chamber. The laser used, is an ultraviolet 248 nm KrF excimer laser. From this, short 20 ns pulses
are used.
Due to the high power density at the target surface, the target material is ablated and a plasma plume is
created [77]. This plasma plume is directed towards the substrate, where the elements condense and are de-
posited one thin layer at a time. The last guiding mirror has two actuator motors attached to the hinges, en-
abling controlled position corrections of the laser focal point at the target. Having the focus point changed
at the target serves two purposes. First, the plume position can be controlled, such that the entire substrate
can be covered by the deposition. Second, by having slight variations in the ablation position on the target,
the target surface will be less worn over time. This is combined with a rotating motor connected to the tar-
get holder. During deposition, the target is rotated, thus the ablation is done over most of the target surface,
and not only on the small area deﬁned by the mirror guidance.
With the laser repeatedly being focused through the chamber window, the transparency of the window de-
cays over time. To minimize the changes in window transparency, an Intelligent Window is used. Here the
window plate is rotating, such that the laser light does not continuously burn the window at the same spot.
By decreasing transmittance ﬂuctuations, the laser ﬂuence will also vary less. The laser ﬂuence is the energy
density on the target surface, and can easily be found as φ =

Cohesive energy is a measure, of the energy difference for an element being arranged in a crystallite state,
compared to having it in gas state [79]. As nature always seeks to relax to the lowest energy, Ecoh acts as an
energetic barrier, when doing PLD. Thus elements with a high Ecoh will require a higher ﬂuence to ablate,
compared to elements with a low Ecoh.
8.2
Sputtering
CZTS samples have also been fabricated via sputtering deposition by Filipe Martinho.
In sputtering, ions from an inert gas is bombarded onto the target material. Due to momentum transfer be-
tween the incident ions and the target atoms, atomically sized target particles are ejected from the surface.
The ejected particles are then sputtered to the substrate, thus making the physical vapour deposition [80].
Recent results from the work group has shown promising results, with uniform deposition on large wafers.
This opens up the possibility of doing detailed analysis study of various fabrication conditions on samples
from the same deposition.
8.3
Annealing
After deposition, the elements will be mixed in an amorphous phase. To form CZTS kesterite structures, the
as-deposited samples are annealed.
The annealing, or thermal treatment, is done over two steps. First an anneal at 200°C is done, followed by
an anneal at elevated temperatures. The two steps will be referred to as 1AN and 2AN respectively. The
reason for this two step process is somewhat empirically found to be advantageous. A possible temperature
proﬁle for the annealing process is shown in Figure 8.2. The two plateaus with constant temperature, are the
1AN and 2AN annealing steps. The temperature ramping between each step was 2°C/min for the samples
characterized in this project.
The annealing process is done in inert N2 atmosphere, where the N2 pressure can be controlled. Many
variations for the annealing process is possible, both varying the annealing temperatures and time duration
for each step.
The thermal treatment is necessary for the formation of kesterites from the amorphous phase. It has though
been reported, that kesterites start to decompose at temperatures beyond 400°C [81, 82]. The samples are
annealed in a closed graphite box. To shift the reaction mechanisms involved in the CZTS formation, S
and Sn powder is therefore added in the graphite box to ensure improved kesterite formation through more
phase stable conditions.

9.3
Annealing optimization
After fabrication of a set of samples, PL measurements have been used to investigate the radiative recombi-
nation processes, which indicates how suitable a given sample is for solar cell usage.
The measure used for determining the activity of the absorber layer, and thereby the potential usage of this
in a solar cell, is the integrated signal (IPL). This is the integral of the PL spectrum, i.e. the area under the
graph. The higher this value, the more radiative recombination has taken place. The width of the peak
should be taken into consideration as well, since a broad peak, would suggest more defect states, and a
potential loss of Voc. Finally the peak wavelength, λp, is an important value to notice, since this will indicate
the main energy state for which photogenerated e/h-pairs will relax to.
9.4
Compositional analysis and grain growth
To support the PL measurements done on the samples, additional SEM images have been taken, to get
knowledge of the grain size and morphology.
One condition for obtaining high efﬁciencies with crystalline solar cells, is having large grains. Larger grains
means fewer grain boundaries and less recombination centers.
Furthermore the elemental composition and cation ratios have proven to be detrimental for kesterite solar
cells, as discussed in Section 6.8. To this, EDX measurements have been used for determining presence of
secondary phases and kesterite composition.
Additionally provided XRD and Raman measurements, taken by the group, have also been used as a means
of quantiﬁcation of crystallinity and determining presence of other phases than the CZTS kesterite.

This behaviour of the elemental composition at increasing temperatures, can be explained by considering
the added energy. During the annealing process, oxides can recrystallise in the precursor, due to adequate
heat to overcome formation barriers. What is meant with recrystallisation is, that the elements were mixed
as oxides in the target composition, before being ablated and deposited through the plasma plume during
PLD. When deposited, it is found in an amorphous mixture. Out of this amorphous phase, oxides can then
recrystallise, as heat is provided during the annealing.
During the anneal, S and Sn is added into the graphite annealing box, in which the sample is placed. As
the temperature increase, this powder evaporates, thus providing S and Sn to the annealing atmosphere.
While heat treated, oxygen in the precursor is exchanged with sulfur, while CZTS is formed from the present
elements, as was described in Section 9.1.

10.4
Effect of annealing pressure
It is important to address the bubble formation during the annealing process, which was observed for the
long time 575°C annealed samples. A likely explanation for the bubble formation, is from the possible route
of formation of kesterite CZTS from CZTO precursors, given in Section 9.1. Here it was seen, that a lot of
gasses are released during the sulfurization process. It is conceivable to imagine some of these gasses being
trapped underneath a crystallite layer. This could explain the observed bubble formation.
To facilitate gas release, the annealing pressure could be lowered below the normal 1 bar condition. The
reasoning for this, is that a pressure gradient would drive the gas formation outside of crystal formation,

a low vapour pressure. It would therefore be expected to have an impact on especially S, as this is a very

10.7
Summary on optimisation of CZTO annealing process
Many results have been presented for the CZTO samples. For clariﬁcation, the main results will be listed
here.
A two step annealing process has been analysed by various measurement techniques. From PL mapping,
high readings were obtained when the ﬁrst step was done for 30 minutes. For the second step, it was found
that elevated temperatures resulted in better grain growth and stronger PL signal. This is in agreement
with literature [28], where elevated temperatures are used for supplying sufﬁcient energy for the solid state
chemical reactions and grain growth mechanisms. To avoid trapping of gas, and thus a rough surface, the
pressure was furthermore decreased below ambient pressure, yielding more uniform, clean and continuous
crystal layers. Finally, the added sulfur content during the anneal was increased. This yielded less PL signal,
but higher crystallinity and fewer secondary phases were observed.
PL measurements of CZTO samples showed a peak wavelength corresponding to about 1.4 eV. Thus band
tailing from interband defects cause roughly 100 mV loss, compared to pure kesterite CZTS.

Characterization of CZTS samples
In the previous section, fabrication of CZTS thin ﬁlm layers were made via an oxide containing CZTO pre-
cursor. This gave rise to some intrinsic problems, such as how to ensure good sulfurisation and overcoming
formation energy barriers for present oxides.
CZTS kesterites can also be fabricated directly from a Cu-Zn-Sn-S containing target in the PLD. The Fabri-
cation and annealing process steps are similar to the steps discussed for the CZTO fabrication. First a PLD
deposition of the sulfurised target material, followed by a two step annealing process in S and Sn containing
atmosphere. There are thus also many possible annealing variations for the CZTS precursors.
11.1
Time variations in ﬁrst annealing step
To start with, the ﬁrst annealing process needs to be optimised. A batch of samples, CZTS AE1, were an-
nealed at 200°C for varying times, followed by an anneal step at 550°C for 30 min, just like the CZTO 12,
discussed previously.
PL mappings of these samples are shown in Figure 11.1. These measurements show, that only very thin lay-
ers were deposited on the substrates. PL spectra in Figure 11.1a, shows the highest PL signals were measured
for the 30 min annealed sample. But due to a thin layer deposition, interferences across the sample was ob-
served. 11.1b shows three different obtained spectra for this sample. They all show comparable IPL, but the
peak position changes about 80 nm. The spectrum named "30 min *", even has two clearly distinct peaks,
where one of them matches with the "30 min **" spectrum. This interference phenomena becomes more
evident, looking at the measured peak wavelength map in 11.1c. From the lower right corner towards the
center, the peak wavelength changes periodically between 980 nm and 830 nm. This interference was also
visibly observable, by looking at the samples in normal daylight, giving rise to similar interference patterns.

Looking at the PL spectra from CZTS precursor based samples, and comparing these to the CZTO precursor
based samples, a pressing question arise: Why is the peak wavelength of the CZTS samples longer than the
CZTO samples?
The PL spectra stems from radiative recombinations, thus showing energetic differences between electron
and exciton states. Longer wavelengths correspond to smaller energies. The peak shift thus indicates pres-
ence of deeper lying interband defects in the CZTS precursor made samples. These defects could likely be
Sn related anti-site defects, which is known to lie deep within the band gap of CZTS [55].
Though PL measurement show decent signal, additional XRD, Raman spectroscopy and SEM images show
a potential problem for this fabrication process for solar cell production. The surfaces are covered in SnS2
platelets, which will cause Voc deﬁcit and form a diode and barrier for carrier collection [93]. Raman and
XRD spectra are presented in Figure 11.2. The surface sensitive Raman spectroscopic measurements, show

The formation of SnS2 secondary phases on the surface of CZTS thin ﬁlms, has been known for several

on the CZTS substrates, facilitating the condensation.

11.3
Summary on annealing of CZTS precursors
From the material presented on annealing of CZTS precursors, similar trends as for CZTO precursors has
been seen.
With regards to the ﬁrst annealing step, PL measurements suggest an improvement by annealing for more
than 20 min. Much presence of secondary phases distorted the measurements. But for consistency and
comparison with CZTO precursors, a 30 min annealing step was chosen.
For the second annealing step, longer annealing conditions were found to be advantageous, as the presence
of secondary phases was seen to decrease. This was observed for two different annealing temperatures.
New measurements by the work group, has shown further improvement in kesterite growth, by increas-
ing the temperature to 600°C and doing the anneal for longer duration. Large grain formation with fewer
secondary phases was obtained, suggesting better conditions for the CZTS phase stability.

Partial cation substitution of Cu with Ag
An interesting topic to investigate, is cationic substitution as discussed in Section 6.8.2. The motivation
for such a substitution was to reduce CuZn antisite defects, by exploiting the larger covalent radius of Ag
compared to Cu.
With Ag substitution the elemental kesterite composition becomes (AgxCu1-x)2ZnSnS4. For clariﬁcation this
is abbreviated ACZTS.
By changing the target material from stoichiometric CZTS, to a 10% Ag substitution of Cu, ACZTS samples
were fabricated by PLD.

CONCLUSION
Experience from CZTS and CZTO sample fabrication would suggest that long high temperature annealing
could be a viable means, but the growth mechanisms, could be affected by the addition of Ag atoms. It
would therefore be necessary to characterize many samples, to gain more insight of the crystal growth and
resulting properties.
It does, however, look promising and worthwhile to investigate further.
13
Conclusion
This project has been focused on characterization of different Cu2ZnSnS4 (CZTS) kesterite thin-ﬁlm sam-
ples. The thin-ﬁlms were fabricated via Pulsed Laser Deposition(PLD), and subsequently thermally treated
in a two step annealing process. Characterization was based on SEM, EDX, PL, XRD and Raman spec-
troscopy.
Three different types of samples have been investigated. Each type is based on different precursor compo-
sitions; an oxide based (CZTO), sulfur based (CZTS) and a sulfur based precursor containing silver (ACZTS).
The chemical route of formation, for making kesterite CZTS is different, depending on the starting precur-
sor material. The fabrication process for each sample type, has therefore been characterized, in order to
optimize this accordingly.
The project has been focused on optimizing the thermal treatment of the samples, and investigate how
varying annealing conditions affect the kesterite growth.
For the CZTO based samples, it was found to be adequate to anneal the samples 30 min for the ﬁrst step.
Characterization was then done, on samples with varying annealing time duration and temperature. Here it
was found to be an advantage to anneal the samples for longer times, 50 min, and at elevated temperatures,
600°C. Hereby less secondary phases and better kesterite crystallinity was obtained. Presence of secondary
phases were also seen to decrease, when more S was added during the annealing.
It was observed, that annealing at high temperatures caused bubble formations in the kesterite layer. By de-
creasing the pressure during the annealing process to 250 mbar, improved morphology with denser kesterite
layer was obtained. By combining these conditions, and anneal the samples at high temperatures while
maintaining a low pressure, yielded smooth and dense samples, with large grain size and high crystallinity.
Characterization of CZTS based precursors, showed similar behaviour. Longer annealing times decreased
the amount of secondary phases while increasing the amount of kesterite. Many secondary phases were
though seen on CZTS based samples, which could be caused by condensation from the Sn rich atmosphere.
Additional measurements was mentioned, where high temperature annealing resulted in larger grain for-
mation and fewer secondary defects.
Motivated by possible decrease in formation of anti-site defect states, partial silver substitution was inves-
tigated. Silver containing ACZTS precursors were annealed and characterized in the same manner as the
CZTO and CZTS samples.
Promising results were found, regarding the PL spectrum of an ACZTS sample, where the highest PL sig-
nals of all samples was measured. Elemental composition and resulting cationic ratios showed a constant
Ag/Cu ratio while also showing similar behaviour, as found for previously characterized CZTS samples. This
indicates, that the elemental composition of deposited precursors is controllable by laser ablation.
On the ACZTS samples, large amount of secondary phases was found, suggesting Sn rich atmosphere during
annealing. This could attribute to stable formation conditions of kesterites, but is also an issue which needs
to be addressed.

Outlook and future work
Though many interesting ﬁndings were made, there is still much to continue working on. More empirical
knowledge on pressure and temperature dependence on the formation mechanisms, could be a way to
improve fabricated samples even more. Especially more knowledge is required on the CZTS samples. More
variations on the annealing process could also be looked into, as for instance the temperature proﬁle and
temperature ramping rate. A way to decrease formation of secondary phases on the CZTS and ACZTS, could
maybe be achieved, if the annealing chamber was vented during cool down.
ACZTS could potentially be a promising thin-ﬁlm absorber. But as for any new material, it takes time to
optimize and improve the fabrication and growth mechanisms. This should therefore be looked more into.
During the data analysis, some interesting features with EDX were observed. It was found, that non-uniform
elemental compositions across a sample occurred, yielding large grains with Zn rich and Cu rich regions
respectively. Such an image is shown in Appendix Figure I.1, where it was found on the 30 min annealed
CZTO 13 sample. Surface defects like these, could also be looked into, and attempt to ascribe a reason for
such formations.
Another thing, which could be considered, is the morphology of the targets. Large surface structure vari-
ations were seen on the ACZTS target, shown in Appendix Figure I.2 and Figure I.3, where Cu, Zn and Sn
rich regions are seen scattered around. The surface morphology stem from the ablation process. After every
PLD cycle, the morphology most likely change, which is conceivable to affect the plasma plume dynamics
and thus the deposition.

Optimizing EDX measurement procedure
After characterizing multiple thin ﬁlm samples, it was found to be connected with difﬁculties to determine
the elemental composition. Using the EDX, the irradiated X-rays from the samples, are correlated to known
characteristic lines from elements, why the irradiated X-rays can be used to determine the elemental com-
position. The energy of the irradiated X-rays cannot exceed the energy of the incoming electrons from the
SEM column, scanned across the surface. Thus increasing the acceleration voltage opens up the possibility
for seeing more ﬁngerprints from the various elements. But at the same time this comes with a cost. The
penetration depth of the electrons into the sample, follows the voltage which drives them. Thus by increas-
ing the acceleration voltage on the electrons, the characterization is less surface dependent, but looks into
the deeper lying layers in the sample.
This is not a problem, having thick depositions of the material in question on the substrate. But when
dealing with thin ﬁlms of a few hundred nanometers, the penetration depth of electrons becomes very
relevant. Crude calculations would estimate the electron penetration depth, from an acceleration voltage
of 15 kV, to be about 1 µm in CZTS.
It therefore seems beneﬁcial to decrease the acceleration voltage, thus only characterizing the very top of
the sample, where the deposited material is located. It should be chosen with caution though, as having
too low acceleration voltage decreases the count rate, since fewer X-rays are irradiated yielding more noise
and fewer characteristic lines can be excited. Too high voltage effectively yields characterization below the
material in question, and thereby analysing the composition of the substrate, rather than the deposited
material.
Especially the overlap of S and Mo characteristic lines close to 2.3 keV, caused many difﬁculties. The samples
are composed of Mo coated soda lime glass (SLG), upon which the thin layer of CZTS is deposited. Thus
the X-ray signal measured with EDX showed traces of both Mo and S, especially with high acceleration
