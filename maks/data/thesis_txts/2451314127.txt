Abstract 
 
ii 
 
Abstract 
 
Application of integrated management of water resources at the river basin level and the 
understanding and quantification of the hydrological processes at different time scales, is 
essential to ensure sustainable development. Hydrological modeling proves to be a useful tool 
for understanding these processes and to provide the water-policy makers and authorities with 
significant information on the catchment’s hydrology. Moreover, the rapid development and 
availability of remote sensing (RS) data in recent years, has shown to be a valuable source for 
hydrological models, since they can replace traditional in-situ model input data especially in 
the case of poorly gauged or ungauged basins. This study explores the use of RS data and 
focuses on their application into a lumped conceptual hydrological model, by examining the 
Guadalquivir River basin in southern Spain. The Guadalquivir river is the fourth longest river 
in Spain and the longest in southern Spain, where the irrigation is the dominant water use, with 
more than 70% of water consumption on average.  
A Budyko type rainfall-runoff model was forced using gridded precipitation and temperature 
data and two calibration approaches were assessed: optimization using only in-situ discharge 
observations and optimization using both discharge and remotely sensed evapotranspiration 
(ET) data. In both cases, the flow duration curve and the climatology benchmark were used for 
the optimization of the model parameters. Four calibration zones were defined, based on land 
use characteristics and topography. However, due to the high regulation of the river (many 
dams), two out of the four zones were chosen (Zone 3 and Zone 4), by including discharge 
stations under natural flow regime. 
Based on the first calibration approach, the model performed better in Zone 4, although the 
model underestimated the discharge in the two zones, including both the calibration and 
validation stations. In the second calibration approach (including also the ET in the calibration), 
the model provided equally consistent results of ET for all the stations of the two zones, but the 
seasonality of discharge was captured better in Zone 4, as it indicated from the climatology 
benchmark. The seasonal patterns of the discharge and evapotranspiration were further 
analyzed, with the ET showing maximum values during spring, while the discharge presented 
more fluctuations due to the high precipitation variability in the catchment. 
In conclusion, calibrating a model for multiple variables (e.g. discharge and ET together), can 
provide useful information about its limitations. Moreover, in the Guadalquivir river basin with 
the high irrigated agriculture, the reservoirs need to be included in the model in order to adapt 
it to the entire basin.

1. Introduction 
 
According to the United Nations report regarding the water resources development, the Earth 
is facing a serious water crisis at the beginning of the 21st century, which is getting worse 
unless remedial action is taken. In order to ensure sustainable development, is to make certain 
that adequate supplies of water of good quality are maintained for the entire population of this 
planet, while preserving the hydrological, biological and chemical functions of ecosystems 
(WWAP, 2015). Improved integrated management of water resources at the river basin level 
and moreover the understanding of local rainfall processes at different time scales and their 
impacts on the basin’s water balance, is one of the tasks that can help to achieve this sustainable 
development. The application of hydrological models can play an important role and provide 
the water-policy makers with significant information, since they can help to make predictions 
(forecasts) and to understand the past (Devia et al. 2015). 
During the last two decades, a wide range of hydrologic applications is benefited by the 
continuous rapid development in remote sensing (RS) techniques. As a result, the monitoring 
of the water and energy fluxes at spatial and temporal scales is becoming more accessible and 
new alternatives for river catchment modeling are proposed (Koppa and Gebremichael 2017). 
Hydrological variables such as precipitation (P), evapotranspiration (ET) and soil moisture can 
be continuously assessed. At the same time, remotely sensed observations are used for forcing 
or calibration into hydrological models (Tang et al. 2009). Especially in the case of ungauged 
basins, RS data can replace traditional model input data including P, temperature (T) and 
reference evapotranspiration (Michailovsky et al. 2012). Moreover, RS data can contribute to 
monitoring changes on the vegetation cover (Xie et al. 2008). For instance, satellite data (2000-
2017) from the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA), showed increasing 
leaf area of vegetation due to direct factors (human land-use management) and indirect factors 
(such as climate change and CO2 fertilization), revealing that the Earth is greener than it was 
20 years ago (Chen et al. 2019). However, the effects of this greening on the hydrological cycle, 
are largely unknown (Zeng et al. 2018). 
Furthermore, due to the climate change many regions of the world can expect a combination of 
increasing temperatures and decreasing precipitation patterns. For example, the Mediterranean 
Basin is considered as a “sensitive region” and is predicted to affected by severe droughts by 
the end of the 21st century. At the same time, an increase in the irrigation water requirements 
is observed in this area, such as in Andalucía (southern Spain) where they are expected to 
increase between 15% and 20% by 2050 (Chavez-Jimenez et al. 2015). The growing water 
demands together with larger climate uncertainty, requires ad-hoc tools to monitor and forecast

the hydrological and ecological systems. Using RS data to monitor the variations in the 
hydrological cycle components is of high importance in this region, which according to Moyano 
et al. (2018) is consistently projected as a “hotspot” of drying trends and prolonged water 
scarcity conditions. 
In this context, this study explores the use of RS data and focuses on their application into a 
lumped conceptual hydrological model in the Guadalquivir River basin in Andalucía (southern 
Spain). Gridded P and T (maximum and minimum) data are used to inform the model, which 
is calibrated against ground observations of streamflow and RS derived ET data. The model 
returns as outputs simulated river discharges and actual-potential evapotranspiration. Such 
model outputs, can contribute to the future water resource management in the area and to a 
better understanding of relations between land use/land cover change and greening (Zhang et 
al. 2015). 
In the sections below, information regarding the state of the art using RS data in hydrological 
models as well as the application of hydrological modeling in Guadalquivir river basin (study 
area), is presented. This is followed by the research objectives and the structure of the report. 
 
1.1  Literature Review 
 
Satellites play an important role in measurements of various dimensions of water cycle 
components (rainfall, soil moisture, ET, surface runoff etc.) in spatial and temporal domains. 
Major components of the land-surface water balance can be estimated from satellite RS and 
during the last decades, earth observations have been used to monitor small as well as large 
scale water bodies. The major focus of RS research in hydrology, is to develop integrated 
satellite system and techniques in order to retrieve different hydro-meteorological components 
and assimilate these data in various process based hydrological models for different 
applications (Tang et al. 2009).  
RS P, T and ET products have been used widely in hydrological modeling and considerable 
literature exists today that describes their application (Tang et al. 2009). Many studies state the 
use of RS products for forcing hydrologic models (i.e. use of RS products as model inputs). In 
every case, in situ observations for algorithm development and validation, as well as the 
uncertainty of the input data, are the main issues and challenges that arise in the context of 
using RS data for forcing hydrologic models. For example, Hansen et al. (2015), developed a 
probabilistic river discharge forecasting system for a poorly gauged river basin. The forecasting 
system was tested for the Okavango river basin in Africa, where different P and T products 
were used and compared to each other. Recently, Kittel et al. (2018) developed a lumped

conceptual model framework coupled to a Muskingum-routing scheme, by applying it in a 
poorly gauged area in Africa ( Ogooué river basin). The model was forced with multi mission 
RS data and different RS P products were compared to each other and were used as forcing 
inputs to the model. The model was calibrated by using both historical in-situ discharge 
observations but also total water storage observations from the Gravity Recovery and Climate 
Experiment (GRACE mission). Moreover, the study explored how new products from the 
European Space Agency (ESA) such as the environmental research satellite CryoSAT-2 and 
Sentinel-1(from the Copernicus program satellite constellation), can be used to derive detailed 
river masks and to extract relevant observations over inland water bodies. This watershed 
research showed, how RS data can be used to obtain useful information in poorly gauged areas 
but also how the hydrological models can be supplemented with the necessary input data and 
useful observations for parameter estimation. 
Furthermore, Thiemig et al. (2013), demonstrated the usefulness of satellite-derived rainfall 
estimates as forcing data for hydrologic applications. Two poorly gauged river basins in Africa 
were tested and four different RS precipitation products were used: CMORPH: Climate 
Prediction Center morphing method, RFE 2.0: NOAA's African Rainfall Estimates version 2, 
TRMM3B42: Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission and PERSIAN: Precipitation Estimation 
from Remotely Sensed Information using Artificial Neural Networks. The results showed 
remarkable differences between the products and the authors concluded that the quality of the 
satellite-derived rainfall estimates, depends on the study area and that before their 
implementation into a hydrological model, the user needs to assess the intrinsic data quality of 
the selected product. 
On the other hand, many studies have been conducted in which RS derived data were used to 
calibrate model parameters and validate model performance (Tang et al. 2009). As the P and 
ET are the largest components  of the water balance in most hydrological systems, many studies 
have focused in these two and in their crucial role in the hydrological cycle (Tobin and Bennett 
2017). Although discharge measurements are used mainly to calibrate-validate the hydrological 
models typically from a single gauge at the basin’s outlet (Michailovsky et al. 2013), many  
watersheds studies have demonstrated the use of RS based-observations of other hydrological 
variables such as GRACE observations (Kittel et al. 2018). Specifically, Tobin and Bennett 
(2017), used Global Land Evapotranspiration: the Amsterdam Model (GLEAM) data to 
constrain the SWAT model parameters that associate with the ET. They demonstrated an 
alternative approach to examine intra-watersheds processes and that the incorporation of 
multiple observations into calibration, will facilitate the understanding of the different 
processes that operate within a watershed.

Jung and Kim (2018), studied how potential CO2 changes can impact the hydrologic 
components in the Seolma-cheon watershed in South Korea. Terra Moderate Resolution 
Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) and Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) data, were used 
together with the Soil and Water Assessment Tool (SWAT) and the Budyko (BD) curve, to 
assess the water cycle changes. The results indicated, that by using CO2 concentrations as an 
additional parameter in the SWAT model, provides a better understanding of the role of ET and 
soil moisture in ongoing and future climate changes. In another study, the SWAT model and 
MODIS imagery were used to estimate monthly flows in a data-scarce Chilean Andean basin. 
The results showed satisfactory to good model performance in terms of representation of long-
term or annual mean discharge at the basin outlet (STEHR et al. 2009).  
Furthermore, the efficacy of the SWAT model to simulate daily and monthly ET for major 
dryland crops in the semiarid Texas High Plains using lysimetric data was analyzed, showing 
that the model underestimates ET at both daily and monthly levels (Marek et al. 2015). Lastly, 
a multi-site calibration and validation of the SWAT model with satellite-based ET, was 
performed in a data sparse catchment in southwestern Nigeria (Odusanya et al. 2019). In this 
study, three different structures of the SWAT model were used, each one by using the three 
available (in SWAT) PET equations: Hargreaves,  Priestley-Taylor and Penman-Monteith. 
Subsequently, two widely used ET products (GLEAM and MODIS) were used to calibrate the 
SWAT simulated ET output for the three model structures. The SWAT model structure based 
on the Hargreaves PET equation performed better and the results demonstrated the suitability 
of using freely available satellite-based ET datasets for calibration/validation, reducing as a 
result the uncertainty associated with the established calibration on a small number of observed 
streamflow datasets.  
 
1.2 River management in the Mediterranean region 
and in the Guadalquivir catchment 
 
According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), the Mediterranean Basin 
is considered as a “sensitive region”, as the consequences of global warming are already more 
evident than in the rest of Europe. For example, by the end of the 21st century, P may undergo 
a decay up to 30%, while the T will continue to increase faster, compared to the European 
average. Moreover, river stream flows are expected to decrease dramatically, triggering 
significant changes in the water resources available for agricultural, industrial and urban water 
supply purposes (IPCC 2007). At the same time, land use shifts represent a major driver of 
change, which will significantly increase water withdrawals (Lambin et al. 2001). Thus, the 
detection of these trends at the river basin scale is very important for the water managers, 
==PAGE:17==
Introduction 
 
5 
 
especially in semi-arid basin Mediterranean catchments where an equilibrium between water 
demand and availability is difficult to manage. Especially for the Guadalquivir River Basin, 
which is an example of a catchment with high hydrologic variability and severe “water deficits” 
according to global scale simulations of river flows, the water allocation will become a big 
challenge in the nearby future (Yeste et al. 2018).  
During the last two decades, the impacts of climate change on hydrological variables have been 
examined in the Mediterranean region and in the Guadalquivir river basin by a small number 
of studies. For example, Gomiz-Pascual et al. (2016), applied the Soil and Water Assessment 
Tool (SWAT) to assess the Guadalquivir river management. A simulated period between 2005 
and 2014 was used and the simulated streamflow was compared with historical flow data at the 
Alcala del Rio dam, located close to Sevilla. A good correlation between the simulated and 
observed data was performed both in the calibration and validation of the model, concluded 
that the SWAT model is an adequate tool to calculate the Guadalquivir river flow that can help 
to assess management practices for use in agro-industry. In another study, several statistical 
methods were applied to annual and seasonal time series of P for the period 1931 until 2006 in 
Southern Portugal, in order to study the P trends and variability and to assess spatial distribution 
patterns. The statistical methods included mainly homogeneity tests and cluster analysis, and 
lack of homogeneity in the P time series and a significant decreasing trend of P was detected. 
The authors concluded that the annual P regime is becoming drier, as the winter and spring also 
become drier (Mourato et al. 2010). 
Recently, Yeste et al. (2018) performed a study in order to identify climate-driver basin-scale 
trends in discharge volumes in the Guadalquivir river basin for a 50 years period (1950 – 2015). 
For this purpose, discharge records from a set of gauging stations in Southern Spain and a 
gridded set of monthly P and PET were used. They concluded, that regional scale streamflows 
have decreased significantly (up to 60%) during the last six decades and that the intra-annual 
redistribution of P was found to be the main statistically significant climate-driver of this 
change, compared to other non-climate factors, such as land use changes. Additionally, Moyano 
et al. (2018) applied three methods to track the spatial and temporal characteristics of ET in the 
Doñana region (Spain). Among them, they found that the PT-JPL-thermal approach is a robust 
RS model for detecting ET at a regional level in Mediterranean environments. Finally, the 
trends in discharge volumes at the regional scale of the Iberian Peninsula were examined in a 
study conducted by Lorenzo-Lacruz et al. (2012). The results revealed a significant decrease in 
annual, winter, and spring streamflow in most of the Iberian sub-basins (Tajo, Júcar, Guadiana 
and Guadalquivir). They concluded that the generalized decrease in P along with higher ET due 
to the increase in T, will lead to a higher decrease in discharge volumes.

1.3 Objectives  
 
While the benefits of using RS data have been investigated in several watershed studies, few 
research works have been used ET data apart from discharge measurements to calibrate-validate 
a hydrological model and also to assess the impact that has on discharge estimations (Tobin 
and Bennett 2017). The overall objective of this project is the analysis and the interpretation of 
the river discharge and simulated ET in the Guadalquivir River basin in southern Spain, using 
a combination of satellite and hydrological modeling.  
To achieve the overarching objective, the following steps have been defined: 
➢ Explore previous hydrological studies and the availability of in-situ and remote 
sensing data in the study area. 
➢ Setting up a lumped conceptual model based on the Budyko framework and using 
the Muskingum routing scheme to simulate river discharge. 
➢ Calibrate and validate the model based on available discharge-ET data. 
➢ Assess calibration procedures and the effects on discharge and ET. 
➢ Assess the overall model performance. 
 
1.4 Selection of the model 
 
In this study, a Budyko type rainfall-runoff model coupled to a Muskingum routing scheme is 
used. Prior to the selection of the model, the semi-distributed SWAT model (Neitsch et al. 
2011), was also investigated but specific limitations didn’t allow its use in this study. The 
SWAT model has the advantage of simulated all the major hydrologic processes (such as ET, 
surface runoff, infiltration and snow accumulation). It divides the watershed into sub basins 
and further, each sub basin into hydrologic response units (HRUs) based on unique 
combinations of land use, soil and slope characteristics (Neitsch et al. 2011). However, SWAT 
does not consider the spatial location of the HRUs present in each sub basin (Tobin and Bennett 
2017). Also, an extensive literature regarding its application in different watershed’s scales can 
be found. The following pros and cons were considered for the selection of the appropriate 
model for this study: 
➢ Operationality: SWAT compared to the Budyko type rainfall-runoff model, 
requires a large amount of input data to calculate the water balance, while the 
method used in this study requires only P and max., min. T.  
==PAGE:19==

➢ Flexibility: The code for the BD rainfall-runoff model can be directly assessed 
allowing modifications, while the SWAT code is encrypted (Kittel et al. 2018). 
➢ Functionality: In the SWAT model there is not a function to aggregate gridded 
climate data and in situ gauging stations (in txt or db format) are required. It uses 
only one climate data per subbasin to compute the water balance (the one nearest 
to the centroid), thus the issue of optimal climate spatial resolution is of high 
significance (Grusson et al. 2017). Additionally, SWAT model uses three methods 
to calculate the potential evapotranspiration (PET) but it offers also the possibility 
to the user to import daily PET values. However, the PET file in the SWAT holds 
only one record for the entire watershed (Neitsch et al. 2011).  
➢ Novelty: The method used in this study (Zhang et al. 2008), was developed to 
integrate RS data into hydrological models in the context of the GlobWetland 
Africa (GW-A) project which was launched from the Ramsar Secretariat and the 
ESA, with the purpose to use earth observations for management and monitoring 
African Wetlands (Kittel et al. 2018). Although Guadalquivir river is located close 
to the north Africa’s wetlands, no previous studies have used this rainfall-runoff 
model to simulate the water balance in the Guadalquivir watershed.

2.3 Ramsar Wetlands and Natura 2000 
 
Protected areas or conservation areas, are those that have been declared subject to special 
protection under a specific Community standard, relating to the protection of their surface or 
groundwater, or to the conservation of habitats and species directly dependent on water. There 
are several kinds of protected areas, which vary by level of protection depending on the 
enabling laws of each country or the regulations of the international organizations involved. 
Two frameworks are considered in this section including the Ramsar Convention on Wetlands 
(Ramsar 2018) and the Natura 2000 network (European Commission 2014). With data obtained 
from MITECO, a map was created (Figure 5) for the depiction of the protected areas under the

Although the wetlands in the Guadalquivir watershed cover a small area (0.75%), Doñana

complexes in Western Europe and is included in the List of Wetlands of International 
Importance. It is located close to the place where the Guadalquivir River reaches the Atlantic 
Ocean (mouth of Guadalquivir river) (Figure 5).

3.1 Hydrological model 
 
By its definition, a model is a simpler and incomplete representation of the reality (Silberstein 
2006). Hydrological models are a powerful tool for water resources management and climate 
change assessment, and they have been widely applied by scientists and stakeholders to make 
watershed decisions. In general, the hydrological models are designed to study the system 
operation, predict its system behavior (i.e. scenario analysis) and to understand various 
hydrological processes. As a model contains various parameters that describe its characteristics, 
a rainfall-runoff (RR) model can be defined as a number of equations that help to estimate 
runoff as a function of different parameters used to describe watershed characteristics. In short, 
the RR model calculates the conversion of rainfall into runoff (Dwarakish and Ganasri 2015). 
Hydrological models can be subdivided into two main categories including the Newtonian and 
the Darwinian models. In the Newtonians models, the hydrologic cycle and behavior is derived 
from Newton’s laws (e.g. the momentum equation, laws of mass and energy). The approach is 
based on cause and effect relationships, having the ability to represent the processes in the 
watershed with high detail, as it builds a mechanistic model of hydrologic processes (i.e. 
describe the physical processes in isolation). On the other hand, the Darwinian approach 
explains the hydrological behavior as a system and does not describe the physical processes in 
isolation. The Darwinian approaches were developed based in the hydrologic behavior and 
empirical data from a population of watersheds.

Each hydrological model has its own characteristics and applications (from small catchments 
to global models or gauged-ungauged catchments) as well as different drawbacks such as large 
data requirements and computational times. In general, models should be as simple as possible 
but as complex as necessary (parsimony). Two types of hydrologic models have been used in 
most applications, including the distributed (or semi-distributed) physically based models and 
the lumped conceptual models (Jajarmizadeh et al. 2012).  
To begin with, the semi distributed models such as SWAT (Newtonian model) are permitted to 
change in space as they allow to divide the catchment into an amount of sub basins (e.g. SWAT 
divides the catchment into hydrologic response units). They require smaller amount of input 
data, in contrary to the distributed models which consider the hydrological processes taking 
place at each grid and define the model variables as functions of space dimensions. However, 
the parameterization of both categories remains a major challenge. On the other hand, lumped 
conceptual models describe the catchment as a single entity or a small group of entities (e.g. 
reservoirs) and they apply in a single point or a region for the simulation of various hydrologic 
processes. Thus, the parameters used in a lumped model represent spatially averaged 
characteristics in a system (Jajarmizadeh et al. 2012). 
For this study, the model used is composed of different submodels to represent different 
hydrological processes: a conceptual lumped RR model was coupled with a linear reservoir 
model (Nash Cascade model) and a one-dimensional routing model (Muskingum River Routing 
model) (Zhang et al. 2008). The description of each submodel is analyzed in the following

3.2 Rainfall-Runoff Model – Budyko Framework 
 
 
The RR model used in this study, is a lumped conceptual model based on the BD framework 
and work of Zhang et al., (2008). In general, the BD framework (Darwinian model) is used to 
quantify the water balance and specifically ET in terms of water supply (P) and atmospheric 
water demand (PET) at the watershed scale. It uses a semi-empirical curve that describes the 
fraction of P that is lost through ET as a non-linear function of the dryness index (ratio of PET 
over P). In short, it relates the evaporative index (ratio of ET over P) with the dryness index. 
The evaporative index is an indicator of the long-term mean annual water balance in the 
watershed, as it is the fraction of the water falling as P that is lost as ET. The dryness index is 
a measure of the long-term mean annual climate (ratio of PET over P).  
More specifically, Budyko (Budyko 1961), assumed that at long-term annual time, scale ET is 
controlled by water availability (represented by P) and atmospheric demand (represented by net 
radiation or PET). Under very dry conditions (Dryness index>1), ET will approach P, meaning 
that ET is limited by water supply. Under very wet conditions (dryness index<1), ET will 
approach PET, reflecting that ET is limited by the atmospheric energy demand. These 
assumptions can be expressed mathematically as following (Malagò et al. 2018):

Calculation of PET 
For the current RR model based on the BD framework, two inputs are required including 
precipitation (P) and max., min. temperature (T) products. While the P is a direct input to the 
RR model, the max. and min. T is used to calculate the potential evapotranspiration (PET). 
 Different methods have been developed for the estimation of PET, which can be grouped into 
five main categories: water budget, mass-transfer, combination, radiation and temperature-
based. However, the most common are the Penman-Monteith method (combination), the 
Priestley-Taylor method (radiation) and the Hargreaves method (temperature-based). Each 
time, the selection of the most appropriate method for calculating PET and ET is a major 
challenge, as the choice is highly dependent of the availability of the data. For example, the 
Penman-Monteith method requires solar radiation, air temperature, relative humidity and wind 
speed, while the Priestley-Taylor method requires solar radiation, relative humidity and air 
temperature. On the other hand, the Hargreaves method which is also used in this model, 
requires only the minimum and maximum air temperature (Neitsch et al. 2011).

3.3 Nash Cascade model 
 
As it is already mentioned in section 3.1, the groundwater storage is treated as a linear reservoir 
and the Nash Cascade model is one of the most common models for river routing. The routing 
for each river reach, is conceptualized as a series of subreaches having the same length, with 
each subreach having the same storage characteristics. Subsequently, the routed outflow from 
the first subreach becomes inflow to the second subreach etc. and as a result the outflow of the 
last subreach is the desired outflow of the whole reach (Vijay P. Singh D.Sc. et al., 2017). The 
Nash Cascade model is incorporated into the RR model in order to handle the tributary 
processes and capture the timing and peak of the observed discharge. 
To be more specific, the Nash Cascade model is used to transform the effective rainfall 
(difference between total rainfall and actual evapotranspiration) into a direct runoff hydrograph, 
which represents a direct runoff at the outlet of a watershed resulting from one unit of an

3.4 Muskingum River Routing Model 
 
As stated in section 3.3, the Nash Cascade model is widely used as part of a conceptual rainfall-
runoff model, but also for flood routing in rivers and channels. In general, the river routing 
methods can be grouped into two categories including (Vijay P. Singh D.Sc. et al., 2017): 
✓ Lumped / Hydrologic flow routing: The flow is calculated as a function of time 
alone at a specific location. 
✓ Distributed / Hydraulic routing: The flow is calculated as a function of space and 
time throughout the system. 
Among the hydrological channel routing methods, the Muskingum method is one of the most 
popular and widely used. The model used in this study, uses the lumped Muskingum routing

3.5.1 Digital Elevation Model (DEM) 
 
Since no in-situ data related to the bathymetry of the Guadalquivir river are available, the DEM 
of the catchment was obtained from the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) - Version 
4 (USGS 2009). The SRTM digital elevation data were produced by NASA originally, in order 
to create the most complete high-resolution digital topographic database of the Earth, by 
covering over 80% of the globe. They had a resolution of approximately 90m and in the late 
2014, a new version was released, proving SRTM DEM’s with 30-meter (1 arc-second) 
resolution. They cover most of the world, having an absolute vertical height accuracy of less 
than 16 meters. Lastly, they are available for global download at 5ox5o tiles.

Using Digital Elevation Models) watershed delineation feature, was used to extract different 
geographical parameters from the drainage catchment. TauDem is a set of DEM tools 
developed at Utah State University (USU), in order to extract and analyze hydrologic 
information from topography as represented by a DEM. The TauDem plugin consists of three 
main tools including: Basic Grid Analysis, Network Analysis and Specialized Grid Analysis 
(Tarboton 2015). 
Specifically, since the concept of the hydrological flow field is represented by flow from each 
grid cell to one or more of its neighbors, a sink fill (pit removal) was firstly performed in order 
to raise the elevation of pits until they drain out (i.e. pixels were filled to the same elevation of 
the lowest of the neighboring cells). Subsequently, the “hydrological correct” DEM was used 
to define the flow direction and slope for each grid cell. Then, based on the flow direction of 
each cell, the number of grid cells draining through each grid cell (flow accumulation) was 
found and selected inlets and outlets were defined. In general, the outlets are placed at sites of 
specific interest and at the main outlet and usually at the confluence of streams. In this study, 
the outlets were placed at points where discharge data are available and at the main outlet of 
the Guadalquivir basin, even though there was not discharge data available at the mouth of the 
basin. Finally, the river network was modelled, and the watershed was delineated and divided 
into subbasins. It is important to note here, that a question that arises each time in stream

Calculation of the geometry (width/depth) of river reaches 
The TauDem hydroprocessing tool, estimates reach geometry including length of the reaches, 
slope and distances from the outlets to the respective downstream and upstream links. 
According to Kittel et al. (2018), in order to decrease the spatial time required by the 
Muskingum routing model, the reaches can be divided further at selected cross sections. Thus, 
each reach is subdivided in a specific number of segments. This was done, by dividing each 
river reach (corresponding to the respective sub-catchment) with 15 km.  
Furthermore, the model assumes that the cross sections have trapezoidal shape and that the 
channel sides have a 2:1 run to rise ratio (i.e. 4m width change per 1m depth change).  While 
the length and the slope from each river reach are created automatically from the TauDem tool, 
the users are required to define/calculate the depth and width of the channel when filled to the 
top of the bank.  
In this study, the Simple Global River Bankfull Width & Depth Database was used (Andreadis 
et al. 2013). This database was developed based on hydraulic geometry equations and the 
HydroSHEDS hydrography dataset. HydroSHEDS (Hydrological data and maps based 
on SHuttle Elevation Derivatives at multiple Scales), is a mapping product that provides 
hydrographic information and is based on high-resolution elevation data obtained during a 
Space Shuttle flight for NASA's Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) (Lehner et al. 
2013). The description of the database can be found in  the paper described by Andreadis et al. 
(2013), while ESRI shapefiles including river networks with depth and width estimations are 
available for download at http://gaia.geosci.unc.edu/rivers/.

3.6 Model Calibration 
 
The purpose of model calibration is to adjust the model parameters over a training period, so 
that the model outputs to match as closely as possible with the observations. More specifically, 
according to Sivakumar et al. (2010), the model calibration includes the selection of a parameter 
set that generates model responses, that reproduce as closely as possible, the historically 
observed hydrologic response of a specific watershed. Subsequently, a model needs to be 
validated, as the model validation provides information about the real functioning of the model. 
The procedure of model calibration and validation is depicted in  Figure 16 (H. Madsen, 2016). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In order to calibrate a model, specific model parameters are selected. The RR BD type model 
used in this study, consists of 6 parameters that need to be calibrated (Kittel et al. 2018). Since 
many of the model parameters are difficult to estimate without measurements or fieldwork, all 
the parameters are included in the calibration. Their description is included in section 3.8. Note 
here, that the ranges for the calibrated parameters, are based on the sensitivity analysis that 
performed in Kittel et al. (2018). 
The calibration procedure can be conducted by two different alternatives. The first is the 
automated calibration, where different computer algorithms are used to reach the best simulated 
reproduction of observations. The advantage of this type is the low personnel requirements, but 
it requires more time and more availability of the observed values. On the other hand, the 
Figure 16: Flow chart of the parameter calibration and model validation process. 
==PAGE:49==
Methodology

second type is the manual calibration, which is “person intensive” as the user needs to 
understand how to alter a given parameter set in order to improve the model performance 
(Sivakumar et al. 2010). In this study, an automated calibration is performed in the 
programming language of Python.  
Moreover, the automated calibration can be based on local or global search methods. Their 
difference, is basically that the local methods include the estimation of a single parameter set 
by following deterministic rules, while the global strategies include different solutions by 
applying both deterministic and stochastic rules and as a result a broad search of the parameter 
space can be achieved (Blasone et al. 2007). For the calibration of the model used in this study, 
the python plugin\package “Statistical Parameter Optimization Tool” (SPOTPY) was used. 
SPOTPY was developed by Houska et al. (2015) and is accessible as open source on the python 
package index PyPI.  
SPOTPY has already been used for a variety of models and its advantage is that allows the use 
of all the algorithms for multi-objective calibration approaches. It has a model-independent 
structure and it can be run efficiently in parallel into large computation clusters, by using a 
Message Passing Interface (MPI) code (Houska et al. 2015).  
Among the 15 available optimization algorithms, the Shuffled Complex Evolution method 
(SCE-UA) is used in this study. This algorithm was developed at the university of Arizona and 
is described in detail in the article of Duan et al. (1992). It has been used in several hydrological 
studies and is considered as capable to deal with complications that meet in conceptual 
watershed model calibration (Duan et al. 1994). At first, the algorithm samples a number of 
random points (a population of points randomly distributed) within a given parameter set and 
then it computes a criterion value at each point (i.e. the model performance is evaluated for 
each parameter set). Additionally, assuming that the optimum is to minimize this criterion 
value, each parameter set is ranked, with the first point representing the smallest criterion value. 
Subsequently, the parameter sets are partitioned into a chosen number of complexes which are 
evolved separately and each one contains 2n + 1 points, where n is the number of parameters 
to calibrate. Periodically, the complexes are shuffled, and points are reassigned to new 
complexes ensured that information gained by the previous complexes is shared. The steps 
continue, until the prescribed convergence criteria are reached, meaning that the entire 
population converged towards the global optimum (Duan et al. 1992, 1994). A flow chart was 
created showing the procedure of the SCE-UA method (Figure 17).

3.6.1 Calibration procedure/Calibration zones 
 
Many references exist in the literature related to calibration procedures and techniques. 
Traditionally, a “single-site” calibration is performed including almost always the main outlet 
of a catchment. However, this assumes that the conditions (including soil classes, topography, 
land uses) across the watershed, are homogeneous (Nkiaka et al. 2018). In this study, due to the 
large surface area of the Guadalquivir catchment as well as the high heterogeneity including 
the different land uses and the elevation differences, a careful calibration of the model is 
required in order to obtain consistent results among all the gauging stations within the 
catchment.  
Moreover, hydrological models are mainly evaluated against discharge stations, by giving 
emphasis in the temporal and not in the spatial component of a model (Mendiguren et al. 2017). 
Thus, in order to account for the spatially variability of the Guadalquivir river basin in the 
calibration process, the catchment was divided into different calibration zones that were 
assumed to have similar biophysical conditions. All the subbasins that belong to a given

3.6.2 Objective functions  
In general, the objective functions are used for the computation of a numerical measure which 
includes the difference between the simulated output (from the model) and the observed 
(measured) output. They aggregate the model errors over time between the two outputs, thus, 
the purpose is to find the values of the model parameters that optimize the numerical values of 
the objective function. There are different objective functions that are used in hydrological 
modeling, such as the root-mean-square error (RMSE), the Nash-Sutcliffe measure (NS) and 
the mean absolute error (MAE). Depending on the model variables to be optimized (e.g.

streamflow and evapotranspiration) as well as the chosen method of the calibration that is used 
in a study, different objective functions have been developed and exist in the literature.  
A key-part is how to divide the dataset for calibration and validation. One of the most common 
calibration approaches is the split-sample approach. This method includes the application of a 
previously calibrated model for a given time period to another unknown period. Typically, if 
the record is long enough, it should be divided in two equal parts (first half for calibration and 
second for validation) (Arsenault et al. 2018). The objective functions that are used in the 
method, put more emphasis on fitting certain parts of a hydrograph (e.g. low or high flows). 
However, in this study we chose to calibrate the model against long-term and seasonal averages 
of flow patterns and discharge stations in subbasins with similar characteristics (based on land 
use and slope classification), were used to validate the results. Thus, a split-sample approach in 
space and not in time was performed and the methods of the climatology benchmark and the 
Flow Duration Curve (FDC) were used along with their objective functions.  
Climatology Benchmark 
The model forecasting skill expresses the performance of the forecast relative to a reference 
forecast or benchmark. Therefore, the choice of the benchmark influences the resulting model 
skill. In general, the benchmarks can be categorized based on their ability to represent potential 
attributes of improvement of the forecasts under evaluation (Pappenberger et al. 2015). Two of 
the most commonly used benchmarks, are the climatology benchmark and the persistence. The 
climatology benchmark, uses the mean of the historical observations for a particular day of the 
year (this is the benchmark to beat in long-term forecasting), while in the persistence, the last 
available observation is used as the reference forecast (this is the benchmark to beat in short-
term forecasting) (Bauer-Gottwein, 2018).

long time period, the seasonal flow patterns will be similar. The Root Mean Square Error

3.9 Modeling Environments 
 
For the current study, different software and modeling environments were used in order to

Remote Sensing P, T and ET products have been widely used in hydrological modeling and 
earth observations have been used to monitor small as well as large scale water bodies. As a 
result, the global monitoring of the hydrological cycle has been improved since a large amount 
of data is readily available from satellite-based RS (Tang et al. 2009). Many studies state the 
use of RS products for forcing hydrologic models (i.e. use of RS products as model inputs). In 
every case, in situ observations for algorithm development and validation as well as the 
uncertainty of the input data, are the main issues and challenges that arise in the context of 
using RS data for forcing hydrologic models. This section describes the climate forcing and 
data inputs used to drive the Budyko type RR model of this study as well as other datasets, 
along with information related to their availability, uncertainty and post processing. The digital 
elevation map was described in section 3.5.1. 
 
4.1 Spatial information/Shapefiles 
 
Vector GIS files from the watershed in .shp format, were downloaded from the Ministry of the 
Environment in Spain-MITECO (Ministerio para la Transición Ecológica) in order to extract, 
compare and analyze the information related to the Guadalquivir catchment and its hydrology 
status. Although the Guadalquivir basin was delineated using the TauDEM tool, the shapefiles 
from MITECO were used to compare and ensure that the final watershed delineation is enough 
close with the original (i.e. assess the accuracy of the delineation). The Guadalquivir model 
outline, the subbasins and the model river network were compared with these from MITECO, 
without observing any deviation between them, but only in the river network where as it is 
already stated in section 3.5.3, a big stream threshold was used for the delineation (smaller river 
lengths and as a result less sub basins) - Figure 66 (Appendix E). Furthermore, other vector 
files described in this study were downloaded also from MITECO and the Guadalquivir River 
Basin Authority ( Confederación Hidrográfica del Guadalquivir – CHG ).  Information related 
to the vector files are presented in Table 13 (Appendix E). 
 
4.2  Precipitation and Temperature Data 
 
In general, P is an important forcing in RR models, and it can be considered as one of the most 
important inputs in hydrological predictions. Many of the surface hydrological processes and 
as a result the response dynamics of a catchment, are highly determined by the amount and 
distribution of the precipitation. Moreover, due to the climate change scenarios, the change of 
==PAGE:60==
Data 
 
48 
 
rainfall and temperature will highly determine the future runoff of a basin (Garambois et al. 
2015). Daily weather data required by the BD type RR model used in this study, include 
minimum and maximum air T and P. Tmax and Tmin are important, since estimations of PET are 
calculated based on inputs of temperature data using the Hargreaves equation (see equation 
(3.17)).  
For this study, daily P, Tmax and Tmin data were obtained based on the work from Vicente-
Serrano et al. (2017) . Specifically, by covering the period 1961 to 2014, they developed a high-
resolution spatial (approx. 1.2 km2) and temporal (weekly) drought dataset for Spain based on 
gridded datasets of max. and min. air temperature, precipitation, relative humidity, wind speed 
and sunshine duration. The grid was divided in three areas including the Peninsular Spain, the 
Balearic Islands and the Canary Islands. By using as auxiliary variable, the latitude, longitude 
and elevation of each grid cell, the grid of each variable was calculated based on the universal 
kriging interpolation method (a gridding interpolation method). In order to obtain a serially 
complete dataset, the missing values were fitted using the 10 surrounding stations for each day 
and location. The daily precipitation estimations were compared to the original observations, 
in order to assess the quality of the gridded dataset. The validation showed low differences 
between the predicted and the observed values for each grid. Finally, based on the complete 
gridded dataset, four daily precipitation indices and nine extreme precipitation indices were 
computed for characterizing the spatial distribution of daily precipitation and the 
frequency/intensity of extreme precipitation events. A more detailed description of the dataset 
and further information can be found in Vicente-Serrano et al. (2017). 
The data were in NetCDF format, thus further processing was performed into Python in order 
to convert them in .tiff format, including the simulated period for this study (2001 to 2017). 
Subsequently, the model assigns the climate information to the virtual stations that the user 
defines. Thus, in QGIS software the centroids of each sub basin were found, giving a total of 
163 virtual stations (since the catchment was divided into 163 sub catchments - see section 
3.5.2). Subsequently, by using Python the virtual stations were filled with P, Tmax and Tmin 
data.

4.5 Evapotranspiration data 
 
Apart from P, ET is the largest term in the terrestrial water balance. According to Garcia et al. 
(2013), an accurate regional estimation of ET is highly important in drylands, since it accounts 
for 90-100% of the total annual precipitation. Moreover, changes in vegetation greenness have 
been reported based on satellite observations and the Iberian Peninsula is among the places that 
are expected to be the most affected areas by severe and recurrent droughts by the end of the 
21st century (IPCC 2007). Thus, global estimates of ET are a requirement, not only because is 
a sole term that links the land surface water balance and the land surface energy balance, but 
also because of its application value in crop water requirement management and drought 
forecasting and monitoring (Zhao et al. 2013). Especially for the Guadalquivir catchment where 
the irrigated agriculture accounts from more than 70% of the total water use, the 
driving mechanisms behind ET and its role are crucial. 
For this study, RS ET data were obtained from the ET dataset generated for Spain based on 
high resolution climatic data and MODIS data. The dataset is based on the Priestley-Taylor Jet 
Propulsion Laboratory (PT-JPL) algorithm, which was originally developed from  Fisher et al. 
(2008). The model was later modified from García et al. (2013), where thermal remote sensing 
data were used in order to derive the soil moisture constraint. This approach was later used, 
tested and validated by Moyano et al. (2018) and is used also in the “construction” of the ET 
dataset for Spain that used in this study. 
In short, the model principle is based on how vegetation adapts to climatic conditions. 
Specifically, under optimum condition the vegetation transpires at its potential, however as 
soon as the conditions change (e.g. increasing T or reducing water availability), the vegetation 
needs to adapt in order to optimize its resources. The model can be divided in two different 
sections. The first one includes the calculation of the net radiation based on different climatic 
inputs (including the DESEMON dataset described in section 4.2 from Vicente-Serrano et al., 
(2017)). In the second section, the PET is calculated based on the PT-JPL model and the ET is 
obtained. Finally, in order to overlap with the MODIS data, the dataset contains daily ET and 
PET data from 2002 to 2017 at 1.1km resolution.

5. Results and Discussion 
 
This chapter presents the results obtained from the preparation of the hydrological model for 
the Guadalquivir catchment, including model calibration and validation. The performance of 
the model was assessed by using two calibration approaches. In the first approach, the model 
was calibrated against in-situ discharge observations, while in the second, ET RS data were 
incorporated, and the model was calibrated based on both discharge and ET time series. 
Regarding the calibration based only on the discharge, two objective functions were used, as 
they described in section 3.6.2. Their combination (equation 3.33) optimizes the parameters by 
comparing the discharge of flow duration along with the mean annual climatology benchmark. 
Additionally, the RMSE objective function (as in climatology benchmark) was used for ET, 
when calibrated the model based on both discharge-ET data.  
Based on the two calibration approaches, the optimal parameter sets for the different calibration 
zones are provided and their performance is described. In short, results from the discharge 
calibration are presented followed by the flow-ET calibration results. Subsequently, for the 
selected subbasins, a visualization of the simulated potential evapotranspiration (PET) against 
the remote sensing PET is illustrated. In every case, realizations on the different approaches 
that used are discussed, while the chapter is finalized with an overall discussion related to the 
model set up and application as well as with suggestions for further analysis. Lastly, modeling 
results can be found both in this chapter but also in the Appendices of the current report.

Looking at the calibration results from Table 6, the optimized parameters vary between the two 
calibration zones, which was expected, due to the different geophysical characteristics of the 
basin.  
Specifically, parameter α1 is the retention efficiency and determines the runoff generated from 
a given rainfall volume. The lower and upper limit for this parameter is 0.1 and 0.9 respectively 
and as it can be seen for both zones was found to be below 0.5. High α1 values mean that more 
water is being retained in the basin (i.e. reduction in direct runoff), thus here the low α1 values 
indicate that a big fraction of the rainfall turns into direct runoff.  Considering that the two 
zones include mainly non-forest areas, there is not so much vegetation cover to slow down the 
direct runoff. Subsequently, the α2 parameter which is the evapotranspiration efficiency, 
governs the distribution between evapotranspiration and groundwater recharge. Both α2 
estimates were found to be very close to the upper limit (0.9) especially for Zone 3, which has 
the higher evapotranspiration. The large α2 values indicate the high partitioning of available 
water into evapotranspiration. 
Furthermore, the parameters where a remarkable difference is observed between the two zones 
are the d and Smax . d is the baseflow recession constant and is used to characterize the interaction 
==PAGE:70==
Results and Discussion 
 
58 
 
of groundwater and surface water systems. It contributes on fitting the low flows since it 
represents the storage-discharge relationship of the groundwater. The parameter is ranging 
between 0 and 1 and the high value in Zone 3 means that the water is stored for a short period 
of time in the aquifer. This indicates that baseflow is strongly linked to precipitation and no 
baseflow will occurred during a long dry period. For Zone 4, the value is smaller, meaning that 
the water is stored for longer time in the aquifer.  The last model parameter, Smax, represents the 
maximum soil water storage in the rootzone. This parameter was found to be very high in Zone 
4, while in Zone 3 the soil appears to have much lower storage capacity.  Since this parameter 
is related to soil and vegetation characteristics, it is quite evident that the variation of Smax is 
due to differences in the soil properties of the area. As it is observed from Figure 62 (Appendix 
B), the calibrated subbasins dominated by different soil types. However, according to the results 
from the sensitivity analysis that performed in the study of  Kittel et al. (2018), this parameter 
was not found to be so sensitive (compared to the α parameters), as it converged to similar 
values in several different calibrations. 
Lastly, the Muskingum parameters were found to be very close to each other for the two Zones. 
Specifically, the Muskingum weighting factor X with lower and upper limit of 0 and 0.5 
respectively, lies within the expected range for natural streams which is typically from 0 to 0.3 
according to Chow et al., (1988). High values of X indicate that there is little attenuation and 
friction within and between the reaches and a value equal to 0.5 means that there is no 
attenuation at all, and it just moves the flow away over space. The Manning’s roughness 
coefficient n is also within the reasonable range for naturals streams according to reference n 
values from Chow (1959). In general, the roughness coefficient is a measure of the effect that 
factors such as the channel geometry, the river bed and the vegetation type in it, have on flow 
resistance. High n values, indicate that the wetted perimeter of a channel applies resistance to 
the flow, contributing as a result to the retention of the water in the stream. 
Although the model parameters are cross-corelated, it might be possible to get similar results 
with different parameters sets (Kittel et al. 2018). However, by obtaining more information 
related to characteristics of the basin (such as soil properties and texture) and by performing a 
sensitivity analysis, the resulted values of the model parameters could be assessed better.

unaffected by the management of the reservoirs. However, the stations are not located in the

are observed, and the river was found to almost dry out in the summer season. However, the

temperature (Neitsch et al. 2011). Thus, the model PET for each of the subbasins for the two 
zones was extracted and it was compared with the PET RS in order to check the fitting of the 
data. The comparison between modeled and RS observed PET can help to address model 
shortcomings.

The last section of the chapter examines the patterns and relation between the discharge and 
evapotranspiration (PET and ET) for the selected subbasins in the Guadalquivir catchment. At 
first, for the examined subbasins, the annual average values of ET, PET and P were calculated 
in order to check whether the subbasins are water or energy limited and that might could explain 
some of the model performance issues. In general, the Budyko curve (see Figure 7) shows the 
relationship between ET divided by P and PET divided by P. If PET exceeds the precipitation, 
PET/P > 1 (aridity index), the basin is water limited because ET is smaller than PET. Otherwise, 
small PET/P (<1) represents a wet climate (energy limited conditions). 
As it can be seen in Figure 54, all the examined subbasins are water-limited, as all modeled 
values plot above an PET/P of 1. The modeled values are below the curve, provided by Budyko 
(1961). Vertical deviations change in partitioning between ET and discharge. The higher ET/P, 
the less discharge is observed. Instead, horizontal deviations change in the climatic conditions, 
meaning that the higher PET/P, the warmer and drier the subbasin is.

6. Conclusion 
 
This thesis demonstrates the use of gridded and remote sensing data to simulate river discharge 
and ET in a lumped hydrological model, by examining the Guadalquivir river basin in Southern 
Spain. A digital elevation model was used to delineate the watershed and derive the 
Guadalquivir river network. The rainfall-runoff model used in this study, is a lumped 
conceptual model based on the Budyko framework and is coupled to a Muskingum routing 
scheme. The model was forced using gridded precipitation and temperature data and model 
calibration of discharge and ET by using two different approaches, was assessed. 
In the first calibration approach, the model was calibrated against in-situ discharge 
observations, while in the second one, evapotranspiration remote sensing derived-observations 
were used to constrain more the model parameters, which was calibrated based on both 
discharge and evapotranspiration data. In both cases, the flow duration curve and the 
climatology benchmark were used for the optimization of the model parameters. To define the 
parameter sets, four calibration zones were included, based on land use characteristics and 
topography. However, due to the high regulation of the river, two out of the four zones were 
chosen (Zone 3 and Zone 4), by including discharge stations under natural flow regime. 
Based on the first calibration approach, the results showed that the model performed better in 
Zone 4 (non-forest and non-flat), where the aggregated objective function (𝑂𝑏𝑗𝑔𝑙𝑜𝑏𝑎𝑙) for the 
calibration station, was found to be closer to the acceptance ranges compared to the calibration 
station of Zone 3 (non-forest and flat). In either case, the model underestimated the discharge 
in the two zones, including both the calibration and validation stations. For the second 
calibration approach (simultaneous calibration), the model provided equally consistent results 
of ET for all the stations of the two zones. However, the seasonality of discharge was captured 
better in zone 4, as it indicated from the climatology benchmark. Additionally, the daily data 
of discharge, evapotranspiration and potential evapotranspiration were aggregated to monthly 
time scale, in order to analyze their seasonality for the simulated period. Concerning the 
potential evapotranspiration, the model provided satisfactory results, with maximum PET 
values during the summer and in accordance with the existing literature. The ET was found to 
present maximum values during spring, while the discharge presented more fluctuations due to 
the high precipitation variability.  
This thesis concludes, that the seasonal patterns of discharge and ET can provide valuable 
information to the water authorities in the Guadalquivir catchment. Calibrating a model for 
multiple variables, can provide useful information about its limitations. Moreover, a better

understanding of the processes happening within a catchment can be achieved and information 
related to the effects of climate change and land uses in the river discharge could be obtained. 
Lastly, we suggest that the reservoirs need to be included in the model in order to adapt it to 
the entire basin.

7. Further studies and model application 
 
The RR model presented in this study, could be an initial step to develop an operational tool 
(e.g. requires few inputs) for water authorities, in order to examine the hydrological dynamics 
of river basins. The model set-up allows its application in poorly- gauged basins, where in-situ 
observations are not available. Moreover, the implementation of the model as an open-source 
QGIS workflow or python package, facilitates the ability of the users to modify the code and 
adapt it according to their needs. 
In the current study, the model was applied to examine the hydrologic responses in the 
Guadalquivir catchment located in Southern Spain and with the specific goal of assessing model 
performance when calibrating with discharge data and with both discharge and ET data 
simultaneously. 
Due to the river regulation by dams constructed across the catchment, hydrologic conditions 
only in subbasins with stations under natural flow regime were included and simulated by the 
model. This didn’t allow the examination of subbasins where the main Guadalquivir river drains 
to and higher flows are observed. Moreover, only subbasins in the south part of the basin were 
included, where non-forests areas dominate. Thus, the further development of the model would 
be to include the reservoirs and dams in the Guadalquivir catchment. By doing that, gauging 
stations downstream of major regulation infrastructures could be used and changes in reservoirs 
releases throughout the year could be included. 
Furthermore, the simulated period used in this study, was chosen in order for the discharge 
observations to overlap with the ET RS data. However, by including longer streamflow records, 
seasonal changes in the discharge could help to better constrain the hydrological modelling 
performed in Yeste et al. (2018). By the same token, the ET RS data used in this study, could 
be integrated in the existing long-term observations provided by a network of automatic 
agrometeorological stations that has been installed throughout the Andalucía (Estevez and 
Gavilán 2008). Thus, a better understanding of the spatial characteristics of ET across the 
catchment could be obtained. In this way, a different calibration approach by including 
separately the dry and wet years of the simulated period could be performed, since the study 
period is characterized by large seasonal variations. 
Additionally, the model used in this study is lumped, meaning that the parameters do not 
directly reflect changes in soil properties and vegetation of the subbasins (therefore, the 
catchment divided in zones). Thus, by applying a semi-distributed or a distributed model for 
the examined subbasins, the spatial-temporal characteristics of ET and discharge could be 
==PAGE:108==
Further studies and model application 
 
96 
 
compared and analyzed further. The finding of effective parameters that reflect the overall 
behavior of the catchment is a challenge. However, some of the parameters or at least their 
ranges, could be better inferred from remote sensing observations or ancillary datasets such as 
the Smax based on land cover or soil maps.   
Another important contribution of the model once is properly calibrated, could be its use to 
simulate impacts of climate change in the catchment. Global climate models provide large 
future rainfall and temperature series for different climate scenarios, which could be used as 
forcing data in the model and evaluate the impacts of climate change in the catchment. 
In every case, the uncertainties in the data used in the study should be considered and evaluated. 
For example, as it is discussed in section 5.4, the ET dataset contains measurements that were 
taken in the sunny days and not in cloudy conditions, contributing into higher ET values. 
Definitely, the model is a simplified representation of real-world system and cannot replace in-
situ observations. However, the results of the spatial analysis of both discharge and ET in the 
Guadalquivir catchment are important for regional water management, as well as for improving 
water use efficiency in the highly irrigated agriculture.
