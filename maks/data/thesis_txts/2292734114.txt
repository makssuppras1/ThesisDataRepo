Abstract
Board games have always been an important area in artificial intelligence research
as they present a very pure form of competition and clearly requires some form of
intelligence to succeed in. Several well-known games have already been solved using
artificial intelligence techniques, but some games are more difficult to design good
artificial intelligence agents for.
This thesis presents the work done for two modern number-matching board games,
2048 and Threes, with the main focus being on 2048. The complexity of the games
is discussed and analysed and three main approaches, that each has succeeded in
games of different characteristics, are compared, namely Minimax, Expectimax and
Monte Carlo Tree Search, and various optimization techniques for each method are
discussed and tested. Eight different heuristic functions are analysed and compared
and a different approach to optimizing weights in an evaluation function based on
a genetic algorithm is presented. This project also considers two ways of combining
the strengths of classic heuristic search with the learning-based tree search approach
of Monte Carlo Tree Search.
Finally, a prototype of a 2048 helper application is
presented, that uses the AI agents developed in this project to help the user to
achieve better gameplay.
The work done in this project shows a superiority of Expectimax compared to
Minimax and Monte Carlo Tree Search and that the most promising heuristic for the
game 2048 is one that arranges the tiles in a snake-like pattern. This work also shows
promising results of applying a genetic algorithm to the field of weight-optimization
in an evaluation function.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the beginning of artificial intelligence board games have been an important area
of research. One of the first examples dates back to 1951, where Christopher Strachey
wrote a Checkers program using the Ferranti Mark 1 machine of the University of
Manchester and Dietrich Prinz wrote an AI program for Chess [12]. In 1997 came
what was considered a major breakthrough for game artificial intelligence with the
defeat of chess grandmaster Garry Kasparov by the IBM supercomputer Deep Blue.
The abstract nature of board games is part of the reason why they have been so
important (and continue to be) for research within artificial intelligence. The states
of a board game are easy to represent and the actions available in the states as well
as their outcomes are well-defined. They are also interesting, because they can be
very hard to solve if not entirely unsolvable1, as most board games have enormous
search trees. Using artificial intelligence in order to make some decisions about which
actions will result in the best outcomes and to create game-playing agents that are
capable of beating humans based on this, is therefore extremely appealing for AI re-
searchers. Such agents have already been made for a number of games, but yet some
games have proved to be more challenging, and heavy research and new techniques
are developed in attempt to create unbeatable game-playing agents for these games.
A well-known example of this is the game Go. Many researches believe that the rea-
son Go is so challenging, is because of the lack of well-defined heuristic evaluation
functions. Another challenging factor is the massive state space of Go and it’s high
branching factor. The classical methods based on a high level of domain knowledge
have proved themselves successful for a number of other games, including chess. Go
researching is now moving into learning-based techniques that requires only little or
no domain knowledge, and recently Monte Carlo-based agents have succeeded in beat-
ing human experts in the game. Go is an excellent example of a very simple game
that is extremely complex to develop a good strategy for.
2048 and Threes are two simple slide-and-merge single-player games that are sim-
ilar in many ways. They both have a maximum of four possible moves for the player
at any time and they are both non-deterministic2, however 2048 has a higher degree

of non-determinism than Threes and due to this also a higher branching factor for
computer actions. Non-deterministic games require different methods than determin-
istic games in order to succeed, and in several cases it has turned out that learning
techniques and Monte Carlo-based methods can be successful for these types of games.
One example being TD-Gammon, a self-teaching Backgammon game-playing agent
based on reinforcement learning [29]. Monte Carlo Tree Search has also been suc-
cessfully applied to Backgammon and single-agent problems containing an element of
chance [9][21]. As both 2048 and Threes are very new games contrary to the other
games mentioned here, research into artificial intelligence strategies for these games
is limited and no thorough analysis or comparison of artificial intelligence techniques
exist yet for these games. The fact that 2048 is a very simple game and easy to learn,
yet hard for most human players to master, makes it an interesting challenge to cre-
ate a successful game-playing agent for. Keeping in mind that 2048 shares properties
with other areas in which Monte Carlo based methods have proved successful before,
being a single-agent non-deterministic game, suggests that these methods could also
be applied successfully to 2048. However, since good domain knowledge about the
game is available, it poses an interesting research question about which approach will
be most successful - a traditional heuristic search-based method or a Monte-Carlo
based approach, or maybe even a combination the two.
In this thesis we will investigate several techniques commonly used for board games
and apply these to 2048 and Threes, with the main focus being on 2048. We will also
analyse the complexity of these games and compare them to other well-known games.
The techniques we will discuss include classic heuristic search algorithms, namely
Minimax and its non-deterministic variant Expectimax, and the newer and popular
Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithm that bases its decisions on random game playouts.
We will also present several optimization techniques for these methods. This work
also includes an analysis of a number of different heuristic functions designed specifi-
cally for this type of game, as well as an attempt to optimize the evaluation function
consisting of a combination of these heuristics by applying a genetic algorithm. Fi-
nally, we will propose different ways of combining the strengths of classic heuristic
search algorithms with the learning-based tree search approach of Monte Carlo Tree
Search.
The aim of this thesis is to gain an understanding of which and why common
methods work (or do not work) for this type of game, as well as providing a thorough
and precise comparison of the various methods and heuristics under the same frame-
work. The results we obtain will then serve as a basis for a 2048 helper application
which we will briefly present.

2.1
2048
2048 is a number-matching game created by 19-year old Gabriele Cirulli from Italy
and released on the open-source platform Github in March 2014. In just a week after
Gabriele released the game it had received over 4 million views. In May 2014 a free
mobile version was released for iOS and Android and since then a number of copies
and variants have been released. 2048 is actually a variant itself of the similar games
1024 and Threes.
Figure 2.1: Search results for 2048 in Google Play
2.1.1
Gameplay
The original version of 2048 is played on a 4 × 4 board and is based on swiping
numbered tiles around the board and combining them with the goal of creating the
number 2048. Initially two tiles are randomly placed on the board, with the proba-
bility 90% for a tile with the value 2 and 10% for a tile with the value 4. Each time
the player has made a move, a new tile with the value 2 or 4 is randomly placed in
an empty cell on the board with the before-mentioned probabilities. The player can
swipe up, down, left or right and a swipe results in all the tiles being shifted as far as
possible in the direction of the swipe until they are stopped by either the edge of the
board or a tile with another value. If two tiles with the same value n collide during

2.1.2
Complexity
When analysing games it is good to know the complexity of the game in question in
order to understand the difficulties of learning to play the game well. Knowing the
complexity of the game can also help in understanding why some artificial intelligence
techniques work and others do not.
2.1.2.1
Highest possible tile and score
The goal of the game is to create the number 2048, but it is possible to continue the
game after reaching the 2048-tile with the potential of reaching even higher tiles. In
theory, the highest tile possible is as high as 131.072, which would require no less than
64 2048-tiles to be created during the game. As most people have difficulties even
reaching the 2048-tile, no one has ever reached this tile without undoes and saves
[36].

2.1.2.2
State space complexity
The state space complexity of a game describes the total number of different board
combinations possible in the game. This number can often be very hard to calculate,
which is why either a lower or an upper bound can be calculated instead. In the case
of 2048, an upper bound can easily be calculated, as there are 16 cells on the board,
and each cell can have 18 different values (2n for n = 0, ..., 17, where 0 represents an
empty tile). This gives us an upper bound of:
1816 = 1.2 ∗1020
The actual number is clearly lower than this bound, as this includes illegal states
such as states with more than 1 instance of a 131.027 tile, which we have already seen
is impossible. This number is however not very big compared to other more complex
games, such as chess which has a state space complexity of 1047 or Go on a 19 x 19
board that has a complexity of as much as 10171. Even a 9 x 9 game of Go still has
a complexity of 1038 due to the relatively large board size. One of the most simple
board games, Tic Tac Toe, which has also been a very popular game in the context
of artificial intelligence analysis has a state space complexity as low as 103 due to its
simplicity.

2.1.2.3
Game tree complexity
The game tree complexity is the total number of different games that can be played
[1]. This can also be viewed as the number of terminal nodes in the complete solution
search tree of the initial position of the game. This number is clearly larger than the
state space complexity as search trees can have several instances of the same states.
As hard as it is to calculate the state space complexity, it is even more difficult to
calculate the game tree complexity.
We can however calculate an estimate if we
know the average branching factor and the average length of a game, i.e. the average
number of plies1 until a game ends in a game over state. The game tree complexity
can then be determined by calculating:
C = bavg
davg ∗cavg
davg
(2.7)
Where bavg is the average branching factor for player nodes (nodes where the
player is about to move), cavg is the average branching factor for chance nodes (nodes
where the computer is about to place a random tile) and davg is the average depth of
the tree (length of the game in plies). These values can be determined by simulation
but will obviously be different for players with different skills. A player that always
achieve the 2048-tile will have a much larger average tree depth than a player that
never achieves it. To get an idea about the size of this tree, 100 games were simulated
using a basic Minimax player of depth 3 (please see section 4.1.1 for information about
Minimax). These simulations resulted in the following values:
bavg = 3.59
cavg = 11.22
davg = 1333.24

This extremely high game tree complexity is purely due to the fact that 2048
games are very long compared to other types of games. For instance, a game of chess
usually lasts for around 80 plies and a game of Backgammon lasts for about 50-60 plies
[1]. However, the state space complexity is significantly larger for chess suggesting
that chess is a more complex game. Indeed if we think about how a game tree for
2048 would look like, there would be a large amount of duplicate states throughout
the tree which would not be the case for a chess game tree, at least not to the same
extent. In addition, each time we combine all the tiles on the board into one large tile
in 2048, the state of the game looks very similar to the initial state of the game. Thus,
the length of the game does not necessarily have anything to do with the complexity
of the game. Another way to look at this is to consider how long thinking time is
acceptable for making a move in a game. Chess players usually take minutes to think
about their next move, indeed most FIDE (World Chess Federation) events use a
time control of 90 minutes for the first 40 moves and 30 minutes for the rest of the
game [14], giving an average time limit of 1.5 minutes per move for a game of 80 plies.
Chess games with no time control probably have a higher average thinking time per
move. For 2048 on the other hand, it would be very odd to think more than a few
seconds about a move. If a player took 2 minutes to think about each move in 2048,
it would take 34 hours just to reach the 2048 tile. Also, since the branching factor is a
lot smaller in 2048 than it is in chess3, and since the exact position of the new random
tile may not have the greatest influence of the move chosen by the player, spending
these 2 minutes would allow the player to look very deep into the game tree, which
is not really natural for a human to do. In chess on the other hand, two minutes can
easily be spent trying to enumerate all the possible moves and subsequently all the
opponents possible moves only a few plies down in the game.

2.2.1
Gameplay
The gameplay is very similar to that of 2048 in the way that it is based on sliding
numbers (which in Threes are numbered cards) around a 4x4 board to combine higher
numbers. The numbers in Threes are, as the name suggests, multiples of 3. Just as
in 2048, the new number created from combining two cards labelled with the number
n will be 2∗n. However, one of the main differences between 2048 and Threes is that
in order to create a 3-card, a 1-card and a 2-card must be merged together, and 1’s
can only be merged with 2’s and vice versa. Also, sliding in a particular direction
will move each card only by one row or column and cards will only combine if they
are up against a wall or a card of a higher or lower number. The biggest difference is
the way new cards are added to the board by the computer, which in 2048 is purely
random. The new card always appears from the direction of the last swipe and only
in rows/columns where cards have been moved or merged as a result of the swipe.
For example, if the player swipes down and only the cards in column 1 and 2 have
moved by this swipe, the new card will appear in the top row either in column 1 or
2. In addition, the value of the next card is always shown to the player. This makes
Threes a lot more deterministic than 2048.

on the game forum toucharcade.com have been trying to analyse the frequency of the
values of new cards in order to determine how the algorithm works. According to
the user kamikaze28, new cards are based on two decks [32]. One deck consists of 12
cards: 4 1’s, 4 2’s and 4 3’s, and this is the main deck the computer uses to draw
cards from. When all 12 cards have been drawn a new deck is created and shuffled.
The other deck consists of bonus cards, and this deck only comes to play once the
highest card on the board has reached 48. The values of the cards in this deck ranges
from 6 being the lowest to the highest card on the board, divided by 8. So if the
highest card is 192, the bonus cards can take on the values 6, 12 and 24. Now, once
the bonus deck comes into play, the chance of the next card being from the bonus
deck and not from the main deck is 1/21. The bonus deck contains only 1 of each
possible card and they all have equal chance of being drawn.

CHAPTER 3
Heuristics
A game can be described as a traditional search problem, containing an initial position
(the initial game configuration), a set of operators (defining the legal moves a player
can make), a terminal test (defining when the game is over) and a heuristic evalua-
tion function (assigning a value for the outcome of the game). As we have already
seen, searching the complete game tree is impossible for most games. The heuristic
evaluation function is therefore used as an estimation of ”how good” a game state is.
It takes as input the game state and returns a score ranking the state. The heuristic
evaluation function is used by search algorithms as a way of comparing states, with
the aim of guiding the search in the most promising direction of a solution.
Designing a good heuristic function requires great domain knowledge, however
sometimes a simple heuristic function can do well. In this thesis both simple and
more complex heuristics are considered along with the combination of the different
heuristics. This chapter will provide an overview and analysis of the different heuris-
tics designed or commonly used for the games and the performance of the heuristics
described is compared individually at the end of the chapter.
In the following sections we will use the following notations: B is a n × n matrix
encoding the n × n board configuration of a game state, where n is the size of the
board, and Bi,j denotes the value of the tile in column i, row j.
3.1

In this section we will describe three very simple heuristics that almost require no
knowledge of the games to use.

game state at all times, and calculating the points score a few plies deeper from the

The heuristic functions described in this section are based on careful analysis of

3.2.1
Monotonicity
This heuristic attempts to measure how monotonic the board configuration of the
state is. This means that it assigns higher values to states where the tiles are increas-

high monotonic score. It does so by assigning a score to each direction and then adds
the maximum of the up/down direction score with the maximum of the left/right
direction score. More formally, the monotonicity score is calculated as follows:
monotonicity(B) = max(up, down) + max(left, right)
(3.4)
Where:
up = ∑(log2(Bi,j) −log2(Bi,j+1)) for all Bi,j < Bi,j+1, Bi,j > 0, Bi,j+1 > 0

left = ∑(log2(Bi+1,j) −log2(Bi,j)) for all Bi,j > Bi+1,j, Bi,j > 0, Bi+1,j > 0
right = ∑(log2(Bi,j) −log2(Bi+1,j)) for all Bi,j < Bi+1,j, Bi,j > 0, Bi+1,j > 0

3.3
Combining heuristics
When dealing with complex problems that has several relevant heuristic functions,
such as 2048 and games in general, it is very common to combine the heuristics by
summing them to take advantage of the different strengths of each heuristic. Since
some heuristics might be more important than others, it is also very common to create
a linear combination of the heuristics and associate each of them with some weight:

(3.9)
Where n is the number of different heuristics (or features), Hi is the heuristic value
of heuristic i and Wi is the associated weight for heuristic i.
Choosing values for the weights is a very difficult task and tuning these values can
sometimes have a drastic effect of the overall performance of the evaluation function.
Tuning of weights is often done manually but can be a very tedious job and always
include a high risk of overlooking the optimal solution. A lot of research have gone into
automatic tuning of weights. Most of this research focuses on reinforcement learning
techniques where moves are trained based on information about the outcome of the
game. The problem about this technique is that the outcome of a game might be
bad, but some of the moves good, or vice versa. However, reinforcement techniques
have been used successfully in the area of evaluation function weight tuning. The
most successful example is probably TD-Gammon, a Backgammon program that has
tuned its evaluation function using a neural network by playing millions of games
against itself, result in expert playing strength [29].
Other approaches takes advantage of expert knowledge. An example of this is the
chess-playing computer DEEP BLUE, developed by IBM, where a small subset of

feature weights (DEEP BLUE uses several thousand features in total) were trained
using existing move choices of master players in a neural network [15].

Expectimax
In this chapter we will present a very well-known artificial intelligence algorithm for
solving games, namely the Minimax algorithm and its non-deterministic variant Ex-
pectimax. We will also present various methods for optimizing these algorithms along
with the experimental results obtained by applying all these optimization methods.
4.1
Background
In order to understand how the Expectimax algorithm operates, it is important to
understand the more classical algorithm Minimax which also forms the basis of Ex-
pectimax.
4.1.1
Minimax
Minimax is a search algorithm that can be applied to two-player games to find the
optimal decision for a player MAX (opponent player is called MIN). It does so by
recursively searching the game tree, starting from the root state and usually continues
until a certain predefined depth has been reached, as it is impossible to search the
complete game tree for most games. When this predefined depth has been reached,
the heuristic values of the leaf nodes are backed up through the tree, where the
highest value of the child nodes is always chosen at MAX nodes and the lowest is
always chosen at MIN nodes.
Minimax assumes that the opponent player MIN always plays optimally, i.e. that
MIN always chooses the action that minimises MAX’s final outcome. For a more
detailed description of the Minimax algorithm, we refer to [25].
4.1.2
Alpha-Beta pruning
Minimax is a full-width tree search with the complexity O(bd) for a fixed depth d and
a branching factor b and it is therefore very computationally expensive, especially for
large branching factors. A well-known method to optimize a Minimax search and to
avoid searching every node in the tree is called Alpha-Beta pruning.
Alpha-Beta pruning eliminates branches of the search tree by realizing when re-
maining child nodes cannot alter the value of the root node, no matter what values
==PAGE:37==
4.1 Background
30
the child nodes have. It does so by maintaining a search window with a lower bound
of α and an upper bound of β. If a value of a node falls outside this window, we
can cut off the search, as there is no reason to search the rest of the successors. The
simplest way to understand how this works is to go through a small example.
Consider the search tree in figure 4.1. Starting at the root node, the search will
do a depth-first search until a predefined depth (in this case 3), starting from the left.
When the search reaches the MIN node with the value 8, it can cut off the remaining
successors. This is because the parent node is a MAX node and it will never choose a
value lower than 8, so the minimum value of the MAX node will be 8, but the parent
of this MAX node is a MIN node and already has an option to choose a node with
a smaller value, namely the MAX node to the left with the value 4. Continuing the
search on the left branch of the root node, we can cut of the search already after
visiting just one of the MAX nodes at depth 2. This MAX node will choose value 2,
as this is the largest child node. Now the parent MIN node will never choose a node
with a higher value, so this MIN node will be at most 2. Since the root node is a
MAX node and already has another option with a higher value (4 in this case) from
the left-most branch, there is no reason to continue the search.

as Minimax. In the best case where the moves are assumed ordered such that the
best node is always searched first, Alpha-beta search will evaluate O(bd/2) leaf nodes
[19]. Please see [25] for more details.

4.3.1
Star1
Star1 was proposed by Bruce Ballard [4] and is a technique that builds on Alpha-Beta
pruning to include pruning of chance nodes.
We have three different types of nodes in Expectimax: MIN-, MAX- and chance-
nodes, so we need to consider three different cases for cutoffs. As Expectimax works
in the exact same way as Minimax for MIN- and MAX-nodes, we can reuse Alpha-
Beta pruning at these nodes. Alpha and beta values are then passed from MIN- and
MAX-nodes to chance nodes, however at chance nodes we cannot use the alpha-beta
window to prune nodes as we do not know if an Expectimax value falls outside the
window until we have searched all the children.
Star1 takes advantage of knowing the bounds of the range of values the evaluation
function can take to derive bounds for the value a chance node can take based on
the best and worst case scenarios. This is done in the following way: Imagine the
algorithm has reached the i’th successor of a chance node. Then in the best case
all the i-1 successors the algorithm has searched so far have the value U (the upper
bound of the evaluation function), and in the worst case all the i-1 successors have
the value L (the lower bound of the evaluation function). The lower bound for the
i’th successor will then be:
BL = 1

(4.3)

4.3.2
Forward pruning
Pruning techniques such as Alpha-Beta and Star1 can be seen as backward pruning
techniques, as the cuts we make are based on previous searches. Backward pruning
also guarantees that we do not cut away important branches of the search tree, i.e.
the search will provide us with the same result as it would have without pruning.
Forward pruning on the other hand cuts branches without previous evaluation. This
means that there is some risk involved using forward pruning, as we could potentially
overlook an important branch, which would influence the final result of the algorithm
negatively. For forward pruning to be effective, specific knowledge about the game is
necessary.
In the case of 2048 we will use the fact that 4-tiles are only generated with a
probability of 10%.
This means that the probability that two 4-tiles will spawn
consecutively is only 1%, thus very unlikely, although still possible. Keeping track
of what type of tile was last spawned and parsing this information along recursively
when we search, allow us to cut of branches whenever we have two consecutive 4-tile
spawns.
4.3.3
Parallelization
Another way to optimize an Expectimax search is to parallelize the search tree. There
are several ways to do this, however in this thesis only one parallelization technique
will be considered, namely parallelization of subtrees.
This is probably the most
obvious way to parallelize a search tree; simply run each subtree search on a different
thread (see figure 4.4).
The drawback of this method is that it does not take unbalanced trees into con-
sideration, which basically means that one process might end up doing a lot more
work than the other processes, and the total execution time is obviously dependent
on the slowest process. However, this is not such a big issue for the game trees we are
dealing with in this project, as the subtrees will be more or less of equal length the
vast majority of the time. Only when one subtree contains a game over node that the
others do not, it will be shorter. Also, the branching factor is more or less the same
for different subtrees considering the same phase of the game. If we are searching
in the beginning of the game, the branching factor will be high for all the subtrees,
and if we are close to a game over node, all the subtrees will have a lower branching
factor.
There is however another challenge when parallelization is used in combination
with pruning techniques like Alpha-Beta or Star1, namely sharing and synchronization
of alpha- and beta-values between processes. As only parallelization of subtrees will be
considered in this thesis we will not be concerned with sharing values across processes.
This means that each child of the root will run as a complete separate search tree and
pruning windows will be kept separate for each process. Of course this also means
the we might loose potential big subtree cuts, where an entire subtree is cut off from
the root node.

4.3.4
Iterative deepening
Iterative Deepening is a technique that runs a depth-limited search (such as Minimax
or Expectimax) iteratively starting at depth 1 and iteratively increases the depth
until either a depth or a time limit has been reached. In the context of this thesis, we
will limit the search by time, as a way of comparing different techniques by allowing
a certain amount of computation time to decide on each move. In practice this is im-
plemented by starting a timer when the algorithm is first called, and then recursively
passing down this timer along with the depth and the alpha- and beta-values. If the
timer reaches the time limit before a full recursion has completed, we return the last
full-recursion result, e.g. if the limit is reached during a search at depth 4 we use the
result we got from searching depth 3.
Although it might seem like iterative deepening is a waste of time, since it searches
the same depths and states multiple times, this is not the case.
First of all, the
majority of the search time is spent on the nodes at the bottom level and the amount of
time used searching the higher levels is very small compared the time spent searching
the bottom level. Secondly, we can reuse the results of earlier search depths so we do
not have to actually execute these searches again. Looking up the results of previous
searches instead of spending time searching allow us to spend this time exploring new
states instead and potentially achieve better results. This idea is implemented by the
use of a transposition table.
A transposition table is a database that stores results of previous searches. In
==PAGE:45==
4.3 Improvements
38
practice this is done by maintaining a hashtable where the keys correspond to game
states and the values correspond to information about previous results. The infor-
mation stored for each state is the heuristic value of the state, the move that has
previously been decided to be the best and the depth at which this information was
stored. The reason for storing the depth as well is so that we know when to update
the entry with new and better results (for higher depths), and when to use what
we already know. The keys need to be unique to every possible game state and in
order to generate these keys, we use something called Zobrist hashing [40]. Zobrist
hashing is a popular method for transforming board positions into unique numbers.
It basically works by creating a table of random numbers at initialization time, one
number for each possible combination of position and value. In our case, this table
will be of size 4 ∗4 ∗17 = 272, since we are dealing with a 4 × 4 board and there are
17 possible values for the tiles. We use 64-bits for each random number in order to
minimize the risk of hash collisions. To calculate the hash of a certain board configu-
ration, we break the board configuration up into cells, and each cell is then mapped
into a random value from the initialization table. All these random numbers are then
XORed together to create the final Zobrist hash key.
Using a transposition table in an iterative deepening framework not only allows
us to efficiently look up earlier results to avoid redoing work and thereby be able to
explore more states and potentially get better results, it also allows us to improve the
search using previous results to order what moves we try first. Good move ordering
is very important for effective pruning, as discussed in section 4.1.2, and this makes
iterative deepening with transposition tables and move ordering a good candidate for
improving the Expectimax procedure.
4.3.5
Genetic Algorithm for tuning of feature weights
Genetic algorithms are a special type of evolutionary algorithms, which uses Dar-
win’s evolutionary theory to generate solutions for optimization problems. The basic
idea behind genetic algorithms is to start with some population containing a set of
solutions (called chromosomes) to the problem in question. This population can be
randomly generated, or it can be a set of solutions already known to be good solu-
tions. In each iteration of the algorithm, a new population is generated from the
old population by mutation and crossover of the old chromosomes. The decision of
which chromosomes are selected to produce offspring for the next generation is based
on their fitness. The fitness is a measure of the correctness of the solution, and the
higher the fitness, the higher the probability that this chromosome will be selected.
This process continues until some condition is satisfied, for example when the correct-
ness of the solution is above some threshold, the amount of improvement of the best
solution in the populations goes below some threshold or simply when a maximum
number of generations has been reached. In short, the four main steps of a genetic
algorithm are:
1. Initialization: Generating the initial population
==PAGE:46==
4.3 Improvements
39
2. Selection: Selecting the chromosomes to breed the new generation
3. Generating offspring for next generation: Based on mutation and crossover
4. Termination: Based on a terminal condition
The description of genetic algorithms just provided is a very general one. The
two main building blocks we need to define in order to use a genetic algorithm for
a specific optimization problem are how to represent the chromosomes and how to
define the fitness function. In the context of this work, where we will use a genetic
algorithm for weight optimization, we will define a chromosome as a set of weights,
where each weight is associated with a specific heuristic.
We will be using the 8
heuristics presented in chapter 3 and order these alphabetically, so a chromosome
will be defined as follows:
c = [wcorner, wempty, whighest, wmonotonicity, wpoints, wsmoothness, wsnake, wtrapped]
As a chromosome is a set of genes, each weight will represent a gene in the chro-
mosome. The fitness function needs to provide a good measurement of how good
the weights are, and rather than basing this on a single game (as is normally done
in reinforcement learning techniques) we will instead run a number of games using
an Expectimax search along with an evaluation function that uses the weights of the
chromosome to be measured. This obviously has the drawback of extending the cal-
culation time of the fitness function, but avoids the problem of overlooking a good
solution because of bad luck in the one game used to evaluate the solution. More
specifically, the fitness function will run 20 games using an Expectimax search of
depth 2 and the final fitness value will be the average score of those 20 games. To
speed things up, the 20 games are run in parallel using 5 threads.
There are many different strategies for the selection part of the algorithm, e.g.
tournament selection, rank selection and roulette wheel selection [24]. In this work,
we chose to implement roulette wheel selection as it is one of the more popular choices
and has proven to perform well.
In roulette wheel selection, each chromosome is
given a probability that is directly proportional to its fitness (see figure 4.5), and two
chromosomes are then chosen randomly based on these probabilities. In figure 4.5,
chromosome 1 has a higher fitness than chromosome 2, which has a higher fitness than
chromosome 3 and so on. The selection process corresponds to throwing a marble
onto the roulette wheel and selecting the chromosome the marble lands on. We also
make use of elitism, which ensures that the best solution is not lost when creating
new populations, and this is done simply by copying a few of the best chromosomes
unchanged to the new population.
In this work, 5 of the best chromosomes are
carried on unchanged to the next population. To enhance the reliability of the fitness
of a chromosome, i.e. the average score, this is also carried on unchanged with the
chromosome to the next population. When the new population is then evaluated the
new fitness is combined with the old for each of the elite chromosomes. This should

ensure that the precision and reliability of the fitness of the elite chromosomes is
enhanced each time they are carried over to a new population.

The operators of the genetic algorithm, crossover and mutation, can be imple-
mented in very different ways as well, and they are also very dependent on the rep-
resentation of chromosomes. Crossover selects genes in the parent chromosomes and
creates child chromosomes based on the selected genes. The most simple way to do
this (and most successful according to results in [22]) is to randomly choose a point
in the chromosome, and then copy everything before this point from the first parent
into the child, and everything after this point from the second parent into the child,
and then do the opposite with the second child. This way of doing crossover is called
single-point crossover and an example of this concept is shown in figure 4.6. Other
ways include two-point crossover and flat crossover (please see [22]).
After new chromosomes have been generated by crossover, we introduce a bit of
randomness, known as mutation (also illustrated in figure 4.6). Mutation is an essen-
tial part of genetic algorithms as it ensures genetic diversity within the population.
Without mutation, every combination of genes we can make would be in the initial
population, and the risk of getting stuck in a local optimum2 would be high.
The mutation in the context of this work is done by choosing a weight in the
chromosome by random, and changing the value of the weight by a random amount.
The random amount will be between the negative and the positive of the old value
of the weight.

population has either decreased or remained the same for the last 10 generations.

of generations with the size of the populations also often in the order of thousands.

CHAPTER 5
Monte Carlo Tree
Search
Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) [8] is an AI technique that has been used in various
areas, although most typically for making optimal decisions in planning problems and
combinatorial games. The main difference of MCTS to the other methods explored in
this thesis is that MCTS does not require an evaluation function to guide the search.
Instead it uses the results of previous explorations to grow its game tree by keeping
statistics of each explored node. This is also the reason that MCTS can be applied
to games with no known strategy, i.e. only the main rules of the game are known.
The main operation of MCTS is based on four steps:
1. Selection: Starting at the root node, the best child is selected for expansion,
continuing descending through the tree until a node with unexplored children
has been reached. Which child is considered the best is decided using a tree
policy.
2. Expansion: The tree is expanded by adding one or more unexplored children
to the tree.
3. Simulation: From one of the child nodes added in the previous step, a playout
is simulated until the game ends. How to make moves in this playout is decided
by what is commonly called a default policy. It is very common to use random
moves for the default policy, especially for problems with no known strategy.
4. Backpropagation: The result of the playout (win or lose, amount of points,
etc.)
is backpropagated through the selected nodes all the way to the root,
while the statistics of the nodes are updated with the new result.
The decision of which child to expand next in the selection step mentioned above
can be treated as a multi-armed bandit problem [2]. The multi-armed bandit prob-
lem is a known probability problem in which a gambler has to decide on which slot
machines to play in order to maximize the payouts after a sequence of plays. These
type of problems lead to a dilemma between exploration (the exploration of unknown
areas) and exploitation (exploiting the areas we already know to have good rewards).
There are different approaches that attempt to overcome this problem, including
==PAGE:57==

greedy strategies and probability matching strategies. A more advanced method is
to know the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) on the rewards of the slot machines.
A simple UCB policy was suggested by Auer et al [2] and balances exploitation and
exploration by choosing the arm that maximises:

The first term of equation 5.2 ensures exploitation as this term will increase for
nodes with higher reward statistics and decrease for nodes with lower rewards. The
second term of the equation ensures exploration, since this term will increase for lesser
visited child nodes. Note especially that if a child node has not been visited before
the second term will be a division with zero, which will evaluate to positive infinity
1, and it thus ensures that every child node will be visited at least once.
It is very common to use the number of wins as the reward function Q(vj), so the
first part of the above equation simply becomes the fraction of wins out of the total
number of simulations. However, in the context of 2048 a win would be considered a
game-state in which the tile 2048 is present, and since this never happens using pure
random play, at least not starting from an initial state in the game (see the results in
section 7.2), it is not feasible to use the win as the reward function here. Also, using
a state with the 2048-tile present as the winning state would limit the search to only
try to achieve the 2048-tile and not to try and achieve something bigger. Therefore
we will instead use the total amount of points as the reward function Q(vj), just as
we did in the example discussed previously. The first term of equation 5.2 will thus
be the average amount of points achieved from playouts including this node.

a bit more tricky when the range of values of the reward function is not between 0
and 1 (win or lose). It is easy to see why when we take a closer look at equation 5.2:
In order for the exploration-term of the equation to have a say in the result, Cp has
to be somewhat close to the values of the exploitation-term (i.e. if it is too small the
results will be exploited too early and if it is larger it will result in more exploration of
low-reward children). Since the average amount of total points from the simulations

Cp accordingly during the game. Using the amount of points of the parent node as
the value of Cp ensures that it will increase in parallel to the value of the average
reward of simulations run from the parent. Section 5.3.1 shows the result of different

5.2
Parallelization of MCTS
An obvious way to improve the number of iterations of the algorithm that can be
run in a given time frame, and thus improve the performance of the Monte Carlo
Tree Search, is to parallelize the search tree. There are many different ways to do
this in practice and [10] compares three different ways, namely leaf parallelization,
root parallelization and tree parallelization. In this work we will only consider root
parallelization, which was shown to be the best one of those three in [10].
Using the root parallelization method, not only a single MCTS tree is generated,
but one tree is generated for each thread. This can be thought of as starting a MCTS
search from the same root node n times, and then combining the results of each search
once they are all done. Then n is the number of threads and each search is started on
a different thread, so that they all run in parallel. In order to avoid synchronization
problems each time a thread reaches the backpropagation step and has to update
statistics of the root node, we create n copies of the root node and run a MCTS
search on each copy. Once all threads have finished, the children of each of these root
node copies will have different statistics attached to them. We then go through each
root node copy and combine the statistics of the children to find the child node with
the best statistics.

Combining classical

In the previous chapters we have discussed two fundamentally different artificial in-
telligence techniques intended to find the best solution to a complex problem, namely
classical heuristic search methods (Minimax and Expectimax) and a learning method
(Monte Carlo Tree Search). Though Minimax and Expectimax can be excellent when
provided with a good evaluation function, they require very good domain knowledge
in order to design such a function, which is not always available. Monte Carlo Tree
Search, on the other hand, does not require any domain knowledge, as it learns from
experiences which moves will be the best.
In this chapter we present several ways of combining the strength of learning with
domain knowledge and classical heuristic search. At the end of the chapter we present
the results of applying these methods to 2048.
6.1
MCTS with domain knowledge
What separates Monte Carlo Tree Search and other learning techniques the most from
classical heuristic search methods, is their ability to learn how to play a game well
without any prior knowledge about the game. It should therefore be a valid theory
that adding domain specific knowledge to Monte Carlo Tree Search could improve
the overall performance.
Monte Carlo Tree Search is still a rather new method in artificial intelligence and
a lot of research is going on in the area, including research of how to exploit domain
knowledge in areas where this knowledge already exist [7]. The methods proposed
until now can be divided into two main categories: Adding domain knowledge to the
tree policy and adding domain knowledge to the default policy.
==PAGE:66==
6.1 MCTS with domain knowledge
59
6.1.1
Adding domain knowledge to the tree policy
A technique known as progressive bias adds domain knowledge to the tree policy. It
does so by the use of an evaluation function in the selection formula. In this work
we use the UCT as the selection formula, as explained in section 5.1. The modified
UCT formula now becomes:

2ln(n)

(6.1)
The added function f(vj) contains heuristic knowledge about the child state vj

(6.2)
Where h(vj) is the heuristic value of the child state vj and N(vj) is as usual
the number of times this child node has been visited. Dividing the heuristic value
by the number of visits ensures that the heuristic knowledge has less influence than
the statistics (which is the core strength of MCTS) when there is a good amount
of statistical information about the node, and a high influence when the amount of
statistical information is sparse.
In theory, progressive bias should help the search in situations where the statistics
for a node is not reliable and direct the search in a promising direction according to
the evaluation function.
6.1.2
Adding domain knowledge to the default policy
In a classic Monte Carlo Tree Search, the default policy is to simulate a game using
completely random moves. As these simulations are very far from realistic, this policy
might not be the most optimal one. The simulations can be thought of as acquiring
knowledge about how good a move is. The more simulations we run from children
and grand children generated by a candidate move, the more we can be sure about
how good this move is, as these simulations tell us something about how the outcome
of our game could be, starting with this move. However, as the statistics we use to
decide on this are based on completely random games, they may not be very reliable,
even if we run many simulations. There is however another way we can evaluate the
move. If we instead of running a simulation at the simulation step run an Expectimax
search to a fixed depth and use the heuristic score returned by the search instead of
the end score of a simulated game, the overall MCTS algorithm will remain the same,
but we may get more reliable statistics.

6.2
Other methods
Among the methods being researched regarding Monte Carlo Tree Search enhance-
ments, many of them focus on the default policy and how to improve this roll-out
phase to be more realistic and less random. One obvious way that springs to mind is
to conduct fixed-depth Minimax- or Expectimax searches to decide on moves during
the simulations. In [3] the performance of MCTS using fixed-depth Minimax searches
in the roll-out phase, which they call MCTS-MR (Monte Carlo Tree Search with Min-
imax Rollouts), is tested against the games Connect-4 and Breakthrough. The results
for Connect-4 are promising while those for Breakthrough are quite weak, and they
suggest these results are due to differences between search spaces. We tested a similar
method against 2048 using a fixed-depth Expectimax search instead of a Minimax
search in the rollout phase. This attempt to improve Monte Carlo Tree Search for
2048 however proved to be a complete failure, as it resulted in only very few iterations
of the algorithm, even when given plenty of thinking time. Results of test runs using
this approach are shown in Appendix A. The reason for this approach failing to work
for 2048, can be found in the length of 2048 games. As we showed in section 2.1.2.2,
the average game length of 2048 is 1333 plies (for a player reaching 2048 only 60%
of the time). This means that in a single simulation, we will be doing between 600
and 700 Expectimax searches! Comparing this to Connect-4 in which this method
was successful, the average game length is only 36 plies.

Both figure 7.1 and 7.3 clearly shows the superiority of Expectimax compared to
Minimax. There is no doubt that when it comes to games involving some kind of
chance, Expectimax is the way to go. When Minimax is applied to 2048 and Threes
(or other single-player games) the computer is treated as the opponent player, and
Minimax assumes optimal play from both players. In the context of 2048 and Threes,
this means that Minimax assumes that the computer will always place the tile at the
worst possible place for the player. This is definitely not the case as the position of
the new tile is determined by random. However, when optimal play is not the case,
using Minimax should simply guard against the worst possible outcome. The results
in figure 7.1 and 7.3 suggest that guarding against the worst possible positions of the
new tiles is not the best strategy and that basing the player’s moves on averaging

the outcomes of chance nodes (which is the essence of Expectimax) is a much more
promising strategy.
Between 2048 and Threes, it appears that 2048 is a more complex game to design
a successful game-playing agent for. As 2048 has been the main focus of this work, we
have not considered Threes-specific heuristics or in any way worked on optimizing the
search techniques for Threes. Considering that the same heuristics and techniques
have been more or less ported from 2048 to Threes, only with the addition of keeping
track of the card-deck (see section 2.2.1), the results obtained for Threes are impres-
sive, especially those using Expectimax. Searching for solutions in Threes is generally
less complex since we can take advantage of always knowing what the next card is
going to be. and since we can keep track of which cards are in the card-deck and thus
which cards can come up next. The number of possible positions for the next cards is
also less than it is for 2048, which makes the algorithms able to search deeper given
the same amount of time. Note also how much better Expectimax is than Minimax
for 2048, where the difference is not as significant for Threes. Though the points
systems are very different in these two games, it is still worth pointing out that Ex-
pectimax more than doubles the average point score compared to Minimax for 2048,
but not for Threes. This could be due to the less amount of randomness in Threes
when we take advantage of the fact that we can count cards. Keeping track of the
card-deck and the possible positions of the new cards is one aspect of Threes that is
very difficult for a human to incorporate, but very easy for a computer game-playing
agent to manage.

Monte Carlo Tree Search-based agents
Figure 7.4 and 7.5 shows how pure Monte Carlo Tree search performs compared to
Minimax and Expectimax for 2048 and Threes, respectively. In both figures, the
techniques were all given 100 ms of thinking time per move.
In these tests the
same optimizations as in the previous section are used for Minimax and Expectimax
and Monte Carlo Tree Search have been optimized by parallelizing the search using 8
threads. What stands out the most in these figures is that MCTS is very clearly better
performing in 2048 than Minimax, but the exact opposite holds for Threes, where
MCTS fails to reach the 768-card even once. We have already seen differences between
the two games in terms of how good Minimax performs compared to Expectimax. The
fact that MCTS is so bad in Threes could very well have something to do with the
bonus card deck in Threes that does not exist in 2048. These bonus cards can appear
at all times and have high values, but they only appear with a probability of 1/21. It
is easy to image a situation where one of the random playouts of MCTS experiences
a high bonus card, or maybe even several, and this situation leads it to game over
at an early stage, where other playouts may not experience bonus card spawns once.
This obviously makes the statistics of the random playouts even more unreliable than
they are for 2048, which could be the reason that the performance of MCTS applied
to Threes suffers as much as we see in figure 7.5.

8.1
Academic work
Although 2048 has proved to be a popular subject for artificial intelligence research
on the internet, published research about the game is limited. The research team
from Poznan University of Technology in Poland, consisting of Marcin Szubert and
Wojciech Jaskowski, seems to be one of the only research teams who have investigated
in artificial intelligence techniques for the game.
Their approach is however very
interesting and different to what people on the internet have done. In their research
paper Temporal Difference Learning of N-Tuple Networks for the Game 2048 [28] they
describe three different approaches of using temporal difference learning to evaluate
player moves, where they use a n-tuple network for their evaluation function. This
n-tuple network calculates the sum of the values returned by each of the n-tuples (n
is the size of the tuple), where each tuple consists of a sequence of board-positions
and a look-up table containing weights for the board-positions. For example, they
use 4-tuples positioned horizontally and vertically on the board, where a horizontal 4-
tuple would return a value for a row on the board. The three approaches they analyse
differ in how the evaluation is implemented. In particular they describe an action-
evaluation where the player action is evaluated, a state-evaluation where the state
is evaluated and an after-state-evaluation, which is a combination of the action- and
state-evaluation that evaluates the state resulting from executing an action. Learning
is then performed after each move and the weights in the look-up tables are adjusted.
Please refer to [28] for more details. Marcin Szubert and Wojciech Jaskowski report
==PAGE:81==

in their paper, that their best-performing AI agent is based on 350.000 training games
and achieves a winning percentage of 98% with an average score of 100.178 points.
On December 21th 2015 they reported on stackoverflow.com [37] that they have now

In [23], Todd Neller describes a variety of exercises and projects using 2048 to

CHAPTER 9
Future Work
This chapter is intended to point out areas in which this work could be taken further
if time has permitted.
First of all, it would be interesting to see what the genetic algorithm could come
up with if it was given more optimal conditions, i.e. a more precise fitness evaluation
in terms of deeper Expectimax searches and more runs for each chromosome, a larger
population and a less strict termination condition. It could also be interesting to see
if the Snake heuristic could be optimized to produce even better results, if the genetic
algorithm was used to optimize the values in the weight matrices.
Secondly, the results we did get from the genetic algorithm, not to mention the
results obtained by Marcin Szubert and Wojciech Jaskowski in [28], show that some
sort of learning of the evaluation function can be very successful in order to opti-
mize the already well-performing Expectimax approach significantly. Based on our
observations of MCTS, it could also be interesting to somehow use MCTS to learn
an evaluation function. Since its strategy approaches well-known strategies used by
human players when given enough time per move, it is reasonable to imagine that it
could be used to learn a good strategy. How this could be done in practical terms is
not entirely clear, as using MCTS for this purpose have not been seen before.
We have analysed different ways of exploiting available domain knowledge to im-
prove on MCTS and it seems logical to assume that improving the default policy to
make the simulations more realistic could have a positive effect. We have shown that
it is not possible to use Expectimax to decide on each move in a simulation due to
the lengths of the games, however we have not tried to replace the simulations with
simulations of a fixed length. Say, we instead of letting simulations run until game
over, we let them run for 50 moves. Then it should be possible to use Expectimax
to decide on the moves, making the simulations realistic and maybe improve on the
overall statistics and thus on the overall success of MCTS.
Finally, more work should be focused on developing Threes-specific heuristics and
optimizing the techniques for Threes.
Our application of the techniques we have
developed for 2048, to Threes, has shown to do surprisingly well, and this could
indicate that a perfect AI agent for Threes is not far away.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
In this project, three different artificial intelligence approaches have been presented
and applied to the games 2048 and Threes: The well-known heuristic search algorithm
Minimax, its non-deterministic variant Expectimax that is often used for games in-
volving chance, and the newer technique Monte Carlo Tree Search that bases its
decision on the statistics of random game simulations. The complexities of 2048 and
Threes have been calculated and analysed in order to understand the challenges faced
by the applied techniques.
We have discussed several optimization techniques for
each approach, including pruning techniques for Minimax and Expectimax, iterative
deepening with transposition tables, and parallelization of search trees, and we have
described two ways of combining heuristic-based tree search with simulation-based
tree search. A new approach for optimizing evaluation functions was also presented,
that uses a genetic algorithm to tune the weights used in a linear combination of dif-
ferent heuristics forming the evaluation function. In connection with the analysis of
the heuristic search-based algorithms, a thorough study of various heuristic functions
for 2048 was conducted. Finally, a prototype of the game was presented, in which the
three artificial intelligence techniques investigated in this work are used to provide
the user with helpful tips while playing the game.
The results presented in this thesis have shown that the performance of Expecti-
max is superior to Minimax and Monte Carlo Tree Search when applied to number-
matching non-deterministic single player games, such as 2048 and Threes, where
domain-specific knowledge is available. We have also shown that the most promising
heuristic function for 2048 is what we call the ”Snake” heuristic, in which the strategy
is to arrange the tiles on the board in a snake-like pattern, and that the choice of
heuristic function is vital for both Minimax and Expectimax to succeed in the games.
Testing various pruning techniques for Minimax and Expectimax has proved how big
an effect Alpha-Beta pruning has on the number of explored nodes compared to a
very small improvement using Star1-pruning for Expectimax. This also showed that
improving the speed of Expectimax through pruning techniques is more challenging
than for Minimax, and that the known Expectimax-pruning methods does not work
very well when the game tree is not regular. The limitations of Expectimax-pruning
do however only make the results of its performance even more convincing compared
to the performance of Minimax, as the searches made by Expectimax are shallower
than the Minimax searches.
We have proved that it is possible to use a genetic algorithm for weight optimiza-

tion in an evaluation function, although the results obtained were affected by the
limited time left for the project. We have also shown that it is possible to improve
on the performance of Monte Carlo Tree Search by using available domain knowledge
both in the tree policy and the default policy, but that the game tree complexity of
2048 limits the ways the default policy can be improved.
Finally, we have showed how the AI agents designed in this project can be used in
a 2048 helper application with the purpose of helping and teaching the user to obtain
better results in the game.
