Abstract
Despite the widespread adoption of facial recognition technology in various applications,
significant challenges persist in ensuring fair and unbiased performance across different
age groups. These challenges are particularly pronounced in scenarios involving children,
who are often underrepresented in training data sets, leading to biased algorithms that
perform with higher accuracy on adults.
This thesis investigates the enhancement of demographic fairness, particularly concern-
ing children, in state-of-the-art face recognition models. It further seeks to align with
a responsible development framework following the novel regulations for biometric iden-
tification systems in the EU AI Act and ISO/IEC standards.
The research aims to
address these issues by the bias mitigation technique of fine-tuning pre-trained face
recognition models using synthetic images of children’s faces. This approach seeks to
mitigate bias while maintaining privacy. The thesis evaluates the fairness of these mod-
els using performance metrics, including the False Positive Identification Rate, False
Negative Identification Rate, and the demographic fairness metric False Negative Dif-
ferential. Performance is assessed across different age subgroups, categorising children
into age ranges from 1 to 15 years and comparing these groups with adults.
Key findings reveal significant disparities in the baseline performance of pre-trained face
recognition models, with adult faces being identified more accurately than children’s
faces. Fine-tuning with synthetic child data generally improved recognition for both
children and adults. However, the improvement in adult performance was significantly
greater than that for children, ultimately widening the gap in identification performance.
Consequently, this led to less fair face recognition models for children compared to the
baseline models.
Further analysis revealed that image quality significantly affects recognition performance
across demographic groups, emphasising the importance of controlling image quality to
confidently isolate and address biases. When controlling for image quality, FNIR was
observed to decrease with age, indicating that the older the children are, the better the
face recognition models identify them.
These insights contribute to enhancing fairness in face identification systems for chil-
dren, suggesting that while fine-tuning on synthetic data improves the overall recogni-
tion performance for children, it does not enhance the demographic fairness of the face
recognition system. To further the development of fair face recognition systems for iden-
tification, this thesis discusses alternative bias mitigation strategies, carefully balancing
the trade-off between privacy and performance. These strategies are evaluated within
the context of compliance with regulatory frameworks governing the field.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction and Motivation
Currently, face recognition systems are among the most accurate biometric identification
methods, thanks to advancements in deep learning technologies driven by the existing,
non-intrusive accumulation of digital images of faces [1]. Face recognition is the process
of identifying or verifying an individual’s identity by the characteristics distinguishing
their face. These characteristics comprise of both biological characteristics e.g. facial
traits, age, height, and skin-lightness, social constructs e.g. ethnicity and gender, and
factors affecting the individual’s temporary state e.g. emotional state and self-styling of
hair and make-up [2]. Face verification technology is widely implemented in smartphones
for user authentication in financial transactions and device unlocking. Face identification
technology is implemented in fields such as surveillance and public service for identifying
unknown individuals.
The widespread adoption of face recognition systems has integrated this technology into
the daily life of modern society. However, managing sensitive biometric information
remains a significant challenge due to privacy concerns, potential misuse, biased perfor-
mance, and security risks associated with data breaches. These challenges raise signif-
icant ethical concerns, particularly when face recognition is used without the subjects’
consent, such as in secret operations or with children, and in areas where the application
greatly impacts the subject’s life. These include areas such as the judicial, healthcare,
and financial industries where equal treatment of all subjects is crucial, and where the
performance discrepancies across different demographic groups, marked by biases based
on e.g. gender, age, and ethnicity, have been observed [3]. This potential variance in
system accuracy underscores the ethical implications of face recognition technology and
data collection process in society. The ethical concerns has led to the development of
both the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the regulatory framework
for artificial intelligence in Europe (The AI Act), as well as the global, non-governmental
ISO (International Organisation for Standardisation) and IEC (the International Elec-
trotechnical Commission) standards for Biometric Performance Testing and Reporting
(ISO/IEC 19795) [4].
Despite ongoing efforts to mitigate bias in face recognition systems, the challenge of
ensuring fairness, particularly for children, remains unsolved [5][6]. This is due to the
sensitivity and vulnerability surrounding the use of such technologies and the impor-
tance of protecting children’s rights during the collection and processing of their face
image data [7][8]. This has led to various public databases of faces being biased toward
adults by mainly containing adult face images, ultimately resulting in many face recog-
nition algorithms being biased to recognise adults better than children [6]. To address
this issue safely, recent research has explored alternative approaches of utilising real
labelled face images to train deep learning-based face recognition systems. Synthetic
data, proposed as an alternative to real data, has the potential to enhance the accuracy
of face recognition systems [9] and to evaluate them in a privacy-preserving manner [10].
==PAGE:11==
1.1 Problem
3
Enhanced accuracy of face recognition systems in critical applications, such as locating
missing children, is crucial due to the global scale of the issue — more than 250,000
children disappear annually in Europe alone, with 460,000 reported missing in the US
annually [11][12]. The use of face recognition in search and investigation of missing per-
sons has been documented to increase effectiveness, and decrease the time spent, before
the person is found [13]. This increased effectiveness has immense societal and lifesaving
impact, as European data, as well as FBI research from 1993 and 2006, and Canadian
data from 2016, all demonstrate that between 70% −76% of abducted children found
deceased had been murdered within the first 3 hours of their disappearance [14][15][16].
This short time frame highlights the race against time that investigators face when deal-
ing with missing child cases [15].
1.1
Problem
Face recognition systems are currently being used by law enforcement agencies [17][18]
and surveillance companies [19][20] on a large scale to locate adult offenders involved in
child traﬀicking and kidnapping. The technology has proven to speed up the search and
detection process [21], and increase the rate of which cases are solved when detectives
use artificial intelligence (AI) tools [22]. Regardless of the successful implementations,
there are fewer face recognition systems deployed to find child victims, partly due to de-
creased recognition of children compared to adults, as well as privacy concerns [23][24].
Beyond the search for missing children, face recognition technology is also crucial in
bioforensic applications, particularly in combating child exploitation. The technology is
used for detecting production, distribution, and possession of child sexual abuse mate-
rial, as well as in the identification of both victim and offender, aiding in the rescue of
victims. Hence, showcasing the potential that face recognition systems for recognising
children holds significant positive impact, despite the ethical and privacy concerns it
raises [25]. For instance, an initiative by the police in New Dehli, India, has conducted
trials using face recognition systems to successfully identify nearly 3,000 lost children in
just four days. However, they state that one of the major challenges in further scaling
this initiative is the need to ensure privacy and data security [26].
Creating a single, fair system for recognising individuals of all ages is crucial. Rather
than implementing separate systems for different demographic groups, one system is
needed to simplify deployment in crucial areas, as well as reducing costs associated
with hardware, software, training, and maintenance [27]. Moreover, a unified system
ensures consistent performance across diverse scenarios and promotes ethical principles
of equality and privacy, while driving technological innovation and facilitating regulatory
compliance [28].

1.2
Aim of the thesis
The aim of this thesis is to explore and enhance the fairness of face recognition systems
in identifying children, with a particular focus on mitigating demographic bias which
may affect performance across different age groups. This is approached through fine-
tuning of state-of-the-art face recognition models with synthetic generated image data
of children’s faces, aiming to address the dual challenges of maintaining privacy and
enhancing system fairness.
The demographic fairness will be evaluated through the metrics measuring demographic
bias in the algorithmic performance, specifically the False Positive Identification Rate
(FPIR), False Negative Identification Rate (FNIR), and False Negative Differential
(FND). The demographic fairness will be initially investigated in a binary setting, eval-
uating the fairness performance between children and adults. Subsequently, the thesis
will evaluate the fairness performance among different subgroups of children, categorised
into age ranges: 1-3, 4-6, 7-9, 10-12, and 13-15 years old. It is noted that 16 years is
the age under GDPR at which a child can provide their own consent [29]. Therefore,
the decision to use age 15 as the maximum age was made to align with this regulation.
The demographic group of adults collectively includes all ages older than 20, to em-
phasise a distinction between the two groups. Furthermore, demographic fairness will
be evaluated in a manner striving to isolate the age bias from other impacting factors
related to the quality of the image data. This thesis seeks to conclude the viability of
synthetic data by fine-tuning two different state-of-the-art face recognition models for
identification on synthetic data, followed by testing the models on real-world data to
closely replicate authentic face identification scenarios.
Fine-tuning models using synthetic data is considered a naive bias mitigation technique.
Additional bias mitigation techniques will be explored if the naive approach does not
result in models satisfying the fairness aim. These evaluations not only assess overall
recognition accuracy but also aim to improve demographic fairness, contributing to dis-
cussions on ethical AI practices. Furthermore, this thesis will analyse the compliance of
current face recognition systems and data practices set forth by AI Act requirements and
ISO/IEC standards, and aims to implement these standards within research constraints.
Fine-tuning models using synthetic data is an initial bias mitigation technique. If this
proves insuﬀicient to achieve fairness objectives, additional bias mitigation strategies
will be explored. These evaluations not only assess overall recognition accuracy but also
aim to improve demographic fairness, contributing to discussions on ethical AI practices.
Furthermore, this thesis critically analyzes the compliance of current face recognition
systems and data practices with AI Act requirements and ISO/IEC standards. Its aim
is to enhance ethical, privacy, and fairness aspects of these systems within research
constraints.

CHAPTER 2
Research questions
The purpose of this thesis is to enhance demographic fairness in the performance of
state-of-the-art face recognition systems using a responsible development framework in
accordance with the AI Act and ISO/IEC standards. The following questions will be
addressed:
1. To what extent do state-of-the-art face recognition models achieve equity in identi-
fication of young and adult faces?
• The performance assessment concerning model accuracy will be conducted by
evaluating metrics FNIR and FPIR. Assessment concerning bias in identifi-
cation systems will be evaluated using the fairness metric FND.
• Image-quality related issues will be addressed by separating the test sets into
canonical images and mixed-quality images. Evaluation will be based on the
metrics mentioned above.
2. Can fine-tuning face recognition models with synthetic children’s face images im-
prove equity in identification of young and adult faces?
• State-of-the-art face recognition models will be retrained on a synthetic data
set constituting of children’s faces and evaluated based on the metrics stated
above.
• The impact of increased intra-class variation in the synthetic training data on
improving equity will be evaluated based on the metrics stated above. This
evaluation will compare performance between adults and children, as well as
across different age groups within the children.
3. How can bias mitigation be performed and what would be a meaningful mitigation
vector, if the performed fine-tuning of models with synthetic children’s face images
does not result in models satisfying the fairness aim?
• Pre-, in-, and post-processing bias mitigation techniques to address underly-
ing biases in face recognition models will be investigated theoretically.
4. How would fine-tuning face recognition systems on synthetic child data for identi-
fying missing children and for bioforensic detection of child exploitation align with
the standards for responsible development of artificial intelligent systems set by
ISO/IEC-standard 9868 and the AI Act?
• Whether the systems and the specific data handling comply with the require-
ments outlined in the regulations, will be analysed by mapping target appli-
cations to risk classes and discussing the ethical implications.

CHAPTER 3
Related Work
This chapter provides an extensive overview of the most recent work necessary for ad-
vancing research on fairness in biometric identification systems, specifically addressing
the critical issue of locating missing children. To ensure a comprehensive review of the
relevant literature, the fields identified as essential for addressing the issue include: face
recognition systems for locating missing children, bias mitigation strategies for demo-
graphic fairness, standardised demographic fairness assessment, and current regulatory
frameworks governing biometric systems. These areas were investigated by searching
Google Scholar for the keywords characteristic herein.
Additionally, relevant papers
were identified from a curated library of citations compiled by the Norwegian Biometric
Laboratory1. Standardised definitions from ISO/IEC standards were accessed through
discussion with Christoph Busch.
Hence, this literature review of related work and
state-of-the-art is, to the best of our knowledge, adequate and exhaustive.
3.1
Face recognition systems for finding missing
children
Face recognition is typically the primary biometric tool used to locate missing children,
as parents and relatives are more likely to possess facial images of the child compared
to other biometrics such as fingerprints or iris scans [30]. Despite this, several studies
[6][30][31][32], including the ones submitted to the face recognition vendor test (FRVT)
[33] report that face recognition for children lags in performance compared to adults.
One of the reasons identified is the bias towards adults in existing face data sets used
for training and evaluation. Medvedev et al. [6] and Chandaliya et al. [30] specifically
note that these biases hinder face recognition systems from accurately discriminating
children’s identities compared to adult’s. They argue that adult faces possess more dis-
tinct characteristics, and emphasise the need for specialised measures to improve the
recognition of children’s faces.
The challenge intensifies during the comparison stage of face recognition systems, as
the time between enrolment and recognition can span many months or years.
Face
recognition systems, as found by Zheng et al. [34] among others, have been observed to
experience a decrease of more than 10% in accuracy when evaluated over large age gaps.
This problem is particularly pronounced for children, as their ageing process is more
rapid and extensive compared to adults [35]. This results in photos of missing children
become outdated at a faster rate compared to adults [36]. An approach to address this
issue is presented by Kumar et al. where they utilise a generative adversarial network
(GAN) as a face-ageing algorithm to age-progress images of children. Specifically, they
demonstrated how incorporating biometric information from images of the parents can

3.2
Bias mitigation strategies for demographic
fairness in face-based biometrics
Recent reports of demographic bias in biometric systems have highlighted the critical
challenge of mitigating algorithmic bias within biometrics, as discussed in Section 3.1
above. Consequently, there has been a significant increase in scientific publications on
this topic, focusing primarily on face-based biometric verification tasks rather than iden-
tification tasks [5]. In the study of bias mitigation using facial biometric characteristics,
approaches can be divided into two catagories, as outlined by Drozdowski et al. in their
survey on demographic bias in biometrics [5]:
1. Mitigation during the training (or fine-tuning) step of learning-based methods such
as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).
2. Mitigation through the dynamic selection of decision thresholds based on demo-
graphic attributes.
Drozdowski et al. highlight that the notion of fairness is multifaceted and can be mutu-
ally exclusive, stating; ”Depending on the definition of fairness one chooses to adopt, a
system can effectively always be shown to exhibit some form of bias” [5]. Nevertheless,
findings in the first category have shown promising results by using demographically
balanced training sets and enhancing these sets with synthetic data. For example, on
racial bias, Kärkkäinen et al. [37] and Wang et al. [38] used pre-processing bias mitiga-
tion methods by constructing balanced racial data sets from existing public image data
sets. They demonstrated that training a ResNet-34 model [39] from scratch on a racially
balanced data set results in more equal verification accuracy across races compared to
training on less balanced data sets.
Similarly on age bias, Medvedev et al. [6] and Kumar et al. [30] have addressed age
bias by collecting a data set of children balanced across African, Asian, Caucasian, and
Indian races. Particularly Medvedev et al. showed that fine-tuning state-of-the-art face
recognition models (ArcFace [40], MagFace [41], and AdaFace [42]) on a combination of
their data set and other less age-balanced data sets reduced the difference in accuracy
in the verification task between children and adults. They state that their data set is
one of the most balanced data sets of children in ages 0 to approximately 18 compared
to other open-source data sets.

3.2.1
Standardised bias assessment
To assess the fairness of state-of-the-art models and the impact of bias mitigation tech-

performance assessments through various tests and evaluations to ensure high accuracy
and reliability in biometric recognition systems [50].
NIST tests face recognition models for demographic bias in face verification and identifi-
cation tasks. These evaluations report on how false error rates vary across demographic
factors like sex, age, and race. Participation is free, and the model performance test

images of approximately 8 million identities, and reports metrics such as the FNIR and
the FPIR [52]. The reports generated by NIST from the FRVT framework is widely re-
garded as benchmarks and quality standards in the biometric development community.
Reflecting the increased focus on demographic bias in face recognition models in recent
years, NIST has increased the frequency of face recognition testing.

CHAPTER 4
Theoretical background
This chapter delves into the theoretical background surrounding the biometric identifica-
tion task, which includes both closed-set and open-set face identification, followed by a
description of the biometric subsystems involved, with a focus on explaining mated and
non-mated comparisons. The face recognition (FR) pipeline including face processing
and feature extraction, and the FR loss functions AdaFace and MagFace are detailed,
before the fairness and bias assessment of FR models are explained.
4.1
Biometric systems terminology
The terminology explained in the following section is aligned with the ISO/IEC 19795-1
concerning biometric performance testing and reporting [4].
Biometric systems are designed to perform biometric recognition, constituting of either
biometric identification or biometric verification of individuals, based on their unique
physiological or behavioural traits called biometric characteristics. The physiological
biometric characteristics can be facial characteristics such as the width between the in-
dividual’s eyes or shape of nose, the colour of their iris, but also their hand geometry or
movement characteristics [62]. The biometric characteristics are captured by a biomet-
ric device e.g. a camera that produces an electronic representation of two-dimensional
signals, e.g. fingerprints or images of a face. These techniques are effective for recog-
nising people, as the various biometric characteristics are distinct to each individual [63].
The individual about whom the biometric system is making a decision is referred to
as the subject. This term will be used henceforth when discussing biometric identifica-
tion at a conceptual level. At a technical level, the biometric system is comparing the
processed information of the biometric characteristics of the subject, and this processed
information is denoted the biometric probe. How the biometric characteristics are pro-
cessed is explained in details in Section 4.3.
The biometric probe is compared to the processed information of biometric character-
istics of themselves, one other subject or multiple other subjects. The information the
biometric probe is compared to is denoted the biometric reference, and all references are
collectively stored in a reference database also denoted a biometric enrolment database.
A biometric probe and biometric reference will henceforth be called the probe and ref-
erence. If a probe and a reference stem from the same subject, then the comparison
between these is denoted a mated comparison, thus non-mated if they stem from two
different subjects and therefore depict two different individuals [64]. The set of subjects
in the test data set i.e. test probes utilised in an evaluation is denoted the test crew.
==PAGE:20==
12
4 Theoretical background
The probes can be enrolled or non-enrolled in the reference database. The probe is
enrolled if the reference database contain references of the same subject as the probe
represents, and it is then denoted that the probe is known. If the probe is not enrolled in
the reference database, the reference database does not contain references representing
the same subject, and then the probe is unknown. This is explained in greater detail in
Section 5.1.1.
In the comparison, which is denoted a comparison trial or transaction attempt, a com-
parison is accepted i.e. identified if the probe and the reference reach a comparison
score exceeding a pre-defined discriminatory threshold, indicating high probability that
the probe and reference stem from the same subject. Comparison scores can be either
similarity scores or dissimilarity scores, naturally following that similarity scores need
to exceed the threshold while dissimilarity scores need to fall below the threshold. This
thesis specifically concerns biometric identification on the biometric characteristics of
faces and the comparison score metric utilised in the biometric system is the cosine
similarity score.
4.2
Identification task
The goal in a biometric identification task is to make a correct decision, which involves
correctly accepting mated transaction attempts while also correctly rejecting non-mated
transaction attempts. Mated transaction attempts are identification attempts on sub-
jects known to be enrolled in the reference database as stated above. Conversely, non-
mated transaction attempts are identification attempts on subjects not present in the
reference database. These attempts consist of two processes. The first process is template
generation, which involves creating a feature embedding of the biometric characteristics
in the captured sample i.e. the image. This is performed in the signal processing sub-
system, and elaborated in details in Section 4.3. The second process is the comparison,
where comparison scores are generated by evaluating the similarity or dissimilarity be-
tween the feature embedding of the captured sample and those in the reference database.
This is performed in Comparison subsystem and elaborated in details in Section 4.3.
4.2.1
Closed-set and open-set face identification
Closed-set face identification assumes that the test crew of probes consists of only sub-
jects who are known to have a reference in the reference database [4]. This means that
trying to identify a subject in the reference database always yields a result. However,
this assumption seldom reflects the real-world use case. In the realistic scenario, an
unknown identity not present in the reference database might be encountered [65].
Open-set face identification resembles the real-world scenario better, as an open set con-
tains both known and unknown subjects. Hence, the open-set face identification needs
to perform an additional task compared to the closed-set identification being not only
==PAGE:21==
4.2 Identification task
13
the correct identification of known probes, but also the correct rejection of unknown
probes [65].
A method to ensure unbiased non-mated comparison trials between unknown probes and
the reference database, in case a large data set of unknown probes is not available, is to
use the jack-knife approach to create the enrolment of references in the reference database
[4]. In this approach, the entire set of subjects is enrolled in the reference database
except for one test subject. Non-mated comparison trials are then generated between
the omitted test subject and all the other enrolled references. This process is repeated by
omitting each subject in turn, ensuring that each subject is both included and excluded
at different times. This systematic omission and inclusion help in generating a large
set of unbiased non-mated comparison scores, when suﬀicient unknown subjects are not
available.
This thesis made use of the jack-knife approach for identification testing
following Clause 8.5.3 in ISO/IEC 19975-1 [4].

4.2.2.4
Comparison subsystem
In the comparison subsystem, the probe is compared against all references from the
reference database.
The comparison process results in comparison scores which are
passed to the decision subsystem. With a well-functioning identification system, it is
presumed that mated comparisons will yield higher comparison scores, if comparison is
based on a similarity metric, and yield low comparison scores for non-mated comparisons.
4.2.2.5
Decision subsystem
The decision subsystem takes the comparison scores and evaluates them against a spec-
ified threshold. If the scores exceed a certain specified threshold, under the assumption
that higher comparison scores indicate greater similarity, the respective reference is
added as a potential candidate for a subject match for the probe. Hence, the threshold
filters out presumed non-mated comparisons, leaving only the proposed candidates for
the candidate list. This process ensures that only the most likely candidates are retained

for further consideration.
The candidate list is processed through a decision policy, which may allow or require
multiple attempts before reaching the final identification decision. This is mostly relevant
in scenarios such as fingerprint identification, where the finger is scanned multiple times.
The identification outcome is then a candidate subject proposed to be matching the
probe, for the mated comparison trials, and a no candidate match, for the non-mated
comparison trials. These correct decisions correspond to either correctly accepting mated
identification attempts, and correctly rejecting non-mated identification attempts.

4.3.1
Quality control process
When the captured biometric sample e.g. an image, enters the signal processing subsys-
tem, various types of processing can be performed. The quality can be assessed with
software such as the Open Source Face Image Quality (OFIQ) used for computing qual-
ity aspects of each captured biometric sample in the data sets in accordance with the
ISO/IEC 29794-5 [66]. The quality of biometric samples is made up of various quality
components, and the components can be either capture related or subject related [67].
The quality of the captured samples can be expressed as a single scalar value, denoted
the unified quality score (UQS) [68], which depends on all quality components relevant
for the recognition performance [67].
The algorithm to compute the UQS is outlined in ISO/IEC 29794-5 clause 7.2.2 [66],
and is based on the deep learning model iResNet50 trained on the Microsoft-Celeb-1M
(MS1MV2) [69] data set with the MagFace loss function [41]. For reference, MS1MV2
contains approximately 4% images of children in the age 0-18, and 76.5% images of
Caucasians [38].
As the quality of biometric samples is heavily dependent on the capturing process, the
information obtained in the quality control can provide valuable feedback to both the
subject and the biometric attendant during biometric capturing, and the quality control
is essential to determine whether a sample is suitable for further processing or if a new
capture is needed. Enhancing quality components during the capturing can significantly
improve the quality of biometric samples, ultimately leading to better system perfor-
mance [68]. Different factors and issues affecting the quality of face biometric samples
and how they are dealt with are presented hereunder:

4.3.2.2
Feature extraction
To create the biometric probe in the format of a feature embedding, the feature extraction
is performed after the face detection and face alignment.
The feature extraction is
performed by CNN, or Recurent Neural Networks (RNNs) to make feature embeddings
[77]. This thesis mainly summarises the architecture of a CNN, which is illustrated in
Figure 4.3.
Figure 4.3: The basic architecture of a CNN. The initial layers act as feature extraction
layers, aiming to reduce the number of features present in the sample. These layers

process and generate the final feature representation as a feature embedding. Illustration

The process begins with an input layer that contains the pixel data of the samples.
Following the input layer, a number of hidden layers incorporate combinations of con-
volutional layers, activation layers, pooling layers, and at the final stage, one or more
fully connected layers. The output layer then uses an appropriate activation function
to convert the network’s raw scores into probabilities, enabling the final creation of the
feature embedding. The hidden layers are elaborated as follows:
1. Convolutional layer: A layer consisting of multiple convolutional filters, or kernels,
which fundamentally extracts the features and spatial relationship between pixels
of the input sample. Kernels of pre-determined sizes slide over the input sample,
and compute the dot product between the kernel weights and the part of the input
==PAGE:27==
4.3 Selected processes of subsystems
19
sample, covered by the kernels. This operation, together with a ReLu activation
produces a feature map which is fed as input data to the next convolutional layer.
The ReLu activation function converts all negative values to zero and keeps posi-
tive values unchanged, making the training of the network more eﬀicient, as this
simplifies the computations of the feature maps [79].
2. Pooling layer: A layer applied after the convolutional layer, reducing the dimen-
sions of the feature maps to improve computational stability of the network. Com-
mon operations in the layer are max-pooling and average-pooling, which slides
kernels over the feature maps of a fixed window size, only extracting the maxi-
mum value or the average value respectively [79].
3. Fully connected layer: The last layer in the hidden layers is similar to traditional
neural networks, where each node is connected to every node in the previous layer.
This layer flattens the features from the preceding layer by performing linear op-
erations to extract feature embeddings or classes of a predefined size.
Finally, the choice of loss function for training the network is what impacts the output
feature embeddings used in the comparison and decision subsystems. The loss func-
tions used in deep FR systems can be categorised into three types [73]. Similar for the
different loss functions is that they require pair of two or three face image samples to
learn discriminate features. Euclidean-based loss functions embeds sample-pairs into an
euclidean space, and reduce intra-class variance and enlarge inter-class variance based
on Euclidean distance [73]. Angular/cosine-margin-based loss separate sample-pairs by
a angular/cosine distance.
The activation function, softmax loss and it’s variations,
modify the softmax loss by normalising the features and weights before computation.
The most recent angular/cosine-margin-based loss functions have shown state-of-the-art
recognition performance [80], and are described in further details in Section 4.4.
Different CNN architectures can be used as backbone to compute the feature embeddings
in FR, such as AlexNet [72], VGGNet [81] and residual networks (ResNet) [39], which
primarily differs in their number of layers and sizes of convolutional filters. ResNet stands
out by using residual blocks, also called skip connections, which facilitates training by
adding the input of a layer to its output of several layers. This way, the network only
needs to learn the difference between the input and output, which skips the need to
separately train all the layers in between. One of the most commonly used open-source
backbone architectures used in combination with angular/cosine-margin loss functions
is the improved residual network (iResNet) [80]. The specific architecture of iResNet is
detailed in the following section.
Improved Residual Network
The iResNet, proposed in 2020 [82] is, as the name implies, an improved version of the
residual network proposed in 2015. The iResNet contains the same number of parameters
and components as the ResNet, thus the model complexity is not increased. Instead the

The original paper by Duta et al. [82] describes three main features that allows for these
improvements, which will be outlined below. For a more detailed explanation, please
refer to the original paper. The first modification is the arrangement of residual blocks
into a number of stages, called ResStages, constituting of a start stage, middle stages,
and an end stage. The number of middle stages depends on the depth of the residual
network. Each middle stage can contain a different number of residual blocks, called
ResBlocks. These are also divided into a Start ResBlock, one or more Middle ResBlocks,
and an End ResBlock, see Figure 4.4 for an illustration. Each ResBlock can differ in
design, but the essential part is that the main propagtation path (where the information
can flow through the skip connections) at a maximum contains four ReLu activation
functions, no matter the network depth. In comparison, the original ResNet contains
the number of ReLu activation functions on the main propagation path proportional to
the depth of the network. The modification enables the input signal to forward more
information to earlier layers.
The second modification lies in the computation of the projection shortcuts, which is
when the input dimensions of the residual block should be reduced to the dimensions
of the output of the residual block, in the skip connections. The original method uses
a 1 × 1 convolution of stride 2, to project the channels of the input dimensions to the
output dimensions. However, this only transfers 25% of arbitrary feature embeddings,

and thus introduces some noise and information loss. Instead, the projection shortcut
presented by Duta et al. performs a 3 × 3 max-pooling layer with stride 2 following a

should be transferred to the subsequent layers.
The third improvement adjusts the standard convolutional layers in the ResNet into
grouped convolutions. This means, that the convolutional operations are applied inde-

4.4
Angular/cosine-margin-based loss functions
The angular/cosine-margin-based loss functions have shown to be suﬀiciently effective
for FR models, as they focus on making features not only more separable between classes
but also more discriminative within classes [73]. One of the most widely adopted loss
functions in both research and industry within this category is the Additive Angular
Margin Loss (ArcFace), which incorporates angular margins between classes directly
into the loss function. This allows the model to learn more discriminative features by
pushing the embeddings of different classes further apart in angular space [80]. Building
on this concept are the Margin-Aware Graph Face (MagFace) and Adaptive Margin Face
Recognition (AdaFace), which proves to introduce additional adjustments and enhance-
ments to further improve FR performance [41]. The following sections describe each of
these loss functions in more detail.
4.4.1
ArcFace
ArcFace is a FR loss function developed in 2018 by Deng et al. [40]. This loss function
introduces the additive angular margin loss, which enhances the softmax loss function
widely used for object recognition. This adaptation improves its suitability for FR tasks,
particularly in data sets with significant intra-class appearance variations such as differ-
ent expressions, lighting conditions, or angles for the same subject. By incorporating the
angular margin, ArcFace enforce inter-class diversity, i.e. that different classes are dis-
tinct from each other, and promotes intra-class compactness, i.e. that samples depicting
the same subject are clustered closely together in the feature space, despite variations
across the different image samples of the same class [40].

MagFace
MagFace introduced in 2021 by Meng et al. is a FR loss function that builds upon the
additive margin introduced in the softmax loss by ArcFace [41]. It incorporates a feature-
magnitude regulariser designed to penalise low-quality face image samples within a class,
which is particularly important in scenarios where each subject exhibits significant vari-
ability in facial appearances between different image samples. The motivation behind
MagFace is to address the issue observed in ArcFace, where both easily recognisable and
challenging face image samples share the same decision boundary. This phenomenon
can lead to overfitting of hard and noisy samples within their respective classes, making
the model less robust to intra-class variations.
MagFace encodes the quality into the loss function as follows. Instead of normalising the
feature embeddings using the l2 norm as in ArcFace, the norm of feature embeddings are
not normalised, thus obtaining the magnitude of the feature embeddings instead. This
characteristic allows MagFace to remain compatible with cosine-based metrics. Specifi-
cally, in MagFace, this is expressed as ai = ||xi||, where xi represents a feature embed-
ding without normalisation. The assumption is that there exists a positive correlation
between the magnitude and quality. Figure 4.6 illustrates face qualities of a feature ai

be separated by the margin m.

4.5
Generation of synthetic data
As described in Section 3.2, synthetic data to mimic real world data has shown to be
a valuable tool in the development of fair FR systems. The most advanced and recent
techniques for generating synthetic data is the use of Generative Adversarial Networks
(GANs) and diffusion probabilistic models (diffusion models for brevity) [43].
GANs was introduced by Godfellow et al. in their paper ”Generative Adversarial Nets”
published in 2014 [84]. GANs consist of two artificial neural network models: the gener-
ator network and the discriminator network. The adversarial term is based on a game-
theoretic scenario where the generator network must compete against an adversary [84].
The principle of a GAN is explained as follows. Initially, the generator network takes
noise (typically from a Gaussian distribution) as input and converts it into complex
data samples, for example, images. This noise input is referred to as the latent space,
which represents a compressed and often lower-dimensional representation of the poten-
tial features in the generated data. The transformations that occur within the generator
network map these points from the latent space to the data space, progressively adding
details and refining the structure to match the target distribution of real data [84].
The discriminator, in turn, receives the generated sample as input and is designed to
discriminate, or classify, a binary problem of whether the input sample is real (for exam-
ple, images in the training data set) or fake (the generated images from the generator
network). The relationship between the two networks in GANs is such that the loss
from the classification of the discriminator is sent back to the generator through back-
propagation, forcing it to change its model parameters to generate samples that are
more similar to the distribution of the real training data.
Alternately, when the generator has produced samples that fool the discriminator into
making false positives, the generator will not update its parameters. Instead, the dis-
criminator will be penalised and will update its model parameters to better distinguish
between real and fake images. This relationship iterates until convergence, where the
generator’s samples become indistinguishable from real data, and the discriminator can-
not tell the difference between real and fake, ideally outputting a probability of 0.5 for
each. At this point, or sooner if the user determines the generator is suﬀicient for gen-
eration, the discriminator may be discarded, and the generator can be used solely to
generate new images, or whatever data type it has been trained to learned [85]. The
architecture of GAN has been enhanced and innovated on to specifically generate syn-
thetic face images of different kinds, such as generating single faces of random identities
or multiple faces of random identities, by popular and recent models like StyleGAN3
and InterFaceGAN respectively, used in various applications [9][43]. Other generative
approaches than GAN is also proposed in the literature, such as variational auto-encoder,
auto-regressive models, and diffusion models [9].
Enhancing GANs to better mimic real-world data can be achieved by incorporating

intra-class variations using diffusion models. The concept of diffusion-based models was
initially introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. in 2015 in the paper ”Deep Unsupervised
Learning using Nonequilibrium Thermodynamics models” [86]. These models belong to
a class of generative models which perceptually adds noise to data (diffusion process),
followed by learning to reverse this process (denoising process) to generate new, realistic
data samples. This approach enables the model to effectively clean up random inputs
and produce high-quality data such as images [87][88].
In particular, text-to-image diffusion models are commonly used to make high quality
and diverse synthetic images from text descriptions, by cleaning up images, filling in
missing parts, and expanding images to produce realistic variations that were not present

4.6
Fairness in biometric systems
As of today, there exists no single agreed coherent definition of algorithmic fairness nor
no international standard for measuring fairness in biometric systems [90][91]. To ad-
dress the current need and growing concerns from the public and policymakers about the
increasing deployment of biometric systems, the ISO/IEC Joint Technical Committee 1,
Subcommittee 37 on Biometrics, has initiated a standard on quantifying biometric sys-
tem performance across demographic groups [92]. This standard, currently referred to as
ISO/IEC Draft International Standard (DIS) 19795-10 [2], is expected to be published
in September 2024 [92]. The scope of this standard is to: ”establish requirements for
estimating and reporting of performance variations observed when cohorts belonging to
different demographic groups engage with biometric enrolment and recognition systems”.
Following the definitions and nomenclature in this standard, and other recent literature
on biometric fairness, this thesis will refer to fairness in biometric recognition systems as
equitability between differential outcomes, caused by demographic performance differ-
entials [90][91]. The demographic performance differential is the difference in biometric
system metrics across different demographic groups (e.g. based on sex, age, and race).
The differential outcomes means that false positive and/or false negative error rates can
differ across the demographic groups [2].
Fairness measures, or differential performance measures, in identification trials, shall by
ISO/IEC DIS 19795-10 Clause 6.6 [2] be calculated using the the False Positive Differ-
ential performance (FPD) and FND. These describe the tendency for mated biometric
samples from subjects in one demographic group not to match relative to another demo-
graphic group, and the tendency for non-mated biometric samples from one demographic
group to falsely match relative to another demographic group, respectively [92]. They

the general topic of fairness in machine learning, this thesis will focus on optimising for
Equal Opportunity. This criterion is adopted as requiring that FNIR is equal across
different groups, hence the FND between the groups is close to 1 [97].
4.6.1
Bias
In relation to the demographic fairness definition described above, based on the defi-
nitions in ISO/IEC DIS 19795-10 [2], a demographically biased model can be defined
as one that produces statistically different outcomes for different demographic groups
[90]. Alternative terms for bias also exist, for example, NIST uses the term demographic
differentials to similarly describe performance variations across population groups [50].
Since FR models are data driven they require a vast amount of data on which to be
trained in order to solve identification or verification task [98]. Consequently, if the
data used for training the models contain bias of any kind, the models will learn these
biases and consecutively produce output reflecting them or even amplify or perpetuate
them [98]. There are different types of biases and these will be presented below based
on the definitions published by Mehrabi et al. [98]. Mehrabi et al. state the definitions
based on how bias manifests in algorithmic and model decisions. The types of biases are
divided into the categories Data to Algorithm, Algorithm to User, and User to Data.
Data to Algorithm concerns bias in data, which when used for training algorithms, might
result in biased algorithmic outcomes. An example of this type of bias is measurement
bias, which occur from the practises of choosing or measuring different features when
creating a data set. For instance, this could involve the estimation of ages of faces in
a data set. Another example of a Data to Algorithm bias is representation bias, which
occur from the sampling process gathering selected data from a population.
==PAGE:37==
4.6 Fairness in biometric systems
29
Algorithm to User concerns bias, which affect user behaviour as a consequence of biased
algorithmic outcomes. An example of this bias type is popularity bias, which occurs
from the practise that more popular items tend to be exposed more by the algorithms.
However, popularity metrics are subject to manipulation which is seen by for example
fake reviews or social bots. Another example is evaluation bias, which arises during
model evaluation e.g. when inappropriate or disproportionate benchmarks are being
used for evaluation.
User to Data concerns bias, which arise in the data due to inherent biases in users
that might be reflected in the data they generate.
An example of this is historical
bias, which is bias seeped into the data through the data collection process due to the
already existing bias and socio-technical issues present in the world. Another example
is population bias, which arises when demographics, statistics, representatives, and user
characteristics are different in the user population of the application than from the
original target population.
4.6.1.1
Demographic bias and image quality
As detailed in Chapter 3, NIST highlights that image quality might not simply be a
correlating factor for demographic bias in FR, but directly causing it [99]. The report
also notes the relevance of these findings to identification algorithms that use one to one
comparisons followed by a sort operation, as done in this thesis.
NIST found that the false negative error rate is strongly dependent on image quality,
where it is seen that poor photography of a face can induce a demographic effect [52].
Examples of poor photography of a face can be under-exposure of darker skinned sub-
jects or over-exposure of lighter skinned subjects. Another scenario which can impose
greater false negative error rates are inadequate camera adjustment, which can lead
to pitch-angle variations of very tall or very short subjects [52]. Additionally, NIST
also found that the false positive rates are seen to vary even with good image quality.
False positive variations arise when an algorithm produces similarity score distributions
that are displaced for one demographic versus another. This can occur due to under-
representation of a demographic in the image data set used for algorithm training.
The FR company Cognitec acknowledges that research has shown FR systems can per-
form very differently across various demographic groups. However, they also emphasise
that not all conclusions from this research point to significant disparities, and that some
studies have found other conclusions on differences in performance among different de-
mographic groups [100]. Cognitec argues that the reason for disparity in performance on
demographic groups is not always rooted in the technology itself, but often in problems
concerning the image quality of test and training sets. As their white paper explains;
”[...] different studies yield differing results, underlining the complexity of accuracy tests

Cognitec reports a study which shows that when evaluating higher-quality photographs

4.6.2
Bias mitigation
In order to obtain a less biased and thus more fair FR model, various bias mitiga-
tion techniques for machine learning models have been proposed in the literature. These
techniques are comprehensively summarised in a survey on ”Bias Mitigation for Machine
Learning Classifiers” by Hort et al. [96], which categorises them into three main groups,
concerning when in the FR pipeline they are performed; pre-processing, in-processing,
and post-processing.
A pre-processing mitigation technique is mitigating bias in the training data, to pre-
vent it from reaching machine learning models. An example of this is creating a more
balanced training data set in terms of demographic composition. This can be achieved
through data gathering choices or through removing the demographic identification of
an individual, and hence anonymising the data. A novel strategy for ensuring balanced
data sets in FR models is through the generation of synthetic data to achieve the desired
balance, as described in 3.2.
An in-processing mitigation technique addresses bias during the training of machine
learning models by adjusting model parameters to reduce discrimination. One approach
involves modifying the model’s loss function, such as by adding a regularisation term to
penalise differences between groups. Constraints can also be applied to the loss function
to enforce specific bias thresholds that should not be exceeded during training. Another
method is to adjust the trained weights through fine-tuning the model with a revised
distribution of training data [101], as mentioned in Section 3.2.
A post-processing mitigation technique is mitigating bias based on the output of trained
machine learning models. Hardt et al. has proposed a widely adopted optimisation tech-
nique to achieve fairness with respect to Equalized Odds and Equality of Opportunity.
This technique aims to adjust the probability threshold of classification outcomes for a

across different groups [102].

influence on GARBE, and conversely if α is close to 0, the FND will have the largest
influence. Following equation 4.12, the smaller the GARBE value is the more fair the
identification performance is, and conversely, the larger the GARBE value is, the more
unfair the performance is.

4.8
Summary
This chapter explored the theoretical background of biometric systems, and explained
how the systems perform recognition by identifying or verifying individuals based on
their unique physiological or behavioural traits, known as biometric characteristics. The
system captures these traits using biometric devices, producing digital representations
for identification. The comparison between a biometric probe and references is called a
comparison trial. If the comparison score exceeds a predefined similarity threshold, it
indicates a high probability that the probe and reference stem from the same subject.
The aim of a biometric identification task is to make accurate decisions by correctly
accepting mated transaction attempts and rejecting non-mated ones.
Closed-set face identification assumes that all individuals in the test set are enrolled
in the reference database, yielding a result accepting the probe as a known identity
for every identification attempt. Open-set face identification, however, better reflects
real-world scenarios by including both mated and non-mated samples, and requires the
system to also correctly reject unknown identities. The jack-knife approach can be used
for generating unbiased non-mated comparison trials.

towards the camera. Consequently, images within each subject that exhibited large SD

is determined as SD larger than the mean SD observed across all subjects. Therefore,
if four out of five images of a subject exhibit rotation in the yaw direction and one

5.2.1.6
Preprocessing data subsets
To test and evaluate the performance of the chosen FR models, experiments are con-

tions on different data subsets sampled from the children data set and the adults data
set respectively. To further strengthen the statistical inference of the obtained results,
the data subset sampling is stratified to ensure an equitable distribution of demographic
factors, as well as an equitable distribution of enrolled identities both between children
and adults, as well as within children age groups. The data subset sampling is consid-
ered a preprocessing procedure, and the functions for data subset sampling can be found
under the data processing utils in the GitHub repository of this thesis.
To construct one children data subset, samples are drawn from the children data set un-
der the following stratifications. Each age group is sampled to contain the same number
of enrolled identities CK as the minority group. This is done by undersampling all age
groups, but the minority group. The second stratification is, that the sampling of each

the case of the data sets before data subset sampling (see Figure 5.9). Hence, all age

Then one adults data subset is sampled to contain the same number of enrolled identi-

amount of images are observed between the children and adults data subsets and within.

In addition, from the children data set, a canonical data set is created. Canonical images
are defined by ISO/IEC 29794-5 [66] to be face images that are centred and frontal
with neutral expression, eyes-open and without eye-wear. ISO/IEC 39794-5:2019 Annex

(ICAO) standards for passport photos [49]. To meet these definitions, the canonical data
set will consist of images exceeding the median UQS of the entire distribution of the
children data set, thereby separating out images with lower quality. This approach serves
as a pragmatic solution since no ISO standard currently specifies a method for separating
face image data sets into canonical images.

facial coordinates, including the five landmarks left eye, right eye, nose, right side of
mouth, and left side of mouth. These coordinates are then used to perform facial align-
ment through geometric transformations [75].
The downloaded test data sets, YLFW and RFW, have already been aligned following
the preprocessing steps in the paper of ArcFace [6][38]. In addition, images in YLFW
have also been cropped, while the images in RFW is cropped using an alignment script

6.1
Face recognition models utilised
To construct the FR models in this thesis, the overall architecture involves selecting a pre-
trained backbone network, incorporating an embedding layer to reduce high-dimensional
features extracted by the backbone into a lower-dimensional space suitable for face em-
beddings, and then integrating the specific loss function.
In this thesis, the state-of-the-art FR loss functions, MagFace and AdaFace, were cho-
sen for their shared architecture designed to achieve high intra-class compactness and
inter-class separation, as detailed in Section 4.4. Hence, these methods were selected
because their design has demonstrated improved recognition accuracy under challeng-
ing conditions, such as variations in illumination, expression, or pose. This attribute is
particularly relevant to the thesis’s focus on evaluating face identification systems using
real-world images, simulating practical scenarios such as locating missing individuals. In
addition, two relatively similar loss functions were selected to enhance confidence in the
results and support statistical significance.
The iResNet18, an enhanced 18-layer residual network detailed in Section 4.3.2.2, pre-
trained on the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CASIA) WebFace data set [116], was
chosen as the pre-trained backbone network for the FR models. The choice of using
this data set and backbone architecture was made after selecting the loss functions, pri-
marily due to their availability as pre-trained models in both the GitHub repositories
for implementing MagFace and AdaFace. This decision ensures consistency in results

54

across experiments. While larger data sets and deeper networks could enhance accuracy,
they were not selected as the focus of the thesis is on fairness rather than optimisation
of accuracy. Additionally, using iResNet18 offers advantages such as reduced training
time and lower resource requirements, thus a smaller carbon footprint.
In summary, the FR models used in this thesis, henceforth referred to as the MagFace
model and the AdaFace model, incorporate the iResNet18 backbone pre-trained with
the MagFace and AdaFace loss functions, respectively.
6.1.1
Backbone architecture and pre-training data
Architecture
The architectures of the two FR models, MagFace and AdaFace, can be broadly under-
stood within the framework of the iResNet18 model. The 18 layers refer to the number
of convolutional layers within the residual blocks and the layers involved in shortcut
connections, as well as the input and output layer. Additional layers for specific func-
tions are not included in this count but all necessary layers and functions to understand
the FR model are detailed below for comprehensive understanding. The specific model
parameters are examined by printing the loaded pre-trained models from the respective
MagFace and AdaFace repositories on GitHub.
• Starting stage (1 convolutional layer)
The starting stage contains a convolutional layer with a kernel of size 3 × 3 with
stride 1, a batch normalisation layer, and a PreRelu activation function, which is
ReLu activation function applied before the addition of the residual connection of
the residual block.
• Middle stages (16 convolutional layers)
The middle stages comprise of four stages, and each of the four stage has two
ResBlocks.
Each ResBlock contains two convolutional layers with three batch
normalisation layers, before and after each convolutional layer. Hence the number
of convolutional layers in the ResBlocks are 4 × 2 × 2 = 16. The kernel size of the
convolutional layers is 3 × 3 with varying stride and padding to gradually increase
the number of channels from 64 to 512, to extract more complex patterns, and to
reduce the feature maps. Between the ResBlocks, a down-sample layer is added to
adjust the output dimensions to the next ResBlock.
• Adaptive average pooling layer
An adaptive average pooling layer is included between the convolutional feature
extractor and the final fully connected layer to ensure a fixed-size output regardless
of input size. The output size specification of 1 × 1 computes the average of the
feature maps reducing it to size 1 × 1 × 512. Hence, for each of the 512 feature
maps, the output will be one single averaged value.
==PAGE:63==
6.1 Face recognition models utilised

• End layers (1 linear layer)
The end layer consists of a batch normalisation layer to normalise the feature maps
along the channel dimension. Regularisation is applied through a dropout layer
of probability 0.4 by default. A fully connected layer is applied to flatten the 3D
vector into a 1D vector. Finally, a 1D batch normalisation layer is applied.
• Model specific layer
The final layer added to the backbone architecture is a linear layer with MagFace
or AdaFace specifications, for generating the final feature embeddings, storing
the unique features of each input face image sample. The output is denoted the
biometric probe (see Section 4.2.2) in the form of a feature embedding.
Pre-training data
The CASIA-WebFace data set [116] which the pre-trained models use, is a public, large-
scale data set comprising approximately 10,000 identities and 500,000 images.
The
identities are celebrities on the IMDb website, from which the corresponding images are
scraped as well. This makes the data set an in the wild data set, though with a tendency
of over-representation of posed red carpet images. Additionally an over-representation

12.50% of the data set, computed in a study by Lee et al. [117]. Other pre-training data

6.1.3
Fine-tuning process as a bias mitigation method
In this thesis, the bias mitigation approach combines both preprocessing and in-processing
methods (further detailed in Section 4.6.2). This approach involves selecting a synthet-
ically generated data set representative of children’s faces — a state-of-the-art method
within preprocessing bias mitigation for FR — to fine-tune pre-trained FR models. The
main objective of this combination is to adjust the weights of an open-source, state-of-
the-art FR model, originally trained primarily on adult data. By doing so, the aim is
to enhance recognition performance for children and reduce the performance disparity
between children and adults, ultimately improving fairness.
The rest of this section will delve into the in-processing method of fine-tuning pre-trained
FR models for identification. Fine-tuning is a transfer learning method to adapt a pre-
trained model to a specific task, through retraining and thereby tuning the weights of
earlier layers to the new task. This method is widely used for tasks with scarce labelled
data, that can make use of similar extracted features from a related task. Furthermore,
this method offers advantages in terms of time complexity, leveraging the computational
effort already invested in training parameters on a large data set. Vrbancic et al. [119]
conducted a study comparing fine-tuning with the conventional transfer learning method
of replacing the last layer(s) in a pre-trained network. They emphasised that fine-tuning
involves adjusting these layer(s) further, and they raise the question of optimal selection
criteria for fine-tuning layers in pre-trained models [119].
The selection of fine-tuning layers in this thesis is made based on the paper Open-Set
Face Identification (OSFI) on Few-Shot Gallery by Fine-Tuning by Park et al. [65] from
January 2023, which claims to be the first to propose an effective fine-tuning scheme for
OSFI. The fine-tuning methodology of their paper suits the task of this thesis, which is
also an OSFI task (See Section 4.2.1). In comparison to closed-set identification, this
resembles real-world scenarios better, as it needs to perform both known probe iden-
tification and unknown probe rejection. In spite of this, they show how FR models
pre-trained on a large-scale public face database are not effective for open-set identifica-
tion. Hence they suggest an overfitting-resilient fine-tuning scheme.
In their fine-tuning scheme they advocate for using a weight initialisation strategy in
the model based on weight imprinting [65], combined with only fine-tuning the batch
normalisation layers, as a method to reduce the risk of overfitting when fine-tuning on
a few-shot reference database. Moreover they introduce a novel Neighborhood Aware
Cosine (NAC) matcher for both effective unknown detection and known probe iden-
tity identification. However, since the training data set used in this thesis does not
resemble a few-shot gallery scenario, only fine-tuning the batch normalisation layers

The process of applying the fine-tuning method described above, will be divided into
the following six generic steps, illustrated in Figure 6.1.
Each step includes specific
parameters for fine-tuning in the context of this thesis, utilising the synthetic child data
set HDA and GANDiff as new training data sets.
1. Load pre-trained model and adapt to specific task
The pre-trained model is loaded, including the final, model specific layer of either
AdaFace or MagFace. The number of classes to predict in the final fully connected
layer is changed from 85, 742, which is the number of classes in the pre-training data
set, to 1, 652, which is the number of classes in the HDA and GANDiff training
data.
2. Prepare custom data set
The implementation of the model specific layers have different assumptions to the
training data format and folder structure. Thus, the training data is prepared to
meet these requirements, which is stated in the respective model repositories, and
described in Section 5.2.2.
3. Freeze all layers except the final layer
In the initial training structure of the two models, all parameters are kept frozen
except for the parameters of the final layers. This means that the weights of the
final layer are randomly initialised, and can be updated to fit the data set, whereas
the weights of the earlier layers will not be updated during the initial training, but
retains the general features learned from the pre-training data set, which can be
applied on the similar new training data set. This ensures that the model does not
overfit on the new training data set, but instead uses the general features learned
by the pre-trained model [119].
4. Train model until stabilisation
The adjusted model is trained using the new training data set, and the model-
specific loss function. In the forward pass, the new training data set is passed
==PAGE:66==
58

through the network in batches, going through the pre-trained weights, and the fi-
nal, model specific layer, with randomly initialised weights. The output of the final
layer returns feature embedding of the input images, on which the model-specific
loss is calculated.
The gradient of the loss is computed and passed backward
through the layers in the network using back-propagation. Only the weights and
biases of the final layer is updated using the stochastic gradient descent optimiser,
based on the average gradient over the batch. A relatively high learning rate of
0.01 is used, enabling the weights of the final layer to relatively quickly adapt to
the unique patterns and characteristics of the new training data set [120]. This
process is repeated iteratively for multiple epochs until the loss converges.
5. Release remaining layers and train with lower learning rate
Using the converged model, all or some of the previous layers can be unfreezed,
and the model can be retrained, using a much lower learning rate than the initial
training. A lower learning rate lets the pre-trained weights to be updated incre-
mentally, and to not overfit on the smaller, new training set [120]. The number of
pre-trained layers to release depends, among other, on how similar the new task is
to the pre-trained task. Nevertheless, this thesis follows the methodology to fine
tune OSFI systems, described earlier in this section. Hence, all batch normalisa-
tion layers (27 layers) in the model will be released. The training of these layers
continues for several epochs, up to a maximum of 26, and the learning rate is set
to decrease by 10% for epoch 12, 16, and 22, to avoid converging to quickly.
6. Save fine-tuned model ready for feature extraction of test data set
The fine-tuned model on the new training data set is saved, ready to perform
feature extraction on the test data set, principally following the process in Section
6.1.2.
Parameter values
To ensure an equatable evaluation of performance metrics from the fine-tuned models
of AdaFace and MagFace, the same hyper-parameter values, data preprocessing tech-
niques and training duration parameters were used for both models, where applicable.
Compared to the original pre-trained hyper-parameters, the batch size was adjusted
from 512 to 256 due to computational resource constraints. Based on experiments on
convergence, the number of epochs was set to 26. The learning rate was set to 0.01,
with a learning rate drop ratio of 10% at epoch 12, 16, and 22. The default embedding

For AdaFace, these were s = 64.0, m = 0.5.

6.2.0.1
Choice of threshold
As emphasised above, the focus of this thesis is to perform an identification task aimed
at fairly identifying children in forensic scenarios, ensuring that the system performs
equally well for both adults and children. Clause 6.3 in ISO/IEC DIS 19795-10 states
that the selected threshold of the biometric system should be reflective of the intended
operational use case [121]. In forensic detection, it is more important to select a threshold
that results in a low FNIR, rather than focusing on minimising the FPIR. This approach
ensures that the system does not mistakenly reject a legitimate candidate, such as a
child. The appropriate threshold needs to be set based on the group which the FR
model performs the worst on. This group is denoted the underprivileged group. Thus,
to determine the appropriate threshold, the average FNIR for the underprivileged group
in two experiment trials is plotted against increasing threshold values. The threshold
value corresponding to a 5% false negative rate is then selected.
6.2.0.2
Controlling factors that influence performance
As stated in ISO/IEC 19795-1:2021(E) Clause 7.3, awareness concerning factors which
can influence biometric performance are important when testing biometric systems in
order to ensure confidence in the results obtained. As justified in Section 4.6.1.1 about
effect of image quality and by the findings of NIST concerning the relation between image
quality and FNIR [52], this thesis investigates the effect of image quality. Maintaining
a consistent image quality ensures that this variable does not affect the outcome of
the experiment, thus making it a controlled factor in the experimental setup. This is
performed in experiment 1.2 and experiment 2.3. The image quality is controlled by
carefully constraining the reduction of images to only include high-quality images in the
data set canonical children data set. Additionally, great attention have been given to
the data refinement and thus general extensive preprocessing as detailed in Section 5.2.1
have been performed.

6.4
Ethical considerations
In this thesis, considerations were made to ensure ethically responsible, transparent, and
accountable AI development. These considerations include choices made about the data
handling, the process documentation as well as the coding practises.
6.4.1
Considerations regarding data
Considerations concerning the selection of test set follows the guidelines stated in ISO/IEC
DIS 19795-1 clause 7.4.2, that the test crew shall not include people whose biometric
characteristics have been used to develop or tune the system which are being tested
[4]. Hence, the real-world data sets were chosen in accordance to not overlap with the
data sets the pre-trained models have been trained on. Additionally, as recommended
in ISO/IEC DIS 19795-1 Clause 7.4.9, this thesis explicitly indicates when results are de-
rived from synthetic samples, specifically in experiments 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3. Furthermore,
the purpose of using synthetic data sets is clearly stated throughout the thesis, which
is to enhance privacy-preserving fairness in line with the AI Act regarding open-source
AI development (detailed in Section 4.7).

6.4.2
Considerations regarding pre-trained models
The pre-trained AdaFace and MagFace models, were chosen to be of backbone iResnet18
trained on CASIA-Webface. This choice was made based on two criteria. The first is
as stated earlier, to not have overlapping images between the chosen real-world test set
and the data set, which the models have been trained on.
Secondly, these models trained on CASIA-Webface were chosen rather than models
trained on ’MS-Celeb-1M’ (MS1M), as Microsoft in April 2019 retracted MS1M and
subsequently advised against its use [69][123]. This was due to concerns regarding pri-
vacy and ethical issues surrounding the data set. MS1M contained biometric data of
nearly 100,000 identities, and its uncontrolled use across various repositories and research
projects raised significant ethical and legal challenges.
6.4.3
Carbon footprint
This thesis also intend to highlight the importance of documenting and reporting on the
environmental impact related to AI development. This thesis strives toward setting an
example for best practise with regards to disclosure as well as reduction of the carbon
footprint related to training and testing of the used models. For that purpose, CodeCar-
bon [124] and Weights & Biases (W&B) [125] are used to track the carbon dioxide (CO2)
emissions related to running the FR models by estimating the amount of CO2 produced
by the cloud and personal computing resources used to execute the code [124]. This will
contribute to the development of ethically responsible, transparent, and accountable AI.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
The five experiments, experiment 1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3 presented above, reveal a
performance bias in state-of-the-art FR models against images of children compared
to adults, and likewise against younger children within the children’s group. In this
chapter, the surrounding factors of these results and related bias mitigation techniques
are discussed. In particular, the potential in fine-tuning models with synthetic data is
addressed, although this approach unexpectedly amplified performance disparities. The
chapter also addresses human cognitive biases, such as the cross-race effect and the need
for fair and accurate FR systems in real-world applications, including border security.
Ethical considerations, including CO2 emissions and compliance with regulations, are
emphasised to ensure responsible AI development. Finally, the limitations of this work,
as well as future work, are presented.
8.1
Unfair performance on children by pre-trained
models
Results of baseline models - Experiment 1.1
Both MagFace and AdaFace showed a state-of-the-art performance, in accordance with
the literature, by producing feature embeddings that are similar for images of the same
identity and dissimilar for images of different identities. However, the false negative dif-
ferential performance observed in experiment 1.1., as indicated by FND>> 1 for both
MagFace and AdaFace, highlights significant disparities in the accuracy of FR systems
when identifying children compared to adults. The baseline results of experiment 1.1 also
reveal that children are identified with a higher FNIR and FPIR, compared to adults.
Several plausible explanations for this discrepancy are worth considering. Firstly, it is
important to note that the baseline models were trained on a data set where faces of 1-18
year-olds constituted only 1
8th of the total data set. This could possibly contribute to
the models’ poorer performance in identifying children. Secondly, the literature suggests
that image quality is a factor contributing to the discrepancy. Although the adult data
set was observed to have a larger amount of images with a high UQS, there are reasons
to believe that this is not the cause of the discrepancy.
Manual inspection revealed that the OFIQ underestimates the quality of children’s im-
ages, likely due to limitations in the MagFace model training, which predominantly in-
cludes adult images (reported in Section 4.3.1). The underestimation of children’s UQS
is evidenced by a right-skewed distribution, contrasting with a more uniformly right-
skewed distribution observed for adults (see the comparisons in Section 4.3.1). Hence,
the distribution of UQS for children is suspected to be inaccurate and unfair. It was
also found that the intra-class variation of UQS was larger for adults’ identities than for
==PAGE:97==
8.2 Model performance and image quality
89
children’s identities. This means that while the adult images scored higher overall, the
identities contained images spanning a broader spectrum of UQS. It was observed that
mated comparison scores between images with very different UQS were generally lower
than the mated comparison scores between images consistently rated as either low or
high in UQS. Hence, the importance of coherence between probe and references in terms
of UQS and the general look of the image is highlighted.
An image quality factor that could contribute to the discrepancy, as referred to in Sec-
tion 4.6.1.1, is the pitch angle of the images. Recall that images with severe pose angles
were filtered out from the adult data set, whereas this filtering was not applied to the
children’s data set due to its limited size. Nonetheless, the higher FNIR observed in
children could be influenced by the tendency for their images to be taken from elevated
angles, which can distort facial traits and make it more challenging for FR systems to ac-
curately process and identify the images. Considering all image quality factors, it would
have been preferable to control image quality by separating the images of adults and
children into canonical groups and making comparisons only within these groups. How-
ever, further investigations of the image quality outside of the time constraints would
be needed. In addition, it is worth noting that the literature shows that recognition
performance can also be influenced by the ethnicity of the individuals (see Section 3.2).
Therefore, the ethnic distributions of children and adults have been stratified to be com-
parable thus minimising this influence.
In summary, the results in experiment 1.1 support the existing literature, indicating that
pre-trained state-of-the-art FR models exhibit a performance bias against children (See
Section 3.1). Albeit, it is important to note that while most existing benchmarks for FR
systems in research focus on 1:1 verification, this thesis’ focus is on 1:N identification,
making direct comparisons to existing literature challenging. To achieve equity, further
application of bias and image-quality mitigation techniques are required in identification
scenarios. Without these advances, state-of-the-art models will continue to exhibit biases
and fail to provide equitable identification outcomes for all age groups.

8 Discussion
Critical towards state-of-the-art results
Many existing studies do not explicitly state whether they separate the effects of various
image quality factors from age bias. This lack of clarification can give the impression
these studies conform to the intuitive notion that children’s facial traits are more similar
to each other than those of adults [6][32][33]. Regarding image quality, the effect of
ethnicity does not appear to be adequately controlled for in these studies. For instance,
the developers of the RFW database [38], used as the adult data set in this thesis,
have shown that ethnicity impacts the performance of pre-trained state-of-the-art FR
models. Therefore, this thesis is critical towards the results of these studies and finds
that the statements about degraded recognition of children cannot be solely attributed
to the different and less distinct facial characteristics of children compared to adults.
This is due to the lack of explicit control for both image quality, ethnicity, and other
demographic factors. To address this clarification gap, this thesis attempts to control
for image quality and ethnic distributions between age groups, ensuring balanced ethnic
groups in both children and adult test data sets. Additionally, this thesis recommends
that further investigations of age bias should be conducted by controlling for both image
quality and demographic distributions between age groups.
8.3
Fairness versus recognition accuracy
Results of bias mitigation through fine-tuning - Experiment 2.1
and 2.2
Experiments 2.1 and 2.2 both produced results that contradicted the hypothesis based
on existing literature, as the FND increased significantly for both MagFace and AdaFace
compared to the baseline FND. These results were obtained regardless of whether the
models were trained on synthetic data with lower intra-class variations (experiment 2.1)
or higher intra-class variation (experiment 2.2).
The hypothesis proposed that fine-
tuning state-of-the-art models with synthetic data of children would primarily enhance
the recognition performance for children, thereby reducing the performance gap between
children and adults and enhancing fairness. Although the findings revealed that the fine-
tuning correctly improved recognition performance for children in terms of lower FNIR,
it also enhanced the recognition performance for adults even further, ultimately leading
to a significantly amplified disparity.
The same contradictory finding has been observed for another biometric characteristic
input, voice. In the paper A Comparison of Differential Performance Metrics for the
Evaluation of Automatic Speaker Verification Fairness [130], the authors found that the
best performing model in terms of fairness, using GARBE as the fairness metric, per-
formed the worst in terms of accuracy measured by the overall ratio of error rates. In
hindsight, Zhang et al. released the study Bias Mitigation in Fine-tuning Pre-trained
Models for Enhanced Fairness and Eﬀiciency in March 2024 [95]. They support the
==PAGE:99==
8.3 Fairness versus recognition accuracy
91
view that ”fine-tuning pre-trained models on new tasks can result in unpredictable un-
fair outcomes” [95]. They only provide a single statement, without further justifications,
attributing the issue primarily to a lack of confidence in the model’s ability to generalise
beyond the specific data it was trained on, especially when applied to new, unseen data.
This is an interesting aspect to explore further.
Another possible explanation for the unexpected findings is detailed here. The method
of fine-tuning only the batch normalisation layers was the recommended strategy for fine-
tuning face identification tasks (See Section 6.1.3). This approach helps preserve the
learned representations in the earlier layers of the pre-trained model while adapting it to
the specific statistics and distributions of the new data set. However, if the pre-trained
model exhibits bias towards adults, as observed in experiment 1.1 and documented in
the literature, this bias could persist even after fine-tuning with additional high-quality
synthetic data of children. This could be because the synthetic data which might intro-
duce new patterns or variations that weren’t as prominent in the original data set. If
these patterns are similar to those found in adults, the model might unintentionally learn
to recognise these features as indicative of adults, especially when the synthetic data
quality is high and the features are consistent across both data sets. This unintended
strengthening of the model’s focus on adults highlights the challenge of balancing and
managing biases in data sets, affecting the model’ performance for different age groups.
Additionally, the performance improvement for the real test set of children compared to
adults might not be as pronounced due to possible overfitting on the relatively homo-
geneous synthetic data set. This overfitting can cause the model to memorise specific
details of children rather than learning the generalisable patterns present in a data set
of real images of children. In contrast, the pre-trained model is primarily trained on real
images of adults. This conclusion holds even for experiment 2.2, which used high varia-
tion synthetic data, as the results did not significantly differ from experiment 2.1. It is
important to note that studying the accuracy on a validation set could have highlighted
potential overfitting issues. However, the original implementation of MagFace [127] did
not implement a validation step, so this aspect was not adjusted. Nevertheless, Zhang
et al. propose a bias mitigation method to address demographic biases in pre-trained
models, regardless of whether they were developed with fairness considerations. They
find that bias is evident in the distinct weights of pre-trained models. Their method neu-
tralises the influential weights, making it challenging to differentiate group information
from the weights. This method show results of both lower biases and reduce prediction
errors [95].
Although this thesis has not reported or optimised for fairness in the objective function
during the fine-tuning phase - a limit not recognised - the bias mitigation strategy of
Zhang et al. seems promising for future application, when using fine-tuning as a bias
mitigation technique. It is also worth noting that fine-tuning AdaFace resulted in a
significant improvement in children’s FNIR compared to the children’s baseline FNIR,
a result not seen with the fine-tuning of MagFace.
This could be explained by the

differences in loss functions. AdaFace incorporates an adaptive loss function, which can
lead to improved generalisation by preventing the model from overfitting to easy samples

Meaningful mitigation vectors
Using bias mitigation through fine-tuning with synthetic data did not result in the
desired level of fairness. Therefore, future research would need to explore other bias
mitigation techniques in practice. This section aims to address these techniques theoret-
ically, which is a part of the third research question. This thesis has mainly focused on
the pre-processing and in-processing bias mitigation methods.
The naive pre-processing technique encompassed the altering of training data with syn-
thetic data to over-represent the underprivileged group. Adjustments to this method
could have been to experiment with balancing the data set to ensure equal representation
of both children and adults, combining this approach with fine-tuning a larger number
of parameters in the pre-trained model. By employing this strategy and avoiding over-
fitting, the middle feature maps could learn to capture general features of both adults
and children on a more equal basis. However, this could also be more computationally
expensive.
The in-processing technique encompassed the fine-tuning of the state-of-the-art pre-
trained models batch normalisation layers. Adjustments to this strategy could include
implementing the fine-tuning method suggested by Zhang et al., which involves neutral-
ising the weights that influence demographic groups, as described above [95]. Other
in-processing techniques could be considered, such as introducing fairness constraints
into the loss function to explicitly penalise disparities based on demographic attributes
using labels of the groups as input as well.
A bias mitigation strategy not explored in this thesis is the post-processing strategy. In
this category, equal opportunity threshold optimisation (EOTO) [97] could be a viable
bias mitigation strategy. This method aims to ensure that the model achieves equal
false positive and false negative rates across different groups, by re-calibrating scores or
thresholds based on demographic attributes, after the predictions are made.
However, using EOTO to achieve equal opportunity between demographic groups usually
comes at the cost of overall model performance, as the performance for the privileged
group is lowered.
Thus while striving for higher fairness with this method, it does
so at the expense of accuracy for the initially better-performing group. This ethical
==PAGE:101==

93
dilemma should be carefully considered. Certainly, models should comply with false
negative levels such as maintaining a FNIR of 5% (or other appropriate levels for the
specific application) for the worst performing group. However, addressing the ethical
dilemma may also establish that security or inconvenience standards are reflected in
fairness metrics, thereby preventing discriminatory outcomes in FR systems.
8.4
Age bias
The results so far have concentrated on identifying the presence of age bias in the
models by comparing performance between children and adults, and the findings indi-
cate an age bias favouring adults over children. The following section investigates the
finer-granularity bias, when children’s performance are compared within the children’s
different age groups.
Differential performance between age groups - Experiment 2.3
Experiment 2.3 involved comparing the predictions of the fine-tuned MagFace model
solely on the canonical images of the different children’s age groups, thereby controlling
for image quality across age groups as detailed in Section 8.2. The results demonstrated
that the youngest children, aged 1-3, exhibit the highest error rates in both FNIR and
FPIR compared to the older age groups. These findings confirms observations found
by Falkenberg et al. who achieved a similar trend on their state-of-the-art pre-trained
FR systems on synthetic data, where d’ decreased for the younger children’s age group
(where higher d’ means that the system can better distinguish between mated and non-
mated distributions) [10]. The benefit of the study of Falkenberg et al. using synthetic
data as test data is that image quality factors can be controlled. Hence, synthetic data
of both children and adults could potentially be used as test data, to control for image
quality when investigating age bias.
While this thesis primarily investigates age bias among different age groups of children
and between children and adults, it is important to acknowledge the existence of age
bias against older individuals as well [131]. Grimmer et al. highlight that young adults
aged 20-40 are typically over-represented in open-source data sets. They suggest that
synthetically generated images can be added to the minority age groups to represent
each age group with the same number of samples, to achieve a more balanced data set.
It is also noteworthy that, among the covariates of the demographic variables sex, eth-
nicity, and age, the age covariate has been the least studied [5]. This gap in research
has resulted in a limited understanding of how age related factors influence the accu-
racy and fairness of FR systems. The findings of this thesis contribute to the literature
on age bias in FR models by using real data, thereby representing real-world scenarios.
By focusing on real data, this study underscores the importance of considering age di-

performance across all age groups.

Ethical development
Carbon emission from fine-tuning
Although this thesis works on relatively shallow models compared to the FR models
deployed by the industry, it was decided to follow best practice for ethical, transparent,
and accountable AI development and report related CO2 emissions. By reporting this
metric, the thesis works on integrating accountability into the AI development process.
Accountability and transparency within development has received increasingly more at-
tention as the environmental effect of AI has come to the attention of society [132]. The
documentation of CO2 emission adds to the considerations of whether the development
of said AI is worth the trade-off between environmental impact and societal impact. This
documentation also has the potential to pressure or motivate organisations and individ-
uals using AI to consider how to make their AI implementations less environmentally
damaging both in the present and in the future [132]. To be noted, using the CodeCar-
bon to track the CO2 emission (See Section 7.6) adds additional CO2 emission, as the
code pipeline becomes heavier to execute. Nevertheless, the package is lightweight [124]
and the trade off between saving the additional CO2 and the knowledge the tracking
brings, the authors of this thesis believe it is justifiable to add for the sake of cultivating
responsible, transparent, and accountable AI.
Compliance with regulations
This section addresses the fourth research question theoretically by exploring how the
integration of additional synthetic child data into FR systems, particularly for the pur-
poses of identifying missing children and bio-forensic detection of child exploitation,
aligns with the standards for responsible development of AI systems as set by ISO/IEC
9868 standard and the AI Act.
ISO/IEC 9868 standard
The ISO/IEC 9868 generally concerns appropriateness of system, system management,
data, data management, security, etc. [61]. As demonstrated, training the specific FR
systems utilised in this thesis on synthetic child data significantly enhances their effec-
tiveness in identifying children. The argument is that to better find children, a higher
system accuracy on children is appropriate, and that the improved identification perfor-
mance on children highlights the need for data sets tailored to the target demographic
of the task.

Higher system accuracy in identifying children is not only advantageous, but essential for
tasks that involve locating missing children. The alignment with ISO/IEC 9868 clause
9.3, which recommends to remove identifiers and delete linked biometric references when
data retention is no longer justified, further strengthens this argument [61]. Data reten-
tion with synthetic data does not reach unjustifiable as real world data does, as no
individual’s privacy is compromised by keeping the data. Additionally, by using syn-
thetically generated child data, the training set is highly customisable, thus unbalanced
and inappropriate data sets will be a problem of the past. By leveraging synthetically
generated data, developers can ensure that the training data precisely meet the system’s
needs.
The AI Act
As stated in the AI Act article 27, the Act builds upon 2019 Ethics guidelines for
trustworthy AI developed by an independent high-level expert group on artificial intel-
ligence [103]. In the ethical guidelines, seven principles are presented. Included in these
are principles concerning privacy and data governance, transparency, diversity, non-
discrimination and fairness. Training FR models on synthetically generated images of
children follows the guideline of privacy and data governance within the FR pipeline,
with the same arguments as presented above that no individual’s privacy is compromised
with synthetically generated data. However, within the pipeline for generating synthetic
data, the guidelines for privacy and data governance are not certain to be followed.
Synthetic data lives up to the guideline of diversity in the data, as developers can
generate and ensure certain diversity on different demographics. However, the use of
synthetic data for fine-tuning, solely based on the findings of this thesis, does not align
with the principles of non-discrimination and fairness, as the models in experiment 2.1
and 2.2 are seen to be less fair and in that connection more discriminating. To verify
or reject this finding, recreation of the experiments with real-world data is suggested.
Hence, it cannot be stated on the basis of the obtained results whether the use of
synthetic data aligns with these guidelines in other cases.
Legacy issues
Important to acknowledge is the potential issues of using synthetic data due to its legacy,
which would lead to violations of ISO/IEC 9868 and the AI Act. The issues are related
to the fact that the generated images are made by models trained on some other data.
This training data might be real-world data, hence issues concerning privacy of the
depicted individuals arise, or the training data might be synthetic data, hence issues
concerning model collapse and the curse of recursion arise. If trained on real-world data,
the data needs to be sourced ethically, and should not violate any GDPR requirements.
The training data should be investigated thoroughly for various biases, as the biases
contained in the training data have the potential to leak into the later generated data

Therefore, developers and creators of synthetic data need to be aware of the privacy

8.6
Technology for real world applications
This paragraph elaborates on how the decisions made and findings obtained in this thesis
relate to real-world applications. In the scenario addressed in this thesis, prioritising the
FNIR over the FPIR is essential. This is because falsely overlooking (i.e. rejecting) a
missing child as not known has far greater severe consequences than falsely labelling a
child as known in the missing child database. While the latter can result in a significant
number of false leads in an investigation, failing to identify a genuine missing child could
potentially endanger their life further. Therefore, the threshold is established based on
an acceptable false negative level of FNIR. In other real-world applications, FPIR may
hold greater importance.
For example, in scenarios where a false positive results in
wrongful accusations of the probe being identified.
Border security and hybrid warfare
At the time of the writing of this thesis, the European Union is facing a significant is-
sue of spies entering European countries through Belarus, disguised as immigrants [134].
The current Belarus-EU border crisis is described as an ongoing hybrid warfare and
migrant crisis, where Belarus is accused of weaponising migrants to destabilise neigh-
bouring countries [134]. Recent news reveals that young people are being lured with
promises of easy and quick money through the messaging service Telegram in exchange
for painting pro-Russian graﬀiti. The payment increases if they agree to monitor infras-
tructure and report back to Russia [135]. In addition, immigrants are promised easy
access to Europe through the Belarusian border. This is explained by Thomas Wegner
Friis, Associate Professor in the Department of Culture and Language at SDU Denmark
[136], in the Danish news media Genstart. In light of these strategies by Russia and
Belarus, the European Union has strengthened their efforts to secure European borders
[134]. By applying remote biometric identification systems, authorities can better screen
individuals entering the country, potentially identifying spies or other security threats,
while also recognising and assisting genuine refugees.
Worth emphasising is that the authors believe FR should be limited to its intended
purposes, within the reasonable capabilities of the technology. Before developing any
application, it is crucial to initially address whether the use of FR is appropriate for

the specific task at hand. The applications’ use should be justified by a clear need and
should not infringe more on individual rights than required to achieve the legitimate

Finding long-term missing children
While FR systems have indeed shown the ability to recognise and find children in today’s
society, e.g. by the Indian police force using this technology [36], the crucial problem
of missing children remains, and it is essential that every aspect of the FR system that
can be improved is addressed. There is an identified gap that includes the longitudinal
performance of FR systems, which is critical to test and improve. A recent study by
Bahman et al. [8] found that state-of-the-art FR models demonstrate significant differ-
ences in match scores when the age gap between probe and reference is above 6 months,
between children in the age 3-14, and adults. To some extent, the authors found that
by using MagFace as a loss function and their controlled cross-age FR data set Young
Face Ageing for verification in FR systems can achieve high accuracy for age gaps up to
three years, with a 0.1% false acceptance rate.
The effect of age gaps was not profoundly tested for in this thesis except for the reduction
of the test data to exclude age gaps larger than 10 years. The mentioned findings suggest,
that there is still a longitudinal performance gap to close. As a step in this direction,
Chandaliya et al [139] has developed ChildGAN, a promising method for age progression
and rejuvenation of face images to synthetically generate face images with realistic traits.
Further work for this thesis could involve estimating the effects of age gaps by using
ChildGAN to expand the current data set of children, and subsequently estimating and
experimenting with bias mitigation techniques to reduce this gap.

8.6.1
Human bias
While this thesis primarily focuses on the bias present in AI models, it is essential not
to overlook the inherent bias in the humans that the FR models are designed to assist.
One prominent example of human bias is the cross-race effect, also known as cross-race
bias, other-race bias, own-race bias, or other-race effect. This bias is the tendency of
humans to more easily recognise faces that belong to one’s own ethnic group, or ethnic
groups that one has a close connection with [140].
The cross-race effect is not only evident in human cognition but also reflected in software
development and the training data used for AI models. Phillips et al. provided evidence
that algorithms developed in Asia are more effective at recognising Asian individuals,
while algorithms developed in Europe are more proficient at recognising Caucasians
[141][5]. These findings indicate that the biases inherent in humans are mirrored in
biometric identification models, as these models are build by and trained on data that
reflects the developers’ demographic context.
Therefore, it is reasonable to assume that the cross-race effect is present among human
investigators manually searching for missing children. Additionally, the use of biometric
identification models will not eliminate all biases, as these models also exhibit such bi-
ases, potentially amplifying human cognitive bias [59]. However, according to Cognitec’s
white paper on demographic effects, the best models in the NIST tests are twenty times
more accurate than even the most skilled human adjudicators [100][142]. This indicates
that despite inherent biases, advanced facial recognition models can significantly surpass
human performance in accuracy.
Therefore, there is a substantial need for largely unbiased biometric identification sys-
tems to mitigate human bias and enhance the accuracy and fairness of FR efforts in lo-
cating missing children more eﬀiciently. A key consideration in developing these systems
is establishing the threshold at which a model is deemed suﬀiciently effective. Should a
model be considered acceptable if it only slightly outperforms humans in the FR task?
There are no clear thresholds provided by regulations or the literature on this matter
yet.
8.7
Limitations and future perspectives
As previously discussed, this thesis employed synthetic data of children to fine-tune
state-of-the-art FR models as a bias mitigation strategy. However, this approach did
not lead to increased fairness between the two demographic groups, children and adults.
This could be explained by several factors and limitations of the work. One limitation
might be assigned to the data choice to fine-tune with, which instead of only contain-
ing synthetic images of children, also could have been of synthetic images of adults, to
avoid problems of overfitting as explained in Section 8.3. Another influencing factor
==PAGE:107==
8.7 Limitations and future perspectives
99
could be the choice of the relatively shallow backbone architecture iResNet18, chosen as
this was the only common backbone architecture between the pre-trained MagFace and
AdaFace models. Fine-tuning of deeper models such as iResNet50 or iResNet100 could
have captured more general patterns of the children, instead of adapting too closely
to the fine-tuning data. For example, ResNet100 has shown to be powerful in other,
similar applications such as Morphing Attack Detection [143]. Additionally, with more
time, further adjustments to data augmentation techniques or experimentation with
hyper-parameters, such as regularisation and margin-based parameters in the FR mod-
els, could have been explored.
Another adjustment could have involved using a test set that includes both demographic
groups within a single data set, collected by the same team and from similar sources,
rather than using two separate test data sets. This approach would ensure more con-
sistent image quality across the demographic groups, allowing for better control and a
clearer evaluation of whether the fine-tuning strategy works equally well for both groups.
Future work
As presented, this thesis has so far contributed to the existing literature by demonstrat-
ing the performance of using synthetic data to fine-tune state-of-the-art FR models as
a bias mitigation technique to enhance fairness between children and adults. To assist
further work in this direction, the GARBE metric was also reported, as this has been
used in state-of-the-art research, although not recommended by ISO/IEC DIS 19795-
10 [2] to be reported as a measure of system performance variation across demographic
groups. Nevertheless, the GARBE metric revealed the same levels of unfairness between
the experiments, as the FND reported in this thesis. Correspondingly, the reporting of
mandatory identification system performance metrics were performed in compliance with
ISO/IEC 19795-1:2021 Subclause 12.7 [2]. Further work would involve specifying the
test details in accordance with ISO/IEC 19795-1:2021 Subclause 12.1, should a FR sys-
tem from this thesis be deployed.
To meet the limitations mentioned, further work could constitute in experimenting with
other bias mitigation strategies such as the post-processing technique EOTO, or in-
processing techniques such as adjusting the weights in the network to give more impor-
tance to children’s images. If post-processing bias mitigation showed promising results,
it furthermore carries the advantage of being less costly in terms of computational com-
plexity, and more easy to adopt, as it could be applied on existing FR models exhibiting
bias.
A strategy to inspect the type of bias could also be to apply explainable AI into the
inference process. For instance, saliency maps can be used to highlight the pixels most
relevant for the CNN’s decision, specifically which pixels to change, to most eﬀiciently
change the model’s prediction [144]. This approach could enable the interpretation of

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
In this thesis, an extensive study on enhancing demographic fairness in state-of-the-art
face identification systems through a responsible development framework in line with
the AI Act and ISO/IEC standards has been conducted. The investigation aimed to ad-
dress several critical questions to ensure these systems perform equitably across different
age groups, particularly between young and adult faces, with the primary motivation of
ensuring equal performance of demographic groups in real-world applications, such as
identifying missing people and forensic analysis using face identification systems.
Firstly, the research assessed the equity of the pre-trained FR models MagFace 1.1 and
AdaFace 1.1 in identifying young and adult faces by evaluating the FNIR and FPIR as
a baseline experiment. It was found that significant disparities exist, with adult faces
generally being identified with higher accuracy than young faces, using a threshold cor-
responding to a false negative level of 5% FNIR for the underprivileged group, children.
The fairness metric, FND, highlighted these biases.
To address image quality issues, a great effort was made to ensure equal image qual-
ity distribution between the demographic groups in the pre-processing step using the
OFIQ software. Further experiments aimed to separate the effects of image quality from
age bias by comparing the identification performance of mixed-quality and high-quality
(canonical) images within the children’s group. The FND indicated unfair performance,
with system performance on canonical images achieving lower false identification rates,
underscoring the need for improved handling of diverse image qualities to mitigate bias.
The second part of the research focused on fine-tuning FR models using synthetic chil-
dren’s face images of varying intra-class variations as a bias mitigation strategy to en-
hance equity. AdaFace 2.1 and AdaFace 2.2 showed significant improvement of up to
20% in identifying young faces. However, for all fine-tuned models, the improvement of
adult performance was significantly higher than for the children, ultimately creating a
larger gap between identification performance and hence less fair FR models for the chil-
dren compared to the baseline models. Investigating the fairness within the children’s
age groups, the largest unfairness in performance was seen between the oldest age group
and every other age group. This highlights inherent age bias in the model’s performance.
With these findings, the fine-tuning approach of this thesis did not entirely eliminate
biases, encouraging further exploration of additional bias mitigation techniques.
Fu-
ture efforts could employ in-processing methods to neutralise the weights influencing
the demographic groups during fine-tuning or adjusting existing loss functions to pe-
nalise privileged groups more effectively. Given suﬀicient time, this thesis recommends
investing additional efforts in ensuring comparable image quality among test groups to
facilitate fair evaluations.

102
9 Conclusion
Moreover, the thesis examined the alignment of training FR systems on synthetic child
data with standards for responsible AI development, particularly for high-risk appli-
cations. The analysis confirmed that compliance with the AI Act permits the use of
FR systems by law enforcement for bio-forensic detection or locating missing children,
despite these systems being classified as high-risk. Nonetheless, the AI Act requires
sensitive data handling of children’s faces and ensures their right to protection from
technology that discriminates based on demographics. Using synthetic data of children
represents a step toward meeting these requirements, but it is crucial to ensure sensitive
data handling throughout the entire pipeline used to generate these images. However
for AI systems released under free and open-source licenses, such as this thesis, the AI
Act permits the use of synthetic data alongside real data in pipelines. While adhering
to ISO/IEC standards for biometrics to the extent possible for research purposes, this
study found that recommended security levels for calculating identification errors are
less established in the literature for identification compared to verification tasks. Con-
sequently, the thesis offers its recommendations at this level for the task of identifying
missing children.
In summary, this thesis contributes valuable insights into enhancing fairness in FR sys-
tems, particularly in the case of identification. The findings advocate for a multi-faceted
approach to bias mitigation, combining data augmentation, algorithmic adjustments,
and compliance with ethical standards, including environmental awareness. As FR tech-
nology continues to evolve, ensuring equitable performance across diverse demographic
groups remains a crucial objective, aligning with broader goals of fairness and responsi-
bility in AI development.
