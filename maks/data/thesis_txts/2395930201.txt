Summary (English)
Cardiovascular diseases are the leading cause of death worldwide, including carotid artery diseases
where plaque are built up in the carotid arteries, causing a narrowing, is known as atherosclerosis.
The pressure gradient across this narrowing, a stenosis, is a valuable tool when assessing the severity
of atherosclerosis. The most common method for estimating the pressure is by the use of ﬂuid-ﬁlled
catheters. The technique contains some disadvantages, including being invasive. In this project,
a ﬁnite element (FE) model is created with the aim of making a realistic representation of an
experimental setup. The FE models are simulated in COMSOL Multiphysics®. The experimental
setup is used to measure pressure and velocities in phantoms mimicking human arteries.
By
creating the model as a ﬂuid-structure-interaction, it is possible to simulate the movement of the
arterial wall associated with a pulsatile ﬂow. The project investigated one carotid artery phantom
from a healthy patient, and one straight tube phantom containing a stenosis. The similarities
between the experimental setup and the simulated model for the straight tube gave the best
results, whereas inconsistencies between the experimental setup and the simulated carotid artery
model aﬀected the outcome when comparing the results. In conclusion, creating the FEM study
as a ﬂuid-solid-interaction model has improved the results and made the model a more realistic
representation of the actual setup - however, it is also seen that lack of consistencies in the geometry
between the two studies aﬀects the result.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Cardiovascular diseases are the leading cause of death worldwide mainly caused by unhealthy diet,
physical inactivity, smoking and high consumption of alcohol.
The eﬀect of this lifestyle may
show up as an increase in blood pressure (BP). Following an unhealthy lifestyle, combined with
an increase in BP, it can lead to atherosclerosis, where plaques are building up inside the arteries.
These plaques contain cholesterol, fat, calcium, and other substances in the blood. Over time,
these plaques harden, causing a narrowing of the arteries which leads to a decrease in the blood
supply to organs and tissue. If plaques are build up in the arteries inside the neck, the carotid
arteries, it can cause an embolism from the plaque, possibly leading to a stroke. By monitoring the
change in BP and the ﬂow patterns in the arteries, an early diagnosis and treatment can be made to
avoid these cases. As of today, Magnetic Resonance Imaging, Particle Image Velocimetry [1], and
Ultrasound can visualize the ﬂow patterns, and ﬂuid-ﬁlled catheters can estimate the pressure. A
change in pressure and ﬂow could be observed after insertion of a catheter. By using ﬁnite element
modeling, it is possible to visualize some of these eﬀects and changes.
1.2
Purpose of the Project
The purpose of this project is continue and reﬁne the development of an existing Computational
Fluid Dynamics (CFD) model of the carotid artery. This model can serve as ground truth when
validating direct pressure measurements with ultrasound. Improvements are made to the model
by including wall movement, and by adjusting the outﬂow.

Background
This chapter describes the background, including a brief description of the cardiovascular system,

2.1
The Cardiovascular System
The cardiovascular system is responsible for the transport of oxygen, nutrient supply and removal
of waste products throughout the body. The cardiovascular system is divided into two system, the
pulmonary and the systemic. The pulmonary transport oxygen-poor blood from the heart to the
lungs, whereas the systemic carries the oxygen-rich blood from the heart to the rest of the body.
Figure 2.1 illustrates the cardiovascular system with some of the major veins and arteries. The
heart functions as a pump with four chambers, right and left atrium and right and left ventricle.
The ventricles act as the main pumps. In both of the two systems, the blood is transported due
to a pressure gradient. [2]
Figure 2.1: Schematic illustration of the cardiovascular system. [3]

2.1.1
Cardiovascular Diseases
Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) are the dominant cause of death where most of the cases occur due
to the formation of plaques in the arteries. It is known that a high level of cholesterol aﬀects the
progression of possible atherosclerosis. Ultrasound is an important tool for assessing the severity
of atherosclerosis. [4]
Atherosclerosis is a widespread disease in the Western world aﬀecting the arteries. The preva-
lence is increasing.
The most common is the narrowing of the arteries, leading to blood ﬂow
reduction which aﬀects the ﬂow to tissue. [5] Several diﬀerent methods are used to quantify the
severity of the stenosis. Figure 2.2 shows an example of the diﬀerent way of calculating the degree
of the stenosis. These three methods are diﬀerent in quantifying the degree of the stenosis in the
internal carotid artery (ICA). The North American Symptomatic Carotid Endarterectomy Trial
(NASCET) method, with the use of this method it is the normal distal ICA which is the denomina-
tor, and it is widely used in North America. The European Carotid Surgery Trial (ECST) method,
uses the width of the original artery at the site of maximal narrowing as the denominator and is
more frequently used in Europe. Some are also using the common carotid (CC) method, here it
is the proximal common carotid artery (CCA) which is the denominator. Despite the diﬀerence
in the methods, all of the methods are well-understood when it comes categorizing the severity of
the stenosis, which vary due to the diﬀerent way of calculating the stenosis degree.

Cardiovascular Fluid Dynamics
Cardiovascular ﬂuid dynamics are used to describe the dynamics occurring in the cardiovascular
system. Within this, steady, turbulent and pulsatile ﬂow can be described. Cardiovascular dy-
namics focuses on the measurement and analysis of ﬂow, volume and blood pressure within the
cardiovascular system. With a system being as complex as this, mechanical models which often
are formulated with mathematical equations are trying to understand and identify physiological
mechanism. Two reasonable assumptions can be made for the blood ﬂow in major vessels with
Newtonian and incompressible behavior [8]. Which result in Navier-Stokes equation

The Navier-Stokes equation descries the ﬂuid mechanics that occur within the blood vessels. How-

describing this motion.
2.2.1
Blood ﬂow and properties
Throughout most of the circulatory system, the blood ﬂow is laminar. The laminar ﬂow consists of
blood layers moving parallel in the blood vessels, with the highest velocity found in the centre and
the lowest along the wall of the blood vessels. When the ﬂow is fully developed, the ﬂow proﬁle
will be of a parabolic shape in straight blood vessels. Blood does not exhibit a constant viscosity
at all ﬂow rates, and when it comes to non-Newtonian1 behavior in the microcirculatory system.
When blood exhibit non-Newtonian behavior it is most evident when the blood clump together
and form large particles at low shear rates. The non-Newtonian behavior occurs in small branches
and capillaries. In most arteries, the blood acts as Newtonian2, and the viscosity can be assumed
to be constant, at 4 · e−3 Pa · s [10]. Furthermore blood is an incompressible ﬂuid with a density
ranging from 1040-1060 kg/m3 [11].
2.3
Carotid Artery
The carotid arteries are the major blood vessels supplying the brain, neck, and face with blood.
The carotid arteries are located on each side of the neck. Each of the two carotid arteries branches
into two, the internal carotid artery (ICA), which supplied the brain with blood, and the external
carotid artery (ECA) which supplies the neck and the face. [7] It is possible for doctors to listen to
the carotid artery using a stethoscope and thus being able to detect a swishing noise. This noise
can be a sign of turbulent ﬂow caused by atherosclerosis. Diagnostics tests can be used to detect
the possible narrowing of the carotid arteries. This could include Doppler US, CT angiogram

2.4
Pressure Catheter
Essential information regarding the severity of a variety of diseases can be provided with accurate
measurements of the blood pressure. The use of a ﬂuid-ﬁlled catheter is the most common method
for estimating the blood pressure, despite improvement in the noninvasive techniques. The accu-
racy of using the catheter contains have limitations. These limitations include the reﬂection of the
pressure wave and distortion inside the probe. The stiﬀness of the catheter will in general increase
the measurement accuracy. However, it is more advantageous to have a compliant catheter mate-
rial when navigating in complicated vessel geometries. Moreover, the ratio between the catheter
radius and the vessel radius can cause a partial obstruction in the vessel lumen, with the result
of an overestimation of the actual pressure. In situations including patients, these artifacts can
result in signiﬁcant disagreements when evaluating the severity of the disease based on the data
obtained from the catheter. [13] The invasive pressure estimation technique also include disadvan-
tages as; air bubbles in the system, clots building up at the tip of the catheter or the catheter tip
is leaning towards the wall of the vessel, and the fact of being invasive includes other risks such as
infection. [14]

Vector Flow Imaging
Ultrasound is an imaging technique, resulting in a 2D image of a 3D structure.
US is used
to visualize structures in the body, including organs and blood vessels. Often US is used as a
diagnostic tool. US is sound waves containing high frequencies above the audible range (20kHz)

2.6 Computational Fluid Dynamics

[15]. The images are created when the pulses are sent into the tissue using a probe and received
as echoes from the tissue, where diﬀerent tissue types reﬂect the sound diﬀerently. US is the ﬁrst
choice when evaluating diﬀerent vascular conditions. US is a non-ionizing image modality and is
relatively inexpensive. The B-mode US is used when looking at the tissue, and the blood ﬂow
can be evaluated with Doppler1. Doppler cannot detect ﬂow perpendicular to the direction of the
beam, and therefore not visualize complex ﬂow patterns. With vector ﬂow imaging (VFI) it is
possible to visualize these complex ﬂow patterns. [16] With VFI it is possible to see and estimate
the direction of the blood ﬂow, along with an estimation of the velocity of the blood ﬂow in all
directions at any given angle.
2.6
Computational Fluid Dynamics
Computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) analyses systems which involve ﬂuid ﬂow, by means of
computer-basedimulation and is used as an application within the biomedical engineering ﬁeld to
analyze blood ﬂow through veins and arteries. The CFD modeling can produce large amounts of
results virtually and is cheap to optimize, compared to, e.g., optimizing or changing experimental
setups. The CFD modeling includes three steps, a pre-processor, a solver and a post processor.
The pre-processing consists of a ﬂow problem, the solver which here is the ﬁnite element method
and a post-processor which include diﬀerent vector, contour and surface plots. [17]
The Finite Element Method and Meshing
Within the ﬁnite element method, the geometry should be deﬁned, the volume including the ﬂuid
is divided into discrete parts which are meshed. The mesh can have diﬀerent geometric structures
(ex. hexahedral, tetrahedral, prismatic or pyramidal). [18] The physical modeling has to be de-
ﬁned, consisting of a set of equations describing the ﬂuid motion - and a set of boundary condition
are deﬁned. The meshing of the geometry is an important part of the FEM. Before being able
to compute the solution, the geometry is assigned a mesh, either consisting of one type or a mix
of these hexahedral, tetrahedral, prismatic or pyramidal elements. The accuracy of the model is
related to the used mesh, which however increases the computation time. [19]

elements. The tetrahedral element shape can mesh any 3D volume, regardless of its shape. In
most cases a ﬁner mesh will give a better result, however, the ﬁner mesh will, of course, increase
the resolution of the mesh, but it will also increase the computation time of the model.

3.4.7
Model Improvement
The US videos processed show a distinct movement of the vessel walls. However, by investigating
the wall motion of the vessel in the simulated 3D model, the movement only caused an approxi-
mately increase in the vessel radius of 4.4% for the straight tube and 0.1% for the patient. It is
desired to reach an increase in the radius of approximately 7-10% [20]. By changing the stiﬀness
of the solid domain, Young’s Modulus, it was possible to meet that criterion. Individual changes
in Young’s Modulus in the two models were made due to the size of the complete models being
diﬀerent. For the straight tubes, studies with Young’s Modulus of 2e5Pa and 1e5Pa was carried
out. For the patient, studies with a Young’s Modulus of 1e5Pa, 4e4Pa, 1e4Pa and 4e3Pa were
carried out. Due to the size diﬀerence, it wasn’t possible to reach the 7-10% radius increase if
the two models have the same Young’s modulus. The initial values for Young’s Modulus, and the
new ones are listed in table 3.12 and in table 3.13 is listed the old and the new radius increases.
The increase in the straight tube vessel radius is calculated before the stenosis at 40mm before the
inlet, and 13mm from the inlet of the patient.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The purpose of this project was to reﬁne a CFD model. In this chapter, the results for the straight
tube and the patient are discussed along with the method used. Furthermore it is explained how
the numerical model diﬀers from that of the setup, and ﬁnally, the limitations of the study are
presented and discussed along with thoughts on a future work and reﬁnement.
5.1
Straight Tube Study
The presented results from this FSI model gives a much better approximation of the detected
velocities from the VFI data, compared to the previous models with rigid walls. The diﬀerence
between the simulated velocities and the experimentally measured velocities has decreased notably.
The velocities in the simulated models are likely to take higher values due to the fact of being able
to extract value from one speciﬁc point in the simulated situation. Whereas the US, ﬁrst of all,
tend to underestimate the velocities as a result of averaging over an area and a directly comparing
is doubtful when chosing the point manually. However, this should demonstrate that a model with
implemented wall movement gives a more realistic description of the velocities.
The pressure measurements diﬀer in the size of the pressure, however, when examining the pres-
sure diﬀerence between the simulated and the measured data, the pressure diﬀerence is not that
evident. The main reason for the diﬀerence observed between FE model and the experimental
measured pressure data is the diﬀerence in the pressure. The outlet pressure is set to 0P (1atm) in
the FE model, whereas the measured pressure at the outlet of the phantom is much higher. When
having this outlet condition in the FE model, the pressure is forced to decrease towards zero.
When comparing the resemblance in the two models, it is clear how the simulated model only shows
a high degree of movement before the stenosis compared to what is observed in the US, where the
==PAGE:60==
42
Discussion
wall is pulsating along the whole length of the wall. The ﬂow pattern seen in the straight tube
also diﬀers from the one in the simulated model due to the phantom being slightly bent which is
a reaction from the modeling process when the phantom undergoes the freeze-thaw cycles.
5.2
Patient Study
In the patient study, the simulated velocities are estimated to be approximately three times the
ones measure with US. The same estimation method is used here as with the straight tube, with
taking the mean of an area which will result in lower velocities. However, in the patient study,
one major factor is sticking out when trying to simulate the experimental setup, namely the lack
of equality in the geometric dimension of the simulated models and the phantoms. The simulated
patient model have a length of approximately 1/4 of the phantom used in the experimental setup.
The pressure measurements are in the patient study also much lower in the simulated model com-
pared to the measured pressure. Again is the situation with the outlet pressure being much lower
than that of the experiment. The outlet pressure was adjusted; however, the values were chosen
arbitrarily.
The idea of using a catheter to measure the pressure has several disadvantages as have been
mentioned earlier. In addition to these, the simulated model gives an illustration of how the pres-
sure and the velocity are changing when a catheter is introduced. The natural ﬂow pattern is
disturbed, and diﬃculties related to controlling the placement of the catheter can also inﬂuence
the measured pressure - and especially if the catheter tip is leaning towards the arterial wall.
5.3
Limitations
With both the experimental setup and the simulated model there some limitations appear. Con-
cerning the experimental setup, the lack of the time synchronizing when subtracting the pressure
data gives a possible incorrect pressure diﬀerence. To compensate for this and being able to syn-
chronize the signals it could be suggested to either get a signal from the pump which could be
used to align the measured signal. Another possibility is to have another catheter present during
all measurements. However, this idea is a very inconvenient- Another foreign material disturbs
pressure and ﬂow, and the size of the catheter is a challenge.
The experimental setup also has some limitations which could aﬀect the results, such as
the resistance from the plastic tube which transport the ﬂuid from the pump to the phantom and
back again. In the setup with the patient phantom, the two tubes running from the ICA and
ECA outlet are connected about 20cm from the outlet which possibly could result in a reﬂection
of the ﬂow in the point at which the ’two’ outlets are combined before the ﬂuid is running back to
the pump. And ﬁnally, the uncertainty of the placement of the US probe which is moved manually.
In the simulated model, the greatest limitation is the geometry and the deﬁnitions of the bound-
aries. The models are constructed with no movement allowed at the inlets and outlets in the radial

direction. This modeling is likely to be the major reason for the velocities estimated in the patient
model are much higher than what is found with US. Another diﬀerence in the simulated situation
is the catheter. The catheter is modeled being completely stiﬀ, and it is placed in the centre of
the inlet, which is not the case of the experiment where the catheter is bent.
5.4
Future Outlook
With the purpose of improving this FSI model, it is suggested that the patient geometry is expanded
to copy the one used in the setup. Another solution to the geometric inconsistencies could be to
allow the inlet and outlets to move in the radial direction, and with that meet the condition
observed with the US. The inﬂuence of assigning pressure to the outlets in both the straight tube
and the patient model, similar to the pressure measured at the outlet could, however, give rise to
other changes in the geometry.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This project has considered the possibility of reﬁning an existing CFD model of the carotid artery.
The purpose of the study was to create a more realistic model than the previous existing one, when
including wall movement and adjusting the outlet pressure. The result of the wall movement im-
plementation deﬁnitely makes the resemblance between the simulated model and the experimental
setup greater. With the two models created, the model with the most simple geometry and the
greatest similarity to the setup showed the best results. The more complex geometry of the carotid
artery, showed less satisfactory results. However, this model has the greatest inconsistencies with
the experimental setup which possibly has aﬀected the outcome.
The comparison of the velocities for the straight tube experiment approaches the velocities mea-
sured in the experimental setup. The pressure diﬀerence across the stenotic part of the phantom
are found to be greater in the FEM study. One possible explanation for that is the lack of move-
ment observed in the last part of the model. The FEM study of the carotid artery geometry needs
further reﬁnement. One possible solution which most likely will improve the results is a change in
the size of the geometry or the boundary conditions.
In conclusion, creating the FEM study as a ﬂuid-solid-interaction model has improved the re-
sults and gives a more realistic representation of the actual setup. However, it is also seen that
lack of consistencies in the geometry aﬀects the result.
