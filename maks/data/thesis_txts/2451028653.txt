Abstract
Variations of the Total Electron Content (TEC) in the ionosphere are known to cause a
spatially-variable phase modulation of SAR interferograms as well as spatially-varying po-
sitioning errors in the ﬂight-path dimension which yield relative mis-registrations between
SAR image pairs. These variations result in an ionospheric phase screen on the interfer-
ogram and in directional ﬂuctuations in the relative position of azimuth pixels on oﬀset
maps. These eﬀects are a major error source in L-band spaceborne data and, therefore,
have to be estimated and corrected. Concerning the compensation of the ionospheric ef-
fects in interferograms, the split-spectrum approach has recently been proposed to correct
Diﬀerential SAR Interferograms. However, no generally applicable approach exits for the
correction of the azimuth streaks. The estimation of the surface motion (oﬀset tracking
maps), which contain this azimuth streaks, requires the separation of the displacement oc-
curring between the acquisition time interval of the image pair and potential ionospheric
eﬀects.
The objective of this project is the implementation of the split-spectrum approach to
compensate for the ionospheric eﬀects in interferograms and to estimate the azimuth
oﬀset produced by the ionosphere. An issue which will need to be assessed is the fact that
the phase modulation eﬀects estimated by the split-spectrum method, might only partly
be related to the ionospheric phenomena causing the azimuth streaks. Assessment of the
ionospheric problem will be carried out in two ways: the ﬁrst one is a simulation of high
levels of electron density in the ionosphere to a simulated SAR signal in order to see the
eﬀects and the azimuth shift produced. The second one is data processing of ALOS-1
PALSAR dataset comprising a ground motion of interest (e.g. a co-seismic deformation
ﬁeld), the application of the split-spectrum algorithm and the estimation of the azimuth
oﬀset.
KeyWords
Ionosphere, Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR), L-band SAR, SAR inter-
ferometry, Total Electron Content (TEC), SAR ionospheric eﬀects, ionosphere estimation,
Split-Spectrum, azimuth oﬀset

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is marking a new era in how to manage the environment,
understand and cope with the eﬀects of climate change and ensure public safety, through
a wide range of information services for many practical applications. SAR is capable of
producing a two-dimensional terrestrial image due to the reﬂectivity of the earth since it is
an active radar. Besides, the range-frequency of operation is the microwave band so that
such propagation is eﬀective through the atmosphere, allowing SAR systems to operate
also in a cloudy sky (with some limitations for particular climatic conditions).
Among the characteristics of a SAR signal, the phase is one of the important ones. This
phase, under ionospheric eﬀects, can disrupt SAR acquisitions and interferometric mea-
surements of ground deformation.
In order to measure the ground scene elevation or
even the ground displacements with high accuracy, these eﬀects must be corrected. Over
the years, diﬀerent techniques have been developed to correct these errors and improve
measurement accuracy.
The selection of the best carrier frequency can improve accuracy. Higher frequencies, as
X-band or C-band, usually have better resolution and also fewer disturbances through the
ionospheric layer than lower frequencies, as L-band. However, the scattering response of
the vegetation, dried soils or ice, are so much better for lower frequencies. The longer
the wavelength, the stronger the penetration into the target.
For instance, the leaves
reﬂect X-band wavelengths but not L-band. Unfortunately, it could be severely aﬀected
by ionospheric phenomena, which is the major error source for L-band synthetic aperture
radar (SAR) signals. Therefore, L-band SAR data are more aﬀected than C-band SAR.

In short, it is a challenge to study and understand these ionospheric problems, and of
course, to improve measurement accuracy by removing these eﬀects.
1.2
Scope
As the ionosphere is an obstacle for the accuracy of the measurements, this thesis aims to
achieve two objectives:
1. To understand the ionospheric eﬀects in the interferograms and the azimuth oﬀset
2. To present and test a correction method to estimate both the ionospheric phase
screen and azimuth oﬀset.
1.3
Thesis Structure
The thesis has been divided into seven chapters, the bibliography and ﬁve annexes. The
ﬁrst chapter (this one) explains the motivation of the project.
The second chapter (ch. 2) explains the basic concepts to understand the ionosphere and
the ionosphere electron content (TEC), an overview of SAR systems and interferometry, to
ﬁnish with the description of the particular eﬀects the ionosphere causes on SAR signals.
The technologies currently used to correct these eﬀects are explained at the end.
Chapter three (ch.3) explains and analyses the SAR data processing techniques developed
in this thesis to produce interferograms. Furthermore, as the objective is to estimate and
compensate for ionospheric eﬀects on the interferograms, the split-spectrum method is
analysed and implemented.
Chapter four (ch. 4) presents a simulation of SAR signals with ionospheric presence to
understand the eﬀects produced as straightforward as possible before analysing them on
real datasets. The chapter also analyses the azimuth shift produced.
Chapter ﬁve (ch. 5) presents ﬁrst the dataset used. Secondly, the result obtained from
the application of data processing techniques. Satellite images, azimuth oﬀset maps and
interferograms are analysed.
Chapter six (ch. 6) presents the results of the implementation of the split-spectrum ap-
proach to estimate the ionospheric phase screen and remove it from the initial interfero-
gram. Moreover, azimuth oﬀset is also estimated with the interferometric phase obtained.

2.1
Ionosphere
The ionosphere is the portion of the Earth’s upper atmosphere where ions and electrons
are present with suﬃcient density to aﬀect the propagation of radio waves signiﬁcantly
[1]. The density of free electrons ηe is a function of the sun’s activity, atmospheric density
proﬁle, geographic location, magnitude and orientation of the Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld, and
time of day. Thus, there is a diurnal eﬀect and a seasonal eﬀect.
The presence of ionospheric charged particles starts at about 60 km altitude, has a peak
between 300 and 400 km typically, and then decreases until about 1000 km [1].
The
ionosphere consists of diﬀerent layers, depending on whether it is night or daytime. The
F2 layer is present at night as the only layer of signiﬁcant ionization. The D and E layers
become much more ionized during the day, as well as the F layers, which develops an
additional and weaker region known as the F1 layer. Figure 2.1 b) illustrates the layers
mentioned above. As it is shown, the ionization also varies with the season. The electron
density reaches the maximum at an altitude of about 400 km, as Figure 2.1 a) shows. The
vertically stratiﬁed structure of the ionosphere is often approximated by an idealized thin
layer positioned at the barycenter of the electron density. In the following chapters, the
single layer approximation is used [2].

2.2
Pulse compression of Linear FM Signal
Pulse compression is a signal processing technique used in radar, sonar seismic, and other
“probing” systems. It is designed to minimize peak power, maximize signal-to-noise ratio,
and obtain an excellent resolution of the sensed object (e.g.
to achieve a good image
quality).
The pulse compression is achieved with a matched ﬁlter.
The discussion of
matched ﬁltering is based on linear FM signals, which are of fundamental importance in
the theory and applications of SAR systems [7]. The linear FM signal is also known as
“chirp”.
Now the linear frequency-modulated (FM) signal will be introduced. This leads into the
pulse compression section, which will brieﬂy explain what this is about by ending with
matched ﬁltering, the tool with which high resolution is obtained in the signal processor.
Following chapter 3 of [7], an ideal linear FM signal or pulse in the time domain is:
s(t) = e(jπKt2)

where t is the time variable in seconds, and K is the linear FM rate in hertz per seconds.
2.2.1
Pulse Compression
In SAR systems, a pulse of energy is used to measure diﬀerent parameters. The received
pulse must be strong enough and have a ﬁne enough resolution for the measurement to be
useful. Moreover, the SNR must be high enough to obtain the required object parameters
accurately, a quality often in conﬂict with resolution. The SNR increase can be done by
raising the average transmitted power [7]. One of the ways of doing it is by transmitting an
“expanded” pulse and later compressing it to the desired resolution in the signal processor.
The technique is known as pulse compressing.
The objective of pulse compression is to obtain a ﬁne-range resolution from the received
data giving a well-focused radar image. The transmitted pulse is the expanded pulse, and
the processed pulse is the compressed pulse.
To achieve good pulse compression, following [7] and without going into details, the spec-
trum of the linear FM signal must be multiplied by a signal with a similar spectrum but
has the conjugate quadratic phase property. This operation is the essence of pulse com-
pression, and is also known as “matched ﬁltering”. The terminology comes from the fact
that the ﬁlter is “matched” to the expected phase of the received signal.

2.3
Synthetic Aperture Concepts
The purpose here is to explain the “synthetic aperture” in the radar context, especially in
the Azimuth direction due to the azimuth eﬀects causes by the ionosphere. Moreover, the
simulation carries out in chapter 4 is based on the SAR signal in the azimuth direction.
SAR is an abbreviation for Synthetic Aperture Radar. A SAR is a high-resolution mi-
crowave imaging system. It is operated either from aircraft or satellite, in order to achieve
high-resolution images. The essential characteristic of SAR is that it is a coherent radar
in order to measure the phase between transmitted pulses and received echoes. The echo
of a point scatterer is a good approximation of an impulse (amplitude scaled), and its
presence in the image approximates the impulse response. The Synthetic Aperture Radar,
therefore, measures the amplitude and phase of the echo obtained. The amplitude is pro-
portional to the strength of the echo, whereas the phase is more diﬃcult to understand,
but still a key characteristic of a SAR. It is in this phase where essential information
resides. At the reception, the phase of the reference oscillator signal is compared to the
echo and measured along with the amplitude. The measured phase of point target echo is
[8]:

2.4
SAR interferometry
This section gives a brief overview of SAR interferometry. A detail explanation can be
found in [9]. As stated before, the phase includes the signal’s most signiﬁcant information.
A SAR satellite can observe the same area from two diﬀerent look angles; one way is by
repeating the orbits of the same satellite. A single antenna repeating its path can form
an interferometer to measure long-term deformation.
An interferogram is a complex image where its phase is related to geometry diﬀerences.
The SAR interferogram is generated by cross-multiplying, pixel by pixel, the ﬁrst SAR
image with the complex conjugate of the second [9]. Therefore, the interferogram ampli-
tude is the amplitude of the ﬁrst image multiplied by that of the second one, whereas its
phase is the phase diﬀerence between the image.
Interferograms are used to measure the terrain elevation and its changes and also to
produce topographic maps, for instance, digital elevations models (DEMs). Thus, they
are important for terrain mapping and detection of environmental changes such as moving
targets. With a range change ∆r the interferometric phase is:
∆φ = 4π

times, the interferometric phase, as shown in equation 2.11, is directly proportional to any
change in the measurements of a surface attribute.

Oﬀset Tracking is a technique that measures feature motion between two images using
patch intensity cross-correlation optimization [17]. With oﬀset tracking the registration
oﬀset of two SAR images in both slant-range (i.e. in the line-of-sight of the satellite) and
azimuth (i.e. along the orbit of the satellite) directions are generated [18].

of detected real-valued SAR intensity images. Motion in the range and azimuth dimensions
causes a 2D shift of the NCC peak concerning the centre of the search window [17]. The
location of the peak of the two-dimensional cross-correlation function yields the image
oﬀset. The more important parameters for search are the number and size of the oﬀset
estimation windows in range and azimuth and should be large enough to accommodate
the largest expected motion. Moreover, to increase the estimation accuracy, oversampling

A. Interferogram Flattening
The complex interferogram consist of several contributions (curved Earth, topography,
noise and eventually atmospheric distortions and displacements). The operation, called
interferogram ﬂattening, removes the azimuth and range phase eﬀects due to the shape
of the earth ellipsoid.As a result, it generates a phase map proportional to the relative
terrain altitude [9].
B. Multilooking
With multilooking, it is possible to average or oversample an image. Averaging reduces
the speckle noise [24], decreasing at the same time, the spatial resolution. Oversampling
can be used to improve the spatial resolution of the image, e.g. in order to facilitate phase
unwrapping under challenging regions such as those with dense fringes. It often includes
a sub-sampling to reduce the number of the image pixel.
In this implementation, the multilooking process is divided into two steps: The ﬁrst one
is a ﬁltering process of the resulting interferogram, and the second one is a sub-sampling
of the data. The ﬁltering process uses a 2D digital ﬁlter [25] which applies low-pass FIR
ﬁlter to a matrix of data X according to coeﬃcients in a matrix H to reduce the Speckle
Noise of the interferograms.
After ﬁltering, the amount of sub-sampling is the same used in the box ﬁlter. The coeﬃ-
cients of H are the same values as the multilooking factors.
==PAGE:42==

The selection of the coeﬃcients (amount of multilooking) is a fact of balance. It has to be
done such that the number of looks is small enough to obtain high resolution and also big
enough such that the phase unwrapping is possible. Often, the resulting amount of looks
is hard to calculate as it depends on the image’s resolution, i.e. its spectral properties.
Therefore, measuring the amount of speckle reduction for the ﬁlter is sometimes essential,
and then calculate the equivalent number of looks for the ﬁlter.
C. Phase Unwrapping
As stated in theory section 2.4 one cycle of colour represents the cycle of relative phase
(0-2π), which is a change in the terrain altitude when the colour change. However, there is
the altitude of ambiguity, which is the altitude diﬀerence that generates an interferometric
phase change of 2π after interferogram ﬂattening. This ﬂattened interferogram gives an
ambiguous measurement of the relative terrain altitude due to the 2π cyclic nature of the
interferometric phase. Therefore, it must be deleted any integer number of altitudes of
ambiguity (equivalent to an integer number of 2π phase cycles). The process of adding
the correct integer multiple of 2π to the interferometric fringes is called phase unwrapping
[9].
D. Filtering
This ﬁltering process is just applied to the delta-k phase because the values obtained are
minimal, and hence, the phase obtained is very noisy. This adaptive ﬁltering algorithm can
reduce the phase noise and improve both measurement accuracy and phase unwrapping
(even though for this case phase unwrapping is not necessary). It is called Goldstein ﬁlter
[26].
This ﬁlter will create an adaptive window applied to the interferogram. The eﬀect of this
window is to obtain the sum of weighted contributions from each of the patches that cover
a particular interferogram sample [26]. The ﬁlter will compute the interferogram power
spectrum and will design a ﬁlter based on the power spectrum. The spatial resolution of
the ﬁlter adapts to the local phase variation in such a way that the smooth phase regions
are strongly ﬁltered, whereas the regions with high phase variance are weakly ﬁltered [26].

4.1
Implementation
This section introduces the program developed in Matlab. It has two purposes: the ﬁrst
one is to understand and produce an azimuth shift. The second one is to estimate the
azimuth shift by derivation of the SAR signal phase (practical demonstration of equation
2.19).
The starting point is the simulation of a compressed azimuthal signal along with a TEC
distribution.
Azimuth focussed SAR can be simulated by compression of several signals described in
equation 2.10 in an array of points targets. In order to execute it, equations 2.7 and 2.9 are
solved and the results replaced in equation 2.10. Thus, the signal in the azimuth direction
is obtained but not yet compressed. Once the array of targets is produced, and following
section 2.3.1, the “received” signal is compressed. The matched ﬁlter can be applied in
the time domain, using a linear convolution. However, as the matched ﬁlter is usually very

long in SAR applications, the matched ﬁlter is applied in the frequency domain. There are
diﬀerent options to generate the frequency domain matched ﬁlter described in [7]. The one
chose here is computed by taking the complex conjugate of the DFT of the zero padded
pulse replica (not time-reversed).
So far, the simulation produces the azimuth compressed signal without ionospheric eﬀect.
The ionospheric simulation is carried out by creating a geometric model whereby the radar
beam views the Earth through a ’phase modulation screen’ as Figure 2.5 shown. This
simulation is designed to simulate, as directly as possible, the eﬀect of varying electron
density at an intermediate range to the scene being imaged.
To determine the sensitivity of an ALOS-1 PALSAR system to TEC ﬂuctuations of a
short spatial extent, a Gaussian TEC anomaly is assumed. To develop an anomaly model
that matches typical ionospheric ﬂuctuations, it considered the same TEC distribution
published in [4]. According to this simulation, a typical anomaly may have an amplitude
of about 0.3 TECU and width comparable to the synthetic aperture length (Ta · Vr).
In order to display the signal under the ionospheric eﬀect, the ionospheric phase contri-
bution is computed (eq. 2.18) and multiplied to the Azimuth Signal. Subsequently, the
matched ﬁlter is applied.
At this point, it is already possible to calculate the azimuth shift by computing the posi-
tion diﬀerence of the signal with and without ionospheric eﬀect. However, a previous step
of interpolation is done. The reason comes from the calculation of the azimuth oﬀset esti-
mation (eq. 2.19). The derivation is carried out through the approximation by diﬀerences
of the signal phase, so it is an important point to select these values with high accuracy
to obtain the best estimate. Therefore, the equation is solved by making the diﬀerence
between the signal phase values. If the amount of points is not enough, the selected phase
value of the target would be biased and could change the result considerably increasing
the diﬀerence error. Consequently, 1000 accuracy points have been selected to interpolate
between samples.
As pointed out in section 2.5.3, the contribution of the ionosphere to the azimuth oﬀset
can be associated to the along-track derivative of the interferometric phase.

Discussion
Thanks to the simulated scenario, and from the results obtained, it is easy to conclude
that a relatively small eﬀect of TEC distribution can lead to azimuth shifts. Figure 4.3
yields two conclusions:
The ﬁrst one, the signal in the Azimuth direction without any ionospheric eﬀect (plot
(a)) keeps its spectrum ﬂat and undisplaced.
However, under ionospheric eﬀects, the
plot (b) yields diﬀerent results.
The magnitude is aﬀected in the middle of the TEC
distribution, as well as the targets under this eﬀect. As theory section 2.5.2 explained,
the TEC distribution aﬀects the phase of the signal. Even though it is not possible to
appreciate an azimuth shift concerning plot (a), each target has been shifted. The change
is too small (below 2 pixels). Figure 4.4 shows this shift.
When it is applied without ionospheric inﬂuence, the phase screen is recovered in the pro-
cessed diﬀerential phase, and there is no azimuth shift (red line). In the other hand, with
ionospheric inﬂuence, the peak-to-peak azimuth shift is around ∼2 pixels. The synthetic
aperture length is around 22 km, which is the value used as the deviation of the TEC dis-
tribution. This value leads to an azimuth shift eﬀect along 100 km. Also, this shift shows
that relatively small phase modulation in a phase screen can lead to the type of azimuth
shift simulated. As seen in chapter 2.1 of the theory, TEC values may vary depending on
solar activity and time of day. If with a value of 0.3 TECU there is an azimuth shift of 2
pixels, the impact will be much higher if this value increases.
The ionosphere will inﬂuence an L-band SAR more than an equivalent C-band SAR for
two reasons: Firstly, from formula 2.2 it is extracted that the frequency is inversely pro-
portional to the refractive index, so the derived phase shift for a given integrated electron
density will be higher, and secondly, the beamwidth is higher, so the possibility of changes
in electron density in the aperture time is larger, as has been proven.
Finally, the equality of equation 2.19 is veriﬁed. Similarities between the shape of azimuth
oﬀset correction estimation (Fig. 4.5) and the shape of the Azimuth Shift due to Iono-
spheric phase (Fig. 4.4) conﬁrm the validity of the former as well as the α value calculated
in Appendix A. No less, [15] reached the same result of the beneﬁts of the phase derivative
image.
In real cases, the veracity of the equation and the information that gives is beneﬁcial
because it could be used to calculate the interferometric phase screen starting with the
azimuth oﬀset, but under a speciﬁc scenario, i.e. the azimuth oﬀset must be produced
just by the ionosphere (remember that azimuth oﬀset can also come from the ground
deformation).

The α parameter, as it is shown, depending on characteristics of a given sensor in a given
mode. Therefore, once α is estimated on a given radar track/frame, one can use them for

To conclude, the presence of the ionosphere has a signiﬁcant impact on the measurements

Discussion
In the simulation, was verify the derivative of the interferometric phase leads to the az-
imuth oﬀset over a simulated scenario. Here, the same estimation will be tested with the
dataset used to verify that it is possible to obtain a reasonable estimation of the azimuth
oﬀset due to the ionosphere contribution.
The ionosphere contribution to the phase is derived in the same way as the simulation,
by a diﬀerence approximation. The result obtained is again noisy; that is why a ﬁltering
process is applied before scaling with the factor α.
The optimal window for the ﬁlter that was found is 40 x 3. The aspect ratio of the window
has to be large in favour of the direction of azimuth; if it is the other way around, the
image obtained would have horizontal lines (for being longer in range). The relation has
to be around 10; otherwise, the image is blurred, and the details are not appreciated. If
the ratio becomes very large (100 x 3) the accuracy decreases enormously because the
azimuth streaks fatten, implying that there is a shift of pixels when there are none. If the
ratio is minimal, resolution and hence, accuracy is lost.
Finally, the result is multiplied by the α factor. If the estimation is correct, the result
should look like the azimuth oﬀset calculated in the previous chapter. In order to perform

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The presence of the ionosphere has an impact on the SAR signals. The ionosphere can
aﬀect the phase of the SAR signal producing changes in the measurements. These wrong
measurements can lead to low spatial resolution, and inaccurate interferograms and az-
imuth oﬀset maps. Therefore, the ionospheric contribution to the SAR signal has to be
estimated and corrected.
In this project, diﬀerent techniques of SAR data processing have been explained to produce
images, azimuth oﬀset and interferograms, and the Split-Spectrum method for detecting
and correcting ionospheric variations has been introduced. The method allows to estimate
ionospheric phase screens and remove them from interferograms, so it is essential for the
ground deformation estimation accuracy. The approach explained has been developed and
tested on diﬀerent datasets.
Firstly, a simulation has been carried out to understand the ionospheric eﬀects on the
SAR signals and see the azimuth shift produced under an ionospheric scenario simulated.
Besides, the relation between the azimuth oﬀset obtained and the Interferometric phase
has also been demonstrated in the simulation.
The diﬀerent techniques of data processing have been applied to two data sets of ALOS-1
images from the Sichuan earthquake. The applied techniques help to understand how
to focus the raw data and to perform a co-registration of the images (necessary, among
other things, to apply the Split-Spectrum method). The dataset analysed is far away from
the epicentre; in this way, it avoids strong ground movements variations focuses only on
the ionospheric eﬀect for the correction method. The results show how the ionosphere
presence aﬀects the interferograms produced.
So far, the ﬁrst objective is addressed. The ionospheric eﬀects in the interferograms and

the azimuth shifts produced is understood as well as the diﬀerent SAR data processing

Afterwards, the split-spectrum method is applied. The estimate of the ionosphere phase

phase.
The results show the robustness of the method: the ionospheric contribution
is removed obtaining the expected interferogram. However, additional testing in more
datasets (with ground motions) is still necessary to validate the use of the method for the
estimation of ionospheric phase screen. Furthermore, the interferometric phase obtained

because it is not far away to reach the expected accuracy.
Here is where the second objective is achieved. To prove the robustness and eﬀectiveness
of the correction method to correct interferograms and estimate azimuth oﬀset.
There are some methods in the literature to improve accuracy. Various methods can be
used to extract information about the ionosphere with diﬀerent accuracies. The combina-
tion of more ionosphere estimation methods can make the phase screen more precise. For
instance, the one presented on [16], which is based on a Bayesian inverse problem, that
combines various information sources in order to increase the estimation accuracy and
thus improve the correction. An extension of the split-spectrum method is a topic worthy
of further research. As future work, more ionosphere estimation methods should be tested
as well as the Bayesian inverse problem and then try the azimuth oﬀset estimation again.

Appendix D
Ethical, economic, social and
environmental aspects
D.1
Introduction
This Master’s thesis is framed within the technological sector of communication systems,
expressly, within SAR technology. The project developed in the Technical University of
Denmark, in the department of DTU Space. The objective of this project is to investi-
gate and implement the split-spectrum approach to correct interferograms and estimate
azimuth oﬀset. An issue will be assessed throughout the project concerns the fact that the
phase modulation eﬀects estimated by the split-spectrum method, are partly be related
to the ionospheric phenomena causing the azimuth streaks. This eﬀect is investigated and
proved. It is essential to note that the satellite data used are public and provided by the
American space agency NASA.
Therefore, it is possible to improve the precision of Earth displacements predictions, and
in general, any data taken and aﬀected by the ionosphere. Here is where the main impact
appears, the environmental impact. As the Earth is observed by satellite, depending on
the application, the environmental impact may be diﬀerent.
Besides, a social impact can also be distinguished. Firstly, indirectly, the ﬁnal objective
of the project is human, i.e. the accuracy of the measures could prevent risks and help
when making a rescue in an earthquake. Regarding human rights, we could all beneﬁt
from technology, regardless of the country of origin.
