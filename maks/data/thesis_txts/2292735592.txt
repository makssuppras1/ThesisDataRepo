Abstract
This thesis investigates the opportunities of lidar-assisted feed-forward control of wind turbines for
load reduction. A continuous-wave, hub mounted lidar system with four beams is modelled and was
combined with a collective pitch FF+FB controller. The system underwent a design and tuning process
which optimized the focus point location, low-pass filter, prediction time shift and gains. The set-up
was then tested against a standard feed-back controller. For above-rated turbulent wind fields, an
average tower DEL reduction of 7% and blade DEL decrease of 1% is shown. Extreme tower and
blade loads displayed a reduction of 25% and 15%, respectively. The feed-back and feed-forward gains
proved to have a crucial impact on the load decrease and must be tuned carefully.

1. Introduction
In the recent years, wind energy has played an important role for meeting the ambitious climate goals
of many countries and is one of the most promising source to do so in the future as well, GWEC [2014].
Hence, wind turbines have increased tremendously in size and complexity to utilize economies of scale
effects. Still, in order to make wind generated electricity production more efficient, and thus lower the
costs of electricity production, further innovation is needed. One method is to try to minimize the
structural loads on wind turbines and make even bigger turbine feasible or increase the lifetime. The
control system having a great influence on these loads is an ideal candidate to achieve load reduction.
Not only can it keep the turbine running at optimal efficiency but it is also able to reduce fatigue and
extreme loads.
For a wind turbine controller the designer is faced with the problem of being unable to measure the
upstream wind speed. Even though on many turbines a nacelle-mounted anemometer is installed,
these measurements are not sufficient to estimate the rotor effective wind speed (REWS), because
the device is disturbed by passing blades and only gives a point measurement. The REWS however
requires a weighted average of the wind speed over the whole rotor plane. Thus feed-back controllers
that are gain-scheduled based on another measurable variable have to be used. In full load operation,
a feed-back from the blade pitch angle is often used for gain-scheduling of the pitch controller, Hansen
et al. [2005].
Lidar systems are able to remotely measure the velocity of particles suspended in air by shining laser
light onto a target volume. If the concentration of particles is high enough to backscatter a processable
amount of light, the combined air movement can be derived via the optical Doppler effect. Mounted
on a wind turbine such a system can measure the wind speed that will be experienced by a turbine
after the wind has been advected from the point of measurement to the rotor plane. This information
posses great value for control strategies since controllers can be designed that are able to directly react
to wind speed disturbances.
Several studies have already shown via simulations that lidar-augmented wind turbine controllers can
have a beneficial effect on loads and energy production.
Additionally, two field tests exist which
show promising results. These are presented below. However since research is still in an early stage, a
comprehensive analysis is not available yet. The reason lies in the fact that sometimes assumptions have
been made which neglect important effects of lidar principles, control realization, turbine modelling
and wind simulation. These often include the following:
∙Lidar beam averaging,
∙Lidar availability,
∙Unrealisable control inputs (for real application),
∙Sensor noise and faults,
∙turbine dynamics (e. g. using a stiff turbine or turning off aerodynamical effects)
∙Evolution of turbulence,
∙Transportation time of wind (i. e. how long it takes the wind to travel from the measurement
plane to the rotor plane.) and

1.1. Motivation, Objective and Approach
Motivation to investigate lidar-assisted wind turbine control stems mainly from the author’s experience
of having a student job at Windar Photonics A/S. During this time an upgrade of an existing 2-beam
lidar, which is used for yaw alignment, was in development in cooperation with DTU Wind Energy.
This allowed for very close participation in the design process and facilitated a thesis topic that is
mutually beneficial.
This master thesis wished to investigate the use of lidar-assisted feed-forward control of wind turbines
in order to reduce structural loads during operation.
Even though there are many different lidar
systems and different mounting positions available for wind energy applications, the current thesis is
using a model of a system in development by Windar Photonics A/S. It is a continuous-wave, nacelle-
mounted device which uses a relatively inexpensive all-semiconductor laser source. The feed-forward
(FF) controller has been taken from Schlipf and Kühn [2008] and Schlipf et al. [2010a]. It can calculate
a contribution to the feed-back (FB) pitch angle demand based on lidar look-ahead measurements and
is used in above-rated operation only. This allows not only for a fast numerical implementation but
also simplifies a possible real-life application because the FF pitch angle can be added after the FB
term is calculated; no changes of the FB control strategy or design are needed.
Hence, in short the question that is addressed in this thesis can be formulated as: Does the load
reduction of lidar-assisted collective FF pitch controller still exist when realistic measurements of a
cost-efficient lidar are used?
Servo-aeroelastic simulations of a 5MW reference wind turbine developed at the National Renewable
Energy Laboratory (NREL) (see Jonkman et al. [2009]) in HAWC21 are used to evaluate the perfor-
mance of the FF controller against a FB controller only. For this purpose a generic lidar model was
added as a sensor to HAWC2 and the above mentioned FF controller was implemented as an external
dynamic link library (DLL). At first the optimal controller and lidar set-up is found through simula-
tions. Then the control performance is evaluated for the design load case 1.2 of IEC-61400 and two
extreme operating gusts.
The remainder of the thesis is structures as follows: The rest of this chapter gives a quick overview of
the usage of lidar systems in wind turbine control. The focus lies on lidar-assisted pitch control and its
load reduction opportunity. Chapter 2 introduces the fundamentals of coherent Doppler wind lidars
and explains its modelling in HAWC2. Some model results are also presented. In chapter 3 the control
strategy, design and tuning are described and the implementation is tested against a deterministic
wind field. Chapter 4 briefly introduces the turbine under analysis and presents the simulation results
for turbulent wind fields and an extreme wind case. Finally, chapter 5 summarizes the thesis and gives
an outlook to future work.

conditions and lidar designs are necessary.
1.2.2. Lidar-Assisted Yaw Control
Self-alignment of modern-scale horizontal-axis wind turbines to the incoming wind in reasonable time
is prevented by several reasons (e.g. the high moment of inertia of the rotor around the yaw axis and
the uneconomic large dimensions of wind vanes, Hau [2008]). Thus active yaw controllers and actuators
are required, which usually rely on measurements from a cup or sonic anemometer that is mounted on
the nacelle. A lidar system that can measure the incoming wind speeds at several points in front of
the turbine can overcome the disadvantages of cup or sonic anemometers (e.g. wind disturbances due
to the rotor and single-point measurements).
Two mounting locations have been investigated: spinner-integrated and nacelle-mounted lidars. Re-
cently spinner-based lidar simulations have been studied in Kragh et al. [2013]. An extensive parameter
study showed that the a lidar model can measure misalignment with a precision of a few degrees. Sev-
eral beam set-ups showed that distributing points over the entire rotor plane does not yield advantages

Hub-mounted lidar offer an alternative where the information transport through the shaft is avoided,

average misalignment over a finite period) and dynamic alignment (the lidar is used continuously).
In Fleming et al. [2014] a nacelle-mounted lidar was field tested on a research turbine and it showed
an effective yaw control that resulted in an increased power curve. As a result though, the loads on
tower and blades were not consistently decreased but also showed an increase under certain conditions.
Unlike spinner lidars, hub-mounted system have made it to commercial applications. For example
both Windar Photonics A/S2 and Avent Lidar Technology3 offer lidars placed on a tripod onto of the
nacelle.

The first work on lidar-assisted pitch control has been done by Harris et al. [2006], where basics of
lidar measurements are described and suggestion on lidar designs are given.
His study marks the

tower and blades while also using less pitch actuation. Still, the non-adaptive controller showed load
reductions as well.
Cyclic Pitch Control
Simulation of a cyclic pitch FF controller using a lidar sensor and a cyclic pitch ℋ∞controller have
been compared to a collective pitch FB controller in Schlipf et al. [2010b]. A quite sophisticated lidar
model using a total of 60 focus points is used. Result for a turbulent wind field reveal significantly
decreased blade flapwise bending moments at the cost of increased pitch actuation against the FB
controller. While both controllers mitigate loads at the 1P frequency, the FF controller is able to
reduce loads below that frequency as well.
Individual Pitch Control
Significant improvements of FF augmented individual pitch controllers have been reported in Laks
et al. [2010]. Simplified lidar simulation were used for both a rotating and stationary lidar device.
Results showed that individual pitch controllers heavily rely on a rotating lidar as input. In addition,
it was shown that highly turbulent field negatively influence the shear estimation and that FF control
should be switch off in these scenarios.
In Dunne et al. [2011] three different control strategies using a realistic lidar model were tested (non-
causal series expansion, preview control and optimized FIR filter). However it was found that only
the FIR filter was beneficial because both other controllers increased tower sway.
Compared to a
FB individual pitch controller an average DEL reduction over eight turbine components of 5% was
achieved. It should be noted that the FIR filter design process was very computationally expensive
compared to the design of the other controllers.
The collective pitch FF controller described first in the previous section is compared to a FF individ-
ual pitch controller in Dunne et al. [2012] which also uses a simple lidar model. Even though both
controllers vary in many aspects, they can achieve similar results. Additionally, the individual pitch
controller can reduce 3P frequency loads, but requires larger pitch actuation and increases tower top
motion. It was also shown that the required preview time is mainly due to low-pass filtering.

Field Testing
Fields tests of a collective pitch FF controller have been conducted on NREL’s CART4 turbines; results
for CART2 are presented in Schlipf et al. [2014a] and for CART3 in Scholbrock et al. [2013]. A very
similar control strategy to the one in this thesis is used for both campaigns.
The CART2 tests use a pulsed lidar with one laser that follows a circular trajectory. Six focus points
at five different focus distances along the trajectory are measured and their average is the estimate of
the REWS. The experiment shows that the beneficial effects that have been reported for simulations
can also be achieved under real conditions. However, the REWS estimate from the lidar system and
the turbine signals need to have a high correlation to achieve beneficial effects. If this is the case, the
standard deviation (STD) of the rotor speed and the DEL on the fore-aft tower base bending moment
can be reduced by 30% and 10%, respectively. But for low correlation both measures are increased by
30% and 20%. The DEL of flapwise blade bending moment was reduced by 10% in both cases.
In the CART3 campaign a commercial lidar from BlueScout Technologies was used. It uses three fixed
pulsed laser beams and measures at three different range gates, but only one distance was used. The
experienced benefits were a 13.6% reduction in the STD of rotor speed, around 2% in the DEL of
the tower fore-aft bending and 7% for the blade flapwise bending. The authors note that the primary
focus of the work was not structural loads and hence the FB controller was not optimized for load
reduction.
1.2.4. Model Predictive Control
Model predictive control (MPC) is an advanced control mechanism where future behaviour of a system
is predicted from an internal model and present measurements. The prediction is used to calculate
control actuations to regulate the wind turbine. Lidar systems can improve the prediction by giving
wind speed estimates of incoming wind conditions.
The main advantage of MPC is that it is an
optimal controller in a mathematical sense, which means that the control variables are described by
a set of differential equations to minimize a cost function, Schlipf et al. [2013b]. Further it is easy to
include additional control in- or outputs and it considers actuator and system constraints naturally.
One drawback is the high computational cost.
Both linear and nonlinear MPC have been applied to the pitch control of wind turbines; for a com-
parison see Schlipf et al. [2014b]. In the article it was shown that both controllers can significantly
improve the rotor speed regulation compare to a FB controller. The nonlinear MPC only shows advan-
tages over linear MPC at wind speed far off the linearisation point. The work was extended in Schlipf
et al. [2013b] to demonstrate load reduction, which exceed those of collective pitch FB and FF+FB
controllers. Tower base fore-aft and blade flapwise DELs were reduced by 28% and 12%, respectively.
Similar results have been presented in Laks et al. [2011].

2. Lidar System and Modelling
Laser anemometry has been in use since the 1970s but large scale usage has been prevented by high
costs, complexity and reliability issues, Jelalian [1992]. However, developments in optical fiber produc-
tion and in the telecommunication industry have mitigated these disadvantages and promise light-based
systems to become a viable option for velocimetry of incoming wind fields in front of turbines, Harris
et al. [2006]. Contrary to many claims in the literature of the word lidar being an acronym for light
detection and ranging, it actually originates of the blend formed by the words ’light’ and ’radar’, Ring
[1963]1.
While there are different mechanisms to utilize light for anemometry like incoherent Doppler lidar, laser
Doppler anemometry, particle image velocimetry and sodar, this thesis will use a coherent Doppler
lidar (CDL), also referred to as coherent laser radar or simply lidar. More specifically it will model a
continuous-wave, monostatic, coherent lidar system with a short-wavelength infrared laser.
This chapter will present the relevant basics of lidar measurements and its implementation in HAWC2.
Also the reconstructing wind speeds and estimating effective wind speeds will be discussed. Finally,
sample measurements will be shown and lidar shortcomings are going to be analysed.

2.6. Shortcomings of Lidar measurements
This section presents some shortcomings inherent to the lidar sensors that are relevant when estimating
the wind speed at the focus point. The evaluation will be in time domain and qualitatively only.
Throughout this section the same wind field from the previous section is used and 𝑁= 100 and
𝑀= 4.5. First of all, as mentioned before the lidar will act as a moving average filter. The weighting
function is determined by eq. (2.1) and the further away the focus distance lies from the lidar device
the stronger the filter effect is.
This can be seen in fig. 2.8 where the time series of the true wind speed at focus point and lidar
measurements with four different focal lengths are shown. The beam points straight upstream again
and the signals have been shifted according to Taylor’s frozen hypothesis (see Taylor [1938]) so that all
measurements arrive 50 m in front of the rotor at the same time. Note here that HAWC2, like many
other aeroelastic simulation codes, can not model evolution of turbulence.
The increasing low-pass filtering effect can be seen as 𝑍increases. Note that Γ is proportional to the the
focal length squared. For some application, for example pitch control, this effect can be beneficial since
actuation is only desired at low frequencies. Thus a focal length that gives the requested frequency
cut-off can be chosen. On the other hand, in reality turbulence does evolve when travelling from the

the rotor plane to minimize changing turbulence errors.

3. Controller
In the chapter at first the collective pitch FB controller is described. Then a FF control strategy that
was presented in a previous study is introduced and an ideal simulation in a deterministic wind field
is shown. Finally, the design and tuning processes, whose results are presented in the next chapter, is
described.
3.1. The Collective Pitch Feed-back Controller
So far commercial wind turbines are controlled by feed-back controllers that use control variable errors
to derive actuations to counteract these deviations.
This allows only for a delayed compensation
because a disturbance has to travel through the entire wind turbine to show an effect in the control
variables. Thus perfect control is theoretical and even under ideal conditions not possible.
Nowadays many wind turbines are variable speed, variable pitch (VSVP) turbines. This allows for an
improved peak power point tracking for below rated wind speeds (variable speed) and more effective
power limitation once rated wind speed is reached (variable pitch). The total control action can be
separated into three different regions.
In region I the objective is to maximize the power production. This is done by letting the rotational
speed of the rotor Ω vary linearly with wind speed 𝑣0 so that the turbine will operate at a constant tip
speed ratio (TSR), 𝜆= Ω𝑅
𝑣0 ; this TSR is chosen where the maximum power coefficient can be reached.
The rotor speed regulation is achieved through changing the generator torque. From the definition of
the power coefficient and the TSR it can be shown that the generator torque demand 𝑄𝑑should vary
as follows

The second region begins as soon as the maximum allowable tip speed is reached (onshore usually

their rated values. This is accomplished by holding the torque demand constant and adjust the pitch
angle based on the rotor speed deviations from rated rotor speed with a PI or PID controller. An
effective method to combine the torque and pitch loop is to run both in parallel and include variables
that saturate one of the two when far away from rated power, see Burton et al. [2011].

3.3. Feed-forward Control Design
The control design involves finding the optimal focus points in front of the turbine, the low-pass filter
frequency and the necessary prediction time shift. Here this was done by brute force, i. e. the parameter
were changed for a number of simulation and the best-performing parameter were chosen.
The 4-beam lidar set-up is shown in fig. 3.5. In the controller design process the circle on which the
focus points are placed is defined by the axial distance from the rotor plane (𝑑axial) and its radius
(𝑟trans). The four focus points are then positioned on the circle at angles 45∘, 135∘, 225∘, 315∘starting
at the top position. Also keep in mind that only one circle can be chosen since a continuous-wave lidar
with only one focus point per beam can be selected.
Figure 3.5.: Possible lidar focus point set-ups of the 4 beams for different focus distances, modified figure 4 in
Schlipf et al. [2010a].
Even though lidar measurements already give a low-pass filtering effect, it is necessary to use an
additional low-pass filter to avoid unnecessary pitch actuation, which would increase blade and tower
loads. This will introduce a time lag in the REWS estimate signal. Hence, a prediction time shift
is needed to account for the delay. It will also incorporate the delay coming from the pitch actuator
model and a possible rising time difference between ΣΩ𝜃and ΣΩ𝑣.
While it is possible to use zero-phase filtering, the available preview time is insufficient for the low
cut-off frequencies of the necessary filter. For example, a 0.1 Hz zero-phase low-pass filter requires 10 s
of preview time. This amount of look-ahead is in most cases not available (especially for large mean
wind speeds). The low-pass filter that is used in this thesis is a discrete, second-order filter and is
described in appendix B.2 in Hansen and Henriksen [2013].
In many previous studies the transportation time from the plane of measurement and the rotor plane
assumes Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis, see Taylor [1938]. Briefly said, it states that turbulence
structures do not change as they are advected downstream.
However, it has been reported that
turbulence does change during transportation, see for example Schlipf et al. [2010c]. Many aeroelastic
codes (including HAWC2) model turbulence by precalculating a turbulence box that is then transported
with the speed of the mean wind. Hence, the effect of changing turbulence structures can not be
modelled.

speed and distance to the measurement plane. However, in reality the mean wind speed is not known
by the controller and a measurement signal needs to be used. The REWS including a low-pass filter
with a very low cut-off frequency can be used, which was also done here.
3.4. Feed-forward Control Tuning
For the collective pitch FB controller that is used the gains of the torque and pitch controller have
already been tuned. No changes have been made when only the FB controller is used. However, for
the FF+FB controller duty can be taken off the FB part by reducing pitch controller gains. This has a
positive effect on the tower and blade loads, but at the same time the rotor speed regulation is reduced.
A positive load reduction of reduced FB loads has been reported in Schlipf et al. [2010a].

5. Summary and Conclusion
This work studied the load reduction potential of a FB collective pitch controller assisted by a four
beam lidar system. At first the optimal control set-up and tuning was found through simulations. It has
been shown that positioning the beams approximately 0.5R from the centre of the rotor is the optimal
set-up independent of the focus distance. The optimal distance between rotor and measurement plane
was found to lie between 1D and 1.4D. While low-pass filter frequencies and prediction time shifts have
a smaller influence on the performance, gains play an important role for this controller combination
and should be tuned. The proportional and integral gain were reduced by 54% and 81%, respectively.
The FF gain was kept variable.
The system was then tested in turbulent wind fields and two extreme gusts. Even though the FF
controller was optimized only using one seed per wind speed, turbulent simulations of six seeds displayed
loads reduction. For above-rated wind speeds, the average reduction of 7.2% of the fore-aft tower
bending DELs and 1.0% for the blade loads could be achieved. In the extreme case, tower bending
load were reduced up to 25% and blade bending loads by 15% for a 25 m/s gust. The load reduction
for a gust close to rated wind speed is much smaller.
In summary, this thesis shows that there is a load reduction potential for inexpensive continuous-wave
lidar that are nacelle-mounted and only have a small number of beams. However, tuning the FB and
FF controller plays a crucial role to achieve load mitigation.
Future work includes the inclusion of wing blockage influences and the beam switching. These ef-
fects will reduce the performance of the FF controller since information is removed from the system.
Additionally, lidar systems usually do not experience full availability. Thus, load simulation where
the lidar system suddenly becomes unavailable have to be considered. Also in this thesis only wind
fields that are aligned with the rotor axis have been considered. The analysis need to be extended to
misaligned wind fields, since during operation a turbine is usually not perfectly aligned to the wind
direction. The lidar sensor needs to incorporate the tower deflection and the tower top velocity. These
effects will increase inaccuracies of the lidar measurements and might have an effect on the controller
performance.
With respect to the controller design, it is possible to analytically calculate the optimal number and
distribution of the focal points in front of the turbine where the best coherence between the REWS
and the lidar-estimated REWS is reached. This calculation requires much less computational effort
than fining optimal points via simulations. Also the low-pass filter frequency can be derived.
