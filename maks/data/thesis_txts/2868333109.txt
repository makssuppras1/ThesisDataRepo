Experimental and Numerical Optimization of Clean rooms
Abstract
• This master thesis is related to a research project from Novo Nordisk’s cleanroom design.
• This project has two main goals: First design a cleanroom setup that is separated by a wall
that has door gaps and study the effects of those in particle contamination, and second,
simulation of the entire lab setup in a CFD model to validate the results.
• The first part of the project, examined the effect of three door gap designs on the particle
concentration from one room to another.
Specifically, the door gaps had the same width
but different heights, and variables such as velocities, temperature, pressure, volumetric flow
rates, tracer gas concentration, and particle concentration were included in the measurements.
Great emphasis was given on measuring leakage volumetric flow rates on the gaps, by using
three different methods. These methods consisted of inviscid theory, viscous theory and power
law.
• The latter part included a model that simulated the entire lab setup for the same variables.
Particularly, the model in Star ccm+, focused on studying velocities, pressure, temperature,
volumetric flow rate, mass flow rate, and particle contamination via a threshold at the door
gap area. The simulation was transiet and also included steady state runs, and the results
were used in order to validate the lab conditions. Such conditions included a fixed volumetric
flow rate of 124 L/s, a four- way inlet, and a fixed contamination source of 0.15 kg/s.

Introduction
1.1
Cleanroom Background
Cleanrooms have become a necessity in today’s Pharmaceutical industry, because of the need to
keep a room free of contaminants during the manufacturing of sterile products [2]. Such structures go
back more than a hundred years ago in medical facilities where the doctors needed a clean operating
table for surgery and to avoid bacterial infection of the patient [1]. Though the technology has
changed, the concept of creating such an environment has remained the same, and the method used
is through proper ventilation [1]. This method involves pressure differential between two rooms,
that either can drive the particles away from a room or keep them inside [3].
As a result of that, every attempt to maintain the particle concentration at a specific level
inside a cleanroom was classified for future use and cataloged in order to create standards for every
industry to use. Specifically, till 2001 the standards for a cleanroom were defined by FS209, an
American Air Force list of parameters for cleanroom environments from the 1960s [1,2].
Later on due to the need for global use, a set of standards by ISO (International Organization
of Standards) was published [4]. As shown in Figure 1, the size of particles and its allowable concen-
tration was defined based on which classification of cleanroom the industry needs - the specifications
are named ISO 146441 (1 till 9) [4].
Figure 1: ISO standards and cleanroom classification [1]
Additionally, it is important to highlight that particle concentration is a definitive factor in
terms of defining the air cleanness of a room [5]. People working inside the room can be considered
source of contamination, as well as the process of operating machines of production [4,5]. Based on
this concept the global organization of standards (ISO) has graded the allowable amount of particles
when the production is functioning and at rest [5]. This helps the industry keep the cleanness level
and particle sizes to a specific number [5]. Specifically, for particles equal to or greater than 0.5
and 5 µm the ISO guide provides different grades and acceptable amount for being at rest or in
operation of manufacturing pharmaceutical products [5]. Therefore every pharmaceutical company,

1.2
Ventilation
In order to achieve the filtration of particles inside the room, proper filters are used and should
be 99.97 % efficient in removing particles greater than 0.3 µm from the room air inlet. Such filters
are called High Efficiency Particle Air (HEPA) filters that maintain the particle concentrations at
low levels and they are installed just before the air enters the room from the ventilation [4,5,6,9].
Ventilation systems that are commonly used to control variables such as pressure, temperature,
velocity etc., in a cleanroom are called HVAC (Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning) [7]. Such
systems are used to control the concentration of particles inside a cleanroom depending on the type
of flow the user needs. The most commonly used categories of flows are called unidirectional and
non-unidirectional [1,5,7]. The unidirectional flow is characterized by laminar flow patterns that
can be either vertical or horizontal as can be seen in Figure 3 [8,9]. A good example of that is
displacement ventilation, the cleanness is achieved when the air entering the room from the supply
has parallel patterns to the floor or the wall of the room till it reaches the exhaust [9]. On the other
hand, the non-unidirectional flow has turbulent flow patterns as can be seen in Figure 4 [8,9]. In
this case a good example is mixed ventilation, the air entering from the inlet creates non parallel
circular patterns that help mixing the air of the room faster [9]

1.3
Pressure difference
With the use of HVAC a clean room can maintain the contamination standards on acceptable
levels, but in order to make this, pressure difference must be established between two rooms [10].
Pressurization can help keep contaminants out of the room or drive them away from a room de-
pending on the needs of the company [11,12]. By supplying more air than the air being exhausted
by the outlet, a room maintains positive pressure. In this case, the particles are driven towards the
exhaust and away from the inlet room [11,12]. On the contrary, by supplying less air than what is
exhausted, the room maintains negative pressure, and the particles stay in the room of supply inlet
[1,9,11,12].
According to the ISO global standards, the pressure difference between two rooms should be
from 5 to 20 Pa, since it is easier to achieve and maintain [4]. Whyte’s research affirms that the

1.4
Leakage measurement on gaps
As a result of the pressure difference between 2 rooms, air can escape through gaps or small
openings, assuming there’s positive pressure in the inlet room. Leakage is defined as the result of a
high speed outflow through a crack, that looks as a jet flow [14]. Generally, in cleanrooms, there are
controlled leakages through door gaps, in which the positive pressurized airflow can be controlled,
and as a result of that any unwanted contamination can be prevented [9].
Door gaps are also
beneficial because they help control the pressure differential between rooms easier [1,11,12,13,14].
The two main ways used by the industry to define the flow rate of leakages, are based on inviscid
leakage theory and the viscous leakage theory [13,15,16,21,22].
1.4.1

Literature Review and Objectives
2.1
Literature Review
This research project focused on studying the effect of three different door gaps sizes between
two rooms. The findings can be described as :
• the gap height doesn’t affect the velocities at the gap area [3,25]
• the Bernoulli principle is accurate for measuring flow rates from leakages [11,12,14,15,17,18]
• the gap size affects the particle contamination of room two [3,26,27]
In Kim’s research [3] the contamination of an isolation room was studied in regards with how the
gaps affect the particle concentration from the isolation room to the corridor.
Various sizes of
door gaps were introduced, but the experimental set up had negative pressure differential. This
research did not expand on positive pressure difference between the isolation room and the corridor
[3]. In Tailhan’s research [25] various crack geometries were examined for leakage properties. The
results show that velocity of air was not a necessary contributing factor in the volumetric flow rate
measurements, but only the Power law equation was used for calculations [25].
In Jia’s research [26] a steady pressure difference was maintained between two rooms, with
gaps of various sizes. The gaps affected the particle contamination through the experiments but the
research mostly focused on negative pressure between the rooms [26]. Additionally, in Wu’s study
[27], various gap sizes were modeled in a Computational Fluid Dynamics software called Star ccm
+, in order to report wether the transfer of contaminants has an effect by the gaps sizes. The results
reported that smaller gaps had higher particle concentration , but in bigger gaps the concentration
was lower [27].
Regarding the Bernoulli principle, a majority of studies use this method for measuring leakages
on cracks [11,12,14,15,17,18]. However those studies did not compare results with the viscous theory
or any other version of the Power law, additionally various researchers have a different opinion for
the discharge coefficient’s value for sharp edges [1,11,12,15,18,50].

2.2
Problem statement
2.2
Problem statement
This study originates from a Novo Nordisk installation of clean rooms, with grade classification
C and D, and door gaps in each room to control the pressure difference. Generally, the purpose of
these facilities, include a variety of tasks related to the aseptic production of pharmaceutical prod-
ucts. Specifically, the production involves processing of active pharmaceutical ingredients (API) of
a product, the part that is responsible for the drug’s main effect. Such processes demand a clean-
room free of contaminants. Subsequently, door gaps of various sizes are designed in the cleanroom
installations that help control the particle concentration between rooms.
With the above literature and case from Novo Nordisk, the purpose of this research project
is to examine which method for measuring leakage through three different crack sizes is the most
efficient. That involves , inviscid Bernoulli principle, viscous theory with a quadratic equation and
the general Power Law. Additionally, a pressure difference of 10 Pa was maintained with positive
pressurization in the main room , and three different gap sizes to compare results ,with 20 mm,
40 mm, and 60 mm different heights, and the same 70 cm withdrawal. Furthermore, the particle
concentration will be measured in order to understand if the gaps have an effect on the pollution
from one room to another.
Aside from CFD and Lab experiments, regarding the velocities of the particles in the gap
area. Particle image velocimetry experiments were ambitiously planned for the setup, but due to
limitations they were not carried out.
The hypotheses made in the beginning of the project:
• The supply inlet flow rate that is adjusted to 124 L/s can maintain 10 Pa pressure difference
between the two rooms in all gap cases.
• The velocities measured in the gap area have different values between lab measurements and
CFD simulations.
• The particle contamination measured in the smallest and the biggest gap height shows con-
nection between the height and pollution spread from room 1 to room 2.
• The system in both CFD and Lab setup is air-tight, which means the mass flow rate from
inlet to gap and oulet is the same. .
2.3
Objectives and goals
The main objectives of the project are :
• Establish a contamination pattern between room 1 and room 2 on each gap case.
• Examine if the gap affects particle contamination.
• Determine whether the Bernoulli principle is accurate for leakage measurements on the gap
area.
• Apply Computational Fluid Dynamics to develop a numerical model that simulates the same
conditions of the lab experiments.
• Validate and compare the simulation data with data from the physical lab experiments.
• Compare viscous, inviscid theory, and Power law on measuring leakage on the gap.

Methods
In this research project, measurements were carried out in a lab inside DTU sustain area. The
lab was adjusted in order to match the criteria of a working clean room in Novo Nordisk ’s facilities.
It is crucial to mention that only the interaction between two rooms was included in the experiments
not the entire setup of Figure 6. One wall was placed between the two rooms and a gap of three
different heights was adjusted every time.
The measurement data involved temperature, velocity, pressure, particle concentration, trace
gas concentration, and volumetric flow rate. The same data were utilized as a starting point for the
CFD simulations, since the entire numerical model was made in accordance to them. Furthermore,
in order to validate the CFD simulation results, the experimental data from the lab were used for
comparison.

3.1
Cleanroom experimental setup
Aside from validating the experimental data from the simulation results, a great emphasis was
given in reporting which variables the different gap sizes have an effect on. The methods included
flow rate adjustments of supply and exhaust diffusers and pressure difference at 10 pascal across all
gap sizes. Following the results, a proper set of ventilation adjustments was suggested.
Having set all those parameters in both lab and simulations, velocity results will be compared
with each other. This focus on velocities was adopted in order to determine whether the Bernoulli
Principle is accurate in measuring the leakage flow rate of the three gap sizes. Proper comparison
of other methods for measuring leakage flow rate was also made in accordance to the literature.
The steps of the project were as follows:
• Adjustment of the supply and exhausts flow rates in order to create a 10 pascal pressure
difference between the two rooms, in every gap variation.
• Perform tracer gas measurements to examine the effectiveness of the air mixing process.
• Measure Velocities in the gap area and also in a fixed location of the room, for every gap
variation.
• Set up a CFD model based on the results of the lab measurements.
• Perform particle concentration measurements for 2 gap sizes.
• Use 4 different equations to measure flow rates in the gap area.
For the CFD simulations Star ccm+ (2024 version) was used

3.10.1
Physics of the model:
• Gravity: Set at -9.81 m/s2 on Y axis because of the convected effect of the heat cylinder
(worker in the lab).
• Gas: Air flow inside a room
• Ideal Gas: A relation to temperature was needed, since all boundary conditions involve
temperature. Temperature relates to density, and the buoyancy effect, so in order to have
accurate temperature values in each boundary surface the ideal gas option was selected [46].
• Implicit Unsteady: An unsteady option was selected that can offer a bigger range of courant
number for the time step [38,39].
Furthermore, in Babich et al.
[39] research, a transiet
simulation gave accurate results for a ceiling fan simulation.
• Steady: This option was chosen because according to the literature, the K- epsilon model
gave accurate results with a steady state [42,43,46]. Therefore the residuals will include both
steady and unsteady, as will be detailed further in the results Chapter 4.
31
==PAGE:37==

CFD Modeling
• K-epsilon Turbulence: This model was selected because in similar turbulent models in
aseptic environments in Catalin’s and Murakami et al. researches, the k-epsilon model offered
accurate results [41,42].
• Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS): Accurate boundary layer effects are required
in this simulations as will be showcased in results section [43].
• Wall Distance and 2 -layer all Wall y+ treatment: This simulation involves boundary
layers, therefore flow on a solid surface will be included [43].
• Realizable k-epsilon Two-Layer Model: This is an HVAC application, and having accu-
racy for boundary layer and near wall flow is demanded.
• Segregated Flow and Segregated Flow Temperature: the governing equations of the
fluid flow (continuity, momentum, and energy), are solved separately for saving space and time
in computational cost. Additionally, the temperature option is selected because temperature
is involved in the simulation and all boundary conditions have a fixed value.
• Passive Scalar: This option was selected as a way to simulate tracer concentration in the
simulation.
• Adaptive time step: A CFL (courant number) provider was applied with max value 1 and
mean value 0.5 as suggested by Babich et al. research [39].
• No initial conditions were adjusted in any option.
3.10.2
Boundary Conditions of the model
The room design was split into 26 boundaries, and all adjustments were based on the experi-
mental lab setup of the 60mm gap case 4. Particularly, the adjustments included:
• Inlet room 1: the inlet of the system was a block with five surfaces front, back, left, right and
down. The down surface is a wall and had no adjustment. The remaining four part shaped
the 4-way diffuser that was used in the lab experiments. Specifically, the flow rate from the
supply is 0.124 m3/s and according to ASHRAE’s handbook [11,12]:
Qsupply = Vsupply · Asupply
(16)
– Asupply: dimensions of the supply (width x height)
– Qsupply: Volumetric flow rate of supply
– Vsupply: Air velocity of the supply
The width of the supply is 0.4 m and the height is 0.04 m, so from the above equation the
total air velocity should be 7.75 m/s. Since the supply is four-way, the total air velocity is
divided equally in four parts which results in 1.93 m/s, and the system has four velocity inlets.

6.2
Objectives completion
This section sums up the objectives that were accomplished during the project. The objectives
at the start of the project were:
• 1-Establish a contamination pattern between room 1 and room 2 on each gap case.
• 2-Examine if the gap affects particle contamination.
• 3-Determine whether the Bernoulli principle is accurate for leakage measurements on the gap
area.
• 4-Apply Computational Fluid Dynamics to develop a numerical model that simulates the
same conditions of the lab experiments.
• 5-Validate and compare the simulation data with data from the physical lab experiments.
• 6-Compare viscous, inviscid theory, and Power law on measuring leakage on the gap.
Objectives 1 and 2 are connected and accomplished via a particle concentration measurement
in the Case 4 and Case 2, which concluded that the gaps affect contamination, and that smaller
gaps can prevent better the contamination than bigger gaps.
Objectives 3 is related to the comparison made in the leakage calculation section of chapter 4.
The velocities from both lab and CFD were compared and show small difference with the Bernoulli
equation results for velocity.
Objectives 4-5 is accomplished and shown extensively in chapter 4. For the same conditions
applied in the lab, the CFD simulations offered results, that helped highlighting the non air-tight
conditions of the lab measurements.
Objective 6 is covered in the last section of results chapter, in which all methods are compared
for volumetric flow rate at the case 4 door gap. The highest values were obtained by Bernoulli,
followed by the two solutions of the Power law, and lastly the smallest values were calculated by
viscous theory.

7
Future implications and limitations
In this section limitations that occurred during the project will be presented as well as any
suggestions for future research.
The experimental setup could not offer an air- tight condition, because the rooms were not
design to simulate a clean room. Therefore, there were additional air leakages all over the setup.
Additionally, due to limited time of the lab’s availabilit , not a lot of pressure difference cases were
explored.
It would be very beneficial in a future study if lower or higher than 10 Pa pressure
differences between the rooms were applied.
Furthermore, due to limited amount of time and
generally the high complexity of the project, the CFD simulations did not include a Lagragian
multiphase addition in the simulation. Such method could simulate directly the chemical used for
tracer gas, and show the mixing pattern in the room. In addition, the project at the start had an
ambitious schedule for including Particle image velocimetry experiments but due to lack of proper
equipment the results obtained were not useful. Such method could provide velocity measurements
for all particles in the gap area with very high accuracy and therefore establish a leakage pattern
for each door gap case.
Another interesting suggestions could be the design of a new gap size
on a different position of the wall.
It would be very intriguing to compare it with the leakage
measurement results from this project.
