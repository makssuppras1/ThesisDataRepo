Abstract
The objective of this thesis was to assess the accuracy of the large-scale climatological models
for wind resource assessment at micro-sitting level. The task was carried out by comparing
the AEP from production data of 12 onshore and 7 offshore wind farms around Denmark, to
the estimated AEP obtained from large-scale models through a downscaling process using
WAsP (software for wind resource assessment). The power production was obtained from the
wind turbine register Stamdata, being long-term corrected with a wind energy index. The
large-scale models evaluated were the WRF mesoscale model and GWA reanalysis model.
Some wind speed measurements in the vicinity of the wind farms were also used for assisting
in the evaluation process. Furthermore, the uncertainties related to the AEP estimation
were investigated through sensitivity analyses, in order to quantify how much they affect the
AEP, and which are the possible ways to mitigate their impact. The results were considered
acceptable for onshore wind farms, being the aggregated error for all offshore wind farms
around -5 %. However, the onshore wind farms as a whole gave a bigger aggregated error,
with AEP overestimations of roughly 14 % and 23 % for the mesoscale and reanalysis models
respectively. The outcomes of the sensitivity analysis pinpointed the roughness length effect
as the main driver affecting AEP estimations. The CORINE roughness maps used for the main
calculations seemed to have too low roughness length values for Denmark, while GlobCover
maps improved the overall results, passing from a common overestimation to underestimate
almost all wind farms analysed. This highlighted a possible too high roughness length of
GlobCover maps, and hence roughness maps with values similar to GlobCover but a bit
lowered seemed to be the more suitable solution. In this matter, from the large-scale models,
the error could be also induced during their generalization process. If maps, with a wrong
interpretation of the roughness land covers, are employed for the generalization process, then
the generalized wind climate is not completely generalize and hence the errors are translated
to the downscaling process.
Keywords.
Wind energy, Downscaling, Large-scale, Reanalysis, GWA, Mesoscale, WRF,
Microscale, WAsP.

1 Introduction
In this chapter the thesis is presented along with the motivation. Some literature
review is described, and the problem statement is also presented. At the end, an
outline of the project introduces the main chapters.
1.1
Motivation
Years of measured wind speed is traditionally used to estimate the Annual Energy Production
(AEP) of wind turbines as wind speed is directly related to wind turbine power output. Using
microscale models as WAsP, the wind climate can be extrapolated to nearby wind turbine
sites, to estimate the production of a whole wind farm. However, there are modelling errors
associated with model extrapolation, and accurate long-term estimates require several years of
time-consuming measurements as the mean wind ﬂuctuates from year to year. The expensive
and time-consuming measurement task can be avoided by using large-scale climatological
models for wind resource assessment. Two of the most important large-scale models are the
Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) mesoscale model, and the Global Wind Atlas based
in reanalysis data. However, there are uncertainties in using modelled data as this need to be
scaled to microscales.
Denmark currently has nearly 6000 wind turbines in operation (onshore and offshore) [1], and
has set its own target of 100 % renewable energy in the entire energy supply and transport
sectors by 2050. For achieving this target, in the energy sector, by 2020 the energy agreement
sets plans for 1000 MW of offshore wind turbines, 500 MW of near-shore wind turbines and
500 MW of onshore wind turbines [2].
With such mix of existing and planned wind turbines, different sources of information for
future wind resource assessment of a speciﬁc site will be available: In some cases power
measurements of exiting wind turbines, or wind speed measurements in the vicinity of the site
assessed, but in many future cases, only large-scale climatological models will be available for
AEP estimations.

Chapter 1. Introduction
In some countries, like Denmark or Germany, a large amount of wind turbines have been
already installed and the “best” spots for wind exploitation have been taken. Site assessment
is crucial for ﬁnding new locations where the wind resource ensures an acceptable capacity
factor of the wind turbines. Therefore, large-scale models are ideal for this task, since the wind
resource of a region can be assessed without any wind speed measurements from the site.
Zhang [3], in his book “Wind Resource Assessment and Micro-Siting”, wrote about this prac-
tices, “Mesoscale NWP models are commonly used nowadays in wind resource assessments
in terms of a site hunt, that is searching for possible sites for wind power projects. In contrast
with micro-siting, which deals with the wind resource within a given project area, a site hunt
using mesoscale models can be referred to as macro-siting” [3].
Nonetheless, the focus of this thesis is not to assess the viability of the large-scale models for
site hunt, but to quantify the error incurred by the models when they are used for micro-siting.
Several studies have assessed the accuracy of large-scale models through wind speed esti-
mation [4], [5], [6] or energy ﬂux density [7]. However, this study assesses the accuracy of
large-scale models with real wind turbine production. The Danish Energy Agency (Ener-
gistyrelsen) provided this possibility by means of a national public database (Stamdata). This
database includes the production data and characteristics of all the installed wind turbines in
Denmark, as well as the wind turbines which have been decommissioned.
Therefore, some wind farms spread around Denmark were selected for the study. First, the
long-term AEP from production data will be calculated in a developed Matlab code, and
subsequently the long-term AEP from the large-scale models will be calculated with the WAsP
wind resource assessment tool. Thereafter, the performance of the large-scale models will be
assessed by comparison of AEPs. Consequently, the study intends to give an overview of how
accurate the WRF-Mesoscale and GWA-Reanalysis models are, for micro-sitting, in Denmark.
Additionally, one of the aims of the thesis is also to identify the sources of error and uncertain-
ties involved in the downscaling and AEP estimation process from the large-scale models, and
in general, from any wind climate used as input. Ways to mitigate the errors will be proposed
in order to improve the forecasting of wind energy production.
1.2
Literature review
The Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) large-scale models can generate long-term wind
data time series from stored weather measurements based on several decades. This wind data
series is continuously corrected based on short-term measurements.
Due to the coarse resolution of large-scale models, they cannot capture all the weather phe-
nomena of local wind climates. They cover weather phenomena of greater magnitudes.
However, the continuous development of these models makes them more sophisticated and
with a higher resolution [3].

1.3. Problem statement
Large scale models are insufﬁcient to capture all weather phenomena related to the local wind
resource. Meso-scale models zoom in from such large scale models and look at the missing
scales between large scales and local or micro-scales. It covers special wind phenomena such
as land and sea breezes, tropical hurricanes and convective storms. In the meantime, the
meso-scale is also commonly used to explicit large scale ﬂows modiﬁed by terrain features
such as hills, mountains and surface features, which are not resolved in the larger scale models.
As large scale and global models become more and more sophisticated and of increasing
resolution, the division between large scale and meso-scale becomes blurred [3].
The combination of WAsP with large-scale models to calculate local wind climates has been
described by Helmut P. Frank in [7], where the mesoscale model “KAMM” in combination
with WAsP was used for local predictions in Denmark, Ireland and Portugal (simple, slightly
complex and complex terrain). The results were compared with the reanalysis model NCEP
in combination also with WAsP, and with wind speed measurements. The mesoscale model
performed quite well for all sites with RMS values between 18 and 27 %, while the reanalysis
data set performed well only in Denmark (23.5 % error), having a big overestimation in Ireland
and Portugal (47 % error for both sites).
Zhang states that mesoscale models have a better performance in hilly regions than reanalysis
models due to its coarse resolution[8], and suggests mesoscale models for site hunt but with a
on-site wind data support.
The WRF mesoscale model in combination with WAsP was also study by Aparicio [9]. Through
the WAsP climatological outputs and with a developed Matlab code, the author calculated the
aggregated power curve for two areas in Denmark, and compare it to measured aggregated
power curve. The ﬁndings revealed a relative error of 25 %.
Wei et al. [4] predicted the wind speed at 40 m a.g.l. with a 5 km resolution mesoscale model
and a developed tool-kit. The results were evaluated with tower observations, and they re-
sulted in a 12.6 % wind speed overestimation from the mesoscale model.
Bayon i Barrachina [6] tried to simulate wind speed time series. The author created a downscal-
ing tool for obtaining the wind speed time series from the NCEP/NCAR Reanalysis Data 1 using
WAsP and a Matlab routine. The roughness maps used were from CORINE but the results were
not very satisfactory. The results obtained with the reanalysis model for two locations across
Europe were, as RMSE, 24 % and 63 % of wind speed underestimation in comparison with
measured data.
1.3
Problem statement
The aim of this thesis is to give some insight about the suitability of large-scale models for
wind farm resource assessment. If the large-scale models are able to represent closely local
wind climates, the expensive and long task of measuring wind speed can be avoided. Till now,
in the wind energy ﬁeld, these models have been used for analyse big regions in search of the
most windy areas. Nonetheless, the rapid development and increasing resolution of these

2.1.1
Power production data (Stamdata)
The power production data used in this thesis was taken from the “Stamdata” database. It is
an open national public database from Energistyrelsen (Danish Energy Agency in English).
This database is an Excel ﬁle, which contains technical information and production data of all
the operational as well as decommissioned wind turbines registered in Denmark.
The power production data is presented in a yearly basis for the previous years, and in a
monthly basis for the current year. By petition, the production data can be obtained in a
monthly basis for the previous years as well. Therefore, for this project data with a monthly
basis for every year was used since it maximizes the amount of valid data.
The ﬁrst records of production data are from 1980 in the case of yearly production. The
monthly production, which is the data chosen for this study, has a time span from January
2005 till today, but only data till March 2016 was considered.
Besides the power production, Stamdata provides more detailed information for each wind
turbine generator in Denmark. The most relevant information for this thesis was:
• Power capacity
• Rotor diameter
• Hub height
• Manufacturer
• Generator model
• Turbine position

2.1. Actual long-term AEP from production
The power production stored in Stamdata is the measured power production of the turbine
in kWh which is fed into the electrical grid (also called point of Common Coupling - PCC).
Therefore, the production stored is the net production after technical losses. The nature of
these technical losses is detailed explained in subsection 2.2.5, under the technical losses
paragraph.
Usually, and in the case of wind farms, the production data stored was not the individual power
production of each single wind turbine but the redistributed power production of the whole
wind farm among all the wind turbines forming part of the wind farm. This circumstance was
particularly more common for the offshore wind farms.
2.1.2
Wind energy index
The wind energy index was used in order to do a long-term correction of the production data
of a wind turbine, and subsequently calculate the long-term AEP.
Deﬁnition and purpose
The wind energy index is a tool for assessing how well a Wind Turbine Generator (WTG)
performed in a particular month. This is possible because the wind energy index corrects the
actual production of the WTG and compare it to long-term normalized wind conditions.
The wind energy index represents the average wind speed of a month compare it to a long-
term average (based on e.g. 10 years). The long-term level of a wind index shall be 100 %, and
it shall represent the available wind energy seen from a wind turbine [10]. The wind energy
index is typically presented on a monthly basis.
If a month had a wind index of 90 %, it indicated that the average wind speed of the month
was 10 % below of the long-term average.
Therefore, the wind energy index can be used for calculating the long-term Wind Corrected
Production (WCP) of a wind turbine and asses how well the wind turbine performed in a
month by subtracting the wind variability. Equation 2.1 is used for this purpose [11].
WCP = Actual Production·100

DK wind index
In Denmark, since the number of wind turbines is quite large, the wind energy index is
calculated using the monthly energy production of the WTGs and calibrated with MERRA
reanalysis data.
Remark that, as mentioned, the wind index should be representative of a long period. A
meteorological long-term period is normally 30 years, but for wind indices based in power
production data, the lifetime, lose of performance, and technology development of the wind
turbines makes difﬁcult to create a perfect wind index. Therefore, the wind index is made with
10 years periods, and it is constantly revised [10].
The DK wind energy index is calculated as the ratio between the monthly energy produc-
tion from a number of WTGs (named reference WTGs) and the same WTGs expected long
term average monthly production based on longer production period corrected with index.

EMD ConWx wind index
The EMD ConWx wind index is a model base wind index made in collaboration between EMD
International A/S and ConWx. The model uses mesoscale climatological data for creating a
monthly wind index. The mesoscale model has a high spatial resolution of 3 × 3 km with an
hourly temporal resolution. Spatial coverage is most of Europe [14].
Merra wind index
MERRA wind index is also a model base wind index based on wind speed from reanalysis
data sets. It has a spatial resolution of 55 × 70 km and a temporal resolution of 1 hour. The
wind index is calculated as the average between, the square root of each minimum wind
speed sample in a month, and, the number of samples available in the corresponding month.
The minimum wind speed sample is deﬁned as the minimum value between the wind speed
sample and the standard wind speed at which a power curve reaches the rated power. Spatial
coverage is global but limited to the land-areas and approximately 50 km into the ocean/sea
areas [14].

2.2
Estimated long-term AEP from wind climate models
This section explains how the estimated long-term AEP was obtained from large-scale wind
climates and in addition from wind speed measurements from different locations. WAsP,
which is the acronym of Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program, was the software used
in this thesis for calculating the AEP from the different wind climate models. This software
was developed by DTU Wind Energy department.
2.2.1
Microscale WAsP model
The WAsP software is a tool for modelling of wind ﬂow over terrain for wind resource assess-
ment and site assessment for wind turbines and wind farms [15].
The wind resource assessment is made with the climatological and map inputs. By inserting
the turbine positions and wind turbine generator power curves, WAsP is able to calculate the
potential yield AEP of the wind turbines.
The wind-climatological input to WAsP contains the wind direction distribution (wind rose)
and the sector-wise distributions of mean wind speed (histograms), as it can be seen in the
example of Figure 2.8. The wind speed is given in m/s, and the wind direction in degrees (from
0◦to 360◦) being north 0◦.
Figure 2.8: Observed wind climate from the LIDAR situated in Anholt wind farm
WAsP estimates the wind resource through the Weibull parameters A and k for the 12 sectors
in which the wind rose is divided.
17
==PAGE:36==
Chapter 2. Materials and Methods
In order to calculate the wind resource in different sites, WAsP uses a Generalize Wind Climate
(GWC). This GWC can be generated by WAsP from local wind speed measurements following
the Wind Atlas Methodology, or it can be obtained directly in the form of GWC as .g wc or
.lib ﬁles. These GWCs can also be obtained from the large-scale (mesoscale and reanalysis)
climatological models.
Figure 2.9 represents the Wind Atlas Methodology [16] implemented in WAsP.
The Wind Atlas Methodology is based in the assumption that the geostrophic conditions at the
two sites studied are the same. Therefore, if wind speed measurements from a site (point A)
are available the wind resource for the turbine site (point B) can be obtained. This is made by
removing, from the wind resource at the measured site, the local effects and creating a regional
wind climate. This regional wind climate is extrapolated by Geostrophic Drag Law [17] to the
new site, and thereafter the local effects of the new site are applied in the downscaling process.
The local effects accounted in the calculations are: sheltering obstacles, roughness of the land
and orography of the terrain.

climate is generalized, extrapolated and downscaled to the calculation site

As can be deduced from Figure 2.9, WAsP is based on two fundamental assumptions: ﬁrst,
the generalised wind climate is assumed to be the same at the predictor (met. station) and
predicted sites (wind turbines) and, secondly, the past (historic wind data) are assumed to be
representative of the future (the 20-y life time of the wind turbines). The reliability of WAsP
predictions depends very much on the extent to which these two assumptions are fulﬁlled
[18].
This procedure of generalizing and downscaling is applied when the local wind climate has to
be extrapolated to another point. In the case of using large-scale models, the wind climate is
already generalized, and only the downscaling process has to be made for the site of interest.

LIDAR Anholt special case
For the case of the LIDAR situated in Anholt some processing of the data was needed. The
wind speed and wind direction time series were downloaded from a SQL database, which
access was provided by DTU. The period of measurements covered 2 years and 6 months
with a time span resolution of 10 minutes. These sets of data could be obtained from several
heights between 25 m to 315 m a.s.l. However, at low heights, the wake effects could affect the
LIDAR measurements when the wind was blowing from the wind farm direction. Furthermore,
since the wake is expanded after the turbine rotor, it was necessary to calculate the diameter
of the expanded wake in the LIDAR position to check if it could affect the measurements. This
diameter of the wake was calculated accordingly to Equation 2.6 [25].
Dw = D +2kX
[m]
(2.6)
being Dw the diameter of the expansion of the wake, D the rotor diameter, X the distance to
the calculation point, and k the wake decay that will be explained in subsection 2.2.5.
The distance X was considered as the distance from the further wind turbine to the LIDAR
(12 km), as wake effects can persist approximately 30-60 km [26]. This gave an expansion diam-
eter in the LIDAR site of 1320 m, meaning that effects could be seen ∼742 m high. Therefore,
as measurements did not reached that height, it was decided that, since the total height of the
wind turbines was 141.6 m, the measurements from 185 m height would be taken. Therefore,
the measurements could be affected by the wake effects, but they would be rather low, and
besides, the LIDAR was situated on the west part of the wind farm, which is from where the
predominant winds come.
Once the data was obtained it was inserted into WAsP Climate Analyst for post processing.
The observed wind climate (OWC) should represent as closely as possible the long-term wind
climate at anemometer height at the position of the meteorological mast. Therefore, an integer
number of full years must be used when calculating the OWC, in order to avoid any seasonal
bias. For the same reason, the data recovery rate must be quite high (> 90-95 %) and any
missing observations should preferably be distributed randomly over the entire period [18].
Therefore, after the data was inspected visually for anomalies, 2 completed years were taken
from the entire period. It is important to mention that besides the annual seasonal bias, care
must be also taken when making an OWC from a small amount of years. The period should be
representative of a long period of years, i.e. it should be considered a normal wind energy year,
and hence its wind index should be close to 100 %.
Finally, the period with the higher data recovery rate and a normal wind index was chosen.
The period covered from March 2013 to March 2015, with an acceptable data recovery rate of
97.42 %. Finally, the observed wind climate was calculated and exported to an OWC ﬁle.

Wake losses
One of the main energy losses in a wind farm are the wake losses, also called array losses since
these much depend on the array conﬁguration of the wind farm.
The European Wind Energy Association, in their report The Economics of Wind Energy, quantify
these losses: Array losses, or park effects, which occur due to wind turbines shadowing one
another in a wind farm, leaving less energy in the wind downstream of each wind turbine.
These losses may account for 5-10 % of the theoretical output described by the power curves,
depending on the turbine rotors, the layout of the wind farm and the turbulence intensity [28].
The wake effects can last for several kilometres downstream of a wind farm. For large wind
farms, the wind ﬂow takes approximately 30-60 km to recover [26]. For this reason, it is
important to include all the large wind farms in the surrounding of the wind farm studied, in
order to account for the external wake effects.
The wake effects were implemented in WAsP by grouping all the individual wind turbine
generators part of a wind farm into a turbine site group. Wake losses between close wind farms
were also implemented, as well as individual wind turbines around the wind farm or wind
farms studied. This was implemented by grouping all the wind farms and individual wind
turbines into the same turbine cluster.
The wake effect modelling were implemented by the k value which is the Wake Decay Constant.
The Wake Decay Constant k gives the rate of expansion of the wake and at the same time
the rate at which the wind speed deﬁcit recovers [29]. In WAsP a default value of k = 0.075
was implemented for onshore conditions. However, for offshore wind farms this value was
changed to 0.05 as recommended in [18].
Eventually, the offshore wind farms were also studied in Fuga, which is an extension from
WAsP, speciﬁc for studying wake losses in large offshore wind farms. However, the results
were practically identical to the results from WAsP, and hence they were not considered in the
results section.

Technical Losses
WAsP takes into account the wake losses due to the interaction between wind turbines. After
the wake modelling, the potential AEP of a wind farm was obtained, however, for obtaining the
net AEP of the wind farm, other losses, which are not implemented in WAsP, had to be taken
into account. These losses are categorized in availability, electrical, turbine performance,
environmental, curtailments and others.
These losses must be estimated for each project in question and subtracted from the AEP
calculated by WAsP, in order to obtain the metered production at the PCC. Because the losses
are given as a fraction of the WAsP result, these need to be factored together. The additional
losses vary greatly, but are often about 5-10 % of the total WAsP AEP [18].
After the technical losses were discounted from the long-term potential AEP of WAsP, the result
was the long-term net AEP, also known as P50 in wind farm economic analyses. P50 means that
there is 50 % chance that the actual production will be lower and a 50 % chance that the actual
production will be higher than the predicted long-term net yield AEP.

The AEP difference between power curves from WAsP or WindPro will depend upon how
the wind speed distribution is at the calculation height. Higher winds will reduce the AEP
differences since both power curves will produce most of the time at rated power. On the other
hand, if the wind speed distribution is shifted towards low winds, the differences might be
higher due to the different conversion values (zone of the power curve between cut-in and
rated speed).
Therefore, an analysis was performed for the three wind climates, though similar results were
expected between them since the three wind models should have a similar wind distribution
for the same site.
2.4.2
Technical losses uncertainty
Even though there are some reports assessing the technical losses on wind turbines, there
is still a wide uncertainty range about these losses. Since these losses have a big impact on
AEP estimations, a sensitivity analysis was performed by implementing different percentages.
Therefore the change on net AEP was studied for 3 different percentages, which were the
minimum and maximum from the range proposed in [18] and the value implemented in
subsection 2.2.5, to say 5 %, 10 % and 7.96 %.
2.4.3

The roughness effect is assessed in this section. The main land cover, which corresponds to
farm land, was increased from its default roughness value of 5 cm. Therefore, the new maps

maps were inserted into the WAsP model, and the effect on AEP was analysed by comparing
the roughness increase with its consequent decrease on AEP.

power production was 90 %.

2.4.6
WCP ﬁtting method
The previous sections analyse the impact of different factors for AEP estimation. This section,
in contrast, analyses how the long-term AEP from actual production is calculated. A difference
on the ﬁnal AEP result could occur because of a different procedure for cleaning of outliers, or
a different ﬁtting method. In the present study, since it is expected that the ﬁtting method has
a higher impact in the ﬁnal AEP result, a different ﬁtting method was analysed.
In subsection 2.1.4 the ﬁtting method for the actual production data was explained. It consisted
in ﬁt a line to the data points but forcing it through the origin (0,0). This was done because
for a wind index of 0 %, the production should be 0 as well. If it was not forced to zero this
condition was not fulﬁlled (though the power output corresponding to a wind index of zero
was very low). However, EMD International A/S (software and consultancy company) does
this procedure without forcing the ﬁt through zero, and this can be observed because the
trend-line equation has some offset [36]. This is also happening in the Turbine Performance
feature from myWindTurbine project.

4 Discussion
This chapter gives a ﬁnal discussion about the use of large-scale models for wind
resource assessment, in order to assess their feasibility. The results from the local
wind climates are also accounted as support in the discussion of the large-scale
models. Moreover, the uncertainties are evaluated in order to ﬁnd out potential
improvements.
Wind farm AEP estimation from large-scale models
The results obtained from the large-
scale models in comparison with the AEP from production were similar for all wind energy
indices, though Merra performs better in four out of the six cases (Table 3.4). The large-
scale models gave a small AEP underestimation for the case of offshore wind farms, and a
considerable overestimation for the onshore wind farms.
The accuracy of the large-scale models for offshore wind farms, could be considered quite
acceptable with an aggregate error around -5 % and a %RMSE around 8 %. This was not
the case for onshore wind farms, where the aggregate errors (for DK and EMD ConWx wind
indices) were around 14 % and 22.6 % for mesoscale and reanalysis respectively with amplitude
errors around 20 % and 25 %. These errors were quite close to the ﬁndings presented by Frank
et al. [7] in Denmark, obtaining for the KAMM mesoscale model a RMSE value of 22 %, and for
the NCEP reanalysis model 23.5 %.
However, with the Merra wind index the results were a bit better achieving, in this thesis,
aggregated errors of 11.4 % and 20 %, with a RMSE of roughly 17 % and 23 %.
Inside the offshore wind farm category, the wind farm situated next to Sprogø is a particular
case. With wind speed measurements, the AEP estimation was very accurate. However, for the
large-scale models, the AEP estimated for Sprogø did not seem to follow the trend of results
from the other offshore wind farms. While in most of the offshore wind farms the AEP from
large-scale models was underestimated, for the case of Sprogø the AEP was overestimated
with a considerably error of around 15 % (Tables 3.1 to 3.3).
One of the reasons for this explanation could be that the Sprogø wind farm could be considered
more as onshore wind farm since the coastlines of Sjælland and Fyn are respectively 8.5 km
57
==PAGE:76==
Chapter 4. Discussion
and 9 km far. Therefore, as the large-scale models showed an AEP overestimation trend for
onshore wind farms, the AEP error incurred in Sprogø could be considered natural.
However, when the map sensitivity analysis was performed for Sprogø (section 2.4), it showed
that the wind farm is not so affected by the roughness from the surrounding land. This meant
that the 8 km of open sea around Sprogø mitigated the roughness effects from the land.
In order to dismiss any wrong data from the real production, the production of the wind
turbines from Sprogø was compared with the production of the same WTG model of other
wind turbines spread around Denmark. It was found that the production data from the WTGs
of Sprogø did match quite well with the production data from other WTGs, and hence the
error could not be linked to production data. Besides, as mentioned before, the error from
wind speed measurements was really low.
Therefore, for this reason, it seemed that the large-scale models did not “see” the Sprogø
location as an offshore wind farm, and the large-scale models did not work so well for this
particular case.
From Table 3.1 it can be seen that the error of the reanalysis model for the Billund 2 wind farm
is striking, with a value of almost 69 %. As presented in section 3.1, this particular wind farm
showed a low efﬁciency in comparison with others wind farms using the same WTG model.
However, later, it was found out that the big error of the wind farms situated in Billund was
mainly due to the wrong interpretation of the roughness lengths of the surroundings.
The error incurred from reanalysis model in Randers 2 was also quite high with 36.3 %. The
hub heights from Billund 2 and Randers 2 are 46 m and 64 m respectively, highlighting that the
reanalysis model gives worst results for low heights. But this was just a consequence of a low
roughness length in the surroundings. Since the turbines are low, they are more affected by
the roughness. But there is another key point, and it is related with the generalization process
of the large-scale models. If the maps used for the generalization process did not have the
right roughness length values, then the GWC will not be really generalize since the local effects
have not been completely removed.
As an example, if the roughness length of the maps used for the generalization process were
higher than the map use in the wind resource assessment, then the mean wind speed will be
much higher. This is probably what happened with the onshore wind farms studied. This is
also the reason why the offshore wind farms studied had better results, because the roughness
length is zero on the sea and there are not mismatches between the roughness of the maps
used for the generalization and the map of the studied site. If the site is several kilometres
away from the coast there are no roughness effects.
Wind farm AEP estimation from measurements
For the case when the assessment was
made with available wind speed measurements, the estimated AEP from offshore wind farms
became quite close to the actual AEP. DK and Merra wind indices had an aggregate error of
only -0.1 %.
58
==PAGE:77==
The results for onshore wind farms, as the results from large-scale models, were quite bad and
showed a common overestimation of around 10 %.
The main driver for such bad results was the site differences between the measurement sites
and the wind farm sites. One of the principles for wind resource assessment from wind speed
measurements is that the measurements should be representative of the site, i.e. similar
surrounding conditions, like: elevation, land cover, closeness to the coastline, etc. According
to Landberg et al. [38], the wind resource will be underestimated if the observations were
made in a high-roughness or sheltered areas, and it will be overestimated if the observations
were from a low-roughness area.
By studying the map from Figure 2.3, and taking into account that, the predominant winds in
Denmark are westerly winds, it can be seen which <masts-wind farms> groups fulﬁl or do not
fulﬁl the similarity principle.
The following wind farms had wind measurements from representative sites: Anholt, Ringkøbing-
Skjern 1&2, Sprogø, Rødsand, Nysted, and partially Randers 1&2. The %RMSE of all these wind
farms was 2.3 %, and accounting only with the onshore wind farms the value was 10.6 %.
The wind farms Billun 1&2, Kerteminde and Holstebro did not fulﬁl the similarity principle. For
example, the Billund 1&2 wind farms are located in the central part of Jylland (high-roughness),
while the reference mast is in the west coast next to the Ringkøbing fjord (low-roughness). The
%RMSE of these four wind farms was 31.7 %.
Besides the similarity principle issue, wind speed measurements for wind farm calculations
should be taken from at least 2/3 the height of the wind turbines, and some of the masts
were only 10 m high. Moreover, usually the wind speed measurements are averaged every 10
minutes, while for the low masts the measurement interval was 60 minutes, and for Beldringe
mast it was 180 minutes, which corresponded to the higher error in Kerteminde wind farm.
The results were also presented graphically in Figures 3.1 to 3.8. The plots highlighted the
high correlation between actual and estimated production in the offshore wind farms, and the
deviation for onshore wind farms. The onshore overestimation showed a +17.7 % offset (for
the large-scale models) from actual production (Figure 3.2). However, for onshore wind farms
with wind speed measurements, Figure 3.4 did not showed anything coherent.
Regarding the different wind energy indices, if any conclusions have to be taken, for offshore
conditions the EMD ConWx wind index performs better with large-scale models, but really
bad with wind speed measurements, but mainly caused because of the deviation in Anholt
wind farm. For wind speed measurements Merra performs really well. For onshore wind farms
the Merra wind index is the best for all models. The better performance of EMD ConWx and
Merra could be due to their smaller spatial resolution compared with the DK wind index.
Still, the differences obtained between the wind indices, although signiﬁcant, are not so big.
Furthermore, the performance of these wind indices could have change just by implementing
more or less technical losses. No ﬁrm conclusion can be extracted about which wind index is
59
==PAGE:78==

clearly the best, and hence all three are suitable for AEP corrections in Denmark. Nevertheless,
calculating the AEP from all three wind indices reveals uncertainties also on the AEP from
production.
AEP estimation in mast positions
Additionally to the wind farms estimated AEP calculation,
the large-scale models were tested in the masts positions. It helped for understanding more
about the nature of the error in the AEP estimation with the large-scale models, specially for
onshore conditions.
Table 3.5 showed the error on AEP with the large-scale models in comparison with the AEP
from the wind speed measurements.
The results from low measurements showed that the mesoscale model is clearly underestimat-
ing the AEP for very low heights, as contrary as found in section 3.1. The reanalysis model, on
the contrary, not only keeps overestimating the wind resource but the overestimation is even
more remarkable. The deviations were -11.5 % for mesoscale and 23.9 % for reanalysis
The results from the high measurements supports the previous ﬁndings in section 3.1. The
error from both large-scale models are quite similar (∼11 %), but looking at the masts with
similar hub heights to the one of a WTG, the mesoscale model performs better than the
reanalysis. However, for a height of 125 m the reanalysis model performed better with half the
error of the mesoscale model. It pointed out that for very high heights the reanalysis performs
better than the mesoscale model. However, in order to extract a ﬁrm conclusion about how
the models perform, more cases at high heights should be examined.
For the case of the offshore measurements from the LIDAR situated in Anholt, the results also
reﬂect the ﬁndings in section 3.1. The large-scale models tend to underestimate the AEP in
offshore conditions, and in this case with a higher height it is more remarkable.
Comparing AEP results between modelled data ignores four important uncertainties: turbine
positions, WTG power curve, technical losses and long-term AEP estimation from production
data. Looking at the project diagram (Figure 2.1) the main uncertainties left are the maps and
wind climate models.
The maps, still can have an inﬂuence in the error because the large-scale models need to be
downscaled, while the reference, which is the OWC, was taken from the calculation point and
does not need any downscaling or generalization process. Therefore, the error on the AEP
estimation of the models can be due to the models itself or from the roughness lengths of the
used map in the WAsP project. The errors of the models might come from: the data sets used
(wrong measurements), wrong roughness maps for the generalization process, failures in the
code of the model, etc.
Therefore, it can be concluded that is quite difﬁcult to quantify how much of the error from the
large-scale models come from the models itself or from the maps used in the wind resource
assessment. But for the present project, with the maps used, the error of the large-scale
60
==PAGE:79==
models respect to the OWCs (at masts height) is around 11 % (as seen in section 3.2), although
more measurements from heights above 50 m could have helped for a more precise and
reliable ﬁgure. Consequently, the difference between this percentage and the aggregated
errors calculated in section 3.1 are due to other uncertainties.
Uncertainties in AEP estimations
Following, the main uncertainties analysed in this thesis
are discussed by measuring its impact on the estimated AEP calculation. Suggestions for
reducing the uncertainty in these ﬁelds are also given.
• Power curve
The sensitivity analysis of the power curves highlighted the importance
of using the right WTG power curve for the power conversion from wind speed. Reliable
power curves from producers are sometimes very difﬁcult to obtain. The library of power
curves in WAsP is very limited, and therefore some power curves had to be created. This
task was infeasible since detailed power curves with the power and thrust values could
not been found in the web page of the manufacturer. Finally, the power curves were
obtained from the library of another software, WindPro.
Since the WTGs from the WAsP library were also in the WindPro library, it was study the
implementation of the same model, with the same air density, from both libraries into
the same WAsP project.
Table 3.6 shows the differences on AEP obtained from both sources. From the six
generators analysed, only one model gave the same power production. Two of them gave
practically the same power, and one of them had a small difference of 0.6 %. However,
for both Siemens models, the mean AEP difference for four wind farms studied with the
same model was around 2.1 %, and for one wind farm with another Siemens model it
was 2.5 %. These differences are quite considerable, and highlighted the importance of
using the right power curve which represents more closely the real power conversion of
the wind turbine studied.
Therefore, careful must be taken when applying the WTG into WAsP, since the results
showed that for the same model AEP differences can be up to 3 %.
• Technical losses
The technical losses are one of the main uncertainties which have
more impact in the net AEP estimation. Nevertheless, these losses are very easy to
implement during the AEP estimation since they are discounted directly from the po-
tential AEP calculated in WAsP. Normally the technical losses account for 5∼10 % of the
potential AEP of the wind farm [18], [39], being a considerable percentage that should
be studied in depth.
Therefore, it is important to quantify these losses as accurate as possible for each partic-
ular wind farm. Spengemann and Borget [32] suggested that, energy yield assessments
should be related to the real technical operation of wind farms and to the site-speciﬁc
losses. These losses can vary from one location to another even if the same wind farm
61
==PAGE:80==
Chapter 4. Discussion
is built. Therefore, a precise study for every wind farm in terms of technical losses is
needed.
Needless to say, that, the percentage difference between the implemented technical
losses and the real technical losses of the wind farm, amounts directly to the the total
error between real and estimated AEP.
• Roughness length
The roughness length can have different interpretations for the
same land cover. Figure A.2 in appendix shows a land cover classiﬁcation with roughness
lengths from three sources, CORINE, KNMI (Koninklijk Nederlands Meteorologisch
Instituut) and European Wind Atlas. Figure A.1 in appendix shows the GlobCover land
cover classiﬁcation with the roughness length values used in the Global Wind Atlas.
In general, the roughness length corresponding to one land cover use is not the same in
all the aforementioned classiﬁcations, and hence, the estimated AEP from a wind farm
will depend upon how the surroundings are interpreted.
Figures 3.10 to 3.12 highlighted that even small differences in the interpretation of the
roughness lengths lead to big changes in AEP estimation. The plots also depicted that
lower wind turbines are more affected by the roughness changes. This is due to the
logarithmic proﬁle of the wind.
Usually, the main land cover type in Denmark is the farm land (also called cropland).
This land cover type has a roughness length of 5 cm for CORINE, between 3 to 17 cm for
KNMI, between 3 to 5 cm for European Wind Atlas and 10 cm or 30 cm for GlobCover.
The main calculation of this thesis used the maps from myWindTurbine which have the
CORINE values, therefore for farm land the roughness length was 5 cm. By increasing
the roughness length of this land cover to the values speciﬁed by other sources, the AEP
decreased considerably. For the four wind farms studied, as average, the AEP decreased
around 7 % for 10 cm, 13 % for 17 cm and 20 % for 30 cm.
This pointed out the huge error that can be incurred in the estimated AEP if the rough-
ness lengths of the wind farms surroundings are not well speciﬁed. The surface rough-
ness based on satellite images may be very different from on-site observations [40].
Therefore, care must be taken regards this uncertainty, and the surrounding area of the
projected wind farm should be visited in-situ for a reliable interpretation of the wind
farm area.
• Map source
The maps give the orography and roughness effects around the wind
farm site. Three different map sources were analysed in subsection 3.3.4. Besides the
important factor of the roughness length of the land cover of these maps, the level of
detail of the land cover contours and the orography was also different between the
sources. In Figure 2.17 the different level of detail of the roughness contours can be
seen.
Table 3.8 summarizes the results from Table 3.7, where the errors expressed the differ-
ences between estimated AEP obtained with the chosen map, and the AEP from actual
production. The results highlighted that using the GlobCover maps, the estimated AEP
62
==PAGE:81==
from large-scale models became quite closer to the real AEP. Specially the GWA reanaly-
sis model had the lowest error by the fact that this model uses GlobCover maps for its
generalization process [23] (globalwindatlas.com/datasets). However, GlobCover maps
worsened the results with the wind speed measurements.
The maps giving the highest AEP deviation were the SRTM maps. The aggregated
differences between these maps and the maps giving the lower error were around
13 % with mesoscale models, 24 % with reanalysis models, and 5 % with wind speed
measurements. Looking at the wind farms individually, some differences amounted up
to 83.4 % as it is the case of Billund 2.
Considering only the aggregated errors these differences were very big, but in some
individual cases as the aforementioned in Billund, they were huge.
The main reason for such huge differences, apart for the level of detail of the maps, was
the roughness length values of the GlobCover maps. These values were higher than in
the other sources. For the farm land and forests, which were the bigger areas in that
region, the roughness length of the SRTM maps were 5 cm for both land cover types,
which means that they were not differentiated. CORINE maps from myWindTurbine
differentiated most of the forest areas, and the roughness lengths were 5 cm for farm
land and 50 cm for forests. For the GlobCover maps the values were 30 cm and 150 cm
respectively, pointing out the big difference.
Taking into account this roughness lengths differences and the ﬁndings in subsec-
tion 3.3.3, the big differences in the results were not surprising.
In conclusion, the roughness values from the CORINE and SRTM maps seem too low.
However the values from GlobCover, tough they gave better results for the large scale
models, are a bit too high since the AEP is usually a bit underestimated. Therefore, a
map with similar roughness length values to the ones from GlobCover but a bit lowered
seems to be the best solution. This idea is also supported by the fact that for the case
of wind measurements, which should be more reliable than the large-scale models,
GlobCover gives the lower mean error but with an underestimation of -9.7 % (however it
has not the lower RMSE).
With wind speed measurements the CORINE maps in overall gave a better result because
GlobCover underestimated too much. However, with a bit lower values than GlobCover,
but higher than CORINE, the error could be reduced in the large-scale models and
wind measurements, giving the best results. Furthermore, this analysis highlighted that,
results from large-scale models are much more accurate if the map used in the site
assessment has the same roughness length classiﬁcation than the roughness map used
in the generalization process. In this case, if an error is induced from these maps in
the GWC, then the same error, but with opposite direction, will be induced during the
downscaling process.
• Air density
The air density, despite the other uncertainties, had the lower impact
on AEP estimation. Specially for Denmark, due to its climatological and orographic
63
==PAGE:82==

conditions, the air density does not differs that much from the default air density used
for wind energy assessment.
For the four wind farms analysed, the speciﬁc air density at hub height increased the
AEP about 0.5 % for all the wind models, as it can be seen in Table 3.9.
Therefore, the speciﬁc air density of a site is something to take into account, but it does
not affect that much the AEP estimation.
• WCP ﬁtting method
Errors can also come from the AEP used as reference. Depending
how the production data is treated and how the outliers are deﬁned, the real AEP of a
wind turbine or wind farm can differ lightly. The alternative ﬁtting method analysed in
subsection 3.3.6, which is the method used by EMD in WindPro, did not show big differ-
ences. In general terms, the analysed ﬁtting method without forcing the ﬁtting through
the origin (0,0) gave higher AEP values with the DK wind index, and lower AEP values
for the EMD ConWx and Merra wind indices. However, the biggest %RMSE difference
with the ﬁtting method forced through zero was 3.4 %. All the other combinations of
wind indices and climate models were below this value.
In general, the method implemented in this thesis gave a bit better results, but both
methods could be used since the differences were quite low. Just maybe it is more
coherent to use the main method since the ﬁt is forced trough the origin. In any case,
care should be taken since the ﬁtting method applied can change the interpretation of
the results.
• Stamdata
Another factor which has not been mentioned before is the uncertainty
around the production database used. From Energistyrelsen it was made known that the
database could had some mistakes, as it was conﬁrmed during the proceedings, where
some mistakes and inconsistencies were found. Hopefully, the wind farms selected did
not have mistakes, but it could be the case when some errors were unusually high.
In wind resource assessment, one of the main uncertainties are the wind speed measurements.
However, as the focus in this thesis was put on the large-scale models, this uncertainty was not
assessed. From all the discussed uncertainties, the map source in common with the roughness
length, are the uncertainties which can have a bigger inﬂuence on AEP estimations. This is
followed by the level of technical losses implemented, which sometimes are difﬁcult to deﬁne.
Then the power curves and the method for calculating the real AEP of the wind farm/turbine
have a similar uncertainty level. At last, the air density of the speciﬁc site, which can alter
slightly the power conversion.
To conclude the discussion, the previous analyses suggest to increase the roughness length
values almost to the GlobCover level. This will decrease the error of large-scale models, as well
as the error with wind speed measurements, to acceptable values.

5 Conclusion
This chapter presents the ﬁnal conclusions of this master thesis and suggest some
future works to be made in order to to obtain more reliable results with the large-
scale models.
5.1
General conclusion
In this master thesis the accuracy of the large-scale climatological models for wind resource
assessment have been investigated. The large-scale models studied were the WRF mesoscale
model and the GWA reanalysis model. Some wind speed time series which were available
from different points in Denmark were also included in the study in order to help in the
identiﬁcation of errors.
The accuracy of the models was assessed by comparison of the estimated AEP with the
production from the national public database Stamdata, which includes all the installed and
decommissioned wind turbines in Denmark. This database is made by Energistyrelsen and it
is available online. The production data from seven offshore and twelve onshore wind farms
from around the whole Denmark were analysed. The ﬁndings revealed that, when calculating
the estimated AEP of the wind farms, the large-scale models can be quite accurate for offshore
wind farms, showing a slightly underestimation. For the onshore wind farms, the opposite
trend was found. The results showed a common AEP overestimation, with a much higher error
than offshore wind farms.
For some wind farms, the estimated AEP was also calculated with wind speed measurements.
For offshore wind farms the result was excellent while for onshore conditions the error was
almost as bad as with the large-scale models. The reasons for the bad results were because
some OWCs were not representative of the wind farm sites.
A posteriori analysis of uncertainties was performed in order to identify the sources of error.
This analysis underlined the high impact of the maps in the process of AEP estimation. Ac-
tually, the high error of the climatological models was mainly due to a low roughness length
65
==PAGE:84==
Chapter 5. Conclusion
interpretation of the surroundings of the wind farms. Better results were achieve using Glob-
Cover maps (high roughness length values) instead of CORINE maps, but roughness values
between the range of GlobCover and CORINE appeared to be the best solution.
Therefore, it was conclude that large-scale models are quite suitable for offshore wind resource
assessment, being ideal since it is difﬁcult to obtain offshore wind speed measurements.
For onshore conditions, the errors makes them still not reliable for wind resource assess-
ment, although maps with roughness values similar to GlobCover can decrease the errors to
trustworthy values. Nevertheless, large-scale models are ideal for site hunting.
5.2
Future works
In order to gain a better understanding of the accuracy of the large-scale models, more wind
farms should be analysed. The research could be extended to wind farms with smaller wind
turbine generators, or stand alone wind turbines.
Moreover, in order to make the AEP results from the large-scale models reliable, ﬁrst further
investigations should be directed into the roughness length classiﬁcations and land cover
identiﬁcations. The roughness length values from different sources should be revised and
veriﬁed for the speciﬁc region. This process could be done by studying a large amount of wind
farms, and adjust the roughness values so the maps give more accurate AEP estimations. This
study can be doable in Denmark thanks to the power production of all WTGs stored in the
Stamdata register.
Linked with the roughness length, research should be made about the roughness maps used
for the generalization process of large-scale models. Maps used for the site assessment should
be of the same characteristics than the maps used for generalizing the large-scale models. A
database containing roughness maps for each large-scale model with their corresponding
roughnesses could improve the outcomes, in terms of AEP calculations, from the models.
Also, more comparisons of the large-scale models with wind speed measurements, from
Denmark or from around the world, could give a better approximation of the error incurred by
the large-scale models. The wind speed time series from different heights could explain the
performance of these models for different hub heights. Studies in mountainous terrain should
be addressed in order evaluate different orographic effects as the ones in Denmark.
Furthermore, studies should be addressed to differentiate the performance of large-scale
models for different regions, since it seemed that for some speciﬁc sites the large-scale models
performed worst. One of the examples was Sprogø. This wind farm that should be considered
offshore had a quite big error from the large-scale models in comparison with the others
offshore wind farms studied. The sensitivity analysis of the maps showed that the roughness
effects from the land had almost no effect in its AEP. Therefore, for some special locations as
the Sprogø wind farm, studies should be made for assessing the behaviour of the models.
