Abstract
Operation and maintenance (OM) cost accounts for a substantial part of a wind turbine’s life-
time cost, especially for turbines located oﬀshore. One method to reduce this cost is to monitor
the condition of the wind turbine, detect faults and provide timely alarms, in an eﬀort to take
optimal maintenance, reduce downtime of turbines, and avoid catastrophic failures and related
expensive replacement of major components. Thus, wind turbine fault detection becomes a
task of central importance for this eﬀort. Nowadays, with more and more sensors available in
turbines, data driven methods with machine learning techniques have great potential to uti-
lize the SCADA and other measured data in fault detection. In this project we ﬁrst generate
SCADA data set by aero-servo-elastic simulations of both normal and faulty turbines using
HAWC2, and then apply diﬀerent machine learning techniques to detect the fault based solely
on the simulated SCADA data. Among various possible faults, this project is mainly focusing
on the yaw misalignment.

Introduction and purpose
1.1
Introduction
Over the past few decades, the global demand for renewable energy sources has increased
signiﬁcantly. In fact, this tremendous change seems to be only the beginning, compared to the
"green" future that is coming. Only in Europe by 2030 the 60% of the power production will
be based on renewable sources, where wind and solar will occupy 40% of the total generation,
and more speciﬁcally the land-based wind will reach the 17% of the total power generation [1].
Consequently, it seems that wind energy is going to be thriving over the next decade.
However, as it is logical, many problems still need to be solved in this ﬁeld. A very important
sector in wind energy is the OM, that is being handled with the condition monitoring. More
speciﬁcally, the condition monitoring ensures the operational health of the turbine.
The target of this project is to solve a part of one of the condition monitoring issues, the
so-called "Yaw misalignment". Characteristically, ROMO WIND [2] uses the expression "Yaw
misalignment – The scourge of wind farm operators", in order to underline the severity of this
problem. Indeed, yaw misalignment is a problem that is still under optimization process, while
it is mostly detected with anemometers, spinner anemometers or even LIDAR.
On the other hand, there is an enormous trend in many industries for adopting AI solutions
[3]. Certainly, this trend has aﬀected the wind energy sector, too. More speciﬁcally, AI has
already given quite powerful solutions in the wind energy sector through OM, wind forecasting
and wind farm control [3].
The objective of this research is to start an investigation regarding the yaw misalignment
detection using AI. In fact, this might lead to more accurate and cheaper detection ways, in
comparison with today’s methods.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE
1.2
Purpose - Process and Structure
The purpose of this project is to build an ANN model that can detect the two major cases,
yaw misalignment or not. However, there was no access at any SCADA data that labels the
degree of misalignment, so the database had also to be built from the scratch. The turbine that
was analyzed was the onshore DTU 10 MW reference turbine (Class-IA), while the simulations
were carried out in HAWC2.
Since related studies were not found, it was decided to follow more conservative steps for this
study. More speciﬁcally, the ﬁrst goal was to ﬁnd a promising ANN model assuming that there
is no wind shear and tower shadow, and then to build a robust ANN model by including them.
As a result, two databases were built in total, one with wind shear and tower shadow, and one
without. The main idea was to check if the results are promising for a simpler case and then
going deeper making the problem more realistic.
The process started by building the 1st database (without wind shear and tower shadow), and
then many models were trained until a promising model was found. The next step was to build
the 2nd database (with wind shear and tower shadow), and follow a similar methodology to
train the ﬁnal model.
Nevertheless, in this project 4 ANN models will be presented in total. The ﬁrst 3 models will
be without tower shadow and wind shear, while the last one will include them. In the end, the
ﬁnal model will be further assessed with the confusion matrix and the sensitivity analysis.
This thesis is divided into the following chapters:

2. Literature Review: Introduces all the relative research that was done until today.

given during the process.

2.1
The eﬀect of Yaw Misalignment in Power Production
Nowadays, wind energy has become one of the most attractive energy sources, which has already
started replacing fossil fuels on a large scale. However, as a newly developed energy production
technique, it has many areas that are still under optimization process. One of these areas is
the so-called "Yaw Misalignment". The eﬀect of yaw misalignment can be described by the
misalignment between the rotor axis and the wind direction [4].
Apparently, yaw misalignment is something that should be avoided in terms of power, when
considering an individual turbine [4]. Indeed, the industry has developed the answer that copes
with this phenomenon, but not fully yet. More speciﬁcally, the turbine uses a control system
and a yaw drive mechanism in order to rotate the nacelle until is aligned with the measurements
of the wind direction [5].
A better view for both yaw misalignment and yaw system can be acquired by the following
Figure 2.1:
Figure 2.1: Yaw misalignment & yaw system [6])

Nonetheless, the problem is not entirely solved. Still there are problems in this control system
causing a small yaw misalignment, which can conclude in big AEP losses for a single turbine.
More speciﬁcally, the [7] claims that a 7.5°of yaw misalignment was responsible for a 2.4% loss
of AEP. Additionally, a recent study [6] of the 5-MW reference oﬀshore wind turbine found
that, a 10° yaw misalignment can cause from 3-5% AEP losses.
On the other hand, the active yaw misalignment could be the reason for increasing the AEP of
a wind farm signiﬁcantly [8]. Certainly, this is a factor of many things, like the spacing of the
turbines, the wind direction, the yaw misalignment. All in all, yaw misalignment has a very
big impact on the AEP of either a single turbine or a wind farm, thus, it is crucial to detect it
accurately.
2.2
The eﬀect of Yaw Misalignment in Loads-DELs
The correlation between loads and yaw misalignment is a bit more complicated than power.
Following the conclusion of the research [8], yaw misalignment has a signiﬁcant impact on the
loads and the DELs of the turbine. Nevertheless, the eﬀects of the yaw misalignment on the
loads are not symmetrical and they rely on the direction of the inﬂow. This means that the loads
do not have the same behavior when the turbine has clockwise rotation with the anti-clockwise
rotation.
2.3
The Financial Perspective of Yaw Misalignment
Principally, the total revenues of a wind farm project are highly depended on the power that
will be generated during the operation period. Taking into account that the yaw misalignment
aﬀects the AEP, it can be claimed that it also aﬀects the total revenues of a wind farm.
Regarding the Loads and DELs, a relevant study [9] has shown that correcting the yaw mis-
alignment can give extra proﬁt. This derives from the fact that the loads will be reduced, and
thus the DELs will be lower, too. Correspondingly, the number of maintenance events and
failures will decline. Therefore, a very important cost during the lifetime of the project will
be minimized. The comparison that was conducted in this research is illustrated in the below
Figure 2.2.

3.2
Machine Learning Categories
Machine learning is mainly discretized into four categories:
• Supervised Learning
• Unsupervised Learning
• Semi-supervised Learning
• Reinforcement Learning
Supervised learning is when the dataset provides information for both inputs and outputs.
In other words, the result of each input is known. Additionally, supervised learning problems
can be categorized into classiﬁcation and regression tasks [15].
• The term Classiﬁcation in ML refers to a problem where for each input the output is a
discrete value.
• The term Regression in ML refers to a problem where for each input the output is con-
tinuous.
On the other hand, in unsupervised learning, the dataset does not give any information
about the outputs, and the most popular methodology of this type of learning is clustering.
In practice, this method is grouping the data eﬃciently without having any initial information
for the outputs.
In semi-supervised learning, the dataset is actually a mix of the aforementioned two types
of learning. More speciﬁcally, for some inputs the outputs are known, while others are not. So
the information of the dataset is not complete [16].

As an optimization algorithm, gradient descent is constantly trying to lower the cost in the
cost function1 and thus update the weights of the ANN [25]. For a better understanding of how
gradient descent operates, Figure 3.9 might be suitable. The basic idea of gradient descent is
that it tries to "reach the deepest point"(minimizes the cost). In other words, it is an iteratevely

3.4.4
Backpropagation
The backpropagation is a very important feature for ANN. Likewise before, the cost function
is being minimized using the gradient descent to update the weighs, respectively. However, the
diﬀerence is that in backpropagation the process does not stop when the ﬁnal layer is reached but
it continues once again reversely, from the last until the ﬁrst layer. With this way the weights
of the neurons can be optimized even more, by giving a better model performance at the end.
The big advantage of this algorithm, is that during the backwards process the calculations that
were done initially are being reused [26]. This extra information can lead the gradient descent
to minimize even more the cost, and thus give a higher performance model.

Loss Function
The main goal of the loss function is to optimize the weights of the ANN. In other words to
minimize the loss of the model. The term loss of the model refers to the process of matching
the predicted values with the actual values and calculating the error. However, there is not
only one standard loss function that works eﬃciently for every case.
According to [27], some of the most frequent loss functions in Keras are:
• Mean Squared Error This lost function uses the mean of squared diﬀerences between
predicted and actual values. This loss function is eﬃcient mostly in regression problems.
• Binary Crossentropy The binary crossentropy is considered to be the proper loss func-
tion for a binary classiﬁcation problem, since the output layer must have only one neuron
that will classify the data into two classes. However, this function have as a requirement
to have the sigmoid activation function at the last layer.
• Categorical Crossentropy Respectively, category of loss function is eﬃcient for multi-
class classiﬁcation problems. It is important to note that the number of the neurons in
the output layer must the same with the number of classes. The requirement here is to
use the softmax activation function at the last layer.
Optimizers
The optimizers are algorithms which are based on the idea of gradient descent. Similarly, they
are used for optimizing the weights and the learning rate of the ANN aiming to minimize the
loss. As stated in [28], some of the most common ones are:
• Stohastic gradient descent it is similar with gradient descent. However, the diﬀerence
is that instead of changing the model parameters only one time in each cycle of the
dataset, in SGD the parameters are changing multiple times. The number of the times
that these parameters will change is the same with the number of observations. Therefore,
the loss has more chances to be reduced.
• Adagrad. A basic negative of all the aforementioned optimization algorithms is that the
learning rate remains steady during all the training process. On the other hand, Adagrad
has the ability to change the learning rate for each parameter and in every time step.
• Momentum was mostly developed to complete the SGD in terms of variance and con-
vergence.
16
==PAGE:29==

• AdaDelta is developed in order to correct the Adagrad in terms of learning rate.
• Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is developed based on the Momentum. In this
optimization algorithm the speed of the learning rate is not so fast, which helps for a
more detailed search of the global minimum.

Training the Model
The next step is to train the model. In Keras it is crucial to deﬁne the number of the Epochs,
the Batch size, the data split (train,validation,test), as well as the metrics.
Epochs describe the number of times all the dataset will go forward and backward through
the neural network.
Batch size is the number of samples that will be passed through the network at one time.
Therefore, one complete epoch requires all the batches to go one time forward and backward
through the neural network.
Figure 3.10: Train/Validation/Test Set [29]
The Figure 3.10 explains brieﬂy how the dataset should be split. For a better understanding,
a more careful explanation is followed below:
Training data is the portion of the data that is used for training the model. During the
training process the model that is trained in each epoch is being evaluated with a small part
of the training dataset too. An appropriate training set is considered to be from 60-80%.
Validation data is the portion of the dataset that is used to evaluate the performance of
the model during the training process. More speciﬁcally, the model is being validated in each
epoch in a small sample of the training data and also in the validation data. This can be done
with the help of the "validation_split()" command, and a reasonable set for this is between

Test data are the data that are will be used at the end of the training process in order to
check how accurate the ﬁnal model is. Similarly, a reasonable percentage is also 10-20% of the

The metrics that are used for evaluating the model are the accuracy and the loss.

It can be characterized as the "worst enemy" of machine learning models, because it can give
sometimes the impression that the models perform well but actually not.
Underﬁtting is when the machine learning algorithm trains a model that is not adjusted to
the underlying trend of the data.

6.2.3
Cross-Validation
The next step to prevent overﬁtting and to check the generalization capability of the model is
the so-called cross-validation method. [39]. In other words, cross validation is the next step of
evaluating the model in unseen dataset.
However, as it was aforementioned, Keras is able to split the dataset into training, validation
and test set. By this way it validates the model during the training before the ﬁnal check with
the test data. According to Jason Brownlee [40], this could be a reason for skipping cross-
validation. Nonetheless, in this project it was determined to implement it for decreasing the
uncertainties.
42
==PAGE:55==
CHAPTER 6. MODELS AND RESULTS
The most popular methods for cross-validation are:
• The holdout method
• The k-Fold cross-validation
• The leave-one-out cross-validation
In this project the k-Fold method was used.
Figure 6.23: k-Fold cross-validation for 10 folds [41]
As is described in Figure 6.23, the main idea of k-Fold cross-validation is that the dataset is
split in k folds (k=2,3,...10,...n) which has equal size each other. Moreover, the number of the
folds is also the number of the models that will be trained. Thus, in the case of k=10 folds, 10
neural networks will be trained.
Apparently, for each model, one fold will be used for testing, while remaining folds will be used
for training. This process is giving the chance for each fold to be the test set, and essentially
generates predictions based on these sets. And this is what makes this method so popular, be-
cause it gives less unbiased estimation of the model ability than the aforementioned methods.
An additional warning sign that can be captured during the k-Fold process, is that if there
is consistency within results folds. More explicitly, if the scores of folds have a high standard
deviation, then there might be an outlier in the dataset.
It is worth mentioning that k-Fold cross-validation is not for choosing a ﬁnal model, but for
evaluating.

Conclusions and Further Research
7.1
Conclusions
The main purpose of this project was to investigate, if it is possible to detect the yaw mis-
alignment for the DTU 10 MW Class-IA wind turbine through an ANN model. Starting from
the scratch, it was determined to study 2 main cases. The unyawed case with ∆γ=0° and the
yawed case with ∆γ=30°, from cut-in (4 m/s) until cut-out (25 m/s) wind speed with step 1
m/s. The initial study was conducted assuming that there is no wind shear and tower shadow,
while the ﬁnal study considered also these two factors making the problem more realistic. It
was decided to start conservatively from a binary classiﬁcation problem of yawed and unyawed
case without wind shear an tower shadow. In case that the results would be promising the
study would go deeper in a more realistic problem.
As for all ANN applications, the ﬁrst thing was to ﬁnd the database. In fact, 35,200 HAWC2
simulations were carried out, in order to acquire a big dataset for training a model and validat-
ing its predictions. After many attempts an almost perfect model (2nd Model) was found, with
96.02% accuracy. This model was trained based on 8,800 simulations by using the 6 features
of the following Equation 7.1 (the terms of this equation are explained in 6.2.1).
∆γ = f(Ω, Torqueaero, Pel, TBFA, TBSS, TBTor)
(7.1)
Moreover, the validation of the model on new dataset was done with 1,760 new samples. It
is important to note that, this process utilized the mean values from each time series. Hence,
it was found that the results are promising for going deeper by adding wind shear and tower
shadow.
It is necessary to note that before reaching to this model, many attempts were carried out.
A remarkable outcome came out during the 1st model, which used as feature of the sum of
the turning points. More speciﬁcally, it was computed that the sum of the turning points for
Mechanical Power, from 4 until 8 m/s (low wind speeds) and from 13 until 22 m/s (high wind

speeds) are more in the yawed case in comparison with the unyawed case.
However, before using the 2nd Model’s approach for the ﬁnal model, the 3rd Model was devel-
oped by splitting the time series into 6 pieces and extracting one mean value from each piece.
Although, that the extracted information was more, the 3rd Model performed slightly lower
than the 2nd Model.
Eventually, the ﬁnal model was trained by using 10,560 new simulations with wind shear and
tower shadow.
As it was expected, the model was more complex, with lower performance

The most important outcome was that the vast majority of the model confusion (9.03% out of
9.15%), is when the turbine is "yawed" but it detects "unyawed".
On top of that, sensitivity analysis took place for obtaining a better picture of the ambiguous
areas of the ﬁnal model. Firstly, analysis was done into the number of errors for each wind
speed.
The main result was that the worst performance of the model is in case of 4 m/s
(51.25%), while from 18-25 m/s the model works perfectly (100%).
As for the other wind

The next topic of the sensitivity analysis was the importance of the sample size. Apparently,
it was investigated how the number of samples inﬂuence the performance of the ﬁnal model. It
turned out that after the 2200 samples the model performance does not increase signiﬁcantly.
More speciﬁcally, it goes from 87.22% to 90.85%, which is an amount which probably can be
covered with a better hyperparameter tuning. Therefore, this model could possibly be acquired
using 25% of the dataset or even the 50% (more conservatively).
Finally, the sensitivity analysis was ended up by comparing the model without wind shear and
tower shadow, with the model that has wind shear and tower shadow. In other words, the 2nd
with the ﬁnal model. In fact, this addendum had as an impact a more complex model with a
lower performance.
The ﬁnal conclusion of this project is that ANN has many potentials to provide strong solutions
for the yaw misalignment detection.

7.2
Further research
In this project it was proved that ANN can detect the case of 0°and 30°of yaw misalignment
with 90.85% accuracy.
A potential research could be to solve the problem with another model, like regression, random
forest, support vector machine, k nearest neighbor or even treat the problem as unsupervised
learning and check its performance.
Moreover, the next level of this research could be to complete the initial problem.
More
speciﬁcally to examine more cases of yaw misalignment (e.g. 5%,15%,-30%,etc). This could be
studied, by gathering data for each yaw case and to train a new model that performs decently.
In case that this is feasible the next stage is to make a model for all the possible yaw cases and
try to optimize it.
Lastly, interesting ﬁelds could be to build similar models that can predict or detect other
condition monitoring issues, like icing, pitch error, etc.
