Abstract
Morphing wing structures can continuously deform their geometrical shape to improve aerody-
namic performance by reducing drag, reducing noise levels, increasing air quality, and reducing
climate change impacts from the aviation section. Construction of morphing wing structure
mechanisms is diﬀicult; therefore, was a general topology optimization method for simulta-
neous mechanism and actuator design proposed. The method has been conceived for both
two-dimensional and three-dimensional large-scale problems, where a parallel computational
framework for unstructured mesh is adopted. The topology optimization method utilizes a bi-
material formulation based on Hashin-Shtrikman bound material interpolation, where a struc-
tural, actuating, and void material could be interpolated upon. The actuation material was
based on linear thermal expansion. Two design domain formulations were proposed, one based
on a density approach and another based on a feature mapping approach[40] for constraining
actuating material as geometrically defined shapes, as a way of realizing the actuating mate-
rial to real actuators. The morphing capabilities were achieved by minimization an error to
a target shape. Furthermore, the compliance of the structure was minimized for structural
stiffness. Error and compliance minimization was both implemented as a single objective and
multi-objective formulation. The proposed method was applied using numerical examples on a
2D trailing edge rib and a 3D trailing edge wing section. Here it was found that the method
was capable of designing morphing wing structure mechanisms. Furthermore, it was found that
the 3D optimized wing sections utilized a more advanced structural layout as one can obtain
for a 2D optimization.
In addition to the presented work, the feature mapping approach was generalized for 3D un-
structured grids for any density-based topology optimization problem.

Introduction
The environmental impact of aviation has established a substantial demand for more innova-
tive aviation technologies to reduce noise levels, increase air quality, and reduce climate change
impacts from the aviation sector. Within aviation and aerospace, one technology area that has
gotten much attention is the morphing wing, a concept of having a wing with adaptive flight
control surfaces while maintaining a continuous geometry. Comprehensive research [16, 41],
has shown that morphing wing technology could reduce the aviation sector’s environmental
impact, due to reduction of drag.
In this work, a morphing wing is defined as a wing with a continuous skin with internal mech-
anisms to achieve a morphing of the outer skin.
Mechanism design is often based on experience and trial and error approaches, and hence, the
design of the mechanism cannot ensure an optimal design, especially not when the mechanism’s
objective becomes complex. The mechanism can be subjected to multiple objectives or con-
stants, achieving maximum stiffness, minimum weight, etc. As the mechanism is the impetus
in a morphing wing, it is very relevant that the mechanism is designed for weight and stiffness
considerations, as this is vital elements in environmental pollution.
Sigmund [31] proposed using the topology optimization (TO) [7, 10] method to the systematic
design of mechanisms, both on a micro- and a macro scale. The systematic design of mech-
anisms is obtained by optimizing a domain to generate a specific shape. This is similar to a
compliant mechanism [30] design, where the mechanism has no discontinues hinges. Sigmund
[32, 33] also extended mechanism design to multiphasic and multi-material models by modeling
bi-material electrothemomechanical actuators using TO. The TO method could thus be used
to model morphing wing mechanisms.
Studies of TO of morphing wing mechanisms are limited. However, prominent examples in-
clude Kambayashi et al. [23] who presented a method for obtaining a multi-layered compliant
mechanism for a morphing wing under multiple flight conditions. Tong et al. [36], who studied
TO of composite material integrated into a compliant mechanism for a morphing trailing edge.
Zhang, Ziang et al. [45] who presented a study of a morphing wing leading edge driven by a
compliant mechanism design. De Gaspari [18] have proposes a design of adaptive compliant
wing through a multilevel optimization approach, sizing, shape and TO is adopted. Gomes
and Palacios [20], who presented a method for a two-step optimization of aerodynamic shape
adaptation a compliant mechanism, by modeling fluid-structure interaction problems combin-
ing with density-based TO.
Since morphing wing mechanism design is not a new concept, optimizing the mechanism with
various methods (excluding TO) has long been an active research field. Examples include Yang
et al. [42] who have proposed and tested compliant skin and internal actuating mechanisms,
where the optimal design is achieved through a weighted least-squared error approach. Florian
Dexl et al. [19] studied an active morphing wing section optimized for aerodynamically shape
changes without defining the target shape prior. Method of Moving Morphable Components[21]
is utilized for the proposed optimization.
All of the above studies only address the mechanism as a two-dimensional structure, and as
the movement of a morphing structure is not necessarily restricted to two-dimension, the result
might be inferior. Furthermore, a two-dimensional study limits the potential solution space
for optimization. Hence one might achieve a superior mechanism design if one solves in three-
dimensional space.
The mechanisms presented in the above studies are all driven by an external force or displace-
ment, meaning that in practice, additional space is required for an actuator(s) in the morphing
structure. Actuating, as part of the mechanism, could prove a better overall volume optimum
for a morphing wing.

Systematic Mechanism Design
A mechanism can be defined as an object that transforms one or more inputs to one ore more
outputs. In the context of mechanics, it can be defined as a mechanical device that transforms
or transfers motion, force, or energy.
An example is a lever, which transfers energy from one side of a pivot to the other. If one wants
a specific output from the lever, it is relatively simple to design the lever mechanism. However,
as inputs and outputs get more constrained, the desired mechanism becomes complex; hence,
the design becomes challenging. Traditional mechanism design is often heavily based on expe-
rience and trial and error. This can be problematic, especially for a novel design.
With the use of TO, the mechanism design can become straight forward and systematic, as
formulated by Sigmund [31]. However, this requires the introduction of compliant mechanisms
for a continuous formulation.
Reverting to the lever; the mechanism gains mobility from a system of ridged members, con-
nected by joints; a bar, a pivot, and a shaft. This effectively results in total conversion of input
energy to output in the lever-system if friction is neglected. This type of mechanism can be
classified as a ridged-body mechanism.
The compliant mechanism is contrary to the ridged-body mechanism. The deflection of flexible
members provide the mechanism mobility, thus storing some energy as elastic energy in the
system, hence not all of the input energy is converted to output energy. However, compliant
mechanisms have many benefits; they are usually simplistic and have low maintenance, high
performance, need no lubrication and experience little wear. Advanced compliant mechanisms
are often used in space applications and Micro Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS)[31]. A
lever designed as a compliant mechanism could be constructed by a single object by connecting
the bar and pivot though a flexible joint.
The mechanical mechanism in a morphing wing will aim to obtain and preserve the wings given
shape for different flight modes. The input to such a mechanism is some actuation. However,
it is not trivial to find the location and size of the input actuator(s). One could prescribe the
amount, size, and location of input actuators and find a mechanism based on these precon-
ditions; this method will only guarantee a mechanism as good as the precondition. Hence a
simultaneous shape generating mechanism and actuator design is needed.
This section will propose a TO method that can find a simultaneous shape generating mech-
anism and actuator design on a two-dimensional (2D) domain and in three-dimensional (3D)

2.3
Topology Optimization Formulation
TO can be described as one of three structural optimization classes, with the other two being
size- and shape optimization. TO is the most general form of structural optimization; for this
work, the general method described by Bendsøe and Sigmund [9] is applied. The method’s
general purpose is finding the most optimal distribution of material in a given design domain,
Ω, such that an objective function is minimized or maximized and constraints are satisfied. This
is obtained by discretization of Ωand controlling each element in Ωby design variables, often
having one design variables per element. In order to model both a shape generating mechanism
and actuator design, a bi-material structure must be introduced, as both structural- and an
actuating material are needed. Two design variables, per element, are introduced, so material
properties in an element depend on two design variables ρe and φe. The two design variables
are defined as two septate vectors ρ and φ with the size of number of design elements. The
first design variables, ρ, sets the density of the elements, void, or solid material. The second
design variables, φ, sets the material phase of the elements.
In order to distribute material, the design variables must describe the interpolation of material.
The material interpolation can be achieved in numerous ways; however, a prevalent method
is the Solid Isotropic Material interpolation with Penalization (SIMP) scheme [8, 9], which
interpolate on the stiffness of the material, though a power law, where the exponent to the power
law works as a penalization on intermediate element stiffness, which means that intermediate
stiffness is uneconomical. An excate material model will be introduced in sec. 2.4.
As the material distribute is based on material stiffness, the constitutive law will depend on
the two variables ρ and φ

length, mesh-dependency, and checkerboard pattern will be avoided [13].

2.5
Design Domain Formulation
As already mentioned, the design domain Ωis assumed to be represented by a discretized
mesh. For the 2D model, the mesh is assumed fixed and rectangular so that each element is
of the same size. This also implies that the mesh will be nonconforming to any shape but a
rectangular shape in contemplation of representation of geometric shapes besides rectangular
shapes. For the 3D model the mesh is assumed unstructured and formulated by isoparametric
elements, meaning that the mesh can conform to a given geometry. In order for the ability to
model irregular shapes for the 2D model, passive solid and void elements to represent a given
shape on a fixed mesh.
Material in Ωis controlled by the two element-wise design variables, ρe and φe, this formulated
will be refereed to as a the density approach in this work. A combined design vector is introduced
xd = {ρ, φ}⊤
(2.5.1)
that describes all design variables for the density approach (subscript d). The optimized topol-
ogy from the density approach TO often results in abstract and very ’natural’ looking structures.
From a geometry representation perspective, the density approach, provides little to no control
of specific dimensions of the structure that can provide a geometric representation. There have
been proposed ways of interpreting the optimized topology into a manufacturable structure,
especially with the advancement of additive manufacturing industry[48]. This also includes
actuators material, like shape memory alloys.
However, for this work, it is desired to have the ability to control the geometry representation
of the actuating material, if needed. To do so, while still representing the design domain on a
fixed mesh, feature-mapping is introduced.

2.8
Numerical Implementation
Four implementations of above-formulated TO method have been implemented as a 2D and 3D
formulation with density approach and feature mapping approach. The two approaches share
common code, but the 2D and 3D code do not share any code. All four implementations have
the ability to solve both (2.6.8) and (2.6.9).
The 2D formulation is implemented in a two-dimension custom made finite element code, based
on the 88 line MATLAB code by Andreassen et al. [3].
The 3D formulation is implemented in a parallel 3D unstructured TO framework (utop) by
Träff et al. [37]. The framework is base on work by Aage et al. [1, 2]. The utop framework is
written in C/C++ and is build around the numerical library PETSc[5] for very eﬀicient parallel
computer implementation. The finite element system is solved with a multigrid preconditioned
Krylov method.
The following subsections aim to discuss implementation aspects and numerical problems that
can occur.
2.8.1
Matematical Programming Method
The TO problems, (2.6.8) and (2.6.9), are solved with the iterative optimization scheme, MMA
[34] with the obtained sensitives (2.7.2) and (2.7.3), as discussed in sec. 2.3.
16
==PAGE:22==
2.8.2
Design variables and density variables
Since two density variables are needed to define each element’s material, the densities variables
fields are implemented as two separate vectors for the 2D code. This way, handling the material
model and the feature map approach becomes superficial, as computing sensitivity vectors can
be vectorized and computed separately. The septate vectors are then concatenated for MMA.
For the feature mapping approach, implement the third vector, as design variables and density
variables are no longer the same. This means that there exist two separate density vectors, ρ
and ϱ(κ), where ρ is also a design variable vector. The third vector is the design variable vector
for the feature mapping, ϱ(κ). This means that ρ and ϱ(κ), and their associated sensitivities,
will be concatenated and given to MMA.
For the 3D code, the framework is set up for elements to have more than one design variable,
where design variables are grouped per element in one design vector. The need for vectorization
is not as impotence as it is for the 2D code; hence most computations are based on element
loops, in which each design variable is looped over.
For the feature mapping approach, the feature mapping approach was first extended as a
general extension to the utop framework, as presented in sec. 2.11. From the general extension,
the framework has been heavily altered to conform with septate design variables and density
variables.
Since the utop framework is based on parallel computing, index bookkeeping is
fundamental, as data is scattered across processors and data access is not as trivial for a
sequential data structure. It is assumed that problems solved with the utop framework are
of large scale (> 1e6 elements). Furthermore, it is also assumed that number of features will
remain relatively low in relation to the number of elements, which means that there will be much
fewer design variables for the features than there will for the design density variables. Thus,
it was decided not to make a new parallel setup for the feature design variables but to have
the feature design variables as a sequential vector. This means that there will exist a parallel
density vector, a sequential feature design variables vector, and a combined parallel design
variable vector, where element design variables are stored continues and then concatenated
with the feature design variables vector.
Speedup has not been investigated, but it is assumed that having feature design variables as
a parallel vector will not increase performance significantly because of two reasons; first, the
vector’s size is relatively small, which means from a memory perspective, the vector has little
too on influence. Second, all processors will need every feature design variable to compute
their par of the density field, which means that all feature variables have to be collectively
communicated across all processors, which comes with a communication overhead. This means
that the code has some limitations to the number of features, but as of now, an upper bound
has not been found.
2.8.3
Passive Density Variables
Density variables can be set as passive, such that a density or material phase does not update for
an element during optimization. This gives different possibilities for passive element, as one can
formulate an area of the design domain so that there must be material. Still, the optimization
must determine the material phase, and so on. To ease computational time, only non-passive
density variables that are design variables are given to MMA. Passive density variables are also
filtered.

2.8.5
Continuation Method
The TO problem is highly non-convex; hence, one can not guarantee that the solution will
converge to a global optimum. The optimization scheme seeks to produce consistent solutions
based on the initial guess by adopting a continuation approach. The continuation approach
uses the projection steepness parameter, β from (2.3.6), so that the steepness is gradually
increased during optimization, typically from a very low value at β0 = 0.01. This ensures that
the sensitivities are low and smooth in the beginning of the iterations, thus the problem is
more convex, but as the design domain converges, the steepness is increased to help further the
convergence. For this work β have an upper limit of 64. β is increased every 40 iteration.
Similar approach are adopted for the projection function (2.5.13), for the feature map approach,
for the steepness parameter, α.
However, as mentioned earlier low values of α will cause
overlapping features to change their overlapping density. An example is shown in fig. 8. Two
features are stacked on top of each other, the one feature is half the length of the other. in fig.
8a the two features are shown for three different values of α, notices how the overlapping density
is higher where the features are stacked for a low value of α. This is very clear form fig. 8c,
which shows the cross-section of the feature density. Combining this with the fact that feature
mapping in general is extreme non-convex[6]. It is hence not desired to have α0 at a low value,

2.9
Validation of Implementa-
tion
Different measures have validated the implemented model. One aspect of the validation is the
finite element modeling; the other is the TO formulation. For the finite element modeling, a
bimetallic beam has been solved to check the correctness for the physics, both by the 2D model
and the 3D model, and compared to analytical results. For the TO formulation, finite difference
checks have been used to check the correctness of all implemented sensitives, as this is crucial
for a correct optimization.
2.9.1
Bimetallic Beam Test
A bimetallic beam is a coupled thermo-elasticity problem, where two beams are bonded to-
gether, typical consists of two different materials. Due to different thermal expansion proper-
ties, the two beams will generate different stresses, as they are prohibited to freely deform. This
will cause the whole structure to bend up or downwards. This test case is thus also relevant for
the later wing structure modeling, as this way of generating deformations, could be an viable
strategy for the optimization routine. The beam is modeled with the length, L = 20[−], hight
and width, H = 1[−] and W = 1[−], with the individual beam hight of h = 0.5[−]. One end of
the beam is fixed. The top beam is modeled as a structural material, with E1 = 1[−], ν1 = 0.3
and α1 = 0.0. The bottom beam is modeled as an actuator material, with E1 = 2[−], ν1 = 0.3
and α2 = 0.1. An analytical model is found in [35], it must be noted that the analytical result
is only comparable due to the high slenderness ratio of the beam. The analytical result and
the results obtained are compared, by the percentage error of tip displacement. Different mesh
discriminations are used. The results are seen in fig. 10. As seen from fig. 10, the result
converges. It can be seen that the 3D model have a slight larger error than the 2D model, this
must be due to the iterative solver of the 3D model. The results show that the physics are
implemented correct.

The correctness of the analytical sensitivities, are measured by an error, εFD, calculated based
analytical- and numerical sensitivities. The numerical sensitivities are calculated for different
perturbations.
The numerical sensitivities (2.9.1) is first-order derivatives, hence would expect εFD to converges
lineally with the perturbation. However, when dealing with numerical differentiation, round-off
error is a significant problem, hence one can state the error as εFD = εf + εc, where εf is the
sensitivity error and εc is the round-off error. One would expect to see that, as h →0, then
εf →0 and εc →∞. This means that one would expect to see a linear converges, up until
some point, εc ⪆εf, then εFD will become unstable and start to divergence. This point, is
determined by the machine precision, Scherer [28].
In order to get as good accuracy as possible of the sensitivity error, the solver must be direct
or iterative with strict tolerances. Low relative convergence tolerance are set for for the Krylov
method.
A finite difference check from the 3D model with the feature mapping approach is seen in
fig. 11, where it can be seen that all quantities converges lineally with the perturbation size,
confirming that the sensitives are correct.

One advantages with the feature mapping extension is the facility of postprocessing. One prob-
lem with density based topology optimization is that the solution is represented by voxels,
and almost all popular CAD programs represent geometry based on splines. which means that

extension, the final feature design variables, combined with some boolean operation, is enough
to describe the whole geometry in a spline base CAD program. Furthermore, the viewing ex-
perience of the feature mapping result is also more pleasant for large scale solutions, as there is
no need to view the ’density filed’, but a reconstructed solution of feature design variables can
be used instead. This also allows for changing of the resolution of the result, as is presented in
fig. 18f. Where the solution with 234 features are rendered with matlab’s fimplicit3 function
on a mesh with 24 million cells. This easily runs on a laptop, which might be problematic if
it was 24 million density elements rendered in a software like paraview. I must be noted that
this is heavily dependent on the number of feature’s.

Examples of Morphing Wing Structures
The morphing wing structure based on compliant mechanisms is a prospering area of aerody-
namic technology. A challenging task is to find structural layouts of the wing structure. The
above-proposed Topology Optimization method on compliance minimization and shape gener-
ation will be demonstrated on wing structures, both on a 2D design domain and 3D design
space.
This section aims to demonstrate how the proposed topology optimization method can be ap-
plied to realize a morphing trailing edge.
A trailing edge wing model is conceived for analysis. The model is based on the morphing trail-
ing edge, ATED[26] (Adaptive Trailing EDge) devolved by the SARISTU (SmARt Intelligent
Aircraft STrUctures) project [16, 41]. ATED is developed to maximize the wing aerodynamic
performance in cruise for large commercial airplanes. ATED have morphing range of ±5 de-
grees of the camber.
ATED consists of several ridged body single dof rib mechanism, such that the ribs are places
along the span and in prolongation of the camber line. What makes ATED an adaptive/mor-
phing mechanism is the skin of the trailing edge. The first and last third section of the trailing
edge skin consists of aluminum planes which are connected by a proprietary hyperelastic skin.
The ATED model and the skin is seen in fig. 40b in app. C.
No exact model of the ATED and its target shape have been obtained; however, a model have
been interpreted based on a CAD model of a ATED rib and provided material properties of
the skin, provided by CIRA[14]. The outline of the ATED rib and the skin are mapped as 2D
polygon shapes, described by coordinate vectors, ri. The camber line is measures at 1876 [mm],
while the trailing edge camper line is 489 [mm] in length, which means that the trailing edge
begins at 0.74% of the camber line. The skin have a total thickness of 2.5 [mm], sections of the
skin is divided into two layers, a stiff 1.5 [mm] layer and a soft 1 [mm] layer. This extreme fea-
ture ratio between camber length and skin thickness, combined with the overall wedge-shaped
of an trailing edge, means that a fine mesh resolution have to be adopted, in order to describe
the design domain. This is especially true for the 2D model, as the mesh is not conforming to
the trailing edge.
A morphing function have been conceived in order to obtain target shapes, the morphing func-
tion have not been establish by any aerodynamic theory or modeling, as this lies outside the
scope of the work. The morphing function simply morphs a trailing edge shape by an morphing
angle, αm. The function works by transforming coordinate vectors that describe the outline of
the skin, by rotating each vector by the rotation matrix, A(αm(x)), which is a function of the
normalized camber line, such that the morphing angle, will grow with the normalized trailing
edge camber line.

3D Analysis on a Trailing Edge Section
The following considers examples of 3D analysis of a trailing edge. The airfoil trailing edge
model is extruded out of plane (in the z direction), so a is formed. The pressure load and target
shape as used for the 2D model is also extruded. The internal part of the trailing edge section,
the design domain, is divided into three section, where the middle section have the width LD, so
the design domain can be segmented into sub design domain, Ω1, Ω2 and Ω3. For the following
problems LD = 0.2Lz. The dimentions of the x-y plane is as for the 2D model, the depth of
the section is Lz = 300[mm], as the wing section ATED is design for[26]. An illustration of
the trailing edge section is seen in fig. 28. The material properties are the same as for the 2D
model. As the 3D model have more design freedom, less material is needed for realizing the
structure, thus a total volume fraction of 18% was allowed with 3% actuator material allowed.
An approximately element size of 1 mm was assumed to be adequately fine in order for describe
features in the trailing edge section. The trailing edge section is discretized to approximately
8.5 million elements or 26 million degrees of freedom. The mesh is seen in app. C, fig. 41 and
42. The trailing edge section is modelled with symmetry boundaries on the two faces of the
trailing edge section, at z = 0 and z = Lz. The trailing edge section is fixed at the y −z plane
at x = 0. The target set is achieved by defining all nodes on the outer skin as a nodeset, then
searching all γ(u) target coordinates, which is defined as a point cloud, and finding closet node
to each γ(u). The search routine is achieved with nanoflann’s[12] k-d tree search operations,
which is a very eﬀicient way of searching in extensive data sets. γ(u) is distributed so that
each target coordinate is location on a node. The target cosiest of 671 evenly distributed target
nodes, equivalent to 2013 target dof.
The pressure was ordinal intended to be defined similar to the target shape by making a nears
neighbor search, of pressure point cloud coordinates and element surface centers, from the
skin surface, with inverse distance weighting[29]. As nears neighbor search often results in a
’staircase’ surface, a laplacian smoothing filter was applied over the pressure field. Inspiration
from multigrid preconditioned node search method have been used here.
However, due to
unsolved bugs, with the laplacian smoothing filter, this was not implement in the final model.
Instead, pressure have been addend by dividing the outer skin into thin panels along the span
of the trailing edge section, and the subjecting each panel to a pressure.
The following results have been solved on the DTU Jess cluster, on 15 nodes, resulting in 300
cores. The problems was allowed an maximum of 1500 iterations, as it have been experienced
that the feature mapping approach is slow to converge. Each iteration takes on average 1-2
minutes, which resulted in a total computation times between 25 and 50 hours depending on
design domain formulation. The results have been obtained with 3 multigrid levels.

4
Discussion
The purpose of this work was to propose a general topology optimization method for simulta-
neous mechanism and actuator design of morphing wing structures for both 2D and 3D space.
From the presented results, it is evident that such a topology optimization method has been
accomplished. The general method was first tested on simple examples that would mimic a
wing structure problem’s isolated characteristic. Here it was found that the method could ob-
tain structures that could achieve the morphing target, in both 2D and 3D, for both density
approach and feature mapping approach. Furthermore, when applying a multiobjective for-
mulation, a trade-off between compliance and displacement error was found in the form of a
convex Pareto front. Next, the conceive method was applied to morphing wing structures, in
the form of a 2D trailing edge rib and a 3D trailing edge wing section. Here, the presented
method can conceive morphing structures for a complex model that emphasizes this method’s
application.
Furthermore, it is shown how actuating material can be placed in such a structure to gain
morphing capabilities. For the 3D trailing edge wing section, the feature mapping approach
seems essential, as actuating material becomes very diﬀicult to interpret to a realistic actua-
tor. However, to work effectively for the feature mapping approach, a starting guess based on
the density approach seems indispensable. Despite have constants the actuating material as
features, it is evident that this, alone, is not enough to have strictly defined feature actuators.
Several alternative solutions are proposed that may restrict the actuating material even more:
Define a tight bounding box, so the feature cannot be removed for the design domain. Zhang
et al. [43] have proposed geometric constrains, so features can not overlap. One could also
apply passive padding around a feature, such that the feature only can interacting with other
material, where there is no passing; an example of such padding is seen in fig. 46 in app. D. It
must be noted that the more constraints to the actuators, the harder the problem becomes.
A significant result is a consistency between 2D and 3D results, as the placement of material
does not seem random but is consistent about its placement. As the end of the trailing edge
consists of small spares for stiffness, the trailing edge’s midsection consists of a more flexible
structure as most of the displacement occurs there. The front of the trailing edge consists of a
thick structure to support the trailing edge.
As already discussed, the method can achieve morphing down to a specified error; however,
the variation or dispersion in error data contributes to the skin’s uneven surface. This could
either mean that there are not enough target points to define the surface or that the problem in
itself will produce this roughness. Different target points for the wing structures have not been
studied, but it is assumed that this will not help, only make the problem harder to solve as the
sensitivities get more weakened. The uneven skin surface could also be explained by the skin
modeling, as the skin has not been developed for this specific morphing. It could also be that
the number of elements defining the thickness of the skin is too low and is thus not modeled
correctly.
The general structure that this obtained agrees with the general mechanism and compliance
minimization design found by topology optimization; as an example, thin hinges are formed
between bodies, a classical observation from mechanism design as seen in [39]. Furthermore,
truss structures, closed wall structures, and box section structures are seen to maximize stiff-
ness, as is also seen for compliance minimization[15]. Which consequently is also an effect of
3D modeling. This also would suggest that the 3D structure is superior to the 2D structure.
In the reviewed literature, no true 3D model are presented; both Kambayashi et al. [23] and
De Gaspari [18] present 2.5D results, where they performed optimization on 2D ribs that is
connected to a 3D wing section. Their results can not be compared as they solve different prob-
lems and apply external loading to drive their model. However, the work presented here shows
46
==PAGE:52==
the possibility of morphing model structures in 3D and that the result may give an alternative
structure to the wing structure that the traditional rib structure.
Furthermore, with the additions of actuator design formulation, the possibility of modeling a
complex 3D mechanisms possible. With the feature mapping approach, the design limitation
seen for the density approach almost disappeared, as the design is easier to interpreter into a
manufacture-able object, which makes the method very versatile.
One area of the actuator design modeling that has not been addressed is the actuator ener-
gy/volume needed for the wing structure to achieve the morphing. Sigmund [33] showed how
a theoretical energy bound for a two material actuators could be determined. However, due to
the compliance minimization formulation, minimizing the compliance is equivalent to minimiz-
ing the system’s elastic strain energy, thus minimizing the system’s deformations capabilities.
Even though compliance minimizing is only subjected to the system with no thermal expansion,
the compliance objective will try to distribute both materials to minimize compliance, which
is unaﬀiliated with the actuator’s effectiveness. For this reason, it is tough to determine the
amount of actuator energy needed, especially for an elaborate mechanism for a wing structure.
Furthermore, an unforeseen result of the actuator design, most noticeable for the feature map-
ping approach, is that structural material is seen to restrict actuation forces from actuators, as
the actuator is too powerful or not having the correct characteristics. This could be a sign of
too much actuating material. These are not ideal circumstances as this may result in a trial
and error design iterations process, which is one of the elements a TO systematic mechanism
design should eliminate.
For the feature mapping approach, it was seen that the convergence was very slow compared
to the density approach, especially if the features are not allowed to change the volume. This
is a drawback to using feature mapping, as they are very sensitive to change. For this reason,
a very low move limit was allowed for the design variables. Simultaneously, feature mapping is
prone to local minimum issues that a low move limit does not help with. A thoroughly move
limit study has not been performed, which might give a better convergence if performed.
The skin is an imported component of a morphing trailing edge, as it is transferring external
loading into the structure while it must remain smooth. For the presented work, a skin model
was based on the ATED model, was included as a passive component. One could presume that
if the skin were an active design, including in the modeling as done in [20], a better result would
be obtained. For a 3D model, the skin could also be modeled as a shell structure. If the skin
design was included, small spares of intermediate densities would not form, but instead, the
skin would be optimized for a better transition between internal mechanism structure and skin
structure.
As discussed, the projection method is causing the spares of intermediate densities to disperse
or turn into a checkerboard-like pattern. The minimum length scale has not been implemented,
but it might help converge the intermediate densities better than pure projection. Here it is
proposed to impose a minimum length scale on the density field, as this defines the structural
topology; this will also help to define better hinges, as the hinges seen for the 2D result are
very thin. Robust formulation[39] is a popular method of introducing minimum length scale,
but the formulation has seen to have problems producing a consistent topology, which would
be a problem for a two-field problem, thus one might want to use geometric minimum length
scale constraints as formulated by Zhou et al. [47].
The finite element modeling has been based on the assumption of small displacement and
geometric linear behavior. However, mechanisms are normally bounded to preponderant move-
ment; therefore, one would expect a nonlinear strain-displacement relationship. Pedersen et al.
[27] also show how nonlinear compliant mechanisms would outperform a linear model. Hence,
a correct model would assume geometrical non-linearity as shown in [32] for modeling thermal
actuators.

5
Conclusion
The objective of the thesis has been reached. A general topology optimization method for
achieving design of simultaneous mechanism and actuator design for morphing structures have
been presented. The morphing is achieved by minimizing an error between target nodes and
displaced nodes. The morphing in itself does not converge element densities; for that reason,
compliance minimization is utilized to force converges and ensure a stiff structure with respect
to external loading. The actuator design has been achieved by introducing a bi-material model,
where the density and material phase can be interpolated. This way, one material could be
formulated as a strictly structural material, which forms the mechanism’s primary structure.
A second material could be introduced as an actuating material for stimulating the structure
by specifying a thermal expansion coeﬀicient.
The method was shown to work for both a two-dimensional structure and a three-dimensional
structure, from analyzing a trailing edge section in three-dimensions show apparent effects of
the added dimension as closed wall structures and box section formed.
An eﬀicient 3D parallel framework has been used and extended upon to model large scale wing
structures on an unstructured conforming grid.
When actuators were forced on to a non-uniform layout, the three-dimensional analysis pre-
sented a more unconventional design, which is not based on a rib layout as is the industry
standard. This may suggest that a two-dimensional rib design is inferior to a three-dimensional
design, from pure mechanical observation.
Furthermore, the presented method has shown how actuation design can be included in a mech-
anism design, which is an added design freedom. However, it can be concluded that constraints
to the actuator design are needed for realizing designs, especially for three-dimensional design.
The addition of inclusion of actuator design for a morphing wing structure also clearly sees that
actuation can be located within the structure itself, which may be an inspiration for further
wing structure design, as this proves a better overall volume optimum.
Due to a highly non-convex problem, it can be concluded that the presented density approach
is an tool essential for finding a starting guess for the presented feature mapping formulation.
The method for obtaining a target shape while minimizing compliance, formulated as a single
objective on compliance minimization, can be concluded to work; however, as discuses, the
method can only achieve morphing down to a specified error. When applying the method as
a multiobjective formulation, a good trade-off between stiffness and error were formed in the
form of a convex Pareto front.
In addition to the presented work, the feature mapping approach was generalized for 3D un-
structured grids for any density-based topology optimization problem.
The presented method have shown a superficial analysis for both 2D and 3D design domain,
it is suggested to investigate further. With emphasize on a more in depth analysis of the 3D
wing section, for different load case, deformed/undeformed structure, multi objective analysis
and different morphing targets at once.
The framework should be extend to geometrical non-linearity for a more realistic design.
It is also considered to extend the model to include skin design, and could be coupled with
aerodynamic modeling as seen in [20]. The multiobjective formulation is implemented and ver-
ified for the 3D model but was never analyzed, as a good trade-off was seen for the 2D analysis;
one could analyze if this is also true for the 3D model if this formulation works better than the
single objective formulation.
