Abstract
Micro mobility sharing is a modern and sustainable transportation system that provides
users with convenient access to small vehicles for short-term rides [1]. However, the flexibil-
ity of picking up and dropping off these vehicles at various stations can lead to imbalances
in inventory, posing a significant challenge for operators who must redistribute the ve-
hicles to maintain system equilibrium [2]. To address this challenge, businesses need to
determine the optimal number of vehicles required at each station, which varies through-
out the day based on demand patterns. Thus, accurate predictions of vehicle demand are
crucial for effective redistribution efforts [3]. In this study, we show that effective cluster-
ing of scooter-sharing stations significantly improves the predictive capabilities of vehicle
needs. We propose a K-Means clustering algorithm to cluster scooter-sharing stations by
their weekly temporal patterns, and consequently their location. For these clusters, the
cumulative difference in vehicle arrivals and departures will be predicted using Random
Forest (RF) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM). Our study demonstrates that the
univariate RF regression model outperforms the multivariate LSTM model. We evaluate
our models based on overall R2 scores as well as cluster specific R2 scores. We anticipate
our study to be a starting point for more sophisticated shared mobility clustering and
inventory predictions.

Introduction
In recent years, there has been a remarkable growth in the demand for shared small
vehicles, such as bicycles and e-scooters, giving rise to the concept of ”micro-mobility” [4].
These innovative transportation solutions cater to short-distance travel needs. Offering
on-demand rentals, micro-mobility vehicles provide a convenient and sustainable option
for urban transportation [1]. Given the increasing demand for affordable and eco-friendly
transportation alternatives, micro-mobility sharing has garnered significant attention from
researchers, policymakers, and industry experts [4].
One of the key challenges faced by micro-mobility service providers is maintaining an op-
timal inventory at each station [2]. As the vehicles are available for pick-up and return
at any station, scooter distribution can become uneven, requiring continuous inventory
rebalancing. To address this challenge, businesses need to determine the optimal number
of scooters required at each station, which varies throughout the day based on demand
patterns.
Thus, accurate predictions of vehicle demand are crucial for effective redis-
tribution efforts [3]. In light of these challenges and the importance of accurate demand
predictions, this research aims to answer the following research question: How can machine
learning methods be used to analyze and predict the periodic inventory levels of scooters,
and how can these predictions contribute to effective inventory planning strategies for
micro-mobility sharing businesses?
To achieve this objective, this research will employ machine learning methods to analyze
and predict the periodic inventory levels of scooters at various stations in Paris.
The
data utilized for this study will be obtained from emTransit BV, a company specializing
in the collection and analysis of e-scooter data in the city. Temporal trends within the
scooter sharing network will be analyzed, and clustering techniques will be applied to
identify distinct demand patterns. Additionally, regions within Paris will be constructed
based on their geographic locations and demand patterns, serving as a pre-processing
step for inventory level prediction models. The research will emphasize the significance of
developing representative profiles of demand patterns to enhance the accuracy of future
micro-mobility demand predictions. These predictions can be used in future re-balancing
optimization models, providing businesses with valuable resource allocation strategies to
tailor their inventory accordingly.
Eﬀicient inventory management is crucial for micro-mobility sharing businesses to meet
customer demand, ensure customer satisfaction, and maximize resource utilization. Straight-
forward approaches, such as naive predictions based on historical data or fixed allocation
policies, have limitations in adapting to dynamic demand patterns. Machine learning al-
gorithms offer the potential to analyze complex patterns and relationships within large
datasets, providing more accurate predictions of inventory levels. The findings of this

Literature Review
Micro-mobility sharing, which includes bike-sharing and scooter-sharing, has become an
increasingly popular mode of transportation in cities worldwide due to its convenience,
cost-effectiveness, and eco-friendliness.
Research in micro-mobility sharing focuses on
understanding the behavior of users [5], the impact of micro-mobility on traﬀic and the
environment [1], the effectiveness of various business models and pricing strategies [6], and
many more aspects.
The success of micro-mobility sharing strongly depends on the ability of operators to
effectively manage inventory levels, re-balance vehicles, and predict demand.
In order
to satisfy customers, micro-mobility sharing services must maintain a high level of bike
availability. Which can be a diﬀicult task as customer movements are highly dynamic,
frequently consisting of one-way journeys, and redistributing vehicles is costly [7]. In recent
years, research has been conducted to address these challenges and improve the availability
and user satisfaction of micro-mobility sharing systems [8, 9]. In this chapter, the literature
on four micro-mobility sharing research areas are identified and examined. Specifically,
demand forecasting, inventory level analysis, re-balancing operations and clustering of
micro-mobility sharing stations.
2.0.1
Demand Prediction
Predicting demand is essential for optimizing inventory levels and ensuring adequate avail-
ability of bikes or scooters to meet user needs in micro-mobility sharing systems. Several
studies have investigated the application of machine learning methods in micro-mobility
sharing demand prediction. Gao and Chen (2019) [10] demonstrated that Random For-
est (RF) and Support Vector Machine (SVM) models outperformed K-Nearest Neighbors
(KNN) and linear regression. These models showed improved prediction accuracy by in-
corporating various features such as weather, pollution, traﬀic data, Covid-19 cases, and
social and economic factors. In a comparative analysis by Wang (2016) [11], neural net-
works (NN) and decision trees (tree-based models) consistently achieved the highest levels
of prediction accuracy in forecasting regional bike rental demand.
Pan et al. (2019) [12] proposed the utilization of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) mod-
els to predict system-level hourly pickup and drop-off demands. By incorporating historical
data and weather conditions, their approach significantly enhanced the accuracy of pre-
dictions. Wang and Kim (2018) [13] employed recurrent neural network (RNN) models to
forecast short-term bike availability at docking stations. By comparing RNN models with
the Random Forest (RF) algorithm using one-month historical data and different predic-
tion intervals (1 minute, 5 minutes, and 10 minutes), they found that LSTM models with
more complex architectures outperformed RF in providing superior predictions for longer
intervals, despite RF having lower computational costs during training.

2.0.2
Inventory Levels
Optimizing inventory levels in micro-mobility sharing systems is crucial to meet demand
while minimizing costs associated with excess inventory. A common approach to identi-
fying optimal inventory levels involves establishing upper and lower bounds based on his-
torical data. O’Mahony and Shmoys (2015) [16] do this by interpreting average demand
per time window as the lower bound for the inventory level. Furthermore, O’Mahony and
Shmoys’ research take demand censoring into account by using information on the number
of bikes available at the station at each time.
Cantelmo et al. (2021) [17] utilized Machine Learning (ML) techniques, specifically Ran-
dom Forest and Gradient Tree Boosting classifiers, to address the inventory problem. The
evaluation of these classification models, conducted using real-world data from the Citi-
Bike system in New York, incorporated a novel iterative re-sampling procedure to handle
imbalanced datasets. The results provided dependable upper-bound and lower-bound es-
timates for inventory estimation. Schuijbroek et al. (2017) [8] also define a minimum and
maximum inventory level by assuming a Poisson distribution for arrivals and departures.
Moreover, the study combines this method with a model known as the User Dissatisfaction
Function (UDF) model, which seeks to optimize inventory in terms of the number of failed
pickups and returns.
2.0.3
Rebalancing
In an effort to improve the micro-mobility sharing systems, methods of rebalancing the
system have also been extensively examined to meet the demand better. Rebalancing
involves redistributing bikes or scooters to ensure availability in areas with high demand.
Repositioning vehicles can be done statically or dynamically. Static rebalancing typically
occurs overnight, while dynamic rebalancing takes place throughout the day. Static repo-
sitioning has attracted the most research attention, partly due to its ease of modeling
[9].
Dell’Amico et al. (2014) [18] approached the Bike Sharing Rebalancing Problem (BRP)
as a special one-commodity pickup-and-delivery capacitated vehicle routing problem. The
objective was to minimize cost while limiting the number of vehicles used for rebalancing.
They presented four mixed-integer linear programming (MIP) formulations for the BRP.
Schuijbroek et al. (2017) [8] proposed a cluster-first route-second heuristic that combines
inventory flexibility and routing decisions. This approach enables operators to implement
a tradeoff between user satisfaction and the cost of rebalancing. The authors developed

Furthermore, incentives can be employed to encourage users to pick up vehicles from
stations with high inventory and return them to stations with low inventory. For instance,

to specific stations [9]. Moreover, Pan et al. (2019) [19] proposed a deep reinforcement
learning framework for incentivizing users to rebalance dockless bike sharing systems.
2.0.4
Clustering Stations
Clustering algorithms have been widely employed to investigate activity patterns in micro-
mobility sharing systems. As the datasets generated by such systems are typically large,
it can be crucial to employ methods capable of summarizing its underlying information
[20]. According to O’Mahony and Shmoys (2015) [16], the purpose of clustering stations
together is not to maintain system balance, but to ensure convenient access for users. To
achieve this, they formulate a clustering problem in order to direct rebalancing resources
to stations in the city accordingly.
Froehlich et al. (2009) [21] conducted a spatio-temporal analysis of a bike-sharing system
in Barcelona. They utilized hierarchical clustering techniques and Dynamic Time Warping
(DTW) to identify behavioral patterns based on bike availability. Their algorithm, which
focused on temporal data, identified six clusters with three classes of behavior: outgoing,
incoming and flat.
Furthermore, their results show that neighboring stations tend to
exhibit similar usage patterns. Similarily, Chabchoub and Fricker (2014) [22] employed
K-means clustering algorithm with DTW to measure the similarity between the stations.
Their aim was to classify stations into three clusters based on bike availability: balanced,
overloaded, and underloaded.
For time-series clustering, Euclidean Distance (ED) and Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)
are widely used similarity measurement methods [23].
Li et al.
(2019) [23] compared
the results of K-means clustering using the two distance metrics and found that DTW
provided better results than ED in most cases. Like Froehlich et al (2009), their findings
also revealed that temporally similar stations are usually adjacent spatially, suggesting
that neighboring zones are likely to exhibit similar user demands. Vogel et al. (2011) [7]
compared three different methods using ED to construct clusters of stations with similar
demand patterns using temporal data. Their results showed that Expectation Maximiza-
tion (EM) outperforms K-means (KM) and sequential information bottleneck (sIB).
Etienne and Latifa (2014) [24] propose a model that can account for observed behav-
ioral differences between weekdays and weekends. They used station usage count for each
available day instead of a long-term summaries that may be influenced by seasonal or
weather-related factors. Their model-based clustering algorithm used an EM algorithm to
estimate the model’s parameters. Moreover, they associated station neighborhood char-

Caggiani et al. (2018) [20] suggested a two-step clustering method. Initially, they clus-

second clustering algorithm uses K-means and seeks to minimize the distance between the
centroids of each spatio-temporal cluster and their elements. Their aim was to develop
clusters that were large enough to share their bike availability effectively while remaining
small enough to allow users to pick up bikes within a reasonable distance of their starting

2.0.5
Conclusion
The three sides to the micro-mobility problem, demand prediction (2.0.1), optimizing
inventory levels (2.0.2) and rebalancing (2.0.3), have all been extensively researched and
tested. Machine learning techniques, including Random Forest, Support Vector Machine,
and Long Short-Term Memory models, have been utilized to accurately forecast demand
by incorporating various factors. Researchers have also addressed the inventory problem
using techniques such as establishing bounds and employing machine learning classifiers
like Random Forest and Gradient Tree Boosting.
Rebalancing strategies, including static and dynamic approaches, have been explored, with
optimization methods proposed to minimize costs.
Furthermore, clustering algorithms
have played a significant role in analyzing activity patterns and grouping stations based
on demand using clustering algorithms. Similarity measures such as Euclidean Distance
and Dynamic Time Warping have been employed to identify behavioral patterns and
establish clusters of stations with similar usage characteristics.
This enables effective
resource sharing and ensures convenient access for users.
Furthermore, these research areas have all been explored separately as well as a solution
as a whole. Regue and Recker (2014) [25] proposed a framework that combines demand
forecasting at the station level, with a station inventory model, a redistribution needs
model as well as a vehicle routing model. The first two models utilize historical data and
their outputs serve as an input to the latter two models.
In conclusion, the combined efforts in these research areas have laid a solid foundation for
improving the eﬀiciency, effectiveness, reliability and user satisfaction of micro-mobility
sharing systems. Further research and innovation in this field will continue to contribute
to the development of eﬀicient and dependable micro-mobility sharing networks.

Data
The focus of this chapter is to provide a comprehensive description and thorough analysis
of the dataset graciously provided by emTransit BV. The first step involves presenting the
data and its features, which is followed by data cleaning and pre-processing steps which are
taken to ensure the integrity and reliability of the dataset. Subsequently, exploratory data
analysis is performed, unraveling patterns in the data. The final section of the chapter
presents additional data that can enrich the breadth and depth of this study.
3.1
Description of the Data
The data is provided by emTransit BV which is a private company that specializes in
collecting and analyzing data on micro-mobility services such as shared e-scooters and
e-bikes. The company is a data solutions provider whose mission is to help cities and
transport operators make informed decisions about the deployment of micro-mobility ser-
vices, optimize fleet management, and understand travel patterns and behaviors [26]. The
data provided by emTransit BV for this project includes information on e-scooter rides
and stations in Paris. The scooters themselves are operated by a micro-mobility company
called Dott. Dott provides the physical infrastructure for the scooters and manages the
day-to-day operations of the services [27]. These scooters are distributed throughout the
city of Paris and can be picked up and returned at any station. The stations for the scoot-
ers are virtual, meaning there is no physical infrastructure, however, each station comes
with a radius within which a scooter should be parked.

Data Cleaning and Pre-Processing
The data was subjected to a series of cleaning steps to ensure the quality and reliability
of the data. Initially, the location of all the stations was confirmed and those stations
located outside of Paris were excluded from the analysis. Additionally, it was ensured
that all stations offered scooter services.
In order to have comparable stations, it was determined that only the stations that were
operational for the entire duration of 2022 would be considered for analysis. The year
was chosen as it contained the most data and would be least affected by COVID-19
virus outbreak. This particular selection criterion was deemed appropriate given that the
stations that operated for only part of the year were found to be situated within a 1km
radius of other stations. Based on this finding, it was assumed that renters would opt
to utilize the nearby alternative stations.
Furthermore, no data on rides was omitted
since rides associated with these stations will be connected to their nearest active station.
Following the completion of the data cleaning process, a total of 2380 stations were retained
for analysis. These stations were all open for the entire period of January 1st to December
8th, providing 342 days of data.
Moreover, in order to focus the analysis on rides that are relevant to the micro-mobility
system within the city of Paris, a boundary was established to delineate the city limits.
Rides occurring outside of this boundary were excluded from the analysis as they did not

4
Methodology
This chapter will explain the methods used when analyzing the data. Firstly, the stations
were divided into regions using Voronoi distribution, allowing the rides to be assigned to
their nearest station. After assigning start and end stations to each ride, the data was
aggregated for each station, creating a dataset to be used for further analysis. To identify
patterns within the data, temporal clustering was performed.
Additionally, to create
clusters for re-balancing, spatial clustering was performed, using the temporal clusters
as an input.
These clusters were then explored and finally, models to predict scooter
inventory levels were created. The following subchapters will explain each of these steps
in detail.
4.1
Mapping Rides to Stations
Each ride in the dataset had its start and end positions geolocated.
As a result, the
station where the scooters were picked up and dropped off had to be estimated based
on that information. This was accomplished by the use of a Voronoi distribution. The
approach generates a Voronoi diagram where an area is divided into regions, each of
which contains one of a given set of points [30]. This technique has been widely employed
in transportation research for analyzing spatial patterns of mobility [31]. In this case,
the points are the geolocation of the stations. This allowed for the creation of distinct
polygons surrounding each station. Given this spatial distribution of stations, the origin
and destination of all rides were mapped to the station whose polygon region they were
within. Using this methodology, the spatial distribution of trips could be analyzed in
depth and patterns and tendencies could be identified.
After mapping the rides to the corresponding stations using the Voronoi distribution, the
data was examined to gain insights into the distribution of rides across stations. The area
of the station regions and the total number of arrivals and departures per station were
visualized. Additionally, the total difference between arrivals and departures was ana-
lyzed to provide a quick insight into the rebalancing implemented on the scooter network
throughout 2022. A high positive difference indicated that more scooters arrived at the
station compared to the number of departures, resulting in an accumulation of scooters
over time if not redistributed. Conversely, a negative difference meant that more scooters
departed than arrived, which would be infeasible without rebalancing procedures. More-
over, temporal trends for specific stations were examined to illustrate the variety of the
data.

4.2.1
Net Flow
The net flow represents the cumulative hourly difference in arrivals and departures, set to
zero at the start of each day. This value is derived by subtracting the cumulative hourly
departures from the arrivals and can reach both positive and negative values throughout
the day. A value greater than zero indicates that during the time period from midnight,
until the specific hour being examined, the number of arriving scooters exceeded the num-
ber of departed ones. Conversely, a value less than zero signifies the opposite. Moreover,
a negative net flow implies that there must have been a surplus of scooters at the station
at the start of day, since the data shows that they departed from the station.
To calculate the net flow, the data was grouped by stations, and the rides were aggregated
by summing up the hourly number of arrivals and departures for each station on each
day. The daily cumulative hourly sums of arrivals and departures were also calculated,
resetting to zero at midnight each day. This ensured that the value for each hour reflected
the total number of scooters that had arrived or departed since midnight. Using these
cumulative data, the net flow of scooters was computed, providing a dataset for further
analysis of scooter usage distribution at each station.
4.2.2
Upper and Lower Bounds
The upper and lower boundaries of scooters per station represent the lowest number of
scooters required to meet demand and the maximum number of scooters required to remain
within a station’s capacity. They are a simple way to add the outcome of an inventory
prediction as an input to a re-balancing optimization model. The bounds could be added
as constraints to signify the minimum and maximum amount of scooters to be stored at
each station. Figure 4.1 displays an example of cumulative arrivals and departures as well
as the net flow for one day. The maximum and minimum values of the net flow are used
to calculate the upper and lower bounds as explained in the following sub-chapters. The
method used is the same as Cantelmo et al. (2021) [17] introduced in their analysis of
bike-sharing inventory levels.

4.2.3
Outlier Detection
To detect outliers within the lower bounds, both manual inspection and statistical methods
were employed. Manual inspection involved examining specific stations with exceptionally
high lower bounds and investigating the underlying reasons for these spikes. Subsequently,
statistical methods, such as the Interquartile Range (IQR) test, were applied to identify
outliers based on predefined cutoff limits. The IQR test involved calculating an upper
cutoff limit using a multiplier of 1.5 times the IQR, where the IQR represents the range
between the 25th and 75th percentiles of the lower bounds distribution. A lower cutoff
limit was not used since the lower bounds can not be negative.
After the outliers were detected, a decision was made regarding their treatment. Factors
such as the nature of the outliers, their frequency, and their potential impact on the
analysis were considered.
The decision aimed to strike a balance between preserving
the integrity of the data and ensuring that extreme values did not distort the overall
interpretation of the inventory levels.
4.3
Temporal Clustering
To explore the temporal patterns of the stations, they were clustered based on their
temporal data.
This could lead to the identification of a cluster of stations with, for
example, high traﬀic during rush hour or a specific trend on weekends. The clustering was
performed using two types of input, once by using the net flow of scooters as input, and
again with the hourly difference in arrivals and departures. In both approaches, the data
was aggregated by weekday and hour, using a total sum of the entire period of 2022. This
produced a dataset containing one column for each hour of each weekday, for a total of
168 columns for each station. This aggregation was chosen in order to maintain some of
the trends present in the data, while also limiting the dimensionality.

4.3.1
Distance Metrics
The K-Means algorithm will be used for all temporal clustering, as explained in the next
sub-chapter. Two different distance measures will be used in the algorithm, Euclidean
distance and Dynamic Time Warping (DTW). Euclidean distance is the primary distance
function used with K-Means. It is computed by finding the square of the distance between
each variable, adding the squares, and then taking the square root of that sum [32].
On the other hand, DTW is a distance measure specifically designed to compare sequences
or time series data. Unlike Euclidean distance, DTW aligns the data in time to account for
temporal distortions and variations in the sequences. It measures the similarity between
two sequences by finding the optimal alignment that minimizes the total distance between
corresponding points along the sequences. By allowing warping or stretching of the time
axis, the clustering algorithm can detect clusters with similar structures or patterns, even
if they are shifted in time or have different lengths [33], [34].
In general, the Euclidean distance metric is used to find an average of all the data within
the clusters. However, because of its one-to-one mapping, it is incapable of capturing the
average shape of the two time series, in which case Dynamic Time Warping is preferable
[35]. As this clustering is based on time series data where stations might have similar trends
with a slight difference in time, both methods will be explored in order to determine the
optimal distance metric for computing the temporal clusters.
4.3.2
Time Series K-Means
The Time Series K-means clustering algorithm was chosen for the temporal clustering. The
utilization of Time Series K-means was driven by the objective of conducting a comparative
analysis between clustering techniques employing Euclidean distance and Dynamic Time
Warping distance. When it comes to fixed-length time series data and using Euclidean
distance, Time Series K-means clustering closely resembles the regular K-means clustering
algorithm.
K-means clustering is a well-known unsupervised learning algorithm for detecting pat-
terns and classifying data into distinct clusters. The algorithm has the advantage of being
computationally eﬀicient, simple to implement, and has intuitive parameters such as the
number of clusters. However, K-means clustering also has some limitations. One of them
is that the quality of the clustering can be affected by the initial positioning of the cen-
troids, which can result in different outcomes for different initializations [36]. Furthermore,
determining the optimal number of clusters can be diﬀicult and subjective, as it requires
balancing the trade-off between model complexity and clustering quality [37].
4.3.3
Evaluation Metrics
To assess the performance of the different variations of K-Means clustering, two evaluation
metrics were utilized: the elbow plot and the silhouette score.
The elbow plot, based on the concept of inertia, measures the sum of squared errors
(SSE) or the sum of the square distances between each data point and its assigned cluster

centroid. The goal is to minimize this error, indicating tighter and more compact clusters.
The optimal value of K (the number of clusters) is determined by identifying the point
on the plot where the SSE drops rapidly, forming a smaller angle or an ”elbow” [38].
This plot, in conjunction with the silhouette score, was used to determine the appropriate
number of clusters for the temporal clustering.
The silhouette score, which combines cohesion within clusters and separation between
clusters, provides a measure of how well-defined the clusters are. It quantifies the average
distance between each data point and its assigned cluster centroid, compared to the average
distance between the data point and its nearest neighboring cluster. The silhouette score
ranges from -1 to 1, with a higher value indicating better clustering quality [39].
The choice between using hourly data or cumulative hourly data as input for clustering
was made by comparing their silhouette scores. Additionally, the silhouette score was
employed to determine the optimal initial positioning of the centroids. To account for
the randomness involved in the initial centroid placement, the clustering algorithms were
executed 20 times with different random seeds, and the instance with the highest silhouette
score was selected.
4.3.4
Outlier Detection
In order to analyze the clusters using both Euclidean distance and Dynamic Time Warp-
ing, an outlier detection step was conducted using the Local Outlier Factor and Median
Absolute Deviation (MAD) methods. The Local Outlier Factor (LOF) is an outlier de-
tection algorithm that identifies outliers by measuring the local deviation of a specific
data point. This algorithm determines outliers by assessing the density between each data
point and its neighboring points. When a point has a lower density than its neighbors, it is
more likely to be classified as an outlier [40]. In order to detect outliers using this method,
each station examines its twenty closest neighbors and evaluates its level of isolation in
relation to these neighbors. It was determined that the five percent of stations with the
lowest density would be considered as outliers. The Median Absolute Deviation (MAD)
method computes the median distance from a data point to the cluster centroid, as well
as the absolute median distance for each data point to the cluster centroids. A station is
considered an outlier if it falls outside the range of the median distance plus four times the
median absolute distance from the cluster centroid. The outliers that will be examined
are those identified as outliers in both methods.
4.4
Spatio-Temporal Clustering
Before feeding the data into prediction models, the stations were divided into spatial
clusters, each cluster containing a set of neighbouring stations. The reason for this is
twofold. Firstly, the data was sparse since many stations had few rides which resulted
in an abundance of zeros in the hourly aggregated data. Secondly, from an operational
point of view, it can be diﬀicult to accurately re-balance 2380 stations. By aggregating
the stations into larger areas, these areas can be re-balanced instead of individual stations.

The company can then freely drop off or pick up scooters within the stations in the area
as needed.
4.4.1
Temporal Clusters as Input
The temporal clusters were used as an input to the spatial clustering to ensure that
each cluster contained stations with a comparable temporal pattern. The goal is to avoid
creating clusters with opposing trends that might cancel each other out, and instead create
clusters that could amplify their shared patterns. By clustering the stations, the demand
of all stations within a cluster could be aggregated, treating them as a single entity. This
was accomplished in two ways. Firstly, the spatial clusters were generated within a loop of
all temporal clusters. That is, a K-means algorithm was employed on all temporal clusters
separately and the resulting clusters were then combined. Thus, all stations were clustered
into spatio-temporal clusters where no cluster contained more than one temporal cluster.
This will be referred to as method 1. In the second method, method 2, the temporal
cluster assignments were added as one-hot-encoded features to the clustering. In that case
the entire dataset was normalized before executing the K-means algorithm. These two
methods were then compared in order to find the optimal spatio-temporal clustering of
the stations.
4.4.2
Distance Criteria
To guarantee that a user always has access to a scooter, the size of a cluster must be
small enough so that a re-balanced station is always within walking distance from other
stations in its cluster. Meaning that if one station within the cluster does not have enough
inventory, a user will be able to walk to a station that has been re-balanced to meet the
demand at both stations. For simplicity, the air distance between geopoints was used
instead of ground or road distance. As a starting point, it was determined to aim for a
maximum of 500m from each station to its cluster center. This distance, the air distance
between a station to its respective cluster center, will be referred to as walking distance.
4.4.3
K-Means
For this application, the computational eﬀiciency of the K-means algorithm is ideal. The
K-means algorithm was utilized with an increasing K to find the lowest amount of clusters
that meet the distance criteria. Thus, starting from K equal to one, K-means clustering
was performed until the distance from all stations to their cluster center was within 500m,
which was the predetermined initial distance threshold. The stations’ longitude and lat-
itude values were used as inputs as well as a Boolean variable that indicates whether a
station is north or south of the Seine river. The Boolean prevents stations with a short
air distance but separated by a river from being clustered together. Furthermore, the
temporal clusters were added as input in the two ways mentioned earlier.
4.4.4
Evaluation Metric
The optimal initial positioning of the centroids was found by executing both clustering
methods 20 times with different random seeds. The instance that provided the lowest value
of K was chosen for further analysis for both methods. Given these results, the silhouette

4.5
Predictions
This chapter delves into the prediction methods used for net flow predictions. The decision
to prioritize net flow predictions over lower bounds was influenced by several factors.
Firstly, the available data for lower bounds is significantly more limited compared to net
flow. Specifically, the amount of data per station is 24 times lower for lower bounds as
the lower bounds only capture the most extreme values for each day. By focusing on net
flow predictions, it becomes possible to interpret these extreme values retrospectively in
addition to gaining insights into other trends related to scooter inventory levels.
Additionally, estimating the net flow enables the calculation of lower bounds for spe-
cific time intervals, such as the inventory requirements for morning or afternoon traﬀic
specifically.
This allows for a more targeted understanding of inventory needs during
different periods of the day. Furthermore, although upper bounds were not estimated in

To predict future variables, historical values of the target variable are utilized as inputs.

4.5.3
Feature Selection
Selecting appropriate features is crucial for enhancing model performance as it allows for
effective interpretation and prediction of the underlying trend. Insuﬀicient features can
hinder the model’s ability to capture the trend, while an excessive number of features
can lead to computational ineﬀiciency and distract from the key patterns in the data.
This understanding aids in reducing the dimensionality of the input data by excluding
irrelevant or weakly correlated lagged variables that might introduce noise and compromise
the model’s accuracy.
Lagged variables and moving averages both serve important purposes in time series mod-
eling. Including lagged variables in the model explicitly helps capture the influence of past
values on the current value. They allow the model to learn from the historical patterns and
correlations in the data, enabling better prediction of future values. Moving averages, on
the other hand, are a smoothing technique used to reduce noise and highlight underlying
trends in the time series. This helps to eliminate short-term fluctuations and emphasize
the long-term patterns and trends in the data [43].
The ACF (Autocorrelation Function) plot was utilized to identify significant autocorrela-
tion at different lags and determine appropriate lag values for incorporating the moving
average (MA) component in the modeling [43]. The ACF reveals the correlation between
observations of a time series at different time lags, indicating how the values of the series
are related to their past values [44]. Statistically significant lags indicate that including
these values can improve the accuracy of the time series modeling. The absence of signifi-
cant correlation values in the ACF plot at certain lags indicates that those lagged variables
may not contribute significantly to the predictive performance [45]. Similarly, peaks in
the PACF (Partial Autocorrelation Function) plot indicate the strength and direction of
correlation between the current observation and observations at specific lags, highlighting
the influence of the autoregressive (AR) component in the data [43]. The number of lags

4.5.4
Model Architecture
Two model architectures were explored for modeling spatio-temporal clusters within the
network. The first approach involved training a single model using combined data from all
clusters, while the second approach involved employing separate models for each cluster.
The former, referred to as Model 1, allows the model to learn from different clusters and
provide predictions for all stations simultaneously. However, it might throw the model off
from each clusters distinct pattern and result in predictions that are too elemental. On the
other hand, the latter approach, Model 2, only has access to each clusters data and could
be used to individualize the models to fit each cluster. Both methods were implemented
to compare their performance.
Model 1 was implemented using Random Forests (RF), while Model 2 was implemented
using both Random Forests (RF) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM). The Random
Forest models in both approaches predict each hour individually, following a univariate
approach, and the LSTM model predicts all 24 hours simultaneously, representing a mul-
tivariate model.
Moreover, hyperparameter tuning was conducted using selective cross-validation and trial
and error. When training a model, it is important to recognize that the model’s per-
formance is not determined solely by its input features and algorithm. The selection of
suitable hyperparameters also plays a crucial role in this process. These hyperparameters
are instrumental in addressing issues related to overfitting and underfitting, improving
computational eﬀiciency, and strengthening the overall robustness and stability of the
model [46].
4.5.5
Random Forest
The Random Forest (RF) algorithm, introduced by L. Breiman in 2001, has achieved
remarkable success as a versatile method for both classification and regression tasks, as
highlighted by Biau et al. (2016) [47]. The success is due to its carefully implemented
randomness. Moreover, the RF framework provides valuable insights into its predictive
capabilities by considering the strengths of individual predictors and their correlations
[48].
Random Forest (RF) constructs a set of decision trees. Each decision tree for a RF is
built from a bootstrapped dataset, a random subset of the training dataset, with features
chosen randomly in each step. During training, the algorithm generates multiple decision
trees that independently learn patterns and relationships in the data. RF outperforms
individual decision trees due to the variety of decision trees [48]. The aim is to employ

the RF algorithm to achieve precise predictions of future net flow values using historical
data. In this specific task, a RF regression model was developed for predicting continuous

4.5.6
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), is a type of recurrent neural network (RNN) intro-
duced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber in 1997 [49]. It is a powerful model commonly
used for time series prediction tasks and has shown remarkable effectiveness in capturing
long-term dependencies [50]. The LSTM model learns from historical values and can cap-
ture both short-term fluctuations and long-term trends in the data. The model utilizes a
memory cell structure that enables it to selectively store and update information. Each
memory cell has input and output gates to protect the memory content from irrelevant
inputs [49]. This architecture allows LSTM to enforce a constant error flow and, thus,
overcome the limitations of traditional RNNs, such as the vanishing gradient problem
[51].
In this study, the model architecture consists of three LSTM layers, with 128, 128, and
64 units, respectively. The first two LSTM layers return sequences, while the last LSTM
layer does not. Each LSTM layer is followed by a Leaky Rectified Linear Unit activation
function, which introduces non-linearity to the model [52]. Dropout regularization was
applied after the second and third LSTM layers with a rate of 0.25 to prevent overfitting
[53]. The model’s output was passed through a dense layer with 24 units, with each unit
representing a specific hour of the day. The Adam optimizer was used for training where
the learning rate was 0.004, with a mean squared error loss function. When training the
model, 30 epochs were used and the validation split was 0.1.
4.5.7
Evaluation Metrics
To gain a comprehensive understanding of the model’s performance, multiple metrics were
utilized. Specifically, R-squared (R2) score, Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean
Squared Error (RMSE). These metrics provide valuable insights into the performance of
the prediction models and help quantify their accuracy.
The MAE measures the average difference between the predicted values and the actual
values. It provides direct measures of the magnitude of the prediction errors. The RMSE
calculates the square root of the average squared difference between the predicted values
and the actual values. This measure can also be directly compared to the actual values.
Both MAE and RMSE range from 0 to ∞, where 0 indicates a perfect fit between pre-
dicted and actual values. MAE offers a direct evaluation of the average absolute error,
disregarding the direction of the errors. On the other hand, RMSE assigns greater impor-
tance to larger errors by squaring them before averaging, which amplifies their impact on
the overall metric [54].

5.5.4
Hyperparameters/Cross Validation
The optimal performance of machine learning models relies not only on their default set-
tings but also on carefully chosen hyperparameters. In this study, the Random Forest and
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models underwent experimentation and hyperparam-
eter tuning to enhance their effectiveness.
Initially, the Random Forest model was trained using default settings, which allowed the
model to create decision trees with unrestricted depth, required a minimum of 2 samples
at each node, and utilized 100 decision trees. However, this approach led to severe over-
fitting of the model. To address this issue, hyperparameter tuning was performed. Due to
computational limitations, exhaustive cross-validation on the model and all its variations
was not feasible. Nonetheless, selective cross-validation was conducted on random stations
to gain insights into the optimal hyperparameters. Based on this testing, several adjust-
ments were made. Each decision tree was limited to a maximum depth of 5 leaf nodes,
requiring a minimum of 5 samples to split a node. Additionally, for each individual model,
a total of 300 decision trees were created. These refined hyperparameters aimed to strike
a balance between model complexity, data representation, and computational eﬀiciency.
In the case of the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model, a series of experiments were
conducted to evaluate its performance using various configurations. These experiments
involved testing different combinations of hidden LSTM layers, dropout percentages, learn-

Discussion
This chapter aims to summarize key findings on scooter stations, clustering analysis, and
predictions. Methodologies are critically evaluated, considering both strengths and limi-
tations. Data limitations and assumptions are discussed, emphasizing the need for future
research to improve predictive models. This chapter presents valuable insights that help
draw meaningful conclusions and provide recommendations for the micro-mobility indus-
try.
6.1
Summary of Findings
The analysis of the scooter sharing dataset has yielded several key findings, contributing
to a comprehensive understanding of the inventory levels and usage patterns within the
scooter network in Paris.
6.1.1
Scooter Stations
The size of a station’s region was found to be unrelated to its demand, suggesting the
presence of other influential factors from the area surrounding a station. Moreover, the
analysis of scooter departures and arrivals per station provided insights into the network’s
rebalancing efforts, highlighting stations that had required attention during the year being
analysed. Examination of outliers in the dataset revealed patterns where high lower bounds
were associated with a large number of scooters arriving the previous day.
6.1.2
Clustering
The temporal clustering analysis yielded valuable insights into the daily patterns and
trends of scooter usage. Three distinct types of clusters were identified: neutral, negative,
and positive. The geographic location of these clusters provided a regional perspective on
demand patterns. The preference for the Euclidean distance as a measure emerged due

strategic areas for implementing effective rebalancing efforts within the network.

6.2
Methodology Evaluation
The methodologies employed in this analysis have several strengths and limitations that
need to be considered in the interpretation of the findings.
This subchapter aims to
critically evaluate the methods used and address potential biases.
6.2.1
Voronoi Distribution Method
The Voronoi distribution method proved to be effective in mapping all rides to a nearby
station, indicating its suitability for the analysis. This was necessary since the dataset
used in this study did not contain information on the specific station being used, making
the scooter network a mix of a station-based and a free-floating system.
One limitation of the Voronoi distribution method is its lack of consideration for certain
geographical features, such as the Seine river and other factors like buildings, roads, or
other infrastructure. As a result, some Voronoi regions may cover areas on both sides of the
river, potentially leading to inaccurate station assignments. As the method is based solely
on direct distances, some rides may be categorized with stations that are not necessarily
the nearest when considering the actual walking distance of the user.
6.2.2
Temporal Clustering
The choice of having three clusters for the temporal analysis is not necessarily the optimal
choice for capturing all possible variations in scooter usage patterns. The trade-off between
silhouette scores and inertia could be prioritised differently. By allowing for more clusters,
the clustering algorithm may be able to detect and categorize additional variations within
the three basic trends (neutral, positive, and negative). Additionally, the incorporation of
station neighborhood characteristics, such as proximity to railway stations and parks, as
well as employment and housing rates of the areas, could enhance the clustering process
and play a key role in identifying similar stations.
The DTW distance method stretches and compresses time series, resulting in points from
one time series being compared with arbitrary points from another time series [34]. This
means that time series with similar patterns occurring in different time periods may be
considered similar. In this analysis, the DTW method was implemented without any limi-
tation on what can be compared between the two time series, which may have contributed
to slightly worse clustering performance compared to the Euclidean distance method. An
improved version could involve incorporating a window, such as the Sakoe-Chiba band
[59], to limit the hours that can be compared.
6.2.3
Spatio-Temporal Clustering
Both spatial clustering methods tested in this analysis did eventually not mix the tempo-
ral clusters within the spatio-temporal clustering. Even though the method that clustered

fectively clustered the stations with minimal distance, it could be problematic if more
temporal clusters are introduced. In such cases, it would be beneficial to develop a spatio-
temporal clustering method that considers the trade-off between combining different tem-
poral clusters based on their temporal differences and spatial distance. Moreover, similar
to the Voronoi regions, the distance between stations and their cluster centers was mea-

6.2.4
Predictions
The hyperparameters of the machine learning models were not evaluated using exhaustive
cross-validation methods due to computational limitations. As a result, the same param-
eters were selected for each model without full optimization. Similarly, the selection of
lagged variables was based on average trends of all clusters. Furthermore, the choice of
features incorporated in the model was kept simple and their individual importance was
not fully evaluated. This lack of evaluation may have hindered the model’s prediction
accuracy and potentially compromised computational eﬀiciency.
In addition, it should be noted that Random Forest (RF) and Long short-term memory
(LSTM) were not directly compared as they employ different techniques. The RF models
used in the current implementation are univariate, meaning they predict only one hour at a
time. On the other hand, the LSTM model is multivariate. While the RF models provided
better accuracy in this analysis, incorporating a multivariate model has the potential to
enhance performance. By utilizing a multivariate model that predicts all hours for each
day simultaneously, it could capture the interdependencies and relationships among the
hours, leading to improved accuracy.
6.3
Limitations and Future Directions
This chapter discusses the limitations of the study and outlines potential directions for
future research.
6.3.1
Data Limitations
The dataset used in this study has inherent limitations that need to be considered. One
notable limitation is the lack of information about the current inventory level at each
station, such as the exact number of scooters present at any given time. To estimate this
scooter inventory, the analysis had to rely on the data of arrivals and departures. Addi-
tionally, the dataset does not capture the active rebalancing efforts within the network,
which may introduce discrepancies between the observed data and the actual state of the
system. Although the calculated net flow was reset to zero at midnight to account for
potential rebalancing, this approach may have limitations and could potentially lead to
erroneous data. It is important to acknowledge that these missing data could introduce
biases or inaccuracies in the results.
Furthermore, since the data only captures past rides, it does not accurately display the
demand for scooters at each time. This is an indication of censored data, as the demand

an empty station, their demand is not accounted for in the data. When basing future
demand predictions on such data, the models will not learn to account for this additional
unseen demand.
Lastly, combining data from different years in the analysis poses challenges due to the
limited operational duration of many stations.
Comparing data across different years

6.3.2
Assumptions
The study is based on certain assumptions that should be considered when interpreting
the findings. One significant assumption is that users will walk to a nearby station that
has been rebalanced if their nearest station does not have suﬀicient scooters. However, in
practice, this assumption may not hold true for all users. It is possible that users may
choose alternative modes of transportation.
The decision to treat the spatio-temporal
cluster as a single station for rebalancing purposes may, therefore, have practical impli-
cations as it could cause a loss of customers. However, further investigation is needed to
validate this assumption and understand the potential impact on customer behavior.
6.3.3
Future Directions
Future research could focus on addressing these limitations by further enhancing the
methodologies used.
In particular, the areas used for rebalancing could be progressed
further to ensure more realistic areas and predictions can be refined to provide more
accurate estimates of bounds to utilize for rebalancing efforts.
Defining Areas for Rebalancing
To facilitate the comparison of long-term data despite the challenges posed by alternating
station locations and limited operational duration, future work could focus on enhancing
the spatial division of Paris into station regions. Instead of relying solely on the positions
of currently active stations, these regions could be defined based on other relevant factors,
such as the presence of parks, schools, shopping centers, buildings, roads, and walking
paths. These defined areas would remain constant, while individual stations within these
areas may open and close based on business needs or city policies. This approach would
allow for the inclusion of multiple stations within each area, while their demand could be
aggregated since their positions have already been analyzed and determined to be suitable
for combining.
Additionally, instead of considering the direct air distance between geopoints, actual walk-
ing distance could be taken into account to determine the boundaries of each spatio-
temporal area. By incorporating walking distance, the size of each region could be effec-
tively limited, providing a more realistic representation of accessibility. Moreover, con-
ducting a user behavior analysis can help determine the appropriate walking distance to

Conclusion
In conclusion, this research successfully addresses the research questions pertaining to the
utilization of machine learning methods for analyzing and predicting periodic inventory
levels of scooters in micro-mobility sharing businesses. The findings highlight the eﬀicacy
of clustering techniques in identifying behavioral trends within micro-mobility sharing
stations, thereby improving the predictive capabilities of simple models. While the pre-
dictions achieved a certain level of accuracy, there is room for improvement through more
comprehensive feature selection and model optimization.
By accurately predicting the net flow of scooters throughout the day, businesses gain the
flexibility to determine their own lower bounds in inventory allocation. This allows them
to strategically set limits on scooter availability at each station. For instance, in order to
prioritize rebalancing efforts for serving morning traﬀic, lower bounds can be derived from
the net flow predictions, indicating the minimum number of scooters required to meet the
anticipated demand during that period. Moreover, the integration of these lower bounds
as constraints within optimization problems facilitates a more comprehensive approach
to inventory planning. By considering additional factors such as the cost of rebalancing
and other operational considerations, businesses can develop robust and eﬀicient inventory
management strategies.
This study discovered that K-means clustering, employing the Euclidean distance metric,
outperformed the Dynamic Time Warping metric in effectively comparing stations with
similar trends at the same hours. The clustering methods revealed three fundamental
trends within the stations: neutral, negative, and positive. These trends indicate whether
the stations, as a whole, exhibit a self-serving nature with minimal demand or possess a
substantial number of incoming or outgoing scooters. According to the clustering results,
the positive cluster exhibited the most tightly grouped stations, all following a similar
pattern. Notably, this cluster exhibited superior performance compared to the other tem-
poral clusters in predicting net flow. These findings highlight the potential of proficient
station clustering to significantly enhance predictive capabilities.
By addressing the limitations and considering future directions outlined in this study, fur-
ther advancements can be made in understanding and optimizing scooter-sharing systems.
These insights can inform decision-making, policy development, and operational strate-
gies, ultimately contributing to enhanced eﬀiciency and user satisfaction of micro-mobility
sharing systems.
