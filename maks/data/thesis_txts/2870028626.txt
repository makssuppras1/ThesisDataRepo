Abstract
The metabolic pathways of Lactococcus lactis play a crucial role in sustainable biochemical
production. This study explored the metabolic regulation of five L. lactis strains, including
wild-type and genetically engineered variants, optimized for specific fermentation products
such as acetoin, pyruvate, and α-acetolactate. Using NMR spectroscopy, including Dynamic
Nuclear Polarization (DNP) for real-time metabolic tracking, glucose metabolism under vary-
ing conditions was analyzed to assess carbon flux efficiency and metabolic adaptability. The
results confirmed that the wild-type strains MG1363 and SD96 predominantly followed ho-
mofermentative metabolism, producing lactate as the primary end product. The genetically
modified strains exhibited distinct metabolic redirections: VJ017 efficiently converted glu-
cose to acetoin under aerobic conditions, FS1076 accumulated pyruvate, albeit suboptimal
yields, and RD06, engineered for α-acetolactate production, primarily generated acetoin sug-
gesting spontaneous decarboxylation. Notably, substrate availability and oxygen levels were
critical determinants of metabolic performance, with the genetically modified strain failing
to thrive anaerobically due to NAD+ regeneration constraints.
Advanced NMR techniques provided deeper insights into metabolic bottlenecks and flux
distributions, demonstrating the potential of real-time metabolic imaging for optimizing
biotechnological processes. The study highlights the feasibility of fine-tuning environmental
and genetic parameters to enhance the efficiency of L. lactis as a biocatalyst for sustainable
chemical production. Future research should focus on refining metabolic engineering strate-
gies to increase production yields and ensure robustness in industrial applications. These
findings contribute to advancing microbial biotechnology and green chemistry approaches
for renewable biochemical synthesis.

1
Introduction
Currently, around 95% of carbon-based chemicals are derived from fossil resources. To pro-
mote sustainability, alternative methods focusing on converting biomass into chemicals are
being actively explored through both chemocatalytic and biocatalytic approaches [1]. The
central carbon metabolism of microbes is a key asset for sustainable bioproduction, yet its
regulation requires deeper mechanistic insights to enhance control over activity and selec-
tivity in whole-cell catalysis. Using the unique capabilities of NMR spectroscopy for in-cell
tracking, this project focuses on optimizing metabolic pathways in Lactococcus lactis (L. lac-
tis), with the wild type L. lactis strain MG1363 as a starting point of reference.
The aim is to investigate how substrate mixtures, particularly glucose/pyruvate and glu-
cose/citrate mixtures, influence the metabolism of genetically modified L. lactis strains op-
timized for specific end products such as acetolactate, pyruvic acid (pyruvate), and acetoin.
By comparing these strains to the wild type, the goal is to uncover new opportunities to
improve product yields and reevaluate canonical metabolism to identify the most promising
strains for industrial applications.
1.1
Green chemistry
Acetoin (3-hydroxy-2-butanone) is a volatile compound with applications in the produc-
tion of food, fragrance, pharmaceuticals, and biobased chemicals due to its pleasant buttery
aroma and flavor. Due to its extensive applications, the US Department of Energy iden-
tified acetoin as one of 30 high-priority biobased platform chemicals for development and
utilization, which highlights its importance [2]. Traditionally, acetoin is produced through
chemical synthesis, using petroleum-based precursors such as diacetyl and 2,3-butanediol.
However, this approach has significant limitations. The finite nature of petroleum resources
and the environmental hazards associated with chemical production have raised concerns
about the sustainability of these methods. Moreover, chemical synthesis often generates
toxic by-products, limiting the applicability of acetoin in sensitive industries such as food
Page 1 of 73
==PAGE:8==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
and medicine.
In response to these challenges, biological synthesis methods have gained considerable at-
tention as a sustainable and environmentally friendly alternative. Microbial fermentation,
in particular, offers several advantages. It is less dependent on natural environmental con-
ditions, provides a stable and controlled production process, and supports rapid microbial
growth and metabolism, enabling efficient production [3].
Several microorganisms, including species from the genera Klebsiella, Bacillus, Enterobac-
ter, Lactococcus, and Serratia, naturally synthesize acetoin. However, their natural acetoin
production levels are typically low, as acetoin is often a by-product of metabolic pathways
producing 2,3-butanediol or diacetyl. Furthermore, the pathogenicity of some strains and the
inherent genetic instability introduced by mutagenesis in high-yield strains pose additional
challenges for industrial application. To address these issues, researchers have turned to
metabolic engineering to optimize acetoin production. By manipulating metabolic pathways,
the aim is to redirect carbon flux toward acetoin synthesis while minimizing by-products.
Traditionally, the experimental approaches used for changing the flux or the concentration
of a particular metabolite of a metabolic pathway have mostly been based on the inhibition
or overexpression of the presumed rate-limiting step [4, 5].

Background
2.1
Lactococcus lactis
Lactococcus lactis is a Gram-positive lactic acid bacterium (LAB) extensively used in in-
dustrial and biotechnological applications. Its designation as "generally regarded as safe"
(GRAS) makes it an ideal candidate for producing food, pharmaceuticals, and biochemicals
[6]. Beyond its industrial significance, L. lactis possesses probiotic properties and plays a key
role in the production of fermented dairy products, recombinant proteins, and other valu-
able metabolites. At the heart of L. lactis metabolism lies glycolysis, a central pathway that
defines its homofermentative nature, where lactate serves as the primary end product when

growing on fermentable sugars. Homofermentative organisms ferment one mole of glucose
into two moles of lactate through the Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) glycolytic pathway.
The homofermentation generates a net of two ATP per glucose metabolized which is readily
used for LAB growth [7, 8]. An overview of the homofermentative EMP glycolytic pathway
is shown in Figure 1.

Understanding how L. lactis regulates metabolic flux under varying conditions is crucial, par-
ticularly regarding the formation of intermediate metabolites and the redirection of pyruvate
into alternative products like acetoin. Research has identified a kinetic bottleneck in L. lactis
metabolism at the pyruvate kinase step [10], which leads to the accumulation of upstream
intermediates such as fructose-6-phosphate and fructose-1,6-biphosphate, as well as biomass
precursors dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG). This bot-
tleneck appears to be influenced by factors such as ATP surplus and NAD+ availability. In
addition, overflow at the pyruvate nexus, a critical metabolic intersection where pyruvate
serves as the key intermediate, followed by a glucose pulse, leads to increased acetoin for-
mation. These unique features make L. lactis an excellent model for studying the intricate
balance of metabolic regulation, thermodynamic constraints, and pathway flux, providing
insights that can be applied to both fundamental research and industrial optimization [10].
These metabolic dynamics in L. lactis become even more insightful when compared to those
observed in other microorganisms, such as yeast. Examining these parallels and contrasts
can further elucidate how different organisms adapt their flux patterns in response to sub-
strate composition and metabolic challenges.
The metabolism of non-engineered baker’s yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) and L. lac-
tis shares intriguing parallels and contrasts, particularly in how they respond to substrate
composition and metabolic rerouting strategies. In S. cerevisiae, the use of mixed carbon
substrates such as glucose and pyruvate has been shown to significantly modulate metabolic
fluxes, enhancing the production of valuable compounds like acetoin and 2,3-butanediol.
Similarly, L. lactis demonstrates the ability to redirect carbon flux from lactate to ace-
toin when provided with glucose-pyruvate mixtures [10]. Both organisms exhibit metabolic
plasticity, allowing shifts toward less conventional pathways without requiring genetic mod-
ifications [1].
The addition of pyruvate as an auxiliary substrate plays a pivotal role in
both systems. In S. cerevisiae, pyruvate alters redox states, shifts glycolytic kinetics, and
dramatically enhances the formation of C4 compounds, such as acetoin and 2,3-butanediol,
with 2,3-butanediol levels increasing over 600-fold. In L. lactis, pyruvate similarly facilitates
the rerouting of glycolytic intermediates, enabling acetoin production to compare to that of

lactate under certain conditions and without the need for genetic engineering. The pathway
towards acetoin is illustrated in Figure 2.

These shifts reflect the organisms’ capacity to adapt their metabolic networks to maintain
redox balance and exploit alternative pathways. It is worth noting, that high acetoin con-
centrations causes a disruptive effect in the growth capabilities of L. lactis. The toxicity of
acetoin is due to reactions of the keto group with DNA and proteins and can be directly
seen by monitoring the rate of CO2 accummulation which is a measure of bacterial growth
[12, 13]. Analytical techniques further highlight the nuances of their metabolism. In S.
cerevisiae, dissolution dynamic nuclear polarization (dDNP) NMR and conventional NMR
spectroscopy provide real-time and long-term insights into pathway dynamics, identifying
bottlenecks and tracing carbon flux through various intermediates. While L. lactis studies
also benefit from hyperpolarized NMR, the focus has been on resolving the organisms slower
glycolytic kinetics and its ability to redirect flux at the pyruvate node [1, 10]. Both sys-
tems underscore the potential of optimizing substrate mixtures to drive metabolic changes
comparable to those achieved via genetic engineering. These findings offer a sustainable
and efficient route for producing high-value industrial compounds, leveraging the native ca-
pabilities of these microorganisms. However, L. lactis stands out for its simpler metabolic
framework and industrial relevance in producing dairy-related and probiotic products. To-

2.1.1
An insight into glucose metabolism by various L. lactis strains
It is possible to shift the metabolic flux of wild type L. lactis into valuable metabolites,
such as acetoin, with a substrate engineered approach. However, it is still interesting to
study different L. lactis strains which have either been genetically engineered for specific
end products or have naturally adapted to utilize other substrates.
VJ017, a genetically modified L. lactis strain designed for the production of ace-
toin: A study conducted by Grötering et al. [14] presents an innovative integrated approach
for the production of 2-butanone, a key industrial solvent, by combining biological and elec-
trochemical catalysis. Using genetically modified L. lactis VJ017, the process achieves high
efficiency and sustainability by leveraging renewable resources while minimizing waste and
energy consumption. The process begins with the production of enantiopure acetoin using
L. lactis VJ017. This engineered strain is optimized to direct glucose metabolism exclusively
toward acetoin. In the study they report a remarkable yield of up to 18.3 g L-1 resulting in
90% of the theoretical maximum when utilizing the complex growth medium M17 [14] under
aerobic conditions with a glucose concentration of 40 g L-1. Furthermore, VJ017 may have
acquired some mechanistic traits to tolerate high concentrations of acetoin, which makes
it more suitable for the production of high acetoin titers compared to wild type L. lactis.
These results makes L. lactis VJ017 an interesting candidate for further studies.
L. lactis VJ017 was constructed by Kandasamy et al. in 2016 and the starting point for
constructing this engineered L. lactis strain is to prevent lactate formation in wild type
L. lactis, e.g. through inactivation of the lactate dehydrogenase activity. To maintain a
redox balance L. lactis changes the product formation mainly into formate, ethanol and
acetate, as well as smaller amounts of other products such as butanediol. However, to fully
ensure that the metabolic flux is redirected towards acetoin, inactivation of all the competing
pathways at the pyruvate node is necessary, as illustrated in Figure 3. It is worth noting,

to regenerate NAD+ required for growth. Due to the inactivation of the NAD+ regenerating

ficient, prompting a shift toward microbial fermentation as a more sustainable alternative.
However, achieving high-yield pyruvate production through fermentation presents challenges,
particularly in preventing its further conversion into other metabolites. In the study by Suo

The resulting strain, FS1072, exhibited high pyruvate yield but struggled to grow efficiently,
particularly in defined media. To overcome this, an adaptive evolution experiment was con-
ducted, in which FS1072 was cultivated over multiple generations under selective conditions.
The evolved strain, FS1076, demonstrated significantly improved growth and stability, mak-

FS1076 exhibited
distinct growth characteristics under different conditions.
In rich M17 medium, FS1076
grew efficiently, reaching a high optical density (OD600). Under aerobic conditions, glucose
metabolism in FS1076 was highly efficient, with over 80% of the consumed glucose con-
verted into pyruvate. The strain demonstrated a high glycolytic capacity, and its growth
was strongly influenced by oxygen availability, as oxygen-dependent NADH oxidase (NOX)
played a crucial role in regenerating NAD+ for continuous glycolytic flux [16].
RD06, a genetically modified L. lactis strain designed for the production of
α-acetolactate: In the work by Dorau et al. [17], a significant advancement in the biocat-
alytic production of acetoin was presented, offering an optimized pathway for transforming
fermentation-derived pyruvate into this valuable compound.
The RD06 strain expresses
an efficient α-acetolactate synthase (ALS) derived from Enterococcus faecalis (E. faecalis),
which enhances its catalytic activity [18]. The strain demonstrated superior performance
under controlled anaerobic conditions, achieving high conversion yields while optimizing
growth conditions. RD06 is designed to optimize the biosynthesis of the acetoin precur-
sor, α-acetolactate, which can subsequently be converted into acetoin through controlled
enzymatic processes. Using whole-cell biocatalysis, RD06 offers an additional efficient and
sustainable approach to high-yield acetoin production. RD06 was derived from the L. lactis
chassis strain ll-cat01, which was engineered to eliminate competing pathways that could
divert pyruvate away from α-acetolactate synthesis. The strain harbors a plasmid expressing
ef-ALS from E. faecalis, which exhibits a high affinity for pyruvate. This genetic modifi-
cation resulted in a whole-cell biocatalyst with superior α-acetolactate production capacity.
Temperature, pH, and biomass concentration were identified as critical factors influencing
product stability and yield. RD06 exhibited optimal activity at 20°C, a condition that bal-
anced metabolic activity and prevented excess by-product formation. Higher cell densities

SD96, a naturally abundant citrate-positive L. lactis strain: Citrate-positive L. lac-
tis strains such as SD96 play a crucial role in the fermentation of dairy products, primarily
due to their ability to metabolize citrate and produce key flavor compounds like diacetyl,
acetoin, and acetate. These strains have been extensively studied for their metabolic flexi-
bility, particularly their capacity for citrate and glucose co-metabolism, as described in the
Page 10 of 73
==PAGE:17==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
study by Ramos et al. [21]. Understanding their origin, characteristics, and metabolic path-
ways provides insight into their essential role in dairy fermentation. Citrate-positive L. lactis
strains are predominantly found in dairy environments, particularly in raw milk, fermented
dairy products, and cheese starter cultures. These strains have co-evolved, adapting to utilize
the substrates available in milk, such as lactose, citrate, and casein-derived peptides. The
defining characteristic of citrate-positive strains is their ability to ferment citrate alongside
glucose or lactose. Citrate metabolism in these strains is primarily related to the production
of aroma compounds essential for the development of flavor in dairy products. The citrate
utilization pathway involves:
1. Citrate uptake and cleavage: Citrate is transported into the cell and cleaved by
citrate lyase into oxaloacetate and acetate.
2. Oxaloacetate Decarboxylation: Oxaloacetate is decarboxylated to pyruvate, gen-
erating CO2, which contributes to the eye formation in cheeses like Gouda and Em-
mental.
3. Pyruvate as a Central Intermediate: As previous described L. lactis strains,
pyruvate acts as a metabolic crossroad, being directed toward lactate, acetate, or the
production of flavor compounds like acetoin and diacetyl.
This metabolic adaptability makes citrate-positiveL. lactis strains indispensable in cheese-
making, especially in varieties that require specific aroma profiles and textural characteristics
[22].
The study by Ramos et al. revealed that the co-metabolism of citrate and glucose results
in substantial changes in metabolic fluxes. When glucose is present, citrate metabolism is
significantly stimulated. This interaction occurs because fructose 1,6-bisphosphate, an inter-
mediate in glucose metabolism, activates lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), promoting lactate
production. Citrate consumption is enhanced by the simultaneous presence of glucose which
enables a Shift in Product Formation. In the absence of glucose, citrate metabolism yields
acetoin, acetate, and butanediol. With glucose present, lactate becomes the predominant
product.

2.2
Analytical methods
2.2.1
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy has emerged as an indispensable tool for
chemists, owing to its ability to provide detailed insights into both the structural and dy-
namic properties of molecules. In addition to its traditional applications, NMR has recently
gained prominence in metabolite profiling, where it is used to identify and quantify small
molecules in complex biological samples. One of the most significant advantages of NMR
is its non-invasive and non-destructive nature, which enables the continuous monitoring of
biological systems over time without altering or damaging the sample. This characteristic
makes NMR highly versatile and widely applicable across various scientific disciplines, from
chemistry and biochemistry to material science and medicine.
Despite its versatility, NMR has notable limitations. A primary drawback is its low sensitiv-
ity, as the signal-to-noise ratio is inherently weak, which can make detecting low-abundance
compounds challenging. Additionally, in liquid-state NMR, the technique encounters diffi-
culties with large molecules. As the molecular size increases, the spectra become increasingly
complex due to a greater number of overlapping peaks, and the signal lines broaden, reduc-
ing the resolution. These challenges can complicate the analysis of macromolecules such as
proteins and nucleic acids [23, 24].
Nevertheless, NMR remains a powerful analytical tool because of its many unique strengths
and versatility. It is a quantitative method, capable of producing precise, accurate and re-
producible results. Furthermore, its exceptional resolution and ability to elucidate molecular
structures, combined with its non-invasive and versatile nature, ensure its continued utility
in both research and industrial settings further strengthened by the minimal amount of sam-
ple preparation required.
To fully appreciate the versatile applications of NMR spectroscopy, it is essential to first
understand the fundamental principles behind the technique. A general illustration of how
NMR spectroscopy operates can be seen in Figure 6.

Not all atomic nuclei are NMR-active; only those with a specific property known as "spin"
can interact with the magnetic field in NMR experiments. The spin property arises when the
nucleus has an odd number of protons or neutrons, or both. This results in the nucleus having
a non-zero spin angular momentum (denoted as ⃗S) and consequently a magnetic moment
(⃗µ) when exposed to an external magnetic field. The relationship between nuclear spin and
magnetic moment is directly proportional through a constant called the gyromagnetic ratio
(γ), which varies depending on the type of nucleus. The equation for this relationship is:
⃗µ = γ · ⃗S.
For nuclei with an even number of protons or neutrons, their spins pair up and cancel
Page 13 of 73
==PAGE:20==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
each other out, rendering the nucleus non-NMR active. When no external magnetic field is
applied, the spins of the NMR-active nuclei are oriented randomly, and as a result, the net
magnetization of the sample is zero. Without a net magnetization, the spins do not precess
(rotate) around any magnetic field. However, when an external magnetic field (denoted as B)
is introduced, it interacts with the spins of the NMR-active nuclei, causing them to align with
the applied field. This alignment of the spins creates a net magnetization in the direction of
the magnetic field, which can then be detected and analyzed by NMR spectroscopy.
This fundamental behavior of atomic nuclei in the presence of an external magnetic field
forms the basis of NMR spectroscopy, enabling scientists to probe molecular structures and
dynamics in detail. During an NMR measurement, nuclear spins can align either slightly with
or slightly against the applied magnetic field (B0). The measured spin eigenstate aligned
with the field (α) has a lower energy, while the state opposing the field (β) has a higher
energy. Because it is energetically more favorable for spins to align with the magnetic field,
a slight excess of nuclei will occupy the lower-energy α state. This imbalance results in a
net magnetization vector aligned with the external field along the z-axis. In addition to
aligning with the field, the nuclear spins also precess around the direction of B0 at a specific
frequency known as the Larmor frequency (νL), which is given by:
νL = γ · B0
2π
where γ is the gyromagnetic ratio of the nucleus. The build-up of this longitudinal magneti-
zation (along the z-axis) follows an exponential process, characterized by the relaxation time
constant T1 (spin-lattice relaxation time). To obtain an NMR signal, the system is excited
using an electromagnetic pulse in the radio frequency (RF) range. When an RF pulse is
applied at the Larmor frequency for a sufficient duration, it disturbs the equilibrium magne-
tization and shifts it away from alignment with B0. If the pulse is strong enough to rotate the
net magnetization by 90°, the magnetization moves from the z-axis to the transverse plane
(the xy-plane). This rotation occurs due to resonance between the Larmor frequency and
the applied RF energy. After excitation, the magnetization gradually returns to equilibrium.

This recovery process involves two key relaxation mechanisms:
1. Transverse relaxation ( with time constant T2) - The loss of detectable mag-
netization in the xy-plane due to dephasing of individual spins, which leads to signal
decay.
2. Longitudinal relaxation (with time constant T1) - The realignment of net mag-
netization back along the z-axis as spins return to their lower-energy states.
The loss of transverse magnetization produces an oscillating signal, known as free induction
decay (FID), which is detected by a receiving coil. The FID signal is then processed using
a Fourier transform (FT) to generate an NMR spectrum, revealing information about the
molecular environment of the nuclei. Repeating the experiment multiple times allows signal
accumulation, improving the signal-to-noise ratio and enhancing spectral resolution [26, 27].
2.2.2
1H NMR and 13C NMR Spectroscopy
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is a powerful analytical tool widely used
in chemistry and biochemistry for the elucidation of molecular structures. Among the vari-
ous NMR techniques, proton NMR (1H NMR) and carbon-13 NMR (13C NMR) are the most
commonly employed due to the abundance of CH groups in organic molecules and their abil-
ity to provide detailed information about molecular frameworks and electronic environments.
While both techniques are based on the same fundamental principles, they differ in terms of
sensitivity, spectral complexity, and the type of molecular information they provide.
1H NMR spectroscopy examines the magnetic properties of hydrogen nuclei, which are the
most abundant and NMR-active nuclei in organic compounds. The fundamental principle
of 1H NMR is based on the interaction of nuclear spins with an external magnetic field as
explained in the previous section. The position of proton signals in an NMR spectrum is
determined by the chemical shift (δ, in ppm), which reflects the electronic environment of
the nucleus. Shielded protons, which experience a higher electron density, resonate at lower
δ values, whereas deshielded protons, often located near electronegative atoms or π-systems,
Page 15 of 73
==PAGE:22==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
resonate at higher δ values. Typical chemical shift ranges include 0.5-2.0 ppm for alkane pro-
tons, 4.5-6.5 ppm for alkene protons, 6.5-8.5 ppm for aromatic protons, and 9.0-10.0 ppm for
aldehyde protons [28]. Another key feature of 1H NMR spectroscopy is spin-spin coupling,
which occurs when nonequivalent protons interact through chemical bonds. This interac-
tion leads to signal splitting, forming characteristic multiplets such as doublets, triplets,
and quartets, with the pattern determined by the n+1 rule, where a proton coupled to n
protons with the same coupling appears as n+1 peaks. The coupling constant (J, in Hz)
provides valuable information about molecular conformation and connectivity. Additionally,
peak integration is directly proportional to the number of protons contributing to a given
signal, allowing for quantitative analysis.
1H NMR spectroscopy is widely used in organic
and medicinal chemistry for structure elucidation, reaction monitoring, purity assessment,
and metabolic profiling. Exchangeable protons, such as those in hydroxyl (-OH) and amine
(-NH) groups, can undergo rapid proton exchange with solvent molecules, leading to broad-
ened or absent signals, an effect that can be confirmed by deuterium exchange experiments
using D2O.
13C NMR, in contrast to 1H NMR, focuses on the carbon-13 isotope, which has a natural
abundance of only 1.1%. Due to this low abundance, 13C NMR signals are significantly
weaker than those observed in 1H NMR, requiring longer acquisition times and signal av-
eraging. Nevertheless, 13C NMR provides crucial information about the carbon framework
of a molecule, making it an essential complementary technique. The chemical shift range in
13C NMR is broader (0-220 ppm) compared to 1H NMR, reflecting the greater variability
in electronic environments around carbon atoms. Aliphatic sp3-hybridized carbons typically
appear between 0 and 50 ppm, sp2-hybridized carbons in alkenes and aromatic rings resonate
between 100 and 160 ppm, while carbonyl carbons appear at much higher shifts, typically
between 160 and 220 ppm. The large dispersion of chemical shifts allows for clearer spectral
interpretation, as signals are generally well-separated. Unlike 1H NMR, where J-coupling
results in complex multiplets, 13C NMR spectra are often recorded using broadband proton
decoupling, which removes proton-carbon coupling effects, leading to the appearance of sin-
glets for each distinct carbon environment. However, in coupled 13C NMR spectra, splitting
Page 16 of 73
==PAGE:23==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
patterns such as doublets, triplets, and quartets can be observed, providing additional struc-
tural information [26, 29].
Both 1H NMR and 13C NMR play crucial roles in molecular structure determination, but
they serve distinct purposes. 1H NMR provides detailed information about hydrogen envi-
ronments, proton-proton interactions, and molecular symmetry, while 13C NMR focuses on
the carbon skeleton, offering a broader chemical shift range and a clearer distinction between
different functional groups. Although 13C NMR is less sensitive due to the low natural abun-
dance of 13C, its ability to reveal carbon connectivity and electronic environments makes it
an invaluable tool in structural elucidation. The combination of 1H NMR and 13C NMR
is widely employed in organic synthesis, natural product characterization, pharmaceutical
analysis, and materials science. In drug discovery, these techniques are essential for verifying
molecular structures, assessing compound purity, and studying conformational changes. In
metabolomics, NMR-based profiling of biofluids provides insights into metabolic pathways
and disease biomarkers. The non-destructive nature of NMR spectroscopy further enables
real-time monitoring of chemical reactions, making it a versatile tool across various scien-
tific disciplines. Furthermore, the continuous development of NMR methodologies, including
multidimensional NMR and hyperpolarization techniques, continues to expand the scope and
sensitivity of these powerful spectroscopic tools [30, 31].
2.2.3
Dynamic Nuclear Polarization
Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (DNP) NMR is a groundbreaking technique that enhances the
sensitivity of traditional NMR spectroscopy by orders of magnitude (>10,000 fold), enabling
the detection of low-concentration species and real-time observation of biochemical processes.
The fundamental limitation of conventional NMR lies in the weak thermal polarization
of nuclear spins, which results in an inherently low signal intensity. DNP overcomes this
challenge by transferring the high polarization of unpaired electron spins to nuclear spins,
significantly boosting the NMR signal [10, 29].
The underlying mechanism of DNP is based on the interaction between electron and nuclear

spins, facilitated by microwave irradiation at cryogenic temperatures. Electrons possess a
much larger gyromagnetic ratio compared to nuclei, and their polarization can be efficiently
transferred through several established DNP mechanisms. In conventional NMR the low
sensitivity stems from the relatively small gyromagnetic ratio of the nuclei.
The small
gyromagnetic ratio results in a small population difference between the α and β spin states,

uses this effect by introducing stable radicals (unpaired electrons with high γ). Irradiating
the sample with microwaves at low temperatures transfers electron magnetization to nearby
nuclei, creating a highly polarized state with more spins aligned to the magnetic field. This
results in stronger NMR signals. However, the enhanced polarization is temporary, last-
ing only seconds to minutes, as it decays with the nuclear relaxation time (T1). The most
prominent DNP mechanisms include the Solid Effect, which involves direct two-spin flipping
of electron and nuclear spins; the Overhauser Effect, which operates in liquid systems and
leverages dipolar interactions; Thermal Mixing, which is prevalent in highly electron-dense
systems; and the Cross Effect, which is particularly effective in modern DNP applications
and involves two electron spins interacting with a nuclear spin [32].
Polarization techniques can be broadly categorized into two main approaches: dissolution
DNP (dDNP) and ParaHydrogen Induced Polarization (PHIP). In dDNP, the sample is
mixed with a radical-containing polarizing agent (i.e. organic trityl radicals) and cooled to
∼1 K in a strong magnetic field [29]. Microwave irradiation then induces polarization trans-
fer, after which the sample is rapidly dissolved in a heated solvent to create a hyperpolarized
solution that can be injected into an NMR or MRI system for real-time metabolic imaging.
dDNP has revolutionized metabolic imaging by allowing the study of biochemical pathways

==PAGE:25==

in live cells and organisms. One significant application of dDNP is in the hyperpolarization
of 13C-labeled glucose, which provides insight into glycolysis and cellular energy metabolism.
Since glucose is central to cellular metabolism, it is an ideal target for hyperpolarization.
However, hyperpolarized [1-13C]glucose presents unique challenges due to its short T1 re-
laxation time (typically <1 second) [33]. To extend its usability, deuteration (replacement
of hydrogen with deuterium) has been employed, significantly increasing the T1 time and
allowing for more effective metabolic tracking. Hyperpolarized glucose has been successfully
used in multiple biological systems. In a study of bacterial metabolism, dDNP has been
used to monitor glycolysis in L. lactis, revealing kinetic barriers at key enzymatic steps [10].
dDNP has also been used for cancer imaging, where hyperpolarized glucose provides a direct
measure of glycolytic flux, making it a potential tool for assessing tumor metabolism and
treatment response, and in neurological applications where hyperpolarized glucose is being
explored as an alternative to 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) PET imaging for studying brain
metabolism due to its non-radioactive nature [34].
Despite its transformative potential, DNP NMR faces several challenges. The short-lived na-
ture of hyperpolarization (seconds to minutes) necessitates rapid data acquisition and limits
the timeframe for observing slow biochemical processes. Additionally, the high cost and
infrastructure requirements, including specialized cryogenic cooling and microwave systems,
restrict its widespread adoption. Research efforts are currently focused on developing long-
lived hyperpolarized probes, miniaturizing DNP polarizers, and expanding its applications
to clinical diagnostics, environmental sciences, and drug discovery.
In conclusion, Dynamic Nuclear Polarization has revolutionized NMR spectroscopy by sig-
nificantly enhancing sensitivity, enabling real-time metabolic imaging, and providing new
insights into biological and chemical systems. As technological advancements continue to
refine the method, DNP NMR is assured to become an indispensable tool across multiple sci-
entific disciplines, bridging the gap between molecular analysis and real-world applications.

Discussion
The results of this study provide detailed insights into the metabolic behavior of different
Lactococcus lactis strains, both wild-type and genetically modified, under various growth
conditions. These findings align with established literature while also highlighting new as-
pects of metabolic regulation, particularly in response to external substrates such as pyruvate
and citrate. By comparing the experimental data with prior research, it becomes possible
to understand how metabolic flux in L. lactis can be optimized for biotechnological applica-
tions, particularly in the production of valuable metabolites such as acetoin.
The wild-type strains MG1363 and SD96 followed a classic homofermentative metabolic
pathway, in which glucose was predominantly converted into lactate. The observed results
confirm well-documented findings in the literature, which state that L. lactis primarily uti-
lizes the Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas (EMP) glycolytic pathway to generate ATP and produce
lactate as the main fermentation product. Previous research has identified that homofermen-
tation in L. lactis results in the net production of two ATP molecules per glucose molecule
consumed, making it an efficient pathway for energy generation and bacterial growth. In
the current study, the MG1363 strain showed typical growth and metabolic patterns, with
an OD600 value of 1.6 after 16 hours of anaerobic incubation. The presence of lactate as the
predominant product was further confirmed through NMR spectroscopy. Similarly, SD96, a
citrate-positive strain, also exhibited homofermentative metabolism under standard condi-
tions, with a preference for lactate production.
The addition of external pyruvate significantly influenced the metabolic flux of both MG1363
and SD96. The literature describes pyruvate as a critical metabolic intersection that can be
redirected toward different pathways depending on enzymatic availability and external con-
ditions. In this study, external pyruvate at concentrations up to 300 mM enhanced bacterial
growth in MG1363, with OD600 values increasing to 2.2, while 500 mM had an inhibitory
effect. This trend is consistent with previous reports that suggest high concentrations of
pyruvate can induce acid stress, leading to impaired cell growth. Furthermore, NMR analy-
Page 48 of 73
==PAGE:55==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
sis demonstrated that at 300 mM pyruvate, there was a noticeable shift in metabolic output,
favoring the production of acetoin in addition to lactate. This observation aligns with ex-
isting studies showing that unbalanced carbon flux at the pyruvate kinase step can lead to
the accumulation of intermediates and redirect metabolism toward alternative fermentation
pathways, such as the formation of acetoin and diacetyl.
SD96 exhibited a similar response to external pyruvate, with increased acetoin production
observed at higher concentrations.
This observation is consistent with literature reports
showing that citrate metabolism in L. lactis enhances glucose uptake and subsequently
stimulates metabolic shifts under certain conditions. In previous studies, citrate-positive L.
lactis strains have been shown to co-metabolize citrate and glucose, leading to increased flux
toward flavor compounds such as acetoin and diacetyl. This was partially observed in the
current study, where higher pyruvate concentrations resulted in a metabolic shift in SD96.
However, lactate remained the dominant fermentation product, emphasizing the robustness
of the homofermentative pathway.
The genetically engineered strains VJ017, FS1076, and RD06 displayed distinct metabolic
characteristics that aligned with their intended metabolic modifications. VJ017, which was
designed for acetoin production, demonstrated an almost exclusive conversion of glucose
into acetoin. However, unlike the wild-type strains, VJ017 exhibited very limited growth
under anaerobic conditions. This behavior is consistent with literature findings that indi-
cate acetoin-producing L. lactis strains rely on NADH oxidase (NOX) activity to regenerate
NAD+ under aerobic conditions [7]. The results confirmed this, as VJ017 reached an OD600
of 4.2 aerobically, while anaerobic growth was minimal. The addition of pyruvate and citrate
did not significantly alter its metabolic behavior, further reinforcing literature reports that
suggest the metabolic flux in such engineered strains is tightly controlled to ensure selective
acetoin synthesis.
The pyruvate-producing FS1076 strain efficiently converted glucose into pyruvate, but the
yield was only about 27% of the theoretical maximum when all glucose is consumed. This ob-
servation aligns with previous studies indicating that engineered pyruvate-producing strains
Page 49 of 73
==PAGE:56==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
often suffer from carbon loss due to residual activity in competing pathways, such as lactate
or acetate formation [15]. The metabolic flux in FS1076 was further analyzed under dif-
ferent conditions, and while citrate addition was tested as a potential stimulant for glucose
uptake, it did not significantly enhance pyruvate yield. This observation suggested that
the metabolic network in FS1076 remains constrained, likely due to bottlenecks in enzyme
activity and regulatory feedback mechanisms, as described in the literature.
RD06, a strain engineered for α-acetolactate production, unexpectedly produced high levels
of acetoin instead. Literature findings suggest that α-acetolactate undergoes spontaneous de-
carboxylation to acetoin, particularly under conditions of increased temperature and acidic
pH [17]. This was likely the case in this study, where RD06 was incubated at 30°C, differing
from previous studies where α-acetolactate accumulation was maximized at 20°C. This high-
lights the importance of environmental conditions in regulating metabolic flux in engineered
strains and suggests that fine-tuning growth parameters could help achieve the desired prod-
uct specificity in RD06.
Real-time NMR experiments provided additional insights into metabolic flux by allowing for
direct observation of glycolysis in L. lactis. In conventional 13C-NMR, MG1363 and SD96
displayed clear lactate accumulation, confirming their homofermentative nature. However,
genetically modified strains such as VJ017, FS1076, and RD06 showed minimal activity in
conventional NMR but exhibited distinct metabolic shifts when analyzed using dDNP NMR.
This behavior suggests that oxygen availability and kinetic bottlenecks significantly influ-
ence metabolic activity in engineered strains. The literature describes pyruvate kinase as
a key regulatory step in L. lactis metabolism, and the observed kinetic barriers in dDNP
NMR align with previous findings that indicate metabolic control points at dihydroxyace-
tone phosphate (DHAP), 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG), and pyruvate.
Overall, the experimental findings in this study align closely with established literature on
L. lactis metabolism. The wild-type strains followed classical homofermentative pathways,
while genetic modifications successfully redirected carbon flux toward acetoin or pyruvate
production. However, the results also highlight the metabolic constraints faced by engineered
Page 50 of 73
==PAGE:57==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
strains, particularly in terms of oxygen dependence and bottlenecks of carbon flux in central
metabolism. The use of NMR techniques further reinforced literature claims on glycolytic
regulation, providing real-time metabolic insights that could be applied to industrial biopro-
cess optimization. Future work should focus on fine-tuning metabolic conditions to enhance
product yields and mitigate the observed limitations in genetically engineered strains. By
combining metabolic engineering with advanced analytical tools such as real-time NMR, it
may be possible to further optimize L. lactis strains for industrial applications in sustainable
biotechnology.

Conclusion
This study investigated the metabolic regulation and optimization of Lactococcus lactis
strains, both wild-type and genetically modified, for their potential in biotechnological ap-
plications. The research focused on the glucose metabolism of five strains under various con-
ditions and employed Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, including Dynamic
Nuclear Polarization (DNP), to analyze metabolic fluxes in real time. The findings confirm
the expected homofermentative nature of wild-type strains MG1363 and SD96, with lactate
as the primary fermentation product. The genetically modified strains (VJ017, FS1076, and
RD06) successfully redirected the carbon flux though with varying efficiencies and metabolic
constraints.
A key outcome was the discovery that environmental factors, such as oxygen availability
and substrate concentration, significantly impact the metabolic behavior of modified strains.
VJ017 exhibited robust acetoin production under aerobic conditions but struggled to grow
anaerobically due to NAD+ regeneration limitations, a trait shared by all three modified
strains. FS1076 effectively accumulated pyruvate, though its yield remained below theo-
retical expectations, indicating room for growth optimization. RD06, expected to produce
α-acetolactate, instead predominantly formed acetoin suggesting spontaneous decarboxyla-
tion and highlighting the importance of fine-tuned growth conditions.
Furthermore, real-time dDNP NMR spectroscopy provided novel insights into metabolic in-
termediates and flux regulation, underscoring its potential as a powerful tool in metabolic
engineering. The study’s findings align with existing literature but also reveal new opportuni-
ties to optimize metabolic pathways for sustainable biochemical production. Future research
should focus on refining environmental conditions and genetic modifications to enhance yield
efficiencies and metabolic stability, paving the way for industrial-scale applications of L. lac-
tis in green chemistry and biotechnology.

Experimental
6.1
Materials
Wild type L. lactis MG1363 was provided by DTU health and SD96 as well as modified
L. lactis strains (VJ017, FS1076 and RD06) were provided by the Peter Ruhdal group
(DTU Food). Reagents, buffers, and salts were purchased from commercial suppliers. High
purity water (Milli-Q) was obtained by filtering deionized water through a commercial water
purification system (Merck Millipore Synergy UV) and used for the preparation of all aqueous
solutions. 5 mm NMR tubes were used for conventional 1H- and 13C NMR and a Shigemi
tube was used for D-DNP NMR measurements.
6.2
Instrument and Conventional NMR experiments
Conventional NMR experiments were acquired at 298 K for 1H NMR or 303 K for 13C
NMR on a Bruker 500 MHz NMR instrument equipped with a 5 mm DCH Cryoprobe and a
Sample-Jet sample changer. Each FID for the acquisition of 1H NMR spectra was sampled
by acquiring 16384 complex data points during an acquisition time of 1.7 s. Shimming,
tuning and matching as well as pulse calibration were conducted on a dummy sample. All
1H NMR spectra were acquired with a spectral width of 20.8 ppm. The phosphate buffer
(20 mL, 100 mM, pH 7.4) was prepared by dissolving DSS (2 mg, 9.16 µmol), maleic acid
(4.8 mg, 41.37 µmol) and monosodium phosphate (1.68 g, 14 mmol) in 12 mL D2O and 8 mL
H2O. pH was adjusted to 7.4 and H2O was added to reach a final volume of 20 mL.
For the time series of one-dimensional 13C spectra, the FID was sampled by acquiring 11,264
complex data points during an acquisition time of 345 milliseconds. The time series was
implemented as a pseudo-2D NMR experiment acquiring a 2D NMR data set consisting of
a series of 1D NMR spectra in real time. Similar to 1H NMR acquisition, shimming, tuning
and matching as well as pulse calibration were conducted on a dummy sample in the absence
of isotope-labeled glucose. Experiments commenced by mixing 6 mg [U-13C, 1H] glucose in
50 µL D2O with 330 µL cell suspension in 30 mM MES buffer at pH 5.6. The time between
Page 53 of 73
==PAGE:60==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
mixing glucose and cell suspension was approximately 2 min for each experiment. All NMR
spectra were processed with ample zero filling in all dimensions and baseline correction using
Bruker TopSpin 4.1.4 or MestReNova 15.0.1.
6.3
D-DNP NMR procedure
Hyperpolarization of [U-13C, 2H] glucose was conducted by Assoc. professor Pernille Rose
Jensen or Senior Researcher Per Magnus Albin Karlsson on a Hypersense polarizer operating
at 3.35 T (Oxford Instruments, UK). After ∼60 min of polarization, a solid-state polarization
of 30% was obtained and the sample was dissolved in 5 mL MES buffer. 200 µL Cell suspen-
sions in 30 mM MES buffer (pH 5.6) were placed into the 500 MHz Bruker spectrometer and
250 µL hyperpolarized [U-13C, 2H] glucose was injected into the sample. The 13C spectra
was acquired with 11,264 complex data points.
6.4
Substrate mixture preparations
40% w/v D-(+)-glucose stock solutions were prepared by dissolving glucose (16 g, 89 mmol)
to 40 mL in Milli-Q H2O. The solution was filtered on a 0.2 µm sterile syringe filter. 1%
glucose M17 growth medium was prepared in 50 mL falcon tubes by mixing 1 mL 40%
glucose stock solution in 39 mL M17.
1 M citrate and 1 M pyruvate stock solutions were prepared by dissolving sodium citrate
(11.8 g, 62 mmol) or sodium pyruvate (4.4 g, 40 mmol) to 40 mL in a 1% D-glucose M17
mixture. The stock solutions were filtered on 0.2 µm sterile syringe filters.
6.4.1
Glucose/pyruvate substrate mixtures
Pyruvate stock solution (1 M) was mixed in four different concentrations (0, 100, 300 and
500 mM) with 1% glucose M17 growth medium reaching a total volume of 1.8 mL for the
anaerobic experiments or 500 µL for the aerobic experiments.

==PAGE:61==
DTU Chemistry & Health Technology
6.4.2
Glucose/citrate substrate mixtures
Citrate stock solution (1 M) was mixed in six different concentrations (0, 10, 20, 40, 60 and
100 mM) with 1% glucose M17 growth medium reaching a total volume of 1.8 mL for the
anaerobic experiments or 500 µL for the aerobic experiments.
6.4.3
FS1076 co-culture procedure
The pyruvate producing FS1076 strain was grown aerobically, as described previously, in
M17 growth medium (1% glucose). The supernatant was recovered after 16 h of incubation
(30 °C, 120 rpm). The pH was adjusted to 6.7 and glucose was added to 1% generating a
pyruvate rich fermentation broth (PM17) resembling M17. Wild type MG1363 L. lactis was
subsequently inoculated in the generated PM17 growth medium and incubated ON for 16 h
under anaerobic conditions (30 °C, 120 rpm). The supernatant was recovered as previously
described and the metabolites produced were analyzed by 1H NMR spectroscopy.
6.5
Bacterial growth procedures for real-time NMR analysis
Two different procedures were followed for the growth of L. lactis prior to subsequent real-
time NMR analysis. (i) OD600 was measured in ON cultures and OD600 = 0.05 was diluted
to 45 mL. The cultures were incubated until OD600 = 0.5 (30 °C, 120 rpm) indicating an
exponential state. OD600 = 15 was harvested from the cell suspension. (ii) OD600 = 15 was
harvested directly from an ON culture with OD600 values indicating a resting state. For both
procedures, the cell suspensions were centrifuged (5000 rpm, 5 min), and the supernatants
were discarded. The cell pellet was washed with 5 mL MES buffer, diluted with 300 µL
MES. 6 mg [U-13C, 1H] glucose dissolved in 50 µL D2O was added to the cell suspension at
the spectrometer and the acquisition was started.
