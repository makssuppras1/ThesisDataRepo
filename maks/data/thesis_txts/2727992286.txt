Abstract
The Arctic region is experiencing the consequences of climate change at an alarming
rate, with impacts far more severe than other parts of the globe. Rising atmospheric
and ocean temperatures have led to the decline of sea ice and increased ocean fresh-
ening. These direct effects of climate change give rise to the indirect consequence of
crude oil pollution, driven by intensified shipping and fossil resource extraction ac-
tivities. Such changes are now affecting marine ecosystems, including the copepods
species Calanus finmarchicus and Calanus glacialis, which play vital roles as a link
between primary production and higher trophic levels of the Arctic food web. While
previous studies have investigated the individual effects of rising temperatures and
crude oil pollution, as wells as the combined impact of increasing temperatures and
pyrene pollution, little is known about the influence of salinity changes on the two
copepod species. Furthermore, to date, no studies have investigated the combined
effect of all three stressors. This study aims to fill this knowledge gap by examining
the response of C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis from Disko Bay, West Greenland,
to increasing temperatures, decreasing salinities, and crude oil pollution. The two
species were exposed to six different treatments, comprising two temperatures (0 or
5°C), three salinities (33, 27 or 20 PSU) and crude oil (Light Louisiana Sweet oil, 1
µL L-1) for six days. The six treatments included: control (33 PSU, 0°C), ambient +
oil (33 PSU, 0°C, oil), scenario 1 (27 PSU, 5°C), scenario 1 + oil (27 PSU, 5°C, oil),
scenario 2 (20 PSU, 5°C), and scenario 2 + oil (20 PSU, 5°C, oil). The daily fecal
pellet production (FPP) was assessed, along with the specific pellet production
(SPP), fecal pellet volume (VFP) and FP carbon content (CFP). Moreover, the
prosome length (PL), female carbon content (CFEM), lipid sac area (LSA) and total
lipid content (TL) of the females were measured before and after the exposure,
and respiration rates were measured following the exposure period. Additionally, a
chemical analysis was conducted assessing the composition and concentration of
the crude oil compounds in the exposure water. The main findings of the study
revealed a significant adverse effect of crude oil exposure on the FPP of both species,
causing a decrease of 67-83% for C. finmarchicus and 70-77% for C. glacialis. In
terms of respiration rates, C. glacialis generally exhibited higher rates compared
to C. finmarchicus, though specific conclusions of differences between treatments
could not be drawn. The chemical analysis of the exposure water identified a total
of 32 compounds, including polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), alkylated
PAHs and dibenzothiophenes (DBTs). The predominant compound detected in the
crude oil was Naphthalene at 650 ng L-1. The decrease in FPP was hypothesized
to result from narcosis caused by one or more compounds present in the crude oil.
Although the concentrations of the compounds were generally lower than those
reported in other studies, the possibility of toxicity induced by a cocktail effect
remains a concern. This study emphasizes the vulnerability of Arctic copepods to
the combined stressors of climate change and crude oil pollution. It highlights the

necessity for proactive environmental conservation strategies in the Arctic marine
ecosystem to safeguard these crucial species of the delicate Arctic food web.

Introduction
1.1
Arctic Climate Change
Arctic marine ecosystems suffer from the consequences of climate change. Although,
climate change is a global concern, polar regions of the world are experiencing the
effects far more severely than the rest of the globe. During the past 49 years, the
atmospheric temperature in the Arctic has increased three times faster than the
global average, which has caused, and continues to cause rapid and widespread
changes to the physical environment in the Arctic, such as melting sea ice and land
ice, thawing of permafrost and changing weather patterns (AMAP, 2021; Carvalho
& Wang, 2020; Meredith et al., 2019). This phenomenon is also referred to as
the Arctic Amplification (AA), which is caused by different forcing mechanisms
such as the ice-albedo feedback mechanism.
Here the reduction in ice-cover,
resulting from increased temperatures, triggers a positive feedback loop where
greater heat absorption occurs from the sun to the exposed ground or sea where the
ice normally would have reflected the heat back to the atmosphere. Consequently,
the temperature of the sea and ground is further elevated, additionally accelerating
the ice melting. Figure 1.1 shows the cumulative mass balance of Arctic land ice
between 1971 and 2019, and its impact on sea-level rise through melting. During
the 49-year period, Greenland has experienced the most significant loss of land
ice, accounting for 51% of the total loss in the Arctic. This makes Greenland
a large contributor to ocean freshening and sea-level rise (AMAP, 2021; Serreze
et al., 2009; Smith et al., 2019; Stuecker et al., 2018). Besides the atmosphere,
the temperature of the Arctic Ocean is warming more than two times faster than
the rest of the globe. This is caused by the process of Arctic borealization, a
combination of anomalous inflow of warmer water from the North Atlantic Ocean
into the deeper Arctic Atlantic water layer (∼150-900 meters) (atlantification) and
anomalous inflow of Pacific water (pacification). This heat inflow is outweighing the
heat loss from the sea surface causing a net heating of the Arctic Ocean (Polyakov
et al., 2020; Shu et al., 2022). The surface temperature of the Arctic Ocean during
summer has shown an increase of 0.5°C per decade between 1982 and 2017, and in
western Greenland temperatures have been observed to be as high as 13°C (2010).
In contrast winter surface temperature typically ranges between -1°C to -2°C, but
deeper water layers increase to above 0°C and typically around 2°C due to inflow
of North Atlantic waters. The warming of the Atlantic Water layer began in the
1970s (Kjellerup et al., 2012; Meredith et al., 2019; Polyakov et al., 2020; Riisgaard
et al., 2014; Sejr et al., 2017).

1.1.1
Ocean Freshening
An increased inflow of freshwater to the Arctic Ocean has also been shown during
the past two decades and is predicted to increase further under a business-as-usual
scenario for greenhouse gas emissions. The freshwater inflow is caused by increased
river runoff, precipitation, land ice melting and declining sea ice (Bamber et al.,
2018; Fu et al., 2020; Jahn & Laiho, 2020; Pierce et al., 2012). The increased
freshwater budget changes the salinity in surface layers and enhances stratification.
This affects ocean circulation, weakens vertical mixing, and suppresses the supply
of nutrients from deeper layers to the surface.
During the winter months in
Greenlandic coastal waters, the upper 100 meters of the water column typically
exhibit thorough mixing, resulting in a consistent salinity of 33 PSU (Dünweber
et al., 2010; M. O. Hansen et al., 2012; Kjellerup et al., 2012). During the summer
season, surface salinities in the vicinity of glaciers has been observed to vary from
25 or 30 PSU to as low as 10 PSU (Calbet et al., 2011; Middelbo et al., 2018;
Riisgaard et al., 2014; Sejr et al., 2017).
The three largest outlet glaciers in
Greenland; Helheim and Kangerdlugssuaq glaciers on the southern east coast and
Ilulissat glacier on the west coast are the most rapidly declining glaciers. However,
northeast Greenlandic glaciers have also been observed to decline with reported
subsurface (>30 m) salinity drops from around 33 to below 32 between 2003 and
2015, partly attributed to the melting glaciers in this region (Khan et al., 2014;
Sejr et al., 2017). On the other hand, when the ice freezes during winter, salt is
excreted as the ice forms, creating a layer of high salinity just below the ice. This
occurrence is also called brine formation and can create a salinity as high as 50
PSU underneath the ice and 90 PSU within the brine channels in the ice (Carmack
et al., 2016; Fu et al., 2020; Grant & Horner, 1976).

1.1.2
Declining Sea Ice: Shipping and Resource Extraction
Opportunities
The amplified warming of the Arctic has caused an extensive annual loss in sea ice
observed through all seasons, and accelerating within the past few decades (Halfar
et al., 2013; Kinnard et al., 2011; Onarheim et al., 2018). Moreover, the percentage
of multiyear ice (ice that has survived at least one melt season) is declining, as well
as the ice thickness, and predictions say that the Arctic will experience sea ice free
summers before 2050. Figure 1.2 shows the sea ice thickness and extent between
1971 and 2019 in the Arctic region (AMAP, 2021; Kwok & Rothrock, 2009; Maslanik
et al., 2011; M. Wang & Overland, 2009, 2012). The declining sea ice in the Arctic
Ocean has opened opportunities for new shipping routes, capturing the attention
of the maritime shipping industry. By using the shorter Northern Sea Route (NSR)
instead of the Suez Canal (Figure 1.3) there is potential to significantly reduce the
distance between Europe and Asia by approximately 40%, cut fuel consumption
by more than 50% and lower CO2 emissions by 49-78% in shipping operations.
However, with the potential increase in shipping activity through Arctic regions,
there comes an associated elevated risk of pollution from these ships, including oil
spills (Aksenov et al., 2017; Schøyen & Bråthen, 2011). Alongside these advantages
of shifting routes, there are also notable disadvantages to consider. Safety is a
critical factor when selecting shipping route, and the NSR poses risks due to harsher
weather and free-floating sea ice. To navigate such challenging conditions, vessels
with higher safety standards and ice-classification are required, leading to elevated
financial costs of operations. Moreover, adverse weather could cause navigation
difficulties, slower speeds, variable shipping schedules, and higher expenses for
icebreaker services. Additionally, Arctic regions tend to be more remote and less
populated, which limits the capacity for search and rescue operations (Aksenov
et al., 2017; Buixadé Farré et al., 2014). Accordingly, there are both advantages
and disadvantages of conducting shipping routes through the Arctic. However,
one undeniable fact is that the annual retreat of ice is making these shipping
operations increasingly feasible. Additionally, the loss of sea ice makes the Arctic
a progressively interesting region for resource extraction due to the estimated
abundant petroleum reserves. These reserves were previously inaccessible due to
the ice cover. Ultimately, the combined potential for increased shipping and energy
resource extraction in the Arctic raises concern about an elevated risk of crude oil
pollution, potentially putting strain on the marine ecosystem (Gautier et al., 2009;
Kalter & Passow, 2023).

1.2
Arctic Copepods
The combined effect of rising temperatures, dropping salinities, and oil pollution
represents potential stressors on planktonic organisms in the Arctic Ocean. The
zooplankton genus Calanus, serves as a vital link between primary production and
higher trophic levels within Arctic and subarctic ecosystems (Madsen et al., 2001;
T. Nielsen & Hansen, 1995). Thus, understanding the responses of planktonic
organisms to these stressors is crucial for anticipating future changes in Arctic food
webs. The species Calanus finmarchicus (Gunnerus, 1765) and Calanus glacialis
(Jaschnov, 1955) are two of the most abundant zooplankton species in the Arctic
Ocean in terms of biomass. The two species co-exists in areas of the Arctic Ocean
where Atlantic and Arctic water masses meet. C. finmarchicus originates from the
open waters of the boreal North Atlantic region and was introduced to the Arctic
through advection of Atlantic water masses before the 1980s. Subsequently, its
habitat has expanded northwards due to the greater intrusion of warm Atlantic
currents and the retreat of seasonal sea-ice. C. glacialis on the other hand is native
to the Arctic (Carstensen et al., 2019; R. Conover & Huntley, 1991; Falk-Petersen
et al., 2009; Jaschnov, 1970; T. Nielsen & Hansen, 1995; Tarling et al., 2022).
Various model simulations have provided insight into how climate change may
affect the distribution of the two species. Slagstad et al. (2011) demonstrated
that under a scenario of an 8°C temperature increase, C. glacialis would shift
its distribution northwards and almost disappear from the Northern Barents Sea,
Baffin Bay and western Fram Strait, with C. finmarchicus becoming the dominant
mesozooplankton species in the Barents Sea (Figure 1.4) (Slagstad et al., 2011). In
contrast, Chust et al. (2014) found that C. finmarchicus would consistently shift
poleward, while C. glacialis would shift slightly southward due to the outflow of
cool freshwater from the Arctic into the Labrador Current (Chust et al., 2014).
Additionally, Feng et al. (2018) demonstrated that C. glacialis would expand its
range northwards rather than shift as Slagstad et al., 2011 indicated (Feng et al.,
2018). Despite yielding different results, these studies collectively suggest potential
changes in distribution of the two species in response to climate change.
Calanus spp.
are herbivores primarily grazing on phytoplankton which they
consume through water filtration. In Arctic waters the abundance of phytoplankton
changes through the year. During winter, limited daylight blocked by sea ice results
in a minimal phytoplankton biomass. With the return of sunlight during early
spring, the snow melts and the ice becomes thinner giving rise to the formation
of a layer of ice algae underneath the ice. As the ice breaks up, the spring bloom
is initiated primarily dominated by diatoms. The nutrients at the surface are
rapidly depleted by the uptake from phytoplankton. This coupled with grazing
by zooplankton, contributes to a decline in phytoplankton biomass at the surface.
The excess algae sink to the bottom, exporting organic matter to deeper waters.

==PAGE:22==
Figure 1.4: Overview of the seas and bays of the Arctic (WorldAtlas, n.d.)
During summer the water column becomes more stratified with a warmer and
fresher surface layer above a cooler and saltier layer below. Simultaneously, the
phytoplankton community becomes more diverse, and dominated by smaller diatoms
and autotrophic flagellates. As autumn approaches, a decrease in daylight and
cooling of the surface layer causes convection of the water column. This convection
results in the mixing of the phytoplankton and marks the transition towards
the winter season. C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis have developed strategies to
deal with this fluctuating availability of food. During winter, both species enter
diapause in deeper parts of the water column. Diapause is a state of inactivity
where reproduction and feeding are paused, and metabolism is reduced. As the
spring bloom approaches in March-April, C. glacialis migrates to the surface below
the ice to spawn. C. finmarchicus also travels to the surface though later than
C. glacialis, but still before the spring bloom. C. glacialis uses its lipid reserves
to initiate the egg production prior to the spring bloom and finishes spawning
during the bloom. In contrast C. finmarchicus relies on available food to initiate
spawning. While at the surface, they feed on ice-algae growing underneath the
ice. As the ice breaks and the spring bloom begins, they feed on the abundant
phytoplankton at the surface, and rebuild their lipid reserves for the winter. Shortly
Page | 6
==PAGE:23==
after the spring bloom C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis return to deeper waters
for another period of diapause until next spring (Figure 1.5). The consumed
phytoplankton contain fixed carbon from the process of photosynthesis which is
turned into high-energy lipid compounds inside the copepods. These lipids are the
main energy source for fish, birds, and marine mammals, such as whales, higher
up in the Arctic food chain. This energy transfer highlights the important role of
Arctic copepods as a key link between the base of the food chain and higher trophic
levels, supporting the biodiversity and overall functioning of the Arctic ecosystem.
Moreover, fishing and hunting of these higher trophic species play an important
role in the Greenlandic economy (Falk-Petersen et al., 2009; Kjellerup et al., 2012;
Madsen et al., 2001; T. G. Nielsen, 2005). Additionally, copepods contribute to the
biological pump through a daily vertical migration pattern in their active period.
At night they ascend to surface layers to feed on phytoplankton and descends back
down during the day to avoid predators. On the way down, they release fecal
pellets and carbon-rich waste particles, facilitating the removal of carbon from the
surface to deeper ocean layers. Furthermore, the release of CO2 from respiration of
diapausing copepods leads to the formation of dissolved inorganic carbon which
also is sequestered in the deep ocean (Pinti et al., 2023; Zhang & Dam, 1997).
The lifespan of C. finmarchicus is typically 1 year, whereas C. glacialis can live up
to 2 years (Falk-Petersen et al., 2009; Madsen et al., 2001). The two species can be
difficult to distinguish from each other, however they are somewhat different in
size and pigmentation. The prosome length (PL) of female C. glacialis has been
observed to range between approximately 2.5-4 mm with a mean around 3.5 mm,
whereas females of C. finmarchicus approximately ranged between 2-3 mm with a
mean PL of 2.7 mm. Consequently, there is a large overlap in size between the two
species, so the PL alone is not sufficient for differentiation. However, C. glacialis
typically contain a larger lipid store. Swalethorp et al. (2011) measured a total
lipid content of C. glacialis to range between 82-290 µg fem-1 during March-May
(2008) in Disko Bay, while C. finmarchicus had a total lipid content between 33-92
µg fem-1 in the same period. Red pigmentation has been identified as a more
reliable method in distinguishing the two species. C. glacialis females generally
exhibit red pigment in the antennas as well as the genital somites, whereas C.
finmarchicus only display little to no red pigmentation (Figure 1.6) (Choquet et al.,
2018; Lindeque et al., 2022; T. G. Nielsen et al., 2014; Swalethorp et al., 2011).

1.2.1
Implications of Climate Change and Crude Oil Pollution
on Arctic Copepods
Previous studies have investigated the effect of climate change and other anthro-
pogenic stressors on the Calanus community. Some of these studies show that
in a future warmer ocean with reduced ice cover, the spring bloom may be trig-
gered by sunlight rather than the breakup of ice. This would result in an earlier
occurrence of the spring bloom, potentially diminishing the advantage of early
spawning for C. glacialis. By the time C. glacialis has finished spawning and is
ready to feed, the spring bloom has already occurred, resulting in limited food
availability. As a result, C. glacialis may struggle to replenish its lipid storage
for the following winter. In contrast, C. finmarchicus has shown higher potential
to adapt and utilize the earlier bloom, potentially increasing the dominance of
the less lipid containing C. finmarchicus (Kjellerup et al., 2012). In fact Møller
and Nielsen (2020) demonstrated that C. finmarchicus already is dominating the
Calanus community in Disko Bay. This shift has resulted in a 34% decrease in
lipid content during spring and summer. Furthermore, increasing temperatures up
to 10°C have shown an increase in egg production for C. finmarchicus, whereas C.
glacialis reaches its peak egg production at lower temperatures (Pasternak et al.,
2013). Thus, the timing of the spring bloom as well as the lipid-driven energy flow
are critical features that shape the structure of communities and populations in
high-latitude ecosystems (Falk-Petersen et al., 2009).
C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis are the two species most investigated in terms
of oil pollution in studies of arctic and subarctic invertebrates, and crude oil is
the compound most tested on crustaceans (Kalter & Passow, 2023). Crude oil
is a mixture of chemicals consisting largely of hydrocarbons. However, it can
also contain sulfur, nitrogen, and oxygen as well as various metals. The specific
content depends on the type of crude oil. One particular concern of crude oil is
its content of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH), which are highly toxic
components that can disrupt membrane fluidity and have potential carcinogenic,
teratogenic, and mutagenic effects on humans and aquatic animals (Almeda et al.,
2013; De Flora et al., 1991; Hylland, 2006; US EPA, 2013; Yu, 2002). The effects
of crude oil have been tested on C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis at an exposure
concentration of 1 µL L-1, where both species showed a significant decrease in fecal
pellet production between 34-58% (Rist et al., 2021). In another study C. glacialis
displayed lower sensitivity to crude oil at 2°C, compared to C. finmarchicus at 10°C.
The study utilized different temperatures to simulate the natural habitat conditions
of each species. They hypothesized that the different sensitivities likely are due to C.
glacialis’ higher lipid content, which may slow down the uptake process. The specific
crude oil exposure concentration were however not mentioned (B. H. Hansen et al.,
2011). Other studies have explored the effects of pyrene, a component often found
Page | 9
==PAGE:26==
in crude oil, on C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis, along with elevated temperatures
(0-10°C). Exposure concentrations ranged from 2.02 ng L-1 to 20.2 µg L-1. The
results revealed increased mortality, reduced egg production and growth of nauplii
in both species. However, C. finmarchicus displayed higher sensitivity, particularly
at increasing temperatures, while C. glacialis mainly showed sensitivity at lower
temperatures (around 0°C). Additionally, C. finmarchicus exhibited decreased fecal
pellet production and delayed spawning with rising temperatures (Grenvald et al.,
2013; Hjorth & Nielsen, 2011).
Studies on other copepod species of the Calanoid order (Acartia tonsa, Temora
turbinate, Parvocalanus crassirostris and Pseudodiaptomus pelagicus), have been
conducted on the effect of crude oil at concentrations of 1 µL L-1 for 48 hours
(Almeda, Baca, et al., 2014; Almeda, Connelly, et al., 2016; Almeda, Harvey,
et al., 2016; Toxværd et al., 2018). These studies demonstrated that copepods
ingest crude oil, resulting in reduced egg production, fecal pellet production, egg
hatching, increased nauplii mortality and decreased nauplii growth and swimming
ability. Moreover, the studies revealed that UVB radiation from the sun increased
the toxicity of dispersed crude oil by 1.3-3.8 times, and dispersed crude oil was
approximately 1.6 times more toxic than crude oil alone. The latter was also
displayed for C. glacialis with higher PAH exposure when the oil was dispersed,
attributed to the presence of smaller droplets that are more available for uptake
(Toxværd et al., 2018). Another study found that the presence of protozoans
reduced the non-lethal effects of crude oil, and the accumulation of PAHs in tissue
and fecal pellets. This was due to the protozoans ability to remove crude oil from
the water by absorption or ingestion (Almeda et al., 2013).
So far, studies including the two species have mainly investigated the single effect of
elevated temperatures and crude oil, or the combined effects of pyrene and increased
temperatures. Additionally, there is a lack of research on how copepods react to
an increased freshwater influx. One master thesis study has investigated the effects
of different salinity levels ranging from 5-60 PSU, in combination with elevated
temperatures between 0-10°C on C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis. The findings
revealed that both species were sensitive to extreme salinity levels (below 20 and
above 50), with C. finmarchicus experiencing higher mortality rates than C. glacialis.
Fecal pellet production rates were also affected, showing an increase at salinities
below 25 and above 40 for both species. Additionally, C. finmarchicus exhibited
increased pellet production at increasing temperatures, whereas C. glacialis peaked
at 5°C (Makri, 2021). No studies have yet investigated the combination of all
three anthropogenic stressors on copepods. Since, these stressors likely coexist in
real-world scenarios, it is important to study the combined impact of temperature,
salinity, and crude oil pollution.
Therefore, the purpose of this master thesis is to investigate the combined effects of

salinities and crude oil pollution on C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis from Disko

Materials and Methods
2.1
Sampling
Plankton samples were collected from the ice in Disko Bay, off Qeqertarsuaq
(Figure 2.1 & Figure 2.2), at two different locations (N69°14’832”, W053°35’428” and
N69°14’550”, W053°30’357”) with maximum depths of 87 and 67 meters respectively,
on March 7, 9 and 10 2023. In situ temperature and salinity is shown in Figure
2.3. The two copepod species C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis were collected using
a WP3 plankton net (300 µm mesh size) with a large non-filtering cod end. The
net was lowered down to the bottom through a hole in the ice and slowly hauled
back by hand. The content of the cod end was quickly transferred to a thermo box
containing surface seawater.
Figure 2.1: Map of the sampling area (Makri, 2021).
Within 2 hours of collection, the samples were returned to the laboratory and kept
at approximately 0°C. The following 48 hours, adult females were sorted in seawater
filled Petri dishes, kept in ice-chilled trays under a dissecting microscope, using a
Pasteur pipette. A total of 342 C. finmarchicus and 209 C. glacialis were sorted
using size and red pigmentation of antennas and genital somites as identification
criteria (Figure 2.4) (T. G. Nielsen et al., 2014). The sorted females were kept in
aerated, seawater-filled buckets, at 0°C.

2.2
Phytoplankton Culture
Cultures of the diatom Thalassiosira weissflogii were grown in 2 L aerated glass
jars and kept closed with an aluminium foil lid to minimize contamination (Figure
2.5). Pretreated surface seawater was used for the cultures, heated to 70°C for 2
hours to eliminate other organisms, but not boiled, to avoid salts from precipitating.
Vitamin-enriched B1 medium and silicate (500 µM) were added in a concentration of
1.1 mL L-1 each (P. J. Hansen, 1989). The cultures were grown at room temperature
and exposed to natural light. The algae were kept in exponential growth by daily
monitoring of cell concentrations and dilution of cultures accordingly. For the
monitoring samples of approximately 2 mL were taken after turning the bottles 10
times to ensure homogeneous suspension of the cells. The samples were fixed in
acid Lugol’s solution and 1 mL was transferred to a Sedgewick Rafter chamber and
counted under a microscope. Each count was based on at least 400 cells. A cellular
carbon content of 131 pg C cell-1 and saturation concentration for Calanus spp. on
400 µg C L-1 was used to determine the amount of diatoms needed to saturate the
feeding for the experiment (Dutz et al., 2008; P. J. Hansen et al., 1997).
Figure 2.5: Diatom cultures used in the experiment.

2.3.1
Acclimatization of Individuals
Of each species 60 females were acclimatized to the three salinities prior to the
experiment; 20 individuals to 20 PSU seawater, 20 individuals to 27 PSU seawater
and 20 individuals were kept at ambient 33.2 PSU seawater (Figure 2.6). The
acclimatization was conducted by concentrating the pre-sorted females on a 200
µm submerged sieve and picking 20 individuals of each species and for each of
the three salinities, following the same procedure as the first sorting. During the
sorting, each individual was examined to validate the gender, species and wellbeing,
ensuring only females in good condition were included in the experiment. Of the
sorted individuals 20 of each species were transferred into two buckets with 5 L
surface seawater (33.2 PSU). The remaining 40 individuals of each species were
transferred into four buckets with 5 L, 30 PSU water and a false bottom cylinder
with 400 µm mesh (28.3 cm high and 18 cm in diameter) (Figure 2.7). The 6
buckets were kept aerated at 0°C. After 12 hours the 40 individuals of each species
acclimatized at 30 PSU were gently transferred by the false bottom cylinders to
four new buckets, two containing 27 PSU water and two containing 25 PSU water.
After 18 hours of acclimatization at 0°C, the 20 females of each species at 25 PSU
were transferred to two new buckets containing 20 PSU water following the same
procedure.

2.3.2
Preparation of Exposure Bottles
After 18 hours 60 250 mL bottles were filled 3/4 with seawater of the respective
salinities and crude oil according to the treatment groups in Table 2.1. The crude oil
was added to obtain a concentration of 1 µL L-1 corresponding to the concentration
commonly found after an oil spill (Almeda, Connelly, et al., 2014, 2016). The crude
oil used in this experiment was Light Louisiana Sweet oil, which was prepared as
an oil droplet suspension by adding the oil to seawater under full vortex stirring
(Figure 2.8). A detailed description of the procedure is found in Almeda et al.
(2021). This method produces oil droplets with a mean diameter of 8 µm (>95%
of droplets are between 1 and 20 µm), which previously has been analyzed with an
Imaging Particle Analysis system (FlowSight®) (Almeda, Connelly, et al., 2016).
Thereafter, two females of each species were transferred to the bottles according to
the acclimatized salinity. Finally, diatoms were added to a concentration of 3,000
cells mL-1 and water of the respective salinities to close the bottles air-free. The
bottles were incubated at 0 and 5°C in the dark and inverted every approximately
4 hours during daytime to ensure suspension of the algae. Seawater-oil samples
resembling the content of the ambient+oil exposure bottles, were subsequently
analyzed for metals and polycyclic aromatic compounds (PACs), to determine the
composition and concentration of the crude oil. The analysis was conducted at
Man-Technology-Environment Research Centre (MTM), Örebro University, Sweden.
The method for the chemical analysis can be found in Appendix C.

2.3.4
Sample Analysis and Calculations
To estimate copepod size and lipid content of the individuals, pictures were taken
after the acclimatization but before start of the experiment, and at the end of the
exposure period on day 6. A total of 30 females of each species were photographed
before the experiment, 10 females of each acclimatized salinity (33.2 PSU, 27 PSU
and 20 PSU). On day 6 at the end of the experiment, all 60 exposed individuals of
each species were photographed. The images were taken with a Leica EC3 digital
color camera attached to an Olympus SZ40 stereo microscope with the copepod
placed laterally in a Petri dish. The prosome length (PL, mm) (Figure 2.10A) and
lipid sac area (LSA, mm2) (Figure 2.10B) was measured with image analysis in
ImageJ. The female carbon content was calculated from the prosome length using
the equation described in Swalethorp et al. (2011) for Calanus spp. in pre-bloom
conditions: CFEM = 0.0018×PL4.1. The total lipid content (TL, mg) was calculated
from the lipid sac area following the equation described in Vogedes et al., 2010:
TL = 0.197×A1.38.
Figure 2.10: (A) Example of prosome length and (B) lipid sac area of a female measured in
ImageJ.
The daily fecal pellet production rate (FPP, FP fem-1 day-1) and specific fecal pellet
production (SPP, µg CFP µg CFEM-1 day-1) were calculated from the number of
pellets counted every 24 hours. Pictures were taken of fecal pellets on day 1, 4 and 6
of the experiment, with the same camera used for the copepod pictures. The length
and width of the fecal pellets were measured with image analysis in ImageJ (Figure
2.11) to estimate the pellet volume (VFP, µm3) assuming a cylindrical shape. A
total of 2.090 FP were measured for C. finmarchicus and 1.786 FP for C. glacialis.
The carbon content of the fecal pellets (CFP, µg C FP-1) was calculated from the
fecal pellet volume using a conversion factor of CFP = 4.3×10−8×VFP (Swalethorp
et al., 2011). Finally, the specific fecal pellet production was calculated from the
following equation: SPP = FPP×CFP/CFEM.

2.3.5
Respiration
Respiration was measured of three randomly chosen copepods from each treatment
group and each species. Before measurements the O2 electrode was calibrated by
lowering it into measurement chambers containing 0% and 100% O2 saturated,
GF/F filtered (0.45 µm mesh size) seawater respectively. The individuals were
thereafter placed in 2 mL measurement chambers containing GF/F filtered (0.45
µm mesh size) seawater using a Pasteur pipette and submerged into a manually
temperature-controlled water bath using snow (Figure 2.12). The water bath
was kept at 2°C ± 0.34. After approximately 5 minutes of acclimatization in the
chamber, the O2 electrode was lowered through the chamber lid and measured
for 10 minutes, or without acclimatization for 15 minutes. The respiration was
measured using Unisense Micro Respiration System (www.unisense.dk), consisting
of a Clarke-type oxygen microsensor, an amplifier and a stirring system. The
Clarke-type sensor measures the partial pressure of O2 which is converted to O2
concentration (µM) through the Sensor Trace Rate software. The stirring system
consists of a magnet stirrer with glass coated magnets which are separated from
the copepod in the measuring chamber by a small metal net (200 µm). The O2
concentration (µM) was measured every second, and the data was logged through
the Sensor Trace Rate software. Background measurements of filtered seawater
without copepods, were conducted for approximately 60 minutes to account for
potential oxygen consumption of the electrode, or microbial respiration.

2.5
Statistical Analysis
To decide on the appropriate tools and methods to use for the statistical analysis
the experimental input data was checked for linearity and distribution patterns. In
terms of both the FP numbers (FP fem-1 day-1), FP volume (µm3) and lipid sac
area (mm2) the values by treatment and species appeared normally distributed
(Figure D.1-D.3), while the temporal dynamics by treatment and species showed
to be highly non-linear (Figure 3.1-3.2). Due to the non-linear pattern of the
temporal data, a mixed effect Generalized Additive Model (GAM) was used to
test for the effect of temperature, salinity, and oil. GAMs use smooth functions
to capture non-linear patterns and can estimate the relationship between several
predictors (independent variables) and a response variable (dependent variable)
(Wieling, 2018). The exposure effect was modelled separately for the two species.
Two different GAMs were run for each species. The first investigates the effect of
each treatment compared to the control where the six exposure treatments were
treated as fixed effects. The second GAM investigates the single and joint effects
of temperature, salinity, and oil by considering the interactions among these three
environmental predictors. That is their combined effect is not simply the sum
of their individual effects. In both sets of models, the replicates, nested within
treatments, were included as random effects to account for individual variation
unrelated to the treatments. Lastly, the model predictions were compared to the
observed mean effect of each treatment, shown as boxplots (Figure D.4), to inspect
the accuracy of the model. All statistical tests were conducted in RStudio (R
version 4.0.2).
2.5.1
Respiration rate
The respiration rates were obtained by plotting all measured oxygen concentrations
(µmol L-1) against time (h) (see example in Figure 2.14). Since the oxygen con-
sumption of all the measurements looked more exponential than the expected linear
pattern, it was decided to split the individual measurements in two and conduct
linear regression on the first 5 minutes (R1) and the rest of the measurement (5 or
10 minutes, R2) respectively. The respiration is expected to be linear because this
means that the oxygen consumption of the copepod is not affected (either slowed
down or increased) over time being in the measurement chamber. Furthermore,
because of the similar looking pattern of all measurements, it was decided not to
cut the first 5 minutes of acclimatization out from the 15 minutes measurements.
The respiration rate (the positive slope, µmol L-1 h-1) was given by Microsoft
Excel (Office 365). Additionally, an ANOVA was used to investigate the regression
slope i.e., if there is a significant linear relationship between the dependent and
independent variable. Respiration rates with a significant slope (p-value<0.05)
were included in the presented results. Only two C. glacialis had insignificant
Page | 24
==PAGE:41==
respiration rates, further one C. glacialis had a negative respiration rate, which
was also discarded for further investigation, since copepods do not produce oxygen.
Figure 2.14: Examples of a 10 minute (control) and 15 minute (scenario 1) oxygen consumption
measurement for Calanus finmarchicus. The dashed lines mark 5 minutes and thus where the
measurements have been divided into R1 and R2.
The respiration rates were converted from µmol L-1 h-1 to µL ind-1 h-1. To isolate the
respiration rate specific to copepods, the mean background oxygen consumption
rate, calculated from two background measurements, was subtracted from the
respiration rates of the chambers containing copepods. Lastly, the mean respiration
rate for each treatment group was calculated, for R1 and R2 respectively.

Discussion
4.1
Exposure Effects on Calanus finmarchicus and
Calanus glacialis
4.1.1
Fecal Pellet Production (FPP)
In the present study it was demonstrated that C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis were
both highly sensitive towards low concentrations of crude oil (1 µL L-1) resembling
the concentration of dispersed oil after an oil spill (Almeda, Baca, et al., 2014;
Almeda, Connelly, et al., 2016). Both species responded with a clear decrease
in FPP of 67-83% for C. finmarchicus and 70-77% for C. glacialis, indicating
a negative effect on their feeding activity. The same pattern was discovered by
Rist et al. (2021) who saw a 34-58% decrease in FPP at 2°C and 33 PSU for C.
finmarchicus and C. glacialis using the same type of crude oil (Light Louisiana
Sweet oil). Interestingly, the decrease in FPP observed in this study was generally
higher compared to the study by Rist et al. (2021), where copepods were exposed to
a combination of crude oil and microplastics. The difference might be attributed to
the combined presence of microplastics, which potentially could mitigate the toxicity
of crude oil, e.g., through adsorption, making the crude oil less available for uptake
by the copepods. However, Rist et al. (2021) also found no significant differences
in oil effects between treatments with and without microplastics. Alternatively,
the variation could be influenced by the combination of several stressors (lower
salinities, and elevated temperatures), potentially causing a synergistic effect that
enhances the toxicity of crude oil. Yet, the reduction in FPP of this study did not
exhibit substantial variation across different salinities and temperatures. Therefore,
the difference between the two studies could potentially be attributed to variations
in experimental conditions, sample size or methodologies.
In the study of Hjorth and Nielsen (2011) they discovered a decrease in FPP of
approximately 50% only for C. finmarchicus when exposed to low concentrations
of pyrene (2.02 ng L-1) at elevated temperatures (8°C). C. glacialis on the other
hand did not exhibit significant sensitivity towards exposure to pyrene. These
result differ from the present study. Hjorth and Nielsen (2011) solely exposed
the copepods to pyrene, which is a single component of crude oil. As previously
described, crude oil contains a diverse range of compounds. Thus, the sensitivity
exhibited by C. glacialis in the current study could be attributed to other existing
compounds of crude oil, or the combination of multiple compounds found in the oil.
Additionally, the concentration of pyrene, in the current study, was found to be
4.2 ng L-1, which is twice the concentration that were used in Hjorth and Nielsen
(2011). This difference in pyrene exposure concentration could also potentially
Page | 39
==PAGE:56==
explain the effect on C. finmarchicus at lower temperatures as well as potentially
the effect on C. glacialis in the current study.
In terms of salinity variations, Makri (2021) found that both species exhibited
increased FPP at extreme salinity concentrations beyond the range of 25-40 PSU.
Despite, scenario 2 + oil (20 PSU, 5°C) falling below this limit, there was still
a significant decrease in FPP. This suggests, in line with the results obtained in
this study, that the impact of crude oil on FPP was more pronounced for both
species compared to the effects of decreased salinities and elevated temperatures.
Regarding scenario 1 and 2 (without oil), the FPP of both species displayed
distinct patterns compared to the oil treatments. For C. finmarchicus, there was
an overall significant increase in FPP, mainly attributed to the rise in temperature,
which had the most significant single effect compared to salinity. Additionally, the
temporal dynamics showed an overall increase in FPP during the exposure period,
with scenario 2 demonstrating the most pronounced increase. These results are
consistent with Makri (2021), who also observed increased FPP for C. finmarchicus
at elevated temperatures. Makri (2021) hypothesized that the increased FPP
could be attributed to osmotic stress triggering osmoregulation in the copepods.
Osmoregulation is a process where copepods work to maintain a stable balance of
water and salt within their bodies, despite changes in their external environment
(osmotic stress). This process requires significant energy, therefore she hypothesized
that the higher energy use lead to higher ingestion rates and subsequently higher
FPP. Alternatively, she hypothesized that a decrease in FPP could arise from a
reduced efficiency in assimilating the ingested food, caused by the stress.
Similarly, the studies by Hjorth and Nielsen (2011) and Grenvald et al. (2013)
also found C. finmarchicus to be more sensitive at elevated temperatures. In their
case, Hjorth and Nielsen (2011) observed decreased FPP at 8°C, while Grenvald
et al. (2013) reported increased mortality at early life stages (sum of five nauplii
stages) in response to 20.2 µg L-1 pyrene at 0, 5 and 10°C. For C. glacialis, the
FPP patterns in scenario 1 and 2 were more inconsistent in this study. Scenario 1
showed an overall increase (41%), whereas scenario 2 exhibited an overall decrease
(-21%), although neither of these trends were as significant as the oil treatments,
both in terms of mean difference from control and the GAM results. These findings
somewhat align with Makri (2021), who observed a peak in FPP at 5°C within the
salinity range of 25-40 PSU, including 27 PSU. However, her results contradict
with this study at 20 PSU, where she found a further increase. Hjorth and Nielsen
(2011), on the other hand, did not find any differences in FPP for C. glacialis at a
temperature range from 0.5 to 8°C.
The current study does not allow for a complete differentiation of the effects of
temperature, salinity and crude oil since they were not tested separately. Therefore,
direct comparison with previous studies investigating the individual effects of these
Page | 40
==PAGE:57==
parameters are not feasible. In ecosystems, several stressors can interact in various
ways, leading to additive, antagonistic, or synergistic effects. Additive interaction
occur when the combined effect of all stressors equals the sum of each single
stressor’s effect in isolation. Antagonistic interactions, on the other hand, results in
a combined effect that is less than the additive interaction, meaning some stressors
mitigate each others effects. Conversely, synergistic interactions involve stressors
that enhance each other’s effects, leading to a combined impact greater than the
additive interaction (Turschwell et al., 2022). The FPP responses in both species
indicate no clear interaction pattern among the three environmental stressors. This
is evident as there is no clear difference between the ambient + oil (33 PSU, 0°C)
and scenario 1 + oil (27 PSU, 5°C) and 2 + oil (20 PSU, 5°C) treatments for both
species, indicating that variations in salinity and temperature do not directly affect
the oil treatments. When comparing the non-oil treatments to the oil treatments,
there is and indication of opposite effects of oil versus salinity and temperature.
This is evident as treatments without oil in general showed an increase in FPP,
while the oil treatments exhibited a strong decrease in FPP, indicating that the
influence of crude oil outweighs the influence of the other stressors, causing an
overall net decrease. The difference between the oil and non-oil treatments could
be due to copepods experiencing narcosis, as observed in Almeda et al. (2013), who
used the same type of crude oil on mesozooplankton communities from the Gulf
of Mexico (the details of this study will be described later). Narcosis is a state of
inactivity or reduced responsiveness caused by exposure to toxic chemicals. It has
been associated with PAHs and volatile compounds of petroleum; benzene, toluene,
ethylbenzene and xylene (BTEX) in crude oil. Narcosis in copepods is reversible
when exposed to unpolluted water, but if they remain in a narcotic state for an
extended period, it may reduce feeding and thus FPP, and additionally make the
copepods more vulnerable to predators. Besides ingestion of crude oil, there is
potential for adhesion to the copepods or diffusion though their cell membranes.
Adhesion to their bodies could hinder their mobility and potentially clog their
mouths, preventing them from accessing food (Almeda et al., 2013).
4.1.2
Fecal Pellet Volume and Lipid Sac Area
The results align with the initial expectation that the lipid sac area and prosome
length of the copepods would remain unchanged during the short period of ex-
posure across all treatments. This was anticipated because, major alterations in
morphology typically require a much longer time frame than the six-day duration
of this experiment, especially for adult copepods like those used in this study. The
LSA, TL, PL and CFEM generally confirmed that C. glacialis is larger and has a
higher lipid content than C. finmarchicus.
The volume of the FP was hypothesized to decrease in response to gastrointestinal
Page | 41
==PAGE:58==
distress cause by the oil exposure. However, this hypothesis was not supported by
other research findings from literature. Furthermore, the results did not align with
this hypothesis. Nonetheless, it would be interesting to explore whether the density
of the FP remained unchanged, like the volume, or if it decreased as a result of the
oil exposure, resulting in the same volume but with reduced mass.
4.1.3
Respiration Rate
The respiration rates of C. glacialis were generally higher than of C. finmarchicus
for both R1 and R2. Additionally, R1 for both species was significantly higher
than R2, ranging between 3.2-4.3 µL ind-1 h-1 for C. finmarchicus and 3.1-9.5
µL ind-1 h-1 for C. glacialis. In contrast, the R2 rates ranged between 0.6-1.0
µL ind-1 h-1 for C. finmarchicus and 0.6-2.1 µL ind-1 h-1 for C. glacialis. Beside
these variations, there were almost no significant differences between treatments
in both R1 and R2 rates for both species. Only in the case of C. glacialis, R1
exhibited a significant difference between scenario 2 and scenario 2 + oil. However,
this variation is not substantial enough to draw general conclusions regarding the
differences between treatments. Previous studies have reported respiration rates
for C. glacialis in the Barents Sea of 0.6 µL ind-1 h-1 at temperatures between -1.8
and 3.5°C (Båmstedt & Tande, 1985; Ikeda & Skjoldal, 1989) and from the Gulf
of Maine (North Atlantic Ocean) of 0.7 µL ind-1 h-1 at 4-6°C (R. J. Conover &
Corner, 1968). Comparing these values with the measured respiration rates of this
study for C. glacialis, suggests that the R2 rates are more accurate than R1. For
C. finmarchicus, respiration rates of 0.3-0.4 µL ind-1 h-1 have been observed at
temperatures between 0.1 to 15°C in the Barents Sea and the North Atlantic Ocean
(Nova Scotia and Buzzard Bay) (Anraku, 1964; Ikeda & Skjoldal, 1989; Mayzaud,
1976). This also indicates that the R2 rates of the current study are more accurate
than R1 for C. finmarchicus. However, the R2 rates are still slightly higher for both
species than those reported in other studies, especially for C. finmarchicus. This
discrepancy might be attributed to the methodology used for the measurements
and data analysis in the current study, warranting further discussion.
Unexpectedly, all the respiration measurements showed the same exponentially
declining pattern. The oxygen consumption is expected to display a more linear
trend, indicating no abrupt variations in the individual’s respiration due to sudden
external changes while being in the small chamber during the measurement. This
discrepancy gave rise to two hypotheses. Firstly, the steep decline in oxygen during
the initial part of the measurement could be attributed to a stress response from
the copepods, potentially due to inadequate acclimatization beforehand. Secondly,
the strong decrease in oxygen consumption after the first approximately 5 minutes
might be linked to lower oxygen levels negatively impacting respiration. It is
also possible that both factors could be at play simultaneously. However, the
Page | 42
==PAGE:59==
temporal dynamics were identical for both the 10 and 15-minute measurements.
Initially, it was anticipated that differences would be observed between the two
durations, and that removing the first 5 minutes of the 15-minute measurements
would align the patterns, accounting for acclimatization. But since this was not
the case, it was deemed misleading to exclude any parts of the measurements. The
background measurements also exhibited the same exponentially declining pattern,
indicating that the dynamics likely were influenced by the equipment, most likely
the oxygen electrode, rather than stress responses from the copepods. Notably, the
electrode was old and had not been used for a while. Despite this, R1 for both
species remained significantly higher than R2 even after subtracting the background
measurements. This implies that indications of stress might still be present.
The decision to divide each oxygen measurement into two was driven by the
unexpected pattern of the temporal dynamics observed in the oxygen measurements.
Here it was hypothesized that the second half of the measurement would provide a
more accurate respiration rate, since the steep decline in the beginning was excluded.
However, this approach comes with the risk of compromising the respiration results
as it is based on fewer data points.
In 2012 a master student conducted an
experiment to determine the optimal respiration measuring time for Calanus spp.
(Pankoke, 2012). The study used Calanus helgolandicus, an Atlantic originating
species of approximately the same size as C. finmarchicus. The aim was to minimize
measuring time while obtaining a representative respiration rate, to identify an
optimal acclimatization period to achieve a stable subsequent measurement, and to
avoid oxygen concentrations from reaching levels low enough to influence respiration.
It was found that a 10-minute measurement was sufficient to capture a representative
respiration rate, and variability generally decreased with increasing measuring time.
Additionally, a 15-minute acclimatization period before measurements was used,
although it was observed that the length of acclimatization time did not influence
respiration.
The findings of Pankoke (2012) indicates that a respiration rate
based on only 5-minutes of a measurement may not be sufficient to provide a
representative result. A modification that potentially could have improved the
respiration measurements of the present study would be to increase the measuring
time to at least 15 minutes for all measurements. In doing so, the first 5 minutes
of steep decline could be excluded, and the respiration rate would still be based
on sufficient datapoints. Additionally, it would be ideal to either maintain the
5-minute acclimatization period or consider extending it to account for potential
stress of the copepods.
After dividing the measurements in two and obtaining the linear regression slope,
considerable variation in the R2-values was observed in the R2 oxygen measurements
for both species. The R2-values for R1 ranged from 0.7 to 0.9, while the R2
measurement showed variability between 0.1 and 0.8 for both species.
These
Page | 43
==PAGE:60==
variations in the R2-values for R2 indicate significant variation in data, which may
increase the difficulty in detecting difference in respiration rates between treatment
groups.
Concerning the potential risk of low oxygen concentration in the measuring chamber
affecting the respiration rate, previous studies have observed a rapid decrease in
oxygen consumption for C. finmarchicus when oxygen concentration decrease below
3 mL L-1 (94 µmol L-1), and death occurs at about 1 mL L-1 (31 µmol L-1) (Marshall
et al., 1935). These observations were found at 5°C and 15°C. Interestingly, the
oxygen concentrations of this study ranged from approximately 105 µmol L-1
at the start to 78 µmol L-1 at the end of measurements.
This means that a
significant part of the measurements is below the 3 mL L-1 limit, where respiration
rates could be affected. To investigate a potential correlation between decreasing
oxygen concentrations and decreasing respiration rates, the respiration rates were
plotted against the start and end oxygen concentrations. Only for C. glacialis, R1
showed a slight correlation with the start oxygen concentration, suggesting that
C. glacialis may be somewhat affected by the oxygen concentration within the
measuring chamber. Given that C. glacialis is slightly larger than C. finmarchicus,
it consumes oxygen more rapidly, potentially making the small measuring chamber
less suitable for this species.
Other factors that are worth considering are the conditions of temperature and
salinity during the measurements. Oxygen consumption for C. finmarchicus has
been found to increase with higher temperatures, although only small differences
was found between 0 and 5°C of 0.28 and 0.31 mL L-1 h-1 respectively.
This
respiration rate represents a combined measurement for 120 females exposed for
4 hours in a single bottle (Marshall et al., 1935). Additionally, the solubility of
oxygen has shown to decrease with increasing temperatures and increasing salinity
(Ikeda et al., 2000). Since, previous studies have indicated a difference in oxygen
availability as well as respiration rates caused by variation in temperature and
salinity, it is worth discussing whether conducting the measurements under the
same conditions as the exposure treatments (in terms of salinity and temperature)
would have been more ideal. All measurements were conducted at 2°C with ambient
filtered seawater. However, whether this is necessary depends on the duration of
the treatment’s effect.
4.2
Exposure Effects on Thalassiosira weissflogii
The cell concentration of T. weissflogii showed no significant differences between
treatments. This suggest that the diatoms were not negatively affected by the
applied oil concentration, salinities, or temperatures of the experiment. Conse-
quently, any effects observed on the copepods cannot be attributed to changes in
food concentration.

4.3
Chemical Analysis of Exposure Water
The crude oil in the current study contains three different compound groups,
each with its unique characteristics. PAHs constitute a broad class of organic
compounds featuring a fused aromatic ring structure. Alkylated PAHs (alkyl-
PAHs) share this structure but possess additional alkyl groups or chains attached
to the aromatic rings. Dibenzothiophenes (DBTs) contain sulfur atoms within
their aromatic structure. All three categories of compounds are considered toxic
and raise environmental concerns. PAHs are overall more studied compared to the
two other types of compounds. They are known for their persistence, toxicity, and
carcinogenic properties in humans (Wnorowski et al., 2022). Consequently, the US
EPA has identified 16 priority PAHs of particular concern to living organisms. All
three categories of compounds share hydrophobic properties (Almeda et al., 2013;
Juhasz & Naidu, 2000). However, PAHs are particularly lipophilic and exhibit
increased lipophilicity with higher molecular weight compared to the two others.
This lipophilicity leads to PAHs accumulating in the lipid tissue of organisms,
making them highly bioaccumulative compounds (Almeda et al., 2013). In contrast,
DBTs are considered more soluble, enhancing their mobility in water (Wnorowski
et al., 2022).
Various other studies have been conducted investigating the toxicity of single
compounds and mixtures present in crude oil on different copepod species. Some
of these studies have also analyzed the chemical composition of the mixtures. By
comparing the composition and concentrations of the compounds found in the
present study with toxicity data from previous investigations, it might be possible
to gain insight into the specific compounds within crude oil that may be responsible
for the observed effects on the copepods.
In a study by Almeda et al. (2013), a chemical analysis was conducted using the
same type of crude oil (Light Louisiana Sweet oil) as in the current study. In their
research, mesozooplankton communities from the Gulf of Mexico were exposed
to crude oil at concentrations between 10-100 µL L-1. The copepod community
mainly comprised Arcatia tonsa, Paracalanus spp. and Oithona spp. However,
the analysis focused solely on the 16 priority PAHs defined by the US EPA, and
the crude oil was not analyzed in the exposure water but in its pure form. The
concentration of the various PAHs in the exposure treatments were calculated
based on the chemical analysis results and the added concentration of crude oil
(10-100 µL L-1). The chemical analysis of Almeda et al. (2013) revealed many of
the same compounds as those found in the current study. A comparison between
the composition and concentrations of PAHs in 1 µL L-1 crude oil is shown in Table
4.1. The overall composition of PAHs in both studies is nearly identical. However,
Almeda et al. (2013) also detected an amount of Anthracene, another priority
PAH. Overall, the concentrations of all the compounds were considerably higher
Page | 45
==PAGE:62==
in Almeda et al. (2013) compared to the current study, likely due to the different
methods used for chemical analysis. Furthermore, in the copepods after 16 hours
of exposure, Almeda et al. (2013) found the compounds Naphthalene (250-900 ng
g-1 DWzoo), Fluoranthene (50-250 ng g-1 DWzoo), Phenanthrene (200-700 ng g-1
DWzoo), Pyrene (200-800 ng g-1 DWzoo), Chrysene (900-4,500 ng g-1 DWzoo), and
Benzo[b]fluoranthene (250-950 ng g-1 DWzoo) at the highest concentrations. They
also reported that total concentrations of PAHs were 2.5-10 times higher in copepods
exposed to crude oil compared to those not exposed. The results showed increased
mortality rates between 16-96% depending on oil concentration, and increased
narcosis effect between 1-56%. The presence of the same composition of priority
PAHs in both the current study and the study by Almeda et al. (2013), although
different concentrations, suggests that the decreased fecal pellet production of the
present study, could be caused by narcosis, as observed in the study by Almeda
et al. (2013).

studies have hypothesized that C. glacialis exhibited less sensitivity towards pyrene
and crude oil exposure compared to C. finmarchicus, due to delayed uptake caused
by higher lipid content. Nevertheless, C. glacialis may still be more prone to
bioaccumulation even with reduced sensitivity (Grenvald et al., 2013; B. H. Hansen
et al., 2011; Hjorth & Nielsen, 2011). Additionally, the mixture of several PAHs,
alkyl-PAHs and DBTs may lead to a cocktail effect on the copepods. This means
that even at lower concentrations of each individual compound, the combined
effect could potentially still induce narcosis in the copepods, compared to a high
concentration of a single compound.
In a third study Barata et al. (2005) tested the single effect of various PAHs on O.
davisae and found 48-hour LC50 and EC50 values for narcosis displayed in Table 4.2.
Indeed, this study also contains PAHs at significantly higher concentrations com-
pared to the present study. Barata et al. (2005) tested three additional compounds,
1,2-Dimethylnaphthalene, 1-Methylphenanthrene and 3,6-Dimethylpenanthrene,
which were not detected in the crude oil used in the current study. However, the
present study did identify concentrations of other methylphenanthrenes (Table
3.7).

4.4
Reflections on Relevance of Study
I all scientific studies it is important to reflect upon the relevance of the study, in
terms of how realistic the studied conditions are. When examining oil pollution
from shipping in the Arctic, previous studies, also mentioned earlier, have shown
the potential advantages of using the shorter Northern Sea Route (NSR) instead
of the Suez Canal (Aksenov et al., 2017; Schøyen & Bråthen, 2011). This route
could lead to reduced fuel consumption and significant CO2 savings, making it a
more appealing option. In fact Schøyen and Bråthen (2011) stated that shipping
operations through the NSR might already be profitable for minor bulk trades.
Despite certain disadvantages of shipping through Arctic waters, the undeniable
Page | 48
==PAGE:65==
annual retreat of ice is making these shipping operations increasingly feasible.
In relation to fossil fuel extraction operations, these have long been associated
with the risk of oil spills, and the Arctic region is no exception. Several oil spills
have been reported in Canada, Alaska, and Russia (Wright et al., 2022).
In
Alaska, smaller oil spills of approximately 8,000 liters occur frequently, with an
average of about 85 spills annually. Moreover, larger oil spills of a magnitude
between 409,000 to 708 million liters have been reported caused by pipeline leaks,
transportation accidents, offshore drilling accidents and natural disasters. These
spills are examples of reported incidents between the 1900s to the 2010s (Wright
et al., 2022). Introducing fossil resource extraction operations in Greenland would
therefore significantly elevate the risk of such spills in the region. Additionally, as
previously mentioned, the remoteness of the region makes it challenging to respond
rapidly to incidents of oil spills (Wright et al., 2022). Therefore, the exposure of C.
finmarchicus and C. glacialis to crude oil pollution in this study holds significant
relevance as it reflects real-world conditions upon an oil spill.
Regarding potential oil concentrations that marine life might encounter, observa-
tions have found concentrations of 20-40 ppm (about 24-48 µL L-1) at the surface
within 10-15 minutes after the spill. One hour after the spill, concentrations of
10-15 ppm (12-18 µL L-1) have been recorded at depths of 1-6 meters, and 1-3 ppm
(1.2-3.6 µL L-1) at 9 meters (McAuliffe et al., 1981). The concentration of a spill
will however depend on the prevailing weather conditions and current in the spill
area (Wright et al., 2022). Therefore, the applied crude oil concentration of this
study falls within the lower range of what has been observed in the water column.
Considering the temperature conditions, as mentioned, previous literature has
reported an annual increase of 0.5°C in the surface layer of the Arctic. Additionally,
surface temperatures of up to 13°C have been recorded during summer 2010 in
West Greenland. Moreover, studies have documented surface temperatures between
-1.8 to 6.8°C in Disko Bay from May-September (average between 1996-1997) and
between 2.2 to 5.9°C in 1992 (Levinsen & Nielsen, 2002; Madsen et al., 2001;
Meredith et al., 2019; T. Nielsen & Hansen, 1995; Riisgaard et al., 2014). Both
C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis leave the surface layer again during midsummer
(T. G. Nielsen, 2005). Thus, the temperatures of 0 and 5°C applied in the current
study realistically reflect the conditions copepods may encounter at the surface
during late spring and summer when they migrate up to spawn and feed.
In terms of salinity conditions, several studies have reported a freshening of the
surface layer during the summer period in coastal areas of Greenland. As previous
mentioned, observations near glaciers have shown salinities ranging from around
30 to 10 PSU, and an annual drop of 0.12 PSU per year from 33 PSU to below
32 PSU in the subsurface layer (>30 m) during summer in Northeast Greenland.
This drop was observed in a region where the melting of the ice sheet has been less
Page | 49
==PAGE:66==
significant (Calbet et al., 2011; Sejr et al., 2017). Other studies have also reported
similar decreases. A decrease in surface salinities during ice melting in August from
31 to 16 PSU was observed in West Greenland in 2010 (Riisgaard et al., 2014). In
Northeast Greenland in 2014, a drop below 20 PSU was reported in the 5-6 meters
of the surface layer during summer (Middelbo et al., 2018). These studies show
that salinity drops to 27 PSU and 20 PSU, as applied in the present study, are
both realistic scenarios that the copepods may encounter during their time at the
surface layer.
4.5
Outlook
In future experiments, it would be valuable to assess aspects such as egg production,
hatching success and rate, as well as nauplii behavior and development in addition
to the parameters analyzed in this study. This would allow for investigation of
the potential effects on subsequent generations of copepods, understanding the
long-term impacts of these stressors. To achieve this, extending the exposure period
to harvest the eggs for subsequent analysis could be considered. This scenario
would also be relevant in terms of a prolonged oil exposure in cases of larger oil
spills and would offer an opportunity to investigate the copepods’ mortality rate
or potential ability to adapt to these changes. Additionally, another option to
consider is to conduct the exposure closer to the spring bloom in late April. During
this time, both species will produce more eggs, which could be harvested within
the six-day exposure period. Additionally, for future studies, it is recommended to
conduct respiration measurements at longer time intervals. This approach would
yield more reliable and comparable data across different treatments.
The knowledge gained from this research can help in the development of more
informed conservation and marine management strategies for marine ecosystems
and resources, in the Arctic regions that are particularly vulnerable to the effects
of climate change and fossil fuel pollution.

Conclusion
This master thesis investigated the combined effects of direct and indirect climate
change stressors, namely increasing temperature, decreasing salinities, and crude
oil pollution, on two ecologically important copepod species, C. finmarchicus and
C. glacialis, in Disko Bay, West Greenland. The study focused on the vital rate
of fecal pellet production (FPP) and respiration rates. Additionally, the chemical
composition of the exposure water containing crude oil was analysed.
One of the main findings of the study was that crude oil exposure, at a concentration
found in the lower range of what is typically observed after an oil spill (1 µL L-1) led
to a significant decrease in FPP in both C. finmarchicus (67-83%) and C. glacialis
(70-77%). This response was larger than what has been found in previous studies
on the effect of crude oil and pyrene. The decrease in FPP indicates a decreased
feeding activity, which can cause problems for restocking their lipid stores for the
winter diapause, as well as decrease the ability to reproduce, develop and grow.
Moreover, it disrupts the carbon sequestration to deeper ocean layers. Analysis of
the diatom T. weissflogii, the food source of the copepods during the experiment,
revealed that the reduced feeding was not a result of changes in food availability.
While minor significant differences were observed in the respiration rate between
treatments, none were substantial enough to draw specific conclusions regarding
the treatments’ impact on respiration rates. The analysis of the respiration rates
indicated a complex response for both C. finmarchicus and C. glacialis, possibly
influenced by factors such as old equipment, insufficient measurement duration,
potential stress of the copepods and potentially too low oxygen concentrations
during the measurements for C. glacialis. Overall, the R2 respiration rates were
closer to respiration rates observed in previous studies for both species, ranging
from 0.6-1.0 µL ind-1 h-1 for C. finmarchicus and 0.6-2.1 µL ind-1 h-1 for C. glacialis.
However, these rates should be approached with a critical perspective given the
aforementioned challenges of the measurements.
The chemical analysis of the exposure water containing crude oil revealed a total of
32 compounds, including 17 PAHs (10 priority), 12 alkyl-PAHs, 3 DBTs, and 1 metal.
Among the identified compounds, Naphthalene showed the highest concentration
at 650 ng L-1. Comparison with other studies suggested that narcosis induced
by Naphthalene, and possibly other compounds within the crude oil, might be
responsible for the observed decrease in fecal pellet production. Although, the
compounds in the current study were generally present at much lower concentrations
than those tested individually in previous studies, the cocktail effect of multiple
compounds could still induce toxicity, even at these lower concentrations.
Continued research efforts are essential to assess the long-term effects on copepod
Page | 51
==PAGE:68==
populations and monitor the overall health of the Arctic marine environment. The
implications on essential Arctic copepod species extend beyond their direct effects,
potentially causing unpredictable consequences on higher trophic levels within the
Arctic food web. The significance of these copepods as an energy-rich food source
in the ecosystem means that any changes in their population and biomass could
have cascading effects throughout the entire food web, impacting a wide range of
marine predators, that depend on them for sustenance.
The study’s context of Disko Bay in West Greenland is of particular relevance due
to the ongoing environmental changes in the Arctic region. This study can help
provide important insight into the combined effects of human-induced direct and
indirect climate change stressors on Arctic ecosystems. The findings emphasize
the vulnerability of Arctic ecosystems, underscoring the urgency for proactive
environmental conservation strategies and regulation to protect the fragile Arctic
marine ecosystem.

Title: Impact of temperature, salinity, and crude oil pollution on the vital rates of the co-
occurring copepod species; Calanus finmarchicus and Calanus glacialis from West 
Greenland. 
 
Background: Arctic marine ecosystems suffer from the consequences of climate change. 
When the temperature rises, the ice melts and creates a layer of freshwater at the surface. 
On the other hand, when the ice forms during the winter, salt is released and creates a layer 
of high salinity just below the ice. These strong changes in salinity can cause stress to 
planktonic organisms. Moreover, the decreased spread of sea ice makes it possible to 
establish new shipping routes and to extract previously inaccessible resources such as oil. 
This increases the risk of oil pollution. Since coastal fisheries form the economic foundation 
for the Greenlandic population by constituting 90% of the total export earnings, it is important 
to investigate the combined effects of these stressors related to climate change and pollution 
on the coastal ecosystems of Greenland. Plankton are the lowest level in the marine food 
chain, and small effects at this level can cause widespread implications on the whole food 
web. Changing salinity and temperatures on copepods, the most abundant planktonic 
animal, have previously been studied separately, and there is only very limited knowledge on 
the effects of the two in combination. In addition, there is no knowledge on the effects of all 
three stressors in combination on copepods.  
 
Project objective: The aim of this master thesis is to investigate the possible, combined 
effects of the climate change stressors; changing temperatures and salinity, as well as 
exposure to crude oil pollution on the copepod species Calanus finmarchicus and Calanus 
glacialis from the Disko Bay in West Greenland.

C
Materials and Methods for Chemical Analysis of
the Oil-seawater Suspension
C.1
Analysis of Metals
Before the analysis, all samples were diluted ten times with 1% concentrated nitric
acid to minimize any matrix effects (sample components influence on the target
element’s result). Additionally, rhodium (Rh) was added at a known concentration
to serve as an internal standard, to monitor any variations or losses during sample
analysis. The element analysis was performed in an ICP-MS (Agilent 7500 cx)
machine. The sample was introduced to the system through a MicroMist nebulizer
connected to a 2°C Scott double pass spray chamber. The settings were set to a
carrier gas flow of 0.9 L min-1, make-up gas flow of 0.20 L min-1, plasma run at
1500 watt and a torch-to-sampling cone distance of 8 mm. To minimize interference
from di- and polyatomic substances of elements prone to this, the analysis used
helium gas for those elements in a collision mode at a flow rate of 5 mL min-1. The
machine was calibrated using Merck VI 10580 multi element standard solution, in
a range of concentrations between 0.01-10,000 µg L-1. Boron was not tested for.
The dilution process and analysis procedure were conducted in a clean environment
(class 10-100) to minimize contamination from surroundings.
C.2
Analysis of PACs
Solid phase extraction (SPE)
The oil-seawater samples were subject to solid phase extraction (SPE) to extract
and concentrate polycyclic aromatic compounds (PACs). This involved conditioning
Oasis HLB cartridge with 5 mL DCM, then 5 mL methanol, 5 mL milli-Q grade
water and finally spiked with internal standard solutions (12.5-50 ng). Thereafter,
methanol (10%, v/v) was added to the samples to a total volume of 1 L, followed
by loading the samples onto the cartridges at a flow rate of around 5 mL min-1.
Excess water was removed from the adsorbent bed by centrifuging each cartridge at
6,000 g for 10 minutes followed by drying for 30 minutes using a vacuum pump. 12
mL of DCM was used to elute analytes. The eluted compounds were subsequently
evaporated using a gentle flow of nitrogen gas, then transferred to GC vials and
exchanged with 0.1 mL toluene. A recovery standard, deuterium-labelled perylene
(50 ng), was added to the GC vials, which were then stored at -18°C until the time
of the analysis using gas chromatography mass spectrometry (GC/MS).
GC/MS analysis
59 PACs were analyzed using an Agilent 7890A gas chromatograph coupled with a
5975C low-resolution mass spectrometer. 1 µL of the analytes were injected into
the gas chromatograph in the spitless mode and separated using an Agilent Select
PAH capillary column (30 m x 0.25 mm, 0.15 µm film thickness). The temperature
Page | 67
==PAGE:84==
program started at 70°C and increased to different temperature levels at various
rates; first to 205°C for 2 minutes followed by 250°C at a rate of 8°C min-1, then
270°C for 2 minutes at 3°C min-1, 279°C at 9°C min-1, 280°C for 3 minutes at
1°C min-1 and finally 325°C for 10 minutes at 5°C min-1. All measurements were
conducted using selected ion monitoring (SIM) mode. To identify and quantify the
PACs in the sample, a quantification mixture containing all 59 PACs, along with
stable isotopically labeled internal standards were used. PACs concentrations were
calculated using the isotope dilution method. For compounds without matching
labeled standard, the closest internal standard from the same compound group
and with similar retention time was used. The recoveries of internal standards for
methylated PAHs ranged from 50-130%, while native PAHs ranged from 20-80%,
with the highest losses observed for low molecular weight PAHs. The limits of
detection (LOD) were set at three times the concentration in the procedure blank,
or if no blank contamination was present, at the lowest point on the calibration
curve.
