Abstract
The Unicorn paradigm has been linked to mainstream startôÙup discourse in such
a strong way that the two terms could be used interchangeably. However, not
all ventures aspire to become part of this exclusive club, as it is based on a suôÙ
perficial and narrow definition: a one billion USD valuation. The term Unicorn
club, as it exists now, is a creation of a venture capitalist, and is synonymous with
a nonôÙdiverse ecosystem, which it has been since its inception. The paradigm,
while being seen as the symbol of success, now also stands for many issues such
as toxic hustleôÙculture, worker exploitation and value creation for a smaller group
without any restraints. In stark contrast to this, the Zebra movement started to
appear as an alternative approach within the startôÙup ecosystem. However, alterôÙ
native approaches like this are still underrepresented in the startôÙup ecosystem,
and role models are scarce. Nevertheless, founders already operate outside of the
mainstream narrative and thus need to create their own concept of success. As
this paper makes apparent, these concepts often align with the Zebra paradigm.
This thesis ultimately aimed to conceptualise success in alignment with the ZeôÙ
bra paradigm. To do so, characteristics of Zebra startôÙups and academiaãs view
of startôÙup success were extracted from the literature. In a second step, six inôÙ
terviews with interviewees from impactôÙdriven startôÙups were conducted to gather
insights into how these ventures went about defining their own idea of success in
a UnicornôÙdominanted world they do not fit into.
Gathering all this information, a first conceptualisation of success in alignment with
the Zebra paradigm was created. This conceptualisation consists of the following
five dimensions: Dual Value Creation, Impact, Journey, Collaboration and WellôÙ
being.
Having a clearer definition of Zebra success might help Zebras and startôÙups in
general, by making it easier to spot alternative success stories within the startôÙup
environment and ultimately also create broader representation for a more sustainôÙ
able ecosystem.
Keywords: Zebra, Unicorn, StartôÙup, Alternative Success Metrics, Social Impact,
Sustainability

Introduction
Venture Capital funding often dominates media coverage and indicates the crediôÙ
bility and potential of startôÙups, glorifying the idea of ãUnicorn StartôÙupsã with Kraft
(2025) describing Unicorns as ãthe pinnacle of successã. Jha et al. (2024) porôÙ

Unicorns are often perceived as symbols of entrepreneurial success, with their
generally positive associations making such startôÙups appear inherently desirable.
Thus, it makes sense that since the introduction of the term by A. Lee (2013) in
2013, many startôÙups have aspired to become one of these mystical creatures. To
become part of the Unicorn Club, startôÙups have to reach a one billion USD preôÙ
market valuation (Jha et al., 2024). This means that their focus lies on shareholder
profit rather than shared prosperity (Zebras Unite, 2017).
It can be clearly seen that in this definition of the Unicorn, described as a superficial
concept by Kuckertz et al. (2023), only two components are included: the startôÙup
as an entity and its valuation. Kuckertz et al. (2023) discuss that the concept of
Unicorns is nearly impossible to operationalise. This is because not only is the
term ãstartôÙupã incredibly vague, but the definition also does not specify how much
time startôÙups have to reach the valuation threshold and how long they must stay
above it. Moreover, the valuation of Unicorns is just a oneôÙpointôÙinôÙtime metric
that is preôÙmarket, meaning it only represents the price the most recent investor
was willing to pay after lengthy, subjective negotiations (Kuckertz et al., 2023).
Thus, the Unicorn valuation is not direct evidence of societal value creation, and
many newly crowned Unicornsã valuations are decreasing shortly after reaching
this milestone (Kuckertz et al., 2023). The subjectivity of valuations was also made
visible by Warren et al. (2025). They demonstrate that every valuation process can
yield different outcomes, depending on the companyãs focus and structure, such
as investorôÙowned and workerôÙowned models, thereby broadening the discussion
around valuation practices.
This ties into critical perspectives raised by Kraft (2025), who highlights issues of
overvaluation and unrealistic growth expectations that may result in market corôÙ

the Unicorn sector, driven by excessive investment and inflated valuations, thus
raises important concerns regarding the sustainability of growth, market saturaôÙ
tion, and investment risk (Kraft, 2025).
Another issue that is apparent in the discussion about ventureôÙbacked compaôÙ
nies is that while startôÙups are seen as highly impactful on economic growth, jobs

capitalôÙbacked founders are Black and 3% are women (Zepeda & Brandel, 2019).
Thus, it can be argued that the term and conversation surrounding Unicorns were

ãfemale underperformance hypothesis,ã which highlights how women are often

perform rather than challenging the existence or nature of such norms. If the conôÙ

1.1
Problem Statement
The issue with the above, however, is that a clear, empirically grounded concepôÙ
tualisation of what success looks like in the Zebra paradigm remains absent from
the academic literature.
Founders working outside the Unicorn mould may already be redefining success
in practice, yet their experiences and values are underexplored in academia esôÙ
pecially. As we will demonstrate later, the literature on startôÙup success tends
to replicate unicornôÙcentric assumptions, with limited engagement in alternative
paradigms.
To address these gaps, this thesis examines how founders define and experience
success, and analyses how success is conceptualised in academic literature on
startôÙups and related fields such as social entrepreneurship and impactôÙdriven
business. After this, the goal is to propose a conceptualisation of success tailored
to the Zebra movement, grounded in founder narratives and informed by broader
theoretical perspectives.

Literature Review
As already mentioned in our introductory chapter, Unicorns and Zebras are two
contrasting philosophies within the startôÙup jungle (De Gennaro et al., 2023). This
jungle has been introduced, as there are many different ways of building a startôÙup,
and as media and venture capital have started categorising ventures metaphoriôÙ
cally based on their distinct behavioural patterns and characteristics (CastroôÙSilva
et al., 2019). Moreover, Kollmann and PrûÑpper (2025) discuss how this method
of classification can offer a nuanced perspective on the foundersã disposition in
relation to their goals, visions, and attractiveness to investors.
Although both Unicorns and Zebras fall under the broader startôÙup umbrella, their
values and vision diverge significantly in the narratives around them. The Unicorn
model, centred around reaching a valuation of one billion USD, promotes a narrow
definition of success (A. Lee, 2013). As discussed in the introduction, this framing
leaves little room for alternative interpretations of success, as it focuses primarily
on valuation and largely overlooks questions of longôÙterm viability and societal
impact (Kuckertz et al., 2023).
Zebras, by contrast, were created as a direct response to the Unicorn paradigm
and were designed with more facets in mind (Zepeda, 2017). Their goal of being
profitable while also serving a further purpose can show a more varied idea of
success (Zepeda, 2017). Unlike Unicorns, which are defined by a single metric,
Zebras open up space for a diversity of goals, such as those related to sustainôÙ
ability, community wellbeing, or ethical governance (Zepeda, 2017). However,
this openness also makes it more challenging to discuss success for Zebras, as
success is no longer tied to a single quantifiable endpoint, and there is yet no clear
objective to reach.
This difficulty also points to a broader issue in how success tends to be underôÙ
stood, the tyranny of the quantifiable (Brandel & Zepeda, 2016ëƒ Solnit, 2014),
meaning that what can be measured tends to take priority over what cannot (BranôÙ
del & Zepeda, 2016), such as enjoyment, meaning or the public good (Solnit,
2014). This critique highlights how startôÙup success is often framed in terms of valôÙ
uation while less tangible yet equally vital elements, such as sustainability, longôÙ
term impact, and founder wellôÙbeing, are routinely sidelined (A. Lee, 2013ëƒ Zebras
Unite, 2017).
In this chapter, we will delve deeper into the current discourse around the Zebra
movement to analyse what makes a Zebra a Zebra. This is done to later conôÙ
ceptualise a definition of success that is built around the idea of Zebras instead
of Unicorns. Having a clearer definition of Zebra success might help Zebras and
startôÙups in general, by making it easier to spot these alternative success stories
within the startôÙup environment and ultimately also create broader representation.
As Zepeda and Brandel (2019) mention, ãYou canãt be it if you canãt see it.ã We
thus aim to enhance the visibility of these success stories and startôÙup types.

2.1
Properties of a Zebra
Given the limited presence of the Zebra movement in academic discourse, it is
currently defined primarily through columns, blogs, and broader media coverage.
Notably, the concept of Unicorns was similarly introduced (A. Lee, 2013). In this
section, we are now collecting properties commonly used to describe what a Zebra
is within the currently available discourse to later pin down how success could be
conceptualised for this new movement.
2.1.1
Purpose
The main idea behind a Zebra venture is to fuse profit and purpose and not to
sacrifice one for the other (Rice, 2020ëƒ Zebras Unite, 2017). De Gennaro et al.
(2023) take this idea further by describing Zebras as adopting a socioeconomic
perspective, being forôÙprofit while also helping communities, supporting people,
promoting diversity, and fostering longôÙterm progress.
2.1.2
Community
As the above statement makes clear, another cornerstone of the idea behind ZeôÙ
bras is community. This is also touched on by Brandel and Zepeda (2016), pointôÙ
ing out that the focus for Zebras lies on the value for users, elevating communities
and building more equitable and inclusive systems. Zepeda and Brandel (2019)
elaborate further on this by mentioning shared prosperity and social good as more
desirable for Zebras than Silicon Valleyãs ãwinnerôÙtakeôÙallã mentality, which will be
further discussed in Section 2.1.3.
Cristofaro et al. (2023) agrees with the importance of these factors, emphasising
the principles of mutualism and resilience, acknowledging how Zebras challenge
the conventional focus on profit maximisation. Along the same line, OneCFO
(2024) mentions how Zebras prioritise business sustainability without sacrificing
social impact by promoting user success, rather than focusing on growing user
acquisition, as is typical for Unicorns.
De Gennaro et al. (2023) further elaborates through their study on Generation Z
entrepreneurs, discussing that these Gen Z, Zebra founders pay close attention to
workôÙlife balance, mental and work wellôÙbeing, value activities that create a sense
of community, and consider what benefits beyond pay a company can bring to
itself and to society.
2.1.3
Cooperative vs. Competition
Zebras are following an ãallôÙtakesôÙitôÙallã market vision, which means that numerous
ventures share a space within a market (Kollmann & PrûÑpper, 2025). This idea
aligns with why the term Zebra was chosen in the first place, as described by ZeôÙ
bras Unite (2017). The animal is mutuliastic, meaning that Zebras band together
in groups to protect and preserve one another. It is discussed that their individual
input results in stronger collective output (Zebras Unite, 2017). With this longôÙterm

value enhancement perspective, Zebras are bringing a strong willingness to coopôÙ
erate with competitors and also any other organisations (De Gennaro et al., 2023ëƒ
Kollmann & PrûÑpper, 2025). Thus, they have a strong market tolerance towards
competitors (Kollmann & PrûÑpper, 2025). Rice (2020) further elaborates that ZeôÙ

2.1.4
Organizational Structure
When considering organisational structures aligned with the Zebra paradigm, the
TEAL model emerges as a fitting framework. This paradigm represents a new,
emerging, and highly flexible organisational system inspired by a decentralised
structure. It is composed of small teams that take responsibility for different projects.
In principle, the actions of the people are not guided by orders from above them
following a chain of command, but they are guided by listening to the purpose of
the organisation (De Gennaro et al., 2023). Cristofaro et al. (2023) discussed that
adopting this organisational structure can lead to a more sustainable and resilient
business model.
2.1.5
Growth
Exponential growth over a short period of time has been discussed in relation
to company success, which is currently characterised by Unicorn startôÙups (KollôÙ
mann & PrûÑpper, 2025). They combine this notion of company success and ãproôÙ

businesses (Kollmann & PrûÑpper, 2025). In contrast to that, longôÙterm growth,
with prioritisation of building a startôÙup slowly and sustainably over a long period
of time, has received little attention so far, despite its importance (Kollmann &
PrûÑpper, 2025). OneCFO (2024) discusses that the focus on growth and valuaôÙ
tion can be detrimental to a founderãs health, as they can burn out. According to
OneCFO (2024), it can also lead to founders sacrificing their values to strive for
this notion of success. Therefore, the pressure of meeting these Unicorn valuaôÙ
tion targets can compromise the companyãs values and prioritise shortôÙterm gains
over longôÙterm sustainability (OneCFO, 2024).
Given its opposition to UnicornôÙaligned values, the Zebra paradigm promotes a
model of growth that is sustainable and oriented toward longôÙterm resilience (KollôÙ
mann & PrûÑpper, 2025).

2.1.6
Financing
As Zebras are not reliant on external valuation, they can follow a more flexible
approach to funding and can utilise different possibilities and combinations of
debt, equity and revenueôÙbased financing (Folsom, 2024). Even when receiving
money from investors, with their strong founderôÙrelated ownership, which will be
discussed in the next section, Zebras might ultimately aim for investors to exit with

of financing also enables them to focus on a more steadyôÙgrowth revenue and

2.1.7
Ownership
As already mentioned in the previous section, Zebras focus on founderôÙrelated
ownership (Kollmann & PrûÑpper, 2025) and therefore tend to avoid trading eqôÙ

2024). This could potentially be due to the fact that according to Zepeda and
Brandel (2019) evidence proves, that ventureôÙbacked companies breed toxic culôÙ
ture, worker exploitation and homogeneity both in leadership and shareholders.
All things the Zebra movement is working against.
Kollmann and PrûÑpper (2025) summarises the Zebra model as driven by ambiôÙ
tions of steady, longôÙterm value creation, sustained ownership, and cooperative
practices. In contrast, the Unicorn model is characterised by shortôÙterm exponenôÙ
tial growth, a focus on exit strategies, and competitive market dominance.

Justifications and Limitations of the Research

This study employed a twoôÙphase research design: a systematic literature review
using the PRISMA framework and a qualitative thematic analysis (TA) of interôÙ

The use of PRISMA was inspired by prior work by Zaheer et al. (2019), who foôÙ
cused on similar objectives. PRISMA was used as a structured method to gain an

The method of the systematic literature review was determined and implemented
before the research aim was shifted from identifying biases to conceptualising
alternative success metrics. This shift was carried out not only due to the backôÙ
ground interviews, as discussed in Section 3.2.1, but also because of the findings
of the literature review using PRISMA.
Although PRISMA provided transparency and replicability, it was not optimally
aligned with the final goal of this thesis, namely, the proposal of a novel success
framework. PRISMA is primarily designed for aggregating and synthesising existôÙ
ing evidence, not for generating theoretical insightsëƒ therefore, a more interpretive
or integrative review method may have been more appropriate.
The conducted literature review also brought up another limitation. As the excluôÙ
sion and inclusion criteria for the systematic literature review were created before
questioning the mainstream narrative around startôÙups and the Unicorn paradigm
in their entirety, articles were required to define startôÙups as more than a new or
small venture. This, in turn, could have caused a skewed perception of the current
academic discourse towards the current norms and prevented new alternatives
already discussed from being visible at the end of this process.
Moreover, as Critical Success Factors were initially collected to discuss biases in
success metrics, they were grouped on practical dimensions, like founder, prodôÙ
uct and funding, rather than theoretical paradigms, i.e. Unicorn vs. Zebra. While
these findings were still useful to show the framing around startôÙup success, future
work could investigate these CSFs more closely and discuss their alignment with

traditional and alternative metrics are already used in combination, and the proôÙ
posed conceptualisation of Zebraãs success in this thesis is already visible in pracôÙ
tice.
Thematic analysis was chosen for the empirical phase due to its flexibility in capôÙ
