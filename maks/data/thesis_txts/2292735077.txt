Abstract 
In Denmark, the general policy is to encourage recycling of waste products, such as wastewater 
sludge, on agricultural land, as they have a nutritional value and can improve the soil quality. The 
utilization of waste products as fertilizer in the agricultural industry is, however, only applicable as 
long as it does not contain critical levels of xenobiotic compounds. Chemical compounds from 
anthropogenic sources are led, through the wastewater, to the wastewater treatment plants, where the 
various types of compounds are concentrated in the sludge during the wastewater treatment.  
 
The purpose of the present Master Thesis is to examine the toxicity of wastewater sludge. To test the 
toxicity of the wastewater sludge, while including mixture effects and interactions between matrix and 
compounds, a method was developed combining the algal growth inhibition test with a direct contact 
exposure approach. Sludge samples from six wastewater treatment plants were tested along with one 
sample of degassed manure from a biogas plant. The freshwater algae Pseudokirchneriella 
Subcapitata was used as test organism in the growth inhibition test, and to support the toxicity 
assessment, the toxicity of the sludge was furthermore tested on the bacteria Vibrio fischeri by 
including the BioTox test. Additionally, preliminary effect-directed analysis (EDA) was performed in 
order to clarify which groups of toxic compounds were responsible for the observed phytotoxic effect. 
For this purpose, Solid Phase Extraction (SPE) was performed to retain specific compound groups 
prior to toxicity testing, on samples from two locations using two different sorbents (activated carbon 
and a cation exchanger). The change in toxicity did thereby provide knowledge about the compound 
groups responsible for the toxic effects observed.  
 
A reduction of algal growth rate was observed upon exposure to sludge from all seven locations, but 
with differences in the level of toxicity, expressed as EC50s ranging from 50 mg/l to 648 mg/L. The 
trends of the EC50s from the algal test were supported by the results of the less sensitive BioTox test. 
The concentration-response plots of the developed algal growth inhibition test indicated differences in 
the composition of compounds responsible for the toxicity between the samples from the different 
locations. This was illustrated by variations in the shape and slope of the sigmoid curves, indicating 
differences in sorption coefficients of the toxic compounds present. Preliminary EDA clarified this, 
and it was illustrated, that while toxicity of one sample only to a small extend could be explained by 
retaining compounds on the chosen sorbents, toxicity of the other sample could be explained as 
organic compounds and cations. By combining an algal growth inhibition test in a direct contact 
exposure setup with preliminary EDA, a solid assessment of the toxicity was obtained, and the 
importance of testing the toxicity by including the entire matrix was underlined.

1 Introduction 
Chemicals of various types are important and indispensable in modern society. Chemicals used in the 
every day life and the industry are discharged by the wastewater to the wastewater treatment plants 
(WWTPs), where the water is cleaned, and organic matter is gathered in wastewater sludge 
(Lynettefællesskabet I/S n.d.). Wastewater sludge is often applied as a fertilizer on agricultural land, 
and in this case the sludge is referred to as “biosolids”. In 2009, 52% of the produced Danish sludge 
was applied directly on agricultural fields (Miljøstyrelsen 2013).  
 
As the general policy both within EU and Denmark encourage that the wastewater sludge is recycled 
on agricultural land as biosolids (European Comission 2015; Miljøministeriet 2006), the 
environmental consequences of this substrate is relevant to continuously investigate. 
 
The wastewater sludge will, because of its many inputs, be an unspecified mixture of compounds and 
interactions will occur between compounds. Furthermore, a high level of organic matter will result in 
interactions between the xenobiotic compounds and the sludge matrix, and complications in relation to 
exposure concentrations can arise when testing the toxicity of the sludge in aqueous solution. To 
address the complications of performing toxicity tests on this complex substance, the direct contact 
exposure approach is ideal, as this includes the entire matrix in the test setup. This approach will both 
embrace the potential mixture effects as well as matrix-xenobiotic interactions.  
 
The BioTox test is and rapid a cost effective bioassay, and the test can provide additional strength to 
the toxicity assessment, by demonstrating the toxicity of the sludge on a organism of a additional 
organism domain. 
 
To supplement the non-target direct contact exposer approach, preliminary effect-directed analysis 
(EDA) can be a useful tool to clarify which compound types in the sludge mixture, is causing the toxic 
effects (Brack 2003). By retaining specific compounds using Solid Phase Extraction (SPE), and 
comparing toxicity before and after SPE, knowledge about the groups of toxic compounds, present in 
the sludge, will be obtained. 
 
In the following section the relevant theory used in the present project is described, both in regards to 
the toxicity tests performed, but also in regard to wastewater sludge and degassed manure and the 
environmental implications of agricultural application of these.

1.1 Method development of algal growth inhibition test 
To investigate the toxicity of the wastewater sludge, a method has been developed on basis of the ISO 
guideline (8692 Water quality – Freshwater algal growth inhibition test with unicellular green algae), 
which is widely used for toxicity testing. The test is based on the fact that the algae are exponentially 
growing under normal conditions. A reduction in growth rate at increasing concentration can be the 
basis of assessment of the toxicity of a compound (OECD 2002).  The response of the test is not 
influenced by individual tolerance, but of the tolerance of the entire inoculum, as the algal cells are 
clones of each other (Christensen et al. 2009). 
 
The algal growth inhibition test is a chronic test, where the toxicity of one or more chemicals can be 
tested during a timespan of typically between 24 to 96 hours. The experimental uncertainty increases 
with increasing exposure period, and Fu et al (2015) found that 48 hours was a more sensitive 
exposure period than any longer time spans. 
 
The test organism is the freshwater alga Pseudokirchneriella Subcapitata, which is extensively used 
for toxicity testing, as it is regarded as a rather sensitive specie (Alexander and Hughes 1993 in Lewis 
1995). Algae are primary producers in the aquatic ecosystem, and they are thereby an important group 
of organisms, both as feed for higher trophic levels and because of their conversion of CO2 to O2 
during photosynthesis. The test alga P. Subcapitata belongs to the class Chlorophyceae, which is the 
largest class within the phylum of the green algae (Raven et al. 2005; Guiry 2015). This alga has a 
large surface area-to-volume ratio, as it is a unicellular organism. P. Subcapitata is furthermore a 
planktonic organism which reproduces asexually (Guiry 2015; Olesen 2009). 
 
All photosensitizing organisms contain chlorophyll, as the absorption of light by these pigments drives 
the photosynthesis (Taiz & Zeiger 2006). The green algae contains the accessory pigments chlorophyll 
b and carotenoids besides the most abundant chlorophyll a (Kirk 1994; Raven et al. 2005). When a 
chlorophyll pigment absorb light, it makes a transition from its ground state to its exited state, and the 
energy can hereafter be transferred to the reaction center of the photosynthetic apparatus, where the 
chemical reaction of photosynthesis is driven by this energy (Taiz & Zeiger 2006).  
 
Fluorescence is the reaction where the photon of the exited chlorophyll is reemitted instead of 
transferred to the reaction center. As illustrated in Figure 1, the wavelength of the light reemitted as 
fluorescence, will be will be longer than the wavelength of the absorbed light, as some of the energy, 
from the light initially absorbed, will be lost as heat (Taiz & Zeiger 2006).

When performing algal toxicity test, fluorescence is a good estimate of biomass. The fluorescence 
signal from chlorophyll a is the largest contributor to the signal, and it is thereby necessary to have a 
stable chlorophyll a/biomass ratio. It is worth noticing that when performing the algal growth 
inhibition test, the fluorescence is measured in vitro and not in vivo, as there are some limitations to 
measuring fluorescence in the living cell (Mayer et al. 1997).  In the living alga cell, the chlorophyll 
pigments are coupled to the reaction center, and only about 1% of the absorbed light is therefor 
reemitted (Mayer et al. 1997; Kirk 1994). Furthermore, the florescence signal of the living cell is 
controlled by the light conditions the algae have been incubated at (Putt et al. 1987). When the 
fluorescence is measured in vitro, the chlorophyll has been extracted from the cell and uncoupled from 
the biochemical processes of the reaction center, which results in a more stable and sensitive test 
response (Mayer et al. 1997). 
 
Some of the parameters modified in the development of a suitable algal test, for testing the toxicity of 
sludge, will be introduced in the following sections.  
1.1.1 
Light 
Light is an important source of energy for photosynthesising organisms, and it is important to secure 
that the algae are light saturated during a toxicity test, as light limitations can result in a decrease in 
growth rate an thereby in a overestimation of the toxicity. Furthermore, algae growing at their 
maximum growth rate, exhibit a higher sensitivity than those growing slower at light limitation 
(Cleuvers & Weyers 2003; Mayer et al. 1998) This is mostly an issue when dealing with a coloured 
substance (Baun et al. 2002) like biosolids. Shading can furthermore affect the chlorophyll/biomass 
ratio and it is in general important to keep the light conditions homogenous to secure a stable 
==PAGE:12==
 
12
fluorescence/biomass ratio (Mayer et al. 1997). The light intensity in the suspension can be affected by 
two means: Increasing illumination or decreasing the length of the light path (Cleuvers & Weyers 
2003). In the present project illumination was increased to secure that the algal was subjected to 
sufficient light intensities. 
 
The growth rate of the algae will increase with increasing irradiance until an upper limit is reached, 
and the irradiation is excessed (Nyholm & Peterson 1997). It is worth noticing, that a too high light 
intensity can be problematic as well. Under conditions of excessed irradiation, the growth of the algae 
can be photoinhibited, by the excessed excited chlorophyll damaging the membranes of the 
photosynthetic system, to an extend beyond the algae’s ability to repair (Lambers et al. 2008).  
1.1.2 
Background signal 
It can be necessary to increase the fluorescence signal of the test organism, if a strong background 
signal is observed in the tested matrix. This can be accomplished by increasing the algae density 
beyond the standard density (104 cells/mL, ISO 2004). In the present project the algal density was 
increased by five times, to drown out a high background fluorescence from the sludge.  
 
There are, however, some issues when increasing the algae density. Firstly, by increasing the density, 
the risk of self shading increases (Nyholm & Peterson 1997). Secondly, by increasing the density the 
risk of physiochemical changes to the algae medium increases. And thirdly, by depleting the CO2 
supply, the pH will increase, which not only affect the growth rate directly, but also indirectly by 
affecting the accessibility and toxicity of the compounds tested (Nyholm & Peterson 1997; Mayer et 
al. 1997). 
1.1.3 
Closed algae-test 
The test system includes three phases, as there, besides the solid and water phase, also exists an air 
phase. Compounds with high Henrys law constant (KH) are volatile, and are thereby likely to escape, 
once dissolved. Hydrophobic compounds will because of their high octanol-water portioning 
coefficient (kow), tend to accumulate in the living organisms (in this case the algal cell) and thereby 
exhibit high toxicity at low aqueous concentrations. These hydrophobic compounds can however have 
a tendency to escape and the concentration will thereby decrease, which can affect the toxicity of the 
sample (Mayer et al. 2000). To prevent the volatile compounds from escaping the test system, a closed 
test can be performed (Halling-Sørensen et al. 1996), which was the approach used in the present 
project.  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
13
As algae utilizes CO2 for their photosynthesis, it is necessary to ensure that their CO2 demands are met 
in the closed system. This is important, in order to ensure growth and to prevent the pH from drifting. 
HCO3
- will convert to CO2 and OH- if the CO2 in the aqueous phase is not renewed by mass transfer 
from headspace, and the pH will drift (Mayer et al. 1998). To avoid CO2 depletion and secure a stable 
pH, headspace can be enriched with CO2 and the concentration of HCO3
- in the medium can be 
increased (Halling-Sørensen et al. 1996). For compounds with high KH, it should however be noted, 
that that they will escape to headspace, until an equilibrium is reached, and the concentration in the 
suspension will thereby still be diluted (Mayer et al. 2000). 
1.1.4 
Direct contact exposure 
When testing toxicity of a matrix, there are several approaches to how it can be executed. It can 
however be challenging mimicking realistic effective concentration of the different compounds. By 
testing an aqueous extract, the toxicity is often underestimated (Harkey et al. 1994 in Baun et al. 
2002), on the other hand, by extracting the compounds with an organic solvent, the toxicity might very 
well be overestimated (Alexander 1995). To get a realistic assessment of the toxicity, direct contact 
exposure can be the right approach, as the occurrence of the entire matrix in the solution will mimic 
the accessible toxicity to a larger extend than by testing an elutriate or an extract (Baun et al. 2002). 
Baun et al. (2002) found that the toxicity assessment was 2.5-3000 times higher when using a direct 
contact exposure setup as apposed to testing the corresponding aqous extract. This approach was 
adopted and used in the present project, where the entire sludge matrix was included in the test setup. 
 
In a complex matrix like biosolids, many compounds with different chemical properties are present. 
The effective concentration of a compound towards the algal is the freely dissolved concentration, 
which is a result of the phase distribution between the biosolid matrix and the aqueous phase 
(Reichenberg & Mayer 2006).

By including the entire matrix in the test suspension, the exposure parameter is the freely dissolved

Time can furthermore alter the composition of the compounds freely dissolved, as the more 
hydrophobic compounds will need a longer period before they reach equilibrium, in relation to the less 
hydrophobic compounds (Cornelissen et al. 1997). This means that the concentration of the most 
hydrophobic compounds might not have reached equilibrium in the test period and the freely dissolved 
concentration in the aqueous phase will be underestimated (ter Laak et al. 2005). 
 
By using the direct exposure approach and including the entire sludge matrix, to buffer the aqueous 
exposure concentrations in the test system, it is possible to test the complex mixture of compounds, 
while securing realistic effective concentrations of the compounds across sorption factors. When using 
this approach, any potential mixture effects will furthermore be included, in the overall assessment of 
the toxicity. 
1.1.5 
Data treatment 
When describing concentration-response data of the algal growth inhibition tests, it is important to 
chose the right model, as the assessment of the toxicity becomes more powerful with lower 
uncertainties (Christensen et al. 2009). 
 
One of two models is typically used to describe such concentration-response data. The logistic model 
can be used for the purpose (Cedergreen & Streibig 2005), but the Weibull model is at least as 
appropriate for describing the data of the algal growth inhibition test (Christensen & Nyholm 1984; 
Christensen et al. 2009).  In the statistical program R used in the present project for data treatment, the 
Weibull model is divided between the Weibull 1 and Weibull 2, where the Weibull 1 has a steeper 
decrease towards the lower limit and the Weibull 2 is steeper towards the upper limit (Ritz & Streibig 
2012).  
 
In the present project the model used to describe the algal growth inhibition data is a three-parameter 
Weibull 2 model. In the three-parameter Weibull model the lower limit of the model is set to zero, and 
the additional parameters describe the upper limit, the slope and the infliction point, which represents 
the concentration of which the function is asymmetric around (Ritz n.d.; Ritz & Streibig 2012). On 
basis of the model, the effective concentration can be estimated, which is the point where half of the 
test organisms are affected (EC50) (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003). In this case the EC50 is the point where 
the growth rate of the biomass is reduced by 50%.

1.2 BioTox 
The BioTox test is a bacterial bioassay, based on the bioluminescent marine bacteria Vibrio fischeri 
(ABOATOX n.d.), which is commonly used to access acute toxicity of hydrophilic chemicals or 
complex mixtures. The test has the advantages of being both rapid and cost effective as well as 
sensitive in comparison with other bacterial tests based on luminescence (Kokkali & van Delft 2014; 
Parvez et al. 2006).  
 
The basis of the test is the bioluminescence emitted by the bacteria in the range between 475 and 540 
nm (Parvez et al. 2006). The luminescence is emitted from the bioluminescent enzyme system of the 
cell, as a by-product of the cells normal metabolism (Ma et al. 2014; Kokkali & van Delft 2014). The 
intensity of the light emission is a measure of the metabolic state of the bacteria, an decrease in light 
intensity is thereby a measure of inhibition of the metabolic system (Ma et al. 2014). 
 
There are some limitations to the BioTox test. First of all, the bacteria have low sensitivity towards 
heavy metals (Teodorovic et al. 2009), and when it comes to different types of antibiotics, the 
response of the bacteria is selective (Ma et al. 2014). The sensitivity of the test depends, not only on 
the chemical structure of the toxic compounds, but also on the physicochemical parameters of the 
tested mixture, such as pH, Total Inorganic Carbon (TIC) or Total Nitrogen (TN). Low concentrations 
of toxic compounds or nontoxic nutrient furthermore stimulate the luminescence of V. fischeri, as seen 
in other organisms. A stimulation of the luminescence can mask a toxic response or even result in a 
hormetic response (Ma et al. 2014). Reproducibility of the test can be impaired if the tested solution 
contain undissolved, slightly soluble or volatile compounds, and furthermore can luminescence be lost 
when testing coloured or turbid samples, and this will affect the result of the test (ISO 1996). 
 
By including the BioTox test in the present project, an additional organism domain is included, and 
the inclusion of multiple types of organisms creates a more powerful toxicity assessment. 
 
1.3 Preliminary effect-direct analysis 
EDA combines chemical analysis and physiochemical fractionation with biological analysis in order 
to provide information on compounds causing toxic effects in a complex mixture (Brack 2003). In the 
present project, only preliminary EDA was performed, and no chemical analysis were included. As 
illustrated in Figure 3, the preliminary part of EDA consists of the combination of biological analysis 
and fractionation. In the present project the biological analysis was represented as an algal growth 
inhibition test, whereas the fractionation was performed by SPE.

SPE is a sample preparation technique mostly used for concentration of an analyte or to remove 
impurities before doing analytical measurements (Majors 2010). The principal of SPE is based on the 
percolation of a sample through a solid sorbent, and the partitioning of a compound between the liquid 
solution and the solid sorbent (Camel 2003; Berrueta et al. 1995). To successfully remove a specific 
compound or group of compounds, the compounds must have a greater affinity towards the solid 
phase than the sample solution (Berrueta et al. 1995). The distribution coefficient for a compound (A) 
is given by the distribution coefficient KA:

There exists many types of sorbents one can choose, depending on the target of the analysis and the 
properties of the sample solution (pH, ionic strength, stability ect.) (Majors 2010). The sorbents are 
classified as either non-polar, polar or ion exchange sorbents (Berrueta et al. 1995). In the following, 
two sorbents will be addressed: An activated carbon- and a silica based cation exchange sorbent.

While SPE is most often used in combination with analytical measurements, the technique can, 
because of its potential to retain specific components, be a useful technique in toxicity assessment of 
complex mixtures. When the technique is used to eliminate a certain compound group prior to a 
toxicity test, the possible change in toxicity can provide knowledge about the compound groups 
responsible for the toxic effects. 
 
In the present project SPE have been performed on the sludge elutriate and on an aqueous extract of 
the solid sludge. These two liquids were chosen, as only liquids can percolate through the sorbents. It 
should however be noticed, that the toxicity of the aqueous extract can be underestimated, as the solid 
fraction wont be present to buffered the exposure concentrations, as previously described. 
1.3.1 
Activated carbon 
Activated carbon is a material prepared from vegetable charcoal, and it is often used to sorb 
compounds, not only as a SPE-sorbent, but also in remediation contexts (Gonzalez-Serrano et al. 
2004; Rakowska et al. 2014; Yu et al. 2014). The sorbent is known to have a strong sorption capacity 
for organic compounds because of its very large surface area (Camel 2003). There exist two types of 
sorption sites on the activated carbon sorbet. Compounds can be retained on the activated carbon by 
van der Waals forces on graphite-like basal planes, or by ionic interactions of hydrogen bonding at 
present polar groups (Hutchinson & Schilt 1983). 
1.3.2 
Cation exchange 
A cation exchange sorbent is categorised as an ion exchange sorbent and it will retain cations from the 
sample solution. This means that metal ions and other positively charged compounds will be retained 
on the sorbent. A cation exchange sorbents can either be strong or weak. In the weak cation 
exchanger, the ion exchange sites will only be present at pH larger than pKa of the compound, whereas 
the ion exchange sites always will be present in the strong cation exchange sorbent (Camel 2003).  
 
The typical cation exchange sorbent is silica based and chemically modified with an ion exchange 
group. The silica is in general characterized by a high sorption capacity for metal ions. The active sites 
of a strong cation exchange sorbent is typically sulfonic acid groups (Camel 2003; Anthemidis et al. 
2012).   
 
1.4 Wastewater treatment  
Wastewater from industrial and domestic sources enters along with drain water, the WWTPs. Here 
mechanical, biological and chemical processes clean the wastewater, and two end products are 
==PAGE:19==

produced; cleaned water and sludge (Miljøstyrelsen 2013). Xenobiotic components that exist in the 
influent wastewater are likely to be recovered in the cleaned wastewater, the sludge or in the air, 
depending on their properties and degradability (Miljøstyrelsen 2006).  
1.4.1 
Treatment processes 
As illustrated in Figure 4, the typical type of treatment processes, of the WWTPs included in this 
project is mechanical, biological and chemical removal of unwanted components. This type of 
wastewater treatment is much in line with the general trend in at the Danish WWTPs (Miljøstyrelsen 
2006). There are however small differences between plants.

Mechanical removal is the first step in the treatment process (Figure 4-1). During the mechanical 
treatment cloths, cue tips and other large objects are removed on a grid. Hereafter sand and fat is 
removed from the wastewater (Hedensted spildevand 2013; Lynettefællesskabet I/S n.d.; Energi

The microorganisms naturally occurring in the wastewater conduct the biological degradation, as 
illustrated in Figure 4-2 (Lynettefællesskabet I/S n.d.). Under aerobic conditions, the organic content 
is degraded to carbon dioxide (CO2) and water, and ammonium is degraded to nitrate. The nitrate can 
then be degraded further anaerobically to free nitrogen (Hedensted spildevand 2013; 
Lynettefællesskabet I/S n.d.).

Phosphor is mainly removed biologically by anaerobe processes, but in many cases it is necessary to 
supplement the biological phosphor removal with chemical removal. This is done by adding 
aluminium chloride, ferric chloride or ferrous sulphate, which binds the phosphor and precipitate 
(Figure 4-3) (Hedensted spildevand 2013; Energi Viborg A/S n.d.).  
 
After the mechanical, biological and sometimes chemical treatment processes, the sludge will 
precipitate and the cleaned wastewater can be oxygenated and let out in the environment. Some of the 
precipitated sludge will be led back in the system, to participate in further biological degradation, 
while the excess sludge will be collected and led further in the system (Figure 4-4) (Hedensted 
spildevand 2013). As illustrated in Figure 4-5, the excess sludge will, at many plants, be led to 
digestion tanks, where the sludge will be anaerobic stabilized for several weeks, by acid-forming- and 
methane forming bacteria, and the gasses are collected as biogas (Lynettefællesskabet I/S n.d.; Jensen 
& Lomstein 2011).  
 
In some cases the sludge will skip the digestion step and be used for agricultural application after it 
has precipitated (Hedensted spildevand 2013), and in some cases the sludge will be used for 
agricultural application after digestion. In both cases, the sludge will typically be dewatered, to save 
money on transportation (Figure 4-6) (Miljøstyrelsen 2013). To enhance the effect of the dewatering, 
and increase the dry weight further, polymer will be added at this step.  
 
Some WWTPs cannot utilize their sludge for agricultural purposes as it can contain unwanted 
compounds, and this sludge is then often burned (Miljøstyrelsen 2006). Another possibility is to 
mineralize the sludge before agricultural application. Sludge mineralisation takes approximately 10 
years, and there is expected a significant reduction in xenobiotic compounds (Miljøstyrelsen 2013; 
Hedeselskabet 2000). Sludge from small facilities is typically brought to mineralization, where as 
sludge from large facilities often is digested (Miljøstyrelsen 2013). The digested sludge will typically 
have a lower carbon content than non-digested sludge, which reflects a lower content of organic 
matter, and thereby affects the ability of the sludge to bind certain compounds such as heavy metals 
and organic compounds. 
 
Compounds like chlorine, gasoline, oil but also salt can be harmful for the biological processes, even 
in small amounts (Hedensted spildevand 2013; Miljøstyrelsen 2006). The nitrification process is the 
most sensitive process, but the biological processes in the digestion tanks can also be affected by 
compounds from the wastewater. Inhibition of the microorganisms in the digestion tanks is however 
==PAGE:21==
 
21
rare in Denmark. An inhibition of the microorganisms at a WWTP will consequently affect the 
WWTPs ability to clean the wastewater (Miljøstyrelsen 2006). 
 
1.5 Biosolids 
In Denmark, the general policy encourages the recycling of waste products, such as wastewater sludge 
on agricultural land, as long as they have a nutritional value and can improve the soil quality. 
According to Danish legislation it is allowed to apply 7 tons sludge/ha/year (calculated as an average 
over 10 years) (Miljøministeriet 2006). This is however only applicable as long as they do not contain 
critical levels of xenobiotic compounds (Miljøstyrelsen 2013).  
 
Biosolids are rich in several nutrients essential to plants, such as nitrogen, potassium and phosphor, 
and as phosphor is a limited resource, the biosolids are considered a valuable source of this nutrient 
(Miljøstyrelsen 2013). Biosolids can however contain xenobiotic compounds, and these compounds 
enter the environment when the biosolids are distributed on the fields. In the sludge amendment some 
limit values for different compounds, likely to be present in the biosolids are listed. Many heavy 
metals and some organic compounds are regulated under the sludge amendment (Miljøministeriet 
2006). The limit values of the sludge amendment are displayed in Table 1.

1.5.1 
Xenobiotic content of biosolids 
Chemical contaminants in wastewater derive from a variety of diffuse and point sources. As described 
in Figure 5, the diffuse sources are the atmospheric deposition in the runoff water, whereas the point 
sources are the domestic and industrial sources.

There are more than a 100 million chemical registered in the Chemical Abstract Registry (CAS, 2015), 
with over 143,000 of these preregistered under REACH (Euoropean Chemicals Agency n.d.). REACH 
is a regulation adopted by the European Union that serves to improve the protection of the 
environment and human health by registering all chemical substances along with risk assessments of 
the individual compounds, provided by the industry. Reflected in the large number of preregistered 
chemicals, many can enter the wastewater. As wastewater sludge is a known sink for hydrophobic 
chemicals and heavy metals, some of these compounds are most likely present in the biosolids 
(Venkatesan & Halden 2014; Boutrup et al. 2006). The content in the biosolids is depended of the 
xenobiotic content in the wastewater, but also of the degradation proses of the WWTP (Miljøstyrelsen 
2013).  
 
The Danish Environmental Protection Agency (Miljøstyrelsen) and the National Environmental 
Research Institute (the former DMU) examined the content of xenobiotics and heavy metals in the 
aqueous environment, hereunder wastewater sludge, from 1998-2003 (Boutrup et al. 2006). They 
==PAGE:23==
 
23
found that the sludge contained both heavy metals, pesticides, phenols, phthalates, detergents, ethers 
and PAHs (polyaromatic hydrocarbons). Many of the compounds were furthermore present in higher 
concentrations in the sludge than in the wastewater, because of their hydrophobic properties or 
tendency to bind to the organic matter in the sludge (Boutrup et al. 2006).  
 
Metals in wastewater originate mainly from industrial sources, and as they bind to organic matter, they 
are accumulated in the wastewater sludge. Especially cobber, nickel and zinc are often found in high 
amounts in biosolids, but also cadmium and mercury can be present (Boutrup et al. 2006). 
 
In general, 20-40% of the organic compounds in influent wastewater are degraded in the WWTP 
(Hijaz 2007 in Smith 2009), which means that some of these compounds are likely to be present in the 
biosolids. Many of the compounds in the sludge are only present in low amounts, but some 
compounds like, DEHP (di(2-ethylhexyl)phthalate), PAH and NP (nonylphenol) can be present in 
problematic concentrations, exceeding the Danish limit values (Boutrup et al. 2006). Also NPE 
(nonylphenol ethoxylates) and LAS (linear alkylbenzene sulphonate) can be present in large amounts 
in the sludge (Smith 2009).  
 
Pharmaceuticals and personal care products (PCPs) are a group of organic compounds, which have 
received some attention in relation to biosolids recycling, and recycling of biosolids are considered an 
important mechanism of release of these compounds in the environment (McClellan & Halden 2010). 
When a pharmaceutical is consumed, the compound is rarely taken up 100% in the body. 
Pharmaceuticals can conjugated to a more hydrophilic form, within the human body. A part of the 
pharmaceutical will thereby enter the WWTP as a conjugated transformation product, but can during 
the waste water treatment be deconjugated and thereby return to its original form (Lishman et al. 2006; 
Mogensen et al. 2008). The PCPs and pharmaceuticals in waste water mainly originate from domestic 
sources (Ingvertsen et al. 2010), and some PCPs are found in rather large amounts in the biosolids 
(Venkatesan & Halden 2014).  
 
There is some concern considering emerging compounds, which are not yet regulated. Especially 
brominated flame retardant and nanoparticles could be some of the compounds potential to pose an 
environmental threat in the future, and the knowledge of the environmental fate and possible adverse 
effects of distribution of these compounds through agricultural biosolid application are limited 
(Ingvertsen et al. 2010; Venkatesan & Halden 2014).

1.5.2 
Variations in composition of xenobiotic compounds in wastewater 
There can be differences in the composition of the xenobiotic content of the wastewater, depending on 
multiple factors. One determining factor is, the catchment area as there is a difference between the 
composition of compounds in industrial and domestic wastewater. To give a few examples, the levels 
of PCAs (polychlorinated alkenes) are much higher in industrial wastewater (Clarke & Smith 2011), 
while the level of some phthalates are much higher in domestic than in industrial wastewater 
(European Commission 2001).  
 
Another important factor for the wastewater composition is temporal. Yu et al. (2013) found that the 
concentrations of pharmaceuticals and PCPs was significantly higher in the winter than in the summer 
season, because of higher human consumption of these compounds and lower degradation in the 
WWTP during winter, which then resulted in an increase in the concentrations of some of these 
compounds in the wastewater sludge. 
 
As the wastewater contains a complex and fluctuating mixture of compounds, this will also be the case 
for the wastewater sludge and hence the biosolids. Multiple studies have found a large variety of 
compounds in biosolids, and there can possibly be many chemicals not examined in the above 
mentioned studies, present in the sludge. This underlines the necessity of addressing the toxicity 
assessment with a non-target approach that includes the entire matrix and not specific compounds. 
1.5.3 
Fate and effects of the xenobiotic compounds of biosolids in the environment  
Once the biosoilds are delivered on the field, the soil and plants can benefit from the nutrients present. 
However, xenobiotic compounds absorbed in the biosolids will enter the environment simultaneously. 
These xenobiotic compounds will vary in their persistence and toxicity. The consequences, the 
biosolid application will have on the environment, will partly depend on the dry matter content, the 
nutrient content and the content of heavy metals and other xenobiotics (Miljøstyrelsen 2013), but the 
fate of a chemical in the environment is to a large extend determined by the compounds present 
partitioning coefficients (Venkatesan & Halden 2014). 
 
Heavy metals will at typical soil pH be bound tightly to the soil particles. The strength of the bond will 
vary between the heavy metals, but if the pH of the soil decreases, more heavy metals will be present 
in the aqueous phase (Ingvertsen et al. 2010). Some of the metals present in the biosoilds, like cobber, 
zinc, manganese and iron, are essential to the plant in low concentrations, but if the levels are too high, 
these micronutrients will be toxic in the soil. Other metals like cadmium, mercury, nickel and chrome 
are toxic, even at low concentrations. Heavy metals have potential for accumulation in the soil, as the 
==PAGE:25==
 
25
only removal mechanisms are plant uptake, leaching and erosion. An increase in the heavy metal 
concentration can decrease the soil fertility (Ingvertsen et al. 2010).  
 
Organic compounds will to a large extend sorb to the organic matter in the sludge, and once on the 
field, further aerobe degradation can occur. LAS can be present in high concentrations in the biosolids, 
but is degraded rapidly once applied on the field, and there is no risk of adverse effects associated with 
the compound. Volatile organic compounds are not expected to cause any adverse effects in relation to 
agricultural application of biosolids, as these are expected to escape before any accumulation can 
occur, at normal sludge application rates (Smith 2009).  
 
Several of the organic components present in biosolids are suspected of being endocrine disruptive 
(Smith 2009). High concentrations of compounds like PAHs, DEPHs, NP and NPEs (Nonylphenol 
ethoxylates) can affect the populations of different soil dwelling organisms, by increasing the 
mortality or decreasing the fertility (Ingvertsen et al. 2010). NPE is furthermore classified as “not easy 
degradable” (Miljøstyrelsen 2006). The concentration necessary to have hazardous effects on the soil 
microorganisms, are however high compared to the concentrations likely to be applied on the field 
(Ingvertsen et al. 2010).  
 
Pharmaceuticals that are distributed on the field with the biosolids have potential for hazardous effects 
to the ecosystem. As the pharmaceuticals are designed to be biologically active, they are often very 
potent and can exhibit ecotoxic effects on non-target organisms at low concentrations (McClellan & 
Halden 2010). The ecotoxic effects could for example be of a estrogenic- or antibiotic character, 
depending of the type of drug (Ingvertsen et al. 2010). 
 
Many of the compounds present in the biosolids will be hydrophobic and will thereby not have great 
potential to leach. However, some contaminants recovered in the shallow groundwater are expected to 
originate from biosolids applied on agricultural fields (Boutrup et al. 2006). Components like lead, 
PAHs and DEPH will have potential to leach to the groundwater (Miljøstyrelsen 2013). The 
groundwater is however not expected to be under threat, as long as the current limit values are met 
(Ingvertsen et al. 2010). 
 
In general, the recycling of biosolids is considered safe, both in relation to groundwater, soil fertility 
and the surface water (Miljøstyrelsen 2013; Smith 2009; Eriksen et al. 2009).

1.6 Xenobiotic content of biomass for biogas utilization 
One of the facilities included in the present project is a biogas plant. The biomass, utilized for biogas 
is mainly manure from cattle and pigs, but to some extend also industrial waste (S.S.F Energiselskab 
n.d.). 
 
Like biosolids manure contains xenobiotic compounds, but there are differences in the composition of 
xenobiotic compounds between cattle and pig manure. While nonylphenol-polyethoxylat used in 
cleaning agents used to clean the milking equipment is mostly found in cattle manure, zinc and cobber 
that are used as growth stimulant is mostly found in manure form pigs as an example (Schwærter & 
Grant 2003). Heavy metals are however present in both pig and cattle manure. The heavy metals most 
frequently found is aluminium and the more toxic compounds cadmium, cobber, nickel and zinc 
(Schwærter & Grant 2003; Boutrup et al. 2006). Schwærter and Grant (2003) even found few manure-
samples where the concentrations of zinc or cadmium exceeded the limit values of biosolids. 
 
PAHs and LAS’s are some of the most frequently found compound groups in manure. Even though 
LAS’s often are present in manure, the concentrations are low in comparison to that of biosolids 
(Boutrup et al. 2006). Of the PAHs, the lighter compounds of the group as well as phenanthren are the 
ones present in the highest concentrations (Boutrup et al. 2006). Phenols are also frequently found in 
manure, and concentrations of this group of compounds can exceed the levels most often present in 
biosolids (Boutrup et al. 2006). Schwærter and Grant (2003) only found the toxic compound NP in 
few of their tested samples, but the concentrations in these samples, were however high and 
approaching the limit values existing for biosolids. The presence of NP in manure could be 
problematic because of the endocrine disruptive potential of the compound, and as the manure is 
distributed in large amounts in comparison to biosolids (Schwærter & Grant 2003).  
 
Like biosolids, manure can also contains phthalates. The two compounds most abundant in manure 
within this groups are, DBP (dibutylphthalat) and DEPH. The levels of DBH in manure is very similar 
to that of biosolids, whereas  DEPH is only present in low concentrations (Boutrup et al. 2006). 
 
Antibiotics are mostly found in manure from pigs, but also manure from cattle can contain such 
pharmaceuticals (Schwærter & Grant 2003).  Typically the levels of antibiotics are higher in manure 
than in biosolids (Ingvertsen et al. 2010), and there are no existing limit values when it comes to 
antibiotics in manure or biosolids used for agricultural application (Schwærter & Grant 2003). 
 
==PAGE:27==
 
27
Manure is extensively used as fertilizer, and to a greater extent than biosolids. When applied on 
agricultural land, there is a risk of the xenobiotic compounds present, leaching to the aquatic 
environment through drains or surface runoff (Schwærter & Grant 2003). In the present project a 
sample of degassed manure from a biogas plant is included in addition to the wastewater sludge. At 
the biogas plant they are allowed to add industrial waste, which can consist of up to 25% of the total 
biomass (S.S.F Energiselskab n.d.). Compounds are present in the manure itself, but the industrial 
waste provides a unspecified input of xenobiotic compounds, which can consist of multiple substances 
such as food waste, wastewater sludge or waste from the medical industry (S.S.F Energiselskab n.d.).  
 
Like the wastewater sludge, the xenobiotic content of this biomass is unknown. Even though many of 
the same compounds that are found in the two substances, they are not covered by the same 
legislation. While the sludge amendment ensures monitoring of some xenobiotic compounds, the 
biomass used for biogas utilization is covered by the “manure amendment”, where no limit values of 
xenobiotic compounds exist (Miljøministeriet 2015).  
 
In the sections above on biosolids and manure, some compounds are mentioned, but theses will only 
represent a fraction of the actual content. Even though the two matrices are different from each other, 
both matrices will contain an unspecified mixture of xenobiotics, and a non-target approach, such as 
the algal growth inhibition test developed in the direct contact exposure setup, can proof usefull. 
 
1.7 Aims and objectives of the project 
As wastewater sludge is a complex mixture of many unknown compounds, it is necessary to include 
the entire sludge matrix in the test setup when testing toxicity. This secures a non-target toxicity test, 
but there are however some practical difficulties when testing a turbid solution on a photosynthesising 
organism. A method, that enables the algal growth inhibition test to be performed in a direct contact 
exposure setup, is therefore developed and performed.  
 
The main aim of the present project is to examine the phytotoxicity of wastewater sludge. The 
objective is to develop a test method on the basis of the algal growth inhibition test, using a direct 
contact exposure setup, where aqueous exposure concentrations are continuously buffered by 
partitioning from the sludge. This approach provides an accurate concentration-response relationship 
and furthermore potential mixture effects are included, as the entire matrix is present. To support the 
toxicity of the sludge, a BioTox test is performed in addition to the algal growth inhibition test. 
 
==PAGE:28==
 
28
To clarify which groups of phytotoxic compounds are present in the wastewater sludge, preliminary 
EDA is performed. This is done by applying Solid Phase Extraction (SPE) with two types of sorbent 
(activated carbon and a strong cation exchanger), and succeeding the removal of the different 
compound groups with an algal growth inhibition test. The toxicity reduction will thereby serves to 
clarify which groups of compounds are responsible for the phytotoxic effect observed in the initial 
experiment.

2.2 Development of algal growth inhibition test 
2.2.1 
Light 
As the sludge was a dark and turbid substance, the light saturation of the algae within the test solution 
was investigated. To ensure that the growth inhibition observed was a result of a toxic response and 
not shading, this was an important issue to address. Furthermore, inadequate irradiance would affect 
the sensitivity and possibly the chlorophyll/biomass ratio, and thereby complicate the toxicity 
assessment of the algae.  
 
The transparency of the wastewater sludge was investigated by measuring percent light able to pass 
through 4 mL (1.4 cm) of each stock solution, from the different locations of the direct contact 
exposure-experiment. 
 
To investigate how different light intensities affect the algal growth, a range of light intensities from 0 
to 257 μmol/m2/s was fabricated using neutral density filters. The filters were taped on the shaker table 
producing transparency ranging from a 100% to 0% light.

approximately 1% CO2 in the headspace.  The test vials were incubated for 48 hours under continuous 
light intensity in a temperature-controlled chamber (202C). Algae were kept in suspension by 
continuous shaking (approximately 300 rpm).

2.2.2 
NaCl tolerance 
NaCl can be harmful to the algae, as this is a freshwater organism. As one of the WWTPs (Fredericia) 
reported of high levels of chloride in their wastewater, the salt tolerance of the algal was tested. This 
was done by conducting a closed algal growth inhibition test according to the DTU-guidelines, but 
with a higher algal density, as in the previous described test (5*104 cells/mL).  
 
Salt (NaCl) was dissolved in the algal medium, and then diluted with a dilution factor of two, 
producing six concentrations and a control. The highest tested concentration was 1315 mg NaCl/L, 
corresponding to a concentration of 1000 mg Cl-/L, which potentially was present in the highest 
concentration in the direct contact exposure test. Each concentration was tested in triplicates, but the 
control was tested with 6 replicates.  
 
The pH was measured at day 0 and day 2 in the control and highest concentration. According to DTU 
standards, the pH should not have drifted more than 1.5 units from day 0 to day 2 as described above. 
Furthermore the pH should not differ more than one unit between the control and highest 
concentration. This was accomplished. 
 
The chlorophyll was extracted and the in vitro fluorescence was measured as described previously.  
2.2.3 
Algal growth inhibition test 
To test the phytotoxicity of the sludge, a 48 hour closed algal growth inhibition test was developed 
and performed. The test method was developed from the guidelines developed at DTU. Modifications 
to the standard test were made. Firstly, the irradiance level was increased to secure that the algae were 
light saturated even at the highest concentrations. Furthermore, the test cultures were inoculated with 
==PAGE:33==
 
33
an algal density five times higher than the standard test-density (5*104 cells/mL), as the fluorescence 
signal of the sludge itself was strong.  
 
Sludge from the seven different plants described in Table 2 was tested. From Fredericia and 
Hedensted two different samples were tested, and from Middelfart the same sample was tested twice. 
WWTP B has two different digestion tanks, and a sample from each was tested. All samples were 
tested within the first week, after the samples had been collected from the digestion tanks, except the 
test on the second sample from Fredericia, the sample from Marselisborg and the second test of the 
Middelfart sample, which were tested within the first 20 days after collection.  
 
Nutrients were added to the sludge elutriate, to ensure a similar nutrient content as in the algal 
medium. The solid fraction of the sludge was added to the elutriate producing a stock solution of 12 g 
(wet weight)/L. The stock solution was diluted with algal medium by a dilution factor of two, 
producing a concentration range of six concentrations in triplicates and a control with six replicates. 
The pH of the test solutions ranged from 7.61 to 8.62. 
 
As above, the test was performed in closed 20 mL glass-vials, and CO2 was added to each vial to 
ensure a concentration of approximately 1% CO2 in the headspace.  The test vials were incubated for 
48 hours under continuous light intensity in a temperature-controlled chamber (202C). Algae and 
sludge particles were kept in suspension by continuous shaking (approximately 300 rpm). 
 
pH was measured at day 0 and at day 2, in the controls and highest concentration. DTU standards was 
met both in regards to differences between highest and lowest concentration, and in regards to 
difference between day 0 and 2.  
 
Samples were extracted after 0 hours, 24 hours and 48 hours, according to the ISO guideline, as 
described above, and in vitro fluorescence was measured. A sample from the inoculum, as well as a 
non-inoculated blank sample from each concentration was extracted as well, to correct the 
fluorescence measurements for background fluorescence from the sludge.  
 
2.3 BioTox 
To test the toxicity of the sludge elutriate on a second type of organism, a BioTox-luminescence test 
was performed with the marine bacteria Vibrio Fischer. This was done according to DTU guidelines. 
==PAGE:34==
 
34

inhibition test was too turbid, and would have disturbed the luminescence signal of the bacteria. 
The test organism V. Fischer was stored freeze-dried, and was reconstituted before the test, by adding 
12.5 mL of Reagent Diluent (NaCl solution), and left for 10 minutes at 4C followed by 20 minutes at

2% NaCl was added to the elutriate after which the elutriate was diluted in a 2% NaCl solution (miliQ

100 l bacteria suspension was transferred to each test vial and the luminescence was measured on a

2.4 Preliminary effect-directed analysis - Solid Phase Extraction 
Preliminary EDA was performed to investigate which compound groups could be responsible for the

the top and bottom.

4 Discussion 
In the following section the results obtained in the laboratory, described in section 3, is discussed on 
basis on the theory presented in section 1 and additional relevant literature.  
4.1 Dry weight and TOC 
In Table 4 the dry weights of all locations are presented. The only plant that distinguishes itself from 
the other is the Hedensted plant, where the dry weight of the whole sludge sample is much lower than 
in the remaining samples. In Table 4, the TOC of the Fredericia and the Hedensted sample are 
furthermore presented, and as illustrated in the table, the TOC of Hedensted is much higher than that 
of the Fredericia sample. The lower dry weight and higher TOC of the Hedensted plant is due to the 
differences in treatment process in the plants as opposed to the other plants. As the Hedensted sludge 
is not digested like the sludge from the other plants, the sludge will not have completed the first 
dewatering that normally happens before the sludge is transferred to the digestion tanks (see Figure 4). 
The TOC is also higher at this plants because of the lack of digestion, as non-digested sludge will 
contain larger amounts of carbon than digested sludge (Miljøstyrelsen 2013). 
 
4.2 Development of algal growth inhibition method  
4.2.1 
Light 
As mentioned previously, it is important to secure that the algae are saturated with light at all test 
concentrations. This is first of all important, to ensure that that the growth inhibition observed is not 
caused by shading, but also to ensure a high sensitivity and a stabile chlorophyll/biomass ratio across 
concentrations. From Figure 6 it can be seen, that the point where the algae are saturated with light is 
at a light intensity of approximately 100 μmol/s/m2. When comparing this point of light saturation to 
the light intensities able to pass through the highest concentration of the test suspension in Table 5, it 
can be concluded that the algae have been saturated with light in suspensions of all samples, and the 
decrease in growth rate is not due to an insufficient level of light.  
 
It is possible that the chlorophyll/biomass ratio can be increased at the lower light intensities, 
underestimating the point of light saturation. This could consequently mask some decrease in biomass 
at the higher concentrations, resulting in an underestimation of the toxicity of the sludge. As this 
project did not include any measurements of the internal chlorophyll concentrations, it is impossible to 
confirm or deny. 
 
==PAGE:50==
 
50
Mayer et al. (1997) investigated the effect of different light intensities on the growth rate of P. 
Subcapitata, at similar conditions as in the present project. Mayer et al. (1997) recommends that algal 
tests with P. Subcapitata are conducted at a light intensity of at least 100 μmol/s/m2, which is much in 
accordance with the results obtained in the present project, and confirms that the algal have been 
growing at sufficient light intensities even at the highest concentrations of the turbid solutions.  
4.2.2 
Degradation and aging 
Even though most toxicity tests were performed within the first week after collection from the 
digestion tank, some of the samples were stored at up to 20 days before toxicity testing. The samples 
not tested within the first week were the first samples from Fredericia, the sample from Marselisborg, 
and the second test of the sample from Middelfart.  
 
Hedeselskabet (2000) investigated the degradation of xenobiotic compounds in sludge (LAS, NPE, 
PAH and DEPH), when storing the sludge for nine months. For digested sludge, they found that there 
was some degradation in the top layers, but the degradation was limited or non-existing below 20 cm 
into the sludge stack, indicating that the degradation of the compounds investigated, was mostly 
conducted aerobically. They furthermore found that temperature was important for the degradation 
processes, as the degradation rate was higher at high temperatures than at low.  In the present project, 
the sludge was kept at 4°C, and tested within 20 days after collection. The sludge was furthermore 
kept in plastic or glass flasks (either before or after separation by centrifugation), creating an anaerobe 
environment in large parts of the samples. It is thereby not suspected that there has been a significant 
reduction in the levels of xenobiotic compounds by degradation in the samples before testing the 
toxicity.  
 
Baun et al. (2002) found that, when testing the toxicity of soil spiked with phenanthrene in a direct 
contact exposure setup, the toxicity of the spiked soil decreased with increasing incubation time. This 
indicated that the phenanthrene became less available to the algae over time. It is likely that a similar 
mechanism can have happened in the present project. The aging of organic compounds was reviewed 
by Alexander (2000), who proposed that organic compounds, but also some inorganic compounds, 
become less bioavailable in soil over time. The compounds will become resistant to desorption by 
becoming entrapped within the solid phase or in nanopores of the organic matter in the soil. The time 
it takes before a compound is resistant to desorption will vary, but the process can happened within 
days (Alexander 2000). As the organic matter of the soil is the predominant matrix entrapping the 
compounds, this process is likely to happen in the sludge as well. This means that when storing the 
sludge, even only for a week, some of the present compounds can become more resistant to 
desorption, and thereby less available to the algae, which will affect the assessment of the toxicity. 
==PAGE:51==
 
51
The first sample of Fredericia and the sample of Marselisborg were only tested once, and there are 
thereby no results to compare the toxicity to, to determine whether the toxicity have changed over 
time. There was a second sample from Fredericia tested, but this sample was collected from the 
digestion tank more than three months after the first, and they are thereby not necessarily comparable. 
The Middelfart sample was however tested twice, with seven days between the tests, and as the two 
tests originate from the same sample, they are comparable. When looking at the two concentration-
response plots of Middelfart in Figure 8, it seems as the curve flattens in the second tests, which 
indicated that there is a shift in the compounds responsible for the toxicity, in relation to sorption. 
Furthermore the EC50 displayed in Table 6 more than doubles from first to the second test. This 
change in toxicity can to some extend be due to the fact that a change happened in the composition of 
xenobiotics, in the time period between the tests, and that some of the compounds present in the 
sludge had aged, becoming more resistant to desorption, and less available to the algae.  
4.2.3 
Algal growth inhibition test 
In Table 6, it is illustrated that the EC50 values vary somewhat between samples and location. When 
examining the locations, where two tests were performed (Fredericia, Hedensted, Middelfart and 
WWTP B), it is seen, that even these EC50 values vary. From the EC50 of the samples obtained from 
Fredericia (two samples obtained at different points in time), Middelfart (same sample tested twice) 
and WWTP B (two samples obtained at the same time but from different digestion tanks), it can be 
seen that they are significantly different from each other, and there is a factor 2 between all locations 
tested twice. From this it can be deduced, that there will be some variation in toxicity that cannot be 
explained by the differences at the locations. Still there seem to be a trend within the EC50 values, and 
the locations can be divided in two groups: A high toxicity group and a low toxicity group. In the high 
toxicity group the Fredericia, Hedensted and Snertinge plants are located. In the low toxicity group 
Marselisborg, Middelfart, WWTP A and WWTP B are located. The EC50s are in general between a 
factor 3 and 6 higher in the low toxicity group than in the high toxicity group, and even though the 
confidence intervals are not overlapping between all locations within each group, there does however 
seem to be a larger gap between the two groups, than within the groups. The EC50 of WWTP B tank 2 
is in the low end of the low toxicity group, but still it is decided that this sample is included in the low 
toxicity group, along with tank 1 of the same WWTP. 
 
It is noticeable that the sludge from WWTP A, which is the only included sample, that is not allowed 
to be utilized for agricultural application, lies within the low toxicity group. This indicates that the 
compounds considered hazardous in the sludge from this plant is not more toxic towards the algae 
than sludge from plants, where the sludge is applied on agricultural land as fertilizer.

The level of industry in the catchment area of Table 2 is to some extend related to EC50. There is not a 
complete division between level of industry and high or low toxicity, but it can be said that all plants 
in the high toxicity group have a either high or medium level of industry in the catchment area, and all 
plants with a low level of industry lies in the low toxicity group. The only plant that does not display 
this relation between level of toxicity and level of industry in the catchment area is the Marselisborg 
plant. At this location, the sludge displays low level of toxicity, even though they receive large 
amounts of industrial wastewater. The level of industry is not likely to be the only determining factor 
of the toxicity, and there will be many other factors affecting the content of the sludge. This could, as 
an example, be the level of precipitation in the period before the sample was collected. Furthermore all 
vacuum tankers in the area empty their content at this WWTP, and this could also affect the 
composition of the sludge, as the sludge from these vacuum tankers differs. 
 
The plots in Figure 8 describe the response of the algal growth rate to the increasing concentration of 
sludge. For all locations, the growth rate decreases with increasing concentration, but the shape of the 
curves is not alike in all plots. As the algae are clones of each other, it would be expected that the 
decrease in growth rate would have happened within a narrow concentration range, as it is the case for 
the samples from Fredericia, Middelfart (1st sample) and WWTP B, producing a steep slope on the 
sigmoid curve. At some of the locations, the slope is however more flat.  
 
The algae will be kept in the water phase by shaking during the test, and the effective concentration of 
the compounds will be the same as freely dissolved concentration. As described by ter Laak et al 
(2007), the freely dissolved concentration will be controlled by the of sorption coefficient of a 
compound, and not the suspension density. The freely dissolved concentration of the compounds with 
a high sorption coefficient will buffered from the matrix at higher dilution factors, than the compound 
with a lower sorption coefficient who can be depleted at high dilution factors. The differences in the 
shapes of the concentration-response curves will thereby be determined by the sorption coefficients of 
the compounds present, and will vary between locations with different composition of compounds 
with different sorption coefficients.  
 
The catchment area of Middelfart, WWTP A and WWTP B, where the slope of the curve is steep, is 
dominated of domestic wastewater. The steep slope of the concentration-response curve suggests that 
compounds with a low sorption coefficient dominate the toxicity at these locations. The plots 
exhibiting the most flat slope are from the samples of Hedensted, Marselisborg and Snertinge, where 
the flatness is most pronounced in the Hedensted plots (Figure 8C and D).  
 
==PAGE:53==
 
53
The matrix ability to bind the compounds will be of importance for the freely dissolved concentration 
of a compound, and thereby the effective concentration. TOC can be seen as a measure of organic 
matter content in the matrix, as carbon represents a large fraction of the content of organic matter. 
Organic matter contains both hydrophobic and hydrophilic domains, and is an important absorbent of 
organic compounds and heavy metals (Schwarzenbach et al. 2003; Boutrup et al. 2006). It is possible 
that the high TOC level of the Hedensted sample affects the matrix ability to absorb compound, which 
again effects the effective concentration. This could be the reason why the concentration-response of 
this location dose not only have a flat slope, but is almost linear as appose to sigmoid like the curve in 
other plots. 
 
The matrix of Snertinge is different than the wastewater sludge, as this sample was obtained at a 
biogas plant. The composition of xenobiotic will thereby also be different from the sludge as well, and 
probably also more different that the in-between plant differences, as this matrix originate from a 
entirely different source. Domene et al. (2007) found, when testing the response of soil collembolans 
to different waste products used for soil application, that the toxicity of pig slurry was greater than 
wastewater sludge, both in relation to feeding inhibition and lethality. This underlines that the manure 
contains toxic elements, which corresponds to the results obtained in the present project. 
 
is possible that amount of industrial wastewater could explain some of the differences in the 
composition of toxic compounds, observed between the two types of concentration-response curves. 
Hedensted and Marselisborg are plants with high or medium levels of industry in their catchment area. 
This is also the case for Fredericia, but the slope of the samples from this location is steep, suggesting 
that the compounds responsible for the toxicity have a lower sorption coefficient than at the Hedensted 
and Marselisborg plants. From this it can be deduced that, a high level of industry does not necessarily 
result in a flat slope. It could have been interesting to include more parameters of the plants in the 
project, such as size in relation to km2 and information about the type of catchment area (urban or 
rural). This information would in combination with level of precipitation in the weeks before 
sampling, clarify the effects of runoff water (Figure 5), which have been a neglected wastewater type 
in the present project. The runoff water could dilute the compound concentrations at the WWTP, but 
also add xenobiotic compounds to the wastewater pool. 
4.2.4 
Importance of direct contact exposure 
In the present project, a test method has been developed based on the algal growth inhibition test.  
It is important to stress that the specific content and composition of xenobiotic compounds in the 
wastewater sludge is unknown, and many factors affect the composition of compounds in the 
wastewater sludge. As the industry and consumer products are evolving, so will the composition of 
==PAGE:54==
 
54
compounds in the wastewater, and thereby also the composition of compounds in the wastewater 
sludge. Different industries will contribute with different types of compounds, and the wastewater 
treatment will differ between plants (Miljøstyrelsen 2006; Miljøstyrelsen 2013). A part of the 
wastewater will consist of surface runoff, which among others can contain compounds like PAHs that 
is related to combustion of fuels (Smith 2009). The level of surface runoff will vary with precipitation, 
and thereby also differ over time. The bacterial community, crucial to the wastewater treatment, might 
furthermore be affected by the wastewater, and wastewater containing large amounts of antimicrobial 
agents or other compounds hazardous to the microorganism, will effect the general treatment of the 
wastewater (Hedensted spildevand 2013).This means that the content of wastewater and wastewater 
sludge in general is unspecified, and stresses the importance of a non-target approach to toxicity 
testing to embrace this unspecified substance.  
 
By including the entire matrix in the test system, potential mixture effects will be included. As the 
content of the sludge is unknown, it is not possible to know whether there has been any interaction 
between the compounds in relation to the toxic effect. It is, however, very likely, that with a large 
amount of different compounds some synergistic or antagonistic effects will occur. 
 
Baun et al. (2002) found, when using an algal growth inhibition test to examine the toxicity of PAH 
contaminated soil in a direct contact exposure setup, that the EC10 was much lower than when testing 
an elutriate of the same soil. They argued that the increase in toxicity observed, when testing the entire 
matrix, was due to the fact that the soil acted as a reservoir for the phenanthrene and continuously 
desorb during the test period. The approach used by Baun et al. (2002) is similar to the approach used 
in the present project, with differences in the complexity of the composition of xenobiotic compounds 
in the matrix. The complexity of the compound content of the wastewater sludge makes the direct 
contact exposure a suitable choice. By not removing the compounds from the sludge matrix, important 
interactions between matrix and compounds, and compounds in-between will be preserved, creating a 
powerful toxicity assessment. When looking at Figure 8, the differences in shapes of the curves 
demonstrates that the interaction between the matrix and the compounds is affecting the phytotoxic 
response, which supports the importance of this approach for the toxicity assessment of this type of 
substrate. 
 
4.3 BioTox 
The trend of the EC50s of the BioTox tests is consistent with that of the algal tests. As it can be seen in 
Table 7, the locations where the elutriate exhibits the highest toxicity is in the Fredericia and Snertinge 
samples. These are also in the high toxicity group in the direct contact exposure algal tests. The 
==PAGE:55==
 
55
locations with the lowest toxic response to the elutriate, is in the BioTox test Marselisborg, Middelfart, 
WWTP B and WWTP A, which also is in the low toxicity group in the algal test. For WWTP A and 
WWTP B tank 2 the confidence intervals are very wide, but when examining the concentration-
response plots in Appendix V, it is oblivious that these two locations also are within the low toxicity 
group.  
 
In the Hedensted sample, the elutriate seems to stimulate the bacteria, rather than induce a toxic 
response. Ma et al. (2014) reports of observed hormesis effects in V. Fischeri bioassays, as the 
luminescence of the bacteria can be stimulated if inorganic nutrient are present in low concentrations. 
Even though the response of the Hedensted sample does not exhibit a classic hormetic response, there 
does seem to be a stimulation of the luminescence signal by this sample. It is possible that the different 
treatment process of this WWTP, where the digestion step is left out, produces a sludge that have a 
different nutrient composition than the other samples, and is thereby stimulating the luminescent 
response of the algae. 
 
The BioTox test was only conducted on the elutriate of the sludge, and the EC50s of the BioTox are 
thereby not directly comparable to the direct contact exposure algal tests, as this test included the solid 
sludge. In the SPE test, the elutriate was tested separately, as the “before SPE”-toxicity estimate. This 
was however only done for the two locations: Fredericia and Hedensted. In both cases, the algal test 
(Table 8) demonstrates a higher toxicity than the BioTox test (Table 7). For the Fredericia sample, the 
EC50 is more than three times larger in the BioTox test than in the algal test. For the Hedensted sample 
the elutriate is nontoxic in the BioTox test, but in the algal test, the EC50 is as low as the Fredericia 
sample.  
 
When Ventura et al. (2010) tested the toxicity of the imidazolium 1-methyl-3-propylimidazolium 
bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide on a range of aquatic organism, they also found that V. Fischeri was 
less sensitive than P. Subcapitata. This finding is much in line with the findings of van der Grinten et 
al. (2010) who tested the toxicity of different antibiotics and found that P. Subcapitata was more 
sensitive than V. Fischeri, where no toxic response was observed. It is however important to note that 
the BioTox test and algal growth inhibition test are two very different tests. The BioTox test is an 
acute test, with a very limited exposure period in relation to the chronic algal growth inhibition test. 
As the trends of the BioTox test is much in line with that of the algal growth inhibition test, the 
BioTox test confirmed the findings of the algal test, and may serve as a cost-effective screening 
method in relation to toxicity assessment of wastewater sludge, keeping the possible hormetic effects 
in mind.

4.4 Preliminary effect-direct analysis - Composition of phytotoxic compound 
groups 
In the direct contact exposure test it is indicated that there is some relation between the fraction of 
wastewater originating from the industry and the toxicity as well as the composition of compounds. 
The composition of compounds in industrial wastewater is complex and will vary with the type of 
industry (Miljøstyrelsen 2006). While some compounds like heavy metals mainly will originate from 
industrial sources (Boutrup et al. 2006), others are more frequent in domestic sources. It is however 
likely that many other factors also are determining of how the composition of compounds will be in 
the wastewater.  
 
To clarify which groups of compounds that are responsible for the observed phytotoxicity, preliminary 
EDA was conducted. SPE was used to fractionate, and tests were performed on the elutriate and on an 
aqueous extract from two sludge samples, using activated carbon or a cation exchanger as sorbents. 
Fredericia and Hedensted were chosen for the preliminary EDA as both locations exhibits a similar 
high level of phytotoxicity, but only Fredericia makes use of a digestion tank in the sludge treatment. 
The two locations furthermore differs in respect to size and level of industry, where Fredericia is a 
large facility which receives large amounts of industrial wastewater and Hedensted a small facility 
which receive a lower amounts of their wastewater from the industry (Table 2).  
 
In Table 8 it is indicated that both the elutriate and the aqueous extract is toxic before SPE, for both 
samples. As there is no confidence intervals available for all EC50 values of the table, it can be difficult 
to distinguish whether the EC50 of the “before” and “after” SPE-treatment are significantly different 
from each other. The four treatments where the EC50 indicates that there has been an effect by the SPE 
treatments are displayed in Figure 9, where the growth rates before and after SPE are plotted pairwise.  
 
In Figure 9A, the growth rate of the samples, where the aqueous extract has been treated with 
activated carbon, is above the growth rate of the “before SPE” results, but only at the lower 
concentrations. In the highest concentrations, the growth rate of the fraction treated with activated 
carbon drops to the levels of the “before SPE”-results. In combination with the low EC50 of the 
aqueous extract “before SPE” (Table 8), these results indicate, that something highly toxic, which can 
be retained on activated carbon is present in the aqueous extract. It is obvious that the activated carbon 
has not removed all toxicity, and something, not retained on either of the sorbents, is still present in 
both the aqueous extract and also in the elutriate. The activated carbon mainly sorbs organic 
compounds. As the only effect of the activated carbon is seen on the aqueous extract, the types of 
compounds retained on the activated carbon are probably compounds readily sorbed in the sludge 
==PAGE:57==
 
57
matrix. In the Fredericia sample, the cation exchange sorbent did not have an effect, and the toxicity is 
thereby not due to heavy metals or other cationic compounds. None of the sorbents had an effect on 
the elutriate from Fredericia, where the majority of hydrophilic compounds will be present. 
Comparing the results of the preliminary EDA with the shape of the curve in the direct contact 
exposure test (Figure 8A and B), a large fraction of the phytotoxicity of the Fredericia sample is likely 
to be due to compounds with a low sorption coefficient, not retained by the SPE. 
 
The SPE treatment is much more effective on the Hedensted sample than in the Fredericia sample. In 
the Hedensted sample, the activated carbon has removed some of the toxicity of the aqueous extract 
(Figure 9B). The aqueous extract of Hedensted is not as toxic as the aqueous extract of Fredericia, 
illustrated by the EC50 of Table 8. When the growth rate of the “before SPE”-fraction decreases in the 
higher concentrations, it is obvious that the toxicity have been reduced in the fraction treated with 
activated carbon. Also the cation exchanger is decreasing toxicity of the aqueous extract (Figure 9C). 
The toxicity of the aqueous extract, which is related to the solid sludge, can thereby both be explained 
by the presence of organic compounds, but also cations, possibly heavy metals. In the elutriate, the 
cation exchanger have also reduced the toxicity (Figure 9D). The elutriate have, like the aqueous 
extract, been in contact with the sludge, before the two fractions were separated. Some compounds 
normally bound in the sludge will also be present in the elutriate, because of the equilibrium formed 
between the solid sludge and the liquid. The decrease in toxicity might for some parts be in relation to 
the removal of these compounds, or other cations present in the elutriate.  
 
The results of the preliminary EDA indicate that the toxicity of the Fredericia sample, only to some 
extend, can be explained by organic compounds, while the toxicity in the Hedensted sample can be 
explained by both cations and organic compounds. It is, as the initial direct contact exposure test also 
indicated, different compound groups that dominate the toxicity within the two locations. The cation 
exchanger only have an effect on the Hedensted sample, indicating that cations, like heavy metals, are 
more importance for the toxic response in the Hedensted sample, than in the Fredericia sample. This is 
likely to be due to the difference in TOC contents. Metals will bind to organic matter in the 
wastewater treatment process (Boutrup et al. 2006), and as the organic matter content is much higher 
in the Hedensted sludge, the capacity for heavy metal binding is also higher. Differences in treatment 
process might in fact have a larger effect on the composition, than the input from industrial 
wastewater. 
 
Not all toxicity is removed from any of the fractions, and especially the toxicity of the elutriate is 
unaffected by the SPE treatments. An possible explanation of the remaining toxicity in the fractions 
==PAGE:58==
 
58
where SPE did have an effect is that the breakthrough volume can have been reached, and some of the 
compounds, otherwise retained, have leaked from the cartridge and into the solution. For the aqueous 
extract of Hedensted, it can be however be reflected, whether the toxicity would have decreased even 
more, if the sample had been treated with both sorbents. 
 
Given that the elutriate is less affected by the SPE than the aqueous extract, some of the toxicity of the 
sludge is potentially caused by hydrophilic compounds. This can for some part be pharmaceuticals or 
PCPs, and this is a group that have gotten much attention because of their potency (McClellan & 
Halden 2010). While many of these products will be degraded or bound to the sludge during the 
wastewater treatment, some pharmaceuticals will also be present the water phase (Mogensen et al. 
2008). This could either be the original compounds or metabolites, and as mentioned earlier, some of 
the pharmaceuticals will enter the WWTPs as a conjugated hydrophilic compound.  
 
Mogensen et al. (2008) screened for a range of pharmaceuticals in both influent- and effluent 
wastewater as well as in wastewater sludge. 13 of the 15 pharmaceuticals detected in the effluent 
wastewater, were detected in either the effluent water or the sludge. Some of the compounds were 
furthermore found in higher levels in the effluent water than in the influent water, possibly due to 
deconjugation during the treatment processes. They furthermore found two pharmaceuticals in the 
sludge, that they did not detect in influent wastewater, which is because of the ability of the sludge to 
sorb and concentrate the pharmaceuticals. It is however worth noticing that the pharmaceuticals and 
PCPs mainly originate from domestic sources, and the WWTP of Fredericia, only receive small 
amounts of the domestic wastewater in relation to industrial sources. Even though pharmaceuticals are 
known to be potent in their toxic effects, other hydrophilic compounds are also likely to be present in 
this sample. 
 
Combining the results of the preliminary EDA, and the result of the direct contact exposure 
experiment, the compounds likely to dominate the toxicity in Fredericia are probably hydrophilic, but 
also organic compounds sorbed in the sludge to some extend. In Hedensted both organic compounds 
and cations, possibly heavy metals, induces the phytotoxic effects. As heavy metals are expected to 
originate from industrial sources rather than domestic, the treatment process seem to be more 
determining for the composition of phytotoxic compound groups than the level of industrial 
wastewater. Eriksson (2001) did however find, when examining trace elements in sludge of Swedish 
WWTPs, that the concentrations of trace metals in general was highest in sludge from small or 
medium sized plants. This can be another explanation, as Hedensted is a small facility in relation to 
the Fredericia WWTP.

4.5 Assessment of the environmental risk of agricultural waste application 
Even though measures were taken to make the toxicity test environmental realistic, in relation to 
exposure concentrations, there is a large difference from the laboratory to the actual environment. 
Some issues that could alter the composition of compounds, in sludge tested in the present project as 
oppose to the composition in biosolids, are worth mentioning. For one the samples tested in the 
present project was not of biosolids, but wastewater sludge, and the dry weight was much lower in the 
present project than it would be in biosolids. Kinney et al. (2006) found, when examining the 
concentrations of organic contaminants in American biosolids, that hydrophilic compounds were 
present in considerable concentrations in all samples tested. However, the solutions tested in the 
present project have a larger level of elutriate than the biosolids applied as fertilizer (dry weight: ca. 
25% in Danish biosolids, Miljøstyrelsen 2013), as the sludge tested in the present project was not 
dewatered and the elutriate was included in the tested solution, and the fraction of hydrophilic 
compounds is thereby larger in the present project. Furthermore a closed algal growth inhibition test 
was performed, and volatile compounds were therefor prevented from escaping. This is not 
environmentally realistic, as volatile compounds in biosolids, would escape upon application on the 
soil.  
 
The phytotoxic response of the algal does however illustrates, that the exposure of the sludge matrix to 
an organism has a toxic potential, and some soil dwelling organisms could be interesting to examine 
further. Plants would be relevant to investigate, as a terrestrial photosynthesizing organism, but also 
the toxic response of terrestrial bacteria, invertebrates or annelids would be relevant to investigate. 
The community of soil dwelling organisms are not only important in an ecological context, but they 
are also extremely important for the soil fertility as degraders of organic material and in recycling of 
nutrients (Eriksen et al. 2009).  
4.5.1 
Biomass for biogas utilization 
The degassed manure from Snertinge was one of the samples that exhibited a high level of toxicity. 
The biomass is however not covered by the sludge amendment, but by the “Manure amendment” 
(Miljøministeriet 2015). This amendment does not set any limit values to the xenobiotic content. 
Many of the compounds, often found in manure, like PAHs, LAS or NP will bind to the matrix, and to 
a lesser extend be present in the water phase, which consist with the flatness of the concentration-
response curve for this location (Figure 8K). The biomass is however a mixture of manure and 
industrial waste, and the waste fraction might also contribute to the toxicity observed, but in the 
present project no information about the waste products was obtained. Considering the low EC50 and 
the indications of a high level of compounds sorbed to the matrix, it is deduced that there are 
xenobiotic compounds present in this matrix, as there is in the wastewater sludge. Furthermore, the 
==PAGE:60==
 
60
toxicity of this biomass is found to be in the high toxicity group, which is noticeable, as this biomass is 
not by legislation subjected to the same monitoring of xenobiotics as the wastewater sludge. 
4.5.2 
Biosolids 
Even though the tests performed in the present project is all on simple aquatic organisms, the toxic 
effect observed both towards algae and bacteria illustrates that there is a toxic potential within the 
sludge. The highest concentration of the individual samples tested in the algal growth inhibition test is 
all between 1-2.4 g DW/L water. When sludge is applied on agricultural soil, a rough calculation will 
give that the concentration in the soil is around 3.5 g DW/L soil, given that the sludge is evenly 
distributed in the ploughing zone (top 20 cm, Sørensen et al. 2001) and that the maximum allowed  
7 t DW/ha/year is applied at once. The exposure in the water is different than in the soil, as the soil is 
present as an additional phase in the system. The effective concentration will thereby be lower in the 
soil as opposed to the water, as the soil will sorb the compounds released from the biosolids. The 
exposure in the soil is however a complicated matter, as the TOC is higher in the biosolids than in the 
soil, and the TOC thereby will decrease in the soil/biosolid mixture, once the biosolids are ploughed 
into the soil. This decrease in TOC might give occasion for release of some of the bound compounds.  
 
The Norwegian Scientific Committee for Food Safety (VKM) completed an extensive risk assessment 
for the Norwegian Food Safety Authorities of the use of sewage sludge as fertilizer (Eriksen et al. 
2009). The focus of the study was some organic compounds, heavy metals and pharmaceuticals. The 
risk assessment covered the risk of human exposure through routes such as uptake of contaminates in 
crops or food producing animals or consumption of groundwater after possible leaching from sludge 
amended soil, but also the risk for the soil environment and aquatic environment was assessed. The 
risk was evaluated by estimating the PEC (predicted environmental concentration) by modelling the 
exposure in the different compartments, and comparing this concentration to the PNEC (predicted no-
effect concentration). They found that the organic compounds octylphenols, NP and LAS exceeded the 
PNEC in the soil environment, but only for a very limited period immediately after application, 
followed by a rapid decline in concentrations as a result of the degradation of these compounds. They 
furthermore found, that even though no heavy metals would reach the PNEC within a period of 100 
years of continuously sludge application, the soil concentrations of cadmium, mercury, copper and 
zinc would increase. For the aquatic environment they found that none of the investigated compounds 
would reach the PNEC on short or long term. The conclusion of the report was, that there no risk is 
associated with the use of sewage sludge as fertilizer in regards to human consumption or the aquatic 
environment. Furthermore, it was concluded that the sludge application of sewage sludge only was 
associated with low risk for the soil environment, but that the concentrations of the heavy metals with 
potential to accumulate in the soil should be monitored (Eriksen et al. 2009).  
==PAGE:61==
 
61
 
It is important to note, that the abovementioned risk assessment is for Norwegian soil, and cannot be 
directly transferred to Danish conditions. The Danish legislation is different than the Norwegian, both 
in regards to allowed amounts of sludge applied on the soil and in regards to limit values of the heavy 
metals. Most notably is the level of lead, zinc and cobber is allowed to be higher in Danish sludge 
before agricultural application, than in the Norwegian sludge (Eriksen et al. 2009; Miljøministeriet 
2006). Furthermore the risks of both the terrestrial and aquatic environment are tied to local factors 
such as the characteristics of the soil and weather conditions (Eriksen et al. 2009). The 
abovementioned study only evaluates the risk of a few compounds expected to be present in the 
sludge, leaving out a large unknown group of different compounds also likely to be present, and the 
potential mixture effects likely to occur in the complex mixture. 
 
No such extensive study is available for the Danish conditions, but a Danish lifecycle assessment 
conducted by the Danish EPA concludes that environmental risks of the xenobiotic in wastewater 
sludge is low (Miljøstyrelsen 2013). Furthermore a study by Jensen et al. (2012) conducted for the 
Danish EPA evaluates the risk for the terrestrial ecosystem, of five different groups of organic 
compounds brominated flame retardants (BFR), musk substances, pharmaceuticals, polychlorinated 
biphenyls (PCB) and perfluorinated compounds. They found that only the perfluorinated compounds 
could potentially pose a risk.  
 
The approach used in the above mentioned risk assessments have focused on modelling data and the 
relationship between PEC and PNEC of individual compounds. The results of the present project 
provides evidence of a toxic potential of wastewater sludge and degassed manure, when testing the 
toxicity of the entire matrix, as oppose to focusing on specific compounds. Many compounds of the 
sludge are already regulated, and there is focus on emerging compounds in the literature, but the focus 
in both the legislation and the literature is compound specific, and does not embrace the complexity of 
these substances. It is however important to note, that the chemical quality of the sludge is higher now 
than ever. The concentration of persistent organic chemicals (POPs) have declined through strict 
control, and the chemical quality of the sludge is expected to improve further by the implementation of 
REACH (Smith 2009). Furthermore, by using biosolids as a fertilizer on agricultural fields, 
greenhouse gas emissions from e.g. phosphor mining and sludge incineration is avoided (Jensen et al. 
2012), and in this way, the utilization of biosolids may indirectly serve as the least environmentally 
harmful option.

5 Conclusion 
The present project examined the phytotoxicity of wastewater sludge by developing a method for algal 
toxicity testing in a direct contact exposure setup. By applying this method to several sludge samples 
from WWTPs with different characteristics, a powerful assessment of the toxicity was obtained, where 
interactions between matrix and compounds, and compounds in-between were included. The result of 
the toxicity tests illustrated that the sludge was toxic towards the algal, and that treatment processes 
and the wastewater source is in relation to the level of industrial wastewater might be some factors 
affecting the toxicity of the sludge.  
 
By testing the effect of different light intensities and the NaCl tolerance of the algae, it was illustrated 
that the growth inhibition observed in the toxicity tests, is a result of a toxic response from the 
exposure to the wastewater sludge or the degassed manure. This could be concluded as the growth rate 
at the concentration of NaCl, or at the light intensity, present in the highest sludge concentrations, did 
not result in growth inhibition of the algae. 
 
The toxicity observed in the algal growth inhibition test was supported by another type of organism. 
The BioTox test provided results, displaying the same trends in relation to high toxicity samples and 
low toxicity samples, as seen in the algal tests.  
 
Furthermore, the present project was able to clarify to some extent which groups of phytotoxic 
compounds were present in the sludge samples, by using preliminary EDA. By applying SPE to two 
different sludge samples, and comparing the toxicity of the fractions before and after SPE, a large 
difference in the composition of toxic compounds was discovered. For the Fredericia sample, the 
toxicity was to a small extent explained by the present of organic compounds, but much of the toxicity 
remains unexplained. For the Hedensted sample, the toxicity could be explained by both the present of 
organic compounds and also by the presence of cations, possibly heavy metals. 
 
Even though the traditional risk assessments conclude that the wastewater sludge is related to no or 
low risk when applied on agricultural land, for both terrestrial and aquatic organisms, the present 
project illustrates that wastewater sludge does have a high toxic potential at environmentally relevant 
concentrations. The present project provides a new approach for toxicity assessment for complex 
substances, and underlines the importance in testing the toxicity by including the entire matrix.
