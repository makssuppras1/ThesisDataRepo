Abstract 
Since the beginning of the 21st century, the negative impact of climate change has seriously 
jeopardized the human society and natural environment. The concept of low-carbon life has 
been broadly accepted and approved by both individuals and businesses. Organizations have 
adopted specific methods to track the carbon footprint during the production process or life 
cycle of the project. In this paper, a cloudburst tunnel project will be introduced as the study 
case and conducted in-depth analysis including the life cycle assessment and carbon calculation. 
The carbon calculation mainly focuses on the carbon emissions at construction and operational 
phase and existing developed tool will be used to do the calculation. The aim of the study is to 
assess the applicability of the existing tool, identify the key contributors of the project and put 
forward practical and feasible low-carbon solutions for the similar projects in the future. 
 
Several methods are used in this paper in order to achieve the goal explained in previous 
paragraph. The life cycle assessment (LCA) is used to help us understand the whole life cycle 
of tunnel project and thus track down the activities and materials that cause carbon footprint. 
Then, the whole life (construction) carbon planning tool is used for whole life carbon 
calculation. By entering the data of the study case in the format the tool requested, the tool will 
automatically calculate and summary the result in multiple formats. 
 
Overall, carbon modelling tool is not suitable for tunnelling project due to the asset model is 
based on a small database which could get better in the future when a vast database established. 
But carbon calculator is applicable for tunnelling project and the result is reasonable. From the 
result of CC, the embodied carbon of material consumed at the construction phase is the main 
contributor of carbon footprint in this project. And the concrete accounts for 99 % of carbon in 
all materials. Steel bar from the reinforced concrete also generate 347.5 t eco2 carbon when it 
considered separately. The energy consumption accounts for 6% of total emissions in this case 
due to the low emissions factor of electricity for Denmark. Currently, a nice low-carbon solution 
is to adjust the concrete recipe. The carbon emission decreases significantly when replacing a 
part of the cement with fly ash or slag.

1 Introduction 
In recent years, the global warming has heavily influenced the human society and natural environment 
in a negative way. Due to the sharp increase of greenhouse gases (GHG) in the atmosphere, the 
plankton and fishery resources in the ocean has rapidly decrease, and even the marine ecosystem are 
undergoing a serious imbalanced situation (Mandal et al., 2021). The global risks report 2019 declared 
that the extreme weather including floods, droughts and hurricanes will continuously emerge all over 
the world in the future based on the current prediction of global warming (World Economic Forum, 
2019). Those extremes weathers not only cause large economic losses to modern society but also lead 
to heavy casualties in many third countries (Clarke et al., 2021). And the fundamental reason behind 
those abnormal phenomena is the concentration of GHG in earth due to human activities. Therefore, 
from individuals to organizations even countries level, people have realized the importance of 
reducing carbon emissions during daily life (Song et al., 2021).  
 
To maintain the environmental sustainability, the European Union has set a goal to achieve “net zero 
emissions” for greenhouse gases by 2050 (World Economic Forum, 2021). “Net zero emissions” is a 
term used in environmental science that the concentration of GHG in the atmosphere remains constant. 
As a part of the EU, Denmark government also determines to reduce 70% of GHG emissions by 2030 
(compared with 1990) and achieve net-zero emissions by 2050 (Danish Ministry of Climate Energy 
and Utilities, 2019). From Figure 1, 76 % of GHG emissions in 2012 is from energy sector in which 
10.81% of emissions is from manufacturing industries and construction (United Nations Climate 
Change Secretariat, 2012). To accomplish the targets of net-zero emissions, all carbon-related sectors 
need to reduce the carbon footprint including construction which will be the section to focus on this 
project.

The emissions of greenhouse gases have become an increasingly serious problem due to the 
occurrence of global warming (Bachmann, 2020). Hence, the study of reducing GHG emissions is 
thriving in recent years. A series of academic articles and industrial report have introduced a number 
of methods and tools which could help to achieve the lower GHG emissions (Lunetto et al., 2021). 
The carbon footprint calculation is one of those methods that could estimate the GHG emissions 
during the human activities and subsequently instruct the future operation in projects to reduce the 
GHG emissions (Al-Mansour & Jejcic, 2017). 
 
Currently there is no dedicated carbon costing tool for calculating the embodied carbon for the 
construction, operation and maintenance and decommissioning of infrastructure for the water utility 
sector in Denmark. In other countries, some clients are performing carbon footprint calculations 
regularly with their own tools, such as Norway (VegLCA) and UK (ERIC) (Sykkelhåndboka, 2002). 
These tools are urgently needed to help us meet our climate obligations.  
 
An ideal way to carry out the carbon footprint calculation for tunnelling projects in the water utility 
sector in Denmark is to build a customized tool exclusively. However, the establishment of a new 
carbon calculation tool is very time consuming and complicated. It also needs to collect enormous 
data from different tunnel projects in order to set the default emissions factor for each item and 
category. Therefore, in this article, an existing tool that considered is applicable for the study case are 
used for the carbon footprint calculation.  
 
The carbon calculation of construction project is often between the border of environmental, 
==PAGE:22==
3 
 
management and civil engineering. In general, environmentally friendly design on construction 
project helps reduce the carbon footprint of the project such as using recycled materials instead of 
conventional materials. From the perspective of management engineering, a nice project plan could 
also help reduce the carbon emissions. If the arrangement of transport scheme and work schedule is 
appropriate, the project can be carried out efficiently and completed in a short time which decreasing 
the energy consumption on site and transport carbon of laborers. Civil engineering may play the most 
important role among the three specializations, a good design of building or other infrastructure based 
on professional knowledge could minimize the material use which is often the main contributor of 
carbon footprint in projects. In addition, methods for excavation and other activities also influences 
the carbon footprint during construction. All in all, the carbon footprint calculation is often fall 
between branches (environmental, management and civil engineering) and who should physically be 
responsible for the calculation is a problem need to be clarified in the future. 
 
The carbon calculation will be based on an actual tunnelling project as study case. The project is 
called Kalvebod Brygge Cloudburst Tunnel which is a tunnelling project in Copenhagen in order to 
increase the capacity of urban sewer against potential heavy rains. The case contains two most 
common structures of tunnelling, shaft and tunnel, which also are the parts associated with carbon 
footprint calculation. In Figure 3, The blue circles and dark blue line represent shafts and tunnel 
respectively. The project data needed for the carbon calculation is collected from project contactor 
and mentioned in detail in “Study case” chapter.

Problem statement and project objectives: 
Generally, this thesis tries to address the following problems 
⚫ How to calculate carbon footprint for tunnelling project in the water utility sector in Denmark? 
⚫ Could the existing carbon calculation tool be applicable to use for the tunnelling project in 
general? 
⚫ What conclusions could be obtained from the tool after it is applied on the study case and how 
to achieve a low-carbon solution according to the conclusions? 
Subsequently, some works are done in this thesis in order to solve those problems, Firstly, the whole 
life cycle of tunnelling project are investigated, and the source of carbon footprint is break down in 
detail (like specific material or certain construction activities). Then, several existing tools for carbon 
calculation that developed by organizations or companies are reviewed. The strengths and weakness 
of each tool are discovered and understanded. Among all the tools, the most appropriate one is chosen 
for the study case and carry out the carbon footprint calculation. The results from the selected tool 
are discussed including identifying the key contributors of carbon emissions, comparing of two 
different approaches, and providing plausible low-carbon suggestions or solutions. 
 
Non-included part 
The carbon calculation in this thesis still misses some carbon footprints in the life cycle of project. 
For instance, the energy consumption during site establishment is ignored due to the inaccessibility 
of the relevant data. Some non-included parts that also associated with carbon emissions are list 
below. They should also be taken into account when those data are available. 
⚫ Power of computers during the project design phase. 
⚫ Water consumption on site including drinking water for workers, water used in concrete or 
other construction work. 
⚫ Electricity and natural gas consumption for the heating and cooling systems in the site office. 
⚫ Petrol consumption for powering small power tools, such as hammer drills and small 
compressors. 
⚫ Lighting during night and underground construction, this could consume electricity or diesel 
if the energy comes from diesel generator. 
⚫ Materials used for site establishment like fence and temporary site office.

2 State of the art 
2.1 Literature review 
Both industry practitioners and scholars have explored the research of carbon footprint in construction 
area and tried to quantify the specific greenhouse gas emissions from construction projects. Diverse 
research for different type of construction works has been improved over the last decades.  Those 
studies show the various activities, items that lead to carbon emissions and some of them are also 
used in tunnelling project. Besides, those studies demonstrate the possible aspect that could lead to 
carbon reduction like construction method and transport management. 
 
2.1.1 Carbon calculation in road system 
The Shenzhen Science and Technology Plan supports the carbon footprint calculation of roads in 
urban area of Shenzhen (Mao et al., 2017). The life carbon assessment method is employed to 
compute the total carbon emissions from transport sector (Mao et al., 2017). The carbon footprint of 
different levels of roads and different types of road structures are investigated respectively to testify 
the impact on emissions by both road levels and road structures. The result shows the third-class (lane 
width between 6 and 7 m) road has higher GHG emissions than other levels (Mao et al., 2017). 
Moreover, the research of carbon footprint of existing and new-built road infrastructure indicates the 
road assets constructed before 2005 emitted higher carbon emissions than the new-built road. Railway 
and subway contribute the most emissions after the year of 2005 (Wei & Chen, 2020). Life cycle 
carbon emissions of establishing a high-way railway is approximately 3200 t co2 per km. 80% of the 
emissions is originally caused by material production in construction phase (Chang & Kendall, 2011).  
 
2.1.2 Carbon calculation in pipeline system 
The carbon footprint of pipeline system with different excavating methods is an interesting study field 
(Lu et al., 2020). Lu collected and computed energy consumption and GHG emissions of nine 
different trenchless methods and open-cut method for different study cases. The energy consumption 
in pipeline construction based on different trenchless technology and factors are illustrated in Figure 
4 (Lu et al., 2020). The result shows that cured-in-place pipe (CIPP) consumes the minimum amount 
of energy among all six trenchless methods considering in the figure. If parameters like soil type, 
ground conditions, pipe length are available, it is likely to select the best method which emits lowest 
==PAGE:25==
6 
 
carbon emissions and highly reduce the energy consumption (Lu et al., 2020). Those parameters could 
also help to decide the low-carbon excavation approach used in tunnelling project. LCA (life cycle 
assessment) model of oil product pipelines have been established based on the life cycle of pipeline 
projects. A comprehensive inventory of carbon and energy which is particularly built for oil pipelines 
are analyzed and discussed (Liqiao Huang et al., 2021). Huang also collected carbon factors and 
compute the sensitivity of each parameter.

2.1.3 Carbon calculation in tunnelling project 
Rodríguez and Lombardía analyzed the methane emissions during the tunnel projects for coal mining 
==PAGE:26==
7 
 
and built a calculation model. The model is certified by actual case that it is also applicable to 
traditional civil work like traffic tunnels (Rodríguez & Lombardía, 2010). Xu studied the relationship 
between the GHG emissions and surrounding soils based on a tunnel project (Xu et al., 2019). He 
found that carbon emissions increase significantly when surrounding environment of tunnel are weak 
rocks. This is mainly due to the sophisticated construction methods are selected when the surrounding 
rock is fragile and bring more carbon emissions. Meanwhile, transition paths of the greenhouse gases 
in tunnel are discovered and validated (Xu et al., 2019). The diesel consumption during tunnel drilling 
could also increase the carbon emissions and has a negative impact on climate change. This can be 
improved by tunnelling from both sides of the tunnel and replacing the diesel with bio-diesel (Lizhen 
Huang et al., 2015). Rodríguez and Pérez applied the LCA method and build a calculation model for 
the tunneling project. The calculator computes the carbon emissions of each phase in the construction 
work by estimating the amount of fuel, electricity and materials used (Rodríguez & Pérez, 2021). 
Subsequently, the model is tested with an actual tunnel project to analyze the key factors of total 
emissions. On average, 80% of the carbon emissions in the construction phase come from the concrete 
and steel used in tunnel construction, which indicates the carbon footprint of a tunnel can be well 
estimated when the amount of concrete and steel used is known (Rodríguez & Pérez, 2021). 
 
2.1.4 Carbon calculation in environmental impact 
The previous discussion focuses on GHG emissions (expressed as carbon equivalents) of construction 
projects. However, the environmental impact is bigger and broader than the carbon footprint. For 
example, sand is the second most used natural resource in the world and the depletion of natural 
sources of sand creates significant environmental damage. The GHG emissions associated with sand 
are very low, but this is still clearly an issue which needs to be addressed. Thomas discussed the 
possibility of replacing natural sand used in tunnelling project with recycled glass (Thomas, 2021). 
Crushed waste glass (CWG), for example, from wine bottles, could be used to replace sand in 
tunnelling supports as an ingredient in shotcrete. The research team from University of Queensland 
has tested the CWG for a year and the result indicates it is most likely to be part of the next generation 
of sustainable materials in construction industry (Thomas, 2021). The next step is to study the 
combination the CWG with other raw materials to optimize the effect of shotcrete. Sabău examined 
the environmental influence of concrete used in construction with different aggregates and cements. 
The result shows the per cubic meter of natural aggregates concrete with pure cement will emit 
between 323 and 332 kg equivalent carbon dioxide (Sabău et al., 2021). The carbon emissions can be 
==PAGE:27==
8 
 
decreased by 17% when replacing 25% of the cement with fly ash. The concrete combination of 100% 
recycled aggregates and 25% fly ash as cement also gives a lower carbon emission than the 
conventional concrete with only natural aggregate and cement (Sabău et al., 2021). 
 
2.1.5 Key contributor of carbon calculation  
Some studies have stated that around 80% capital carbon emissions are from the concrete and steel 
used in construction site (Monahan & Powell, 2011), even more in some cases (Cho & Chae, 2016). 
This indicates the concrete management could be an important role in carbon reduction. Figure 5 
shows the contribution of materials from a study case in South Korea. Clearly, the ready-mixed 
concrete is the main contributor of carbon footprint in this case.

2.2 Calculation tools 
In addition to the study by various scholars, some government agencies and specialist firms in the 
industry have developed several existing tools to calculate the carbon footprint in actual construction 
projects. Highways England carbon tool is a carbon calculator for road construction works and 
maintenance works in England (Highways England, 2019). The government company has been 
collecting information from their daily construction and maintenance operation that related to carbon 
emissions for more than ten years. With all the information, it is plausible to connect the carbon 
emissions with construction and maintenance activities by specific carbon factors and build a detailed 
and precise database. The data inventory includes ten different data categories that based on the 
certain procedures in road projects. Most categories are considered as materials consuming during 
the road work. Other non-materials categories like energy consumption, employee transport and 
waste disposal are also accounting as a part of the carbon emissions (Highways England, 2019). This 
detailed data collection is closer to the actual emissions situation and allowed the calculation tool to 
give a more comprehensive and accurate results. The outputs from this tool contain both the total 
carbon dioxide equivalent emissions for the project and the equivalent emissions each calendar month 
or financial quarter. Besides this, the result also presents the carbon emissions by different categories 
==PAGE:29==
10 
 
which could help us study the effect of various categories on the total emissions and improve the 
further operation (Highways England, 2019). However, the tool also has several weaknesses. The 
calculation only considers the carbon emissions for the duration of construction and maintenance 
work according to the contract date instead of whole life carbon emissions. The carbon tool is only 
suitable for highway project. If an exact item is not available in the carbon inventory, you can only 
choose the closet option to replace the item which in some case could cause the inaccuracy of final 
result. 
 
The whole life (construction) carbon planning tool is another tool for whole life carbon calculation 
developed by the Environment Agency of the UK. The tool is applicable to a wide range of 
construction projects and the establishment of capital and operational carbon based on the existing 
data (Environment agency of the UK, 2016). The planning tool contains two main parts – the carbon 
modelling tool and the carbon calculator. The carbon modelling tool provides a top-down carbon 
assessment during the appraisal stage which allows the customers to conduct solution optioneering 
based on the carbon footprints of the options.  Afterwards, the carbon calculator will give a detailed 
whole life carbon assessment based on the preferred project options that have minimum carbon 
emissions in the modelling tool (Environment agency of the UK, 2016). The total carbon emissions 
are divided into diverse types according to the possible source of carbon emissions. The construction 
project is considered as a combination of various assets. Each asset will be assigned a specific class 
depending on the emissions stage and link to certain carbon factor. Similar to Highways England tool, 
the whole life carbon planning tool also illustrates the results by different classification. The 
calculator computes not only whole life carbon but also the new build carbon and operational carbon. 
The carbon emissions also separated into different columns in the diagram according to the 
corresponding stage, type and asset (Environment agency of the UK, 2016). Compared to the 
Highways England tool, this tool has several advantages. First it has a detailed database of items that 
is suitable for almost all construction projects. Secondly, the customers are allowed to build their own 
asset if a particular asset is not provided in the current data sheets. Thirdly, the modelling tool enables 
the customers to conduct option selection based on a quick and brief carbon assessment before the 
actual construction activities. Finally, the tool considers the carbon life cycle of projects as 100 years 
which is more thoughtful than thinking only up to the contract award date. 
 
The Norwegian state highways authority (SVV) created a carbon footprint emissions calculator called 
Veg LCA for road infrastructure in 2017. The tool is designed to focus on comprehensive life cycle 
==PAGE:30==
11 
 
assessments of all types of road infrastructure (Hammervold & Sandstrand-Dahlstrøm, 2020). Veg 
LCA includes two tools: the intermediate phase tool and the late phase tool in road engineering. The 
intermediate phase tool is used at early stages in the road project that can make calculations of climate 
budgets (SVV’s term for carbon emissions) based on general information about the project. With 
necessary inputs, the budget tool calculates greenhouse gas emissions and energy consumption from 
the life cycle of road infrastructure which could give a rough magnitude of the carbon emissions in a 
project (Hammervold & Sandstrand-Dahlstrøm, 2020). In contrast, the late phase calculations require 
detailed data for all relevant input factors. Specific and detailed information of roads, tunnels and 
bridges are required and organized in a hierarchical structure in separated spreadsheets. Once the user 
inputs all information to the corresponding cells, the tool will automatically summarize the total 
quantities of material and energy used and reassigned them to different life cycle phases as 
development, operation and maintenance (Hammervold & Sandstrand-Dahlstrøm, 2020). In this page 
customers can submit accounts for the actual use of materials and energy for the same road project 
after construction and compare the difference in greenhouse gas emissions between budget and 
accounts. The tool provides detailed results for each input factor and road element. Other results can 
also be found given on a higher level, as well as presented graphically. Overall, Veg LCA is a 
powerful calculation tool for various road projects. Arrangements have been made for it to be easy to 
analyze which is the main contributions of total greenhouse gas emissions for the project among the 
various input factors (materials and energy), main processes and life cycle phases (development, 
operation and maintenance) (Hammervold & Sandstrand-Dahlstrøm, 2020). Moreover, Veg LCA is 
aligned with the climate module in the Norwegian Public Roads Administration's cost-benefit tool 
and the process codes from its standard specification which makes the tool easier to use and more 
transparent. All above existing tools could address the carbon calculation based on basic project data 
or detailed project data as the top-down or bottom-up method. With more and more detailed data, the 
calculation will get more precise and authentic.

3 Methods 
3.1 Basic concept of carbon footprint 
The carbon dioxide equivalent (co2e) of greenhouse gases is the tonnage of that gases multiplied by 
its greenhouse effect index which is called global warming potential (GWP) (UNFCCC COP 19, 
2013). The global warming potential of a specific gas represents the amount of carbon dioxide with 
the same warm effect of that gas in a hundred years. It transfers the climate effect of different 
greenhouse gases into one common unit and facilitates the future calculation and analysis. According 
to the definition, the GWP of CO2 is 1, and the GWP of other greenhouse gases is generally greater 
than that of carbon dioxide. However, due to their low amount in the air, CO2 is still considered to be 
the main gas causing the greenhouse effect. 
 
The carbon emissions factor is the average value of carbon dioxide equivalent (co2e) to produce a 
certain product or carry out a specific human activity. The Inventory of carbon and energy (ICE) 
launched by University of Bath has summarized the embodied energy and embodied carbon for most 
of the materials in the world. It could be a reliable reference when the carbon coefficient of specific 
material is needed. For example, the carbon emissions factor for steel described as “General - UK 
(EU) Average Recycled Content” is 1.46 kg co2e/kg. 
 
An Environmental Product Declaration (EPD) is a transparent, objective document that provides 
information about the environmental impact of products in their whole life cycle (including raw 
material acquisition stage, manufacturing and processing stage, transport stage, operation stage and 
demolition stage at the end) (Del Borghi, 2013). Companies that prepare EPD for their products have 
distinct advantages. First, clear and unified information in the whole supply chain can more easily 
avoid the use of harmful materials. There are greater opportunities to effectively use materials and 
energy, formulate clean manufacturing procedures and prolong the product life cycle. In addition, a 
verified EPD can earn your products credits in green building rating system such as LEED v4. 
 
Capital carbon (also as new build carbon) is the carbon emissions during the construction of new 
asset. In general, it consists of materials carbon, transport carbon, plant use/energy consumption and 
construction waste. Operational carbon is the carbon emissions during the operation of project like 
maintenance and repair activities. Replacement carbon is the carbon associated with the rebuilding 
==PAGE:32==
13 
 
of specific items or structures. Demolition carbon is the carbon emissions from the demolition of 
expired assets including the material waste, transport and plants for the disposal. 
 
3.2 Organization strategy of carbon calculation 
In order to meet the requirement of low carbon footprint and environmental protection. Organizations 
need to adjust their strategy and try to accomplish low carbon task of their product or project. The 
following steps could be an effective way for organization to build up an assessment system regarding 
low carbon solutions. 
 
3.2.1 Set the goals 
The basic sustainable goal for every product system is to reduce the GHG emissions during the life 
cycle of the product system and meet the requirement to accomplish the net-zero emissions by 2050. 
Several standard documents have been published as a framework for organizations to evaluate the 
carbon reduction in their projects. The Cambridge Centre for Smart Infrastructure and Construction 
has launched “Carbon Reduction Code for the Built Environment” to facilitate organizations 
achieving net zero on schedule (CSIC, 2021). The aim for all organization is to reduce the direct and 
indirect carbon emissions by 75% by 2030 in order to meet zero carbon emissions by 2045 (CSIC, 
2021). 
 
3.2.2 Set a target 
For each project, carbon reduction targets should be clearly illustrated as a deliverable in procurement 
document. It is critical for design team to discuss the carbon reduction target with procurement group 
to reach consensus in the appraisal stage which is the time for discovering low carbon choices and 
making decisions. A series of questions and objectives should be developed to fit the purpose of green 
solution but also considering the project cost.  
 
For instance, transport is an important aspect to focus on during daily construction. Early engagement 
from project manager could find potential carbon emissions that can be improved in appraisal phase. 
For example, the project team can collaborate with their chain members and suppliers to gather key 
information like quarry site, transport route and schedule. With that information, it is possible to 
design a detailed logistic plan in early phase of the construction that provides optimal route and 
==PAGE:33==
14 
 
delivery time. According to the study case of “Heysham to M6 link road project” in Guidance 
Document for PAS 2080, this operation can reduce carbon emissions and mitigate the impact on local 
traffic (Construction Leadership Council, 2016). 
 
3.3.3 Establish carbon baseline  
Establishing a carbon baseline is a key activity for the organization to use as a reference to 
preliminarily understand the carbon emissions from their product. The baseline also helps the 
organization to identify and track the change of emissions during the whole life cycle of product 
system. Therefore, it is necessary to establish a reasonable baseline for a specific project or product 
before comparing it with the low carbon solution. 
 
The carbon baseline of product system can be set by two different methodologies - top-down and 
bottom-up methods. Top-down method is mainly used at the beginning of the project when material 
data and relevant information is not available. Industry average data is considered as reference to 
calculate carbon emissions. The industry average data could be average GHG emissions per £ spent 
or average GHG emissions per unit of specific product (Construction Leadership Council, 2016). 
Then, the estimated carbon footprint can be computed with estimated total cost or designed quantity 
of product. This method enables the organization to be aware of the magnitude of carbon footprint 
before the commencement of the project. 
 
On the contrary, bottom-up method analyses all materials and activities needed for the delivery of the 
infrastructure. This methodology provides not only total carbon emissions but also the emissions of 
different stages or categories. That information allows project team to identify the main contributor 
of carbon emissions and adopt corresponding improving actions. 
 
Most organizations combine both methods to determine the baseline for the project. The intermediate 
phase tool of Veg LCA is an example of the mixture of both methods. The tool provides a detailed 
table where the project manager can fill with the number of various materials used. Otherwise, the 
manager only needs to input length of road infrastructure, lighting length and time to acquire climate 
budget which is the same as carbon baseline (Chang & Kendall, 2011). In the whole life (construction) 
carbon planning tool, the preferred option selected from carbon modelling tool will be used as 
baseline for the detailed calculation in carbon calculator. 
==PAGE:34==
15 
 
 
3.3.4 Develop carbon footprint tools  
One of the most vital steps for carbon calculation is to quantify emissions factors and environmental 
impact of various materials and activities in the whole life of specific product. For construction 
projects, several existing tools have provided various emissions factors for different items and can be 
directly used when the framework suits the case. However, if the tool does not provide data categories 
or carbon emissions factors suitable for certain project. The user could manually add exclusive items 
and customer emissions factors as we needed to obtain the carbon footprint of our project. 
 
Organization needs to prevent green washing during the process of defining the emission factor and 
recognize the environmental impact of the product. Greenwashing has become a common means for 
companies to cover up the high carbon emissions of their products (Wei & Chen, 2020). To prevent 
greenwashing occurring in tunnelling project, the organization should have a close connection with 
their suppliers and guarantee all the data collected and collated are accurate. Moreover, all activities 
in the project should align with the government standards and follow specific industrial certification. 
Sustainability reporting has become an effective way for organizations to prevent greenwashing and 
become more sustainable. 
 
3.3.5 Track during all stages of the project lifetime 
To obtain the precise data for whole life carbon calculation. All stages of the project lifetime should 
be monitoring. First, the whole life carbon emissions of different options should be computed by 
carbon model developed from previous step. The engineers compare the different options and identify 
the solution with lowest carbon footprint (Rodríguez et al., 2012). Once the preferred option has 
determined, the project manager should share the strategy and project plan with all partners and 
suppliers. The manager should explain the system boundary and life cycle assumptions of the project 
and highlight the main contributors of emissions. Whole life of the project will be divided into 
different stages and activities which allow the practitioners easily to understand. Then, partners and 
suppliers should follow the lead of design team and document all data inside the system boundary in 
functional unit that can directly be used for carbon calculation (Copur et al., 2012). Constructors can 
compare the actual number of materials and energy used with the designed data of same activities to 
identify whether capital carbon in these activities can be further reduced or whether it is likely to be 
increased (Cardu & Seccatore, 2016). New-built carbon data should be documented as single file for 
==PAGE:35==
16 
 
the future improvement of carbon modelling. Finally, a monitoring system should be established in 
operational phase to quantify the carbon emissions (Mottahedi et al., 2018). The monitoring system 
should document the information like accurate sources of data, data collectors and collection methods. 
Project managers should consider whether existing management systems can be used to help collect 
and maintain such information until the end of the project whole life (Lizhen Huang et al., 2020).  
 
3.3 LCA methods 
The life cycle assessment (LCA) method is an internationally used methodology to evaluate the 
environmental impact of a specific product, process or service in the whole life cycle. In order to 
employ LCA successfully, the researcher need to identify and quantify the energy, material 
consumption and environmental emissions of waste in the whole life cycle stage of a practical product. 
Afterwards, the impact of these consumption and emissions on the environment should be evaluated. 
Finally, the researcher analyses and evaluates the opportunities to reduce these impacts and improved 
performance for mitigate the negative effect (Bandini et al., 2017). Basically, life cycle assessment is 
considered as a new environmental management tool and preventive means of environmental 
protection. Figure 7 is an example of the life cycle for tunnel project by different stages. 
 
 
Figure 7 The common life cycle of tunnel project 
The life cycle assessment contains four steps and the activities for each step are illustrated below: 
1. Definition of the goal and the scope 
==PAGE:36==
17 
 
This stage is to define the objectives and scope of LCA. It is the first step and the most critical 
part of whole LCA. The objective definition mainly describes the reason and purpose of LCA, 
and the scope definition mainly describes the functional unit, system boundary, allocation 
procedure, reference flow, original data quality requirements and the assumptions and limitations 
of the product system studied (Hernandez et al., 2018). The definition of objective and scope 
directly determines the depth and breadth of LCA. Meanwhile, the research scope may need to be 
continuously adjusted and improved due to the change of study objective. 
2. Inventory analysis 
Inventory analysis is the process of establishing inventory of input and output data in the studied 
product system. It mainly includes data collection and calculation to quantify the relevant inputs 
and outputs according to the system boundary. Firstly, the life cycle model is established 
according to the research scope determined in the goal and scope definition stage to prepare for 
data collection. Then, the data need to be related to unit process and functional unit. Finally, all 
results obtained from above process should list together as the inventory of the product in whole 
life cycle. 
3. Impact assessment 
The purpose of impact assessment is to evaluate the environmental impact of product life cycle 
according to the results of inventory analysis stage. This process transforms the inventory data 
into specific impact types and index, which is more convenient to understand the environmental 
impact of product in life cycle. In addition, this stage also provides necessary information for the 
interpretation stage of life cycle results. 
4. Interpretation 
Interpretation is to identify major problems in the life cycle of study product based on the results 
of inventory analysis and impact assessment. By evaluating the results, including analyzing 
integrity, sensitivity and consistency of previous steps, some conclusions can be drawn regarding 
the LCA. Meanwhile, the limitation of the assessment and suggestion for performance 
improvement should also be presented in this part.

4.4 Data assumptions 
From the original project data, major information is about the materials used and very few other 
information is provided. This could cause a large underestimation on carbon emission due to missing 
data. Those data like energy consumption are not well-documented at construction site and hardly to 
achieve at present. Hence, it is necessary to make a reasonable assumption about missing information 
like construction machine and energy consumption. The assumption about plant use is based on the 
scale and construction time of the project. Those detailed information is discussed with the project 
manager Morten Jensen to ensure the value is not overstated.

To enter reasonable data in the plant use section, it is necessary to understand activities involving the 
use of large plants during the construction works. The general construction works for building shaft 
SB and tunnel section with inner diameter 3000mm are explicitly illustrated below by steps. From 
those steps, the type of plants used in this tunnel jacking project can be summarized.  
1. Setting up the site at the middle shaft (shaft SB), which also involved utility re-locations 
2. Secant pile construction in the middle shaft SB 
3. Casting capping beam for middle shaft SB 
4. Excavating down the middle shaft SB 
5. Installation of thrust wall and sealing device (sealing ring) in the shaft SB 
6. Setting up the plant (muck separator, slurry plant and gantry crane on rails) and lowering down 
the TBM (tunnel boring machine) into the shaft SB (heavy crane involved) 
7. Tunneling from the shaft SB to Shaft KALV (the shaft locates in the harbor) including work of 
bentonite pumping and separation plant, disposal of soil from the site, delivery of pipe segments 
and lowering these items down by gantry crane 
8. Receival of the TBM at the Shaft KALV (procedure involving heavy lifting and cutting) 
9. At shaft SB, connecting the new cloudburst tunnel the existing sewer system.

6 Discussion 
6.1 Evaluation of results from CMT and CC 
From the result of CMT and CC, the whole life carbon is 34863 t co2e (Figure 18) and 3554.8 t co2e 
(Figure 20) respectively which is nearly ten times difference. The main disparity comes from 
replacement carbon and operational carbon which are both important contributors in CMT but are 
close to 0 in CC. The reason could be the reference projects that used to build the asset model in CMT 
have pump station during daily operation. This will generate high operational carbon due to energy 
consumption by pump station. As for replacement carbon, the asset model in CMT conservatively 
assume the standard life of many items are 60 years while in CC the standard life of assets is 
considered as 120 years and the refurbishment type is not possible. This is also why the demolition 
carbon from CMT is 6384 t co2e but only 16.7 t co2e in CC. 
 
In addition, the capital carbon for the tunnel and shaft is 4732 t co2e from CMT which is more than 
twice the value from CC. Several reasons could have caused the difference. 
⚫ In our case, we barely consider the transport carbon in the whole life cycle of tunnel project. 
The information about transport of staff and material is not recorded on site. Therefore, we 
assume the materials are all from local suppliers and the mode of transport is van with a 
distance of 50 km. The reference project is from England which might import some material 
overseas by shipment. And this will cause more transport carbon. 
⚫ The energy consumption in the study case is relatively low due to the use of clean energy. In 
the study case, all plants are driven by electricity and 47% of electricity are generated from 
wind power in Denmark according to an industrial report (Gronholt-Pedersen, 2020). 
Therefore, a low emission factor is selected for electricity in this project and low carbon 
footprint are generated by energy consumption compared to the result from CMT. 
 
Overall, the result from CMT and CC is tremendously different.  The carbon modelling tool could 
calculate whole life carbon with only asset and asset measurement at the appraisal phase of project. 
However, the high uncertainty of asset model indicates a potential large error in the result. Meanwhile, 
the accuracy of result from CMT is highly depended on the similarity between your project and the 
reference project. Therefore, the result from CMT is not credible and only accepted when bottom-up 
==PAGE:62==
43 
 
carbon assessment is unavailable. The result from CC should be the one adopted for further discussion.

Advantage and disadvantage of the tool:

One way to solve the problem about concrete recipe is to use the concrete carbon calculation tool for 
specific concrete recipe to obtain the unique carbon footprint of that concrete. Concrete Embodied 
Carbon Footprint Calculator is a powerful tool to achieve the purpose and is free to all users. The 
embodied carbon database of the tool is from the ICE (inventory of carbon and energy) which is 
created from a research project by the University of Bath (Geoff Hammond, 2008). This ICE database 
is also the same one used in the whole life (construction) carbon planning tool which make sure the 
consistency between the whole life carbon calculation and concrete recipe calculation. The concrete 
calculator allows users to enter the quantity of cement, fly ash, blast furnace slag and limestone fines 
for per cubic meter concrete. Afterwards, the users can enter the w/c ration, reinforcement steel and 
transport distance from concrete producer to construction site. Those parameters can provide more 
precise description of the concrete recipe and contribute to a more accurate result. And the highlight 
of the tool is it could enter the admixtures according to the diverse function. Figure 34 is the list of 
all types of admixtures in the concrete calculator. This is a small contributor of concrete caron

7 Conclusion 
This project aims at calculating the carbon footprint of tunnelling projects for the water utility sector. 
To that end, the whole life cycle of tunnelling project is assessed and all activities involving carbon 
emissions are discovered and quantified. The whole life (construction) carbon planning tool, 
developed by the environment agency of UK, are used for the carbon calculation. The results from 
CMT and CC are evaluated and both advantage and disadvantage of the tool are mentioned. Then, 
key contributors of carbon emissions are identified based on the result and possible low- carbon 
solution and improvement are discussed. 
 
From previous steps, several main conclusions of this project can be summarized as below: 
⚫ LCA method could assess the carbon footprint during the whole life cycle of tunnelling 
project. The carbon footprint is separated into capital carbon, operational carbon, 
replacement carbon and demolition carbon according to the stage of emissions in the life 
cycle of project. And the carbon emissions of each stage can be further separated into more 
detailed categories that can be quantified. 
⚫ CMT gives an exaggerate whole life carbon for tunnelling project due to small project 
database and high-uncertainty model. Therefore, the CMT is not suitable for tunnelling 
project in Denmark. However, this situation could improve in the future when a vast 
database is established. 
⚫ CC is applicable for tunnelling project in Denmark. The data categories in CC meet the 
requirement of carbon footprint calculation for tunnelling project. The carbon emissions for 
each item or activities are reasonable compared with some academic papers. Moreover, the 
concrete carbon from VegLCA and CC are in good agreement which verify both tools are 
given rational results for material carbon. 
⚫ The capital carbon is 2230.4 t co2e and accounts for 63% of whole life carbon which is the 
largest contributor among all stages. Replacement carbon is the second largest contributor 
(36%) that is automatically computed in CC according to the data in capital carbon section. 
⚫ Materials is the main source of carbon emissions for capital carbon which accounts for 85%. 
More than 99% material carbon comes from concrete (including the reinforcement steel) 
which is the dominant part in this whole section. The concrete carbon can reduce around 
45% by adding secondary cementitious materials like slag and fly ash into concrete in this 
==PAGE:89==
70 
 
study case. 
⚫ Energy consumption is a relatively small contributor that accounts for 6 % of total carbon 
which is benefited from the green electricity grid of Denmark. Meanwhile, low emission 
factor of electricity also indicates lower emissions factors for other building materials which 
consume electricity during the production. 
⚫ From the aspect of environmental protection, using recycled material like crushed white 
glass and waste steel fiber can make the project more environmentally friendly and 
sustainable.

8 Future work 
Hypothetically, this project will proceed in the future and develop a new customized tool for 
tunnelling project based on Danish situation. I would like to share several general ideas for the future 
work.  
⚫ The goal of the tool is to provide carbon-related information for local tunnelling projects 
which are in progress at the moment and planned for the near future and make low carbon 
suggestions to project managers. 
⚫ The framework of the new tool should be customized for the Danish context. This includes 
designing the interface and the data categories with the content that could be associated with 
the tunnelling project in Denmark. 
⚫ The data collection for the tool should focus on the information of typical tunnelling project 
from local (within the Denmark) so that the calculator is developed as a plausible tool to be 
practically applied in local tunnelling projects.  
⚫ This tool should be testified with extra tunnel projects to demonstrate its practical benefits. 
And the new data help elaborate the calculator. 
⚫ A methodology will be developed to cover issues such as establishing a clear baseline and 
tracking progress during project evolution. The baseline of carbon footprint provides a starting 
point for studies into how to reduce the overall impact of a project and more specific topics 
such as where materials are coming from and whether this is a sustainable option.
