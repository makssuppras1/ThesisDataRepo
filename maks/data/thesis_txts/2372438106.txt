ABSTRACT 
 
Microplastic pollution in the environment is a widely recognised concern. Recently it was 
estimated that a large fraction of microplastics originate from car tire rubber, i.e. over 50 % of 
the total microplastic emissions in Denmark and Norway. These particles are transported into 
the environment and sewer systems by wind and runoff from roads. However, rubber particles 
originating from car tire abrasion have not been directly found in the wastewater sludge or 
soil, possibly due to their small size (< 20 μm). Therefore, degradability of the rubber in 
different environmental conditions needs to be considered. This study evaluates 
biodegradability of pristine and weathered car tire rubber by lab-scale experiments and 
scanning electron microscope (SEM) analysis. Two different inocula, soil and activated 
sludge, were used in order to assess the particle fate. Furthermore, this study provides an 
assessment of the suitability of the existing biodegradability standard tests, OECD 301 and 
ISO 14851, for testing biodegradability of microplastics. Biodegradable plastic poly(D,L-
lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) was used as a positive control in the biodegradability tests. 
 
In the study, car tire rubber was not found to fulfil the biodegradability criteria of the 
standard guidelines (pass level of 60 % of theoretical oxygen demand production). However, 
pristine rubber and weathered rubber showed some biodegradability with activated sludge 
[pristine 4.1 % (3.8-4.5), weathered 7.2 % (6.8-7.6)] and soil supernatant [pristine 1.5 % (0.8-
2.3), weathered 1.1 % (0.8-1.5)]. Degradation of pristine rubber was not experienced when 
using soil particles as an inoculum. Accelerated weathering was found to increase 
biodegradability of the car tire rubber with activated sludge. Moreover, a 2.5-fold higher 
biomass concentration increased biodegradability of weathered rubber to 8.4 % with activated 
sludge and to 4.5 % with soil supernatant. The positive control, PLGA was not found to fulfil 
the biodegradability criteria of the standard guidelines, reaching 5.3 % biodegradability with 
soil, 3.8 % (3.1-4.5) with soil supernatant and 14.7 % (12.1-17.4) with activated sludge. SEM 
analysis was done to the pristine and weathered rubber particles before and after the 
biodegradability tests to detect structural changes. The analysis confirmed degradation of the 
particle surface after the experiments, seen as porosity and roughness.  
 
Challenges were experienced when using the existing standard methods for testing 
biodegradability of microplastics. Longer lag phase (1 day) was seen when using soil as an 
inoculum, which led to the use of soil supernatant. With soil supernatant the lag phase was 
reduced by 1 day, however, high differences between replicates were observed (pristine 95.7 
==PAGE:5==
v 
 
%, weathered 58.8 %, PLGA 38.6 %). This could be due to the low level degradability of the 
test compounds and the sensitivity of soil supernatant as an inoculum. 
 
As an outcome of the study, car tire rubber degradability needs to be taken into 
consideration when investigating microplastic fate in the environment and during wastewater 
treatment. Furthermore, it is recommended to use longer testing times (180 days to 18 
months), relevant inocula (different soil, activated sludge, water sediment and surface water 
bacteria) and alternating biomass concentrations in future studies. To obtain information 
about the degrading fragment of the rubber and the degradability of the end-products, 
degradability test with different compounds of the rubber is suggested. Lastly, PLGA is not 
recommended to be used as positive control in short-term tests due to its limited 
biodegradability in 28 days. Natural materials, e.g. cellulose and poly-3-hydroxybutyrate 
(PHB) are suggested to be used as positive controls in future studies.

1 INTRODUCTION AND AIM 
 
Plastic pollution in the environment is a widely recognised concern (Van Cauwenberghe et 
al., 2013; Sivan, 2011). The increasing production and use of plastic combined with littering 
and poor recycling has resulted in plastic debris accumulation in natural habitats, including 
water bodies (Thompson et al., 2009; Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013). In recent years the 
main focus has been in microplastics, which are commonly defined as particles with a 
diameter smaller than 5 mm (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013; Arthur & Baker, 2009). 
Microplastics have been found in the marine environment worldwide (Van Cauwenberghe et 
al., 2013; Thompson et al., 2004). Because of the possible sorption of chemicals and ingestion 
by marine organisms, microplastic pollution poses a risk to aquatic life (Van Cauwenberghe 
et al., 2013; Thompson et al., 2009).  
Recent microplastic abundance studies have estimated that a great fraction of particles 
originate from city dust, including car tires (Mepex, 2014). Although these particles have not 
been classified as microplastics before, it might be necessary due to their properties and 
extensive polymer content (Mepex, 2014). The car tire consists of synthetic styrene-butadiene 
rubber (SBR), natural rubber, additives and fillers, including clays, carbon black and silica 
(Mepex, 2014; Genan, 2015; WRAP, 2006). If the emission factors, the amount of different 
vehicles, life-time and the SBR content of the tires are considered, the microplastic pollution 
from tire wear is estimated to be annually over 50 % of the total microplastic emissions in 
Norway and Denmark (Mepex, 2014; Lassen et al., 2015). The particles are transported to the 
water bodies and the environment by wind and runoff from the roads (Mepex, 2014; Lassen et 
al., 2015). Therefore, the emissions from tire rubber can be considered as a potential major 
source of microplastic contamination (Mepex, 2014). 
Microplastics have a potential to undergo degradation (Andrary, 2011). This can occur 
as biodegradation, where the carbon of the polymer is utilised by microbes, converted to CO2 
and integrated into biomass (Andrary, 2011; Müller, 2005). Moreover, abiotic degradation by 
sunlight, temperature or reactions with water may occur as a first stage degradation or parallel 
with the biotic process, causing plastic weathering (Müller, 2005). Rate of degradation 
depends on the environmental conditions and the molecular structure and composition of the 
plastic (Müller, 2005; Andrary, 2011). However, little is known about the degradability of 
microplastics and polymers in the environment. 
In microplastic detection studies, car tire rubber particles have not been found in the 
wastewater sludge or soil, possibly due to their small size (< 20 μm) (Lassen et al., 2015; 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
EPA, 2017). Therefore, degradability of the particles in different environmental conditions 
needs to be investigated.  
 
1.1 Aim and objectives 
 
The overarching aim of the study was to investigate the biodegradability of microplastics 
originating from car tire rubber with different inocula and to evaluate the suitability of 
existing standard methods to testing biodegradability of microplastics.  
 
The objectives of this study were the following: 
 Based on available literature, to characterise microplastics, consider their sources and 
effects including a focus on car tire particles and the degradation pathways of 
microplastics.  
 Evaluate the car tire rubber degradability under different environmental conditions by 
using soil and activated sludge as inocula in order to assess the particle fate. 
 Determine 
the 
influence 
of 
accelerated 
microplastic 
weathering 
on 
the 
biodegradability of car tire rubber, by exposing the particles to UV radiation. 
 Observe the structural changes in the surface of pristine and weathered rubber 
particles before and after the biodegradability tests to observe the influence of 
degradation. 
 Determine and evaluate the suitability of existing, standard biodegradability tests 
(OECD 301 and ISO 14851) for testing biodegradability of microplastics. 
 Analyse the suitability of PLGA as a representable biodegradable plastic and a 
positive control in standard-based biodegradability tests. 
 
The objectives were completed through a literature review and a range of techniques, 
including lab-scale experiments and scanning electron microscope (SEM) analysis.

2 BACKGROUND 
 
The following chapter provides an overview about plastic debris and microplastics, with a 
focus on rubber particles originating from car tires. Moreover, it will include information 
about the principle of the abiotic and biotic degradation processes and different standard tests 
to measure biodegradability.  
 
2.1 Origin of plastic debris in the environment 
 
Plastics are synthetic polymers that can be used for various purposes (Thompson et al., 2009; 
Andrary, 2011). Synthetic polymers are created by polymerisation of monomers derived from 
oil and gas and addition of chemical additives (Thompson et al., 2009; Sivan, 2011). Plastics 
are extremely versatile and have positive qualities, including strength, durability and high 
thermal and electrical insulation properties (Thompson et al., 2009; Barnes et al., 2009). The 
diversity of the polymers and the properties results in the production of vast variety of plastics 
used for different purposes (Thompson et al., 2009). Due to its beneficial features, plastic is a 
widely used material that furthermore exerts as a major environmental threat (Sivan, 2011). 
As a result of inadequate recycling (Sivan, 2011) and modern littering behaviour, plastic 
debris has accumulated natural habitats including water bodies (Thompson et al., 2009). 
Streams and rivers transport plastic debris to the marine and freshwater environment, where it 
poses a risk to aquatic life (Brander et al., 2011). Marine macro- and microplastic 
contamination is resulting in species endangering by injuries, impaired movement and 
ingestion of the debris (Thompson et al., 2009; Brander et al., 2011). Moreover, there are 
concerns of toxic chemicals sorbing to the microplastics that could potentially leach to the 
organisms through the plastic debris (Thompson et al., 2009).  
 
There are various sources of plastic accumulation in the environment, including 
domestic items such as plastic packaging, bags and footwear (Barnes et al., 2009; Müller, 
2005). Several classes of plastics are used for these purposes, including Polyethylene (PE), 
Polypropylene (PP), Polystyrene (PS), Polyethylene terephthalate (PET) and Polyvinyl 
chloride (PVC) (Andrary, 2011). Because of the high-volume use and production, they are 
very likely to be found in the marine environment (Andrary, 2011). Plastic persistency is 
estimated to be from hundreds to thousands of years or even longer in deep sea environments 
with low oxygen content and absence of light (Barnes et al., 2009; Brander et al., 2011).

Therefore, there are growing concerns of the overall impacts of the plastic debris to the 
organisms and the environment. 
 
2.2 Environmental impact of microplastics 
 
Microplastics are small particles that originate from intended production or from the large 
plastic debris by degradation (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013; Lassen et al., 2015). They can 
be defined as particles with a diameter smaller than 5 mm or 1 mm, depending on the 
classification (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013; Arthur & Baker, 2009). Because of the small 
size, they can be ingested by marine organisms and potentially cause harmful effects (Van 
Cauwenberghe et al., 2013; Brander et al., 2011; Cole et al., 2013). Microplastics have been 
discovered in the marine environments worldwide (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013; Mepex, 
2014; Thompson et al., 2004). Since the oceans do not have borders, microplastics have a 
potential to spread interminably and they can therefore be considered as a global 
environmental concern (Mepex, 2014; Brander et al., 2011).  
 
Microplastic pollution can be categorised as primary or secondary microplastics 
(Mepex, 2014; Arthur & Baker, 2009). Primary microplastics are intentionally produced 
particles, including pre-produced granules, abrasives and microbeads used in cosmetic 
products (Mepex, 2014; Arthur & Baker, 2009). Secondary microplastics are formed in the 
environment from the weathering of larger marine debris, and their production rate depends 
on the properties of the plastic, mechanical abrasion, UV radiation and the local 
environmental conditions (Mepex, 2014; Arthur & Baker, 2009; Ivleva et al., 2016).  
Because of the different types and sizes of particles, plastics often occur as a mixture 
in the marine environment and they can be found throughout the water column (Mepex, 2014; 
Ryan et al., 2009; Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013). High density plastic tends to settle, while 
the low density plastic remains buoyant (Cole et al., 2013; Ryan et al., 2009). However, 
oceanographic factors and freshwater inputs cause vertical mixing, resulting as different areal 
concentrations of microplastics and the occurrence of microplastics in remote islands and 
shoreline sediments (Cole et al., 2013; Arthur & Baker, 2009; Van Cauwenberghe et al., 
2013). High concentrations of microplastics are found in oceanic gyres, particularly in the 
North Pacific Gyre known as the Great Garbage Patch (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2013; 
Lassen et al., 2015). Furthermore, microplastics have been found in deep-sea sediments (Van 
Cauwenberghe et al., 2013), remote mountain lakes (Free et al., 2014), several rivers around 
==PAGE:13==
5 
 
the world (Lassen et al., 2015) and even in the oceans surrounding Antarctica (Andrary, 
2011). 
 
Identifying the source of microplastic pollution is difficult due to different locations, 
emission times and stages of degradation (Mepex, 2014; Lassen et al., 2015). Part of the 
emissions can be assumed to have a historical origin but there are new sources from which 
some are unknown or recently found (Mepex, 2014). However, estimations of the emissions 
can be calculated based on the weight of the plastic products and the latest known production 
and use volumes (Mepex, 2014).  
 
2.2.1 Microplastics from car tires  
 
In recent microplastic occurrence studies, Norwegian, Danish and Swedish researchers 
estimated that based on calculations a large fraction of microplastics is originating from city 
dust, including the rubber from car tires (Mepex, 2014; Lassen et al., 2015). Rubber has not 
been formerly classified as microplastic, however, due to its properties and substantial 
polymer based material it might be necessary (Mepex, 2014; RIVM, 2015). Composition of 
passenger car and off-the-road (OTR) tire are presented in Table 1. A common-sized car tire 
made by Goodyear contains 30 kinds of synthetic rubber, 8 kinds of natural rubber, 8 kinds of 
carbon black, steel cord for belts, polyester and nylon fibre, steel bead wire and 40 different 
chemicals, waxes, oils, pigments, silica and clays (WRAP, 2006). The rubber particles 
originating from city dust and the wear of the tire are predominantly coarse particles in the 
size range of 2.5-10 μm (Lassen et al., 2015). However, other sources have indicated the 
particle size being greater, with an estimated length of 220-1230 μm (Lassen et al., 2015).

The wear surface of car tire consists partly of synthetic polymers, specifically from 
styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR) that can be classified as plastic (Mepex, 2014). The fragment

additives and fillers, including clays, carbon black and silica (Mepex, 2014; Genan, 2015; 
WRAP, 2006). The properties of SBR, including its abrasion resistance and beneficial cost-
performance balance, make it a highly desirable material for tires (ICIS Chemical Business, 
2004). Therefore, it is used in large volumes in the tire industry (ICIS Chemical Business,

In studies from the United Kingdom, the emission factor for the particle generation for 
a passenger car is estimated to be 0.1 grams per vehicle-kilometre (Mepex, 2014). For

According to EPA (2017) and Lassen et al. (2015), car tire rubber particles have not 
been found in the soil or the activated sludge in microplastic detection studies, possibly due to 
their small size. However, potential degradation in the environment needs to be taken into 
evaluation when assessing particle fate in the environment. In a previous study, Cadle & 
Williams (1980) used thermogravimetric analysis, pyrolysis-gas-chromatography and 
extraction pyrolysis-GC methods to investigate car tire rubber degradability in soil and 
suggested that atmospheric oxidation is more significant form of degradation than the 
microbial attack. To obtain more information on the pathways of car tire rubber degradation, 
more research with different methods and inocula needs to be conducted.  
 
2.3 Degradation pathways of microplastics 
 
Microplastics can be attacked by a variety of natural and man-made agents, resulting in 
degradation (Fried, 2003; Andrary, 2011). Degradation is a reduction of the molecular weight 
of the polymer that results as weakening of the material (Andrary, 2011). It can occur 
abiotically or biotically, and it is a crucial environmental process when considering the speed 
of chemical disappearance in the nature (Müller, 2005; De Henau, 2009).  
 
From the perspective of industry, degradation is usually undesired (Fried, 2003). For 
instance, the unsaturated elastomers of rubber tires can be extremely degraded by UV 
radiation and ozone, resulting in a reduced life-time of the tire (Fried, 2003). Therefore, 
different fillers and stabilisers are added to the formulation to create resistance towards the 
degrading agents (Fried, 2003). However, in some cases degradation is desirable, for instance 
when considering plastic and packaging that can rapidly degrade into environmentally safe 
by-products, such as CO2 and biomass, contributing less volume to landfills (Fried, 2003).  
 
Due to the difficult decomposability of polymeric materials, degradability of 
microplastics needs to be considered to overcome the environmental problems associated with 
pollution from car tire rubber (Shah et al., 2013). 
 
2.3.1 Biodegradation 
 
Degradation of microplastics can occur by biodegradation, where carbon from the polymer is 
utilised by microbes, converted to CO2 and integrated into biomass. (Andrary, 2011; Lassen 
et al., 2015; Müller, 2005). In biodegradation organic substances are broken down by 
enzymatic processes, leading to the disappearance of the molecular structure and the

formation of smaller organic species that are directly usable for cell anabolism and converted 
into carbon dioxide and water (De Henau, 2009). The biodegradation process of an organic 
substance is described in Figure 1.

Biodegradation can proceed partially or totally, depending on the molecular structure 
(De Henau, 2009). Partial biodegradation is an indication of an inherent persistent residue that 
is unable to go through further biodegradation (De Henau, 2009). Incomplete biodegradation 
may furthermore be caused by unfavourable environmental conditions or the absence of 
acclimatised microbial communities (De Henau, 2009). Complete mineralisation takes place 
once the biodegradation is finalised and all the organic carbon of the polymer is converted 
into carbon dioxide and recycled into biomass (De Henau, 2009). However, there is no 
existing data on mineralisation of microplastics or nano-scale plastics in the marine 
environment (Andrary, 2011; Lassen et al., 2015).  
In general, most of the polymers are highly resistant to the attack of microorganisms 
due to their cross-linked structure, unique chemical composition and the presence of additives 
(Fried, 2003; Shah et al., 2013). Therefore, biodegradation of natural and synthetic polymers 
proceeds at a relatively slow rate in the environment (Shah et al., 2013). However, 
biopolymers and a few synthetic polymers, such as aliphatic polyesters have been found to 
biodegrade rapidly in aquatic environment (Andrary, 2011).  
 
2.3.2 Abiotic degradation 
 
In addition to the biodegradation process, abiotic chemical and physical factors can similarly 
affect to the polymers (Müller, 2005; Gewert et al., 2015). Abiotic effects can occur parallel 
to the biotic process or as a first stage degradation (Müller, 2005). Often, they result in 
accelerated deterioration of the plastics, known as weathering (Fried, 2003).  These effects 
include (a) photodegradation, where the degradation is caused by action of light, (b) thermal 
polymer degradation, where the degradation is caused by high temperatures, (c) thermo-
oxidative degradation, where degradation occurs as slow oxidative breakdown at moderate 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
temperatures and (d) hydrolysis, where the degradation is caused by the reaction with water 
(Müller, 2005; Andrary, 2011). Photo-oxidation is primarily caused by sunlight and the 
exposure to UV radiation (Andrary, 2011). Once photo-oxidation is initiated, degradation can 
proceed thermo-oxidatively without continuing exposure to UV radiation and it is able to 
sequence as long as the system has oxygen (Andrary, 2011). In the degradation process, the 
molecular weight of the polymer is decreased and new, oxygen-rich groups are created within 
the polymer, making the polymer brittle and available for further degradation (Andrary, 2011; 
Gewert et al., 2015). Figure 2 shows the photo-oxidation process for polyisoprene, illustrating 
the formation of new oxygen groups.  
 
Figure 2: Photo-oxidation of polyisoprene (Fried, 2003) 
Degradation of microplastics has been found to proceed faster compared to the 
macroplastics, because of the higher surface to volume ratio (Gewert et al., 2015). Commonly 
plastics degrade first at the surface of the polymer, resulting as the exposure and availability 
for chemical or enzymatic attack (Gewert et al., 2015). The degradation of the polymer can be 
seen visually as crazing, embrittlement and change of color (Gewert et al., 2015).   
 
2.3.3 Conditions affecting degradation  
 
The rate of degradation depends highly on the environment where the plastics are located 
(Lassen et al., 2015). In aquatic environments the degradation and weathering rate is lagged 
due to the lower levels of oxygen and temperature when compared to air and land (Andrary, 
2011; Lassen et al., 2015). In land environments, e.g. beaches, the degradation is several 
orders of magnitude faster than in water column or the marine sediment (Lassen et al., 2015). 
This is caused by differences in several factors, including solar UV radiation, pH, salinity, 
humidity, temperature, nutrients and mechanical abrasion (Müller, 2005; Andrary, 2011). 
Moreover, the environmental factors influence the microbial population and their activity, and 
consequently the biotic degradation (Müller, 2005).

10 
 
 
The structure and composition of the plastics further complicates the degradation 
process (Müller, 2005). Typically plastic materials do not consist of one homogenous 
component but contain several different polymers or additives (Müller, 2005). Within one 
polymer, different structural elements can exist as copolymers and they may be distributed 
alternately or used to build long structural blocks (Müller, 2005). Moreover, branching of 
chains may occur (Müller, 2005). The different structures of polymers can influence the 
degradation behaviour and process (Müller, 2005). Furthermore, additives might extend the 
degradation period, as a result of biofilm formation (Lassen et al., 2015).  
 
2.3.4 Test methods for biodegradability 
 
Test methods for biodegradability have been available for several years for different materials 
(Müller, 2005). When a new chemical product is introduced to the market, its ecological 
impacts need to be evaluated (Müller, 2005). Therefore, a variety of standardised tests have 
been developed for different materials and environments with different analytical methods 
(Müller, 2005). Test methods for polymer degradation by microorganisms have been 
established before the existence of biodegradable plastics (Müller, 2005). Even though 
conventional plastics are relatively resistant to environmental influences, they can be partially 
attacked by microorganisms causing undesired changes in mechanical properties, including 
color and mechanical strength (Müller, 2005). The test methods later developed for 
biodegradable plastics are predominantly based on evaluation of low molecular-weight 
substances (Müller, 2005). However, they have been modified for particular environments, 
considering the complex composition of plastics and the heterogeneous surface degradation 
mechanism (Müller, 2005). 
 
Biodegradation is measured as a rate of oxygen uptake, removal of dissolved organic 
carbon or production of carbon dioxide. In biodegradability analysis, a defined media is 
inoculated with a mixed microbial population or individual microbial strains (Müller, 2005). 
The used inoculum may be derived from activated sludge, sewage effluent, surface water, 
soil, compost or from a mixture of these (OECD, 1992; ISO, 2004). Different options and 
flexibility make biodegradability tests versatile and available for further modification. Table 3 
presents the selected OECD and ISO standards for testing biodegradability. OECD 301 is a 
guideline for measuring Ready Biodegradability of Chemicals in an Aerobic Aqueous 
Medium and ISO 14851 is a guideline for measuring Determination of the Ultimate Aerobic 
Biodegradability of Plastic Materials in an Aqueous Medium.

Higher polymer degradation rate is often observed in the standard-based laboratory

absolute degradation rate of plastics in the natural environment can only be evaluated to a 
certain extent (Müller, 2005). However, biodegradability tests are widely used for systematic 
investigations (Müller, 2005). Therefore, testing the biodegradability of microplastics can be 
used to evaluate the potential of microbial degradation of the polymers in different 
environmental conditions.

3 MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
This study included two biodegradability tests: (a) a preliminary biodegradability test and (b) 
an optimised biodegradability test. In the preliminary test, pristine car tire rubber was used to 
assess the biodegradability of the compound by using two different inocula: soil and activated 
sludge. In the optimised test, weathered car tire rubber was used to evaluate the influence of 
accelerated weathering to the level of biodegradability by two different inocula: soil 
supernatant and activated sludge. Furthermore, scanning electron microscope analysis was 
performed to the rubber particles before and after the tests. 
 
This chapter will give a detailed description of the materials and methods used in the 
study and in the tests. The detailed equations for the biodegradability tests are presented in 
Appendix 1.

3.2 Preliminary biodegradability test 
 
Biodegradability of the car tire rubber particles needs to be considered, since the fate of the 
particles in the environment is unknown. Therefore, ready biodegradability of the particles 
was tested to analyse the biological degradation potential. The preliminary biodegradability 
test was done based on the OECD guideline for testing Ready Biodegradability to evaluate 
the biological degradability of car tire rubber. 
 
The manometric respirometry test (OECD 301 F) in a closed bottle system was chosen 
as an appropriate test method due to the low solubility of the test compound and the desired 
test concentration (75 mg/l). The test for ready biodegradability was completed using 
OxiTop® instrumentation seen in Figure 3. 
Figure 3: Instrumentation for the biodegradability test 
 
 
 
The principle of the biodegradability test was to measure the CO2 production of the 
inoculated bacteria with an addition of the test substance. The produced CO2 over time can be 
presented as oxygen consumption, in particular as biological oxygen demand (BOD). Ready 
biodegradability is measured as the ratio of theoretical oxygen demand (ThOD) and BOD.  
 
In the preliminary test, the biodegradability of the test compound, pristine rubber, and 
the positive control, PLGA, were evaluated. Moreover, the possible inhibitory effects of the 
compounds were considered by preparing flasks with the chosen compound and a reference 
substance, sodium acetate (NaAc). This was done in order to assess the toxicity of the 
compounds. The inhibition flasks were prepared at the same concentrations as the other flasks 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
and with both used inocula. However, due to the higher expected BOD value, a smaller 
volume of the sample was used resulting in a lower amount of added substance. The 
inhibitory flasks were run parallel to the other flasks, giving the same conditions and test 
duration.

3.2.5 Positive control 
 
To analyse the biodegradability of the test compound, a positive control was needed for a 
comparison. For this purpose, biodegradable plastic was ordered from Sigma-Aldrich. The 
chosen plastic was Resomer® RG 502 H Poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA). The 
compound was received as homogenous powder. The flasks with positive control contained 
25.3 mg of PLGA with an exception of the inhibition samples, which contained 17.35 mg of 
PLGA.  
 
3.2.6 Reference substance 
 
Sodium acetate (NaAc) was used as a reference substance to test the functionality of the 
biodegradability test. Flasks with 35.1 mg of NaAc were prepared. Additionally, 24.1 mg of 
NaAc was added to the inhibition flasks with the test compound and the positive control.  
 
3.2.7 Test conditions 
 
The test for biodegradability proceeded for 28 days, according to the OECD standard. The 
solutions were stirred in closed flasks at a constant temperature of 20±1°C.  Each sample was 
prepared in two replicates. To each flask, a drop of nitrification inhibitor Allylthiourea (ATU) 
was added to every 50 ml of liquid. Additionally, two pellets of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) 
were added to the rubber sleeve of each flask to absorb carbon dioxide.   
 
3.3 Accelerated weathering and optimised biodegradability test 
 
Following the preliminary biodegradability test, accelerated weathering and optimised 
biodegradability test were performed. In the second test, the results of the preliminary test 
were evaluated in order to optimise the conditions for biodegradation. As an outcome, 
accelerated weathering was considered as a first stage degradation to observe possible 
enhancing influence on the second stage biodegradation. Therefore, the accelerated 
weathering of the test compound was done prior the biodegradability test to detect potential 
photodegradation. Furthermore, to reduce the experienced lag phase with the soil inoculum 
and observe the influence to the biodegradation process, soil supernatant was used in the 
optimised test instead of soil as is.  
 
==PAGE:26==
18 
 
3.3.1 First stage accelerated weathering 
 
 
The first stage accelerated weathering was done by following the ASTM guideline D6954-04 
for Exposing and Testing Plastics that Degrade in the Environment by a Combination of 
Oxidation and Biodegradation. Since the rubber particles are assumed to be trapped in soil, 
similar conditions were resembled in accelerated weathering.   
 
Three samples, each containing 0.5 g of the test compound, were exposed to 
fluorescent UVA-340 lamps with the conditions of 8 h UV irradiance time followed by 4 h 
dark period. The samples of the test compound were placed to three different locations, 
resulting in different exposure strength to the irradiance. The three irradiance strengths used 
were 9 W/m2, 6 W/m2 and 2 W/m2. Accelerated weathering proceeded for 42 days, with a 
constant temperature of 20±2°C and relative humidity of 36±5 %.   
 
3.3.2 Optimised second stage biodegradability test 
 
The second stage biodegradability test was done based on the DS/EN ISO 14851 standard for 
Determination of the Ultimate Aerobic Biodegradability of Plastic Materials in an Aqueous 
Medium. Corresponding to the preliminary biodegradability test, oxygen demand in a closed 
respirometer was used to measure biodegradability of the test compound. The test for 
biodegradability was completed using OxiTop® instrumentation. 
 
Second stage biodegradability test was modified and optimised based on the results of 
the preliminary biodegradability test. The amount of the compounds and the test conditions 
remained the same. The pristine test compound was used in two of the flasks and the 9 W/m2 
weathered test compound in the rest of the test flasks. The only change was made for the 
inoculum: instead of soil inoculum, soil supernatant was used. Moreover, two different soil 
inoculum concentrations were used, 10 g/100 ml and 25 g/100 ml, resulting as TSS values of 
0.8 g/l for the low concentration and 2.13 g/l for the high concentration. The soil was 
suspended in the amount of mineral medium, allowed to settle for 30 minutes and filtered 
through a coarse, porous filter. To obtain the final concentration of 5 % in the test medium, 
12.5 ml of the soil supernatant was added to the inhibition flasks and 18.25 ml to the rest of 
the flasks. Furthermore, in addition to the sludge concentration used in the preliminary test, a 
higher concentration of activated sludge was used to compare possible intensifying effects to 
the biodegradability of the test compound. The higher concentration was 2.5 times greater 
than the original concentration, and so 1.5 ml of the inoculum was added to the chosen flasks. 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
Therefore, the TSS was 9.1 g/l for the low concentration and 13.7 g/l for the high 
concentration. 
 
3.4 Scanning electron microscope analysis 
 
To observe the influence of weathering and biodegradation, a visual test with an electron 
microscope was completed to detect possible changes in the polymer surface structure. The 
scanning electron microscope (SEM) analysis was performed by using Inspect S50 
microscope in DTU Center for Electron Nanoscopy.  
 
The SEM analysis was done to the pristine rubber particles, the pristine rubber 
particles after the preliminary biodegradability test with soil supernatant and activated sludge 
inoculum, the weathered rubber particles and the weathered rubber particles after the 
optimised biodegradability test with higher soil supernatant and activated sludge 
concentration. After performing the biodegradability tests, the samples were filtered, rinsed 
with ethanol and dried in Petri dishes. The chosen samples were transferred into aluminium 
tape and placed into the sample holder to avoid contamination before inserting them to the 
electron microscope. To perform the analysis and see the possible change in particle surface, 
10-30 μm scale was used. Everhart-Thornley detector (ETD) of the microscope was used to 
obtain the pictures.

4.1 Preliminary biodegradability test 
 
In the preliminary biodegradability test the biodegradability of the pristine rubber particles 
was evaluated. Moreover, the biodegradability of the positive control, PLGA, and the

biodegradability was 60 % of ThOD production for respirometric methods.

5 DISCUSSION 
 
The following chapter is considering and discussing the different aspects of the tests, 
including the degradability of the car tire rubber, choice of positive control (PLGA) and 
inoculum, test method evaluation and the broader perspective. 
 
5.1 Degradability of car tire rubber 
 
The purpose of the study was to evaluate degradability of rubber particles originating from car 
tires in order to assess the persistency and the fate of the particles in the environment and 
during wastewater treatment. The test compound used in the experiment was rubber granulate 
extracted from industrial tires, indicating that the test compound was a composition of several 
elements. These included natural rubber, additives, fillers and the synthetic polymer SBR, 
which can be classified as plastic.  
 
In the tests the pristine test compound did not fulfil the biodegradability criteria 
according to the standard tests (pass level of 60 % of ThOD production) yet some 
degradability was seen with both inocula. A higher level of biodegradability was seen with 
activated sludge [4.1 % (3.8-4.5)] and as well with soil inoculum when using soil supernatant 
[1.5 % (0.8-2.3)] instead of soil (0 %). Furthermore, the weathered car tire rubber did not pass 
the criteria for biodegradability. However, weathering of the rubber particles was found to 
improve biodegradability of the car tire rubber with activated sludge inoculum [7.2 % (6.8-
7.6)] but not with the soil supernatant [1.1 % (0.8-1.5)]. Additionally, weathered rubber 
particles showed greater biodegradability with 2.5-fold higher biomass concentration of both 
used inocula, activated sludge (8.4 %) and soil supernatant (4.5 %). Inhibitory effects were 
not seen for the rubber particles during the biodegradability tests, suggesting that the test 
compound is not toxic.  
 
The influence of weathering was found to improve the biodegradability of rubber 
particles with activated sludge inoculum. In a previous study, Cadle & Williams (1980) 
suggested that atmospheric oxidation is an important mode of degradation of tread-wear 
particulate and it is indicated to be more influential than microbial attack. In the weathering 
process the molecular weight of the polymer is decreased and new oxygen-rich groups are 
created (Andrary, 2011). Therefore, the polymer is more available to further degradation, 
which was seen in the optimised biodegradability test, especially with the activated sludge 
inoculum.  
==PAGE:40==
32 
 
 
In the evaluation of the degradability of car tire rubber, the composition of the test 
compound is notable. The rubber granulate used in the study consists of substantial amount of 
natural rubber (38-42 %), and several additives and fillers. Due to the mixture of different 
components in the test compound, the degrading fragment of the particle cannot be interpreted 
to be merely the synthetic polymer SBR. However, since degradation is making the polymer 
more brittle and changing the molecular structure, higher degradability level could be 
experienced in longer testing time. In the preliminary biodegradability test, the BOD curve of 
the pristine rubber compound with the activated sludge inoculum reached a plateau, indicating 
the incapability of the microorganisms to degrade the resulting transformation products and 
completely access the rubber particles. More information about the degrading fragment, 
changes in the molecular level and the degradation end-products of the compound are needed 
to further analyse the degradability of the rubber particles. 
 
According to Gewert et al. (2015), plastics are found to degrade first from the surface 
of the polymer, seen as crazing and change of color. To detect the possible change, scanning 
electron microscope analysis was done to the particles before and after the degradability tests. 
The SEM analysis showed change in the polymer surface, especially when comparing the 
pristine particle to the particles after the optimised biodegradability test. The surface was 
found to be more rough and porous, and the particle edges more round after weathering and 
second stage biodegradation with the higher biomass concentration, indicating that 
degradation at some level was occurring. It should be understood however, that the level of 
change is dependent on the transformation of an individual particle and does not imply the 
same degradation level of all of the rubber particles. To assess the biodegradability of the car 
tire rubber in environmental conditions, more detailed evaluation of the surface and structural 
changes is required. 
 
5.2 The choice of positive control 
 
PLGA was used as a positive control in the tests in order to compare the degradability of 
biodegradable plastic to microplastics. In the tests PLGA did not pass the standard criteria for 
biodegradability (pass level of 60 % of ThOD production). However, the tests showed the 
positive control to degrade more than the test compound, especially with activated sludge 
inoculum [14.7 % (12.1-17.4)]. Furthermore, in the preliminary biodegradability test the 
curve of BOD was showing continuing increase with both inocula, giving indication of the 
degradability of the transformation products of biodegradation. In the preliminary test with 
==PAGE:41==
33 
 
soil PLGA was degrading more (5.3 %) than the pristine rubber (0 %), in the optimised test 
the difference was not as substantial [PLGA 3.8 % (3.1-4.5), pristine rubber 1.5 % (0.8-2.3)]. 
Moreover, PLGA showed enhancement in the presence of sodium acetate in the preliminary 
test with soil and feasibly with activated sludge, indicating that the reference substance 
improved the biodegradability of PLGA and the conditions for the biomass.   
 
During the experiment it was found that PLGA had a tendency to stick to the walls of 
the flask, causing the PLGA partially not being available for the microorganisms due to 
incomplete mixing. Sticking could explain the high differences between the replicates in the 
biodegradability of PLGA (38.6 % difference with soil supernatant, 26.7 % difference with 
activated sludge) and the variation in the BOD values. Because of the sticking, the suitability 
of PLGA as a positive control is questioned. According to the supplier of PLGA (Sigma-
Aldrich), the degradation timeframe of PLGA is less than 3 months. If the biodegradability 
tests had been continued for a longer time period or PLGA had been included in the first stage 
accelerated weathering, further degradability could have been seen. However, because of the 
limited degradability level of PLGA in 28 days, its representation as a biodegradable plastic 
and therefore a positive control in the used standard tests is not sufficient.  
 
PLGA is a copolymer of polylactic acid (PLA) and polyglycolic acid (PGA) (Makadia 
& Siegel, 2011). The suitability of formulations of biopolymer polylactic acid (PLA) as a 
positive control is further questioned by McDevitt et al. (2017). This is mainly due to the 
insufficient marine degradation. The suitable positive controls for testing biodegradability 
suggested by McDevitt et al. (2017) include natural materials, e.g. cellulose and poly-3-
hydroxybutyrate (PHB) that are already included as positive controls in some biodegradability 
standards (e.g. ISO 17556). These components are recommended to be used as positive 
controls in further studies to compare the level of biodegradability to microplastics. 
 
5.3 The choice of inoculum 
 
Soil and activated sludge were chosen as inocula in order to compare the influence of 
different bacterial communities to the biodegradability of car tire rubber under various 
environmental conditions. It was found that activated sludge inoculum was more efficient in 
degrading the pristine rubber [4.1 % (3.8-4.5)] and the weathered rubber [7.2 % (6.8-7.6)] 
compared to the both used soil inocula, soil particles (pristine rubber 0 %) and soil 
supernatant [pristine rubber 1.5 % (0.8-2.3), weathered rubber 1.1 % (0.8-1.5)]. Furthermore, 
it was found that a higher biomass concentration resulted in a higher biodegradability of the 
==PAGE:42==
34 
 
weathered, abiotically oxidised rubber with both of the used inocula (activated sludge 8.4 %, 
soil supernatant 4.5 %). 
 
The soil inoculum used in the tests was collected next to a highway. Since a 
substantial part of the rubber particles is assumed to be trapped and absorbed by soil, the 
chosen soil inoculum was suspected to be acclimatised to the particles originating from car 
tires. It was found that the level of degradation with the soil was low, especially when using 
soil as is in the preliminary test (pristine rubber 0 %). The higher biodegradability of the car 
tire rubber with the soil supernatant indicates that the bacteria need to be dissolved in order to 
perform biodegradation. However, there were greater differences between the replicates with 
soil inoculum than activated sludge inoculum (pristine rubber 7.1 %, weathered rubber 9.2 
%), particularly when using soil supernatant (pristine rubber 95.7 %, weathered rubber 58.8 
%) instead of soil as is (0 %). Therefore, it could be said that soil supernatant is more 
heterogeneous and sensitive as an inoculum compared to the activated sludge. The high 
differences between the replicates may further be a result of the test equipment sensitivity and 
the uncertainty when testing compounds with low degradability.  
 
The reason for different level and efficiency of degradation could be because of the 
difference of the bacterial population in soil and activated sludge. Because of different 
conditions and type of inoculum, there are assumed to be differences in the microorganism 
communities. The amount of active bacteria in soil and activated sludge is known to be 
different: depending on soil, the amount of active bacteria per gram of dry soil is between 
4×106 and 2×109 (Vieira & Nahas, 2005), whereas in activated sludge the amount is 1-
10×1012 bacterial cells per gram of dry mass (Guo & Zhang, 2012). To obtain more 
information about the specific degrading bacteria and the influence of different bacterial 
inoculum to biodegradability of microplastics, samples before and after the biodegradability 
tests need to be examined from the biological perspective. By performing the biological 
analysis, information about the bacterial communities and their influence on the 
biodegradability of car tire rubber could be obtained and used in future research.  
 
5.4 The evaluation of biodegradability standards for microplastic testing 
 
There is no existing standard test for measuring biodegradability of microplastics. To evaluate 
the biodegradability of the rubber compound, two different standard tests, OECD 301 and 
ISO 14851, were used as guidelines. The preliminary biodegradability test was based on 
OECD standard, while the optimised biodegradability test was based on the ISO standard.  
==PAGE:43==
35 
 
 
The main difference between the test methods and the standard tests applied in this 
study was the preparation of soil inoculum. Soil as is was used in the preliminary 
biodegradability test and it was seen that the pristine rubber did not show biodegradability (0 
%). Furthermore, when comparing the curves of the inhibition samples between the inocula in 
the preliminary test, longer lag phase was experienced with the soil inoculum (2 days) 
compared to the activated sludge inoculum (1 day). Therefore, different standard and 
preparation methods of soil inoculum were used in the optimised biodegradability test in 
order to assess the possible differences in the level of biodegradability and the lag phase. It 
was found that the new method did reduce the lag phase of the inhibition samples with soil by 
1 day and moreover affected to the degradability of both test compounds, pristine and 
weathered rubber. However, with the rubber particles the lag phase was not reduced (7 to 15 
days with soil, 3 to 5 days with activated sludge), suggesting that the presence of the test 
compound is preventing the microorganisms from performing degradation. Another 
difference between the used standard tests was the suggested inoculum concentration. While 
the OECD standard suggested suspended solids to be less than 30 mg/l, the ISO standard 
instructed to use concentration between 30 mg/l and 1000 mg/l, enabling the test to be 
performed with higher concentration of biomass. In the tests performed in the study, similar 
inoculum concentrations (~30 mg TSS/l) were used in order to maintain comparable results. 
The used concentrations were at the interface between the two standard tests.  
 
Currently, the existing biodegradability test methods are created for chemicals 
(OECD) and plastic materials (ISO), and are therefore not fully aimed for testing the 
degradability of microplastics or rubber materials. Because of the lack of specific standard 
tests, the OECD and ISO standards were used as directional methods to evaluate their 
suitability for testing microplastic degradation and to optimise the biodegradability test 
method for microplastics. From the perspective of the degradation ability of soil, the ISO 
method and the use of soil supernatant was more efficient than the OECD method where soil 
as is was used. Moreover, when considering and evaluating the influence of higher inoculum 
concentration to biodegradation, ISO standard is more suitable due to the broader range of 
suspended solids. However, with higher concentrations of biomass, higher level of 
endogenous BOD is experienced, which may be problematic. With the ISO method and the 
use of soil supernatant higher differences between the replicates were experienced (pristine 
rubber 95.7 %, weathered rubber 58.8 %). Despite the working test methods and validity of 
tests, challenges were experienced when using the standards for testing biodegradability of 
==PAGE:44==
36 
 
rubber particles. Therefore, it cannot be concluded that the existing standard tests are 
completely suitable for testing biodegradability of microplastics. 
 
The biodegradability level of the rubber particles was found to be low, which caused 
uncertainties with the values and the replicates. The suitability of the method for the low level 
degradation and measuring biodegradability of microplastics is therefore discussed. 
Furthermore, when considering the life-time of plastics in the environment, the duration of the 
standardised biodegradability test could be prolonged from 28 days. McDevitt et al. (2017) 
suggested a new, Ecocyclable, standard method for measuring biodegradability of 
microplastics, where the recommended test duration would be from 180 days to 18 months to 
assess comprehensive degradability. With the new method, the biodegradability and the 
environmental persistency of the rubber particles could be evaluated more thoroughly.  
 
Lastly, the level of biodegradability considered as pass level (60 % of ThOD 
production) and its suitability for testing microplastic degradability in the existing standards 
could be discussed. Biodegradation of microplastics in the environment is desired however, 
degradation can be harmful, especially if toxic products unable to undergo further degradation 
are left to the environment. According to McDevitt et al. (2017), a biodegradability standard 
should require that the degradation end-products are non-toxic and non-persistent. Moreover, 
it is suggested that the biodegradability level is extended to 90 % and tested with several 
inocula, including (a) aerobic soils, (b) anaerobic methanogenic environments and (c) aquatic 
environments to ensure the environmental safety (McDevitt et al., 2017). Based on these 
suggestions, it is recommended to use the new standard instead of the existing methods when 
testing biodegradability of microplastics. 
 
5.5 Environmental fate of rubber particles 
 
Another objective in the present study was to analyse biodegradability of the car tire rubber 
because of the unknown fate of the particles in the environment. According to The Danish 
Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) (2017), rubber particles originating from car tire 
abrasion have not been directly found in the wastewater sludge or soil, even though based on 
the calculations they are identified to be the biggest source of microplastic pollution in 
Denmark (56 % of the total microplastic emissions). EPA (2017) and Lassen et al. (2015) 
suggest that particles are smaller than the lower size limit used in the detection study (< 20 
μm), which could be a possible reason for not discovering them. Lassen et al. (2015) has 
further estimated that the size of the rubber particles originating from road wear is in the 
==PAGE:45==
37 
 
range 2.5-10 μm. However, as a result of the present study, biodegradability and weathering 
of the rubber particles need to be taken into consideration. Since some of the particles are 
assumed to be trapped in soil, microorganisms could degrade the particles parallel to 
weathering. Furthermore, particles transported into the sewer systems could be degraded more 
efficiently by the microorganisms in activated sludge as discovered in the tests, possibly 
explaining the absence of them in wastewater. The rubber particles used in the study were 
relatively large (90-125 μm) compared to the estimated particle size in the environment and 
activated sludge. Smaller size of the particle results in a higher surface to volume ratio, which 
has been found to expedite degradation (Gewert et al., 2015). Faster degradation of the 
smaller particles in the environment could potentially explain the absence of the car tire 
rubber in the environment. To assess the actual environmental fate of the rubber particles, a 
smaller size limit (< 20 μm) is recommended to be used in further microplastic detection 
studies.  
Furthermore, according to Councell et al. (2004), car tire rubber has been indicated to 
be a significant source of zinc in the environment, resulting in potential health and 
environmental problems.  Zinc is found from car tires as zinc oxide and contributes to 1-2 % 
of the compound of the tire (WRAP, 2006). At high concentrations (> 120 μg/l), zinc has 
been found to cause toxic responses in aquatic organisms (Councell et al., 2004). Therefore, 
concerns regarding zinc releases from care tire rubber have been raised. However, zinc could 
potentially be used to detect the car tire rubber fate in the environment in future studies, by 
using zinc as tracer.   
 
In addition to the possible degradability of the rubber particles in the environment, the 
experienced low degradability of the test compound with the soil inoculum needs to be taken 
into consideration. The low level of degradation with the microorganisms of soil could be 
problematic, especially since the used soil was chosen based on the assumed fate of the 
particles. The microplastic particles are known to have an ability to be transported into 
different environments due to urban runoff and wind (Lassen et al., 2015). This could result in 
microplastic contamination in different types of soil e.g. in forests, that are not necessarily 
acclimatised to the rubber particles from car tires. Furthermore, the rubber particles are likely 
to be transported to aquatic environments, resulting in the contamination of marine and 
freshwater environments. The degradability of the car tire rubber in different environmental 
conditions should be taken into consideration and investigated to minimise the environmental 
harm and microplastic pollution.  
==PAGE:46==
38 
 
 
Additionally, the persistence of the rubber particles in the environment needs to be 
acknowledged. The biodegradability tests showed only minor degradability within 28 days 
and gave merely an indication of the degradability of the test compound. Furthermore, in the 
performed first stage accelerated weathering, the optimum situation was considered: the 
rubber particles were continuously exposed to UV irradiance, maximising the influence of 
accelerated weathering. Additionally, in biodegradability tests, the rubber was used as the 
only carbon and energy source to the microorganisms. In the environment, the circumstances 
are different and the rate of degradation is highly dependent on the environmental conditions 
(Lassen et al., 2015). Studies show that in the laboratory experiments higher polymer 
degradation rate is usually observed compared to the natural conditions (Müller, 2005), 
suggesting that the rate of degradation is slower in the environment. Polymers have been 
found to be resistant to degradation and plastics are estimated to stay in the environment from 
hundreds to thousands of years (Müller, 2005). Moreover, additives have been further found 
to extend the degradation period of the polymers (Lassen et al., 2015). However, the presence 
of other carbon sources and nutrients, and their possible accelerating influence on the 
microbial activity needs to be recognised. Since the rubber particles are in the environment 
for a longer time and the microorganisms have more nutrients than in the completed tests, 
degradation in the natural conditions could be enhanced. Inhibitory effects were not seen for 
the rubber, indicating that the compound is not toxic. To assess and analyse the actual fate and 
persistency of the rubber particles in the environment, more comprehensive research with 
different relevant inocula is recommended.  
 
5.6 Broader perspective 
 
Microplastics from car tire rubber have been estimated to be the main source of microplastic 
pollution in several countries, including Denmark (56 %) and Norway (53.6 %) (Mepex, 
2014; Lassen et al., 2015). According to studies, the most effective solution to reduce 
microplastic emissions is to eliminate the source of contamination. However, when 
considering legal aspects and government initiatives, pollution originating from car tire 
rubber is impossible to ban and difficult to control and restrict when comparing to other 
sources, such as microbeads in cosmetics. This is mainly a result of cosmetic microbeads 
being categorised as intentionally produced, primary microplastics and car tire rubber being 
categorised as secondary microplastics, originating from abrasion and therefore being more 
challenging to control. Since cars are the main form of transport in the present world, the 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
amount of particles originating from car tire rubber in the environment is not expected to 
decrease. Among cars, the contribution of other emission sources using tires and rubber 
materials are needed to be taken into consideration. Since the particles are known to spread in 
the environment due to runoff and wind, the microplastic contamination from car tires is 
uncontrollable. 
 
The issue of eco-friendliness of cars includes the sustainability assessment of car tires. 
Renewable production sources of tires have been discovered, however, that does not reduce 
the microplastic emissions originating from tires. To solve the problem, changes in the tire 
properties and composition need to be made. However, eco-friendly materials and 
biodegradability of the car tire rubber reduces the life-time of the tire. On the other hand, 
increased life-time could potentially reduce microplastic emissions but result as less 
degradable material. To determine the most sustainable solution from the perspective of the 
environment, more comprehensive information of the biodegradability of the car tire rubber is 
needed.

6 CONCLUSION 
 
The overarching aim of the study was to investigate the biodegradability of microplastics 
originating from car tire rubber by using two different inocula, soil and activated sludge. 
Furthermore, the goal was to evaluate the suitability of the existing standard methods to 
testing biodegradability of microplastics. As an outcome of the study, the following can be 
concluded: 
 As expected, pristine rubber did not fulfil the standard criteria for biodegradability 
(pass level of 60 % of ThOD production). However, some biodegradability was seen 
with activated sludge [4.1 % (3.8-4.5)] and soil supernatant [1.5 % (0.8-2.3)] in 28 
days. With soil inoculum biodegradability of the pristine rubber was not seen. 
 Weathered rubber did not fulfil the biodegradability criteria of the standards. In the 
optimised biodegradability test, weathered rubber showed more biodegradability with 
activated sludge [7.2 % (6.8-7.6)] than soil supernatant [1.1 % (0.8-1.5)] in 28 days. 
Accelerated weathering of the rubber particles was found to improve biodegradability 
with activated sludge inoculum. Moreover, 2.5-fold higher biomass concentration 
improved biodegradability of the weathered rubber with both used inocula (activated 
sludge 8.4 %, soil supernatant 4.5 %). 
 Changes in particle surface were detected with SEM after the biodegradability tests. 
The influence of degradation was clearly seen as changes in surface structure when 
comparing pristine rubber to the weathered rubber after the optimised biodegradability 
test: the surface was porous and gaps were detected. 
 Several challenges arose when using the existing standard methods for testing 
biodegradability of microplastics. When using soil as an inoculum in the preliminary 
test, 1 day longer lag phase was experienced in comparison to the activated sludge. In 
the optimised test the lag phase of soil was reduced by 1 day due to the use of soil 
supernatant. However, greater replicate differences were experienced when using soil 
supernatant as an inoculum (pristine rubber 95.7 %, weathered rubber 58.8 %).   
 The positive control, PLGA, did not fulfil the standard criteria for biodegradability, 
reaching 14.7 % (12.1-17.4) biodegradability with activated sludge, 5.3 % with soil 
and 3.8 % (3.1-4.5) with soil supernatant in 28 days. Because of the limited 
biodegradability, great differences between the replicates (activated sludge 26.7 %, 
soil supernatant 38.6 %) and the tendency to stick to the walls of flasks, the use of 
==PAGE:49==
41 
 
PLGA as a representative biodegradable plastic and positive control is not 
recommended in short-term, standard tests. 
 
  
Based on the outcome of the study, degradability of the car tire rubber in the environment and 
during wastewater treatment needs to be considered when analysing the fate of microplastics. 
To obtain further information about the degradability of the rubber particles, more 
comprehensive research with different types and concentrations of the inoculum and longer 
test times (180 days to 18 months) are recommended.

7 FUTURE RECOMMENDATIONS 
  
To assess the biodegradability of the car tire rubber and obtain information about the 
degrading fragment, it is recommended to develop a degradability test with the different 
compounds of the rubber, including the synthetic polymer SBR, natural rubber, additives and 
fillers. In order to assess the biodegradability with different inocula and the fate of the rubber 
particles in different environments, several types and concentrations of soil and activated 
sludge should be used in further studies. Furthermore, the presence and influence of other 
substrates and carbon sources should be taken into account when investigating microplastic 
degradability in the environment. In future studies, water sediment and surface water bacteria 
are recommended to be used as an inoculum to evaluate the degradability in aquatic 
environments and in order to obtain information about the particle fate. To investigate the fate 
of rubber particles in the environment more in depth, smaller size limit (< 20 μm) is 
recommended to be used in further microplastic detection studies. Moreover, since car tire 
rubber has been indicated to be a significant source of zinc in the environment, it is suggested 
to be used as tracer when investigating the fate of car tire rubber. 
 
To observe the car tire rubber degradability in a longer timeframe, it is advised to 
continue the test for a longer time period (180 days to 18 months) as suggested by the 
Ecocyclable standard (McDevitt et al., 2017). Furthermore, to assess the comprehensive 
biodegradability of the compound of interest, a test with pre-adapted bacteria could be 
considered in addition to the test methods used in this study. To evaluate the influence and 
level of weathering, different UV irradiance strengths (e.g. 3-12 W/m2) and longer exposure 
times (3 to 6 months) are suggested to be used. Moreover, different types of biodegradable 
plastics or natural components (e.g. cellulose or PHB) are recommended to use as a positive 
control instead of PLGA. 
 
Lastly, to measure the specific level of degradability, molecular weight or grain size is 
recommended to be measured before and after the test. Electron scanning microscope analysis 
could be used for the particles on a larger scale, to observe the degradability with different 
types and concentrations of biomass and with different times and levels of weathering to 
detect the change in polymer surface structure.
