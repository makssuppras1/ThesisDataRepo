Abstract
Virus-like particles (VLPs) have emerged as a promising platform for vaccine development
due to their strong immunogenicity and favorable safety profile. The production of Chikun-
gunya virus (CHIKV) VLPs in Human Embryonic Kidney 293 (HEK293) cells via transi-
ent gene expression (TGE) presents multiple challenges, including optimizing transfection
conditions and media composition to maximize yield and process consistency. Previous op-
timization efforts have primarily relied on a one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) approach, which
is both time-consuming and limited in its ability to reveal interactions between variables.
This study applied a Design of Experiments (DoE) methodology to systematically invest-
igate key factors that influence the production of CHIKV VLP. Variables such as culture
medium, PEI/DNA ratio and concentration, cell density at transfection, post-transfection
incubation temperature, and the supplementation of GlutaMAX and valproic acid (VPA)
were evaluated for their impact on cell viability, transfection efficiency, and VLP yield. By
using a structured DoE approach, the study aimed to identify optimal conditions while re-
ducing the number of experimental runs needed compared to traditional OFAT methods.
Among the tested media, a cheaper alternative was identified, which under certain conditions
performed comparably or even better than the current media. Switching to this cost-effective
medium could provide significant cost reductions. Despite systematic optimization efforts
for different parameters, the results exhibited high variability across experiments, with sub-
stantial uncertainty impacting the reliability of the identified trends. While factors such as
PEI and DNA concentration and ratio, as well as VPA supplementation, showed potential
for improving transfection efficiency and VLP production, further validation is necessary to
confirm these findings. Similarly, the alternative media formulations demonstrated potential
for supporting HEK293 cell growth and VLP production compared to the current protocol,
but further investigation is needed to assess their effectiveness and consistency. Future stud-
ies should focus on enhancing experimental reproducibility, further optimizing experimantal
factors such as PEI/DNA complex formation, and identifying additional factors enhancing
the stability and efficiency of CHIKV VLP production in HEK293 cells.

Introduction
1.1
Advancements in Vaccine Development
Vaccination has been a crucial factor reducing the global burden of infectious diseases and
is regarded as one of the most impactful achievements.
Traditional vaccines, including
live-attenuated and inactivated vaccines, have long been a central strategy in the field of
immunization. Live-attenuated vaccines contain weakened versions of pathogens capable of
inducing immunity in healthy individuals without causing disease. In contrast, inactivated
vaccines contain pathogens that have been killed or rendered non-replicative yet still eliciting
an immune response [1, 2].
Despite their widespread popularity, traditional vaccines are not without limitations. Al-
though live-attenuated vaccines are highly effective, there is a small risk of reverting to a
harmful form, particularly in individuals with compromised immunity. Furthermore, these
vaccines often require careful storage under specified conditions, which can present logistical
challenges in regions with limited resources [3]. Inactivated vaccinations, while safer in this
regard, can require numerous doses and the addition of adjuvants to elicit a strong immune
response. Additionally, the production procedure is labor-intensive, dangerous, and often
requires large amounts of pathogen material to be cultivated [1, 2, 4].
In recent years, a new generation of vaccines has emerged as promising alternatives to tradi-
tional methods. These include innovative platforms such as subunit vaccines, DNA vaccines,
mRNA vaccines, and virus-like particles (VLPs). Figure 1 gives an overview of the different
types of vaccines. VLPs typically comprise one or more viral structural proteins that as-
semble into a particle resembling a virus. Unlike traditional vaccines, VLPs cannot replicate
within host cells due to their lack of genetic content [5]. This eliminates the possibility of
it reverting, giving them a high safety profile. By displaying viral antigens in a native-like
conformation, VLPs induce immunological responses similar to those caused by real infec-
tions. VLPs effectively trigger innate and adaptive immune responses, which both are key
to generating long-lasting immunity[5, 6, 7].

1.2
Chikungunya Virus
Even though new vaccine techniques have evolved in the last decade, there are still disease-
causing viruses where no vaccine exists yet or the one available gives limited protection. One
of those viruses is the Chikungunya virus (CHIKV) which is an alphavirus transmitted by
mosquitoes belonging to the Togaviridae family. It was first identified in Tanzania in 1952.
The virus is primarily transmitted between hosts by two mosquito species, Aedes aegypti
and Aedes albopictus. Chikungunya fever, which is characterized by a quick onset of fever,
joint and muscle pain, headaches, exhaustion, nausea, and rashes, usually develops after an
infection with CHIKV. Severe joint pain or arthritis is one of the most common symptoms
and can last for months or even years after the original infection [8, 9]. Figure 2 depicts the
CHIKV genome, showcasing its structural and non-structural protein-encoding regions.

A large outbreak on Reunion Island in 2005 that afflicted thousands of people brought
CHIKV international attention. Since then, the virus has spread to more than 21 nations
in Africa, Asia, Europe, and America. resulting in about 450.000 cases of infection globally.
A notable mutation in the CHIKV genome allowed the virus to adapt to Aedes albopictus, a
mosquito species capable of surviving in temperate climates, thereby facilitating its distri-
bution to regions beyond the tropics. This mutation has raised concerns that the virus may
have the potential to trigger epidemics on a global scale [8, 10].
CHIKV infections can lead to chronic joint pain affecting quality of life. With no specific
antiviral treatments available, management focuses on symptomatic relief through NSAIDs,
antirheumatic drugs (hydroxychloroquine, methotrexate), and analgesics [8, 9]. Severe cases
involving meningoencephalitis are treated with mannitol to prevent cerebral oedema, while
rehabilitation addresses mobility issues [9].
Recently, a live-attenuated vaccine, IXCHIQW® was approved for the prevention of Chikun-
gunya virus infection. The virulent reversion is reduced due to the nsP3 deletion in the
IXCHIQW® vaccine; however, no studies have fully clarified the mechanism of attenuation,
and the possibility of reversion to virulence has not been completely ruled out [11]. Further-
more, due to its live nature, IXCHIQW® is unsuitable for individuals with compromised
immune systems or a history of severe allergic reactions [11]. Given the limitations of live-
attenuated vaccines, there is an increasing demand for the development of next-generation
vaccines that offer broader protection across diverse populations while also being safer and
more efficient to produce on a large scale. VLP vaccines have emerged as a promising solution
to meet these needs [9, 11].
1.2.1
CHIKV VLP

by Akahata and colleagues in 2009 [8], culminating in the vaccine candidate CHIKV VLP
(PXVX0317). Figure 3 illustrates the characterization of the CHIKV VLP. This candidate
has successfully completed two Phase 3 clinical trials, meeting all endpoints, and is now
positioned for regulatory submissions, with a potential market launch anticipated in 2025
[11, 12, 13]. The VLPs contain the essential structural proteins of the virus, including the

1.3
Production, purification and formulation of Bavarian Nordics
VLP vaccine
Three main steps are involved in the production of the CHIKV VLP vaccine: the expression,
purification, and formulation of the vaccine [15]. The process followed at Bavarian Nordic
(BN) is outlined below, and figure 4 provides a schematic overview of these steps.
• Production (Upstream Processing): The production process for CHIKV VLPs
begins with upstream processing. The process starts with one vial of VRC293 (Vac-
cine Research Center 293) cells taken from a working cell bank (WCB). These cells are
thawed and cultured in the current growth media within shaker flasks maintained at
37°C with 5% CO2.
The cells are initially expanded in shaker flasks and subsequently transferred to biore-
actors, where the main production takes place. On day 3 post-seeding the bioreactor,
the cells are fed with the transfection media to achieve the desired media ratio. Trans-
fection occurs on day 4, using the CHIKV expression plasmid mixed with polyethyl-
eneimine (PEI). The addition of PEI facilitates the introduction of the plasmid into the
cells, promoting the expression of the CHIKV structural proteins necessary for VLP
assembly. Around 20 hours post-transfection, protein expression is further induced by
adding valproic acid (VPA) and GlutaMAX to ensure maximum VLP production. The
VLP is secreted into the media and thereafter harvested by removing the cells
• Purification (Downstream Processing): Following upstream processing, the down-
stream purification of CHIKV VLPs aims at removing host cell protein and DNA, while
keeping the VLP intact and stable. The downstream process include buffer exchange
and column chromatography steps before a pure VLP protein is ready for being for-
mulated and filled in vials for injections

The bulk drug product is then subjected to a final sterile filtration step to ensure

1.4
Motivation and Objectives for this Master Thesis
The production of VLPs for vaccine development presents significant challenges, particularly
when using HEK293 cells as protein factories. Each cell line and product require specific
optimization, as methods developed for one system may not directly translate to another.
While previous optimization efforts for CHIKV VLP production in VRC-293 cells have fo-
cused on adjusting individual factors such as PEI concentration, DNA amount, and cell
density, these studies have typically uses a one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) approach.
This
method is both time-consuming and limited in its ability to reveal interactions between vari-
ables.
In this thesis, I propose a more efficient strategy using Design of Experiments (DoE) meth-
odologies to identify and optimize key parameters for CHIKV VLP production in HEK293
cells. By simultaneously testing multiple factors, DoE enables a comprehensive evaluation
of interactions between variables, ultimately enhancing expression efficiency and reducing
production costs.
Key parameters to be investigated include pre-transfection cell concentration, DNA/PEI
ratio and concentration, incubation temperature, GlutaMAX concentration, VPA concen-
tration, and the choice of growth media.
While the findings may be specific to VRC-293 cells and CHIKV VLPs, the insights gained
could also inform broader strategies in VLP production at Bavarian Nordic, particularly in
optimizing transfection efficiency and growth conditions.

Background
2.1
HEK293 Cell Line
The HEK293 cell line, which was derived from human embryonic kidney cells that were trans-
formed with sheared fragments of human adenovirus type 5 (Ad5) DNA over 40 years ago,
has become a widely used expression tool in both research and biopharmaceutical industries
[16]. The original transformation, performed by Graham and colleagues in 1977, incorpor-
ated Ad5 DNA into chromosome 19 of the host genome [17]. This cell line’s adaptability to
suspension culture, serum-free media, and compatibility with various transfection reagents
and vectors has made it a versatile platform for the transient expression of proteins, espe-
cially when human-like post-translational modifications (PTMs) are required [18]. PTMs
are chemical alterations that proteins undergo subsequent to their synthesis from mRNA.
These modifications are essential for regulating the protein’s structure, activity, stability,
and interactions with other molecules. In the fields of vaccine development and therapeutic
applications, PTMs, particularly glycosylation, are important for ensuring that proteins are
correctly folded, stable, and able to stimulate the desired immune response [19, 20].
HEK293 cells are frequently used to produce therapeutic proteins and viral vectors. Using
plasmid vectors under strong promoters, such as the CMV promoter, has been demonstrated
to be an effective strategy for the expression of desired proteins [16].
However, despite
their versatility, HEK293 cells face competition from Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells,
which outperform them in growth potential, cultivation duration, and total protein output.
Nevertheless, because of their human origin and ability to handle complex protein expression
systems, HEK293 cells continue to be an essential tool in the field of biotechnology [21].
2.2
Transient Gene Expression
Transient transfection is a technique that facilitates rapid and temporary expression of genes,
with the introduced DNA remaining episomal and not integrated into the host genome. This
approach is well-suited for short-term studies such as the evaluation of gene function, the
rapid production of recombinant proteins, or the assessment of vector efficiency [22]. The
expression typically lasts for a few days, after which the DNA is either degraded or diluted
during cell division. The simplicity and speed of transient transfection make it a suitable
method for experiments that require rapid results, although the lack of long-term expression
limits its applications [23, 24].
Conversely, stable transfection is a process that facilitates the sustained expression of genes
by integrating foreign DNA into the host genome [25]. Integration of foreign DNA into the
host genome is a technically demanding process, which can occur through random insertion
or site-specific integration.
The latter can be achieved using gene editing tools such as
the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats/CRISPR-associated protein 9

genomic location, recognised by the protospacer adjacent motif (PAM), resulting in double-

via homologous recombination [26]. Following transfection, only a small fraction of cells

2.2.1
PEI-Mediated Transfection
PEI is a cationic polymer that is frequently used for the mediation of TGE in mam-
malian cells, particularly HEK293 cells. PEI forms complexes (polyplex) with plasmid DNA
(pDNA), facilitating the delivery of DNA into cells. The simplicity, cost-effectiveness and
relatively high transfection efficiency of PEI have made it a preferred method for large-scale
transfection applications [23].
Nevertheless, PEI-mediated transfection displays considerable variability in terms of trans-
fection efficiency and recombinant protein production. The variability observed is influenced
by several factors, including the molecular weight and structure of PEI, polyplex formation
time, and the type of PEI used, whether branched or linear [28, 29].
The ratio of pDNA to PEI is another critical factor in achieving effective transfection. This
ratio is dependent on several factors, including the specific cell line, the recombinant DNA
used, and other experimental conditions. Consequently, the findings of previous studies may
not be directly applicable to new experiments. This limitation is particularly evident in
cultures with different cell densities where the use of a standard pDNA and PEI quantity
may prove inadequate for optimal transfection efficiency. In such cases, the failure to adjust
these quantities can result in either an excess of the transfection agent which may lead to
cytotoxicity, or an insufficient quantity which may result in poor gene expression. Therefore,
it is essential to tailor the pDNA-to-PEI ratio to specific conditions in order to maximise
transfection success and overall protein yield [24, 29].
Moreover, the composition of nutrient supplements can influence the formation of pDNA-
PEI complexes and cellular uptake. Modifications to physicochemical characteristics, such
as pH, can affect the formation of complexes. Additionally, alterations to the composition
or fluidity of the cell membrane, resulting from changes in nutrient composition can disrupt
cellular uptake pathways. Consequently, optimizing the quantities of pDNA and PEI con-
cerning cell density while accounting for the impact of nutrient supplementation is essential
for achieving optimal transfection efficiency and overall productivity [30].
In short, while PEI-mediated transfection represents an efficient and cost-effective method
for TGE, the achievement of consistent transfection efficiency and protein production neces-
sitates careful optimization of multiple factors, including PEI type, DNA-to-PEI ratio, cell
density, and nutrient conditions.
2.2.2
Mechanism of PEI-Mediated DNA Transfection and Gene Expression
The process of PEI-mediated transfection is initiated when the negatively charged pDNA is
combined with the positively charged PEI, forming polyplexes that are capable of binding to
the negatively charged cell membranes. This is due to the reduction of the negative charges of

==PAGE:22==
Department of Biotechnology and Biomedicine
Technical University of Denmark

the DNA by the cationic amines which serves to protect the DNA from nuclease degradation.
The process of cellular uptake via multiple endocytic pathways is well-documented [31]. One
prominent mechanism involves the facilitation of uptake by heparan sulfate proteoglycans
present on the cell surface [32, 33].
Once bound, the polyplexes are engulfed into the cell through endocytosis. The endosomal
environment is acidic which would typically lead to the degradation of DNA. However, PEI
acts as a ”proton sponge,” buffering the acidic conditions within the endosome, allowing the
release of DNA into the cytoplasm following endosomal lysis [31, 33, 34]. Nevertheless, this
hypothesis has yet to be validated [31].

Delivering plasmids to the nucleus for gene expression is a major challenge in transfection
due to the barrier posed by the nuclear envelope [35]. During mitosis, the nuclear envel-
ope breaks down, allowing plasmids in the cytoplasm easier access to the nuclear space.
Once inside, plasmids may integrate into chromatin, potentially aiding in the reformation
of the nuclear envelope [36]. In non-dividing cells, plasmids must pass through nuclear pore

complexes (NPCs) to enter the nucleus. These are large, multiprotein channels that allow
molecules to pass based on size and specific signals. It is thought that plasmids enter the
nucleus either with the help of nuclear localization signals (NLSs) or by having an intrinsic
signal that enables recognition by the NPC [36]. Therefore, the success of transfection in
HEK293 cells depends not only on the DNA carrier but also on the stage of the cell cycle and
potentially by using DNA targeting sequences, with mitotic cells offering a more favorable
environment for DNA entry into the nucleus [37].
Once inside the nucleus, the pDNA is subjected to transcription, resulting in the generation of
mRNA. The newly synthesized mRNA is then transported from the nucleus to the cytoplasm,
which serves as a translation template, resulting in the encoded protein synthesis. Following
this, the proteins undergo PTMs, such as glycosylation and phosphorylation, critical for
proper folding, stability, and functionality. Subsequently, the proteins are directed through
the secretory pathway for eventual secretion outside the cell [35].
2.3
Adapting HEK293 Cells to Serum-Free Media
The cultivation of HEK293 cells is commonly performed in medium supplemented with fetal
bovine serum (FBS) which provides essential nutrients and growth factors for cell growth
and survival [38]. However, FBS introduces challenges, including batch-to-batch variability,
unpredictable interactions with test substances, and the risk of contamination with bovine
pathogens. To overcome these issues, serum-free media have become the preferred choice in
biopharmaceutical production, offering more consistent and reproducible outcomes [39].
Direct transition to serum-free media can negatively impact cell viability and proliferation
due to the absence of growth factors [38]. Therefore, a gradual reduction in serum concentra-
tion is necessary to allow cells to adapt while maintaining viability during the transition [40].
Serum-free media must meet the nutritional needs of HEK293 cells, including amino acids,
vitamins, glucose, lipids, and growth factors [38]. A critical component in HEK cell media
is glutamine. Standard L-glutamine is prone to degradation due to high process temper-
ature leading to the production of toxic by-products such as ammonia. To address this,
stabilized alternatives like GlutaMAX are used. GlutaMAX is a dipeptide form of L-alanyl-
L-glutamine that gradually releases glutamine, minimizing ammonia buildup and enhances
cell viability and productivity [41, 42].
2.3.1
Additives
In optimising recombinant protein production, additives play a crucial role in transcriptional
regulation, PTMs and cellular metabolism. While many additives, such as dimethyl sulfoxide
(DMSO), lithium acetate and caffeine, have been studied to improve transfection efficiency
Page 15 of 80
==PAGE:24==
Department of Biotechnology and Biomedicine
Technical University of Denmark
Master Thesis
26-02-2025
and protein yield [18, 43, 44], this work focuses on VPA as the primary additive as it has
already been shown that adding VPA approximately 20 h post-transfection greatly increases
VLP production (internal data, not published).
VPA is a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor that enhances the production of recombin-
ant proteins, such as VLPs, in mammalian cells. HDAC enzymes remove acetyl groups from
histones, thereby tightening the chromatin structure and suppressing transcription. VPA
prevents this deacetylation, keeping chromatin relaxed and transcriptionally active, thus in-
creasing gene expression efficiency [44]. Although there are limited sources supporting this,
VPA has been shown to induce cell cycle arrest in the G1 phase. This would reduce cell
division and reallocate cellular resources towards protein production [45, 46].
Careful optimization of the use of VPA is required because of its potential cytotoxicity at
high concentrations. Despite its limitations, VPA remains a valuable additive for improving
productivity in TGE. While VPA is the only additive used in this study, it is important
to acknowledge the wider range of additives explored in previous research. Exploring other
compounds with different mechanisms could potentially further improve protein yields [43,
44].
2.4
DoE and its advantages
DoE is a statistical methodology that is used to systematically and efficiently explore the
influence of multiple factors on a desired outcome. By simultaneously evaluating multiple
variables and their interactions, DoE minimises resource usage while maximising insights into
complex systems. This approach is of particular significance in domains such as pharma-
ceutical production and bioprocessing where it facilitates the identification and optimisation
of critical process parameters (CPPs) and critical material attributes (CMAs).
This, in
turn, ensures the quality of the final products, aligning with the Quality by Design (QbD)
framework [47, 48, 49].
DoE approach has been shown to offer significant advantages over the conventional OFAT
method.
The OFAT method frequently fails to consider significant interactions between
variables and can maintain preexisting biases [48]. In contrast, the DoE approach provides a
structured framework for systematically exploring complex processes. DoE makes it possible
to have a more comprehensive view of the process space by defining the scope of the issue
using scientific intuition and past knowledge [50].
It has been demonstrated that DoE
minimises the number of experiments required, supports robust optimisation, and integrates
quality concerns in the beginning of the development process, lowering the need for end-stage
testing. Consequently, DoE is considered a more efficient and insightful approach to process
optimisation [48, 50].

3.1.1
General Protocol for TGE and VLP Production
VRC-293 cells, initially stored in liquid nitrogen, were thawed and inoculated in the current
serum-free Expression Medium supplemented with GlutaMAX-I reagent (concentration un-
known). The cells were passaged a minimum of three times and a maximum of six times
before being transitioned to the alternative media.
For the experiment, 0.5 × 106 cells/mL were transferred to 25 mL of the tested growth
media in 125 mL Erlenmeyer shaker flasks. The cultures were incubated in an orbital shaker
(Buch & Holm) at 110 rpm, 37◦C, and 5% CO2.
Cells were supplemented with 2 mM
GlutaMAX™(100x) (Gibco™), or as otherwise specified.
After approximately four days
of incubation, the cells were counted and diluted (to 0.5 × 106 or 1 × 106 cells/mL) into
fresh media to maintain optimal cell density for transfection. Typically, two passages were
performed before transfection, which was carried out 3-4 days after the last passage. One
day prior to transfection, the flasks were topped with an additional 25 mL fresh medium and
transferred to 250 mL flasks to further support cell growth and ensure optimal conditions
for the transfection process.
3.1.2
PEI-mediated Transfection Protocol
TGE was carried out using the PEIpro®-GMP reagent (Polyplus) to deliver pDNA into
HEK293 cells. The transfection complexes were prepared by mixing CHIKV pDNA (na-
tional institute of health) with the appropriate amount of PEIpro®-GMP. The ratio and
concentration of pDNA to PEI varied in some of the screens, with the polyplexes allowed to
form at room temperature for a fixed time (8 minutes ±30 seconds).
On the day of transfection (generally day 4 after last passage), the PEI-DNA complexes
were added to each shaker flask containing HEK293 cells. After the addition of the trans-
fection mix, the flasks were incubated at 110 rpm, 37◦C, and 5% CO2. The post-transfection
temperature was adjusted according to the experimental design (37°C being the normal pro-
cedure). To improve protein expression, VPA was added to the culture 20–24 hours after
transfection at a standard concentration of 4 mM, with adjustments made as needed to align
with the experimental design.
Following transfection, the cultures were closely monitored to evaluate cell growth, viability,
metabolite profile, and VLP production. Sampling was conducted at several time points post-
transfection (typically on days 2, 3, 4, and 5). Throughout the course of the experiments,
any necessary adjustments to key parameters were made to optimize transfection outcomes
based on specific experimental requirements.
At each sampling point, 1.5 mL of cell culture was collected for analysis. These samples were
centrifuged and filtered through 0.2 µm Nalgene™Rapid-Flow™Sterile Disposable Filter
Units (Avantor®) prior to further examination.

Fixation and Staining for Transfection Analysis in HEK Cells
The fixation process represents a key step in the preparation of HEK cells for transfection
analysis as it serves to stabilise cellular structures and preserve intracellular components for
subsequent detection. Prior to fixation, cells are stained with Zombie Violet™dye, which
enables the differentiation between viable and non-viable cells. This staining is essential for

membranes while remaining excluded from intact cells. Subsequently, the cells are fixed using

Phosphate-Buffered Saline (PBS) to remove excess fixative, and stored under appropriate
conditions for further analysis.

3.3
DoE setup
A custom DoE approach in JMP statistical software was applied to evaluate the influence
of various parameters on VLP production, with the response variable set to be maximized.
Continuous factors were systematically varied across experiments to assess their impact on
VLP production. To manage the complexity of the experimental setup and avoid an excess-
ive number of factor combinations, a subset of factors was selected for each experiment. This
approach ensured a feasible design while maintaining the ability to identify key influencing
parameters. The model was constructed using a second-order interaction approach to cap-
ture both linear and non-linear effects. Response Surface Methodology (RSM) was applied
to model these relationships and optimize the experimental conditions. RSM is a statistical
technique that uses polynomial equations to approximate the relationship between the re-
sponse variable and the influencing factors. By incorporating both quadratic and interaction
terms, RSM enables the identification of optimal factor levels while accounting for interac-
tions between factors. Without a second-order interaction approach, JMP only includes the
maximum and minimum values of continuous factors as defined by the user. When second-
order interactions are applied, JMP also incorporates a middle value, increasing the number
of required experiments. Once the number of factors and their minimum and maximum
values are defined, JMP suggests the minimum number of experiments needed to sufficiently
cover the RSM model. Additionally, JMP provides a recommendation for the number of ex-
periments required to ensure a robust and reliable predictive model. After determining the
number of experiments, JMP calculates the most optimal experimental design and generates
a table outlining the setup. Upon completion of the experiments, VLP titers are entered
into the results table, enabling the preparation of a statistical model using standard least
squares analysis.

5
Discussion and future work
In this thesis, the optimization of CHIKV VLP production in HEK293 cells was studied.
Optimizing VLP production in mammalian cell lines presents several challenges as each cell-
line requires tailored adjustments to achieve high yields. In this study, a DoE approach was
used to systematically investigate key factors influencing transfection efficiency and VLP
production.
To initiate CHIKV VLP production, transient transfections were performed using PEI as
the DNA carrier. A major challenge associated with transient transfection is the variability
in its efficiency which was evident in the results of this study. Despite cells being exposed
to identical conditions, there was significant variability in both cellular health and VLP
titers. In some instances, cells under the same conditions failed to produce VLPs, while at
other times, high titers were observed. This inherent variability in mammalian cell cultures,
coupled with the challenges of transient transfection, made it difficult to identify optimal
conditions as results could differ considerably between experiments.
Additionally, unex-
plained low titers were observed early in the process, and the root cause of this deviation
was not identified during the course of the thesis, thus preventing the achievement of higher
yields.
Nonetheless, through various screenings and DoE analyses, it was still possible to estimate
optimal conditions for each factor as the low titers were consistent across all samples, provid-
ing a valuable opportunity to investigate the effects of different parameters.
The available resources allowed for approximately 15-30 flasks per experiment, which re-
quired splitting the DoE into smaller subsets to make the experiments manageable. How-
ever, the critical effects of the interaction between factors may have been overlooked when
using this approach [50]. Additionally, the inability to perform multiple replicates for each
parameter was a limitation. Without replicates it was difficult to assess the repeatability of
the results, particularly given the significant variability in transfection efficiency and VLP
production between samples. As a result, the reliability of the findings may be compromised.
To optimize the current process for CHIKV VLP production, a wide set of factors in the
transfection protocol were considered. The selection of these factors was primarily based on
literature and internal discussions. The factors tested included:
• Growth medium
• Incubation temperature 24 hours post-transfection
• Cell density 72 hours prior to transfection
• Concentration of VPA supplementation 24 hours post-transfection
• Concentration of GlutaMAX supplementation
• Effects of PEI/DNA concentration and ratio

• Cell density at the time of transfection
To ensure a manageable experimental design, it was required to narrow down the selection of
media. Investigating multiple media formulations in parallel, each requiring optimization for
several factors, would have resulted in an impractically large number of experimental flasks.
Applying the DoE approach further reduced the required number of flasks while maintaining
statistical robustness.
For example, in Section 4.6, where three conditions were tested for three different factors, a
traditional OFAT approach would have required 33 = 27 individual flasks to fully explore the
parameter space. In contrast, DoE significantly reduces this number while still providing
statistical robustness. JMP analysis suggested that for an experimental setup with three
conditions across three parameters, a minimum of 10 runs is sufficient to build a reliable
model, with the default recommendation being 16 runs. This demonstrates the efficiency of
DoE in optimizing multiple factors simultaneously, enabling a comprehensive analysis while
maintaining a practical and scalable experimental workload.
Following the initial three screening experiments, the decision to proceed with specific media
was evident, as only three formulations yielded promising results. Media C, as discussed
in Section 4.2.1 was selected for internal studies at Bavarian Nordic, leaving Media I and
Media H as the most suitable candidates for further investigation. Both media demonstrated
satisfactory performance compared to the current media. This was particularly clear in the
experiments described in Sections 4.4 and 4.5, where cells cultured in Media I exhibited
comparable or even higher VLP titers than those observed in the control flask. Additionally,
Media H initially showed promising VLP production, especially in the experiment described
in Section 4.3, before the unexplained decline in titers.
With the selection of Media I and Media H, the DoE studies could be initiated, allowing for
a systematic investigation of key process parameters.
5.1
Evaluation of Factors Affecting CHIKV VLP Production
Post-transfection temperature shifts were evaluated to assess their impact on transfection
efficiency and VLP production. Lowering the incubation temperature to 33°C and 35°C did
not enhance VLP yields compared to the standard 37°C condition (section 4.3 and 4.4).
This contrasts with findings from Lin et al.
(2015), who reported a 1.5-fold increase in
recombinant protein expression when reducing the culture temperature to 33°C 24 hours
post-transfection [55].
Temperature reductions are known to influence various cellular processes, including growth,
viability, protein synthesis, and metabolism.
In some cases, lower temperatures can en-
hance recombinant protein expression by extending the duration of protein production and
reducing proteolytic degradation. However, the effect is often cell-line dependent, and not
Page 53 of 80
==PAGE:62==
Department of Biotechnology and Biomedicine
Technical University of Denmark
Master Thesis
26-02-2025
all expression systems benefit from temperature reductions [55]. Given that temperature
adjustments consistently showed minimal impact on VLP production in this study, further
optimization in this area is not considered a priority.
The cell density effect (CDE) is a well-documented phenomenon in animal cell-based biopro-
cessing, where increasing cell density leads to reduced cell-specific productivity due to nu-
trient depletion, metabolite accumulation, and physiological changes [56]. As a result, cell
density at the time of transfection is expected to have a significant impact on transfection
efficiency and VLP production.
Higher densities may reduce efficiency due to increased
cell-to-cell contact and nutrient competition, yet they can also lead to greater total VLP
output. The results of this study indicated that higher cell densities generally led to lower
transfection efficiencies, which aligns with the assumption that increased cell aggregation
may reduce the availability of transfection complexes. However, total VLP production was
not solely dependent on transfection efficiency, as higher cell densities resulted in a greater
number of productive cells, partially compensating for the lower transfection rate. The find-
ings suggest that an optimal cell density range exists, balancing transfection efficiency with
total VLP output. Various strategies, such as fed-batch and perfusion cultures, are being
explored to mitigate the challenges of the CDE [56].
VPA supplementation enhanced VLP production, as flasks without VPA generally exhibited
lower yields. Higher VPA concentrations appeared to further increase VLP yield, with 8
mM resulting in the highest production. For GlutaMAX, supplementation beyond 2 mM
had no significant effect. However, considerable variability was observed in the DoE results,
highlighting the need for further investigation to confirm these findings and provide a more
definitive understanding of their impact.
Based on the DoE results, PEI concentration was the most significant factor affecting VLP
production, showing a strong linear relationship with increasing yield (p = 0.00011, Section
4.5). This confirms PEI’s key role in transfection efficiency. Section 4.6 further showed that
this trend continues at higher PEI concentrations, up to 12 µg/mL. These results suggest
that increasing PEI levels could further improve transfection efficiency, as long as cytotoxic
effects are controlled.
DNA concentration showed a moderate effect but was not statistically significant in Section
4.5 (p = 0.12015). However, Section 4.6 revealed a clear quadratic relationship, indicating
that DNA concentration does not have a simple linear effect on transfection efficiency. This
suggests that excessive DNA may reduce transfection efficiency, likely due to insufficient
polyplex formation between PEI and DNA.
The optimal PEI/DNA ratio was found to be between 1.6 and 2.4, with 1.6 representing
the current procedure. However, the ideal ratio for polyplex formation appears to be highly

dependent on the cell line, recombinant protein, and media composition. Xie et al. (2013)
reported that a PEI/DNA ratio of 5:1 resulted in the highest transcription levels and trans-
fection efficiency, despite reducing cell viability to approximately 40–50% [57]. In contrast,
lower PEI/DNA ratios of 1:1 or 1:3 led to higher cell viability but lower transfection efficiency
and yield [57]. These findings highlight the importance of carefully balancing PEI and DNA
concentrations to maximize transfection efficiency while minimizing cytotoxic effects.
5.2
Final Considerations
Assessing cost reduction in relation to VLP production levels remains challenging due to
the variability in results. Before the decline in titers, the the current media formulation
consistently yielded higher VLP titers than Media I, while Media H occasionally achieved
comparable levels 4.3. However, following the unexpected decrease in titers, Media I out-
performed the current protocol in certain conditions, as observed in Section 4.4.
Considering the cost reduction described in Table 4, switching to a more cost-effective me-
dium appears promising.
However, due to the substantial variability in results, a more
stable production protocol is necessary.
The observed differences in cell behavior across
media suggest that each medium requires specific optimization, as factors influencing trans-
fection efficiency and VLP production likely have distinct optima.
One notable pattern is the adaptation phase seen with Media I, when cells first show reduced
viability before progressively stabilizing to levels that are similar to or higher than those of
the current protocol. This underscores the importance of optimizing adaptation conditions
when transitioning to a new medium.
Establishing a stable cell line could reduce many of the current challenges, particularly
the high variability in transfection efficiency and VLP production. While generating and
screening stable cell lines is resource-intensive and time-consuming, it would enhance pro-
cess consistency and predictability, addressing the main limitation of the current system
with transient transfection.
Such unpredictability is not viable for large-scale production, as it introduces uncertainty
in yield estimation, complicating both process management and production planning. Es-
tablishing a stable, well-adapted cell line would enhance reproducibility, improve overall
efficiency, and support a more robust manufacturing process, making it a valuable long-term
investment despite the initial effort required.
However, TGE remains an accessible and cost-effective technology that allows for the rapid
production of high titers of biological products within weeks. Its straightforward implement-
ation, lower costs, and shorter timelines make it a highly valuable approach, especially for
early-stage process development. More importantly, TGE offers flexibility, enabling manufac-
turers to quickly adapt production to different products by simply modifying the transfected

plasmid DNA, without altering the cellular platform [56]. For a process still in development,
a stable cell line should not be implemented until the optimal conditions have been identified
and established.
5.3
Future work
The optimization of biological processes, like producing VLPs in HEK cells, is inherently
complex since even small parameter adjustments can impact the entire process. While this
study has identified optimal conditions for some factors, others require additional research.
A larger dataset would help determine these optima with greater precision, leading to better
process optimization.
Looking beyond this study’s scope, lactate concentration deserves further investigation. Re-
search has shown that pH variations during and after transfection can affect protein expres-
sion [58]. Specifically, when too much lactate builds up, it can lower the culture pH, poten-
tially harming cells and reducing transfection efficiency [59]. Recent advances by Hooman et
al. (2025) demonstrate a promising genome-editing approach that effectively reduces lactate
production in both CHO and HEK cells by simultaneously targeting multiple genes. Import-
antly, these modified cells maintain normal growth rates while producing minimal lactate
[60].
Another important consideration is the choice of the HEK293 cell line. The VRC293 cell
line, which is used for this process, is derived from the HEK293H cell line at the Vaccine
Research Center and is cGMP-compliant [61]. HEK293H cell lines are clonal isolates of the
HEK293 line, selected for their rapid growth in serum-free media, high transfection efficiency,
and high protein production capacity [62]. Other derivatives like HEK293T, HEK293E, and
HEK293F have been created for different biotechnological applications, each with unique
properties [63]. For instance, HEK293-6E stably expresses the EBNA-1 protein, enabling
recognition of the OriP origin of replication and supporting episomal DNA replication. A
study demonstrated that HEK293-6E cells transfected with pOriP produced three times
more VLPs than those transfected with a control plasmid, while maintaining similar cell
densities and viability [29].
Exploring alternative cell lines for CHIKV VLP production
could offer potential benefits, but it would also involve significant regulatory work and may
require new clinical trials.
The role of various additives enhancing CHIKV VLP production should also be investigated.
Studies show that compounds like DMSO and lithium acetate can improve transfection effi-
ciency by increasing cell membrane permeability [44]. Similarly, caffeine can enhance protein
yields by inhibiting DNA damage response kinases and modifying cell cycle progression [43].
Nocodazole, which arrests the cell cycle in G2/M phase, have been shown to increase trans-
fection efficiency to 94%, from 58% in the control, and doubled protein production in CHO

==PAGE:65==

Technical University of Denmark
Master Thesis

cells. No decrease in cell viability was observed [64]. This is just a few out of many additives
to consider.
At stated in Section 2.2.2 transport of DNA from the cytoplasm to the nucleus remains a
key challenge in non-viral gene delivery. While PEI is commonly used as a DNA carrier,
its effectiveness varies based on its branching structure and charge distribution. Research
has shown that incorporating DNA nuclear targeting sequences (DTS) into plasmid design
can enhance DNA nuclear entry and improve transfection outcomes. Therefore, investig-
ating combinations of different PEI variants with DTS-modified plasmids could potentially
optimize transfection efficiency and increase VLP production yields [37].
The range of parameters to explore is large, and each parameter examined contributes to a
deeper understanding of the mechanisms underlying transient transfection in HEK293 cells
and VLP production. By systematically optimizing these parameters, it will be possible to
develop a more efficient transfection protocol, ultimately leading to enhanced VLP yields.

Conclusion
The production of CHIKV VLPs in HEK293 cells requires process-specific optimization, as
methods developed for other systems are not directly transferable. By utilizing a structured
DoE methodology, optimal conditions were identified while drastically reducing the number
of experimental runs compared to traditional OFAT methods.
The study was initiated with a media screen based on the current protocol to identify suit-
able candidates for further optimization in the DoE. Out of 11 tested media alternatives,
two were selected for further investigation.
Among the parameters evaluated, PEI concentration emerged as the most significant factor,
demonstrating a strong linear correlation with VLP yield even at higher concentrations. In
contrast, DNA concentration exhibited a quadratic effect, indicating that excessive amounts
could reduce transfection efficiency. The optimal PEI/DNA ratio was identified as between
1.6 and 2.4, balancing efficiency and cell viability. Subsequent to transfection, reduction of
temperature did not enhance VLP yield.
Supplementation with VPA led to improved VLP production, with 8 mM yielding the highest
titers, though variability in results suggests the need for further refinement. GlutaMAX bey-
ond 2 mM had no significant effect. Additionally, higher cell densities reduced transfection
efficiency but increased total VLP yield, highlighting the trade-off between transfection ef-
ficiency and overall productivity.
However, the overall results were highly inconsistent, with substantial variability across
experiments. The large uncertainty suggests that the observed trends may not be robust,
and further studies are required to confirm the findings. Additional research is required
to refine the experimental setup, minimize variability, and confirm whether the identified
parameters truly enhance CHIKV VLP production. Moreover, future studies should explore
alternative process parameters to improve transfection efficiency, maximize VLP yield and
minimize production costs.
