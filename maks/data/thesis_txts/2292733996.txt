Summary
In this project, we have developed an agent based model, used to describe
interactions between ﬁsh in a school.
The model is built to describe social dynamics, in which individual behavior
and collective consensus decision-making are resolved using stochastic pro-
cesses. Each agent have two traits indicating the abilities of leadership and
perform social interactions. It is the combination of these two traits within
the particualr environmental context that determine the behaviour of the
given agent and that of the group.
The model is tested in six diﬀerent cases, each mimicking migrations of a ﬁsh
population in terms of group movement.
The eﬃciency in model’s implementation allows for large-scale simulations in
which algorithm optimization is required. To do this, we investigated imple-
mentations of GPGPU and explicit parallel programming. These have been
developed and tested to optimize usage of the available computational resour-
ces.
The results of the cases show what prerequisites are necessary for grouping,
the eﬀects of elimination of agents and change of climate and the bolstering
of performance by using evolution of traits.
The results show that an increase of leadership is always in the interest of
both the group and the agent itself as it increases group performance. Sso-
me results suggest absence of sociality even when the cost of ﬁtness is zero
and the environment is constant. However when the environmental gradient
changes (e.g. since climate change) high sociality is evolved at the expenses
of a reduced leadership trait.
This speciﬁc results will require further investigations in how the code is
implemented (i.e., the selection algorithm) and the speciﬁc method we used
forssocial interactions. In terms of implementation, a proof-of-concept imple-
mentation using GPGPU have proven more eﬃcient than using external pa-
rallel programming. However, since the maximum memory allowed on GPUs
are still relatively small to CPUs, the limit of how many agents can be simu-
lated are vastly smaller. It was however possible to reach the requirement of
30.000 agents using GPGPU.

Introduction
A large number of animal species live in groups forming societies ranging
from the complexity of the modern human societies to simpler organizations
such as those widespread in ﬁsh (Figure 1), birds (Figure (2) and several ter-
restrial animals. Fish schooling, birds ﬂocking or animal herding are typical
examples of such natural groups where individuals appear to act collectively
without centralized directions. However, to beneﬁt from the advantage in li-
ving in a group, individuals need to compromise their personal interests and
behaviours to reach a collective decision [6]. This task is known as a con-
sensus decision, which describes the process of balancing between personal
needs and gains obtained by living in a group [13]. The collective decision
process is a diﬃcult task for a large group, especially when individuals can-
not easily communicate.
Recent research in group dynamics and collective behaviour has analysed
how collective decision can be reached in natural groups through appropria-
te pooling of individual estimates [12]. Here it is described as a stabilization
of movement, meaning that if all within a group behaves the same, the same
patterns of stabilized movement occurs.

It remains unclear however how in such groups on the move the social role
is determined and how the performance of the group and its structure might
be aﬀected by the presence of leading individuals or individuals acting only
in response to their personal interest.
It has been suggested [5] that a possible societal structure of a group is one in
which organisms have one out of two roles, leaders and followers. Individuals
of the ﬁrst type are able to take decisions gathering information from the
society in which they live as well as from their environment. While follower
can only move as a response to social cues obtained from other organisms
around them. Moreover, even though the size of groups can diﬀer between
species, the basic dynamics, structure and roles remains [9].
One example of the advantages of using group structure, can be found in ﬁsh
schools, where the ﬁsh groups together to obtain for example better survi-
val against predators or increased performances in migrating [14]. Previous
studies [8] showed that in order to achieve great accuracy on getting the
migration on target, only a very small proportion of the group needs to be
informed and with greater groups, this proportion can decrease even further.
This occurs without communication between organisms and possibly with no
public information about the level of individuals knowledge. This can be ex-
plained as a result of the Many Wrongs Principle, which foretells that groups
usually outperform singletons through cohesion [10], even if the singleton is
more skilled than any of the group members [15].
The principle show that through reasonable pooling of estimates from a
crowd, an accurate collected estimate can be derived1. Grouping together
is then an advantage for ﬁsh migration but not all compositions of grouping
are equally eﬀective [5]. It has been shown that in aggregations with a mix-
ture of leaders and followers individuals can enjoy signiﬁcantly higher growth
rates than in any other group comprising a single behavioural type [11]. Hen-
ce to identify the migratory performances of ﬁsh school under diﬀerent social
structures of leaders and followers is key to understand the ecology of these
species.
Leadership traits are hard to be described in general as the tasks of this ro-
le are diﬀerent between diﬀerent species. Nonetheless, leaders in nature are
usually excelling in a key attribute, e.g. strength in predatory groups, color
traits or singing skills in birds, etc., and we consider here the ability in en-
vironmental detection. Environment detection deﬁnes the ability of leaders
to see and change direction accordingly to environmental cues (temperature,

We create hypothesis to link the social structure to group performance:
Hypothesis: The social structure of a group can aﬀect the performance of
the group to complete complex tasks, like e.g. long distance migrations.
In this project we analyse with a numerical model the eﬀect of changes in
social structure on group performance. Hence we study how ﬁsh population
can improve performance by changing towards a particular social structure.
Since ﬁsh groups can be tasked with many diﬀerent problems we will also
study perturbed scenarios, in which ﬁsh schools are exposed to ﬁshing and
changing environment due to e.g. climate changes.
To determine leaders and followers in our simulations, we use diﬀerent hypo-
theses:
 Inherent role: The role of an individual is chosen by birth. This means
that an individual is either born follower or leader by chance, which
does not change throughout time.

 Group-chosen role: The role of an individual is chosen by birth, but

like the inherent role, but bounded such a speciﬁc portion of the group
is leaders.
 Role by need: The role of an individual is chosen and changed by need.

on their neighbouring (if any) individuals (See Case 5).
Inherent and group-chosen roles are the simplest of implementation, but yi-
elds very basic and unnatural scenarios. Studies show that the role by need
yield a more natural and resilient composition of grouping [7] [4], as it allows
a dynamic learning of roles, depending on the scenario. The realism of this
are however limited, as the learning process of leadership or sociality are just
as unknown as how individuals are given natural roles in the ﬁrst place.
So conclusively, this leads to the questions of how an group performance are
aﬀected by social structure, how group performance changes after pertur-
bations, like e.g. climate changes, and which factors within group composi-
tion that increases the resistance and resilience towards these perturbations.
The main questions to be answered in this thesis are:
1. How are the composition in ﬁsh migrations aﬀected when performing
long distant migrations?
2. Given a social structure of followers and leaders, how will the group
react to changing environmental conditions? What is the role of lear-
ning?
3. What are the conditions for resilience and resistance of the migration
paths under climate change and ﬁshing?

Description of simulations
For this project, the migration of ﬁsh population are analysed developing
a numerical model that uses an agent based description of the interactions
within the migrating population. The migrating ﬁsh school is travelling in
hazardous waters, exposing the group to dangers such as predation and ﬁs-
hing.
The model is used to test how social structure within the migrating popula-
tion can aﬀect group formation and resistance to perturbations.
The performance of the group when migrating is assessed by counting the
total number of agents that are able to follow the environmental cues.

Social dynamics are deﬁned as all the interactions occurring between agents.
Interactions are possible when individual encounter each other and are based
on the identiﬁcation of the closest neighbours. Two radii are taken into ac-
count, perception radius (Re) and repulsion radius (Rr) (Table 1 and Figure
3).

For the social dynamics, there are three possible outcomes from encountering:
 No neighbours: The agent has not found any neighbour, and does by
default not change direction of movement, other than the consistent
error.
 Aggregation & cohesion: The closest neighbour is within Re but outside
Rr. This means the agent want to aggregate and cohere toward the
nearest neighbour, in order to form a group. For illustration, see Figure
5 for cohesion and Figure 4 for aggregation.
 Repulsion: Triggered by either the closest neighbour is within Rr or
random chance, it will try to repulse itself away from the neighbour;
breaking from the group formation. The agent changes towards the
opposite direction from the nearest neighbour, which can be seen at
Figure 6.
Only one of these three actions can be taken on each time-step, in which is
chosen by the distance to the closest neighbour or by the random defection.

2.1.2
Environmental detection
We stated earlier that the main trait of leaders in the population is the abili-
ty to ﬁnd the environmental cues and adjust their movement towards them.
This leaves that the presence of leadership movement is critical, in order for
a group to reach a certain target. If no leaders are present in the group, the
model predicts the movement of agents to group together by random enco-
unters and then move aimlessly throughout all time-steps. This means that
the leadership trait are the pointer of grouping, allowing and ensuring that
movement towards the designated target is made.

small. Hence for now, these aspects will not be discussed.

3.3
Reasoning
Before beginning to make the diﬀerent implementations, we need to ask how
and why usages of parallel programming and GPU programming would in-
crease performance. While the technicality of how memory are stored and
computed on CPUs and GPUs are quite complicated, a simpliﬁed version of

Just like the diﬀerent resources of a system can predict the performance of

gives an overall understanding of the system, but might be inaccurate when

3.3.1
Parallel Programming
When speaking of parallel programming, the kind of structure are split in
two parts: implicit and explicit parallelism.
Implicit parallel programming
Implicit parallel programming are the implemented usage of multi-threading,
which mostly comes from the underlying libraries in Matlab, like BLAS,
LAPACK, FFTW, etc. The number of threads can be controlled, with some
exceptions, but is beginning to grow obsolete with newer versions of Matlab.
Explicit parallel programming
In this execution model, MATLAB uses a pool of workers, i.e. additional
MATLAB instances, to distribute the work. A workpool of instances are
started, using speciﬁed resources and mechanisms of the speciﬁc cluster, see
Figure 7. While we only use a single computer in this project, this form of
parallelism can be divided between several computers, with each having se-
veral workers.
s103228
Page 19
==PAGE:20==
Jonas Thor Pedersen
31\01-2016
The function parfor (also called SPMD constructs) sends the enclosed code
to each worker, which will be written by the client (called the master pro-
cess). The master process writes the code to a pre-deﬁned storage, of which
all works read and execute their given tasks; each yielding an output to a new
output-ﬁle, which are accumulated by the master process and then yielded
out as the ﬁnal results.
From this, it is clear that a lot of potential overhead can be created, which
sets an upper limit of how many workers can be eﬃciently used in parallel
on a given system.
For this project, the only focus of parallelism is on the explicit parallel pro-
gramming. The implicit parallel programming are already highly regulated
automatically for each system, while the explicit parallelism have higher po-
tential of increasing performance.
When using standard settings, a single session of Matlab is generally single-
threaded on a CPU. This means that even given that most CPUs in newer
times have multiple cores within them, Matlab only makes use of one core.
This leaves a potential performance increase by using more working resour-
ces, if the tasks in Matlab can be run over multiple cores (multithreading,
implicit parallelism) or several Matlab sessions (explicit parallelism) can be
run on multiple cores.

small-scaled projects might not be aﬀected or even decrease in performance,
as the constructing and assembling of the parallel model takes calculations
by themselves.
3.3.2
GPU Usage
General-Purpose computing on Graphics Processing Units (GPGPU) is the
usage of GPUs to increase performance of applications usually handled by
CPUs. As a relatively new concept, GPGPU is increasingly being incorpora-
ted in large-scaled simulations, as potential computational gains can be achie-
ved, by making a GPU-CPU framework which makes correct uses of CPUs
and GPUs. However, usage of both CPUs and GPUs have strengths and we-
aknesses; in which the understanding of structures of CPUs and GPUs are
essential to optimize the usage of both. A generic GPGPU model can be seen
in ﬁgure (8).

diﬀerent components. The section is simpliﬁed, focusing on the aspects of

CPUs
The Central Processing Units (CPUs) are the main-cores of computations
today. Being signiﬁcantly faster than GPU cores and having speciﬁc archi-
tectures to handle more complex5 calculations, the CPU excel in complicated
calculations, which are mostly required by modern operation systems. Howe-
ver, the amount of threads CPUs have are very limited compared to GPUs,
and are by this extremely ineﬃcient for simple compute-intensive operations
like e.g. video processing and physics simulations.
Conclusively for this, the CPU is best used for few, complex calculations.
Combined with the parallel programming, multiple threads are available,
but this is still only a far cry from the amount of threads GPUs have.
GPUs
Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) are the hardware of which normally are
used to handle computer graphics. Usually structured by a relatively large
amount of cores and being specialized to operate on ﬂoating-point numbers,
the GPUs excel in making many calculations simultaneously, though con-
strained under simplicity of operations. As each individual GPU core are
usually vastly slower than a CPU core, using a GPU core to compute some
complex calculations would drastically decrease performance, compared to a
CPU core.
Conclusively, using GPUs on a large-scale amount of relatively simple calcu-
lations is optimal. For this project, many vectorized (simultaneous) ﬂoating-
point calculations are used, in which usage of GPGPU is very likely to be
optimal.

How does the sociality term aﬀect grouping?
Higher sociality decreases the error, no matter the composition. Sociality are

To form groups, a certain threshold of sociality needs to be achieved for the
population. For complete grouping the level is approximately ≥0.5, while no
grouping is around ≤0.04.
More leaders decrease the deviance of the group, where a minimum amount
are needed for the entire group to move towards their target.

leaders; which requires a speciﬁc amount of sociality for groups to form. This

sociality level (or vice versa), in order to form an optimal group composition.

Conclusion
This project, has built a basic workframe for an agent based model, using
social and individual dynamics. This framework has been implemented with
parallel programming using multiple CPU cores and with GPGPU, using
CUDA cores; in order to ﬁnd the optimal usage of computational resources.
After testing computational eﬃciency, the framework has been customized to
simulate speciﬁc scenarios, namely ﬁve cases testing a speciﬁc problem within
long distance ﬁsh migrations. Furthermore a major case have been created
and tested, using combinations of these ﬁve cases, testing if the combining
the individual cases yields some unforeseen eﬀects.
Generally the results of the individual cases show that being a leader is
an advantage for both personal and grouping gain of performance. In most
cases, when increasing the amount of leaders, the deviation and dispersion
of group performance decreases signiﬁcantly a the steady-state. Furthermore
the response to perturbation is faster with more leaders; indicating that more
leaders strengthen the group to adapt quicker and better to environmental
changes.
The premises of grouping are however independent of the social structure (see
Sociality in Case 1) and even though no clear deﬁnition of sociality exists,
an approximation of the general expectation of grouping is presented.
The greatest surprise for this project however, is shown in Case 5, where no
agent learned sociality, even though it had no cost on the individual ﬁtness.
These results does not match the earlier research from Guttal & Couzin, but
is probably linked to the diﬀerence in the modelling of social dynamics.
Shown from Case 2, we ﬁnd that removing sociality after converging towards
a target does not do much harm to group performance. Having already con-
verged, the agents stay more or less on target for a long time after losing
sociality. Thus, to enforce a constant need of grouping (and leadership in
particular), we combined Case 3 and Case 5 to see the inﬂuence of an chan-
ging environment on the learning of traits.
The results showed two things. First, that by increasing cost of a trait, the
learning process of that trait is suppressed, not very surprising.

This could be due to the error as in Case 5, but now the results actually

A last notation is to Case 4, where the dynamic roles of agents show an

From this project, we have learned about the social and individual dyna-

Forecasting group behaviour on changing environments have been used wide-

optimizing the design of escape routes to evacuate a building as fast as po-

about carriers.
Beyond the results of this thesis, the agent based model used to explain inte-

7.4
Explanation of kNN and k-d trees
The kNN-algorithm are used in order to ﬁnd the nearest neighbour(s, to
use in the interactions between agents. The algorithm makes use of kd-trees,
which are grown using the createns function in Matlab.
A k-d tree is a method of splitting up a domain into subspaces, dividing the
population into portions. Each population portion are evaluated, and if any
portion are found still too big, the domain are divided once again to create
subsubspaces, and so on, until a reasonable population size are made. When
reaching the reasonable population, a distance search are made for each node
the tree has grown, assembling all results and yielding them as a collected
output.
Growing a Kd-tree is however expensive, having a O(K · n), K being num-
ber of iterations and n the amount of agents, while the M-nearest neighbour
search are merely O(M log(N)). A simpliﬁed version of the algorithm can be
explained as follows:
s103228
Page 61
==PAGE:62==
Jonas Thor Pedersen
31\01-2016
kNN-Search
Data: A set of datapoints (population)
Result: The data of nearest neighbour(s) or set of subdata
Initialize root-node (entire dataset);
K-d Tree(data);
if Population good to nearest neighbour search;
then
Make distance search for all data points and sort to ﬁnd M-nearest
neighbours;
else
Split the population by creating child-nodes of input;
kNN-search(children)
end
Algorithm 1: kNN-algorithm explained
Speciﬁcally for the k-d tree, is that the splitting to children are cycled through
the dimensions; meaning for this 2-dimensional problem, it ﬁrst cuts along
the x-axis, then the y-axis, repeating this until splitting is done.
K-d trees are chosen above other trees, as k-d trees are better for dynamic da-
tasets, which are non-sparse and where the majority of points changes; while
e.g. quadtrees are better used stationary, as the computational complexity
in most cases will be greater. Also, k-d trees are inherently used for ﬁnding
nearest neighbours and are in Matlab implemented with diﬀerent heuristics
(a median-ﬁnding algorithm), to balance the grown k-d tree; minimizing the
number of times children nodes are needed to be grown.
It should be noted that a smarter implementation of Quadtrees have been
tested, trying to exploit the agent velocity to predict how much of the sear-
chtree change, indicating that only certain children of the tree needs to be
recalculated. However, making these checks while building and maintaining
the quadtrees did not yield improvement in calculation time; in which k-d
trees have been chosen as the ﬁnal algorithm for this project. It should be
noted that increasing the population size, a combination of a stationary tree
(e.g. quadtrees) and a dynamic k-d tree, might increase performance as cre-
ating an entire k-d tree every iteration grows very expensive; but might be
necessary for portions of the population, while the stationary quad-tree can
take care of those agents which are caught within the smarter implementation
mentioned above.
