 Abstract 
ABSTRACT 
In this thesis, a Radio over Ethernet mapper and demapper is designed in VHDL, in accordance to a 
requirement specification based on the IEEE 1904.3 Radio over Ethernet Encapsulations and Mappings draft 
standard.  
The system is simulated and verified in Modelsim, and synthesized in Quartus II. 
Simulations show promising results, as the design is able to map and demap I/Q data from four parallel 
antenna flows, without error and with the required 8ns precision for the presentation time.  
Further improvements can be made to the system by increasing the test cases, and also increasing the 
speed of the circuit, as it did not meet the requirements of 10Gbps which were set before the design phase.

 Introduction 
INTRODUCTION 
In order to meet the increasing demands for bandwidth and lower cost of the mobile networks, steps are 
taken to centralize the expensive components, which are currently placed at the antenna site.  
This form of functional split, is referred to as Centralized Radio Acess Network (C-RAN).  
By using this kind of functional split, several antennas can share the expensive component, hence lowering 
the total cost. 
Common Public Radio Interface (CPRI)[2], is currently the most used protocol to transfer I/Q samples 
between the antenna site and the baseband unit. This protocol has strict requirements to bandwidth, 
latency and jitter, hence more expensive solutions such as OTN or dark fiber is used for transport. To 
further decrease the total cost, it would be better to transport the data over Ethernet, which is a common 
part of the infrastructure in metro areas.  
If Ethernet should be used for transporting CPRI data or raw I/Q samples, several strict requirements need 
to be met, such as synchronization, latencty and jitter. Furthermore, the radio data would need to 
beencapsulated and mapped to Ethernet. For this, the IEEE 1904.3 draft standard for Radio over Ethernet 
Encapsulations and Mappings[3] is used. 
In this thesis, a Radio over Ethernet mapper and demapper is made in VHDL, which aims to map raw I/Q 
data into the Ethernet and RoE frame format. The thesis will go through the requirement specification for 
each component and after that the design will be presented. Lastly, the system is simulated in Modelsim 
with the help of a testbench and Analyzer component.

1 PACKET BASED FRONTHAUL NETWORK 
This chapter serves to describe the relevant technologies and theory needed to understand the use cases 
and requirements for the Radio over Ethernet implementation.  
The first section will describe Cloud Radio Access Network (C-RAN), while the next section will describe the 
protocol used in C-RAN, to transport IQ samples from the Remote Radio Head (RRH) to the Base Band Unit 
(BBU). The last section describes Radio over Ethernet, why it’s needed and what the requirements are. 
 
1.1 CLOUD RADIO ACCESS NETWORK (C-RAN) 
In a traditional mobile network, a single base station consists of a BBU, typically located in a container at 
the bottom of the antenna tower. From the container, typically a coaxial cable is used to transmit the Radio 
Frequency (RF) signal up to the antenna. An amplifier is needed to amplify the RF signal accordingly, as the 
attenuation in coaxial cables are relatively high – around 3dB / 100m.  
In cases where the antennas are located far away from the BBU, a lot of power will be lost from the signal, 
hence a high power amplification is needed. This leads to a lot of power consumption.  
Numbers show, that the cell site accounts for 72% of the power used in a mobile network, and at the cell 
site 46% of the power is used for air conditioners, and 51% is used for major equipment [1]. Hence, in the 
traditional base station configuration, there are significant cost to site rental and electricity, and also 
decreased flexibility for rollout of new base stations.  
To address some of these issues, and for operators to decrease cost, but increase efficiency and flexibility 
the C-RAN architecture is proposed. The C-RAN architecture can reduce cost (CAPEX and OPEX), lower the 
power consumption, and increase flexibility for rolling out new base stations.  
The C-RAN architecture can be divided into three main components, the first component consist of the cell 
site, which has the Remote Radio Head (RRH), and the antennas.  
The second component is a BBU pool, which is a centralized BBU pool, which serves multiple base stations. 
The third and final component, is the connection between the cell site, which has strict requirements to 
bandwidth, latency and jitter.  
The link between BBU and RRH uses Common Public Radio Interface (CPRI), usually over a dark fiber or 
Optical Transport Network (OTN), which has to carry raw I/Q samples from the antennas, hence the high 
bandwidth requirements.  
In Figure 1-1 , a visualization of the C-RAN architecture can be seen. It is worth to notice, that the amount 
of X2 interfaces between cell sites also can be reduced with this architecture, and is only needed in the case 
where two cells sites need to coordinate and belong in two different BBU pools. If they share the same BBU 
pool, they can easily coordinate in case of Coordinated Multi Point transmission (CoMP) or handovers.

A LTE cell site can support carriers ranging from 1.4MHz to 20MHz and with several carriers per site, this 
puts pressure on current fronthaul networks, but also future fronthaul networks will be challenged, with 5G 
and the support of Internet of Things (IoT) coming up.  
Modern cell sites, uses a Multiple Input and Multiple Output (MIMO) configuration, with up to 8 
Transmitter (Tx) and 8 Receiver (Rx) antennas, which requires the signal between BBU-RRH to be in the 
order of several Gbps, hence putting pressure on the fronthaul network. In 5G, Massive MIMO might be 
implemented, which has to make the fronthaul network scale even further. 
By centralizing and sharing the processing power the energy used at the cell site is reduced, as the need for 
air conditioning and a separate BBU per cell site is reduced. It also increases the utilization of the 
processing power, as the processing power is shared between multiple base stations.  
As cell load during the day differs a lot between cells, especially cells in residential and office areas, the C-
RAN architecture enables dynamic adapting to the traffic patterns, hence utilizing and saving resources 
better.  
It also allows for easy cooperation between cells, in the case for Carrier Aggregation (CA) or Coordinated 
Multi-Point transmission for LTE and LTE-A.

Lastly, the on-site rental for base station equipment is reduced, as it only consist of the RRH and antenna, 
hence saving money and simplifying the operation of a large number of base stations.  
Another advantage also comes when rolling out new base stations, due to less installation of equipment 
and remote configuration.  
In urban areas, this solution is ideal, as an operator can have a variety of cell sizes – Macro to small cell, 
where a shared BBU pool makes sense, and lowers the OPEX and CAPEX due to the above mentioned 
features.  
As explained, there are numerous advantages to C-RAN, compared to a normal RAN. However, C-RAN also 
introduces some challenges, which only becomes bigger as we introduce 5G and other new technologies, 
hence we need a scalable solution for these challenges. The technical challenges will be introduced in the 
next section, and serve as an introduction for the requirements of the implementation of a Radio over 
Ethernet Mapper and Demapper.   
 
 
1.2 COMMON PUBLIC RADIO INTERFACE (CPRI) 
CPRI is a protocol, which specifies the interface between the radio and the control part of a base station. It 
carries the data between the BBU and the RRH, and defines the interfaces between these two entities. It 
opens up for a simpler base station architecture. As mentioned in the previous section, with CPRI it is 
possible to locate the BBU further away from the antenna site, hence increasing flexibility of the 
architecture.  
CPRI carries low encoded digital IQ samples, which results in bit rates of several Gbps per site, depending 
on the number of antennas, and bandwidth of carriers. Roughly, one 20Mhz LTE carrier requires 1Gbps, 
and with MIMO and Massive MIMO, this could put pressure on the fronthaul network. The bandwidth can 
be lowered depending on which line coding is used but also different functional splits are being 
investigated, to further increase the scalability. This however, is out of the scope for this thesis. 
However, there are many other strict requirements for CPRI. The end to end delay can be no more than 
100us, and the differential delay can’t exceed + / - 8.138ns [2]. These strict timing requirements are due to 
the need for coordination between antennas and different cell sites, in case of CoMP or MIMO 
configurations at the cell site. In both cases, the data that need to be sent, is split up between multiple 
antennas / cell sites, hence increasing the actual throughput to the user.  
Because of this, CPRI needs to know the exact link delay between RRH and BBU, and to recover the CPRI 
clock in both ends for synchronization. This can be done over dark fiber, where the CPRI clock is used in 
both ends, hence making it easy to recover the timing. 
Optical Transport Network (OTN), can also be used as transport of CPRI data. In this setup, the Generic 
Mapping Procedure (GMP), is used to recover the CPRI timing, even though OTN runs on a separate OTN 
clock.  
The end-to-end delay is found through roundtrip delays.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:11==

These requirements, are used in the implementation of the Radio over Ethernet mapper / Demapper 
components, however done in a different way, as Ethernet is a packet based technology, and not 
synchronous.  
In the context of C-RAN, there are several problems with CPRI, which disables some of the advantages that 
C-RAN could offer.  
The first problem, is that CPRI uses a constant bit rate. This is a problem, as the mobile traffic often varies a 
lot over the day – daytime and nighttime, but also if the cell is located in a residential area, or an office 
area. This means that the data rate can’t be adjusted to the traffic patterns, hence decreasing the 
utilization of a shared BBU pool. Statistical multiplexing is needed to take advantage of these traffic 
patterns, hence a packet switched technology should be used, e.g. Ethernet.  
The second problem, is the scalability of the bandwidth required for CPRI. The bandwidth is proportional to 
the number of antennas, and in future mobile networks (5G and beyond), this could be a problem due to 
massive MIMO configurations.  
The last problem to address, is that CPRI links a RRH to a BBU in a 1:1 relationship, hence decreasing 
flexibility, but also resiliency in a C-RAN context, as it removes the ability to change a RRH to another BBU 
dynamically.  
To address these problems, several propositions have been made.  
First, the use of Ethernet has been presented, to transport radio protocols between the BBU pool and the 
RRH. This is known as Radio over Ethernet (RoE). With Ethernet, it is possible to take advantage of 
statistical multiplexing. Another advantage of Ethernet is its cost, and how it is widely used in most network 
equipment. It also gives the opportunity of flexible routing, resiliency and the ability to provide operations, 
administration and maintenance (OAM) functions, such as fault finding and monitoring of the network 
performance.  
Second, several functional splits have been proposed. The functional splits, will move some of the 
functionality either from the BBU to the RRH, or from the RRH to the BBU, each giving their own 
advantages. The different functional splits and their advantages, are out of the scope in this thesis, but they 
are able to further reduce the bandwidth required for CPRI, hence making the protocol more scalable for 
future fronthaul networks.

1.3 RADIO OVER ETHERNET (ROE) 
Radio over Ethernet enables the use of Ethernet, to encapsulate and map radio data into Ethernet packets. 
It can vary from CPRI over Ethernet, or even map raw I/Q data directly into the Radio over Ethernet 
payload.  
In this thesis, an implementation of a Radio over Ethernet mapper / demapper is presented in VHDL, with 
the purpose of being able to transport a raw I/Q data stream, which the antennas should receive or 
transmit at a given presentation time.  
The presentation time can be seen as RoE’s way of synchronizing the cellsite and BBU, which enables it to 
take advantage of CoMP and MIMO, but also adjust the frequency of the given BTS. The synchronization 
needed for the presentation time, can be achieved through e.g. Precision Time Protocol (PTP) [6] or 
Synchronous Ethernet [7]. In this implementation, it is assumed that PTP will be used.  
PTP gives an 80-bit time of day timestamp, which then gives the two RoE endpoints the same 
understanding of time of day. 
The implementation is based on the IEEE 1904.3 D/0.3 [3] draft standard, for Radio over Ethernet 
Encapsulations and Mappings. As the standard is a draft version, changes are being made quite often, 
hence this specific solution might not be correct according to the final release of the standard.  
 
As mentioned in the previous section, Ethernet brings numerous advantages for transport in the fronthaul 
network. The key points are:  
 
Industry standard 
 
Low cost equipment  
 
Statistical multiplexing gain  
 
OAM  
 
Standard IP / Ethernet and routing / switching 
While Ethernet brings advantages for transport in the fronthaul network, it also introduces some technical 
requirements, which need to be solved.  
First, CPRI is tightly synchronized, hence the two Ethernet end points needs to be tightly synchronized as 
well. The synchronization is needed, as the RoE mapper and Demapper, need to share the same 
understanding of Time of Day (ToD). This is due to the presentation time, which is introduced in RoE. The 
presentation time, is a timestamp in the RoE header, which tells when the RoE data packet should be 
played out from the demapper to the antenna. The timestamp is 32 bits long, where the 30 most significant 
bits (MSB) counts nanoseconds, and the 2 least significant bits (LSB) represents values of 0.25ns, hence it 
can at best achieve 1/4 nanosecond precision. The 1/4 ns precision will not be used in this implementation, 
but it is something that is supported in the standard for future use. Especially in the IEEE 802.1CM – Time 
Sensitive Networking for Fronthaul [12], the sub ns precision could be useful.  
Synchronization is also needed at the base stations, for frequency adjustments. Frequency errors due to 
jitter in the network should be less than 2 ppb [2]. 
The synchronization can be done through e.g. Synchronous Ethernet or IEEE 1588v2 Precision Time 
Protocol (PTP). In this implementation, it is assumed that PTP will be used, hence the system will receive an 
80-bit Time of Day timestamp, which is synchronized between the two components. This means they have 
the same understanding of Time of Day. With the use of PTP or Synchronous Ethernet, the demand for 
==PAGE:13==

measuring the end to end delay, which is done in CPRI disappears, and is replaced with presentation time 
which is an easier approach, as long as the two Ethernet end points share the same understanding of time 
of day.  
The requirements to synchronization in this design are set according to the equivalent CPRI requirements, 
hence the presentation time should have an accuracy of + / - 8.138ns, and the end to end delay can be no 
more than 100us [2].

As seen in table 2, the RoE header consist of a version field, which indicates the RoE version used. 
Next is the packet type field, which defines what kind of packet. There are several options, such as control 
packet, CPRI tunneling packet, structure aware or agnostic CPRI packet and finally the native RoE data flow 
packet, which is what this implementation is using. The packet options and its binary values are described 
in 1904.3, table 2, but for reference, the native RoE data flow packet value is 00 0100b, which is used in this 
implementation.  
The next value is the flow_ID, which describes different data flows between two end points. Hence, 
between a given destination MAC address and source MAC address, there can be 28 = 256 flows, as the 
flow_id value consists of 8 bits. The flow_id value, can be used to identify different antennas at a cell site, 
as the cell site would be a given MAC address, but it can support multiple antennas, which has to be 
identified – in this case through the flow_id value.  
The length field, tells how many bytes are following after the header, for this specific RoE packet.  
Last is the timestamp field, which is the presentation time, e.g. the time where this RoE packet should be 
played out on the antenna. The presentation time has to be defined as the current ToD + offset, where the 
offset should be the maximum end-to-end delay, which is the network delay, processing and buffering 
delay.  
The offset should be set accordingly to the specific network that the solution is implemented in, and it can 
be manually set through a CPU interface to the mapper, or it can be edited in the code pre compile time. By 
==PAGE:14==
 14

adding the signal to the mapper entity, it provides flexible ways of changing it – during operation also. 
Another solution to find a proper value for the offset, would be to measure the delay with OAM packets 
between the two endpoints. By doing this, the highest delay value could be taken over some continuous 
stream of data in a given time interval. The offset can be said to take account for non-determinstic jitter in 
the Ethernet network, as it is the maximum of delay which the network can guarantee, e.g. worst case 
delay through the network. 
The offset should be as low as possible in a good engineered network, as it increases the static delay per 
packet, e.g. increases the minimum delay for users, furthermore the delay can be critical for services such 
as self driving cars in future networks. 
This definition of the presentation time requires the implementation to have buffers which are big enough 
to store the packets before the presentation time is reached at the demapper. In the mapper component, 
buffers should also be present, as it should be able to store a timestamp, as the first I/Q sample of a packet 
is received and then store I/Q samples until the RoE packet size is reached.  
One of the challenges with the + / - 8 ns precision of the presentation time, is that the LTE sampling clock is 
30.72MHz, which is the clock used for that part of the circuit. This clock has a period of 32.55ns, hence with 
this only a 32ns precision can be guaranteed. In the requirement and design section there will be more 
information about this and the solution provided for the design.

mapper component.  
The mapper will then insert the I/Q data in the RoE frame and insert the RoE frame in the Ethernet frame. It 
will then send the RoE packets to the demapper component, which extracts the relevant header fields and 
buffer the packets. After this, it will send them to the correct antenna interface, at the correct presentation 
time. The output interface for the demapper, is also in the 30.72MHz clock domain.

2.1 RADIO OVER ETHERNET MAPPER REQUIREMENTS 
The mappers main functionality is to be able to store incoming I/Q data samples from multiple antennas, 
and set a timestamp as the first IQ sample is received for a given RoE packet. The I/Q data samples have to 
be stored until it reaches the size of a RoE packet, which can be up to 1500B, due to the Maximum 
Transmission Unit (MTU) being 1500B for a normal Ethernet frame.  
The packet size has influence on the delay before the first I/Q sample is transmitted, but also the efficiency 
of actual data vs overhead being transmitted in the system. In this design, the packet size is set to 1000B, in 
order to guarantee a low delay, low buffer size and a decent overhead versus actual data ratio. 
Lastly, the mapper should be able to map I/Q samples into a RoE frame and then the RoE frame into the 
Ethernet frame. The RoE header and Ethernet header is described in table 2.

to their flow_id.

30.72MHz, hence the period of the clock is

The received timestamp will have to be synchronized to the 30.72MHz clock domain and then converted

3.1 MAPPER 
As described in the specification of requirements for the mapper, its main functionalities are:  
 
Buffer I/Q samples for each antenna  
 
Set timestamp for each RoE packet  
 
Add Ethernet and RoE headers to packets  
The mapper will use an Altera IP core FIFO [9] for each antenna, to buffer the incoming I/Q data. It will also 
set a timestamp, as the 1st I/Q sample of a packet is received for an antenna, in a FIFO. Hence, for this 
implementation a total of 4*2 = 8 FIFOs is needed to support 4 antennas. Two separate FIFOs per flow_id is 
choosen, as it simplifies the operation required when assembling a RoE packet.  
With this solution, it is just required to read one time from FIFO_PresTime(flow_id) to get the correct 
timestamp for this packet. For the I/Q data, it is required to read  
𝑃𝑐𝑘_𝑙𝑒𝑛𝑔𝑡ℎ
64
 times from FIFO_IQ(flow_id) 
to send a packet.  
 
Another solution which were thought of during the design phase, was to just use one FIFO per flow_id, and 
then make the width of the FIFO = data_bus_width + timestamp_width.  
However, with this solution, the 32bits from the timestamp would only be used every 250 write operation, 
as it takes 250 samples * 32bit in order to fill a packet of 1000Bytes. Due to this, a lot of space in the FIFO 
would be unessecary, as it would not be utilizied.  
The FIFO size is determined in the requirement specification, and for the implementation a size of 512*32 = 
16384 bits is used, which ensures that the FIFO will not become full. 
The FIFO data structure is used as the sequence of data is important, and timestamps and I/Q data should 
come out in the same sequence as it came in. We also assume that the RoE packet will not reorder during 
transmission, hence simplifying the design.  
If a packet were able to reorder during transmission, a different approach and solution is needed.  
The solution should be able to deal with the case where a reordering has occurred, which is something that 
the FIFO memory structure is not good at. For this case, a RAM could be used. It would require a bit more 
logic and complexity to keep track of all the RAM addresses, but could be a viable solution for the 
reordering problem.  
The timestamps come from the 80 bit ToD value, which are converted to a timestamp with a 32-bit format 
according to the 1904.3 draft [3]. The 32-bit timestamp format according to the 1904.3 draft defines the 
30MSB as the nanoseconds value, and the 2LSB defines 0.25ns values.  
Lastly, the mapper needs to be aware of when a new packet starts to get buffered (set presentation time, 
and save it in the FIFO), but also what part of the packet is currently being sent out, so it can add the 
==PAGE:24==

header and indicate Start of Frame and End of Frame to the demapper, but also know when to sent header 
values, and when to sent actual I/Q data. In the implementation, a state machine is used to keep track of 
which part of the packet is being sent out. For the timestamp, a 250 integer counter is used to know when 
to save a new timestamp, as 250*32 = 1000B (packet size). A counter which increments every clock cycle 
can be used, as the datastream is continuously.

The mapper can be divided into two main components, which is needed to realize the above design.  
First a FIFO for buffering the incoming I/Q data is needed. The FIFO will also act as a synchronizer between 
the two clock domains (30.72MHz and 156.25MHz). This component is instantiated 4 times for fulfilling the 
design to handle 4 antennas simultaneously.

A second FIFO for buffering the timestamps is also needed. The timestamps will be read out, when a full 
RoE packet has been buffered in the I/Q data FIFO. As before, this component will also be instantiated 4 
times.  
The second component is a Finite State Machine (FSM) of type Moore, which is responsible for monitoring 
the I/Q data FIFOs, and how many I/Q samples is stored there. The Moore state machine can be defined as 
a statemachine, which output values solely dependant on the current state.  
When a FIFO reaches the size of a RoE packet, the FSM should start the transmission by sending the correct 
header values, and control read signals to the respective FIFOs.  
The two components will be described more thoroughly in the following sub section.  
 
FIFO buffers  
As explained above, two different FIFOs are required per antenna. One for the I/Q samples and one for the 
timestamps. All the FIFOs used in this design, except the one called “Asynchronous FIFO” is made with the 
Altera MegaWizard FIFO, which automatically generates a FIFO with the desired number of memory and  
in/out buswidths for the FIFO. 
First, the FIFO for the I/Q data. It interfaces directly to the JESD204B interface, where the 30.72MHz LTE 
sampling clock transmits the 32 bit I/Q data. The I/Q data comes at a constant rate, hence the write_enable 
signal is per default set to ‘1’.  
The output interface is of width 64 bit, and runs on the system clock which is 156.25MHz. This means, that 
a packet will be read out roughly 10 times faster than it took to write it to the FIFO. The FIFO also sends out 
a “rdused” signal out on the output interface, which is used to determine how many words of length 64 is 
stored in the FIFO. This signal is used in the FSM, to trigger when to start reading out from a FIFO, and 
encapsulate it in a RoE frame. In Figure 3-2,  the FIFO interface and its clock domains can be seen.

occurred, hence the 80-bit presentation time is incremented with one second.

intermediate result in a register and introducing several clock delays.



The demapper will receive RoE packets, and extract relevant header information, such as flow_id, 
packet_length and presentation time.  
The flow_id is used to determine which FIFO the I/Q data and presentation time should be stored in, as 
each antenna has their own FIFO.  
The presentation time, will be synchronized to the 30.72MHz clock domain through a FIFO, as the Time of 
Day signal also lies in this clock domain. After the synchronization, the presentation time can be converted 
to an 80-bit time of day value. The 80-bit value is then buffered in a FIFO, where a check will be done to see 
if the packet should be played out on the antenna. It is converted to an 80-bit Time of Day value, in order to 
compare itself with the ToD from the PTP equipment. 
As the 30.72MHz clock can only guarantee 32ns precision, a solution where 3 additional 30.72MHz clocks 
are generated through an Altera IP core Phase Lock Loop (PLL) [10], each shifted in phase, e.g. 90, 180 and 
270 degrees is presented.   
The presentation time will then be compared with the current Time of Day, and with that result the correct 
phase shifted clock can be chosen, with the help of an Altera IP core Clock Multiplexer [11]. The Clock 
Multiplexer can take up to 4 clocks as input, and chose one of them as output, with the help of a 
clock_select control signal. The Clock Mux ensures a glitch free switchover between clocks, which a normal 
multiplexer would not guarantee.  A description of the clocks used in the component, can be seen in the

Finally, the delay through the system has to be determined in order to guarantee that the presentation 
time will be played out on the antenna with 8ns precision. Especially the delay through the FIFO which is 
driven by the output from the clock mux is important. In order to measure this, the read and write pointer, 
which describes the occupancy in each clock domain, will be synchronized to a clock domain which is 7/8 of 
the 30.72MHz clock. The occupancy can be described as the write_pointer subtracted from the 
read_pointer. This occupancy is measured over 128 clock cycles, and averaged, to find the precise delay 
through the FIFO. The reasoning behind choosing a 7/8 of the original clock to measure the delay, is that it 
guarantees that we measure the occupancy in several phases of one single clock cycle. As it can be seen in 
the simulation and test section, the occupancy can take a sequence of values e.g. ‘3334444’, hence 
measuring with a 7/8 of the original clock frequency, over 128 samples, guarantee us the necessary 
precision on the delay through the FIFO.

3.2.1 Demapper components  
The demapper will be divided into different parts, each responsible for a specific set of tasks which is 
needed in order to fulfill the requirements for the component.  
 
The first part is a Finite State Machine, which is responsible for handling incoming packets, extract relevant 
header information, and forward the specific bits to the correct place. The primary tasks are to extract the 
presentation time and forward it to the synchronization part, and afterwards forward the I/Q data to the 
correct FIFO buffer given the flow_id.  
The second part is the buffering of I/Q data and presentation time. Two FIFOs are used per antenna, in 
order to guarantee that 4 antenna flows can be supported. A register for each antenna is also used, to store 
==PAGE:32==

 Design 
the oldest presentation time, hence it will be able to check that register when to start sending out data in 
order to ensure the timing requirements. The register’s value is the presentation time for the next 1000B in 
the FIFO. 
The third part is a Phase Lock Loop and a Clock Mux. The PLL is responsible for generating 4 outputs which 
are equal to the 30.72MHz clock, in 4 different phases. The clock is shifted 90, 180 and 270 degrees, which 
can guarantee a precision of 
1
30.72𝑀𝐻𝑧
4

The PLL also gives an output which is equal to 7/8 of the 30.72MHz clock. The use of this clock will be 
explained in the Asynchronous FIFO part.  
The Clock Mux is used in order to choose between the four different clocks, and enables a glitch free 
switchover between clocks.  
The fourth and final part is the asynchronous FIFO, which is used in order to determine the correct delay 
through the FIFO. The delay is measured by a read and write pointer. The pointers are incremented every 
time it reads or writes to the FIFO. By synchronizing these two pointers to a third clock domain, and 
subtracting them, the correct FIFO delay can be determined, by averaging over a large number of clock 
cycles.  
 
Demapper Finite State Machine  
The FSM in the demapper, has some similarities to the one used in the mapper component. It is now 
responsible for receiving packets, and forwarding the bits to the correct blocks, in contrast to the mapper 
being responsible for assembling packets and sending them.  
The demapper FSM will extract the length, flow_id and presentation time field from the header, and then 
forward the payload to the correct I/Q data buffer. It will also perform a simple check on the incoming 
packets presentation time, to see if the received presentation time is older than the previous one received 
for this flow_id. If that is the case, an error has occurred, and the packet will be discarded, resulting in a 
packet loss. It can happen if there has been a change in the order of the packet during transmission, but it is 
assumed in the implementation that it will not happen. However, the check is implemented as it is simple 
to do, and if a packet with a newer presentation time is buffered in the FIFO, it could potentially corrupt a 
lot of packets in the FIFO.  
To be able to perform the above actions, 4 states are used: Idle, Eth_Header2, Eth_Header3 and Empt_IQ. 
An overview of the state diagram, the state tasks and the relations can be seen Figure 3-5.

Eth_header3 
This state, will check if the received presentation time is newer than the previous received for this flow_id, 
if that is the case, it will keep the packet and otherwise it’s a packet loss and it moves back to Idle state.

Empt_IQ 
This state is responsible for forwarding all the I/Q data bits to the FIFO responsible for the specific flow_id.  
As the total number of header bits are not divisible with 64, the state will use a similar approach as in the 
mapper FSM. The incoming I/Q data bits will be stored in a register, so that the previous value and the new 
value is accessible at any time.  
The state will distinguish between two scenarios, one where the End of Frame bit is ‘1’ and one where it is 
‘0’. In the case where it is ‘0’ it will take 16 bits from the previous value and 48 from the new value and 
forward to the I/Q data FIFO. The next_state is Empt_IQ.  
In the second case, where EoF = ‘1’ it will also forward the relevant bits to the I/Q data FIFO, but set 
next_state to Idle, as the packet has now been processed and stored, ready to send out to the antenna.

First, the presentation time is synchronized to the 30.72MHz domain, because the ToD value also lies in this 
clock domain, which it should be able to compare itself with. When converting the presentation time, it 
should do a check to see if there has been a wrap-around, e.g. the nanosecond value has surpassed 1*109 = 
1 second. The following pseudo code can describe the check [3]:  
 
diff = timestamp – local_PTP_ToD 
Windowsize = 100000000 
Wrap = ‘0’ 
if abs(diff) > Windowsize then wrap = ‘1’ else wrap = ‘0’ 
end if 
if (diff < 0) then (secondsfield + wrap ) 
else (secondsfield – wrap) 
 
The pseudo code is an example algorithm from the 1904.3 draft standard, Annex B.  
It is implemented in VHDL for the actual implementation. It should be noted that the actual VHDL 
implementation may seem different, as some operations are not doable in hardware.  
The windowsize is a predefined size, which describes a value which is considered “fair” for latency. In this 
specific case, the offset value (100us) is chosen.  
The problem with this algorithm is, that it does not know if a packet is e.g. one, two or three second 
delayed. That scenario is unlikely, but can happen due to badly designed networks or human errors. 
The 1904.3 draft standard, does not specify any solution as of now, to determine how “late” packets can be 
recognized, e.g. several seconds late.  
As the only information carried out by the timestamp is nanoseconds, it does not know what the actual 
second value was at the given time of the timestamp. 
A solution could be to carry another field in the header, with a size of e.g. 2-4 bits. able to represent what 
the second value was at the time of the timestamp. Another solution could be a sequence field, which is 
actually used in some of the CPRI over Ethernet solutions, to be able to ensure all control data being 
received correct, as they are of higher priority than normal data packets for the system.

Asynchronous FIFO  
The asynchronous FIFO is used in order to determine the exact delay through the FIFO. Altera’s FIFO IP 
core, does not support a 3rd clock, hence this FIFO is made manually.  
This is done by synchronizing the read and write pointer to a third clock domain, and measuring the 
occupancy of the FIFO which is given by subtracting the two pointers. The read and write pointer is 
incremented for each read/write operation to the RAM. In order to move the pointers from one clock 
domain to another the following circuit is used:  
 
Figure 3-6 - The circuit used to synchronize read and write pointer [5].  
Source: 34349 – FPGA cource, exercise manual 2. 
The grey code is used in order to ensure that only one bit will change at a time. Furthermore, a series of 
registers is used to achieve synchronization and avoid meta stability.  
The pointer is synchronized to the clk_shifted domain, which is the clock that runs at 7/8 of the 30.72MHz 
clock. By measuring the occupancy of the FIFO with a fraction of the normal clock, a precise value for the 
delay can be given. By doing tests, the accuracy of this method is within an acceptable range of the 
expected value, which will be shown in the simulation section. By choosing another clock fraction, e.g. 
15/16, 31/32 etc. a higher precision could be achieved.  
The delay in the FIFO is then found, by summing up the occupancy over 128 clock cycles, and then 
averaging it. By choosing such a high number of iterations, we get an average value with an acceptable 
precision. The choice of iterations is also because dividing by 128 in binary is just a shift in bits, which is 
easy to do in hardware and lowers the complexity of the circuit. 
As there is a delay in the read signal operation, the sum starts after 10 clock cycles, ensuring that the FIFO 
will reach its ‘balance’. The FIFOs read and write enable signal, is set to always ‘1’ unless full or empty for 
the respective signals, in order to sum over a continuous delay. The delay value for the FIFO is calculated 
after 130 clock cycles from the system start up. This is in a valid time range, as the first packet isn’t received 
before at least 250 clock cycles from system start. The expected delay values should be a natural number x 
+ phase_shift. This means there are 4 possible combinations:

starting at 0. By using this kind of sequence, verification of correct order of the samples received is easily 
seen in Modelsim. When testing the final system, an additional test sequence would be used, in order to 
guarantee that the system handles the MSBs aswell. If the 32-bit test sequence with a incrementation of 
one each clock was to be used, more than a billion clock cycles would be needed, in order to verify the 32th 
bit in test sequence. 
In a real system, a Pseudo Random Binary Sequence (PRBS) would be used to test the system, in order to 
simulate a real data input, and to test as many random inputs as possible for verification.

timespan, in order to ensure the correctness of the sequence and precision of presentation time. 
Parameters such as SA, DA and offset is also set directly in the testbench. Finally, the ToD is generated in 
the testbench aswell, incrementing the time of day for each clock cycle.

 Simulation & Verification 
4.3 FULL SYSTEM TEST  
In this section, a full system test is done, where automated tests will keep track of the sequence of the 
output, that it matches the one generated in the testbench. Furthermore, the test sequence will change so 
all bits in the 32 long sequence are asserted at least once.  
Lastly, the precision time precision will be shown aswell.  
A toplevel view of the test system can be seen in Figure 4-11 below.  
 
Figure 4-11 - Toplevel test system overview 
It is easy to test the setup, as data is generated in the testbench, then it will be mapped into the Radio over 
Ethernet and Ethernet frame, send to a demapper component which unwraps it and store the data until it 
should be send to the analyzer. The analyzer can then see if any packet loss, reordering or wrong 
presentation time happens, as it knows what the data generator (testbench) feeds into the mapper. Instead 
of inspecting waveforms, which are prone to errors, control signals are made which can be inserted if the 
data is valid, and deasserted if not valid, hence you can run the tests over long timespans and check the 
correctness of the data.  
Ideally, a PRBS sequence should be used to test as many inputs as possible, but in this thesis, the focus has 
been on implementing a full functional system, being able to handle 4 antennas with 8ns precision, and not 
on implementing complex test scenarios.  
However, tests will be made where the 32 bit input to the system is split up in 4 byte sequences, where 
each will be incremented seperatly. In this case, it can be guaranteed that all of the 32 bits will be asserted 
at some point. Testing big complex digital systems, is not an easy task, as the possible combinations of 
signals can be in the order of billions.  
As the simulations are quite heavy for an ordinary PC, the time span simulated is about 500us.

Sequence testing 
The first thing to check in the system test, is the order of the data sequence. In the first case where a 32-bit 
sequence which is incremented by one every clock cycle, it is easy to test in the analyzer component.  
As it receives its first sample, it should take that value, and expect the next value to be current_value + 1. If 
this is the case, a “valid” signal is asserted, as seen in Figure 4-12. 
 
Figure 4-12 – Automatic test of data sequence over 540us 
As seen in the figure above, all of the four valid signals stays valid, hence all values received is equal to the 
previous value plus one. The simulation is done with 4 different phases on the flows, and ToD set to 0 from 
system start.  
 
Last, for the test sequence testing, the byte wise incrementing is tested. With this method, all bits get 
asserted at least once in the input, hence testing if there is any problem with the logic handling the MSBs, 
which the other test sequence could not test in these timespans.  
 
Figure 4-13 - Bytewise sequence testing 
In figure Figure 4-13, the bytewise testing can be done. Each byte gets incremented seperatly, hence it will 
only require 255 clock cycles to ensure that all bits in the 32-bit sequence has been asserted at least once. 
Again, this test should ideally use a PRBS sequence, and run for a very long time, in order to cover as many 
combinations of input as possible.

Presentation time precision 
When testing the precision time, the 8ns precision can’t be automated without adding some complexity to 
the analyzer. This is because the Time of Day is only updated every ~32ns, hence it can’t check the phase 
delay precision. However, tests have been made over 500us to check if all presentations time is within 32ns 
of time of day, as a new packet is send. For the correct phase change, the waveforms where the actual 
phase offset of each data out are referred to.  
The tests are using the fact that we have a continuous stream of data coming in after the first packet 
arrives. Hence, the new presentation time will need to be checked at time: 
 
𝑁𝑒𝑤 𝑝𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑒= 𝑜𝑙𝑑 𝑝𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑒𝑛𝑡𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑒+ 8000 
So a counter is needed to keep track of the presentation time, and another counter is needed for when to 
do the actual check of presentation time. The counter which needs to keep track of when to do the check, 
is a simple counter which increments by 1 every clock cycle, and checks makes the presentation time check 
every 250 clock cycle. The value 250 comes from the fact that a packet size is of 8000 bits, and each clock 
sends 32 bit, hence 250 times 32 gives the packet size, 8000. 
 
Figure 4-14 - 500us test period of presentation time 
Referring to Figure 4-14 - 500us test period of presentation timeFigure 4-14, the test sequence can be seen. 
It can verify that each presentation time is presented within 32 ns of ToD, but not the 8 ns precision. 
However, when the waves are expected, it can be seen that each of the inputs to the analyzer are phase 
changed by the correct value.  
The full system tests have shown that the system works correct for timespans of 500us. The correct 
sequence, and presentation time have been proved, however, the presentation time precision of 8ns did 
not get tested by the automated tests. A possible solution to this, could be to make a new clock which runs 
with a period 4 times shorter than 30.72, which a parallel Time of Day value resides in, incrementing by 8 ns 
per period. Then the analyzer inputs would have to be synchronized to this domain, hence making the

 Future work 
6 FUTURE WORK 
If more time were available, several improvements and new features could be investigated for the RoE 
mapper/demapper implementation.  
First, more exceptions could be handled, e.g. handling out of order packets, detecting “late” packets which 
is defined as more than 1 second late. This would make the implementation more flexible, and closer to a 
solution which could be used in a real network. The core design would need to change, in order to support 
this feature.  
 
Second, the support of an even more accurate precision time could be implemented. As the RoE draft 
standard supports timestamps with a 1/4ns precision, this could be a subject for further research, and 
which possibilities that are available without adding to much complexity, as the price should not be too 
high in the RRH. The more accurate timestamp is something that is actively researched, in the 802.1CM 
standard [12], for time sensitive networking for fronthaul. It is just a matter of time, before future network 
applications require this kind of timing, in order to support new services.  
 
Last, the implementation should be implemented on a FPGA, with real network tests. This is something that 
could be very valueable, as results in the Modelsim simulation can be different compared to when it is 
implemented on an actual FPGA, where things as wiredelay and timing constraints can be a problem. If the 
timing constraints can’t be met, pipelining of the critical paths would be needed.  
In this specific case, the Quartus report shows that the maximum frequency of the clk_sys circuit is 
50.74Mhz, hence an improvement to this would be an important step for fulfilling the 10Gbps requirement.

 Conclusion 
7 CONCLUSION 
In this thesis, a requirement specification has been presented, with origin from the IEEE 1904.3 draft 
standard and CPRI standard, and a design was made according to these requirements.  
The design was split in two main componenets, a mapper and a demapper, and each of these component 
split in smaller sub parts required in order to ensure the desired functionalities. A long with explanations of 
each part, arguments justifying the given solution was given.  
In some parts of the describtion of the components, alternative solutions were given which had other 
advantages or disadvantages and arguments where made for why one was chosen over the other.  
After designing the componenets, each component was succesfully tested and verified in Modelsim with 
waveforms, to check if the component acted as expected.  
Last, the components were succesfully tested as a combined system, with the help of a testbench and a 
data analyzer, which could guarantee that the data sequences and presentation times were correct over a 
longer time span. The testbench provided different kind of input test sequences, in order to test different 
input combinations. The results were satisfying, as they showed what was expected. However, the 8 ns 
precision on presentation time, was not tested over a long time span, but instead waveforms inspected 
several times to ensure it worked.  
Lastly, the system was synthesized in Quartus II, and it was able to present a synthesis report and timing 
constraints. It was shown that the Clk_sys could not deliver 10Gbps in the given design, and improvements 
will need to be done in order to meet this requirement. All other requirements were fulfilled, according to 
the requirement specification.
