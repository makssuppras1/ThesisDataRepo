Abstract
Metabolic dysregulation is a critical factor in the pathogenesis of neurodegenerative diseases, particu-
larly Alzheimer’s disease. Early detection and intervention are pivotal, yet current diagnostic methods
remain limited. This thesis explores the implementation and evaluation of Proton Observed Carbon
Edited (POCE) spectroscopy for identifying and quantifying metabolites in complex biological sam-
ples, focusing on glucose metabolism in the cerebral cortex using 13C-labelled glucose as a tracer for
ﬂux studies.
A POCE spectroscopy pulse sequence was developed and implemented. Issues such as decoupling and
water suppression were addressed and solved, in order to optimise the technique for quantiﬁcation
and identiﬁcation of metabolites.
The POCE pulse sequence was tested on the identiﬁcation of compounds in a constructed test sam-
ple. Identiﬁcation proved possible and aspects such as multiplicity and coupling constants assisted in
identiﬁcation. Diﬀerent amounts of scans was also tested and it was found that it made no signiﬁcant
diﬀerence with regards to ease of identiﬁcation.
Further validation of POCE spectroscopy was conducted using mice brain astrocyte cell extract sam-
ples. Though the identiﬁcation of metabolites in actual biological samples proved more challenging,
the substrate and metabolites were identiﬁed. The performance of 500 MHz and 800 MHz spectrom-
eters was assessed to understand the impact of magnetic ﬁeld strength on metabolite identiﬁcation.
Results demonstrated that POCE spectroscopy could eﬀectively distinguish labelled metabolites,
showing potential advantages over traditional methods in terms of experiment time. However, a no-
table issue was identiﬁed in the form of a signal strength diﬀerence between the spectra with and
without the inversion pulse, posing a challenge for precise quantiﬁcation. Despite this, the relative
quantiﬁcation of metabolites like hydroxybutyric acid was promising, indicating the method’s poten-
tial utility.
The ﬁndings indicate that POCE spectroscopy is a viable technique for metabolic ﬂux studies, pre-
senting an attractive alternative to HSQC due to its shorter experiment times and theoretical simpler
quantiﬁcation, though further research is needed to actually use POCE spectra quantitatively.

Resumé
Metabolisk dysregulering spiller en væsentlig rolle i udviklingen af neurodegenerative hjernesygdomme
såsom Alzheimers sygdom. Dette projekt undersøger anvendeligheden af POCE spektroskopi til iden-
tiﬁkation og kvantiﬁcering af metabolitter i komplekse biologiske prøver, med særligt fokus på hjernens
energimetabolisme.
I projektet blev en POCE pulssekvens, udviklet i samarbejde med Jens Christian Madsen fra Bruker,
implementeret og optimeret. Optimeringen omfattede justeringer af dekoblingsstyrke og presaturation
af vandsignalet.
Først blev metoden evalueret på baggrund af identiﬁkation af testprøver, formuleret med 13C mærkede
molekyler. Bagefter blev den også testet på metabolit ekstrakter af astrocytter fra musehjerner inku-
beret med 13C-mærket glucose. Identiﬁkation af molekylerne i både testprøverne og de biologiske
prøver, var mulig og relativ uproblematisk.
Kvantitativ analyse af POCE spektre afslørede en forskel i signalstyrken mellem spektre med og uden
inversionspuls. Selvom absolut kvantiﬁcering ikke var mulig, viste den relative kvantiﬁcering lovende
resultater, idet at forholdet mellem de forskellige typer af protoner i 3HB passede. Dette fremhæver
behovet for yderligere undersøgelser af acquisition parametre og pulssekvensen for forhåbentligt at
opnå pålidelig kvantitativ analyse i fremtiden.
Sammenligningen mellem POCE spektroskopi og NMR-teknikker, såsom 1D 1H spektroskopi og HSQC
spektroskopi, viste, at POCE spektroskopi har fordele i kortere eksperimenttider og teoretisk simplere
kvantiﬁcering. Dog gjorde POCE spektroskopis dårligere spektrale opløsning at identiﬁkation af nogle
metabolitter var sværere end i et HSQC spektrum.
Det konkluderes, at POCE spektroskopi er anvendelig til identiﬁkation af metabolitter i komplekse
biologiske prøver. Teknikkens hurtigere eksperimenttider er en fordel og kan, med yderligere optimer-
ing, og måske særligt i kombination med andre NMR teknikker, blive et robust værktøj til metaboliske
ﬂux-studier. Det er dog nødvendigt at adressere forskellen i signalstyrken mellem de to subspektre i
yderligere forsking.

Introduction
Metabolic dysregulation plays a signiﬁcant role in the pathogenesis of neurodegenerative brain diseases
such as Alzheimer’s disease. The progression of Alzheimer’s disease is gradual, unfolding over many
years. Although the mechanisms underlying the evolution of the disease are largely unknown, it is
well documented that brain metabolism in a sick patient deviates from that of a healthy person
early in the process, long before symptoms of cognitive impairment and memory loss occur [1–4].
Speciﬁcally, the metabolism of glucose in the cerebral cortex has been linked to the illness [5–7].
Enhanced understanding of brain metabolism is therefore useful in the early detection of Alzheimer’s
disease and could lead to earlier diagnosis and even treatment of the disease [8, 9].
When approaching the subject of studying brain metabolism, many techniques can be applied de-
pending on the scope of the investigation. The ﬁrst concern is whether to proceed in vivo or in vitro.
In vivo, methods such as magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS) and positron emission tomography
(PET) are often used [10, 11]. Both are non-invasive imaging techniques and rely on the injection of a
contrast molecule to visualize the metabolism of a body part or organism. In vitro techniques are also
plentiful. The most commonly used method is mass spectrometry (MS), often in combination with a
separation technique such as liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS) or gas chromatog-
raphy–mass spectrometry (GC-MS) [11–14]. However, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) has several
advantages over MS. It requires minimal sample preparation, the samples are not destroyed during
acquisition, and it is inherently quantitative [15]. NMR spectroscopy is a broad term encompassing
a vast array of experiments. The standard NMR experiment used for quantiﬁcation in metabolomics
is 1D 1H spectroscopy. However, this method lacks compatibility with isotopic tracers, rendering it
irrelevant for investigations focusing on speciﬁc pathways or enzymatic processes, which are crucial for
brain energy metabolism research. Heteronuclear single-quantum correlation (HSQC) spectroscopy is
compatible with isotopic tracers and is already an established method for metabolite identiﬁcation,
demonstrating quantiﬁability even in complex mixtures [16, 17]. New studies are using proton ob-
served carbon edited (POCE) spectroscopy as well, even though it has previously been used only
in vivo and not quantitatively [18–20]. POCE spectroscopy has lower experiment times than HSQC,
which is favorable when handling delicate biological samples and also less expensive in spectrometer
time usage. Therefore, exploring POCE spectroscopy, comparing it to established standards such as
HSQC and 1D 1H spectroscopy, and assessing its usage for metabolite identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation
in ﬂux studies would be valuable eﬀorts.
This project involves implementing and testing POCE spectroscopy, as a novel technique for brain
energy metabolite quantiﬁcation. The eﬃcacy of POCE spectroscopy is evaluated by comparing the

1.1
Project aim
The aim of this project is to investigate the applicability of POCE spectroscopy for metabolite iden-
tiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation in complex biological samples with a labelled substrate. To succeed with
this aim I have the following objectives:
• Optimize experimental parameters for maximizing the eﬀectiveness of POCE spectroscopy in
metabolite proﬁling and quantiﬁcation.
• Address the challenges and advantages of POCE spectroscopy with regards to identifying and
quantifying metabolites in biological samples.
• Compare the performance of POCE spectroscopy with established NMR techniques, such as 1D
1H spectroscopy and HSQC spectroscopy, in terms of metabolite identiﬁcation and quantiﬁca-
tion.

Background
Given that the primary objective of this project is to identify metabolites within complex biological
samples, it would be valuable to have an understanding of the expected metabolites in such sam-
ples. Therefore, background knowledge of brain energy metabolism is advantageous. This section is
dedicated to exploring brain glucose metabolism and the techniques employed to investigate it. Sub-
sequently, the background knowledge required to understand the NMR spectroscopy of this project
is elaborated upon, providing the theoretical foundation necessary for comprehending the methods
employed.
2.1
Brain energy metabolism
The brain accounts for 25% of the human body’s glucose consumption, even though it only amounts to
2% of the total body weight [21]. Glucose is the main energy substrate of the brain. It is metabolised
ﬁrst through the glycolysis, which takes place in all cells, and yields two molecules of pyruvate and two
ATP.

2.1.1
Biomarkers
A biomarker is a measurable medical sign or set of signs,
that indicate a disease in an organism [11]. A medical sign
is not the same as a symptom, since a sign is not detectable
by the patient themselves [24]. Because of this, a biomarker
may, in theory, be found well before a patient is diagnosed
by symptoms. This is why a biomarker is so valuable, if working towards an earlier diagnosis of a
disease.
As an example, Alzheimer’s disease is closely related to brain metabolism, so a biomarker might be
possible to ﬁnd in the metabolome of the brain. It could be both the presence or absence of a certain

2.2
Techniques for studying metabolism
Studying metabolism can be approached in two primary ways: targeted and untargeted, also called
metabonomics and metabolomics, respectively. Both ﬁelds focus on the metabolism of organisms, cells,
or organs, but they diﬀer in scope and objective. Metabolomics aims to identify and quantify the entire
metabolome of these biological systems, while metabonomics investigates the metabolic response to
speciﬁc biological stimuli [26]. However, these terms are often used interchangeably.
Metabolomics involves comprehensive proﬁling of all metabolites in the chosen organism. MS is fre-
quently employed for this purpose due to its high sensitivity and resolution [12, 27, 28]. Despite
the advantages of MS, NMR spectroscopy is a valuable technique in targeted metabolomics, where
speciﬁc pathways or enzymatic processes are examined. Unlike MS, which primarily provides atomic
mass and fragmentation patterns, NMR can oﬀer detailed information about nuclear environments,
chemical surroundings, and neighboring atoms. Additionally, NMR often requires minimal sample
preparation.
An important aspect of studying brain energy metabolism is not just the choice of analytical method
but also the selection of an appropriate model system. Human brain samples are diﬃcult to obtain,
making it necessary to use model organisms. These model systems are designed to replicate the symp-
toms and characteristics of the human brain, allowing researchers to study metabolic mechanisms that
can then be translated to human physiology.

2.3
Background of NMR
This section explores the fundamental principles of NMR, focusing on the elements needed to under-
stand the pulse sequences and experimental designs of this project. It covers the key concepts of NMR
from the nucleus to the NMR spectrum. By understanding the background of NMR, it is possible to
see the reasons for the results and the challenges of the experiments further in this project.
2.3.1
Spin and Larmor frequency
NMR utilises the fact that atomic spins can be aligned in a magnetic ﬁeld, and that the frequency of
their precessing vary depending on their chemical environment. Spin is an intrinsic quantum value and
possesses an angular momentum: a charge and a motion of this charge. This makes spins inherently
magnetic, which is why it is possible to align atomic spins in an external magnetic ﬁeld, which in
NMR is denoted B0 [29].

often denoted a radio frequency (rf) pulse.

The vector model of NMR
To understand what happens on an nuclear level in NMR, a vector model can be taken into use. This
is pictured in ﬁgure 2 [31].
Figure 2: The vector model of the spins in the magnetic ﬁeld [31]
B0 is the applied magnetic ﬁeld, and is aligned with the z-axis. As seen with the vector on the right
side of the ﬁgure, all the nuclei precess with their Larmor frequency around the z-axis. The vector
in the vector model represents the mean of all the spins in the magnetic ﬁeld. In reality a little over

system along the negative part of the z-axis, but still precessing with their Larmor frequency.

When the bulk magnetisation is perturbed by an rf pulse, the magnetisation will be transferred the
denoted angle of that pulse. Almost all NMR experiments contain a 90°pulse at some point, which will
transfer the bulk magnetisation into the x-y plane. This is useful since only the magnetisation in the
x-y plane will be visible in the ﬁnal spectrum. However a 90° pulse does not mean that all the spins
are shifted into the x-y plane. Since spin states are quantum states this is not possible, they can only
be aligned with the magnetic ﬁeld or perpendicular to it. What it does mean, is that half of the excess
low energy spins, that are aligned with the external magnetic ﬁeld, are shifted into the high energy
state, which is that perpendicular to the B0. This results in a 90° shift of the bulk magnetisation.
2.3.3
Relaxation
Spin relaxation is the rate at which the spins decay towards the bulk magnetisation after excitation.
Spin relaxation happens in two major ways: longitudinal and transverse relaxation. The time at which
these two relations occur are called T1 and T2, respectively. Relaxation times are extremely important
for quantitative NMR, since the recovery of signal along the Z axis is often done just before transferring
the magnetisation into the x-y plane and acquiring. That means, that if the bulk magnetisation is not
recovered fully along the z-axis, before a 90° pulse is applied, the signal acquired will not correspond
to the actual amount of spins and therefore nuclei in the sample.
Diﬀerent factors contribute to relaxation. The most contributing factors, especially for small molcules,
are the spins from the nuclei closely bound [32]. Since spins are small magnetic ﬁelds, they aﬀect one
another. As the molecule tumbles in the liquid solution the position of the spins with regard to each
other will vary and therefore induce relaxation.
Longitudinal relaxation
Longitudinal relaxation, as the name implies, is the relaxation that returns the bulk magnetisation to
the z-axis, after a rf pulse. When an rf pulse is applied, it excites some of the spins of the low energy
state into the high energy state. As they relax back towards the equilibrium and the z-axis, this energy
is transferred to the surroundings as heat. However, the temperature change is to small to detect in the
sample, but it is the origin of another name for longitudinal relaxation which is spin-lattice relaxation
[31].
The T1 of a nuclei is tied closely to its chemical properties. The T1 is important for quantitative NMR,
since recovery along the z-axis is imperative for quantiﬁcation of an NMR spectrum. Since chemically
diﬀerent nuclei have diﬀerent T1’s, it is important to set the relaxation times to an appropriate length,
often chosen as 5 times the typical T1, which recovers 99.3% of net magnetisation [31].

Transverse relaxation
Transverse relaxation is the spreading out of signal in the transverse plane. Often denoted as the spins
"fanning out". T1 is always longer than T2 because longitudinal relaxation contributes to the decay of
transverse magnetization. T2 measures the time it takes for the spins to fully dephase, which cannot
occur once they are aligned with the z-axis and have returned to thermal equilibrium [31]. This also
makes the T2 less important for quantitative NMR, since if the delay is enough to fully relax the spins
along the z-axis, there is no transverse relaxation.

To generate a spectrum of two dimensions, data sampling based on two time variables is necessary.
Preparation and mixing is the combination of pulses and delays that makes up the experiment. Evo-
lution is the waiting period, that varies with each scan, thus creating the second dimension, where the
time variable is t1. Detection is the acquisition of the signal and is pictured by the FID, and creates
the ﬁrst dimension in the same way as with a 1D experiment. The diﬀerences of the t1 allows for the
detection of the diﬀerences between the couplings to other nuclei of the spins, and is what generates
the second dimension, which is always about the coupling of two nuclei [31].
The FID and fourier transformation
The FID is the signal that the receiver coil of the spectrometer obtains. The receiver coil is, as is clear
from the name, a coil in which it is possible to induce an electric current. As is known from basic
electromagnetism anything with a magnetic moment is able to induce a current. The receiver coil thus
collects an electrical current as a result of the oscillating signal of the spins [31].
The FID is a signal in the time domain, which means that it is a function where the variable is time - in
most cases miliseconds. It is a sinusoidal wave, which also means that it can be seen as a combination
of many sinusoidal waves, which it is. It is the product of frequencies of all the spins. The FID decays
dependent on the T1 as it is with the rate of T1 that the spins return to the z-axis and therefore no
signal remain in the x-y plane.
The FID is converted from the time domain into the frequency domain via Fourier transformation.
Fourier transformation is basically reversing the process of combining all of the spins, and taking apart
the FID into all the diﬀerent sinusoidal waves of the spins. This is what gives the NMR spectrum
which ideally has a distinguishable frequency for each chemically unique nuclei.

Spin coupling
A single type of nuclei and therefore spin, will not always have only a single peak in the spectrum.
This is due to the fact that spins aﬀect spins that are close by. As mentioned in section 2.3.3, this

leads to relaxation, but it also results in spin coupling.
Spin coupling is the change in the frequency of a spin, due to another spin close by. As an example
we can consider two spins close to each other in a molecule, A and B. Spin B is from a CH group, so
there is only two options for that spin - aligned or perpendicular to the B0. The spin will be aligned
with the magnetic ﬁeld in slightly more than half of the molecules. That means that the spin A, will
experience a slightly heightened magnetic ﬁeld if the spin of B is aligned with the magnetic ﬁeld,
and a slightly decreased magnetic ﬁeld if B is perpendicular in the magnetic ﬁeld. Thus the spin A is
deshielded by B in half of the molecules and shielded by B in the other half. This leads to a splitting of
the signal into a doublet, with the downﬁeld peak being due to the B spin aligned with the magnetic
ﬁeld and the upﬁeld peak arising from the perpendicular spin B. When spin B is a more proton rich
functional group, spin A will split into more complex peaks. The number of peaks follows the n+1n+1
rule, where nn is the number of neighbouring protons. The coupling constant, which is the distance
in Hz between these peaks, provides information about the spatial relationship between the coupled
protons in the molecule [31].
Decoupling
Decoupling is the practice of applying a pulse during acquisition to prevent the observed nucleus
from coupling with a chosen other nucleus [33]. The most common approach involves applying a pulse
at the frequency of the second nucleus to ensure that the observed nucleus does not couple to it.
This technique is widely used in almost all 1D 13C spectra to decouple protons, since multiplicity
is not required to identify peaks in a 1D 13C NMR spectra, because the spectral width is large
enough that the positions of the peaks are suﬃcient for identiﬁcation. In contrast, 1D 1H spectra
of organic compounds typically do not require decoupling. This is because other common nuclei in
organic chemistry do not frequently have spin-active isotopes, as shown in Table 1. For instance, in
a 1D 1H spectrum, the coupling to the 1.1% of 13C atoms will still be present but will only account
for 1.1% of a peak equivalent to one proton. However, in the spectrum of a labeled compound, where
a higher percentage of carbon is 13C, decoupling should be applied to obtain an easily interpretable
spectrum.
Diﬀerent decoupling approaches can be taken [31]. The most common method, and the one used in this
project, is broadband decoupling during acquisition. This involves applying a relatively broad pulse
through the carbon channel during acquisition, thereby eliminating the proton couplings to carbon.

Integrals and internal standards
Since the amount of signal is directly related to the amount of spins, NMR is inherently quantitative
[31]. The integrals of the NMR spectrum are essential to quantiﬁcation, since the size of the integrals is
directly correlated to the amount of spins precessing. Since the processing of a NMR spectrum aﬀects
the integrals, it is important for quantiﬁcation that it is done the same way with every spectrum, if
the aim is to compare between spectra.
Furthermore, for quantiﬁcation to be possible, either an internal standard or a standard curve is
needed. An internal standard is a known amount of a chosen substance added to the samples. An
ideal internal standard fulﬁls certain criteria: it does not interfere with the sample and it has peaks
in a region of the spectrum that is relatively free of other peaks, to ensure that the integral of the
standard is only due to nuclei of that compound. International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry
(IUPAC) has a guide to internal standards in NMR in which sodium trimethylsilylpropanesulfonate
(DSS) is recommended for aqueous solutions.
Spectral resolution and signal to noise
The spectral resolution is deﬁned as the minimum frequency diﬀerence between two peaks in a spec-
trum that can be diﬀerentiated [31]. The spectral resolution is directly related to how long the FID
is acquired, since the ﬁne structure of peaks in an NMR spectrum lies in the end of the FID. This
is also why "zero-ﬁlling" of the FID is often used to increase the resolution of the ﬁne structure [31].
Zero-ﬁlling is digitally adding zeros at the end of the FID and thus artiﬁcially increasing the amount
of time the spectrum was acquired for, and therefore the ﬁne structure in the spectrum.
The signal to noise ratio (SNR) of a NMR spectrum is the ratio between the average peak height in
the spectrum and the standard deviation of the noise height in the baseline. The SNR is often used
as a way to evaluate how easily interpretable an NMR spectrum is.
The water signal and solvent presaturation
With some samples and NMR experiments, it can be useful to try to eliminate the water signal. In
biological samples it can be hard to bypass the strong water signal, due to the high content of water or
compounds with exchangeable protons in cells and other biological ﬂuids. However, some measures can
be taken to minimise the water signal in the ﬁnal NMR spectrum. One of them is solvent presaturation,
which is a tried and tested technique, that is a standard in many NMR experiments [10]. It is the
application of a pulse at the exact frequency of the water protons, to saturate and suppress the
water signal. Since this pulse will also suppress other frequencies around the water signal, it makes
quantiﬁcation harder, since signals are aﬀected by it. Therefore, if the aim is to quantify a spectrum

2.3.5
Isotopic tracers
Since only some nuclei are spin-active and therefore detectable in the NMR spectrum, certain atoms
in a molecule can be replaced with spin-active nuclei, eﬀectively spiking the natural concentration of
that molecule with a tracer nuclei. If the molecule undergoes any chemical transformations, the new
positions of the spin-active nuclei can be observed in the NMR spectrum. While many atoms have
naturally abundant nuclei that are also NMR active, making them less useful for tracer applications,
this is not true for all. The natural abundance, gyromagnetic ratio, and spins for some nuclei are
shown in Table 1. The spins and natural abundance of these nuclei highlight why 13C is an ideal
isotopic tracer. 13C is spin-active and thus visible in the NMR spectrum, but it has a very low
natural abundance. In contrast, the most abundant carbon isotope, 12C, is not spin-active and remains
invisible in the NMR spectrum. Therefore, when an isotopic tracer with 13C is used, the resulting NMR
spectrum predominantly reﬂects the 13C-labelled molecules. In a spectrum, only 1.1% of the carbon
signal would come from natural carbon, with the remaining signal originating from the 13C-labelled
tracer.
The use of isotopic tracers is valuable when studying speciﬁc metabolic pathways or time frames in
an experiment. By labelling a tracer molecule and then introducing it into an organism or cells, it
becomes possible to track the tracer through the system and observe which metabolites it integrates
into. This process, combined with a suitable NMR experiment that highlights the labelled nucleus,
allows for precise monitoring of metabolic processes [11].
It is both possible to label speciﬁc positions in the isotopic tracer, and label the molecule fully. These
approaches have diﬀerent purposes and advantages. A speciﬁc labelling might allow for elucidation of
how a speciﬁc enzymatic process changes a metabolite. If only labelling in one position takes place,
it is then possible to follow that speciﬁc atom, whereas if all positions are labelled, there is no way to
distinguish between the diﬀerent atoms. However, full labelling of the tracer allows for the detection
of the complete metabolism of the tracer. All metabolites will then be visible in the spectrum - at
least all metabolites to feature that nuclei.

3
Methods
Understanding the evolution of NMR techniques for quantifying metabolism provides essential con-
text for choosing the right experiment. This section investigates the evolution of NMR quantiﬁcation
methods, with a particular focus on techniques compatible with metabolic ﬂux studies.
Afterwards the relevant NMR experiments used in this project to identify and quantify metabolites
within complex biological samples, are presented and described. The selection process for an appro-
priate NMR experiment that aligns with the use of an isotopic tracer, facilitating the investigation of
glucose metabolism in the cerebral cortex is then detailed.
3.1
Development of NMR techniques for quantifying metabolism
To implement and understand a new method for quantifying metabolites in complex biological mix-
tures, it is essential to investigate the established techniques that have preceded it. Throughout the
history of NMR techniques for quantifying metabolism, several methods have consistently stood out
in the literature.
The ﬁrst to approach the subject of quantifying any type of NMR were Jungnickel and Forbes in 1963
[34]. They quantiﬁed proton signals in 26 diﬀerent compounds with a relative standard deviation from
theory of 0.3% for triplets. The NMR spectra were acquired on a 60 MHz spectrometer. Jungnickel
and Forbes did not use an internal standard, but instead quantiﬁed each proton signal as a percent-
age of total protons in the molecule, which is relative quantiﬁcation. They concluded that the small
deviations from theory were due to impurities in the sample, and that under optimal conditions the
results would be accurate to within a few tenths of 1% of the total hydrogen content. This paper laid
the groundwork for later quantiﬁcation, as it was clear that with the small addition of an internal
standard, this method would be able to do absolute quantiﬁcation of these compounds.
Later that same year, 1963, and in that same journal, Analytical Chemistry, D.P. Hollis was able to
quantify mixtures of aspirin, caﬀeine and phenaticine [35]. Hollis used an external standard, which
means that he obtained a proton spectrum, with all the same acquisition parameters, of a sample
containing a known concentration of caﬀeine right after the original spectrum. He then quantiﬁed
the spectrum of the mixture by comparing the integrals with that of the known caﬀeine sample. The
result was quantiﬁcation of aspirin, caﬀeine and phenaticine with a standard deviation from reality of
respectively 1.1%, 3.2% and 2.2%.
So quantiﬁcation of NMR began with 1D 1H spectroscopy. NMR spectroscopy was advanced in the
1970’s with the development of fourier transformed NMR and higher magnetic ﬁelds of the spectrom-
eters [10]. This progress in the ﬁeld of NMR made it possible to analyze more complex mixtures,

When examining the history of qNMR in metabolomics, it is clear that the most used method, espe-

Throughout the 00’s several developments in software methods, made the use of NMR in metabolomics
more relevant than ever. Xi et. al. developed a model in 2008, that identiﬁed metabolites in complex
mixtures using HSQC experiments and speciﬁcally the intensity data from these experiments [39].
Reynolds et. al. and Crockford et. al. made algorithms that could quantify peaks in a 1D 1H NMR
spectrum [40, 41]. These developments made it easier to quantify spectra in the same way across
experiments. Heikkinen et. al. showed a quantitative HSQC experiment in 2002 that works on com-
plex mixtures [17]. It was not on a metabolite sample though, but on wood lignin, a complex polymer
originating from, in this case, spruce wood. However, this development makes it clear that HSQC spec-
troscopy shows promise for complex mixtures in general, which includes metabolite extracts. Wolfram
Gronwald showed in 2008 that metabolites in urin samples could be reliably quentiﬁed using HSQC
spectroscopy [42]. These two developments in the 00’s laid the groundwork for later use of HSQC
experiments in metabolite identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation.
As is clear from this literature review, 1D 1H spectroscopy is the standard when quantifying metabo-
lites in complex mixtures, such as a brain cell metabolite extracts. However, a regular 1D 1H spectrum
is not compatible with an isotopic tracer. Therefore the isotopic tracer compatible HSQC experiment,
that has also been shown to be both able to identify metabolites in complex mixtures and quantify
them, is a good alternative. However the POCE NMR experiment has, in theory, a lot of advantages
over HSQC, especially with regards to shorter experiment times and easier quantiﬁable spectrum
acquisition.

3.2.1
The POCE pulse sequence
The pulse sequence used in this project is depicted in ﬁgure 7 and is modelled after pulse sequences
described by McNair et. al. in 2022 and Henry et. al. in 2006 [20, 44]. The pulse sequence begins with
relaxation delay of 25 s. This is traditionally not depicted in the schematic pulse sequence, and thus is
not shown in 7. The protons are then excited by a 90° pulse, to transfer them into the x-y plane. After
waiting half a second divided by a typical 1JC-H coupling, a 180° pulse is applied in the proton channel.
In the inversion spectrum a 180° pulse is applied in the carbon channel as well. This pulse selectively
excites protons attached to 13C. After waiting another half second divided by a typical 1JC-H, in
order for the magnetisation from the carbons to transfer to the protons, the spectrum is acquired
with broadband decoupling. This pulse sequence results in half of the spectra to have inverted peaks
for the protons attached to 13C. Subtracting these two types of spectra, also called editing, results in
the diﬀerence spectrum, which contains only the protons bound to 13C at double intensity. The three
diﬀerent spectra can be seen in ﬁgure 6.

3.2.2
Quantifying POCE spectroscopy
The important aspects to consider, when striving to quantify 1D 1H NMR experiments, are the
relaxation delay and the 90° pulse. The 90° pulse is what transfers the net magnetisation into the x-y
plane, and thus makes the magnetisation visible. Therefore, if the pulse is not up to par, not all of
the magnetisation will be visible and therefore it will lead to a lower signal intensity, than what is
actually in the sample. The same is true for the relaxation delay. If the delay is not long enough, not
all of the net magnetisation is recovered along the z-axis, and therefore not all of it will be transferred
into the x-y plane with the next 90° pulse. A value of 5·T1 for the relaxation delay of d1 recovers
99.3% of the net magnetisation, which is used in most NMR experiments.
An important aspect of quantitative POCE spectroscopy, that is not necessary to consider with regular
1D 1H NMR, is the 180° pulse via the carbon channel. It needs to fully and selectively excite the protons
bound to a 13C. This is dependent on the delay after the inversion pulse being the right length. It needs
to be long enough for magnetisation transfer between the 13C and the attached proton(s), however
not that long that it transfers further out into the spin system. This has previously been shown to be
a challenge by Henry et. al. in 2006 [44]. They showed longer couplings in a POCE spectrum of [3-13C]
glutamate, speciﬁcally the protons at C4. This could be a problem for interpreting a POCE spectrum,
with regards to labelling, since the position of the 13C is important for identiﬁcation of a metabolite.
However, if quantiﬁcation takes place via. the right peak, the protons attached to an actually labelled
carbon, it should not be a problem with magnetisation transfer further than intended. This is because,
it can only transfer internally in an already labelled compound, so the worst thing that can happen
is a peak, from protons not attached to a labelled carbon showing up. As Henry et. al. showed in
the paper, the peak would be at considerably lower intensity than the directly coupled protons [44].
But it is important to keep in mind, when considering the noise and artefacts in the POCE spectra,
especially at lower ﬁelds, as strong coupling eﬀects are more prominent at lower ﬁelds.
3.3
1H-13C-HSQC spectroscopy
HSQC is a 2D NMR experiment that shows one-bond correlations, between two diﬀerent nuclei, of-
ten 1H and 13C. The HSQC spectrum thus contains two chemical shifts for each peak, one for each
nucleus.
HSQC spectra has been used as a way to identify metabolites as mentioned in section 3.1, and will
therefore serve as standard in this project to compare POCE spectroscopy against.
A HSQC spectrum can be many things, depending on on the pulse sequence and acquisition parame-

The biological samples
The biological samples were metabolite extracts of astrocytes isolated from mice brains. They were
prepared by Laura Escudé in the following way: The cerebellum from a mouse brain is removed

before incubation with isotopically labelled tracer glucose takes place. The samples were incubated
with either [U-13C6]-D-Glucose, [U-13C3]-Pyruvate or both [U-13C3]-Pyruvate and unlabelled glucose.

which was incubated with D-Glucose-13C6 for 1 hour. The incubation was terminated by removing
the incubation medium and then placing the cells on ice and washing with ice cold PBS. The water
soluble metabolites were extracted extracted upon by adding ice cold ethanol, scraping the cells up and
centrifuging. The supernatant was then freeze dried, before D2O phosphate buﬀer was added. These
samples contain the products that the astrocytes managed to produce from the labelled substrate

5.1
Implementation of a POCE pulse sequence
A POCE pulsesequence is not included in the standard Bruker pulse catalogue, making it necessary
to program it for this project. Jens Christian Madsen from Bruker provided assistance by coding a
POCE pulse sequence. The code for this custom sequence is available in Appendix A, and the pulse
sequence is illustrated in Figure 7 in Section 3.2, where it is also described.
For any NMR experiment the pulse sequence is only half of the development, the acquisition param-
eters making up the other half. Jens Christian Madsen provided some instructions for the acquisition
parameters along with the pulse sequence, which was used as a starting point for setting up the ex-
periment. The setup of the speciﬁc acquisition parameters will depend on the spectrometer and the
sample.
5.1.1
Decoupling
One of the challenges with the POCE pulse sequence turned out to be the strength of decoupling.
Decoupling, as described in section 2.3.4, is, in this case, a continuously applied broadband rf pulse
during acquisition. This can lead to a heating of the sample and the probe, since it is basically just
energy applied directly to it. However, since decoupling is also very important in an already crowded
1D spectrum, the perfect middleground between no decoupling and heating needed to be found. Jens
Christian Madsen wrote in the pulse program, that if using broadband decoupling, the acquisition time
and thus the decoupling was supposed to be kept below 50 ms. However, keeping the acquisition time
below 50 ms was not really a viable option, since the strength of decoupling needed would then be too
high. POCE spectra with diﬀerent strengths of decoupling was run on ﬁrst the 800 MHz spectrometer
to determine the right approach. The diﬀerence between successful and lacking decoupling is shown
in ﬁgure 10. The ﬁgure depicts the alanine methyl group of the test sample. In the blue spectrum the
protons are coupled not just to the alpha proton, but also the 13C they are directly bound to. The

5.1.2
Saturation of the water signal
The water signal is prominent in the POCE spectra. Water signals will always be prominent in proton
spectra, but since the aim is to quantify the POCE spectra, the water signal is an even greater
challenge. This is because it usually messes with the baseline of the spectrum, which is important
to keep even, in order to quantify the integrals. Especially if, as in POCE, some signals are negative
and some are positive, this is important, since the large water signal in the middle of the spectrum
obscures the baseline, with such a degree that quantiﬁcation is diﬃcult.
This was not a challenge with the test samples, since they were formulated with a D2O buﬀer and
not long before the acquisition, and thus little to no exchange had taken place. This is clear in one of
the ﬁrst POCE spectra acquired in this project of sample 10 in appendix B, where almost no water
signal is present.
However, in the case of the biological samples the story was quite diﬀerent. Even though they were
also, theoretically, free of water, water was almost the only thing that was visible in ﬁrst couple of
spectra. An example of this can be seen in appendix C with sample L1.3. Some peaks are clearly visible,
but the signal is very overpowered by the water signal, and if any signals were to be negative around

the spectrum is on top of the water signal. This leads to a suppression of the water signal. However
this was already done in the case of the spectrum in appendix C, so other measures had to be taken

5.3.2
Possible reasons for diﬀerence in signal intensity
The diﬀerence in signal strength for the peaks of 3HB and glucose ranges from 15% to 26%, with little
to no consistency across samples and number of scans. A larger amount of samples would be needed
for further investigation, but this inconsistency makes it hard to reliably quantify a POCE spectrum.
Furthermore, this inconsistency would make it hard to diﬀerentiate whether or not peaks in the
diﬀerence spectrum, was due to this diﬀerence in signal strength, or the actual presence of labelled
metabolites. This is not a problem as long as the labelled positions in the sample are 99.99% pure, as
is the case with the test samples, however when working with real biological samples there is going to
be both labelled and unlabelled versions of the same metabolites, and thus, if there is a reduction of
signal in the inversion spectrum, it is not possible to know whether it is from the presence of labelled
metabolites or this reduction in signal strength.
In the end this diﬀerence in signal strength leads to less signal in the diﬀerence spectrum, than what
there should be. This is due to the fact, that if there is less negative signal in the inversion spectrum,
then when subtracting the inversion spectrum from the normal spectrum, the diﬀerence ends up being
less than double the signal. It can be looked at like this: [12C+13C]-[12C-0.8·13C]=1.8x13C, instead
of the expected 2x13C, as mentioned in section 3.2.
A reason for this diﬀerence in signal strength could be the fact that the pulse sequence for the two
spectra are diﬀerent lengths. This is due to the fact that the 180° inversion pulse is not necessarily the
same length as the 180° pulse through the proton channel. The 180° inversion pulse was 20 µs. The
standard proton 90° pulse is around 10 µs, however it may wary with the given day, when a pulsecal
command is performed. Since the 180° pulse through the proton channel per deﬁnition is the double
length of the 90° pulse, depending on the length of the proton 90° pulse that day, the diﬀerence in

the length of the pulse sequence with the inversion pulse and without may wary from non-existent to
several microseconds.
The spectrum in ﬁgure 21 was acquired on the 500 MHz spectrometer with a p1 of 10.09 µs, which
gives a 180° proton pulse of 20.18 µs, which is 0.18 µs longer than the inversion pulse. The delay
between the inversion pulse and acquisition is half a second divided by constant 2, which is the typical
coupling constant between 1JC-H. As mentioned in section 3.2.1, for this project a coupling of 145
Hz was chosen, which lead to a delay of 3.45 ms. 3.45 ms is almost 20.000 longer than 0.18 µs, which
means that it is probably not the diﬀerence of 0.18 µs, that makes a diﬀerence as to whether or not
the nucleus is properly relaxed.
Since the diﬀerence in pulse length depends on the calibration of the 90° proton pulse, it is diﬀerent
on the 800MHz spectrometer from the 500MHz spectrometer. The POCE spectra acquired on May
13th on the 800MHz spectrometer, were obtained with a 180° pulse of 15 µs, whereas the 180° pulse
via the carbon channel were 24 µs. That is a diﬀerence of 9 µs. But even 9 µs is still 400 times less
than the relaxation period of 3.45 ms, so that hardly makes a diﬀerence.
Since the diﬀerence in signal mostly, if not only, aﬀects the inverted peaks, it could also be that the
inversion pulse through the carbon channel is not working ideally. It could either be that that it is
not exciting all the nuclei, leading to not all signal being inverted 180° in the spectrum, or that the
magnetisation transfer is not working.
It could also be eﬀects of what Henry et. al. described in 2006, which was previously mentioned in
section 3.2.1. Since both glucose and 3HB contain a strongly coupled spin system, magnetisation
transfer further than the 1JC-H could happen [44]. A way to investigate this would be to acquire
POCE spectra of the test samples with an internal standard on the 800MHz spectrometer. Since
coupling eﬀects are stronger at lower ﬁeld strength, one would expect the signal intensity diﬀerence
to be smaller when acquiring the spectra on an 800MHz spectrometer. This was not done due to time
restrictions.
Since POCE spectroscopy has previously been used quantitatively, as documented in section 3.2, it
should either be possible to eliminate this diﬀerence in signal intensity or ensure that it is uniform
across all labelled compounds and thus account for it in quantiﬁcation.

Absolute quantiﬁcation
Because of the signal strength diﬀerence, absolute quantiﬁcation of the POCE diﬀerence spectra were
not attempted, since it was clear that it would be impossible. However, since it could be possible in
the future, a few important aspects of quantitative POCE spectroscopy is elaborated upon.
First and foremost it is important to choose the right peaks. An isolated peak as far away from the
water presaturation as possible is ideal. Furthermore, as mentioned in section 3.2.2, since magnetisation
transfer further than 1JC-H is possible, quantiﬁcation should happen through a position in a metabolite
that is known to get the labelled 13C. This is not a problem when using a fully labelled substrate,
since all metabolites of a fully labelled substrate will be labelled, however if using POCE spectroscopy
with a substrate with only one or two labelled positions, this would be crucial. If choosing to quantify
via a peak further away than 1JC-H, it would result in thinking that there is less of that metabolite,
than is actually in the sample.
As mentioned in section 2.3.4, spectra with presaturation can be harder to reliably quantify, speciﬁcally
with regards to the peaks around the frequency of water. But since this is a test of POCE spectroscopy
and its limitations, it would be valuable to quantify a POCE spectrum both based on a peak far
from the water signal and thus the presaturation and one close by, to test how much it aﬀects the
quantiﬁcation. However, this was not possible in this project as explained in section 5.3.

5.5
Usefulness of POCE spectroscopy in metabolic ﬂux studies
The usefulness of POCE spectroscopy in vitro metabolic ﬂux studies is discussed and compared to
established NMR methods, primarily HSQC.
5.5.1
Spectral resolution
The spectral resolution of a 1D spectrum will always be worse than with a 2D spectrum. This is not
inherently a problem, but it makes it harder to quantify the metabolites in the 1D spectrum, since a
peak that is not overlapping with anything else is needed in order for quantiﬁcation to be possible.
Since the spectral resolution of POCE spectroscopy is worse than that of a HSQC spectrum, it might
not be ideal to use in combination with fully 13C labelled glucose. It was not a problem in this project,
but the metabolite extract sample was quite simple. The spectral resolution of the POCE diﬀerence
spectrum could be a challenge with more complex samples, as it was already more diﬃcult to decipher
the glucose peaks than in a HSQC spectrum.
The same comparison between a POCE diﬀerence spectrum and an HSQC would be useful done with
[U-13C1]-D-Glucose as a substrate. But if using labelling of a speciﬁc atom in the tracer compound,
it would be necessary to be attentive to the expected metabolites. If the labelling ends up on a
quarternary carbon it would be invisible in a POCE spectrum.
5.5.2
Identiﬁcation of peaks
Identiﬁcation of peaks in the POCE diﬀerence spectrum was relatively easy, both in the test sample
and the biological sample L1.3. However, in both these samples I knew going in, what was in the
samples. It would be useful to test identiﬁcation in a POCE diﬀerence spectrum on samples where
the compounds were unknown to me.
Due to the nature of the 2D spectrum, every peak comes with two chemical shifts and therefore a
more robust identiﬁcation is possible. Also because of the greater spectral resolution, it is less likely
for peaks to overlap. This made the distinguishing of peaks between the glucose anomers easier in
the HSQC spectrum. Since the peaks of glucose overlapped in the POCE spectrum, the multiplicities
were also less usable. This combined made the HSQC spectrum the better technique for identifying
the peaks of glucose. However identiﬁcation could be made easier by employing a program to identify

6
Conclusion
In this project POCE spectroscopy was tested as a method for identifying and quantifying metabolites
in brain cell extract samples with fully [U-13C6]-D-Glucose as a substrate.
First a POCE pulse sequence was established and the experimental parameters were optimised. A
suitable decoupling strength was determined by testing diﬀerent values. The strong water signal of
the biological samples was eliminated by adding presaturation to the POCE pulse sequence. These
two measures resulted in an easily interpretable POCE diﬀerence spectrum, suitable for identifying
metabolites.
Identiﬁcation of compounds in the test samples was performed, and it was found that the POCE
diﬀerence spectrum created from spectra obtained with 32 scans was adequate for identiﬁcation. It
was also found that the amount of scans made no distinguishable diﬀerence for identiﬁcation of the
test sample compounds. Afterwards, metabolites in the astrocyte cell extract sample were identiﬁed
using a POCE diﬀerence spectrum. The spectrum was harder to interpret than that of the test sample,
however identiﬁcation of the substrate and metabolite was still possible, especially since the biological
sample was still not overly complex. The identiﬁcation based on the POCE diﬀerence spectrum was
compared to that based on a HSQC spectrum, and it was found that while the distinguishing between
the peaks of glucose were easier in the HSQC, information such as multiplicities and coupling con-
stants, that helped with identiﬁcation in the POCE diﬀerence spectrum, were lost.
Upon close inspection of POCE spectra, a diﬀerence in signal intensity was discovered between peaks
in the two spectra that comprise a POCE diﬀerence spectrum. It was discovered by comparing the
signal strength of three peaks in the two spectra, with regards to the internal standard. It was found
that the diﬀerence in signal strength for [U-13C4]-Hydroxybutyric acid was around 20% whereas the
diﬀerence for the methyl group of [U-13C1]-L-Alanine was below 5%. This led to the hypothesis that
the reason for this diﬀerence in signal intensity lay with the inversion pulse; however, further investi-
gation would be needed to conﬁrm this.
POCE spectroscopy was used to attempt to quantify the constructed test samples, to determine
whether or not this was possible. Absolute quantiﬁcation was impossible, however relative quantiﬁca-
tion, especially with regards to [U-13C4]-Hydroxybutyric acid, was promising.
POCE spectroscopy shows promise with regards to identiﬁcation and quantiﬁcation of complex metabo-
lite mixtures. If the quantiﬁcation of POCE spectroscopy were reliable, it would be a valuable tool,
since it holds information that other techniques do not, such as multiplicities, coupling constants, and
an overview of not only labelled compounds but also the entire contents of the sample.

Future perspectives
Further investigation into POCE spectroscopy would be useful with regard to more biological samples,
diﬀerent substrates of those samples, and the factors leading to the diﬀerence in signal intensity. The
ﬁrst step would be to acquire POCE spectra of all the astrocyte metabolite extracts samples. Since
a portion of those had pyruvate as a substrate, this would also look into what a substrate with only
three labelled nuclei would do for the spectral resolution of the POCE diﬀerence spectrum.
The diﬀerence in signal intensity should also be addressed in further research. Firstly, POCE spectra
of the test samples with an internal standard should be acquired on the 800MHz spectrometer to
investigate whether or not the signal diﬀerence could be due to strong coupling in the spin system.
It would also be interesting to study whether or not the diﬀerence in signal intensity aﬀected the
compounds of the test sample diﬀerently. This would be done by acquiring POCE spectra of more
samples.
It would also be useful to explore other applications for POCE spectroscopy or its eﬀectiveness in
combination with other NMR techniques. A unique aspect of POCE spectroscopy is that it not only
provides information on labeled compounds but also obtains a spectrum of all compounds in the
sample, with the labeled compounds clearly distinguishable—the inversion spectrum. This technique
could be used to assess the amount of metabolites derived from a labeled substrate compared to the
entire metabolism during that period, giving a useful overview of how readily the cell utilizes the
labeled substrate.
New research is being conducted into HSQC and its use in identifying and quantifying metabolites
in complex mixtures [50, 51]. This is promising for the future of metabolic ﬂux studies and would
therefore be the standard to compare POCE spectroscopy against in further research.
