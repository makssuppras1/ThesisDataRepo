English Abstract
Energy demand has experienced an exponential growth for the last decades and predic-
tions augur that it will not stop there. Fossil resources depletion is closer than never
and the pollution derived from their consumption is having a major impact in Earth’s
ecosystems. Bioenergy and biofuels represent one of the most promising alternatives to
replace traditional activities due to the transformation of renewable bioresources into
alcohols, oils and biogas, among others.
Seaweed bioreﬁnery represents a bright hope to partially settle the energy transition
globally, as seaweed is accessible all around the planet and does not compete for land and
food supply. In this project, wet cast seaweed collected in the Eastern coast of Denmark
was pretreated with diﬀerent methods, involving changes in temperature, pH and dura-
tion (aqueous ammonia soaking pretreatment or AAS, hydrothermal and thermoacidic
pretreatments). Pretreatment eﬀects on sugars solubilisation were investigated. Fer-
mentations were accomplished with mixed microbial consortia, reducing operating costs
and favoring the higher exploitation of the substrate complex internal structures. Eﬀects
from two diﬀerent inocula, incubation temperature and pretreatments in the fermenta-
tion were analysed in screening tests, and the most promising conditions were scaled
up to 2.5 L batch bioreactors. The objective was to obtain a signiﬁcant production of
volatile fatty acids (VFAs), which are fundamental intermediates in the production of
biofuels and bulk chemicals. Gas and liquid products were monitored throughout the
experiments, and the possible interactions between hydrogen, ethanol and carboxylates
were discussed.
All pretreatments showed an improvement in soluble sugars and aﬀected the biomass
diﬀerently. Eﬀects on the main algal carbohydrates were determined. For example, the
thermoacid treatment provided the solubilisation of 30% and 50% of the total avail-
able glucose and xylose in the cast seaweed, respectively. Thermophilic conditions (55
℃) resulted the most beneﬁcial for medium chain fatty acids (MCFAs) synthesis. Hy-
drothermal seaweed was more successful than the raw, the AAS and the thermoacidic
biomasses in the screening tests. In contrast, the fermentation of thermoacidic seaweed
provided a 40% yield increment (g total products/g VS) over the fermentation of raw
seaweed in the bioreactors. Hydrogen, ethanol and short fatty acids demonstrated their
inﬂuence in the generation of MCFAs and alcohols.

Preface
This Master Thesis, “Processing of seaweed for biological production of bulk chemicals”,
is submitted to the Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering at Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) in partial fulﬁlment of the requirements for the degree
Master of Science (MSc) in Chemical and Biochemical Engineering. The thesis consists
of a workload equivalent to 35 ECTS of MSc Program curriculum. The project was
accomplished at the Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering in DTU
Building 227 (ground ﬂoor) from January 27 to September 23, 2020.
This project aims to uncover seaweed potential as biomass to partially satisfy the
energy and bulk chemicals demand through a sustainable bio-based process. The present
document follows KT Guidelines for Student Projects, and is constituted by: project fun-
damentals and speciﬁc goals; a review of the literature concerning the topic; a description
of the steps carried out throughout the experimental phase; and the presentation and
discussion of the results obtained.

Introduction
The Earth has a ﬁnite amount of non-renewable resources that humans constantly use
to generate the energy and goods needed for the daily life. Over-consumption and lack
of application of renewable technologies have been setting the Overshoot Day in mid
Summer for the last years, consuming the resources equivalent to the available in 1.75
Earths by the end of the year [1]. The ascending energy demand - expected in a 26%
increment over the next decades- and the environmental eﬀects associated to it (e.g.
GHG emissions, raising sea levels, etc.) [2] have raised awareness among institutions,
researchers and population all over the world.
Bio-based technology represents one of the main alternatives for the production of
fuels and commodities through sustainable processes. Application of lignocellulosic raw
materials for the production of 1st and 2nd generation biofuels has consolidated in the
sector for the last years and has been adapted to the geographical and agricultural
conditions of the placements. However, the processing of these biomasses continue to
present technological issues that make the implementation of most of them still unfeasible
in large scale [3].
In view of the diﬃculties presented by lignocellulosic feedstocks, seaweed appeared as
a possible candidate for the production of the so-called 3rd generation biofuels. The use
of seaweed as biomass may provide other ecological beneﬁts, such as: non competition
with food supply [3, 4]; no need of land and fertilizers [3, 5, 6]; fast growth in multiple
aquatic environments (salt, fresh or wastewater) [2, 7]; and higher CO2 ﬁxation potential
[2, 8]. In addition, seaweeds could lead to more eﬃcient bioconversion processes due to
its low lignin content [2, 7].
Interest in macroalgae for the production of fuels was investigated in the past (20th
century), but it became eclipsed by the sudden rise of other technologies [6, 9]. Attention
for seaweed derived biofuels has resumed at present, although the research available is
still very limited [9]. Their attractive anatomy and adaptability to new environments
and conditions make them a good source of bioactive compounds for pharmaceuticals
and fertilizers [6, 10], enabling the development of cost-eﬀective biocascading processes
[5]. Nevertheless, seaweed processing at large scale may primarily bring unavoidable
constraints related to their high moisture content or their structural heterogeneity among
species, aﬀecting yields and expenses [7].
Volatile Fatty Acids (VFAs) are liquid intermediate compounds formed during biolog-
ical synthesis of fuels (alcohols, H2, biomethane, etc.). VFAs are obtaining considerable
attention due to their role in the production of alcohols and long and medium chain fatty
acids [11], and are grouped within bulk or commodity chemicals due to their broad im-
plications. However, the scarce research in seaweed-based technologies has not provided
remarkable advances on VFAs production from macroalgae fermentation yet [8].

1 Introduction
12
Scope of the Project and Objectives
Processing of seaweed for biological production of bulk chemicals is a project that em-
braces the generation of volatile fatty acids (VFAs) from one of the most abundant and
unused bio-resources found in the Earth. The project aims to develop eﬃcient treatment
solutions for obtaining liquid chemicals from wet cast seaweed originating in the East
coast of Denmark (Solrød municipality area).
Seaweed is pretreated through various methodologies attending to diﬀerent operat-
ing factors, ranging from temperatures to pH. An ammonia based pretreatment, which
previously showed a 46% increment in the biomethane yield from the same algae [12], is
tested at diﬀerent ammonia concentrations to observe its eﬀect on VFAs generation. Hy-
drothermal and thermoacidic methods are also applied to the biomass to observe changes
in sugar release and subsequently, in the yields, productivities and product distributions.
Fermentation is carried out with mixed microbial cultures as novelty in seaweed
bioconversion. Mixed microbial consortia may facilitate the interaction with complex
biomasses due to their selective growth oriented towards the available substrates. Si-
multaneously, expenses associated to the fermentation are reduced due to their higher
resistance (no sterilization required). Incubation conditions and two diﬀerent inocula
are initially tested to determine the best match for seaweed fermentation.
Thus, wet seaweed is processed as collected in the beach, trying to engineer fea-
sible solutions that reduce drying expenses and debilitates the eﬀect of miscellaneous
structures. Environmentally speaking, ﬁnding an application to seaweed will help to re-
duce soil contamination and bad odour in coastal areas, while providing a lower carbon
footprint in comparison with competing technologies.
Therefore, the speciﬁc objectives of the research can be listed as:
1. To unveil the potential of wet seaweed in the synthesis of biofuels and commodities;
2. To enhance biomass degradability and culture accessibility to convertible sugars;
3. To determine the optimal conditions for the biological production of bulk chemicals
from cast macroalgae;
4. To orient the fermentation towards the generation of the most valuable products
(longer chain fatty acids);
5. To explore the metabolic pathways and the behavior the consortia according to
products evolution throughout the fermentation.

2.1
Seaweed as Feedstock
Interest in seaweed for producing 3rd generation biofuels has raised in the last years due
to its widespread distribution and its low implication in the human food chain [4, 13].
More than 9,000 diﬀerent seaweed species have been identiﬁed and coexist in the seas
[7].
Macroalgae cultivation and growth is not restricted by land availability as they natu-
rally grow in underwater sand and rocks. Cultivation can take place onshore or oﬀshore,
frequently combining the latter with wind farms [14]. They can reach tens of meters
long, since their growth is favored by the higher CO2 sequestration capacity of the sea
and by a more eﬃcient photosynthesis than land plants [4, 13].
Macroalgae are autotrophic, multicellular and eukaryotic organisms that reproduce
asexually and generate new constitutionally identical individuals [3, 13]. Depending of
the photosynthetic pigments, they can be classiﬁed in three diﬀerent families: brown,
green and red seaweeds. Their pigments and internal structure are inﬂuenced by the
exposure to sunlight and the geographical, meteorological and environmental conditions
[9, 13, 15]. Seaweed composition (Table 1) varies seasonally, but the three groups have
in common a higher content in carbohydrates during Summer and Autumn months [9].
Figure 1 [16] presents examples from each of the families. In comparison to terrestrial
plants, seaweeds anatomy is simpler and do not show roots or leaves. Polysaccharides
are abundant and lignin is not a major compound in the internal structure [4, 7, 9].
Water represents between 80 and 90 % of the wet weight [7, 13].

Current Situation in VFAs Manufacture
Volatile Fatty Acids (VFAs) are enlarging their presence in bioreﬁnery due to their
involvement in the production of alcohols, high value chemicals or pharmaceuticals,
among others [11]. Indeed, their esteem is such that a third fermentatation platform
- the carboxylate platform (see Section 2.4)- has been designated for them. Therefore,
their value reside in their function as intermediates rather than in their use as bulk
chemicals. In the fermentation process, they are cogenerated with hydrogen by anaerobic
fermentation and digestion as intermediate products before the methanogenesis (Figure
3).
Acetic, butyric and propionic acids are the most abundant fatty acids resulting from
anaerobic fermentation of algal biomass, although their concentrations vary according
to the conditions given in the medium, inocula preparation, feedstock nature and fer-
mentation duration [11]. Kidanu et al. [11] analysed mixed cultures fermentation of
diﬀerent algae under diﬀerent conditions, such as temperature range, inhibitor addition,
pH and pretreatment inﬂuence in air-dried seaweed, getting a maximum concentration
of 8.0 g/L of VFAs from a 35 g/L of substrate. Fermentation length was found to be 5
days [11]. For diﬀerent conditions, Pham et al.[22] reported a VFAs concentration over
15 g/L for the brown algae Laminaria japonica, with an initial substrate concentration
of 50 g/L.
Challenges in biologically produced VFAs come from the puriﬁcation of the acids
after the fermentation. Their short carbon chains and hydrophilic properties complicate
the separation. However, methods such as fermentation with anion exchange resin have
been successfully developed [11].
VFAs production from seaweed still remains an unexplored territory, as demonstrated
the limited literature found about this biobased technology. However, In the future,
cascade bioreﬁnery or integrated processes are expected to develop bulk chemicals man-
ufacture from macroalgae in large scale and make the process cost-eﬀective [5]. Other
products obtained from algal biomass and the current trends are shortly introduced in
the next sections.
Production of Biogas
Seaweed resulted a very suitable candidate for the generation of biogas in terms of degra-
dation, providing high eﬃciency and conversion rates [4, 9, 10]. The lack of lignin in
algal structures eases carbohydrates isolation and sugars conversion by anaerobic diges-
tion (AD). However, the high moisture content and expenses derived from drying and
transport of large volumes suggest that seaweed processing might be only economically
viable if it is integrated with Combined Heat and Power (CHP) generation system [4, 7].
Other costs are associated to the removal of sulfurs, nitrogen and cadmium [4], although
the presence of those chemicals seemed to be lower in the products coming from brown
seaweed than from green algae or land crops [9].
Seaweed is a seasonal raw material with changing compositions throughout the year,
and any variables need to be considered for biogas production: temperature, residence

Production of Bioalcohols: Ethanol and Butanol
Seaweeds, in special brown macroalgae, stand out as raw materials for the production
of ethanol due to the high content in hydrolysable carbohydrates and presenting sugar
alcohols, such mannitol [4]. In comparison to other algaes, brown seaweed also oﬀers
higher amount of cellulose in its structure. However, and despite not having lignin, this
quantity of cellulose is not suﬃcient enough to accomplish a fermentation similar to the
land-crop ethanol bioconversion. Pretreatments playing with e.g. temperature, pH or
enzymatic hydrolysis, are carried out to reduce the long sugars chains into monomers

Hydrolysis and Fermentation) or SSF (Simultaneous Sacchariﬁcation and Fermentation)

Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most common yeast used in industry to produce

or its pH ﬂexibility [25].
Other researchers have implemented diﬀerent scopes to boost the production of
ethanol from seaweed. Wargacki et al. [26] modiﬁed E. coli to report a yield equal to
80% of the maximum theoretical yield by transforming all the sugar-based compounds:
alginate, cellulose, laminarin and mannitol [9, 26]. Enquist-Newman et al. [27], instead,
modiﬁed S. cerevisiae to convert alginate and get a yield over 80% of the maximum
theoretical [9, 27].
Brown algae is considered to produce butanol in the future on large scale manufac-
ture. Huesemann et al. [28] were conﬁdent about the potential of an integrated process
to generate acetone, butanol and ethanol (ABE) through fermentation of glucose and
mannitol by Clostridium acetobutylicum [10, 28]. The fermentation showed a triauxic
proﬁle of the fermentation by consuming glucose, mannitol and laminarin, in this respec-
tive order [28]. Results seemed promising for being a ﬁrst approach, and improvements
in alginate reduction technologies could lead to higher yields [4, 28].
All in all, and as with biogas, the conversion of macroalgal feedstock into bioethanol
is only economically viable if it is accomplished to generate other high value products in
an integrated system [9]. The potential of butanol production is still being investigated
and optimized [28].
Generation of Hydrogen and Syngas
Bio-based hydrogen could be generated from algal biomsass in three processes: fermen-
tation, gasiﬁcation or photobiological process [3, 9]. Nevertheless, the current hydrogen
in the market proceeding from biorenewable sources does not exceed 5% [25].
Fermentation of macroalgae has proved better results in hydrogen production than
2nd generation biomasses. Suutari et al. [9] provided yields such as 71 mL H2 g−1 TS for
untreated seaweed, exceeding the yield got for land crops [9]. Torres et al. [10] supported
the application of dark fermentation - fermentation in absence of light - with MMC as
it requires lower energy input and major adaptability to diﬀerent feedstocks [10].
Gasiﬁcation of macroalgal biomass is said to produce seaweed based hydrogen and
syngas in competitive prices [13]. Seaweed gasiﬁcation carried out with supercritical wa-
ter increased the yields obtained from land-based materials. Brown seaweed processing

Production of Biodiesel
Macroalgae does not represent a suitable biomass for producing biodiesel. The low lipids
content and the high moisture concentration in the wet seaweed are constraints hardly
salvageable [4, 10, 13]. However, the production of biodiesel from macroalgae could be
carried out integrated in other process as bioethanol or biogas manufacture.
Biodiesel could be obtained from seaweed by the traditional two-stages process: oil
extraction from seaweed with solvents and catalysed transesteriﬁcation with methanol
[10]. The remaining algae could then be treated to produce other chemicals and energy
[10]. New procedures aiming to reduce operational costs, such as direct methanolysis,
approach the production of biodiesel in a single stage. Methanol is used as solvent for
oil extraction and as transesteriﬁcation alcohol, acting directly over the algal biomass
[3].
High biodiesel yields are feasible to obtain as the triglycerides content in lipids is
very high and conversion reaches 99% [9, 13]. Nevertheless, this could be harmful as an
alkaline catalyst could react with triglycerides and water and lead to saponiﬁcation [9,
13], diﬃculting the biodiesel puriﬁcation. Other catalysts could be used to avoid soap
formation, such as acid catalyst, lipases or heterogeneous catalyst. The ﬁrst one provides
a very slow reaction and needs more solvent and energy input than the alkaline. Lipases
require ambient conditions, but makes the process even less cost-eﬀective. Heterogeneous
catalysts have been only tried in microalgae and could result expensive [9].
Implications in Pharmaceuticals and Cosmetics
Application of seaweeds in medicines comes from several decades ago, when they were
already involved in the production of 35% of the therapeutic chemicals [21].
Macroalgae represents an incredible source of bioactive compounds used in many
commercial pharmaceutical and cosmetics.
Seaweeds are continuously acquiring new
components that allow them to resist bacterial attacks caused by sea contamination and
environmental pollution. Algae obtain properties that can be added to drugs and cos-
metics in form of algal extracts or bioactive compounds [29]. They could be used as
emulsiﬁers or stabilizers because of their gelling qualities, or as suppliers of therapeutic
properties. Anti-bacterial and anti-viral, anti-tumoral, anti-oxidant, anti-aging or im-
munogenic potential are some of the properties that seaweeds strengthen in healthcare
products [5, 21, 29]. In case of brown seaweed, alginate is used as excipient and bio-
adhesive, presents potential as anticoagulant and is essential in biodegradable wound
healing products, as it is a water-binding agent helpful in skin hydration [29, 30]. Fu-

More applications
Macroalgae has a very broad range of applications apart from the previously introduced.
There is room for seaweed in biomaterials, food, animal feed or agriculture. Due to its
high biocompatibility and adhesive properties, Torres et al. [10] explored the use of
biocomposites made from algal residues to sustainably complement the packaging of
foods, medicines and plastics [10]. Seaweed is considered as a food ingredient in many

total market values of over a half billion US dollars [3]. Animal feed has incorporated

help to keep the soil humid and soft [15], but contaminants present in their structure,
such as cadmium, need to be eliminated before their application [4].

Pretreatment Methods
Pretreatment methods are processing techniques that allow manufacturers to set the
crude raw material ready for the conversion into chemicals with a market interest. Ap-
plied to biological feedstocks, the pretreatment helps to release the maximum amount of
convertible sugars (or extracts of interest) from the internal structure of the biomass and,
at the same time, reduce long chain polysaccharides into monomers that can be easily
transformed into the desired products by fermentation. Figure 5 [31] shows a glimpse of
the structural change in lignocellulosic biomass after the application of pretreatments.
The output shown after the pretreatment is a midway structure, since these operations
aim to reduce the long carbon chains to simple sugars or to provoke an alteration in the
complex structures (e.g. swelling). In case of algal feedstock, lignin would not likely be
present on the process due to its low content in the biomass.
Pretreatments can be classiﬁed attending to the type of process carried out over
the biomass. Physical, thermal, chemical and biological are the major categories for
pretreatments. However, sometimes those families are grouped together for a uniﬁed
treatment, resulting for example in physico-chemical or thermo-chemical processes. Ta-
ble 3 shows the functions and limitations of some of the pretreatments commented in
the next sections.

Thermal Pretreatments
This group involves all the processes that work under high temperatures in order to
remove moisture or volatile compounds from the biomass. High temperatures help to
degrade biomass internal structure by breaking down cell walls [17]. Temperatures can
reach over 200℃, although it generates inhibitors that diﬃcult the digestion and fermen-
tation processes. Biomass solubilisation and methane potential are higher at elevated
temperatures [17].
Thermal processes are applied directly to the biomass by subjecting the substrate
to high temperatures, e.g. heating, drying, torrefaction, pyrolysis, etc.; or by using a
chemical agent in what is known as thermochemical or physicochemical processes. This
is the case of steam or ammonia ﬁber explosions, or supercritical CO2 explosion.
Sharma et al. [35] reported higher sugar concentrations for brown seaweed (S. latis-
sima) dried at 30℃before an enzymatic hydrolysis [35]. The assessment considered
diﬀerent temperatures between 30℃and 105℃, as well as wet and freeze-dried biomass,
being the mild temperatures the more consistent for polysaccharides reduction [35].
Adams et al. [38] found out that unwashed freeze-dried brown algae L. digitata pro-
vided a higher fermentation yield than the substrate dried at 70℃[38].
Bae et al. [40] assessed the pyrolysis of seaweed for the production of bio-oils and
carbon-rich char. The study arrived to the conclusion that 500℃in inert atmosphere
was the optimum condition for bio-oil generation for all the species analysed. Lower
temperatures favored the formation of char [40].
Steam explosion has shown beneﬁts when accomplished before enzymatic hydrolysis
[17]. This pretreatment has been mainly applied for production of methane and uses
temperatures around 150℃. Vivekanand et al. [41] obtained a rise of 20% in the methane
yield after applying steam explosion to brown algae [41]. Steam can be replaced by hot
water at temperatures near 80℃in order to reduce the energy input and the residence
time in the fermentation or digestion [17].
No reported results for Ammonia Fiber Explosion (AFEX) or supercritical CO2 ap-
plied to seaweed were found. Both of them proceed similarly to steam explosion. AFEX
enhances the fermentation rate and digestibility without removing lignin or hemicellulose
[31]. Supercritical CO2 treatment results cheaper than AFEX and operates at milder
conditions than steam explosion [31].
Chemical Pretreatments
Chemical pretreatments are the techniques that include the action of a chemical agent
to degrade biomass into simpler structures easy to dissolve or extract. According to the
properties of the solvent used in the hydrolysis, chemical operations could be classiﬁed
under acid, alkaline or organic acid treatments.

1. Alkaline hydrolysis. It involves the use of a base as chemical agent to alter the
biomass structure, for example, by decreasing cellulose crystallinity or reducing
polysaccharides into monomers ready to be consumed. It runs under low pres-
sures and temperatures (e.g. ambient conditions) and can easily remove lignin and
hemicellulose [31]. Long treatment times, salts formation and lower depolymerisa-
tion eﬃciency in comparison to acid hydrolysis are the main constraints [17, 31].
Alkaline agents include from hydroxides to lime or ammonia [31].
Pham et al. [22] applied alkaline hydrolysis with sodium hydroxide (0.5N at room
conditions) to brown seaweed to investigate the eﬀect in VFAs production [22].
Results showed that product concentration and productivity were improved by the
pretreatment at high substrate concentrations, whilst the yield decreased at those
concentrations [22]. In comparison, alkaline hydrolysis provided better results than
the thermal treatment [22].
No previous work for VFAs production from aqueous ammonia soaked (AAS) sea-
weed was found. AAS results a mild alkaline treatment carried out at atmospheric
pressure that oﬀers the total recovery of the catalyst [42]. The latter results a
big advantage, as the harshness of the treatment (ammonia concentration) can be
adapted to each biomass according to their needs. This pretreatment has proved
good hemicellulose solubilisation for other biomasses as wheat straw [42]. Engelsen
[12] obtained a 46% improvement in the ultimate biomethane yield after treating
seaweed with AAS 15% ammonia (w/w) [12].
2. Acid hydrolysis. Acid pretreatment is commonly carried out using a diluted
solution of a strong acid as chemical agent to hydrolyse polyssacharides into fer-
mentable sugars. Acids act fast and eﬃciently -as they are able to dissolve and
hydrolyse hemicellulose- but provide diﬃculties related to corrosion, acid recov-
ery and intoxication [17, 31]. It usually requires warm temperatures and the pH
needs to be adjusted to neutral before enzymatic hydrolysis or fermentation [31].
Dilute sulfuric acid is one of the most common agents employed in this process, fol-
lowed by nitric and hydrochloric acids [31]. Acid treatments can provide a glucose
recovery over 90% of the available in the original feedstock [5, 31].
Adams et al. [43] compared acid and thermal treatments with the brown algae S.
latissima for ethanol fermentation. The highest ethanol yield came from ambient
temperature (T = 23℃) and no acid pretreatment. However, comparing the rest
of the conditions, results showed that the acid hydrolysis at pH = 2 caused a
better eﬀect than the thermal treatment at T = 70℃[43]. Probably, the acidiﬁed
biomass leaded to a lower yield than untreated biomass due to salt and inhibitors
formation [43]. Barbot et al. [44] highlighted the eﬀect of an acid hydrolysis with
hydrochloric acid 0.2M and heat treatment at 80℃, enhancing the methane yield
in 140% [17, 44].
The higher costs associated to this pretreatment makes the alkaline hydrolysis
often the preferred option [31].

3. Organic acid pretreatment.
Some organic acid can substitute strong acids
during the acid hydrolysis providing similar results in biomass depolymerisation.
Organic acids do not only represent a more sustainable measure to process the raw
biomass, but also add an extra source of nutrients to the medium, which organisms
can use during the fermentation [37].
Vanegas et al. [37] assessed the pretreatment of S. latissima and L. digitata with
four organic acids (acetic, citric, lactic and oxalic). Sugars release was proportional
to the concentration of most of the acids and declared oxalic acid as the most
promising option.Nevertheless, it is expected that the results vary according to the
species and type of algal biomass treated [37].
Other chemical processes that are bursting into industry, and whose potential has
already been proved for lignocellulosic biomass, are Organosolv and Ozonolysis. Suitabil-
ity of Ozonolysis for seaweed can be questioned since its main purpose is lignin removal
[31]. The Organosolv pretreatment could bring some interest due to the application of
an organic solvent as catalyst, after observing the achievements given by Vanegas et al.
[37] with the organic acids.
Biological Pretreatment
Biological pretreatment is applied to biomass in terms of enzymatic hydrolysis. Rot
fungi or enzymes cocktails are added to the feedstock to remove lignin and hemicel-
lulose, degrade long chain polymers and reduce the crystallinity of the biomass. This
pretreatment becomes one of the most sustainable due to its low energy requirements
and waste generation, although it triggers longer hydrolysis times [31]. In addition, en-
zymes become really expensive for large scale production [37] and their cost can only be
recovered with high value products. The studies available centered in the application of
enzymes to seaweed are still very limited [17].
Cellulases and alginate lyase are the predominant enzymes used to hydrolyse seaweed
[37].
Alginate lyase degrades alginate and eases the access of cellulases to cellulose
and laminarin [34].
Vanegas et al.
[37] obtained sugars concentrations rounding 20
g/L for S. latissima and L. digitata after enzymatic hydrolysis with those enzymes
[37]. However, the election of the microorganisms, dosage and operating conditions (pH,
agitation, residence time,...) must be adapted to the raw materials according to species,
composition or structure [37].
Manns et al. [34] carried out a study to assess the most beneﬁcial dosage and ratio for
the combination of alginate lyase and cellulase in brown seaweed hydrolysis. Optimal
concentrations for dosage resulted in 1% of alginate lyase and 10% of cellulase (% in
w/w) for 8 hours of hydrolysis [34]. Sharma et al. [35] played with the concentration of
those two enzymes in a dose of 7 mg enzymes/g dry matter. The optimal combination
was 10% of alginate lyase and 90% of cellulase. It was found that the ﬁrst hydrolysis
with alginate lyase at 37℃followed by the treatment with cellulase at 50℃released
more sugars than the hydrolysis with the mixture of both for 8 hours. However, this

two-stages hydrolysis needed an incubation time of 20 hours [35]. The authors foresee
better results if alginate lyase resistance at 50℃is improved [35].
Pham et al. [8] used bacteria Vibrio spp. to biologically treat brown seaweed in the

Absence of Pretreatment
In some studies it was questioned the use of a pretreatment to enhance sugars availability
in the substrate. In case of ethanol production, sacchariﬁcation/hydrolysis is always
required and therefore, the lack of pretreatments referred to previous stages to SSF.
Adams et al. [43] research for the production of bioethanol provided results advocat-
ing for the use of ambient temperatures (T = 23℃) and slightly acidic environment (pH
= 6) in brown seaweed fermentation. Analysis concerning also pH = 2 and warm temper-
atures (T = 70℃) were accomplished, providing lower fermentation yields. Theoretically,
this could happen because the heating induced inhibitors solubilisation. Therefore, the
suggested procedure would be base on cutting the wet seaweed to few centimeters and
proceed with the SSF [43]. Manns et al. [34] arrived to the same conclusion at T = 40℃
[34]. Kostas et al. [5] obtained a glucose yield of 50% of the theoretical yield in L.
digitata only with enzymatic sacchariﬁcation [5].
2.4
Mixed Microbial Cultures Fermentation
Fermentation is an anaerobic bioconversion process where carbon-based biomass is trans-
formed by microorganisms (bacteria, archae, or yeasts) into acids, alcohols, hydro-
gen, carbon dioxide and methane. Complex macromolecules are hydrolysed to monos-
sacharides and other products, so then those can be converted into valuable products by
coupling oxidation and reduction reactions in absence of external oxidizing agents, such
as O2 or NO−
3 [25]. Anaerobic operation provides lower operational costs resulting from
lack of oxygen supply, and a lower energy loss on electrons exchange [45]. The anaerobic
degradation of biological compounds happens in four phases: hydrolysis, fermentation
(or acidogenesis), acetogenesis and methanogenesis [25]. However, when methane is not
the target product, the last phase is suppressed by, for example, the addition of an in-
hibitory compound or by the treatment of the inoculum. Fermentation processes are also
provided with a growth medium to ensure the adequate conditions for the microorganism
to grow, reproduce and transform the sugars into attractive molecules.
Fermentation Cultures
Bioconversion takes place by the action of microorganisms present in the inoculum.
Pure or axenic cultures fermentation is the name given to the fermentation developed
==PAGE:29==

Mixed Microbial Cultures Fermentation
27
with a single isolated microoganism. When there are two or more diﬀerent but known
strains, the name given is deﬁned co-culture. If the inoculum contains undeﬁned and
mixed organisms, usually proceeding from industrial or wastewater sludge, fermentation
is commonly said to happen with mixed cultures. Each of them serve to diﬀerent pur-
poses and have their advantages and disadvantages. Technologies implementing pure
cultures are already well established, mostly in the biotechnological and pharmaceutical
sectors, while the interest in undeﬁned mixed cultures is still very recent.
This project focuses on the application of undeﬁned mixed microbial cultures (MMC)
to produce valuable products from heterogeneous biomass. Adaptability to diﬀerent,
mixed and complex substrates is one of the multiple advantages MMC have over pure
strains [46–49]. More beneﬁts, attained to techno-economic factors, are: capacity to
operate in (semi-)continuous processes [47, 48]; lower inoculum and production costs [47];
no need for sterilization [46–50]; and selective bacterial growth according to the nature
of the substrate and the manipulable process conditions [47, 50]. From a sustainable
perspective, if upcoming advances prove comparable yields, quality and eﬃciencies, there
is no doubt that undeﬁned MMC can replace pure cultures in some industrial activities.
The Carboxylate Platform
Bioreﬁnery comprises the transformation of biomass into valuable fuels and chemicals
through chemical and biological processes. Three diﬀerent platforms can be distinguished
according to the substrate used in the bioconversion: sugar platform; syngas platform;
and carboxylate platform [46]. The fermentations developed in this project only embrace
the latter system, as sugars platform requires the use of pure enzymes [46]. Therefore, the
objective of the carboxylate platform is the conversion of waste into functional products
by the implementation of undeﬁned MMC [46, 50].
Agler et al. [46] distinguished two big steps in the carboxylate platform: a primary
fermentation, where polyssacharides are turned into short sugars by hydrolysis, and
those converted into short fatty acids (carboxylic acids), ethanol, hydrogen and carbon
dioxide; and a second fermentation, where the carboxylates are reduced into alcohols or
converted into medium chain fatty acids (MCFA) by chain elongation [46]. This platform
faces challenges the other bioreﬁneries do not. Separation of short chain acids becomes
very diﬃcult and expensive, reason why sometimes they are reduced to alcohols or
longer chain acids as butyric or caproic [46]. Methane can be formed during the process,
consuming part of the hydrogen and acetate required for other metabolic reactions [46].
Possible Metabolic Pathways and Mechanisms
Catabolic and anabolic reactions fostered by the microbial community cover the majority
of the processes happening throughout the fermentations. Transformations carried out
in the primary fermentation seem straightforward: polymers are turned into accessible
sugars by hydrolysis, which then are consumed in the acidogenesis phase to produce
hydrogen, carbon dioxide, ethanol and C2-C4 carboxylic acids [46, 48]. All these com-
pounds will react between themselves in the secondary fermentation to generate alcohols

and longer chain acids. The reactions involved in the secondary fermentation are col-
lected in Table 4, concerning chain elongation and carbon dioxide and acids reductions.
Chain elongation is the result of the reversed β-oxidation pathway happening in
presence of short carboxylates and ethanol, where the alcohol plays as electron donor
and carbon source [45].
In the reversed β-oxidation cycle, short chain carboxylates
are reduced with acetyl-CoA, derived from ethanol oxidation, to generate longer chain
carboxylates through the addition of two carbons to their carbon chain (e.g.: acetate
leads to n-butyrate; propionate to n-valerate; n-butyrate to n-caproate; etc.) [45, 46,
48]. Studies also suggest DL-lactate as reducing agent for acetate chain elongation to
n-butyrate [45, 48, 49], and methanol or n-propanol for n-butyrate conversion into n-
caproate [45, 49]. Clostridium spp. - C. kluyveri, among others- seem to be the bacteria
responsible of the majority of these elongations [45, 46, 48–50].
Alcohols, such as propanol, butanol or hexanol, are generated by the reduction of the
corresponding carboxylate with the molecular hydrogen present in the environment. H2
is initially generated by sugars oxidation (Equation 1)[47] in the primary fermentation,

than other reactions, while high H2 partial pressures facilitate both homoacetogenesis
and acetate reduction to ethanol [46, 48].

Yields, product distribution and fermentation length rely on diﬀerent variables, such
as pH, temperature or inhibitory compounds. Buﬀer solutions contained in the growth
medium, pre-fermentation methods or incubation conditions are manipulated to guide
the fermentation towards the target outcomes.

Introduction to the Experiments
The experimental phase of the project took place from February to early August. Ex-
periments were oriented towards seaweed characterization and fermentation, through
the evaluation of the biological and physicochemical properties and of the results given
by seaweed processing. Seaweed was pretreated under diﬀerent methods and fermented
at diﬀerent conditions. Hereby, the activities developed during the experimental phase
concern: biomass characterization; pretreatment methods applied to crude seaweed;
screening fermentations and BMP test; and scaled-up fermentation in semi-pilot batch
reactors of 2.5 L.
3.1
Biomass Characterization.
Cast seaweed from Solrød Beach, used as substrate in the municipality’s biogas plant,
was collected in October 2019 and stored in a freezer at -20℃.
The biomass was not washed or dried for the experiments. Its size was hand-cut to
ca. 1 cm long before being used. Total solids (TS) and volatile solids (VS) analysis were
performed to determine the amount of water, convertible substrate and ash (or residue)
present in raw and pretreated biomasses.
Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) analyses were implemented to the feedstock. To-
tal COD was measured in milled seaweed to establish the potential of the biomass as
substrate. Soluble COD was analysed for raw and pretreated biomasses, to indicate
the fraction of COD equivalent solubilised in the liquid phases. Ammonia content was
measured in pretreatments involving the use of NH4OH.
Diluted Acid Hydrolysis (DAH) was carried out to diﬀerentiate pretreatment eﬀects
on sugars release and solubilisation. Strong Acid Hydrolysis (SAH), developed to sol-
ubilise all sugars and sugar acids present in the biomass, was not performed in these
experiments, as the results were provided by previous studies.
The species present in the cast seaweed have not been investigated enough to be
accurately deﬁned, but the biomass is most likely composed by brown species.
3.2
Pretreatment Methods
Screening fermentation tests of raw seaweed allowed the selection of the most suitable
conditions for the inoculum. The biomass, however, was subjected to diﬀerent treatments
so as to uncover a higher potential as a fermentable source of sugars. Seaweed can be
subjected to inﬁnity of pretreatments attending to temperature, pH or pressure, as
detailed in Section 2.3. In this experience, the most exotic options leaned towards a
mild-basic, a neutral and an acid pH pretreatments.
Aqueous Ammonia Soaking (AAS) was the ﬁrst pretreatment implemented to the
biomass.
Previous experiences in methane production (Engelsen [12]) supported its
application. Diﬀerent NH4OH concentrations (5%, 15%, 25% and 32%) were investigated
in the screening fermentations, and the most promising was scaled-up to a pilot reactor.

4.1.5
Dilute Acid Hydrolysis
Soluble sugars available in the pretreatment liquid phases were determined by Dilute
Acid Hydrolysis (DAH). The procedure consisted on the hydrolysis of the liquors with
H2SO4 8% (w/v) and the addition of a spike solution or Millipore water. The spike
solution contained 80 g/L of D-xylose, 33 g/L of D-glucose monohydrate and 30 g/L of
L-arabinose.
The liquors were centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 30 minutes and ﬁltered by 0.45 µm
membrane ﬁlters. 5 mL of each pretreated liquor were pipetted into four diﬀerent 50-
mL Pyrex tubes. 5 mL of sulfuric acid 8% were introduced in each of the tubes. For

The heat treatment applied to the inocula consisted on the immersion of inoculum blue-

minutes and then cooled down to room temperature.
Nitrogen was ﬂushed into the
inoculum during the entire process to keep the anaerobic conditions. The set-up of the

For each experiment round, approximately 250 mL of each inocula were prepared and

at the end was around two-thirds of the initial. The volume evaporated in both inocula
in the ﬁrst round of experiments was the same and it was assumed that the VS ratio
remained constant.

The pH of inocula were controlled in all the screening test to favor the microbial growth
and were initially set in pH = 6 −6.5. This step took place right before setting the
fermentation in the vials.
The initial pH of the sludge was over pH = 8. Drops of HCl 1 M and KOH 3 M were
added to regulate it. The main inconvenience was the pH tendency to increase due to
CO2 evaporation. As done in the heat treatment, anaerobic conditions were kept but by
ﬂushing a N2/CO2 (80%/20%) mixture.

Methane was observed in raw seaweed GC ﬁve days after the ﬁrst fermentation began,

4.8.3
Kinetic Constants
The kinetic constants were obtained by linear regression in total liquid products basis.
The linearization was developed in MS Excel spreadsheets, assuming 1st grade reactions
and considering the all the sugars and all the products as wholes (one reactant, one

The thermoacidic pretreatment provided the highest solubilisation of glucose (30%);
xylose (50%); fucose (37%); rhamnose (90%); mannose (57%); and mannuronic acid
(88%). Other sugars, such as galactose or guluronic acid, presented a release higher
(>100%) than the sugars detected in the raw seaweed after the SAH. This could be
explained by the degradation of the sugars present in the SAH samples, or by the overlap
of other compounds in the integration of the DAH samples.
If this inconsistency is
applied in all the pretreatments and for all sugars, the thermoacidic condition would
have provided the maximum release in galactose and guluronic acid as well.
Results of the hydrothermal pretreatment showed a minor eﬀect of the pretreatment
in the bioreactor batch, which resulted in a lower solubilisation for most of the sugars
(Table 11). Both pretreatment batches were carried out at the same conditions and ratio
solids:liquid, and the only diﬀerence was given by the volume of the batch (screening
batch: 0.6 L active volume in 1-L blue-cap; bioreactor batch: 1.2 L in 2-L blue-cap). The
hydrothermal pretreatment (screening) provided the highest release of galacturonic acid
(50%) and mannuronic acid, which resulted over the limit of the available in the seaweed
(>100%). However, diﬀerences between both hydrothermal batches in the mannuronic
release suggest a wrong integration or detection of the peak in the chromatograph, as
there were many unidentiﬁed compounds in the analysis – especially around sugar acids
retention times.
AAS pretreatments did not provide a degree of solubilisation as high as the ther-
moacidic and hydrotermal pretreatments (Tables 10 and 11).
Ammonia – and mild
alkaline- methods are pretreatments not only designed for the release of sugars. They
provide other eﬀects, as it could be swelling of the internal structure to make internal
sugars more accessible, or the removal of lignin [31, 32, 42] (see Table 3). All AAS con-
centrations provided a low release of glucose (1-2%), but had huge impacts in rhamnose,
galactose and guluronic and mannuronic acids. Xylose, fucose and mannose, among oth-
ers, were also released in signiﬁcant amounts (5-20%). AAS 32% provided an unusual
eﬀect on mannitol solubilisation, as it achieved a 74% that is not comparable with any
other pretreatment. The two AAS 25% batches resulted more similar among themselves
than the two hydrothermal. In this case, both pretreatments were prepared with the the
same volumes. The increase in ammonia concentration seemed to favor sugars release,
although the higher the concentration, the lower the diﬀerence between the eﬀects on
the biomass (overall, AAS 25% and 32% resulted very similar). AAS 25% provided a
slightly higher release than the other AAS concentrations for most of the sugars. Re-
sults from the ammonia tests carried out to the AAS pretreatments are listed in Table
9. The amounts are believed to be low enough to not produce inhibition during the
fermentation, although sugar-nitrogen complexes could have been formed [56].
In general, it can be observed that glucose was diﬃcult to solubilise in all pretreat-
ments except in the thermoacidic, while other sugars such as rhamnose or galactose
seemed to be easy to extract. Nevertheless, there is a signiﬁcant uncertainty in these
results. Several unidentiﬁed peaks were registered in the chromtography and some of
them had similar detection times than the known sugars and sugar acids.

In this study, thermal pretreatments, in acidic and neutral conditions, have provided
a better solubilisation than the alkaline at atmospheric temperature, although as com-
mented before, each pretreatment can enhance substrate consumption in a diﬀerent way.
Thompson et al. [17] recommended the use of thermal treatments with a T ∈[80, 180] ℃
to favor sugars solubilisation without the formation of inhibitors [17]. The same authors
defended that acidic treatments are more eﬃcient at removing lignin from the biomass
and depolymerising hemicellulose [17]. On the other hand, acid hydrolysis per se of-
ten generates inhibitory compounds and can damage the equipment [31, 32]. Alkaline
pretreatments present some advantages and disadvantages in comparison to acid agents
or thermal treatments. Alkaline treatments improve accessibility to the biomass (e.g.
swelling cellulosic structures), operate at milder conditions and can remove lignin and
hemicellulose without generating compromising inhibitors. However, it needs of longer
application times and can lead to soap formation [31, 32].
Acid pretreatments results more convenient and economically viable than other pro-
cesses [58]. Indeed, Park et al. [58] explored and optimised the application of acidic
hydrolysis in brown seaweed in order to prevent the formation of inhibitors and by-
products, such as furan aldehydes [58]. The model developed by the authors provided
an optimal HCl concentration of 0.1N applied for nearly 20 minutes at 140℃, becoming
more eﬃcient than higher/lower concentrations at diﬀerent temperatures and duration.
The pretreatment yielded 113 mg of reducing sugars/g of dried seaweed [58]. Hamouda
et al. [18] obtained a higher yield by pretreating brown seaweed with HCl 3% (264 mg/g
dry biomass) rather than NaOH 4% (197 mg/g) [18]. Azizi et al. [59] presented yields
of 120-200 mg reducing sugars/g dried biomass and 50 mg of glucose/g dried biomass
for the treatment of air-dried and washed brown seaweed, with the combination of an
acid pretreatment (H2SO4 0.15N) followed by an enzymatic hydrolysis [59].
The results obtained in this project from the thermoacidic pretreatment with HCl
0.15M (Table 11, tranforming the units: 3 mg reducing sugars/g biomass and 1.3 mg
glucose/g biomass) are far from the provided in Azizi et al. [59] and other references.
However, the diﬀerences can be give due to multiple reasons, such as the use of collected
and wet seaweed instead of harvested and dried and washed; the application of enzymatic
hydrolysis; or the use of a stronger acid. The eﬀect of the enzymatic pretreatment was
notable, as the glucose concentration reported before it was 2.4-fold lower than at the end
of their experiment [59]. At same conditions, the authors also reported a worse sugars
concentration for HCl pretreated biomass [59], and other studies found a higher VFAs
yield after fermentation by treating dried seaweed and applying enzymatic or alkaline
pretreatments [8, 11, 22].

remarkable, as this inoculum was designed for thermophilic operation in a biogas plant.

exhibited a peak on day 2 in all cases, but then became depleted in the thermophilic

towards the end of the fermentation. Mesophilic industrial consortia, whose ethanol pro-

signiﬁcant production of MCFAs.

Inoculum and Conditions Election
Several factors can be assessed to decide which condition suits better seaweed fermenta-
tion and VFAs production. Yield, fermentation length, productivity, product selectivity
or product concentration are some examples. In this case, the decision was made con-
sidering the four screening tests with heat-treated inoculum. Yields at the end resulted
higher for the mesophilic experiments, and so did the kinetic constant, but MCFAs
formation prevailed in the decision.
Thermophilic fermentation presented a higher selectivity and amount of caproic acid

Absence of Inoculum Heat-shock and Eﬀect on the Fermentation
Inocula for the two diﬀerent raw conditions were heat-treated to avoid methanogenesis
and direct the fermentation towards VFAs production. The industrial inoculum operated
at thermophilic conditions was utilised for the rest of the fermentations. Against all odds,
methane was formed on day 4 in the thermophilic ﬂasks, leading to the addition of BES
as methanogenesis inhibitor for the rest of the project.
The reason that explains heat-treatment ineﬃciency in the ﬁrst fermentation round
could be the time-span between its application and the set-up of the fermentations (2 -
3 days). BES addition invited to test the eﬀect of treating or not the inoculum before
the fermentation, as other bacterial families may have been inactivated by it.
A fermentation of raw seaweed with the industrial inoculum without heat treatment
was carried out. Results were collected in Table 12 and Figures 14 and 15. Yields and
productivities became the lowest among the experiments. The kinetic constant k resulted
very similar to the heat-treated inoculum, and the small diﬀerences could result from
a longer competition for the substrate among new diﬀerent cultures that were removed
with the heat-shock.
This case conﬁrmed the trend seen before for thermophilic fermentation. Generation
of H2 in this experiment was higher than at 37℃in the initial days, but the peak wa
signiﬁcantly lower than its homologous.

Biochemical Methane Potential (BMP) Test
A BMP test was carried out aside for raw and AAS 15% seaweed to evaluate the eﬀect
of the industrial inoculum at thermophilic conditions using seaweed as sole biomass. En-
gelsen [12] studied the digestion of the same seaweed (raw and pretreated with ammonia
15%) in mesophilic conditions with a diﬀerent inoculum, obtaining a production of 65±1
and 95±3 mL CH4/g TS for raw and AAS, respectively [12]. The AAS pretreatment
aﬀected positively the biomethane generation, entailing an ultimate increase of 46% over
the raw biomass[12].
The results provided in this assessment at thermophilic conditions and with the in-
dustrial inoculum used for the fermentations agreed on the yield upgrade by applying
AAS. However, raw seaweed yielded 129±13 mL CH4/g TS and AAS produced 139±12
mL CH4/g TS. Despite the production is superior to the obtained at mesophilic condi-
tions, the increment caused by pretreating the biomass is not as notable (8% increase).

The reason why yields and product distribution diﬀered among fermentations was
connected to the role of hydrogen and ethanol in the fermentation.
Hydrogen plays
as reducing agent in homoacetogenesis and carboxylates reduction into alcohols, at the
same time that is produced in the chain elongation process, short fatty acids oxidation
and/or by sugars fermentation (see Table 4). Acetate reduction into ethanol (or other
carboxylates into their corresponding alcohols) needs of H2 as electron donor [46] and
therefore, it could most likely happened when H2 concentration was high. On the other
hand, the ethanol produced during the fermentation is commonly consumed in the chain
elongation process [45, 46], which provides hydrogen as a product. With this relations
set, the amount of hydrogen generated by the fermentation is key if the gas is not
externally fed. While a high partial pressure boost homoacetogenesis and favors ethanol
production, it also reduces the probability of MCFAs generation as hydrogen is in the
products side of the reaction. For this reason, it can be demonstrated that the highest
hydrogen producer conditions (thermoacidic and hydrothermal, Figure 19) are the ones
with lower C5-C6 yields (Figure 21). Aside, they became the ones with the highest
distributions for alcohols. Sparging N2 caused the opposite eﬀect. If H2 partial pressure
is very low, one of the few reactions that may happen using H2 as reducing agent is
homoacetogenesis. Chain elongation was actually favored from this, as the culture will
try to cover the hydrogen demand in the broth by chain elongation of carboxylates with
ethanol. Thus, all ethanol present may be consumed in the fermentations with sparging
(as shows its distribution in Figure 20b or its proﬁle in Figure 24 Appendix A). The raw
fermentation without sparging presented a half-way distribution between the extremes:
H2 production was high enough to provide alcohols at the end of the fermentation, and
at the same time, low enough to present a signiﬁcant yield of MCFAs (Figure 19). In
conclusion, sparging may result a good option if the desire outcome is to obtain a higher
yield of MCFAs, while it should be avoided if the operation is oriented towards alcohols.
At practice, VFAs - or MCFAs with short chain- present several issues for their extraction
and often they are transformed into their equivalent alcohol so as to ease the separation
and save expenses, what could lead to the direct use of a thermoacidic system.
Thermoacidic fermentation turned out to be the fastest according to its kinetic con-
stant.
This fact may be connected with owning the highest degree of solubilisation
(Table 11) among the treatments tried, making sugars more accessible for the consortia
and fostering their consumption. It should be pointed out the case of glucose, whose
release has been extremely favored by this pretreatment and might have played a part
of the ﬁnal yield. Indeed, sugars solubilisation might be connected with hydrogen gen-
eration. While raw is the ﬁrst fermentation if reaching the maximum production of H2,

Conclusion
Raw and pretreated seaweeds were characterized and pretreatment eﬀects on COD and
sugars solubilisation were studied. All pretreatments favored sugar release, especially the
thermoacidic pretreatment (e.g. 30% glucose solubilisation). Increase in AAS concen-
trations resulted positively correlated to sugars solubilisation. The results were diﬃcult
to compare with the provided in literature, as in this case the biomass was not farmed
or harvested, and there was some uncertainty regarding the biomass and the results.
Screening tests were accomplished for raw and pretreated seaweed following an ini-
tial substrate concentration of 24 g TS/L. Raw was initially tested with two diﬀerent
inocula at mesophilic and thermophilic conditions. Mesophilic inocula provided higher
yields, but thermophilic temperature favored the formation of MCFAs (Figure 15b). The
thermophilic industrial inoculum was chosen for the rest of the experiments as MCFAs
are key intermediates in biofuels manufacture. From the diﬀerent ammonia concentra-
tions tested in the AAS pretreatment, AAS 25% resulted the most favorable for the
macroalgae fermentation (0.15 g products g VS−1). Fermentation of the hydrothermal
seaweed provided the highest yield of the screening tests (0.19 g g VS−1). Nevertheless,
no pretreatment provided a higher yield for MCFAs than the raw experiment (Figure
18). The fermentation of pretreated seaweed was performed for hydrolysates and total
solids, evidencing the contribution of the solid fraction to the production of VFAs.
Raw, AAS 25%, hydrothermal and thermoacidic seaweeds were scaled-up to 2.5L
bioreactors. Raw was tested with and without sparging of N2 to provide insights about
the eﬀect of hydrogen in the fermentation. AAS fermentation also happened with sparg-
ing of N2, but the hydrothermal and thermoacidic experiments were carried out without
H2 removal. Thermoacidic (0.25 g g VS−1) and hydrothermal (0.18 g products g VS−1)
conditions showed an improvement over the raw yield (0.17 g g VS−1). However, AAS
25% provided a higher yield for C5-C6 acids (Figure 21).
Hydrogen had a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the experiments.
High H2 partial pressure
favored the reduction of carboxylates into alcohols and prevented higher activity in
chain elongation. In contrast, low H2 avoided the formation of alcohols, but drove the
synthesis to MCFAs.
Higher acetate concentrations have beneﬁted chain elongation
reactions (negatively increasing their ∆G) (Figure 22). In the screening experiments,
higher MCFAs concentrations were given on the fermentations with an earlier rise and
depletion of H2 yield (thermophilic raw and AAS experiments, Figures 14 and 25a). For
the bioreactors, thermoacidic and hydrothermal fermentations provided the highest H2
production proﬁles (Figure 19) and the lowest C5-C6 yields (Figure 21). Conversely,
the highest yield for MCFAs were obtained with sparging and low H2 proﬁle (raw w/o
sparging).
Mesophilic fermentation of seaweed is proposed as an alternative for the next step
in the exploration of seaweed potential. Mesophilic inocula provided higher SFAs yields
than thermophilic in the raw screening tests. Scaling them up into the 2.5L bioreactors
with sparging of N2 could improve their MCFAs yield, specially in the case of the KT
inoculum, where signiﬁcant valeric and caproic acids were formed (Figure 15b).
