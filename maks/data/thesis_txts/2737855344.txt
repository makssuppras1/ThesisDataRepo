Abstract 
This thesis evaluated subjects' sleep quality and following-day performance influenced by air 
temperature in bedrooms in Copenhagen, Denmark. Twelve students of DTU, six females and six 
males, aged 24.5±2.8, participated in the experiment where they had to sleep in their bedroom for 
one week at their normal preferred temperature and another week with elevated temperature. 
Subjects were asked to sleep alone for four nights each week and keep their sleeping environment 
unchanged. They played a crucial role in this experiment as they were responsible for elevating 
the temperature, which they did by maximising their radiator thermostatic valve for the night.  This 
field study examined the impact of transitioning from normal to elevated temperatures on subjects' 
sleep and the next day’s performance, simulating overheating inside the bedroom. The experiment 
was done in three different periods; in the first period, the first four subjects participated; the next 
week of the first period, 5th and 6th subjects joined the study. The remaining subjects participated 
in the last period. Between the first two periods and the third period, the outside temperature was 
different, making the experiment difficult for the last-period subjects to have differences in 
temperature in their bedrooms. The objective evaluation was made using data from the “HOBO 
logger”, which were air temperature, relative humidity, and CO2 concentration measured and 
collected; the skin temperature was measured with “iButton”, and sleep records were recorded 
with “Fitbit” Actiwatch. Subjective evaluation was made using responses from morning and 
evening dairies, which included the acceptability scale of thermal comfort and air quality, 
temperature, humidity and air freshness perceptions, GSQs, and cognitive tests. All parameters 
were evaluated regarding temperature to identify how the subject’s physical and psychological 
responses would change while increasing temperature. The average temperature of the normal 
temperature setting for eight subjects with successful temperature change between two weeks was 
measured to be 22.7±2.1 °C, and the other week with elevated temperatures, the average 
temperature for all subjects was 25.8±1.8. Unfortunately, sleep quality did not significantly differ 
between conditions, and performance did not vary as subjects tended to improve daily test scores. 
The skin temperature reacted in the way that it was expected – in warmer bedroom temperatures, 
the skin temperature increased. Questionaries gave the outlook that subjects felt uncomfortable in 
warmer temperatures.  
Keywords: overheating, thermal environment, sleep quality, thermal comfort, cognitive test.

Resume 
Denne afhandling evaluerede emnet søvnkvalitet og præstation den følgende dag, påvirket af 
lufttemperatur i soveværelser i København, Danmark. 12 studerende fra DTU, heraf seks kvinder 
og seks mænd, i alderen 24.5±2.8 år, deltog i eksperimentet, hvor de skulle sove i deres

temperatur. Deltagerne blev bedt om at sove alene i fire nætter begge uger og holde deres sovemiljø 
uændret. De spillede en afgørende rolle i dette eksperiment, da de var ansvarlige for at hæve 
temperaturen, hvilket de gjorde ved at åbne helt op for deres radiatorers termostatventil over natten. 
Denne feltundersøgelse undersøgte virkningen af at skifte fra normale til forhøjede temperaturer 
på deltagernes søvn og på næste dags præstation, ved simulering af overophedning i soveværelset. 
Eksperimentet blev udført i tre forskellige perioder; i den første periode deltog de første fire 
deltagere; i den næste uge af den første periode deltog 5. og 6. deltager i undersøgelsen. De 
resterende deltagere deltog i den sidste periode. Mellem de to første perioder og den tredje periode 
havde udendørstemperaturen ændret sig, hvilket gjorde det vanskeligt for deltagerne i den sidste

koncentration blev målt og indsamlet; hudtemperaturen blev målt med “iButton”, og 
søvnregistreringer blev optaget med “Fitbit” Actiwatch. Den subjektive evaluering blev udført 
baseret på svar fra morgen- og aften-dagbøger, som inkluderede acceptabilitetsskalaen for termisk 
komfort og luftkvalitet, temperatur, luftfugtighed og luftfriskhedsopfattelser, GSQ'er og kognitive

fysiske og psykologiske reaktioner ville ændre sig ved stigende temperatur. Den gennemsnitlige

præstationen varierede ikke, da deltagerne havde tendens til at forbedre daglige testresultater. 
Hudtemperaturen reagerede som forventet – ved varmere soveværelsestemperaturer steg 
hudtemperaturen. Spørgeskemaerne gav det indtryk, at deltagerne følte sig utilpas ved varmere 
temperaturer.

Introduction 
Given the rapidly escalating impact of climate change, overheating has emerged as a significant 
global concern. Despite Denmark's location in the Nordic region, where occurrences of hot days 
(>30°C) are rare [1], temperatures are still on the rise. The most pessimistic scenario (SSP5-8.5) 
forecasts a temperature increase of 4-5°C for Denmark by the end of the 21st century [1], see Figure 
1. This combination of rising outdoor temperatures and insufficient cooling infrastructure is 
resulting in overheating in buildings, posing significant challenges to human health and well-
being. Considering that people spend most of their time indoors, with nearly one-third of their lives 
spent sleeping [2], it is crucial to create suitable sleeping conditions for everyone. The thermal 
environment and sleep are closely related, as the body's temperature regulation system is closely 
linked to the mechanisms that regulate sleep [3].  
This study aims to investigate how an increase in bedroom temperature during the night would 
affect a person's sleep and cognitive performance the following day. It begins with a literature 
review of previous research on similar topics, providing a strong foundation for the relevant issues 
analysed in the project. Following the literature review, a field study was conducted to test subjects' 
sleep quality, first at their normal and then at an elevated sleeping temperature. In total, 12 subjects 
were tested over three periods of two weeks, with one week at a typical setting and the other at an 
elevated temperature setting.

Literature review 
The literature review was crucial for laying the groundwork for this project by delving into past 
research and identifying potential areas for improvement. An extensive investigation will be 
conducted on sleep and temperature to identify pivotal elements and evaluate previous study 
results. 
2.1 
Importance of the topic 
Sleep is an essential aspect of our lives, serving as a pivotal contributor to physical and mental 
well-being. It holds immense significance as it allows our bodies to recharge, promoting mental 
clarity, restoring energy levels, and helping to manage daily stress and anxiety [5]. The sleep topic 
is necessary to investigate more as one-third of adults are affected by sleeping disorders, with 20% 
of it being insomnia, which includes difficulty falling asleep, keeping asleep and early morning 
awakening [6].  Many things can prevent people from getting a good night's sleep, including stress, 
diet, and the temperature of their bedroom. Although some factors, such as stress, can be 
challenging to manage, the temperature of a person's bedroom is much easier to control. The 
temperature of our sleeping environment is vital in achieving quality sleep, as it impacts the 
thermoregulatory control centre, also known as POAH. This control centre regulates both our body 
temperature and our sleep cycles. Interestingly, as we progress through different sleep stages, our 
thermoregulatory response also changes [2].  
The year 2021 highlighted the importance of considering indoor climate, as most people spent 
most, if not all, of their time at home. Before this, building planners didn't pay much attention to 
overheating if people would spend their days outside of their homes as usual [7]. It is crucial to 
address this issue, particularly during extreme weather events such as heatwaves [8]. Ensuring 
adequate cooling measures are in place is essential to maintaining comfortable indoor 
temperatures, as excessively high indoor temperatures can have detrimental effects on health, 
disrupt comfort, and impede restfulness during the night [9].  
Climate change is currently posing a significant challenge in the modern world. As temperatures 
rise everywhere, especially in more urbanised areas facing the heat island effect [10]. Buildings 
struggle to keep cool, resulting in uncomfortable living conditions for people [11]. This can 
negatively affect sleep, as increased heat at night can raise heart and sweat rates, creating a thermal 
load that interferes with deep, restful sleep and promotes wakefulness [12]. The latest studies show 
that there is no standard temperature suitable for everybody, as every person is different and, 
therefore, prefers different bedroom temperatures while sleeping [2]. Since each person has unique 
preferences, no fixed temperature guarantees comfort for all. A strategic way to improve sleep 
quality and save energy is to consider everyone's behaviours and preferences. Customising the 
bedroom temperature to meet specific needs can provide a comfortable sleeping environment, 
minimising the need for excessive heating or cooling. This customised approach caters to 
individual preferences, improving sleep quality while conserving energy.

2.2 
Climate change and overheating 
Climate change indicates long-term shifts in temperature and weather patterns [13]. While it may 
occur naturally, over the past two centuries, its escalation can be attributed to human activities 
such as the combustion of fossil fuels, deforestation, and agricultural practices, coinciding with 
the beginning of the industrial era [14]. Summer in some regions has become intolerable for 
individuals unaccustomed to such conditions. Consequently, the hot summer of 2003 claimed over 
35,000 lives in Europe due to extreme heat stress [15].  
The outlook for climate change is increasingly frightful. Projections indicate that if current trends 
persist in human activities, temperatures could rise by 2.6 to 4.9°C by 2100. Urban areas will face 
even greater challenges due to the urban heat island effect, characterised by higher temperatures 
compared to rural areas. This phenomenon has been extensively studied in 400 cities worldwide, 
revealing anticipated temperature increases ranging from 5 to 10°C, which will impact indoor 
environments and clothing requirements and lead to heightened energy consumption for cooling 
[16].  
Flores-Larsen's research in Argentina [17] predicts a 23-59% decrease in heating energy 
consumption by 2080 and a 360-790% surge in cooling energy usage. This poses a significant 
issue as the decrease in heating energy is outweighed by the substantial growth in cooling demands. 
Similarly, Wand and Chen's study in the US [18] found that by 2080, there will be a net increase 
in primary energy required for heating and cooling, with some cities facing challenges due to 
inadequate natural ventilation for buildings. Buildings are designed to withstand different weather 
conditions but often lack natural energy efficiency. Older buildings contribute to high energy 
demands and overheating due to poor insulation. Retrofitting older residential properties could 
mitigate overheating risks and decrease cooling demands, especially considering that 50% of the 
residential building stock in the EU was built before energy efficiency regulations in the 1970s  
[19], [20]. 
Researcher Attia defines cooling resistance as the building system's capacity to maintain initial 
design conditions during disturbances such as heat waves or power outages [21]. The greater the 
resistance of the cooling strategy, the better it can sustain a comfortable and healthy environment 
for building occupants [22]. In a case study in Belgium [23], researchers examined a Passive House 
to explore the impact of climate change on occupant thermal comfort and the risk of overheating. 
They found that buildings with lower ventilation rates are more vulnerable to overheating with 
rising outdoor temperatures. Interestingly, a higher ventilation rate decreased the building's 
vulnerability to climate change by 53%. 
Various solutions can be implemented to mitigate overheating in buildings. These include using 
dynamic insulation materials and designing the building's orientation to facilitate passive cooling  
[24]. The simple design of the orientation of the building plays a crucial role in addressing 
overheating issues; for instance, a passive cooling solution could involve placing bedrooms in the 
north [20]. Additionally, an effective ventilation system and the use of a variable refrigerant flow

2.3 
Influence of human activity on sleep 
This thesis primarily focuses on the environmental factors within the bedroom that affect sleep. 
Other significant factors influencing sleep quality, such as caffeine consumption, physical activity, 
dietary habits, and alcohol and smoking tendencies, must also be considered. However, factors 
beyond personal control, such as stress, allergies, or medical conditions, will not be examined. 
Firstly, the most apparent factor that the general population believes affects sleep is caffeine, which 
is consumed by approximately 80% of the public [25]. It has long been a conventional belief that 
a person should avoid consuming coffee in the evening, as it may disrupt falling asleep or decrease 
sleep quality. This thinking holds partial truth, as evening consumption of caffeine can indeed 
postpone the sleep onset and reduce sleep depth in most cases[26]. This occurrence arises from the 
peak effect of caffeine, which causes peak wakefulness approximately 30-75 minutes post-
consumption [27]. Evening caffeine intake correlates with a longer SOL and decreased TST, SE, 
REM, and light sleep stages, although it remains unrelated to WASO or deep sleep [28]. However, 
when discussing daytime caffeine intake, its impact on night sleep quality appears less noticeable 
among regular caffeine consumers who are healthy and naturally good sleepers [26]. It can be 
concluded that caffeine may not pose a significant detriment when consumed earlier in the day, 
highlighting the importance of timing. Unfortunately, a notable portion of the daily caffeine intake 
among regular consumers occurs during the evening hours [26]. 
Another essential factor that undeniably affects sleep quality is alcohol consumption. Poor sleep 
health, which includes aspects such as shortened sleep duration, difficulties in falling or staying 
asleep, irregular sleep patterns, and decreased alertness, has been identified as a risk factor for 
individuals who drink alcohol [29]. A study conducted on college students revealed that one in 
four participants experienced poor sleep quality, and 36% of heavy drinkers failed to get the 
recommended minimum of 7 hours of sleep per night [30]. The issue of poor sleep, particularly 
prevalent among heavy drinkers, has significant public health costs, impacting entire populations 
beyond just individuals or families [31]. 
The relationship between food and sleep is vital. Hunger often accompanies restricted sleep, and 
inadequate sleep correlates with increased obesity risk [32]. Poor sleep patterns may lead to 
unhealthy dietary habits, and vice versa [33]. A balanced diet has the potential to enhance sleep

fats, resveratrol, polyphenols, and antioxidants [34], [35]. Plant-based proteins, rich in tryptophan,

Despite the belief that evening exercise negatively impacts sleep, evidence from 23 studies

2.4 
Indoor climate influence on sleep 
The relationship between comfort and sleep quality is significant. With the evolving nature of 
work, people often experience less physical exhaustion, making comfort a crucial factor in 
facilitating sleep. Thermal comfort, encompassing air temperature, mean radiant temperature, air 
velocity, and humidity, is an essential element influencing a person's ability to achieve restful sleep 
[39]. Research indicates that an optimal thermal environment reduces movement during sleep, 
improving sleep quality. Studies have shown that higher air velocity can hinder sleep, particularly 
in hot and humid conditions and during later sleep periods [40]. Additionally, a shift from a 
thermally neutral room temperature can lead to increased wakefulness and decreased REM and 
SWS in semi-nude individuals [2]. Moreover, a humid sleeping environment can elevate heart rate, 
sweat rate, and thermal load, leading to reduced SWS and increased wakefulness [41]. Despite the 
multifaceted nature of indoor climate, temperature remains the predominant factor influencing 
sleep quality, complemented by other contributing factors. 
2.5 
Next day’s cognitive performance 
The next day’s cognitive performance does not directly result from discomfort in the indoor 
environment. Uncomfortable air temperature could disturb sleep, decreasing the next day's 
cognitive performance. Some studies investigated cognitive performance, which resulted in a 
reduction for older adults when sleep was poor [42]. The study done measuring the effects of poor 
sleep recorded that adolescents who reported poorer sleep quality had struggles in more decision-
making; in general, poorer sleep was associated with more apathy and less self-esteem when 
making decisions [43].  
A combination of appropriate indoor climate parameters can increase people’s cognitive 
performance as they sleep better and are well-rested. The study tested how air quality in the 
bedroom affects sleep and next-day cognitive performance; it resulted in a better ability to 
concentrate and logical thinking with better air quality as the CO2 level was lower [44].

was established by adjusting it for two and a half hours throughout both experiments. The 
participants remained seated during the experiments, and their skin temperature, rectal 
temperature, metabolic rate, and evaporative weight loss were all measured. 
Results: Research revealed that skin temperatures were notably lower during the night compared 
to the day (P<5%). The difference was approximately 0.4°C, and there was also a decrease in 
metabolic rate, although not as pronounced. Females exhibited lower evaporative loss and 
metabolic rate than males (P<5%) and slightly lower skin temperatures. Despite these gender 
differences in skin temperatures, there was no significant variation in temperature preference 
between males and females. 
Insights: Using a previous research method, the study was conducted in a controlled chamber with 
easily adjustable thermal conditions. An exciting aspect of this study is its consideration of 
potential gender differences and their potential influence. While the study indicated that other 
periods were less significant for analysis, it suggested that full-day measurements would provide 
more precise insights into how and when individuals experience skin temperature changes. 
The effect of deviations from personal room temperature preference on sleep and 
performance (2015) [45] 
Description: The research was conducted at Aarhus University, where 20 participants were enlisted 
to explore the impact of ambient temperature in the sleeping area on the subjects, its influence on 
sleep quality and efficiency, and its effects on the following day's performance. The study occurred 
in an older dormitory with substantial heat loss and fully controllable heaters. 
Results: In the study, participants consistently reported feeling warmer in warm conditions and 
colder in cold conditions. Generally, they experienced better GSQ scores, sleep quality, and short-
term memory in normal conditions compared to warm conditions, and they also felt less SBS. 
However, the study also revealed that participants perceived a shorter sleep latency in normal 
conditions, with females showing better short-term memory and males reporting better sleep 
quality and feeling more rested in the morning. It was noted that all subjects experienced fewer 
SBS symptoms and perceived better air quality in cold conditions compared to warm conditions. 
Interestingly, there were no differences in sleep efficiency between the conditions. The study did 
not identify an optimal temperature for all subjects, underscoring the intricate relationship between 
temperature and sleep quality.

Effect of thermal environment on sleep quality in actual bedroom in summer by sleep stage

Description: During August and September 2015, a field survey was conducted in Japan with 16

measurements for five nights, during which the entire measuring period was considered. This

better sleep in the IoT-based controlled room, with only one participant experiencing better sleep

waking phase of sleep, but the temperature needs to be increased as sleep progresses to REM

The effects of air temperature on sleep quality (2021) [49] 
Description: The field study was conducted in Denmark, with ten students (four females and six 
males) participating over four nights. The first night served as an adaptation period, while the 
second, third, and fourth nights were conducted under cool, neutral, and warm conditions, 
respectively. The study meticulously recorded parameters such as temperature, relative humidity,

Results: The participants' experiences played a crucial role in this study. They reported better air

than maintaining a constant temperature throughout.

Results: Temperature is the most significant indoor factor influencing sleep quality, outweighing 
the impact of other factors. This study recommends a combination of 20°C temperature, 55%

clarification.

3 
Hypothesis 
This research study explores the potential impact of raising bedroom temperature from the usual 
comfortable sleeping temperature on people's sleep. Existing literature suggests that a deviation 
from the normal thermal environment can lead to disrupted sleep, which in turn can result in poorer 
cognitive performance and increased tiredness the next day, see paragraph 2.6. Consequently, it is 
important to examine whether an elevated bedroom temperature influences sleep quality and, if 
so, to what extent. Furthermore, if air temperature does not affect sleep quality, will it still impact 
next-day cognitive performance? The study outlines two primary hypotheses and one additional 
hypothesis; see Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2: Map of hypothesis. 
1st hypothesis: The bedroom temperature influences sleep quality. People tend to sleep better at 
their normal bedroom temperature. The sleep quality is decreased if the bedroom temperature is 
elevated from the normal temperature.  
2nd hypothesis: The bedroom temperature influences the next day's cognitive performance. The 
next day's cognitive performance decreases if the bedroom temperature is elevated from the normal 
temperature.  
Additional hypothesis: The bedroom temperature influences indoor climate perception and 
comfort. If the bedroom temperature is increased, indoor climate perception and comfort is 
negative.  
In addition to these hypotheses, the study will investigate different sleep parameters and bedroom 
temperature relations, as well as the relation between skin temperature and bedroom temperature.

4 
Methods 
This thesis section will describe a field study in the individual bedrooms of Denmark’s Technical 
University students. In total, 28 volunteers wanted to participate in the study, and 12 were 
recruited. Due to time constraints and the need to engage more volunteers directly while testing 
various conditions for participants over a longer period, personal participant bedrooms for the field 
study were selected. The bedroom with an adjustable radiator temperature was chosen to create 
warmer conditions for the participants. 
4.1 
Preparation and procedure for the experiment 
The research project commenced on January 29th, and preparations for the experiment began 
immediately. The entire month of February was dedicated to organising and recruiting participants. 
The actual experiment took place over three periods, each lasting two weeks. The first period began 
on March 4th with four participants, the second on March 11th with two additional participants, and 
the third after the Easter vacation on April 8th, when the remaining six participants joined the study. 
The university provided six sets of measuring tools, limiting the maximum number of participants 
in each period to six. Due to slower than expected recruitment, the first measuring period had four 
participants, and two more participants joined the experiment the following week. Recruitment 
efforts included utilising various online platforms such as university student forums, student 
groups online, and physical posters (see Appendix 1) in student dorms and campus buildings. The 
posters included a barcode linking to an online form through which potential participants were 
required to answer specific questions (see Appendix 2). Additionally, each participant was 
required to have a heater that could be regulated to increase room temperature and preferably live 
alone in their bedroom. The overall procedure of the experiment is presented in Figure 3.

The experiments took place in participants' bedrooms. Before the start of the experiment, all 
participants were briefed on the study, given instructions, and provided with a guide (see Appendix 
3). They were also asked to complete a General Survey (see Appendix 4). Throughout the study, 
participants played an essential role in assisting, and there was ongoing conversation and guidance 
during all measuring periods. Each participant was required to sketch their bedroom, which was

4.5.3 Sleep records 
The sleep data was collected using the graphs 
and metrics the "Fitbit" Actiwatch provided. 
These graphs indicate the time range of the 
actual sleep period each night and display the 
beginning and end of sleep. Each participant 
wore the watch before falling asleep, enabling it 
to measure sleep stages, including Deep sleep, 
Light sleep, REM, and any brief awakenings 
during the night. Following each night, the data 
was collected to calculate sleep parameters 
such as sleep duration, latency, efficiency, and 
wakefulness after sleep onset. See Figure 12 for visualising the process of sleep quality evaluation.  
Sleep onset latency (SOL) is the time it takes for a person to fall asleep and was gathered directly 
from the graph by taking the first awake stage until the next sleeping stage. For adults, a SOL of 
no longer than 30 minutes is considered appropriate [54]. 
Wakefulness after sleep onset (WASO) is the total time a person is awake during sleep, calculated 
from "Fitbit" data by considering all "awake" stage time, excluding SOL, and the time when the 
person is finally awake. For adults, a WASO of no longer than 20 minutes is considered appropriate 
[54]. 
Sleep efficiency (SE) was calculated from the ratio of total sleep time (sleep duration) to total time 
in bed. For young adults and adults, an SE higher than 85% is considered appropriate [54].  
For raw sleep records and graphs of each subject each night, see Appendix 12.

recorded a person’s sleep start and end right. 
• Most of the first nights of elevated temperature condition week were eliminated as it was 
not heating up that quickly.

• Cognitive performance test, see section 3 hypothesis 2.

• Subjective humidity sensation vote.

• Thermal comfort and thermal sensation vote, as it was expected that subjects would feel

Discussion 
This section discusses the results and outlines observations. The following paragraphs also discuss 
failures and limitations, making most parameters insignificant.  
7.1 
Change in temperature 
The research investigated the impact of increasing bedroom temperature on sleep and next-day 
cognitive performance. Each participant experienced a unique variance in the two temperature 
settings, as normal temperature settings varied across subjects; see thermostatic valve positions for 
each participant in Appendix 6, and the maximum variance was achieved by adjusting the 
thermostatic valve instead of setting the temperature on the thermostat. Some participants achieved 
significant differences between the two temperature settings; for instance, Subject 1 had a 6.6°C 
variance. On average, participants with successful temperature changes experienced a 3.1°C 
difference, insufficient to induce a heatwave effect or cause significant discomfort. Even though 
the average difference is higher than a previous similar study [45], data analysis revealed no 
significant difference in sleep quality and next-day cognitive performance between the two 
temperature settings. However, there was a notable difference in subjective thermal comfort and 
perception responses. Participants reported feeling warmer in elevated temperatures and more 
neutral in normal temperature settings. 
7.2 
Interpretation of results 
7.2.1 Sleep 
Sleep quality was investigated in two ways: first, by looking at graphs and calculating SOL, 
WASO, and SE, and second, by the GSQ score that they got after filling out the GSQ questionnaire. 
Both methods lacked significance in the difference between the two temperature settings, possibly 
due to various factors that need further exploration. 
Before the experiment, most participants had sleeping problems, as indicated by their high PSQI 
scores. In fact, only one participant was identified as a healthy sleeper according to the PSQI, see 
Figure 29. Previous studies on similar topics (see Table 1) have indicated that they recruited only 
participants with low PSQI scores, as those with scores higher than five are considered to have a 
sleep disorder. However, recruiting only participants with low PSQI scores was impossible in this 
study. Therefore, our subjects were poor sleepers to begin with. It can be argued that the 
temperature change in the experiment could not have disrupted their sleep more than it is naturally 
disturbed. 
==PAGE:55==

Figure 29: PSQI scores of subjects. 
In the literature review, it was noted that several factors can impact sleep quality, including the 
consumption of caffeinated beverages, alcohol, food, and exercise, see section 2.3. One study 
indicated that individuals who consumed alcohol experienced difficulties falling asleep [29]. 
Despite advising participants against the use of coffee or alcohol before bedtime, it was 
challenging to enforce these recommendations. Some participants reported using these substances 
for multiple nights, as evidenced by their diaries. However, it remains unclear whether other 
participants also consumed substances that could affect sleep quality but did not report it. This 
highlights the difficulty of controlling such variables in field studies. Participants who had trouble 
falling asleep (high SOL) and staying asleep (high WASO) mentioned using either coffee or 
alcohol. It's worth noting that the consumables and an individual's emotional and mental well-
being can influence sleep, a factor to consider given that the recruited subjects were students 
experiencing some stress. Additionally, the absence of a consistent sleep routine could have further 
impacted their sleep quality. 
The National Sleep Foundation has established guidelines [54] for appropriate sleep parameters 
across different age groups. After analysing the sleep parameters of the participants, it is evident 
that the time it took for the subjects to fall asleep (SOL) was suitable, as it was under 30 minutes, 
indicating that they did not have difficulties falling asleep regardless of the bedroom temperature. 
However, the time spent awake after sleep onset (WASO) for most participants, except for Subject 
2, during the normal temperature setting week, exceeded the recommended threshold of 20 
minutes. On average, all subjects were awake for 36 minutes in the normal temperature setting 
week during sleep, which falls within an uncertain range but is not considered inappropriate. In 
the elevated temperature setting, the average time spent awake after sleep onset was 41 minutes, 
which is inappropriate. These findings suggest that temperature might not have contributed to 
disturbances, as participants experienced high WASO in both weeks, depending on the 
temperature. Except for subject 7, sleep efficiency for all subjects in both weeks was acceptable, 
as it exceeded 85%, indicating that temperature changes did not significantly impact sleep 
efficiency for the subjects.

7.2.2 Cognitive performance 
Cognitive performance data showed no significant differences between the two temperature 
settings either. Cognitive tests revealed that subjects consistently achieved higher scores each time 
they were administered. According to the literature review in the section 2.5, discomfort in the 
thermal environment minimises an individual's cognitive performance the following day. Air 
quality, notably lower CO2 levels, appears to significantly influence concentration and logical 
thinking. It is possible that during the warmer temperature week, some individuals opened 
windows to cool down, leading to improved air quality and, subsequently, better cognitive 
performance scores. Another study focusing on older adults found that uncomfortable 
temperatures resulted in poorer sleep, impacting cognitive performance the next day [42]. 
However, in the current study, the recruited subjects were young adults/adults, and the 
temperatures were not disruptive enough to affect sleep and subsequent cognitive performance. 
7.2.3 Subjective evaluation 
Indoor climate parameters such as thermal comfort, perceived air quality, thermal and humidity 
sensation votes, and air freshness perception were asked in daily diaries in the morning and 
evening. The results from the subject evaluation were the most promising, showing that subjects 
who experienced significant changes in temperature during those two weeks felt that their 
perceptions were affected. In the investigation, eight subjects had successful changes in 
temperature between weeks. Participants 1, 3, 5 and 6 showed that they felt warm after sleeping in 
the elevated temperature week, showing that they followed protocol and kept the bedroom 
environment unchanged for the whole night; the other four who had a change in temperature during 
the weeks did not report that felt slightly warm or more neutral which means they could have used 
air conditioning or opening of the window to feel more comfortable, which implies overheating 
was not achieved. In both morning and evening, they preferred normal temperature compared to 
elevated; they felt more comfortable, the air quality was better, they felt more neutral, and the air 
was fresher while sleeping. Still, none of the participants were blinded by the experiment's purpose 
and controls; they were responsible for changing and keeping the temperature as asked. Therefore, 
it can be that subjects knew that their temperature was elevated and psychologically thought that 
they felt a certain way. The same happened to the previous study, which was also done in the 
subjects’ bedrooms, as subjects were the ones who changed temperatures [45]. Therefore, 
subjective evaluation is not the strongest argument that subjects felt uncomfortable during the 
elevated temperature week. It can only prove that not all subjects followed the protocol and kept 
the environment unchanged. Also, it can be debated that they slept without a duvet for the rest of 
the night if they felt too hot.

7.3 
Comparison with past studies on the topic  
Comparing the results from this study to past studies has proven challenging since previous 
researches were conducted in controlled chambers where temperature and other indoor climate 
parameters could be closely regulated. In contrast, this experiment took place in individual 
bedrooms to minimise the discomfort of participants sleeping in unfamiliar settings. The aim was 
to gather as much data as possible over two weeks. A similar study conducted in Denmark [45] 
involved investigating students' dormitories, with a temperature range very comparable to this 
study. These earlier findings echo current results, as participants in both studies reported feeling 
warmer as the temperature increased. Interestingly, both studies found insignificant effects on 
sleep quality and cognitive performance. Another related field study in Japan [40] discovered that 
sleep quality was higher at 26°C, which they considered a neutral temperature. In this study, the 
temperature of 26°C was classified as high compared to normal temperatures. It is worth noting 
that previous studies conducted in controlled environments found significantly better sleep quality 
in thermally neutral settings, although results vary across studies (see paragraph 2.6). 
7.4 
Limitations 
In this thesis, some limitations were discovered during experiments and after analysing the data: 
• The primary limitation was the lack of control over the environment, with subjects tasked 
with managing the temperature. The temperature in the bedrooms of four subjects did not 
vary between the two settings, and when there was a difference, it was often negligible. 
The absence of temperature variation could be attributed to individuals adjusting to 
enhance their comfort, such as changing clothes and bedding or opening windows in 
response to elevated temperatures. 
• Figure 30 illustrates the variation in outdoor temperatures during three measurement 
periods, highlighting a potential impact on indoor temperature. The first period, from 
March 4th to 15th, involved subjects 1-4; the second period, from March 11th to 22nd, 
involved subjects 5 and 6; and the final period, from April 8th to 15th, involved subjects 7-
12. The first and second periods had similar outdoor temperatures, whereas the third had 
consistently high outdoor temperatures. Individual outdoor temperatures were collected 
using the living postcodes of the subjects and nearby weather stations, as detailed in 
Appendix 13. Notably, during the warmer weather of the third period, bedroom 
temperatures showed only minor differences compared to the colder weather period, 
suggesting a potential influence of outdoor temperature on indoor temperature. 
==PAGE:58==

Figure 30: Outside temperatures for all nights in three periods. 
• The study's findings are limited as the temperature range was relatively narrow, with the 
highest temperature recorded being 29°C for only one subject, which may not accurately 
represent real-life heatwave conditions. Additionally, participants' bodies may have been 
able to adjust to the slight air temperature increase during the experimental period. 
Furthermore, the duration of the measurements was brief, potentially limiting the ability to 
observe the full extent of the impact; a more extended experimental period could yield 
more comprehensive results. 
• The participants were not blinded to the experiment's aim or to the modified temperature 
for one week, as they were responsible for adjusting the temperature. Therefore, being 
aware of temperature changes could impact their subjective responses. Perhaps they 
prepared for higher temperatures by switching to lighter clothing or a lighter duvet, and so 
on. 
• The number of recruited subjects was too small. A significant amount of data was discarded 
during the sorting process, and four subjects were ultimately eliminated from the final data 
analysis due to an experiment failure. The thesis utilised statistical power to determine the 
necessary sample size for future studies to yield meaningful results. The calculated power 
value for objective results was found to be substantial, while the effect size was minimal. 
However, it was determined that around 20 subjects would be sufficient to achieve 
significant results for subjective responses. See all calculated power for each parameter in 
Table 18. 
7.5 
Summary of discussion  
The study found no significant differences in sleep quality or next-day cognitive performance 
based on bedroom temperature. However, subjects who experienced successful temperature 
changes showed slightly improved sleep quality. Further investigation is warranted for potential 
substantial outcomes. For improvements and recommendations for future study, see the section 8.

Recommendations 
Just because the study was not the expected way, thinking about what might have gone wrong and 
how things could have been done led to ideas and recommendations for future investigations on 
this or similar topics. The listed recommendations will be discussed below: 
• Number of subjects 
Getting quality data with a small group is challenging, as every experiment has misfortunes, and 
some data will be lost; therefore, getting more subjects provides more data and avoids gaps in data. 
In this experiment, the sample size decreased to just eight subjects who successfully raised their 
temperature the following week. Analysing the subjects by gender would have been interesting, 
but out of the eight, only three women remained after sorting, making it impossible to conduct 
gender-based analysis. 
• Variety of subjects 
In this research, the participants were exclusively students, many of whom lacked consistent 
sleeping patterns. This factor influenced the outcomes, prompting consideration for expanding the 
study to include older adults as well. To enhance the study's impact, it is advisable to recruit 
individuals who typically sleep in colder temperatures, thus yielding more pronounced differences 
when assessing the influence of temperature changes.  
• Stricter selection 
As this thesis had a time limit, it was hard to recruit people longer and select only healthy subjects. 
All subjects who were sleeping alone and had no serious health problems were invited. However, 
after seeing their PSQI, it was clear that almost all had trouble with sleep. It would be a huge 
improvement to have only subjects with lower than 5 PSQI. 
• Diaries and cognitive performance tests 
Daily reporting and test participation are necessary to prevent data loss. Filling diaries at least 
twice a week is not enough. Participants should also practice the cognitive tests before the 
experiment begins or have the tests changed daily to avoid improvements over time. 
• Control 
Simple thermometers would be helpful to see if the temperature changes between weeks. Data 
transfer each week could also improve the quality of the experiment and avoid data loss. 
• Real overheating 
It could be interesting to plan a similar study for one week in cold weather conditions and another 
week when the heat wave and the overheating would be real. 
• Different intervention methods 
As was proven by this study and previous similar study [45], changing the position of the 
thermostatic valve may not be the most effective way to increase the temperature.

Conclusion 
In this study, a combination of objective and subjective assessments was used to collect 
comprehensive data. Various parameters, including sleep quality and next-day cognitive 
performance, were measured and evaluated based on participants' daily diaries. The research aimed 
to explore the impact of different bedroom air temperatures on sleep quality and next-day cognitive 
performance. 
The study lasted for two weeks. During the first week, participants experienced their typical 
bedroom temperature, followed by an increased temperature in the second week. Only eight out of 
the 12 subjects experienced a change in temperature between the two weeks. The average normal 
bedroom temperature was recorded as 22.7±2.1°C, while the average elevated temperature was 
25.8±1.8°C, resulting in an average difference of approximately 3°C. 
The study was unable to reject the main null hypotheses, which state that increasing the 
temperature has a negative effect on sleep quality and cognitive performance. While the study did 
not yield conclusive results, it does suggest that within the tested temperature range, there may not 
be significant effects on sleep quality and cognitive performance. Reasons for this could include 
quick adaptation to the temperature, the small sample size, and the fact that the participants may 
have already had poor sleep quality to begin with.
