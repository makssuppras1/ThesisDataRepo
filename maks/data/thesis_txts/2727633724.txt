1.1
Background
Continue writing:
Systolic array is chosen as the hardware architecture for matrix inversion due to its promising
characteristics. Systolic arrays offer regular and pipelined architectures, which are well-suited
for parallel and efficient data processing. They provide high throughput, data reuse, and power
efficiency, making them an attractive option for compute-intensive tasks like matrix operations.
However, executing matrix inversion on a systolic array is not a straightforward task. Matrix
inversion involves complex operations, such as division, square roots, and trigonometric functions,
which require additional hardware support and specialized algorithms. The challenge lies in de-
signing a systolic array that can efficiently handle matrix inversion operations of varying sizes
without underutilizing the hardware.
To address this challenge, flexibility needs to be introduced into the systolic array architecture.
This flexibility allows the hardware to adapt to different input matrix sizes and efficiently utilize
the processing elements. By incorporating flexible hardware designs and control strategies, the
systolic array can dynamically adjust its configuration based on the input matrix size, optimizing
resource utilization and minimizing underutilization.
The choice of systolic array as the hardware architecture for matrix inversion is driven by its
potential to provide the required flexibility. The regular and pipelined nature of systolic arrays
enables efficient parallelism and data movement, which are essential for matrix inversion opera-
tions. Furthermore, the scalability of systolic arrays allows for handling matrices of different sizes,
accommodating the evolving requirements of baseband processing as new standards emerge.

Matrix Inversion Algorithm
This section aims to provide a comprehensive analysis of algorithms and architecture in the
literature review. The focus will be on matrix inversion algorithms, including LU, QR, SVD, and
Cholesky decomposition, which are commonly employed in practical engineering applications.
Special attention will be given to SVD and Cholesky decomposition, where new and modified
algorithms will be introduced alongside their corresponding hardware architectures as the project
progresses.
Traditional methods for finding the inverse matrix of a simple matrix are no longer suitable
for multidimensional matrices encountered in practical engineering implementations. To address
this, researchers have proposed the Gauss-Jordan elimination method, which reduces the coeffi-
cient matrix to a unit array, eliminating the need for back substitution and enabling direct calcula-
tion of the inverse matrix. However, this method often incurs significant time overhead, requires
substantial resources, and can be computationally expensive. In response, matrix theory offers
triangular decomposition methods for matrix inversion. These methods involve decomposing the
matrix into multiple matrices that are easier to invert, such as triangular matrices or diagonal ar-
rays. Commonly used matrix decomposition methods include Cholesky decomposition [1] and QR
decomposition-based matrix inversion methods [2]. Some basic matrix decompositions are:
• LU decomposition: A = LU, where where L and U are lower and upper triangular matrix.
• Cholesky decomposition: A = LLH, where L is lower triangular matrix and LT is the
Hermitian transpose of L.
• LDL decomposition: A = LDLH, where L is lower triangular matrix and D is diagonal
matrix.
• QR decomposition: A = QR, where Q is a unitary matrix and R is an upper triangular
matrix.
• SVD decompostion: A = USV T, where U and V are the left and right singular vectors of
A, and S is the diagonal matrix.
The process of solving equations using backward-substitution is an efficient technique when
implemented properly. However, it is important to note that LU decomposition is applicable only
to square matrices. On the other hand, Cholesky decomposition is specifically designed for positive
definite matrices, which ensures that certain conditions are met for the decomposition to be valid.
LDL decomposition, on the other hand, can be applied to symmetric matrices. QR decomposition
and SVD are more general approaches that can be applied to both square and rectangular matrices,
making them versatile methods for matrix decomposition.
The LU decomposition, QR decomposition, and SVD are commonly used for calculating ma-
trix inverses. The computation of matrix decomposition is inherently more complex than simple
matrix multiplication, involving a large number of calculations that may require iterative processes.
However, despite the complexity, matrix decomposition calculations often exhibit certain patterns
that can be leveraged to design efficient hardware architectures. By understanding these patterns

Back substitution is a method used to solve an upper triangular system of linear equations.

size of the system of equations. This complexity arises from the need to perform n iterations, each
involving a summation over n terms.
Forward and back substitution play a crucial role in numerical algorithms such as LU de-
composition, Cholesky decomposition, and solving systems of linear equations in sparse matrices.
These methods allow for the efficient solution of linear systems by leveraging the triangular struc-
ture of the matrices involved.

Cholesky Decomposition
The Cholesky decomposition was introduced by Andre-Louis Cholesky in his work. Cholesky
decomposition is specifically designed for symmetric positive definite matrices. It decomposes a
symmetric positive definite matrix A into the product of a lower triangular matrix L and its trans-
pose. Cholesky decomposition is computationally more efficient compared to LU decomposition
for symmetric positive definite matrices.
Cholesky decomposition offers several advantages when dealing with symmetric positive def-
inite matrices. It provides a computationally efficient factorization that requires only half the op-
erations compared to LU decomposition. This efficiency arises from the special structure of the
decomposition, utilizing the lower triangular matrix. As a result, solving linear systems and per-
forming other computations become simplified.

The main advantage of LDL decomposition is that it can be computed more efficiently than

2.5.1

numerical technique used to compute the QR decomposition of a matrix. This method involves a
sequence of orthogonal transformations called Givens rotations, which transform the matrix into
an upper triangular form. The Givens rotation is a simple orthogonal transformation that zeros out
specific elements of the matrix.

The numerical calculation of the singular value decomposition (SVD) of a matrix has been
extensively studied. In 1965, Golub and Kahan proposed a numerically stable algorithm for com-
puting the SVD [5], which served as the foundation for many subsequent SVD algorithms, includ-
ing those still used in LINPACK. The Golub-Kahan algorithm involves bidiagonalizing the matrix
as an initial step.
Another approach to achieving a stable SVD calculation is the Jacobi method. This method
iteratively applies plane rotations to the matrix, resulting in a stable computation of the SVD.
While the convergence speed of the Jacobi method may be slower compared to other methods, it
offers the advantage of being able to be calculated in parallel. This parallelizability compensates
for its potential drawback in terms of speed. Furthermore, the Jacobi method can achieve higher
precision without requiring the bidiagonalization step performed by Golub-Kahan’s algorithm.

Hardware Architecture
Systolic arrays, first introduced by H. T. Kung and Charles E. Leiserson in 1979, offer a
solution to the limitations of datalow architectures. These arrays utilize a regular grid of processing
elements (PEs) with static interconnections to achieve high power efficiency and peak throughput.
Unlike dataflow architectures that require complex microarchitectures and reconfigurable in-
terconnections, systolic arrays adopt simplistic processing element designs and fixed interconnec-
tions. This approach reduces power consumption by minimizing memory access and data move-
ment. As a result, systolic arrays outperform datalow architectures in terms of power efficiency.
The systolic array architecture features pipelined operations, where data flows through the
array in a synchronized manner. Each processing element performs specific computations on the
data as it passes through, and the output becomes the input for neighboring elements in subsequent
clock cycles. This pipelined and synchronized processing allows for continuous data processing
with high throughput and low latency.
The regular structure of systolic arrays enables efficient parallelism and data reuse. The com-
putational units within each processing element operate synchronously, and data moves through
the array without the need for complex routing or arbitration. This regularity and locality of data
movement contribute to the array’s high performance.
To design systolic arrays, the dependence mapping method, introduced by P. Quinton, is
often used. This method characterizes systolic arrays that are solutions to algorithms expressed as
systems of uniform recurrent equations. It involves finding a timing function for the computations
and an allocation function to map the equations onto a finite architecture.

3.5
Hardware Architecture for SVD
The Jacobi SVD method was introduced by Forsythe and Henrici [14] where they used left
and right multiplications of a matrix of size m ˘A n by some unitary transformation matrices to
calculate SVD of the matrix. These unitary matrices are identity matrices except for 4 entries at
positions (p, p), (p, q), (q, p) and (q, q). This method solves the SVD problem by iterating over
enough sweeps including all possible pairs of p and q. In 1982, Brent et.al. proposed the so-
called Brent-Luk-VanLoan (BLV) architecture [15], a distributed memory multiprocessor system,
to calculate the SVD of a matrix of size m x n using one-sided Jacobi rotation. They used a new
parallel ordering instead of the conventional cyclic ordering based on which they could introduce
parallelism into the SVD problem. However, their design is not scalable, being of order O(n2).

Systolic arrays have been widely used in various matrix decomposition methods, such as LU
decomposition, Cholesky decomposition, and QR decomposition. These arrays offer regular and
pipelined architectures that enable efficient parallelism, data reuse, and high throughput. They also
provide power-efficient solutions by simplifying processing element designs and reducing memory
access and data movement.
For LU decomposition, the SALIMS architecture combines LU decomposition, backward and
forward substitutions, and matrix multiplication in an array of processing elements. It achieves ef-
ficient computations while storing the necessary information for solving linear systems and finding
the matrix inverse.

enhanced efficiency and reduced interconnect overhead.

However, it is not scalable in terms of performance efficiency and resource utilization, potentially
limiting its efficiency as the problem size increases.
In conclusion, systolic arrays have proven to be effective architectures for various matrix de-
composition methods, offering power efficiency, high throughput, and parallel processing. How-
ever, the choice of the specific array design should consider factors such as hardware utilization,
scalability, and resource requirements to optimize performance for a given problem size.

nication. OS excels in computation efficiency but demands a larger number of PEs, particularly
for larger output feature maps.
Figure 13: Weight stationary [22]
Weight Stationary (WS) mapping allocates each weight element to a dedicated Multiply-
Accumulate (MAC) unit within the array. Once weights are mapped, they remain fixed until all
computations relying on them are completed. Inputs are streamed in from the left edge, and partial
sums are stored within the array. Reduction operations occur across the MAC units, necessitating
communication between neighboring PEs. WS reduces the number of required SRAM banks com-
pared to OS, as partial sums are stored instead of output pixels. However, the storage of partial
sums increases the implementation cost.

5.1
Dataflow Patterns
Figure 14: Input stationary [22]
Input Stationary (IS) mapping shares similarities with WS, but with input pixels "pinned" to
the PEs and weights streamed in. Each column in the array is assigned to a convolution window,
defining the set of input pixels required to generate a single output pixel. Input pixels are streamed
from the left edge, while weights are streamed from the top edge. Reduction operations take
place within columns, and convolution windows are maintained until all computations depending
on them are completed. IS also reduces the number of required SRAM banks compared to OS,
similar to WS. However, the cost and runtime of IS mapping vary depending on the workload, as
the timing and order of computations differ.
Each dataflow mapping offers unique advantages and trade-offs in terms of computation ef-
ficiency, resource utilization, and implementation costs. OS excels in computation efficiency but
requires a larger number of PEs. WS reduces SRAM bank requirements, but the storage of partial
sums increases implementation costs. IS also reduces SRAM banks but exhibits varying costs and
runtimes based on the workload. The selection of a dataflow mapping depends on specific applica-
tion requirements, such as resource constraints, computation efficiency goals, and implementation
cost considerations.
The OS dataflow in a systolic array is well-suited for matrix decomposition methods like LU,
Cholesky, and QR decomposition. It dedicates each processing element (PE) to generate a specific
output pixel, leading to improved computation efficiency and reduced latency. In contrast, the WS
dataflow, commonly used in CNNs, optimizes weight reuse but may not be as efficient for matrix
decomposition.
The OS dataflow allows for efficient resource utilization by parallelizing computations across
dedicated PEs. It minimizes data movement within the array, reducing communication overhead
and enhancing computation efficiency. This aligns well with matrix decomposition tasks that pri-
marily focus on computations involving the input matrix.

Tiling is not typically used in the context of Forward Substitution (FS) in LU decomposition.
Tiling is a technique commonly employed in matrix multiplication to break down large matrices
into smaller submatrices for efficient processing in systolic arrays. It allows for localized data
reuse and minimizes data movement within the array.
In cases where the size of the matrix exceeds the capacity of a systolic array, tiling can be
employed as a technique to facilitate the computation of Forward Substitution (FS). Tiling involves
dividing the large matrix into smaller submatrices, known as tiles, that can fit within the systolic
array. By processing these tiles sequentially, the overall computation can be effectively performed.

the previous tile. It may be advantageous to include the extra cycle for triangular tiles as well to
simplify the hardware implementation.

6
Conclusion
Theoretical analysis of matrix inversion algorithms and hardware architectures for matrix de-
composition reveals that the LU, Cholesky, and LDL algorithms have lower complexity and are
suitable for smaller to moderate-sized matrices. On the other hand, the QR and SVD methods ex-
hibit higher complexity but are valuable for larger matrices. The analysis of hardware architectures
considers factors like the number of processing elements, clock cycles, complexity, and scalability.
Based on these considerations, the Cholesky and LDL algorithms emerge as promising options due
to their low complexity and scalability.
Future Work In order to advance the proposed Cholesky and LDL systolic array designs,
tiling and dataflow analysis are essential. Tiling techniques need to be explored to efficiently
process large matrices by breaking them down into smaller submatrices. Additionally, a thorough
analysis of dataflow patterns is necessary to optimize the computation flow within the systolic
array.
Another crucial aspect to consider is the clustering analysis for scalability. The systolic array
should be designed in a way that allows for easy scalability, accommodating matrices of varying
sizes. Clustering techniques can help identify the optimal arrangement of processing elements to
achieve efficient parallelism and resource utilization.
To develop a comprehensive understanding of the systolic array’s performance, utilization
and granularity analysis is required. This analysis involves evaluating the utilization of system
resources and the granularity of computations to identify potential bottlenecks and areas for im-
provement. It aids in optimizing the design and enhancing the overall efficiency of the systolic
array.
To validate the proposed designs and assess their feasibility, a high-level synthesis hardware
implementation is necessary. This step involves converting the design specifications into a hard-
ware description language and synthesizing it to obtain a hardware implementation. Through this
process, the functionality and performance of the systolic array can be verified, allowing for ad-
justments and refinements as needed.
In summary, to advance the proposed systolic array designs for Cholesky and LDL decom-
position, tiling and dataflow analysis, clustering analysis, utilization and granularity analysis, and
high-level synthesis hardware implementation are crucial steps. These steps contribute to refining
the designs, optimizing performance, and ensuring the feasibility of the proposed systolic array
architectures.
