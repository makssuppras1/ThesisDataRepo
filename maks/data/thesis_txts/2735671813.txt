Abstract
This thesis explores the potential of using a SE3-equivariant diffusion model to identify potential
inhibitors for BCL-2 and KRAS mutations. By leveraging advanced computational strategies,
this research aims to bridge the gap between theoretical models and practical drug discovery,
generating novel molecular candidates targeting these pivotal cancer-related proteins. The study
successfully identified several promising molecules, such as MKG12C1, MKG12A2, MKG12S2,
MKG12V2, MKG12R1 and MKG13C1 for KRAS mutations, alongside MB2 and MB3 for
BCL-2, showcasing significant binding affinities and desirable physicochemical profiles. While
not every molecule outperformed existing inhibitors, their identification could still offer insight
into potential substructures that could be used in future drug development.
==PAGE:4==
Limitations
This thesis, while showcasing the potential of generative artificial intelligence (AI) in the realm
of drug discovery, encountered certain limitations that should be acknowledged to provide a
comprehensive understanding of the context and scope of the research made.
A significant limitation of this study was the depth of molecular and biochemical knowledge.
Originating from a background in machine learning and mathematics, the thesis strives for clear
and contextual discussions on molecular interactions and properties. However, this background
may lead to limitations in the depth of certain explanations, particularly in the details of
molecular science. The primary focus was to illustrate AI’s capability in identifying inhibitors
for BCL-2 and KRAS mutations, which might have led to a less detailed examination of the
complex interactions between molecules.
In continuation of this, the results section of the thesis could also have been enriched with a more
detailed analysis of the functional groups and molecular composition of the identified molecules
deemed interesting. Such an analysis would offer deeper insights into the specific elements
contributing to a molecule’s binding affinity and its physicochemical properties, enhancing the
understanding of what makes certain molecules more promising.
A decision was made to exclude CDK4/6 from the thesis, despite generating molecules for these
proteins. Although CDK4/6 findings were part of the research, they did not yield as promising
results compared to those related to BCL-2 and KRAS mutations. Including an in-depth analysis
of CDK4/6 would have extended the thesis without substantially contributing to its main focus.
Therefore, they were excluded. However, for the sake of completeness and transparency, the
results obtained for CDK4/6 have been included in the appendix, where readers can access the
full results for all proteins, Appendix D.1.

Introduction
The search for novel therapeutic agents, particularly those that can inhibit crucial cancer proteins
such as BCL-2 and KRAS, remains a pivotal challenge in the fight against cancer. These proteins
play critical roles in the development and progression of various cancers, making them prime
targets for drug discovery. However, the complexity of molecular interactions and the specificity
required to effectively target these proteins pose significant challenges.
The emergence of generative artificial intelligence (AI) models in drug discovery heralds a
promising avenue for accelerating the identification of potential inhibitors. These models leverage
computational power to simulate and predict molecular structures and their interactions, identi-
fying molecules with potential inhibitory properties against significant cancer proteins. This
thesis employs a SE3-equivariant diffusion model, a state-of-the-art model for particularly image
generation but also applied broadly within generative modeling, to generate drug candidates
targeting BCL-2 and KRAS mutations, which play crucial roles in various cancers. By aiming
to bridge the gap between theoretical models and practical drug discovery, this work contributes
to the evolving field of AI-driven therapeutic discovery. It also demonstrates the model’s capa-
bility to predict molecules with high binding affinities and favorable physicochemical profiles,
showcasing the potential of generative AI to open new pathways in the design of inhibitors.
Additionally, this thesis aims to demonstrate and emphasize the potential of AI in such significant
field of research, as the ongoing fight against cancer is.

Theory
2.1
Molecular Interactions
Molecular interactions are fundamental in all biological functions and systems. They define
the structure, stability, and dynamics of molecules and their interactions with each other. The
various types of molecular interactions are described in the following sections.
2.1.1
Covalent Bonds
A covalent bond forms when two atoms share a pair of electrons, allowing both atoms to achieve
a stable electronic configuration. The shared electrons are attracted to the nuclei of both atoms,
creating a strong bond that holds the atoms together. The electrons can either be shared equally,
like an oxygen molecule O2, or unequally, as observed in water H2O, where atoms with higher
electronegativity, such as oxygen or nitrogen, attract the shared electrons more strongly, resulting
in a polar molecule. [4]
2.1.2
Ionic Bonds
Some groups of molecules can have a degree of polarity, that makes them either completely
donate or accept electrons, creating ions. A positively charged ion (cation) is formed when an
atom donates an electron, whereas a negatively charged ion (anion) is formed when an atom
accepts an electron. Oppositely charged ions can form ionic bonds with each other which are
the strongest among non-covalent bonds. [4]
2.1.3
Van der Waals forces
Van der Waals forces are a collection of intermolecular forces, including dipole-dipole interactions,
dipole-induced dipole interactions, and dispersion forces [7].
Dipole-Dipole Interactions
Dipole-dipole interactions occur between molecules with permanent dipole moments, meaning
that the molecules have a partial positive and negative region. The positive region of one molecule
is attracted to the negative region of the neighbouring molecule, resulting in the dipole-dipole
interaction. [4]
Hydrogen Bonds
Hydrogen bonds are a particularly stable type of dipole-dipole interaction, where an electronega-
tive atom, such as nitrogen (N) or oxygen (O), interacts with a hydrogen atom bonded to another
electronegative atom. Hydrogen bonds are stronger than regular dipole-dipole interactions but
weaker than covalent or ionic bonds. [4]
Dipole Induced Dipole
Dipole-induced dipole interactions happen when a polar molecule makes a nearby non-polar
molecule temporarily polar. This happens because the polarity influences the distribution of
electrons in the non-polar molecule and causes it to become temporarily polar. This causes a
weak interaction between the polar and the non-polar molecule. [7]
Dispersion Forces (London Dispersion Forces)
2
==PAGE:9==
Dispersion forces are present in all interactions between molecules or atoms. They emerge
because of fluctuations in the electron distribution of a molecule or atom. This creates temporary
dipoles, which causes temporary dipoles in neighboring molecules and in that way creates an
attraction. These forces are the least powerful among all inter molecular forces but especially
gain importance in larger molecules. [22]
Pi Interactions
π-interactions are a type of attractive force that involves an aromatic molecule. Aromatic
molecules contain at least one aromatic ring, which is typically constructed of carbon atoms
with alternating single and double bonds. This arrangement creates a delocalization of electrons
above and below the ring. When another molecule or atom with a suitable charge approaches, it
results in an attractive force with these regions. This can occur with a metal, an ion, simple
molecules or other aromatic molecules. [8]
2.1.4
Ion-Dipole Interactions
An ion group can also interact with a polarized group in an ion-dipole interaction. In this type
of interaction, the ion is attracted to the end of the dipole molecule that is oppositely charged.
Water is frequently involved in ion-dipole interactions because all charged or partly charged
molecules are soluble in water. [4]
2.1.5
Hydrophobic Interactions
While earlier described interactions focused on attractions between atoms and molecules, hy-
drophobic interactions is a repulsion between a water molecule and a hydrophobic molecule.
This occurs because the hydrophobic molecule is unable to disrupt or replace the hydrogen
bonds between water molecules. Consequently, hydrophobic molecules tend to cluster together,
minimizing their exposure to water. [4]
2.2
Proteins
Proteins are essential for most biological functions within the human body, playing a crucial
role in cellular repair and growth, metabolism, immune defense, hormone production, and the
transmission of nerve signals. In cancer cells, specific proteins become dysregulated or mutated,
leading to abnormal growth and cell divison.
2.2.1
Protein Structure
Proteins are created by linking amino acids together through peptide bonds to form peptide
chains. A peptide bond is a specific covalent bond between the carboxylic group of one amino
acid and the amino group of another amino acid. Figure 1 illustrates how the peptide bond is
created.
Figure 1: Peptide bond of two aminoacids [4]
3
==PAGE:10==
If a peptide chain consists of more than 50 amino acids and has a well defined structure it is
generally considered a protein, while a peptide chain with less than 50 is considered a peptide.
Proteins may consist of multiple peptide chains linked by interactions between their side chains.
After the formation of a peptide chain it still has ionizable properties in its N-terminal or
C-terminal ends, where free amino and carboxyl groups are present. Its polar structure in its
carbonyl (-C=O) and amide (-N-H) groups within the peptide chain makes it able to form
hydrogen bonds. [4]
2.2.2
Protein Folding
The three-dimensional structure of a protein is critical in defining its functionality within the
body. Its structure is defined by the amino acid sequence, which determines the folding in a
number of hierarchical steps. First a protein’s primary structure is defined by its linear amino
acid sequence connected by peptide bonds. Then its secondary structure is defined through
hydrogen bonds between the carbonyl and amide groups. The tertiary structure is defined by
diverse interactions between the amino acids side chains and finally its quaternary structure is
defined, and is the assembly of multiple peptide chains through interactions of their amino acid
side chains. Each level of structure plays a crucial role in the ultimate shape and function of the
protein. [4]
Primary Structure
A proteins primary structure is its linear amino acid sequence, which dictates the higher-level
structure. The ordering and composition of amino acids is determined by the gene that encodes
for the protein. [4]
Secondary Structure
The secondary structure is determined by how the amino acids of the proteins peptide chains
create hydrogen bonds. The hydrogen bonds arise between the polar carbonyl- and amide-group
of two different peptide bonds in the peptide chains. The initial three dimensional structure of
the protein emerges from these hydrogen bonds, leading to the peptide chain forming helices,
sheets, turns, bends or loops. The most common and well defined secondary structures are the
α-helix and the β-sheet. [4]
The α-helix has the shape of a spiral and is formed due to hydrogen bonds between a carbonyl
group and an amide group that is located 4 amino acids later in the peptide chain. The amino
acids side chains will point out from the spiral and are a crucial factor in stabilizing the structure
of the spiral. [4]
The β-sheet has the shape of a zigzagging sheet. This is formed by the hydrogen bonds between
peptide chains that run parallel to each other. The peptide chain segments can either be from
the same or a different peptide chain, and can run parallel or anti parallel to each other. Mostly
anti parallel β-sheets will be bound together by β-turns, which consists of 4 amino acids creating
a 180o degree turn, while parallel β-sheets will be bound by β-loops which are more flexible and
longer chains. The side chains of the amino acids that contribute to the β-sheet will be pointing
either up or down from the sheet. [4]
Tertiary Structure
The tertiary structure of the protein represents the three-dimensional conformation of the
peptide chain. This is determined by the interactions of the amino acid side chains. These
4
==PAGE:11==
interactions include hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions and disulfide bonds.
The hydrophobic interactions are playing a particularly important role in determining the folding
of the peptide chain into a functional structure, as they are pushing non-polar residues away
from water. The disulfide bonds, which are covalent bonds formed between the sulfur atom
of two cysteine amino acids, will typically form late and stabilize the tertiary structure of the
peptide chain. [4]
Quaternary Structure
When a protein consists of multiple peptide chains, it has a quaternary structure. The qua-
ternary structure is defined by the interactions between the amino acid side chains of different
peptide chains. In a multi peptide chain protein each peptide chain is responsible for a specific
functionality, and together they combine into the overall function of the protein.
2.3
Molecular Descriptors
Molecular descriptors are an important aspect of evaluating a molecules potential as a drug
candidate. This thesis focuses on a molecules binding affinity through docking, its quantitative
estimate of drug-likeness (QED), its lipophilicity (LogP), its oral bioavailability (Lipinski and
Veber rules) and its synthetic accessibility (SA).
2.3.1
Molecular Docking
Molecular docking is a computational technique utilized in drug discovery to predict the optimal
conformation and binding affinity between two molecules. In this project, the two molecules
under study will be a target protein intended for inhibition and a smaller inhibitory molecule.
Docking consists of two main components, a sampling algorithm and a scoring algorithm. The
sampling algorithm explores and optimizes the orientation and position of the inhibitor molecule
and the scoring algorithm evaluates the binding affinity of the inhibitor molecule with the given
position and orientation. The two methods are used in an iterative optimization approach, where
the sampling algorithm proposes a position and an orientation and the scoring algorithm predicts
the binding affinity. This process continues a certain amount of time, a certain number of steps or
until some stopping criteria is met. The docking method used for this project is the QVina 2 al-
gorithm, which is an improvement of QVina 1 that is originally derived from AutoDock Vina. [29]
AutoDock Vina [15]
AutoDock Vina uses a combination of a global and local search algorithm to estimate the best
binding affinity for a given molecule and protein. The pseudo code for the global optimization
algorithm can be seen in Figure 2a. In each iteration of the global optimization algorithm a
random change in position, orientation or conformation of the current solution is made. Then
the local optimization algorithm is used on the changed version, making smaller adjustments,
until a new local minima is found. The metropolis criterion is used to decide whether to accept
the new candidate solution. It favors new solutions that exceed previous solutions, but also
allows for exploration by occasionally accepting sub optimal steps. If the solution is accepted
and currently the best solution further refinements are made by an additional local search. If
the score is still the best it is stored, and a new iteration is started. Pseoudo code for the local
search algorithm can be seen on Figure 2b, and uses the Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno
(BFGS) algorithm, which relies on first- and second-order derivatives of the scoring function,
with regards to position, conformation and orientation, to find a local minima. [15]

2.3.2
Wildman-Crippen LogP [33]
The Wildman-Crippen LogP is a measure of a molecules lipophilicity. This is an important
measure in drug discovery as it is highly related to a molecules bioavailability. LogP is calculated
by first determining the concentration of a molecule in its neutral (non-ionized) state within two
distinct phases: an organic solvent (typically octanol) and water. This is achieved by allowing
the molecule to reach equilibrium between these phases, and then measuring how much of the
molecule is dissolved in each. The LogP value is then obtained by taking the logarithm of the
ratio of these two concentrations. In drug discovery, an optimal LogP value indicates a molecule
has the ideal balance of lipophilicity, enabling it to permeate cell membranes effectively while
also ensuring that it can be transported to its target. The LogP value also plays a significant
role in the absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties. A high
lipophilicity can also result in the molecule binding to other hydrophobic targets than the desired,
which creates an increased toxicity risk.
Specifically a molecule should not have a LogP of over 5, as this often contributes to high
metabolic turnover, poor oral absorption and increased risk of toxicity [23]. Generally molecules
with a LogP greater than 1 and less than 4 are more likely to have optimal physiochemical and
ADME properties for oral drugs. When looking at the mean LogP of patented drugs across 18
of the biggest pharmaceutical companies they are typically within a range of 3.5-4.5. [23]
2.3.3
Lipinski and Veber rules [20]
The Lipinski Rule of 5 (Ro5), is a simple yet effective method to evaluate drugs in early stages of
drug discovery. The Rule of 5 (Ro5) is based on data from the World Drug Index (WDI), focusing
on molecules with a United States Adopted Name (USAN) or International Non-proprietary
Name (INN), indicating their advancement to phase 2 in the drug approval process. Out of 3,704
compounds identified for clinical exposure, 2,245 were selected based on the absence of ’POLY’,
’PEPTIDE’, ’QUAT’ text strings or the O=P-O fragment. By comparison with the entire WDI
dataset, molecular weight, LogP and the number of hydrogen donors and acceptors were deemed

significantly different. From the specific values of the selected compound the specific cutoff
values where chosen, and the Ro5 states ”Poor absorption or permeation are more likely when:
There are more than 5 H-bond donors (expressed as the sum og OHs and NHs), The molecular
weight is over 500, the LogP is over 5, or there are more than 10 H-bond acceptors (expressed as
the sum of Ns and Os).” Lipinski paper p. 7-8. [20]. Veber’s research build upon the Lipinski
Ro5, arguing that a compound which meet the criteria of 10 or fewer rotatable bonds and a

2.3.4
Synthetic Accessibility Score [13]
The Synthetic Accessibility Score (SA Score) is a quantitative metric designed to predict, how
easily a molecule can be synthesized. This metric is extremely helpful in early stages of drug
discovery, as molecules with higher synthetic accessibility generally require less time, effort and
resources to synthesis. By prioritizing molecules with higher synthetic accessibility, it decreases
the timeline and reduces the cost of developing a drug. The synthetic accessibility score is a
combination of a fragment score and a complexity penalty, Equation 3.
SAscore = fragmentScore −complexityPenalty
(3)
The fragment score is based on the frequency of each of the molecular fragments in a large
fragment database (based on PubChem). The score assumes a correlation between the frequency
of the fragment and its synthetic accessibility. The fragmentScore will be positive for common
fragments and negative for rare fragments.
The complexity penalty is a measure for the complexity of a molecule. This includes ring
complexities, stereochemistry, macrocyclic structures and the overall molecular size, which
results in the score increasing for molecules that are difficult to synthesize.
When the complexityPenalty has been subtracted from the fragmentScore, the result, which
commonly lies in the range [-4,2.5], is multiplied by -1 and scaled to be between 1 and 10.
Molecules that are easily synthesized will have a value closer to 1 while difficult to synthesize
molecules will have a value closer to 10.

2.4.3
SE(3)-Equivariance
A function is considered equivariant to a group of transformations if applying the transformation
and then the function is the same as applying the function and then the transformation. In this
project, the focus will be on SE(3)-equivariance, covering all possible rotations and translations
in a three-dimensional space. All components of the model must satisfy this property, in regards
to the positions of the molecular structures in 3D. This consistency is crucial as if the model were
to yield different results for the same molecular complex in various orientations or translations,
it would significantly hinder the models ability to generalize, and would ultimately make the
model unable to perform the task. This concept is shown mathematically in Equation 23.
Rf(x) + t = f(Rx + t)
(23)
Where R ∈R3×3 is a matrix fulfilling the orthogonality condition RT R = I and the determinant
condition det(R) = 1. The orthogonal condition ensures that the matrix preserves its inner
product, which in the context of three-dimensional transformations implies that R does not
change angles and distances between points in the molecular system. The determinant condition
ensures that the system is not scaled. In Eq. 23 t is added column wise and represents the
translation component. In practice it means that applying a rotation or translation to the whole
molecular system should not impact the final evaluation of likelihood and that for a specific
protein pocket x(P) a ligand x(L) should be sampled with the same probability as its transformed
counterpart Rx(L) + t if the protein pocket had the same transformation. In SE(3)-equivariance
reflections are omitted due to their association with chirality, which can significantly change the
properties of a molecule. [17] [27]

probabilities, and the mean vector of these distributions transforms equivariantly with respect
to rotations [27]. The update step in each EGCL layer is also seen to be rotationally equivariant.

which do not change under rotations. The update of the position of a node depends only on
other nodes, and as all nodes are rotated, the change in position is consistent with the rotation
of the entire system.

is at all times during the sampling process maintained with a center of mass (CoM) at zero.
This ensures that translations do not affect the system as the entire system is held in place.
The EGCL layers are also translation equivariant because the inputs to the MLP consists solely
of features and distances, which are unaffected by translations. The position update is also
unaffected by translation, as it is dependend only on the relative position to other points.

2.5
Equivalent Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model with inpainting
The final model used to generate molecules according to a protein binding pocket is an Equivalent
Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (EDDPM), which utilizes the concepts and methods
explained in Sections 2.4.1-2.4.4. In the DiffSBDD paper [27] whose implementation is used in
this project, they test two different models, a conditional DDPM that based on the protein pocket
performs the denoising steps, and an inpainting model that approximates the joint ligand-pocket
distribution. The inpainting model was seen to generally have a better performance and this
was chosen for this thesis.
The molecular system of a protein and ligand is modeled as a graph. Each node vi in the graph
represents an atom and is comprised of a feature vector hi and a coordinate vector xi. The
feature vector is an embedding generated by a trainable MLP, representing atom attributes such
as charge, type, and residue. Within the graph, each node of the ligand is interconnected, while

protein nodes are connected solely among themselves if their distance is less than 4 angstroms,
and to ligand nodes within a distance of 7 angstroms. This approach is adopted to mitigate
computational burden, considering that a fully connected graph becomes excessively demanding

conditioning on the protein binding pocket but also on predetermined substructures that may
be essential in some problems.
2.5.4
Resampling
When sampling with the inpainting model, the authors of the DiffSBDD paper [27] observed
that the model sometimes produced molecules that where dislocated in regards to the protein
target pocket. This happened due to the method trying to merge a newly generated segment
with an independently sampled noisy representation of the fixed regions. Despite the models
effort to align and integrate the two parts, this is difficult as a new noisy representation of the
fixed part is sampled in each step. To fix this, Lugmayr [21] proposed a simple resampling
strategy. This method repeatedly applies the model at every denoising step prior to moving
to the next time step. This technique gives the model more time to adapt to the conditional
information, and makes it able to refine the generated part to align with the fixed part. This
approach, while effective, adds a computational burden by extending the total computation
with a factor of resampling steps. However, [27] demonstrates that introducing resampling
steps r while decreasing the number of diffusion steps T proportional, still results in increased
performance and saturates at r = 10.
2.5.5
Scaling Features
Since coordinates, atom types, and charges are different physical entities, it’s beneficial to
establish a relative scale among them, and in the ”Molecule Generation in 3D” paper [17], they
determined that scaling the node type features by a factor of 0.25 and the atom charge features
by a factor of 0.1 relative to the coordinates x enhances model performance.
2.5.6
Number of Atoms
During the process of creating molecules, the total number of atoms within the molecule plays a
critical role. Throughout the training phase, this number is presumed to be known. However,
when generating molecules tailored for a specific pocket during sampling, the precise number
of atoms required is not known and has to be determined prior to using the model. In the
DiffSBDD paper[27], they utilize the training dataset to construct a joint distribution of ligand
nodes NL and pocket nodes NP . Based on this, the conditional distribution p(NL | NP ) was
calculated, and gives a distribution over potential ligand sizes given the pocket size. With this
conditional distribution established, the size of the ligand can either be sampled or given as a
manual input prior to using the model.

3
Methodology
3.1
Proteins
This section explains the functionalities of wild-type BCL-2 and KRAS as well as their connection
to cancer. BCL-2 is a key protein in cell survival and is closely associated with lymphoma.
KRAS mutations, are a big reason for unchecked cell proliferation and are commonly found in
various types of cancer.
3.1.1
BCL-2 family
Apoptosis, also known as programmed cell death, is a process essential for the elimination of
cells that are damaged, diseased, or no longer needed. Cells can start the apoptosis process
in two ways, either by receiving external (extrinsic) or internal (intrinsic) signals. The latter
of which is regulated by a balance of BCL-2 family proteins. This family includes both pro-
survival proteins, like BCL-2 and BCL-X, and pro-apoptotic proteins like BAX and BAK. In a
healthy cell, anti-apoptotic proteins bind to pro-apoptotic proteins, ensuring that apoptosis is
not initialized. However, if the cell becomes damaged, diseased, or no longer receives survival
signals, pro-apoptotic proteins are upregulated disrupting the balance between anti-apoptotic
and pro-apoptotic proteins. This frees up the pro-apoptotic proteins to initiate a chain of events,
that ultimately leads to cell death. [10]
3.1.2
Function of BCL-2 Protein in Cancer
The BCL-2 protein plays a crucial role in cancer by preventing apoptosis, allowing cancer cells to
survive longer than they should. BCL-2 is particularly associated with various types of cancers,
including chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) [5] and follicular lymphoma [9]. In CLL BCL-2
is often overexpressed due to genetic mutation or other abnormalities. In Follicular lymphoma,
which is the most common type of non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma, the translocation t(14;18) is the
reason for BCL-2 overexpression. The overexpression of BCL-2 ensures that the balance between
anti-apoptotic and pro-apoptotic proteins leans towards preventing apoptosis. Thus, BCL-2
contributes to the growth and survival of cancer cells, highlighting its potential to be targeted
in the development of cancer therapy.
To target BCL-2 in cancer treatment, therapies like BH3 mimetics are developed. These drugs,
such as Venetoclax, are designed to inhibit and inactivate BCL-2, and are called BH3 mimetics
as they mimic the function of the natural BCL-2 antagonist and pro-apoptotic protein BH3. By
mimicking the actions of BH3, these treatments inactivate BCL-2 and makes the cell trigger
apoptosis. [12] This strategy has proven effective, notably with Venetoclax in treating CLL.
However, challenges such as the emergence of the G101V mutation in some patients failing
treatment, alongside the discovery of G101A and F104L mutations, have been noted. [3] In
this thesis, our goal is to generate new BH3 mimetic molecules that are effective against both
mutated and non-mutated forms of the BCL-2 protein.
3.1.3
KRAS Protein
KRAS is a crucial protein within the RAS superfamily, a large group of proteins known for
their role in transmitting signals within cells. These proteins are characterized by their ability
to bind to GTP (guanosine triphosphate) for activation and GDP (guanosine diphosphate) for
deactivation. Specifically the KRAS protein is involved in signaling pathways that regulate cell
division, growth, and death. Upon activation by GTP, KRAS undergoes a structural change
that initiates a sequence of signaling pathways within the cell. Afterwards its GTPase activity
converts GTP to GDP, returning KRAS to its inactive state and stops it from sending signals.
20
==PAGE:27==
This cycle of activation and deactivation is a vital regulatory mechanism that ensures cellular
processes are tightly regulated, preventing uncontrolled cell growth and division. [18]
3.1.4
Function of KRAS Protein in Cancer
While the non-mutated form of KRAS is essential for regulating cellular functions, mutations in
the KRAS gene leading to altered KRAS proteins can cause uncontrolled cell groth and division.
These mutations create hyperactive KRAS proteins that disrupt normal cell regulation, and in
that way contributes to the development of various forms of cancer. For example, more than
80% of pancreatic cancers have KRAS mutations. They are also quite common in colon cancers
(40–50%) and lung cancers (30–50%). Beyond these, KRAS mutations play a role in cancers of
the biliary tract, uterus (endometrial cancer), cervix, bladder, liver, blood (myeloid leukemia),
and breast. This shows the significance of KRAS in various cancers, emphasizing its potential as
a crucial target for developing cancer treatments. [18]
The most common mutations of the KRAS gene are found at positions 12 and 13, which both
code for the amino acid Glycine (G). These mutations result in Glycine being substituted by
other amino acids such as Aspartic acid (D), Alanine (A), Arginine (R), Serine (S), Valine (V),
or Cysteine (C). These substitutions are denoted as G12D, G12A, G12R, G12S, G12V, and
G12C for codon 12, and G13C and G13D for codon 13. While less frequent mutations also
occur in codons 61, 63, 117, 119, and 146, this thesis will primarily concentrate on developing
molecules to target mutations at codons 12 and 13. To mitigate treatment-related side effects
and avoid unintended targeting of healthy cells, it’s essential to account for wild-type KRAS
when targeting mutations, and make sure that it is only the mutated KRAS that is targeted.
[18] [14]
3.2
Determining Binding Pocket
Obtaining a three-dimensional structure of the KRAS and BCL-2 proteins, including mutations,
from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) is necessary to identify their binding pockets.
3.2.1
Finding PDB file
Locating a PDB file for a protein was done by utilizing the PDBminer tool [11]. This tool scans
both the Protein Data Bank (PDB) and AlphaFold Database for the specific protein, compiling
a detailed list of PDB files. The list includes details on mutations, resolution, and coverage
among other features, offering a comprehensive insight into known mutations and files available
for the protein. [11]
3.2.2
PyMol Pocket identification
Once a PDB file was obtained, identifying the binding pocket was done using the molecular
visualization tool PyMol [28]. In this thesis, a binding pocket was defined as all residues within
a 5-angstrom radius of any part of a known inhibitor. This method assumes that the known
inhibitor is correctly positioned for effective protein inhibition, and that all important protein
residues are within 5 angstroms. This range was chosen as all important molecular interactions
have a distance of below 5 angstroms [6]. This methodology provides a reliable estimate of the
binding pocket, without needing an exhaustive analysis of the protein’s functional dynamics,
a topic beyond this thesis. The details regarding which inhibitor was utilized to identify the
binding pocket for the different proteins is detailed in Appendix C.

3.4
Molecular Generation
3.4.1
Generating Molecules
The Equivalent Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model, explained in sections 2.4.1-2.5 was used
for molecular generation. The model was trained on the Binding MOAD dataset and utilized
the inpainting with resampling method for generation. As input the model required a list of
residues for the binding pocket, a PDB file containing only the target protein, the number
of molecules to be generated, the size of the molecules and number of resampling steps. The
residues used as binding pocket for the specific proteins can be seen in Appendix C. Initially, to
determine the molecular size, the strategy outlined in Section 2.5.6, which allowed for conditional
sampling of the molecular size based on pocket size was used. However, this method did not
yield optimal results as it seemed to limit the molecular size to a narrow range of atom numbers.
To ensure exploration, 16 molecules where generated for each molecule size, spanning from 5 to
80. Although the choices of 5 and 80 atoms may seem extreme, these limits were selected to be
certain that an appropriate molecular size fell within the range. This method produced a total
of 1216 (76 sizes multiplied by batch size of 16) molecules for each mutation or wild-type of the
proteins. This resulted in a total of 4864 generated molecules for BCL-2 and 9728 for KRAS.
3.4.2
Optimizing Molecules
After generation each molecule went through two optimization steps. First RDKit’s Chem.SanitizeMol
was applied [24], which is a fundamental function in RDKit that cleans up and fixes the molecular
structure of a molecule to make sure that it follows the basic rules of chemistry. It validates and
corrects the type of bond between atoms, how many bonds an atom can have, and whether a
ring of atoms is correctly marked as aromatic. This makes sure that molecules are accurately
represented, and do not violate any basic chemical rules. Following this, RDKits UFF Optimiza-
tion function [26] is applied to the molecule. This function fine-tunes the shape and form of
the molecule by adjusting atoms and bonds to lower the molecule’s energy, aiming for the most
stable and realistic conformation possible.

3.5
Calculating Molecular Descriptors
3.5.1
Docking
Following the creation of molecules for the four BCL-2 configurations and the eight KRAS
configurations, each molecule was docked against the wild-type of the protein and all mutations
using the QVina2 algorithm. For further details on QVina2, refer to Section 2.3.1. For both
BCL-2 and KRAS, it was essential to dock all molecules against every configuration. In the case
of BCL-2, achieving a strong binding affinity across all mutations was ideal. Similarly, for KRAS,
a molecule designed for one mutation could also exhibit effective binding to another mutation.
Additionally, for KRAS, securing a low binding score with the wild-type protein would be ideal
to spare healthy cells.
3.5.2
Wildman-Crippen LogP
The LogP value, indicative of a molecule’s lipophilicity, was computed using the
rdkit.Chem.Crippen.MolLogP function within RDKit which a fast and effective way to determine
LogP. See Section 2.3.2 for a full explanation.
3.5.3
Lipinski’s Rule of Five
RDKit was used to calculate the properties related to each of the six Lipinski and Veber rules.
From this it was easy to determine if the rules were met. These properties are detailed in Section
2.3.3.
3.5.4
Synthetic Accessibility (SA) Score
The Synthetic Accessibility (SA) score, which estimates the ease of synthesizing a molecule,
was calculated employing the rdkit.Chem.SA Score.sascorer module in RDKit. For an in-depth
explanation of the SA score and its calculation, see Section 2.3.4.
3.5.5
Quantitative Estimation of Drug-likeness (QED)
The Quantitative Estimation of Drug-likeness (QED) score of a molecule was determined using
the rdkit.Chem.QED function in RDKit. See Section 2.3.5 for further details on QED.

5
Discussion
5.1
Methods
The first component of being able to generate molecules was to find a PDB file of the target
protein with an associated known inhibitor. This was done utilizing the PDBminer tool which
conducts a thorough search of the Protein Data Bank and the AlphaFold Database. For all
targets in this thesis a file containing the protein with an inhibitor was found. However, it is
possible that there are other data bases or sources out there that could have provided additional
PDB files. The lists obtained from the PDBminer tool were extensive, and in this thesis it
was not possible examining all files. Looking through all the files in the list or exploring other
databases, could have given additional insight into which residuals make up the binding pocket
and given more benchmark property values to compare the generated molecules with. It could
also have ensured that a known inhibitor was available for comparison for all mutations of
KRAS, and potentially more were available for BCL-2. The inhibitors used in this thesis not all
thoroughly examined and might be in early drug development stages or potentially not started
drug development.
The next step in molecule generation was identifying the binding pocket. This was determined as
all residues within 5 ˚Angstr¨oms of the inhibitor molecule. This was a fast and easy way of defining
a good approximation of the binding pocket, as it is known that important molecular interaction
span below 5 ˚Angstr¨oms. However, this method assumes that the inhibitor found binds in the
complete binding pocket, and that no part of the binding pocket is beyond 5 ˚Angstr¨oms of
the inhibitor. This is not always the case, and maybe there are also other substructures of
the protein outside the range that potential could be used to bind more effectively. Given the
potential limitations of the pocket identification method used, improvements could have been
made by individually analyzing each target protein and identifying the binding pocket based on
this.
The next step was generating molecules were a EDDPM model with inpainting and resampling
was used. When it was initially applied in this thesis it showed promising results, and therefore
no other models were explored. However, other models may have shown better performance
for the proteins targeted in this thesis. It would also have given a more nuanced image of the
performance of generative models in a field like this to have tested multiple models. It could
also have been beneficial to make a hyper parameter study exploring if a different number of
EGCL layers, number of resampling steps etc, could have increased the performance of the
model. The model could also have been trained on other datasets, or a combination of datasets,
to potentially gain an increase in performance. These options were mainly not explored due to
time and computational limitations but could have added additional depth to the thesis.
A choice was made to generate the same amount of molecules, for each molecular size spanning
from 5 to 80 atoms. This was found to clearly not be optimal choices as the majority of
interesting molecules generated had a size of 20-45 atoms, with no relevant generated molecules
outside of a range of 15-65 atoms. The idea behind setting a broad range was to make sure that
the selection of molecular sizes did not limit the potential to generate promising molecules. The
minimum threshold of 5 was chosen as no molecule with less atoms could be a inhibitor, while
the upper limit of 80 atoms was chosen to stay just under the model’s maximum capacity. In
hindsight this range should have been narrowed to focus computational resources on molecular
sizes that had a higher potential to generate interesting candidates. Additionally, implementing
an optimization study through a Bayesian framework could have helped with this, focusing the
generation to favorable molecular sizes for the specific protein pocket.
45
==PAGE:52==
The optimization of molecular structures, after generation, included RDKit’s Chem.SanitizeMol
that ensured chemical validity and UFF Optimization to refine their conformation towards
energetic stability. This guaranteed chemically plausible and conformationally stable molecules.
The UFF optimization may have been redundant as the docking process also changes the
molecules conformation, and the computational overhead by doing the optimization might not
have been worth it. However, initiating the docking process with molecules in a more stable
conformation could make the docking conformations more realistic. It might also have been
beneficial to explore if more molecule optimization methods could have been applied before
docking.
In this thesis, the selection and analysis of molecular descriptors such as Docking Scores,
Wildman-Crippen LogP, Lipinski/Veber Rules, Synthetic Accessibility Score, and Quantitative
Estimation of Drug-likeness (QED) were important in evaluating the generated molecules. It is
worth noting that these properties are all estimations, and there is no guarantee that a molecule
will behave as predicted in practice. However, they provide a great tool to prioritize some
molecules over others for further research. Incorporating additional molecular descriptors, such
as ADMET properties and PAINS filters, could have provided more wasy of evaluating the
generated molecules.
In this project, QVina2 was selected as the docking tool, as it is both efficient and comparable
in performance to other docking tools and simulations. The biggest advantage of QVina2, is its
speed, which is a big factor when docking a wide range of molecules as in this thesis. However,
exploring alternative docking tools, like AutoDock4, could have provided different perspectives
and given more accurate results.
5.2
BCL2
For the BCL-2 protein the criteria for success for a potential inhibitor was achieving high
binding affinity for BCL-2 and all its mutation, while having a high drug-likeliness and good
physicochemical profile. The generated molecules were compared with the known inhibitor
Venetoclax that has shown promising results in inhibiting BCL-2. Of the generated molecules 4
were considered particularly interesting.
The first molecule was MB1, which had the highest binding affinity to both wild-type BCL-2
and its mutations of all molecules, including Venetoclax. Its QED value was also higher than
the QED of Venetoclax, but low compared to the mean QED value of approved drugs. However,
the biggest concern for MB1 is its LogP value, which is significantly exceeding the optimal
range. The low QED value and high LogP suggests that the molecule cannot directly be
applied as a drug candidate. However, it is possible that it can be used as a starting point
and that the QED and LogP values can be improved by molecular engineering. While this
could be possible, changing the molecule might also worsen its binding affinity. If it is not
able to be optimized, some substructures of MB1 might still be valuable to identify and fur-
ther investigate, as these might give insight to what is needed for a high binding affinity to
BCL-2. No notable structural changes are observed in the images comparing MB1 docked to
wild-type BCL-2 and BCL-2 with the G101V mutation, which is consistent with the unchanged
binding affinity observed for the two proteins. Compared to this, the images of Venetoclax re-
veal changes in conformations in the two cases, and could explain the difference in binding affinity.
Although MB2 exhibits a lower binding affinity compared to MB1, it demonstrates only a slightly
reduced binding affinity to wild-type BCL-2 when compared to Venetoclax and outperforms
Venetoclax for binding to the G101V mutation. When comparing MB2’s physicochemical profile
with MB1 it is improved for all metrics. Especially its synthetic accessibility score is a lot higher
46
==PAGE:53==
and also exceeds the value at which a molecule is considered easy to synthesise. Its QED of 0.46
also shows a big improvement over MB1 and is close to the mean for approved drugs. However,
with a LogP value of 6.9, which, although nearer to the desired range, remains considerably
beyond the acceptable limits. Despite its LogP value of 6.9 still being outside the optimal range,
the prospects for adjusting MB2 to achieve an acceptable LogP are more promising than for
MB1. Additionally, its high synthetic accessibility score indicates potential lower experimental
costs and a more favorable risk-reward profile, making MB2 an attractive option for further drug
development efforts. MB2 is also seen to have no notable changes when comparing the images
of wild-type BCL-2 and the G101V mutated BCL-2 with the highest binding conformation of
MB2, suggesting that it is not affected by the mutation.
If the LogP value of MB2 is still too far from an acceptable value, MB3 and MB4 could be
considered as alternatives. While both MB3 and MB4 displays lower affinities, they are still
below -10. Their physicochemical profiles are better compared to MB1 and their LogP improved
compared to MB2. Specifically, MB3 has the highest QED of all the molecules with a value of
0.68, which is also higher than the mean of approved drugs. The LogP value of MB3 is within
the optimal range and its synthetic accessibility indicates that it would not be too difficult to
synthesis and is still higher than the SA score of Venetoclax. With MB3 the question becomes
if its lower binding affinities are high enough to still justify the synthesis efforts in terms of risk
versus reward. While not showing as high potential as MB3, MB4 has a high SA score and
still a QED higher than MB1 but lower than the mean of approved drugs. Its LogP is within
the Lipinski rule regarding LogP and MB4 is therefore considered a less promising but more
cost-effective candidate compared to MB3.
Overall the profiles of especially MB2 and MB3 seem promising as potential BCL-2 inhibitors
due to binding affinities and favorable physicochemical characteristics respectivly, whereas the
binding affinity combined with a lower QED for MB4 and an extremely high LogP and low
QED value for MB1 makes them less attractive.
5.3
KRas
The criteria for success for molecules targeting the mutated versions of KRAS was high binding
affinity towards the mutated KRAS, low binding affinity towards wild-type KRAS and a good
physicochemical profile.
For generated molecules targeting the G12C mutated KRAS, MKG12C1 was seen to be the
only molecule that could match the performance of the established inhibitor AMG-510. It was
seen that the two molecules had similar properties with regarding to binding affinity, LogP and
Weight. MKG12C1 had a slightly higher QED while AMG-510 had a higher SA score. This
would indicate that MKG12C1 had the potential to become a drug candidate, but its QED
value is below the average QED for approved drugs, and its SA score slightly outside the range
of what is considered easy to synthesis. Therefore there is risk in proceeding with MKG12C1
as a drug candidate without further optimization. However, its comparable performance to
AMG-510, especially in terms of binding affinity, suggests that it has potential. It is also worth
noting that AMG-510 is a covalent inhibitor, and if MKG12C1 could be chemically engineered
to also covalently bind to G12C, it could enhance its binding affinity further, making MKG12C1
a good starting point as a drug candidate. It is also notet that a structural change is observed
in the images comparing MKG12C1 docked to wild-type KRAS and KRAS with the G12C
mutation, which might be the reason for the different in binding affinity between the two.
In examining the generated molecules for targeting the G12D mutated KRAS, the molecules
where compared with known inhibitor MRTX1133. This sets a high standard with a notable
47
==PAGE:54==
docking score of −13.1 and having a good physicochemical profile, meeting all Lipinski and Veber
rules besides slightly exceeding the maximum weight. The model did not find any molecules
that surpassed MRTX1133 in terms of binding affinity to the G12D mutation. Where the model
found some interesting molecules, MKG12D1 and MKG12D2, where mainly in regards to their
QED and SA scores where both where better compared to MRTX1133. However, besides their
lower binding affinity, MKG12D1 was also seen to have a high binding affinity towards wild-type
KRAS, which could cause unwanted side-effects. Given the existence of MRTX1133, a candidate
already demonstrating high binding affinity towards the G12D mutation, careful consideration
should be made before advancing with MKG12D1 and MKG12D2 for future research. However,
their high SA scores suggest potentially low synthesis and testing costs, and therefore might still
be worth the risk. Examining the plots of MKG12D1 with its highest conformation in regards
to the wild-type KRAS and G12D mutated KRAS, it seems to not interact with the mutated
part, and it would therefore also make sense that it has high binding affinity to both, as it does
not utilize the difference of the proteins.
Given the absence of a specific inhibitor targeting the G12A mutation, GppNHp, a non-
hydrolyzable analogue of GTP, was used to identify the binding pocket. Among the generated
molecules two were particularly interesting. The first was MKG12A1, mainly due to its high
affinity towards the G12A mutation and lower affinity towards the wild-type KRAS. It had a
QED score of 0.444 which is lower than the mean for approved drugs. Its SA score classifies
it as easy to synthesise and its LogP value was almost inside the optimal range. The high SA
score suggests low synthesis and testing costs, and with chemical engineering the QED value
could potentially improve. As an alternative to this is the MKG12A2 has a higher QED score of
0.828, which is also significantly higher than the mean QED for approved drugs. The downside
of MKG12A2 is however its higher affinity towards wild-type KRAS, which means higher risk
for interrupting KRAS important function in healthy cells. Both MKG12A1 and MKG12A2
show potential as drug candidates and indicate good starting points for chemical engineering.
Examining the plots of MKG12A1 with its highest conformation in regards to the wild-type
KRAS and G12A mutated KRAS, it is seen to be very different, which could be the reason
for the difference in binding affinity towards the two. However, the difference in proteins from
wild-type KRAS to the G12A mutated KRAS is also noticeable and makes it harder to compare.
In examining the generated molecules for targeting the G12S mutated KRAS, they were compared
with the known inhibitor beta-lactone5, whose binding affinity with the G12S mutation was
seen to be the best of all molecules. Where the model found some interesting molecules was in
regards to the physicochemical profile, where MKG12S1 and in particularly MKG12S2 where
interesting. MKG12S1 had a notable affinity for the G12S mutation, a better QED score,
slightly higher than the mean for approved drugs, and a higher SA score, still not considered
easy to synthesise. Even though its binding affinity towards wild-type KRAS is higher than for
beta-lactone5, it is still high enough to have a risk of interrupting KRAS’s important function
in healthy cells. It is here that MKG12S2 shows promise, while having a lower binding affinity
towards G12S KRAS, it has a much lower binding affinity towards wild-type KRAS of only -9.7.
It also has a QED above the mean of orally approved drugs, and a SA score suggesting it being
easy to synthesize. Compared to beta-lactone-5 MKG12S1 and in particularly MKG12S2 have
a much better physicochemical profile and show potential as drug candidates and indicate good
starting points for chemical engineering. Examining the plots of MKG12S2 with its highest
conformation in regards to the wild-type KRAS and G12S mutated KRAS, it is also seen to be
different indicating a difference in the interaction between it and wild-type KRAS and G12S
KRAS.
For targeting the G12V mutation in KRAS, without a direct comparison to the known in-
hibitor BI-2865 due to docking difficulties, MKG12V1 and MKG12V2 stand out based on their
48
==PAGE:55==
promising profiles. MKG12V1 displays a slightly higher affinity towards the G12V mutation,
while MKG12V2, has a superior QED. Both molecules has a relativly high affinity towards
wild-type KRAS, which means higher risk for interrupting KRAS important function in healthy
cells. Importantly, their high synthetic accessibility scores not only suggest that they could be
synthesized with relative ease, but also potentially reducing developmental costs. The low QED
of MKG12V1, which is also lower than the mean of approved drugs, compared to the high QED
of MKG12V2, which is above the mean of approved oral drugs, makes MKG12V2 the more
promising candidate of the two. Examining the plots of MKG12V2 with its highest conformation
in regards to the wild-type KRAS and G12V mutated KRAS, do not show a huge difference in
conformation, but still makes it a potential as starting point for chemically engineering a G12V
inhibitor.
In investigating the KRAS G12R mutation, our study revealed two standout molecules, MKG12R1
and MKG12R2, each presenting a unique set of advantages when compared to the specific G12R
inhibitor, alpha-beta-ketomide 4. MKG12R1 had a really high binding affinity to the G12R
mutation, indicating strong potential for effective targeting. Its desirable QED and synthetic
accessibility suggest a good balance between drug-like properties and ability to be synthesised in
a cost efficient manner, and despite a LogP value slightly above the optimal range it shows a
really good physicochemical profile. A possible concern with this molecule is its strong affinity for
wild-type KRAS, which makes careful monitoring in initial phases of potential drug development
necessary. As an alternative MKG12R2 offers a compelling profile with significantly lower
affinity for wild-type KRAS, an exceptional physicochemical profile, and still having a relativly
high binding affinity towards G12R KRAS. This means less risk of interrupting KRAS important
function in healthy cells and having really good drug-like properties. If the binding affinity is high
enough for MKG12R2 to have inhibitory properties towards the G12 R mutation it is a really
promising candidate. Research on G12R also showed that the compound MRTX1133, already
recognized as an inhibitor of G12D, has the highest binding affinity among all molecules studied.
This suggests that, if not previously considered for the G12R mutation, MRTX1133 could
also be a promising drug candidate for targeting the G12R mutation. Examining the plots of
MKG12R2 with its highest conformation in regards to the wild-type KRAS and G12R mutated
KRAS a big difference in conformation is seen, and its affinity towards the G12R mutation is clear.
In targeting the KRAS G13C mutation, initial filters revealed molecules with superior affinity
and drug-like qualities compared to the covalently-binding inhibitor edaGDP. Among these,
MKG13C1 and MKG13C2 were particularly interesting. MKG13C1 combines a high affinity
for the G13C mutation with favorable synthetic accessibility and a low affinity for wild-type
KRAS, suggesting lower risk for interrupting KRAS’s important function in healthy cell. Its
profile is balanced, although with room for improvement in drug-likeness. MKG13C2, on the
other hand, has exceptional physicochemical properties, including a QED score way above the
mean for approved oral drugs and even lower binding affinity with wild-type KRAS, although at
a slightly lower affinity for the G13C mutation. Depending on if the drug-like properties for
MKG13C1 are good enough and the binding affinity towards the G13C mutation is high enough
for MKG13C2, both molecules demonstrate the potential for initial drug candidates towards
inhibiting the G13C mutation of KRAS. Examining the plots of MKG13C1 with its highest
conformation in regards to the wild-type KRAS and G13C mutated KRAS, a big difference is
seen, indicating the difference in binding affinity. However, the conformation of KRAS for G13C
compared to the wild-type KRAS is also big.
In the exploration of inhibitors for the KRAS G13D mutation, it was noted that BI-2865, a
known inhibitor, was not dockable, preventing a known inhibitor to be directly compared with
the generated molecules. Among the identified candidates, MKG13D1 emerged with high

its affinity for wild-type KRAS could potentially be a problem in practice and cause unwanted
off-target effects. The alternatives MKG13D2 and MKG13D3 offer promising profiles with
outstanding QED scores, and lower binding affinity towards the wild-type KRAS, but at the
cost of lower binding affinity towards the G13D mutation as well. This highlights the complexity

Future Work
The research presented herein lays a solid foundation for further investigation into the develop-
ment of inhibitors for KRAS mutations, BCL-2 and potentially other cancer-related proteins
as well. Future studies should extend beyond the existing model implementation, exploring
additional models within the DiffSBDD paper and investigating alternative computational
methods available in the latest research. Such an exploration could reveal varied capabilities for
generating molecules, and possibly lead to other potential inhibitor candidates for the KRAS
mutations and BCL-2.
Future efforts should focus on utilizing computational resources more efficiently by generating
molecules within a narrower, more optimal size range that is more likely to yield effective
inhibitors for specific mutations. A dynamic approach, like Bayesian optimization, could enhance
this process. Starting with a broad spectrum of molecule sizes and gradually narrowing down to
those sizes showing the most promise based on performance feedback, could help more efficient
utilization of computational resources.
The research conducted here identified several promising drug candidates. Future efforts should
involve a detailed analysis of these candidates and their substructures to identify those that
contribute most to binding affinity and drug-like properties. By preserving these crucial sub-
structures, the inpainting method could be used to generate the rest of the molecule, potentially
leading to the creation of even more effective inhibitors. Specifically for KRAS mutations,
an interesting approach could include retaining the part of the known inhibitors which forms
covalent bonds to the mutation and then generate the remaining complementary structure to
see if this could lead to more effective inhibitors.
While this thesis has concentrated on BCL-2 and KRAS mutations, the methodologies and
insights gained could be applied to other cancer-related proteins, such as EGFR, HER2, VEGF
etc. By broadening the research to encompass a wider array of targets, there’s potential to
identify inhibitors for these proteins, thereby aiding in the development of treatments for the
cancers they are associated with as well.
To bridge the gap between computational predictions and practical application, future work
should prioritize experimental validation of promising candidates. This step is crucial because,
while the metrics used in this thesis are carefully considered, they remain estimations and may
not fully reflect real-world complexities involved in drug development.
Beyond generating and identifying promising molecules, subsequent efforts should also explore
optimization strategies tailored to enhance drug-like properties and mitigate potential off-target
effects of the promising candidates found. This might include chemical modifications to improve
solubility, distribution, and metabolic stability, ensuring that the molecules not only bind
effectively to their targets but are also viable as drugs.

7
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to identify potential inhibitor candidates for the cancer related proteins
BCL-2, and mutated KRAS. This was indicated by a high binding affinity estimated with docking.
For BCL-2, the molecules MB2 and MB3, where the most promising candidates, where MB2
had high binding affinity to BCL-2 and its mutations, and MB3 a favorable physicochemical
profile. This indicates that both have potential as starting points for further optimization and
development into viable drugs.
For the KRAS mutations, especially MKG12C1, MKG12A2, MKG12S2, MKG12V2, MKG12R1
and MKG13C1 showed promise and could be used as starting points for developing an inhibitor
for KRAS with G12C, G12A, G12S, G12V, G12R and G13C mutations respectively. These
where not perfect drug candidates but they either had good phisicochemical profiles, or high
binding affinities towards the mutations.
This thesis also demonstrated the potential of using generative AI in the research area of drug
discovery, and despite constraints in computational resources and existing knowledge, this study
successfully generated a number of intriguing molecules aimed at inhibiting BCL-2 and its
mutations, alongside KRAS mutations. While not all identified molecules outperformed known
inhibitors, they still presented valuable properties that could help gain insights and foundational
knowledge for further research.
The results of this thesis also gives an opportunity for exciting future work, including validating
the effectiveness of the proposed inhibitor candidates in a practical setting, altering the generation
approach fixing some important substructures and generating the remainder of the molecule and
expanding the use of generative AI to other cancer related proteins.
