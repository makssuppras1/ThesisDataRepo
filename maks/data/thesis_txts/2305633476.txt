Preface 
The master project is the final step in acquiring the degree of Master of Science in Chemical 
Engineering and Biotechnology. Worth 30 ECTS, it is performed within 4 to 6 months, after the 
completion of the so-called Master Cycle which encompasses all courses and formal examinations of 
the Masters, as well as a mandatory Engineering Internship in the industry. Altogether the project 
accounts for a quarter of the overall credit count and grade of the Masters; as a whole worth 120 
ECTS. 
The work was carried in the research group of Professor Kaj Thomsen at the Center for Energy 
Resource Engineering (CERE) at the technical University of Denmark (DTU). It consisted entirely of 
modelling work, i.e. no experiments were carried in the lab. 
This project was particularly challenging, as throughout our studies (Bachelors and Masters), we 
virtually never covered any topic in modelling nor in relation with the oil & gas or geothermal 
industries. Despite having gathered very diverse experiences through 3 internships in significantly 
different fields throughout my studies (nanotechnology for NRC, fermentation process development 
for Bayer and pilot plant new product development for NestlГ©), the novelty of this last project proved 
to be the most challenging of all.

Abstract 
In this master project solid-liquid equilibrium data for lead(II) with several co-cations and anions in 
water is processed to obtain Extended UNIQUAC parameters. The focus is on volume q and surface 
area r for lead(II) itself as well as temperature binary interaction parameters u0 and ut for several lead-
ion pairs. Specifically, the binary interactions with the aqueous solvent alongside cations Na+, Mg2+, 
Ca2+ and Sr2+, as well as anions Cl- und SO42- are estimated and compared to the literature using macro-
enabled Excel files coupled with proprietary modelling software and libraries. Additionally, some 
thermodynamic properties such as the Gibbs Energy of Formation and Heat Capacity are fitted to 
account further for temperature dependency. Finally, the resulting scale formation tendency, 
thermodynamically predicted by this set of parameters, is compared to solid-liquid equilibrium data 
found in the literature, namely the systems PbCl2вҖ“H2O, PbCl2вҖ“HClвҖ“H2O, PbCl2вҖ“NaClвҖ“H2O, PbCl2вҖ“CaCl2вҖ“
H2O, PbCl2вҖ“MgCl2вҖ“H2O, PbCl2вҖ“SrCl2вҖ“H2O, PbSO4вҖ“H2O and PbSO4вҖ“Na2SO4вҖ“H2O.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Context 
Given that the results from this project apply mostly to the oil and gas, and to a minor extent, to the 
geothermal industries, and that these fields are poorly covered in Swiss chemical engineering 
curricula, a brief introduction to these industries would bring a valuable input to readers unfamiliar 
with these topics. The purpose of this section is to provide a brief overview before proceeding with 
the underlying technical aspects. 
1.1.1. Oil and Gas 
1.1.1.1. History 
Oil has been used for more than 5000 years. In Antiquity, people used mainly bitumen collected from 
oil seeps for marginal yet key applications. In the Genesis, the Bible reports the usage of вҖңpitchвҖқ to 
cover NoaвҖҷs Ark. In ancient and medieval naval warfare, the famous вҖңGreek fireвҖқ providing continuous 
burning while floating on water, is strongly suspected to originate from a volatile fraction of petroleum 
called naphtha, combined with heat generating compounds such as quicklime (рқҗ¶рқ‘Һрқ‘Ӯ)1. Beyond naval 
applications, its hydrophobic properties allowed it to be used as mortars in buildings but also for 
covering water conducts and tanks. It was used for road construction, for torches and lamps, as well 
as for a wide range of medicinal usages already in the 15th century. Exploitation of natural gas leakages 
to produce salt from sea water is reported for the middle of the first millennium B.C. in China (Encarta 
2006; Reinicke et al. 2000; Speight 2014).  
Over the centuries a lot of this knowledge was lost and until the second half of the 19th century, the 
usage of oil and gas was limited to a select few of the aforementioned applications. By then, the 
industrial revolution and the subsequent exponential population growth in western countries led to 
profound socio economical changes, requiring cheaper combustibles for lighting lamps. Oil coming 
from animals, such as whales, was commonly used but increasingly unaffordable while вҖңrock oilвҖқ 
coming from seeps (вҖңhuile de rocheвҖқ in 19th century French or вҖңErdГ¶lвҖқ literally вҖҳEarth oilвҖҷ still in use in 
modern German)2 was not sufficient to meet the high demand. From observations of oil seeping into 
wells drilled for water and salt, attempts were made to drill for oil. The most famous of these is 
credited to Colonel Drake in Titusville, Pennsylvania, on August 27th, 1859. With a depth of only 21m, 
it was initially capable of producing 8 to 10 barrels a day. In 1901, the use of the rotary drilling method 
in the Spindletop oil field, in Beaumont, Texas, down to a depth of 347m yielded an estimated 100вҖҷ000 
barrels a day. Along with coal and other energy sources, crude oil has proved to be one of the main 
drives of the second industrial revolution, from the second half of the 19th century till World War I 
(Reinicke et al. 2000; Encarta 2006) and beyond (Figure 1).

1.1.2. Geothermal Energy 
1.1.2.1. History 
Natural sources of hot water were used since antiquity for their therapeutic properties as well as a 
means of relaxation. The first immigrants to Iceland carried water from thermal sources to their homes 
through wooden conducts. Rational usage of hot springs appeared only later in history. A manuscript 
from the year 1334 describes hot water distribution through pine wood conducts to houses of the 
town of Chaudes-Aigues in France. This place is believed to be the warmest natural hot spring in 
continental Europe with a surface temperature of 82В°C. In 1827, Frenchman FranГ§ois Larderel 
attempted replacing heating from wood with vapor from hot springs. The amount being insufficient, 
he made a first drilling in 1833 to obtain higher volumes at higher temperatures. His work was carried 
further by Prince Pierro Conti in Tuscany who managed in 1904 to lighten up 5 bulbs with the help of 
a dynamo run by a turbine using geothermal vapor (Encarta 2007).

2. Scale Formation 
The definition of scale, and in particular its distinction with deposits, is not always unanimous. 
Essentially, scale originates from materials dissolved in fluids and solutes which deposit to form a solid 
layer that attaches to surfaces, typically of industrial equipment such as piping systems, wells, heat 
exchangers and membranes through a process called вҖҳscalingвҖҷ or вҖҳscale formationвҖҷ. This results in a 
dense crystalline deposits, well bonded to the aforementioned surfaces. Loosely defined deposits on 
the other hand, are sometimes termed вҖңsoft scaleвҖқ, вҖңpowdery depositвҖқ, or вҖңsludgeвҖқ among other 
names. They commonly refer to weakly bonded solid layers. Usually, the longer a scale is exposed to 
the same conditions, the more permanent and resilient to removal it becomes. Common industrial 
problems include reduced thermal efficiency, induced under-deposit corrosion, increased pressure 
drop and flow perturbations such as cavitation and blockage. Beyond reducing the cost effectiveness 
of industrial units, this can seriously affect the safety of the personnel operating the equipment as 
well as the quality of intermediate or final products, typically through contamination of fluids coming 
in contact with the scale. 
2.1. Non Thermodynamic Considerations 
Although this work focuses on thermodynamic aspects of scale formation, a proper understanding of 
this phenomenon would not be complete without the inclusion of some basic aspects of kinetics and 
transport phenomena. 
The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry defines supersaturation as an unstable system 
whose concentration of a given material in solution is greater than it would exist at equilibrium (IUPAC 
2014). A prerequisite for any precipitation process to occur, it is also its main driving force. Conversely,

precisely between both states and is at thermodynamic equilibrium between the liquid and solid 
phases, resulting in neither of the latter phenomena taking place globally. This is quantified by the 
saturation index (рқ‘Ҷрқҗј[вҲ’]), as expressed in [1], which is defined for a salt S as the product of the activities 
of its constituents in solution to the power of their stoichiometric contributions, divided by its 
solubility product. The values for each case are < 1, = 1 and > 1 for undersaturation, saturation and 
supersaturation respectively:

2.1.2. Formations stages 
Assuming thermodynamic driving forces are in place, scale formation can be described as occuring 
through a series of 5 consecutive and partly overlapping stages identified by (Hoang 2015): (1) 
initiation, (2) transport, (3) attachement, (4) removal and (5) aging. Intuitevely, this corresponds to 
the transport of the precipitant from the bulk to a given surface (1-3), followed by the formation and 
consolidation of a scale (3-5).  
2.1.2.1. Initiation 
Here the surface on which the scale will form is being conditioned. This step can last from several 
weeks to only a few seconds. Its duration depends on the surface (surface temperature, materials, 
roughness, coating, etc.) as well as the solution (scalant concentration, flow characteristics, pressure, 
etc.). Essentially, anything that increases the supersaturation degree, locally or over the entire 
solution, be it concentration, temperature or pressure variations, subsequently provides a stronger 
driving force which in turn results in a shorter initiation. 
Within this stage occurs a specific time period called the induction period (П„) and defined as the delay 
between achievement of supersaturation and the formation of the first crystals in solution. The latter 
has been found for several salts to correlate with the supersaturation ratio S, through a logarithmic 
relationship, specifically log рқңҸвҲқ(log рқ‘Ҷ)вҲ’2, as can be seen in Figure 13.

boundary layer, and driven by the concentration difference between the latter two regions. Assuming

2.1.2.3. Attachement 
Deposits adhere to the surface and among themselves, with a gradual growth over time ending up in 
the formation of a scale layer. Key factors here include surface conditions and scalant properties. 
2.1.2.4. Removal 
During this stage a competition between removal and deposition of scalants takes place. The main 
removal force is hypothesized to originate in shear forces at the interface between the fluid and the 
deposited scale layers, mainly dynamic (e.g. velocity gradients at the scale surface) and static (e.g. 
viscosity) properties of the fluid, as well as the deposited-upon surface characteristics (material, 
roughness, etc.). 
2.1.2.5. Aging 
The aging process starts already with the first deposition layers of scale. Here crystal formation and 
transformation leads to an either increased or decreased deposition strength over time. Chemical 
reactions may also occur and thereafter modify the mechanical structure of the scale. 
 
2.1.3. Prerequisites 
In order for scale formation to take place, three simultaneous factors must intervene: (1) 
thermodynamically governed supersaturation coupled with heavily kinetic and transport phenomena 
dependent (2) nucleation and (3) sufficient contact time.

model will deal with statistical quantities (i.e. with a certain probability) as input and/or output of the 
model. Another major distinction is between steady-state and non-stready-state models, based on 
their respective exclusion or inclusion of changes in the variables over time (Bockhorn 2000). Under 
the aforementioned terminology, the Extended UNIQUAC would fall into a steady-state, deterministic 
model.

the implementation used in this work and not other possible features of the program which were not 
disclosed during the project.

4.2. Approach 
Every time, after a comparison was made, readjustments of the data in the Data Files were performed, 
using the aforementioned 3-point scheme (Figure 14). This included adjusting the options in the вҖҳScript 
FileвҖҷ and determining which data to consider and which to exclude for a given Estim run or better yet 
the addition of newly found relevant datapoints from the literature when available. 
Given the scarcity of data for some of the systems, a very careful determination of the precise dataset 
on which to base the parameter estimation from the Data Files had to be operated. Effectively, 
datapoints which were far from the rest (visually or through statistical indicators18), contradictory 
from what the literature reported in similar conditions, or related to doubtful claims or experimental 
techniques, had to be disregarded. 
Once a set of parameters was calculated alongside fitting of thermodynamic properties, a meticulous 
approach followed. The validation, besides the use of statistical tools, was ultimately graphical. Most 
of the difficulty then was in finding a balance and identifying which parameters had the highest impact 
on which system. In one instance, a newly found combination of parameters that yielded a fitting 
improvement in a given ternary system made another worse. In another instance, for a same system, 
an improvement in one temperature range yielded a terrible misfit in another. Ultimately, it was a 
careful compromise between the different modelled systems, sharing common parameters which 
predicted solubility through a very sensitive interplay.  
A careful follow-up of the different parameter combinations, on which datasets and hypotheses they 
were estimated, through historical parameters proved quite useful in favoring one set over another. 
Practically, besides the inherent modelling challenges, difficulties related to the software tools 
themselves were unfortunately numerous and are deemed accountable for the relative low amount 
of results obtained. 
Although in theory the usage of Estim may seem very straightforward, in practice one needs a 
considerable amount of time to harness it properly. With the wrong input, initial guesses, or selection 
of considered datasets for a given run, the resulting parameters can be completely erroneous, yielding 
solid-liquid equilibrium predictions far from actual data found in the literature. In the absence of a 
deeper knowledge of how the core of Estim runs, many of the procedures needed to run the program 
smoothly were learnt empirically by trial and error. These include for instance the necessity to start 
with low concentration data for the first parameter estimations, and then progressively increase the 
latter, alongside considered temperature ranges. Of course, this has to be coordinated with proper 
options in the вҖҳScriptвҖҷ File, from the straightforward weight-balancing in the objective function [48] to 
the indistinct choice of the precise numerical methods and their implementation details. 
The second software tool, the Macro-Enabled Excel File, also came with significant issues. As shown 
in Figure 16, it has a built-in routine to identify the architecture (32 vs. 64 bits) on which it is runs. For 
instance, this feature had to manually be removed from the small part of the code that was accessible. 
It caused referencing errors upon opening existing files or transferring them from one computer to 
another, in which case the files were thereafter irreversibly corrupt. Moreover, the thermodynamic 
routines often yielded indistinctive error messages, whose source could not be traced, leading to a 
very time consuming indistinctive debugging. Note that these issues could arguably be in large part

5.1.2.1. Lead(II) вҖ“ Water 
After the r and q, because of its ubiquity, the binary interaction of lead with water was the second 
most sensitive combination to estimate. As all systems were aqueous and in mass percent water was 
always the main compound, it impacted greatly on all modelled systems. Upon inspecting the binary 
interactions of water with other species in Table 4 and Table 5, two observations come to light. First, 
the u0 parameter is unusually large. It is the highest in the table. It is also much higher than fellow 
heavy metals iron(II) and zinc(II) which lie between 750 and 80021. Second, the ut parameter is negative 
and of relatively large magnitude. This negative sign with cations appears only for alkaline earth metals 
magnesium(II) and calcium(II), and not for ion(II) and zinc(II). The magnitude is also quite large, much 
larger than for all other water-species ut interactions, and quite distinct from fellow heavy metals 
iron(II) and zinc(II), both positive of magnitude less than 3. 
It should be noted however that across almost all historical estimation runs, this ut value was negative 
and of similarly high magnitude, while the u0 value varied greatly, exhibiting both negative and positive 
values, always from a few hundreds up to c.a. 2300. A lot of satisfactory fits were obtained with u0 
values close to that figure (u0=2300Вұ50). 
Being the most two sensitive parameters, it may be that the oddity of the r and q values subsequently 
produce an unusual (рқ‘ў0, рқ‘ўрқ‘Ў) combination for water-lead.

5.1.2.3. Lead(II) вҖ“ Sulfate 
Similar to the case of lead-chloride, a proper binary interaction fit is critical in ternary systems 
containing lead sulfate. Table 4 shows large variations (700-2100) of u0 values across sulfate-species 
interactions. Zinc(II) and iron(II) u0 interactions range themselves within 1100-1500. As for ut values 
reported in Table 5, the high variability is also present with figures within 3-51, but notably all positive. 
Zinc(II) and iron(II) ut values follow that trend with figures within 14 to 18. For both binary parameters, 
the variability in the case of the sulfate anion relative to its chloride counterpart is noteworthy. In this 
light the u0 value found for lead(II)-sulfate interaction seems acceptable. A negative value of ut 
however seems odd relative to the other species-sulfate interactions. 
Historical values however corroborate the negative ut value. While the u0 parameters exhibits a certain 
variability ranging first broadly within 200-800 and then narrowing down to 500-700 by the end of the 
work, the ut parameter is consistently negative with absolute magnitudes from near zero to 30. 
That being said, it should be noted that in contrast to its chloride anion counterpart, the binary data 
used to fit lead-sulfate was far less numerous and much less uniform in terms of trends across different 
authors. Moreover, the temperature ranges were of 0-60В°C for lead(II)-sulfate binary data, while 
lead(II)-chloride benefited from a much wider 0-200В°C set. In fitting an expression such as 
temperature-dependent [34], the wider the temperature set, the more precise the fitted parameters 
can be expected. This is deemed to be the main reason for the greater variability in historical values 
for lead(II)-sulfate in contrast to lead(II)-chloride. Furthermore, this dataset size disparity renders the 
sensitivity of sulfate interaction parameters with lead(II) far less statistically heavy than in the case of 
the chloride anion.

The following ternary systems, present a common trend characteristic, well reported in the literature 
(Winand 1991; Tan, Bartels, and Bedard 1987), easily visualized in the figures below. As mentioned in 
the first part of the resultsвҖҷ presentation, it should be kept in mind how critical the binary parameters 
for lead(II) with the chloride anion are on all the systems presented below. 
The common trend is a steep initial depression in solubility, until reaching a flat minimum, whose 
values increase with temperature. Increasing minimum with temperature is particularly visible in 
Figure 25. 
Below are presented the ternary systems of lead(II) chloride in hydrochloric acid solutions, as well as

5.2.1.2.1. PbCl2-HCl-H2O 
This first ternary Chlorolead system comes with a вҖҳbuilt-in thresholdвҖҷ at 6 [рқ‘ҡрқ‘ңрқ‘ҷ рқҗ»рқҗ¶рқ‘ҷ/рқ‘ҳрқ‘” рқҗ»2рқ‘Ӯ]. As we 
know from basic chemistry, electrolytes in water dissociate to a certain extent, not always fully. HCl, 
which is considered a strong acid, is typically regarded as fully dissociated in aqueous media, although 
the actual extent is unlikely 100% at all concentrations. Yet, as it is presented by (Thomsen, 
Rasmussen, and Gani 1996) the precise extent of dissociation is not reported in the literature and 
consequently an arbitrary threshold of 6 [рқ‘ҡрқ‘ңрқ‘ҷ рқҗ»рқҗ¶рқ‘ҷ/рқ‘ҳрқ‘” рқҗ»2рқ‘Ӯ] has been implemented historically into 
the model, so that at any lower concentrations, HCl is regarded as fully dissociated.  
In the graphic representations below, SLE is presented with the common trend shape described earlier 
for ternary systems, at different temperatures, with extrapolations at those temperatures where 
experimental data does not go all the way (e.g. at 0В°C in Figure 20 below). The threshold is indicated 
by a vertical dashed line. Numerically, the вҖҳMacro-Enabled Excel FileвҖҷ experiences difficulties in yielding 
SLE values beyond that point. Not very visible at lower temperatures, the difficulties became more 
and more serious with increasing temperature, up to the point were simply no SLE output was issued. 
In the next four figures below HCl is plotted against PbCl2 in aqueous media at 0В°C, 25В°C, 50В°C and 
80В°C in Figure 20, Figure 21, Figure 22 and Figure 23 respectively. These temperatures were chosen 
because they included sufficient ternary experimental data for a graphic comparison with the 
predicted SLE trend. 
At 0В°C, the predicted SLE is strictly speaking mostly an extrapolation as experimental data is available 
at less than 5% weight HCl. The single point reported by (D M Lichty 1903) is actually a binary point 
extracted from the PbCl2вҖ”H2O dataset to increase parameter estimation and enhance the resulting 
visualization. This is a common strategy when processing ternary data. Other than that, only one 
dataset is available by (Engel 1889), one of the oldest references in this work. The fit is moderately 
good. 
At 25В°C, the standard temperature, a lot of data is available. It should be noted that the data from 
(von Ende 1901), unlike the rest, was reported at 25.2В°C. However, given the equipment in 1901, it 
was deemed negligible enough to include this data in the parameter estimation and subsequent SLE 
prediction comparison. Altogether this fit is considered of very good visual quality. Reverting back to 
the binary Figure 18, presenting also a very good fit at the standard temperature, and given that both

mentioned in previous section 5.1.2.2. Lead(II) вҖ“ Chloride on page 27.

5.2.1.2.2. PbCl2-NaCl-H2O 
With 157 initial experimental datapoints, the PbCl2вҖ”NaClвҖ”H2O system provided a numerically rich 
basis on which to estimate (u0, ut) binary interaction parameters for lead(II) with Na+, alongside 
offering a likely improvement to the lead(II)вҖ”chloride anion binary fit. 
Below, Figure 27, Figure 28 and Figure 29 present the corresponding SLE predictions at isotherms 13В°C, 
25В°C and 50В°C respectively, each time indicating the type of salt (PbCl2 or NaCl) over the corresponding 
wt% contribution point from both NaCl on the x axis and PbCl2 on the y axis. The question of which 
salt forms, and in which concentrations is, not unlike for the complexation рқ‘ғрқ‘Ҹрқҗ¶рқ‘ҷрқ‘ӣ2вҲ’рқ‘ӣ reported by (Liu 
and Papangelakis 2006) contentious in the literature. On one hand, authors such as (Demassieux 1923) 
and (Deacon 1927) initially reported solely NaCl and PbCl2 in the PbCl2вҖ”NaClвҖ”H2O ternary system. 
On the other authors like (Kendall, J.; Sloan 1925) report a NaClвҖў2PbCl2 salt. Most recent authors, 
including (Liu and Papangelakis 2006), however do not consider double salts. They do however 
consider the aforementioned lead-chloride complexation. In this work, only the simplest forms of 
PbCl2 and NaCl were considered. 
Across the various temperatures the fit is relatively good at low NaCl concentrations and at what was 
considered to be the SLE curve with the solid phase NaCl. The two-point salt however was not as well

6. Conclusions 
Several conclusions arise from this work. First and foremost, the very large impact that experimental 
data in terms of quantity, diversity and quality has on fitting Extended UNIQUAC parameters. Second, 
the strong interdependence and interplay parameters have on each other, with an almost hierarchical 
structure, from ion-dependent r and q, to binary (u0, ut) to finally specific ternary (u0, ut) within each 
subsystem. Third, the fundamental importance of the salts considered in the solid-liquid equilibrium, 
not only their thermodynamic properties, but also their speciation, complexation and presence or 
absence of double salts and/or hydrates, highlighting the utmost importance of a proper 
characterization. 
For the particular systems studied, the consistency of the lead(II) chloride data allowed for a relatively 
good solid liquid equilibrium prediction of system PbCl2вҖ”H2O up to 140В°C out of the 200В°C covered 
by binary data altogether. Ternary data for systems PbCl2вҖ”HClвҖ”H2O and PbCl2вҖ”NaClвҖ”H2O were also 
relatively well predicted, whereas ternary systems PbCl2вҖ“CaCl2вҖ“H2O, PbCl2вҖ“MgCl2вҖ“H2O and PbCl2вҖ“
SrCl2вҖ“H2O have to be considered a (starting) work in progress, far from final parameter values and 
cruelly lacking experimental data at more temperature and concentration ranges. 
As for lead (II) sulfate systems, it seems the very low solubility of lead(II) relative to other species on 
one hand and in quantity relative to water on the other, made its вҖҳstatistical weightвҖҷ quite low in the 
interplay of parameters, leading to a definitely incomplete fit of the ternary PbSO4вҖ”Na2SO4вҖ”H2O data 
in spite of a relatively good binary PbSO4вҖ”H2O prediction. 
7. Future Perspectives 
The most important contribution that would benefit this work is indubitably additional data, in terms 
of quality and quantity, well distributed with proper characterization, especially beyond 150В°C. Once 
this has been established, the inclusion of pressure-dependent parameters, as previously done by 
(VillafГЎfila GarcГӯa, Thomsen, and Stenby 2005) to much higher pressures would further the 
applicability of the Extended UNIQUAC Model to increasingly important HP/HT reservoirs, not only 
from the oil and gas, but also the very promising geothermal industries.

A3. Additional Concepts and Definitions 
A3.1. Nucleation 
Nucleation, is a complex and often loosely defined phenomenon (Mullin 2012). It may occur 
spontaneously or induced artificially in conditions of fluid agitation, mechanical shock, friction, 
extreme pressures and even cavitation. Although there has been no final agreement on nomenclature, 
nucleation can be classified into two types and two sub-tybes, as summarized in Figure 43. Overall, it 
is defined as the process in which the smallest stable aggregates of a crystalline phase are formed in 
a crystallizing system within a supersaturated fluid, starting already at the aforementioned initiation 
stage. (Mullin 2001; Mullin 2012; Hoang 2015).

A3.3. Excess Function 
A3.3.1. Concept 
A detailed discussion on the concept and often ambiguous usage of the term вҖңexcess functionвҖқ is 
provided by (Friedman 1960) and (Missen 1969) in both the cases of solutions of electrolytes and non-
electrolytes respectively. 
(Missen 1969) presents the following general formalization. For a property рқ‘Җ in a given mixture, the 
excess function рқ‘Җрқҗё is defined for a specific set of temperature рқ‘Ү, composition variable25 рқ‘Ҙ and 
specified property26 рқҗҪ as: 
рқ‘Җрқҗё(рқ‘Ү, рқҗҪ, рқ‘Ҙ) вүқрқ‘Җ(рқ‘Ү, рқҗҪ, рқ‘Ҙ) вҲ’рқ‘Җрқ‘–рқ‘‘(рқ‘Ү, рқҗҪ, рқ‘Ҙ) = рқӣҘрқ‘Җ(рқ‘Ү, рқҗҪ, рқ‘Ҙ) вҲ’рқӣҘрқ‘Җрқ‘–рқ‘‘(рқ‘Ү, рқҗҪ, рқ‘Ҙ) 
[61] 
The presence of the superscript рқ‘–в…Ҷ refers to an ideal solution model (which must be specified and can 
thus lead to confusion) whereas its absence indicates reference to the considered real solution. This 
definition automatically extends to corresponding increment functions, indicated by the symbol О”. 
In words, the excess function can be defined as В«the difference between the function for the mixture 
in some specified state and that for an ideal solution of the same composition, or the difference 
between the increment in the function on forming the mixture from the reference state and that on 
forming an ideal solutionВ» (Missen 1969).  
(Graetzel and Infelta 2006) introduce the concept in the specific case of mixtures of real gases, defining 
excess functions as the difference between real and ideal changes in the system due to mixing. 
Practically, (Friedman 1960) considers excess functions to offer the most convenient way to describe 
the deviation from ideality for a given solution.
