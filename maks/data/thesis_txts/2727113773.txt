Abstract
In this thesis we establish a method for solving chance constrained optimal control
problems through two cases. This method approximates the chance constraints using
the distribution dynamics of a linearization of the model of interest. We find that the
method is usable for a broad range of chance constrained problems, as long as the
distribution dynamics can be estimated properly by a linearization. The first case
concerns a linear model for the temperature dynamics of a cooling house with simple
chance constraints and a cost function that describe the price of electricity used for
cooling. With this model we approximate the chance constraints in two different ways,
giving us two different optimal control problems. One with dynamic variance and one
with a stationary variance. The solution to both problems is a control variable that
cools the room more when the price of electricity is low and less when the price
of electricity is high. For the optimal control problem with a dynamic variance the
optimal control results in a set of temperatures that constantly decreases, whereas the
optimal control problem with a stationary variance results in a somewhat constant
set of temperatures. Of these two problems the stationary variance results in the
lowest cost. The second case is with a non-linear model of a power grid with both
controllable and renewable energy sources and a set of chance constraints concerning
synchronisation and stability. We define two different optimal control problems for the
power grid. One where we estimate the dynamics of the expectation linearly and one
where we estimate the dynamics of the expectation non-linearly using the uncertainty
principle. The solution to the optimal control problem with the linear expectation
has a control variable that is almost constant. But when used in a simulation of the
original stochastic dynamics the chance constraints are violated. The solution to the
non-linear optimal control problem has a rapidly fluctuating control variable, that
when used to simulate the original stochastic dynamics results in trajectories that
meets the chance constraints.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The focus of this thesis is to establish and implement a method for solving a stochastic
optimal control problem with chance constraints and test it on two relevant cases. An
increasing part of the worlds energy production comes from renewable energy sources
such as sun and wind. This gives us new challenges as the power generation becomes
fluctuating and uncontrollable [1]. At times the sun shines and the wind blows and we
get plenty of renewable energy. At other times it is cloudy and windless, resulting in
less. For the purpose of this project we part the power chain into three layers. On the
upper layer we have the transmission, on the middle layer we have the distribution,
and on the lowest layer we have the consumer. In this project we work with two
cases, one from the upper layer and one from the lowest. As a consumer you are
confronted with this uncontrollably of the renewable energy production through the
price of power, as the price is often the result of supply and demand. A way of coping
with this, and thereby decrease the price of energy, is by having a flexible energy
use. Lineage is a worldwide company working with cold storage solutions. One such
solution is a cooling house placed in Aarhus, Denmark. The inside of this cooling
house is kept at freezing temperatures all year round, resulting in a massive cost
of power. Finding a temperature control that minimizes the price of power, while
keeping the temperatures in an acceptable range, is thereby of great interest for the
company. On the uppermost layer the stability of power grids are affected by the
fluctuating power generation [2]. An unstable power grid can lead to shutdowns of
powerplants and blockouts in large areas. To keep the power grids stable we therefore
have to control the generation of power from dispatchable sources such as gas. This
leads us to our problem statement.
Problem statement
Maintaining the temperature in an industrial cooling house
in the required range takes a lot of power which costs a lot off money. The interest
of this project is to find a control that keeps this temperature while minimising the
price thereof. Furthermore we wish to expand this method to solving a control problem
concerning synchronization and stability of power grids.
The two constrained control problems described above can both be stated as
stochastic optimal control problems with chance constrains. As stochastic systems
contain uncertainties we state constraints using probabilities. Approximation of these
chance constraints are in the literature done using a broad range of methods, where

include Lagrangian relaxation and the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation [3], polyno-

In this project we choose to approximate the chance constraints using the distribution

In reality we establish and define the solution algorithm for a stochastic optimal
control problem through the two cases. With the first case, in Chapter 4, we define
a deterministic and a stochastic optimal control problem for the temperatures of
the Lineage industrial cooling house. We include chance constraints in the stochastic
optimal control problem. Due to the dynamics of our model we do this in two different
ways. We construct a solution algorithm that discretizes the deterministic optimal
control problem according to a multiple shooting algorithm, and solves it using the
IPOPT software package. The solution algorithm is implemented in Python using
the CasADi toolbox. We then expand the algorithm to solve the stochastic optimal
control problem with chance constraints. The second case includes a more intricate
model and more complicated constraints. To accommodate this the solution algorithm
is once again expanded. The end result is a solution algorithm that can easily be
modified to a broad range stochastic optimal control problems.

3.1
Thermal energy transfer
In this section we review the theory behind heat transfer. We use this to form a
model for temperature dynamics in Section 4.2. All theory is from Ghoshdastidar
[12].
3.1.1
Newton’s law of cooling
Newton’s law of cooling describes the heat transfer mechanism called convection.
With this we describe the thermal energy transferred from a body, b, to its surround-
ings, s, as
q = hA(Tb −Ts)
(3.1)
where q is the rate of heat flow [kJ/h], h is the heat transfer coeﬀicient [W/(m2◦C)], A
is the surface area between the body an the surroundings [m2], Tb is the temperature
[◦C] of the body and Ts is the temperature [◦C] of the surroundings.
3.1.2
Fourier’s law of heat conduction
Fourier’s law of heat conduction describe the heat transfer through a wall separating
two environments of different temperatures. The rate of heat transfer, q, is described
as
q = −kA
L (Tb −Ta)
(3.2)
where k is the thermal conductivity of the wall [kJ/(hm◦C)], A is the surface area
of the wall [m2], L is the thickness of the wall [m], Ta is the temperature [◦C] of
environment a and Tb is the temperature [◦C] of environment b.

3.2
Power grids
In this section we introduce the mathematical model used for describing the dynamics
of a power grid. The theory is from Nishikawa [13] and Dorfler [14].
A power grid is build up of generators and loads, together called nodes/busses,
and the connections between these called links. A generator represents a production
of energy in the grid, which may come from both controllable sources such as the
burning of fossil fuels and uncontrollable sources such as wind. The load represents
the consumers, i.e. the extraction of energy from the grid. In the following we define
ng as the number of generators and n as the total number of nodes.
3.2.1
Admittance matrix
The physical structure of the power grid can be described by the admittance matrix,
Y0, in which element (i, j) is denoted Y0ij. In this matrix the off-diagonal elements,
Y0ij for i ̸= j, is the negative of the admittance between node i and j. The diagonal

The specific case of this section concerns a cooling house owned by Lineage located in
Aarhus, Denmark. Our goal is to create a control that minimises the cost of power,

The case specific information introduced in this section comes from [15] together

The amount of

it has the space to store more than 450 tons of goods, and in one day around 250

Our primary focus is on the latter.
The freezing room consists of three

relatively thin and made of concrete it is assumed that they have no noticeable effect
on the temperature of the room. In each section the room has three evaporators for
cooling placed imediately below the ceiling. The cooling room has two evaporators.

separating the rooms. A sketch of the cooling house is seen in Figure 4.1.

4.2 The model

4.2
The model
For the purpose of describing the temperature dynamics of the cooling house we
introduce the collective internal energy of the stored goods, Ug, and the internal
energy of the indoor air, Ur. We also introduce the temperatures Tg and Tr of the
goods and the indoor air, respectively. All four of these map the time interval [t0, tf]
to real values. The relation between the temperatures and the internal energy can
be described using the heat capacities, cr and cg, of the indoor air and the goods
respectively.
Ur(t) = crTr(t)
Ug(t) = cgTg(t)
The cooling house contains several types of goods with different parameters for
each good. These include heat capacities, heat transfer flows and temperatures. How-
ever to simplify the model we only use a single state to describe them, and all param-
eters related to the goods represent a mean or overall value for the set of goods. We
assume that the internal energy of the indoor air is only affected by the temperature
of the goods, the evaporators, the radiation from the sun and the outside tempera-
ture. Furthermore we assume that the internal energy of the goods is only affected
by the temperature difference between itself and the indoor air. The thermal energy
exchange between the indoor air and the goods can be described using Newtons law
of cooling.
Qgr(t) = −kgr(Tr(t) −Tg(t))
The parameter kgr > 0 describes the rate of the thermal exchange and is specific for
the goods and the air. As the equation describes the heat exchange from the goods,
Ug, to the air, Ur, we add the negative sign. The exchange of thermal energy between
the indoor air and the outside is described using Fourier’s law of heat conduction.
Qor(t) = −kor(Tr(t) −To(t))
where To : [t0, tf] 7→R describes the outside temperature at time t, and kor > 0
describes the conductivity of the building. We again include a negative sign, as the
equation describe the heat exchange from the outside to the indoor air. We simplify
the effect of the evaporators using the function Qre : [t0, tf] 7→R, which describes the
rate of thermal energy removed being from the indoor air using the evaporators. This
function is also used as our control variable for the system. The last source of change
to the system is Qsr : [t0, tf] 7→R, which describe the amount of energy added to the
system due to radiation from the sun. Collecting all the different thermal exchanges
we can describe the dynamics as
˙Ur(t) = Qgr(t) −Qre(t) + Qsr(t) + Qor(t)
˙Ug(t) = −Qgr(t)

4.6 Discussion

4.6
Discussion
As described in section 4.2.4 we had to scale the parameters from kJ to mJ as the
function nlpsol was not able to solve the problem, as it converged to a point of local
infeasibility with the larger values. As this scaling of parameters did not change the
model, the problem is most likely related to the numeric method.
For the three different optimization problems we chose three different sets of initial
values, Ur0 and Ug0. After running tests on the problems we quickly observed that the
optimal control, independent on initial values, resulted in temperatures that lay close
to the minimum possible value, Tmax for (4.10), Tmax −
p
ρj,j(t)γ1−ϵ/2 for (4.11) and
Tmax −pρ∗
j,jγ1−ϵ/2 for (4.12). If a lower initial value were chosen, the solution would
result in a control, Qre, which moved the temperatures up to the before-mentioned
values. We chose initial values, Ur0 and Ug0, which where as close to this minimum
temperature as possible, to minimise the initial values’ influence on the solution.
By doing this the behavior of the control variable, Qre, dependent on the price of
electricity, w(t), became more clear as it did not have to move the temperature to a
more optimal area.
In section 4.4 we note that in the solutions to the deterministic, (4.10), and both
of the stochastic optimization problems, (4.11) and (4.12), the control variable, Qref,
changes its behaviour at the end of the time interval.
This is likely because the
optimization does not take the time after the interval of interest, t > tf, into account.
In all three solutions we observed that the control variable assumed low values despite
the low energy prices. This keeps the temperature at its minimum (Tmax for (4.10),
Tmax −
p
ρj,j(t)γ1−ϵ/2 for (4.11) and Tmax −pρ∗
j,jγ1−ϵ/2 for (4.12)), however we
cannot decrease the control value if and when the energy prices increases in the
future without increasing the temperatures, thereby violating the constraints. This
would be a problem if we were in a situation where the time after tf was important,
e.g. with an infinite timeline and a rolling time interval. With a rolling time line
we split a larger time interval, [t0, tf], into smaller pieces [t0i, tfi] for i = 1, ..., m
and solve these iteratively. We can avoid the unwanted behavior by making the time
interval, [t0, tf], larger than needed for the specific situation. In the case of a short
and limited time interval, we would choose tf larger than needed. And in the case of
a rolling time interval, we would choose tfi+1 > t0i for i indicating the the ith interval
in a set of consecutive time intervals, [t0i, tfi].
In Section 4.4 we found the solutions to two different versions of the stochastic
optimal control problem with chance constraints, (4.11) and (4.12). With (4.12) we
consider the same number of constraints as with the deterministic problem (4.10),
whereas with (4.11) we add constraints for the confidence intervals.
Despite this
it only takes one more iteration for nlpsol to find the optimal solution (see Table
4.2). Looking at the cost function for these two solutions (Table 4.2) we observe that
cost is lowest for (4.12). The reason is likely that this control (Figure 4.8) keeps
the overall temperatures at a constant value, whereas the overall temperature of
the other solution (Figure 4.7) is constantly decreasing, which requires more cooling.
The downside of the solution for (4.12) is however that it keeps the temperature much

5.5
Discussion
Due to rapidly increasing variances of the generator frequencies, we decided to set the
standard deviation of all parameters in dt equal to 0.0002. This is quite a small value
given that the mean, µd, has values close to one. It is likely that the distribution
for these parameters is larger in reality. In the stochastic simulation of the system
in Figure 5.4 we observed that the noise was not visible despite the large confidence
intervals indicating that it should be. It is possible that our realisations have only
captured the part of the distribution that lies closest to the mean. It is more reason-
able to assume that this approximation of the non-linear distribution dynamics is not
adequate. Furthermore we observed that the simulations of the stochastic non-linear
model with the control from the solution to the linear optimal control problem did
not comply with the chance constraints. Again this could be due to our specific re-
alisations, and not a characteristic of the controlled system. As the solution to the
non-linear optimal control problem did satisfy our chance constrains it seems that
this approximation of the expectation is more accurate. Due to the large confidence
interval of our approximative states we had to choose a quite large value for ϵω, and
our constraints did therefore allow large fluctuations in the frequency of the genera-
tors. In our simulation in Figure 5.4 the frequencies of the generators took unequal
values and the system was therefore not synchronous. When we look at the angle
of rotation of the generators we however observed that they had an almost constant
decrease, and that none of them diverged. We will need more knowledge about the
physical system before we can make any conclusions about whether the solution is
still viable. Looking at the active power generation in the solution to the non-linear
system (Figure 5.4) we observed that it fluctuated rapidly changing values three times
per second. This is perhaps more often than possible for the real power plant. A more
realistic solution might therefore be found with a control function that is piece-wise
constant for longer time periods.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In Section 2.3 we introduced two different ways of stating the optimal control problem.
One where constraints state that the confidence interval of the linearly transformed
process, µz,j, has to stay inside a set of bounds. The other with constraints stating
that µz,j has to stay inside a set of tighter bounds defined using the stationary
variance. If the system has a stationary variance, this variance can be reached either
inside or outside of the time interval used for control. In Section 4.4 we found that
the stationary variance of the temperatures, Tr and Tg, was found after 333 days,
which was 333 times later than the end of time period of interest.
Furthermore
the stationary variance was much larger than the dynamic variance. With this we
get a much tighter set of constraints than necessary. These tighter constraints could
exclude an optimal solution with a more optimal cost, that otherwise could have been
reached. As stated in Section 4.6 we can loosen the constraints by using the largest
variance in our time frame rather than the stationary variance. A clear difference
between the two optimal control problems is that with the first method we work
with a dynamic variance, and in the second we work with a constant variance. This
difference will likely give us different solutions. But is one better than the other?
Not necessarily. This depends on our model of interest. With the Lineage case we
observed that the constant variance gave us an optimum with a lower cost. With
a completely different model, this might not have been the case. Another question
that comes up when looking at these two methods is: which solution is easiest to
compute? We can part the solution method into two parts. The first part where
we define the inputs to nlpsol, and the second part where we use nlpsol to solve
the system. For the first part the difference lies in when we calculate the variance,
and what objects we store them in. With the stationary variance we simulate the
variance, and from this estimate the stationary values. If the bounds are applied
directly to the states, the variance is then used directly for the bounds of these. If
the bounds are on a linear combination of the states, we instead fist define this linear
combination and then use the variance to define the bounds on this linear combination.
For the dynamic variance, we calculate the variance at every time step. This variance
is then used to state the constraint equations. The largest difference between the
method with the dynamic variance and the stationary variance is then the number
of times we calculate the confidence interval, which is one or n times. We cannot
say much about the difference in the number of constraints, other than that the
method with the dynamic variance always will result in at least as many constraints
==PAGE:58==
6 Discussion
51
as with the constant variance. The largest difference might therefore lie in the second
part of the solution method. Here the difference depends on how nlpsol handles
the constraints. In the case where a state variable has no constraints, we define its
bounds as −∞≤µxt ≤∞. If nlpsol handles these constraints the same as with
the constraints −xmin ≤µxt ≤xmax, then the method with the dynamic variance
will always have most constraints, making this problem larger. With a few states this
might not make a considerable difference to the solver, but with larger problems it
might. Which method is best, is therefore not easily determined and depends on the
problem.
Does the method of using chance constraints for stochastic optimal control guar-
antee a solution where all realizations of the stochastic process, xt, stays inside of the
bounds for t ∈[t0, tf]? No. With the chance constraints we define a set of constraints
such that at every time, t, there is a probability that the stochastic process stays
inside of the bounds. If we set α = 0.01 (as we do in this project), then at every time,
t, 99% of all realisations will stay inside of the bounds.
For a nonlinear system, our method is an approximation of the linearization made
at the initial point. Our control is thus based on an estimate of xt with an error
that increases with time. In this project we attempted to decrease by linearizing the
system anew at every time step i. For this we used the CasADi function jacobian, to
differentiate the symbolic expressions. With every time step these expressions became
increasingly complex and after a few time steps (less than 20) the function used for
defining the inputs to nlpsol broke down. By breaking down we mean that that it
would take increasingly longer for the function to do the calculations in every step
and the computer would freeze. We believe that a reason behind this might be that
CasADi constantly keeps track of the derivatives of the objects, and that they then
became to complex. With more knowledge about CasADi, this might be avoidable.
We define the bounds on our constraints such that they are constant at all time.
So what would happen if an initial value lies outside of the bounds or close enough
to the limits such that the confidence interval lies outside the bounds for the few first
time steps? With the current algorithm this makes the problem infeasible. With the
Lineage case we could be in a situation like this, if they got a large load of warmer
goods, or if temperature requirements suddenly changed due to a change in the nature
of the goods. At the moment the algorithm is not able to handle varying bounds,
however this is a change that could easily be implemented. A way of handling the
situation with an initial value outside of the bounds would be to loosen the bounds
such that the constraints had to be inside the ’correct’ bounds before some time s.
This could be done by making broader or by removing bounds for t < s.
As described in the introduction, chance constraints are in the literature approx-
imated using a broad range of methods.
According to [27] a large part of these
methods are quite conservative. The method used in this project should not be as
conservative, and the method that is most similar is the sampling method. With the
sampling method the state distribution is found through trajectory samples, whereas
in this project we find it using a linear approximation. In both methods it is possible
to use the wrong distribution. With sampling we might avoid this by having enough
==PAGE:59==
6 Discussion
52
samples. With our linear approximation this error in the distribution depends on the
state dynamics and the time interval of interest (as described earlier in this chapter).
This makes the sampling method more versatile. Furthermore the sampling method
is useful for a broad range of distributions, whereas our method is only valid for a
Gaussian distribution.

CHAPTER 7
Further use of the
methods
The algorithm in this project is usable for a broad range of stochastic systems, with
the only strict restriction being that they follow a normal distribution at every time
t. The method relies on a linearization of the system and the most obvious choice is
to use it for linear systems. For non-linear systems the method might still be very
useful. In these cases we however have to be aware that the linearization introduces
errors that increase with time.
At the moment the optimal control algorithm presented in this project is in the
form of an open loop control, i.e.
we have no feedback.
We can introduce some
feedback by including it in an online loop. For this we can involve the Kalman filter.
This algorithm would use three steps in every iteration. The first step would be were
data is collected from the true system. This true system is where a control input is
used to control a process and the data comes from. This process could be areal life
process such as the Lineage cooling house, but it could also be a simulated process.
It is important that we know or have an estimate of the dynamics in the form of a
system of stochastic differential equations. The observations of this system can either
be some or all states directly or a transformation of these. With this data we then
move to the next step. In this step we filter the data, such that we get an estimate
of all states and the covariance matrix of these. Our suggestion is to do this using an
extended Kalman filter. Before we can use the online control algorithm we need more
than one data point. This is because the Kalman filter needs several iterations before
reaching a good estimate. With the state estimates we go on to the third step. At
this step we compute the control. We do this using the state estimate as our initial
values for the mean, and with the estimate of the covariance as our initial covariance.
If we use the control method with the stationary confidence interval, we do not use
the estimated covariance matrix. The optimal control is found for a time period that
is longer than the time between the used observations. In this way we avoid the
problems discussed in Section 4.6 about the control at the end of the time interval
of interest. This control is then fed to the first step, and the algorithm moves on to
the next iteration. A sketch of the algorithm is included in Figure 7.1. By iteratively
finding a new control, like described above, we obtain a control that matches the true
state more accurately. For non-linear systems this algorithm gives us a chance to use

in every iteration, and thereby limit the errors of the linear approximation.

Conclusion
With a simple stochastic model describing the temperature dynamics of an industrial
cooling house we were able to find a control that that minimized the cost of power,
while satisfying chance constraints concerning a set of temperature bounds.
The
solution algorithm used the linear distribution dynamics of the confidence intervals
of the states as chance constraint approximaters. As the covariance of the system
reached a stationary value for t →∞, we approximated the chance constrained
optimal control problem in two different ways. One using the dynamic covariance
and one using the stationary covariance. Both problems resulted in a control that
took a minimum value when the electricity price was high and a maximum value
when the price was low.
The difference between the two solutions were that the
solution to the problem with the dynamic covariance had a constantly decreasing set of
temperatures, while the stationary covariance resulted in near constant temperatures.
Due to this the latter resulted in a lower price for electricity.
A disadvantage to
the problem statement with the stationary covariance was that it resulted in a quite
large confidence interval, which kept the temperatures very low. We found that a
way to lessen this disadvantage was to replace the stationary covariance with the
largest covariance reached in the desired time interval. Furthermore we found that
the difference in computational requirements for the two methods were negligible for
our specific problem.
We were able to expand the solution method, such that we could solve a chance
constrained optimal control problem concerning the synchronization and stability of a
power grid. As the stochastic dynamics of the power grid were non-linear, the method
suggested that we optimized the linearized distribution dynamics. The solution to
this problem was a control variable that took fluctuating values in the beginning of
the time interval and then reached a constant value. A simulation of the non-linear
dynamics with this optimal control showed us that it did not satisfy the chance con-
straints. Using the uncertainty principle we then restated the problem such that the
expectation followed the non-linear dynamics. This resulted in a rapidly fluctuat-
ing control, that did indeed satisfy the chance constrains. We could therefore infer
that the non-linear expectation was a better approximation than the linearized. The
linearized dynamics of the covariance resulted in large confidence intervals. Due to
this some fluctuations of the generator frequencies were allowed, and the system was
therefore not fully synchronous. However as the system did not diverge as a result
of this, the system might still be stable in the physical sense. The large variance
==PAGE:64==
8 Conclusion
57
of the noise was not visible in the simulations, we thereby have reason to believe
that the linearization is not an adequate approximation of the non-linear distribution
dynamics.
A result of the two cases was a solution algorithm which, depending on accuracy of
the linearization, could solve an optimal control problem with chance constraints. For
future uses the solution method can easily be adapted to the specifics of a dynamic
model, making it usable for a large range of problems. Furthermore it can together
be a part of an online optimization algorithm.

A plot of the variance in the time interval t ∈[0, 5] is plotted for all states in
Figure C.1. Here we observe that some variances reach a stationary value, where
other variances are constantly increasing.
