Abstract
Intelligent Transportation Systems bring the possibility of introducing innovation in transportation
systems, aiming to make them safer, more accessible, more reliable and improve the users’ general
satisfaction. This is conceivable implementing disruptive technologies with the goal making traﬀic more
eﬀicient and minimizing the possible problems that occur on the roads. As part of the traﬀic, public
transports have potential enhancements. One of these is providing users with an accurate system of
arrival to the destination.
This Master Thesis presents a design and implementation of a system that predicts the Estimated
Time of Arrival of a bus to chosen destination. A process to transform GPS data captured by Ride
the Future project buses circulating in Linköping (Sweden) is presented. Raw data is treated to obtain
a dataset formed by segments that compose the entire bus route. Thereafter, the obtained dataset is
prepared and encoded in order to be fitted in a Machine Learning model, with the goal of predicting
the travel time along every segment in different moments of the day. With the travel predictions for
every segment that makes part of a route, the Estimated Time of Arrival is computed.
During the development of the project, several Machine Learning models had been examined, expos-
ing the benefits and downsides of all each of them and evaluating their performance on faced prediction
problem. The most advanced presented model is a Double Graph Neural Network, which is the model
that achieves the best results, outperforming the established baselines and simpler models. Therefore,
this model is the used as basis to build the final arrival time estimator, getting a predictor with an
satisfactory performance.

to take into consideration their importance in urban planning.
Sometimes traﬀic in the cities is a

In recent years, the number of passenger cars and other types of vehicles on the roads has increased,
provoking also a rise in road congestion [unk; KW18]. According to studies carried out in the most
important cities in the world, in cities like London or Paris, the average time that a person spends in
congestion is above 140 hours per year [Man]. Road congestion is a considerable nuisance to travelers,
constitutes a large annual societal loss, and has a significant environmental impact. In order to be able
to limit the consequences of congestion and reduce the environmental impact, it is important that the
current road network is utilized as well as possible and that new infrastructures are planned taking into
account their impact.

1.2
Problem statement
One of the experimental AVs is running in the city of Linköping (Sweden) under the surveillance of
Linköping University and the Swedish National Road and Transport Research Institute. Concretely,
there are three autonomous buses (shuttles) operating in a 3,7 kilometers-long route across the university
campus and a close urban district [fut21], in which they collect people at a diverse number of stops.
During the journeys, the buses collect several types of data, being GPS position one of them. GPS data
can be used to determine the bus position at every moment and to establish the followed route with the
times needed to go from point A to point B. The analysis of different travel times between the two same
points provides valuable knowledge that can be used in future rides to establish the ETA (Estimated
Time of Arrival) of the bus. ETA is a commonly used term in Intelligent Transportation Systems that
refers to the expected time that a journey will take, informing receivers about the estimated date and
time in which the destination will be reached.
1.3
Goals
The main goal of this master thesis is to analyze and use the data related to the position of the described
shuttles, in order to predict the ETA to the next station, using Machine Learning approaches and taking
into account different parameters involved in traﬀic forecasting.
This project aims to design, develop and evaluate a valid procedure able to estimate the ETA to
every position of the route using data acquired by Linköping’s shuttles and other parameters that may
have an influence on the driving conditions, e.g. the day of the week. In order to predict the ETA to
the next station, Machine Learning approaches will be used taking into account different parameters
involved in traﬀic forecasting. As the most advanced approach, the route followed by the buses will be
modeled as a graph and Deep Learning techniques will be applied in order to accomplish the established
goal. In particular, Graph Neural Networks (GNN) will be used to perform the estimations. Thanks to
their ability to capture spatial dependence, GNNs are the most appropriate method to perform traﬀic
forecasting problems [JL22].
1.4
Methodology
The steps to perform during the development of the project will be:
1. Acquire knowledge about the topic and its state of the art, focusing on traﬀic forecasting and the
necessary tools to perform the tasks.
2. Understand the data captured by the shuttles, as well as its relationship with external parameters
that may influence the road condition.
3. Design of a transformation method for the data to fit it in a Machine Learning method.
4. Fit the data in designed ML models. First in classical models; later on, in a neural network, such
as Feed-forward Neural Network; and finally, in a more advanced model, such as a Graph Neural
Network.
5. Evaluate the results and compare them with results obtained from baseline models.
6. Write a report presenting the development carried out and the obtained results.
A more detailed planning can be seen in the Appendix D.

2.1
Machine Learning
In the current world, more data is collected every day and it is becoming a more important and valuable
asset for companies, being even the most valuable resource of some of them [Bha19]. Data helps to have
a better understanding of the world through the identification of patterns that can be used to foresee
events creating an advantage. Data has been always analyzed in order to extract value from it, but due
to the high amounts handled nowadays that surpass humans’ abilities to make sense of it, the process
must be automated creating systems that can learn from the data and its changes.
Machine Learning (ML) [RN09], a subset of Artificial Intelligence, is ”the field of study that gives
computers the ability to learn without being explicitly programmed”, as Arthur Samuel, a pioneer in the
field, stated in 1959. ML makes use of a set of computational methods based on statistics, pattern recog-
nition, optimization, and data mining to answer questions related to the data, bringing the possibility
of deriving meaning and learning from it in order to perform predictions of future unseen data.
Since data can have different structures, not all the data can be used for the same purpose. All ML
algorithms take data as input, but their goal can be different, therefore it is possible to define three
main, but not unique, ML tasks:
• Classification.It is the task of predicting a class label (category) among a discrete set of labels
learned from the training data. An example of a classification problem is the categorization of a
handwritten character as its corresponding letter from the alphabet.
• Regression.
It is the task of predicting a continuous value taking into account the existing
values and relationships in the input attributes. An example of regression is the prediction of an
employee’s salary based on features like age, job level and experience.
• Clustering. It is the task consisting of creating sets of unlabelled samples such samples in the
same group are more similar to other samples in the same group than to the samples from other
groups. An example of clustering is client segmentation.
The learning process performed by different ML algorithms is adapted to the data. According to
the used data structures, ML methods can be classified in three levels:
• Supervised ML. The algorithm is trained on labeled data. Using several data attributes, rela-
tionships between the label and the data are established. So on, the predictions are performed by
identifying patterns in the new data and associating the most similar label. Supervised learning
includes a large list of models, among the most used ones are Linear/Logistic Regression, Decision
trees, K-Nearest Neighbours, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting...

• Unsupervised ML. The algorithm is not told about what is represented in the data, so its task
is to discover patterns and correlations in it creating subsets of data. This can be used for different
assignments like clustering or anomaly detection. Some of the most popular algorithms that are
part of this level are hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN, K-Means clustering, Gaussian Mixture
models, etc.
• Reinforcement ML. As well as Unsupervised Learning, it is used with unlabelled but the goal
is to ask questions about the data to get an outcome (typically an action) and grade it using a
reward. The goal of the algorithm is to maximize the reward.
Today Machine Learning algorithms can be found in a wide range of fields with many different
applications with high impact on the real world, e.g. healthcare, transportation, finances, energy, and
manufacturing, among others. Some more specific examples are the adaption of the content to users’
interests in social media, improvement of Internet searches, recommendation systems, energy price
forecasting, prediction of diseases...

between the ground truth and the model outputs. In general, the goal of a neural network can be

The learning process in Neural Networks consists in minimizing an objective function (loss function)
that measures the error between the ground truth and the prediction made by the model. This opti-
mization process is performed during the training part, in which the network adjusts its parameters
(weights of the neurons and their connections) to get closer to the optimal result. In order to correctly
modify the weights, the model uses a gradient vector that indicates the error increase or decrease if
the corresponding weight would be increased by an infinitesimal amount, i.e. computes the gradient
of the loss function with respect to the weight of each neuron. Then, the weight vector is adjusted in
the opposite direction to the gradient vector since the goal is to minimize the objective function. This
approach, when used repeatedly for many small sets of samples (batches) and computing the average
gradient until it stops decreasing, is known as Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). SGD speeds up the

data can be modeled as a graph... The next section in this Chapter will describe more in the detail
GNNs, since they play an important role in this project.
Deep Learning differs from classical Machine Learning models by the used data: it can use as
input unstructured data, like images or text, automating the feature extraction. While in classical ML
approaches the features’ importance must be established by a human expert, DL algorithms are able to
decide which features are more important to determine the output allowing to obtain predictions with
increased accuracy.
2.2.1
Hyperparameters
Machine Learning models and, especially, Deep Learning models use a high number of computations
to give the final output.
In this process, the Machine Learning engineer setting up the model has
the responsibility of controlling the learning process. This is done by selecting the desired training
hyperparameters.
A hyperparameter is a parameter that is set up before the start of the training
process and it has a direct influence on the model’s training and its later performance [Dee20].
==PAGE:16==
2.2 Deep Learning
6
The next elements related to the training process can be found among the hyperparameters:
• Batch size. The training size is divided into different subsets (batches) to speed up the learning
process. These batches are inputted into the model. The ideal situation would be to have bigger
batches, but bigger batches make the optimization slower due to the number of gradients that
must be computed.
• Number of epochs. An epoch corresponds to a complete training cycle using all the data from the
training set. The number of epochs establishes how many times the model should be trained on
the data.
• Learning rate. It controls the size of the step that the model needs to perform to get closer to the
optimal value of the loss function. It is one of the most important hyperparameters in ANN.
The process of selecting the right hyperparameter combination to maximize the performance is
known as hyperparameter tuning. This process does not have a best value for each model, but it is
more a task that requires several trials and errors until finding the values that lead to the best results.
Since this can lead to execute the training step multiple times, with the corresponding time that it
requires, there are methods that perform this searching process among a selected hyperparameter space.
Grid Search trains and evaluates a model for all the possible hyperparameter combinations. Random
Search chooses combinations of random hyperparameters from a statistical distribution provided for
each hyperparameter.
These two mentioned methods are easy to implement, but computationally
expensive due to the number of tried models that must be evaluated, moreover, they do not guarantee
finding the optimal values. For a more optimal tuning, Bayesian Optimization can be used. It creates a
probabilistic model of a function mapping hyperparameters with previous model performances in order
to choose the new hyperparameters. Like this past evaluations are taken into account when choosing
the next hyperparameters set to evaluate accelerating the process of finding the most appropriate values
[FH19].
2.2.2
Regularization techniques
A ML model is considered to be good if it is capable to generalize in the proper way a new input sample
corresponding to the same problem, being able in this way of making predictions of future data with
samples never seen by the model. In some situations, ML models can have a poor performance after
being trained in a specific dataset. This can be produced by overfitting or underfitting episodes, due to
the fact that the complexity of the model is not properly adjusted to the data. Regularization techniques
are used to control models’ complexity by calibrating them in order to reduce the generalization error
and prevent the model from overfitting or underfitting.
Overfitting occurs when the model fits exactly its training data without enough generalization.
Therefore, when to model is evaluated in unseen data, its performance is not accurate. Low error in the
train set predictions and high error in predictions using the test set are a clear sign of overfitting. This
phenomenon is in general produced by an excessive complexity of a model or overtraining. So a logical
solution to this problem could be to stop the training process earlier. This is known as early stopping,
and it is a regularization technique, but it can also result in other issues if the model is not trained for
enough time to generalize well to the data resulting in an underfitting situation.
Other regularization techniques besides early stopping are:
• L1 regularization. It adds a penalty term to the loss function that is equal to the absolute value
of weight parameters, trying to estimate the median of the data.
• L2 regularization. This technique adds a penalty term equal to the square of the weight parameters,
as means of estimating the mean of the data.
==PAGE:17==
2.3 Graph Neural Networks
7
• Dropout. While training of the model is performed, the results coming from randomly selected
hidden neurons are ignored in order to stimulate the other hidden neurons. Like this, interdepen-
dence among neurons in learning is reduced.
• Data augmentation. The simplest way of avoiding overfitting is to increase the size of the training
data, but this is not always possible. This technique consists in introducing copied samples but
with slight modifications or creating synthetic samples.

2.3.1
Graphs
The world is full of entities with all types of relations between them. These entities and their connections
can be represented using graphs, since they are a general non-linear structure used for describing and
analyzing entities with relations or interactions. There are many types of data that can be represented
naturally as graphs, as for example food webs, underground networks, roads, social networks, commu-
nication networks, molecules and any other data in which relations are established. The modeling of
this data as graphs helps to get easily the context knowledge contained in the entities and capture the
relationships between different entities. In this way, complex data can be structured as a relational
graph to synthesize the contained information.

2.3.2
Graphs applications in Machine Learning
Graphs include large amounts of information about the represented data. Using the appropriate tech-
niques it is possible to learn encoded information. Machine Learning aims to build models that learn
from data in order to be able to solve some tasks related to it. Making use of graphs, it is possible
to formulate several Machine Learning related problems, in different abstraction levels in the graph:
graph-level, subgraph-level, node-level or edge-level [Les21b].
Starting from the lowest level, node-level tasks try to predict a property of a node, classify it or
predict the position of a new node taking into account the attributes of the node and its relationships
with other nodes.
An application of it in the real world is AlphaFold, which can predict protein
structures helping to accelerate progress in many areas of research related with health [Jum+20]. Link
prediction aims to predict whether new links between a pair of nodes can be added since the relationship
between them is similar to the relationship between two other nodes that are related. Nowadays, this
technique is often used by companies in their recommender systems in which users get recommendations
about products depending on the previously purchased products [Yin+18].
Subgraph-level tasks are performed on a portion of a graph. They introduce some extra challenges,
like non-trivial internal topology or varying sizes in dynamic graphs [Als+20].
These tasks are for
instance used, among others, in the estimation of traveling time used in Google Maps [Der+21]. This
application is directly related with the goal of this thesis, and further explanations about this topic will
be covered in next sections.
In higher-level tasks, different tasks can be accomplished, such as graph classification, regression and
clustering. In this kind of application, the objective is to learn over a dataset with multiple graphs to
make predictions related to each of the graphs. It has been used, for example, for drug discovery: given
that molecules can be represented as graphs, it is possible to classify different molecules and promising
molecules from a pool of billions of candidates allowing the discovery of new drugs and new uses of
molecules [Sto+20].
In most of the cases, the previously mentioned tasks are supervised learning, since the data is labeled
and the correct solution to the task is known, but graphs are also suitable to apply unsupervised learning,
in which the model identifies patterns in not labeled data. Clustering and community detection are one
of these tasks. There the goal is to examine the graph with the purpose of creating clusters of nodes
grouped together based on some common features [Ham].
2.3.3
Graph Neural Layers
Graphs have arbitrary sizes and complex spatial structures since there are no reference points, so it
is not possible to define where a graph starts or finishes. Also, graphs are often dynamic and with
==PAGE:19==
2.3 Graph Neural Networks
9
multiple features assigned to their nodes and edges. Applying Deep Learning techniques to graphs is
not trivial [San+21].
A first approach to input a graph into a neural network would be representing the graph with its
adjacency matrix appending the corresponding features. This idea has several issues due to the fact that
the number of parameters is multiple times the number of nodes, the graphs do not have a clear order,
so the adjacency matrix is sensitive to node ordering, and this approach would not be able to represent
graphs of different sizes. Therefore, the idea behind how a GNN works is based on how Convolutional
Neural Networks work, where a convolutional operator (window) slides through the data. The issue is
that, due to the graph structure, it is not clear how to define a sliding window over the data. So, in
GNN if the goal is to predict the features from a node i, this node is going to take information from its
neighboring (adjacent) nodes and these are going to aggregate information from their neighbors. Figure
2.3 shows an example of node aggregation.
Figure 2.3: Each node aggregates information from its adjacent nodes following the graph architecture
(left). This example shows the nodes aggregation when a prediction for the target node A
is done (right). The target node takes the information from its neighbors averaging it and
applying a neural network (in the Figure, represented as grey boxes). Source: [Les21a].
An important difference between classical ANN and GNN is that there is a different neural network
architecture defined for each node based on the structure around the target node, so GNNs learn over
multiple architectures simultaneously.
A single GNN layer with the process in which information is taken from adjacent nodes is called
node embedding and consists of two steps. First, a message transformation in which information of
a neighbor node passes through a transformation function.
Second, message aggregation using an
aggregation operator. This operator must be permutation invariant since the ordering of the nodes in
a graph is arbitrary, i.e. the aggregation result must still be the same independently of the order of
the nodes. Some examples of aggregation functions are the mean, the sum or the maximum. Equation
2.5 shows the general expression that describes the update of a node embedding of a single target node
i at level l + 1 in which information from neighboring nodes j is aggregated. Nodes features hj are
multiplied by a weighted matrix W in order to be converted into higher-level features. At this point is
where the learning takes place, since the weight matrix comes from a fully connected neural network
that takes as input the node features. In other terms, when multiplying by this weighted matrix, each
of the node features vectors are passed through a single layer neural network. For each individual target
node, this process must be done only for the neighbors defined by the adjacency matrix, and then the
results are summed up into one embedding. Finally, an activation function σ is applied. The output of
the GNN are the updated node embeddings.

Performance of the models and error metrics
A fundamental step in every Machine Learning process is the train-test split, which indirectly allows
to evaluate the performance of a model. The procedure consists in dividing the used dataset into two
subsets, making possible the evaluation of the model using a different subset of data than the one used
for training it, and simulating like this the real use that will be given to the model in practice. By
splitting the dataset fed into the models, it is possible to evaluate the level of generalization that they
are achieving and the training and testing processes can be monitored to detect possible issues on it, e.g.
overfitting. Additionally, in some cases, a validation set is used in order to evaluate the performance of
the model on a small set of data formed by samples used during training.
Machine Learning algorithms try to learn the behavior of a target attribute making use of computa-
tional methods applied on training data features. Afterwards, the trained model can be used to make
predictions about a target variable. It is a complex task to decide if a model will perform better than
another one in a specific problem, therefore the verification of how accurate is the model prediction is
made by measuring errors.
There are multitude of error metrics [Bot19] than can be used, but, in order to accomplish these
comparisons during the development of this project, three error metrics will be used. The main criteria
for choosing these metrics is that they best adjust to the type of faced problem, which will be described
in next sections.

of the data, the bigger MSE can appear. A disadvantage of this metric is its weakness against
the presence of outliers in the data, which highly contribute to the error if the prediction is not
correct.

CHAPTER 3
Literature Review
Intelligent Transportation Systems aim to transform transportation methods to be safer, more con-
nected, more affordable and less detrimental to the environment. To achieve this goal, a long variety
of technologies is applied to the transportation systems. Among other multiple benefits, these enable
the travelers to be better informed and make a more coordinated and smarter use of transportation
networks making it possible to save money and time. The goal of this Chapter is to provide the reader
with an overview of the performed progress in the field of ETA estimations and its state of the art. Since
the final goal of this project is the design of an ETA prediction system using Graph Neural Networks,
the literature clearly distinguishes between approaches that use GNN and the ones that do not, but
first, a description of traﬀic forecasting is given.
3.1
Traﬀic forecasting
Traﬀic forecasting is the term that blends all tasks involved in the prediction of traﬀic behavior, based
on the collected data in transportation infrastructure. Several methods can be used to collect these
data, e.g. connected vehicles or road sensors. The understanding and analysis of these data make
possible to predict a long variety of events, from common places in which traﬀic incidents occur to the
duration of traﬀic jams, but one of the main areas in traﬀic forecasting is traﬀic flow prediction. This
essential component of ITS seeks to get accurate estimations about the traﬀic speed, density and travel
times at a future specific interval of time in a certain thoroughfare.
The term Traﬀic Forecasting is widely mentioned in the literature as it has high relevance due to
its impact on urban mobility, transport and logistics [Lan+18]. It has been an active research area
among the engineering community trying to improve transportation systems, but it gained even more
relevance in recent years with the development of new technologies and techniques related to massive
data. Data Science and ML play a determining role in traﬀic forecasting.
Nowadays, Traﬀic Forecasting discoveries are applied in multiple domains, for instance, they are
useful in modeling and planning roadway infrastructure, like the capacity of a highway or the required
bike lines in response to a predicted growth of traﬀic volumes, parking occupancy, bike-sharing demand
or finding the route with the shortest travel time (i.e. the fastest route) between two points [Lan+18].
The last example is the most related one with the main topic of this Master Thesis, since ETA pre-
dictions also require computing the travel times along a specific route. When making traﬀic predictions,
many different approaches can be used and, as the Machine Learning discipline is in constant evolution
and improvement, new techniques are implemented with possible applications in Traﬀic Forecasting.
Among the literature, it is feasible to distinguish between approaches that make use of graphs and the
ones that do not.
3.2
Approaches without Graph Neural Networks
Estimation of arrival times is a common topic among Traﬀic Forecasting since it has applications in
several domains.
Many of them are similar to this thesis, due to the fact that they also estimate
the arrival time of buses. Before the proliferation of Deep Learning techniques, several attempts were
==PAGE:24==
3.3 Approaches with Graph Neural Networks
14
performed using statistics and Classical Machine Learning models. In [RZ04] and [Cha+10], Linear
Regression, Principal Components and Nearest Neighbors were used as a basis to predict travel times
on freeways and for buses journeys. The authors of [WHL05] and [Men+] propose models based on
Support Vectors, while in [Cho+09] a simple approach based on statistical methods that makes use of
average historical data is exposed. All these implementations have several limitations due to their low
complexity and are clearly surpassed by Deep Learning models.
A remarkable experiment that does not make use of neural networks and aims to predict bus travel
times is the proposed in [Bai+15].
There, the authors describe a model based on Support Vector
Machines, which gives an offline estimation for the entire bus journey using historical data, and a
Kalman filtering-based algorithm, responsible of dynamically adjusting the travel time with the latest
operation information obtained from the bus. This model’s performance was tested in a dataset with
multiple bus routes in China, turning out to obtain better results than other simpler models defined
as a way of comparison, and even improving the results of an Artificial Neural Network, with a final
MAPE error of 5.2% and which corresponds to an average error of 22.7 seconds.
DeepTTE [Wan+18] defines a multi-approach model that makes use of both most common method-
ologies in travel time estimations: 1) split the journey into several segments and compute the times for
each of them, and 2) a general travel time estimation for the entire trip. This model makes use of the
feature extraction ability of Deep Neural Networks to create a model with several components: first,
a convolutional component that transforms GPS sequences into a series of feature maps, capturing in
this way possible local correlations; second, recurrent neural networks that learn temporal dependencies
from the previously extracted features; and lastly, an attribute component that incorporates external
conditions, like weather, path length, driver habit and day of the week. These components’ outputs
are used as input for fully-connected layers. In addition, to estimate entire path times, an attention
mechanism is used in order to learn the impact of the segments in the entire trip. DeepTTE model
was tested on two datasets with GPS tracks from Chinese taxis, obtaining MAPE errors of 11.89% and
10.92%, which in each case correspond to errors of 16.88 and 18.15 seconds. From the results of this
experiment, it should be pointed out the fact that the error rates decrease while the trip distance is
increased, e.g. the prediction for a 5 km trip has a higher error than the one for a 35 km trip.
In [Bar+20], the authors present under the name of BusTr a Machine Learning model based on
two Feed Forward Neural Networks fed with real-time traﬀic data in order to predict ETA of a bus.
These take charge of making predictions for different segments of a bus route. The model separately
predicts the time is taken to traverse each road segment and service every stop along the route and
the predictions are summed. This project, implemented in Google Maps as a forecasting system for
public buses, uses a GTFS dataset (General Transit Feed Specification [API22], which defines a common
format for public transit data). It includes two levels of features: first, full-trip context features, e.g. the
trip interval or real-time speed forecasts along the route; and second, features relative to each segment
and stop, like the distance traveled on each segment or a representation of the temporal moment in
which the journey will happen, using the day of the week and a half-hour slice for the moment of the
day. Each FFNN is customized to the type of segment on which is working. After training the model
using MSE as loss function and aiming to capture weekly trends more than the seasonality effect, they
obtain a MAPE of 15.16 %
3.3
Approaches with Graph Neural Networks
Routes representation by means of graphs allows to expand the dimensionality of the problem by
representing spatial information of the route. Like this, traﬀic forecasting problems with only a temporal
dimension can be turned into spatio-temporal traﬀic forecastings. This new representation requires the
usage of a different type of neural networks, Graph Neural Networks. These are a topic undergoing
intense study during last years and about which new methods are being published.
==PAGE:25==
3.3 Approaches with Graph Neural Networks
15
One of the most relevant research outputs is the one from Google in collaboration with Deep Mind
[Der+21].
There, a Graph Neural Network estimator able to perform complex spatiotemporal pre-
dictions is described. In order to give an ETA prediction, this powerful model takes features related
to the spatial information of the road network and anticipates events that may occur in the future
(temporal information). To perform the estimations, first it is necessary to define the problem at a
lower level. The entire route between the origin and destination points is modeled using supersegments,
representing adjacent road segments followed by typical traﬀic routes. Each supersegment is adapted
to a graph structure, in which roads are represented by nodes and edges indicate connections between
roads. Each supersegment learns from historic data the travel times for different fixed times. For each
supersegment a Graph Network (GN) block is defined with three functions (MultiLayer Perceptrons), in
charge of learning from the features at different levels: edge, nodes and global supersegment. The global
architecture consists in an encoder-processor-decoder process in which the encoder is applied to the raw
features of a supersegment (real-time travel times and historical data specific to a time of the day and
day of the week) producing a first representation of nodes and edges; later on, the processor updates
the representation with node and edge features (average real-time and historical segment travel speeds,
segment length and priority); and finally the decoder transforms the graph representation into a final
prediction. In this way, predictions for several time horizons in the same supersegment are obtained.
For the training of this network, the authors use a combination of various loss functions, obtaining a
strong performance of the network in several experiments with data across worldwide regions. Due to
the good results of the developed dynamic ETA predictor, it is implemented in Google Maps and used
by their customers in their everyday journeys.
In [Jin+21], a Dual Graph Neural Network for Travel Time Estimation is proposed.
Following
the same previous representations of the road, an improvement in the accuracy of the travel times
estimations can be done by establishing two different graphs with their corresponding neural network.
In that way, a node-wise graph represents road intersections and their respective links, while an edge-
wise graph models the edges between intersections.
Both graph representations include a weighted
adjacency matrix in which the relations between intersections and edges are represented. At a higher
level, the model architecture performs temporal convolutions making use of graph layers. This model
is trained using MAPE as loss function, with a parameter that avoids exploding values in situations in
which the denominator is too small. The Dual GNN was tested in several traﬀic datasets with samples
from China and Portugal, presenting results with an average error of 15.01 seconds and MAPE of 23%.
With A3-TGCN [Zhu+20], the researchers capture the dependencies among traﬀic flows present in
the spatial dimension and the relations between adjacent temporal points in the temporal dimension.
This is done by using an Attention Temporal Graph Convolutional Network. This model is composed
by a combination of a GCN model, responsible of capturing the spatial dependencies on the road, and
a GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit), in charge of the temporal dependencies present in the time-series of
the nodes, with an attention mechanism. During the training, the loss function is based on absolute
errors plus a normalization parameter to avoid overfitting. The evaluation of the model is performed
on two real-world datasets with speed information measured on fixed points of a road, bringing results
that improve previously tested models on the same datasets.
Another interesting research output with more relation with public transport is [Ma+22a]. There, a
similar representation to the previous one is used: the bus network is modeled as a graph, in which the
nodes represent travel segments between pairs of stops while the edges represent dependencies between
nodes. In addition, the edges are weighted according to different perspectives, e.g. spatial distances.
As a way of capturing the spatial correlations that change along the time, an attention module based
on a Multi-Attention Graph Neural Network is used, while the temporal dependencies are captured by
a temporal attention module, which includes a LSTM block.
Other relevant investigation outputs in relation to this Master Thesis are [TPR22] and [RBO21],
since their general goal is similar to the one described in this Master Thesis.

4.1.1
Quality of the data
Before starting to work with big quantities of data, and as part of the analysis of the dataset, it is
important to check the data quality.
It is not just about big quantities of data, but good quality
data which helps to build better performing ML models, capturing critical patterns in the data. Data
quality refers to the checking activities performed to ensure that a specific set of data fits the needs
that a solution to a certain problem requires. During this process some data quality issues can be
detected, e.g.
incompleteness, incorrectness, inconsistency...
There are several dimensions of data
quality: availability, usability, reliability, relevance, presentation quality; and at the same time, each
dimension can be divided into other elements associated with it [CZ15]. In terms of this project, since
the generation of the data and its storage is out of the scope of this project, the most important data
features to be considered are timeliness (as part of availability), accuracy, integrity and completeness
(reliability), fitness (relevance) and readability (presentation quality).
Timeliness refers to the time delay between data generation and its utilization and to the frequency
with which the data is updated.
In the particular case of this project, it is necessary to count on
regularly updated data. Samples from several consecutive days and weeks are needed to be able to
use them as input for the Machine Learning model. Like this, possible patterns can be more usual,
improving their detection and leading to an increase in the prediction’s accuracy. Data obtained from
==PAGE:29==

the SHOW project completely fulfill this requirement, since the mini-buses travel several times a day
and every day of the week capturing data with a high temporal frequency (on average, four samples
per second). In addition, three different vehicles are collecting data, so if there are any issues with any
of them, the others can continue with the data collection.
Accuracy denotes whether data values reflect the true state of the event that is described. Integrity
relates if the data format is clear and consistent. Accuracy together with integrity and completeness,
which explains the deficiency of some attributes from the dataset, are three extremely important data
features that decide if it is a good idea to rely on the data for a certain problem. In this particular case,
after analyzing the dataset, some issues have been detected:
• Since several vehicles operated by different systems are collecting information, the datasets ob-
tained from each vehicle are not identical. As can be seen in Table 4.1 and Table 4.2, corresponding
to vehicles type 1 and type 2, the attributes’ names are different despite representing the same
feature. This issue can be easily solved as one of the first steps of the data processing part. As a
way of solving the issue, all the attributes from the dataset of vehicle 1 will be renamed with the
same names as the attributes from the dataset of vehicle 2, obtaining in this way a standardized
name for all the common attributes.
• The quantity of information gathered by different vehicles is not the same: vehicle 1 collects
more information than vehicle 2. Paying attention to the differences between the datasets, it can
be noticed that the additional attributes are not critical for the goal of this project, since the
only essential attributes are the ones corresponding to the GPS coordinates and the record time.
Therefore, the extra attributes in each dataset can be overlooked and may be useful to be used
as part of the verification process of new generated attributes.
• Some of the attributes are not complete, since they have missing values.
Several incomplete
features, such as indoors_temp_value, outdoors_temp_value, battery_level and theta, among
others, are not important for the purpose of this project. But the missing values in the attributes
next_station and activity_next_stop can be considered a relevant issue, since this information
could be useful to define the segments between stops, as described in Section 4.2.2. Since there
is no possible way to fix these missing values, this information must be regenerated based on the
GPS position of the bus.
• Regarding attributes speed_value and speed, besides having some missing values corresponding to
when the bus is parked and that can be easily fixed by filling them with zeros, there are some
inconsistent values of the speed that are negative. Since speed is a scalar quantity and it does not
have a direction, it cannot be negative. After a brief analysis of the data, it is likely that negative
speeds correspond to moments in which the bus moves in reverse. Seeing that this only happens
in the parts where the bus is parking in the garage and it is not traveling anymore along the route,
this issue is not important for the analysis and also can be easily transformed to positive values
keeping the absolute value of the samples with negative speed. Moreover, speed values included
in attributes speed_value and speed do not seem to be very accurate, so new speed values will be
computed using GPS coordinates and timestamps.
• Taking a first look at the data, it is not easy to indicate if the values of the attributes geo_lat,
geo_lon, lat and lon are always correct. In consequence, it is essential to use a representation
method to visualize if the position data contained in each file is correct. As it will be described
with more detail in Section 4.2, some of the generated by the buses files have a few erroneous
coordinates points in which the GPS position quickly moves to an impossible location, as can be
seen in Figure 4.3. Due to this kind of problem, and given that the mentioned attributes related
with the coordinates are the most important attributes in the dataset, some data files must be
fixed and others must be completely rejected given the high amount of errors. These issues must
be taken into consideration and be evaluated individually, since some of them can severely affect
the quality of the data.

Route followed by the buses
Once the quality of the data is accepted, the next step is to get more information about how the route
of the buses looks like. This operation will allow to distinguish crucial points in the route where the
buses often stop, which can be identified as bus stops. During this process, it is essential to consider
that the buses can also stop because of other types of events, such as traﬀic lights, areas in which traﬀic
jams are common or parking spots in which the buses recharge their batteries.
To visualize the journey of the buses, the only indispensable attributes are the ones containing
the latitude and the longitude of the shuttle at every moment (lat, lon, last_seen), but these three
parameters are not enough to extract additional knowledge from the representation.
So, a fourth
attribute related to the speed must be used. As a way of increasing the accuracy of the vehicles’ speeds,
a new speed attribute is computed making use of the coordinates and the timestamp. To make so, just
a basic physics formula is necessary.
speed (m/s) = distance (m)
time (s)
(4.1)
It is important to mention that the distance can not be calculated as the direct difference between
two points because this could generate wrong results, but must be calculated taking into account the
ellipsoidal shape of the Earth. Thus, the model WGS-84 (World Geodetic System) of the Earth is
used as a reference coordinate system. This system is included and easily accessible using distance
functions from the GeoPy library [Geo]. Time can be computed immediately as the difference between
the timestamp in the previous point and the actual point.

4.2.1
Position of the bus stops
As the final goal of the procedure described in
this Chapter is to obtain the travel times from
one stop to another as well that the time spent at
the stops, an essential procedure is to define the
position of the bus stops. Knowing this, it will be feasible to define segments between two stops and
compute the time that it takes for the bus to travel among them.
A first approach to the position of stops can be that they are located in the places in which the
vehicles are not moving or moving slowly (see Figure 4.2). But that is not completely accurate, since
==PAGE:32==
4.2 Route followed by the buses
22
the buses stop as well in other places different than the stops, e.g.
traﬀic lights, obstacles in the
road...
So not only these points should be used, but this approach must be combined with some
additional information obtained from some external sources as verification of the stops positions. In
this case, it would be possible to use satellite pictures (e.g. Google Maps) or street maps of the area
(e.g. OpenStreetMap). The combination of the two approaches gives a first list of coordinates in which
the bus stops are located. Even so, this method is entirely manual and not as precise as needed, since
the location of the stops in the maps can have on average up to 6 meters of error [Hak10], provoking
that the bus is being detected at a stop when it is still driving in a segment, or the opposite. Also, this
process for the location of the stops can not be automated.
Since GPS latitude and longitude along the route are points in a coordinates system, a clustering
method may be applied for the purpose of getting the position of the stops, as it was done in some
projects from the literature [Ma+22b]. Clustering is a fundamental task in Data Mining and it consists
in grouping a set of data points such that similar points are grouped together, discovering like this new
sets of categories. Clustering is an unsupervised learning method, since the used data is not labeled
and the algorithm tries to recognize some hidden (not visible at first sight) information. In order to
determine if two points are part of the same cluster, they must meet some requirements in terms of the
similarity between them, so a similarity measure must be set up. A valid similarity measure should be
symmetric and obtain the minimum value zero in case of identical points [RM05]. In the present case,
it is trivial to determine that the similarity measure must be the distance between points represented
by the latitude and longitude, keeping in mind the shape of the Earth modelled with WGS-84 ellipsoid.
Before choosing the clustering algorithm the type of data that is going to be clustered must be
considered. Samples collected by the shuttles have a high frequency, since there are, on average, around
four samples per second. Due to this fact, the recorded routes are formed by a large number of coordinate
points. This, added to the fact that the speed of the bus is not high, causes that a lot of points are
really close to each other complicating the clustering task. The goal of the clustering will be to find
areas in the route in which the density of points is higher (i.e. places in which vehicles always stop),
ignoring the noisy clusters with lower density (i.e. stops due to unpredictable events). For this reason,
the chosen clustering algorithm must have a good performance in density based clustering. As suggested
in [Kri+11], DBSCAN (Density Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) is a good choice
for this task, since it is able to discover clusters with arbitrary shapes and densities and it is eﬀicient for
large amounts of data. Other benefits of DBSCAN are that it does not require specifying the number
of clusters beforehand, but the algorithm itself finds possible clusters.
DBSCAN works locating regions with a high density of points separated by regions of low density. In
order to determine the density of a region, two main parameters are necessary: eps, which indicates the
maximum distance between two points to consider them as neighbors; and minPts, minimum number of
points in a neighborhood to be considered as a cluster. Through these parameters, DBSCAN classifies
all the data points into three categories [Est+96]:
• Core point: point that has at least minPts points in its neighborhood defined with radius eps.
• Border point: point reachable from a core point and with less than minPts in its neighborhood.
• Outlier or noise: point not considered as core point and not reachable from any core point.

4.2.2
Definition of segments
As the final goal is to predict the Estimated Time of Arrival to any of the stops on the route, a required
step is to obtain an estimation of the travel time in all the sections of the route that the bus will travel
through for the specific temporal moment. To make accurate predictions, it is necessary to follow a
consistent process distinguishing when the bus is close nearby a stop or when it is traveling between
two stops. A clear definition of several segments in the course will be helpful.
Once the positions of the bus stops are clearly established, it is possible to continue with the
definition of segments. In this project, the concept of segment refers to the sections in which the route
can be split up in order to contrast the behavior of the bus/traﬀic in diverse locations along the route.
In this way, the previously described route can be modeled using two types of segments:
• Segments corresponding to a bus stop, identified with the id of the bus stop, as showed in Table
4.5 (e.g. s6). The bus will be considered to be at a stop when the distance between the location
of the vehicle and the location of the stop is 5 meters or smaller.
• Segments corresponding to a road between two stops, identified with the id of the two stops that
are connected through the segment (e.g. s3_s4 will identify the segment between the stops s3
and s4). When the vehicle is not close to a bus stop, it will be considered that it is in this type
of segments.
Defining a consistent method to subdivide the route on its segments makes possible to model every
journey of the bus as a set of connected segments that are consecutively visited. As was done in [PRP19]
in the literature, once a ML model will be defined, a logical method to compute the ETA will be to
accumulate the predictions for each of the segments that the bus needs to go across in order to reach
the destination stop, adding it to the actual time. Thus, in the example shown in Figure 4.6, the ETA
to s4 would be the travel time for the segments s2_s3, s3, s3_s4 added to the actual time. The time of
s4 should not be considered since it accounts for the time that the vehicle waits at the stop.
Figure 4.6: Diagram representation of a bus traveling through different segments on the route between
the stops s2 and s4. The ETA prediction can be performed by adding the estimated travel
time of each segment.
4.3
Creation of a new dataset with segments
In previous parts of this Chapter the original raw data and its attributes have been described. Despite
their consistent format after a few modifications, these files cannot be used as input for a ML model
since they do not contain any information about the segments and just GPS coordinates with other data.
Due to this, a procedure to process the data must be defined in order to continue with the problem
approach based on segments. This process consists on the next steps and they must be followed for
each file with raw data:
1. Import the file and adapt its useful attributes (i.e. timestamp of the sample, latitude and longi-
tude) to the format that will be used in the next steps.
==PAGE:36==
4.3 Creation of a new dataset with segments
26
2. Add information related to the bus stops. When the GPS position of the bus is close to the
stop location (i.e. the distance is 5 meters or less), it is considered that the shuttle is at the
corresponding stop. Additionally, the expected next stop in the route will be necessary.
3. Since the files contain multiple rounds around the route, to differentiate the segments from several
laps, it is fundamental to enumerate each of the laps. Thus, each time that the vehicle is close to
the initial stop in the route (stop s0), the laps counter is increased.
4. Next, the segments relative to each stop and between each pair of consecutive stops in every lap
around the route are obtained grouping the data by actual stop, next stop and number of round in
the route. In this step, the aggregation conducted during the ”group by” computes the difference
between the timestamp at the last point of the segment and the timestamp at the beginning of
the segment. In this way, the travel time in each of the segments is obtained.
5. Finally, extra attributes are computed, such as the time of the day and the day of the week in

Feature transformation
The obtained dataset can be already used as input for a ML model, but, since the models work only
with numerical values, the categorical attributes must be converted into numerical ones so the model
can learn from them. This process is called encoding of the categorical values and must be executed on
the attributes segment_id, dow, tod and condition.
segment_id is a categorical attribute that can take the values from all the segments that appear
on the dataset. For its encoding it is possible to use One Hot Encoding technique. For each sample,
it produces a vector with the same length as the number of categories [AI], such each element of the
vector representing one category. The encoding consists of assigning a value of 0 to all the elements of
the vector, except to the one corresponding to the actual category of the sample. Like this, this method
allows to keep track of the categories in a numerical way.

mathematical formulation computing sine and cosine of the feature that represents the day of the week

CHAPTER 5
From Classical Machine
Learning to Graph Neural
Networks
After completing the transformation of the data, the next step in the process is to fit the dataset
into different Machine Learning models to check how well they can generalize the data by detecting
the existing patterns and connections between different events. This Chapter describes the process
of implementation, adjustment and comparison of the different models when fed with the previously
created dataset, as well as the obtained results that will be analyzed in the final discussion.
5.1
Machine Learning problem definition
Once the dataset is ready and prepared to be inputted into the models it is possible to clearly define
the type of ML problem faced problem in this Master Thesis.
As has been stated previously, the
final goal is to give an estimation of the arrival time to a specific destination. For that purpose, it is
necessary to estimate the travel times along all the segments between the actual location of the bus and
its destination, to, later on, add them all to the actual time, obtaining in this way an estimation of the
time at which the bus will reach the destination. Thus, the faced problem is a regression problem in
which the target variable is the attribute travel_time.
5.2
Baseline models
During the Machine Learning process several models will be implemented and tried in order to check
their performance on the segments dataset. Some models are simpler, and others more complex, but
the fact that a model is more complex does not mean that its performance will be better. Due to that,
comparisons between models must be conducted. Comparing models’ errors makes easier the decision
about which model performs the best in a particular problem but, since each problem presents different
characteristics and distinct results are expected, it is not trivial to choose a model with which start the
model building process. Due to this, it is common to define baseline models to be used as a starting
reference with which a more advanced model can be compared.
A baseline model is a very simple model that can be easily set up and that makes naïve predictions
based on the training data, or even, on some occasions, random predictions. It can be considered a
preliminary model fitting that is used as a basic reference, working as a ”threshold” which allows to
decide if a model is valid. Baselines can be used to prove that a defined ML model is learning useful
aspects from the data and performs better than the naïve predictor. In addition, performance results
of baseline models give a first idea about the diﬀiculty of the problem that is handled. Therefore, poor
results in these models are not bad and it just indicates that the ML models have a lot of room for
improvement.
==PAGE:47==
5.3 Classical Machine Learning approaches
37
In this project, four different baseline models with different levels of simplicity have been imple-
mented:
• Baseline 1 (B1). A mean regressor that computes the mean of travel_time of the whole dataset
and, later, uses it as prediction for every segment (Zero Rule method). That is to say that this
model always will predict the same value for every segment, no matter which are the values of the
other attributes.
• Baseline 2 (B2). A mean regressor for each segment that computes the mean of travel_time on
each of the segments. In this case, the prediction for each segment will be different, but the model
will still ignore attributes other than the segment_id.
• Baseline 3 (B3).
A naïve model that uses as prediction the closest past sample in the same
segment, i.e. the prediction for time t in a segment will be the travel_time in that segment in
t −1, or the closest one in the past available in the training data.
• Baseline 4 (B4). A baseline that uses as prediction the average travel_time on each segment at
each time of the day (tod).
5.2.1
Evaluation of baselines
In order to obtain a first idea about the diﬀiculty of the faced problem, an evaluation of the baselines is
performed using the segments dataset. Each baseline takes its described input and makes predictions
based on it. Table 5.1 shows the baselines performance differentiating between the segments along the
followed route and using MSE and MAPE metrics.
Inspecting the table, it is possible to determine that Baselines 2 and 4 are performing the best,
obtaining general errors for all the segments of 265.31 and 270.16, respectively, which correspond to an
average error in seconds of 16.29 and 16.43. Looking into details, it is easy to notice that the predictions
on the segments between stops are more accurate than the ones corresponding to stop segments, since
the errors are lower in the first ones in most of the cases. This is mainly due to the variance of the data,
which is higher in the segments corresponding to stops since it is highly dependant of how many people
are traveling on the bus or waiting at the bus stops. Said already gives a first idea about one of the
biggest challenges covered in this project: the predictions for the travel_time in segments corresponding
to stops are going to be more diﬀicult to perform, because the bus can stop or not at a specific stop,
depending on if there are people waiting or if someone wants to get off. Since the mentioned baselines
already have an acceptable performance (MAPE below 20%) in the segments between stops, the goal
of the Machine Learning models will be to mainly improve the predictions in the stops.
5.3

Approaches that are computationally cheaper to perform and require less computational power are
considered Classical Machine Learning models. Their greatest advantage compared to Deep Learning
approaches is the ease with which their outcomes can be interpreted. Therefore, before starting to
train Deep Learning models, three Classical ML approaches are used to test their performances on the
segments dataset: Linear Regression, Random Forest and Gradient Boosting.
In addition, during the evaluation of these models, the influence of the attributes related to weather
is going to be examined. Since these weather-related features are not part of the original data, it is
necessary to verify that they have a positive impact on the learning process, improving the process of
finding patterns in the data.

Linear Regression (LR) is a supervised basic Machine Learning model based on the linear regression

variable. This is set up using a multidimensional linear expression. This model is one of the most basic
ML algorithms that can be used.

5.3.2
Random Forest
To understand the concept of Random Forest (RF), first the decision trees must be mentioned.
A
decision tree is a ML structure with a tree-like structure that tests the values of different attributes
and, based on that, a prediction is done. The trees are formed by nodes, branches and leaves, where
the nodes represent a test on an attribute, the branches symbolize the outcomes of the test and each
leaf node illustrates the final decision used as prediction. The learning process in which the structure
of the tree is formed is made by splitting the dataset into subsets based on the value of an attribute.
Random Forest is a ML technique that combines several decision trees and takes an average of all the
predictions to determine the final prediction.
The combination of multiple trees creates a forest. Whereas randomness is introduced in the building
process of the trees using bagging. This consists in that different trees (weak learners) are built based
on different subsets of the dataset, so none of the trees uses all training data. Like this, the used weak
learners are barely biased and with high variance, which is reduced with the aggregation of all decision
trees’ predictions. In that way, the combination of the results from various trees compensates for the
errors caused by single trees, obtaining a better generalization of the problem.
Random Forest is an ensemble method, since it combines multiple not powerful models in order to
improve the results. It provides a great accuracy, due to a good generalization of the faced problem, and
low risk to present overfitting as long as the best hyperparameters are chosen. The implementation of
Random Forest has been done using SciKit-Learn. There, it is necessary to define the hyperparameters
of the model. Below, the most significant ones due to their influence on the results are described [Coe20]:
• n_estimators controls the numbers the number of trees used in the RF. A higher number of trees
not always will give a better result, but it only will increase the complexity of the model, and
therefore, the execution time.
• max_depth monitors the number of decision nodes that each tree can contain. This is one of the
most important hyperparameters when increasing the accuracy of the model, since its extreme
increase could cause overfitting.
• max_features states the maximum number of features that the algorithm takes to build every
tree.
In order to find the best combination of hyperparameters a tuning process was performed making
use of a Bayesian Search. The search space was defined with the following intervals: for n_estimators
from 10 to 1000, and for max_depth from 10 to 10000. The value of max_features was set to auto,
which is the recommended option by SciKit-Learn and uses a value corresponding to the root square of
the total amount of attributes in the dataset.
After several iterations of the algorithm, the best combination of hyperparameters was the composed
of 2326 n_estimators and 500 as max_depth. These are used the evaluate the performance of this model
with the segments dataset. As previously done with the Linear Regression model, a comparison relative
to the usage of weather features was performed. The results are showed in Table 5.3.

5.3.3
Gradient Boosting
Next evaluated model, Gradient Boosting (GB), is also based on decision trees.
In this case, the
individual trees are trained one by one sequentially, such that every new trained tree tries to improve
the errors made by the previous tree, obtaining in this way a better result. The predictions performed
by the model are obtained by aggregating the results of all the trees that are part of the model.
Gradient Boosting, as well as a Random Forest, is an ensemble method. But GB makes use of
a different technique to get a balance between bias and variance, boosting. It lies in the sequential
training of several weak learners, in this case individual decision trees. The employed learners have low
variance but high bias, which is reduced with the sequential adjustment of the models.
SciKit-Learn also provides an implementation of GB that has been used with the segments dataset.
In order to make use of it, it is also necessary to provide the hyperparameters. In this case, there are
two important hyperparameters:
• n_estimators refers to the number of weak learners used while fitting the data. Boosting methods
can easily have overfitting if this value is to high, therefore a regularization term learning_rate is
used.
• learning_rate is the value that decides the impact that each decision tree has on the final result,
i.e. it controls the pace with which the ensemble model learns. There is an important trade-off
in the setting of this hyperparameter, since the smaller the learning_rate, the bigger the number
of decision trees needed to achieve good results, with the corresponding impact that this has on
the execution time of the algorithm.

A MultiLayer Perceptron is a specific case of FFNN
where all the layers are fully connected, i.e. every neuron
from a layer is connected to all the neurons in the next
layer, and the activation function used after each layer is
the same. As this is the first Deep Learning model applied
to the segments dataset, the reason behind using a MLP
is to analyze in more detail the structure of the problem
plus have an intermediate complexity model between the
Classical ML approaches and Graph Neural Networks.
Before training the model, it is essential to define its
architecture. The input layer must be formed by as many
neurons as attributes are in the dataset, in this case 63.
The output layer must be composed by only one neuron,
since the addressed question is a regression problem in
which the model is aiming to return just one value that cor-
responds to the prediction. MLPs are composed of multi-
ple hidden layers in order to be able to detect non-linear re-
lations between the attributes. There are no specific rules
to define the number of hidden layers and their hidden neu-
rons, but in general, for small datasets like the one used in
this project, one hidden layer is suﬀicient for most of the
problems and adding extra layers can slightly improve the
results or, even sometimes, lead to overfitting. Another
important aspect is the number of hidden neurons in each
hidden layer, since only a few of them can make that the
model will not be able to generalize enough to solve the
faced problem, while an excess can cause the appearance
of overfitting issues. Some guidelines can be used in this
process [Pan+11]: the number of hidden neurons should
be greater than 2/3 the size of the input layer plus the size
of the output layer and smaller than twice the size of the
input layer. Taking this into account and after some trials,
a neural network with two hidden layers with 128 and 64
neurons, respectively, is defined as a suitable architecture.
In addition, two dropout layers were added in between the
hidden layers to prevent the model from overfitting.
In practice, these neural networks are implemented us-
ing PyTorch.

5.4.2
Graph Neural Network
The last implemented models during this project, and as stated on the goals, are based on Graph
Neural Networks. In this case, it is necessary to follow a radically different strategy at the time of
feeding the neural network with the data, since GNNs expect to receive graphs as input. Thus, the
segments dataset must be modeled as a graph. Given the fact that a bus journey can be considered
a sequence of followed segments (categorized in segments between stops and stops), it is reasonable to
assign a graph node to each of the segments of the route. These nodes are connected to other nodes by
means of edges representing the connection between adjacent nodes. In this way, generalizing this idea
to the entire route followed by the buses from Ride the future project, it can be modeled as a graph.
The resulting graph is a directed cyclic graph, since all the edges have only one direction clearly defined
by the circulation direction of the buses and it contains a cycle, i.e. there is a path from a starting node
traversing all nodes ending up back at the starting node.
Modeling the route as a graph brings benefits at the moment of predicting new values of the target
variable travel_time, since one additional dimension is added to the model. This is the spatial dimen-
sion, which is represented by the graph’s topology, but also the temporal dimension is conserved and
represented by the nodes’ attributes. In such manner, it is possible to convert the segments dataset
into a spatio-temporal forecasting problem.
As can be seen in the literature review exposed in Chapter 3, there are several approaches that
can be followed in order to solve a problem with these characteristics. In the next points, each of the
possible approaches will be exposed, with its description, result and found diﬀiculties.
5.4.3
Approach 1 - One unique graph with stops and segments between stops
Following the previously described idea, the entire route can be represented as one unique graph which
includes both type of segments present in the route. In this way, the obtained graph is formed by 27
nodes, which is the sum of the number of stops (13) and segments between stops (14), considering the
segment s6_s10 which is not included in the main route but often done by the buses when they do not
complete an entire lap around the route.
It is feasible to use this graph to feed a GNN. In order to accomplish this, a few data transformations
must be performed with the objective of assigning to each node only samples corresponding to that
specific node. Since the number of samples available in the dataset per each segment is not the same
(see Table 4.6), it is needed to find a way to deal with this issue, due to the fact that each node of
the graph must have the same dimensions to allow the GNN work performing the necessary matrix
computations. A potential solution is to complete the nodes’ matrices with synthetic data (vectors with
zeros). To identify them it is necessary to add to all samples another attribute that indicates if the
sample is true or not [Roz]. In this way, the computations can be performed without errors and, thanks
to the binary indicators, it is possible to create a boolean mask that shows which samples and errors
performed on them during the training of the network must be ignored in the loss function and the
later backpropagation process.
This approach aims to concentrate all the data in one unique graph, so possible existent correlations
in the journeys could be detected. But also this approach has a big issue related to the data corre-
sponding to the different types of segments: the travel_time across a segment corresponding to a stop

Approach 2 - Two graphs: one representing segments between stops; and
another, stops

between the travel_time in segments between stops and the ones in stops, the previously created graph
can be split in two. In such way, one graph captures the information from the segments between stops,
while the other takes charge of the segments corresponding to stops. The obtained graphs have different
topologies: the one used for the segments between stops is formed by 14 nodes, while the other one by
13.
With this approach the correlations that may appear in the different types of segments, can be
captured without being interfered by the other type of segments. To illustrate this idea, an example
can be proposed to the reader in order to facilitate the understanding of possible correlations. If the
temperature is cold and there is snow on the streets, it is likely that the bus will go slower in the
segments as a countermeasure to the risk that the snow adds to the traﬀic. This event should not have
any impact on the travel_time along a stop segment, since the snow will not interfere on the action of
getting on and off the shuttle. To exemplify the opposite situation, it is enough to imagine a situation
in which a person on a wheelchair wants to make use of the bus. In this case, the bus must take more
time than normally at the stops at which the mentioned person wants to get on and off, but this event
should not have any correlation with the travel_time on the segments between stops.

5.4.5
Approach 3 - Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Networks
Among the models that can be found in the literature, it should be pointed out the usage of models
which employ spatio-temporal GNNs, e.g. [Zhu+20]. These continue using a graph as input to the
model, but another dimension is added: the attributes of the nodes are time series. In this way, on each
step of the learning and predicting process the model is able to visualize a certain number of previous
steps and make the prediction based on the evolution of the temporal series. In most of the cases this
approach is used in popular traﬀic forecasting datasets, like PeMS-BAY or METRLA.
The mentioned datasets have an important characteristic that makes possible to apply this type
of GNN: they are captured in a synchronous way, i.e. all the sensors set out in the streets captured
a sample at the same time instant. This aspect makes possible to build a graph for each temporal
step and give to the attributes a temporal series with the previously recorded values. In such way, the
spatio-temporal GNN is fed with the graphs corresponding to each temporal step and it is able to make
predictions for a certain number of time steps in advance.
Trying to apply this approach to the segments dataset used in this project, it is noticeable that the
samples here contained are not synchronous. This is mainly due to the fact that in this dataset the data
is captured by the elements in movement (the buses), while in the previously mentioned ones, the data
had been captured by fixed elements in the streets. Therefore, since the used data is asynchronous, it is
not possible to apply this approach for the segments dataset. Even if a process of ’data synchronization’
would be performed, i.e. define a temporal step and assign the samples to the corresponding time step

6.2
ETA estimator based on Machine Learning models
In order to fulfill the objectives established at the beginning of the project an ETA estimator is required.
It must make use of one or more ML models to be able to perform the ETA predictions. In this way,
once an ETA prediction is required, the estimator just has to compute the route between the actual
stop in which the bus is (origin stop) and the desired destination stop. Later on, a sample with the
corresponding time and weather attributes (properly encoded) can be created for each segment and
used as input in the model that will perform the prediction of the target variable travel_time. These
predictions can be cumulated obtaining in such a way an estimation of the total travel time (TTT) that
is required to complete the selected journey. Finally, adding the TTT to the initial starting hour in
which the bus was at the origin stops, the ETA is obtained.
In the process of building the estimator there is a crucial decision to take, since it will decide its
general performance: the ML model used to make the predictions of the segments. After analysis carried
out in the previous Section, there are clearly two models that complete the prediction tasks better than
the rest, Double GNN and Random Forest. Double GNN model brings the best results in the segments
between stops, while RF stands out in the stops. Since, it is not trivial to differentiate which model
from these outperforms the other one because each of them is better than the other one in a different
type of segments. For this reason, three ETA estimators are built and analyzed. This strategy aims to
find the best models combination, taking into account the contrary advantages and disadvantages that
the tried models have on the distinct types of segments. The mentioned estimators are:
• An estimator which only makes use of the Random Forest model in both types of segments.
• An estimator based on the Double Graph Neural Network in both types of segments.
• An estimator that uses a single Graph Neural Network for the predictions in segments between
stops and a Random Forest in the stops. So every model is used in the type of segments where it
operates the best.
6.2.1
ETA estimations
These estimators are evaluated on an equal basis: a test set with randomized journeys is defined by
taking data corresponding to the month of March 2022. Like that, the random journeys, with their
corresponding tod, dow and weather attributes, are passed to the estimators.
Each of them works
independently and provides an ETA prediction.
In order to evaluate the estimators’ accuracy, another method that finds the ground truth of the
arrival time is required.
At a first sight, its implementation can appear to be simple, because the
total travel time from one stop to another can be computed by subtracting the time from the sample
corresponding to the destination stop from the time of the sample in which the bus is in the origin
stop. But this is not so due to the fact that the dataset samples are captured by several shuttles at
the same time. So, when the total travel time of a bus journey must be computed, there is a chance
that another bus is in a further stop part of the journey and, if the total travel time is computed as the
difference between the two closest samples corresponding to the first and last stops of the journey, it
could bring wrong results. For instance, considering a journey from s2 to s6 made by bus 1, bus 2 can
be at s6 when bus 1 did not finish yet its journey. So computing the total travel time with the exposed
method would lead to an incorrect result, since the starting time of the desired journey would be the
corresponding to bus 1 and the finish time would correspond to bus 2. Therefore, the calculation of
the ETA ground truth must be performed in the same way as the predictions made by the estimators,
i.e. finding among the test data the samples corresponding to each segment at each specific temporal
moment and accumulating their travel_times. In this way, the TTT can be added to the starting time
obtaining the ground truth of the arrival time to the destination stop.
The estimators have been tested in a total of 156 test cases, which correspond to all possible route
journey combinations in the bus route without crossing two times the same stop. The starting time for

each of the journeys corresponds to a randomly chosen time from a test set sample in which the bus
was at the stop corresponding to the origin stop of the journey. In such way, the computation of the
ground truth is possible since can be directly obtained from the dataset.
Testing the estimators in a variety of journeys (shorts, longs, several moments of the day...) provides
a reliable overview of their performance, allowing to choose the best estimator based on these results.

7.1.2
MSE or MAPE as loss function
The loss function defines how good are the predictions performed by the neural network. Its choice
implies important changes in the training process, therefore it has a considerable impact on the final
model’s performance, since it is used as basis to adjust model’s parameters during the training process.
In a regression problem, like the one faced in this project, the most typical and simplest choice is to use
MSE as loss function. But this has some disadvantages like the magnification of the errors due to the
squaring term or the fact that it is not trivial to associate the output error with the measured variable,
since the units are different.
Considering this, some research projects from the literature decide to use MAPE as loss function,
due to its advantages compared to MSE. MAPE is more understandable by humans do the fact that it
is scale independent and expressed in percentages. Despite this, the usage of MAPE as part of the loss
function is not appropriate. At first sight, MAPE can be seemed like a suitable option for the segments
dataset problem, since the lengths of the segments are not the same, so a relative loss function would
fit better the problem than an absolute one. But, assuming a solution to the problem of samples with
value 0 on the target variable that, theoretically, would bring an infinite error, MAPE brings several
problems. The model training process relies on minimizing errors by making use of optimizers based
on first and second derivatives. This can be problematic since MAPE is not differentiable on the points
where the prediction is the same as the true value [Kol].
Another aspect is that MAPE is biased towards high values of the predictions, e.g. if the true
travel_time in a segment is 120 seconds and the prediction is 115 seconds, MAPE shows that this
prediction is worse (4.16%) than another in which 145 seconds are predicted in a segment with real
value of 150 seconds (3.3%), despite the absolute error is the same in both cases, 5 seconds.
Finally, predictions over the ground truth and under it are not treated equally. An under-prediction
will never have a higher contribution than 100% (e.g. true value of 10 seconds and prediction of 0
seconds), while there is no upper limit for over-predictions and they can even have values above 100%
(for instance, 30 seconds when the ground truth is 10 seconds, gives as result 200%) [Kol].
To compare the influence of the loss function choice on the final GNN model, an experiment was
performed. Despite using the same architecture with the corresponding hyperparameters that minimize
the error in each case, results in Table 7.1 show that a lower MAPE does not imply lower error on the
segments predictions.

7.1.3
Graph Neural Networks architectures
As way of summarizing, in this Master Thesis three possible GNN architectures had been proposed and
analyzed in order to be used to extract information from the segments dataset. Since the dataset is
structured by segments and two different types of segments are present in it, two separate graphs seemed
to be the most appropriate choice. Like that, the problem can be divided into two more defined tasks,
since there are not any dependencies between both types of segments. With this approach each type of
segment is isolated, allowing for implementing a different neural network that can be more specialized
on each specific type of segment. Also, when only one mixed graph is used, the neural network tends,
during the training process, to improve only the weights corresponding to the segments between stops,
since the error in this case is always bigger than in the stops because of the higher travel_times on this
type of segment.
7.2
Conclusion
This Master Thesis describes a complete Machine Learning process including all the steps until the
generation of a final model able to predict the Estimated Time of Arrival of the buses taking part in
Ride the future project. During the process several challenges were faced.
Firstly, the raw data obtained from the shuttles have been analyzed, understood and transformed,
ensuring the same format for data from different vehicles. Later on, after identifying the route followed
by the buses and the location of their stops, a systematic and consistent process has been defined as a
means of identification of the segments in which the route can be subdivided. This was an essential step
in the method used to define the segments dataset, and further feature extraction. the segments dataset
contains a collection of samples obtained from the buses’ GPS locations that describe the required times
to travel along every segment. After removing some inaccurate samples, additional features related to
the weather have been added due to the high association between the traﬀic flow and the weather
conditions. Finally, a feature encoding process has been applied on the dataset.
Secondly, several Machine Learning models have been fitted to the previously prepared data, with the
final goal of predicting the travel times on each segment of the route. Different models were trained and
evaluated, starting with simple baselines and ending with complex Deep Learning approaches, trying
also some Classical Machine Learning models. Among the best performing models, a Double Graph
Neural Network with a specialized part on each type of segment can be found. This model, after its
corresponding hyperparameter tuning, is able to predict the travel times on each type of segment with
an average error of 16.88 seconds on the segments between stops and 15.62 on the stops. The specified
Double GNN not only makes better predictions than the baselines but also overcomes Classical ML
approaches and Feed Forward Neural Networks.
Finally, as last result of the process, an ETA estimator has been defined, considering as base various
model combinations. Nevertheless, the best performing estimator is based on the Double GNN model,
which obtains a general relative error of 10.22% on a set of test journeys with various lengths.
7.3
Diﬀiculties, possible improvements and future work
Graph Neural Networks are widely used in similar problems to the one treated in this project, due to
their performance and ability to represent several dimensions of the same problem. Nonetheless, in most
cases related to traﬀic forecasting, the used datasets are composed of samples captured by fixed elements
on the roads every period of time, e.g. loop detectors, making the data synchronous. In the segments
dataset, the data is captured by the buses (moving elements in the system) and, later, the segments are
identified and a sample is extracted from each time a bus crosses a segment. These samples constitute
an asynchronous dataset, i.e. the spacing between samples is not the same and some temporal intervals
have much more samples than others. This fact causes some diﬀiculties at the moment of fitting the
==PAGE:70==
7.3 Diﬀiculties, possible improvements and future work
60
data to the required format in the GNNs, since for their appropriate operation the same amount of
samples on each segment would be hypothetically ideal. Furthermore, Deep Learning algorithms work
better on bigger amounts of data, so a larger dataset would make possible to obtain a better problem
generalization, as well as maybe make the dataset synchronous.
Another minor diﬀiculty was the identification of the correspondence between the segments dataset
samples and the corresponding bus that capture the sample at the moment of reconstructing the ground
truth of the travel time for a specific journey. This can be easily solved at the beginning of the pre-
processing task, assigning to each sample an identifier of the bus that captured it.
Also, one aspect that could be improved related to the predictions, is that in the developed version
the predictions are always performed between pairs of stops. So when a prediction is required when the
bus is in between two stops, an additional error is added.
As future work, the developed ETA estimator could be adapted to be used with a real-time API
provided by Ride the future project and already used on the oﬀicial website of the project [fut21] to
visualize the buses in their route in real time. This API would provide live data captured by the buses.
It could be processed following a similar procedure to the described in this project, to finally add new
samples to the dataset improving the training set for the models.

APPENDIX A
Impact of the project
This appendix contains the possible impacts and implications of the project considering different points
of view: social, environmental, economical and ethical.
A.1
Social impact
The usage of public transport was, and still is, highly encouraged by the authorities in recent years due
to the growth of population and the resulting increase in the number of vehicles in the streets. But
many people continue preferring to not to follow the mobility recommendations due to diverse reasons.
One of them is that travel times making use of public transports can be sometimes longer than using
another means of transport and irritating, because of unexpected events.
Intelligent Transportation Systems can be a feasible solution to this problematic, since they aim to
reduce congestion on the roads and improve traﬀic flow. Like that, this could have a positive impact on
public transport since the travel times would be more controllable due to the fact that fewer unexpected
events would happen.
The system designed during this project could bring some of these benefits, in combination with
other systems. If a bus user knows when the bus will arrive at the stop at which he is waiting and to the
destination, it is more likely that this person will take the bus instead of choosing another alternative
option. This, in a longer term, could cause that more people use public transports, which would lead
to the creation of new routes reaching more destinations extending the public transport network.
A.2
Environmental impact
Transportation systems have a high contribution to CO2 emissions. This, together with the increase of
emissions during traﬀic congestion [BB08], causes that the environmental impact is one of the points
in which Intelligent Transportation Systems have a lot of benefits to bring.
Improving traﬀic flows and vehicles’ general organization on the roads, can help to reduce polluting
emissions as well as decrease energy consumption. A higher usage of public transport, enhanced by
systems like the designed in this thesis, can have a positive impact on the environment, especially in
big urban areas, where traﬀic density is higher.
Buses that take part in Ride the future project, which are fully electrical, are a good example of a
project directed towards lower environmental impact. Public means of transport that adapt innovative
non combustion based engines are part of a potential solution to ecologically neutral transportation.
A.3
Economical impact
Time is a valuable asset with economical repercussions. To move from one point to another is always
time-consuming, and, considering the popular expression that says ”Time is money”, travel times can
be a crucial aspect whenever a person is deciding how to move. A system that estimates travel times
==PAGE:77==
A.4 Ethical implication
67
along several journeys can help to transport public users at the moment of taking the decision on how to
arrive to their desired destination, choosing the most appropriate one according to their expectations.
It is also possible to consider the economical impact from another point of view: the one of public
transport companies. Having travel time predictions at different moments could bring the possibility
of optimizing the number of buses circulating along each of the routes. In addition, a system like the
one designed in this thesis could have a positive impact on the usage of public transport, increasing the
demand, with its corresponding economical consequences for these companies.
A.4
Ethical implication
Just like providing ETA estimations could bring several benefits to both users and public transport
operators, it could also have some disadvantages if the predictions are not accomplished. Since some
users can base their choice on the provided estimation, it is necessary to make all the efforts to fulfill
the expectations.
