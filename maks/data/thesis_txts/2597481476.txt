Abstract
In recent years data-driven modelling of wastewater treatment plants has seen a rise in popularity within
academia yet there is a lack of guidelines for model development and the published literature seldomly
shares data or software implementations leading to replication issues. The goal of this thesis is to develop
guidelines for transparent model development to improve model quality and replicability while investigating
the impact of sensor location, non-stationary data and autocorrelative data on the ordinary least squares,
partial least squares, k-nearest-neighbours, random forest and vector autoregression methodologies.
An
extended benchmark simulation model is used to simulate two years of dynamic data from a municipal
wastewater treatment plant and generate data sets from diﬀerent plant locations. Regular and dynamic
versions of the aforementioned regression methods are used to predict biogas production and CH4 fraction
in the anaerobic digester, and TSS, NOx, NH4 and PO3−
4
in the wastewater treatment plant eﬄuent with
mean absolute prediction errors as low as 0.32%. A scaleable framework is developed that allows for swift
model development and comparison between regression methods. The dynamic models signiﬁcantly improve
the root-mean-square error on an independent test set compared to the regular models aﬃrming the need for
addressing non-stationarity and autocorrelation while further studies are required to investigate the impact
of sensor location on model development. A guideline is presented for model development and model sharing
that enables end-users to partake in data-driven modelling which has primarily been an academic exercise up
to this point. Two proposals are made to 1) extend the developed framework to enable eﬃcient soft-sensor
design and 2) extend the developed framework to enable it for optimisation and control purposes.

Introduction
In 2007, the development of sanitation was voted to be the greatest medical advance in the last 166 years in
a contest run by the British Medical Journal [1]. Sanitation is important because it lowers the transmission
of diseases such as diarrhoea, typhoid, cholera, polio and hepatitis. Successful wastewater treatment includes
the removal of pathogens which prevents their accumulation and further spreading of disease. A noteworthy
event is that of the 1854 cholera epidemic in London where the source of disease was a speciﬁc water pump
that had been contaminated with human faeces. This event led to the promotion of sewage disposal and
running water in domestic homes which marked the beginning of the so-called "Great Sanitary Awakening"
[2].
Apart from removing pathogens from wastewater there is also a need to remove nitrogen and phospho-
rus due to their fertilising eﬀect on plants which can lead to eutrophication. Eutrophication is the explosive
growth of algae and other water plants which can have two major consequences, forming algal blooms and
aquatic hypoxia. Harmful algal blooms can produce toxins capable of killing birds, mammals and ﬁsh [3].
Aquatic hypoxia is oxygen depletion of aquatic environments where the dissolved oxygen concentration dips
below 30% oxygen saturation whereas a "healthy" aquatic environment should not be below 80% oxygen
saturation. Most ﬁsh cannot live below 30% saturation and low dissolved oxygen concentrations can greatly
aﬀect their reproductive abilities [4].
If wastewater isn’t handled properly there is a risk of eutrophication as mentioned above, but the wastewater
also drains back into drinking water reservoirs eventually. The eﬀect of nitrogen and phosphorus in drinking
water is hard to trace since it takes a long time to reach the depths of the drinking water reservoirs [5]. A high
concentration of nitrate in drinking water is toxic to humans and animals, and leads to methemoglobinemia,
where the iron(II) in hemoglobin oxidizes disabling oxygen transfer in the body. The disorders main victims
are infants who have been fed formula with water with an excessive amount of nitrate leading to blue baby
syndrome [6].
Treating wastewater is a short term expense that serves as a long term investment in public sanitation and
the environment. Typically when we consider investments we would evaluate our decisions based on the
net present value of the investment - If we take the monetary gain for the course of the investment and
discontinue it then we would know the proﬁt of our investment. There is however a slight caveat in the case
of investing in sanitation and the environment - we have no reliable way to estimate the monetary value of
improving the environment or sanitation. If we could evaluate the investment in terms of monetary value,
then we could objectively justify if the investment would be worthwhile. This small caveat means that we are
typically left to compare the monetary cost of an investment with a non-monetary metric of environmental
health or sanitation - we are left comparing apples and oranges so to speak.
If we could compare the costs of operation with the monetary gain of treating wastewater we could simply
ask the question: At what point does it become economically infeasible to treat wastewater further? However
we are left with two separate questions: How much do we wish to treat our wastewater and how much are
we willing to pay for said treatment.
A commonly adopted approach for solving problems with an environmental-economical trade-oﬀis the so-
called BAT principle.
The best available technology (BAT) principle describes using the best available

from the wastewater treatment plants. In Denmark the plants are taxed based on the total nitrogen, total

The volumetric tax is based on the sophistication of the treatment system with more sophisticated treatment
being taxed less than simpler systems in order to encourage cleaner eﬄuents. A treatment plant with me-
chanical treatment, biological treatment, nitriﬁcation, denitriﬁcation, chemical precipitation and ﬁltration

In general wastewater taxes appear to have had a positive eﬀect on the discharge from municipal wastewater
treatment plants [8]. We could therefor create an overall cost function that included both operational costs
as well as wastewater tax rates in order to evaluate changes made to the plant. This cost function would
be subject to change for diﬀering wastewater tax rates, but it should provide an objective function that
accounts for the operational cost while encouraging a clean eﬄuent beyond what is mandatory.

In order to improve the operation of wastewater treatment plants with respect to the aforementioned qual-
ity parameters the wastewater treatment community have relied on process modelling to understand the
underlying processes. Wastewater treatment process understanding enables process optimisation which in
return improves the quality of the wastewater treatment plant operation and as a consequence thereof the
quality of life of both local communities and ecosystems. Process modelling can be divided into two cate-
gories which are mechanistic and black-box models. Mechanistic models, also called white box models, focus
on modelling the cause and eﬀect of changes applied to the systems in question. Black-box models on the
other hand, do not focus on modelling the cause and eﬀect of changes applied to the system, but rather
black-box models solely focus on the mathematical relationship between an input and output for a given
system. The mechanistic models are often labour-intensive to develop for the ﬁeld of wastewater treatment
systems since they often include parameters that have to be adjusted for diﬀerent scenarios due to changes
in the local conditions for each plant. The fundamental processes of wastewater treatment plants are carried
out by complex cultures of bacteria operating under diﬀerent conditions from plant to plant. Furthermore,
the processes at wastewater treatment plants are also subject to daily, weekly, monthly and seasonal changes
that aﬀect the bacterial cultures carrying out the processes, and the wastewater treatment plant is therefor
often characterised as inherently non-linear and non-stationary. Within recent years black-box models have
seen a rise in popularity within the ﬁeld of wastewater treatment as the popularity of data-driven analysis
techniques overall increased within the ﬁeld. Black-box models are less labour intensive than mechanistic
models, and can if applied correctly be used where process knowledge in lacking but where there is still
a need for process modelling in order to optimise plant performance. The most popular black-box model
within the wastewater treatment community over the past couple of years has been artiﬁcial neural networks
(ANN). When a model, such as an artiﬁcial neural network, has been established for a given plant, it can be
used to predict quality parameters or future plant performance which can then be used as a basis for making
decisions of whether or not to change the operational conditions in order to improve plant performance [10].
A predictive model can also be used to approximate the outcome of a given control action before applying it
to the wastewater treatment plant which can prevent sub-optimal and costly outcomes - a methodology also
referred to as a digital twin, where one or more models represent a digital version of the system in question.
The ﬁrst publications using artiﬁcial neural networks within the ﬁeld of wastewater treatment comes from
the early nineties and to this day the method is still the most popular data-driven topic within the ﬁeld
of wastewater treatment [11]. However despite its predictive power there is one fundamental property that
feed forward neural networks do not account for, which is time. Wastewater treatment data is notorious for
being non-linear and non-stationary and the concern of autocorrelation and changing statistical properties
as a function of time with-in the data are seldomly commented on within published articles or accounted for
in the modelling.
The principle of parsimony suggests that we should use the simplest solution that solves a given prob-
lem. The published articles within the wastewater treatment literature typically focus on one and sometimes
two methods for a given prediction purpose and do not include the results of simpler models such as linear re-
gression which can be used as a baseline to justify if the more sophisticated and computationally demanding
models are necessary for the given purpose. Simple models can perform satisfactorily and provide adequate
solutions and they are common knowledge for most engineers, scientists and practitioners unlike artiﬁcial
neural networks and other more sophisticated prediction methods that are prevalent within the wastewater
treatment literature.
3
==PAGE:19==
A fundamental tenet of scientiﬁc research is that experiments must be reproducible and in current years
there has been ongoing discussion about a replication crisis within academia across a wide range of academic
areas [12]. The majority of articles within the wastewater treatment community seldomly provide the data
or software implementation of their work. Reproducing the results of other researchers without the under-
lying data or software implementation makes it infeasible to validate the academic research and develop it
for use in practical applications. Published articles about data-driven modelling become anecdotal without
thorough descriptions of the workﬂow, or access to the underlying data and software implementation. If the
data used within the articles is conﬁdential a surrogate data-set can be provided. A surrogate data-set could
be data that has been standardised by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation of each
variable, which would produce a data-set that has a diﬀerent mean and variance from the original data-set,
but with the same shape and statistical distribution as the original data-set.
The data-driven tools created within wastewater treatment academia are often too complex for end-users
such as plant operators, and as a consequence the developed methods end up being an academic exercise
instead of a valuable asset for the end-user [11]. The replication crisis and the complexity of the methods
used within the wastewater treatment ﬁeld create a situation where both end-users and other academics are
unable to beneﬁt from the conducted research.
The goal of this thesis is to demonstrate the capabilities of diﬀerent predictive models within a wastew-
ater treatment framework and to create a transparent workﬂow that can serve as a guide for future projects
on how to handle data and develop models in a reproducible fashion that can be applied at scale. More
speciﬁcally the following ﬁve goals can be deﬁned:
• Compare diﬀerent data-driven prediction methodologies within a wastewater treatment framework
• Investigate the impact of sensor locations within a wastewater treatment plant for developing predictive
models
• Investigate the dynamics and time-trends of diﬀerent state-variables in wastewater treatment plants
• Compare diﬀerent dynamic data-driven prediction methodologies with their non-dynamic versions
within a wastewater treatment framework
• Develop a reproducible and scalable workﬂow that allows for the application and comparison of pre-
diction methodologies
The thesis starts with a description of the main biochemical processes that are used to treat wastewater at
a modern wastewater treatment plant. After the description of the underlying processes, a section follows
that describes the computational implementation of a wastewater treatment plant in MATLAB / Simulink
and how the data used for the predictive modelling is sampled.
After this the used methodologies and
concepts relating to the data-driven modelling are described. The result section is then provided which can
be divided into two parts, one section with models that do not account for temporal dependencies, and one
section that accounts for temporal dependencies. For each of these sections there are essentially two parts,
a part describing modelling related to the activated sludge process and a part describing modelling related
to the anaerobic digester. After the results section the key ﬁndings of the results, future perspectives and
the proposed workﬂow is discussed before a conclusion is given.

Literature Review
In table 3 and 4 an overview of some of the articles utilising predictive models within the ﬁeld of wastewater
treatment is given. The majority of articles on predictive modelling within the ﬁeld of wastewater treat-
ment use the models for two purposes, forecasting state-variables and optimisation of operational conditions.
Forecasting state-variables can be used to determine aeration requirements or quantify the addition of extra
chemicals to the wastewater treatment process. The predictive models can be used alongside optimisation
algorithms, such as particle swarm optimisation (PSO), to determine the optimal operational conditions
for a given process [13] [11]. Most of the articles utilise a multilayer perceptron artiﬁcial neural network
(MLP-ANN) model for predictive modelling which does not account for temporal dependencies, however
examples exist where the input data is altered to account for temporal dependencies [14] [15]. Wastewa-
ter treatment processes exhibit nonlinear, non-stationary, auto-correlated, cross-correlated behaviour and
are characterised by changes occurring at many diﬀerent time-scales and these properties make wastewater
treatment modelling diﬃcult [16].
Artiﬁcial neural networks and its many variations stem from the ﬁeld of computer engineering and are
not methods that are common for chemical engineers. There is a need for more in-depth interactions be-
tween disciplines when developing predictive models, and also a need for more in-depth interactions with
end-users if the predictive models are to be applied outside of academia. The majority of the papers focus
on one method, while those that consider more on average focus on two techniques. There does not exist a
transparent comparison of a large subset of methods that can provide guidance for practitioners within the
wastewater ﬁeld. There are few guidelines for how to develop models and which methods to apply which
makes it diﬃcult to select the best method for a given situation [11].

Biochemical Processes
3.1
Nitriﬁcation & Denitriﬁcation
Nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation occur inside the activated sludge process and the two processes are used to
reduce the nitrogen content of the wastewater and ensure that the nitrogen content is up to the described
eﬄuent standards.
Nitriﬁcation takes place in two sequential oxidation steps: (i) ammonia oxidizing organisms convert free
and saline ammonia to nitrite, and (ii) nitrite oxidizing organisms convert nitrite to nitrate. The organisms
are obligate aerobes meaning that they only grow in aerobic zones. [26]. The two stoichiometric redox
reactions in nitriﬁcation are:
NH+
4 (aq) + 3
2O2 (g) →NO−
2 (aq) + H2O (l) + 2H+ (aq)
(1)
NO2 (aq) + 1
2O2 (g) →NO−
3 (aq)
(2)
Nitriﬁcation is a prerequisite for denitriﬁcation - without it biological nitrogen removal is not possible. In
biological nitrogen removal systems the nitrogen is removed by transfer from the liquid phase to the solid
and gas phases. About 20 % of the inﬂuent nitrogen is incorporated in the sludge mass but the bulk of
the nitrogen is removed via the gas phase. In the nitriﬁcation step the nitrogen remains in the liquid phase
because it is transformed from one ion to another, but for denitriﬁcation the nitrate is converted from an
ion to atmospheric nitrogen, N2 by ordinary heterotrophic organisms [26].
3.2
Enhanced Biological Phosphorus Recovery (EBPR)
Enhanced biological phosphorus removal is the biological uptake and removal of excess phosphorus in the
activated sludge process.
The excess phosphorus accounts for the phosphorus that is not consumed by
other organisms such as those that carry out the nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation process.
Phosphorus
accumulating organisms (PAOs) can store large quantities of phosphorus internally in long chains called
polyphosphates. Under anaerobic conditions PAOs can digest volatile fatty acids (VFAs) and store them
as poly-β-hydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) and under aerobic conditions the PAOs utilize the stored PHAs as a
carbon and energy source for energy generation and growth of new cells. The ﬁrst two tanks in the activated
sludge system used in this thesis are anaerobic tanks designed for the PAOs to uptake VFAs, since under
anoxic conditions in the third and fourth tanks ordinary heterotrophic bacteria can outcompete the PAOs
for the uptake of VFAs, so the two ﬁrst tanks are used to give the PAOs access to VFAs [27].
3.3
Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion is a complex chemical and biochemical process which converts biodegradable organic
matter into a mixture of methane and carbon dioxide, commonly referred to as biogas. The conversion
of biodegradable organic matter by means of anaerobic digestion can help with reducing the overall excess
sludge production from a WWTP while producing biogas which can be burnt oﬀto produce energy limiting
the need for fossil fuels [28]. Furthermore, the sludge that is produced through anaerobic digestion can be
used as a valuable fertiliser due to an increase in nitrogen availability and removal of pathogens [29].
Anaerobic digestion is separated into four biochemical processes: hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis and
8
==PAGE:24==
methanogenesis. An overview of the biochemical processes is depicted in ﬁgure 1 which has been adapted
from Henze et al [30].
The ﬁrst process, hydrolysis, is the degradation of bio-polymers into their constituent monomers and
oligomers. The bio-polymers are divided into three groups carbohydrates, proteins and lipids which are
converted to sugars, amino acids and long chain fatty acids respectively.
The process occurs with the
excretion of enzymes from fermentative bacteria which convert the bio-polymers into their less complex con-
stituents.
The sugars, amino acids and long chain fatty acids obtained from hydrolysis can pass through the cell
walls and membranes of fermentative bacteria where they are converted into simpler compounds and then
excreted. This conversion is the second process, acidogenesis, which is the anaerobic oxidation of sugars,
amino acids and long chain fatty acids. Acidogenesis produces volatile fatty acids (VFA), alcohols, lactic
acid, CO2, H2, NH3 and H2S as well as new cell material.
Acidogenesis produces acetate and other volatile fatty acids such as propionate and butyrate. The third
process, acetogenesis, converts the volatile fatty acids other than acetate into acetate, hydrogen and carbon
dioxide.
The ﬁnal process in anaerobic digestion is methanogenesis.
Methanogenesis describes the formation of
methane by two types of bacteria, aceticlastic methanogens and hydrogenotrophic methanogens. Aceticlas-
tic bacteria carry out aceticlastic methanogenesis which converts acetate to methane and carbon dioxide,
and hydrogenotrophic methanogens convert hydrogen and carbon dioxide to methane and water.
The fermentative bacteria responsible for the hydrolysis and acidogenesis are a mixture of mostly strict
anaerobes and facultative anaerobes which diﬀers from the bacteria used for acetogenesis and methanogen-
esis which are only strict anaerobes.

Benchmark Simulation Model No. 2 (BSM2)
The following section begins with a description of the wastewater treatment plant studied in this thesis
and gives an overview of the software implementation of the wastewater treatment plant. The section also
describes the modelling experiments and how the data is generated from the extended benchmark simulation
model no. 2 with the addition of noise.
4.1
Plant Description
The treatment units at a wastewater treatment plant can be seen as independent units within an integrated
framework. The diﬀerent units are combined to create a functional treatment plant and the diﬀerent units
rely on the performance on each other. The benchmark simulation model framework used within the wastew-
ater treatment community is a model interface approach that integrates several existing and well-accepted
process models for diﬀerent process units by using appropriate conversions, since the diﬀerent independent
models do not necessarily have the same number of state variables. A key advantage of the plant-wide
modelling approach is that when the models are co-integrated the outcome of one alters that of the others
creating a realistic representation of a wastewater treatment plant. This allows the user to evaluate the
impact of control and operational strategies on both individual units, other units and the plant as a whole.
The extended benchmark simulation no. 2 (BSM2) is used in this thesis and this plant-wide model combines
state-of-the-art models for anaerobic digestion, the activated sludge process and enhanced biological phos-
phorus removal [31].
In ﬁgure 2 an overview of the BSM2 plant layout within Simulink is shown.
Figure 2: Overview of the BSM2 plant layout.
The ﬁrst unit in the BSM2 plant is a bypass which can be used to redirect water away from the primary
clariﬁer. In the primary clariﬁer, settler, thickener and dewatering units particulate matter settles to the
bottom of the clariﬁer before being scraped away - producing a concentrated sludge and a eﬄuent of water
which has less suspended solids. The eﬄuent of the primary clariﬁer is passed on to the activated sludge
11
==PAGE:27==
process after being combined with any bypassed water. Before the activated sludge there is another bypass
that can be used to redirect water in order to skip the activated sludge process. The bypasses are used to
avoid exceeding the maximum plant capacity during heavy rainfall. The activated sludge unit represents a
series of seven tanks designed for enhanced biological phosphorus removal, nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation.
In ﬁgure 4 an overview of the activated sludge sensors is given. The ﬁrst two tanks in the activated sludge
process are anaerobic tanks which are implemented for the production of volatile fatty acids (VFAs) and the
uptake of VFAs by phosphorus accumulating organisms. The two tanks after the two anaerobic tanks are
anoxic tanks where no oxygen is present and NOx is converted into atmospheric nitrogen through denitriﬁca-
tion. The ﬁnal three tanks are aerobic tanks where ammonia is converted to NOx through nitriﬁcation. The
activated sludge system has an internal recirculation so that the formed NOx in the last three aerobic tanks
is recycled back two the third and fourth reactor for denitriﬁcation in order to reduce the NOx content. This
method of internal recirculation is referred to as a pre-denitrifying system and has a higher eﬃciency than
a post-denitriﬁcation system where the aerobic tanks are placed before the anoxic tanks without internal
recirculation [32].. The bacteria cultures that grow in the activated sludge process accumulate as they grow
and eventually the sludge that builds up in the system requires disposal. The sludge exiting the activated
sludge process is referred to as waste activated sludge and is sent to the anaerobic digester. The anaerobic
digester feed consists of sludge from the primary clariﬁer and waste activated sludge from the activated
sludge process, and the anaerobic digester consumes the sludge from the primary clariﬁer and the activated
sludge process to produce biogas. This reduces the aoverall amount of sludge that has to be disposed oﬀand
the biogas that is produced can be burnt oﬀand used to generate electricity which in turn improves plant
economy [33].
To summarize the role of the two processes the activated sludge serves as a method to produce a clean
eﬄuent and the anaerobic digester reduces the waste produced by the system and improves the overall plant
economy.
4.2
Computational Implementation of the extended BSM2
The BSM2 implementation used in this thesis is based on a combination of MATLAB, C, and Simulink ﬁles.
MATLAB is a programming software commonly used by engineers and scientist to program mathematical
models and Simulink is an MATLAB integrated block diagram environment used for model-based design.
Simulink uses block diagrams to contain the underlying code of mathematical models. The blocks can then be
interconnected much like integrated process units in a plant connecting inputs and outputs of diﬀerent mod-
els to create the ﬁnal plant-wide model in an graphical environment instead of long sections of written code.
MATLAB is a relatively user-friendly programming language, but it is not a fast programming language with
respect to the speed at which it carries out calculations and therefor C is used for the underlying calculations.
The BSM2 framework can be run in both a steady-state and a dynamic fashion. The steady-state method
uses a constant inﬂuent for the course of the entirety of its simulation, whereas the dynamic method uses
a dynamic inﬂuent where a predeﬁned inﬂuent ﬁle is used. The inﬂuent ﬁles used in BSM2 describe the
inﬂuent stream with respect to ﬂowrate, temperature, pH and variables that describe the content of nitrogen,
phosphorus and carbon within the inﬂuent. The inﬂuent ﬁles can be generated with a phenomenological
model developed by Gernaey et al. (2011) [34][35]. The inﬂuent ﬁle used for this project was generated by
Dr. Flores-Alsina to which I owe my thanks. The inﬂuent ﬁle is generated to mimic the conditions seen
at municipal treatment plants and features daily variations, rain periods with increased ﬂow and seasonal
12
==PAGE:28==
changes relating to temperature and the chemical distribution of the inﬂuent.
Before running the dynamic simulation a steady-state simulation is run to ensure that the BSM2 imple-
mentation is functional. After a full steady-state simulation the dynamic simulation is carried out using the
generated inﬂuent ﬁle. The inﬂuent ﬁle has data corresponding to 973 days of operation. The ﬁrst 245 days
are used to reach steady-state and the following 728 days correspond to two years of operation, starting and
ending during the summer.
The simulation was run with the following settings in the extended BSM2 implementation.
• The decay of heterotrophs and autotrophs depend on the present electron acceptors
• The inﬂuent wastewater temperature is passed through the reactors
• All the biological phosphorus variables are kinetically described within the anaerobic digester model
• There is sulfate in the inﬂuent and S-related processes are activated
• Only hydrogenotrophic sulfate-reducing bacteria are responsible for sulfate reduction in the anaerobic
digester
• The default primary clariﬁer model is used
• Iron related processes are deactivated in the activated sludge process
For an indepth explanation model description and available parameters see Solon et al. (2017) [31].

4.6
Variable Descriptions
The following variable descriptions are short and superﬁcial descriptions of the variables and what informa-
tion they convey.
The ﬂow rate, Q (m3/day), describes the volumetric ﬂow in the system.
This variable contains infor-
mation on the quantity of wastewater that has to be processed and can in combination with concentration
measurements give an indication of how much nitrogen and phosphorus needs to be removed from the system.
The temperature, T (K), describes the temperature in the system. This variable relates to the speed of
chemical and biochemical reactions within the system and can be used to give an indication of how well the
diﬀerent units in the system are operating.
The total suspended solids, TSS (kg SS/day), describes the ﬂow of particulate matter in the system. This
16

4.7
Sensors
A key diﬀerence between the BSM2 model and a real WWTP is the use of sensors. Sensors interpret the
true state of a system and outputs a measurement signal, and there is typically a discrepancy between the
two values called measurement noise. The presence of measurement noise adds an element of uncertainty
that can make modelling of the system more challenging. The absence of measurement noise and realistic
sensors in BSM2 reduces the comparability between studies done based on simulations in BSM2 and a real
WWTP.
In 2003 Rieger et al. proposed a series of sensors for implementation in the wastewater treatment simulation
models. The suggested sensors were simpliﬁed for the ease of implementation and the suggested parameters
for a handful of diﬀerent sensors were provided. The suggested sensors transformed the signal through either
a ﬁrst or second order Laplace transfer function and then added normally distributed white noise with a
standard deviation of 2.5 % of the maximum of the measurement range.
The suggested sensor parameters by Rieger only covered certain measurement ranges which might diﬀer at
other locations in the plant. Adding 2.5% white noise of a measurement range that is unrealistic for a certain
location in the plant could result in measurements with a signal to noise ratio that is both too low or too
high. Developing realistic sensor models for implementation in BSM2 is out of the scope of this project, so in
order to add an element of measurement noise to all the measurements an even simpler approach was taken;
Add normally distributed white noise to each measurement signal as a percentage of the measurement signal.
Initially 2.5% was used, however for some signals the 2.5% would be larger than the variance of the signal
resulting in a signal where the underlying trend in the signal was unidentiﬁable. The noise was adjusted to
0.25% resulting in a signal with a noisy pattern where the underlying trend could be identiﬁed. For the pH
variable only 0.15 % normally distributed white noise is added to the measurement signal. For a pH of 7
the 0.15 % is approximately equivalent to a 2.5 % change in the concentration of H+ ions after the log10
transformation to pH.
The noise can be added both in Simulink and after the simulation is done by selecting the desired vari-
able and multiplying it with a normally distributed random signal with a mean of 1 and a variance of
18
==PAGE:34==
0.00252. It is important to make sure that the seed used to generate the random signal is diﬀerent for each
Simulink block or that the function used to generate the random signal does not reproduce the same noise
signal for diﬀerent variables. An overview of both a Simulink setup and a script that generates the before
mentioned variables with noise is given in appendix A3 and A4 respectively.
Another aspect of sensors are measurement errors which are not included due to the scope of the project.
WWTP are notorious for poor data quality and sensor reliability due to the harsh environment that the
sensors are placed in [38]. The goal of this project is to make predictions from pre-processed data where
data reconciliation has been carried out, and therefor the addition measurement errors has been omitted.

5.1
Model Selection & Assessment
When developing a predictive model there are two goals:
• Model selection - estimating the performance of diﬀerent models in order to choose the best one
• Model assessment - estimating the ﬁnal models prediction error on new data
Model selection relates to model hyper-parameters that need to be selected in order to develop the models.
The hyper-parameters for a given model changes the performance, and the hyper-parameters that give the
best ﬁnal model should therefor be selected. The best hyper-parameters are those that minimise the test
error which is the models future performance so to speak. Estimating the test error is not straight forward,
and using the training error is not a good estimate of the test error. In general, the more complex the
model is made via hyper-parameters the better it will adapt to the underlying structure of the training
data, eﬀectively decreasing the bias of the model, however the test data may not have the same underlying
structure as the training data, and therefor increasing the model complexity will increase the variance of
the model, a concept that is also referred to as over-ﬁtting. A simple model will have a higher bias but will
generalise better than a model that has been over-ﬁt. There is an intermediate level of model complexity
that gives the minimum expected test error, where the chosen hyper-parameters result in a model that is
neither over-ﬁtting nor under-ﬁtting the data [39].
Model assessment is done after a model has been selected with its respective hyper-parameters. Ideally,
the model assessment is done on data that hasn’t been used for training or hyper-parameter selection, since
using the same data results in underestimating the true test error. For data-rich situations using the same
data for model selection and assessment can be avoided by using a validation set. The validation set is used
for model selection and a test set is used for model assessment. The data is split into three sets: Train,
validation, and test set. The models are ﬁt with the training set, the hyper-parameter choices are selected
on the validation set, and the performance is assessed on the test set. The size of the training, validation and
test set can vary depending on the available data, but a typical split could be 50% training, 25% validation
and 25% test. An illustration is given in ﬁgure 7.
Figure 7: Illustration of using a train, validation and test set. The original data-set is split into three diﬀerent
sets that are used to train the model, select model hyper-parameters, and assess the model performance
An alternative method to having separate train, validation and test sets is to use cross-validation. Cross
validation or K-fold cross validation is a methodology where the data is split into K even folds and then
each fold is used as a train, validation and test set over diﬀerent iterations. Imagine splitting the data into

hyper-parameters that perform the best over the ﬁve folds of validation are then kept, and the test error

the training sample means that one must extrapolate from neighbours rather than interpolate between them
[43].

Principal Component Analysis
Principal component analysis is a method for reconstructing a data set from linear combinations of the
original features. These linear combinations correspond to eigenvectors of the co-variance matrix of the
original data set, and the eigenvalues of those eigenvectors represent how much variance of the data set is
explained by that eigenvector.
If we have a data matrix X of n × p size, where n is the number of observations and p is the number of

of the original data on the principal directions are called principal components, also referred to as PC scores;
these can be regarded as new latent variables. The j-th principal component is given by j-th column of XV.
The coordinates of the i-th data point in the new PC space are given by the i-th row of XV .

5.5
Partial Least Squares
Partial least squares is a widely used technique in chemometrics, especially in the case where the number of
variables is larger than the number of data points [44]. With a large number of variables some of them are
bound to be redundant, and for this we are interested in models that can distinguish between the importance
of diﬀerent variables.
In order to explain partial least squares we ﬁrst need to understand principal component regression. Prin-
cipal component regression is a combination of ordinary least squares, and principal component analysis.
Principal component analysis is a way of reducing the input data to a set of linear combinations wherein
information is kept - a way to reduce dimensionality. The updated data-space from principal component
analysis can then be used with the response variable, y, to construct a linear regression model resulting in
a principal component regression model. The diﬀerence between principal component regression and partial
least squares lies in the way the updated data-space is created. The partial least squares directions are based
on the singular value decomposition of XT Y , whereas the principal component regression was based on the
singular value decomposition of X. During principal component analysis we found linear combinations in X
that explained the most variance, however for partial least squares we search for linear combinations that
take into account correlation with the response variable, y. The ﬁrst linear combination in partial least
squares is the most important linear combination for predicting the response variable, y, and the last is
the least important. If all linear combinations are taken into account then partial least squares becomes
equivalent to ordinary least squares [46]. Selecting the number of linear combinations (components) to keep
can be done via cross-validation, to see which number of components gives the lowest prediction error. For
a thorough mathematical explanation see Ng (2013) [47].

In total the trees can split into as many sections as there are observations. This produces an over-ﬁt regression

Ensemble methods build a prediction model by combining the strengths of a collection of simpler base
models. An ensemble method is a "democratic" method, where a committee of simple models cast a vote for
the prediction of the ensemble method. Ensemble methods work well for low-bias and high-variance methods
such as trees and a committee of trees is called a forest. The trees cannot be grown on the same data, since
this would result in a committee of identical trees, since they would all split identically. In order to grow
diﬀerent trees the trees are grown via bootstrap aggregation also called bagging [50].
Bagging is a technique for reducing the variance of an estimated prediction function. The same regres-
sion tree is ﬁt many times to bootstrap samples of the training data, and the results are averaged. Bootstrap
samples are samples created by sampling the original data with replacement, an illustration is given in ﬁgure
13. The main idea behind bagging and averaging models ﬁt on a large number of bootstrap samples is to
average out many noisy, but approximately unbiased models reducing the overall variance and improving
the predictive power of the ensemble model [51].

For a large number of bootstrap samples the samples will be approximately identically distributed, and

of the bagged trees can.

bagged ensemble into random forest is to improve the variance reduction by reducing the correlation between
the trees. This is achieved by in the tree-growing process through random selection of the input variables.

The ordinary least squares regression can be written as:
Y = β0 + β1X1 + β2X2 + ... + βpXp + e
(30)
Where β0 is the intercept, β are the regression coeﬃcients, and e is the error. The autoregressive model is
a variation where instead of using the explanatory variables X for the regression, prior values of y are used
instead.
Yt = β0 + β1Yt−1 + β2Yt−2 + ... + βpYt−p + et
(31)

a vector autoregressive model (VAR). Suppose we have two response variables, Y1 and Y2, which we wish to

versa for Y2.

Before developing the predictive models the data is standardised to have a mean of zero and a variance
of one. This is done for each variable by subtracting the mean and dividing by the variance of the respective
variable. Standardisation puts the values on a common scale where it becomes easy to analyse if a obser-
vation is above or below average, and model constants that are not scale invariant such as linear regression
coeﬃcients become comparable. It is common procedure to standardise the regression variables, X, and not
the response variables, y, since standardising y changes the scale of all the model constants equally which is
unnecessary since it does not aid interpretation.
In order to automatise the process of developing models for each variable at the diﬀerent locations a se-
ries of nested programming objects were used. A programming object has a series of attributes that work
like a mathematical function. The developed object takes the data from a given location and uses it to pre-
dict the response variables with the diﬀerent regression methods. For instance the Activated Sludge model
object has an attribute corresponding to each of the four eﬄuent parameters that are being predicted. These
four attributes are in of themselves also objects that contain four attributes corresponding to each of the
four regression methods. The regression attributes are also objects that contain the respective model ﬁt to
the given data, predictions for the test-set based on the ﬁt model, residuals of those predictions and the

In ﬁgure 14 an illustration is given of the 3-fold cross-validation for selecting the hyper-parameters for
the models. The PLS, KNN and RF models have hyper-parameters that need to be determined. The hyper-
parameters are determined via 3-fold cross-validation of the training-data. For each data-set the ﬁrst 80%
of the 2 years of data are used as training data with the last 20% of the data being used as an independent
test-set. The training-data is then split into three equally sized parts that are used for 3-fold cross-validation
in order to determine the hyper-parameters. For each hyper-parameter combination the data is trained with
that hyper-parameter combination on two thirds of the training data and the last one third of the training
data is then used to calculate a test score. This test-score can be predeﬁned, but by default the sklearn
software implementation uses the scorer of the method, which varies from method to method but all of them
provide a measure of performance. This process is then repeated where the third of the data that was pre-
viously used to calculate the test-score is now used to train the data, and one of the two thirds of data that
was used to train the data is now used to calculate a new test-score. This process is repeated one last time,
so that each one third of the training-data is used twice for training a model with a given hyper-parameter
combination and once as a validation set to calculate a test-score. This procedure generates three test-scores
that are averaged into a mean-test score, and this process is carried out for each hyper-parameter combi-
nation. The hyper-parameter combination with the best test-score is then selected as the hyper-parameter
combination of choice, and the model is reﬁt on all of the training-data to produce one ﬁnal model. The
ﬁnal model is then used to predict the values of the test-set and calculate the root-mean-squared error on
the test-set.

Variable importance in random forest can be derived by looking at the improvement at each split for the
splitting variable. The more often a variable is used to split, and the greater the reduction it provides in the
mean square error for each node in a regression tree, the more important the variable is for the random forest
model. The variable importance in random forest gives an idea of which variables are useful predictors for
building a regression tree to make a given prediction. The variable importances are normalised, so that for
a given prediction model the variable importances sum to one. In table 7 and 8 the variable importance for
the RF models built on the anaerobic digester inﬂuent and eﬄuent are shown respectively. There is no clear
relationship between the regression coeﬃcients and the variable importance in random forest - for instance
the regression coeﬃcient for NH4, -6.673, for predicting biogas production from the anaerobic digester eﬄu-
ent is the ﬁfth largest numerically of the regression coeﬃcients in that OLS model, but for the corresponding
variable importance in random forest it is the one with the second largest variable importance, 0.219.

6.4.1
Cross-validation of Model Parameters for the Activated Sludge Models
For the PLS, KNN, and RF models one hyper-parameter was tuned for each model through 3-fold cross-
validation on the training set. For PLS the number of components to keep was tuned, selecting between one
and nine components. For KNN the number of neighbors, K, was tuned by selecting between every second
value between 2 and 200. For RF the number of max estimators was tuned, selecting between one and nine
max features. I did not tune the number of trees for random forest for the activated sludge process due
to computational limitations, and instead the number of trees was set to 100 which is the default value for
the software package, but also the point where the anaerobic digester models showed diminishing returns
from adding more trees. The cross-validation results for each hyper-parameter optimisation for the activated
sludge models can be found in appendix B3.

Time-Series Analysis
In the following section a description will be given as to how the data is handled with respect to making
the data non-stationary. The example given is for the activated sludge process data at location i, however
the same procedure is applied to all of the other data-sets for both the activated sludge and anaerobic digester.
In ﬁgure 48 the ﬁrst daily, weekly, monthly and yearly data from the eﬄuent of the last aerobic tank
in the activated sludge process are shown. The variables shown are used as exogenous/predictive variables
in the developed regression models. The weekly subplots show that Q, TSS, NOx, CH3COO−, kLa and pH
exhibit a daily pattern with two peaks. The ﬁrst of the two peaks most likely corresponds to the increased
consumption of water from households during the morning for preparing for a day of work, and the second
peak corresponds to preparing dinner and showering after a day of work. The weekly subplot for temper-
ature, T, exhibits a daily peak corresponding to the natural cycle of day and night. The weekly subplot
for phosphate, PO3−
4 , and ammonia, NH4, do not exhibit as distinct a daily pattern however a daily peak
is still noticeable. The yearly subplots for T, NOx, NH4, kLa and pH exhibit noticeable yearly patterns
corresponding to seasonal changes.
Stationary implies that the mean and variance do not change over time.
In ﬁgure 48 there were both
daily and seasonal patterns present in the data, however it is diﬃcult to infer the means and variances over
time just from looking at ﬁgure 48. In ﬁgure 49 three diﬀerent subplot types are shown: means, variances
and a histogram. The data is split into four consecutive pieces for which the means and variances are cal-
culated in order to evaluate if the means and variances change across these four sections of the data. The
data-set consists of two years of data, so comparing two sections of the means and variances corresponds to
comparing the mean and variance of the ﬁrst year with those of the second year, which are similar. Com-
paring the means and variances of four sections corresponds to comparing four halves of two years of data,
wherein the means and variances should reﬂect seasonal changes. The data is standardised by subtracting
the mean and dividing by the standard deviation for each variable, resulting in data with a zero mean and
a standard deviation of one. The means are plotted from (-1;1) with a horizontal red line at height zero.
The horizontal red line for the variances is the mean of the four variances, and the plot is then plotted
from (mean(variances)-1;mean(variances)+1). The better the four dots lie on the line the more stationary
the data is, since this would imply that the means and variances are not changing over time. A normally
distributed histogram can also indicate stationarity, since a non-stationary series will more likely have an
73
==PAGE:89==
asymmetric distribution, for instance the temperature histogram in ﬁgure 49 shows a bimodal distribution,
and from the plot of the means we can see that the temperature clearly alternates between a warmer and a
colder half year.

7
Discussion
In the following section a discussion of the results and the goals of the thesis are given, suggestions for future
work is given and ﬁnally a suggested guideline for developing and sharing predictive models is given.
In the initial modelling phase OLS, PLS, KNN and RF were used to predict variables relating to the
anaerobic digester and the activated sludge process. For the anaerobic digester the linear models, OLS and
PLS, performed better than the non-linear KNN and RF. The anaerobic digester is typically run with a low
organic loading rate as a safety precaution and under these conditions the system becomes linearisable [57].
When predicting an unseen value the OLS and PLS models can extrapolate from the training data, whereas
KNN and RF cannot extrapolate and use an average of values found within the training data to assign to
the unseen value that is to be predicted. If the system is linearisable then the OLS and PLS models can
successfully extrapolate from the training data to the test data, whereas the KNN and RF models are forced
to assign average values found within the training data to the values of the test set.
For predicting TSS, NOx, NH4 and PO3−
4
in the settler eﬄuent the RF model was the best for all sce-
narios. The linear models OLS and PLS predicted negative values for some of the points in the test-set as
seen in ﬁgure 38 and 43 unlike random forest and KNN which cannot predict negative values unless negative
values are present in the training-set. If the distribution of the test set is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from the
distribution of the training data then the linear models will have diﬃculty extrapolating to the test set and
as a result the predictions will suﬀer. The test set featured the last 20% of the two years of data correspond-
ing to summertime data, and if the intercept is ﬁt on training data that includes one summer period and
two winter periods, then the models will be biased towards predicting for winter periods, which can result
in a systematic prediction error as seen in ﬁgure 38. This is remedied for the dynamic models when the
seasonality is removed by diﬀerencing, and the training data and test set have similar distributions and values.
The sampled data that was used to develop the regression models was non-stationary as evident by the
seasonal and daily patterns. If two variables exhibit the same temporal pattern and a regression model is
built then a suitable regression model can be developed without the two variables being causally related,
such a regression is called a spurious regression [58]. If the developed predictive models are interpreted and
there are spurious correlations present then the spurious correlations can result in wrong conclusions about
the underlying system. In order to make the data stationary the data was diﬀerenced with respect to the
prior days diﬀerence which removed the daily patterns, and then diﬀerenced with respect to the prior value
which stabilised the variance. The performance of the OLS, PLS and RF models on the diﬀerenced data was
signiﬁcantly better than the OLS, PLS and RF models built on the data before applying the diﬀerencing.
In addition to diﬀerencing the data to make it stationary the introduction of lagged variables provides the
models with information of prior states of the underlying system. For predicting biogas production, CH4
fraction, NOx and PO3−
4
the addition of lagged variables did not result in signiﬁcant model improvement,
but for predicting the TSS and NH4 of the system there was a noticeable diﬀerence when compared with
the models built on the diﬀerenced data without lagged variables.
The number of lagged variables and the order of the developed VAR model was determined with a combi-
nation of the lag determination methods developed by Vanhatalo et al. and Rato & Reis which suggested a
common lag structure for both the regression, X, and response variables, y. The number of lagged variables
describes how long the "memory" of the process is and for each variable there can be diﬀerent eﬀects on
the system at diﬀerent time scales requiring unique lags for each variable. Rato & Reis suggested a method
105
==PAGE:121==
for determining a ﬁne lag structure where the number of lagged versions to add to the model is determined
for each variable separately, however this method was not investigated in this thesis but could be applied in
future work. The number of lagged variables determined also indicate the number of terms to include in an
autoregressive model such as VAR [55]. The VAR(2) model performed better than all of the OLS, PLS, KNN
and RF models for predicting the diﬀerent response variables of the anaerobic digester and the activated
sludge process. The VAR models use prior values of all of the variables to predict the response variables for
the anaerobic digester and activated sludge process, whereas the OLS, PLS, KNN and RF models only use
prior values of the input variables to predict the response variables of the anaerobic digester and activated
sludge process. Any regression method can be made autoregressive by adding prior values of the response
variables to the regression variables and this could present a simple strategy to improve the performance of
PLS, KNN and RF or other simple methods.
For the initial modelling there was a noticeable diﬀerence between model performance at the diﬀerent loca-
tions for predicting all variables except the CH4 fraction. After diﬀerencing the data and adding the lagged
variables there was an increase in performance for all locations and the diﬀerence in performance between
locations was less when compared with the diﬀerence during the initial modelling.
In the introduction the following ﬁve goals were deﬁned for the thesis:
• Compare diﬀerent data-driven prediction methodologies within a wastewater treatment framework
• Investigate the impact of sensor locations within a wastewater treatment plant for developing predictive
models
• Investigate the dynamics and time-trends of diﬀerent state-variables in wastewater treatment plants
• Compare diﬀerent dynamic data-driven prediction methodologies with their non-dynamic versions
within a wastewater treatment framework
• Develop a reproducible and scaleable workﬂow that allows for the application and comparison of
prediction methodologies
To summarise on the ﬁve goals based on the discussion above:
• When developing predictive models diﬀerent methods should be tested to determine which method has
the best performance as diﬀerent purposes require the application of diﬀerent methods. For linearisable
systems linear models such as OLS and PLS perform well, but for non-linear systems non-linear models
such as KNN and RF may perform better
• The sensor location played an important role in the initial modelling but after diﬀerencing the data
and adding lagged variables the sensor location appeared to have a smaller impact. This study was
conducted for simulated data without accounting for fouling of sensors or the cost of sensors which
should be taken into account alongside model performance in the development of predictive models and
further studies are required to estimate the importance of the sensor location for predictive modelling
• The measured variables displayed signs of non-stationary patterns. More speciﬁcally, seasonal and
daily patterns were identiﬁed. To make the data stationary the prior and prior days measurement were
subtracted for each value. A course lag structure of two lags was determined indicating that if the data
sets were analysed as a whole then the two prior measurements corresponding to 15 and 30 minutes
prior had an impact on the system.
106
==PAGE:122==
• The best predictors of the predicted variables appeared to be the variables themselves and a vector
auto-regressive model of order two outperformed the OLS, PLS and RF models.
• A scaleable framework has been produced and shared on Github. The framework displays how to load
data and create a nested model object that makes predicting several variables from diﬀerent data sets
with diﬀerent methods eﬀortless

Future Work
In the literature review many of the published articles featured artiﬁcial neural networks citing their predictive
capabilities and while artiﬁcial neural networks are know for their predictive capabilities other considerations
also have to be taken into account when developing a predictive model. The default artiﬁcial neural network,
the MLP-ANN, is poor when dealing with missing values, outliers in the data set, dealing with irrelevant
variables, interpretation of the models and they are hard to scale computationally for large data sets, however
the predictive power of the MLP-ANN is good if there are no missing values, outliers or irrelevant variables
and suﬃcient computational power to handle a large data set. Variations of ANN exist that tackle these
issues, however the majority of the published literature have utilised the MLP-ANN without addressing the
issues above. Regression trees which are the underlying models in RF are commonly regarded as the best
oﬀ-the-shelf procedure for developing data-driven predictive models and they are good at handling missing
values, outliers and large data sets [59].
Wastewater treatment plants are notorious for bad data quality [60], and for development of a suﬃcient
predictive model the data would either have to be cleaned and pre-processed, or input to a model that can
account for low data quality - such as the RF model. There is a general lack of knowledge among practition-
ers at wastewater treatment plants and this emphasises the need for use of robust models that can handle
poor data quality if the practitioners are inexperienced with data cleaning [11]. The simulated data used in
this thesis did not have to be cleaned due to the absence of outliers and a low level of noise being applied to
the measurements.
A similar predictive accuracy as that obtained in this thesis should not be expected from models built
on data from industrial and municipal wastewater treatment plants, however the methodology and workﬂow
is scaleable and transparent which allows for the reapplication to real world data in order to validate the
workﬂow and establish a set of guidelines for developing predictive models on real world data.
Once a predictive model has been developed it can be used for optimisation, control and forecasting pur-
poses. For optimisation the developed models can include manipulated variables representing operational
conditions, and an optimisation algorithm such as particle swarm optimisation can then be used to ﬁnd the
optimal operational conditions [10] [13]. For control and forecasting the predictive models can be used to
quantify the addition of extra chemicals needed in the future in order to optimise the eﬃciency of the process
[17]. In the methods section multi-step forecasting was brieﬂy discussed however it was out of the scope of
this project to implement in the workﬂow, but the implementation is straight forward and can contribute to
forecasting the demand of bulk chemicals that need to be added to the chemical processes several time-steps
ahead [24] [15].
Once a model is ﬁt to a set of training data then the expected performance on a test set will improve
107
==PAGE:123==
if the test set distribution resembles that of the training data. If the underlying system is non-stationary and
evolving over time then the test set distribution resembles the training data less as time goes on resulting in
gradually decreasing model performance. To avoid gradually decreasing model performance over time the
developed models should be reﬁt to new available data - a concept referred to as a moving window.
In the initial modelling section the regression coeﬃcients of OLS, the number of components of PLS and the
random forest feature importance was investigated for each of the models at each of the locations to deter-
mine if any of the variables were redundant. The values of the variables in these three metrics showed some
patterns and some of them changed values from location to location indicating diﬀerences in the underlying
model structure. Each of these three metrics are dependent on all of the variables, and removing a variable
will change the OLS regression coeﬃcient, performance across the number of PLS components and the ran-
dom forest feature importance of other variables. Developing simple predictive models require less sensors for
monitoring data in order to develop the data-driven models. Removing variables should not only be based on
the merits of the three metrics mentioned above, but on the change in model accuracy when removing a given
variable. Methods such as best-subset selection, and forward- and backward-stepwise selection are developed
for reducing the number of variables to a subset by evaluating the change in model accuracy [61]. These
methods can help guide the development of simple predictive models that require fewer sensors to predict a
given variable. Measured variables can be separated into two groups: continuous measurements and discrete
measurements. Continuous measurements are measurements that are monitored online with sensors, and
discrete measurements are measurements that cannot be monitored via sensor equipment, but have to be
analysed in a laboratory an example of a discrete measurements are COD and BOD. Predictive models can
be used to estimate the value of discrete measurements from continuous measurements - a concept referred
to as a soft-sensor [62]. The workﬂow described in this thesis can be used for the development of soft-sensors,
and addition of a subset selection method to the workﬂow would create a framework for creating soft-sensors
based on simple predictive models that require a minimal amount of sensors. A factor that inﬂuences the
data quality and indirectly the model development is the sensor location. Wastewater treatment plants are
notorious for harsh environments and sensor fouling, and the placement of the sensors has to be taken into
account when developing data-driven models, since the placement aﬀects both the data-quality and the cost
of sensor maintenance [60].
To summarise the main takeaways of the above with respect to future work:
• New regression methods can be tested to compare their performance with the methods tested here
• Application to real world data to validate the proposed workﬂow and guidelines
• Development of a data cleaning workﬂow for handling missing values and outliers
• Application to soft-sensoring where discrete measurements are estimated with the use of online mea-
surements
• Development of a workﬂow for testing ﬁne lag structures instead of having a common lag structure for
all variables
• Development of a workﬂow for testing moving-window size in order to determine how often a model
should be reﬁt and how much data should be used to reﬁt the model
• Development of a workﬂow for testing auto-regressive versions of diﬀerent methodologies
• Development of a workﬂow for implementing subset selection methods to simplify predictive models
108
==PAGE:124==
• Development of a workﬂow that integrates multistep forecasting from predictive models with optimi-
sation algorithms for optimisation and control purposes
The modelling framework presented here is developed to help practitioners improve the operation of wastew-
ater treatment plants. Developing soft-sensors and integrating the workﬂow for optimisation and control
purposes extends the framework and allows the end-user to go from data to actionable knowledge that can
help improve plant operation. The addition of a ﬁne lag structure, more methodologies, auto-regressive
versions, moving-windows and data cleaning helps with developing more sophisticated prediction models,
but does not extend the developed framework to improve plant operation directly.
Combining subset selection with soft-sensor development could extend the framework to enable the end-
user to create simple models for predicting hard to measure variables such as COD or BOD with fewer
sensors. This increases the available knowledge and improves the basis of decision for the end-user.
Integrating the framework for optimisation and control purposes allows end-users to determine optimal
operational conditions and control the underlying processes improving plant operation. Two main projects
can therefor be deﬁned for future work:
• Integration of the developed framework with subset selection methods for developing soft-sensors
• Integration of optimisation algorithms and multistep forecasting within the developed framework for
optimisation and control purposes
8.1
Proposed Guidelines for Developing and Sharing Predictive Models
For developing predictive models the following guidelines should be consulted:
• Ensure that the data has been cleaned by handling missing values and outliers if the intended predictive
methods cannot process missing values and outliers
• Investigate both the regression variables and predicted variables for non-stationarity and auto-correlation.
– For investigating non-stationarity the data can be split into a number of sections to investigate
the means and variances of the diﬀerent sections. Alternatively the time-series literature can be
consulted for parametric tests or other procedures. Subsequently, the data can be made stationary
through diﬀerencing
– For investigating auto-correlation the methods used for lag determination in DPCA provide an
easy method for analysing both the regression and predictive variables simultaneously
• If parametric methods are used the hyper-parameters should be determined with the use of an inde-
pendent validation set or through cross-validation
• Train the model with the best hyper-parameter combination
• Estimate the model performance on a test set
For sharing predictive models the following guidelines should be consulted:
• When publishing articles ensure that the data and software implementation is available upon request
or accessible in a public repository such as Github or via other cloud storage services. If the data is
conﬁdential, provide a standardised version of the data that conceals the original values of the data
and allows for replication of the published results
109
==PAGE:125==
• When publishing code used to develop models the code should satisfy the following criteria:
– The code should be readable. Long lines of code should be separated into several lines to improve
readability
– Use meaningful names for variables, functions, methods and objects

9
Conclusion
In the introduction the following ﬁve goals were deﬁned:
1. Compare diﬀerent data-driven prediction methodologies within a wastewater treatment framework
2. Investigate the impact of sensor locations within a wastewater treatment plant for developing predictive
models
3. Investigate the dynamics and time-trends of diﬀerent state-variables in wastewater treatment plants
4. Compare diﬀerent dynamic data-driven prediction methodologies with their non-dynamic versions
within a wastewater treatment framework
5. Develop a reproducible and scaleable workﬂow that allows for the application and comparison of
prediction methodologies
1) For predicting a variety of quality parameters related to the anaerobic digester and the activated sludge
process several OLS, PLS, KNN and RF models were trained on diﬀerent input data from diﬀerent plant
locations. For predicting the biogas production and CH4 fraction of the anaerobic digester OLS outper-
formed the other models, and for predicting TSS, NOx, NH4 and PO3−
4
in the settler eﬄuent the random
forest model outperformed the other models. To conclude no model was superior and developing data-driven
prediction methodologies requires comparison of diﬀerent regression methods in order to determine which is
best suited for the speciﬁc case.
2) The predictive models developed based on diﬀerent plant locations showed noticeable changes in pre-
diction accuracy. In order to validate the true impact of sensor location on the development of predictive
models the environment the sensors are placed in should be taken into consideration. Sensors in wastewater
treatment plants are subject to harsh conditions that can aﬀect data-quality. This study did not consider the
diﬀerence in data-quality for diﬀerent sensor locations and further studies are required to properly determine
the impact of sensor location on developing predictive models.
3) The data used in this study was investigated for non-stationarity through a visual inspection method
based on evaluating the mean, variances and histograms for four consecutive sections of the data. The visual
inspection method revealed daily and seasonal patterns. Diﬀerencing was used to make the data stationary
by subtracting the prior days measurement and the prior measurement for each observation. The data was
investigated for auto-correlation with a combination of two methods for lag determination developed within
the ﬁeld of DPCA and a common lag structure with two lags was used for all of the state-variables to ac-
count for auto-correlation. A coarse lag structure was used to simplify the workﬂow for the data-sets, and for
further studies ﬁne lag structures with speciﬁc lags for each state-variable should be investigated. The diﬀer-
encing procedure and the two lags were used to develop dynamic predictive models that showed signiﬁcant
improvement compared to before applying the diﬀerencing procedure and adding the two lags illustrating the
importance of analysing wastewater treatment plant data for non-stationarity and auto-correlation before
developing predictive models.
4) For predicting a variety of quality parameters related to the anaerobic digester and the activated sludge
process several OLS, PLS, RF and VAR(2) models were trained on diﬀerent input data from diﬀerent plant
locations. For OLS, PLS and RF models were developed on data after applying the diﬀerencing procedure
111
==PAGE:127==
and with and without including lagged variables. The dynamic versions of OLS, PLS and RF models resulted
in 6.25%-89.36% reductions in model RMSE compared to the non-dynamic versions. The VAR(2) model
was built on the data after applying the diﬀerencing procedure and the VAR(2) model outperformed OLS,
PLS and RF. The MAPE of the VAR(2) model for the diﬀerent variables was between 0.32%-1.16%.
5) A scaleable framework has been provided that can train, test and compare several predictive methods
simultaneously enabling practitioners to copy the framework and apply it to their own user-cases. Guide-
lines have been provided for developing and sharing predictive models to increase transparency within the
published wastewater literature going forward.
Several options have been discussed for future work but two projects have been identiﬁed that can help
practitioners improve wastewater treatment plant operation in the near future.
• Integration of the developed framework with subset selection methods for developing soft-sensors
• Integration of optimisation algorithms and multistep forecasting within the developed framework for
optimisation and control purposes
