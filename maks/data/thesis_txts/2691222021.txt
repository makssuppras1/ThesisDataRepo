Abstract 
A global goal on adaptation was established in the Paris agreement to enhance adaptive 
capacity, strengthen resilience, reduce vulnerability of countries to climate change, 
requiring the Parties to submit periodically an adaptation communication. This thesis 
focuses on how and what are countries reporting about their adaptation actions to climate 
change under the UNFCCC through their National Communications (NC), and to assess 
how prepared they are to meet the requirements from the A-BTR guidelines, which will 
come into force 2024. The data was systematically obtained by a qualitative coding exercise 
of 33 countries’ NC reports, which was used to analyze trends and to create a 
Comprehensiveness Index. A total of 4.991 actions were identified. Annex I parties reported 
on average 147 initiatives per report compared to 52 of non-Annex I countries. Groundwork 
actions represented 56% of the total codable actions, the rest being tangible actions, being 
relatively balanced, and 12% of the adaptation measures were found to be mainstreamed 
into existing programmes. Some sectors were more frequently reported, such as emergency 
management and water. Regarding the Comprehensiveness Index, it was found that only 
10 countries scored as Moderate comprehensive reporting, while all the others ranked as 
Poorly comprehensive. There is no significant correlation between how extensive countries 
report and countries’ capacities, such as GDP and Government Effectiveness. From this 
analysis it was found that countries don’t currently report what is required for the A-BTR 
and that further analysis should be made to assess if countries are or not prepared.

1. Introduction 
In recent decades, the world has been affected by an increase in the average global temperature. The 
year 2020 has been one of the warmest years on record (UNEP, 2021), countries have been facing 
more frequent extreme weather events, such as droughts, storms, floods, wildfires (Thomas & Lopez, 
2018). These are, at the same time, having an impact on human communities and natural ecosystems. 
Several assessments have shown impacts that include alteration of food production, human 
displacement, availability and quality of water resources; freshwater, marine and terrestrial species 
migration patterns and abundance (IPCC, 2014). These impacts are expected to intensify; hence 
countries need to promote and implement adaptation solutions, among other measures, as fast as 
possible to reduce impacts and costs. However, these vulnerabilities and impacts are different for 
every nation, being affected by different factors such as each countries’ climate, resources, capacities, 
political awareness, among others (Massey, et al., 2014), making it difficult to come with a common 
solution. 
In December 2015, the Paris Agreement was established in the 21st session of the Conference of the 
Parties (COP21) to the United Nations Framework of Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), 
in which more than 190 countries committed to a global goal to combat climate change, by holding 
the global average temperature below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit 
the temperature increase no more than 1.5 °C (UNFCCC, 2015). To achieve this goal, actions are 
being done towards mitigation and adaptation. However, historically mitigation has received more 
attention than adaptation planning (IPCC, 2014) and even when there are synergies between these 
both, not all dangerous climate impacts can be avoided by mitigation (ecbi, 2019). The Paris 
agreement recognizes that need and takes a step forward by establishing a global goal on adaptation 
(Art.7) to enhance the adaptive capacity, strengthen resilience, reduce vulnerability of countries to 
climate change, recognizing it is a global challenge. 
In order to provide information to the UNFCCC, a “loose system” with different instruments are 
submitted regularly to facilitate inter-country communication (Dale & Christiansen, 2020). The 
National Communication (NC), a reporting-oriented instrument, was established to provide an 
overview of the actions being carried out by each country, including adaptation. Specifically, the 
objective of including information related to adaptation in the NC, for both developed (Annex I 
Parties) and developing countries (non-Annex I Parties), is to encourage transparency, consistency, 
and comparability of information. However, a need for a clearer understanding of the adaptation 
efforts, good practices, priorities, needs and gaps was identified. For this reason, a new instrument 
regarding reporting to climate change under the Enhanced Transparency Framework (ETF) in 
Article 13 of the Paris Agreement will come to force in 2024 called the Biennial Transparency Report 
(BTR) for Annex I and non-Annex I Parties and will become the main reporting instrument. The 
BTR will enable countries to report on progress towards all substantive aspects of the Paris 
Agreement, including adaptation – A-BTR (UNFCCC, 2019a; Dale & Christiansen, 2020). 
The A-BTR is expected to facilitate the collective understanding of adaptation in a national level, 
by including adaptation priorities, status of previously published actions, planned and implemented 
adaptation actions (referred also as initiatives, measures, among others). However, it is yet unknown 
if countries, mainly developing countries, will be able to fully address the proposed guidelines, as a 
struggle to fulfill the pre-Paris Agreement transparency requirements has been previously observed 
(Dagnet, et al., 2019).

Therefore, the aim of this work is to learn how countries are presently reporting on adaptation 
through their NC reports to assess how prepared they are to meet the required information for the 
A-BTR document. Moreover, this work also aims to generate a baseline of knowledge regarding the 
extensiveness of current adaptation reporting and to assess if there are any trends between core 
aspects of reporting and different national characteristics (e.g. income bracket, number of NC). This 
thesis project is done in collaboration with the UNEP-DTU partnership. 
Furthermore, through the identification of information requested by the guidance’s sections of the 
A-BTR within the latest NC reports of a sample of 33 countries, an assessment on how prepared 
countries are to meet the A-BTR guidelines will be performed. As well, to know the 
comprehensiveness of the initiatives being reported, a statistically significant relationship between a 
Comprehensiveness Index (CI) and countries’ capacities indicators such as GDP and Government 
Effectiveness Index will be measured.  
The central research questions in this context are: 
• 
How and what are countries reporting on their national communications about their 
adaptation actions to climate change under the UNFCCC? 
• 
How prepared are countries to meet the requirements from the A-BTR guidelines? 
To address the second question the following hypothesis of expected outcomes will be assessed 
• 
Countries with greater capacities are more prepared to report climate adaptation 
actions under the A-BTR framework 
The thesis comprises the following chapters: in chapter 2, the basic principles and historical 
background is outlined with the main concepts of interest related to adaptation to climate change. 
The next chapter explains the methodological procedures applied, including the data source, 
collection, and analysis, and the explanation of the CI. Followed in chapter 4 by the literature review, 
providing an overview of previous studies done in the matter. Then, the general results are presented 
in chapter 5, including also the trends observed by the different categories such as convention group, 
income bracket, region, and NC, the CI and the correlation between the comprehensiveness of 
reporting and different indicators in regards of countries’ capacities (GDP and Government 
effectiveness). Chapter 6 contains the discussion of the observed trends within the coded results, the 
CI and the preparedness to report under the A-BTR, as well as the main limitations found in the 
study and further work. Finally, Chapter 7 includes the overall conclusions.

2. Background 
The following section will define, explain and/or contextualize different concepts that are of interest 
for this research such as the UNFCCC, climate change adaptation, NCs and the A-BTR framework. 
2.1. 
UN FCCC  
The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) was adopted and opened 
to signature at the Rio Earth Summit on 1992, entering into force in 1994 with 196 countries, also 
known as Parties. The ultimate objective of the UNFCCC is to “achieve the stabilization of 
greenhouse gas concentration in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous 
anthropogenic interference with the climate system” (United Nations, 1992). Every year the Parties 
meet at a Conference of the Parties (COP) were they review the implementation of the Convention 
and negotiate multilateral responses to climate change (UNFCCC, 2017). 
In order to establish quantitative parameters on the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction, on 
December 1997, the Kyoto Protocol was adopted. However, it entered into force in 2005. A 5% GHG 
reduction below 1990 levels was established during the first commitment period of 2008-2012 for 
developed countries (UNFCCC, 1997), known as “Annex I Parties”.  
In 2009, the Copenhagen Climate Change Conference resulted in the Copenhagen Accord, which 
started with the long term goal of limiting the global average temperature  increase below 2 C, where 
also developed countries provided US $30 billion to mobilize long term finance for the period 2010-
2012 (UNFCCC, 2009). This accord foreshadowed a movement towards a voluntary global climate 
governance (Held & Roger, 2018). On December 2010, at COP16, the Cancun Agreement was created 
including a comprehensive package to assist developing countries regarding finance, technology, and 
capacity building support. Additionally, during this COP, it was also recognized that “adaptation 
must be addressed with the same priority as mitigation, requiring institutional arrangements to 
enhance adaptation action and support” (UNFCCC, 2010). Subsequently, for the second commitment 
period (2013-2020) of the Kyoto Protocol, developed nations set a goal of 20% reduction of emissions 
compared to 1990 under the Doha Amendment. However, these Parties represented only 14% of the 
total global emissions (De Gregorio, 2016).  
In COP 21, 195 nations signed the Paris Agreement in order to combat climate change, recognizing 
that an effort from all Parties is needed to meet the common objectives, which is “holding the rise 
in global average temperature bellow 2 C above pre-industrial levels, and pursuing efforts to limit the 
increase to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels”, as well as to increase the ability to adapt to the 
adverse impacts of climate change and low greenhouse gas emissions (UNFCCC, 2015). Each party 
can establish their voluntary action plan and their Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC), 
which are the GHG emissions each party commits to reduce, ensuring all nations contribute somehow 
(UNFCCC, 2015). The Paris Agreement addresses the urgency of reducing vulnerability by 
establishing an explicit adaptation goal in Article 7. What adaptation to climate change means and 
to what Parties committed to, will be explained in more detail in the following sections. 
2.2. 
Adaptation to climate change 
Adaptation to climate change is defined by the IPCC (2014) as “the process of adjustment to actual 
or expected climate change and its effects”, therefore, adaptation aims to reduce the vulnerability of 
ecological, social and/or economic systems to climate change; and to reduce the damage (or to

maximize the benefits) related to climate change and its effects. Thus, vulnerability to climate change 
is defined as “the propensity or predisposition to be adversely affected”, include elements such as lack 
of adaptive capacity, which is defined as “ability of systems, institutions, humans, and other 
organisms to adjust to potential damage, to take advantage of opportunities, or to respond to 
consequences”  (IPCC, 2014; ecbi, 2019). 
Climate change is going to increase existing climate-related risks and create new for natural and 
human systems, dependent on the magnitude and rate of climate change and on the vulnerability. 
Some of these risks are related to a particular sector or region, and others will have cascading effect. 
All socioeconomic systems are sensitive to climate change, some are particularly vulnerable (such as 
food production and human health). For its part, natural systems are also vulnerable to climate 
change, being even irreversibly damaged on human time scales. Notwithstanding the above, 
adaptation remained a secondary concern for the international negotiations for over a decade, as the 
focus was on mitigation. This was mainly because not all developed countries saw adaptation as a 
global problem (IPCC, 2014; ecbi, 2019). 
There are some differences in vulnerability and exposure that arise from non-climatic factors, and 
from multidimensional inequalities, generally caused by uneven development processes. The adaptive 
capacity of countries to climate change can depend on level of economic development, where 
developed countries have more resources to reduce impacts of climate change through institutional, 
financial, and human and technologies developments that developing countries do not have. Yet in 
contrast to the above, in general, the countries that have contributed the least to climate change 
(through fossil fuel-based economic development) are the most affected by its impacts. As an 
example, developing countries, and especially least developed countries and small island developing 
states, are already facing difficulties in coping with the impacts of climate change that are devastating 
their ecosystems and economies, and threatening to wipe out development progress (ecbi, 2019; 
IPCC, 2014; UN Adaption Committee, 2019). 
However, as climate change has become more tangible, developed countries have placed greater 
emphasis on adaptation. Still, the adaptation finance gap is not closing as a result of the current 
efforts. Providers of development finance are not integrating adaptation effectively enough across 
their activities. Therefore, there are various categories of adaptation needs including: biophysical and 
environmental needs, social needs, institutional needs, needs of engaging the private sector, and 
information, capacity, and resource needs (UN Adaption Committee, 2019). 
In the same line, adaptation is currently transitioning from the awareness-raising phase to the 
construction of actual strategies and plans at the societal level. Thus, even when climate adaptation 
is widely embedded in policy and planning across countries, levels of engagement and the quality of 
instruments are different from country to country, furthermore, from developed to developing nations  
(IPCC, 2014; UNEP, 2021). 
Adaptation is an important part of the responses of countries to climate change. As mentioned 
before, Article 7 of the Paris Agreement recognizes this within the UNCCC, reinforcing this 
framework by establishing a global objective to address adaptive capacity, strengthen resilience and 
reduce vulnerability. The Paris Agreement also establishes that Parties must engage appropriately 
in adaptation planning processes and implementation of adaptation actions (Kato & Ellis, 2016).  
Additionally, in Article 14, the Global Stocktake (GST) was established to collect progress towards 
fulfilling the Paris Agreement’s goals. It will be conducted every 5 years, starting in 2023. For 
adaptation, it will highlight the adaptation efforts done by developing countries, enhance action, 
review the effectiveness of adaptation and support, as well as the overall progress made. The outputs

2.3. 
N ational Communications 
The Article 7.9 of the Paris Agreement states that “each party shall as appropriate, engage in 
adaptation planning processes and the implementation of actions, including the development or 
enhancement of relevant plans, policies and/or contributions” which can include the process to 
formulate national adaptation plans, implementation of adaptation actions, assessment of climate 
change and vulnerability, M&E and building resilience of socioeconomic and ecological systems. This 
article is the only legally binding element for all Parties, which requires them to undertake efforts in 
the adaptation planning processes and in the implementation of adaptation actions (UNFCCC, 
2015). 
Articles 7.10 and 7.11 of the Agreement states that each party should submit and update periodically 
an adaptation communication, which might be a National Adaptation Plan (NAPs), a NDC and/or 
a NC to describe its priorities, implementation and support needs, plans and actions, which also 
contributes to sharing information, good practices and lessons learned. From the aforementioned, 
the NAPs and the NDC are planning-oriented instruments, whilst the NC is a reporting-oriented 
instrument (UNFCCC, 2015).  
Reporting on what has already been done gives the opportunity to share the progress towards goals 
and results of implemented actions. At the moment, countries report their efforts through the NC, 
as well as with Biennial reports (BR) for Annex I Parties and Biennial Update Reports (BUR) for 
non-Annex I Parties. However, the last two cover mainly mitigation actions and GHG inventory 
(Desgain & Sharma, 2016).  
For its part, the NC was the first instrument to be established by the UNFCCC in 1995. It must be 
submitted every 4 years with the objective of providing an overview of the actions being carried out 
by each country, in matter of national GHG inventory, adaptation and mitigation measures to 
climate change and the financial, technical and capacity-building needs (Desgain & Sharma, 2016; 
UNFCCC, 1995). For its adaptation section, it shall include information on the expected impacts of 
climate change and an outline of the actions taken regarding adaptation. It is important to mention 
that the guidelines of the requested information for the NCs for Annex I and non-Annex I countries 
are different. 
For Annex I countries, the guidelines have been revised several times since its publication in 1994. 
Regarding the adaptation relevant sections, the first guidelines encouraged the Parties to report on 
the expected impacts of climate change and outline the actions taken to implement Article 4.1(b) 
and (e) in regard to adaptation. For the 1999 guidelines, in decision 4/CP.5, the encouragement from 
the previous guidelines became a mandatory element and it also included a structure of the chapter 
including three main sections: a) expected impacts of climate change; b) vulnerability assessment 
and c) adaptation measures (UNFCCC, 2000). From the last revision in 2019, 6/CP.25, the Parties 
are encouraged to include the following information in the section: a) climate modelling, projections 
and scenarios, b) assessment of risks and vulnerability to climate change, c) climate change impacts, 
d) domestic adaptation policies and strategies, e) monitoring and evaluation framework and f) 
progress and outcomes of adaptation action (UNFCCC, 2020). However, at the starting time of this 
study, there were not any released reports following the las update of the guidelines. 
For non-Annex I Parties, the first guidelines were adopted in Geneva in 1996 at COP 2. They were 
then revised at COP 8 in 2002, Decision 17/CP.8, suggesting to provide information on the scope of

methodologies and tools used for the climate change scenarios, information on their adaptation to

NC forms part of the measurement, reporting and verification (MRV) framework for developing

2.4. 
A-BTR framework 
The Enhanced Transparency Framework (ETF) on action and support was established under Art. 
13 of the Paris Agreement aiming to build mutual trust and confidence and to promote effective 
implementation taking into account each Parties’ different capacities (UNFCCC, 2015).  
However, it was until 2018 that an agreement to operate the ETF was reached as a response to the 
need of improving reporting and transparency (UNFCCC, 2019c). This agreement, which will be 
applicable to all countries, defines the adoption of a detailed set of modalities, procedures and 
guidelines (MPGs). Through this harmonization for both Parties that will come into force in 2024, 
it will be possible to track progress made by each country, also leaving behind the BUR and BR and 
introducing the Biennial Transparency Reports (BTR) for all Parties (Dale & Christiansen, 2020). 
The BTR, which will become the main reporting instrument, will enable countries to report on 
progress towards all aspects of the Paris Agreement, such as each Parties’ emissions, mitigation, and 
adaptation actions, technology development, and capacity building activities as response to climate 
change in the country (Dale & Christiansen, 2020; UNFCCC, 2019a). It will have to be submitted 
once every two years, while, as already mentioned, NC are reported every 4 years, meaning an 
overlap will occur every four years, and in this case, countries might decide to submit a combined 
NC-BTR report to reduce the reporting burden. 
The adaptation chapter of the BTR (A-BTR) aims to include all the relevant aspects regarding 
adaptation under the ETF, including good practices, priorities, needs, gaps and supporting activities. 
The guidelines for reporting the A-BTR, as it can be found in decision 18/CMA.1, include nine 
different sections as observed in Figure 1. Two sections regarding background information in the 
national adaptation processes to explain national circumstances and to identify vulnerabilities. It 
will also include two sections regarding future activities, which include the definition of adaptation 
priorities and targets, and adaptation policies, plans and strategies that will enable adaptation. As 
well, two sections to include results from previous activities, consisting of the progress and monitoring 
and evaluation of implemented adaptation actions. The three remaining sections consist of losses and 
damages, supporting activities and any other information related to adaptation the country would 
like to communicate. They are much detailed than the ones existing for the NC, however they are 
not mandatory (UNFCCC, 2019c; Dale & Christiansen, 2020).

3.1. 
Literature Review 
An extensive literature review on adaptation to climate change was conducted as a first step. The 
search engines used were DTU Findit, Google Scholar and Web of Science, including key words such 
as: “adaptation to climate change”, “UNFCCC”, “National Communications””, “guidelines”, 
“transparency”. However, not many results were obtained in regards of “reporting adaptation 
initiatives to climate change”. Therefore, the aforementioned results were complemented by some 
articles provided by UNEP-DTU Partnership, and a second approach was taken by looking into the 
bibliography of the documents and identifying the key authors in the field to identify additional 
literature. The articles found relevant for the study were read and analyzed to identify the main 
findings, observations, debates, and gaps within reporting adaptation to climate change. Further 
research was also done for topics out of scope of the thesis, but that supported the arguments, by 
using the first approach of search engines with keywords depending on what was being pursued. 
3.2. 
Data source 
The NC reports were selected as the most appropriate data source for this study as it has been 
considered the best proxy to compare adaptation between countries  (Lesnikowki, et al., 2015b). This 
for two main reasons: one being that it’s available for a wide range of countries (Parties under the 
Paris Agreement), and second, because they demonstrate priorities and trends in adaptation efforts, 
even when a complete inventory of action is not reported (Lesnikowski, et al., 2011), thus providing 
a snapshot of adaptation activity occurring in each country (Lesnikowki, et al., 2015b). It is unlikely 
that the NCs report all the adaptation efforts being carried out by each country given the practical 
and logistical considerations of doing so. Notwithstanding, it is relevant to have an overview of the 
adaptation trends and priorities on a national level.  
A sample of NCs from non-Annex I Parties was selected based on the document’s release date, 
reducing it to documents dated during the 2019-2020 period. Only the reports available in the 
UNFCCC website were considered. Six Annex I Parties from different regions were included to be 
able to make a comparison between non-Annex I and Annex I Parties in terms of the quality and 
quantity of information provided. These criteria were also selected trying to maintain a 
representative sample according to the different income brackets, regions, and national 
communication number. A total of 33 countries were included in the analysis (see Appendix B – List 
of countries (sample)). It is also important to mention that the majority of the documents were 
reported in English, however, seven NC were reported in other languages, from which four were in 
Spanish, and the remaining three in French, based on the language skills, the ones in French had to 
be translated to English using Google Translate document tool. Another important point to consider 
is that Annex I countries were using the guidelines from Decision 4/CP.5, and non-Annex I parties 
used the guidelines from Decision 17/CP.8 (see Appendix A – Comparison guidelines).

Data analysis 
The analysis was conducted using ATLAS.ti 8. The chapters of interest coded for each NC report

3.4.1. Comprehensiveness Index 
The Comprehensiveness Index (CI) aims to represent the extent of information given or reported for 
each adaptation initiatives in the NC, and then, with this information, assess to what extent are 
countries prepared to report under the A-BTR framework.  
Therefore, in order to elaborate this index, a first approach was taken regarding the A-BTR 
framework. As mentioned before, the primary aim of the A-BTR is to “facilitate national reporting 
that is capable of contributing to the collective understanding of adaptation”, and thus, the reporting 
that is conducted under the A-BTR needs to be comprehensive enough, with sufficient levels of detail 
and clarity, to enable “the report’s audience to better understand what has been done so far by the 
reporting country and what the results of their actions have been” (Dale & Christiansen, 2020; 
UNFCCC, 2019a). Hence, considering the information required for each guidance section of it, some 
codes related to the description of the different initiatives were selected. Where an approach of how 
much information per initiative is reported was taken. 
As the national circumstances, impacts, risks and vulnerabilities, and adaptation priorities and 
barriers, sections A, B and C of the A-BTR respectively, are already part of the requirements for 
the NCs they were not integrated in the index as all countries evaluated already consider them. 
Therefore, variables from the Description code group that are, or interpretated to have been, 
requested from the A-BTR guidelines within sections D, E and F were selected. Then, it can argued 
that in order to assess the posed question as how ready are countries to report against the BTR 
guidelines, providing information related to these variables could represent comprehensive reporting 
in regards of the reporting framework provided by the A-BTR guidelines. 
On the other hand, the variables from the Description code group that were not considered, e.g. 
Cost/budget, Geographic cope, implementing actors, among others; are not explicitly or interpretable 
to be requested by the guidelines. 
It is important to mention that it is up to interpretation whether the guidelines request the mentioned 
variables or not. However, for the sake of the present research, the interpretations provided by Dale 
and Christiansen (2020) in the A-BTR guidance document were followed. 
The Table 2 shows how every selected code of the Description code group was used for the index

estimated by country in order to assess, as mentioned, if the country is prepared to report under the 
A-BTR framework. Also, a ranking among countries was constructed for further comparison and 
analysis.

3.4.1.1. Correlation evaluation with countries’ capacities  
In order to evaluate the hypothesis that countries with greater capacities are more prepared to report 
climate adaptation actions under the A-BTR framework, the correlation between the CI and two 
indicators of countries’ capacities was studied.  
The first indicators selected was the GDP per capita (current US$ - 2021) of the most recent year 
when accessed, corresponding to 2019 for all countries but Cuba which is 2018 (Bank, n.d.). This to 
assess whether there is a statistically significant relationship between the countries’ economic growth 
and the comprehensiveness when reporting adaption. This indicator was selected as an unambiguous 
and positive link between the countries’ economic growth has been found (Bowen, et al., 2012). 
Moreover, GDP has been also reported to be significantly related to adaptation, for example in the 
health sector (Lesnikowski, et al., 2013), and as a commonly reported barrier for climate change 
adaptation (Eisenack, et al., 2014). 
The second indicator selected was Government Effectiveness for the year 20191 as it captures 
perceptions of the quality of policy formulation and implementation, credibility of the government’s 
commitment to such policies, among others (World Bank, n.d.). As GDP, this indicator has been 
also found related to climate change adaptation. Engle et al. (2013) within they work mentioned 
several sources contributing to the fact that governance, institutions, and security are related to the 
ability to cope with and adapt to climate change. Furthermore, it has been stated that better 
governed countries have better adaptative capacity, being less vulnerable to climate change (Weiler, 
et al., 2018). 
The values for GDP and Government Effectiveness can be found in Appendix B – List of countries 
(sample). 
It is important to mention that these two indicators do not represent the capacities of a country in 
their totality, but they do give an overview of these. Moreover, as argued, they are also related to 
aspects of interest of each country's capacities regarding climate change adaptation.

4. Literature Review 
The following section will navigate through past work on reporting on climate change adaptation 
and NCs. Key findings, observations, debates, and gaps will be presented, as well as, based on the 
above, the state of knowledge on the topic. It is important to mention that the references presented 
in the following section are not the only ones that have carried out work in the area, rather, they 
are the ones that present scopes and results that are of interest to the present research. 
Since the NC reports began, there have been several investigations to track the progress of climate 
change actions. These studies vary in terms of geographic scope, income bracket countries, or actions 
related to sectors. The different assessments have been hampered by problems with data quality and 
accessibility, leading to the use of proxy measurements to capture relative states (UNEP, 2018). 
Gagnon-Lebrun and Agrawala (2007) performed one of the first reviews done on NCs, where they 
examined the attention given to, and progress made on adaptation in the NCs of developed countries. 
The analysis was done using an empirical comparison through content analysis of the NCs, and an 
in-depth qualitative assessment of progress made by the different countries reported on their NCs 
and other illustrative examples of initiatives. 
Gagnon-Lebrun and Agrawala (2007) found that impacts and adaptation received less attention 
within the NCs than GHG emissions and mitigation policies and measures. Also, that within the 
discussion on impacts and adaptation, the assessment of future climatic changes and impacts 
dominates, while the coverage of adaptation measures is often limited and generic. Although many 
developed countries identified adaptation options, they were often only at a generic level. 
For its part, Ford et al. (2011) did a systematic review of observed climate change adaptation in 
developed nations. The review was performed on peer reviewed publications documenting adaptation 
actions to climate change, where a distinction between proposals and adaptations actions themselves 
was established.  
Ford et al. (2011) offered a “snapshot of what is going on” regarding climate change adaptation in 
developed nations. It was found that adaptation initiatives were most commonly documented in 
transportation, infrastructure and utilities sector. And that to answer a question as “are developed 
nations adapting to climate change?”, a systematic review methodology where explicit inclusion and 
exclusion criteria is needed. It was also found that, while mainstreaming is evident, few studies 
describe the development of specific adaptation plans. Also, even though there is an assumed high 
adaptive capacity for developed nations, this does not necessarily translate into effective adaptation 
actions. It was also stated that systematic review methodologies, in regards of the politicized nature 
of climate change, offer an approach with greater openness and rigor, where such methodology holds 
insights for future climate change metanalyses more broadly. 
Same year, Lesnikowski et al. (2011) performed a study regarding the adaptation to climate change 
actions within the health sector of the UNFCCC Annex I Parties. For the assessment, a codebook 
was designed to extract data quantitatively and systematically regarding health adaptation 
references or initiatives in the NCs. One of the main classifications was the level of action, were 
groundwork and adaptation actions were identified. Groundwork actions were defined as the “first 
steps necessary to inform and prepare for adaptation, but do not explicitly indicate tangible changes 
in policy or delivery of government services that improve resilience”. On the other hand, adaptation 
actions were defined as “changes made to build environments, the delivery of government services, 
==PAGE:23==
 
Literature Review 
DTU Environment

organizational mandates, or regulations in response to precited or experienced impacts of climate 
change”.  
Lesnikowski et al. (2011) found that there was an imbalance in reporting between groundwork, 80% 
of the initiatives, and adaptation initiatives for the health sector, suggesting that the health 
adaptations are still maturing. Moreover, that the understanding of how much adaptation was 
occurring was limited. It was also stated that the systematic approach used allowed for comparative 
analyses, identifying leaders and laggards on adaptation, and to set an overall picture of the state of 
adaptation actions. 
Then, Lesnikowski et al. (2015a) performed, as far as the present investigation is aware, the largest 
comparative study on climate change adaptation initiatives. In this research, NCs from 117 countries 
were studied, systematically identifying more than 4,000 initiatives. For the analysis, a codebook 
was also designed, where the information was categorized on 12 indicators and the typology of action 
was adapted from Lesnikowski et al. (2011) for groundwork and adaptation actions. The initiatives 
analyzed in the study were explicitly concerned with the impacts of climatic change, including both 
positive and negative impacts of climate change. 
From the main results, Lesnikowski et al. (2015a) found 3,395 codable adaptation initiatives with 
709 recommendations. With 73% initiatives at the groundwork level, being most of them 
implemented through mainstreaming into existing frameworks, policies, institutions and programs. 
23% of the actions were tangible adaptation actions, and 4% of them were overlapped. It was noticed 
also that all countries report at least one impact and vulnerability assessment. Regarding sectors, it 
was identified that adaptation is reported as a priority in sectors such as agriculture, water and 
environment. While, specifically for tangible action, they occur more frequently in the category of 
infrastructure, technology and innovation. 
Lesnikowski et al. (2015a) found again the abovementioned imbalance between groundwork and 
adaptation initiatives, but not only for the health sector. It was concluded that although progress 
was noted in both impact and vulnerability assessment and adaptation research, the translation of 
this knowledge into adaptation initiatives was still limited. Also, that while the NCs provide a 
valuable source of information for tracking adaptation to climate change, there is a lack of consistency 
between countries. Moreover, due to this inconsistency, the realization of a global adaptation 
inventory is hampered. It was also stated, mentioning several references, that there was a growing 
recognition about the benefits of systematic tracking of adaptation. 
The same year, Lesnikowki et al. (2015b) assessed national-level progress on adaptation for Annex I 
Parties by using an indicator-based methodology. This approach was used to identify general trends 
in policy change of government-led adaptation over the period between NC5 and NC6. It was also 
stated that NCs are the best available proxy to compare adaptation across countries and over time, 
but that they are also an imperfect information source that provides only a snapshot of adaptation 
activity per country. 
Lesnikowki et al. (2015b) found that adaptation activity was increasing across the Annex I group. 
Also, that the adaptation landscape within the sample was reaching a greater diversity regarding the 
range of policy instruments being implemented. The bigger share of groundwork activities was also 
identified in this work. On the other hand, it was observed that the mainstreaming of existing policies 
increased, with a total of 57% of the initiatives following this. 
During this work, Lesnikowki et al. (2015b) also mentioned the inconsistency between NCs, 
explaining that is due to the lack of coherence in the UNFCCC reporting guidelines about what

constitutes adaptation. And this, as found before, impedes efforts to evaluate adaptation progress 
across countries. 
Thus, while the different research reviewed have different scopes, certain patterns were identified. 
For example, as can be seen, from the observations of Gagnon-Lebrun and Agrawala (2007), to the 
work of Lesnikowski et al. (2015b), and based on what has been reported across the NCs, there has 
been progress in terms of tangible climate change initiatives. However, over time, the imbalance 
between groundwork and adaptation actions has remained. This imbalance was also found by other 
works on specific regions, such as Africa and Asia (Ford, et al., 2014). 
It was also commonly commented that there is a lack of consistency on reporting between NCs, 
making it difficult to track progress in adaptation between countries. Recognizing that this may be 
due to the lack of specificity of the respective guidelines (Lesnikowski, et al., 2015a; Lesnikowki, et 
al., 2015b). Moreover, in the work of Ellis and Moarif (2015) “Identifying and Addressing Gaps in 
the UNFCCC Reporting Framework” for the Climate Change Expert Group, it is mentioned that 
the reporting requirements for NCs are insufficient to ensure that countries will provide complete, 
consistent or transparent reporting of their adaptation activities. 
Complementary to the above, it was also recognized that there is a need to add evaluation as a 
component in adaptation reporting, in order to measure effectiveness of adaptation actions and 
progress. Also, aiming to develop methods for tracking adaptation progress, more rigorous, 
transparent and consistent guidelines and tools for reporting through the UNFCCC need to be 
established (Lesnikowski, et al., 2015a; Lesnikowki, et al., 2015b; Craft & Fisher, 2018). 
As mentioned, adaptation remained a secondary concern for the international negotiations for over 
a decade, as the focus was on mitigation (IPCC, 2014; ecbi, 2019). Moreover, Lesnikowski et al. 
(2017) found that adaptation is still behind mitigation at the country level regarding political 
leadership and resource allocation. However, as Dale and Christiansen (2020) mentioned, as 
recognition of the need to adapt has increased, the demand and need for better and more insightful 
communications and reporting on adaptation needs, priorities, plans and actions under the UNFCCC 
has also increased. Furthermore, Dale and Christiansen (2020) listed, as part of the diverse range of 
drivers of why countries are seeking to establish more comprehensive communication and reporting 
systems, the following reasons: to make adaptation more visible (to balance with mitigation), to 
improve the accessibility to lessons learned and good practices, to raise levels of accountability for 
international support, and to report on the lack of support in the face of the increased adaptation 
challenges posed by climate change. 
Furthermore, it is well documented that there are no agreed-upon methods, indicators, metrics, or 
frameworks designed for an assessment of progress of adaptation to climate change globally (UNEP, 
2018; Leiter, et al., 2019). In the same line, after a review of existing frameworks by the UNEP 
(2018), desirable criteria for a global assessment framework were identified: capacity to aggregate or 
synthesize country-level data, transparency, consideration of progress over time, avoiding undue 
burden on countries, inclusion of proxy indicators that are consistent with a collective understanding 
of adaptation, and sensitive to national vulnerabilities, resources and context.  
In regards of the scope of the present work, it is also important to include some research that has 
been done regarding the upcoming ETF (and therefore BTR) and its implications. For example, 
Weikmans et al. (2020) studied the relationship between transparency and progressive ambition. 
Where several challenges that pose barriers to generate comparable, intelligible, and complete 
information under the ETF to compare adaptation actions and policies were identified. These 
challenges are mainly related to the flexibility of the framework as in reporting requirements change

according to countries' capacities, as well as other issues related to the scope of transparency, 
countries' ability to report, and the politicization of transparency agreements. 
For its part, it has been already analyzed that because many developing country Parties have struggle 
to implement the existing transparency requirements, the operationalization of the ETF will present 
greater challenges. Therefore, a big need for capacity building to support countries as they begin to 
prepare for the ETF exists (Dagnet, et al., 2019). Moreover, further research has been done on the 
GHG inventory and Mitigation sections of the ETF (Gladov, et al., 2020; Zahar, 2019). 
As can be seen, and as far as the present investigation is aware, most of the assessments reviewed 
are focused on measuring progress of adaptation across different countries, without going into further 
detail as to how the initiatives are reported within the different NCs. Furthermore, the only perceived 
insight in regards of the above is that the NCs are inconsistent across countries in reporting on 
different adaptation initiatives. Therefore, the above implies that the results of the present study are 
relevant to the field.

5.1. 
Results per coding category 
From the 33 coded reports, a total of 4,991 actions were identified, from which 46% (2,295 actions) 
were classified as Codable actions, meaning they were qualified as relevant for the study and were 
continued to be coded. Almost one third were Proposed actions, as there was no evidence of 
implementation. Twelve percent were coded as Unlinked to adaptation, as a clear link between 
adaptation and climate change was not stated. The rest were classified as Indeterminate, Duplicate 
and Not Adaptation, with 6%, 4% and 3%, respectively.  The median number of initiatives considered 
codable was 46. 
From the Codable actions, 56% were found to be Groundwork actions, while the remaining 44% were 
Adaptation actions. Almost half of the Groundwork actions were Conceptual tools, followed by 20% 
of Impact/vulnerability assessments, and 14% of Adaptation research. Regarding the Adaptation 
actions, the most common action type was Infrastructure development/technology, with actions such 
as crop technology, infrastructure projects, among others. Other common adaptation actions were 
Public awareness/outreach and Surveillance/monitoring with 14% each. 
Regarding the Action aggregation, almost 60% of the actions were aggregated as Projects, 12% as 
Programs and 9% for both Plans and Independent actions. Ten percent of these actions were part of 
another Project, Program, Strategy or Plan.  
The amount of information reported was gathered to assess the amount and quality of information 
given for each action. 71% of the actions had a description of the work done, 67% included the 
Geographic scope, 50% included Name/Title, while only 32% included an Output (either ongoing or 
finalized), and the Description of objectives and Status were included in 16% of the actions. The 
results can be observed in Figure 2.

The stage of the actions was also included in the coding. It was observed that most of the reported

Among the coded initiatives, the action approach was also applied to determine if countries were

insurance, culture/arts/heritage, technology, tourism, while for National Security no action was 
found in any report.

countries are while reporting their actions. Almost half of the coded actions were described in just 
one sentence, while 39% were more than one sentence (in text). Also, 21% of the actions were 
reported in Tables, 6% were cross-references, meaning they led to links or external documents. It’s 
important to mention that these codes were not mutually exclusive, therefore some actions might 
have two or three of the Format codes, e.g. an action presented in a Table but with more than one 
sentence received the code Tabular and In text.

6. Discussion 
The aim of this thesis is to generate a baseline of knowledge regarding current adaptation reporting 
and to identify trends in reporting across countries with different national characteristics (e.g. income 
bracket, regions), and assess how prepared they are to meet the required information for the A-BTR 
document. In the current section, a reflection on the results found, how they differ from those of 
previous studies, considerations and limitations will be discussed. 
The chapter will be divided in two main sections according to the main research questions, the first 
one discussing about how and what are countries reporting on their national communications, 
followed by a discussion of the country’s preparedness to meet the A-BTR requirements according 
to the CI. 
The codebook was designed to capture individual action oriented. Nevertheless, complementary 
insights were obtained from the perception after performing the codification, therefore such 
observations were used throughout the discussion process. 
6.1. 
Current reporting trends and challenges 
Trends in certain codes were noticed for some of the classifications, as the number of adaptation 
actions relevant for this study, having more initiatives as higher was the income bracket and as more 
NCs have been the country submitted, as well as more actions for Annex I Parties compared to non-
Annex I.  
The classification that showed a more noticeable difference between the aggregated groups was the 
Convention group, therefore between Annex I and non-Annex I countries. This makes sense as the 
guidelines are different for Annex I and non-Annex I countries, and also as they are categorized in 
only two groups and differences can be easier perceived. Therefore, this classification will be mostly 
used for comparison purposed throughout the discussion.  
The classifications can also be interlinked, as Annex I Parties are composed of the industrialized 
countries, which are high income economies, and non-Annex I Parties are mostly developing countries 
(UNFCCC, u.d.). The same was observed for the NCs as, for example, Annex I countries are all in 
their 7th NC. Therefore, it would be expected that Annex I countries perform better as they have 
usually a higher income bracket than non-Annex I and they have also submitted more NCs. On the 
contrary, the region classification was not found to show any important trends. One possible reason 
for this is that each country has different circumstances, being those really different from neighbor 
countries. However, the sample used was small to perform a proper analysis per region, therefore 
further work can be done in this aspect. 
6.1.1. General findings 
Some interesting highlights from the different coded categories will be discussed. The number of 
actions considered relevant for the study (codable actions) for the 33 NCs analyzed were found to 
be 2,295, while the Proposed actions were 1,438. A previous study from Lesnikowski et al. (2015a), 
found 3,395 codable actions and 709 recommendations for a sample of 117 countries. The considerably 
big difference, taking into account the number of the sample, is interesting due to the fact that the 
same methodology was used to collect and analyze the data. This can be due to different decisions 
taken while coding, or it can also suggest an increase in the number of adaptation actions being 
reported.  
==PAGE:50==
 
Discussion

Regarding the action types, the most reported type for groundwork and adaptation initiatives for all 
countries were Conceptual tools and Infrastructure development/technology respectively. However. 
the following most reported actions were different for Annex I and non-Annex I countries. Where, 
for example, for Annex I Parties, Adaptation research was the secondly more often found in their 
reported groundwork initiatives, as well as Public awareness for adaptation measures. This makes 
sense, as it is addressed in Article 6 of the convention, on Education, Training and Public Awareness, 
that educational and public awareness programmes on climate change and its effects shall be 
developed and implemented (United Nations, 1992). Additionally, it was observed that in developed 
countries climate change awareness is much advanced (Khan Abbasi & Nawaz, 2020) and it might 
be a result of this activities. 
While for non-Annex I Parties, the following most reported action type were Impact/vulnerability 
assessments for groundwork and Regulation for adaptation. The last was perceived during the coding 
exercise, where many developing countries (majority of non-Annex I) were reporting the definition 
and implementation of new climate change adaptation policies. The last giving the impression that 
such efforts are new for developing countries because they are still establishing them, while Annex I 
already defined them.  
Another observation was that every country had a Climate change scenario, Dominica being the only 
exception. These are essential for having a successful vulnerability assessment, even though they 
only represent 3% of the total actions performed for both convention groups, which makes sense as 
they are usually just reported once per NC. 
It was also found that most of the groundwork and adaptation initiatives were reported under the 
sectors Water, Agriculture, and Emergency management, being those 43% of the total sectors. Water 
and Agriculture were also the sectors with more reported actions in previous studies (Lesnikowski, 
et al., 2015a; UNEP, 2021). While 10 sectors of the 21 coded were less frequented reported, each 
only representing 1% of the actions. The perceived unbalanced reported within the different sectors 
may suggest that countries tend to report on their most climate-sensitive sectors or that priority is 
being given to address the main humans needs first, which therefore need more urgent attention. 
In terms of the action description, more than half of the actions reported on Description of work, the 
Geographic scope and the Title of the initiative which can be perceived as the easiest information to 
report and thus probably the easiest to access to. It could also be due to the fact that countries can 
perceive as unpractical reporting on specific information that is not being required (Dale & 
Christiansen, 2020). Furthermore, a small amount of actions, 1% of the total codable initiatives, 
were found to be including information such as targets, identification of barriers, and procedural 
transparency when being reported. Adding to this, the outcomes were rarely reported as well, as only 
6% of the actions included this information. It was found that the most common way countries 
reported outcomes was in a qualitative way with 3%, followed by 2% of the quantitative and 1% 
reported on both. This might reassure the idea that as a clear metric or goal to adaptation is not 
yet in place, in comparison to mitigation’s goal to hold the temperature below 2 °C, adaptation 
outcomes are hard to quantify, and usually rather qualitative (UNEP, 2021). 
From the results of the coded Status category, it was also observed a lack of status as such, where 
only 16% reported the current situation of the action. For the remaining actions, the status was 
inferred in accordance to the information given. Notwithstanding, 17% were coded as Indeterminate 
as the status was not stated, nor it could be inferred by the text. On the other hand, when coding 
it was difficult to differentiate between Proposed and Planned actions, regarding the amount of 
==PAGE:51==
 
Discussion  
DTU Environment

information given. Nonetheless, when not established, based on the reported information, actions 
that gave the impression of being planned were coded as such.  
In relation with financing adaptation, in the adaptation actions reported on the NCs, 7% of the 
actions reported the donor of the activities, whereas only 4% of the actions reported the cost or 
budget allocated for the measure to be implemented, almost 80% being reported by non-Annex I 
countries. The latter makes sense, as developing countries shall be assisted with financial resources 
by developed countries, as stipulated in Art. 9 of the Paris Agreement (UNFCCC, 2015). 
Notwithstanding, it was also observed that none of the 3 low income countries included in the sample 
reported on cost/budget, while only Burundi reported two times on donors. A possible reason for 
this is that, even when the non-Annex I guidelines suggest that they “may provide information on 
how support from Annex II Parties meet specific needs and concerns relating to V&A” (UNFCCC, 
2003), it does not explicitly mention to report on the received amount of support. Another potential 
explanation is that they might not be even reporting on the supported adaptation measures, however 
this cannot be assessed only by the information collected from this work. 
Another interesting result was the reduced amount of mainstreaming activities, consisting of only 
12% of all the codable actions, compared to previous literature showing mainstreamed actions being 
around 60% (61%, 63%, 57%) (Lesnikowski, et al., 2011; Lesnikowski, et al., 2015a; Lesnikowki, et 
al., 2015b). There are several potential reasons for the discrepancy. The first one might be 
acknowledged to the limitations of what was considered as mainstreaming for this study, e.g. actions 
that were leading to mainstreaming were not regarded as such. Another possible reason might be 
that within the sample selected, the countries did not report many mainstreamed actions. Finally, it 
was also observed that Low income bracket countries presented a lower amount of mainstreamed 
actions compared to the other income brackets.  
Regarding the format when reporting, 48% of the actions were only described in one sentence, while 
40% were coded as “In text”, meaning they were described in more than one sentence. This might be 
a reflection of the quantity of information given, however it is not a reflection of the quality or 
coherence of it, as “In text” initiatives were not always comprehensive or the other way around, 
having sentences that were descriptive and broad. Additionally, 14 countries used cross-referencing 
meaning they had a link to websites or reports with further information, as an example, Australia 
was mainly including a small description of each initiative, however they had cross reference for 
almost one third of their actions. The amount of information given in the cross references was not 
coded, but it might be a good way to communicate detailed information and to reduce the reporting 
burden (Dale & Christiansen, 2020). 
6.1.2. Adaptation progress  
It was observed that almost 40% of the codable actions were captured from Annex I Parties, that 
constitute only 18% of the chosen country sample. Specifically, Annex I Parties reported on an 
average of 147 actions per country, while non-Annex I reported an average of 52 actions per country. 
This might be a result of the technical capacities and/or financial resources that Annex I countries 
have compared to non-Annex I, which thus can keep a record and report their adaptation actions. 
Another possibility may be that Annex I Parties are not only reporting, but actually doing more 
adaptation actions than non-Annex I countries. This sounds reasonable due to the same arguments 
given before, assuming that they can use their resources to carry out more adaptation actions. 
Nonetheless, considering that countries are not required to report on every action they are doing, 
this assumption is subject to many uncertainties. Furthermore, it would be complex to do so as an 
integrated system would be needed to ensure good communication between the different actors and 
==PAGE:52==
 
Discussion  
DTU Environment 
 
44 
 
stakeholders, which will also require more resources to be allocated to the reporting. This representing 
a reporting burden on countries, that might not be efficient as many actions can be neglected in 
regards to their scope or/and effectiveness to reduce vulnerability (UNEP, 2021). 
Additionally, the guidelines do not specify the level of approach in which an action should be 
reported, and each country has their own way of doing this. As an example, Republic of Korea 
reports on several plans without specifying each of the many actions that might be needed in order 
to implement the plan. This case can also be applied to other approaches such as programs, strategies, 
frameworks and even policies. Another difference can be observed in regards on how countries assess 
some initiatives, this was noted mainly in the case of vulnerability assessments, were some countries 
reported only one action including a vulnerability assessment of all sectors, while other reported 
several vulnerability assessments as each of them was done by different actors and in some cases for 
different sectors. These two cases support the idea that countries do report differently, being this 
one of the potential factors affecting the number of reported actions. However, the number of actions 
does not give an indication whether successful adaptation is occurring (UNEP, 2021).  
In the same line, results can be perceived as a relevant aspect to track progress towards adaptation 
(Dale & Christiansen, 2020). Nonetheless, it represents a challenge as the outcomes are not always 
reported, as from the results gathered from this study show that only one third of the actions reported 
outputs and only 6% reported outcomes. However, on the bigger picture, there is still a need of 
understanding the effectiveness of the outcomes of the adaptation measures to reduce climate risks, 
and whether a progress is done or if maladaptation is occurring (Ford, et al., 2013; Ford & Berrang-
Ford, 2015; UNEP, 2021). These challenges reflect that there is still a gap on the methodology to 
evaluate adaptation success, adding to the fact that many countries have not yet established and/or 
fully operationalized their M&E systems (UNEP, 2018; Hammill, et al., 2014). Australia is one of 
the countries that reported some progress in the matter, by reporting for each territory a monitoring 
and evaluation section, most of them being evaluation frameworks to ensure their adaptation 
measures are successfully contributing to reduce the climate change risks and impacts. 
More than half, 56%, of the reported adaptation actions were classified as groundwork level, 
compared to Lesnikowski et al. (2015a) were groundwork constituted 73% of the initiatives. 
Nevertheless, even though these cannot be compared directly as in the previous study 
recommendations were included for actions as groundwork, an advance towards a balance between 
groundwork and adaptation initiatives can be inferred. Meaning that there are still new initiatives 
being created and research going on, which should continue as they are the base of adaptation 
initiatives. However as they are not considered sufficient to combat climate change (Ford, et al., 
2014), also tangible actions are needed to improve resilience. 
Additionally, it was also found that the proportion for groundwork and adaptation was similar 
between Annex I and Non-Annex I, with 55% and 57% respectively. However, within the groundwork 
actions, non-Annex I Parties on their firsts few NCs (1st, 2nd) were reporting more activities on 
Impact Assessment, while countries with more NCs, like Annex I Parties (7th NC), reported more on 
Conceptual tools. Even though both type of actions are groundwork, the definition of plans, 
guidelines or projects can be perceived as a “next step” towards adaptation to climate change after 
identifying the vulnerabilities, according to the national adaptation process (Berrang-Ford, et al., 
2019), and therefore this might also reflect that Annex I countries are generally further ahead in 
their adaptation processes than many non-Annex I countries.  
The aforementioned can be considered as evidence of adaptation progress, however there is a need 
for longitudinal tracking of this over time (Ford 2019). There are some countries that have been

already taking steps towards tracking, such as Cuba and Burundi, that have included a section on 
progress in the implementation of adaptation initiatives from previous NCs, giving the opportunity 
to report outputs and outcomes. Another interesting case was Uruguay, were they reported a section

which is an interesting approach, as it can be perceived that they have an integral assessment of the 
progress of their different initiatives, going beyond each instrument. These cases were not common 
regarding the other countries from the sample, probably due to the fact that it is not part of the

specifically on section E, which states that each party “should provide the implementation of 
adaptation actions identified in current and past adaptation communications, including efforts 
towards meeting adaptation needs, as appropriate” (UNFCCC, 2019a). 
6.1.3. Barriers in tracking progress and exceptions 
A challenge found in different reports was a perceived lack of internal communication within the 
different actors, whereby most of the actions were reported by the national government, while actions 
by other actors such as NGOs, private sector and sometimes local government were rarely or not 
reported at all. This might be due to a data challenge were some of the actions being implemented 
might not be documented or communicated to the responsible reporting entity (UNEP, 2021), 
making it easier for government agencies to report on the initiatives done nationally. Another possible 
reason might be that adaptation policy has not yet been embedded in regional/sub-national 
governments. However, this was out of the scope of this work, and will therefore not be assessed. 
Unlike countries such as Canada, Australia or Denmark, possibly due to their governmental systems, 
they reported in provinces, states and territories, respectively, giving space for different actors to 
demonstrate their contributions to adaptation. Additionally, based on the Government Effectiveness 
index, these three countries are perceived to have a strong governance performance, which may also 
suggest that the information is more efficiently shared within the regional governments, and therefore 
more available to report. However, in any of the analyzed reports was found this type of institutional 
arrangements between the private sector and the governments. This has been addressed before by 
Lesnikowski et al. (2015a), confirming that “private adaptation efforts are likely underreported to the 
UNFCCC”. 
For its part, several countries reported vulnerability/impact assessments in multiple sectors 
identifying the risk for each one of them. Moreover, countries like Denmark, Estonia, and Equatorial 
Guinea, by doing this, identified their most vulnerable sectors, then reporting the adaptation 
initiatives under those sectors. In contrast, there were cases when countries reported by sector but 
without stating how they defined them, giving an impression of uncertainty. Notwithstanding the 
above, the guidelines for non-Annex I Parties suggests to countries to report their initiatives in key 
vulnerable areas, therefore it can also be inferred that the different countries were following such 
structure, being this a potential reason of the lack of explanation. 
During the course of this work, and based on the definitions provided in the codebook, a certain 
proportion of actions were not perceived as adaptation to climate change. It is important to add that 
such initiatives, under other definitions, might have the nature of adaptation to climate change, but 
for the sake of efficiency and scope of the present work, they were not defined as such. In other 
words, under the premise that any action might indirectly help to adapt to climate change, this 
categorization was made to avoid considering initiatives with a link to climate change that was not 
stated nor well explained/justified. Therefore, some cases of adaptation actions not classified as such 
were observed while capturing the initiatives throughout the report.

One of the cases was observed when countries were reporting on initiatives that were not directly

action that can help to adapt to climate change, however its first nature is to adapt to unsustainable

In the same line, when the structure of the report was done by sector, some actions were often found

some of the areas found to have many Unlinked to adaptation actions, for instance reporting on a 
health system in preventive health for the poorest communities or improving the quality of drinking 
water and laboratory standards. 
Other case was observed when countries were reporting actions related to weather or extreme events 
not relating them to climate change. As a case in point, Dominica reported mainly on a tropical 
storm and a hurricane undergone within the reported period. Moreover, they reported on a 
descriptive way, the estimation of losses and damages in different sectors after these natural disasters, 
which is also not perceived as an adaptation measure. However, this could have been linked to 
climate change by considering it as a first step performed before a restoration of the damages, keeping 
into consideration climate-resilient solutions. 
Moreover, from the total of Unlinked to adaptation and Indeterminate actions, 85% were captured 
for non-Annex I Parties. As the majority of the non-Annex I Parties are developing countries, the 
above can be explained considering that such countries, as mentioned, are already facing difficulties 
in coping with the impacts to climate change that are affecting their economy, threatening their 
development (IPCC, 2014; UN Adaption Committee, 2019). Therefore, they may find interesting to 
report on development (not adaptation initiatives in regards of the used definition) to communicate 
the complex context and difficulties that they face when trying to adapt to climate change. Another 
potential explanation is related to the fact that climate change and development are intertwined, 
where to address vulnerability to climate change, adaptation and development play a crucial role. 
Moreover, any effective development process needs to consider adaptation to climate change, and in 
turn, adaptation initiatives themselves often need development interventions to be successful 
(McGray, et al., 2007; Madhuri & Kumar, 2021). Therefore, as these two concepts are closely related, 
developing countries may find interesting to report on both as their current efforts to adapt to 
climate change. 
Notwithstanding the above, it could be also the case that there are constraints while assessing the 
progress of adaptation to define what can be considered as adaptation (Ford & Berrang-Ford, 2015; 
Lesnikowki, et al., 2015b; UNEP, 2021). This was not always the case, as Viet Nam, for example, 
reported several actions that were coded as Unlinked to adaptation, however, they acknowledge that 
these do not respond directly to climate change, but rather to more sustainable practices. 
As it was observed from the previous examples, it can be perceived that countries might have a 
misunderstanding of the difference between adaptation, mitigation, sustainability and weather 
processes, they might also carry out actions that may respond to a risk, but that may also lead to 
increase the vulnerability to climate change, resulting in maladaptation (ecbi, 2019). An example 
was found in Nigeria’s NC, where one reported action was to keep livestock in confinement instead 
of in free range (extensive), justifying it by saying it might help farmers to reduce disease infestation, 
however this may also cause higher GHG emissions, animal welfare and other economic issues 
(Breuer, 2019). As this example, other actions could have also been classified as Maladaptation, but 
in order to do so, it is necessary to perform a more extensive assessment/evaluation of the action,

6.1.4. Functions of the A-BTR observed in N C 
The preparedness of the countries to comply with the new requirements of the A-BTR will be 
analyzed in section 6.2.4. However, some relevant observations of what some countries have already 
been doing will be underlined here. 
As already mentioned before, one of the additional communication functions of the A-BTR is to 
communicate information about specific good practices, experiences and lessons learned, which can 
be potentially in the form of case studies. This type of case studies with successful adaptation actions 
have been already identified in NC, such as Cook Islands, Canada, and Australia, with the majority 
of them giving more detailed information compared to other adaptation initiatives. Different aspects 
of the initiatives were described such as the contextualization of it, where insights of the situation of 
the country in regards of specific initiatives were reported. Also, in some cases, illustrative 
information on the different steps taken to implement the initiatives were given. 
In the same line, other reporting formats also show different ways to deliver the information. Ghana, 
for example, reported many adaptation measures in tables, having elements such as name of the 
initiative, objectives, impacts, implementing actors, funding, funding agency, while also including 
footnotes with references for further information, delivering a clear and structured overview of each 
action. Therefore, even when the respective guideline does not ask for some of this information, 
Ghana uses tables efficiently to provide extra aspects that may compile with some of the A-BTR 
requirements. 
Other additional function of the A-BTR that was observed on the NCs is the emphasis of developing 
countries to highlight their barriers or the need of capacity building, financial, infrastructure or 
technology support. Gambia as an example highlighted the absence of monitoring and evaluation 
system and lack of stakeholder commitment to providing data which resulted in missing narrative in 
different chapters of the report. As well as countries like Tonga and Botswana also reported lack of 
financial resources to carry out adaptation measures. 
With the previous examples and as mentioned by Dale and Christiansen (2020) countries are 
acknowledging the need of comprehensive systems for more in-depth communication in adaptation 
initiatives. Moreover, when coding it was perceived that there is a sort of freedom of reporting, and 
that this could be allowed by the openness of the respective guidelines. Furthermore, this was also 
stated by Lesnikowski et al. (2015a), where they mentioned that the depth of reporting across 
countries is a reflection of the respective guidelines “… which are brief and non-specific…”. Being 
this, as mentioned, one of the bases for the need of a more detailed guide, such as the A-BTR.  
Therefore, even though some functions of the upcoming A-BTR framework were observed when 
coding the different reports, it is important to analyze to what extent are countries prepared to 
report under the mentioned framework as a reflection of comprehensiveness. Being this the second 
research question assessed by the CI.

6.2.4. CI and preparedness to report under the A -BTR framework 
As mentioned, in line with the CI values, during the coding process there was a general impression 
that most of the countries were lacking information asked by the A-BTR framework. This may be 
self-explanatory considering that in regards of the A-BTR reporting requirements, just some aspects 
are currently in place by the guidelines for Annex and non-Annex I countries. Moreover, those 
requirements, as mentioned for the guidelines, are loose, where for example, as Table 4 in the 
Appendix A – Comparison guidelines shows, the non-Annex I Parties are just encouraged to provide

information. However, and as stated, the assessment performed in the present investigation is merely 
to see how prepared countries are to comply with the A-BTR demands of information.  
Therefore, answering the research question regarding how ready countries are to report under the A-
BTR guidelines, the above suggest that countries are not prepared, however, considering that the CI 
and assessment of the present work are focus on a specific area of the framework, such statement

hampered even for the countries with access to experts and M&E infrastructure. Moreover, as

6.3. 
Summary of main findings 
In the next section, some of the most relevant and common findings between the two main sections 
of this chapter, Current reporting trends and challenges and Comprehensiveness Index, will be 
discussed.  
The present investigation was performed following a systematic approach of the adaptation 
initiatives to climate change reported through NCs. Such approach has been acknowledged to be 
helpful in finding important insights when it comes to the state of adaptation to climate change 
among countries (Ford, et al., 2011; Lesnikowski, et al., 2011; Lesnikowski, et al., 2015a), For 
example, considering that there are currently no frameworks in place to track adaptation, the 
systematic approach has been able to provide, as mentioned, insights on this. 
The above also applies to the present work, where the systematic approach used provided important 
insights on how and what countries are reporting on climate change initiatives through their NCs. 
Where the NCs served as a proper data source since they are considered the best proxy to compare 
adaptation between countries (Lesnikowki, et al., 2015b). Moreover, the different analyses allowed 
to further assess how prepared countries are to report under the A-BTR framework. 
Convention group was the classification in which big differences could be noticed between both of 
the classification groups (Annex I and non-Annex I), this sounds logical as they have different 
guidelines. The income bracket and NC could be related to the convention group classification and 
some trends were also found in these two classifications. Concerning the region classification, no 
trends were found in this matter as the sample was too small to find any significant differences. 
Annex I Parties reported more initiatives on average than non-Annex I countries, with 147 compared 
to 52. However, it is uncertain whether countries are reporting the total amount of adaptation actions

specific (Lesnikowski, et al., 2015a). Even when some countries followed fairly similar format, the

this own style reports the countries of Republic of Korea and Uruguay.

explanation for the different levels of comprehensiveness by country is related to the freedom allowed 
by the different guidelines. 
In regards of the research question as how ready countries are to report under the A-BTR guidelines, 
it was found that considering the CI assessment, countries are not prepared to do so. Where the less 
preparedness in regards of the requirements was observed for report on progress and results (core 
questions E and F), and that, considering that those results need to be determined by M&E processes, 
this adds an additional technical necessity, that is considered highly challenging even for the 
countries with the highest levels of technical capacity. The last related to the fact that there is no 
agreed definitions or methodologies for assessing adaptation, making the assessment of outcomes and 
impacts of adaptation hampered even for the most capable countries that may do have access to 
experts and M&E infrastructure. Furthermore, to answer more accurately the posed research 
question, further investigation should be performed on other aspects of the A-BTR framework not 
assessed by the present assessment (e.g. M&E systems, Loss and damage, among others), and the 
willingness and capability of countries to cope with the A-BTR requirements.  
6.4. 
Limitations  
It is important to highlight some of the limitations that were observed while conducting the current 
work. The first element is related to the codebook, as it was not designed to capture the overall 
impressions of the report, e.g., structure of the report, but was individual action oriented. As a result 
it was mainly oriented on the what and only one dimension of the how (as it does answer the how 
of each individual action) of the RQ, How and what are countries reporting on their national 
communications about their adaptation actions to climate change under the UNFCCC?. Therefore, 
categories regarding the structure and the content of the NC could be included and expanded to 
analyze if the Parties are complying with the current guidelines and to obtain general impressions of 
the report. 
The second element is that only nine categories were selected to be coded in order to attempt to 
capture and examine what and how is adaptation being reported across different countries, basing it 
==PAGE:63==
 
Discussion  
DTU Environment 
 
55 
 
on Lesnikowski (2015a). However, some categories that may have provided relevant information were 
neglected, this was done in regards of the scope of the current research and due to time constraints. 
One of these categories is Actors, coding which would have allowed to capture the involvement or 
participation of the different type of actors such as government, private sector, university, etc., as 
well as if adaptation being implemented by actors outside of the national government were being 
recorded in NCs. Something that would represent a significant blind spot in the ability of NCs to 
capture and document the full spectrum of adaptation occurring "on-the-ground". Another example 
that could have been interesting is the tracking of donors and funding to understand progress made 
in addressing adaptation challenges. 
There are several limitations in regards of the CI. The main one is related to the nature of the index, 
being its aim to measure the preparedness of countries to report under the A-BTR framework’s 
requirements, the CI does not take into account the number of codable actions, neither the type of 
them, but rather the amount of information reported for each one. This limited the scope of the 
analysis in regards that the index by itself does not allows to analyze other interesting aspects of 
adaptation to climate change such as progress. In the same line, related to the way the CI was 
estimated, some initiatives that were noteworthy were not captured. Moreover, as the CI considered 
the overall of initiatives per country, in some cases, the general impression that some reports were 
more informative was not backed up.  
It also relevant to point out that the CI creation was also bounded by the codebook limitations. This 
as it was based on the variables considered in the codebook that the different components of the 
index were selected regarding what was requested or interpreted to be, by the A-BTR framework 
guidelines. 
It is important to mention that regardless the abovementioned limitations, the CI allowed to find 
interesting insights in regards of what defines the comprehensiveness on which countries report. In 
the same line, it facilitated the estimation of correlations with indicative country indices, where such 
an assessment would have been challenging considering the coding results alone, and from which 
important outcomes were found. 
6.5. 
Further work 
Adaptation to climate change has been practiced for several decades now, however there is still a 
long road to achieve adaptation goals to reduce vulnerability. Adaptation measurement is important 
to track what has been done and the impact it has had, as well as to know how much more needs to 
be done. However, further work needs to be done to advance towards a more consistent and 
comparable adaptation reporting, and this can be achieved by setting mechanisms in order for 
countries to provide the same type of information and format. 
As the A-BTR framework will come into force in 2024 with new guidelines and therefore, information 
requirements, further recommendations regarding the inconsistencies and gaps when reporting that 
were observed during the current investigation may not be relevant. However, it will be interesting 
to investigate whether A-BTR will deal effectively with these gaps in the future as some potential 
challenges for the ETF have been already recognized (Weikmans, et al., 2020). 
Furthermore, it is worthwhile to mention that the NC of Annex I countries are from 2017 and 2018, 
therefore they were guiding their reports on the guidelines from decision 4/CP.5 , section II 
(UNFCCC, 2000), so it can also be interesting to do an assessment of how their NC reporting changes 
with the guidelines of Decision 6/CP.25 (UNFCCC, 2020).

Additionally, interviews can be carried out with the people in charge of making the NCs, in order to 
find out what factors are influencing how they report and to better understand the national 
circumstances. 
Notwithstanding the above, and regarding the current work, different matters were observed, which 
were beyond the scope of the thesis, but that would have been interesting to look at: 
• 
A regional assessment with a bigger sample can be done in order to assess if a trend can be 
found within countries in the same region, e.g. North America, Western Africa. As the 
sample from this study was rather small to be able to group the countries and find trends 
for the different regions. 
• 
Some codes can be included in the action type of the codebook, such as technical assistance, 
creation of websites, restoration of land, among others. Because there were initiatives that 
at the time of evaluating their type of action did not seem to fit any of the codes used, and 
were, therefore coded as Other. 
• 
The cross referenced initiatives could be analyzed and codified in order to know how much 
information these sources are providing compared to what is being reported on the NC and 
to evaluate if it really does help the lector to get a better overview of what was done. 
• 
Proposed actions were not codified further than as such, therefore it can be interesting to 
see the direction of adaptation by looking at trends on what countries want to be do. 
• 
One aspect that might have been interesting to include in a broader way is Maladaptation. 
At the moment there is still a lack of knowledge in how adaptation might be reducing the 
risk of climate related outcomes in the long term. However, to assess this more information 
of the initiatives would be required. 
• 
In regards of the CI, in order to answer more accurately whether or not countries are 
prepared to report under the A-BTR, more research should be done. For example, the areas 
of the A-BTR framework that were not considered within the index should be also assessed, 
as if, for example, how prepared are countries to meet the M&E systems requirements. 
Another example is the further research that should be done in regards if countries have the 
willingness and resources (economic, technical, among others) to cope with the A-BTR 
framework.

7. Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to explore the way countries are currently reporting through their national 
communication reports to generate a baseline of knowledge regarding the extensiveness of current 
adaptation reporting. The main research questions intended to be addressed were: 
1. How and what are countries reporting on their national communications about their 
adaptation actions to climate change under the UNFCCC? 
2. How prepared are countries to meet the requirements from the A-BTR guidelines? 
As well, as to prove the following hypothesis for the second research question: 
• 
Countries with greater capacities are more prepared to report climate adaptation actions 
under the A-BTR framework 
To do so, the approach adopted was a systematic approach, where a codebook including different 
categories was build based on past works. Thus, performing a screening of climate change adaptation 
initiatives reported through NCs to the UNFCCC using ATLAS.ti 8. Then, a Comprehensiveness 
Index was created based on the A-BTR framework information requirements in order to assess how 
prepared are countries to meet those. 
Through the assessment conducted, important insights were found in terms of answering the research 
questions.  For the how of the first RQ, it was generally found that the most common information 
presented in the initiatives was description of work, geographic scope and title, suggesting that these 
may be the easiest information to access and therefore to report. On the other hand, it was also 
found that almost half of the initiatives were presented only in one sentence.  
For the what of the first RQ, several results were found, such as that more than half of the CAs were 
classified as groundwork, and where it was also found that the action type suggested that Annex I 
was generally further ahead in their adaptation processes than most non-Annex I. In addition, the 
sectors under which most initiatives were reported were water, agriculture and emergency 
management, suggesting that countries tend to report on their most climate-sensitive sectors or that 
priority is given to address the main human needs (sectors with more urgent attention). 
Compared to past research results, a reduced number of mainstreaming activities was found. Which 
can be due to different approaches when coding and/or that the countries within the sample might 
not report many mainstreamed actions. Also, that there was a relative misunderstanding of 
adaptation, more representative in developing countries, where this can be explained by how 
interconnected the concepts of adaptation and development are; or that there is no clear definition 
of adaptation. In addition, there were also functions of A-BTR being reported in the NCs, such as 
the use of case studies, the use of tables to report information not required by the guidelines but by 
A-BTR, and that some developing countries highlighted their barriers or the need for capacity 
building, financial, infrastructure or technology support. 
As for the second RQ, it was found that most of the countries were classified as Poorly comprehensive 
reporting, with only 10 out of 33 being classified as Moderate comprehensive. The scores of the 
different went from 2.05 to 3.20 out of 8, indicating that level of comprehensiveness when reporting 
in regards of the A-BTR requirements were low, as the highest CI value is two-fifths of the total 
score achievable. Therefore, considering the CI results by themselves, it can be stated that countries 
are not prepared to report under the A-BTR framework, however, to fully assess the posed RQ, 
==PAGE:66==
 
Conclusion  
DTU Environment 
 
58 
 
further research must be done. Examples of this are the need to study how prepared are countries 
to other sections of the A-BTR such as the M&E, Loss and damage, among others. Also, if countries 
have the capacities (economically, technically) and the willingness to cope with the A-BTR 
requirements. 
For its part, the posed hypothesis was rejected as no significant correlation between the CI and 
countries’ capacities indicators were found. Thus, the comprehensiveness of reporting, as an approach 
of quality, is not meaningfully explained by the capacities of the countries, neither to the effectiveness 
of adaptation to climate change.  
It can be concluded that the what and how countries are reporting through their NCs are a reflection 
of the freedom that the guidelines posed, as they are brief and non-specific, allowing countries to 
follow own styles when reporting, creating a lack of consistency between countries. The above was 
also confirmed by the CI results, where no trends or correlation among potential explanations of 
such were found, adding that the different values and rankings are only related to the freedom that 
the guidelines provide. 
Furthermore, in regards of the CI, and considering the mentioned freedom, it is not feasible to state 
that one style of reporting is better than another. Nonetheless, the CI helped by providing insights 
about comprehensiveness, and that the NCs are not being comprehensive in regards of the A-BTR 
requirements of information. 
Moreover, the freedom of the guidelines allows countries to politically choose what to report and 
how, under the premise of what they individually consider important for an issue, which as 
mentioned, is of global interest. 
It is important to mention that even considering the different limitations that were identified, the 
present investigation successfully achieved the stated objective, effectively answering the RQs and 
evaluating the defined hypothesis. Notwithstanding the above, further work that concerns the present 
investigation can be done in order to complement the findings and answer more accurately, for 
example, the second RQ. 
Considering that the A-BTR framework will come into force in 2024, further recommendations 
regarding gaps and inconsistencies in the guidelines may not be relevant. However, it may be 
interesting to assess whether the A-BTR will be able to deal with those gaps and further reduce the 
inconsistencies. 
For its part, and as far as the present research is aware, further information on how climate change 
adaptation initiatives were reported in NCs has not been provided by past works. Thus, giving to 
the results a certain relevance in the field. It is also important to mention that while it was not the 
scope of the present investigation to assess progress on climate change, comparison with past research 
results were done, from which findings can be rescued to further research on the matter. 
Finally, the different results and discussions of the present research serve as an additional argument 
to establish the need for more detailed guidelines such as the A-BTR framework for the sake of the 
global stocktake and global adaptation to climate change as a whole.

Appendix C – Codebook 
The codification of the NCs is done by identifying each adaptation actions and by assigning a code 
for each category of the 9 selected for this study, which are: action, action typology, action type, 
action aggregation, action description, stage, sector, action format.  
Definitions  
 
Some important terms that were used across the codebook are defined hereafter: 
 
• 
Climate change: “a change of climate which is attributed directly or indirectly to human 
activity that alters the composition of the global atmosphere and which is in addition to natural 
climate variability observed over comparable time periods.” (United Nations, 1992)  
• 
Adaptation: “Actual adjustments, or changes in decision environments, which might 
ultimately enhance resilience or reduce vulnerability observed or expected changes in climate”  
(IPCC, 2014) 
“In human systems, the process of adjustment to actual or expected climate and its effects 
in order to moderate harm or exploit beneficial opportunities. In natural systems, the process of 
adjustment to actual climate and its effects; human intervention may facilitate adjustment to expected 
climate.” (UNEP, 2018) 
• 
Output: “goods or services delivered by an intervention (project or program) and used by 
others” (Brooks, et al., 2013) 
• 
Outcome: “short term changes in the population or system targeted by the intervention that 
result from delivering or using outputs” (Brooks, et al., 2013) 
• 
Impacts: “longer term changes that result from outputs and outcomes, either within or 
outside the population or system the intervention targets (e.g., the intervention may produce or 
influence wider societal changes)” (Brooks, et al., 2013)

01  
Action  
To identify if an adaptation action within the NC qualifies as 
relevant for the study, the respective action must meet the 
following criteria (Lesnikowski, et al., 2015a): 
• 
Actions must be concerned with impacts of climate 
changes, not impacts of general climate processes or weather, 
introductory or background statements, methodological 
descriptions, or sectoral trends.   
• 
Actions must be concerned with adapting to climate 
change, not to mitigate it.  
• 
Actions can relate to the effect of climate change on any 
type of system (human, marine, terrestrial, or riparian) and 
any type of vulnerability associated with a changing climate. 
Both positive and negative impacts of climate change will be 
included. Where climate change is not specifically identified 
as a risk component, the action will only be included if it can 
be inferred that climate change is taken into account. General 
monitoring of temperature, precipitation, extreme events, etc. 
will be excluded unless climate change is clearly related. 
• 
The reporting on the delivery of the iteration of the 
national communication being analyzed is not going to be 
coded.  
• 
Likewise, attendance at international conferences (such as 
the COP events) is not coded as an adaptation in of itself, 
unless it is explicitly mentioned, or strongly inferred, that part 
of the attendance was directly informing the countries 
response to climate change stressors in the reporting country 
(i.e., negotiating international adaptation frameworks is not 
classed as adaptation here). 
• 
As the minimum level of information, an action should be 
coded only if the 02 Action level and 03 Action type can be 
identified or strongly inferred

The information presented in the entry specifically or strongly
