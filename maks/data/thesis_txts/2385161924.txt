Abstract 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a biological process in which organic material is degraded by 
microorganisms by producing biogas (methane and carbon dioxide). AD is an attractive waste 
treatment practice that can provide not only energy but also control of pollution, making AD a 
sustainable source of bioenergy. However, ammonia inhibition, a substance that is produced 
through biological degradation of nitrogenous matter, is an extremely common problem that 
remains and needs to be addressed due to significant operational problems and economic losses 
that it causes for the biogas plants. Up to now, many methods to counteract ammonia inhibition 
have been proven either too expensive or time consuming.  Thus, it is urgent that sustainable and 
practical solutions should be found to address the ammonia inhibition. A possible solution to the 
ammonia toxicity is the bioaugmentation of ammonia tolerant methanogenic inoculum (ATMI). 
Nevertheless, suitable technology to generate big amount of volume of ATMI or with high biomass 
concentration during the scale up of bioaugmentation technology  is still lacking and this was the 
object of the present thesis. More specific this study was consisted of two main aspects:  
A. The first aspect aimed to optimize the cultivation processes of ammonia tolerant 
methanogenic enriched cultures. Firstly, the cultivation growth medium was assessed, 
based on methane production efficiency and incubation time. Both mesophilic and 
thermophilic ammonia tolerant methanogenic cultures M1 and T1 were tested. The results 
revealed that the basal anaerobic medium was the best growth medium for both M1 and 
T1. Then, the kinetics of M1 and T1 at different acetate levels were studied and the 
maximum growth rates as well as the biomass concentrations (VSS L-1) were calculated. 
The results revealed that M1 and T1 at acetate 6 and 3 g L-1 had the maximum biomass 
concentration (121.67 and 66.67 VSS L-1 respectively) and at the same time the maximum 
==PAGE:5==
 
v 
growth rates were 0.0118 and 0.0151 h-1 respectively. Furthermore, ammonia and acetate 
inhibition were also investigated. The inhibitory concentrations IC50 for M1 and T1 were 
calculated. IC50 concerning the ammonia were 10.63 and 6.76 g NH4+-N L-1  and regarding 
the acetate 8.9 and 5.92 g L-1 respectively. Last but not least, the inoculum ratio in batch 
reactor was also investigated. The results revealed that 10% was the best inoculum ratio 
compared to the tested for both M1 and T1. However, 2% could support satisfactory, when 
thermophilic conditions are tested. 
B. The second aspect aimed to characterize the ammonia tolerant enriched methanogenic 
cultures, through the methanogenic pathway and the microbial dynamicity of the 
communities. The methanogenic pathway of the acclimatized inoculum was determined by 
Radioisotopic analysis. The results showed that aceticlastic was the dominant 
methanogenic pathway in all the tested samples. Finally, the results from metagenomic 
analysis revealed that the predominant genera common to the tested communities were 
affiliated to Firmicutes, Synergistetes, Bacteroidetes and Thermotogae. Concerning the 
archaeal community the dominant genera were represented by Methanosarcina, 
Methanomassiliicoccus, Methanoculleus and Methanothermobacter.

1 Introduction 
The global primary energy consumption originated from fossil fuels was 86% in 2015 (British 
Petroleum, 2006). Nevertheless, the use of fossil fuels is associated with severe environmental 
impacts such as air pollution, climate change and depletion of natural landscapes (IPCC, 2013). 
Climate change is the most imminent environmental worldwide issue during the last decades. The 
rise in global temperature can cause detrimental effects on ecosystems, wildlife, food chains and 
in human life as well (Appels, et al., 2011).  
On the contrary, there are alternative sources of energy that are available and environmentally 
friendly. For instance renewables are considered as an advantageous way to partly replace the 
fossils fuels. It is clear that renewable resources will play an important role in the current carbon 
dioxide –mitigation policy. For instance, energy from biomass and waste is seen as one of the most 
dominant future renewable energy sources that can support a continuous power generation, while 
at the same time treats waste  (Appels, et al., 2011).   
 Furthermore, European Union has put a target to achieve 20% share of renewable energy in overall 
energy consumption until 2020. Following this concept, the anaerobic digestion (AD), as a 
renewable source of energy, can successfully support the needs of the growing demands for energy.

1.1 Anaerobic digestion steps and anaerobic microbiology 
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a biological process in which organic materials are degraded and 
stabilized under anaerobic conditions by microbial organisms and this leads to the formation of 
biogas (55-65% methane and 35-45% carbon dioxide) and other gases but in smaller 
concentrations such as H2S (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). AD is a rapidly developing technology 
because of its economic and environmental benefits (Angelidaki, et al., 2009). The use of biogas 
has a lot of advantages such as depletion of Global Warming Potential (GWP), reduced use of 
artificial fertilizers and continuous energy production (Singh & Sankarlal, 2015 ). 
Ad is a natural phenomenon that can be observed in the anaerobic environments such as the 
sediments of lakes (Melville, et al., 2014). AD is a biochemical process and it is widely known 
that it is mediated by different microorganisms. Three are three main groups of microorganisms: 
fermenting bacteria (hydrolytic–acidogenic), anaerobic oxidizing bacteria (syntrophic–
acetogenic) and methanogenic archaea   (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). Fermenting bacteria hydrolyze 
polymers (polysaccharides, proteins and lipids) to monomers (sugars, amino acids and long-chain 
fatty acids) that are further converted to short chain fatty acids (butyrate, acetate , propionate etc.), 
hydrogen, alcohols (ethanol and methanol),  and carbon dioxide. The oxidizing bacteria oxidize 
the latter to acetate, hydrogen, formate and CO2 (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). These end products 
are finally transformed to methane and carbon dioxide by the methanogenic archaea. The archaea 
involved in the anaerobic digestion process are members of acetate utilising order 
Methanosarcinales (consisting of two families: Methanosaetaceae, strictly acetoclastic 
methanogens and Methanosarcinaceae, versatile acetoclastic methanogens) and strictly

AD can be categorized in four stages: hydrolysis, fermentation or acidogenesis, acetogenesis and 
methanogenesis (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). These steps are depicted in Figure 1.

In each stage, there is specific community of synergistic microorganisms that use enzymes 
(intracellular or extracellular) to break down the complex compounds into simpler (Melville, et

al., 2014). The products of each stage are used by the microorganisms for their growth. If the 
growth rate of any appropriate community is inhibited or the balance of populations shifts, this can 
affect the overall rate and efficiency of digestion. 
 Hydrolysis 
Hydrolysis is the first stage of the AD process where polymeric compounds such as carbohydrates, 
proteins and fats are converted into smaller units. Different groups of fermentative bacteria excrete 
extracellular enzymes such as proteases, cellulases, xylanases and lipases and convert these 
compounds into monosaccharides, amino acids and long chain fatty acids (LCFA) respectively 
(Melville, et al., 2014). The hydrolysis of each compound is mediated under different time frame 
(Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). For example, proteins are hydrolysed faster than carbohydrates. 
Carbohydrates are the most oxidized compound in AD and depending on their type are solubilized 
easily, in the case of sugars starch and pectin, while more difficult in the case of lignocellulose 
(Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). Lignocellulose, which is a combination of lignin, cellulose and 
hemicellulose is hard to degrade by microorganisms. Finally, fats break down to LCFA and 
glycerol (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). 
 Fermentation  
During this stage sugars and amino acids are converted to hydrogen, alcohols, carbon dioxide and 
volatile fatty acids (VFA) such as propionate or butyrate. The amino acids release ammonium 
during this process. The main VFA of acidogenesis are acetate and butyrate. In general, 
acidogenesis is a fast process and involves numerous intermediated products and end-products. 
When the system is operating under balanced conditions most the substrate is converted to acetate, 
carbon dioxide and hydrogen directly (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). However, under unstable 
==PAGE:14==
 
5 
 
conditions, products such as butyrate, propionate and ethanol appear in much higher 
concentrations (Angelidaki, et al., 2011b).  
 Acetogenesis  
Acetogenesis refers to the synthesis of acetate and the formation of acetate from organics acids 
(Angelidaki, et al., 2011b). Acetogenesis is mediated by acetogenic bacteria, either the hydrogen-
producing acetogens or the hydrogen-utilizing acetogens. The fermentation products of the present 
stage such as VFA and alcohols, are utilized by hydrogen producing acetogens, using carbon 
dioxide and hydrogen ions as electron acceptors. This bioconversion process is not exergonic. 
Thus, a syntrophic relationship with methanogens is mandatory in order to keep the hydrogen 
partial pressure low for acetogenic reactions to be energetic favourable (Treu, et al., 2016a). The 
obligatory syntrophic relation between hydrogen-producing acetogens and hydrogenotrophic 
methanogens has the following characteristics:  
 Propionate and butyrate are degraded based on the cooperation of acetogens and 
methanogens.  
 The specific growth rates are affected by the interspecies distances between 
hydrogenotrophic methanogens and  hydrogen-producing acetogens 
 The share of available energy during the syntrophic fermentation is pivotal (Kougias, et 
al., 2016) 
 Methanogenesis 
Methanogenesis is the final step of anaerobic digestion process, mediated by three main pathways: 
aceticlastic methanogenesis where acetate is cleaved to methane and carbon dioxide, 
==PAGE:15==
 
6 
 
hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis where carbon dioxide is reduced to methane and 
methylotrophic methanogenesis where methylated compounds such as methanol, methylamines, 
methylmercaptopropionate etc., are converted to methane. However, the larger contribution of 
methane formation is derived from acetate (70% of methane formation) and the rest is primarily 
produced from H2/CO2 (30% of methane formation) and formate.  
Methanogenesis from acetate is mediated by two different pathways: aceticlastic methanogenesis 
and syntrophic acetate oxidation coupled with hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis (SAO-HM). 
Aceticlastic mechanism (reaction 1) is the direct acetate transformation to methane and carbon 
dioxide and it is carried out by aceticlastic methanogens originated by two groups of Archaea, the 
Methanosaetaceae spp. and Methanosarcinaceae spp. within a single family Methanosarcinales 
(Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011a; Karakashev, et al., 2006). Syntrophic acetate oxidation is a two-
step reaction (reaction 2 and 3) where acetate is first oxidized to hydrogen and carbon dioxide and 
subsequently converted to methane and it is carried out by acetate-oxidizing bacteria, such as 
Clostridium spp. in a syntrophic association with hydrogenotrophic methanogens such as 
Methanobacteriales spp. or Methanomicrobiales spp. (Fotidis, et al., 2013c;Hattori, et al., 2000; 
Karakashev, et al., 2006).

1.2 Ammonia inhibition on anaerobic digestion  
There are many substances reported in the literature that inhibit the anaerobic digestion process. 
A substance can be judged inhibitory when it provokes inhibition on microbial growth or an 
adverse shift in the microbial population. A decrease of the steady-state rate of CH4 production 
and accumulation of organic acids, usually indicate inhibition (Chen, et al., 2008). Some of the 
main inhibitors are the ammonia (free ammonia and ammonium ion), VFA, LCFA and heavy 
metals.  Among the aforementioned factors affecting the performance of the process, ammonia has 
been considered as the major toxicant factor that affects the stability of anaerobic digestion and 
then causes inhibition and suboptimal biogas production in full scale digesters (Westerholm, et al., 
2011).  
Free ammonia nitrogen (NH3) and ammonium ion (NH4+) are the two main forms of inorganic 
ammonia nitrogen found in anaerobic digestion process (Yenigün & Demirel, 2013). Both forms 
can provoke inhibition in anaerobic digestion process. Even though low concentrations of 
ammonium are beneficial for bacterial growth, high concentrations can be deleterious for the 
bacterial community. Ammonia is produced by the breakdown of the nitrogenous matters such as 
proteins and urea (Gonzalez-Fernandez & Garcıa-Encina, 2009). Various mechanisms for 
ammonia inhibition are known such as an increase of energy requirement, change in the 
intracellular pH, exhaustion of intracellular potassium and inhibition of specific enzymes 
(Wittmann, et al., 1995). To date, it is not clear the influence of ammonia concentration on the 
methanogenic community composition and on methanogenic pathway (Fotidis, et al., 2013b). In 
this manner, many researchers claimed that hydrogenotrophic methanogens are more resistant to 
ammonia toxicity (Koster & Koomen, 1988; Chen et al., 2008; Hao et al., 2012), while others have 
indicated the opposite (Zeeman , et al., 1985). Regarding the tolerance of aceticlastic methanogens 
==PAGE:17==
 
8 
 
to ammonia toxicity, it seems that Methanosarcinaceae spp. is the dominant aceticlastic 
methanogen at increased ammonia concentrations compared with Methanosaetaceae spp. which 
is more sensitive to high FAN concentrations (Karakashev, et al., 2005).  
Finally, there are several factors affecting the toxicity of ammonia inhibition that are introduced 
as follow: 
 Ammonia concentration   
In the literature, it has been reported a wide range of total ammonia nitrogen (TAN) concentrations 
(1500 to 7000 mg L-1) according to Hejnfelt and Angelidaki (2009). This wide range is maybe due 
to the different nature of substrate tested, acclimation periods and environmental conditions in 
terms of pH and temperature (Chen, et al., 2008). TAN concentration up to 4000 mg NH4+-N L-1 
is known to provoke inhibition to methanogenesis (Chen, et al., 2008) Other studies have reported 
that when TAN concentration is over 3000 mg/L, then the anaerobic digestion process is inhibited 
independent the pH value (McCarty, 1964). Similarly, Hobson and Shaw (1976) reported that TAN 
concentration of 2500 mg L-1 inhibited partial the methanogenesis, while TAN of 3300 mg L-
1inhibited totally the methanogenesis. Nonetheless, if there is adaptation to high ammonia levels, 
the microorganisms can tolerate TAN concentrations above 3000-4000 mg L-1 (Angelidaki & 
Ahring, 1993) or even higher with TAN concentrations above 6000 mg L-1 NH4+-N L-1 according 
to Lauterboeck et al. (2012). 
However, ammonia inhibition should not always related to TAN concentration but mostly to FAN 
levels (Chen, et al., 2008). FAN concentration depends on temperature, pH and TAN (Rajagopal, 
et al., 2013). Angelidaki and Ahring (1994) observed that FAN concentrations higher than 700 mg 
L-1 caused inhibition in the thermophilic reactor, resulting to rapid increase of VFA concentrations 
==PAGE:18==
 
9 
 
and low methane production. In another study contacted by Borja et al. (1996), inhibition of 
methanogenesis of cattle manure occurred at FAN value of 995 mg L-1. Finally, Nielsen and 
Angelidaki (2008) reported that anaerobic digestion of cattle manure was inhibited at pH 7.6 and 
at 550C at FAN concentration equal to 1200 mg L-1 (Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008b).  
However, there are several methods to control the ammonia inhibition (Chen, et al., 2008). To 
begin with, acclimation of microflora could be a possible way to alleviate the ammonia toxicity 
(Sung & Liu, 2003). Acclimation of microorganisms to high ammonia concentration could 
increase the ammonia tolerance (Chen, et al., 2008). Another way to alleviate ammonia toxicity 
could be the lowering of the temperature of the digester (Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008b) or the 
addition of ammonium- binding ions (Kougias, et al., 2013). Furthermore, increasing the C/N ratio 
of feedstock or dilution with water are also two additional ways to counteract ammonia inhibition 
(Chen, et al., 2008). However, these traditional ways are time-consuming and cost-expensive 
(Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008b).  
Bioaugmentation of ammonia tolerant methanogens in the anaerobic reactors could be a realistic 
way to control the ammonia inhibition (Fotidis, et al., 2014; Fotidis, et al., 2013c; Westerholma, 
et al., 2012; Chen, et al., 2008). Bioaugmentation is the process where specific microorganisms 
are added in a microbiological system to improve process operations (Stephenson & Stephenson, 
1992). There have been reported many successful studies of bioaugmentation of ammonia-tolerant 
methanogenic cultures that had been used for different purposes. For instance, to improve the 
degradation of specific organic compounds (Cavaleiro, et al., 2010), start-up of new reactors 
(Saravanane, et al., 2001) and odor reduction (Duran, et al., 2006). Furthermore, recovery after 
organic overloading reactor (Tale, et al., 2011) and improved performance of mesophilic reactor 
degrading distillery wastewater (increased methane production with simultaneously reduction of 
==PAGE:19==
 
10 
 
VFA) with the addition of hydrogen-utilizing methanogens (Savant & Ranade, 2004). Moreover, 
Fotidis et al. (2014) reported a successful bioaugmentation of a fast growing hydrogenotrophic 
methanogen (Methanoculleus bourgensis MS2T) in a mesophilic continuous reactors with manure 
as substrate, operating under “inhibited steady state”. In the latter study, the methane production 
yield was increased to 31.3% by a bioaugmentation concentration of 11% of the reactor’s working 
volume.  
 
 Temperature  
Anaerobic digestion takes place under different temperature conditions such as Psychrophilic (12-
18°C), mesophilic (25–40°C), thermophilic (50-55°C) (Angelidaki & Batstone, 2011a). These 
three temperature conditions are illustrated in Figure 2. Nevertheless, the two most commonly 
applied temperature conditions are thermophilic and mesophilic. There are several benefits to the 
utilisation of thermophilic process compared with mesophilic. For example, thermophilic process 
has reduced residence time in the plant and a higher organic loading capacity, better degradation 
of LCFAs and other, but the most important is the destruction of pathogens. However, there are 
also some limitations compared with the mesophilic process such as the demands of larger amount 
of energy, the larger risk of ammonia inhibition and degree of instability. Temperature influences 
the ammonia toxicity, which increases when the temperature increases. The variation in 
temperature provokes many different phenomena. For instance, by lowering the temperature, the 
VFA production as well as the substrate composition rate decreases  (Bowen, et al., 2014).

 pH 
Methanogenesis is limited to a narrow pH range. The optimum pH range that has been reported in 
anaerobic digestion is 6.6 to 8.2 (Mao, et al., 2015), although some studies have reported that AD 
performs sufficiently in higher pH values. Other studies have reported that the optimal range of 
pH to achieve the maximum biogas yield in AD process is 6.5–7.5 (Liu, et al., 2008) and that 
outside of this pH range, the biogas yield is decreased immediately until anaerobic digestion ceased 
(Hwang, et al., 2004). Most methanogens perform better at a pH range between 7 and 8, while the 
acid forming bacteria perform better at a lower pH range. PH not only affects the growth of the 
microorganism growth but also other factors, such as the dissociation of crucial elements 
(ammonia, organic acids, sulphide), that are very important for the anaerobic digestion. Moreover, 
pH plays an important role when it comes to control free ammonia (FAN) that is toxic to 
==PAGE:21==
 
12 
 
microorganisms. The actual pH value in thermophilic biogas reactor is higher compared with 
mesophilic reactor because the dissolved carbon dioxide forms carbonic acid by reaction with 
water. Bicarbonate buffer system can mainly control the pH in anaerobic reactor (Angelidaki, 
2004). 
 
1.3 Kinetics 
Anaerobic digestion has been applied for a many purposes, such as the conversion of biomass crop 
to methane and the production of saleable methane from cattle dung. However, anaerobic treatment 
of complex waste material presents a set of problems to scientists who try to improve the treatment 
process (Kishor & Singh, 1988).  
Simulation of the kinetics for microbial growth and substrate utilization is an important area in 
which more research work is required (Kishor & Singh, 1988). The first kinetic observations were 
of a pure culture on synthetic medium in a batch test (Angelidaki, 2004). The number of cells over 
time is increasing in numbers during substrate consumption. Microbial kinetics always indicate a 
great influence of substrate concentration on microbial growth.  For instance, at low concentration 
of substrate, the specific growth rate (μ) is very close to zero, which indicate an insignificant 
change in microbial population. On the other hand when substrate concentration is increasing 
above a threshold limit, the specific growth rate is proportional to an increase in the substrate level 
and approaches a maximum value. If there is an increase in substrate concentration above this 
threshold limit, the decrease in specific growth rate takes place (Mulchandani & Luong, 1989 ). 
There are four stages of cell growth in a batch reactor as can be shown schematically in Figure 3. 
==PAGE:22==
 
13 
 
 
Figure 3 Cell concentration at a batch experiment [adapted from (Angelidaki, 2004)] 
Phase I. Initially, a small number of cells is added into the batch reactor where there are the 
nutrients necessary for the growth of microorganisms. When cells are introduced to a substrate in 
batch, there is an initial delay due to the time needed from the microorganisms to adjust to the 
substrate and the environmental conditions. This phase is called lag phase and there is little 
increase in cell concentration. During this phase, a series of important functions take place such as 
the synthesizing of transport proteins and enzymes for utilizing the new substrate. The duration of 
the lag phase is related to the growth medium from which the inoculum was take as well as the 
medium in which it is placed.  
Phase II. Following the lag phase, namely exponential growth phase, the cells are dividing at the 
maximum rate. All of the enzyme's pathways for metabolizing the substrate are in place and 
subsequently, the cells can use the nutrients most efficiently. The substrate is consumed according 
to zeroth order kinetics. This means that the substrate is consumed at a constant rate (for example, 
rS=-k1, where k is the zeroth order parameter in mass volume-1 time-1 units). As substrate is 
==PAGE:23==
 
14 
 
reducing, it changes to first order kinetics (for example, rS=-k2S where k2 is the first order kinetic 
parameter in time-1 units, and S is the substrate in mass volume-1 units). 
Phase III. During this phase, namely stationery phase, the lack of one or more nutrients limits the 
cell growth. Furthermore, the growth rate of cells is also influenced by the buildup of organic acids 
and toxic materials formed during the exponential phase.  
Phase IV. This is the final phase, namely death phase. During the death phase, there is decrease 
in live cell concentration. This decrease is due to the accumulation of toxic by-products, decrease 
of nutrient supply and harsh environments. 
 In non ideal conditions death or inactivation of microorganisms can occur during all phases in a 
batch reactor. In continuous fed systems, both death and respiration take place simultaneously. 
The uptake rate of substrate depends on the substrate concentration and the kinetics are similar to 
the kinetics of the pure culture. Accordingly, there are two processes (Angelidaki, 2004): 
(1) Biomass death represented by a first order process in biomass concentration. 
(2) Combined respiration and anabolism that is zeroth order at high substrate concentrations, 
while first order at low substrate concentrations.

2 Aim of the study 
It has been reported that many full-scale anaerobic digesters are operating at “inhibited steady-
state” condition with up to 30% losses of potential methane production rate that leads to serious 
economic losses for the biogas plant, due to ammonia inhibition  (Fotidis, et al., 2014).  
To date, there  have been suggested several methods to address this problem, such as lowering the 
operating temperature of the reactors (Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008a), adjusting the C/N ratio, 
diluting the reactor content with water  (Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008a) and adding ammonium 
binding ions (Kougias, et al., 2013). However, these methods are time-consuming and cost-
expensive (Nielsen & Angelidaki, 2008b). 
Ammonia inhibition could be solved by bioaugmentation of ATMI. However, appropriate 
technology to generate big volume of ATMI or ATMI with high biomass concentration through 
the transition from lab scale of bioaugmentation technology to full scale (i.e. a full-scale biogas 
plant) is still lacking and this was the object of the present thesis. At DTU environment, 
bioaugmentation succeed in lab scale. Therefore the main challenge to generate ATMI for full 
scale implementation remains. Following this concept, ATMI (M1, T1) were cultivated at both 
mesophilic and thermophilic conditions at the higher FAN and pH level. To accomplish the aim 
of the present study, both optimization of the cultivation strategies -bioaugmentation technology 
(1st experimental assay) and characterization (2nd experimental assay) of ATMI were elucidated.  
 
==PAGE:29==
 
20 
 
During the first experimental assay, the used growth medium for the cultivation of ATMI and their 
kinetics were assessed. In addition, different acetate concentrations were tested to determine the 
highest biomass concentration (VSS L-1) in which the ATMI could grow efficiently. The final 
choice of acetate concentration was assessed based on the biomass synthesis/conversion rate and 
maxiumum specific growth rate (μmax). Finally, an extra target was added, which was the effect of 
medium to inoculum ratio on the growth of ATMI in batch reactor. The aim was to to determine 
the minimum amount of ATMI that could growth efficiently within a short incubation time (a 
realistic timeframe). 
For the performance of the second experimental assay, both the methanogenic pathway and the 
effect of ammonia acclimation on the methanogenic microbial community were assessed. 
Methanogenic pathway was assessed by Radioisotopic analysis of ATMI, while the microbial 
structure by 16S rRNA gene sequencing.

Radioisotopic analysis 
The methanogenic pathway of acetate degradation was determined by measuring the production 
of 14CH4 and 14CO2 from acetate labelled in the methyl group (C-2). In case the syntrophic acetate 
oxidation coupled with hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis (SAO-HM) is the dominant 
methanogenic pathway, the methyl group of the [2-14C] acetate is converted to 14CO2. Thus, the 
microorganisms involved in hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis convert only a very small part of 
14CO2 molecules to 14CH4. Accordingly, the concentration of 14CO2 will be larger compared to the 
concentration of 14CH4 formed during the oxidation of acetate. On the other hand, when aceticlastic 
methanogenesis (ACM) is the dominant methanogenic pathway, all 14C carbon ends up to 14CH4. 
Therefore, the concentration of 14CH4 in the gas phase will be larger compared to the concentration 
of 14CO2 formed during the oxidation of [2-14C] acetate. It is a general assumption that when the 
ratio 14CO2/14CH4 is below or above 1, the dominant pathway is ACM or SAO-HM respectively. 
When the methane production ceased, and remained stable, batch reactors containing 14C carbon 
were acidified with 7.2 M HCl (final pH = 0.95 ± 0.1). Thus, the dissolved 14C bicarbonate was 
converted to 14CO2 and then trapped with a CO2 absorber for liquid scintillation counting. The 
14CH4 was combusted to 14CO2 in a tube furnace, where the temperature was above 850 °C. The 
14CO2 produced was trapped in 10 mL of CarbosorbR-E. For the radioactivity counting, 10 mL of 
CarbosorbR-E were mixed together with 10 mL PermaflourR E+ (Perkin-Elmer Company) 
scintillation fluid. Measurements were performed in triplicates using a liquid scintillation counter 
(Tri-Carb 1600; Perkin-Elmer Company) (Fotidis, et al., 2013b).

16s rRNA gene sequencing 
DNA extraction and sequencing  
Triplicate samples for each ammonia-tolerant methanogenic culture were taken from both the 
initial inoculum obtained from the biogas plants and at the higher total ammonia nitrogen 
concentration where the corresponding cultures were acclimatized to identify the microbial 
dynamics. Genomic DNA was extracted from each sample according to PowerSoil® DNA 
Isolation Kit (MoBio PowerSoil, Carlsbad, CA USA) by applying extra phenol cleaning steps. 
During this procedure, addition of 2 ml of phenol: chloroform: isoamylalcohol (25:24:1, v/v, 
Sigma-Aldrich) to the supernatant of the fist step of extraction was performed and then shaked 
briefly and centrifuged. The V4 regions of 16S rRNA gene were amplified with PCR using 
515f/806r primer, and next generation sequencing was performed by Illumina MiSeq platform 
(Ramaciotti Centre for Genomics, Kensington, Australia). 
Data analysis 
The raw sequences were analysed using CLC Workbench software (V.8.0.2) with Microbial 
genomics module plug in (QIAGEN). A trimming procedure and chimera crossover filter were 
applied according to default parameters provided by the software to ensure the high quality of the 
reads. Phylogenetic assignment of Operational taxonomic units’ (OTUs) was performed with 
multiple sequence comparison of fixed length trimmed (240 bp) sequences using MUSCLE 
software (Edgar 2004) and Greengenes v13_5 database as reference (clustered at 97%). When 
taxonomy similarity percentage was lower than 80%, new OTUs were identified with a minimum 
occurrence of five reads. Maximum likelihood phylogeny was performed with Neighbour Joining 
==PAGE:39==
 
30 
 
as construction method and Jukes Cantor as the nucleotide substitution model to estimate the 
evolutionary between sequences. Bootstrap analysis with 100 replicates was used to test the 
certainty of the evolutionary relation and distance. Rarefaction curve (Fig. 10) was used to evaluate 
the sequencing depth for each sample. Shannon index was used to evaluate Alpha diversity. 
Furthermore, Beta diversity was performed by STAMP software and represented as Principal 
Component Analysis (PCA). The taxonomic assignment of the most interesting OTUs with high 
abundance (relative abundance of bacteria and archaea > 0.5% in at least one of the triplicate) was 
based on the results from BLASTN search against 16S ribosomal RNA sequences (Bacteria and 
Archaea) database and CLC software). Microbial relative abundance among the cultures were also 
represented (Fig. 8) using Microsoft software excel 2013

abundance (relative abundance higher than 0.5% with respect to the total number of sequences that 
were present in at least one of the triplicate) were taken into account in the present study.  
 
Taxonomical characterization  
To make it more clear the taxonomic assignment was divided to the taxonomic assignment for 
mesophilic (M1, M2) and thermophilic cultures (T1, T2, T3). However, the vast majority of the 
microbial community was assigned to phylum, class, order and family level. Only a few OTUs 
were assigned to genus level. The results based on the high relative abundance of OTUs showed 
that the majority of the community for all the tested cultures consisted of bacteria and archaea and 
that the bacterial community was both more abundant and more diverse than the archaeal. The 
bacterial community’s dominance and bigger diversity compared with the archaeal community 
found in the present study were in agreement with previous studies (Sundberg, et al., 2013). The 
taxonomic assignments obtained for Euryarchaeota (where the Archaea domain belongs) was 
easier making possible to be assigned even in a genus level (100% of the total high relative 
abundant). On the contrary, bacteria were in the majority unclassified at genus level (only 35.6% 
of the total high relative abundant), indicating that archaea were better characterized than bacteria 
in the tested communities. The precis percentages of these two domains are illustrated in Table 11 
(Appendix). The predominant genera common to the tested communities were affiliated to 
Firmicutes, Synergistetes, Bacteroidetes and Thermotogae. The presence of the previous phyla 
and especially Firmicutes and Thermotogae was in accordance with previous studies  (Sundberg,

bacteria by producing acetic acid, carbon dioxide and hydrogen that can be used by the

4.2.3.1 Batch experiment VS methanogen’s biomass inoculation ratio 
Figure 19 illustrate the methane yield of M1 (a) and T1 (b) at different inoculum ratio. As it can 
be seen in case of M1, 10% inoculum ratio performed better than the rest tested inoculum ratios. 
Batch reactors  corresponding to 10% inoculum ratio reached the maximum theoretical within 23 
days, while at the same time 20% inoculum ratio, which was the second best performing inoculum 
ratio, had 43% lower methane production yield compared to 10%. These results showed that the 
inoculum ratio in mesophilic conditions was of outmost importance, indicating that the 
concentration of BA medium and therefore the concentration of trace elements present in BA 
medium was very important factor that finally affected the performance and the properties of the 
microorganisms. Thus, it can be assumed that the concentration of the trace elements in the case 
of 20% inoculum ratio was less than the optimal, leading to lower methane production compared 
==PAGE:71==
 
62 
 
to 10% inoculum ratio. Therefore, the right concentrations of trace elements is required to prevent 
disturbances in reactor performance when less amount of trace elements is used (Zandvoort, et al., 
2006).   
Regarding T1 the results showed that the inoculum ratio didn’t affect in a significant way the 
biomethanation process, indicating that the BA medium concentration was of minor importance 
for the performance of the microorganisms. The results demonstrated that even 2% inoculum ratio 
could efficiently support the batch cultivation process, even though within the same incubation 
time the methane production was lower compared to the rest inoculum ratio. This finding was also 
supported by the One-way ANOVA which showed that methanogenesis was not significant 
different (p>0.05). 
Nevertheless, the results could be well grouped in two groups. The first group was consisted of 2 
and 5% inoculum ratio, while the second group was consisted of 10 and 20% respectively, based 
on the production efficiency and the tested incubation time. The increase in the inoculum ratio 
from 10% to 20% was in this case of minor importance. In addition, 10 % inoculum ratio 
performed almost better and reached faster the maximum theoretical compared to 20%.  
A common characteristic of M1 and T1 was that 10% inoculum ratio performed better. Therefore 
it could be assumed that 10% inoculum ratio could be set as the optimal threshold in batch reactor 
experiments, taking into account the tested incubation time.

5 Conclusions 
Based on the results of the present study, the following conclusions were drawn: 
1.  Aceticlastic pathway was found to be dominant for all the tested samples, mediated by 
Methanosarcinales, even though their low relative abundance compared to the 
hydrogenotrophic methanogens in case of M1 and M2. Furthermore, there was not always 
correlation between metagenomic analysis and methanogenic pathway revealed by 
Radioisotopic analysis, meaning that it was not possible to identify, with certainty, the 
dominant methanogenic pathway. 
2. The 16S rRNA sequencing for microbiome analyses revealed that the majority of the 
microbial community consisted of bacteria represented mostly by Firmicutes, 
Synergistetes, Bacteroidetes and Thermotogae and archaea represented by the predominant 
genera 
of 
Methanosarcina, 
Methanomassiliicoccus 
and 
Methanothermobacter. 
Nevertheless, their high relative abundance was not distributed equally within the tested 
samples. Furthermore, metagenomics analysis revealed some novel species such as 
Methanosarcina soligelidi and Methanomassiliicoccus luminyensis. 
3. Basal Anaerobic medium was the best growth medium for both mesophilic and 
thermophilic conditions. The addition of different amount of digestate effluent did not 
==PAGE:74==
 
65 
 
improve the methane production yield for the tested ammonia tolerant methanogenic 
inoculum. 
4.  The higher ammonia and acetate concentration, the stronger inhibitory pressure exerted 
on methanogenic microorganisms. The increase in the concentration of the aforementioned 
solutions revealed lower maximum growth rates. However, the acetate concentration of 6 
and 3 g L-1 showed both high cell concentration (VSS L-1) and satisfactory maximum 
growth rates. TAN concentration of 12 and 7 g NH4+-N L-1 and acetate concentration of 6 
and 3 g HAc L-1 found to totally inhibit the methanogenic activity for ATMI M1 and T1 
respectively.  
5. The inoculum ratio in the case of M1 was of major importance, since the tested inoculum 
ratio showed different performance of the microorganisms. On the contrary, the inoculum 
ratio was of minor importance in the case of T1. To conclude, 10 % was the inoculum ratio 
in which both cultures performed better. However 2 % could efficiently be used as 
inoculum ratio in case of thermophilic conditions.

6 Future perspectives  
Ammonia inhibition in anaerobic digestion process is a highly debated issue with many assays to 
have been conducted until now (Rajagopal, et al., 2013). The present study was performed to 
develop the appropriate technology to generate ATMI. The study focused on two aspects. The first 
was related to the technical- implementation part, while the second focused on the biological part 
(cultivation of ATMI). The reason was togenerate ATMI to the higher TAN level that could 
potentially be used for bioaugmentation. The second part tried to decipher the microbial 
community dynamicity and the importance of different microbial groups in anaerobic digestion.  
In general the following experiments are suggested: 
1. Investigation of the effect of medium to inoculum ratio in different proportions than the 
tested in the present study. 
2.  Implementation of fed-batch experiment VS methanogen’s biomass inoculation ratio. 
Previous studies showed that that fed-batch was the best acclimation method based on the 
incubation time, production efficiency, TAN/FAN levels achieved and methanogenic 
==PAGE:76==
 
67 
 
activity. Fed-batch reactor offers a more stable microorganism growth that can be 
controlled by exponential feeding strategy (Tian, et al., 2017) without washout effect.  
3. Implementation of the optimal cultivation strategies step in bigger plateau of TAN and 
acetate concentrations than the present. 
4. Isolation and cultivation of the most predominant species that the 16S rRNA sequencing 
analysis revealed, with aim to increase the probabilities of acclimation  in even higher TAN 
level than the tested in the present study. Thus, production of suitable ammonia-tolerant 
methanogenic consortia that could be used for a successful bioaugmentation process.
