Abstract
Honeypots are decoy systems or protocols designed to appear vulnerable, with their primary
value lying in being probed, attacked, or compromised. They serve as early warning mecha-

cates that LLMsÔÇÖ ability to generalize, reason, memorize, and diversiţÇĽy helps address persistent
challenges in traditional emulated honeypots, such as fexibility, interaction depth, and deceptive
eţüÉectiveness. However, the stochastic nature oţÇĽLLMs introduces new issues, the most signiţüôcant
being inconsistencies in expected outputs. Additionally, relying solely on LLM-based emulation
results in drawbacks such as the absence oţÇĽreal-time data, having increased latency and ţÇĽully
interactive environments.
In this thesis, we examine the strengths and limitations oţÇĽLLM-powered honeypots and present
HybridHoney, a honeypot that adopts a hybrid approach, drawing on the strengths oţÇĽboth
traditional emulated honeypots and LLM honeypots.
Through user studies, we demonstrate

evaluating entire system interactions rather than isolated response replication, we establish a
higher benchmark ţÇĽor success, providing a much-needed reality check on the current state oţÇĽ
LLM-based honeypots. Ultimately, LLM-based honeypots still involve too many issues to be
viable, but using a hybrid approach to handle these problems could be a step in the right
direction.

The results oţÇĽthe user studies show challenges in both design and ţÇĽunctionality oţÇĽLLM-based

6.5.1

An issue observed with HybridHoney is its inability to handle commonly used applications in
