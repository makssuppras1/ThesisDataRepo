Title: Electrochemical properties of ceria-based thin-film electrodes for

Department: Energy Conversion and Storage

Abstract 
Development of thin films nano-porous electrodes with low ohmic resistance 
and high catalytic activity for intermediate temperature solid oxide cells can 
compensate the performance losses associated to lower the operating 
temperature. 
Symmetric cells with thin electrodes based on Ce0.9Gd0.1O1.95 (CGO) and a 
cermet CGO with 37 vol.% Ni (Ni-CGO) using (Y2O3)0.8(ZrO2)0.92 (8YSZ) as 
electrolyte were fabricated in order to investigate the effect of electrode 
composition, the electrode thickness and the use of Pt and Ni-YSZ as 
contacting layers in the performance of the electrodes. 
The deposition of 10, 20 and 30 thin films on the electrolyte was done using 
spin coating. The precursor solutions Ni-CGO and CGO were prepared and 
optimized by controlling their viscosity (∼80  mPa·s) and the surface tension 
(∼40 mNs-1). Drying and calcination of the thin electrodes was carried out using 
different thermal treatments for each case. The microstructure of the cells was 
analysed using SEM-EDS and HTXRD. The electrochemical characterization 
was studied through EIS using a thermal cycling profile between 650ºC and 
800ºC at reducing conditions H2:H2O 80:20. 
It was found that the electrochemical properties of the Ni-CGO and CGO thin 
electrodes are strongly linked to the microstructure, which in turn is 
dependent on the manufacturing parameters of the thin films. 
Extensive delamination of the electrode – electrolyte interface was observed in 
some of the cells. High densification of the CGO during the pre-sintering in air, 
TEC mismatch between the CGO and YSZ under reducing conditions and the 
use of two drying-calcination profiles during the fabrication of the electrodes 
might result in considerable mechanical stress along the electrode – electrolyte 
interface that might lead to structural damage. The porosity created during the 
reduction of the NiO probably somewhat decreased the excessive mechanical 
stress. 
In cells using Ni-CGO electrodes, serial resistance Rs, may be mainly 
attributable to the electrolyte since obtained Rs values (∼1.9 – 2.0 Ωcm2) are 
close to the calculated ohmic resistance of the 8YSZ (1.8 Ωcm2). Polarization 
resistances Rp, per electrode of 0.04 Ωcm2, 0.03 Ωcm2 and 0.04 Ωcm2 were 
obtained for the cells based on Ni-CGO electrodes with 10, 20 and 30 coatings 
respectively, Increments of 0.1 Ωcm2, 0.03 Ωcm2 and 0.01 Ωcm2 in Rp values 
were obtained after the EIS test, which last 128 hr. Thus, it was possible to 
observe a reciprocal relation between the thickness of the electrodes and the 
electrochemical performance of the cells, i.e. the polarization resistance 
decreases by increasing the thickness of the electrode.  
It is noteworthy that due to the scattered data obtained from the cells based on 
CGO electrodes, a reliable comparison between cells with CGO and Ni-CGO 
was not achieved. 
The main impedance contributors of the cells with Ni-CGO electrodes related 
to polarization resistance were found at 1) low frequencies between 1 kHz – 3 
kHz (concentration), which was associated with diffusion issues from the flow 
bulk to the electrode’s surface and 2) intermediate frequencies between 50 Hz – 
70 Hz (activation), which may be associated with hydrogen dissociation 
processes; however, additional experimental setups and impedance analysis 
are needed to define the processes. 
In cells with Ni-CGO electrodes, the use of Ni-YSZ as conducting layer 
provided similar results than using Pt; however, an slightly higher 
contribution in the arc of the concentration polarization was observed, which 
may indicate interferences in the gas diffusion due to coarsening of the Ni of 
the Ni-YSZ. In the case of the CGO electrodes, the impact of the Ni-YSZ is 
considerable, since Rp values per electrode increase around 0.04 Ωcm2 in 
comparison with CGO using Pt as conducting layer. 
Overall, the use of thin films of Ni-CGO with 37 vol. % Ni as electrodes 
provides good catalytic and electrochemical activity, low ohmic resistances and 
stability that could make it a subject for further studies. However, it is 
necessary to optimize the thin film fabrication process, in order to improve the 
microstructure of the electrodes and the electrode – electrolyte interface. 
Additional studies regarding the electrode thickness could allow determining 
the optimal thin film that compromises the thermal and microstructural 
stability of the electrodes and their electrochemical and catalytic properties.

1. Introduction and motivation 
1.1 Energy conversion and solid oxide fuel cells 
Long term energy security, environmental consequences caused by the use of fossil fuels 
and the expansion of energy access are important challenges facing both, developed and 
developing countries [1][2]. The use of renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar, is 
a feasible option to reduce the dependence on fossil fuels in the electricity generation sector, 
providing a diversification of energy sources. However, an intermittent output of these 
sources leads to concerns regarding the reliability in the electric power system [3]. 
Denmark has an electric system based on a high level of renewable electricity. Nowadays 
more than 20 % of the electricity is produced by wind power, but this ability relies on good 
inter-connection to the German and Nordic grids for back up and export [2]. Furthermore, 
Denmark’s Energy Strategy contemplates the decarbonisation of the entire energy sector 
and the independence from fossil fuels by 2050. This brings a number of significant 
challenges and opportunities to the electricity sector [4]. In the period 2035 – 2050, a 
radical conversion of the transport sector is expected, primarily to electricity, biofuels and 
hydrogen [5]. In this context, energy storage technologies are essential components for the 
integration of large amounts of variable energy sources in the energy systems [6], keeping a 
balance of energy supply and demand and equalizing the effect of the fluctuating renewable 
energy into the grid [2]. Using electrolyzers, renewable energy can be used to generate 
hydrogen or synthetic fuels during high electricity generation. Once the energy demand 
needs to be supplied, different technologies for reconversion of the fuel into electricity can 
be used [6]. A promising technology for this purpose is the so-called fuel cells. 
Fuel cells are promising electrochemical devices, in which the free energy of the oxidation 
reaction of hydrogen based fuels is converted directly into electricity [7][8]. In solid oxide 
fuel cells (SOFC) a solid electrolyte is used to conduct oxygen ions, O2- between two porous 
electrodes (anode and cathode). At the cathode, the O2, usually from the air, is reduced to 
produce O2- ions, which are transported through the ionically conducting electrolyte. In the 
anode, the ions O2- oxidise the hydrogen-containing fuel, releasing electrons to external 
circuits and generating heat, water and CO2 in case of using hydrocarbons as fuel. During 
this process, the operating temperature can reach up to 1000ºC [9]. Fig. 1.1 shows a 
schematic of SOFC and the chemical processes carried out in the electrodes.

During the last decades, SOFC technology has been under constant research to reach 
commercial purposes due to their high efficiency, low generation of pollutants, fuel 
flexibility and the ability to generate high-grade heat usable for other purposes [11][12]. 
Nowadays, SOFCs are focused in systems working in the region 700ºC and 750 ºC based on 
yttria-stabilized-zirconia (YSZ) electrolyte, a porous ceramic – metal composite (cermet) Ni-
YSZ anode and lanthanide-based perovskites cathode, where lanthanum strontium 
manganite (LSM) has been widely studied [7]. 
However, in terms of cost, reliability and long-term stability, SOFCs are still not 
competitive in comparison with other energy conversion technologies [13]. Working at high 
temperatures involves mechanical and chemical stability issues and the use of specialized 
materials. In addition, the Ni-YSZ anode is very susceptible to sulphur poisoning and 
carbon deposition, which segregates to the triple phase boundaries (TPBs) [14]. 
Intermediate temperature solid oxide fuel cells (IT-SOFC) operate at temperatures between 
800ºC and 500ºC [15], in order to minimize problems related to thermo – mechanical stress, 
chemical reactivity among the different components of the cells [16], as well as the 
possibility to use metals for the interconnects, which could reduce the cost of the materials 
and manufacturing of the cells, furthermore improving their reliability, portability, and 
operational life [17]. Nevertheless, one of the main challenges to overcome when the 
operating temperature is lowered is providing high electrochemical activity, as well as 
tolerance towards impurities [16]. Therefore, the development of new electrode materials, 
as well as manufacturing techniques that offer good compromise between the performance 
and stability of the cells, as well as economical feasibility are essential to commercialize the 
IT-SOFC. 
Ceria gadolinium oxide (CGO) has been of great interest for IT-SOFC due to its high ionic 
conductivity at intermediate temperatures and its oxidising catalytic activity and its mixed 
ionic-electronic 
conduction 
(MIEC) 
behaviour 
in

atmospheres 
[18][19]. 
Nevertheless, its electronic conductivity is low at IT-SOFC operating environment in 
comparison with electronic conductor materials. The electronic conductivity of CGO can be 
enhanced using a network of nickel in a cermet Ni-CGO, which also acts as catalyst for the 
fuel [20]. Due to the stronger chemical affinity between CeO2 and S than between Ni and S, 
the Ni-CGO tolerates high sulphur levels in the fuel [20]. In addition, like YSZ, the CGO 
acts as a matrix support and prevents the Ni particles agglomeration [20]. These features 
make the Ni-CGO cermet a feasible option as anode material in IT-SOFC. 
1.2 Thin film electrodes: use and manufacturing 
Incorporating thin film electrodes enhances the performance of SOFCs. Thin films 
components have lower ohmic resistance and compensate the losses associated with the 
decrease in the reaction kinetics caused by lowering the temperature, since the path for the 
oxygen ion diffusion decreases [21][22]. This technique, also allows lower processing 
temperatures of the components, easing the thermal compatibility of the films with the 
substrate [23]. Extensive research has been done in the use of Ni-CGO cermet as anode 
material in traditional cells (anode thickness >10 µm) [24][25]; however, not many studies 
have been carried out about the use of micron to sub-micron range thin film Ni-CGO anodes 
and little is known about the correlation between their microstructure and electrochemical 
performance [26]. 
Nowadays, several manufacturing methods are available to fabricate thin films; 
nevertheless, the selection of the manufacturing technique is based on the features of the 
final product, the nature of the used materials and the cost of the fabrication. 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
9 
Beckel et. al [21] lists different thin film deposition techniques for Solid Oxide Cells (SOC) 
in two groups: Vacuum deposition techniques, such as physical and chemical vapour 
deposition (PVD and CVD) and liquid-precursor-based thin film deposition techniques. 
Several vacuum depositions techniques have been studied for tailoring of thin electrodes 
Ni-CGO; however, these techniques can have a high production cost due to the specialized 
equipment and required conditions. Within the liquid-precursor-based techniques, spin 
coating is one of the most usual methods for applying uniform thin films on substrates over 
relatively large areas (diameters < 30 cm). This technique is quick, inexpensive and simple 
to produce films from few nanometres to few micrometres in thickness [27]. 
 
Fig. 1.2 Principle of the spin coating process. a) Initially the precursor solution is deposited on the 
substrate. b) The substrate is rotated until reaching the desired spin speed, ω. c) The centrifugal force 
created by ω removes the excess of solution. d) The evaporation of the solvent leaves a thin film of the 
material on the substrate surface [27]. Image borrowed from S.L. Hellstrom [29]. 
Fig. 1.2 shows a schematic of the spin coating process. It consists basically on spreading a 
thin axisymmetric film of a liquid, usually a polymeric material and solvent, on a flat 
rotating substrate at constant angular velocity. The rotation of the substrate at high speed 
creates a centrifugal force, which drives the liquid radial outward. During this process, 
most of the fluid is removed; however, the viscous force and surface tension retain a 
residual film on the surface of the substrate. Depending on the composition of the solution, 
the solvent evaporates partially or completely, leaving the desired material in an even 
covering [27][28]. 
Despite spin coating is a batch process and the usage of the material is typically less than 
10%, it has been widely used in different industrial and technological sectors, where 
manufacturing of semiconductors is one of the most important fields [27]. This 
demonstrates that production of thin electrodes for IT-SOFC using spin coating can be 
feasible and easily scalable. 
1.3 Measuring and analysis techniques 
In order to assess the performance of the different components of a fuel cell, different 
analysis techniques can be used and complemented by each other. Among many other 
techniques, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), scanning electron microscopy - 
energy dispersive spectroscopy (SEM-EDS) and high temperature X-ray diffraction 
(HTXRD) are some of the most important for the analysis of SOFCs.  
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
 
“Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy is a basic analysis technique to study the 
electrochemical performance of the fuel cells and it is used to characterize electrode 
processes and complex interfaces” [30]. This method studies the system response to the 
application of a periodic small-amplitude AC potential. The measurements are carried out 
at different AC frequencies that simulate a range of electrode reaction rates providing 
information about the interface, its structure and the reaction that takes place [30][31]. 
Thus, EIS has been a useful tool to determine not only the total loss in the performance but 
also to identify the different resistance contributions of the components of the fuel cells 
[32].  
 
Fig. 1.3 Basic analysis techniques to determine the performance of the components of a solid oxide fuel cell. Each 
technique provides information that is complemented by others.  
It is known that performance of the cells is strongly related to the morphology of the micro 
and nano structures of the electrodes and electrolyte of the cell, as well as to the possible 
micro-failures that are not possible to be observed at macro-scale. SEM is fundamental in 
the development of SOFC technology. SEM is a method for high-resolution imaging of 
surfaces that makes it possible to obtain micrographs with more than 100,000 
magnifications and depth of field of around 100 times that obtained with an optical 
microscope. EDS can be attached to the SEM to carry out qualitative and quantitative 
chemical analysis of features or phases to obtain the elemental composition of the specimen 
[33]. 
HTXRD is an in-situ analysis technique to study the structural changes of materials under 
specific conditions that can simulate real operating conditions of atmosphere and 
temperature. An X-ray diffractometer equipped with a high temperature chamber is used to 
record the diffraction patterns. Using this technique, information of thermal behaviour, 
crystallization processes and thermal expansion-contraction of the SOFC can be obtained 
and analysed. 
It is noteworthy that the results obtained from the above-described basic measuring 
techniques are complementary of each other to provide a more complete overview of the 
performance of the SOFC’s components. However, additional tests and analysis methods 
can be used, in order to explain the complex behaviour of the SOFCs components.

1.4 Motivation and aims of this work 
The motivation to carry out this master thesis project came from the necessity to improve 
the performance and stability of the materials used in IT-SOFC, considering simple and 
scalable manufacturing methods, as well as use of economically feasible materials, in order 
to develop this technology for commercial purposes. 
Graves and Sudireddy obtained polarization resistances values of 0.01 Ωcm2 at 650ºC using 
thin film electrodes based on CGO prepared by spin coating. However, low electronic 
conductivity limited the performance of the electrodes. Thus, the addition of Ni as current 
collector could improve the electronic conductivity and the performance of the electrode. 
The aim is to fabricate thin film electrodes based on Ce0.9Gd0.1O1.95 (herein referred to as 
CGO) and a cermet CGO with 37 vol.% Ni load (herein referred to as Ni-CGO), in order to 
investigate the effect of electrode composition, the electrode thickness and microstructure 
on the electrochemical performance. Fabrication of symmetric cells was done using 
(Y2O3)0.8(ZrO2)0.92 – 8YSZ – as electrolyte and Ni-CGO and CGO as thin electrodes. In order 
to characterise the electrochemical performance and the microstructure of the symmetric 
cells, EIS and SEM techniques are considered to be used. The evolution of the CGO 
formation and the reduction of NiO to Ni are studied using HTXRD. It is attempted to 
analyse the effect of electrode thickness, stability under thermal cycling and the influence 
of different current collectors, such as Pt and Ni. 
Initially, it was planned to fabricate Ni-CGO electrodes using both: spin coating and 
templating. However, only spin coating was studied, since the project work was delayed due 
to unforeseen technical issues during the precursor solutions preparation and on-going 
implementation of laboratory safety regulations, which restricted the availability of the 
required furnaces for sample preparation. Furthermore, technical malfunctions of EIS 
testing equipment delayed the work and the restricted availability of SUPRA35 Field 
Emission SEM microscope limited a complete study of the electrodes’ microstructure. 
However, SEM analysis allowed relating the scattered EIS data with the microstructure of 
the cells, since extensive mechanical failures across all the cells were observed in all the 
tested samples.

2. Theoretical concepts 
In this chapter some basic concepts concerning the materials and analysis techniques used 
in this study are reviewed. An initial introduction regarding some properties of the 
gadolinium doped ceria (CGO) and the cermet Nickel-CGO (Ni-CGO) is discussed. 
Following, the spin coating process, which includes dispense techniques, rotational speed, 
drawbacks and advantages is described. A brief introduction behind the basics of the main 
measuring techniques used in this work is shown. Basic concepts of Electrochemical 
Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and X-ray Diffraction 
(XRD) are described.  
2.1 Ni-CGO as material for Solid Oxide Fuel Cells. 
Cerium oxide (CeO2), or usually called ceria, has found numerous applications in different 
fields. Cerium oxide is a highly stable, nontoxic, refractory ceramic material with a melting 
point of 2600ºC and a density of 7.13 g/cm3. It has a cubic centred face crystal structure 
with a lattice constant of 5.11 Å and where Ce+4 is 8 and the O-2 is 4 coordinated [34]. It 
does not undergo any crystallographic transition from room temperature to its melting 
point in air [35]. 
 
Fig. 2.1. Model of the CeO2 showing the possible oxygen vacancies [34]. 
The mechanical properties of pure ceria ceramics are usually rather poor; however, as an 
ionic conductor ceria has a good performance, which is increased doping it with various rare 
earth elements, as Gadolinium (Gd+3), whereby the negative charge of the acceptor with 
respect to the host cation (Ce+4) is accommodated by the formation of oxygen vacancies 
[34][36], in order to keep the charge compensation. For each mole of Gd+3 incorporated into 
the ceria lattice, the charge neutrality is kept by forming ½ mol of oxygen vacancies.

In order to improve the mechanical stability of the CGO electrode, it is favourable to

Ceria doped with rare earth elements shows a higher ionic conductivity than does stabilized 
ZrO2 [34], especially at a temperature range of 500ºC – 800ºC, which allows its use in 
Intermediate Temperature Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (ITSOFC). Several advantages when the 
SOFCs are operated at an intermediate temperature are observed, such as lower 
degradation rate of cell performance, higher theoretical voltage, and lower fabrication costs 
through the use of metallic components [39]. 
The electronic conductivity of the CGO in reducing environments avoids its use as 
electrolyte in ITSOFC but allows its application as anode material due to its mixed ionic 
electronic conductor (MIEC) properties, nevertheless, the electronic conductivity is low. 
Then the use of a cermet (ceramic – metal composites) with Ni as metallic phase, overcomes 
this drawback. 
Ni – Ce0.9Gd0.1O1.95 (Ni-CGO) is widely used as anode material. Ni provides the electronic 
conductivity and shows catalytic activity for the fuel oxidation, whereas CGO acts as porous 
matrix to support the Ni, preventing its agglomeration during sintering and operation of 
the cell [20]. It also transfers the oxygen ions to the reaction site to complete the catalytic 
cycle [19]. 
A property of the Ni-CGO is its tolerance to high sulphur levels in the fuel, due to the 
better affinity of the CeO2 – S, instead the Ni – S [20]. This could allow the use of the Ni-
CGO in SOFCs using hydrocarbon fuels, since CeO2 also improves the catalytic activity 
towards hydrocarbons [17]. 
The electrochemical reactions occur in a limited active area where the fuel, the electronic 
conductor (Ni) and the ionic conductor (GCO) meet together. This area, called triple phase 
boundary (TPB). is critically dependant on the microstructure, especially grain size, 
porosity, surface area, tortuosity and connectivity [20]. Thus, optimizing the TPB becomes 
an important factor to improve the anode performance [17]. 
It is known that fine Ni and oxide ion conducting ceramic particles yield better electrical 
and electrochemical anodic performance than coarse particles due to high density of Ni – 
CGO contact areas. In the cermet Ni-CGO the coarsening of the Ni grains due to sintering, 
is one of the major problems that reduces the TPB area and in consequence a loss of 
performance in the cell will be observed [20]. Thus, it is important to extent the length of 
the TPB by tailoring the anode morphology through favourable manufacturing process of 
the electrodes [17]. 
Incorporating Ni-CGO thin film electrodes reduces the ohmic resistance [21] improves the 
ionic conductivity and compensates the losses associated with the decrease in the reaction 
kinetics caused by lowering the temperature, since the path for the oxygen ion diffusion is 
decreased [22] Coarsening and segregation of the Ni to the electrode surface is also 
diminished because smaller grains reduce the total surface energy of the Ni [23]. 
Nevertheless, the electrical conductivity of the CeO2 based materials is affected by the 
space charge effect. If the grain size approaches to the nano-metric scale, the electronic 
component of the conductivity will dominate the charge carriers.

2.2 Thin film deposition. 
Spin coating is a widespread technique for applying uniform thin films on flat substrates. 
The deposition of a liquid is placed in a substrate that rotates at high speed. The 
centrifugal forces drive the liquid outward. The viscosity and the surface tension of the 
liquid allow the formation of a thin residual film on the substrate [27]. The final thickness 
of the thin film depends on the liquid properties (viscosity, drying rate, surface tension, 
solution concentration and density) and the parameters of the spin process (rotational 
speed, acceleration, spinning time) [28]. Because of the different processing parameters, as 
well as the liquid’s rheology and evaporation process, the development of a mathematical 
model for spin coating has been complex [40]. 
2.2.1 
Spin coating. 
The spin coating process can be split in four different stages: (1) Dispense of the liquid, (2) 
spin up (3) fluid flow dominated thinning and (4) spin off and solvent evaporation [28]. 
Stages 3 and 4 are carried out simultaneously and have the most impact in the coating 
thickness [27]. Fig.1.2 shows the different stages of the spin coating. 
During dispense, the liquid is deposited on the substrate, which is immediately wetted. Due 
to the centrifugal force the liquid is spread on the entire substrate until the thin film is 
reduced to its critical height [28][41]. Two main dispensing methods can be used, where its 
use depends on the nature of the solutions. 1) Dynamic dispensing, where the substrate is 
already spinning at low rotational speed during the application of the liquid. 2) Static 
dispensing, where the liquid is applied to the substrate before spinning. The first method is 
usually used for solutions where the solvent has a high evaporation rate, in order to avoid 
the deposition of solids in specific locations, which would create irregularities on the thin 
film. Static dispensing is usually used for solutions with high vapour pressure liquids [27]. 
Spinning up is carried out accelerating the substrate using a ramping rate until reaching 
the final rotational speed. The ramping rate can impact the final coating thickness and its 
uniformity.

X-ray diffraction (XRD) is a technique that relies on constructive interference of 
monochromatic X-rays and a crystalline sample when Bragg’s Law is satisfied [56].

Converting the diffraction peaks to inter-planar distances dhkl allows the identification of 
the crystal, since each crystal has a unique set of inter-planar distances.  This is usually 
done by comparison with standards. [56]. 
The X-rays are generated by a cathode ray tube, filtered to produce monochromatic 
radiation, collimated to concentrate, and directed toward the sample [56]. Wavelengths of 
the X-rays used in crystallography vary in the interval 0.5 – 2.0 Å [57]. 
In-situ X-Ray Diffraction is an analysis technique that allows to analyse the formation, 
growth and phase transition of crystalline materials in real time and under specific 
conditions of atmosphere composition and temperature. An X-ray diffractometer equipped 
with a high temperature chamber is used to record the diffraction patterns. Using this 
technique, information of thermal behaviour, crystallization processes and thermal 
expansion-contraction of the specimen can be obtained and analysed. 
2.4.2 
Diffraction-line broadening 
Diffraction lines of crystalline materials contain vast microstructural quantitative 
information, such as crystallite size, crystallographic orientation, and distribution of phases 
among many others. Relative ratios peak highs are used as a measure of qualitative phase 
while more sophisticated techniques are required for quantitative phase analysis. However, 
if the sample is polycrystalline material, the line overlapping can be severe, making 
difficult an accurate analysis of some reflections [58][59]. In addition, sources of peak 
broadening, such as dislocations, micro-stress and grain boundaries can also make more 
challenging the analysis [60]. 
Several methods based in analytical functions have been developed for the structure 
refinement of diffraction data. Two approaches will be used in this work: Whole Profile 
Pattern Decomposition (WPPD) and Halder – Wagner approximation.

When a material is bombarded with high-energy electrons, an emission of characteristic X-
ray correspondent to the material occurs. The characteristic X-rays results when the X-rays 
beam ejects inner shell electrons (K-shell) of the specimen atoms. An electron from an outer 
shell subsequently fills the hole in the K-shell. The energy released by the outer shell 
electron is emitted as a characteristic X-rays quantum with specific energy, which is 
characteristic of the specimen atomic number from which it is derived. Measuring the 
energy of the emitted X-rays provides a direct method of elemental identification [67][70]. 
Figure 2.14 shows a sketch of the interaction between the electron beam and the generation

In electron microscopy the technique that uses this principle is called Energy Dispersive 
Spectroscopy (EDS) and it allows determining the composition and relative proportions in 
at.% of a sample. 
Different events can occur during the characteristic X-ray generation. Depending on the 
outer electron that fills the gap in the inner shell, characteristic X-rays will be produced. It 
is usual to use the Siegbahn notation to name the different events. The nomenclature is 
based on denoting the electron shell that was ionized to produce the X-ray (K, L, M) 
followed by a component that gives the relative intensity of the line within each cell, α, β, γ, 
where α is the most intense line. E.g. Kα, is the event with more ionization energy [67][69].

3.2.2 
Spin Coating 
Spin processor 
The used spin coater is intended for spin coating of thin films from solutions and other 
dispersions. It provides a maximum rotational speed of 12,000 rpm (revolutions per minute) 
with controllable increments of 1 rpm and can hold wafers of up to 200 mm of diameter. 
The spin processor is designed to run in ambient temperatures between 20ºC and 24ºC [79]. 
Vacuum source with ≥15 mmHg is needed to hold down the wafers during the thin film 
deposition. A vacuum interlock assures that vacuum is activated before any program can 
run and it will not disengage until the wafer stops totally. The spinning processor requires 
at least 4.13 to 4.83 bar of clean dry air (CDA) or N2 with consumption of 0.085 m3h-1 to 
pressurize the motor seal. If the CDA pressure is below 4.13 bar, the processor will not 
operate. The purpose of the motor seal is to separate the process chamber from the motor 
and electronics, so as to extend the lifetime of the device [79]. 
The spin processor is provided with a table – top control panel, where different coating 
programs can be stored. The control panel can be programmed on-site or using a PC. 
During the experimental work, different programs of rotational speed and spinning time 
were used to get a uniform thin film of the precursor solution on the surface of the studied 
substrate [79]. 
Formation of films: Rotational speed and spinning time 
Rotational speed and spinning time are important parameters, since they determine the 
thickness and the uniformity of the film. Previous works of Plonczak [72] and Wang [74] 
were used as reference to define a range of rotational speed (2000 – 3000 rpm) and spinning 
time (1 min). Different rotational speeds of 2000, 2500 and 3000 rpm, as well as spinning 
times of 15 seconds and 1 minute were tested during the experimental work. 
In order to study the impact of the thickness in the performance of the cells, symmetric 
cells with 10, 20 and 30 spin-coated cycles were fabricated. Two 8YSZ tapes were used in 
each case. (Sc2O3)0.1(Y2O3)0.01(ZrO2)0.89 – 10Sc1YSZ – tapes with irregular geometry, area in 
the region of 4 cm2 and thickness of 160 µm were used as substrate for film deposition tests 
using different Ni-CGO precursor solutions. Rotational speed and spinning time were 
defined for the 10Sc1YSZ substrates; nevertheless, once the 8YSZ substrate was used for 
film depositions, the defined parameters for the 10Sc1YSZ changed and new tests were 
necessary. The 8YSZ tapes used for the spin coating were 2.5 cm x 2.5 cm and 200 µm 
thick. Due to their size, it was necessary to use a glass of 10 cm x 10 cm and tape to support 
the 8YSZ substrate during the spin coating process. A static dispense of solution was used 
since evaporation of the solvent before the start of the spinning is not critical because 
ethylene glycol is used as main solvent; nevertheless, the use of dynamic dispense can also 
be considered. Approximately 3 seconds between dispense of the solution on the 8YSZ 
surface and the spinning-up of the substrate were taken. 
It is worth pointing out that this work did not intend to optimize the spin coating process 
but only to create the thin films required for this study. Only spinning time and rotational 
speed were roughly studied; nevertheless, parameters such as acceleration and slowdown in 
the rotational speed, environment’s moisture, among other factors were not considered. 
3.2.3 
Heat treatment 
Drying and calcination are the final step to form the coated film on the electrolyte 
substrate. Spin coating and heat treatment of CGO solutions have been widely studied and 
different fabrication methodologies can be found. Plonczak et al. describes three different 
heat treatment procedures for the CGO, involving sequential heating [72], gradual heating 
[80], and two-step temperature treatment [81]. 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
37 
The heat treatment method using gradual heating at 300ºC for 2 hours [80] was used as 
reference; however, reducing the time of heat treatment per cycle was necessary due to the 
limited time. Heat treatment at 350ºC for 30 minutes was applied in each cycle to fix the 
precursors to the substrate’s surface and then, after all the necessary cycles, heat 
treatment using 350ºC for 2 hours was carried out, in order to degrade all the nitrate 
precursors. During the experimental work, the first 10 coating cycles with CGO precursor 
solutions were done setting the samples in the furnace at 350ºC for 2h then cooling down at 
room temperature before the deposition of the next thin film. The followed 9 cycles were 
carried out using 350ºC for 30 minutes and for the 20th cycle 350ºC were applied for two 
hours. 
In the case of the Ni-CGO samples, the heat treatment profile is described by Wang et al. 
[74], where the spin coated substrate is dried at 200ºC for 15 min, followed by a calcination 
at 600ºC for 5 minutes. It was intended to follow this procedure for all the cycle coatings of 
the Ni-CGO; however, due to on-going implementation of laboratory safety regulations was 
not possible to carry out this process as described. 
Initially two chamber furnaces were used for the heat treatment. One of them set at 300ºC, 
and the second one at 200ºC. After deposition of the spin coated film, the samples were 
placed in the furnace at 200ºC for 15 minutes for drying of the film, then transferred to the 
furnace at 300ºC, which was warmed up till 600ºC with a ramp of 180ºC/h for the 
calcination. Afterwards, the temperature was decreased with the same ramp until reaching 
300ºC. Finally the sample was cooled down at room temperature. 
Following this procedure only one cycle per day was achieved. 10 cycles were done following 
this method. The next cycles were done in a vertical tubular furnace, which allowed 
following the procedure described by Wang et al.; however, an additional step was included 
in the reference procedure. After the treatment at 600ºC, the cells were transferred to a 
furnace at 200ºC for 10 minutes, in order to avoid a severe thermal stress on the samples. 
This procedure provided uniform and smooth films with complete coverage on the 
electrolyte’s surface. 
It is worth to point out that the manufacturing method of the CGO with high loads of NiO 
has not been studied as deeply as the CGO. A few additional trials were carried out, in 
order to reduce the number of manufacturing steps and to match the conditions of the 
initial heat treatment used with the CGO (350ºC for 2 hours), however, the results were not 
satisfactory. 
3.2.4 
Measurement of the viscosity 
Several hourly samples of the precursor solutions were taken during the preparation, in 
order to monitor the viscosity. A rheometer with a stainless steel parallel plate of 50 mm 
diameter (PP50) was used to take the measurements. The gap between the PP50 and the 
rheometer’s fixed plate was set at 0.6 mm. Approximately 1.5 ml of solution was poured on 
the centre of the fixed plate and then the PP50 was lowered to the set gap distance. The 
excess solution was removed from the edges of the plates using a plastic spatula, in order to 
avoid possible distortions on the measurements. 
Viscosity was measured in a shear rate interval between 1 s-1 to 100 s-1, taking 45 points 
per measurement and 10 s of delay between each point, in order to allow the stabilization of 
the conditions. The used measuring method to determine the viscosity during the 
preparation of the solutions was the FFT (Fast Fourier Transformed) Smoothing.

Measurement of the surface tension

3.4 Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) 
In this work, backscatter electron detectors were used to obtain information of cross section 
samples, in order to define and link the different results obtained during the EIS tests with 
the microstructure and morphology of the cells. Both samples, embedded in epoxy and 
fractured, were analysed. 
The sample preparation was carried out prior to the SEM experimental session. Sample 
were embedded in epoxy, polished metallographically and coated with carbon to provide 
electrical conductivity. The samples were used to obtain information regarding the 
compositional contrast of the different phases and the failures of each layer. 
Two different electronic microscopes were used during the experimental work. A Hitachi 
TM3000, working with a backscattered electron detector and accelerating voltage of 15 kV, 
was used to obtain initial micrographs of the samples, in order to identify specific features 
for further studies. Micrographs with magnifications at x300, x1000, x3000, x10000 and 
x25000 were analysed. 
To identify the material of each layer of the cell, EDS was performed at different locations 
of the layers. The atomic percentage of Ce, Gd and Ni was obtained and compared with the 
nominal composition of the CGO and the expected content of Ni after reduction of the NiO. 
A FESEM was used to obtain information regarding the microstructure of the electrodes 
and study the specific features observed in the TM3000. The FESEM was set at an 
accelerating voltage of 15 kV. Micrographs of the electrodes with image’s widths of 400 µm, 
10 µm, 4 µm and 2 µm were taken at three different locations of each cell, in order to obtain 
details regarding morphology, porosity and grain size. Only EIS and HTXRD tested cells 
with 20 coatings were analysed using the FESEM. 
Due to the structure of the cell with thin electrodes, it was not possible to obtain 
micrographs using In-Lens at a voltage of 1 kV to analyse the percolation of the Ni on the 
electrode material. The thin electrodes have thicknesses of around 1 µm. Because of this, in 
embedded cross section samples, the electrode’s edge and interface electrode – electrolyte 
==PAGE:42==
 
42 
 
are very close to the epoxy resin, which tends to charge after only few minutes due to the 
interaction of the electron beam of the microscope. This creates distorted images with very 
high brightness.  
The limited availability of the FESEM allowed only the analysis of the 4 cells of Test 2 and 
the cells tested in the HTXRD analysis. The rest of the cells were analysed using the 
TM3000 as an alternative option; nevertheless, it is important to mention that TM3000 
does not provide detailed information of the microstructure. EDS was carried out on the

3.5 High temperature X-ray diffraction (HTXRD) 
HTXRD was performed to define the evolution in the crystalline structure of the CGO and 
the Ni formation at high temperature and reducing atmosphere. The tests were carried out 
on two symmetric cells, Ni-CGO and CGO, both with 20 spin coated cycles. 
A high temperature non-ambient chamber attached to the XRD was used to perform the 
analysis. In order to provide similar operation conditions than the EIS tests. A mixture of 9 
vol.% H2:N2 humidified at room temperature to provide approximately 2 – 3  vol.% H2O, 
was fed to the non-ambient chamber. 
The temperature profile simulated the reducing conditions of the EIS test. Thus, the 
samples were exposed to the H2:N2:H2O atmosphere at 650ºC for 1 hour to reduce the NiO 
to Ni and afterwards, the measurements were carried out at 700ºC, 750ºC 800ºC for periods 
of approximately 2 hours in each dwell. A final measurement was done at 650ºC.  
The XRD patterns were obtained varying 2θ, where θ is the angle of incidence of the X-ray 
beam on the cell’s surface, in the interval from 25º to 80º with a step of 0.019º, counting 
2823 steps for the complete interval. The used voltage was 40 kV and a current of 40 mA.

4. Results and discussion 
In this chapter, the results of the preparation of the precursor solutions and fabrication of 
the cells, as well as their characterization are shown and discussed. 
Initially, data obtained during the viscosity monitoring and the surface tension of the final 
precursor solutions for spin coating are presented and assessed. The viscosities and surface 
tensions of the final precursor solutions are 78 mPa·s and 85 mPa·s and 33 mNm-1 and 38 
mNm-1 for the Ni-CGO and CGO respectively. Average thickness of the electrodes of 350 
nm (±11%), 680 nm (±29%), 830 nm (±13%), were obtained for the cells with 10, 20 and 30 
coatings of Ni-CGO and an average thickness of 700 nm (±28%) was obtained for the CGO 
electrodes with 20 coatings. 
The results of the characterization are shown and discussed. Initially, results of HTXRD 
showed that the formation of the CGO cubic structure is already completed at 650ºC, while 
the reduction of the NiO to Ni is still registered at 800ºC at reducing conditions given by a 
mix of 9 vol.% H2:N2 humidified at approximately 2 – 3 vol.% H2O. The relation between the 
crystallite size of the CGO and the temperature is discussed.  
SEM micrographs revealed an extensive structural damage in the electrode – electrolyte 
interface. Serious delamination of the electrode material, as well as some fractures in the 
electrolyte were observed in all the tested samples under a thermal cycling profile (EIS and 
XRD). The electrodes based on CGO presented high densification of the material, while the 
Ni-CGO based electrodes show porosity in their structure. Some possible causes of the 
structural damage are discussed. Structural stressed caused by the mismatch in thermal 
expansion coefficient (TEC) of the materials, chemical expansion of the CGO, porosity of the 
electrodes and possible damage during the handling of the cells are some of the possible 
causes related to the delamination of the electrodes. 
EIS analysis showed that three main impedance contributors might be involved in the 
performance of the cells based on Ni-CGO. However, scattered data of the cells based on 
CGO electrodes was obtained during the experimental work, making the analysis and 
comparison of these cells difficult. The relation between the values of Rs and the structural 
failures of the electrodes is discussed, as well as the relation of the thickness of the 
electrodes and their performance, which may follow the “thickness effect” described by 
Kenjo et at.

Triton X100 accelerated the polymerization process, since the CGO precursor solution

Rheological measurements were carried out in the final solutions, in order to identify their 
nature. Fig. 4.2 shows the flow curve measurements of the final precursor solution Ni-CGO. 
It is recognizable that the solution present Newtonian behaviour, since the relation 
between the shear rate and the shear stress is linear. A period of stabilization of the 
solution is observed in the shear rate between 0 and 10 s-1. [28][83]. 
According to Huang et at. [84] and Sahu et al. [28], it is usual that the fluids used in spin 
coating have a Newtonian viscosity, in order to allows the formation of uniform film 
thickness on a flat substrate. If non-Newtonian materials are used, it is likely to have 
defects on the films during the spin coating deposition [85]. 
Despite the Newtonian behaviour, the high surface tension of the precursor solutions did 
not allow the formation of uniform thin films and the use of an organic surfactant, Triton 
X100, was necessary.

4.3 Formation of precursor thin films on the substrate 
Several tests of film formation varying the rotational speed and the spinning time were 
made using the different prepared precursor solutions. Initially, pieces of approximately 4 
cm2 of 10Sc1YSZ were used as test substrate and later tests using tapes of 5 cm x 5 cm of 
8YSZ were used to verify the correct coverage of the film on larger surfaces. The final 
substrate to be coated was the 8YSZ electrolyte. After several attempts, it was obtained 
that rotational speed of 2000 rpm for 1 min of spinning time provided the best cover for 
both final precursor solutions. 
==PAGE:47==
 
 
47 
After successful correct formation of even films, heat treatment was carried out based on 
literature references. In the case of the CGO spin coated cells a methodology previously 
used by Plonczak et al. [72] was applied. By drying at 350ºC for two hours, a correct film 
was formed on the surface of the electrolyte without shrinkage, delamination or any other 
kind of perceptible anomaly. However, in order to speed up the process, the drying time was 
reduced to 30 min at the same temperature, with a final cycle of 2h as described in chapter 
3. The formation of the oxide films was successful using this procedure. 
In the case of the Ni-CGO spin coated films, initial tests were carried depositing the thin 
film, followed by drying at 200ºC for 15 minutes. The first cycle produced a uniform thin 
film; however, after the second and third deposition and drying, dragged precursor material 
was observed close to the substrate edges due to the centrifugal force of the spin coating 
process and because the precursor material was not totally dried and adhered to the 
substrate’s surface. 
Some tests were carried out using the drying conditions for the CGO cells (350ºC for two 
hours). The first layer of Ni-CGO dried properly; nevertheless, after the second layer, the 
formation of lose precursor’s powder was observed on the surface of the electrolyte. It may 
possible that the poor coalescence of the NiO particles at the drying temperatures cause 
that effect.  
Finally, the method described by Wang et al. [74] was used for the fabrication of the Ni-
CGO films. This procedure provided uniform and smooth films with complete coverage on 
the electrolyte’s surface. 
A summary that shows different parameters of all the prepared and tested solutions, which 
includes viscosity, surface tension, spinning time, rotational speed, as well as heat 
treatment, is shown in Table 4.2 and Table 4.3.  
It was necessary to control several parameters to obtain films with the necessary 
characteristics. Solutions with high viscosity (∼800 mPa·s) tend to form thick films; 
however, during the drying process, delamination, cracks and loss of the material was 
observed. On the other hand, the formation of very thin films due to higher rotational 
speeds or spinning time, as well as low viscosities can lead in dewetting of the film or 
several cycles would be needed to reach a specific electrode thickness.

From Table 4.4 and Table 4.5, it is noticed that the crystallite size increases by increasing 
the temperature, which is a phenomenon related to the thermally induced grain 
growth/coarsening. [91]. Levy et al. [92] has published that CGO crystallites have a slow 
growth between 215ºC and 700ºC using an air stream; nevertheless above 700ºC the 
crystallite size increase rapidly with increased temperature. In their work, Levy et al. 
reported a growth in the crystallite size of CGO from 9 to 22 nm between 500ºC and 700ºC 
with a fast increase to 81 nm at 1000ºC. This behaviour is also observed in the cell 
20_CGONi_XRD, where during the initial step from 650ºC to 700ºC an increase <30 nm is 
observed; however, in the next steps between 700ºC – 750ºC and 750ºC – 800ºC, a difference 
of about 60 nm was noticed. 
The growth of the crystallite size might increase also the grain size, which is also related to 
the electrochemical activity. Muecke et al. [93] have studied the behaviour of thin film Ni-
CGO anodes and defined that small grains provide lower polarization resistance. The 
increase in the size of the CGO might be related to the decrease in the performance of the 
studied cells in this work during the EIS thermal cycling profile, which is analysed in the 
section 4.6.  
The crystallite size is also affected by the processing method of the electrodes [94], such as 
firing temperature or fabrication techniques, as well as the addition of other components in 
the electrode [92]. Comparing the data between Table 4.4 and Table 4.5 it is noticed that 
the cell 20_CGONi_XRD present smaller crystallite sizes than the cell 20_CGO_XRD. It is 
known that in a cermet Ni-CGO, the nickel provides the electronic conductivity and 
catalytic activity, while the CGO works as support for the Ni and prevents its coarsening 
[20]. However, the obtained results may suggest that the Ni might have an effect in the 
stabilization of the crystallite size of the CGO.  
Muecke et al. [95] have developed studies on thin films Ni-CGO with different 
concentrations. They demonstrated that the introduction of NiO as secondary phase into 
the CGO hinders its grain growth. Thus, the grain size of the CGO decreases with 
increasing the NiO. 
In addition, it is worth pointing out that there is a relation between the thickness of the 
electrodes and the crystallite size. This analysis was not carried out in the experimental 
work, since only the cells with 20 coatings based on electrodes Ni-CGO and CGO were 
analysed. However, works of Sakaliuniene et al. [91] and Baqué et al. [96] have 
demonstrated that the crystallite size decrease by increasing the number of layers of the 
thin film.

Van Gestel et al. [101] deposited CGO thin films as anode material on 8YSZ electrolyte via 
physical vapour deposition (PVD). Pre-sintering of the cells was carried out at 1000ºC and 
1400ºC followed by reduction of the anodes at 900ºC using 4 vol.% H2:Ar. 
In the case of the cells pre-sintered at 1400ºC, some transversal cracks were found; 
however, the cells pre-sintered at 1000ºC showed serious delamination of the electrode 
layers. In addition, Kaiser et al. [102] observed that CGO fabricated under reducing 
conditions leads to excessive grain growth and cracks along the grains. 
Taking as reference the results of Van Gestel et al. and Kaiser at al. and comparing them 
with the obtained results in this work, it may be possible that pre-sintering temperature, as 
well as the reduction atmosphere affected the electrode material, weakening the electrode – 
electrolyte interface and causing the delamination of the electrode layers. 
Under reducing atmosphere and at elevated temperatures, the Ce+4 reduces to Ce+3, 
resulting in higher oxygen deficiency and n-type conductivity; however, an expansion in the 
lattice of the CGO is also observed due to formation of lattice defects [34][97]. This 
expansion of the CGO and the difference in the TEC between the 8YSZ and CGO may 
induce significant mechanical stress in the electrode – electrolyte interface. 
 
As it was noticed, the cell 20_CGO_01 presents the most extensive damage along the 
electrodes in comparison with the Ni-CGO based cells. The high densification of the 
electrode layers may be related to this failure. After reduction, the cells based on Ni-CGO 
have some porosity that may allow absorbing to some degree the stress created by the 
expansion of the CGO by reduction conditions and TEC mismatch. On the other hand, the 
cells based on CGO do not have such degree of freedom, which may create more stress in 
the bulk material and along the electrode – electrolyte interface, leading to delamination of 
the electrodes. 
In the case of the cells based on Ni-CGO with 10 coatings, the structural stability of the 
electrodes may be related to the fabrication method of the cells, since only one single heat 
treatment was used for the coating formation. Thus, no internal interfaces are observed, 
which may create a more stable electrode. 
In addition, studies developed by Fang and Lo [103] have demonstrated that in some 
materials the effect of the TEC mismatch tends to decrease in thin films in comparison with 
the effect of the TEC mismatch observed in bulk materials. The thinner the electrode the 
more stress it can take before it delaminates, due to the fact that the energy released upon 
delamination scales with the volume of the strained layer. It might be possible that this 
phenomenon occurs with the TEC mismatch between the CGO and the 8YSZ. 
In summary, the mismatch of the TEC, the expansion of the CGO lattice due to reducing 
conditions and the difference of the fabrication of the cells during the drying/calcination 
could be related to the structural failures observed in the tested cells.

4.6 Electrochemical performance 
EIS was used to study the electrochemical performance of the symmetric cells. Three 
different tests were done to analyse the impact of the electrode thickness, the stability of 
the cells and the influence of the current collector on the performance of the cells. The cells 
were treated under a thermal cycling profile, as described in the Chapter 3. The main

Fig. 4.15 shows the Bode plot of the cells 10_CGONi_03, 20_CGONi_04 and 30_CGONi_03 
when they are operating at peak temperatures of 700ºC, 750ºC and 800ºC. Data at the 
beginning of the ST1 at 650ºC is shown as comparison. It is noticed that the contribution 
previously identified in the range 1 kHz – 3 kHz tends to decreases in magnitude of Z” and 
in frequency with increasing the temperature in all the cases. Thus it is assumed that this 
contribution is related to the kinetics of the reactions. 
In contrast, the contribution found in the range 50 Hz – 70 Hz remains almost constant in 
the cells 20_CGONi_04 and 30_CGONi_03 after the thermal cycling profile, which indicates 
that this process might be less dependent of the temperature and it is not related to the 
activation of the electrodes. This suggests that this contribution may be related to 
concentration polarization processes of the electrodes. In the case of the cell 10_CGONi_03, 
the contribution in the interval 50 Hz – 70 Hz has another behaviour, since the impedance 
related to the concentration processes increases by increasing the temperature. The cells 
10_CGONi_03 and 10_CGONi_04 show the same characteristics. Although all the 
electrodes of the EIS tested cells have thicknesses below 1 µm, it may be possible that 
temperature have an influence on the microstructure of very thin the electrodes (∼350 nm 
for 10 coating electrodes), affecting the performance of the cell. 
Nielsen et al. [13] have described that in infiltrated system with Ni-CGO20, the arc in 
middle frequencies is attributable to the electrochemistry of the electrode reactions and the 
arc at low frequencies is related to the gas composition. This reinforces the assumptions of 
the kinetics and the concentration polarization as contributors of the arcs in intermediate 
frequencies and low frequencies respectively. 
It is noteworthy that more detailed analysis can be done in future studies, in order to 
determine with precision the frequency of the different contributions related to the 
polarization resistance, as well as identify the related processes. 
It is know that in low impedance systems, such as SOFC, the parasitic inductance can 
affect the impedance data, especially at high frequencies. Such effect is originated by the 
setup and configuration of the measurement cell, including the cables connected with the 
electrochemical interface [54]. “The theoretical analysis and topology simulation show that 
the errors caused by the parasitic inductance limit the measurement frequency, lead to 
inaccurate parametric identification and deform the impedance diagram” [54]. 
In order to allow the reliability of the impedance data, a correction by inductance must be 
done. In their study, Raikova et al. [54] showed that in some systems using YSZ as 
electrolyte, the high frequency inductance “tail” is replaced by a section of a semicircle, 
which represents the response of the electrolyte. Therefore, it may be possible that the arc 
observed in high frequencies in all the studied cells the may be related to the parasitic 
inductive effect.

Prior to analyse the inductive effect as possible cause of the wide incomplete arc observed 
at frequencies 200 kHz – 600 kHz, two other possible explanations were evaluated. 
In works developed by Primdahl and Mogensen [106], a comparison of impedance 
measurements using two different setups with Ni/YSZ cermet and Ni anodes is given. The 
impedance was taken in a frequency range between 500 kHz and 0.01 Hz working at 
1000ºC under a reducing atmosphere H2:H2O 97:03. In their results, the Ni anode showed a 
wide incomplete arc between 10 kHz and 500 kHz. This reference could be used to explain 
the wide incomplete arc in the cells based on Ni-CGO electrodes. However, the incomplete 
arc was also observed in the cells based on CGO electrodes, which indicates that the 
contribution of the high frequency arc is not only given by the Ni. 
Studies carried out by Nielsen et al. [13] have obtained incomplete arcs in high frequencies 
during investigations of infiltrated CGO20:Ni anodes. In their work, they attributed this 
arc to the grain boundaries of the electrolyte and oxide ion charge transfer resistance 
between the electrolyte and the infiltrated anode. 
Thus, it is probable that the wide incomplete arc at high frequencies may be attributed to 
three possible processes: 1) inductive effect created by the wiring; 2) the possible 
contribution of the grain boundaries of the electrolyte and the oxide ion charge transfer 
resistance between the electrolyte and the electrodes and, 3) the effect of the metallic Ni in 
the Ni-CGO based electrodes

4.6.3 
Effect of the electrode thickness in the performance of the cells. 
In the previous sections, the possible contributions to the impedance were described. It was 
noticed that cells based on 20 and 30 coatings electrodes showed similar behaviour despite 
the difference in the Rs values; however, cells based on 10 coatings electrodes showed clear 
differences mainly in the Rp values. Following a more detail analysis of the different 
behaviours is described. 
Concentration polarization. 
Regarding the concentration polarization processes associated with frequencies between 50 
Hz and 70 Hz. It was suggested that an increase in the temperature could have an 
influence on the cells with 10 coatings electrodes. From Fig. 4.13a it is possible to see that 
cells based on 10 coatings electrodes show higher values of polarization resistance. E.g. 
cells 10_CGONi_03, 10_CGONi_03, 20_CGONi_03 and 30_CGONi_03 show 0.0789 Ωcm2, 
0.037 Ωcm2 and 0.042 Ωcm2 at the beginning of the ST2, with increase of 0.034 Ωcm2, 0.005 
Ωcm2 and 0.001 Ωcm2 at the end of the ST2. From these data, it is noticed that also the 
exposition time at 650ºC at the EIS test conditions have an effect on the 10 coating 
electrodes, since they present higher degradation than the electrodes based on 20 and 30 
coatings. 
Although the thickness of all the EIS tested cells are below than 1 µm, it might be possible 
that very thin electrodes, e.g. 10 coatings electrodes with ∼350 nm, tend to be more affected 
by the temperature because they have less surface area. This could cause coarsening of the 
electrode material, blocking the paths for the correct gas diffusion and increasing the 
impedance contribution related to the concentration process during the EIS test. It is 
necessary to analyse the electrodes of the cells 10_CGONi_03 and 10_CGO_Ni_04 with 
FESEM, in order to link the electrochemical behaviour with the microstructure. 
On the other hand, it was described that the concentration polarization processes of the 
cells based on 20 and 30 coatings electrodes are practically independent of the temperature, 
which may indicate that the electrodes’ microstructure is more stable. Thus, the impedance 
contribution for these cells might be related to the gas diffusion in the electrodes or from 
the flow bulk to the electrode’s surface. 
It is expected that working with thin films allows having less impedance contributions 
related to diffusion through the electrode’s porous structure, since gasses do not have to 
cross different layers to reach the active zones. Porosity in the electrodes was found in the 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
67 
EIS tested cell 20_CGONi_01 (Fig. 4.9b). Considering this, it might be that the main 
concentration polarization processes observed at low frequencies is not related to the 
diffusion in the electrode but with the flow bulk to the electrode’s surface. 
The design of the used EIS-test rig only allows low flows of gasses and the gases do not flow 
directly to the electrodes cells but to a chamber (Fig. 3.2a). In addition, the alumina holders 
and the Pt meshes can reduce the flow of gasses to the electrode’s surface. This can create 
thicker boundary layers between the electrode’s surface and the flow bulk, affecting the 
concentration of the gasses on the electrode’s surface [53]. This stagnation effect may be the 
cause of the impedance contribution observed at low frequencies in the cells 20_CGONi_04 
and 30_CGONi_03, since it is independent of the temperature. 
Activation polarization. 
From the Nyquist plots shown in Fig. 13 and the Bode plots in Fig. 15, it is observed that 
the main contributor of the polarization resistance is found in frequencies between 1 kHz 
and 3 kHz, which is related to activation processes, i.e. where the kinetic of the electrode’s, 
adsorption/desorption processes and charge transfer can take part. [107].  
Different authors have studied different phenomena related to the activation polarization. 
As described, Ni is used in the cermet Ni-CGO as anode material because CGO presents 
low electronic conductivity under reducing conditions. However, Ni also presents high 
catalytic activity, dissociating the hydrogen into protons and transferring them to the 
electrolyte. It has been suggested that oxygen ions are transferred from the electrolyte to 
the nickel where it reacts with hydrogen [108].  
On the other hand, Primdahl and Mogensen [109] assumed that the major rate-limiting 
step for SOFC anodes based on CGO is the hydrogen adsorption/dissociation on the surface 
of the electrodes, which is observed as a small arc in low frequencies (∼5 Hz).

In the case of the activation energies related to the polarization resistances (EaRp), Fig. 4.21 
shows the Arrhenius plots based on the total Rp values. In the case of the cells with 10 
coatings (Fig 4.21b), EaRp was calculated in 0,96 and 0,95 for the cells 10_CGONi_03 and 
10_CGONi_04. The similitude can be observed in the slope of the curves for these two cells, 
which practically is the same and even Rp values are very approximated. Cells based on 20 
coatings electrodes (Fig 4.21a) have variation in the values of EaRp, since the slope of each 
curve is different. EaRp values of 0.75 eV, 1.09 eV and 0.81 eV for the cells 20_CGONi_02, 
20_CGONi_03 and 20_CGONi_04 were obtained respectively. For the cells with 30 
coatings, a considerable variation of the slope can be observed in Fig. 4.21b. A difference of 
0.32 eV between the EaRp of the two cells was calculated. This may indicate that the 
observed structural damage during the SEM analysis may have an important influence in 
the electrochemical activity of the thin film electrodes, since delamination of the electrodes 
affect the quantity of active zones to carry out the reactions, thus affecting the performance 
of the electrodes. E.g. lower structural damaged was observed in the cells with 10 coatings 
electrodes and their activation energies are practically the same (0.95 – 0.96 eV).  
The low EaRp values of the cells 20_CGONi_03 and 30_CGONi_03 may be related to the 
stress of the films due to delamination. Studies carried out by Beckel et al. [21] describe 
that thin films with high compressive stress show lower activation energies than those with 
lower compressive stress. 
It is worth pointing out that EaRp values were calculated based on the total polarization 
resistance, which involves the concentration and activation processes. In case to split the 
processes, different activation energies will be obtained, since some processes depend on the 
temperature changes (activation processes) than others, which are practically independent 
of the temperature (concentration processes), e.g. the stagnation effect related to the 
diffusion. However, concentration polarization is only a small fraction of the total 
polarization resistance in thes cells, which may not impact considerably in the activation 
energy of the activation polarization processes.

If the degradation of the electrodes occurred after applying 750ºC, then a similar behaviour 
would be expected from the analysis of changes in Rp, since the ionic current would be 
practically blocked, making the active area less than the measured geometrically, unless 
other simultaneous mechanisms would be involved, such as the NiO reduction. 
During the HTXRD analysis, it was defined that NiO continued reducing at 800ºC. It is 
probable that during the first stage of the EIS tests, the reduction of the NiO was not 
completed, thus lower catalytic activity and issues with the diffusion would be observed due 
to low porosity. The performance of the electrodes would increase by the NiO reduction 
during the next stages of the EIS test.  
This would explain the difference in the behaviour of the changes observed in the Rs and 
Rp values during the stability tests. However, more studies are necessary to analyse the 
behaviour of the NiO reduction in the studied thin films.

5. Conclusions 
In this work, the microstructure and electrochemical performance of symmetric cells based 
on thin nano-porous electrodes Ni-CGO/8YSZ/Ni-CGO with 37 vol.% of Ni were studied. In 
order to analyse the effect of the electrode thickness on the performance of the cells, thin 
electrodes were fabricated by spin coating of Newtonian nitrate precursor solutions thin 
films on 8YSZ electrolyte. Different thermal treatment profiles to obtain the Ni-CGO 
coatings were used. Symmetric cells with CGO/8YSZ/CGO were fabricated as reference. 
The effect of two different collecting layers between the electrodes and the external current 
collectors was studied in the cells with 20 coatings. The microstructure of the cells was 
analysed using SEM-EDS and HTXRD. The electrochemical characterization was studied 
through a specific thermal cycling profile in EIS tests. 
The cells based on Ni-CGO electrodes showed average thicknesses of 350 nm, 680 nm and 
830 nm after 10, 20 and 30 coatings respectively. The cells based on CGO with 20 coatings 
presented a thickness of 700 nm, which demonstrates the effect of the viscosity and solid 
content of the precursor solution on the thin film formation. The viscosities of the precursor 
solutions Ni-CGO and CGO were 80 and 85 mPa·s respectively. 
After the EIS testing, extensive delamination of the electrode – electrolyte interface was 
observed in different cells. The major structural damages were found in the cells based on 
CGO electrodes with 20 coatings. High densification of the CGO during the pre-sintering in 
air and TEC mismatch between the CGO and 8YSZ under reducing conditions could cause 
a considerable mechanical stress in the electrode – electrolyte interface that might lead to 
delamination. In the case of the Ni-CGO electrodes, the reduction of the NiO resulted in 
porosity, which may absorb part of the stress caused by the CGO’s expansion. This was 
noticed during the SEM analysis of the different cells, since less delamination in the cells 
based on Ni-CGO electrodes was observed. 
Another factor that may contribute to the structural damage is the heat treatment of the 
cells. The use of two drying-calcination profiles during the fabrication of the Ni-CGO 
electrodes resulted in different densities across the electrode’s internal layers, that may add 
stress among the electrode layers and in the electrode – electrolyte interface. The 
interlayers were easily visible during the SEM analysis. The Ni-CGO electrodes with 10 
coatings showed the best electrode – electrolyte contact among the studied cells, probably 
due to only one drying-calcination profile was used, which in turn may avoid having 
different porosities within the electrodes. However, the performance of the electrodes with 
10 coatings was lower than those with 20 and 30 coatings Ni-CGO electrodes. 
Nickel is used in cermet electrodes due to its electronic conductivity and high electro-
catalytic activity; however, it was observed that its precursor, NiO, also decreases the 
CGO’s crystallite size. From the results of the HTXRD, a decrease in the crystallite size 
around 60 Å by adding NiO 50 vol.% in the CGO precursors was observed, since the 
addition of NiO as second phase to the CGO matrix hinders the grain growth. The decrease 
in the crystallite size may improve the electrochemical activity of the electrodes.  
Regarding the possible contributions in the serial and polarization resistance, it was 
noticed that in cells using Ni-CGO electrodes, the serial resistance provided values in the 
region of 1.9 – 2.0 Ωcm2, which may indicate that Rs is mainly attributable to the 
electrolyte (1.818 Ωcm2 at 650ºC, electrolyte thickness of 200 µm). This indicates that the 
ohmic resistance associated with the electrodes or other processes is relatively low. In 
addition, lower values of Rp suggest a better electrochemical activity in comparison with 
==PAGE:84==
 
84 
 
the results of the CGO electrodes. However, it is noteworthy that due to the scattered data 
obtained from the cells based on CGO electrodes, a reliable comparison between cells based 
on CGO and Ni-CGO electrodes was not achieved. 
Rp values per electrode of 0.04 Ωcm2, 0.03 Ωcm2 and 0.04 Ωcm2 were obtained for the cells 
based on Ni-CGO electrodes with 10, 20 and 30 coatings respectively, with a degradation 
after the EIS tests of 0.1 Ωcm2, 0.03 Ωcm2 and 0.01 Ωcm2 respectively. Thus, it was possible 
to observe a reciprocal relation between the thickness of the electrodes and the 
electrochemical performance of the cells, i.e. the polarization resistance decreases by 
increasing the thickness of the electrode. 
Different contributions of polarization resistance were observed in the cells based on Ni-
CGO electrodes between 650ºC and 800ºC at reducing conditions H2:H2O 80:20. The 
frequencies of the different processes were obtained using Bode plots. The higher 
contributor was found at frequencies between 1 kHz and 3 kHz, which was associated with 
activation polarization processes, such as H2 adsorption/dissociation. A second arc, in lower 
frequencies around 50 Hz – 70 Hz, was connected with concentration processes, in specific 
gas diffusion from the flow bulk to the electrodes’ surface caused by the rig’s design. 
However, it may be possible that degradation of the electrodes during the thermal cycling 
tests may be also related to the correct diffusion of the gases through the electrode. In all 
the cases a third wide incomplete arc was found between 200 kHz and 600 kHz. This arc 
was associated mainly to grain boundaries of the electrolyte; however, possible effects 
caused by inductance were also considered. 
The study of the different thickness showed a reciprocal relation between the thickness of 
the electrodes and the electrochemical performance of the cells, i.e. the polarization 
resistance decreases by increasing the thickness of the electrode. This may be related to the 
coarsening of the thin films and the loss of active areas in the electrodes to carry out the 
electrochemical reactions, since thinner films tend to lose percolation of the material. Thus, 
thicker layers could allow more stability in the microstructure and keep the electrochemical 
activity of the electrodes. 
The effect of Pt and Ni-YSZ as collecting interface between the electrodes and the external 
current collectors was studied in the cells based on 20 coatings. It was found that the use of 
Ni-YSZ as conducting layer does not show considerable differences in the ohmic resistance 
in comparison with the cells using Pt as collecting interface. However, slightly higher 
contribution in the arc of the concentration polarization was observed, which may be 
associated to the coarsening of the contacting layer material during the thermal cycling 
profile of the EIS. This may affect the correct diffusion of the gases. SEM analysis is needed 
to study the microstructure of the tested cells and link this hypothesis.  
Summarizing, it was found that the electrochemical properties of the Ni-CGO and CGO 
thin electrodes are strongly linked to the microstructure, which in turn is dependent of the 
manufacturing parameters of the thin films. The use of thin films of Ni-CGO of 800 nm 
with 37 vol.% Ni as electrodes provides good electro-catalytic activity, low ohmic resistances 
and stability through the used thermal cycling profile that could make it a subject for 
further studies.

6. Future scope 
It is necessary to optimize the thin film tailoring process, in order to improve the 
microstructure of the electrodes and the electrode – electrolyte interface. It is advisable to 
carry out the preparation of the precursor solutions in a clean environment to avoid the 
depositions of powder particles to reduce the quantity of micro-defects in the electrode due 
to deposition of fine particles on the thin films. The use of more viscous precursor solutions 
could reduce the quantity of spin coating cycles, which would be a key point for mass 
production.  
Special attention must be taken during the electrodes tailoring, since only one drying-
calcination profile must be used, in order to avoid the formation of internal layers within 
the electrode, which may result in structural failures.  
The assessment of different heat treatment profiles should be studied in order to define the 
parameters (temperature ramp, pre-sintering temperature, exposition time) that 
compromise a strong electrode – electrolyte interface and a favourable microstructure of the 
electrodes. It may be necessary to reduce the ramp, in order to avoid the grain size growth 
and provide better electrochemical performance. It is also necessary to clarify the effect of 
the NiO/Ni on the formation of the CGO.  
In addition more studies regarding the electrode thickness could allow determining the 
optimal thin film that compromises the thermal and microstructural stability of the 
electrodes and their electrochemical and catalytic properties. It has been seen that thin 
films with 830 nm provided good results of stability and electrochemical activity. This 
thickness could be taken as reference for future studies. 
A deeper impedance analysis is required to identify with precision the features of each 
contribution, such as frequency and capacitance and associate them to a specific process 
related to concentration and activation polarization. Analysis techniques, such as 
distribution of relaxation times (DRT) may allow to find with precision the frequency of the 
processes, as well as other processes that are not possible to perceive in the Nyquist and 
Bode plots. In addition, deeper microstructural analysis would allow linking with more 
certainty the microstructure of the cells with the electrochemical performance. E.g. the use 
of low voltage In-lens SEM would allow the identification of the Ni percolation in the 
electrodes and the possible coarsening of the materials in cells with different thicknesses. 
These deeper analyses may allow identifying the manufacturing – operating parameters 
that could be improved to achieve better electro-catalytic performances.  
Although the results obtained between the cells using Pt and Ni-YSZ as contacting layer 
are similar and the use of Ni-YSZ paste seems to be an option to be used as contacting layer 
material. More studies using Ni-YSZ are needed, in order to probe its long-term stability, as 
well as the effect in the performance of the cell.
