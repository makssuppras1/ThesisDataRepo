ABSTRACT
Presently grid follower technology is utilised to control inverter-interfaced units in power
systems.
There is an upper-limit to the share of generation from inverter-interfaced
units using this technology, since they rely on other generators to deο§¹ne grid voltage and
frequency. By introducing grid former inverters to the power system this limit can be
eliminated and stabilise inverter dominated power systems. For this reason, a study is
conducted on grid former technology in fully inverter-interfaced wind turbines and its
application in DenmarkβÄôs western transmission system as replacement of conventional large
synchronous generators.
Three diο§Äerent grid former control principles are investigated, and they are all designed to
make inverters mimic the dynamics of large synchronous generators. Additionally, eο§Äects
on short-circuit contribution and inertia when replacing large synchronous generators
with grid former inverters are studied. A dynamic model of a grid former inverter is
implemented in PowerFactory, in order to make RMS transient simulations of its behaviour.
This benchmark model is used to replace large synchronous generators in EnerginetβÄôs model
of DenmarkβÄôs western transmission system. All simulations are performed in PowerFactory
to perform diο§Äerent case studies and analyse eο§Äects of these replacements.
The ο§¹ndings demonstrate that a wind farm using grid former technology, which is modelled
using a simple wind turbine model together with a grid former inverter model, can operate
eο§Éciently in transmission systems with the ability to deο§¹ne voltage and frequency. Three
diο§Äerent control strategies are implemented and generally they are all successful in emulating
synchronous machine behaviour as well as synchronising with synchronous machines and
exhibiting fair load sharing.
Furthermore, concerning short-circuit contribution it is
reduced when large synchronous generators are replaced with wind farms using grid former
inverters.

1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
This report focuses on grid former inverters (GFIs), a relatively new power electronics
technology used to control grid connected inverters. GFIs are able to deο§¹ne the voltage
and frequency of a power system and behave similar to a synchronous generator. This is
in contrast to the well-known and widely used grid follower technology, which relies on
other generators in the power system to deο§¹ne voltage and frequency.
The number of inverter-interfaced units (IIUs) in power systems increases rapidly, mainly
in distribution grids. According to [1] "more and more inverters will be connected to the
grid and will eventually dominate power generation", and there are several reasons why this
is happening. One of them is political decisions to change from fossil fuel based generation
to renewable, and as an example this is still the political direction taken in the European
Union (EU) [2].
This is what is currently happening in Denmark, where the share of conventional generation
has been decreasing since 1990, while the share of renewable generation from Wind Turbines
(WTs) has been steadily increasing (slide 14 and 18 in [3] and page 4 in [4]). This trend
is expected to continue in the near future [4] (page 4), and thus it will be the focus of
this thesis to investigate WTs, and how grid former technology can be implemented in
them. More speciο§¹cally, focus will be on WTs that are fully inverter-interfaced to the grid
through GFIs.
This change from conventional to inverter-interfaced generation aο§Äects the operation and
stability of power systems in various ways, and thus the behaviour and dynamics of IIUs
during transients and faults becomes essential for stability and reliability of the electric
energy supply.
1.2
Motivation
Due to the fact that grid follower control is currently used to control IIUs, there is an
upper-limit to the share of generation from IIUs, since they rely on other generators to
deο§¹ne grid voltage and frequency [5]. This limit can be eliminated by introducing GFIs to
the power system, which can in theory allow 100 % penetration of generation from IIUs.
This is one of the reasons why it is interesting to investigate behavior and dynamics of
GFIs. Another reason is that GFIs are also necessary for stabilising inverter dominated
power systems [5], i.e. power systems with IIU penetration level a little below 100 %.
Three diο§Äerent aspects of GFIs are especially interesting for grid stability, and these are
control without frequency measurement, short-circuit (SC) contribution and provision of
1
==PAGE:14==
1. Introduction
synthetic inertia. With regards to the ο§¹rst mentioned, grid former technology is capable
of operating without measuring grid frequency, which is not possible with grid follower
technology. Tracking of grid frequency is usually done using a Phase-Lock-Loop (PLL)
and use of these in controllers for IIUs gives rise to problems. First of all, according to [6]
"PLLs introduce a non-negligible delay which can limit the performance of the controllers
that depend on the frequency estimation of the PLL." In addition, during severe power
system faults, e.g. SC faults, PLLs can contribute to cause instability of controllers [7] and
in worst case cause loss of synchronism [8]. An inverter needs a PLL to synchronise with
the grid before connection, so a PLL cannot be omitted in inverter control design. However,
using grid former instead of grid follower technology, the PLL can be deactivated after
synchronisation, and thereby avoid problems caused by the PLL during normal operation
and system faults.
Assuming that inverters with grid former technology will be used to some extent in large
power systems, it is important to investigate SC contribution of these. According to [9]
SC contribution from IIUs is lower than the contribution from synchronous generators,
but "the eο§Äect is, however, dependent on control design" [9]. It might be possible to design
a grid former control strategy, that shows better SC contribution than what the present
grid follower control strategies do. If that is possible, it would be helpful for Transmission
System Operators (TSOs).
Likewise, it is interesting for TSOs to understand the ability of GFIs to provide inertia,
which is closely related to two kinds of frequency ancillary services. These are the classical
and well-known frequency containment reserve (FCR) and the relatively new fast frequency
response (FFR). When using grid follower technology. an IIU does not at all provide inertia,
at least not when it is just controlled to generate maximum active power. According to [10]
(page 12), such IIUs "behave passively towards frequency changes and, as a result, the total
system inertia is decreased". In other words, replacing a large conventional synchronous
generator with an IIU using grid former instead of grid follower technology might retain
some inertia, that would otherwise have been lost.
1.3
Problem Statement
This report conducts a study on grid former technology and its application in transmission
power grids as replacement of conventional large synchronous generators. The following
question will be answered: Which grid former control principles for fully inverter-interfaced
WTs with GFIs can mimic the dynamics of large synchronous generators, and what are
the eο§Äects on SC contribution and inertia when replacing large synchronous generators
with such GFIs? Also, the following sub-questions must be answered:
1. What is state-of-the-art of the grid former technology and utilisation in transmission
power grids with focus on provision of SC contribution and inertia?
2. Which simple models can represent the behavior of fully inverter-interfaced WTs
using GFIs, while providing SC contribution and inertia to the power system?
3. Which GFI control principles are suitable for delivering SC contribution and inertia
to the power system?

4. How does fully inverter-interfaced WTs with GFIs perform when providing SC

5. What are the technical speciο§¹cations of a park of WTs using GFIs, including all

The behaviour of GFIs in power systems is studied using simulations, and in this report
only Root Mean Square (RMS) transient simulations are performed with balanced phasors
and only a positive sequence component. According to [12] (page 5) this is over-simpliο§¹ed,
since "the line dynamics cannot be neglected in the presence of grid-forming converters
due to potential adverse interactions between their fast response and the line dynamics".
Contrary to RMS transient simulation, electro-magnetic transient (EMT) simulation shows
these interactions with line dynamics, but in this report the time span of simulations
must be several seconds, so they are too time consuming to be applied to DenmarkβÄôs
western transmission system. In addition, the Danish TSO, Energinet, primarily uses RMS
transient simulations, so as a consequence of this RMS transient simulation is used in this
report as well.
As stated above, simulations performed on DenmarkβÄôs western transmission grid assumes
only a positive sequence component, i.e. it is assumed that there are no zero- and negative
sequence components. This means that only balanced three-phase faults can be simulated,
and according to [13] (page 239) these are not the most common type of faults to occur,
but they are the type that causes the largest fault current. In other words, this report
investigates the fault with worst case fault current, and not the faults that are most
probable to occur in reality.

Besides unbalanced faults, it could also be interesting to investigate black-start capability
of GFIs. However, this is beyond the scope of this report.
Many control strategies for GFIs have been suggested in literature. However, in this report
only control strategies that are inspired by the dynamics of synchronous machines (SMs)

Power systems with 100 % penetration of IIUs is a scenario that lies far into the future,

THEORY
2.1
Type IV Wind Turbines
In 2017 the authors of [15] stated that DFIG WTs were dominating the market, but
also that the direct-drive permanent magnet synchronous generator (PMSG) WT were
increasing its market share year by year. Direct-drive PMSG is a generator technology that
is most suited for type IV WTs [11] (page 93), so in other words partially inverter-interfaced
WTs had the greatest market share in 2017, and the market share of fully inverter-interfaced
WTs were increasing. So even though type IV WTs are not currently the dominating type,
the recent increase in installation of this type of WTs makes it interesting to focus on
them.
Both type III and type IV WTs are variable speed WTs, and their rotor speed can be
controlled using various concepts. However, back in 2012 it was stated in [11] (page 77)
that "the variable speed concept is used in practice today only together with a fast-pitch
mechanism", which means that a variable speed WT controls its blade pitch angle in
order to regulate its rotor speed. In addition, type IV WTs can adjust their rotor speed
electrically using its power converters [16] (page 164). Due to the speed-power characteristic
of WTs, rotor speed can be used to control active power injection from the WT generator.
To sum up, active power injection from a type IV WT generator is controlled partly by
blade pitching and partly by control of its converters.
Type IV WTs are conο§¹gured with rotor- and grid-side converters as illustrated in ο§¹gure
2.1, and as mentioned earlier it is the grid-side converter that is implemented using grid
former technology. The DC link consists of a DC capacitor Cdc and furthermore a DC
chopper is used as suggested in [17] (page 10) and [16] (page 172), which protects the
capacitor against harmful overvoltages.
It is assumed that the amount of active power extracted from the wind is controlled via the
rotor-side converter and blade pitching. The DC link capacitor voltage reο§²ects imbalance
between generated DC power and AC power injected into the grid [16] (page 160), so as
a result the rotor-side converter control and pitch control are jointly regulating the DC
voltage. Furthermore, type IV WTs that use a PMSG needs active damping of speed
oscillations in the generator [16] (page 164), and for simplicity the rotor-side converter is
assumed to perform any necessary active damping perfectly.
Consequently, the generator and rotor-side converter can be roughly modelled as a
controllable DC energy source, and likewise the control for the rotor-side converter together
with pitch control can be roughly modelled as a DC voltage controller, just as it is illustrated
in ο§¹gure 2.2. Here, the energy source is a controllable current source in parallel with a
conductance Gdc, which is the model suggested in both [12] (page 3) and [18] (page 19).

2.2
Inverter Interfaced Generation
The transient behaviour of SMs is well-known, also with respect to SC contribution and
inertia, and according to [5] "regardless of the behavior of controls (e.g., excitation and
governors), the systemβÄôs performance is largely predictable." On the other hand, transient
behaviour of IIUs is primarily determined by their control, and in the worst case their
transient behaviour can be unpredictable.
2.2.1
Inverter Behavior During Short-Circuit Faults
Short-Circuit Contribution
With regards to SC current contribution of inverters it highly depends on the inverter
control strategy. However, controlling a grid follower inverter to simply inject maximum
6
==PAGE:19==
2. Theory
power causes it to contribute with less SC current than for any type of conventional
generator [9].
According to [20] SC contribution of any IIU is "largely determined by its control", and this
also applies to type IV WTs, because they are fully inverter-interfaced to the grid. For
a WT of type III, i.e. a DFIG, SC contribution is not solely deο§¹ned by control strategy
of converters because the generator stator is connected directly to the grid. Still, SC
contribution from a DFIG is less than that of a SM, because induction generators generally
provide less SC current than SMs [9].
For SMs the SC current is divided into sub-transient, transient and synchronous parts [9] [21],
but for IIUs this division makes no sense. Instead, the response time from the fault appears
until the controller of an IIU reacts is critical. In addition, any inverter controller has some
kind of current limitation implemented, with the main objective of preventing overheating
of inverter switches by keeping currents through them below rated values. This current
limitation does not have to be implemented as a simple saturation with minimum and
maximum current. It can be implemented with various functions and strategies, and which
implementation is used also has inο§²uence on SC contribution.
According to [21] (page 428) type IV WTs "can generate constant current during faults."
Furthermore, unlike other types of WTs, the generator of a type IV WT always generate
balanced voltages and currents because it is fully inverter-interfaced. In other words, the
generator of a type IV WT does not generate negative- and zero sequence voltages and
currents during SC faults. Instead, it is the grid-side converter that generate these, and
this might stress some switches in this converter more than others. Balanced three phase
short circuits stress these converter switches evenly, but line-to-ground, line-to-line and
double line-to-ground faults causes zero-sequence and negative sequence currents to ο§²ow,
and it is non-trivial to determine which fault type is the most stressful for the converter
switches.
Besides the problem of overcurrent through converter switches, there is another potential
problem that can arise during SC faults. According to [22] (page 204) "phase-to-ground
faults can produce power-frequency phase-to-ground overvoltages on the unfaulted phases".
This kind of overvoltage is called a temporary overvoltage [23] [22] (page 203), and it
is characterised by the overvoltage lasting for the entire duration of a fault. Temporary
overvoltages can also occur for other faults than just phase-to-ground faults. If a temporary
overvoltage causes the instantaneous voltage of a phase to exceed the DC voltage, active
power will ο§²ow from the AC side of the inverter to the DC side. This can cause overvoltage
of the DC capacitor, but another possible consequence is that the inverter disconnects due
to this reversed ο§²ow of active power.

Synthetic Inertia
Unlike conventional generators, IIUs do not naturally provide much inertia to the power
system, and some provide absolute none. As a result, the total system inertia constant
Htot decreases as low or zero-inertia IIUs dominates power generation [29].
However, IIUs are characterised by being fully controllable, which means that active power
injection can be easily changed, and furthermore this can be done quite fast and well
within the time frame of inertial response [28] (page 24). This kind of control is called
synthetic inertia, but in some literature it is also called virtual inertia or artiο§¹cial inertia.
For an IIU to be able to provide synthetic inertia, its operating point in terms of power
must be set carefully. According to [28] (page 24) "if it operates close to its maximum
power output, a very low inertia in the upward direction can be provided, and vice versa
for the minimum operating point".
Wind energy is intermittent and non-dispatchable. This causes problems when it comes
to the frequency ancillary services of FCR and secondary reserves, since the intermittent
behaviour prevents WTs from guaranteeing reserves [6]. However, this intermittency "is in
time scales larger than that of the inertial response of synchronous machines", according
to [6]. As a result, intermittency of wind energy does not hinder WTs from providing
synthetic inertia.
The rotating mass of a WT is relatively large, and it can indeed be used to provide some
inertia. However, a drawback of this is that [10] (page 13) "the rotational speed of the wind
turbine decreases and, therefore, the in-feed from the [WT] will drop considerably when the
frequency support is ended". Instead, provision of synthetic inertia by WTs "is done by
operating the wind generation at a de-loaded value", according to [11] (page 909). In order
to operate at a de-loaded value, the power output of the WT is controlled such that the
WT operates below the maximum value for a given wind speed.

active and reactive power supplied to the grid by injecting current into the energized power
grid.
Utilising grid former control it is expected that power systems of any size can ensure stable
operation even with a high share of IIUs and achieve 100 % penetration in the grid of the
future. On the other hand, with the grid follower control it is not possible to achieve 100 %
penetration [34], because grid follower inverters act solely as voltage-following current
sources [33].

2.6
State-of-the-Art GFI
If an inverterβÄôs control is designed to interact with non-stiο§Äpower grid or weak grid
similarly as SMs, then the inverter is termed grid former. Thereby the name Grid former
inverter [19], and it is a power electronics-based DC/AC converter. The GFI control needs
to be compatible with all presently connected devices and this includes SMs and other
grid follower controlled units in the power grid.
As power systems gradually become dominated by IIUs, GFIs can play a big role in
improving the frequency dynamics and other measures of stability. The dependence of
frequency stability on the mechanical inertia of SMs can be decreased by introducing active
frequency control provided by GFIs and thereby help to stabilise the grid [37]. Looking at
the power system from a certain perspective the GFI can be described with a black box
representation in the power system, and according to [18] (page 8) "From a viewpoint of
the power system, the voltage source converter can be seen as a black box that modulates
an AC voltage with a magnitude V and frequency œâ and injects active and reactive power
P and Q into the grid through its terminal".
GFIs have two main objectives, which are one for islanded operation and another for
grid connected operation. When utilising GFIs in grid connected operation they regulate
active and reactive power injected to the AC bus and in some cases improving the power
quality. A GFI is capable of independently generating sinusoidal voltage at the bus, which
is necessary in islanded operation [38].
Mimicking the electro-mechanical interaction of a SM with the grid is generally the
deο§¹nition of the objective of grid former control. This means a GFI is capable of matching
the SMβÄôs dynamics. Thus, a GFI is expected to exhibit load-sharing, frequency regulation,
voltage regulation, black start capabilities and drooping similar to SMs. However, it should
be noted that a GFI can deο§¹ne voltage and frequency without necessarily mimic SM
dynamics e.g. utilising the control strategy dispatchable virtual oscillator control explained
in [12] (page 10), which is not based on emulating SM dynamics. GFIs do not have inherent
features such as inertial response, synchronisation- and damping mechanisms, so these key

features have to be implemented utilising controls and separate energy storage. However,
GFIs have a fast response time, which enables control at much faster time scales than with
primary control of a SM [39].

simpliο§¹ed to an ideal AC voltage source with a low output impedance, which sets the

for future grid codes for GFIs will be given.

The response times and limitation of current during voltage and frequency steps are

DISCUSSION
This section discusses the results presented above, and furthermore it provides technical
speciο§¹cations for GFIs that can replace large synchronous generators. Finally, this section
gives recommendations for future grid codes for GFIs.
As a starting point, the capability of GFIs to provide virtual inertia to power systems
is discussed. The results from the test system indicates that regardless of which control
strategy for SM emulation is used, GFIs are capable of synchronising with SMs, and
they exhibit load sharing as well. However, the load event tested in DenmarkβÄôs western
transmission system is far too small to reveal the RoCoF, which is the only useful measure
of system inertia. The results from this case study shows that replacing SMs with GFIs
causes larger amplitudes of transient frequency oscillations at PCC compared to the
base case. However, the oscillations during this transient state cannot be used to draw
conclusions on the eο§Äect of these replacements on system inertia.
The authors of [12] (page 13) ο§¹nds that regardless of whether droop, VSM or matching
control is used, the RoCoF as well as frequency nadir/zenith is generally lower when
replacing SMs with GFIs. In other words, they ο§¹nd that all of the three control strategies
for SM emulation are successful in providing virtual inertia. According to the authors, this
is due to the fact that converters generally respond faster than SMs.
This points at a source of error in the GFI model developed in this report, namely that
low-level control responds much slower than in reality. The low-level voltage controller
presented in this report responds within approximately half a second, but in reality it
is only in the order of magnitude of tens of ms. This means that the response time of
low-level control is about an order of magnitude higher than it is in practice. Unfortunately,
tuning the low-level voltage controller more aggressively causes instability when it is used
together with high-level control.
There can be several diο§Äerent reasons for this, and one possibility is that high-level control
is tuned too aggressively, and thereby causes instability. However, this seems unlikely since
high-level controllers and their tuning are very similar to the ones presented by the authors
of [12], and their EMT model of a GFI shows stable behaviour. This leads to another
possible reason, which is that the PowerFactory model of the low-level voltage PI controller
might not represent the actual behaviour of this controller well. The low-level voltage
controller is built upon electro-magnetic equations, and these are disregarded in an RMS
modelling environment. This means that the RMS model of the controller implemented in
PowerFactory takes into consideration some electro-magnetic signals, that are not present
in the RMS grid model. This causes a mismatch between the signals in the controller and
the signals appearing in the grid, and this can potentially be the reason why the low-level
voltage controller is unstable when tuned aggressively. If this appears to be the actual
79
==PAGE:92==
5. Discussion
reason, then there are two possible solutions to this problem. One is to develop a model
of the controller that is adapted to the RMS modelling environment, and the other is to
change the modelling environment to EMT instead of RMS.
The case study of SC faults in DenmarkβÄôs western transmission system shows a signiο§¹cant
diο§Äerence between the base case and the case with ο§¹ve large synchronous generators
replaced by GFIs. SC contribution is generally smaller for GFIs and furthermore, they do
not inject maximum reactive current, even though a severe fault is simulated. Fault current
contribution can easily be increased in FM control by increasing the value of K, which is
the slope of added reactive current, as required in the current Danish grid codes for grid
follower wind and PV power plants. Increasing K cannot make the SC contribution of a
wind farm with GFIs completely match that of a similar sized SM, but it can get such a
wind farm closer to that.
FM control causes the inverter to switch to grid follower control during SC faults, and it
does not switch back to grid former control before the inverter output voltage has recovered.
If the grid is critically weak, there is no guarantee that the voltage will recover, and that
will cause the inverter to be stuck in grid follower mode. As a result, the inverter control
presented in this report is in fact not strictly grid former control. It is better practice to
design the control to be strictly grid former control, also during SC faults. This can for
example be done using the control suggested in [41] or [57].
With regards to the current limitation used in PIPI low-level control, it can cause instability
of high-level control as described in section 4.1.3. The authors of [12] observes similar
problems, and they propose a diο§Äerent current limitation strategy that solves them. The
strategy is to reduce the active power reference pβà½
ac when the ο§¹lter current magnitude
exceeds its limit, and according to [12] (page 17-18) this strategy succeeds in preventing
instability of high-level controllers. Unfortunately, it has the disadvantage of causing
unequal load sharing [12] (page 17-18), and it is unknown how this strategy responds to
SC faults.
On the other hand, the paramount beneο§¹t of this solution is that it is much less costly than
simply over-sizing the inverter, in order to avoid current limitation completely. The authors
of [57] suggests the same basic idea, i.e. limiting ο§¹lter current by reducing the active power
reference, but they take a fundamentally diο§Äerent approach of how to implement it.
With regards to active power injection from a WT, this report assumes that it can be
regulated freely from 0 to 1 pu. However, this is not realistic for a WT. First of all, it
has a certain cut-in wind speed, so it must inject some minimum amount of active power.
Secondly, it is seldom that the rated power of a WT can be extracted from the wind, so
most often the available power is lower than 1 pu. The simple WT model used in this
report disregards this completely, and it is possible that this simpliο§¹cation is a source of
errors in the results presented in this report. However, hybrid power plants like the ones
presented in [58] might be able to solve this problem, such that a hybrid power plant with
grid former technology always has full range of available active power.
Similar to the limits of generated active power, the limits of the DC current source assumed
in this report are not realistic. The lower limit is set to βà£imaxDC, just as it is done in [12]
(page 3). This is not a realistic way to model DC voltage control in a WT. It might be
80
==PAGE:93==

possible for the WT rotor-side converter to allow a negative value of idcs, but only for
short periods of time, because this means that the WT generator absorbs active power. In
steady state the generator of a WT must inject some minimum amount of active power,
and this amount is always larger than zero. As a consequence of that, idcs can at best be
zero or negative during short-lived transients, and in steady state it must be larger than
some positive lower limit.
5.1
Technical Speciο§¹cations for GFIs
In the light of the results presented in section 4, this section provides general
recommendation for technical speciο§¹cations of a wind farm with GFIs that is to replace a
given large synchronous generator. It is recommended to choose the ratings for a single
GFI such that they are at least close to the ratings given in table 3.1. With regards to
control parameters, it is in general recommended to use the values listed in appendix B.
These are the general recommendations, and the following subsections elaborates more on
these and also gives some exceptions.
5.1.1
Inverter Ratings
Regarding inverter ratings there are some recommendations for power rating of the wind
farm to replace a SM, and also for voltage rating of the GFIs in this wind farm.
Firstly, there are some concerns about the active power setpoint, pset. The maximum
value of pset is less than 1 pu due to instability of controllers caused by low-level current
limitation as described in section 4.1.3. This type of instability appears when pset is set to
0.9 pu and not when it is reduced to 0.8 pu, so this entails that pset < 0.9 pu. Furthermore,
WTs are characterised by a minimum active power injection as described above. The active
power rating of a wind farm, i.e. the number of parallel units N, must be set according to
these maximum and minimum limits.
Secondly, the voltage rating of inverters must be high enough to withstand voltage that
the inverter switches can output given the DC voltage. This voltage is given by equation
3.8, so the inverter must be rated to at least 1.04 kV line-to-line.
5.1.2
Low-Level Control
Concerning tuning of low-level control the gains chosen in this report will most likely
not work in practice, since the model responds to slowly. Hence, they are required to be
adjusted.
Furthermore, the current limitation does successfully limit inverter ο§¹lter current in steady
state, but not during transient periods. As a consequence of that, the maximum current
magnitude imax needs to be set conservatively to 1.0 pu.
5.1.3

With respect to tuning of the three diο§Äerent control strategies for SM emulation, the tuning
criteria described in section 2.7 and 3.6.2 must be utilised. Unlike the tuning of low-level

5.1.4
DC side
The central tuning parameters that aο§Äect DC voltage controller stability are the DC
capacitor cdc and the time constant œ³dc. As stated in the sensitivity analysis of the DC
voltage controller in section 4.1.2, the time constant œ³dc can maximally be 30 ms. What
this means in practice is that the rotor-side converter of a type IV WT must be able to
regulate DC current with a response time, that can be roughly modelled by a low-pass
ο§¹lter with a time constant of 30 ms. Of course, the rotor-side converter is allowed to
respond faster than that, and in that case the performance will only be better than what
is required. This requirement does not specify exactly how the rotor-side converter must
behave, but it serves as an indicator of how fast the rotor-side converter must respond
in order to ensure stability when using matching control. If a larger phase margin and
thereby faster damping of harmful oscillations is desired, then the rotor-side converter
must respond faster than what is required here.
Furthermore, the DC capacitor can minimally be 0.05 pu. It can be increased in the case
that oscillations of DC voltage appearing when using matching control needs to be reduced
in frequency. Increasing the size of cdc also gives rise to more damping of these oscillations,
but it is much more eο§Äective to make the rotor side converter respond faster if that is
technically possible. This minimum value of cdc and maximum value of time constant œ³dc
are chosen, in order to achieve a reasonable phase margin of the DC voltage controller,
but they can be increased and reduced, respectively, to improve the phase margin.
The proportional gain in the DC voltage controller is required to be kdc βâΞ10 pu. As stated
in section 4.1.2 this gain has direct impact on load sharing when using matching control,
and in order to obtain equal load sharing with a given SM, kdc must be set to the inverse
of the governor droop for that SM. This is the tuning criterion for matching control, and
in this report kdc is tuned against the SM governor droop from the test system, which has
kSM
droop = 0.1 pu. Therefore, kdc is set to 10 pu in all simulations performed. For the other
control strategies, i.e. droop and VSM control, this gain does not have any impact on load
sharing. Thus, it can with advantage be increased, e.g. to 20 pu, or tuned manually when
having one of these control strategies assigned to a GFI.

5. Discussion
5.2
Recommendations for Grid Codes
Currently there are no Danish grid codes for units using grid former technology, but this
subsection gives a few recommendations for such grid codes. The recommendations given
here only concern SC current contribution.
First of all, the current Danish grid codes for grid follower wind and PV power plants
require units to inject some speciο§¹ed amount of added reactive current during faults. As
stated in the discussion above, following these grid codes cause inverters to inject reactive
fault current that does not even reach Imax during a severe fault at a 400 kV bus. Thus,
it is possible to have SC current contribution from GFIs that is larger than the results
obtained in this report. In order to better match the SC contribution of SMs, future
grid codes for GFIs should somehow require higher SC current contribution, than what is
required in the present grid codes for grid follower wind and PV power plants.
Secondly, it is recommended to make grid codes for GFIs in a way that does not explicitly
require the inverters to switch to grid follower control during SC faults. The FM control
presented in this report causes inverters to switch to grid follower control during severe SC
faults, and this is the most straightforward way to comply with the current grid codes.
However, as stated in the discussion above it is better practice to design the control to be
strictly grid former control, and that is why it is recommended to make requirements for
SC current contribution of GFIs, such that they allow for grid former control during the
fault.

CONCLUSION
6.1
Future Work
One subject for future work is to design FM control that does not make GFIs switch to
grid follower technology during SC faults. As a starting point, it would be interesting
to study if virtual admittance in GFI control can be useful in such a design. The use of
virtual admittance in GFI control is common, see for example [59], [18], [40] or [41].
Furthermore, it would be interesting to make a study of black-start capability of GFIs.
The authors of [60] concludes that GFIs perform better than conventional synchronous
generators during black-start, which makes this study even more interesting.
As stated in the discussion section above, the low-level control presented in this report
responds much slower than it does in reality. Thus, another subject for future work is to
make an EMT model of the GFI and test system presented in this report. With such an
EMT model, it would be possible to check the validity of the RMS model presented in
this report. Furthermore, it would help to conclude whether or not the model of low-level
voltage control presented in this report is in fact suitable for RMS transient simulation.
Finally, the case study of a load event in DenmarkβÄôs western transmission system presented
in section 4.2.2 does not disturb the system enough to reveal any eο§Äect on system inertia.
Setting up a new case study that causes a much larger disturbance is also a subject for
future work, because that would help to conclude what the eο§Äect on system inertia is when
replacing large synchronous generators with GFIs.
6.2
Conclusion
Grid former technology enables inverters to deο§¹ne voltage and frequency in a power system,
which is something that grid follower technology cannot do. At the time of writing, grid
former technology is already being used in practice in microgrids, but this report studies the
use of the technology in transmission systems. The control for a GFI is always separated
into low- and high-level control, and various methods and strategies exists for both levels.
It is however most common to use cascaded PI controllers with current limitation as
low-level control, and emulation of SM behaviour as high-level control.
This report focuses on type IV WTs that use a GFI as their grid-side converter. The
rotor-side converter along with its control and the AC generator are altogether modelled
as a simple DC circuit, that has a current source controlled by a DC voltage controller.
This simple WT model is combined with a GFI model, in order to develop a benchmark
model of a wind farm using grid former technology. All technical speciο§¹cations such as
inverter ratings and values of control parameters are given in this report.
84
==PAGE:97==
6. Conclusion
With regards to high-level control of a GFI it consists of several parts, but most importantly
it has control that emulate SM behaviour. This report considers three diο§Äerent control
strategies, and these are droop control, VSM and matching of SM, respectively. Analysis
of the benchmark model shows that all of the three strategies are generally successful
in emulating SM behaviour, as well as synchronising with SMs and exhibiting fair load
sharing. However, it appears that matching of SM interacts with the DC voltage controller
of the WT, which causes rapid and harmful oscillations of frequency. In order to dampen
these oscillations, the DC link capacitor of each WT must be at least 0.05 pu. Furthermore,
the DC voltage controller must respond at least fast enough to be modelled by a low-pass
ο§¹lter with a time constant of 30 ms.
This is found by analysing the behaviour of the benchmark model in a small test system.
The same analysis also shows that current limitation in the GFI low-level control can cause
instability if the GFI is operated at a high active power setpoint. Thus, wind farms with
GFIs must always be operated at a de-loaded value.
The developed benchmark model is used to replace large synchronous generators in
EnerginetβÄôs model of DenmarkβÄôs western transmission system. SC analyses are conducted
in this model, and they show that SC current contribution is generally reduced when large
synchronous generators are replaced by wind farms using GFIs. Furthermore, a load event
is analysed in the model, with the purpose of investigating the eο§Äect of these replacements
on RoCoF and system inertia. Unfortunately, the load event appears not to be large
enough for this study, so no conclusions can be drawn on the eο§Äect on system inertia.
