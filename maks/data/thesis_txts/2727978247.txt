Abstract
This thesis covers the topic of automating the scheduling process for train cleaning. It
is based on real life data provided by the company DSB. The train cleaning scheduling
problem, TCSP, defined in this thesis is a problem for which a schedule for a fleet of trains
is presented, with the task of finding the best times to perform one of two types of clean­
ing, without impacting the original schedule. The problem is not well researched and only
one instance of a similar problem has been found in the literature. Finding solutions to
the TCSP could reduce scheduling times for railway companies significantly, improve re­
sponse time to unscheduled events, and potentially reveal better solutions than the ones
in use by companies today.
The thesis attempts to create a MILP model capable of matching the efficiency of the
solution in use by DSB. The efficiency is evaluated based on cost and the distances trav­
elled by trains between cleanings. To create the model, data from DSB was filtered and
converted to appropriate data types. The model was built and programmed in Julia in sev­
eral steps, gradually increasing complexity. The final model was solved using the Gurobi
solver in order to calibrate the three main parameters, the cleaning α­values, the distance
limit and the cost of exceeding the distance limit.
The model did not match the efficiency of the solution proposed by DSB. The tuning of
parameters was more difficult and time demanding than initially assumed. The model
was able to find solutions within range of the proposed solution, so better solutions may
be possible with additional tuning and extensions to the model.

Introduction
The efficient scheduling of train cleanings is a crucial aspect of maintaining a high standard
of cleanliness and hygiene in the transportation sector. However, the existing approach
employed by Danish company DSB relies heavily on manual scheduling, lacking the sup­
port of computer­based systems. This manual process poses significant challenges in
terms of time consumption and the ability to explore alternative, more efficient schedules.
In this thesis, I aim to address these challenges by proposing and implementing auto­
mated and simplified scheduling methods for train cleanings.
Figure 1.1: A map of the train lines operated by DSB throughout Denmark as of 2023.
This thesis primarily focuses on the lines: InterCityLyn, InterCity and Regional [1].
DSB is one of the biggest train operating companies in Denmark and is responsible for
running several train lines with different types of trains throughout Denmark, with some
extending into Sweden and Germany, as seen on figure 1.1. The train lines that this thesis
will focus on are the DSB InterCityLyn, InterCity and Regional lines from figure 1.1. These

for transportation across long distances throughout Denmark.
DSB is also responsible for running the DSB S­tog lines from figure 1.1. The DSB S­train
lines are the most used trains in Denmark, transporting up to 15 million passengers every
month in and around the area of Copenhagen, the capital of Denmark [2]. It consist of
7 individual train lines distinguishable by letters, as seen on figure 1.2. The S­train lines

With more passengers on the trains, the inside of the trains can also be assumed to
become more dirty than before. As passengers enter and leave the trains, they will carry

dirt and trash with them, some passengers put their shoes on the seats, some might eat
or drink and some might leave their trash behind in the carriage. All this leads to the train
carriages requiring more cleaning to uphold a certain standard of cleanliness and hygiene
inside the carriages.
Scheduling cleanings for trains, which are constantly moving across the country, is a very
complex task. Carriages are independent from the trains they make up, thus not all car­
riages will have travelled the same distance when they arrive for cleaning, not all stations
will have the necessary crew and items needed to clean trains, and trains will not always
have stops long enough to be able to clean them. This problem will be referred to as the
Train Cleaning Scheduling Problem (TCSP) from here on.
An example of an instance in the TCSP:
Carriage 1 is a part of train A and has travelled all the way from Aarhus to Hillerød and back
to København H. Here it is uncoupled from train A and attached to train B. Train B already
consists of carriages 2, 3 and 4 and has travelled from Høje Taastrup to København H. In
it’s current state, train B now consists of carriages 1, 2, 3 and 4. Carriage 1 has travelled
many times further than carriages 2,3, and 4 and therefore it is much more dirty inside,
but if train B was to be cleaned now, regulations state that all of the carriages would be
cleaned at the same time.
The problem here is that either one carriage is left much more dirty than the others, or
time and money is spent cleaning three carriages that do not need to be cleaned yet.
When scaled up to the full fleet of trains, this type of complexity makes the TCSP very
difficult for the human brain to solve, therefore it is in the interest of DSB to improve the
scheduling process for train cleaning by adding some kind of support system that can aid
in creating these schedules.
The support system to solve the TCSP proposed here is a mixed integer linear program­
ming, MILP, model, which can take an existing train schedule and find the best cleaning
schedule, without making any changes to the train schedule, based on cleaning prereq­
uisites determined by the user. The best cleaning schedule is evaluated by the shortest
average distance travelled between cleanings of all carriages throughout the duration of
the train schedule and the total cost of the cleaning schedule.
To achieve this, the data provided by DSB is analyzed and preprocessed. Cleaning pa­
rameters are organized into tables. These include a list a of all stations with cleaning crew
and facilities, and a list of cleaning durations for each litra type, i.e. each type of train, see
tables 1.1 and 1.2. A depiction of some of the different trains in use by DSB can be seen
on figure 1.3. There is an unexplained difference in the litra type abbreviations on figure
1.3, which was retrieved from DSBs website, and the abbreviations in the data received.
This, however, is not of great importance, so it is not considered any further.
The two types of cleanings in table 1.2, OR cleanings and TR cleanings, hereafter sim­
ply ORs and TRs, correspond to a lighter and faster cleaning and a slower but deeper
cleaning. They can be thought of as an ordinary and a thorough cleaning respectively.

The scheduling process takes into account the constraints imposed by the existing train

their development. Advanced ideas, such as cleaning crews working while trains are in
operation and inputting previous schedules to better track dirtiness, were excluded due
to complexity. Nevertheless, the models are designed with the goal of the final model
being able to be implementable in real­life scenarios, considering practical constraints
and resources.
The findings and insights obtained from this research contribute to the field of automated
planning and scheduling, particularly in the transportation sector. The TCSP, addressed
in this thesis, is an underexplored area of research, making this work valuable for inspiring
further investigations and more detailed models.

1.1
Motivation
Currently the prior work published regarding scheduling of train cleaning is very limited.
While the train cleaning schedule problem shares some similarities with problems such
as the parallel machine scheduling problem, the train scheduling problem is much more
complex, as the schedule has to fit not just the time schedule, but also the physical place­
ment of the trains as they travel around in Denmark.
DSB is currently looking for input and ideas to reduce the complexity of planning the train
cleaning schedule.
One way to do this is to involve some type of support system, that will suggest a schedule
which can then be fine tuned by employees.
This thesis deals with real life data delivered by DSB and will attempt to create a model
which can be used to support in the creation of a train cleaning schedule.
1.2
Objective
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop a MILP model that leverages data ob­
tained from DSB to optimize the scheduling of train cleaning. By utilizing the dataset,
my goal is to create a model that can match or improve upon the current train cleaning

schedule established by DSB.
The second objective is to evaluate the effectiveness and accuracy of the proposed mod­
els. To do this a comparison will be made between the solutions presented by the model
and the existing cleaning schedule suggested by DSB. Average distances traveled by
trains between cleanings will serve as a metric for evaluating performance as well as the
total cost of the solutions.

Data Processing
The modelling is performed using real­life data received from DSB. The data is contained
within two Microsoft Excel documents and has to be extracted from these to be used in
the Julia model.
The data processing plays a crucial role in the success of programming and solving the
mathematical models. Good data processing is required for transforming raw data into
a format that can be utilized by the models. For this thesis, this mostly involves opera­
tions such as organizing and converting the data to ensure that the models can run as
efficiently as possible, by removing the need to convert between data types when solving.
Furthermore, data processing enables the identification and removal of outliers or errors
that could otherwise negatively impact the model’s performance.
The quality of the data and the accuracy of its processing can significantly influence the
reliability and effectiveness when solving the mathematical model, leading to more robust
and insightful results.
2.1
The Data
The data in the first excel sheet, hereafter referred to as ”the data”, can be thought of as
a collection of schedules for each train carriage in DSBs fleet, for a full normal week in
2023.
A normal week refers to a full week of running with no external disruptions or changes
to arrival or departure times for all trains. The normal week is used as a baseline for
scheduling trains, and events which affect the normal week schedule are to be dealt with
by human employees as they occur. The data in this project is based on the normal week,
as this is the schedule that would be most accurate when compared to the actual sched­
ule for any given week throughout 2023.
Each row in the data corresponds to one train ride for a specific carriage. A train ride
consists of a departure time, station and date, an arrival time, station and date, and a
distance in kilometers. Each row also includes carriage information such as a carriage
id number, a litra type, a train id number and a total distance driven in kilometers, along
with other elements, most of which are not relevant for this thesis. The data is sorted by
carriage id, meaning that each carriages full schedule appears as one set of rows. A full
list of elements in each row can be found in appendix A.1.
A litra type is a specific type of train signified by a string of letters. For the purposes of this
thesis the litra type only affects how much time is needed to clean the train. The different
litra types and their respective cleaning durations can be found in table1.2.
There is a total of 22 columns in the data. The columns of interest for this thesis are:
• Løbsnr: The ID number for each carriage. This number will stay constant for a
specific carriage.
• Litra: The litra is an identifier for different types of trains. This will be expanded upon
in the parameters section.
• AfgangsDato: The departure date. This explains on which date the train departure
takes place.

2.3
Data preparation
Before the data and the parameters can be used in a MILP model I have to prepare both
of them for modelling. This includes filtering out unnecessary or incomplete data and
converting remaining data to the proper format.
I start by preprocessing the parameters. Since the parameters excel file contains two
separate tables in a single sheet, I load them into Julia over two times using the XLSX
package. Specifying a segment of the sheet, I can load table 1.2 into a data frame which
I call CleaningDuration.
Excel has already specified the cleaning durations as a time element, so when they are
loaded into Julia they are of a DateTime format. Julia reads DateTime as a point in time
and I want the elements as periods of time. Therefore I go through the whole CleaningDu­
ration dataframe and convert the DateTime elements to Period elements. For this I use
the Dates package. Since both the duration counter and the DateTime elements start at
00:00 I can use the Dates.Value() funtion to find the amount of time passed from midnight
until the time specified at each element.
10

==PAGE:19==
So for an OR cleaning of an ABS type train, it takes 47 minutes. Dates.Value() then reads
this as the time passed from 00:00, i.e. midnight, until 00:47 at night, which corresponds
to a time period of 47 minutes. Dates.value() outputs each time period in nanoseconds,
which allows for the use of the function Dates.canonicalize() to convert each time period
to the most fitting Dates.Period() element. I use Dates.canonicalize() as it automatically
converts the time periods from just nanoseconds to both hours and minutes. Having the
durations in hours and minutes makes it easier for users to quickly understand the time­
frames in question and better allows for times to be added and subtracted from each other
when programming.
After having imported the CleaningDuration I now want to import the station cleaning fa­
cility data. This data is read into a data frame using the same method as the duration
data and it does not require any transformation after being imported into Julia. I call this
dataframe CleaningStations.
Now I want to import the train schedule. I start by reading the normal week data into a
data frame called df.
The first thing to do here is to filter out data that is irrelevant or incomplete. I do not need
any data for train rides with trains of the litra type EA/EB or ME, since these trains do
not carry passengers and are thus cleaned differently, so all rows containing any of these
three litra types are removed from the dataframe. Additionally all rows in the dataframe
containing dates outside the normal week are removed. This includes 9 dates all of which
are before 2023 and the first Sunday of the normal week data to make the normal week
data span over exactly 7 days. The first Sunday was removed as it was significantly
smaller than the last Sunday, consisting of 45 train rides compared to 1349 train rides.
The data supplies us with a departure date, departure time and arrival time, but there is
no arrival date. Given the geographical size of Denmark I assume that no train rides have
a duration of more than 1 day. Using this assumption I can easily find the arrival date
by checking if the arrival time is before the departure time. If that is the case, then that
means that the arrival time must take place the day after the departure date. I use this to
create a new column containing arrival dates for all train rides.
Afterwards, I then calculate the difference between the arrival time and the next departure
time. I do this by subtracting the time and date of arrival from the time and date of the
next departure. The date is included so that overnight stops are calculated correctly. I
add the difference between arrival and departure to the data frame as a column called
TimeStationary.
I use the TimeStationary column to create two binary vectors called ORAvailability and
TRAvailability. These vectors indicate whether there is enough time available to perform
either an OR or a TR at any stop along the schedule. I calculate these by checking if the
duration of each cleaning method is less than or equal to the time spent stationary at each
stop.
I assume that during rush hour there must be more passengers and thus the trains must
need more cleaning. I have based my definition of rush hour on DSBs guidelines for their
pensioners travel card, which does not work between 07:00 and 09:00 during weekdays,
as this is what DSB considers rush hour. A column is added to the data frame with bi­
nary values indicating whether a departure takes place between 07:00 and 09:00 monday
through friday. Using a for loop over each row in the data frame, each rush hour indicator
is assigned based on the departure time and date for the corresponding row.
With all this done, the data frame is ready to be used in the models.

3
Mathematical models
For the mathematical modelling the problem was split into 3 sub­problems, each adding
a layer of complexity on top of the previous one. Each of these sub­problems were then
modelled, with the goal of creating one final model that could solve the problem based
on all the constraints. The model was created with the goal of being able to take the
processed data as input and output a cleaning schedule that would be equal to or better
than the schedule proposed by DSB.
The solutions generated by the models are represented by two binary columns, one for
each type of cleaning. Each column row contains a binary value with a 1 if a cleaning of
the corresponding type is scheduled for the corresponding arrival in the dataset, and a 0
otherwise. The output of the model is an Excel sheet.
• The first model produces a schedule for just one carriage and ignores all other car­
riages in the dataset.
• The second model produces a schedule for all the carriages available, taking into
consideration a prescribed linking of carriages.
• The third model introduces a new more realistic objective function, cost of cleaning,
a limit on distance driven between cleanings and a penalty for exceeding the limit.
Initially the models were created without any time tracking. This meant that cleanings
would only be assigned to an arrival, but no time­frame would be assigned for when the
cleaning was actually supposed to be done by cleaning crew. When taking time into
consideration for the solution, a time variable is added to the model. This variable is
represented by a matrix with the same number of rows as in the dataset, one row for each
train ride, and each column representing a 10 minute interval, starting from the first day
and continuing until the last day of the normal week. The time variable is added in order
to track exactly at which time a train is to undergo cleaning.
After implementing the time variable a second sheet is added to the output. This sheet
shows a detailed breakdown of exactly when a cleaning is scheduled for.
All models will be shown with the time variable implemented.
Each model was created from scratch by me, and at the end of this chapter I compare
the final model to previous work in order to investigate if the model share similarities with
other well­known problems within the field of operations research.

The objective function (3.1) attempts to minimize the dirtiness, where 1 unit of dirtiness is

To do this the sum of every train rides length is calculated where a penalty of 0.2 units
of dirtiness pr kilometer is added if a train ride begins during rush hour. The penalty is
an estimate based on numbers from Trafikstyrelsen for how many more passengers are
present during rush hour[5]. For every time a cleaning starts and ends, a penalty of 10

a train and start cleaning. Thus to eliminate time waste I try to schedule cleaning staff to
fully clean one train before moving on to the next, it is however not a necessity, so if it is
strictly better to move between trains whilst cleaning, that is still allowed.

money, it is in the interest of DSB to schedule their cleanings so that trains are cleaned

model a few times.

rides in the subset K must also have a cleaning of type j assigned to them. Here subset
K is the subset of train rides in the set N, which share train ID number, arrival time, arrival

train. This is done to simplify the workflow for cleaning crew and make tracking of cleanli­

the assignment of cleaning crew would become much more complex and in practice may
lead to more mistakes if wrong carriages are cleaned during stops due to human error.

In model 3 the new objective function 3.17, is introduced. To enable the use of this new
objective function five new variables are added; a cost variable, a dirtiness variable, a
dirtiness slack variable, a dirtiness slack tracking variable, and a linearization variable,
all of which are described in table 3.1.
The objective function 3.17 states that the aim of the model is to minimize the total cost
of cleanings, plus the sum of the start up cost for each cleaning started, plus a penalty for
each kilometer tracked by the dirtiness slack tracking variable. The size of the penalty
for the slack variable will be discussed in chapter 4.
The constraints 3.18 to 3.23 remain unchanged and function the same as in model 2.
Constraint 3.24 states that y has to be equal to the cost per time period times the amount
of time periods assigned for cleaning. Constraint 3.25 states that the dirtiness variable
for every train ride in the set Nt, which is the subset of set N containing every carriages
first scheduled train ride, is equal to the length of that train ride scaled to 1.2 times
the train ride if it takes place during rush hour. Constraint 3.26 tracks the dirtiness for
each carriage throughout the duration of the schedule. The dirtiness variable is equal
to the dirtiness variable at the previous stop, plus the length of the last train ride, adding
a penalty when the rush hour indicator equals 1. When a train is cleaned part of the
dirtiness is removed, based on the cleaning parameter value, α.
Constraints 3.27 to 3.30 are linearization constraints used to determine how much of
the dirtiness variable is reduced by at each instance. Constraint 3.27 states that zi,c can
at most be equal to M if a cleaning of type c is performed and at most be equal to 0 if no
cleaning is performed. Constraint 3.28 states that zi,c can at most be equal to the total
dirtiness accumulated so far. Constraint 3.29 states that if a cleaning is not performed
zi,c has to be at least equal to the total dirtiness accumulated so far. Constraint 3.30
states that at all times zi,c must be at least 0.
Constraint 3.31 states that the slack variable di has to be less than or equal to the sum of
a kilometer limit set by the user plus the slack variable. Constraints 3.32 states that the
slack variable has to be greater than or equal to 0, as the slack cannot remove kilometers
driven without cleaning. Constraint 3.33 states that d_trackeri has to be greater than or
equal to 0 at all times. Constraints 3.34 and 3.35 state that for each train ride in subset
Nt, d_trackeri has to be greater than or equal to the slack variable d_slacki and for
every subsequent train ride d_trackeri has to be greater than or equal to the change
from d_slacki−1 to d_slacki. Together, constraints 3.33 and 3.35 enables the tracking of
only positive changes in d_slack, as whenever d_slack decreases, d_tracker will always
remain greater than or equal to 0.
The purpose of having the slack and tracking variable split up is to ensure that kilometers
driven past the limit are not counted more than once in the objective function. The slack
variable is necessary for allowing di to exceed the limit. For example, if a train exceeds
the limit by 20 km during train ride i, and then by another 30 km during train ride i+1,
the slack variable would be: d_slacki = 20 km, d_slacki+1 = 50 km. However, because
the variable is summed up in the objective function this returns 70 km of slack, when in
reality only 50 km were used. Therefore the tracking variable is introduced to track the

changes in the slack. The tracker would return: d_trackeri = 20 km, d_trackeri+1 = 30
km. Thus allowing the model to accurately track how many kilometers of slack are being
used.
After testing, covered in chapter 4, it was deemed that d_trackeri did not sufficiently
penalize the use of slack. It was belived that d_trackeri would have introduced enough
cost to deter the use of slack, but updates were necessary to enforce the penalty of
slack properly. d_trackeri was removed and replaced with d_slacki and d_slack_twoi.
The use of these two slack variables meant that in the objective function the penalty for
using slack scaled with the amount of times slack was used in a row. Since d_slacki just
tracks how much slack is needed to keep going, the first use of slack will carry over to
the next and keep stacking until the train is cleaned. Thus the longer the train continues
to travel with slack, the penalty will increase exponentially. The second slack variable
d_slack_twoi was introduced to add an additional penalty to the slack variable after the
d_slacki reached the same size as the limit set. This was done in order to try to keep
dirtiness within at least twice the limit set, but allowing it to move beyond this limit. In
this way the limit works more like a wanted value, whereas the limit on d_slacki is the
cutoff, after which as little slack as possible is wanted at all cost.

The objective function for the majority of studies investigated by the team aimed to
minimize the makespan. The makespan is an effective way to balance the load between
machines and achieve a high level of utilization for all machines. The makespan is also
easy to measure and makes the process of finding lower bounds more straight forward
compared to other measurements. There also exists a number of algorithms that can
efficiently solve for minimum makespan in the classic PMSP[6].
Depending on how many additional resources are added and how these resources are
treated, the team found six version of the classical PSMP that can be classified as its
own type. Based on this the team found that there are three approaches to the classical
PSMP with additional resources: polynomial­time algorithms, exact approaches, and

heuristic/approximation approaches. The heuristic/approximation approach is out of
scope for this thesis, so it will not be discussed.
The team found that there exists polynomial­time algorithms, but many problems are
identified as NP­hard and that for NP­hard problems the research focuses on either
using an exact approach on special cases, or on approximation to find near­optimal so­
lutions.
Exact algorithms are rarely used in the literature but branch and bound and dynamic pro­
gramming were found to optimally solve several special cases. A branch and bound al­
gorithm was able to solve cases with 1000 jobs for one additional resource and with 100
jobs for more than one additional resource, in less than 10 seconds. The dynamic pro­
gramming approach solved knapsack sub­problems for each machine and afterwards
comparing the different solutions found. The team did not mention any cases for which
the dynamic programming approach was used.
For the machine environment, most studies involved in this paper solve versions of the
classical PSMP with more than three machines. The studies with two or three ma­
chines almost all prove the problem to be NP­hard and solvable in polynomial time. The
machines used in the classical PMSP are classified by three main classes: identical,
uniform and unrelated. With the inclusion of additional resources to the system a new
class is introduced: parallel dedicated machines, where sets of jobs are pre­determined
to be assigned a specific machine. This extension removes the job­machine assign­
ment sub­problem. Another class of the PMSP is: identical parallel machines. This will
be the focus of the next section.
Before moving on, I want to compare what has been covered so far with model 3.
Looking at this model and comparing to model 3, there are some key differences to
note. Model 3 does not have any constraint on the resources available. Cleaning crew
are assumed to have all the necessary resources available at all times, and there is
assumed to be no cap on the budget for cleaning the trains. For model 3 the jobs
being performed are also optional. The assignment of jobs happens when the jobs are
needed, not because the jobs need to be completed.
The overlap between the RCPMSP and the TCSP is in the way that both of these prob­
lems are approached. Both problems deal with unrelated parallel machines for which
some type of job is assigned. The jobs require a certain amount of time to be com­
pleted and the timing is tracked and used to evaluate the effectiveness of the model.
If the assignment criteria in model 3 was that a certain number of cleanings had to be
performed for each train, then the models would begin to look quite similar. The re­
source constraints in the RCPMSP would be an interesting addition to the TCSP. Right
now model 3 assumes that cleaning crew are available in infinite amounts at all times,
but that is not a realistic assumption, instead resources could be an amount of workers
available at certain times.

Parallel identical machine scheduling
Since the data used to run model 3 is split into several dataframes each containing data
for just one litra type, except for ABS and BK, though these are treated the same when it
comes to cleaning, the problem is similar to that of parallel identical machine scheduling.
In this extension on the PMSP all machines are able to be assigned any of the available
jobs and the processing time of the job does not depend on which machine it has been
assigned, just like the cleanings are not dependent on the specific train being cleaned,
when solved for only one litra type.

objective function they used was based on minimizing Lmax. To solve this problem
they design a branch and bound algorithm to find the optimal solution, using different
properties for what defines the optimal solution.

preference and the amount of resources available. It continues to do this until it runs

many resources that will free up. It then assigns the highest preference cleaning that is

doesn’t consider what is better long term.
The results from this model were able to improve on the real life schedule and was found

The problem tackled by the team could be argued to be a variation of the TCSP, in which
the preferred length of time between cleanings are defined as input for the model. This
approach is very different from the one I have presented for the TCSP, since in my
version the model is purely based on the cost and the dirtiness, i.e., the distance driven.
If it is assumed that the trains follow the same schedule with a cycle of one or two days,
then the approach used by Adenso­Dìaz et. al seems to be a good approximation to
real life, as is the case for the company in their article. However it will fall short if the
trains schedule has a cycle of perhaps one week or more, as the train might travel a
lot during some periods of its schedule and not a lot during other periods, before the
schedule repeats. Then it is not an easy task to set a preferred period of time between
each cleaning and the model is no longer applicable. There I see their model as solving
a simplification of the TCSP.
Given that the topic is on train cleaning scheduling this model is by far the closest one I
have found to the TCSP and it does provide some insight into how the problem can be
approached. The idea of minimizing the tardiness is again a main component of how the
model evaluates its solutions and I believe that it justifies my use of slack as a means
of evaluating the effectiveness of my model.
Since the team had a different problem to solve, I do not see much relevance in the
constraints that they have formulated, with regard to my own model. The use of a daily
budget could be an interesting implementation to the TCSP, but it would most likely need
to be redefined to be on a the scale of a weekly or monthly budget, to allow for more
flexibility in the model.

Analysis
In this chapter, I evaluate the effectiveness and reliability of the developed mathematical
model, model 3. Building upon the data provided by DSB, the solutions proposed by
the model are processed and analyzed, enabling me to better understand the problem
at hand and the performance of the proposed model.
The solutions are represented as binary columns, indicating when a specific type of

with respect to minimizing the distance between cleanings and ensuring that all trains

By thoroughly analyzing the data and evaluating the solutions, insight into the effective­

as the foundation for assessing the benefits of the approach used in this thesis and
its potential impact on DSB’s operations. Overall, the data analysis chapter provides a
comprehensive evaluation of the models performance and evaluates potential contribu­
tions to the field of automated planning for train cleaning.
4.1
Parameters
In the models there are several parameters that can be modified.
• Cleaning α­values: The amount of the train that each type of cleaning will clean.
• Limit: The desired distance driven between each cleaning by each train.
• Penalty: A cost added for each kilometer driven above the limit until the next clean­
ing is performed.
We consider that cleaning crew are paid an hourly rate of 135 DKK, based on informa­
tion from [10]. In reality this parameter is subject to change based on union agreements,
seniority, overtime and bonuses, but for the purposes of this thesis the hourly rate will
be kept constant for all cleaning crew employees.
The time limit is determined by the user. Note that when using the time limit in the
Gurobi solver, the time limit only starts counting once the model has been set up and
begins optimizing. This means that the time required to set up all the constraints does
not count into the time available based on the time limit.
A time limit of 300 seconds, has been used for the initial testing of model 3.

is to be expected as the model is now free to clean carriages individually as in model 1,
but the objective value is still too big to be realistic, so it is ruled out that this constraint
causes any problems in the model.
Having run the model several times to test different issues, it is found that the solutions
are all feasible and that without being able to find any reason as to why, the model does
not value the penalty as highly as the cost, which means that it assigns more d_slack
than expected. In order to fix this, the variable d_slack_tracker is removed and the
objective function is updated to include d_slack instead. Using d_slack directly in the
objective function means that when slack is applied several times in a row, the penalty
scales exponentially, since the first slack applied continues to be applied for every sub­
sequent use of slack. Additionally in order to further reduce the use of slack a limit is
set on d_slack, which limits it to be the same distance as the limit on d. A second slack
variable called d_slack_two is then introduced. This second slack variable will be used
after d_slack reaches its limit, however d_slack_two will have a multiplier of 10 in the
objective function, meaning that the penalty will increase ten­fold when above twice the
limit.

the limit. This is in line with how different trains travel different distances. For a train of

for a train of litra type B going 100 km past the limit only means surpassing the limit

limit, it is much more impactful for the train of litra type MGA and should therefore be
penalized more. By treating the limit as a goal for the median length driven by trains
between cleanings, and then tuning the penalty to allow a degree of freedom for going
above the limit, results might improve.

5
Conclusions
The conclusion will cover the findings in the Analysis chapter and then proceed to cover
some topics for future work.
The Analysis chapter has evaluated the effectiveness and reliability of the developed
mathematical model, based on the data provided by DSB. In the chapter the solutions
proposed by the model were explored and analysed with regard to their effectiveness in
comparison to the DSB solution. Additionally, the impact of different objective functions
and parameters on the models performance were touched upon.
The Parameters section defined the parameters that can be modified in the model.
These included, but are not exclusive to, the cleaning alpha­values, the limit, and the
penalty.
The chapter discussed the process of finding realistic values for the cleaning alpha­
values and the kilometer limit by analyzing the data from DSB. The cleaning alpha­
values were determined based on the assumption that each type of cleaning is stan­
dardized independently of litra type. The average ratio of ORs to TRs was used as a
baseline to evaluate the initial alpha­value for an OR. The kilometer limits were calcu­
lated for each litra type based on the median dirtiness for all carriages of that litra type
in the solution provided by DSB.
In the Testing section, the model was tested using different combinations of parameters
to determine their impact on the models performance. The model was run with varying
cleaning alpha­values and the results were evaluated to determine the most appropri­
ate values. It was observed that reducing the alpha­value for ORs brings the models
solution closer to the DSB solution in terms of which types of cleanings were assigned in
greater numbers. However, a very low alpha­value might not represent realistic cleaning
effectiveness for ORs and thus it was not reduced further after a certain point.
The section Reviewing the Model analyzed the models constraints and objective func­
tion to identify potential reasons for the overuse of slack. The model was updated to
include d_slack directly in the objective function to address the issue. Additionally, a
second slack variable d_slack_two was introduced in order to further restrict and con­
trol the use of slack. The results of the updated model with varying alpha­values and
penalty limits were discussed in order to find new appropriate values. This significantly
changed the results and re­calibration was performed alongside testing of the penalty
in the following section.
The section Tuning the Penalty examined the impact of different penalty values on the
model’s performance. It was observed that the penalty seems to have a significant
influence on the results, and finding appropriate penalty values is challenging. The
penalty was changed from its initial value for all litra types, to be litra specific. It was
then adjusted based on a user­defined acceptable distance travelled above the limits

set, in order to make the model penalize exceeding the limit more accurately. This lead
to much better results than the initial version of the model.
Finally, the section Tuning the Limit investigates the impact of reducing the kilometer
limits by 25% and increasing the penalty. The results indicate a strong correlation be­
tween reducing the limit and reducing the median and third quartile distances. It was
found that by reducing the limit to 25% below the wanted median value, the model was
able to return solution with medians closer to the values desired.
All in all, the model was not able to compete with the solution proposed by DSB. The
solutions proposed by the model were slightly cheaper than the one proposed by DSB,
however they were much less efficient in cleaning the trains compared to the cost in­
volved. The model was able to run all tests within a reasonable time frame, so it may still
prove useful after some more work. Some topics for future work are suggested below.
5.1
Future work
Many simplifications were needed in order to create a working running model within the
scope of 35 ECTS points for this project. There were many interesting and exciting
ideas discussed throughout this project, that would make for interesting additions to the
TSCP. Future work could focus on expanding upon the simplifications that were used in
order to make a working model 3, in order to create a more realistic version to be used
in real life cases.
With more time available I would suggest investigating model 3 further in order to fully
understand the balance between cost of cleaning and the penalty for exceeding the limit.
This seemingly small issue turned out to be very difficult to work with during analysis
and might have significantly impacted the tuning of parameters.
A simplification that has been touched upon in this project, that might benefit from ad­
ditional work in the future is the use of the time variable. The time variable has many
more uses than just allowing tracking of the cost of cleaning. It can be used to limit
when cleanings can be performed throughout the day, add extra cost to evening, night
or weekend shifts and can be used to track how many trains are cleaned at the same
time at each station. This could significantly improve the real life uses for this model.
Additionally future work could include the aspect of how much personnel is available for
cleaning at each cleaning station and how much personnel is required for each type of
cleaning. DSB has stated that at some stations additional personnel is available and
for an additional cost trains could potentially be cleaned faster than their regular times.
A very interesting addition to the model, which could still be solved within a reasonable
time frame, is the addition of cleaning crew on the trains that clean during train rides.
This would add a third type of cleaning and could be treated as a type of Travelling
Salesman Problem, where cleaning crew start their shift at one station and travel with

trains around the country only to end their shift at the station they started at.
Another aspect that could be considered in the future would be to use historical data
as input for the model. Right now all the trains start with 0 dirtiness, but in reality,
some trains will have just been cleaned, while others will have accumulated a signifi­
cant amount of dirtiness. Allowing the model to use this data from the previous schedule,
could potentially improve the solutions. It could also allow for the inclusion of unplanned
events, such as a passenger vomiting in a carriage. The extra cleaning performed after
such an event would simply be added to the input and the model would correct the new
schedule accordingly.
I believe that there is potential for this model to become a useful tool for the specific
problem of train cleaning scheduling, but there is still a long way to go. Others like
me that specialize in mathematical modelling could add and expand on this model in a
multitude of ways and turn it into a very realistic tool and others more skilled in computer
programming could improve my code to run significantly faster. I am yet to find a model
which attempts to solve this specific issue, so I believe that there is reason to continue
development until either the model is ready for deployment in real life or another model
is published.
