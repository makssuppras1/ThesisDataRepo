Abstract
The objective of this project is the study of machine learning applications within the ﬁeld
of shipbuilding market analysis. This is achieved by an extensive study of shipping market
litterature as well as a quantitative analysis and modelling of maritime data.
The shipping market study ﬁnds that, while shipping is an industry that knows drastic changes
in demand and high inertia in the supply of new ships, the growing ﬁeld of machine learning
seems to be widely under-utilized in the ﬁeld of shipbuilding demand forecasting. Addition-
ally, many academic studies use statistical methods to predict speciﬁc market aspects such as
sea cargo movements, but only limited attempts have been made to take a holistic modelling
approach of the global shipping market.
Motivated by these ﬁndings, the research takes a deep dive into shipping market indicators
available in the form of time series data and explores their potential for predicting the ship-
building demand by help of a classic time series modelling approach. Following up on these
elements, the eﬀorts are oriented towards developing a modelling framework that allows a
holistic approach for shipbuilding demand forecasting in combination with various possible
machine learning algorithms. This results in a code module developed in Python and avail-
able in an open online repository for potential future research in the ﬁeld. Thereafter, the
framework is applied and tested on diﬀerent time intervals, model architectures and machine
learning algorithms. In particular, this project makes use of ﬁve classic supervised machine
learning methods, namely linear regressions, random forests, gradient boosting, gaussian pro-
cesses and neural networks.
Finally, the forecasts of the shipbuilding demand obtained with the developped framework
are discussed in terms of quality and relevance to the shipbuilding industry. The discussion
observes that, while the absolute results yielded in this project can certainly not be considered
as groundbreaking in the ﬁeld of maritime forecasting, the general approach of machine learn-
ing and data science shows promising future developments as part of the digitization strategies
adopted by the shipping industry. In that sense, this project achieves its ambition of exploring
the application of machine learning to shipbuilding market analysis.
Lastly, it is important to note that, while machine learning is a broad ﬁeld which involves a
heavy underlying framework of statistics, mathematical modelling and computational science,
the objective of this project is not the study machine learning litterature. Accordingly, the
theoretical background of machine learning has been willingly kept to the strict minimum.
Instead, for a thorough theoretical approach, the interested reader is recommended the classic
book by Christopher M. Bishop, Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning [Bishop, 2009].

1.1
Shipbuilding Industry
1.1
Shipbuilding Industry
With regards to international trades, seaborne transportation remains the most eﬃcient, cost-
eﬀective and widely used method of transportation for most goods worldwide, and the Interna-
tional Maritime Organization (IMO) estimates that over 80% of the global trade is transported
by ship [IMO, 2021].
Many diﬀerent ship types operate in the global merchant ﬂeet, and the vessels are distinguished
by cargo types: bulk carriers for grains, coal, iron ore, steel or cement, container ships for
containerized cargo, crude oil tankers for unreﬁned petroleum, product tankers for reﬁned
petrolum products, etc... Currently, the global merchant ﬂeet consists of more than 90,000
ships capable of carrying more than 2,000 tons each, which represents a total capacity of more
than 2 billion tons [IHS, 2021].
The shipbuilding industry provides the merchant ﬂeet with new cargo ships of all sizes and,
accordingly, the sector is a heavy engineering business which requires substantial capital in-
vestment as well as a high level of technical and management expertise. Globally, the industry
is constituted of a network of shipbuilders and shipyards mainly located in industrialized ar-
eas such as Japan, Europe, South Korea and China, which also makes shipbuilding one of the
world’s most open and competitive enterprises. Indeed, considering the heavy investments
involved in the construction of a new ship, the shipbuilding industry is free of common trade
barriers such as distance, transport costs and tariﬀs [Stopford, 2008].
Many diﬀerent actors operate in the shipbuilding business.
A shipbuilder is a company,
person or location working with the construction of ships, and a shipyard refers to the
location where ships are built or repaired.
There are approximately 300 major shipyards
worldwide [Clarksons, 2021] and the largest South Korean yards employ up to 10,000 workers
[Stopford, 2008]. Engine designers such as MAN Energy Solutions play a direct role in equip-
ing the newly built ships in terms of propulsion and auxiliary power.
New ships are ordered for construction by shipowners to shipyards, which in the shipbuilding
business is called contracting. Contracting a new ship for construction is a long-term and
substantial investment for the shipowners and the main source of income for shipyards. As
a consequence of the major undertaking of building a cargo ship, the contracting process is
complex and the negotiations alone can take up to one year [Stopford, 2008]. Once a new
cargo ship is contracted, the construction process usually takes between two and three years
from the contract date to the delivery date. When a ship is then delivered by a shipyard, it
commonly operates for a period of 25 to 30 years. A consequence of these long lead times in
the shipbuilding industry is a high inertia in the supply of ships in the world ﬂeet [Volk, 1994].

1.2
Shipping Market Model
1.2
Shipping Market Model
From a macroeconomic perspective, global shipping is composed of four distinct but related
markets, namely the freight market for the trade of cargo transportation, the shipbuilding
market to trade new ships, the sale-and-purchase market to trade secondhand ships and, ﬁ-
nally, the demolition market to trade ships for scrapping [Stopford, 2008]. Each market trades
a diﬀerent commodity but, since shipowners operate in all four of them, their dynamics are
closely related and it is relevant to consider the shipping market as a whole when studying
shipbuilding [Gasparotti and Rusu, 2018]. This introductory section will therefore focus on
giving a holistic approach to the global shipping market with shipbuilding included.
Generally speaking, shipping is a sector operating all over the world and is therefore inﬂu-
enced by local and global economic trends [Stopford, 2008]. This inﬂuence can be illustrated
by analysing the ﬂuctuations in shipbuilding demand represented in ﬁgure 1.1. The ”ups and
downs” of the shipbuilding market can indeed be linked to some major historical events af-
fecting the global economy.
The post-war period from 1945 to the early 70’s presented an exceptional growth for the sector,
with a high demand due to a global lack of shipbuilding capacity. This period ended with the
1973 oil crisis which started a long-term recession that would last almost 15 years. Then, not
before 1987 did the industry regain strength and did the contracting levels increase durably
again, which coincided with a growing shipbuilding capacity needed to replace the old ships
constructed in the 70’s peak [Stopford, 2008]. The industry then experienced an alltime peak
right before the ﬁnancial crisis of 2007-2008, which was followed by a period of great volatility
and slow recession.
The economic factor in shipbuilding introduces the notion of external and internal inﬂuences
in the market. In econometrics, when describing an economic model, this is translated into
exogenous and endogenous variables. These variables diﬀer in the sense that the value of the
former is assumed to be determined outside the model and imposed on the model, whereas the
value of the latter is assumed to be determined by the model. In the case of the shipbuilding
market, empirical research has identiﬁed inﬂuence factors that aﬀect the market demand and
separates them into external and internal environments [Li et al., 2014].
In the internal environment of the global market, the shipping demand can be measured in
tons of shipped cargo, which is commonly multiplied by the average shipping distance in
order to get a measure in ton-miles.
It is also common to take into account operational
maritime transportation costs, which have been shown to have a strong impact on trade
[Korinek and Sourdin, 2009]. As to the shipping supply, it can be measured by estimating the

A fundamental concept in maritime forecasting is the notion of market cycles [Stopford, 2008]
and the shipbuilding demand cycles have been plotted in ﬁgure 1.3. In time series analysis,
a cycle is deﬁned as a sequence of ﬂuctuations repeated at an unﬁxed frequency in a series,
and this concept also applies to shipping time series. Economic cycles are present in many
industries and, in shipbuilding, three timespan levels of a cyclical behaviour can be identiﬁed.
The ﬁrst level is the long-term cycle, which can also be referred to as the ”secular trend”.
This level indeed indicates the trend within the sector i.e. whether the general tendency of
the business is to grow or to shrink over a period of 15 to 20 years. The second level is the
medium-term cycle, also called ”business cycle”, which is deﬁned over a shorter period of time
than the long-term level: approximately 5 to 10 years. The last level is the short-term cycle,
also referred to as ”seasonal cycle”, which in turn is deﬁned over a period of only 1 to 2 years.
Together, these cycles form a decomposing of shipbuilding demand.

Introduction
1.3
Maritime Forecasting
Statistical methods, such as regression analysis and time series analysis, are widely used in
maritime forecasting.
In 2005, Thien Le Duc suggested a model to forecast the dry bulk
market freight rates based on both methods [Thien et al., 2005]. Indeed, the model combined
multiple linear regression (MLR) with time series analysis to forecast bulk shipping freight
rates from 2005 to 2006. The model also took into account the dry cargo trade volumes and
the dry bulk ﬂeet. In 2009, Luo et al. presented a dynamic economic model to predict ﬂeet
capacity and freight rates in the container shipping market [Luo et al., 2009]. Their approach
used regression analysis to estimate the relationships of ﬂeet capacity and freight rates with
exogenous variables such as the change in international trade. Both publications represented
the shipping supply and demand balance using the ﬂuctuations in freight rates.
In 2017, Tsioumas et al. suggested the use of multivariate vector autoregression with exoge-
nous variables (VARX) to forecast the baltic dry index (BDI) [Tsioumas et al., 2017]. Their
results showed that the model could outperform a univariate autoregressive moving average
method (ARIMA), a classic time series forecasting method. In the same year, a study from
Wada et al. suggested a dynamic model for shipbuilding demand forecasting based on system
dynamics (SD) [Wada et al., 2017]. The uniqueness in their approach was to consider simul-
taneously complex relationships between sea cargo movement, contracting/deliveries of new
ships and scrapping.
Despite the ﬁelds of regression analysis and machine learning sharing popular algorithms such
as linear regression, very little mention of more elaborate machine learning methods is made
in the maritime forecasting litterature. Only in 2008 did a study from Akdemir et al. sug-
gest the use of artiﬁcial neural networks (ANN) to forecast the total world dry bulk ﬂeet
[Akdemir et al., 2008]. The study had promising results showing that neural networks could
outperform the more conventional regression analysis in terms of forecasting. To this date,
however, other publications involving advanced machine learning methods for maritime fore-
casting are unknown to the author of this project.
Two observations can be made from the above elements. The ﬁrst is that the growing ﬁeld
of machine learning seems to be under-utilized for forecasting in the shipping sector, despite
at least one study showing promising results [Akdemir et al., 2008]. The second observation
is that, as pointed out by Wada et al. [Wada et al., 2017], most studies have so far focused
on sea cargo movements, and only limited attempts have been made to take a holistic ap-
proach of the global shipping market. As an example, in Luo et al. [Luo et al., 2009], despite
the model being characterized by parameters like container shipping freight rates and the
global container ﬂeet capacity, several simplifying assumptions are made, notably ignoring the
impacts of the newbuilding, seconhand and scrapping markets on the container freight market.

2.1
Maritime Intelligence
Maritime intelligence is the business of information sharing for global seaborne transport and
trade [Lloyd’s, 2021]. The aim of this section is therefore to introduce the various maritime
intelligence sources used in this project and to give an overview of the available data covering
the diﬀerent aspects of the global shipping market. In particular, the focus is put on the

2.1.1
Data Sources
Two main data sources have been used for this project. The ﬁrst, IHS Fairplay, provides ex-
tensive information about ship characteristics, including unique IMO number, contract date,
delivery date, potential scrapping date and deadweight tonnage for each registered vessel. The
Fairplay register has been the industry benchmark since 1764 and is still nowadays a widely

Temporal Coverage
The temporal coverage refers to the time period during which a given market indicator is
available, and this period can vary signiﬁcantly from one indicator to another. The coverage
is characterized by respectively a start and an end date, before and after which the market
indicator is not recorded. This data property has to be distinguished from sparsity, which is
addressed later on in section 2.3.

the early 1950’s to most recent months. Typically, alternative fuel prices such as Liqueﬁed

Machine learning algorithms are not designed to handle missing data, and therefore the re-
stricted temporal coverage of some indicators will limit their possible usage in the implemented
models. Even if data science presents methods that would allow to ”guess” the missing values
of indicators outside their temporal coverage [Richman et al., 2009] [Tiwari, 2020], these impu-
tation methods are complex and considered way out of scope to be implemented in this project.
Still, to strike a balance between using enough indicators that make sense of the shipping mar-
ket and a suﬃcient timespan of data for training and testing the models, it has been decided
to mainly use the period from January 2000 to December 2020. Qualitatively, this decision
is supported by the idea that today’s shipping market is diﬀerent from the shipping market
30 years ago [Stopford, 2008], which is a statement that further analysis in this section also
supports quantitatively.

2.2.3
Stationarity
Stationarity is a central concept used in time series analysis to describe a type of statistical
equilibrium or stability in the data [Montgomery et al., 2008]. A series is called strictly sta-
tionary if its properties, such as mean and variance, are not aﬀected by changes in the time
origin. A series that has a trend and/or a seasonal component is called non-stationary, and
precisely these non-stationary components can deteriorate the quality of the forecast if not
accounted for in a model. Therefore, an accurate description of the stationarity of a series is
a safe step towards adapted forecasting models.
The stationarity of a time series can be evaluated with unit root tests. These tests check the
validity of a so-called null hypothesis whose deﬁnition and meaning can vary depending on
the model ﬁtted in the test. It is the validity (or not) of the null hypothesis that enables to
draw conclusions with regards to the series’ stationarity.
The tests used in this instance are the classic Augmented Dickey–Fuller test (ADF) and the
Kwiatkowski–Phillips–Schmidt–Shin test (KPSS). In the ADF test, the null hypothesis is that
the series has a unit root and its validity indicates that the series may be non-stationary.
In the KPSS test, in contrast to most other unit root tests, the alternative null hypothesis
is that the series has a unit root, and a valid null hypothesis indicates that the series may
be trend-stationary e.g. has an underlying trend that can be removed. The tests have been
applied to two version of the contracting series: the original version and a diﬀerenced version.
More details on diﬀerentiation are given in section 2.3.

3.1
Model Architectures
As observed in the previous sections, the shipping market can be decomposed into four
groups of indicators representing the market segments of demand, supply, prices and exter-
nal economic factors. This decomposition has been extensively researched in publications such
as [Wada et al., 2017], [Gasparotti and Rusu, 2018], [Li et al., 2014] or [Wheelen et al., 2017].
Given this setup, empirical research identiﬁed causal relations between indicators and market
dynamics such as shipping cycles in e.g. [Volk, 1994], [Stopford, 2008], [Kim and Park, 2017]
and [Lun et al., 2010]. Therefore, the idea behind this section is to explain how the resulting
macroeconomic model can be transposed to speciﬁc machine learning model architectures.
3.1.1
Autoregressive Models
In an autoregressive model, the variable of interest is forecasted based on the past values of
the variable. This means that the output of an autoregressive model can be represented as a
function f with lagged input features as in equation 3.1, where y is the time series to predict
and w is the parameter controlling the autoregressive order of the model.
yt = f (yt−1, yt−2, . . . , yt−w)
(3.1)
This concept is utilized in this project by estimating the function f with machine learning
algorithms.
However, while the classic autoregressive model uses a linear combination of
lagged features [Hyndman, 2018], a particularity of the autoregressive models implemented in
this project is that the algorithms used can be strongly non-linear.
3.1.2
Multiple Regression Models
In an autogressive model, the target variable is forecasted based on the historical values of
this one variable, but the concept can also be extended to multiple variables. This has been
represented in equation 3.2, similar to equation 3.1, with the notable diﬀerence that the inputs
are now observations of multiple time series.
yt = f (Xt−1, Xt−2, . . . , Xt−w)
(3.2)
The multiple regression thus deﬁned translates a fundamental idea behind this project. While
an autoregressive model imposes unidirectional relationships between the variables [Hyndman, 2018],
a multiple regression model allows multidirectional relations. In other words, this means that
the forecasted variable can be inﬂuenced by multiple other variables, which is more represen-
tative of the actual shipping market dynamics introduced in section 1. In continuation of this,
the following sections will further develop the idea of multiple regression by distinguishing
endogenous and exogenous variables in the models.

learning provides an extensive framework of algorithms to learn these relations.

estimator can usually be expressed by a simple arithmetic function. In the case of a time

Random Forests
As presented in [Breiman, 2001], random forest is an approach based on decision trees be-
longing to the algorithm class of ensemble methods. The idea behind ensemble methods is
to combine an ensemble of sub-estimators to build a meta-estimator that performs better
than a sub-estimator alone [Scikit-Learn, 2021]. In the case of a random forest method, the
sub-estimators considered are regression trees in which branches represent input data values
and leaves represent output data values.
Decision trees are often quoted among the most popular machine learning algorithms for their
simplicity and intuitiveness [Wu et al., 2008] [Piryonesi and El-Diraby, 2020]. For a visual and
intuitive introduction to decision trees, the author highly recommends the website from Tony
Chu and Stephanie Yee, A Visual Introduction to Machine Learning [Chu and Yee, 2021].
3.2.4
Gradient Boosting
As presented in [Friedman, 2001], the gradient boosting algorithm used in this project is again
an ensemble method based on decision trees but, in this case, instead of ﬁtting the regression
trees on random subsets as in random forest, the algorithm uses a loss function. This function
provides a gradient that optimizes the selection of the subsets used to ﬁt the regression trees.
In this project, the loss function is the least squares method commonly used in regression
analysis.
3.2.5
Gaussian Processes
In a gaussian process algorithm, the output is evaluated based on the similarity with the
training data. The similarity measure is deﬁned by a kernel function such as the multivari-
ate gaussian distribution in the present case. Gaussian processes have the advantage of being
versatile and applicable to many diﬀerent problems thanks to the ﬂexible choice of kernel func-
tion [Scikit-Learn, 2021]. Notably, gaussian processes have been revealed to be particularly
performant with multi-output problems [Chen et al., 2020b], and the application of gaussian
process has known an increasing interest within the ﬁeld of machine learning over the recent
years [Chen et al., 2020a].

3.2.6
Neural Networks
Neural networks, or artiﬁcial neural networks, denote a class of algorithms inspired by the
biological neuron structures in the brain. While many complex neural network structures are
available in the ﬁeld of machine learning, the present project will exclusively use the so-called
feedforward structure as neural network estimator in the models.
Neural networks make use of information processing units called neurons, and each neuron
is connected to other neurons by weighted connections [Herlau et al., 2019]. Neurons are or-
ganized in interconnected layers to process the information, therefore always including an
input and an output layer. Between these two layers can be included one or several hidden
layers inﬂuencing the complexity of the network and the propagation of information. The
feedforward structure is characteristic in the sense that connections between neurons never
form a cycle [Zell, 1995], so that the information always ﬂows forward from the input layer
to the output layer, which diﬀers from other classic NN structures such as Recurrent Neural
Networks (RNN) or Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN).
Neural networks provide the base for the very topical deep learning methods and belong
to the forefront of machine learning research [Camara Pereira and Rodrigues, 2020]. Given
these learning algorithms and the model architectures previously deﬁned, the next section will
describe how this setup can be used to forecast the demand of the shipbuilding market.

3.3
Prediction Method
3.3
Prediction Method
As described in section 1.3, maritime forecasting research is widely dominated by classic
statistical methods and seemingly little focus has been put on machine learning methods so
far. Therefore, this section will present the practical steps applied in section 4 to forecast the
shipbuilding market demand over diﬀerent time horizons with machine learning models.
3.3.1
Prediction Intervals
Based on internal MAN shipping intelligence [MAN-ES, 2021a], three forecasting periods have
been selected in this project. On the short-term, the prediction interval will have a range of
two years corresponding to a focus on monthly variations of the shipbuilding demand. In
particular, the overall ships contracted for construction will be forecasted from January 2019
to December 2020 included with adapted choices of data transformations described later on.
For the medium-term, the prediction interval will have a total range of ﬁve years in order to
focus on the quarterly ﬂuctuations in demand. Accordingly, the input and output data will be
adapted to this range to ﬁlter out part of the monthly variations which become less relevant
on the medium-term. More speciﬁcally, the demand will be forecasted from January 2016 to
December 2020 included.
Finally, on the long-term, the prediction interval will have a range totalling ten years. In this
case, the data will be ﬁltered even more to allow the models to capture the yearly variations
in particular. The start and end dates of the long-term forecasts will respectively be set to
January 2011 and December 2020.
3.3.2
Prediction Steps
Time series analysis provides various methods to predict values ahead of time [Brownlee, 2020].
In particular, two main methods can be distinguished wrt. the number of steps forecasted
ahead by the models, namely single-step predictions (SSP) and multi-step predictions (MSP).
In single-step prediction, the model is used to predict only one single step ahead of historical
observations, without reusing the predicted values. In other words, this means that predicting
over the intervals deﬁned above using an SSP method can be made only by inputting the true
historical observations to the model in each step.
This can be contrasted with the multi-step prediction method in which the feedback of pre-
vious predictions is reused by the models to continue the forecasts [Landassuri et al., 2013],
therefore limiting the amount of historical observations needed to forecast over longer intervals.
28
==PAGE:36==
3

3.3
Prediction Method
While MSP has the advantage over SSP to allow actual forecasts of the ”future”, it also has
the drawback of making the forecasts signiﬁcantly more unstable due to the propagation of
noise in the forecasted values. Therefore, with regards to the supplementary amount of work
involved in creating MSP forecasts as well as the limited timescope of this project, it has been
decided to perform and present exclusively single-step forecasts in this report. Nevertheless,
for future developments and interested readers, the framework described in appendix E also
includes an experimental but functional multi-step method.
3.3.3
Prediction Parameters
Accordingly with the data transformations described in section 2.3 and the inherent autore-
gressive model architecture described in section 3.1, the data input parameters for the models
will be (1) the smoothing window size s, (2) the diﬀerentiation order d and (3) the number of
lagged features w.
In terms of feature smoothing, the window size s will be considered for values ranging from
3 to 18 months trailing moving averages. This is based on the shipping cycles introduced in
section 1.3 that can be decomposed into short-, medium- and long-term cycles. Intuitively,
the more the smoothing window applied to the input features is large, the more informative
these features will be for long-term cycles, and vice versa for small window sizes.
The diﬀerenced features will be considered based on the stationarity analysis of section 2.2.3,
which concluded that the overall ship contracting series could be made stationary using a
ﬁrst order diﬀerentiation. Therefore, the diﬀerentiation order d will be considered with values
ranging from 1 to 12 months. In theory, the lower values should allow to remove other poten-
tial trends while the higher values should allow to remove potential seasonalies.
Based on the autocorrelation analysis performed in section 2.2.4, the number of lagged features
w to input will been considered for a range between 1 and 36 months of historical observa-
tions. Intuitively, the number of lagged features should be adapted to the prediction intervals
discussed previously, and the respective number of lags for short-, medium- and long- term
forecasts will be chosen accordingly in section 4.
Finally, the next section will deﬁne the error measures and scoring used to evaluate the quality
of a forecast and more generally the performance of a model, thus laying the ﬁnal brick to the
modelling framework developed in this project.

5.2
Modelling Improvements
The ﬁeld of data science oﬀers many techniques to improve the performance of machine learn-
ing models [Arabnia et al., 2020] and, therefore, this section introduces and discusses some
of the concepts that would be expected to yield the best modelling improvements for future
machine learning research applied to shipbuilding market analysis.
5.2.1
Feature Selection
In machine learning, feature selection refers to the process of selecting a subset of relevant
features for the model [Wikip´edia, 2021]. Therefore, in consideration of the varying informa-
tive values of the diﬀerent market indicators, feature selection appears as a safe step to take
towards improving the performance of the models with endogenous and exogenous features.
Among others, feature selection can have have the beneﬁt of simplifying the interpretation
of the models for researchers [James et al., 2013] since fewer input features are used. Ad-
ditionally, the process reduces training times and prevents overﬁtting. A simple and com-
putationally eﬃcient method to start with in this case could be sequential feature selection
[Herlau et al., 2019].
Following up on the observations made in section 4, feature selection therefore allows to select
the most informative features for each model. In other words, the process would also allow to
determine ideal smoothing window sizes, diﬀerentiation orders and number of lagged features
to use.
5.2.2
Hyperparameters Optimization
In machine learning, hyperparameters are speciﬁc parameters used to control the learning
process of a model, and their value can have signiﬁcant impacts on the performance of the
resulting models [Claesen and Moor, 2015]. In this project, hyperparameters to consider could
be related to both feature selection and learning algorithms.
As presented in section 3.2, learning algorithms also have hyperparameters such as the num-
ber of hidden layers or the choice of activation functions in the case of neural networks, and
these parameters are typically optimized using cross-validation [Bergstra and Bengio, 2012].
Generally speaking, hyperparameters optimization is a discipline of its own within the already
vast ﬁeld of machine learning.
Due to the limited timescope of the project, this report doesn’t address hyperparameters
optimization for demand forecasting. Instead, the hyperparameters for the learning algorithms

The main idea behind cross-validation is to partition the available dataset into multiple known
and unknown subsets to evaluate how a model’s performance generalizes to unseen data
[Stone, 1974] or, in the case of time series, to unknown periods of time.

as many independent subsets as possible. However, in the case of time series, where temporal
dependencies in the data are essential, it would be necessary to adopt a cross-validation method
that preserves the order of the observations. For instance, the models in this project could be

5.3
Development Perspectives
5.3
Development Perspectives
Just as in many other sectors, digital trends are shaping the future of the maritime indus-
try [MAN-ES, 2021b], and the global transportation system is evolving towards data-driven
integrated systems [Bhavsar et al., 2017]. Consequently, the increasing volumes of available
information are opening new possibilities for the marine sector such as big data, smart-ships
or artiﬁcial intelligence.
Big data is the ﬁeld that deals with handling and analyzing massive amounts of data and is
typically described by the three key concepts of volume, variety and velocity [Laney, 2001]. In
the transportation sector, complex data collection technologies and intelligent transportation
systems are reinforcing the exponential growth in terms of data availability [Khan et al., 2017].
Given this context, complex market modelling methods thriving on the use of massive amounts
of data are of increasing interest for the shipbuilding industry.
In this project, the volume and value of available data has been identiﬁed as a limiting factor
for the performance of the models and, from that perspective, big data opens new market
modelling possibilties in terms of machine learning. For instance, given the appropriate tools
and technologies, it is possible to imagine models including extended factors inﬂuencing the
shipping market such as political, legal, technological, environmental and socio-cultural inﬂu-
ences [Wheelen et al., 2017].
Moreover, the demand forecasting results in this project have shown the importance of ap-
propriately handling the input data in applied machine learning models. Notably, the use of
larger volumes of data is no guarantee for improved performances if the quality of the data is
insuﬃcient or if the models are not able to handle the supplementary inputs. In that sense, the
ﬁelds of data science and data engineering also have a major role to play in the development
of maritime forecasting.
Generally speaking, intelligent transportation systems are in increasing need for data infras-
tructures and systems which are able to handle large amounts of historic and real-time data
[Khan et al., 2017]. In this context, smart ships will open new machine learning possibilities
and challenges with regards to navigation, controlling and tracking [Dijk et al., 2018]. Fur-
thermore, as a subarea of artiﬁcial intelligence [Herlau et al., 2019], machine learning can also
be expected to play an important role for the fast growing ﬁeld of computer-aided decision
making in the shipping industry [Lindahl, Michael, 2018].

Conclusion
The objective of this project was the study of machine learning applications within the ﬁeld
of shipbuilding market analysis, which has been achieved by an extensive study of shipping
market litterature as well as a quantitative analysis and modelling of maritime data. Con-
sequently, this conclusive section summarizes the main ﬁndings and perspectives of the project.
From the market study, the main ﬁnding is the under-utilization of machine learning tech-
niques in shipping market forecasting. In particular, since shipping is an industry that knows
drastic changes in demand and high inertia in the supply of new ships, the research of new and
potentially better forecasting methods is fully justiﬁed. Therefore, assuming the increasing
availability of massive amounts of data, complex machine learning methods such as gaussian
processes and neural networks are expected receive growing attention in the ﬁeld of shipping
market analysis over the coming years.
With regards to the database analysis, the key takeaways from this project are the following.
Despite a large amount of available market indicators, many revealed to have an insuﬃcient
coverage or quality for proper training of advanced machine learning models. In parallel, even
if some market indicators are recorded back to the 1950’s and earlier, it hardly makes sense
from an economic perspective to train models on market data prior to 21st century. Indeed,
the digital trends in the maritime industry make today’s market hardly comparable to the
market that existed 20 years ago. Therefore, the data availability is for now considered a lim-
itation to the developement of machine learning learning applications for maritime forecasting.
In terms of actual forecasts, the results yielded by the models implemented in this project are
not considered suﬃciently performing to serve for market forecasting as such. However, the
added value of the implementations lies more in the general trends that could be observed.
Generally speaking, a rather simple model with limited input data is suﬃcient to evaluate the
long-term trends in the market, and more complex models are not guaranteed to yield better
results on that behalf. Instead, advanced machine learning models are expected to reveal
their true potential when applied to short-term or even real-time forecasts, which require the
ability to model complex and non-linear relations in massive amounts of data. Therefore,
this observation naturally goes hand in hand with the current digital trends observed in the
maritime industry.
Given that perspective, this project is considered to have achieved its goal not through the ob-
tention of groundbreaking machine learning results, but more through modest and argumented
observations with the intention of triggering the innovativeness of an interested reader.
